The estate sale people had come on Friday to price everything, and they had done a thorough job. Ruth saw that the moment she turned off the highway and pulled up the gravel drive: little orange stickers on everything visible through the front window, on the lamp with the amber shade that had stood beside her mother's chair for as long as Ruth could remember, on the mirror in the hallway that caught the light just so on winter mornings. The estate sale people had an eye for what things were worth, and they had priced accordingly, and now it was Saturday and the first customers were already walking up the porch steps before Ruth had even gotten out of her car.

She had driven four hours from Columbus and she needed to use the bathroom and she needed coffee and she needed, she understood now, to have had some other plan entirely. To have hired someone to be here in her place. To have donated the contents to a church or a Goodwill or simply had the place emptied by people she would never see and never have to thank.

But she had not done any of those things. She had driven four hours and here she was, and a couple in their sixties were already examining the birdcages on the porch—her mother had kept three canaries in the last years, yellow birds who had died before her mother died, in what order Ruth no longer remembered—and the woman in the couple was asking the estate sale manager, a solid woman named Connie who wore a money apron and had the practiced pleasantness of someone who did this every weekend, asking Connie whether she could take the birdcages off the porch hooks now or whether she had to wait.

"You can take them now if you're going to buy them," Connie said. "Just come see me when you're ready to pay."

Ruth got out of the car.

Connie had explained it all on the phone. How she and her two assistants would run the sale, manage the cash box and the card reader, keep people from pocketing things, make sure the flow through the house went smoothly. How Ruth didn't need to be there at all if she'd prefer not to be. Many families preferred not to be. Connie said this in a voice that made no judgment and offered no guidance. Many families preferred not to be was all she said.

Ruth had said she would be there.

She went up the porch steps and nodded at Connie and went inside.

The house smelled exactly as it always had. That was the thing she had not prepared for—not the orange stickers, not the strangers handling objects she had touched ten thousand times, but the smell, which was her mother's house and nothing else on earth, some combination of old wood and the particular soap her mother bought at the IGA and something underneath those things that was just the house itself, the accumulated weather of sixty years, the breath of old plaster walls. Ruth stood in the front hallway with her coat still on and felt the smell move through her like water finding its own level.

She went to use the bathroom. Somebody had put an orange sticker on the wooden toilet paper holder that her father had made in the garage sometime in the 1980s. Twelve dollars. Ruth stood looking at it and then washed her hands and dried them on the hand towel that was still there on the ring by the sink, a cream-colored towel with a crocheted edge that her mother had made herself decades ago and that had hung on that ring for so long it had the particular softness of something washed hundreds of times. She looked at it in the mirror. No orange sticker. She went back out.

In the kitchen, a young woman—late twenties, Ruth guessed, wearing overalls and her hair in a knot—was standing in front of the open cupboard where Ruth's mother had kept her good dishes, the Blue Willow set that had been a wedding gift in 1959 and that Ruth had eaten Sunday dinners from every week of her childhood. The young woman was turning a plate over in her hands, looking at the mark on the back.

"These are beautiful," the young woman said. She said it to no one in particular, or perhaps she meant it for Ruth, who was standing in the doorway.

"Yes," Ruth said.

"Do you know anything about them? The history?"

Ruth looked at the plate, which was white with the blue willows and the blue bridge and the little blue figures that Ruth had stared at through so many Sunday dinners while her mother and father talked about things that did not concern her. "They're Blue Willow," Ruth said. "It's a pattern. Very common."

"Oh." The young woman turned the plate over again. "Still. They're lovely." She said it without any reduction, without the slight deflation Ruth had expected after the word *common*. The young woman was right, Ruth thought. They were lovely. Thirty-five dollars for a full set of eight, service included, which was probably about right, which was probably what they would have cost when new.

"I think I'll get them," the young woman said, more to herself than to Ruth, and she began carefully wrapping the plates in the newspaper that Connie's assistants had left in stacks on the counter for exactly this purpose.

Ruth moved through the kitchen without touching anything. Out the back door and then back in again because the cold was sudden. Late October, and the yard was all fallen leaves, the maple her father had planted when Ruth was small now enormous and bare, its leaves matted on the ground in a dense copper layer that wanted raking and would not be raked. The owners who bought the house would rake it or not rake it according to their own lights. Ruth was done with the yard. She had been done with the yard for a long time, she supposed. You had to become done with things gradually, or else you didn't and then you were undone by them.

She went to the living room.

Two men were carrying the couch out the front door. It was a heavy couch, a rust-colored brocade thing that had been in the living room since before Ruth was born, that her mother had sat on to watch the news every night for forty years, and the two men were angled through the doorway with the concentrated effort of it, one of them saying to the other, *watch it, watch it, left a little*, and then they were through and down the porch steps and Ruth heard the sound of a truck tailgate lowering.

The room looked different without it. Lighter, somehow, and smaller. The rectangle of carpet where it had stood was a different shade from the rest, a little more saturated, like a photograph of the carpet rather than the carpet itself, and Ruth stood in the middle of the room looking at it until she became aware of a man in a canvas jacket standing near the fireplace, bending to look at the brass fireplace tools in their stand, the poker and the tongs and the little brush that her father had used on Sunday evenings in winter, banking the fire for the night. The man straightened up and looked at Ruth.

"You the family?" he said.

"Yes."

"Nice place," he said. "Good bones." He looked at the fireplace. "Your folks build it?"

"My parents bought it in 1961," Ruth said. "They didn't build it. It was already old then."

"Good bones anyway," he said, and bent back to the fireplace tools.

She watched him take them to Connie and pay for them—eight dollars, the sticker said, eight dollars for the tools that had leaned by that fireplace for her entire life—and then she moved to stand near the window and watched the room empty and fill and empty again, the way a tide pool empties and fills with creatures who have no idea where they are. An older woman who reminded Ruth of no one bought the standing lamp with the amber shade, and when she tilted it to unhook it from the outlet the light died and the room went briefly gray, the afternoon outside being overcast, the clouds flat and unanimous. Someone bought the end table. Someone else stood for a long time in front of the built-in bookshelves and then bought a small armful of paperbacks, mysteries, her mother had loved mysteries in her last decade, her eyes failing slowly and the large-print mysteries stacked beside her chair, and Ruth had kept up with her mother's reading in order to have something to talk about on the phone and had found she liked them too, the clean resolution of them, the way the world was finally explicable.

Her mother had died in March. A Tuesday, early morning, which was how these things usually happened, Ruth understood, the body choosing its own time without reference to anyone's schedule. Ruth had not been there. She had been in Columbus, asleep, and had gotten the call at six-fifteen from the overnight nurse at the hospice, a young man who was sorry for her loss and who had waited, he said, a decent interval before calling, wanting to let the morning come first. Ruth had not known what to say to that. She had thanked him.

The clearing out of the house had taken most of the spring and summer, Ruth driving down on weekends, sorting and bagging and donating in slow increments, working through her mother's closets and her mother's desk and the boxes in the basement that contained the evidence of a life—Christmas cards going back thirty years, saved rubber bands, a coffee can full of mismatched buttons, receipts for appliances that had long since been replaced, photographs of people Ruth could not name. Her cousin Deborah had helped for two weekends and then stopped returning calls. Ruth had not minded, or had minded only in the way you minded things that were also not surprising. She had continued on her own.

Now what remained was the furniture and the kitchen things and a few miscellaneous items in the bedrooms, the beds and the dressers and the nightstands, and Connie's people were managing all of that efficiently, directing traffic through the narrow hallway, keeping people moving. Ruth had not gone upstairs yet.

She went upstairs.

Her room had been the small one at the end of the hall, the one with the slanted ceiling that followed the roofline and the single window that looked out over the backyard and the maple tree. The bed was still there, a narrow twin with a white iron headboard, and someone had put an orange sticker on the headboard and another on the small dresser that had been hers. On the dresser's surface there was nothing. She had cleared it herself months ago, had boxed up the things that had accumulated there in the years after she moved out, the spare keys and the small bottle of perfume her mother had liked, the framed photograph of Ruth and her mother at a beach in—1987? 1988?—Ruth's hair enormous in the way of the decade and her mother smiling in a hat. The photograph was in Ruth's car now, wrapped in a sweater. She had taken it on one of the first trips down in April. She had known to take it early.

She sat on the edge of the bed. The mattress gave in the familiar way of old mattresses, the springs settling under her with a small soft protest. She had slept in this bed on visits home for twenty-five years after moving away. She had slept in it badly and then better as she got older, as the visits became what they were, finite and known, as she stopped being angry at the house for being small and her mother for being lonely and herself for living far away. The anger had taken years to pass through, longer than she had expected. On the other side of it was just this: a narrow bed and a window and the bare maple in the gray afternoon.

A child's voice from downstairs, high and particular, asking about something, and a parent's voice answering, and then footsteps on the stairs, and Ruth stood up before anyone appeared in the doorway. It was a woman about Ruth's own age, with a small boy of perhaps five who held her hand and looked at everything with the calm attention of children who have learned that interesting things are everywhere.

"Oh, sorry," the woman said. "I didn't know anyone was in here."

"No, it's fine," Ruth said. "Come in."

The woman moved to the dresser and looked at the orange sticker. The boy detached from her hand and went to the window and stood on his toes to see out.

"Is this the whole set?" the woman asked. "Dresser and bed frame?"

"I think so," Ruth said. "The mattress probably isn't for sale. I don't think they'd sell the mattress."

The woman nodded. She was wearing her gray hair cut close, and she had good posture, the kind you either had or worked for. "This is my daughter's furniture," she said, almost to herself, and then seemed to hear what she'd said and looked at Ruth. "I mean—something for my daughter. Her bedroom."

"How old?" Ruth said.

"Seven."

Ruth looked at the boy at the window. "That your son?"

"Mm. Leo." The woman called to him gently—*Leo, don't put your hands on the glass*—and he stepped back without complaint and came to stand beside his mother and looked at the dresser with the same level regard he'd given the window.

"It was mine," Ruth said. "When I was small. The dresser." She did not know why she said this. It was not information the woman needed.

But the woman received it simply. "It's good quality," she said. "You can tell."

"My parents bought it at a furniture store in town that isn't there anymore. They bought this whole room together, I think. As a set." Ruth put her hand on the top of the dresser, on the bare wood where the perfume bottle had stood. "My father drove it home in a borrowed truck."

The woman nodded. She did not fill the silence with anything, which Ruth appreciated.

"I hope your daughter likes it," Ruth said.

"I think she will. She likes white."

They went out together into the hallway, the woman calling for Leo who had already found the stairs, and Ruth watched them go down and then stood at the top of the stairs looking at the hallway below, at the mirror on the wall that had the orange sticker in the corner of the glass, and she saw herself in it: a woman in a gray coat, standing at the top of her mother's stairs, looking at herself looking back.

She came down.

In the kitchen the Blue Willow dishes were gone. The young woman in overalls had bought them and taken them out to her car, and the cupboard stood open and empty, the shelf paper her mother had put down—decades ago, white with a small yellow flower at the border—visible in a way it had not been in Ruth's memory, hidden always behind the stack of plates. Ruth looked at the shelf paper. It was very clean. Her mother had been a clean woman.

She found herself standing at the kitchen table, which had been sold but not yet carried out. The table was maple, solid and plain, the table she had eaten every meal at until she left for college. There were four chairs. The estate sale priced them as a set, and someone had apparently bought the set, because all four chairs had orange stickers with the word SOLD written across them in black marker. Ruth put her hand on the back of the chair that had been hers, that had been hers by custom rather than assignment, in the way families arrange these things without ever discussing them.

Connie appeared in the doorway. "You want some coffee? I've got a thermos out front."

"Please," Ruth said.

She followed Connie out to the porch where a folding table held the cash box and the card reader and a thermos and a stack of paper cups. Connie poured and handed it over and Ruth held the cup in both hands and looked out at the yard, at the people moving up the front walk with the purposeful leisure of Saturday morning, carrying their finds back to their cars.

"You're doing great," Connie said.

Ruth looked at her.

"Family members," Connie said. "It's hard. You're doing great."

Ruth drank the coffee, which was gas-station coffee, hot and bitter and good. "Does it seem hard to you?" she said. "From the outside?"

Connie considered this with the seriousness it deserved. She had been doing this for, she had told Ruth on the phone, seventeen years. Ruth imagined her moving through house after house across seventeen years of Ohio Saturdays, presiding over the dispersal of the things people had lived among, being professional and decent about it. "Sometimes," Connie said. "When people are present. You get a sense of it."

"My mother would have hated this," Ruth said. "She didn't like strangers in the house."

"Most people's mothers would have hated it," Connie said, without unkindness. "But they're not here to hate it."

"No," Ruth said.

She watched a man carry the kitchen table down the porch steps. He had a friend helping him, and they had it balanced on their forearms, and they moved with the careful economy of people who had moved furniture before, and the table went down the steps and across the yard and into the back of a blue pickup truck and the man climbed in after it and lashed it with tie-downs, working efficiently, done in a minute, a man with somewhere to be.

The maple tree stood in the backyard, bare against the flat sky. Her father had planted it when it was a sapling barely taller than Ruth was at age six, and now it was vast, now it cast a shadow over half the yard in summer. Her father had been dead for eighteen years. Ruth had spent a long time grieving for him and then the grief had found its place in her, the particular storage the body finds for things it has decided to keep, and he was there now, present and finished, the way the tree was present and finished. Not gone. Just no longer waiting.

Her mother had been different. Her mother had always been waiting for something, though Ruth had never been able to determine what. Perhaps just for Ruth to come home, which Ruth had done on schedule but never quite enough, never quite as thoroughly as her mother needed. She had spent years being not quite enough for her mother and had made her peace with it, or what passed for peace, the acceptance that you could not be different than you were and that loving someone did not mean fitting them perfectly. She had held her mother's hand in the hospice in February and told her—what had she told her? The truth, mostly. That she had tried. That she had thought about her mother every day of her life. That the sound of her voice on the phone was something Ruth would look for in every subsequent silence.

Her mother had been mostly asleep by then and Ruth did not know how much she'd heard.

There were fewer customers now, the afternoon thinning out in the way of estate sales, the best things gone, the browsers who remained picking through what was left with the calm diminished expectations of late arrivals. Connie was adding up figures on a clipboard. An assistant was carrying a box of unsold kitchen items to one side—things that would be donated, Connie had explained on the phone, anything unsold by four o'clock. Ruth watched the assistant move through the house with the box and she thought about the shelf paper in the kitchen cupboard, her mother's careful shelf paper, the little yellow flower at the border, which no one would buy and which would stay in the house until the house was sold and the new owners renovated the kitchen and pulled it out.

She went to get her coat, which she had left somewhere, and found it on the banister post in the front hallway. She put it on. The hallway mirror still had its orange sticker. Fourteen dollars. She found Connie on the porch and told her she would buy the mirror.

"Oh, sure," Connie said, without any particular register, and wrote it in the ledger and took Ruth's card and ran it through the reader. "Want to take it now?"

"I'll get it in a minute," Ruth said. "I need to get my car."

She went down the porch steps and across the yard to her car, which was parked in the gravel where she'd left it that morning. She sat in the driver's seat for a moment. Through the windshield she could see the house, the front porch, the two front windows, the maple tree in the backyard visible above the roofline. A woman she didn't know came out the front door carrying a reading lamp. Two children, bundled against the cold, ran across the yard with their father telling them to slow down.

She got out and opened the trunk, where she had a moving blanket she had brought specifically for this purpose. She went back up the porch and into the house and stood in front of the mirror. She peeled the orange sticker off the glass carefully, peeled it all the way off and folded it over on itself so it wasn't sticky anymore, and put it in her coat pocket. In the mirror she looked at herself—gray coat, gray afternoon, her mother's house behind her, nearly empty now—and then she lifted the mirror off its hook.

It was lighter than she expected. She carried it out the front door and down the porch steps and across the yard to her car, and she wrapped it in the moving blanket and settled it in the trunk and closed the trunk softly.

She went back up the porch and looked in the open front door. The house was still. Connie was counting the cash box at the folding table, her assistant moving through the rooms with quiet purpose, straightening what remained. The floors held the shapes of what had stood on them. The walls were bright where pictures had hung. The house was absolutely itself, Ruth thought, emptied down to what it had always been: wood and plaster and the smell of sixty years of weather, the light coming in the windows just as it always had, indifferent to what it fell on.

She said goodbye to Connie, who thanked her and said she would be in touch about the final figures.

Ruth drove back toward the highway. The town was small enough that she was through it in four minutes—the gas station, the IGA, the grain elevator, the water tower with the town's name on it—and then she was in the country, the fields flat and dark with turned earth on both sides, the sky the same gray it had been all day, and she drove with the mirror in the trunk and her mother's house behind her, growing smaller and staying exactly where it was.
