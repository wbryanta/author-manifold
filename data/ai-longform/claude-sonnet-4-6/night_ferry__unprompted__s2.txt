The ferry departed at eleven, which gave him three hours to sit in the plastic bucket seat near the stern lounge and watch the lights of the mainland recede into a single amber smear on the water. He had bought a coffee from the vending machine that tasted of the cup more than anything else and he held it in both hands not for warmth but for something to hold. The other passengers were few: a young couple who had already disappeared into one of the berth corridors, a heavyset man in a high-vis vest sleeping with his chin on his chest, two teenage boys playing something on a phone with the volume low enough that he could hear only the percussion of it, like distant gunfire. He watched the amber smear until it was gone and then he watched the black water instead, the way the ship's running lights caught the wave tops and turned them briefly white before they vanished back into the dark.

His name was Paul and he was fifty-one years old and he had not spoken to his brother in nine years. The last time had been a phone call that lasted four minutes, which he knew because he had looked at the call log afterward with a feeling he could not entirely name—relief, perhaps, or its more shameful cousin, satisfaction. His brother's name was Daniel and Daniel had been living, as far as Paul knew, or had been living as far as anyone knew, in a small fishing town on the northern island, in a rented room above a hardware store. This was the information the constable had given him over the phone in a voice that was professional and not unkind. There's a man, the constable had said. We believe he may have been there some time. We found this number in his effects. Paul had said, What kind of effects. The constable had paused and said, A wallet. A library card. Some correspondence. Paul had asked if there was a name on the library card and the constable said yes, there was, and told him what it was, and Paul had stood in his kitchen holding the phone and looking at the magnetic letters his daughter had arranged on the refrigerator years ago, long before she left for university, letters that still spelled out the word PLUM in the center of the door as if the refrigerator were making a small announcement about itself.

He had told his wife where he was going. She had listened and then said, carefully, Do you want me to come with you. He had said no, and she had nodded in a way that meant she understood the no to be several things at once, and he had packed a bag with one change of clothes and his toiletry kit and the folded printout of the ferry schedule, and she had driven him to the terminal without the radio on.

The ferry was called the *Sigrid Larssen* and it was not a large vessel. It smelled of diesel and cafeteria grease and underneath those things a deeper marine smell, salt and rot and cold metal, the smell of serious water. He had crossed this strait once before, decades ago, with Daniel, when they were young men going north for reasons that had seemed important then and which he could no longer reconstruct with any confidence. He remembered only that Daniel had stood at the bow rail for most of the crossing and Paul had stayed inside drinking beer, and that when they arrived Daniel's face had been wind-raw and his eyes had been bright with something that Paul had recognized even then as a species of happiness he himself did not possess and perhaps had never possessed. Daniel was two years younger but had always seemed, in some vertical sense, further along.

He finished the coffee and put the cup on the empty seat beside him. The ferry was making a low continuous vibration that he felt through the seat and through the soles of his feet and he found it, after a time, not unpleasant. The two teenage boys had stopped playing their game and were now talking quietly about someone named either Jamie or Janie, he could not tell which, and their voices had taken on a different quality, more careful, the way young people's voices got when they were talking about someone they were both in love with and neither would admit it. He looked away from them, toward the windows where the darkness outside was complete and occasionally interrupted by nothing.

He thought about the library card. Daniel had always been a reader, voracious and indiscriminate, the kind of person who read the back of cereal boxes and the small print on rental agreements and who had once, on a long car journey they had taken as children, read aloud from a repair manual their father kept in the glove box, narrating the torque specifications for wheel bolts as if they were poetry, which at the time had made Paul want to push him out of the moving car and which now, sitting on the ferry in the dark, made him put his face briefly into his hands.

He had not cried yet. He was not certain he would, and he was not certain what that meant about him.

The constable had said that the body—he had said the body, there had been no softening of it, no euphemism, which Paul had respected—had been found by the landlord when the rent went three weeks unpaid. The room had been locked from the inside. The constable did not say how the body had been found, meaning in what condition, meaning after what interval, and Paul had not asked because he had understood without asking that there would be a visual identification and that the nature of the identification might be complicated by time. He had looked at PLUM on the refrigerator. He had said, I'll come tomorrow.

The heavy-vis man was still sleeping. His breath came in long slow intervals and once he made a sound that was not quite a snore, more like a question without words. The teenage boys had gone quiet. Paul got up and walked the length of the passenger lounge, past the vending machines and the unstaffed service counter with its laminated menu, past the door to the berth corridor, and out onto the rear deck. The cold was immediate and serious. The wake stretched back in a white road that narrowed to nothing. He stood at the rail and gripped it and felt the cold come through his palms and he looked at the wake for a long time.

He and Daniel had shared a room until Paul was twelve and Daniel was ten, and in that room, in the dark, Daniel used to talk. He talked about things he had read, things he had thought about, things he had noticed and which no one else had apparently noticed or considered worth discussing. He talked about how the second hand on their alarm clock moved in steps rather than smoothly, each second a distinct jerk forward, and how he found this disturbing because it meant that inside the clock there was a mechanism whose entire purpose was to stop as much as to go, that stopping was built into the heart of timekeeping, which seemed to him wrong somehow. He talked about a boy at school who collected the foil wrappers from cigarette packs and kept them folded in a tin and who, when Daniel had asked why, had said he was saving them but could not say for what. He talked about their father's hands, which were large and very still when the man was sitting, as though they belonged to a different, more patient person than the one he usually was. Paul had mostly fallen asleep to this talking. Occasionally he had said shut up, Daniel, and Daniel had, for a few minutes, and then resumed. He thought now that this was perhaps the greatest intimacy he had ever been permitted with another person: to be talked to in the dark by someone who did not require response, only presence.

The wake was still white and still narrowing. He went back inside because the cold had become something he was fighting rather than simply feeling, and there was a difference.

He considered getting a berth but decided against it. Sleep seemed beside the point tonight. He sat back down in his bucket seat and pulled his jacket tighter and looked at the windows, which were now showing him his own reflection, a middle-aged man sitting alone with no book and no phone in his hands, which struck him as either honest or slightly pathetic, he couldn't decide. He looked like someone waiting for something without certainty that it would come, which was perhaps an accurate description of what he was.

Nine years. Before the phone call that lasted four minutes there had been a longer silence, and before that silence there had been an argument, and before the argument there had been a series of smaller arguments, and before those there had been years of a relationship that had been conducted mostly by its failures, by the missed occasions, the birthdays noted but not marked, the news that arrived late and secondhand. He and Daniel had not grown apart so much as they had simply grown, and the directions had not been the same. Paul had made a life that was organized and warm and populated with people who relied on him and whom he reliable in return. He had a job that was not interesting but was solid, an accountancy practice that he had built himself over twenty years, that provided for his family and that required of him a kind of careful attention he had turned out to be good at. He lived in a house with a garden. His wife's name was Helen and she was a person of genuine virtue and she loved him, he believed, in a way that was durable if not spectacular. His daughter was called Mira and she was studying architecture in another city and called him on Sunday evenings with reports of her life that were cheerful and selective, as children's reports always are, and he loved her with a thoroughness that sometimes startled him.

Daniel had made no such life, or had made different versions of one repeatedly, none of them lasting. There had been a period in a city in the south, something to do with music, Paul had never been entirely clear on the specifics. There had been a woman—there had been several women—one of whom Paul had met once, at their mother's funeral, a small dark-haired woman with serious eyes who had stood at Daniel's shoulder and said very little, and Paul had thought at the time that she was the right person for his brother, and then three years later Daniel had mentioned in passing that she had gone. There had been a period of difficulty that Daniel had referred to, in the four-minute phone call, as his troubles, which was the word their mother had used for such things, her people's word, the northern word that put a roof over the unspeakable and let you live under it. Paul had not known what to say to troubles and so had said something brisk and practical about resources, about getting help, about the number of a service he had looked up in advance of the call, and Daniel had been quiet for a moment and then had said, Yes, right, and the call had ended shortly after. Paul had written down the number of the service on a piece of paper and put it in a drawer.

He had taken the piece of paper out of the drawer this afternoon, before Helen drove him to the terminal. It was still there. The number was still on it. He had looked at it for a long time and then put it back.

The ferry moved on through the dark. He could feel the sea becoming more serious, a longer swell beginning to assert itself as they got out from the shelter of the mainland coast, the ship's movement changing from a shallow rocking to something with more patience in it, a great slow pendulum. He was not a good sailor and he kept his eyes on the fixed middle distance and breathed carefully and after a while his body accepted the motion and stopped objecting.

He tried to think about what identification would require of him. He had identified a body once before, their mother, in a hospital in the town where she had lived, a room that was very clean and very quiet, and the experience had been unlike what he had expected in that it was entirely unlike representation—unlike films and unlike the idea of it he had carried all his life—it had been simply his mother, smaller than he remembered her, and very still, and that was all. She had looked like a person who was doing something private. He had said yes, that's her, to the attendant, and the attendant had made a small mark on a form, and Paul had walked out into the hospital corridor and stood there for a moment and then walked to the car park, and that had been that. It had been simple in the way that irreversible things sometimes were.

He did not know if Daniel would be simple. He did not know what time did to a person, in a locked room, in the north.

He thought about Daniel at the bow rail, wind-raw, eyes bright. He thought about Daniel reading the repair manual aloud in the car. He thought about Daniel at eighteen, at twenty, at thirty, the way the shape of a person changes and stays the same at once, the way you can always see the child in a middle-aged face if you have known the child, the continuous thread of the person running through all their iterations. He did not know what Daniel looked like now. He had a photograph from fifteen years ago, and he had the continuous thread, and perhaps that would be enough.

After a time he slept without meaning to, and dreamed with unusual clarity: he was in the childhood bedroom and Daniel was talking in the dark, but in the dream he could not make out the words, only the tone of them, which was neither happy nor unhappy but interested, deeply interested, in the way that Daniel had always been interested in things, the way some people are interested in the world as a matter of primary orientation, as a basic fact about themselves, and he was lying in his bed listening and he felt the particular comfort of having somewhere to be that did not require him to be anything other than horizontal and present, and then the dream changed without changing, the room becoming at once the room and the ferry and the dark outside becoming the same dark, and Daniel's voice continuing, talking about the second hand on the clock, about how the stopping was built into the heart of timekeeping, and Paul tried to listen more carefully, tried to hold on to the words, but they moved away from him the way words in dreams do, the meaning running out of them as you reach for it.

He woke to the overhead lights coming on and the PA system making a sound before the voice came through to say they were forty minutes from the port. The high-vis man was awake and eating something from a paper bag. The teenage boys were gone. Paul sat up and put his feet on the floor and looked at his hands on his knees for a moment, the way his father's hands had looked, large and still, belonging to a more patient person. He could see the lights of the island through the windows now, scattered and amber, not so different from the mainland lights that had disappeared behind him in the night except that they were closer and more distinct, each one a specific place.

He went to the bathroom and ran cold water over his face and looked in the mirror above the sink. He looked like a man who had slept in his clothes, which he was, but he also looked like something else, something he examined for a moment without quite arriving at a name for. There was something around his eyes. He could not tell if it was grief or its absence and he stood there long enough that the automatic light turned off and he was briefly in total darkness, and he did not move, and then he moved his hand and the light came back on and he was still there.

The ferry made the port with a grinding of engines going into reverse and then the long soft collision with the dock, the ropes going out, the whole vessel shuddering once and then going still. He walked down the vehicle ramp behind the high-vis man and out into the cold of the port, where the sky was beginning to grey at its eastern edge with a light that was not yet light but was the idea of it, the suggestion. The town was small and quiet and smelled of fish and diesel, the same diesel as the ferry, as if the whole north ran on a single engine.

The constable's office was a ten-minute walk from the dock according to the printout in his jacket pocket. He did not check it. He walked through the quiet streets and his footsteps were loud on the wet pavement and the grey light grew incrementally and a gull crossed overhead without making any sound. He passed the hardware store and he looked up at the windows on the first floor without meaning to and saw nothing, curtains, a windowpane with condensation on the inside. He kept walking.

He was thinking about what he would say. Not to the constable, he had the words for the constable. He was thinking about what he would say to Daniel, if it was Daniel, after the identification and the form-signing and the arrangements, all the machinery of it—what he would say in some private moment that he would make, or perhaps didn't need to make, perhaps would simply find, in the way that you find yourself somewhere without entirely having decided to go there. He did not know if you spoke to the dead. He had not spoken to their mother. But he and his mother had been, by the end, at peace with one another, or near enough, and he and Daniel had not been anything, they had been a silence, and silence, he thought, was something you could still do something about. Even now. Especially now, maybe, when the silence would have to change shape to continue.

He would say: I got the ferry.

He would say: I came.

It was not nothing, he thought. It was perhaps not enough, but it was not nothing, and he had learned over fifty-one years to make a careful distinction between those two things, and to understand that the distance between them was sometimes smaller than it appeared.

The constable's office had a light on inside. He stood outside it for a moment on the wet pavement with the grey light coming up around him and the gull somewhere nearby, crying now, and he thought of Daniel at the bow rail, wind-raw, looking out at whatever it was he had been looking at, happy in his vertical way, further along. He put his hand on the door.
