The smoke came up from the Kessler drainage at ten past two, a thin gray finger at first, the kind that could be a campfire or a burning barrel or someone's lazy afternoon with a pile of deadfall. Maren watched it through the Osborne firefinder the way she had watched every anomaly of light and haze that summer, her chin hovering above the brass fittings, her eye traveling the crosshair to the ridge. She took a bearing: two hundred and twelve degrees. She wrote it in the log. She looked again.

By two-fifteen the column had thickened at its base.

She knew the drainage. She had hiked into it twice in three years, not because the terrain demanded it but because she permitted herself a kind of surveillance from a distance, a tenderness she could only practice when she was certain no one would see her practicing it. The drainage ran south-southwest off the back of Pinecrest Ridge and opened into a wide bench that caught the afternoon sun and held it the way a cupped hand holds water. Ruth had chosen it for that reason, or so Maren had assumed, because Ruth had always been drawn to light the way their mother had been drawn to trouble — instinctively, without apology. The cabin sat on that bench. A Forest Service permit, a one-room structure with a sleeping loft and a woodstove and, Ruth had told her once in a letter, a porch where you could watch the elk come down at dusk. She had never been invited to see the porch. She had never asked.

Maren stood back from the firefinder and looked at the smoke with her naked eye. It was the color of dirty wool now, and it was moving, pulling east with the afternoon drainage wind. She felt the knowledge arrange itself inside her chest before she was willing to think it plainly: that was not a campfire. Campfires in August went white or transparent with the heat. This was a structural fire or a ground fire with material — lumber, furniture, something with density and memory.

She crossed to the radio and lifted the handset.

The static was the same static that had been answering her all morning. She had reported it at eight a.m., had run through the diagnostic checklist twice, had climbed out onto the catwalk to look at the antenna though she knew the problem wasn't the antenna, the problem was somewhere in the repeater system on Bald Peak that the district office had been meaning to address since June and had not addressed. She had filed the maintenance request. She had been told someone would come up before the end of the week. It was Wednesday.

She keyed the handset three times and listened. Nothing resolved out of the white noise. She set the handset down with a care that surprised her, as though it were breakable, as though what she needed to do next required a certain deliberate gentleness with objects.

The phone was a landline, a relic the district kept operational out of what seemed like institutional nostalgia, and it had gone out in the third week of July during a lightning storm that had hit the ridge with enough voltage to blow two breakers and leave a char mark on the outside wall the shape of a splayed hand. Maren had reported that too. She had been offered a satellite phone as a replacement and had been waiting for it since the first of August, which was two weeks ago, which was now relevant in a way it had not been before.

She stood in the center of the cab and let herself feel what she was feeling, which was something she had learned to do in the years after her divorce and before she had taken the first lookout posting, when a therapist in Missoula had told her that naming the sensation was the first step toward not being governed by it. She felt afraid. She felt a specific variety of afraid that had to do with distance and with helplessness, the fear of watching something happen without the ability to alter its course. She had felt this before, at sixteen, standing outside her parents' bedroom door listening to her mother cry with a sound like tearing fabric while Ruth, who was twelve, sat at the kitchen table doing homework with a concentration so fierce and so false that Maren had wanted to shake her and hold her at the same time.

She checked the smoke column again. It was not larger. It was not smaller. It was the same gray-brown pillar maintaining itself against the blue August sky, steady as a declaration.

Maren had a bicycle. The district did not require her to have one, but the previous occupant of the tower — a retired biology teacher named Harold Fitch who had done fourteen seasons — had left it chained to one of the support legs, and when she had inquired about claiming it, the district had told her to do whatever she liked with Harold Fitch's bicycle, as Harold Fitch had not been heard from since he'd driven away at the end of his last season with a wave out the window of a pickup loaded with houseplants. The bicycle was a mountain bike, olive green, with tires that Maren had kept inflated because there was little else to do at certain hours and the small discipline of it had pleased her. She had ridden it twice on the service road, testing the brakes, learning which stretch of the switchbacks would require her to walk it. She had not ridden it down the mountain, not all the way, not the eight miles of loose dirt and embedded rock and the two stream crossings where the road washed out each spring and was only partially restored.

She did not let herself deliberate about this. Deliberation, she had found, was what you did when you wanted to arrive at the same answer you had started with but needed the scenery of a decision. She changed into her trail shoes. She filled both water bottles and put them in the pack along with the first-aid kit, which felt like either practicality or an admission she was not prepared to articulate. She wrote in the log the time and the bearing and the estimated column height and the notation *radio non-operational, telephone non-operational, proceeding to investigate/render assistance, road departure 14:31.* She took the binoculars and went down the stairs.

The road began at the base of the tower as a two-track through open rock and lodgepole and descended the north face of the ridge in a series of switchbacks that she had walked many times, going up to the trailhead and back in the evenings when she needed to hear something other than her own thinking. In good weather the walk was forty minutes. On the bicycle the descent would be faster, controlled primarily by how much she was willing to trust the brakes, which she trusted in the way she trusted most things — provisionally, and with a portion of her attention held in reserve.

She rode. The first switchback was the worst, a tight left with a drop on the outside edge where the roadbed had crumbled away to reveal the gray subsoil, and she took it wide and felt the rear tire slip on a patch of decomposed granite and held herself steady through the instinct she had developed in forty-three years of being a body in motion through unreliable terrain. After that the road opened slightly. The trees came in close on both sides, their shadows making a strobe of the afternoon light. She could feel the speed in her sternum, the specific vibration of fast travel over bad ground.

She thought about Ruth.

This was not new. She had spent the summer thinking about Ruth in the methodical way she had once prepared for difficult conversations, rehearsing positions, testing arguments, abandoning them and beginning again. They had not spoken in two years. The silence between them was of the particular kind that forms not from a single rupture but from an accumulation of smaller failures, each one individually manageable and collectively impossible. There had been the summer of their mother's illness, which had lasted eight months and ended in a rehabilitation facility outside Billings, where their mother had spent her last three weeks staring at the window with an expression of such concentrated disappointment that Maren had not known whether she was disappointed in them or in herself or in the view. Maren had come in August. Ruth had come in June and again in September, missing the death by four days, arriving to a room already stripped of personal effects. She had not blamed Maren for this, at least not in words. She had blamed her in the way people blame each other for things that no one could have controlled, which is to say she had been very precise and very quiet about everything else.

Then there had been the question of the property, their mother's house in Deer Lodge, which was not worth arguing over and which they had argued over for seven months with the assistance of two attorneys and a real estate agent who had called Maren once in tears and said she had never in thirty years of real estate seen sisters behave this way. Maren had accepted this assessment. She thought it was probably accurate. They had behaved with a focus and a terrible efficiency, the way people behave when property is not the thing they are arguing about.

She crossed the first stream at speed, felt the water against her ankles, kept moving.

The thing they had been arguing about was too old and too shapeless to be called a thing. It was more like a weather system, a pressure differential that had existed between them as long as Maren could remember, which was as long as Ruth had existed in the world as a fact that required Maren to reorganize her understanding of herself. Ruth had arrived when Maren was four and had proceeded to be a different kind of person in the same family, which should have been ordinary and was instead somehow unbearable in ways that shifted form over time. As children it had been a competition for a mother who was too distracted to be won. As adults it had become something harder to name, a mutual watching, a vigilance that looked like indifference from the outside and from the inside felt like a prolonged act of holding one's breath.

Maren came around a long curve and stopped.

The road ahead was blocked by a tree, a lodgepole of medium diameter that had come down sometime in the last week across the full width of the two-track, its root ball a dark tangle of earth and rock on the uphill side. She got off the bicycle and walked to it and stood there looking at it the way she looked at the smoke that morning, letting the information arrange itself. The tree was too large to lift. The branches on the uphill side were dense enough that she could not push the bicycle over without risk of fouling the wheels. She could go around through the timber on the downhill side, where the drop was manageable, fifteen or twenty feet of slope before the ground leveled again into the road's continuation.

She lifted the bicycle over the roadside edge and worked it down the slope, holding the frame, feeling the dirt give under her trail shoes, the dry August soil sliding in small cascades around her feet. She got the bicycle to the road below the tree and got back on and kept going.

The smoke, when she could see the sky through the trees, had changed. She caught glimpses of it between the canopy, darker now, more substantial, no longer a column but something more lateral, spreading east with increased urgency along the ridge. She registered this and filed it in the part of herself that was still doing the work of a lookout and kept pedaling.

She had not seen Ruth in three years. The last time had been at a mutual friend's wedding in Missoula, a brief, too-bright encounter at the reception where they had each performed warmth for the benefit of the other guests and then stood beside the dessert table in silence eating the same lemon cake with the focused attention of people who needed something to do with their hands. Ruth had looked thin. She had been living in the cabin for two years already by then, teaching online, she said, doing some consulting work she had been vague about. She had cut her hair, which had always been long, their mother's hair, and the shortness of it made her face look exposed in a way that Maren had found hard to look at directly.

*You could come up sometime,* Ruth had said, the cake fork tilted at an angle in her hand. *In the summer. It's good in the summer.*

*I know,* Maren had said, meaning she knew it was good in the summer, having seen it from above, having taken the bearing, having written the coordinates in no log but kept them in the way you keep something you are not ready to use. She had not said that she had been coming up, in a manner of speaking. She had not said anything useful. She had said she might do that, someday, and had watched Ruth nod with the expression of a person absorbing a refusal that they had expected and that still cost them something.

The second stream crossing was worse than the first. She hit it wrong and the front tire caught on a submerged rock and she went over the handlebars into six inches of water and landed on her hands and knees in the streambed with the bicycle on top of her. She lay there for a moment with the cold water running over her wrists. A dipper worked the far bank, oblivious. The water was very clear and she could see the individual stones on the bottom with a clarity that seemed excessive, almost accusatory, each pebble present and distinct in the way that things become present and distinct when you are lying on the ground.

She got up. She checked her hands — scraped, bleeding mildly at the left palm — and her knees and her face, which had not hit anything. She lifted the bicycle and walked it through the rest of the crossing and mounted again on the far bank, breathing hard, her wet clothes immediate against her skin in the afternoon heat.

She was thinking about what Ruth had put in the cabin. She was making a list without meaning to, the way the mind catalogs what might be lost in order to reckon with loss before it arrives. The sleeping loft. The books Ruth collected with the indiscriminate enthusiasm of someone who had grown up in a house with too few of them. The woodstove, which was not sentimental but which was integral. The porch. Whatever was on the porch — a chair, a table, the particular quality of light Ruth had described in the one letter Maren had kept, the only letter in which her sister had sounded like herself rather than like someone performing the neutral transaction of information, in which she had written *the light in the evenings is the kind of light that makes you feel the day was worth something even if it wasn't,* and Maren had read it three times and sat with it and put it in the drawer where she kept her passport and other documents of essential identification.

She came out of the trees at the trailhead parking lot, which was empty except for a green Subaru that she recognized from the two times she had seen it, once at the wedding in Missoula and once, she now admitted to herself, from the cab of the tower through the binoculars on a clear morning in late June when she had watched it come up the road and park in what she supposed was a kind of ritual proximity, the closest she had permitted herself.

She left the bicycle against the trailhead sign and started up the trail at a run.

The trail into the Kessler drainage was two and a half miles, steep for the first mile and then gradual through a series of meadow openings before the final drop to the bench. She had run trails enough to know her pace and she ran her pace, steady, economical, saving something for the unknown event she was running toward. The smoke smell reached her on the wind before she cleared the first ridge, a specific smell, not just wood smoke but something synthetic in it, something that confirmed what she had been unwilling to confirm until now.

She came over the ridge and saw it.

The cabin was not gone. This was the first thing she established, scanning from the ridge with the binoculars, and the relief of it was so physical it felt like being struck. The cabin stood. The fire was in the structure behind it — a small outbuilding, a shed, entirely involved now, burning with the bright orange competence of a thing fully committed. The wind was pushing the fire east and away from the cabin, and the ground around the cabin was rocky and poor, not the kind of ground that carried fire easily. The cabin was not gone.

Ruth was standing in the yard.

Maren could see her through the binoculars from three hundred yards away, a small figure in a dark shirt standing at a distance from the burning shed with her arms at her sides, and the posture of her — the particular stillness of it, the way she held herself in the face of the burning — was so familiar, so devastatingly recognizable from decades of watching her sister absorb things without moving, that Maren felt something rupture quietly in the center of her chest.

She ran the last section of trail without the binoculars, letting the footing demand all her attention. She came down off the trail and into the meadow above the bench and crossed the meadow at full speed and came through the fence gap that marked the edge of Ruth's property and stopped.

Ruth turned.

They looked at each other across thirty feet of August grass. The shed burned behind Ruth with a sound like sustained applause. The smoke column, Maren's smoke column, two-twelve degrees, column height approximately sixty feet, the thing she had been watching all afternoon from above, rose between them and the sky.

Ruth's face was doing several things at once. Her face had always done this — registered multiple contradictory weathers simultaneously, which was something Maren had cataloged as a child and then stopped attending to as an adult because it required too much of her. Now she attended to it. She stood in the grass and she looked at her sister's face and she attended to it.

"The radio's out," Maren said. This was not what she had meant to say, but it was true, and it established the reason for her presence without requiring her to establish the other reasons, the ones she had been carrying up the mountain for three years.

Ruth looked at the shed. She looked back at Maren. "I know," she said. "I called the district on my sat phone an hour ago. They're sending a crew up the back road." She paused. "An engine. Maybe two."

Maren absorbed this. "You're okay," she said.

"I'm okay. It was the wiring in the shed. I thought I'd put it out but—" Ruth stopped. She gestured at the burning structure with a motion that said everything necessary about the distance between what we think we have managed and what we have managed.

Maren looked at the cabin. At the porch, which had a chair and a small table on it, and a coffee mug on the table that had probably been sitting there since morning. At the elk-gnawed fence posts along the property edge and the light on the meadow grass, late afternoon light now, the kind Ruth had written to her about, the kind that made the day feel worth something.

"I got here as fast as I could," Maren said. She meant this to cover more than the afternoon.

Ruth was quiet for a moment. The shed made its sound. An ember rose and went east and disappeared.

"I know," Ruth said. "I know you did."

They stood in the grass in the smoke-altered light and watched the shed burn down to its frame, which was all it could do now, all either of them could do, and the crew would come up the back road and manage what remained to be managed, and the smoke column would thin and resolve and eventually, by evening, be nothing, an absence in the sky where the smoke had been, over the drainage, over the bench, over the place where Maren was standing two feet from her sister without touching her and feeling the distance between two feet and touching as something that could, possibly, be crossed.
