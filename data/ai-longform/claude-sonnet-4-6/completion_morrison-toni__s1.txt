He kept his pistol loose in its holster and his eyes moving, noting the angle of light through the canopy, the quality of silence beneath the oaks and hickory, the way Regina's ears swiveled and settled. She was a better instrument than any compass. When her ears went flat he slowed; when they swung forward, curious and unafraid, he nudged her on. The trail was old enough that roots had grown across it in ridges, and in places the undergrowth had crept inward until only a narrow corridor remained, enough for a man and horse but not much more. Jacob Vaark was not a large man but he had learned to sit a horse as though he were, shoulders back, chin level, a posture that had saved him from trouble on at least three occasions he could count and likely others he could not.

The Lenape had walked this ground for time beyond memory, and though they were mostly gone from this particular corridor now—pushed back, traded out, sold into arrangements they had not fully understood—their trail remained as evidence of their intelligence. They had found the high ground between the swamps, the route that curved away from the worst of the bogs and arrived at the river crossings where the water ran shallow over flat stone. Jacob appreciated this as he appreciated most practical wisdom, regardless of its source. He was not a man given to sentiment about the vanished, but he was not given to contempt for them either, which put him at some distance from many of his neighbors and most of his acquaintances in the tidewaters.

His business today was with D'Ortega. Senhor D'Ortega, as the man styled himself, though Jacob had heard it said the family's connection to Portugal was three generations back and the title was something the grandfather had simply decided upon and the sons had inherited without irony. The plantation was called Jubilo, which was either evidence of a sense of humor or its complete absence, Jacob could not determine which. D'Ortega owed him a debt. Not a small one. The particulars were these: Jacob had stood surety for a shipment of tobacco that D'Ortega had consigned through a merchant in Rotterdam, a man named Pieters whom Jacob knew from his years in the low countries and still maintained correspondence with. The shipment had arrived damaged—spoilage in the hold, water having gotten into the lower tier of hogsheads through a caulking failure the captain disputed but could not disprove. Pieters had taken the loss partially but not wholly, and what remained had come to rest, as these things do, upon the man least able to argue his way free of it. That man was Jacob Vaark.

He had not been angry about it. Or rather, he had been angry in the way a man is angry at weather—fully, briefly, and without expecting the weather to apologize. What he required now was not apology but settlement. D'Ortega had written twice promising resolution. The letters were pleasant and elaborate and contained nothing of substance. Jacob had therefore decided to come in person, unannounced, which was not the custom but which had the advantage of preventing further pleasant and elaborate letters.

The trail opened onto a cart road and here the character of the land changed. Someone had cleared the verge back fifteen or twenty feet on either side, and though the clearing was not recent—the saplings coming in were already knee high in places—it gave the road a purposeful, managed look. Regina lifted her head. Jacob let her choose her pace and she settled into a willing trot that suggested she smelled water ahead or simply preferred the open road to the close trail, or both. The fog had burned entirely away now and the sun was full and the heat was the particular wet heat of the tidewater in this season, the kind that sat on a man's shoulders like a living thing and breathed against the back of his neck.

He was thirsty and had finished the water in his flask somewhere in the fog. He thought about the cold spring on his own land, the way it ran out from under a shelf of granite year round at the same temperature regardless of season, so cold in summer it ached in the teeth. He had built near it deliberately, had in fact changed the orientation of the house to put the kitchen within reasonable distance of the water. Rebekka had appreciated this. She was a practical woman and did not require the kitchen to be far from the house for reasons of fashion or appearances. What she required was water close and wood close and a floor that could be scrubbed, and he had done his best to provide these things, which was not as good a best as he intended but better than nothing, and she knew it, and they had reached, through some negotiation that was mostly silent, an understanding about this that constituted a kind of happiness. He thought of her now as he rode and felt the familiar combination of gratitude and inadequacy that she inspired in him, not unpleasantly.

The first sign of Jubilo was the smell. Even before the fields came into view Jacob's nose registered it—the particular dense sweetness of tobacco in flower mixed with something underneath it, midden and woodsmoke and the smell of many bodies in close quarters, which was not precisely unpleasant but was not precisely not unpleasant either. It was the smell of scale, of accumulation, of a thing grown past what one man could oversee with his own eyes. Jacob's operation was nothing like this. He had four people bound to him in various arrangements, none of them fixed in the way D'Ortega's were, and his fields were modest—he supplemented through trade more than cultivation, which suited him and which Rebekka called sensible though he suspected she sometimes wished for more certainty. You cannot eat a trade deal, she had said once, and he had laughed, and she had not, quite.

The house came into view at the end of a long allée of young elms not yet grown to the grandeur they were intended to represent. It was a brick house, larger than it needed to be, with pretensions toward a formal garden at the front that the climate was slowly defeating. Roses that had been planted in a geometric pattern had grown into each other and beyond their borders; the low boxwood hedges were uneven; the central path was mossy and slightly sunken on one side. Jacob noted all of this in the automatic way he noted things—not critically, but accurately. The house itself was sound. Good brick, good mortar, good proportions. Someone had known what he was doing. Whether that person was D'Ortega or a builder D'Ortega had hired and failed to acknowledge Jacob could not say.

A boy of perhaps twelve came running from the direction of the stable when Jacob rode in. He was barefoot and wore a rough shirt too large for him and he reached for Regina's bridle with the practiced ease of a child who had been doing this since he could stand. Jacob dismounted and handed him the reins and asked for water for the horse first and then, if it was not too much, for himself. The boy said nothing but nodded with the particular nod of someone who has learned that nodding is safer than speaking, and led Regina away. Jacob watched him go and then turned to face the house.

He was made to wait. This was itself a communication. The woman who answered the door—she was not a servant in any obvious livery but was not, from her bearing, the mistress of the house—told him Senhor D'Ortega was engaged and would attend to him presently. She indicated a chair in a dark hallway that smelled of tallow and dried herbs and something beneath those that Jacob could not name and did not try to. He sat. He was patient in the way of a man who has prepared himself for this particular inconvenience. He had not expected to be received immediately. He had expected to wait somewhat longer than necessary as a demonstration of relative standing, and he had come prepared to find this neither surprising nor diminishing.

While he waited he looked at what there was to look at, which was not much. A table against the wall held a pewter basin and a crucifix of some dark wood that was either very old or made to appear so. Above it on the wall was a painting in a heavy gilt frame—a landscape, European, a river valley with castles, the kind of scene painted in large quantities for large quantities of drawing rooms, not a bad painting but not a painting that had been looked at in a long time. The floor was wide pine boards, waxed but not recently. Through a half-open door to his left came the sound of a woman's voice and a child's intermittent response and then silence and then a slow, heavy footfall on stairs above.

D'Ortega was a man in his late forties who had been handsome once and retained the vestiges of it in the structure of his face—good bones, wide-set eyes, a chin that still suggested decision even if the flesh around it no longer confirmed this. He was not fat but he was softened, as men who eat and drink well and work little tend to be softened, the body making its accommodations to what is asked of it. He wore a good coat, better than the house perhaps warranted on a weekday afternoon, and Jacob understood this as another communication: I knew you were coming or I dress this way always, take your pick. Jacob offered neither admiration nor offense. He shook the man's hand and named himself and named his business with the directness he had found was either respected or resented but in either case moved things along faster than circumlocution.

D'Ortega received him in a room off the hallway—not the formal parlor, Jacob noted, but a working room of sorts, with maps on the table and a ledger open, though the ledger looked to have been recently placed there rather than recently consulted. They sat across from each other. A girl appeared with wine and Jacob accepted it and drank because he was still thirsty but he did not let the wine move him toward warmth because that was its purpose in this context and he was not unaware of this.

The debt, D'Ortega said, was of course on his mind constantly. He said this with feeling and Jacob believed the feeling was real—not guilt precisely but the particular discomfort of a man who has made a great many things someone else's problem and is intermittently troubled by this. There had been difficulties this past year. The weather, as Jacob surely knew. The price of tobacco had not recovered as hoped. There were labor problems of a persistent kind that D'Ortega described in terms that managed to make the people laboring sound like a problem they were creating for themselves, which Jacob found both familiar and wearisome. He said nothing.

He thought about the amount owed. He thought about what form of settlement was practical. D'Ortega's land was valuable but land could not be easily translated into the kind of capital Jacob needed for the transaction he was planning, the transaction he had been planning for two years, the one that Rebekka knew about in outline and not in detail, partly because the detail was still forming and partly because the detail, if she knew it, might cause her to look at him in a way he did not wish to be looked at. Not with contempt. Rebekka was not a contemptuous woman. But with a kind of knowing that would require him to know it too, and he preferred for a while longer to examine the proposition on his own terms.

The proposition was rum. Specifically, the construction of what they were calling, with the cheerful imprecision of men in new enterprises, a still house, though what was intended was a distillery of modest but scaleable capacity, in partnership with a man named Pursey who had the technical knowledge and needed the capital and the land, and Jacob had both. The ingredients could be obtained cheaply. The market was not only reliable but growing in ways that a man watching the trade patterns could see clearly if he was inclined to look. Jacob was inclined to look. He looked at things steadily and without flinching and he recorded what he saw and he thought about it and then he acted, usually correctly, occasionally not, and on the occasions when not he absorbed the loss and looked again. This was not philosophy. This was simply how he operated and he had operated this way since he was seventeen years old and arrived in this country with nothing except a facility for arithmetic and an absence of illusions about his own importance, which had turned out to be the most useful thing he owned.

What D'Ortega owed him, translated into what Jacob needed, meant something besides money. He looked at the man across the table and waited for the opening that would let him state this.

It came, as these things do, through D'Ortega's own discomfort. He spread his hands—a gesture of a man showing he has nothing in them, though this was not quite true—and said that he wished very much to satisfy the debt in full and that he was prepared to offer, as partial settlement, goods in kind, if Jacob would consider this, given the current illiquidity of his position. Goods, Jacob said. What goods. D'Ortega looked at his hands. Labor, he said. People. He had more than he required at present and was in fact facing the cost of maintaining them through a period where the work they could do would not pay for their maintenance, and if Jacob were willing to take one or perhaps two of them against the debt—skilled, D'Ortega said, though Jacob noticed the word came a half beat late, as though it had been searched for—this would go a substantial way toward resolution and the remainder could be addressed over the next two seasons in tobacco or in cash as circumstances allowed.

Jacob drank the rest of his wine and set the cup down carefully. He did not want people. He had made this calculation before and arrived at the same place each time: the costs were not only financial. There was the matter of the management of people, which was a skill he did not have in the way D'Ortega had it, or rather in the way D'Ortega's overseers had it, and which in any case required a scale of operation to make economic sense that Jacob had not achieved and was not certain he wished to achieve. His arrangement with the four people currently on his land was peculiar and impermanent and Rebekka said it was also impractical but it was what it was and he had not had occasion to deeply regret it. Adding to it through a debt settlement was not the plan.

And yet. He thought about the still house and Pursey and what Pursey had said last month about the need for hands during the building phase, strong hands and capable ones, not requiring the kind of oversight that unskilled labor required. He thought about the boy who had taken Regina without being asked, who had done it with the ease and confidence of a boy who had learned something real and internalized it. He thought about whether D'Ortega's people would have skills that Pursey could use.

He told D'Ortega he would need to see them first. D'Ortega nodded as though this were the most natural thing, which it was. He called for someone and there was a wait and then through the window that looked toward the back of the property Jacob could see a figure moving, and then two, and then a small group assembling in the yard with the particular assembling quality of people who have assembled like this before and know what it means and make their faces into whatever their faces need to be for this occasion. Jacob watched them through the window and felt the thing he always felt at this moment, which was not quite guilt and was not quite nothing, and which he had never been able to name to his own satisfaction and had mostly stopped trying to name, because naming it would require doing something about it, and he was not prepared to do something about it in any terms that were available to him in the world as it was currently arranged.

He stood. He would go and look, because he had said he would, and because not looking once you had said you would look was its own kind of dishonesty, a more insidious kind than looking and deciding against, which was at least direct. He followed D'Ortega through the half-open door and down a passage that smelled of tallow and dried herbs and the same unnameable thing as before, which he now thought might simply be age, the smell of a building that had been housing human life and its residue for long enough that the walls had absorbed it past any cleaning out.

In the yard the light was hard after the dark passage. He stood and let his eyes adjust. There were five of them. Three men, two of them past forty by his guess, one much younger, barely a man. And two women, one older, one—he stopped his assessment here because she was looking at him with an expression that he recognized from no previous experience and could therefore not categorize and that unsettled him in a way he would think about later, on the road, with Regina under him and the fog long gone and the Atlantic smell replaced by pine and wood smoke and ordinary evening air. She was young and very dark and her face was entirely composed and her eyes said something he could not hear, exactly as the woman in the first passage of his memory, the one he kept returning to, had said something he could not hear, raising them to look at him hard, saying something important to him. But she was not that woman. He did not know her. He did not know any of them.

D'Ortega was speaking at his elbow, naming capabilities, naming histories, naming values with the casual authority of a man naming the qualities of his horses. Jacob heard this without really listening, because what he was doing was looking at each person's hands—carpenter's hands, field hands, a woman's hands reddened from work he could not specify—and thinking about Pursey and the still house and the winter coming and the spring after that and the shape of the future which was always, when he looked at it directly, both more and less legible than he expected.

He made no decision that afternoon. He told D'Ortega he needed to think on it. D'Ortega said of course and offered more wine and supper and a bed for the night and Jacob declined all three, not rudely, simply firmly, the way he declined things he had already assessed and found unnecessary. He retrieved Regina from the boy, who had watered her and brushed her and treated her better than the stable suggested he would have, and he rode out of Jubilo as the sun was moving toward the tree line and the shadows were lengthening across the cart road.

For a while he thought about nothing in particular, or rather he thought about everything at once in the undifferentiated way of a man who is tired and has taken in more than he has processed. The tidewater smell came and went. A heron lifted from a ditch and flew low over the road ahead of him and then banked away south. Regina's hooves were steady on the dry road.

He would write to Rebekka tonight if he reached the ordinary before full dark. He would tell her the debt was not yet resolved but that it might be. He would not tell her the form it might take because he had not decided and because he did not yet know what words to use, and with Rebekka the words mattered—not because she was particular about language, she was not, but because she was particular about honesty, and honesty required that he have thought a thing through before he offered it to her, or else he was not being honest so much as thinking aloud in her direction, which was a different thing and which she had told him once, not unkindly, she preferred he not do.

The trail entered the old Lenape corridor again and the trees closed overhead. In the dimness the roots across the path were harder to see and he slowed Regina to a walk. He thought about the girl in D'Ortega's yard, her composed face, her eyes saying what he could not hear. He thought about the boy in the surf this morning, or rather the suggestion of the boy, because in the fog he had not seen him clearly, had only known he was there. He thought about the sloop somewhere out in the bay. He thought about Rebekka's hands. He thought about Pursey and the still house and the smell of rum which was the smell of money which was the smell, if you stood far enough back, of everything that moved in this world and determined who arrived where and what they found when they got there. He thought about the nature of debt and the nature of settlement and whether they were ever, when considered honestly, the same thing.

Regina walked on. The trail held. Above him, between the interlaced branches, the sky was still pale though the light was going. He had perhaps an hour before dark. He was thirsty again. He was thinking, still thinking, and he did not stop thinking, and no conclusion came to him, and he did not expect one, and the horse moved steadily under him through the old corridor of the old trail between the old trees in the new world which was not new at all but only new to him, and to men like him, and to the particular hungers they carried here and fed here and called by names that sounded, in his better moments, nothing like what they were.
