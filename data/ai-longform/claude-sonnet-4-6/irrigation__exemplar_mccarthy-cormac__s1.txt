The elder brother was already in the field when the younger came down from the house. He was walking the row of peach trees with his hands in his pockets and the early light came through the branches and lay in broken pieces on the ground. The younger crossed the fence and came on through the dead grass. The trees stood in their rows like penitents, the fruit small and hard and the leaves yellowed at the edges in a way that had nothing to do with the season.

Hector, the younger said.

The elder looked up. He had their father's face, the long jaw, the pale eyes. You ready, he said.

Let me get some water.

There's water on the truck.

They walked to where the truck sat at the end of the row and the younger took up a canteen and drank. The sun was low yet and the Catalinas to the east were dark and ribbed against the pale sky. Hector had already loaded the shovel and the pipe wrench and a length of two-inch PVC in the bed. He looked at his brother.

We'll start at the head, he said. Work down.

I know how to fix a waterline, Memo.

I didn't say you didn't.

The younger, whose name was Guillermo, got in the truck. They drove along the fence road without speaking, the truck jostling in the ruts, a thin plume of dust rising behind them in the windless morning. At the head of the main lateral they stopped and got out and Hector stood looking down the row of emitters, some buried in the blown dirt, some standing up out of the ground at wrong angles.

Half of these are clogged, Hector said.

All of them need to be dug out and reset.

They've been this way two seasons.

I know it.

Hector crouched at the first emitter and worked it loose with his fingers. Memo took up the shovel and began to clear the soil from the line. They worked without talking. A quail called from somewhere in the trees and its mate answered from a greater distance and then there was nothing. The light came on across the field.

This section here's cracked, Memo said.

He was pointing at a length of pipe he'd uncovered. Hector came and looked. The crack ran the full circumference of the pipe and the soil around it was dark from the slow seep.

I told Dad, Memo said.

When.

Before he got sick. Spring before last. I told him this whole stretch needed to come out.

Hector took the pipe wrench from the truck bed. Well it's out now, he said.

They cut the line and pulled the cracked section free and Hector went to the truck for the replacement pipe while Memo shaped the trench with the shovel, cutting the sides square. He worked steadily. His shirt was dark across the back already. He looked out at the trees, at the small hard fruit and the yellowed leaves.

What kind of yield you think we'll get, Memo said when Hector came back.

Hector didn't answer right away. He was threading the new pipe into the coupling. Not enough, he said.

Same as last year.

Worse than last year.

Memo stood the shovel in the dirt. He looked at his brother's back. We need to talk about this, he said.

I'm working.

You can work and listen.

Hector stood and wiped his hands on his pants. He turned around. He had the pipe wrench in his hand and he held it at his side. All right, he said.

I talked to the Brewer outfit. They came out two weeks ago and walked the property.

I know they did. Teresa told me.

And.

And what.

What did you tell her.

I told her it wasn't any of their business and she agreed with me.

Memo took off his hat and held it at his side. Hector, he said. Listen to me.

I'm listening. I been listening. You've been saying the same thing for two years.

I've been saying it because it's true. We're running a deficit. The equipment's twenty years old. The—

The equipment's fine.

The tractor needs a new hydraulic pump. The spray rig—

I can fix the spray rig.

When.

When I get to it.

You said that in April.

A mockingbird had lit in one of the peach trees and sat there going through its repertoire in the hot building morning, one song and then another, each one borrowed and made strange in the borrowing. Both men watched it a moment. It flew and was gone into the trees farther off.

This was his place, Memo said.

I know whose place it is.

Was. And he's dead. He left it to both of us.

Hector squatted back down at the pipe coupling. He began to work the wrench. I didn't need you to tell me he was dead, he said.

I'm not trying to pick a wound.

Then don't.

I'm trying to talk sense.

Your sense and mine are different.

Memo replaced his hat. He picked up the shovel and began to move dirt back into the trench, tamping it around the new pipe. He worked the shovel methodically, the way a man works when he is doing something with his hands because the rest of him needs to be still a moment.

What's your sense then, Memo said. Tell me.

Hector tightened the coupling and checked it with his hand. My sense is that we water these trees and tend this ground and we put up what we can put up and we sell what we can sell and that's what we do.

And if it's not enough.

It's got to be enough.

That's not an answer.

It's the only one I have.

They moved down the line to the next break. This one had blown at the coupling and the pieces were still half-connected, held together by the intact emitter tube, and a crust of dried soil marked where the water had run before it stopped running. Memo cut out the fitting with a hacksaw while Hector held the pipe steady. The sun was higher now and there was no mercy in it. The mountains to the north were brown and the sky above them was white.

How much did Brewer offer, Hector said.

Memo looked at him. Four-forty an acre.

Hector was quiet.

That's better than we could have hoped two years ago. Three years ago it wasn't even on the table.

It's still not on the table.

Memo stopped sawing. He looked at his brother. Hector. I have a family. My kids—

So did he. So did our father.

Your kids are grown.

My daughter is fifteen years old.

I know how old Elena is.

Then you know I have obligations.

So do I. Mine are just older than yours.

Memo resumed the hacksaw. What obligations, he said. You're not married. Your life is—

Don't.

I'm not—

I said don't.

The hacksaw worked through the last of the fitting and the pipe end dropped free. Memo held it in his hand. Old plastic, weathered and brittle, the kind that would go on failing all down the line. He turned it over in his hands and set it in the dirt.

Your life has always been your own, Hector, he said. That's not an accusation. I'm just saying. You don't have anybody counting on you but yourself.

Hector stood and walked to the truck. He stood at the truck bed looking into it. He wasn't looking for anything. He stood there.

That's not true, he said.

Memo waited.

Hector turned around. He leaned against the truck. There are ways a man can be obligated that you can't put in a column or show to a real estate company, he said. There are things you owe that you never get a bill for. He looked out at the trees. Dad was out here every morning of his life, he said. Every morning that he was able and some that he wasn't. The summer he had his hip done he was out here on crutches. You remember that.

I remember.

He came out here when it was dark still. He came out here in August when the temperature was a hundred and four degrees.

I know he did.

Then you know what that means.

I know what it meant to him.

Hector took off his hat. He was squinting in the white light. His face was dry and dark from years of this same light, light accumulated and permanent. It means something to me too, he said.

Memo walked to the truck and took up the canteen and drank from it. He held it out and Hector shook his head. Memo drank again.

What happens in five years, Memo said. If we keep on. If things go the way they've been going.

We'll figure it out in five years.

Figure it out now. Play it out in your head.

Hector put his hat back on. In five years this orchard is producing again if we get the water right. The price on late-season peaches—

The Marconas.

What.

The Marconas up north are planting three thousand acres in stone fruit. They've got water rights we can't compete with. The packing house—

I know about the packing house.

The packing house won't take our fruit at the same price in three years. Maybe two. They told me that. That's not something I'm guessing at.

Hector looked at him. When did they tell you that.

March. I went up there in March.

You didn't tell me.

I'm telling you now.

They were quiet. A hot wind had come up off the flat and it moved through the orchard in a low continuous sound, the leaves all turning and showing their pale undersides. Two of the trees at the end of the row were dead entirely, their branches leafless and gray, their bark beginning to come away in long strips that curled in the wind.

Those two need to come out, Memo said.

I know it.

They went back to work. They fitted the new coupling and tamped the trench and moved on down the line. The morning wore on. They stopped and ate sandwiches under one of the larger trees at midday and drank from the canteen and Memo smoked a cigarette. He smoked it slowly and looked out at the property, the dry grass in the unfenced areas, the equipment shed that needed its roof patched, the main house up the slope with the garden gone to weeds along its south wall.

We grew up here, he said.

Hector looked at him.

I know what this place is. I'm not saying I don't.

I know you're not.

I'm saying it's changing. It's not what it was even when we were kids. It's less every year.

Everything's less, Hector said.

That's not a—

It's drier and the water's going to keep costing more and we're going to keep getting older and all that's true. But you sell something like this and you can't buy it back.

I know that.

You don't know it. Not all the way down. Memo.

What.

Hector looked at him a long time. He looked at him the way men look at each other when they need a thing to be understood without the machinery of words. The wind moved through the trees. The leaves shifted and showed their pale undersides and showed their green again.

There are things that shouldn't be given up, Hector said.

Even if they're lost already.

They're not lost.

Hector.

I'm fixing the goddamn waterline, aren't I.

Memo smoked. He ground the cigarette in the dirt and stood. All right, he said.

They worked through the afternoon. They found three more cracked sections and replaced them. They found a coupling that had never been properly glued and had probably been seeping for two years at least. They reset the emitters and Hector bled the line and they watched the water begin to come through, slow at first and then steady, darkening the soil around each emitter in a widening circle.

It's moving, Memo said.

Hector nodded. He stood watching the water soak into the dry earth.

That one's weeping still, Memo said.

Where.

There. Third one down.

Hector walked to it and crouched. He worked the emitter in his fingers. It's just the connection, he said. He tightened it by hand. The weeping stopped.

They stood in the row and looked at the water coming. The emitters at the head of the line had been running long enough that the moisture had spread and in places met and the soil was dark in a long connected wetness down the row.

That'll help, Memo said.

Hector didn't say anything. He picked up the pipe wrench and walked back toward the truck.

I'm not trying to take anything from you, Memo said. When he came to the truck he set the wrench in the bed and turned around.

I know it.

I just need you to think about it.

I think about it.

Hector lifted the tailgate and latched it. He stood looking off toward the mountains. They were purple now in the late afternoon light, the shadows coming down off them long and cool-looking.

When you were up here with him, Memo said. Those last couple summers, before he got really bad. What did you two talk about?

I don't know. Everything. Nothing.

Did he talk about the place? About what he wanted done with it?

Hector looked at him. He was quiet for a while. He said the thing about land is it outlasts you, he said. He said that was either a comfort or it wasn't, depending on how you thought about it. Hector paused. He said he hoped we'd work it together. He said me and you.

Memo nodded. He looked out at the trees in the long light.

That's what he said, Hector said. I'm not making it into something it wasn't.

I know you're not.

He wasn't foolish about it. He knew what things cost. He'd been broke enough times.

Yes.

But that's what he said.

The light was coming in low and amber through the trees now and the leaves caught it and held it and the small hard fruit was bright against the yellowed green. Down the row the water came steadily to each tree and soaked into the ground and was taken in silence into the dark.

It's late, Hector said.

I know it.

We got most of it.

We'll come back in the morning.

Hector opened the truck door. The interior smelled of old sweat and dust and the particular smell of a working truck, oil and heat and something sweet and decayed from the floorboards. He sat in the seat and Memo came around and got in on the other side and they sat there a moment without starting the engine.

I'll call Brewer and tell them we're not interested, Memo said.

Hector looked at the windshield, the cracked corner of it, the road ahead going up through the trees toward the house. All right, he said.

This year, anyway.

Hector nodded.

But we have to do it right, Hector. We have to fix the spray rig and we have to get somebody to look at the tractor and we have to pull those two dead trees and replant. We have to do it like it's actually what we're doing.

I know it.

I mean we can't just put our heads down and hope.

I know it. I understand you.

Memo was quiet. All right, he said. All right then.

Hector started the truck. The engine turned over and settled into its idle and he sat a moment listening to it, some irregular knock in it that had been there a long time.

You hear that? he said.

The engine?

Yeah.

Doesn't sound right.

No.

You know what it is?

Probably the same thing it was last time. I can fix it.

Memo looked at him.

I can fix it, Hector said again. He put the truck in gear. The tires found the ruts and they moved up the road through the orchard toward the house. The irrigation water was running behind them in the dark. The trees stood in their rows against the last of the light, the fruit that was on them small and imperfect and alive, taking into themselves the water that had traveled through the new white pipe and the old cracked lengths they hadn't gotten to yet, that they would come back to in the morning.
