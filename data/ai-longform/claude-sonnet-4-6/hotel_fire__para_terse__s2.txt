She had told herself the drive would be the difficult part, that once she arrived the worst would be behind her, but standing now in the portico while a young man in a burgundy vest reached for her overnight bag, Eleanor understood that she had been wrong about almost everything. The hotel was white. That was the first thing. The old one had been red brick, Georgian in its symmetry, with black shutters and window boxes that in summer held geraniums. This building was white stucco with a kind of Mediterranean pretension, all arches and terracotta detail, and she thought: good. She thought: this is a different place. She repeated it to herself while the young man told her his name was Justin and asked if she'd be joining the Halvorsen wedding party, and she said yes, she'd be joining the Halvorsen wedding party, and she followed him through the automatic doors that opened with a sigh of climate-controlled air.

The lobby smelled of cedar and some synthetic floral that she couldn't identify. Eucalyptus, maybe. Whatever it was, it was not the smell she remembered, and this too she catalogued as a mercy. She stood at the check-in desk and watched a woman type and listened to the fountain somewhere behind her, a small burbling thing that was meant to suggest tranquility, and she let herself be tranquil. She accepted her key card in its little paper sleeve. She accepted directions to the elevator. She smiled at people who were already wearing wedding weekend lanyards, people who knew the bride or the groom, people who were already a little luminous with anticipatory celebration, and she thought how strange it was to be here, how strange and how manageable. She had been wrong about the drive. The drive had been fine.

The room was on the third floor. She had specifically requested not the fourth floor, which was as high as this building went, and she had not explained why, and no one had asked. The room was clean and unremarkable. A king bed with white linens, a gray accent wall, a flat-screen television bolted to its surface. A window that looked not toward the back gardens as she'd been told it would but toward the parking lot, which was full now with cars bearing out-of-state plates, but this didn't bother her. The parking lot was fine. The parking lot was not something she had opinions about.

She put her garment bag over the back of the desk chair and sat on the edge of the bed and looked at her hands.

Twenty-two years. Her daughter had been eleven months old. Eleanor had been working the early shift, seven to three, because the overnight woman, a Haitian grandmother named Celestine who Eleanor had loved, had a grandchild being baptized that Sunday and had asked Eleanor to cover her Friday overnight, which meant Eleanor was there at two in the morning when the fire started in the mechanical room on the fourth floor and moved, the investigators would later say, with an unusual speed attributable to a combination of factors including the age of the ductwork and a recent renovation that had, whether through negligence or cost-cutting or simple bad luck, left a section of fireproofing compromised. She had been in room 112, changing the shower curtain liner, which was something she did sometimes on slow nights when she wanted the feeling of having accomplished a task, even a small unnecessary one. She had stepped out into the hallway and seen the smoke moving along the ceiling like something alive and purposeful, like a dark idea taking shape.

What she had done then was not heroic. She had not, in the years since, tried to make it heroic. She had panicked. She had stood in the hallway for what felt like a long time but was probably three seconds and felt the cold absolute clarity of a body recognizing danger and then she had run, not toward the stairwell, not at first, but toward the housekeeping cart she'd left outside 114, because on that cart was her phone, and she needed her phone, she needed to hear her daughter's breathing on the baby monitor app she ran in the background of everything, her daughter who was at home with Eleanor's sister, who was sleeping in a crib in a room with a nightlight shaped like a crescent moon, who was fine, who was fine, who would always be fine whether Eleanor could verify it or not. She grabbed the phone. She ran toward the stairwell. She banged on doors as she went, six doors, maybe seven, banging and shouting and not stopping, and behind one of those doors was a family from Cincinnati who had come to Vermont for a leaf-peeping weekend and all four of them got out because Eleanor had banged and shouted. She knew this because they had found her afterward, in the parking lot, in the cold, wrapped in a mylar blanket, and the father had said thank you in a voice that was barely a voice, and she had looked at the four of them standing there—two adults, two teenage girls who were wearing pajamas and holding each other—and she had felt something too large and too complicated to name.

Three guests had not gotten out. A man in his sixties, traveling alone. A couple on their honeymoon. The couple had been on the fourth floor, at the end of the hall, farthest from the stairwell. The man had been in 108, close enough that for a long time Eleanor had lived with the question of why she hadn't gotten to him, why she had gone for her phone instead of banging on more doors, whether the seconds she'd spent at the cart had been the seconds that mattered. A therapist had eventually helped her understand that she could not know this and would never know this and that the not-knowing was something she would have to carry rather than resolve, and Eleanor had found this to be true. She carried it. It was not light, but she carried it.

She sat on the edge of the bed in the rebuilt hotel and looked at her hands.

The wedding was tomorrow at four. Tonight there was a rehearsal dinner in the ballroom for family and the wedding party, and then there was an informal gathering in the bar for everyone else—friends, colleagues, the various peripheral people whom Suki Halvorsen had accumulated over a life spent moving from city to city, collecting people the way some people collected beautiful things, not acquisitively but out of a genuine and almost helpless appreciation. Eleanor had known Suki since they'd both been twenty-six and working at the same nonprofit in Burlington, and they had stayed friends through all the years and distances, and when Suki had called to tell her about the wedding Eleanor had said immediately and without thinking, I'll be there, I'm so happy for you, before she'd understood where the wedding would be. And then she'd understood. And then there had been a silence of perhaps four seconds, which Suki had filled by saying, El, I should have asked you first, I'm sorry, I didn't even think, and Eleanor had said, No, no, don't be sorry, I want to come, and she had meant it. She had meant it completely, and she had also not entirely meant it, and this was simply how it was.

She changed out of her traveling clothes and put on the gray dress she'd brought for the informal gathering. She looked at herself in the bathroom mirror for a moment, not with any particular scrutiny, just acknowledging herself. Fifty-one years old. Her hair was fully silver now and she kept it short. Her daughter was twenty-two and had her father's eyes, which was a thing Eleanor still noticed every time she looked at her, not with grief anymore but with something more settled and more bittersweet, a recognition of how long it had been and how much had happened and how the person she'd been when she fell in love with that man felt like a figure she could see clearly but not quite reach. She was not unhappy. This was important to her, the precision of it. She was not unhappy. There were difficulties, and there was the thing she carried, and there was the ordinary accumulation of loss that came with being a person of a certain age, but there was also her work, which she found genuinely absorbing, and her daughter, who was extraordinary, and her friends, and occasionally men, and the way the light fell on Lake Champlain in October, and coffee in the morning, and books. She was not unhappy. She put on earrings, small gold loops, and went downstairs.

The bar was called something—she looked at the menu to check—the bar was called the Terrace Lounge, and it occupied the west wing of the ground floor in a space that Eleanor could not map onto her memory of the original hotel because the original hotel had been arranged differently, had opened onto its public spaces from a different angle, and this was helpful, this disorientation. She did not know where she was in relation to where she had been, and so she was simply here. She ordered a gin and tonic from a bartender who was very young and very efficient and she turned to find Suki already crossing the room toward her with her arms open.

Suki was wearing a yellow dress that she could not have worn at twenty-six and could now wear completely. She had that quality some women developed in their late forties, a fluency in themselves, a sense of having finally arrived at the right vocabulary for their own appearance, and she looked wonderful, Eleanor told her, she looked genuinely wonderful, and Suki laughed and held Eleanor by both arms and looked at her and said, I'm so glad you came. I was worried you wouldn't come. I kept almost calling you to say you didn't have to come.

I wanted to come, Eleanor said. And she did. She did want to be here.

They found a table near the window and Suki told her about the groom, whose name was David, whom Eleanor had met once at a conference Suki had organized and whom she remembered as a man with a quality of attention that was unusual, a man who listened like he was building something from what you said, and she told Suki she'd liked him very much, which was true. Suki talked about the last three years, about the apartment in Portland they'd rented together and were now in the process of buying, about the garden that was mostly David's project but that Suki had developed opinions about, particular opinions about the roses, about whether roses were worth the trouble they required and whether the answer to that question revealed something essential about a person's temperament. I've decided they're worth it, Suki said. The trouble. I've completely come around.

Other people joined them. A man named Greg who had worked with Suki in the Portland office of an environmental organization and who had brought his boyfriend, a quiet Englishman named Thomas who was a cellist and who said very little but listened with the same quality of attention Eleanor had noticed in David, and she wondered if people chose others who resembled what they admired. A woman named Priya who had been Suki's roommate in Chicago and who was a pediatric nurse and who had strong opinions about the cheese plate that had arrived and was almost all brie, which she felt was a failure of ambition. People with names Eleanor caught and then lost, people who were warm and easy, people who had the slightly heightened affect of people at the beginning of a celebratory weekend. Eleanor drank her gin and tonic. She ordered another. She felt herself settling into the evening like settling into a warm bath, the way you let the temperature find you.

At some point Suki was pulled away toward the rehearsal dinner overflow, some family logistics that needed managing, and Eleanor sat with Greg and Thomas and Priya and talked about things she had no strong feelings about, which was restful. She liked strangers. She liked the particular freedom of talking to people who had no history of her. She talked about her work, which was grant writing for a land trust, which most people found either boring or surprisingly interesting, and Priya found it surprisingly interesting and asked several questions that Eleanor enjoyed answering. She talked about her daughter's work, which was in urban planning in Montreal, which allowed Eleanor the pleasure of mentioning Montreal, which she loved, and describing its qualities to Thomas, who had never been, who listened carefully as though constructing it in his mind.

She was on her third drink, which was one more than she usually had, when she became aware of where the bathrooms were. They were down a hallway to the left of the bar, past a set of double doors, and she had not thought about this hallway or these doors until she found herself walking toward them, and then she was standing at the entrance to the hallway and something was wrong with the light or with her relationship to the light, something was shifting in a way she recognized, a change in the quality of her attention, a narrowing.

The hallway was carpeted in a burgundy and gold pattern that was generic and plentiful in hotels and meant nothing. The walls were off-white, hung with sepia photographs of Vermont landscapes. There was nothing significant about this hallway. She knew there was nothing significant about this hallway. She also knew that she was standing in it and that her breathing had changed, had become something she was conscious of, which was not how breathing was supposed to be. She stood still and looked at the photographs. A covered bridge. A hillside of birch trees. A man standing in a field with his back to the camera, looking at something outside the frame, something the photographer had seen and wanted to see again. She looked at this photograph for a moment, at the man's back, at his posture which was neither troubled nor relaxed but simply present, attending to something, and she thought: all right. She breathed. She walked to the bathroom, which was clean and well lit, and she stood at the sink and ran cold water over her wrists.

In the mirror she looked like herself. This was reassuring, or rather it was neither reassuring nor alarming, it was simply accurate. She looked like a fifty-one-year-old woman who had driven three hours to attend a friend's wedding at a place where she had once survived something, and she was doing fine, and tomorrow she would sit in a white chair in a garden and watch her friend marry a man who listened well and had opinions about roses, or rather who had been changed by proximity to someone who had opinions about roses, which was perhaps better, and this was worth the drive and worth the night and worth the third drink she probably shouldn't have had.

She dried her hands on a towel that was thick and warm from whatever heating element kept hotel towels thick and warm, and she thought, not for the first time, about the guests who had died here. Not in this hotel, because this hotel had not existed when they died, but in this place, in the space that this hotel now occupied, on this specific piece of earth. The man in 108. She did not know his name, had been told it once by a journalist who was writing something and had called to speak with her, but she had not been able to hold it, the name had not stayed with her, which was a failure she had made her peace with imperfectly. The couple—she didn't know their names either, had never learned them, had chosen not to learn them, which was a different kind of thing. She had understood early on that she could not mourn people whose names she knew without being destroyed by the mourning, and so she had maintained this not-knowing with a care that she recognized as cowardly and necessary in equal measure.

She wondered sometimes if they had been happy, the couple. If the honeymoon had been good up until it wasn't. If they'd eaten a good dinner that night, if they'd sat in the bar downstairs, the old bar that had been called something different, the bar she'd sometimes walked through at the end of her shift on her way to the staff exit, and if they'd been easy with each other, the particular ease of two people who have just promised each other everything and are still close enough to the moment that the promise feels both enormous and weightless, which she understood was how it felt, or could feel, because she had felt it once herself, at a different wedding in a different place, and the feeling had been true even though the marriage had eventually become something else and then ended.

She thought: they were probably happy. She let them be happy. She gave them the good dinner and the ease and the bar and the warmth of the room and then she let them go, let them become again what they always were in her mind, which was the place where the road stopped, the end of the knowable.

She went back to the bar.

Greg and Thomas were saying goodnight, Thomas gentle and a little formal in his leave-taking, Greg exuberant, pulling Eleanor into a hug as though they'd been friends for years. Priya stayed and ordered another glass of wine and asked Eleanor about the land trust and whether it was possible to feel, in that work, that anything was actually being saved, or whether the scale of what was being lost made every small preservation feel futile. Eleanor said she'd spent a lot of time with this question. She said there were days when it felt futile and days when it didn't, and that the days when it didn't were often the days when she was standing on a specific piece of land, when she was physically present on the ground that was being protected, because it was hard to feel futile about a thing you could stand on and look at and hear. Priya nodded slowly. She said she thought the same was probably true of medicine, that the generalization was crushing and the specific was survivable.

Survivable, Eleanor repeated. That's a good word for it.

Suki came back around eleven, glowing with the managed chaos of family, and pulled Eleanor aside and asked, with a careful directness, how she was doing, not in the casual social sense but in the actual sense, and Eleanor said she was doing well, actually, she was doing surprisingly well. Suki searched her face for a moment and then accepted this, because she was someone who had learned over the years to trust Eleanor's self-assessments, who had sat with Eleanor through enough difficult periods to know the difference between fine and performing fine.

I thought a lot about whether to have it here, Suki said. David and I talked about it. He said it was your call entirely, that if you said you were fine with it then you were fine with it, and I think I needed him to say that before I could believe it.

He's right, Eleanor said. And I am.

Can I ask you something? Suki said. She was looking at the middle distance, at the bar and the people still gathered at it, at the girl behind the bar who was making something complicated with muddled fruit. Did it feel different when you got here? The building, I mean. Different enough.

Eleanor thought about this. She thought about the white stucco and the automatic doors and the cedar and fake eucalyptus smell and the carpet in the hallway, and she thought about standing at the sink with cold water on her wrists, and she thought about the man in the photograph looking at something outside the frame.

It's different enough, she said. And then she said: but it's the same too, in some way. It's the same ground.

Suki was quiet.

I don't mean that badly, Eleanor said. I think I mean—she searched for it—I think I mean that the sameness is okay. That the ground can hold both things. The building, and what was here before the building. I've been thinking about that.

Suki took her hand and held it. This was something Suki did, had always done, and Eleanor had always received it as the gift it was, this simple willingness to hold a hand without needing to resolve anything with it.

They stayed at the bar another hour. The crowd thinned. The chandelier above the bar, which had been set at a calculated amber warmth, shifted slightly cooler as the evening moved toward midnight. Eleanor drank water and felt pleasantly tired, the kind of tiredness that came from being fully present in a succession of hours, from having arrived somewhere and stayed. Priya left, hugging Eleanor with the warmth of someone who meant it even recently met. Suki's David appeared to collect her, and he shook Eleanor's hand with both of his, which she remembered as a thing he did, and said he was really glad she'd made it, and she could see that he was, that it wasn't the thing people said.

She took the elevator to the third floor. She walked down the hallway with her key card and opened the door to the room with the view of the parking lot, which was still full, which would still be full in the morning when she woke. She took off the gray dress and the gold loops and stood at the window for a moment in the dark. The parking lot below was still and orange-lit and entirely ordinary. Somewhere behind her in the building she could feel its new shape, all the rooms occupied or soon to be occupied, all the sleeping and near-sleeping, all the small ceremonies of people making themselves comfortable in unfamiliar beds, and beneath all of it the ground, which held everything, which had always held everything, which would go on holding everything long after the building was something else.

She got into the white bed. She thought briefly about her daughter in Montreal, asleep in her own apartment, asleep without needing monitoring, asleep and fine, fine, fine. She thought about tomorrow's garden and the white chairs and Suki in whatever dress she'd chosen, which she'd refused to describe, about which she had maintained a secrecy that Eleanor found touching, as though the dress were still capable of being a genuine surprise in a world that had largely surrendered the concept. She thought about David listening.

She closed her eyes. The room held its ordinary darkness. Outside, the parking lot held its orange light, and below that the ground held its long, patient memory of everything, and Eleanor slept.
