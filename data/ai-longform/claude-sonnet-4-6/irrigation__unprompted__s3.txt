The leak had been there since April, maybe longer, a slow weeping in the second row of trees that had turned the soil dark and soft as a bruise. Martin found it first, crouching down with his hands pressed into the wet earth while the morning was still cool and the light came sideways through the apricot trees, turning everything amber and provisional. He didn't call to his brother right away. He stayed there a moment with the mud between his fingers, thinking about nothing in particular, or thinking about everything at once, which was the same thing these days.

Daniel was at the truck when Martin finally stood and waved him over, one arm raised the way their father used to signal from across a field, a gesture so automatic Martin sometimes caught himself doing it alone. Daniel came without hurrying. He was forty-three, two years older, and he'd developed the deliberate pace of a man who had decided somewhere along the way that urgency was a form of vanity. He wore their father's canvas hat, the one with the sweat stain at the crown shaped vaguely like the state of Nevada, and he carried the pipe wrench loosely at his side.

He stood next to Martin and looked at the dark soil, at the thin silver thread of water still working its way up through the ground.

"Main line or lateral?" he said.

"Lateral, I think. Down maybe a foot."

Daniel set the wrench against the trunk of the nearest tree and crouched where Martin had crouched. He pressed two fingers into the earth, tilted his head slightly.

"We'll need to dig it up. Could be a joint."

"Could be a crack."

"Could be," Daniel said. He stood and brushed his hands on his jeans without looking at Martin. "I'll get the shovels."

They worked in silence for the first hour, trading off when one of them tired, piling the dug earth to the side in a way that was careful, almost respectful. The orchard was thirty-eight acres of mostly apricots with a section of peaches at the north end that their father had planted late, almost as an afterthought, trees that were only now coming into anything like reliable production. The irrigation lines were old, installed in 1979 when their father had first run water to the upper rows, and then extended piecemeal over the following decade as the orchard grew. There were joints every several feet that had been patched and re-patched until the whole system was a record of small emergencies, a map of where things had gone wrong and been temporarily fixed. Martin knew this system the way he knew his own body, meaning he understood it mostly through its failures.

They found the crack at about fourteen inches. A hairline fracture in a length of PVC that had spread, over winter probably, and then given way under the spring pressure. Daniel lifted the pipe section from the trench and held it up, and the split ran about three inches along the underside, clean as a knife cut.

"We've got coupling and a good length of replacement in the truck," Martin said.

"I know what we have."

Martin let that sit. He climbed out of the trench and walked back to the truck and got the water and drank from the jug and looked down the row of trees, which were heavy with small green apricots not yet close to ripe. In six weeks they would be ripe. Someone would need to harvest them, which meant hiring people, which meant spending money they didn't have to harvest fruit that might or might not sell for enough to cover the cost of having harvested it, plus the irrigation repair and the costs that had come before and the costs that were already waiting for next month, patient as creditors, which was what they were.

He brought water back for Daniel, who took it without thanks, which was not rudeness but just their way with each other, the shorthand of brothers who had spent enough time together that the formalities had worn smooth.

"I talked to Reyes on Thursday," Martin said.

Daniel was fitting the new coupling, turning it by hand to feel the threads. He didn't stop.

"Reyes from the water board?"

"No. Reyes from Pinnacle."

Daniel stopped then. He set the coupling down on the edge of the trench and looked up at Martin with an expression that was not quite anger, not yet, but that held anger somewhere behind it the way clouds hold rain.

"You talked to a buyer."

"I talked to a man who buys land. I didn't agree to anything. I didn't even agree to talk to him, really. He called me."

"He called you. They just call people now."

"They know we're in trouble, Daniel. Everyone knows. You know that."

Daniel picked up the coupling again and went back to fitting it, but his movements had changed, had lost their easiness. "What did he say."

It wasn't a question exactly. Martin heard it as an opening, a door held slightly ajar, and he stepped through carefully.

"Two million four. For the whole parcel. Land and buildings, equipment if we want, or they'll let us dispose of it ourselves."

"Two four," Daniel said. "For thirty-eight acres of the best stone fruit land in this county."

"Land that hasn't turned a profit in four years."

"Land that hasn't turned a profit because of the drought years. Because of Bartlett going under and pulling half the regional pricing with it. Because of things that aren't permanent."

"The drought might be permanent. Lots of people think the drought is the new normal now."

"Lots of people," Daniel said, with a flatness that dismissed the category entirely.

Martin crouched at the edge of the trench and watched his brother work. Daniel had their father's hands, wider across the palm than Martin's, with thick fingers that seemed clumsy until you watched them do something precise, and then they didn't seem clumsy at all. He was fitting the coupling with the wrench now, and the sound of it rang in the still morning air.

"You could pay off the note on your house," Martin said. "You could put money away for the kids. Actual money, not money we keep saying will come from a good year. Ana could—"

"Don't," Daniel said. Not loudly. Just the word.

"I'm not trying to—"

"Don't use Ana to make your argument. Or the kids. That's not a fair way to make an argument."

Martin sat all the way down on the pile of dark earth and rested his forearms on his knees. The sun had moved and now the light came more directly, hot on the back of his neck. A bee worked through the grass near the trench, purposeful, uninterested in either of them.

"Dad lost thirty thousand on this orchard in the last two years he was alive," Martin said. "We kept that from him. We both know that. We moved money around and we kept it from him because we didn't want him to know."

"He knew," Daniel said.

"What?"

"He knew. He wasn't stupid. He wasn't even that sick, not at the end, not in his mind. He knew and he let us pretend we were keeping it from him, because that was what we needed." Daniel sat back on his heels and looked at the coupling, then at Martin. "He didn't want to sell then either. That was never something he wanted."

"He's not here now."

The bluntness of it surprised even Martin as he said it. Daniel's face went through something, a movement under the surface, and then settled.

"No. He's not."

"I'm not saying his wishes don't matter. I'm saying they can't be the only thing that matters. We're in debt. Real debt. Not debt we're managing, debt we're losing to. Every year we hold this place we go deeper. You know the numbers as well as I do."

"I know the numbers," Daniel said. "I also know that numbers are a way of telling a story, and that you can choose which numbers you use to tell it."

"What's that supposed to mean?"

Daniel climbed up out of the trench with a movement that was slightly effortful, slower than it would have been ten years ago. He sat on the opposite side from Martin, the trench between them, and drank from the water jug.

"You're counting what we lose. You're counting debt service and labor and water costs and revenue shortfalls. You're building a case." He wiped his mouth. "You're good at building cases. You always were, even when we were kids. You could build a case for anything."

"That's not fair."

"I'm not trying to be unfair. I'm saying you're a lawyer by training and you think in arguments, and that's not a criticism, it's just a fact. But the numbers you're not counting—what does the Reyes family do with two million four worth of orchard land? They don't grow apricots. Nobody who buys orchard land at that price grows apricots. They plant grapes, or they build houses, or they put in one of those agricultural tourism things where people come and make their own jam on the weekend."

"Maybe that's what this land becomes. Maybe that's not the worst thing."

"Dad planted these trees."

"Some of them. Some of them we planted. Some of them need to come out—you know that. The southwest block is half-dead."

"The southwest block had a virus. We treated it."

"Two trees are still showing it, Daniel."

"Two trees out of—"

"I know how many trees. I know exactly how many trees." Martin heard his voice rising and made himself stop. He looked down the row again. The light was full and hot now and the green apricots hung in clusters, and there was something about the sight of them that he found he couldn't be purely rational about, which was exactly the problem. He had come here meaning to have a practical conversation, a conversation between two adults about their shared financial situation, and it kept turning into something else, something with the texture of grief.

"I don't want to sell," Martin said, and surprised himself again.

Daniel looked at him.

"I know that's what it sounds like I want. But I don't want to. I want us to be able to keep it. I want there to be a version of this where we keep it and we make it work and we're here when the kids are old enough to understand what it is." He paused. "I just don't think that version exists."

Daniel was quiet for a moment. Below them, somewhere at the base of the slope, a tractor was running on someone else's property, a steady mechanical sound that rose and fell on the air.

"Do you remember when the pump failed?" Daniel said. "The big one, the main pump, that would've been—"

"Ninety-one."

"Ninety-one. July. Middle of the summer. We were—"

"I was seventeen."

"Right. And Dad didn't have the money to replace it. Literally didn't have it. I found him sitting at the kitchen table at two in the morning and he had all the papers out, all the account statements, and he was just looking at them." Daniel pulled a length of grass from the ground near where he sat and turned it between his fingers. "I asked him what he was going to do and he said he wasn't sure. He said maybe nothing. Maybe just wait and see. And I thought he meant wait and see if the money appeared somehow, but I've wondered since if he meant wait and see if the problem got worse or better on its own. Like he was just—sitting with it."

"We borrowed against the house," Martin said. "He and Mom did. That's how they paid for the pump."

"I know. But that night, before he did that, he was just sitting with it. Not trying to solve it. And I think about that sometimes when we talk about all this. Whether there's something useful in that."

"Sitting with it didn't fix the pump."

"No. But he wasn't ready to fix it yet. He was still deciding what it cost."

Martin looked at his brother across the trench, which was maybe three feet wide and just over a foot deep, a small excavation, minor surgery. The replaced pipe section lay off to the side, the crack in it visible if you knew to look. In an hour they would backfill the trench and restore the water pressure and the leak would be fixed, and next month or next year there would be another one, because that was the nature of a system this old, that was what it meant to maintain something past its designed lifespan.

"What's your number?" Martin asked.

"My number for what?"

"For selling. If you had one."

Daniel looked up at the sky, which was deep and cloudless, the specific blue of a California June that looks painted rather than real.

"I don't think I have one."

"You have to have one. Every farmer who says he'll never sell has a number. That's not cynicism, that's just—there's always a number."

"Maybe. Maybe not." Daniel stood up and began collecting the tools they'd need to backfill. "Pinnacle doesn't want this land because of the trees. You understand that. They want it because the county rezoning proposal goes through planning in September, and if it does, this parcel gets reclassified and they can build sixty, seventy homes on it. That's what two million four is. It's the price of sixty houses that don't exist yet. It's not what this land is worth as an orchard."

"As an orchard it's worth substantially less."

"As an orchard it's worth what we make it worth. If we make it work."

"And if we can't?"

Daniel handed Martin a shovel. Martin took it. They stood on either side of the trench for a moment, and there was something almost ceremonial about the posture, something that made Martin think of funerals, though he didn't want to think about funerals, not with his brother standing in their father's hat in the middle of their father's orchard on a morning that was so beautiful it seemed almost cruel.

"Mom's in that house," Daniel said. "You know that. You think about her anywhere else?"

"She'd go to Sara's. Sara's been asking her."

"She doesn't want to go to Sara's. She wants to be in her house. She wants to look out the kitchen window and see the trees her husband planted."

"She's seventy-four and the stairs are getting hard for her and she's three miles from any—"

"I know how old she is."

They began to shovel. The earth went back into the trench in a rhythm that developed naturally between them, one lifting while the other packed, the old collaborative instinct of brothers who had done physical work together since they were large enough to hold a tool. The soil was dark and rich down where the moisture had been, and Martin noticed this, as he always noticed things about this land that he was trying to be practical about, that he was trying to think of as an asset that could be converted into other assets.

He thought about the summer he was twelve and Daniel fourteen and they had picked apricots from dawn to midmorning every day for three weeks and their hands had turned rough and their shoulders had ached and their father had paid them fifty cents a lug box, which was almost nothing but which they hadn't cared about because the money hadn't been the point. The point had been working alongside their father, matching his pace, earning some version of his wordless approval. The apricots had been extraordinary that year, heavy and deeply colored, the flesh so ripe it gave when you pressed it, and there had been a smell in the orchard those mornings that Martin could still recall precisely, that combination of warm fruit and turned earth and the specific dry quality of summer air in this valley that he had never encountered anywhere else.

He knew that memory was not an argument. He knew that memory was in fact the enemy of clear thinking, that it worked against the kind of assessment this situation required. He had told himself this, told himself that grief for a version of his childhood was not a reason to deepen a financial hole that would eventually swallow the very things he was grieving for. He believed this. He just couldn't make it feel true.

"He talked to me about it," Daniel said, after they'd been shoveling a while. He said it without preamble, as if continuing a thought rather than beginning one.

"About selling?"

"About the whole thing. The orchard, the debt, the future. This was maybe six months before he died. He was pretty clear-eyed still, you remember how he was, that kind of reckoning he could do." Daniel didn't stop shoveling. "He said he didn't expect us to hold it forever. He said he'd always understood that nothing lasted, that was just the nature of things, everything had its season. He said what he hoped was that we wouldn't let it go before we were sure we'd tried everything." He paused. "He said we'd know when we'd tried everything."

"Did he say how we'd know?"

"He said we'd know because we'd stop arguing about it."

Martin was still for a moment. Then he laughed, a short involuntary sound, and Daniel looked up and there was something in his face that hadn't been there before, something loosened.

"He knew us pretty well," Martin said.

"Better than we knew ourselves, probably."

They finished backfilling the trench and tamped the soil down. Daniel went to the head of the lateral line and opened the valve slowly, and they both waited, watching the ground. The restored section held. No welling, no dark spread of moisture. Martin walked the length of the pipe to the end of the row and back, checking, and everything was dry.

"We should look at the north junction while we're out here," Daniel said. "I've been meaning to check it."

"I know. I thought the same thing."

They gathered the tools and began walking north, through the apricot rows, toward the peach section at the top of the slope. The fruit on the trees moved very slightly in a wind Martin hadn't noticed until now, a light and intermittent wind that came from the west and carried with it the dusty green smell of the hills, and Martin walked through it and thought that Daniel was right that they hadn't tried everything, and also that he, Martin, was right that the trying had a cost, and that both of these things were true, and that they would keep being true for some time, perhaps for the rest of the summer, the rest of the year, perhaps beyond that. They would have this argument in different forms for as long as there was something to argue about, and the argument would be the sound of two brothers who loved the same thing differently, which was not so different from how they had always been.

He didn't say any of this to Daniel. He carried his shovel over his shoulder and watched the ground for irregularities, soft places, signs of water where water wasn't supposed to be. The peach trees came into view at the top of the row, a little taller than the apricots, their leaves larger and a slightly different shade of green, and there was fruit on them too, still weeks away, and the trees looked like what they were: living things in the middle of their season, indifferent to ownership, requiring care regardless.

Martin stopped at the north junction and crouched down and pressed his hand into the soil. It was dry. He looked up at Daniel, who was looking back at him.

"Dry," Martin said.

"For now," Daniel said.

Martin stood. The sun was high and the valley spread out below them, the ordered rows of the orchard and then the road and then other properties, other orchards, other people's complicated arrangements with land and money and the things they had inherited. Somewhere in there was his mother at her kitchen window. Somewhere there were the sixty houses that didn't exist yet and might or might not exist, depending on a planning board, depending on Pinnacle, depending on the two of them.

"You want to eat?" Daniel said. "I've got food in the truck."

"Yeah," Martin said. "Give me a minute."

He stood a moment longer at the top of the row while Daniel went ahead, walking back through the trees the way they'd come, the canvas hat moving away through the green light. The valley was very bright and very still. Martin looked at it. Then he picked up his shovel and followed his brother.
