The older one was already in the field when the boy came down from the house and the dew was still on the grass and the mountains to the east held the first light in their peaks like something poured there and cooling. Harlan stood at the head of the irrigation row with a shovel and did not look up when his brother came across the field toward him. The younger boy whose name was Curtis walked with his hands in his pockets and he looked at the trees as he came between them. The trees were old and many of them were sick. Galls on the bark like fists. Branches gone grey and skeletal among the green. He stopped beside his brother and looked down into the shallow ditch where the water should have been running.

You find the break, Curtis said.

Somewhere down past the Gravensteins. I didn't walk it all the way out.

Harlan handed him a shovel and picked up his own and they walked down through the orchard in the early morning with the light coming sideways through the trees and laying long shadows across the dry grass. An owl called somewhere in the dark interior of an old pippin tree and then was silent.

The ditch ran between two rows and they walked the bank of it picking their way around the volunteer weeds and the places where the bank had crumbled. In places the ditch was dry and in places there was standing water gone dark and rimmed with a white mineral crust. Harlan looked at this and looked away.

Here, Curtis said.

The plastic line had failed at a coupling and was bent back on itself and lay pushed into the bank by the pressure of some earlier water or perhaps just by time and the working of the earth. The ground around it was soft and black and smelled of must.

Hand me that tape, Harlan said.

Curtis set down his shovel and went into his back pocket and came out with a roll of pipe tape and handed it over. Harlan knelt at the ditch and pulled the coupling apart and looked at where it had split. He worked without speaking. His hands were large and deliberate and he turned the fitting in them studying the failure.

This is the third time on this section, Curtis said.

I know it.

The fitting's garbage. The whole line probably is.

Harlan did not answer. Curtis watched him. He could see the tendons working in the back of his brother's neck.

It'll hold a little while, Harlan said.

And then what.

And then we fix it again.

Curtis stood up and looked down the row of trees. The fruit was coming in sparse and small and even from here he could see the russeting on some of the apples, the dull rough patches that meant something wrong in the skin of the thing itself, some failure of the world to provide what was needed.

Harlan, Curtis said.

Don't.

I said don't.

They worked through the morning, the two of them, going down through the orchard checking each coupling and each section of pipe. The sun came up over the mountains and the dew burned off and the air grew dry and warm with the particular warmth of that valley in June which was the warmth of the world preparing to be merciless. The older brother worked steadily and without complaint. He did not look at the sick trees or at the places where the grass had gone yellow or at the collapsed section of the old wooden flume that had served their grandfather's system and which now sat rotting in the weeds at the edge of the property like the ribcage of something enormous.

Curtis watched all of it. He watched it deliberately, letting himself see it.

They found another failed coupling midway through the Gravensteins and replaced it with a section of new pipe Harlan had in a coil over his shoulder. They found a place where a gopher had worked up under the line and displaced it and they dug that out and relaid it and tamped the earth back. They found a section where the pipe had been abraded against the edge of a rock and worn through to a slow weep and they cut it out and spliced it.

All that work just to water trees that aren't producing, Curtis said. They were sitting on the bank of the ditch eating the sandwiches their mother had sent with them. It's been three dry years.

Harlan bit his sandwich and chewed and looked away. Dad planted half these trees, Curtis said.

I know who planted them.

And his dad planted the other half.

Then you know what I'm saying. You know I'm not saying it lightly.

That's not what I'm saying.

That's what you're saying when you talk about selling.

Curtis looked at him. His brother's face was angular and sun-darkened and there was a stubbornness in it that went all the way to the bone. It was their father's face. He had always wondered what face he himself wore and had never been able to say.

I'm saying we have sixty acres of trees that are failing, Curtis said. I'm saying the water costs have tripled. I'm saying the market for heritage apples is not what it was supposed to be. I'm saying there are men who will pay good money for this land and that money would set us both up for the rest of our lives and that there is nothing shameful in that.

Harlan stood and picked up his shovel. There is something shameful in it, he said. He said it without heat, as if he were stating the properties of soil. There is exactly that.

They went back to work.

By midday the heat was considerable and they had worked their way back toward the head of the system. There it goes, Curtis said.

Harlan watched it. Yes, he said.

The shadows had drawn back under the trees and the grass was pale with heat. What?

They are to me.
