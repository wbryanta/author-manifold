The pipe had split somewhere near the base of the old Bartlett row, and water was finding its way through the cracked clay in thin silver threads that caught the morning light before vanishing into the earth. Dominic saw it first, from the high ground near the barn where he'd gone to check the pressure gauge, and he stood there a moment watching the waste of it before he went to find his brother.

Paul was in the kitchen eating toast over the sink, looking out at the trees the way their father used to look at the television—present in body only, his eyes focused on something that wasn't there. He was forty-one years old and his hair had gone the color of January grass, that indeterminate gray-blond that made him seem both younger and older than he was, depending on the light. Dominic came through the screen door and Paul didn't turn.

"Line's split in the Bartletts," Dominic said.

"How bad."

"Bad enough. We'll need to dig."

Paul set down his toast and rinsed his hands under the tap, dried them on the back of his jeans. "Of course we will," he said, not bitterly, just as a fact, the way you'd note that it was Tuesday.

They collected what they needed from the equipment shed—two spades, the pipe cutter, a length of one-inch poly that Dominic wasn't entirely sure was the right diameter, the coupling kit their father had bought the previous September and never opened. The cardboard box was damp at the corners from where it had sat on the shed's concrete floor through winter, and when Paul picked it up he held it carefully, as though its dissolution might be prevented by gentleness.

The morning was already warm with the particular warmth of Central Valley June, which was not the pleasant warmth of the coast but the heavy, mineralogical warmth of a place that has been retaining heat since April and will not release it until October. The sky was white at the horizon and deepening to a true blue only directly overhead. The orchard rows ran east to west, and they walked south along the access road, past the early Babcock peaches that would need picking in three weeks if anyone could be found to pick them, past the nectarines that were coming in late and sparse this year, into the Bartletts where the trees were old—their grandfather's trees, planted in the forties, with trunks the circumference of a man's embrace and bark that had grown so deeply furrowed over the decades that it held its own small ecosystem of lichen and spider and dark.

The split was where Dominic had seen it. A section of lateral pipe about fourteen inches long had developed a longitudinal fracture, and the water was seeping out in a fan shape, soaking a dark patch of earth that was already being attended to by a delegation of yellow jackets. Paul crouched and pushed his finger against the fracture and the seam opened wider.

"We should shut off the zone," Dominic said.

"I know that."

"I'm just saying."

"I know what you're saying, Nic."

Dominic walked back to the valve box near the road and turned off the zone and walked back. Paul was still crouching, his forearms on his knees, looking at the pipe with an expression Dominic recognized as the expression their father wore when the accountant came in March.

They began to dig. The soil was dry on the surface and clenched hard two inches down, and they worked in the wordless rhythm of people who have performed the same task together many times, one spade's work offset from the other's, clearing a trench around the pipe's path. Dominic was sweating through his shirt within ten minutes. A mockingbird ran through its repertoire in one of the Bartlett trees above them, cycling through imitations of birds that had been here earlier in the season and were now gone, so that the bird was performing a kind of memorial to the spring.

"I talked to Hendricks again," Paul said.

Dominic's spade hit a stone and he worked it loose with the blade. "I figured."

"He's coming up. Friday. Just to walk the property."

"Just to walk it."

"That's what I said."

Dominic threw the stone out of the trench. It landed in the dry grass of the row middle and raised a small cloud of dust. He said nothing.

"He's offering two-nine," Paul said. "Potentially. He used that word. Potentially two-nine."

"Potentially."

"For ninety-two acres, Nic. You do that math."

"I know the math."

"Then do it out loud. Tell me what two-nine divided by ninety-two looks like."

"I know what it looks like. I also know what this looks like." Dominic gestured with his spade, a wide arc that took in the trees, the dry grass, the shimmering white distances at the edge of the property. "I know what this looks like in my sleep."

Paul stood up and stretched his back, pressing his hands against his lumbar the way a man twice his age might do. He looked down the row at the Bartlett trees, their great dark canopies casting the ground into a green-gray shade. "Dad looked at this in his sleep too," he said. "It didn't save it."

"It's not gone yet."

"No. Not yet."

They continued digging. When the trench was wide enough and deep enough they could see the full extent of the fracture, and it was worse than it had looked at the surface—the pipe had not simply cracked but had begun to separate at a joint some eight inches north of the split, the rubber gasket dried and crumbled to the texture of old tobacco. Dominic crouched in the trench and examined it and felt the familiar sinking that was not despair exactly but was adjacent to despair, was despair's responsible, work-shirted cousin.

"We need to replace a longer section," he said.

"How much longer."

"Maybe two feet. Maybe more. Let me see if the coupling's the right size first."

He climbed out of the trench and opened the cardboard box. The coupling kit was correct—one-inch barbed fittings, two of them, and a length of flex connector—and he held up the connector to show Paul, who nodded and began to dig again, extending the trench.

The two-nine that Paul was talking about was two million nine hundred thousand dollars. Dominic had done the math. He'd done it at three in the morning lying next to his wife in the dark, and he'd done it in the truck on the way to the hardware store in Visalia, and he'd done it sitting in the bleachers at his daughter's soccer game, watching her run in the heat without looking like it cost her anything at all, the way children run. The math was not complicated. The math was, in fact, seductive in its simplicity, which was part of the problem with the math. Two million nine hundred thousand dollars was enough to pay off the operating debt, the line of credit they'd extended twice in the last four years, the equipment loan, the property taxes that were two cycles behind, and still have enough left to put Dominic's daughter and Paul's two boys through whatever school they chose and have something remaining that could be called a future. The math made a kind of sense that could not be argued with in the daylight, and which became, in the dark, unbearable.

"You know what Hendricks wants to do with it," Dominic said.

"Build houses."

"Sixty-three units, Paul. He had a rendering. I saw it on his phone. Sixty-three units on ninety-two acres of—"

"I know what's on ninety-two acres."

"Pear trees that are forty years older than we are."

"That are producing seventeen percent under projections and have been for six years." Paul stopped digging and leaned on his spade. His face was red with heat, and there was a streak of clay on his jaw that made him look younger, that made him look, for a moment, like the boy he'd been when they first worked this ground together, following their father between the rows in the early morning learning the names of things. "I'm not the villain here, Nic. I don't want to be the villain."

"I'm not calling you the villain."

"You have this way—"

"I know I have a way. I'm not calling you the villain."

Paul went back to digging. The trench had reached its necessary length and they began to cut the damaged section of pipe. Dominic used the cutter, pressing the blade against the pipe wall and rotating, and the cut was clean. He moved to the second mark and cut again and lifted the damaged section out and laid it on the ground beside the trench. It looked smaller out of context, that ruined piece of pipe—a foot and a half of white poly that had given up, that had finally admitted what the heat and pressure and years had been asking it to admit.

Paul picked it up and turned it in his hands. He put his finger into the fracture. "What's the water pressure running at on this zone?"

"Forty-five, maybe fifty."

"Should be forty."

"Yeah. The regulator's been drifting."

"We need to fix the regulator."

"We need to fix a lot of things."

"That's not an argument for selling. That's an argument for fixing things."

"I know it is," Paul said. He set down the pipe section. "I know that's what it is."

They worked without talking for a while. Dominic fitted the barbed connector into one end of the new pipe section and Paul fitted the other, and they used the small worm-gear clamps to secure the connections and lowered the assembly into the trench, connecting it to the existing line at both ends. It was not elegant work but it was solid work, and when they were done and had packed the clay back around the pipe and turned the zone valve back on and walked back to check for leaks they found none, only the wet dark earth around the repair and the resuming sigh of emitters opening further down the row, the ground beginning to accept the water it had been waiting for.

"He wants to walk it on Friday," Paul said again. They were standing in the shade of the biggest Bartlett, the patriarch tree, the one their grandfather had brought as a whip from a nursery in Modesto and planted by hand. Its canopy was perhaps forty feet across. In June the fruits were still green and hard, two months from harvest. "Just a walk. I'm not saying it's a decision."

"Bringing him out here is most of a decision."

"It's a conversation."

"With a developer."

"With a man who's offering us a way out of a situation that is—" Paul stopped. He pressed the back of his hand to his mouth and looked down the row and then looked at his brother. "Nic, I have sixty thousand dollars in operating debt that I personally guaranteed. Not the partnership. Me. My name. I did that two years ago when we needed the equipment and I didn't tell you and I'm telling you now." He said it plainly, without dramatics, the way you'd confess to a small but important lie, the way the weight of having carried it was audible in the telling. "I didn't tell you because you would have argued and you would have been right and we needed the harvester. But that's where I am. That's what my name is attached to."

Dominic looked at his brother for a long time. The mockingbird had followed them down the row and was still at its performance in the canopy above, its inventions becoming more abstract, further from any original, cycling now through something that might have been a red-winged blackbird's call or might have been pure fiction. "Sixty," Dominic said.

"Yes."

"Personally guaranteed."

"Yes."

"Jesus, Paul."

"I know."

"You should have—"

"I know I should have. I know it."

Dominic sat down on the ground at the base of the old Bartlett, his back against the tree's great furrowed trunk. He'd sat in this spot as a child and as a young man and in his thirties and now, at thirty-eight, he sat here again and looked at the same view—the rows running east, the almond blocks of the neighboring property just visible at the boundary, the water tower their father had painted white in 1987 and which had been weathering back to rust ever since, patiently resuming its original color. His father had stood on a ladder to paint it, and Dominic had been nine years old, and he remembered his mother calling from the porch that it was time for lunch and his father saying five more minutes in the voice he used when he meant twenty.

Paul remained standing. He was not good at sitting in the dirt; he'd never been, even as a kid. He stood with his arms folded against the heat and watched his brother and waited.

"If we sell," Dominic said, "where do you go."

"What do you mean."

"I mean where do you go. Where does Paul Serrano go when there's no orchard."

"I go wherever. I get a job. I figure it out." But his voice was careful around the words, as though they were load-bearing and he was uncertain of them.

"You've been here your whole life."

"So have you."

"I have a wife. I have Sophie. I have—there are other things around me."

Paul unfolded his arms and put his hands in his pockets. He was quiet for a moment. "That's a kind of cruelty, Nic. You know that."

"I'm not trying to be cruel. I'm asking a real question."

"The question implies I have nothing."

"The question implies you're rooted here in a way that I'm not sure the money accounts for."

A hawk crossed the open sky at the end of the row, tilting without effort on a thermal, moving south. They both watched it. This was something their father had also done, stopping whatever he was doing to watch a hawk, and neither of them had ever remarked on this inheritance until right now, when Paul said quietly, "Dad used to watch them like he was expecting a message," and Dominic said, "I know," and for a moment they were entirely in agreement about something.

"I'll talk to Teresa about the debt," Dominic said. "Before Friday."

"You don't have to—"

"I'm going to talk to her. We'll figure out what we can do. Maybe there's a refinance option we haven't—"

"We've looked at the refinance options."

"Maybe there's one we haven't."

Paul was quiet. His face had something in it that was either gratitude or the reluctance to receive gratitude, and with Paul it had always been hard to tell the difference. "I'm not trying to lose this," he said at last. "I need you to know that. I'm not trying to lose it. I'm trying to not lose everything else along with it."

"I know," Dominic said.

"The trees don't know that, maybe. But I know it."

"The trees don't know anything."

"Dad thought they did."

"Dad thought the almighty Bartlett pear tree had opinions about the harvest schedule. Dad thought the tractor had feelings."

"He talked to the tractor," Paul said, and something shifted in his face, some old grief and old love moving through him, and he laughed—a short, private laugh that was really a different kind of sound wearing a laugh's clothing. "He apologized to it. When it wouldn't start. He'd say, now listen, and then apologize."

"He apologized to the pipe once," Dominic said. "I watched him. He was fixing a split—different pipe, different row, but the same business—and he got it open and looked at it and said, I'm sorry, I should have caught this sooner."

They were both quiet with the memory of it. The heat was fully established now, that mid-morning heat of the valley that was not going anywhere. The emitters ticked quietly in the soil down the row, the repaired zone performing its function, water finding its way to the roots of the old trees.

"Let him walk it," Dominic said.

Paul looked at him.

"On Friday. Let him walk it. That doesn't commit us to anything and it might—I don't know. It might show him something he doesn't want to build on. These trees." He looked up at the canopy, at the green fruit hanging in the leaf shadows. "Maybe when he sees them."

"Men like Hendricks don't see trees," Paul said, not unkindly. "They see land. They see zoning classifications and water rights and setbacks."

"Maybe. But let him walk."

Paul nodded slowly, not agreement so much as acknowledgment—the way you nod when someone offers a compromise that is not quite right but is not quite wrong either, that lives in the honest middle country between yes and no. He picked up his spade. Dominic picked up his. They collected the pipe cutter and the remnant poly and the empty coupling box and began to walk back north along the row, back toward the access road and the equipment shed, into the increasing white heat of the morning.

They passed the nectarines. Dominic stopped and turned a fruit in his hand without picking it, feeling its density. Still two weeks from ready, maybe three, but there was a blush starting at the shoulder of it that was encouraging. He let it go and it turned back to its original position on the branch, as though it had not been touched.

Paul had stopped a little ahead and was looking at a tree Dominic had been watching for two weeks, a Bartlett at the south end of the row whose foliage had been thinning at the crown—not fire blight, the extension agent had said when Dominic sent photos, more likely crown rot at the root collar, the result of repeated over-irrigation that had compromised the rootstock. The tree could be managed, the agent had said, with adjusted irrigation scheduling and possibly a fungicide drench, but it would be a slow recovery if it recovered at all, and it would not be a productive tree in the meantime.

"This one's worse," Paul said.

Dominic came and stood beside him. The crown thinning had progressed; what had been concerning two weeks ago was now definitive, the sparse upper foliage yellowing against the blue sky, the small fruits still clinging but dull, without the green vitality of the trees around it.

"I've been adjusting the emitter run times on this zone," Dominic said. "Trying to let it dry down a little between cycles."

"Will it help?"

"Might. Might be too far along." He studied the tree, its great old trunk showing no outward sign of the rot that the agent believed was working at its base, the bark still dense and complex as ever. It was possible to stand at the trunk and look up through the crown and see the patches of sky that shouldn't be there, sky showing through where there should be leaf and branch and fruit. "Grandfather planted this one," Dominic said. "This specific tree."

"He planted all of them."

"I know. But I always think of this one. He's got it in the photograph. The one in the album. He's holding it like a—it's a whip, it's nothing, it's a four-foot stick with roots, and he's holding it out at arm's length like he's showing off a fish."

Paul smiled, looking at the tree. "He'd have hated to hear you call it a stick."

"He would have apologized to it for me."

The hawk, or another hawk, crossed the sky at the row's end again. Dominic watched it until it was gone. Behind them the irrigation emitters continued their small patient work, water moving through the repaired pipe into the cracked earth, reaching for the roots of trees that had been in this ground longer than either of them had been alive, that had been fruiting and resting and fruiting again through their childhood and their father's middle age and their grandfather's old age and were still, in this present June, performing their slow annual transformation of water and light into something that could be eaten, something that could be held in the hand and carried away and tasted, containing within it, in whatever sense such things are ever contained, all the years of its making.
