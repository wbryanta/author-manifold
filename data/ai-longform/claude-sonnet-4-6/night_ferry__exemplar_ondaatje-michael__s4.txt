He had bought the ticket in the afternoon when the light was still flat and grey over the harbour, the kind of light that made everything look like a photograph of itself. The ferry did not leave until nine. He had five hours and he spent them in the terminal café drinking coffee that tasted of the plastic cup it came in, watching the trawlers come in with their loads, the gulls working the air above them with a patience that was not patience at all but pure mechanical hunger. His name was David Calder. He had not seen his brother in eleven years.

The call had come from a woman named Svensson at the regional office in Hammerfest, her voice careful and official the way voices are when they are required to deliver uncertain news. We have found a man, she said. We cannot yet confirm. There are some difficulties with the identification. She used that word, difficulties. He had stood in his kitchen in Tromsø holding the phone and looking out at the water and thinking about how the word difficulties could mean so many things, could hold inside it a body so altered by cold or time or water that the usual methods had failed, or could mean simply that the paperwork was incomplete, that his brother had once again moved through the world leaving no proper trace of himself. Both of these possibilities seemed equally like Morten.

Now it was just past eight and he carried his bag down to the dock. The ferry was already lit, its white hull luminous against the darkening water, and somewhere deep in its machinery a low vibration had begun, barely perceptible, the way you feel rather than hear a sound when you press your ear against something living.

He found a seat near the stern on the upper passenger deck. A few other travellers arranged themselves at intervals around the long room, each carefully separate from the others in the way of people on night journeys who have something private they are carrying. A woman in her sixties was reading a novel with her finger keeping place on the page even when she looked up. Two men in work clothes slept already, their heads tipped back, mouths open to the recirculated air. David put his bag on the seat beside him and looked out the window at the dock and at the lit windows of the terminal building and at the dark water gathering between the hull and the pier as the ferry began, almost imperceptibly, to move.

He thought about the last time he had seen Morten. It was not at a moment of drama or crisis, nothing you could point to and say: there, that was when the cord finally snapped. It had been at their mother's apartment after the funeral, the two of them standing in the kitchen while the few remaining guests finished their drinks in the next room and the smell of too many flowers came in under the door. Morten was wearing their father's old cardigan, the brown one with the wooden buttons, which none of them had ever seen their mother throw away. He looked thin in it, the shoulders too wide for him though Morten was not a small man. He was eating a piece of bread and butter and staring at the kitchen wall. David could not remember now what he had said, or even whether he had said anything. He remembered the light in that kitchen, the afternoon light coming through the yellow curtain, and Morten's hand holding the bread, and the way the wooden buttons of the cardigan were worn smooth at their edges from years of use. Then Morten set the bread down on the counter and walked out of the kitchen and David heard him say something brief and unintelligible to the people in the next room and then heard the apartment door close, not slammed, just firmly shut, with a sound that contained no uncertainty about itself.

The ferry moved through the inner harbour and then turned north into the strait. The water was calm but not glassy, with a low swell running from the northwest that gave the ship a slow, almost meditative roll. David watched the lights of the city diminish. He had grown up around water, had spent his childhood watching his father move through boats the way other men move through rooms, with an authority that seemed effortless and was not. His father had died believing that Morten would eventually come right. He had used that phrase, come right, as though his younger son were a navigational problem that would resolve itself given time and correct instrumentation.

What David knew about the last eleven years came to him in fragments, relayed through his mother before she died and afterward through Morten's occasional girlfriend, Anita, who called twice and then stopped. Morten had worked on supply boats out of Bodø for three years. He had lived for a while in a cabin near Kautokeino with a man whose name David was never told. He had been in Tromsø once, perhaps two years ago, staying with someone from the boats, and had not called. This last detail his mother had kept from him until she was already sick and the keeping no longer seemed necessary, or perhaps she told him because she wanted him to carry it too, the weight of that unreturned call. He had been angry at the time. Now he thought he understood something about it, though he could not have said precisely what.

A young man came down the aisle selling coffee from a cart. David bought a cup and held it in both hands and watched the darkness where the strait opened out, the scattered lights of what might have been a coastal settlement or a group of boats, impossible to tell at this distance. He thought about bodies. He thought about how a body becomes unfamiliar to you over time, how the body you knew at thirty is not the same body you would be asked to identify at forty-three, the face thickened or hollowed, the hands altered by the work they had done, the particular way a person held themselves in the world changed by years of weather or alcohol or simply by the slow pressure of unhappiness. He had a photograph in his wallet, taken on a boat in the summer their father was still healthy, Morten twenty-six, squinting into the sun with his hand raised not quite to shade his eyes, a gesture that looked like the beginning of a wave. He had looked at it in the café that afternoon until it ceased to mean anything and became instead pure shape and light, the way a word does when you say it too many times.

The thing he most remembered about Morten's face was the quality of his attention. When Morten looked at something he looked at it completely, no part of him withheld. This was the quality that had made him difficult, and also the quality that David had most envied and most feared in him. He remembered watching Morten look at a woman at a party once, years ago, long before the trouble between them. She had been talking to someone else and was unaware of Morten's regard, and there was nothing predatory in it, nothing that could have been called desire exactly, more a quality of witness, as though Morten were memorising her in case she were needed later for some purpose he had not yet determined. The woman had eventually turned and seen Morten looking and had smiled, uncertain, the way you smile when you cannot interpret what you're being offered.

David got up and went out to the stern deck. The air was very cold and smelled of salt and diesel and something faintly organic he could not identify. The wake of the ferry spread behind them, white against the black water, lit by the ship's own lights, and beyond it the darkness where Tromsø had been was now only a faint paleness on the horizon that might have been cloud. He stood at the railing and let the cold come into him. He had not slept the previous night and his exhaustion by now had passed through the stage of heaviness and become instead a kind of lightness, a slight unreality in everything he touched and heard, as though the world were rehearsing itself, not quite committed yet to being what it was.

He thought about what Svensson had said when he asked how the body had been found. She paused before answering. He was found by the woman who owned the cabin, she said. He had been there for some time. David did not ask her to be more specific. He knew what some time meant in that climate. He knew what it meant to a body.

Morten had always loved the furthest north. Even as a child he had talked about it the way other children talked about places they had seen in films, with the authority of someone who has been there in a dream so vivid it constitutes a kind of travel. Their father had taken them both to Nordkapp when Morten was twelve and David fifteen, a summer trip, and while David had been disappointed by the tourist infrastructure, the coaches and cafes at the top of the world, Morten had walked to the edge of the cliff and stood there a long time looking north across the open Barents Sea with an expression that David could only describe as recognition. As though the sea had finally confirmed something he had always suspected about the nature of things.

The cold was becoming serious and he went back inside. The woman with the novel had fallen asleep, the book open face-down on her lap at an angle that would have troubled its spine. One of the working men had pulled a jacket over himself. The fluorescent light in the cabin was the colour of something institutional, a hospital corridor or a waiting room, and under it everyone's face looked simplified, all the nuance drained out, what remained the essential structure, the arrangement of bone.

David sat down and closed his eyes. He tried to sleep and instead found himself thinking about a summer when he and Morten were very young, before any of the trouble, before they understood the particular way their family had been assembled wrong. They had been in the garden of their grandmother's house in the Lofotens for two weeks one August, the kind of summer that seems in retrospect to have had no bad weather, though this was obviously not true. He remembered Morten building something in the corner of the garden with whatever material came to hand, pieces of driftwood and rope and flat stones from the path. It was a structure with no clear purpose, not quite a house, not quite a boat, something between. David had asked him what he was building and Morten looked up with that focused, complete attention and said, I don't know yet. As though the building itself would eventually clarify its own intention.

This seemed to David now a way of describing something about his brother's life. That he had been building something from available materials whose purpose was never quite revealed to him, or to anyone else.

The ferry moved north through the strait. Around three in the morning David woke in his chair to find the cabin darker and quieter, the overhead fluorescents dimmed to a few emergency-level bulbs, the other passengers sleeping. The motion of the ship had changed slightly, the roll more pronounced, and outside the windows he could see nothing at all, only the darkness and occasional whitecaps catching the light from the hull. He felt for a moment entirely outside of time, the journey itself a kind of suspension, neither the city behind him nor the harbour ahead, only this crossing, this being-between.

He thought about what he would say when he saw the body. He had assumed there were things you said. That there was a procedure for this. He had identified his father's body eleven years ago in the hospital in Tromsø after the stroke, and he remembered the nurse lifting the sheet and his father's face being so profoundly still that it seemed to David less like death than like the idea of his father, the way you might see someone's face in the moment before you've fully recognised them, just the pure undetermined arrangement, before memory and history overlay it with everything you know. He had stood there for longer than was necessary. He had felt no desire to touch the hand.

With Morten it would be different or it would be the same. He did not know. There was a possibility he was preparing himself for that it was not Morten at all, that the body in the cabin near Hammerfest had nothing to do with him, that somewhere Morten was still moving through the world leaving his incomplete traces, sleeping on boats or in borrowed cabins, looking at the sea with that quality of total attention, building his purposeless structures from whatever materials the place provided. This possibility lived alongside the other one, and David found he could not yet determine which he was hoping for.

He remembered once, perhaps the last good time between them, a drive up the E6 in autumn when Morten had borrowed a car from someone and appeared at David's apartment at six in the morning and would not tell him where they were going. The birch trees had been turning, long slopes of yellow and orange against the dark rock, and Morten drove with one hand on the wheel, very relaxed, occasionally pointing out something in the landscape without speaking. They had stopped at a lake somewhere north of Narvik and eaten sandwiches that Morten had brought and sat on the rocks for a long time. Morten had been between jobs, or between lives perhaps. He had talked about a woman in Bodø, not Anita, someone earlier, and his voice when he talked about her had a particular quality, measured and careful, as though he were describing something breakable. They had driven home in the dark and Morten had dropped him at his apartment and driven away in the borrowed car and David stood on the pavement and watched the tail-lights diminish down the road and had the feeling, not a premonition exactly, more a species of clarity, that this was one of the last uncomplicated times, that something was already shifting beneath the surface of their lives. He had gone upstairs and made tea and stood at his window drinking it, unable to sleep.

He had never asked Morten the right questions. He understood this clearly now, in the dimmed cabin of the ferry moving north through the darkness, the roll of the water under them like breathing. There were things he should have asked. He had been afraid of the answers or had thought the time for asking had not yet come, that there was still time, that time was something that would continue to be available to them. He wanted now to be able to go back to that kitchen in his mother's apartment, the yellow curtain and the afternoon light, and ask Morten before he picked up the piece of bread, before he walked out through the door. Something simple. What is happening to you. Or even simpler. Are you all right. Three words that might have changed the angle of things.

But you could not. The past was not a place you could re-enter and make different. It sat behind you, finished, the way the wake of the ferry sat behind the ship, white and fully formed and rapidly becoming dark.

Around five in the morning a thin grey light began at the edge of the sky to the east. David had slept a little more and woke with the specific disorientation of a face that has been leaning against a cold window. He sat up and rubbed his neck and looked out. They were entering the outer harbour, the hills visible as dark shapes against the lighter sky, a few lights on in the town, a crane at the dock lit like a question mark against the pale air. The engine note changed, dropping as the ship began to slow, and around the cabin people stirred and gathered their things.

He sat still a moment before standing. He looked at the photograph again in the pale light coming through the window, Morten squinting into the summer sun with his hand raised in that ambiguous gesture, half-greeting and half-shielding. David put it back in his wallet.

He would walk off the ferry into the cold morning. He would take the taxi to the regional office and meet Svensson, whose voice he already knew. She would take him somewhere, a room in the hospital or the police building, and she would open a door, and there would be a body under a sheet, and she would fold the sheet back. And then he would know something, one thing or the other, and the not-knowing that had sat inside him for eleven years like a stone in water would be replaced by something harder or something else entirely, and he would have to begin the long work of living with whichever it was.

The ferry docked and the passengers moved toward the exits. The woman with the novel tucked it under her arm still open at her page, still keeping her place, still refusing to let go of wherever she was in it. David picked up his bag and followed the others down the corridor toward the cold air and the grey morning and the town he did not know, waiting on the other side of the door.
