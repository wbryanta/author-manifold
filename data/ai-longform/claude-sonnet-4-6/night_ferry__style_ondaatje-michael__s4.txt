The ferry leaves at eleven, which means he has two hours in the terminal building drinking bad coffee from a machine that takes his coins without acknowledgment. Outside, the strait is invisible. He knows it is there because he was born thirty miles east of here and because the water has always been the organizing fact of this landscape, the way a wound organizes a body, pulling everything toward itself. But tonight there is only fog and the orange smear of dock lights, and he sits in the plastic chair with his bag between his feet and tries to remember his brother's hands.

This is what he has been doing for six hours, since the call came. Going through the inventory. The hands first, because hands are what you notice about a person when you stop looking at their face. The face you learn too well, you stop seeing it, but hands remain strange. His brother's hands were wide across the knuckles, the fingers slightly too short for the palm, and there was a scar along the base of the left thumb from a gutting knife that had slipped in 1987, the year their father was dying and they were both working the processing plant on rotating shifts, sleeping in the same bed at different hours, leaving notes on the kitchen table. *Milk. Your turn for dishes. I'll be back by six.* Communication reduced to its functions. He hadn't thought about those notes in years. He thinks about them now, in the terminal, the coffee cooling in his hand.

The woman at the information desk has given up pretending to work and is looking at her phone with the open, uncomplicated attention people give to things they know won't judge them. He is the only passenger in the waiting area. There are two other men near the door who seem to belong to the ferry itself, in the way of people who are always slightly ahead of a departure, who know things about the machinery that makes motion possible.

He thinks: it may not be him.

He has been thinking this since the call. The police in Fagerström — a constable with a voice like water running under ice, careful and specific — had said *a man approximately your brother's age* and had said *no identification on the body* and had said *we found your name in a notebook.* Which means almost nothing. His brother knew many people. His brother was the kind of man who collected the names of people he intended to return to and often didn't, which was different from not caring, though it took years to understand the difference.

He met a woman once, at a party in the city, who had known his brother the way you know someone in a period of your life you've since sealed off. *He was very present,* she said, which seemed to him an odd thing to say about a person who was known for disappearing. But he understood it. When his brother was there he was absolutely there, all his attention directed at you like a kind of pressure, like weather. And then he wasn't there, and the absence had the same quality — enormous, specific, hard to look away from.

He boards the ferry at ten-fifty. A deckhand in orange rain gear takes his ticket without looking at him and he finds a seat in the main cabin near a salt-smeared window. The engines, already running, change in tone as the gangway lifts. He has crossed this strait perhaps thirty times in his life. He knows its moods. In summer it is blue-grey and cold even on clear days, and the islands rise out of it like the backs of sleeping animals, and you can stand on the upper deck and watch the birds working the wake. In winter it is this: opaque, the water black when you can see it, the fog sitting down on everything, the ferry moving through something that is less like water than like the interior of a decision not yet made.

He doesn't sleep. He has a book in his bag but doesn't take it out. A family three rows ahead of him — two parents, a child of perhaps four who should be sleeping and isn't — make the sounds of people contained in a moving space, the child's occasional question, the murmured answer, the quality of attention you give to a small person who doesn't yet understand that the world continues when they're not looking at it. He watches them without watching them. He is thinking about the summer his brother turned twenty-three.

This was after the fish plant and after their father and after the winter that came so early it caught the boats. His brother had gone north on a hunch, or what passed for a hunch in a person who didn't believe in luck but acted as though he did. He'd taken work on a survey crew, the kind of work that put you in small planes over landscapes that had no interest in being named. He came back that autumn changed in a way that neither of them could articulate. Not hardened — the word people use when they mean someone has been disappointed. More like something had been added. Some orientation, some knowledge of where he stood in relation to things. He talked less about the future. He began to be good at sitting still.

That was also the year their mother started forgetting things. Small things at first: names of people she'd known for decades, the location of the spare key. Then larger things, the shape of a day, the tense of certain events, and she would talk about their father as though he were merely away somewhere, as though the elsewhere he inhabited was geographic rather than permanent. His brother handled this with a patience that astonished him. He would sit with her for hours in the kitchen, not trying to correct her, just keeping her company in whatever time she was living in. He thought then that his brother understood something about loss that he didn't, some way of being inside it without being destroyed by it. He has spent the years since trying to understand what that way was.

The ferry moves. Around him people sleep in the uncomfortable positions of people who have learned to sleep anywhere. The child is finally quiet. He presses his face briefly to the window and sees nothing, only his own reflection made partial by the dark — an eye, the side of a jaw, the pale fact of his forehead — and beneath it the vague running of water against the hull.

There was a period, ten years ago, or perhaps twelve, when they had stopped speaking. He tries to fix the beginning of this in time and cannot. It was not a single event. It was more like a shore that keeps receding while you're not paying attention, and then one day you look up and the water is very deep and the shore is far away and you can't quite remember when you last felt the ground. There had been an argument, yes — about their mother's house, about what should be done with it after she went into the care facility, about whether the past was something you owed to or something you left. But the argument was not the cause. The argument was only where the distance became visible.

He had told himself, over the years, that it was his brother who'd pulled away. But sitting in the cabin of a ferry in the middle of a fog-covered strait, he understands that this is the story you tell when you need one person to be responsible for a space that two people made.

His brother sent a postcard four years ago from somewhere in the interior — a photograph of spruce trees in late light, the sky that particular shade of amber that only exists for twenty minutes in autumn in high latitudes. On the back, in his brother's cramped and slightly upright hand: *Still here. Hope you are somewhere good.* He had put it on his kitchen windowsill and it had moved through several iterations of his life since then, following him from apartment to apartment with the accumulating indifference of objects that once mattered and whose mattering you can't bring yourself to end.

The crossing takes four hours in good weather. Tonight will take longer. He goes to the small cafeteria at the stern and buys another coffee and a sandwich he doesn't eat, and sits at a table bolted to the floor and watches the other insomniac passengers with the solidarity of people who are all, in their different ways, moving toward something they haven't seen yet.

He is thinking about identification. What it means to identify a body. The word used in its legal sense, which is also its most primitive sense — to make identical, to declare sameness, to say: this is the one. He has never done this before. He knows, in the abstract, what it involves: a room, a face, the particular bureaucratic tenderness of people who do this kind of work, who have learned to create space for a thing that has no ordinary place in ordinary life.

What he doesn't know is what he will feel.

He has always believed that he would know his brother anywhere, in any condition, the way you believe you know your own name — not because you've tested it but because it is the thing against which all other knowing is measured. But sitting in this cafeteria that smells of old coffee and diesel and the specific stale warmth of enclosed spaces, he is less certain. His brother is fifty-one years old. He hasn't seen him in four years, and before that infrequently, and before that only in the way of people who are in the same orbit but moving at different speeds. He knows his brother's face at thirty, at forty. He knows the way the grey came in at the temples, the way certain expressions had deepened into permanent geography around the eyes and mouth. But fifty-one is a decade he hasn't seen. And the body they will show him — the constable with the water voice had said *found near the water, outside Fagerström, conditions consistent with exposure* — will be a body that has been outside in November, which does certain things to a face.

He wraps his hands around the coffee cup. The sea is doing something against the hull, a different rhythm than before, and the lights in the cafeteria sway incrementally, the way they do when the crossing gets serious.

He had a dream, in the years of the silence between them, that he has never told anyone. In the dream he and his brother were in the water — not drowning, not struggling, but simply in the water of the strait, at night, and the lights of the ferry were visible in the distance, moving away from them. They were not frightened. The water held them. They were talking, though he could never remember afterward what they said, only the feeling of it, the ease of the conversation, the way you speak to someone when the stakes of the moment have stripped everything down to what you actually mean. He woke from this dream several times over the years and lay in the dark feeling the specific grief of having been close to something.

He wonders if his brother dreamed of him.

This is a foolish thing to wonder and he wonders it anyway.

The family he observed earlier — the parents, the child — come into the cafeteria and the child, now awake with the complete commitment of a small person who has decided to be awake, climbs onto a seat across from him and stares at him with the candid attention of someone who has not yet learned that staring is supposed to be impolite. He looks back at the child. The child has dark hair cut bluntly across the forehead and eyes that are not quite any color in the cafeteria light and the expression of someone listening to something he can't hear. After a long moment the child says: *where are you going.*

He says: to Fagerström.

The child considers this. The mother, settling into the seat beside the child with a container of juice, gives him the brief apologetic look of a person managing a small force of nature. He shakes his head: it's fine.

*Why,* the child says.

He says: to see someone.

This appears to satisfy the child, who turns to the juice container with the focused application you give to a mechanical problem. He watches the family assemble itself around the small table, the minor ordinances of it, the passing of things, the quiet negotiation of space and attention. The father catches his eye and nods in the way of men who are not natural nodders but have learned to do it as a form of acknowledgment, and he nods back, and this is enough.

He thinks about what he will say if it is his brother. He thinks about what he will say if it isn't.

In either case there will be a version of the same action: a returning. To his car, to the highway, to his apartment in the city with its kitchen windowsill and its accumulated evidence of a life. He will drive back in the early morning, the fog perhaps lifting or perhaps not, and the landscape on either side of the highway will be the landscape he has always known, the spruce and the granite and the occasional farm with its yard light burning against whatever is out there. And he will carry with him, as he has been carrying it for twenty years, the specific weight of an unfinished thing.

But if it is his brother.

He will stand in a room in Fagerström and he will look at a face and he will say yes or no, and in that moment something will close, some long circuit of uncertainty will complete itself, and he will know not only that his brother is dead but that the years of distance and the argument about the house and the postcards and the silence — that all of this is now final in a way it wasn't before, because before, at least, there was the theoretical possibility of a conversation. A phone call. A Saturday afternoon when one of them finally drove to where the other was.

He knew a man once who had lost a twin, and the man said that the worst part was not the grief, which was enormous and expected, but the phantom quality of his own reflection afterward. *I kept thinking,* the man said, *I kept thinking that I should look different. That I would look different. But I looked exactly the same, and that was the wrong thing.* He has thought about this for years, though he is not a twin and his brother is — was — is — a year older than him, and the situation is not analogous. But there is something in it he recognizes, the way the self uses the other person as a reference point without knowing it, and then one day the reference is gone, and you are no longer sure of your own coordinates.

The ferry shudders through a larger wave and the lights hold. The cafeteria attendant, a young man with the resigned expression of someone working a shift that ends nowhere near morning, wipes down the counter with the thoroughness of a person who has decided that the quality of this one action matters absolutely. The child is asleep against the mother's arm. He watches the attendant wipe the counter. He watches the father looking at his phone and then out the window at the dark and then at his sleeping child with an expression that is not quite any single thing.

He came to identify his father's body on a Tuesday in November. Twenty-three years ago. He remembers the drive to the hospital, the early dark, the way the road felt unfamiliar even though he had driven it hundreds of times. He remembers the attendant at the hospital who spoke to him with professional gentleness, and the corridor, and the door, and the room with its particular quality of light. He had not been prepared for how much his father looked like himself. Like the specific, ordinary person he had been. He had expected something translated, something that announced its own finality. But his father looked like his father, only still, only quiet, only no longer using his face for anything.

He had stood there for a long time.

His brother, who had arrived an hour later because he was coming from farther away, stood beside him and neither of them spoke and then his brother put his hand on his shoulder and they stood that way for a while, and this was the last time he could remember being in a room with his brother and feeling that they were both fully present, both in the same hour.

He finishes the coffee. The cafeteria is very quiet now, the engines a low constant chord beneath everything. Outside, the fog is doing what fog does, which is to make the world intimate and without distances, to make everything equally close. He has perhaps an hour before they dock. He should try to sleep. He knows he won't.

He takes the postcard from his jacket pocket. He has been carrying it for days, since before the call, one of those small private habits you don't notice until you notice them. The amber sky, the spruce trees, the brief handwriting on the back. *Still here. Hope you are somewhere good.* He looks at it for a long time. He turns it over and looks at the spruce trees and the sky. He thinks about his brother standing somewhere in the interior, in that light, taking a photograph he thought was worth sending. Deciding to send it. Writing those words.

The somewhere good. He has often wondered if his brother knew, when he wrote it, how that phrase would sound. Whether it was simple — a wish, uncomplicated — or whether there was something else in it, some awareness of everything that the word *somewhere* was doing, holding all the unspoken geography of their estrangement.

He puts the postcard back in his pocket.

The ferry horn sounds once, distant and absolute, the sound of something announcing its own existence to a world that can't see it. The engines change. He feels in the soles of his feet the subtle alteration that means they are slowing, means the crossing is nearly done, means the lights of Fagerström are somewhere ahead in the fog, not yet visible, but there. The constable will meet him at the terminal. There will be a car, a drive, a building, a room, a door. He will walk through it.

He sits with his hands open on the table in front of him, the way his brother used to sit when he was thinking — palms up, not quite an offering, more like a readiness. He is not aware that he is doing this. The child sleeps. The attendant stacks cups. The father puts his phone away and looks at his wife and she looks back and something passes between them that has no name but which he recognizes, the look of two people who have been through a night together.

The fog presses against the windows. He breathes. He is here, moving, and the water is doing its work beneath him, and somewhere ahead the lights are already on in the building where he is expected.
