The hotel had been rebuilt in the same proportions as the old one, which was the first thing she noticed — that the architects had been faithful, or perhaps simply unimaginative, and had reproduced the original facade with only minor gestures toward modernity. The limestone facing was paler now, almost white in the October light, and the windows on the upper floors were slightly wider than she remembered, but the general arrangement of the place, the way it presented itself at the end of the long drive with that particular combination of grandeur and apology, was exactly as it had been. Eleanor stood beside the hired car while the driver removed her bag from the boot, and she looked at the hotel the way you might look at someone you once knew in a condition of great intimacy and have since been asked to meet again in ordinary social circumstances.

She was fifty-three years old. She had been nineteen when she worked here, when the fire started in what they eventually determined was a linen cupboard on the fourth floor, an electrical fault, a frayed wire behind the shelving where the extra pillows were stored. She had been on the second floor when the alarms went. She remembered the sound of them, which she had heard in tests before and which had always produced in her a mild and dutiful response, as if the alarm were a kind of civic request she would honor without enthusiasm. But that night the sound had been different in quality or she had heard it differently, and before she'd understood anything consciously she had already left the room she was turning down and was moving toward the service stairs.

Twenty-two people had been in the hotel that night. Seventeen had gotten out. The three chambermaids on duty — Eleanor, a Polish woman named Basia who was ten years older and whose surname Eleanor had never learned, and a young man named Cormac who everyone called by his surname, Finneran — had all survived. The four who died were two guests on the fourth floor, one of the night porters, and a kitchen hand who had gone back in, it was said, because he'd left his phone. He was from Laos and his name was reported in the newspapers as Khamla, though one paper rendered it incorrectly and Eleanor, reading the coverage from her mother's kitchen table, had felt a specific, unreasonable anger at this, an anger she later understood was misdirected grief. She had never met Khamla. She had eaten food he had plated and sent up on trays, but she had never seen his face.

The driver said something about the time, about needing to be back in Galway before seven, and she thanked him and took her bag. The doorman — there was a doorman again, this had been a feature of the original — offered to take it, but she said she was fine. She crossed the forecourt on the gravel that was fresh and white and did not crunch in quite the same way as old gravel does.

Inside, the lobby was arranged similarly to how it had been, which was to say there was a large central space with a patterned floor, a desk to the right, and a staircase that rose and then divided at a landing. But the carpet was different, a deep burgundy now instead of the dark green she remembered, and the light fittings were modern, low and recessed in the ceiling. Where there had once been a fireplace in the alcove to the left there was now a seating area with low chairs in a pale fabric. Eleanor stood still for a moment. A young woman at the desk smiled at her with professional warmth.

She said her name and the young woman found her in the system and processed the booking and said everything you'd expect a person behind a hotel desk to say. Eleanor accepted her key card — they used key cards now; in her time the keys had been actual keys on heavy brass fobs — and was told the function room was at the end of the corridor on the ground floor, that the evening reception began at six, that if she needed anything. She nodded through it. She was thinking about where the linen cupboard had been. It would have been directly above her, on the fourth floor, at the far end of the corridor that ran behind this lobby. She imagined the framing of the rebuilt hotel, the timber and steel inside the limestone skin, and the place within that structure where a new cupboard now existed with its own shelves and its own linens and its own buried wires.

Her room was on the second floor. She took the stairs. The corridor was narrower than she remembered, or perhaps she had simply grown larger in her imagination of it. She found her room and put her bag on the luggage stand and sat on the edge of the bed for a while, not thinking about anything in particular, looking at the window and the oak trees beyond it that had not burned because the fire had been confined mostly to the east wing. The trees were the same trees. She found this consoling in a way she couldn't immediately account for.

She had agreed to come to this wedding reluctantly, for Siobhán's sake. Siobhán was the daughter of Eleanor's closest friend, Máire, who had died two years ago of a swift and unceremonious cancer that had taken her between diagnosis and death in eleven weeks. Siobhán was marrying a man named Ferdia who worked in renewable energy and who had, the one time Eleanor met him, talked at length about battery storage solutions in a way that conveyed both expertise and a genuine inability to gauge other people's interest. Eleanor liked him well enough. She had no particular feelings about the wedding. She had said yes because Siobhán had asked and because Máire wasn't there to be Siobhán's mother at her wedding and because it seemed to Eleanor that the accumulation of refusals, the life of steady avoidance she had been quietly assembling, had lately begun to press against her with a weight she could no longer dismiss.

She had told no one about her history with the hotel. She had mentioned it only once, years ago, to Máire, who had held her hand across a kitchen table and said that must have been terrifying, that it must have changed you. And Eleanor had said yes, it had, it changed the way she slept for years, I still don't sleep well in unfamiliar rooms, I lie there too alert, listening for the particular quality of silence that hotels have, which is not really silence but a kind of compressed busyness, all those lives going on behind all those closed doors. Máire had understood this perfectly, as she understood most things, and they had not spoken of it again.

Eleanor washed her face and changed into the clothes she'd brought for the evening reception. A dark blue dress, low-heeled shoes. She looked at herself in the mirror with neither satisfaction nor displeasure. She had a broad face and strong hands and her hair was still mostly its original dark brown with grey only at the temples. When she was nineteen she had been unremarkable-looking, neither pretty nor unpretty, with an energy about her that came from being always slightly harried, slightly behind, in the way of people who have taken on more responsibilities than they can comfortably carry. Her mother was unwell then, not terminally but persistently, and Eleanor was the older of two daughters and the one who'd stayed close to home. The chambermaiding had been practical and convenient. She had not particularly minded it. There was a satisfaction in the work that she'd been slightly ashamed of at the time, a satisfaction in making rooms orderly, in the specific pleasure of a clean surface, a tight-cornered sheet.

She thought about Finneran sometimes. She had not seen him since the night of the fire. After it was over, after the statements and the inquiries, they had simply gone back to their respective lives, which were not lives that overlapped in any ordinary way. He was from somewhere in the west, she seemed to remember, had taken the job seasonally, and she had the sense that he'd moved away eventually, perhaps to England or farther. She thought about him not with any particular sentiment but with the specific quality of thought you give to people who were present with you at something extreme. There is a version of intimacy that has nothing to do with knowledge or affection. Finneran had that kind of presence in her memory: a face she would still recognize, she felt sure, though she had not seen it in thirty-four years, because her brain had stored it with the heightened encoding that terror provides.

Basia she had actually seen once, about eight years after the fire. At a bus stop in Dublin. Basia had been heavier and her hair was different and they had looked at each other with the brief suspended certainty of people who know each other from a context they cannot immediately place, and then they had placed it simultaneously and Basia had said something in Polish first and then in English, she had said, I think about it still, and Eleanor had said, I do too, and the bus had come and Basia had gotten on it, and that was the whole of their reunion.

The reception room was filling when Eleanor arrived. Tables with white cloths and small arrangements of white and copper-colored flowers. The light was warm and deliberate. Siobhán, across the room, was radiantly, helplessly happy, the way people are on the day of a wedding, when the happiness is almost too large for the person containing it. She was wearing an ivory dress with a low back and she had her mother's way of holding her head, slightly tilted, that caused Eleanor to have to look away for a moment. She took a glass of prosecco from a passing tray and stood near the wall and watched the room.

It was a large party, perhaps eighty people. She knew only a handful of them, friends of Máire's, some cousins of Siobhán's she'd met at the funeral. She made the rounds that politeness required. She spoke about Máire with the people who wanted to speak about Máire and about Ferdia's battery storage solutions with the people who wanted to speak about that. She ate the dinner, which was good, a lamb dish with small roasted root vegetables. She drank two glasses of wine and then switched to water. She danced once, briefly, when Siobhán pulled her onto the floor and would not be refused.

It was during the dancing that she looked up and saw the door to the service corridor. It was set into the wall behind the bar, painted the same cream as the wall, easily ignorable. But she saw it with a recognition that was physical, a tightening in her chest that she breathed carefully through. The service corridors in hotels like this one run behind the public rooms, they carry the work of the hotel behind a scrim of decorum, and she had spent more time in those corridors than in any of the guest-facing spaces. She knew the logic of them, the rhythm of their functionality. She stood on the edge of the dance floor and looked at that door.

She had gone through a door like it — the corresponding door in the original hotel, perhaps the door that stood in this exact position — on the night of the fire. She had been carrying a stack of turned-down-room cards and she had pushed through the door and seen the smoke moving in the corridor in a way that smoke had no right to be moving, low along the floor and purposeful-seeming, as if it had somewhere to be. She had stood there with the cards in her hands. She had been, for a moment that felt long but was surely only a second, completely unable to understand what she was seeing, the way the brain sometimes refuses the evidence presented to it. And then she had understood and had turned back through the door and begun, with a composure that was itself a kind of shock, to do the things that needed doing.

She had knocked on doors. She remembered this clearly: the sound of her own fist on door after door, the specific hollow knock of hotel doors, and the confused faces of the guests who opened them. She had knocked and said there was a fire and they needed to leave now, please, the stairs were at the end, please bring your key, no please don't wait for your things. A man had argued with her about his laptop. She had said sir, please. She had said sir, the stairs. He had taken his laptop and they had both gotten out. She did not know if she had saved this man's life or if he would have woken and gotten out regardless. She had been told, afterward, by the fire investigator who interviewed her, that her actions had likely made a difference. She had accepted this with the numbness that characterized her entire relationship to the event in the months following it.

She went back to the wall with her water glass. The dancing continued. Siobhán was dancing with Ferdia now, they were laughing at something, and the sight of them laughing had the complex quality of all such sights: the pleasure of witnessing happiness alongside the small, unavoidable sorrow of everything that the happy people did not yet know, all the future that lay ahead of them unconfigured and waiting.

An elderly man sat down beside her. He had the careful movements of someone whose joints were unreliable and he held a whiskey glass in both hands as if it were something that required cradling. He said her name with a slight interrogation and she said yes, and he said he'd known Máire, they'd grown up in the same townland, and his name was Tomás. She said she remembered Máire mentioning him and this was only partly true but seemed kind. They sat together watching the dancers, and the silence between them was the easy silence of two people who are not required to be anything to each other but who find themselves in sufficient sympathy to sit quietly without awkwardness.

After a while Tomás said, you've been here before.

She looked at him.

You have the look of someone who knows where things are, he said. The way you came in this evening, you knew where you were going.

She told him, then. She did not plan to; it was simply that his observation was accurate enough, his manner gentle enough, and she was tired in the way you become tired when you have been not-saying something for a long time. She told him about the chambermaiding, about the fire, about the linen cupboard on the fourth floor. She told him about Khamla and his name being printed wrong. She told him about Basia at the bus stop. She did not tell him about the man with the laptop, though she thought about it.

He listened without interrupting, which is rarer than it should be. His whiskey glass sat on the table and he turned it slowly by its base while she talked, a small rotation, back and forth.

When she finished he said, and what do you make of coming back.

She thought about this honestly. Outside the tall windows the hotel grounds were dark. The oak trees would be out there in the dark, the ones that had not burned, putting down their roots another thirty-four years deeper into the ground.

I don't know yet, she said. I'm not sure I feel what I expected to feel.

What did you expect?

Something larger. More definitive. She paused. The way you imagine a conclusion will feel.

Tomás nodded slowly. He understood conclusions. He was, she guessed, nearly eighty, and had the settled look of a man who had outlived a number of his certainties without becoming bitter about it.

I think, she said, and then stopped. I think the hotel is just a hotel. I think that's the thing I didn't know until tonight.

He said nothing, which was the right response.

She looked at the room again, the dancing and the flowers and the candlelight, Siobhán's ivory back moving through the crowd. She thought about what the room had been before it was a function room, before the rebuilding. This part of the hotel had been the old ballroom, or something near it, and had been the furthest from the fire, and had come through the night relatively undamaged, which meant it had been demolished not by burning but by the decision to rebuild, by the desire to start again with clean materials. She found herself thinking about that — about the rooms that had survived the fire but been demolished anyway, about the particular kind of loss that is not destruction but replacement, which is quieter and less dramatic and in some ways harder to grieve.

Her mother had died nine years ago. There was no one left now who had known her at nineteen. There was no one left, really, who knew the shape of the person she'd been before. Not better or worse than the person she was now, simply continuous with her, the earlier draft of the same long document. She thought about the girl moving down the corridor with her turned-down-room cards, making her small practiced dispensations of care, putting chocolates on pillows, drawing curtains, smoothing the disturbed surfaces of strangers' days. There had been a goodness in it she had never fully acknowledged. The work had a goodness that had nothing to do with scale or recognition. She had been twenty-two rooms into a thirty-room corridor when the smoke came under the door.

Siobhán appeared beside her, glowing and slightly breathless, and put her hands on Eleanor's shoulders from behind and said are you all right, you've been sitting here for ages. Eleanor said she was fine, she was enjoying watching, which was true. Siobhán bent down and kissed the top of her head and said I'm so glad you came, I'm so glad you're here, and her voice cracked slightly on the last word in the way it sometimes did when she was speaking to someone who stood in for her mother, someone around whom the absence was particularly shaped. Eleanor reached up and covered one of Siobhán's hands with her own.

After Siobhán went back to her party, Tomás stood with some difficulty and said he was going to find his bed, that he was past the age for late nights. He shook Eleanor's hand with his large dry hand and wished her well and moved carefully across the room. She watched him go. She finished her water.

She stayed another hour. She watched the cake being cut. She watched Ferdia make a speech that was funny in places and earnest in others and hit its marks with more precision than she'd expected given the battery storage impression. She watched Siobhán laugh. She thought, once, clearly and with intention, about Khamla, whose name had been mispronounced and misprinted and who had gone back for his phone and who had been twenty-four years old, and she let herself think about him fully, without deflection, which she had not always been able to do.

At eleven o'clock she went up to her room. The corridor on the second floor was quiet and the carpet absorbed her footsteps and she walked its length slowly, looking at the numbered doors, thinking about all the sleeping and the not-sleeping going on behind them, all the versions of the night being had simultaneously in those small parallel chambers. She stopped outside her room. The key card felt unfamiliar in her hand, too light, lacking the authority of the old brass fobs.

She stood for a moment in the corridor before going in. The corridor was identical to dozens of corridors she had stood in at nineteen with her cart and her cloths and her small stack of folded towels. The overhead lights were recessed and warm. Everything was clean and ordered and utterly indifferent, in the way of hotels, in the way that is their great practical virtue and their peculiar sadness.

She went in. She drew the curtain and looked out briefly at the grounds, at the darkness where the oaks were, at the faint smear of light from the town beyond the fields. She turned down the bed with the habit of someone who has always turned her own bed down, no one ever having done it for her. She put herself into the clean white architecture of the sheets.

She expected not to sleep, and for a while she did not. She lay and listened to the hotel's compressed and busy silence, all the lives going on behind all the closed doors. But she was not on alert in the old way. She was simply there, present in the building, a guest in the rebuilt place, her body warm and the sheets tight-cornered and the darkness complete and ordinary. Somewhere below her the wedding continued, the music coming up through the floors as a vibration rather than a sound, felt more than heard, a pulse in the structure of the place, and she lay with it, and gradually, without her quite noticing the moment of it, she slept.
