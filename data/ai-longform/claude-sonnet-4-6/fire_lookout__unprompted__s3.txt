The smoke came up thin at first, the kind a person could mistake for morning haze burning off the ridge, but Vera had been watching sky from this tower for eleven summers and she knew the difference between haze and smoke the way a mother knows the difference between a child sleeping and a child gone quiet for wrong reasons. She knew by the color—this was not the soft grey of dissipating moisture but something with blue in it, blue and then yellow at the edges where the light caught it—and she knew by the way it moved, not drifting but rising with intention, with the vertical certainty of something burning at its source.

She put the binoculars to her eyes. The Osborne fire finder sat in the center of the cab, its brass ring tarnished to the color of old pennies, its map disc faded from years of sun through the wraparound glass, and she crossed to it and sighted down the alidade and placed the smoke at the far end of Picket Creek drainage, somewhere in the lodgepole and subalpine fir below the talus fields, somewhere in the country she had spent a decade not thinking about in any sustained way.

The radio was a grey box on the shelf beneath the south-facing windows and she already knew what she would find when she reached for it, because she had spent the last three days knowing, the way you carry a dull specific weight without naming it. The handset gave her only static, a white and indifferent sound, and she tried the frequency twice, then the emergency frequency, then cycled through everything the radio could reach. The repeater on Harlow Peak had been down since Thursday when lightning took out the antenna feed, and the Forest Service radio tech was coming sometime next week or the week after, or possibly never, because the whole fire lookout program was being decommissioned and she was the last one staffing any tower in the district, a fact that had seemed poignant and even somewhat beautiful to her in early June when the fireweed was blooming and she was reading Pessoa in the evenings and feeling philosophical about solitude. It seemed considerably less beautiful now.

She tried her personal cell phone, knowing as she extended it toward each window in turn that there was no signal from this elevation—counterintuitive but true, the tower sat in a geographic notch that blocked the nearest cell infrastructure, something she had confirmed in the first week of her first summer here and never had reason to reconsider. The phone showed one bar and then none and then one again, mocking.

Vera set the phone face down on the map table and looked at the smoke.

It was growing. In the time it had taken her to work through the radio frequencies and the phone, the column had thickened and the blue had gone to grey and the grey was now touched with the first suggestions of brown, which meant material, which meant not a campfire left unattended or a smudge pot or anything benign. Brown smoke was structure or heavy fuel. Brown smoke was the smell of things that were meant to last.

She knew the drainage. She had not been in it in six years but she knew it the way you know the architecture of a house you grew up in, knew where the drainage bent west below the second crossing and where the water slowed in a wide pool beneath a leaning spruce and where the old miner's trail had been rerouted after the washout in 2003. She knew where the cabin sat, back from the creek on a small bench of land, surrounded by the lodgepoles on three sides with the fourth side open to the meadow. Ruth had bought it from the estate of a man named Gallagher, a retired hydrologist who had built it by hand over five summers in the 1970s and who had died without children. Ruth had called Vera when she signed the papers, six years before their falling out, or rather six years before the falling out clarified itself into something neither of them could climb back over, and Vera had understood even then that the cabin was a way of being close to her without being close to her, close to the mountains where Vera spent her summers, a kind of adjacency that Ruth had always preferred to the direct approach.

The direct approach. That was not Ruth's way, or it had not been, in the years Vera had known her well enough to have opinions about her ways. Ruth came at things from angles. She would talk around a grief for months before naming it. She had called their mother's stroke a setback, had called the end of her marriage a change of plans, had referred to Vera's leaving home at seventeen—which was really Vera's fleeing, which was really Vera's responding to years of something that took Vera until her mid-thirties to find the language for—as Vera's adventure. As if the whole of it had been merely a choice about style.

Vera stopped herself. She was thinking about Ruth because the smoke was coming from where Ruth was, and thinking about Ruth when she wasn't in danger was already something Vera limited carefully, the way you limit how often you press on a bruise to see if it still hurts. You already know it hurts. You don't need the information.

She looked at the clock on the wall: 9:47 in the morning. She looked at the thermometer outside the south window: sixty-one degrees. She looked at the flag on its short pole at the northeast corner of the catwalk: not moving. No wind, or wind below the threshold of the flag, a few miles an hour at most. The fire weather had been building for a week, the relative humidity dropping each afternoon into the single digits in the lower drainages, the fuel moistures in the duff going critical, the ten-hour fuels drying out, but this morning was still, still and clear and cool the way mornings at seventy-eight hundred feet are cool even in July, and the smoke was rising straight because there was no wind to push it, and if a wind came the fire would move in whatever direction the wind chose and it would move faster than she could think about clearly.

She should go down. She knew she should go down. The road was eight miles of two-track starting at the base of the tower's access trail, which itself was a mile and a half of switchbacks through the krummholz and then into the timber, and the truck was at the trailhead, and if she drove eight miles to the ranger station she could get someone on a radio or a landline, she could report the fire, she could send help into the Picket Creek drainage, she could do the rational and proper thing. She would be descending from the tower she was supposed to be staffing—a tower that was, she reminded herself, being decommissioned, that was already in its bureaucratic afterlife, that she was staffing as a kind of courtesy gesture the district had offered her because she had asked for one more summer and they had not known how to say no to a woman who had spent eleven summers alone above timberline watching for smoke. They had given her this last summer the way you give someone a piece of the demolished house, a tile or a doorknob, something to carry away.

The smoke column was casting a shadow now. She could see the shadow of it lying across the upper drainage, a grey wash on the green, and the column itself was fully committed now, no longer suggesting but declaring, and she watched it for another moment in the way she had learned to watch fire, completely, without looking away, the way you look at something you are trying to memorize because you understand you will need to describe it later and your description will matter.

She went to the equipment locker and took out the day pack. She had kept it packed for eleven summers, had never once needed it in any way beyond the hypothetical, had checked and repacked it each June with the deliberate patience of someone who hoped never to use what they were preparing. Water filter. First aid kit. Emergency blanket. A compass. A headlamp. Fire shelter, which she had never trained to deploy but which the Forest Service required her to carry. A small amount of food. She added her field notebook and the cell phone and the hand-drawn map of the district she had made herself over years of walking the trails and which she trusted more than the official maps, which had last been updated in 1997.

She looked around the cab. It had been her home for eleven summers in the way that an inhabited space becomes home through accumulation rather than intention: the coffee can on the windowsill with paintbrushes she used on her days off, the stack of novels on the shelf beside the fire finder, the wool blanket on the cot in the corner. She had never brought much. She had never needed much. The person she was up here was a simplified version of the person she was in the rest of her life, and she had come to prefer the simplified version, had spent the other nine months of each year trying to preserve that simplification against the friction of the world.

She locked the trap door from below and descended the stairs, nine flights of steel grating that rang under her boots, and came out into the morning.

The smoke was a column you could see from here without binoculars, rising above the ridge to the south, and it was darker than it had been. She stood for a moment at the base of the tower and looked at it and felt the thing she always felt when she finally committed to leaving the tower, which was a sensation of falling forward, of having handed something off to gravity.

She went down the trail at a pace she could sustain, not running—running on a steep rocky trail was how you broke an ankle and became your own emergency—but moving with the particular efficiency of someone who knows the terrain and is afraid in a useful way. The krummholz gave way to open forest and the trail dropped through a series of long traverses and she moved through the shadow of the trees and tried not to think too specifically about what she was moving toward.

They had not spoken in six years. The arithmetic of estrangement was simple once you stopped rounding: six years, two months, some days. The last conversation had been on the phone. It had gone the way their difficult conversations had always gone, which was poorly but not loudly, badly but with control, the damage subcutaneous. Ruth had said a thing that could not be taken back and Vera had let it sit between them on the phone line in the silence that followed—not stunned silence, not hurt silence exactly, but the silence of recognition, of hearing something confirmed that you had spent years hoping was not true—and then had said she had to go, and had gone, and had not called again.

She was not going because it was Ruth. She told herself this on the trail, watching her feet on the loose shale of the switchbacks. She was going because there was a fire and someone was likely in the drainage and there was no other option for getting that information to people who could act on it. She would have gone for anyone. She had built eleven summers on the premise that she would go for anyone.

The trail bottomed out at the trailhead and she found her truck where she had left it, facing out, because she always parked facing out. The engine started with its familiar reluctance, the old diesel shuddering to life, and she pulled onto the road and drove.

The road wound through the forest, following the contours of the mountain, and in the places where it topped small ridges she could see the smoke, closer now, and larger, and she could see that it had what firefighters called a head, a leading edge that was moving, that had found some fuel or some air or some combination and was going. She gripped the wheel and drove.

She thought about the last time she had seen Ruth in person, which was before the phone call, a year before, at their mother's house in Spokane at Christmas. Ruth had been contained that visit, which was how Ruth was when she was managing something, and Vera had moved around her in the old way, the way she had moved around Ruth for most of their shared childhood, alert to the changes in atmosphere, reading them. She had learned to read Ruth before she had learned to read anything else. Ruth was her first text, her primer in the grammar of other people's pain. Their mother had made a turkey and they had eaten it and talked about inconsequential things and Vera had driven back to Spokane the next morning in a early light feeling the specific relief of having survived something, not knowing that it was the last time she would see her.

She came around a corner and had to brake hard. A tree had come down across the road, not recently—the needles were brown, it had been down for weeks—and it was a tree the road crew had not gotten to, would not get to, because the road was on the decommissioning list along with the tower and the whole infrastructure of watching. She sat in the truck and looked at the tree. It was a big lodgepole, two feet in diameter at the base, lying across the full width of the road, and she did not have a chainsaw.

She sat for a moment in the seat of the idling truck and rearranged her understanding of the situation. Then she turned off the engine and got out and took the day pack from the passenger seat and looked up the road and down the road and looked at the tree once more as if it might change its mind.

She knew the road. She could walk the remaining six miles to the ranger station. She could walk it in two hours, a little more. Or she could go into the drainage.

The Picket Creek trailhead was a mile back up the road. She had passed it ninety seconds ago, had registered it without considering it because she had been going to the ranger station, because the ranger station was the right choice, the sanctioned choice, the choice that kept her out of the drainage and out of the proximity of Ruth and on the correct side of the boundary they had established with their silence. The fire was somewhere in the lower drainage. The cabin was in the lower drainage. The trail went seven miles from the trailhead to the far end of the drainage and the cabin was three miles in.

Vera stood on the road and looked at the smoke. It was overhead now, the column deflecting south as it rose and spread, and the smell of it reached her here, that specific smell of combustion in timber, sweet and wrong. She could see the shadow of it on the mountain across the valley.

She walked back up the road.

The Picket Creek trailhead was a pullout with a brown carsonite sign and a register box no one had maintained, and the trail went down from the road steeply through spruce and fir, dropping toward the creek. She went down it. The pack on her back was familiar and the ground under her feet was soft with duff and she moved through the trees in the smell of smoke and thought about nothing that was not immediately necessary.

The creek was low for July, the drought having drawn it down to a series of linked pools with gravel bars between them, and she crossed it on stones and climbed the far bank and came into the lodgepole forest where the trail opened up and she could move faster. The smoke smell was constant now and there was an orange quality to the light filtering through the canopy, a filtered quality, amber, the light of a different atmosphere than the one she had woken up in.

She smelled the fire before she heard it. Then she heard it: the low sound that people describe as roaring but which is more accurately a kind of white noise with mass, a textured sound, the sound of material being consumed faster than the mind can track. She came out of the trees at the upper edge of the meadow and stopped.

The fire was in the lodgepoles to the east, working through the canopy, throwing embers into the meadow grass, and the meadow grass was catching at its edges. The cabin was at the far end of the meadow, or it had been, and there was no longer a structure where the cabin had been, only a low mound of burning material, collapsed, still showing its bones in the orange frame of what she thought might have been a wall or a roof beam. The whole thing was perhaps twenty minutes past the point of helping.

She stood in the meadow and watched the fire move and tried to locate Ruth. She walked the meadow edge, staying low, moving away from the fire's head, calling. She felt foolish calling because she did not know what Ruth would hear in her voice after six years, did not know what she herself was offering with the sound of her own name for someone else.

She found her at the creek. Ruth was sitting in the water, up to her waist, her grey hair loose and wet on her shoulders, and she was looking at the fire the way a person looks at something that used to be their place in the world, with recognition but not with hope. She had a burn on her forearm that was serious. She had no shoes. She was holding a photograph in one hand, a waterlogged square of color held by its edges, and she looked at Vera across the water with an expression that Vera could not at first interpret and then recognized, because she had worn it herself, as the expression of someone who has just used the last of their reserves of surprise.

Vera crossed the creek. She took Ruth's uninjured arm and helped her up out of the water and Ruth came up out of it the way things come up that have been resting at the bottom, slowly, weighted.

"I tried the road," Ruth said. Her voice was rough from smoke. "The tree."

"I know," Vera said.

"I was going to walk out."

"We'll walk out together."

Ruth looked down at the photograph. It was too damaged to read. Whatever had been in it was gone, the image dissolved to a smear of light and color, and Ruth looked at it for a moment longer and then let it fall into the current. Vera watched it go.

They stood on the bank. The fire sound was closer and there were embers in the meadow grass now, small fires starting at the margins, and Vera felt the urgency of it without having to calculate it, the way bodies know. She got Ruth's arm over her shoulder and felt the weight of her sister against her side, the specific weight of someone she had once known better than any other person and had spent years unlearning, and it was still familiar. The weight was still familiar.

"There was a box," Ruth said. "Letters. I went back for them."

"I know," Vera said again, though she didn't, though she was saying it the way you say what is needed before you say what is true.

"They burned," Ruth said.

"We have to go," Vera said.

Ruth nodded, still looking at the place where the cabin had been, and Vera turned her gently away from it, toward the trail, toward the creek and the forest and the long climb back up to the road. The smoke came with them in the way smoke does, touching everything, settling into the fabric of their clothes and the fiber of their hair, marking them. Vera thought about the tower empty above them on the mountain, the binoculars on their hook, the Osborne fire finder pointing at a column of smoke that was no longer a column but a spreading thing, a rearrangement of the world that would take years to understand. She thought about the eleven summers. She thought about the simplified version of herself she had been trying to preserve.

The trail went up through the trees and they went up it. The morning burned around them, specific and irreversible. Ruth limped beside her and did not speak and Vera matched her pace, and the trees received them into their shadow, and above them on the ridge the mountain stood unchanged in all its difficult permanence, and they walked.
