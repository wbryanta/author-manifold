They had been in Guatemala for three months, and Anil had learned to carry certain things with her always: a thermos of water, rubber gloves folded into her back pocket, a small notebook with a waterproof cover in which she recorded measurements and observations in a handwriting that grew smaller and more compressed as the days went on, as if the work were slowly teaching her to take up less space in the world. She had learned too the proper distance from grief. Not the cold remove of a surgeon, but something more deliberate—the understanding that if you let yourself feel everything, you would feel nothing useful, and the dead needed your usefulness above all else.

She was twenty-nine years old and had already spent time on excavation sites in Afghanistan and Cyprus and had written her doctoral thesis on the chemical analysis of bone degradation in tropical climates. Her supervisor at Guy's Hospital had called her the most gifted student he had seen in a decade, and she had accepted this praise the way she accepted most things about herself, with a kind of wary acknowledgment, the way you might acknowledge the existence of a peculiar weather system. It was there. It had consequences. It was not something she had chosen.

She was Sri Lankan. Born in Colombo, raised partly there and partly in England, partly in the company of her uncle Gamini who was a doctor and a difficult man and whom she loved with a loyalty she could not always account for. Her parents had died when she was a child, a car accident on the Kandy road in the rain, and she had been passed between relatives with the particular gentle deliberateness of a family managing guilt, each household feeding her and educating her and ensuring her comfort while also, she understood only later, ensuring that she did not stay too long.

She had become a person who knew how to leave.

The forensic team she worked with in Guatemala was international, assembled by a human rights organization whose office in Geneva occupied three rooms above a watch repair shop. There was Clyde Martine, the team leader, American, a man of fifty with a methodical temperament and a habit of humming tunelessly while he worked, which the others had initially found irritating and had come to find necessary, the way you come to need certain ambient sounds. There was Bettina Schutte from the Netherlands, the team's photographer, who documented each stage of each excavation with a precision that approached the devotional. There was Emmanuel, the local coordinator, a quiet man who had been born in the highlands and who translated not only language but the subtler information embedded in silences, in the particular way a family elder stood, in the direction someone's eyes moved when asked a question.

And there was Anil.

Her specialty was the reading of bones. She could determine age and sex and in many cases the particular biography of violence that had ended a life. She had learned to see the body as a document. She had learned that documents could be falsified, could be damaged, could be read incorrectly by those who brought their assumptions to the text. She had learned the discipline of letting the evidence speak before she spoke herself. This was harder than it sounded.

In the evenings, back at the small house the team rented near the town of Chimaltenango, she would sit on the low wall outside and write up her notes while the last light held, and sometimes Bettina would sit beside her with a glass of whatever local liquor had been acquired that week, and they would talk, or not talk. Bettina was a woman who understood silence as a form of company. Anil had not had many friends like that.

She had received a letter two weeks ago from an organization called the Human Rights Commission in Sri Lanka. It was a formal letter, carefully worded, requesting her participation in an investigation that was, the letter said, of the utmost sensitivity. There had been discoveries. Skeletal remains in locations that suggested systematic concealment. The commission had the authority, recently granted, to conduct independent investigations, and they required a forensic specialist of international standing who could be seen as neutral, as someone without entanglement in the political arrangements of the country.

Someone who knew the ground but had been away long enough.

She had read the letter twice and then set it on the table and gone outside and looked at the mountains.

She had been away from Sri Lanka for fifteen years. She had left at fourteen, in the care of an aunt who had married an Englishman and lived in Gloucestershire, and she had not gone back. She had told herself many times, in many different formulations, why this was. The reasons were real. Her work took her elsewhere. Her life had organized itself around a kind of mobility. But she knew, in the way that you know certain true things about yourself that you have agreed not to examine too closely, that there was more to it than that.

She had written back accepting the commission's request.

Now Guatemala was nearly done. They were close to completing the documentation of a mass grave site that held the remains of approximately forty individuals, men and women and some who from their size appeared to be adolescents, killed between 1982 and 1983. The forensic evidence would be submitted to the commission that had engaged them and would eventually, if conditions permitted, be used in legal proceedings. Anil had been part of similar processes before and had given testimony at tribunal hearings in The Hague. She understood the gap between evidence and justice and had learned not to let that gap become a source of despair, because despair was a luxury she did not permit herself. Rage, occasionally. Sorrow in controlled doses. But not despair.

In the afternoons when the heat became extreme they rested under a canvas awning and Clyde would review the day's documentation, cross-checking measurements, going over Bettina's photographs against the hand-drawn site maps. Anil would often use this time to write in the notebook, not official notes but the other kind, the kind she had kept since she was a teenager, observations about what she saw that were not evidence exactly but were not nothing either. She wrote about the woman in the grave. She wrote: the grief of love in that shoulder. She had written those words immediately after, while the image was still complete, because she understood that images like that were not permanently available, that the mind edited them over time, softened them for its own protection.

She did not want this one softened.

On their last evening in Guatemala, Clyde opened a bottle of something he had been carrying in his bag since Bogotá, a rum that was very good, and they sat together in the small courtyard and Emmanuel told a story about his father that they did not entirely follow because of the translation difficulties but that ended with his father standing in a river holding a shoe, and it was clearly very funny, and they laughed, and Emmanuel looked pleased and then solemn the way people sometimes do when they realize they have successfully communicated something of themselves to strangers.

Anil told them she was going to Sri Lanka.

She had not announced this before, though Clyde had known since the letter arrived. He looked at her across the table without saying anything for a moment. He had worked in a dozen countries in conflict and he had opinions about going into certain situations, about the specific risks that attached to a person who was returning home to look at what home had become. He did not say any of this. He refilled her glass.

Bettina asked what it was like, Sri Lanka. Anil thought about this.

She said: green. She said: there is a particular quality of light in the late afternoon, in the low hills, that she had not found anywhere else. She said: the people are very warm and also very capable of terrible things, and this combination is not unique to Sri Lanka but it is very concentrated there, she had always felt.

She said: I have not been back since I was a girl.

There was a silence that was not uncomfortable. Emmanuel looked into his glass. Somewhere in the hills above the town a dog was barking with the mechanical repetition of a dog that has forgotten why it started.

She flew from Guatemala City to Miami and then to London and then to Colombo, three flights over two days with long intervals in airports that have a way of making time feel hypothetical. She slept in fragments and read and ate food from packages without tasting it. Somewhere over the Atlantic she took out the commission's letter and read it again, and the supplementary documents that had accompanied it: brief descriptions of four sites where remains had been discovered, photographs taken by local officials that were not forensic in quality but that showed enough. She studied the photographs carefully. She was looking at a darkness she had spent fifteen years learning to see clearly.

She arrived at Katunayake airport at four in the morning, local time, on a Tuesday in April. The air when she stepped off the plane was thick with moisture and the smell of things growing, a fecundity that felt almost aggressive after Guatemala's dry highlands. She had forgotten how much was alive here all at once.

Her contact at the commission was a man named Sarath Diyasena. He was waiting for her at the arrivals barrier in a blue shirt that was already limp from the heat, a man of perhaps fifty with a careful face and the particular watchfulness of someone accustomed to calibrating how much of himself to show in a given situation. He held a sign with her name on it though she would have found him anyway. She had been told to look for a man who would seem to be waiting while actually observing everything around him, and this man was doing that. She found it reassuring. Paranoia in the circumstances was appropriate.

He drove her to a government guesthouse in Colombo 7, a colonial-era building set back from a street of other colonial-era buildings in various states of managed decline. The guesthouse was clean and slightly formal in the manner of places that have been maintained without being renewed, the furniture arranged with care, the curtains white and freshly starched. She was shown to a room on the second floor with a ceiling fan that turned slowly and a window that looked out onto a garden where a man was already working with a rake in the pre-dawn dark, the sound of his work a kind of reassurance.

She unpacked the essentials: her field kit, the notebooks, the photographs she always carried when traveling, three images of people she had loved. She placed these last on the dresser.

Then she sat on the edge of the bed and did not sleep.

She had expected to feel something on arrival that was nameable, a recognition or a return of some kind, the landscape of the interior self corresponding to the landscape of the exterior place. People talked about this. The homeland working on you from the outside. What she felt was more complicated and less dramatic, a low continuous awareness that the coordinates of things were different here, that her usual competence was operating in a slightly altered field, as if she were a compass that had been moved nearer to a pole.

In the morning Sarath came to collect her and they drove through the city in his small grey car with the windows down. She watched Colombo from the passenger seat with a methodical attentiveness, noting what had changed in fifteen years and what had not. There had been bombings. Buildings had been repaired or replaced or left in ruin, and you could sometimes tell which by the color of the masonry, the new stone brighter than the old. At certain intersections there were military checkpoints, young men with rifles who waved most vehicles through but stopped others with the arbitrary authority of the bored and the powerful. Sarath navigated this with a practiced ease, reaching for identification before it was requested, maintaining a specific quality of expression that Anil catalogued as: authority acknowledged.

He told her what he could. He was cautious in what he said, and she noticed that he did not name certain things directly, used circumlocutions, spoke about the situation the way people speak about an illness that has not yet been given a formal diagnosis. She understood this. Language itself was contested here. To name something was to take a position.

What she gathered was this: the commission had been established six months ago with a mandate that was, in Sarath's word, provisional. They had been given access to certain sites and denied access to others. They operated with the awareness that they were observed, that their findings would be scrutinized not only by the international community but by parties within the government who had an interest in particular versions of events. The remains that had been found were located across a geographic range that suggested multiple agencies responsible. This was itself politically complex. It was easier for all sides when atrocities could be attributed to the enemy.

What they had was a skeleton. What they needed was to establish identity, cause of death, and if possible timeframe.

What they had, she already understood, was also an argument. The bones were an argument against silence.

They drove east out of the city, the road eventually climbing into the hill country, the vegetation thickening as the elevation increased. She had been right about the light. In the late morning it fell through the trees and across the terraced hillsides with a quality she had carried somewhere in herself for fifteen years without knowing she had kept it, and seeing it now she felt the curious sensation of recognizing something she had forgotten she knew.

Sarath pointed out villages as they passed. He told her small histories. He had a scholar's relationship to the landscape, seeing it layered, the present moment resting on sequences of other moments. He was a man, she began to understand, who had spent a long time looking at things very carefully and who had made this careful looking into a kind of armor.

She asked him directly: who do you think is responsible for the sites?

He was quiet for a moment. He said: we don't know. He said: that is what we need you to tell us.

She said: I can tell you things about the bones. About when these people died and how. About who they were if there's enough to go on. I cannot tell you who put them in the ground.

He said: no. But you can tell us whether what we're being told about them is true.

This was the core of it. She understood now.

The official account existed already. There was a version of events that had been issued, carefully, by people in positions to issue such things. What the bones might say could confirm that version or complicate it or refute it entirely. She had seen this dynamic in other countries, the way official narrative preceded evidence and then had to be reconciled with it or defended against it. The bones were not political. But they existed inside politics, and everything done with them and about them was political.

She thought of the woman sitting in the grave in Guatemala, sitting among the sleeping men.

There are certain kinds of knowledge that are not abstract. The woman in the grave had not needed forensic analysis. She had known her husband's bones by something older than science. What science offered was language for what was already felt, and the ability to carry that knowledge into rooms where it could be heard and recorded and held accountable.

They arrived at the first site in the early afternoon. It was in a cleared area near a reservoir in the central highlands, a depression in the ground that had been partly excavated by local investigators and then stopped, covered over with a tarpaulin that had been inadequate against the rains. A police officer stood nearby in the shade of a tree. He nodded at Sarath without warmth.

Anil crouched at the edge of the depression and looked at what was visible. The rains had done some damage but not catastrophic damage. She could see the pale curves of bone in the earth.

She opened her field kit.

She had learned this in her first week of training in forensic anthropology: the discipline required you to begin always with what you knew, not what you suspected. What she knew was limited to the visible and the measurable. What she suspected was required to wait, sometimes for days, sometimes for weeks, sometimes indefinitely.

She pulled on her gloves and took out the small folding rule and leaned into the depression.

The light was very good here. She noted this. The light was clean and direct, coming from the west, illuminating the profile of the earth with a clarity that would help.

She thought: I have come back.

She thought: let's see what the ground has kept.

The police officer in the shade of the tree was watching her with an expression she would have called neutral if she had not already learned to read the many different densities of neutral. This one was a border between hostility and indifference, which meant that someone had instructed him to be neutral and that naturally he was neither.

Sarath stood back. He had the quality of some surgeons she had worked beside, the ability to give another person the full space of their competence without interrupting it. She had not yet decided whether she trusted him. She had not yet decided whether trust was the right category to apply.

What she decided was to work. The bones did not require her to resolve her questions about Sarath or about the commission or about what she was doing in a country she had spent fifteen years not returning to. The bones required only that she come to them without assumptions and record what she found.

She began.
