The truck knew the roads better than he did now. That was how Bernard thought of it on mornings when his hands were stiff and the sky above the ridge came up the color of a bruise healing—that the truck, a 1994 Ford with a cracked dashboard and a heater that worked only when it felt inclined, had memorized these particular hollows and switchbacks in a way that his own body had not so much memorized as absorbed, the way wood absorbs weather. He let it carry him. He rested his hands in his lap and watched the fields go gray and then gold in the early light, the corn stubble standing in rows like the ruins of something, and he thought: this is the last time I will do this. He had thought this before. He had thought it in September when the arthritis moved into his right index finger like a tenant who intended to stay, and he had thought it in August when Gerald Purcell out on Route 9 had asked him whether it was still worth tuning a piano that nobody touched, and Bernard had said yes, without hesitation, because he could not imagine what else to say. But today he had not invented the thought. Today it had arrived fully formed, the way certain chords arrive—inevitable, bearing the harmonic weight of everything that preceded them.

He had his bag on the seat beside him. The canvas was soft with decades of use, worn shiny at the corners, and inside it the tuning lever, the rubber mutes, the felt strips in their cloth roll, the electronic tuner he had bought in 2003 and still mistrusted, preferring his ear, which was imperfect now but still—he believed, he needed to believe—more truthful than the machine. The bag smelled of wood and of something harder to name, something close to the smell of the inside of old churches, though he supposed that was partly because so many of the instruments he visited existed in the orbit of churches and church-adjacent lives: the faith that one ought to keep a piano, the guilt of not playing it, the occasional resurrection of someone's daughter home for Thanksgiving who would sit down and pick through a few bars of something she'd learned at twelve and then stand up and say, there, and never sit down again.

The first stop was the Kellermans'. He had been tuning their Kimball upright since 1987, longer than their marriage, longer certainly than their interest in music. Donna Kellerman met him at the door in a housecoat, holding coffee in both hands as though it were keeping her upright.

"Bernard," she said, not exactly warmly and not exactly coldly, in the tone reserved for things that arrived on schedule.

"Donna."

She let him in and he moved through the living room without guidance, past the school photographs arranged on the wall in a climbing diagonal—her grandchildren now, he supposed, faces he didn't recognize—and into the small back room where the Kimball stood against the wall beside a window that looked out on a feeder gone empty. The piano was covered with a crocheted runner and on top of it: a ceramic hen, three paperback novels, a photograph in a drugstore frame of what appeared to be a cruise ship. Bernard moved these items to the floor and lifted the lid and pressed middle C and listened.

It was badly flat, as it always was. The room held no humidity control and the radiator beside it went through violent cycles of heat, and the result was that the piano, tuned once a year, spent eleven months stretched between the tuning it had received and the tuning it was always trying to escape. He thought sometimes that pianos were like people in this regard. They tended back toward their natural disorder. The work was never permanent.

He opened the lid, removed the fallboard, and set to work. Donna did not stay. He heard the television come on in another part of the house, some morning program with the relentless cheerful urgency of morning programs. He worked in the way he always worked, methodically and without haste, setting the temperament in the middle octave first and then stretching outward in both directions, tuning octaves by eliminating the beats in the interval until what remained was clean and still, or as clean and still as this instrument would allow. The Kimball had a bright, slightly nasal tone that no amount of tuning would alter—that was a matter of construction, of the quality of the soundboard and the age of the strings—but he could make it accurate, and accuracy was what he could offer.

He was there forty minutes. On his way out, Donna handed him a check already written and he noticed she'd spelled his name wrong, as she always did, Bernerd, and he pocketed it without comment as he always did.

The next stop was eight miles down a county road that the county had mostly stopped maintaining, and he drove it slowly, watching the verge for deer. The farm belonged to a man named Hutchcraft who had died four years ago, and whose wife, Mabel, had called Bernard the following spring and said she supposed the piano needed tuning, even though, she noted, Harold hadn't played it since his stroke in 2009 and she herself had never played at all, she'd married into the piano, was how she put it. He tuned it every year since. She was always in the kitchen when he arrived and she was there now, a small woman in her eighties with close-cropped white hair and the careful movements of someone who had recently renegotiated the terms of her body.

The piano was a Mason & Hamlin from the late 1940s, a good instrument, better than the room it lived in deserved, and Bernard suspected Harold had bought it at a time when he still played regularly and still believed he would continue. The tone was dark and warm and the action, despite its age, was responsive, the hammers needing voicing more than anything else but holding up reasonably well. Bernard loved this piano in the way that he loved certain things quietly and without expectation of return: with a kind of custodial tenderness. He took more time than he needed to. He played small intervals after tuning them, listening not just for accuracy but for the quality of the sustain, the way the note opened and breathed in the room.

Mabel came to the doorway once and watched him.

"You look tired," she said.

"I'm all right."

"Harold used to play hymns," she said. "Early in the morning before I was up. I'd hear it from the bedroom and I'd think: there he is. Just to know where he was." She paused. "I don't know why I'm telling you that."

"I don't mind," Bernard said, which was true.

She went back to the kitchen and he finished the tuning and played a C major scale very slowly, one note at a time, listening to each note decay into the next, and the morning light through the window fell across the keys and he sat for a moment after the last note was gone, in the particular silence that follows a piano being played in an empty room, which is different from ordinary silence, fuller, as though the air has been reminded of something.

He ate his lunch in the truck in the parking lot of a gas station that had stopped selling gas sometime in the previous decade but still functioned as a convenience store. A sandwich he'd made that morning—cheese and mustard, nothing elaborate—and a thermos of coffee that was lukewarm now but still coffee. He was not a man who required much. This was something he had come to understand about himself late, later than he should have, after Margaret had left and the house had contracted around him and he'd learned, slowly, what it took to sustain a single life. Not very much. The discovery had been neither liberating nor depressing. It was simply accurate.

He thought about Thomas while he ate. He often thought about Thomas on this route because the route ended at St. Andrew's, and St. Andrew's was where Thomas had played his recital, and today—today was the reason for the finality that had settled over the morning like the low cloud cover that was moving in from the west. Today was not incidental. He had planned it, in the way that people plan things they cannot quite bring themselves to name directly: by arranging the circumstances and then waiting to see what he would feel.

Thomas had been eleven. Bernard had been—he calculated—forty-three. The recital had been in late October, a Sunday afternoon, the church's small sanctuary given over for the occasion, the pews filled with parents and siblings and grandparents arranged in the postures of polite anticipation. Thomas had played two pieces: a Bach minuet from the Anna Magdalena notebook and a simplified arrangement of a Chopin nocturne that his teacher, Mrs. Paulson, had marked up in pencil with fingering suggestions and dynamic reminders, the page so annotated it was barely legible. Bernard had sat in the third pew with Margaret's hand in his and he had watched his son walk to the bench with the particular walk of an eleven-year-old boy attempting dignity, and his chest had tightened in a way he recognized now as a form of grief pre-emptive, grief banked against the future, the body knowing what the mind had not yet admitted.

Thomas had played adequately. Not brilliantly—he was not a gifted pianist, he had decent technique and poor instinct for phrasing, which was perhaps Bernard's fault, Bernard who could hear the music so clearly and could not always find words for what he heard—but he had played without stopping, without the catastrophic memory lapse that all the children feared, and when he finished the Chopin he had sat for a moment with his hands in his lap before standing to bow, and Bernard had seen on his son's face an expression he would spend years trying to describe to himself. Relief, yes, but also something else—a kind of surprise, as though Thomas had not expected the music to be there when he reached for it, as though he'd set out on a journey uncertain of the destination and found it anyway, and found it was real.

That was the last recital. Not because Thomas was forced to stop—Mrs. Paulson continued teaching him for another two years—but because Thomas gradually, without announcement or drama, simply stopped practicing, and one day did not go to his lesson and then did not go again, and eventually Mrs. Paulson called and Bernard answered and she said she thought Thomas had decided to discontinue, and Bernard said yes, I suppose he has, and that was that. Later, when Thomas was in high school and then in college and then in the series of cities that had claimed him—Portland, Denver, Portland again, now somewhere in the southwest that Bernard always forgot to specify when people asked—the piano in their house collected the same sort of debris as the Kellermans' Kimball: items placed for a moment that became permanent by default. Bernard had kept the piano tuned. He had tuned it himself, which was its own kind of statement, a man bent over his own household's instrument making accurate what no one played.

He did not know exactly when he had stopped expecting Thomas to call. It was not an event. It was a gradual receding, like the tide going out so slowly you could not say when the water had been and when it wasn't. Thomas called on birthdays, usually. Called at Christmas, usually. The calls had a quality of completion about them, each one a small closed unit, self-contained, not pointing toward the next. Bernard was not estranged from his son. That was not the right word. The right word did not, as far as he knew, exist. There was a distance that was not hostility, an absence that was not rejection, a silence that was not—he believed, he had to believe—anger. It was simply what had happened. They were men who did not know how to be in each other's company, who had never learned the frequency on which they might reach each other, and who had both, separately, made peace with this in ways that were not quite peace but were the best available approximation.

The afternoon's third stop was the Delaney house, a farmhouse south of town that was being sold, the old woman who'd owned it having died the previous winter. The estate wanted the piano appraised; Bernard had said he could tune it as well while he was there. A nephew let him in and then disappeared, leaving Bernard alone with the house, which had the quality of houses recently vacated by long habitations—not empty so much as suspended, the furniture still in place but the life extracted, the rooms left holding the shapes of gestures no longer made. The piano was a Steinway upright from around 1910, extraordinary, the kind of instrument Bernard encountered rarely in domestic settings and when he did felt a particular solemn happiness, the way he imagined archaeologists felt when they found something intact. The tone was still there, under the detuning, under the age of the strings and the loosening of the pin block—the essential voice, that irreducible quality that Steinway had built into the instrument a hundred years ago and that had somehow survived the decades of a farmhouse in a county where winters were long and people did not always remember that wood breathed.

He tuned it carefully, more carefully than the job strictly required, because the instrument merited it. He noted in his book that the treble pin block was deteriorating and that two of the strings in the upper register were false and would need replacing before the next tuning, and he wrote out these observations in the small precise handwriting he'd developed over decades for exactly this purpose, and put the note on top of the piano for the nephew to find. Then he sat on the bench—the bench with its needlepoint cover, roses and leaves—and played a few bars of a Schubert impromptu he had memorized in his twenties and never entirely lost. He was not a skilled pianist. He had enough technique to demonstrate to his clients what a properly tuned instrument sounded like, to play a few notes of something so they could hear the difference, but he was not and had never been a performer. His relationship with the piano was diagnostic. He understood it from the inside, from the mechanism outward, and this was its own form of intimacy, perhaps more intimate than performance in some ways—he knew these instruments the way a doctor knows a body, by its failures, its compensations, its small everyday persistence against entropy—but it was not the intimacy of music-making, and he knew this.

He drove to St. Andrew's in the late afternoon, the light going long and amber the way it did in November, flattening itself against the face of things and making everything briefly beautiful and a little sad. The church was a modest white clapboard building at the edge of the town proper, set back from the road by a short gravel drive and flanked by maples that had mostly dropped their leaves. The pastor, a young woman named Ruth who'd been there only a few years and who regarded Bernard with a kind of benevolent patience, as one regards old arrangements that still function—she met him at the side door and handed him the key to the sanctuary and said she'd leave him to it, she had a meeting.

The sanctuary was cool and smelled of candles and old wood and the particular dusty perfume of hymnals. He had been in this room three hundred times, perhaps more, and it still did what it did to him—quieted something, focused something—in a way that he supposed was what churches were designed to do and that he experienced without belief, purely as architecture, purely as the effect of high ceilings and colored light and silence maintained by habit.

The piano was a Yamaha studio upright against the wall to the left of the chancel, not a remarkable instrument, kept reasonably well because Ruth had some sense of music and because the congregation had a choir that used it weekly. It was the third piano the church had owned in the time Bernard had been tuning here. The original instrument, the one present at Thomas's recital, had been replaced in 1998 by a different upright that had lasted until 2011. There was no material continuity. The piano Thomas had played no longer existed.

Bernard understood this and yet the room remained the room.

He opened the Yamaha and began his checks, pressing each key slowly through the full compass, listening. It was in reasonable shape. About an eighth of a tone flat overall, a few unisons that had drifted, one key in the tenor that stuck slightly—he worked on the key's mechanism until it moved freely, something minor, a small obstruction in the balance rail. He set the temperament, A440, the machine telling him one thing and his ear telling him a slightly different thing, and he split the difference as he always did, trusting the ear more than he admitted. He worked through the bass, through the middle octaves, into the treble, tuning octaves and then checking them again, listening for the sustained note to bloom open and hold still, the thing he had spent a life learning to hear: that moment of correctness, unmistakable, when the beating stopped and the intervals resolved into something that was not silence but the closest approach to it.

He worked until the light through the high windows had deepened to something nearly purple, and then he was done, and he sat on the bench.

He did not intend to sit long. He intended to pack his bag and lock up and drive home and eat something and sleep and in the morning begin the business of thinking about what came next—whether he would sell the truck and the equipment or give it away, whether he would call the few clients who weren't on this route and explain that he was retiring, whether retirement was even the right word for the ending of work that had never felt like work in the way that work was said to feel. But he sat for a moment and then he sat longer, and the room held him in the way that rooms sometimes do, not through any quality of warmth but through familiarity so deep it had become indistinguishable from comfort.

He placed his hands on the keys. He thought of Thomas's hands, which had been his own hands in miniature—the same square palms, the same slight crook in the ring fingers—bent over these very same keys, or keys in the same position in a room that was the same room. He thought of the expression on Thomas's face when he'd finished and stood to bow. That surprise. That discovery of the thing being real.

He called Thomas so rarely. Less than Thomas called him, he suspected, though he was not certain. There was always the sense that the call would be interruption, that Thomas was in the middle of something—his life, the ongoing specific weather of it—and that Bernard's voice would arrive like an announcement of an obligation, something requiring acknowledgment. He did not want to be an obligation. He had perhaps confused this preference with generosity, which it was not. He saw this now, sitting in the cooling sanctuary with his hands on the keys of a tuned piano in a town at the edge of the dark. He had called it giving space and it was a form of absence. He had called it not imposing and it was—he searched for what it was, the precise word—it was cowardice. Not of the dramatic kind. The small domestic kind, the fear that runs along the surface of things and prevents the smallest reaching, the almost-nothing it would have taken to say: I want to know you. I am here.

He pressed middle C and let it ring. The note rose into the high space of the sanctuary and widened and breathed and slowly surrendered itself to silence. He pressed it again. He thought of Mabel Hutchcraft saying I'd hear it from the bedroom and think: there he is. Just to know where he was.

He reached into his coat pocket for his phone. It was a simple phone, not smart, that he used primarily for confirming appointments. Thomas's number was in it. He had called it last on a Sunday in June—Thomas's birthday, Bernard had been slightly late in the day, it had been after dinner—and Thomas had answered and they had talked for eleven minutes about nothing essential, weather and health and a film Thomas had seen, and Bernard had hung up feeling the familiar mild desolation of the conversation that had passed without saying anything, without the single true sentence he had been constructing for years and could not deliver.

He held the phone. He pressed middle C one more time.

Outside, the last light was leaving the maples. The gravel drive was going gray. In the parking lot, his truck waited the way his truck always waited—without implication, without need, ready to carry him back through the dark over roads it knew by heart.

He looked at the number. He thought: he will answer or he will not. He thought: it is late in the year. He thought: the piano is in tune.

He pressed call and raised the phone to his ear and listened to it ring in that other place where his son was, wherever he was, in whatever room, with whatever light coming through whatever window—and he waited, with his free hand resting flat on the closed lid of the Yamaha, feeling through his palm the faint residual vibration of the note he had just played, still moving in the wood, not yet entirely still.
