The ferry left at eleven and he was the last to board, coming up the metal gangway with a bag that held one change of clothes and a toiletry kit and the photograph his mother had pressed into his hand four hours ago at the bus station in Tromsø, her fingers cold from standing outside and her eyes already doing the work of grief without waiting for confirmation. He had looked at the photograph on the bus. It was taken at a Christmas gathering perhaps fifteen years ago and showed his brother Erik standing in the particular way he had always stood, weight shifted to the left, chin slightly raised, as if he were about to say something worth hearing. The photograph was creased along one diagonal and the colours had gone the pale chalky way of old prints, so that Erik looked less like himself and more like a suggestion of himself, a rough version from which the final man had been refined. He had put it in the breast pocket of his jacket and had not looked at it again.

The vessel was called Nordlys and it was not a large ship. It served the crossing between the mainland and the island three times weekly in winter, and in summer added a fourth run on Fridays. He had crossed on it before, years ago, though he could not now remember when or in what circumstances. It smelled of diesel and wet wool and something cooked that had been cooling for a long time. He found a seat in the forward lounge along the port side, a row of bolted chairs facing a window already blackened by the water outside, and he set his bag at his feet and kept his jacket on.

There were perhaps thirty other passengers. Most had settled already, some sleeping with their heads against the cold glass, some eating from wax paper parcels. A family with two children occupied the cluster of seats near the vending machines; the younger child slept across three chairs with her feet in her mother's lap, and the older one, a boy of maybe ten, sat watching his father with an expression of solemn attention as the father spoke in a low voice about something the man could not hear. He watched this for a moment and then looked away.

His name was Kjetil. He was forty-four years old and he had not spoken to his brother in eleven years, not since the summer their father died and the things that needed to be said were said badly or not said at all, and the silence that followed had thickened into something that came to feel structural, like a wall you eventually stopped trying to see through and began to treat as a boundary of the known world. He did not hate his brother. He was not certain Erik had hated him. What had happened between them was less than hatred and in some ways worse: a mutual withdrawal, each of them receding from the other the way continents recede, slowly and without drama, so that you could say there was no single moment it happened, only the accumulated fact of the distance.

He took out his phone. His wife had sent a message asking if he had boarded safely and he typed back that he had. She had offered to come with him and he had said it was better that he go alone, and she had accepted this without argument, which was one of the things he valued most about her: the way she understood when he needed to go toward a difficult thing without company. He put the phone in his jacket pocket. He tried to think of nothing.

Outside the windows the water was black and the sky was black and the difference between them was only the stars, which were out in considerable numbers, though the glass was too dirty and the interior light too bright for him to see them clearly. He could see his own reflection instead, and behind it, fainter, the reflections of the sleeping passengers, so that the window held a kind of ghost lounge suspended against the darkness.

He went to the galley and bought a coffee in a paper cup and stood at the counter drinking it without tasting it. The man behind the counter was young and had the polished look of someone who had not yet been surprised by his own life. He asked if the crossing would be smooth and the young man said the forecast was good, the wind had dropped, and Kjetil nodded and carried his coffee back to his seat.

The body had been found three days ago on the eastern shore of the island, in a cove below a headland the locals called Skarvet. A fisherman had found it early in the morning, tangled partly in kelp, and had reported it to the police, who had in turn contacted the mainland office, which had contacted his mother. The police had not said outright that it was Erik. They had said there was a man, and that the man carried no identification, and that his approximate age and physical description were consistent with information the police had on file. What information, his mother had asked, and the officer had paused and said there had been a report, some months ago, of a man matching Erik's description living rough near the harbour on the island's north side. Someone had called it in as a welfare concern. When officers went to the location the man was gone.

His mother had not known Erik was on the island. She had not known where Erik was for some years. She received a card at Christmas with no return address. The card last year had a painted reindeer on the cover and inside only his name, written in the slightly formal script Erik had always used, as if every letter were a document.

The ferry moved out into the strait and the mainland lights diminished behind them. The strait was called Fugløysund and it was not wide, but at night in winter it felt wide. He thought about Erik at twenty, the year Kjetil had left for the university in Bergen. Erik had been finishing his final year of secondary school and had come to the bus station to see him off. They had stood together on the pavement not knowing how to be men to each other yet. Erik had shaken his hand and then pulled him quickly into an embrace and then stepped back and looked at some middle distance and said, well, and Kjetil had said, well. The bus had come. He had not known that this was the high point, in some ways, of their brotherhood: two young men on a pavement not knowing how to speak, the embrace rushed and insufficient, the bus coming. He would have spoken better if he had known.

He took the photograph out of his breast pocket and held it. The thing about the photograph was that it showed Erik happy. Not theatrically happy, not performing for the camera, but happy the way people sometimes are at Christmas when the year is ending and there is warmth and food and they have managed to get through it, another year, and the new one has not yet asked anything of them. Erik was holding a glass and looking at something just off-frame and there was the beginning of a smile on his face that might have been the end of a laugh. He was thirty at the time of the photograph. He had not yet lost the job at the fishing cooperative, had not yet started the two years in Narvik, had not yet married and un-married Silje, had not yet—but the list of what had not yet happened became too long to hold, because the photograph was fifteen years ago and a great deal can befall a man in fifteen years.

A woman sat down in the row behind him. She was perhaps sixty, well dressed in the way of a person who has made a long careful decision to always be well dressed. She had a small hardback book that she opened without first looking around the lounge, which he respected. The light was not very good for reading but she read. He could hear the faint sound of pages turning.

He thought about the last time he had seen Erik, which was at the house in Tromsø just before their father's funeral. They had arrived within an hour of each other and had stood in the kitchen while their mother sat with relatives in the living room. Erik had lost weight since Kjetil had seen him last and there was something in his eyes that had the quality of permanent vigilance, the look of a man who had spent too long in situations that required him to be alert. They had talked about the arrangements for the funeral, the practical things, the flowers and the cars and who would speak. Erik had said he wanted to say something and Kjetil had said of course, and then Kjetil had said something—he could not fully remember what, something about their father's wishes, something about the estate—and Erik had taken it as a kind of claim of authority, a positioning, and what followed was not quite an argument but the skeleton of one, and by the time their father was in the ground they were barely speaking and by the time a week had passed they were not speaking at all.

What he remembered most clearly about that last conversation in the kitchen was not what they had said but what he had noticed while they were saying it: that Erik had a small cut on his left hand, between the thumb and forefinger, that was still slightly fresh. He had looked at this cut and thought, my brother has been doing something with his hands, and there had been something in this thought that was almost unbearably ordinary, almost unbearably tender, and he had not known what to do with this and so he had continued saying whatever he was saying about the estate or the arrangements, and the moment had passed.

He put the photograph back in his pocket.

There was a low vibration through the floor from the engines and the sound of the hull working against the water. The boy near the vending machines had fallen asleep against his father's arm. The father was still awake, looking toward the windows. His face in the reflection had the composed and somewhat vacant expression of a man who had accepted that he was in transit and there was nothing to do about it.

Kjetil thought about what would happen at the other end. The ferry arrived at six in the morning. The police station was near the harbour and a Sergeant Halvorsen had said she would meet him there or, if there was a delay, at the hotel three streets up which was the only hotel open in winter. The viewing of the body—she had not called it that, she had said the identification—would be at the hospital, which was twenty minutes from the harbour town. He would be taken by car. It would not take long.

He had tried to construct the scene in his mind and the scene would not be constructed. He could not imagine what it would be like to stand in a room with what might be his brother. He was not afraid of it exactly. He was afraid of something adjacent to it: the possibility that he would stand there and feel nothing properly, that the grief or the relief or whatever was required of him would fail to arrive in the right form, that he would stand there dry and procedural, confirming or not confirming, and then walk back out into the winter morning, and that this inadequacy would be the final measure of whatever he and Erik had been to each other.

His coffee had gone cold. He set the cup on the floor beside his bag.

He thought: if it is him, I will tell my mother. If it is him, there will be a second funeral in a family that has already had its difficult funeral. He thought about Erik's hands—they had been Erik's best feature, not in the way of hands that are manicured or refined but in the way of hands that knew what they were for and went to work—and he thought about what condition the hands of a man found tangled in kelp might be in, and he stopped thinking about this.

He tried instead to think of what Erik had been good at. He was good at certain kinds of patience that Kjetil had never mastered: the patience for waiting out weather, for sitting still in a boat, for not speaking when there was nothing useful to say. He had a low fine singing voice and did not know many songs but the ones he knew he sang with conviction. He could fix an outboard motor under difficult conditions. He had, at one point, grown tomatoes very successfully in a climate that was not suited to tomatoes. He had loved Silje very much and this had been clear to anyone watching, and when it ended it had broken something in him that Kjetil was not sure had ever been repaired.

The woman with the book turned another page. Outside the window the darkness was complete and uniform. Somewhere to the north there were islands and beyond the islands open water that ran all the way to the ice, and he thought about what it meant to live on one of these islands in winter, to wake each morning to water on all sides and the mainland a boat-ride away. He had never done this. He had always chosen the mainland. He had always, when he examined himself honestly, chosen proximity to things: to cities, to roads, to whatever had the quality of continuation.

Erik had done otherwise. He did not know exactly when this had become true of his brother, but at some point Erik had begun choosing the periphery—the smaller harbours, the coastal jobs, the island postings, the places that were inconvenient to get to and from. Maybe this was just how Erik was. Maybe it was something that had happened to him. He thought about the welfare report, someone calling in their concern about a man living rough near a harbour on the north side of an island in the middle of winter. He thought about what kind of life produced this outcome: not a terrible outcome necessarily, not an ending, just a man in a situation that prompted a stranger to call. He felt something that was partly grief and partly anger and partly something else that had no name, something that was made of all the years of silence and the ease with which they had allowed themselves the silence.

He could have called. Erik could have called. Each had had the other's number, or some version of it, or could have obtained it. The silence had been a choice made daily for eleven years and he had never been able to decide which of them bore more of its weight. He leaned his head back against the seat and closed his eyes.

He slept briefly, without meaning to, and woke to the sound of the ferry's horn, two low blasts. He looked at his watch. It was ten past three. The lounge had the depleted feel of deep night; several people had moved to the reclining seats in the aft lounge or found floor space, and the remaining lights had been dimmed. The family near the vending machines was asleep in a collective pile. The woman with the book was still there, still reading, though she had taken out a small torch and was reading by that, which was either consideration for others or an act of private discipline, he could not tell.

He went to the bathroom at the rear of the lounge and ran cold water over his hands and looked at himself in the mirror. He was not a man who spent time looking at himself in mirrors and the face that returned his gaze had the slightly surprised quality of something seen unexpectedly. He was older than he generally considered himself. There were the particular lines around the eyes that his father had also had. He thought that Erik would have these lines too if Erik was the kind of man who accumulated lines, and he thought that he was.

He dried his hands and went back to his seat. The photograph was in his pocket and he did not take it out. He had looked at it enough. He knew the face in it and he knew that the face would be different now, fourteen or fifteen years on, worn or obscured by whatever those years had held, and he thought about how identification worked, how you looked past the surface alterations to some more fundamental shape underneath, the architecture of a person that persists despite everything. He thought: I will know him if it is him. Whatever else has happened, whatever the water and the years have done, I will know my own brother. He believed this and was afraid of it equally.

He looked out the window and saw that the darkness had changed in quality, had become the darkness before light rather than the darkness of pure night. The horizon was not yet visible but there was a sense of its imminence, a preparation in the sky. He thought about the island ahead of them, a mass of rock and scrub and winter grass and the remains of fishing operations both ongoing and abandoned, the small harbour town with its hotel and its police station and the road running twenty minutes out to the hospital, and he thought about Erik, who had perhaps been there, living rough near the harbour, watching the water with whatever expression he wore when he thought no one was watching.

He thought: what was it like, the last day? But this was not a thought he could afford before he knew whose last day he was thinking about, and he put it down.

The island appeared in the pre-dawn as a dark elevation against the slightly lighter sky. He watched it come forward out of the darkness, growing more particular as the ferry drew close, until he could see the lights of the harbour and the shapes of the buildings, small and steady in the cold. The engines changed their pitch. There was a grinding sound as something was prepared below.

He picked up his bag. He put on his jacket and then took it off and folded it over his arm. He stood there a moment in the aisle between the sleeping passengers, holding his jacket, looking toward the island in the window. The woman with the book had closed it and was putting on her coat. The boy by the vending machines had woken and was looking around with the clear-eyed blankness of a child who has come awake in the wrong place and not yet understood how he got there.

Outside the window the harbour was close. He could see the dock lights on the water and beyond them the streets going up from the waterfront, a few lit windows, a dog moving along the base of a building. Somewhere in the town there was a police station where Sergeant Halvorsen was either already there or would be there soon, and somewhere beyond the town was a hospital where a body lay in the condition that bodies lie in when they have come from the water, and the body was or was not his brother.

He put on his jacket and zipped it. He took the photograph out and looked at it once more: Erik at thirty, glass in hand, looking at something off-frame, the beginning or the end of a laugh on his face. He put the photograph back and the ferry touched the dock and the horn sounded once, a single note that went out over the dark water and did not come back.
