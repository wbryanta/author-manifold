The ferry leaves Ullapool at nine and the man does not go below. He stands at the stern rail while the harbour lights shrink and then vanish, and when there is nothing left to watch he remains there anyway, his jacket collar turned against the rain. His name is Daniel. He is forty-three years old and he has not slept.

The woman at the Caledonian MacBrayne desk had looked at him with the particular sympathy that attaches itself to late-night travellers, that face people make when they suppose you are grieving or escaping something, and she wasn't wrong on either count, though Daniel would have said he wasn't sure which was which anymore. He'd asked for a cabin and then cancelled it. A cabin seemed like a decision about what kind of night this was going to be, and he didn't know yet.

He finds a seat in the upper lounge. Plastic chairs in interlocking rows the colour of old mustard, the strip lighting making everyone look autopsy-pale. A young couple sleeps draped across each other, the girl's head on the boy's chest, his chin resting on her hair. An older man in a waxed jacket reads something on a tablet, its screen trembling with the ship's motion. There is a smell of diesel and something fried from the cafeteria below. Daniel buys a coffee from a vending machine and doesn't drink it. He sets it on the chair arm and watches it.

His brother's name is Martin. Was Martin. May be Martin. The constable he'd spoken to on the phone — a woman with a Highland accent he found oddly steadying, like someone reading from a very clear map — had been careful with her language. A man found near Stornoway. Some items near the body. A wallet was mentioned, a specific jacket. She said they would be grateful if someone could come. She said they understood it was a significant journey. She used the word significant the way people use it when they mean hard and she was right about that too.

Daniel had written Martin's address in his phone two years ago after their mother's funeral, when Martin had appeared at the crematorium in a linen jacket with grass stains on both knees and smelling of something chemical and sweet, and Daniel's wife Karen had said just get the address at least, and so he had, and Martin had recited it with that terrible brightness he put on sometimes, that performance of a man who was doing very well, thank you. Daniel had typed it in and they had not spoken since. He'd looked the address up on a map once. A village eleven miles outside Stornoway. He'd looked at the satellite image: a grey-rendered house, some black land, the Atlantic framing everything.

There were two years between them, Daniel older. Growing up there had been a period — he was perhaps eight, Martin six — when they were so close in register that people took them for twins. The same gap-toothed smile, the same way of leaning forward when they were interested in something, chins out, brows down, as if ideas were physical things you had to square up against. He remembers this in the lounge of the ferry with some precision, the way you remember things when you're afraid you're going to need them.

Martin had always been different in a way that eluded clean description. Not troubled, exactly. Not brilliant, though he was that too. He was porous, Daniel used to think. The world got into Martin at a cellular level and Martin had to metabolize it somehow, and the metabolizing had gone various ways over the years, some of them fine and some of them not. There had been a PhD — philosophy of mind — abandoned in the third year. A period making furniture in Devon with a woman named Helen who Daniel had liked very much and who had called him once, after, to say she was sorry that it had come to this, though she hadn't specified what this was. Various addresses. Various absences. The chemical smell at their mother's funeral, and that terrible brightness.

Daniel thinks: he is probably not dead.

He thinks: but if he isn't, where has he been.

Outside the portholes the Minch is black. He can hear it working against the hull, that deep systemic pressure, and he thinks about the water's temperature, which he knows from something he read once — five or six degrees Celsius, even in summer, cold enough to incapacitate a swimmer in minutes. He stops thinking about this.

He calls Karen but it goes to voicemail. She will be asleep. He listens to her outgoing message — she uses her full name, Karen Elliot, as if the voicemail were a professional transaction — and he finds this unbearably tender, this small formality she maintains. He doesn't leave a message. He doesn't know what he would say. He is on a boat in the dark going to look at a dead man's face.

They had a fight, the last time they spoke. Not the funeral — he means the time before, which was perhaps eighteen months before the funeral, when Martin had turned up at their house in Edinburgh unannounced and stayed four days and on the fourth day had borrowed three hundred pounds and then left before Daniel was awake. The note said: *You know I'm good for it*. He was not good for it. That wasn't the point, Karen said, the point is the pattern, and she was right. She was usually right. Daniel had called Martin and they had said things, and the things had been true and also unforgivable in the way that true things sometimes are, and then there had been the funeral and the address and then two years.

What he said on the phone was: I don't know how to help you anymore.

What he meant was something else and he'd known it even then. What he meant was: *I am afraid of what it costs to try*. But you can't say that. You can't hold someone at arm's length and then confess that the arm's length is about your own fear, because then you're not even protecting yourself with the dignity of principle, you're just a coward who wanted to believe he had reasons.

He gets up and walks the length of the lounge and back. The older man with the tablet looks up briefly and returns to his screen. The young couple does not stir. Daniel goes out onto the side deck and holds the rail. The wind has changed direction, coming off the beam now, and the rain has relented into something that is almost mist. He can see no stars. He can see no land. The ship's lights cast a yellowish nimbus on the water directly below him and he watches it, that moving circle of pallor, the sea green-black at its edges.

He is trying to remember Martin's face and he finds he can only retrieve it in specific contexts. Martin at fourteen trying to teach him to play a chord on a guitar he'd found in a skip. Martin at nineteen at their father's hospital bedside doing a crossword in a very quiet voice, reading the clues aloud as if their father were asleep and might be gently woken. Martin at maybe thirty in a pub in Glasgow after a Celtic match, happy in that easy way he could be sometimes, that way of inhabiting a moment without apparent cost, and Daniel had thought: this is it, this is the version that gets to stay. But it hadn't stayed.

And then Martin at the crematorium, that jacket, that brightness.

Daniel thinks: what I know about my brother is all the shapes of his absence.

He doesn't mean this as a fine thought. He means it as a fact, assembled here on the deck while the ferry makes its way across the water, while the body in Stornoway is or isn't his brother's body. He has been rehearsing his brother's absence for so long that the loss has worn grooves in him, that particular kind of grief that happens before the death and leaves you feeling robbed twice.

He goes to the cafeteria and orders something he doesn't want and sits with it. There is a man two tables over eating a full Scottish at what must now be past midnight, eating it with complete seriousness, and Daniel watches him and feels something — not quite hunger, not quite comfort, something in the neighbourhood of both. He eats his chips. He drinks a Coke. The ship rides a slow swell and the overhead lights sway by about two degrees and then return.

Their father died when Daniel was twenty-three and Martin twenty-one. It had been expected — the heart had been failing for two years — and still it wasn't. Martin had come home from university and spent a week and gone back and then come home again when it was close and been there at the end, which Daniel had not been, having been on a placement in London and having miscalculated. He'd arrived four hours after. He'd stood in the hospital corridor and Martin had hugged him and said nothing, just held on, and Daniel had thought then and thinks now that Martin was trying to give him something — to transfer some portion of those four hours, that deathbed, that knowledge — and that this was an act of extraordinary generosity that Daniel had never acknowledged, had in fact run from, had begun in some obscure way to resent, as if Martin holding what Daniel had missed meant Martin had something over him.

He is seeing this for the first time with something like clarity and he wishes he were somewhere he could sit still with it, not on a boat moving toward a body.

He goes back to the lounge. The couple is awake now, talking in low voices, and the man with the tablet is gone. Daniel sits and takes out his phone and looks at Martin's number. He doesn't call it. What would it mean, to call a number whose owner might be in a mortuary in Stornoway? He thinks about the phone, wherever it is, ringing in some evidence bag or some damp field. He thinks about the ringtone Martin had on his phone at their mother's funeral, which had gone off once and been swiftly silenced — he can't remember the song.

He opens his photos. He rarely photographs Martin but he finds three. One is from the funeral, taken without Martin's knowledge: Martin standing a little apart from the main group of mourners, smoking, looking at something off to the left. He is in profile. His hair is longer than Daniel remembers expecting. There is something in the posture that Daniel recognises from his own posture in certain moods — that combination of wanting to be present and wanting to be anywhere else — and he finds this recognition unbearable and comforting at once.

The second photo is from a Christmas, years ago, both of them and their mother. Martin is grinning at something out of frame. He has his arm around their mother and she is barely up to his shoulder and she looks proud in that way she sometimes did, not of anything particular, just proud that they existed and were standing next to her.

The third photo is a mistake, taken when the phone was in his pocket, a blur of floor or ceiling, nothing.

He falls asleep sitting up, which surprises him since he'd believed he wouldn't. He dreams of something without images, just a sound like the sea, which may just be the sea.

He wakes when the lounge fills with people coming up from the cabins, assembling their bags, the particular choreography of arrival. Through the porthole he can see the lights of Stornoway harbour, weak in the pre-dawn grey, and the outline of the town coming out of the water. He sits up. His neck aches. He watches the land take shape.

He had been to Lewis once, years ago, on a camping trip with Karen before they were married. They had driven to the Callanish Stones and stood among them in a light rain and Karen had put her hand on one of the stones and said something about the people who put them there, wondering what they understood that we'd forgotten. He had been dismissive, or not dismissive exactly but insufficient, had not matched what she was offering, and she'd withdrawn it quietly and they'd walked back to the car. He had thought about this moment many times since. He thinks about it now.

The ferry docks with a long metallic shudder. He joins the queue for the car deck, though he has no car, and then peels off toward the main disembarkation ramp. Outside the air is cold and salt and there are herring gulls screaming somewhere above the dock in the grey light. He puts his bag over his shoulder and walks toward a town he doesn't know.

The police station is not far. He'd looked it up. He walks with his hands in his jacket pockets and tries to decide what he is prepared for. He passes a bakery that is already lit, a woman stacking rolls in the window, and the smell of bread coming from the ventilation is so acute and so kind that it stops him for a moment. He stands on the pavement. He breathes.

The constable is not the woman from the phone but a young man with a careful manner who shakes Daniel's hand and thanks him for coming such a long way and offers him tea, which Daniel accepts without meaning to. They sit in a small room with a table. The constable says that there are some items — would he like to see the items before or. Daniel says before.

There is a wallet. Brown leather, worn through on the fold. Daniel opens it and there is a Tesco Clubcard and a library card and a card for some loyalty scheme at a hardware shop and some money. The library card is Lewisham, which would be from years ago, and the name on it is Martin Philip Elliot, and seeing the full name — the same middle name as Daniel's, Philip, for their father — does something to Daniel's breathing that he has to work to correct.

There is also a book. A small paperback, very battered, water-damaged at the bottom half so that the pages have swollen and warped and dried in crumpled shapes. He can read the spine: it is the Penguin edition of *The Notebooks of Malte Laurids Brigge*, and Daniel holds it for a moment and feels something go through him like a current, because it had been him who'd given Martin this book, it had been him, twenty years ago, pressing it on him, saying you'll love this, not sure anymore why he'd thought that, but Martin had loved it, had read it twice on the same holiday and reported back with a kind of fervent seriousness. It had been a point of contact. It had been one of their things.

He sets the book down on the table.

He says: when did you find him.

The constable says: three days ago. And he says: it was out near the Butt. He says: it would appear he'd been living rough for some time. He says other things about the condition of the body and what the doctor had said and Daniel hears the words but they do not assemble into what they're supposed to mean, they just come at him like weather.

He says: okay.

He says: alright.

He says: I'd like to see him now.

The room they take him to is not cold, or not theatrically cold — it is just a room, and Martin is there under a white sheet and a man in a jumper — not a doctor, something else, an administrator of some kind — stands to one side and nods and folds back the sheet to the shoulders.

Martin's face is not peaceful, which is what people say. It is not much of anything anymore. It is a face being reclaimed by something more basic than expression. But it is Martin's face. He is certain. He is so certain that the certainty arrives before the grief, as if his whole body has been waiting to confirm the thing it already knew and needed only to ratify, and then the grief arrives behind it like a second wave.

He says yes.

He is in a corridor later, he is not sure how long later, and the constable has given him more tea and a Tunnock's from somewhere and he is holding both and doing nothing with either. Through a window at the end of the corridor the sky is coming up properly now, the specific blue-grey of a Lewis dawn, the colour of neither one thing nor another.

He thinks about the Callanish Stones and Karen's hand on the stone and his own insufficiency. He thinks about a pub in Glasgow and a moment that was supposed to stay. He thinks about the phone call and the arm's length and the truth that was really about fear.

He thinks about his brother reading a crossword aloud to a dying man, gently, so as not to wake him, or so as to wake him, or just because Martin had always known instinctively that the things you do in terrible moments are the small things, the crossword, the quiet voice, the staying.

He takes out his phone and calls Karen. It is half six. It rings three times.

She says: Daniel.

He says: it's him.

She says nothing for a moment. He can hear her breathing and the sound of her shifting in bed, the small creak of it, and he knows she is sitting up and he knows her face, the one she makes when she is gathering herself to hold something, and the knowing of it is so physical that he has to close his eyes.

She says: are you alright.

He says: no.

She says: I know. I know you're not.

He looks out the window at the pale sky coming in over the harbour, over the water he crossed in the night, over all that dark distance between where he is and where he started. He holds the phone with both hands.

He says: I don't want to be someone who doesn't know how to stay.

She is quiet. Outside a gull is crying. The sky is getting lighter in the way that it does this far north, slowly, as if the dark is reluctant, as if it knows something about the value of what it's giving up.

She says: come home, Daniel.

He drinks the tea. It has gone cold but he drinks it anyway, down to the bottom, and he sets the cup on the windowsill and looks at the sea, which is the colour of old pewter, which is the colour of something that has been here far longer than any of this and will be here long after, which is neither a comfort nor not a comfort, which is just what's there.
