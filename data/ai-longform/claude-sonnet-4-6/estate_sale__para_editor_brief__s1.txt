The card table was the first to go. Marion watched from the kitchen doorway as two teenage boys in matching orange t-shirts — BULLDOG MOVING & ESTATE SERVICES stenciled across the back — lifted it from the corner where it had stood for as long as she could remember and carried it out through the front door without ceremony, without even pausing to acknowledge what it was. She heard the screen door bang shut behind them. Then the sound of a van door sliding open on its track.

She had arrived at seven that morning, three hours before the sale was scheduled to begin, intending to go through the house one final time, to confirm that everything belonging to the category of *keep* had already been removed and that everything remaining truly belonged to the category of *sell*, which she'd been told by her brother Dennis was simple and obvious and required no further deliberation. Dennis lived in Scottsdale now and had not come. He had, however, sent a very organized spreadsheet.

The house was a Dutch Colonial on Mercer Street, buff-colored with dark green shutters, built in 1951. Marion had grown up here from the age of two until she left for Columbus at eighteen. Her mother had lived here for fifty-three years, and the accumulation of fifty-three years was everywhere — in the closets and the basement and in particular in the formal living room that no one had ever used formally, which was lined along two walls with her mother's books and along the other two walls with her mother's collections, the ceramic owls, the commemorative plates from national parks, the small brass figurines of uncertain origin that Marion had never been able to identify as anything in particular. They lined the windowsills too, and they stood on the mantelpiece beside the carriage clock that Dennis had already taken, leaving a clean rectangle in the dust.

The estate sale company was run by a woman named Connie Parrish, who was approximately Marion's age, sixty, perhaps sixty-two, and who had the efficient brightness of someone accustomed to managing other people's grief without being infected by it. Connie had small laminated price tags on everything. The card table: twelve dollars. The wingback chair: forty-five. The set of Encyclopaedia Britannica from 1974, complete, which Marion's mother had bought on installment from a door-to-door salesman because she believed in the transmission of knowledge even though Marion and Dennis had never once opened them: eight dollars for the set.

"People don't really buy encyclopedias," Connie had explained when they'd spoken on the phone two weeks earlier. "But sometimes a decorator will take them for a bookshelf. Or a photographer. Staging, you know. They look nice."

Marion had said that was fine. She had said that everything was fine, meaning she consented to the prices, meaning she understood the logic of liquidation. She had driven up from Cincinnati on Friday afternoon and had spent that evening and all of Saturday sorting through her mother's bedroom, removing what the spreadsheet designated as family items: the jewelry, a few photographs, the Bible with the family names written inside the front cover in several different hands going back to 1887, her grandmother's embroidered pillowcases. She had taken these things and placed them in cardboard boxes in the back of her Subaru, and she had driven to the Hampton Inn on Route 40 and eaten a club sandwich in bed and watched television without knowing what she was watching.

Now it was Sunday. The sale had begun at ten and it was nearly noon and the house was full of strangers.

She had not expected the strangers. That is, she had expected them intellectually, had understood that an estate sale required buyers, required the public admission of people who did not know her mother, who had no framework for understanding that the card table was where her mother had played bridge every Thursday for thirty years, where she had sat doing her taxes, where she had spread out the pieces of jigsaw puzzles in the evenings when she couldn't sleep. Marion had understood all of this abstractly, and yet she was still somehow surprised by the specific reality of the strangers, by their normalcy, their ordinariness, the way they moved through the rooms touching things and discussing prices in low voices with companions, treating the objects with neither reverence nor disrespect but with a kind of calm commercial consideration that was somehow more disorienting than either.

A man in his seventies was examining the toaster. He turned it over and looked at the bottom and set it back down. Four dollars, the tag said. He didn't take it.

A young couple — late twenties, the woman visibly pregnant — stood in the doorway to the formal living room looking at the wingback chair. The woman sat down in it experimentally and looked at her partner, who made a face that was not yes and not no. Marion looked away.

She had positioned herself in the kitchen because the kitchen felt most like a neutral territory, a room she associated less intensely than the others with specific memories, though this was not entirely accurate. The kitchen was where her mother had stood every morning of Marion's childhood making oatmeal, standing at the stove in her housecoat with her hair not yet done, and Marion had always sat at the kitchen table and watched her mother's back and listened to the specific sounds of the morning — the gas burner catching, the wooden spoon against the pot, the particular tone of the radio her mother kept on the counter tuned to the local station that played easy listening and farm reports. The radio was gone. Dennis had taken it in an earlier visit, though it wasn't on the spreadsheet.

She should call Dennis, she thought. She should call Dennis and tell him the radio wasn't on the spreadsheet. But she knew that if she called Dennis he would apologize efficiently and the conversation would turn immediately to the question of the house itself, the sale of the house, the timeline, the realtor, the questions of disbursement and taxes, and she was not ready to have that conversation today. She was already having enough conversations.

A woman about her own age touched her arm. "Excuse me, hon, do you know if this is the original range?"

Marion looked at the range. Her mother's range, white and slightly yellowed, with four burners and a clock above the dial panel that had read 11:47 for as long as Marion could remember because her mother had never figured out how to change it after the power went out one summer.

"I believe so," Marion said. "It came with the house."

"Nineteen fifty-one?"

"Yes."

The woman nodded as if confirming a theory. "My sister has the exact same one. They last forever, those old ones." She looked at it with affection, with a kind of collector's warmth. "Is it included?"

Marion said she believed Connie would know. She pointed the woman toward the front of the house and the woman thanked her and moved away through the dining room, where the table and six chairs were tagged collectively at two hundred and twenty dollars, which Marion had thought was very low and then had wondered why she thought so, since she didn't want the table, had no room for the table, and the price being low only meant that it would sell, which was the point.

She went to the back door and stood on the small concrete stoop that looked out over her mother's yard. The yard was still beautiful, absurdly so, because her mother had tended it until the last year of her life with a devotion that Marion had always slightly envied, the devotion of someone who understood what she was doing and did it without questioning whether it was the right use of her time. The hostas were up along the back fence. The forsythia her mother had planted the year Marion was born had grown into a substantial shrub and was just past blooming, the last yellow flowers browning at the edges. The vegetable garden was unplanted this year, a rectangle of turned earth that Marion's neighbor Tom Grady had apparently tilled out of loyalty or habit in April, not knowing or perhaps not accepting that there would be no one to put anything in it.

Marion stood looking at the vegetable garden for a while. She thought about taking something from the yard. A cutting of the forsythia, to plant somewhere. Her backyard in Cincinnati was not much of a backyard — a rectangle of grass, a dying maple she kept meaning to have assessed. She had no natural aptitude for plants. She had never understood, when she was young and her mother was in the garden, what the appeal was, what patience felt like as a pleasure rather than an imposition. She had grown into an appreciation of it, or rather she had grown into a regret that she hadn't inherited it, which was not quite the same thing.

She heard Connie's voice inside, doing something managerial. She heard the screen door bang again.

The wingback chair came out while she was standing there. She didn't see it but she heard it, heard the scrape of the legs on the hardwood floor — a sound she recognized without wanting to — and then the bump of the legs against the door frame. The pregnant woman's partner said "almost" and then "got it." Then the screen door. Then quiet.

Marion went back inside.

In the formal living room, which looked emptier now without the chair, an elderly man was going through the books on the shelves with the slow pleasure of someone who reads shelf labels the way other people read menus. He was pulling volumes out and reading the title pages and putting them back. He was taking his time. He had the look of someone who had all afternoon, which Marion supposed he did.

On the shelf beside where the man was browsing there was a framed photograph that should have been in the box in her car. Marion couldn't understand how she'd missed it. It was a photograph of her parents taken sometime in the early 1960s, before Marion was born, on what appeared to be a picnic or an outdoor gathering of some kind. Her father had his arm around her mother's shoulders, and her mother was laughing at something off-camera. Her father was looking at her mother. Marion had seen this photograph her entire life without looking at it carefully, and now she lifted it from the shelf and looked at it carefully and did not understand, as she often had not understood, how a person could look at another person that way and then one day simply be gone from the world. Her father had died in 1987. Her mother had kept this photograph on the shelf for thirty-six years.

The elderly man glanced at her and then back at the books with the tact of someone who had been in many houses and understood the social contract of estate sales, which required pretending that the person standing in the middle of the room holding a photograph was not doing what they were obviously doing.

"Are these priced individually?" he asked, without turning from the shelf.

"I think so," Marion said. "There should be tags." She could hear that her voice sounded like her ordinary voice and not like a voice that needed assistance, which was something she had always been told was a gift and had occasionally suspected was a deficit.

The man found a volume and held it up. It was the poems of Edna St. Vincent Millay, a paperback, the spine cracked from use. The tag on the inside cover said fifty cents.

"Your mother's?" the man said.

"Yes."

"Good taste." He held the book as if weighing it in his palm. Then he set it back on the shelf. "I already have it," he said, by way of explanation, and moved down to the next section.

Marion tucked the photograph under her arm and went through the dining room, where a woman was asking Connie whether the china cabinet came with the china, and through the front hallway, and out the front door, and down the porch steps to her car. She put the photograph on the passenger seat and sat down in the driver's seat and did not start the car. The door to the house was propped open with a brick. She watched a man carry out two of the dining room chairs, one under each arm, and load them into a pickup truck that was parked in front of the house. He went back in. She watched him come back out with two more chairs.

The light through the maple tree in the front yard was the particular light of late May in Ohio, which was not so different from late May anywhere but which Marion had always associated with a specific feeling that she had never found a name for, a feeling that had something to do with the end of the school year and the beginning of summer and the particular quality of suspension between one kind of time and another. Her mother had been healthy in late May. In late May she had still been in the garden. She had been in the hospital by August and had died in October, a death the doctors characterized as having gone well, which Marion had thought about often in the months since.

She took out her phone and looked at it. Dennis had sent a text at 9:47 that morning: *How's it going?* She had not answered it. She typed *Fine, busy* and sent it and put the phone in her pocket.

She went back inside.

The afternoon wore on. The encyclopedias went to a young woman who seemed genuinely pleased, who said she was furnishing her first apartment and had always wanted a set, which was not something Marion had expected to hear and which moved her in a way that was disproportionate to the transaction. She thanked the woman in a way that was probably too warm for the context. The young woman seemed not to notice or not to mind. The toaster was still on the counter. The four-dollar tag fluttered when the door opened.

Around two o'clock the traffic in the house slowed. Connie came into the kitchen and reported on the progress of the sale with the practiced calm of someone delivering a weather forecast, noting what had sold and what had not and what she intended to mark down if the afternoon was slow. The range had sold. The dining table and remaining chairs had sold. The wingback chair had sold. The card table had sold. The ceramic owls had sold in a lot to a woman who collected them, which Connie said happened fairly regularly with ceramics — collectors appeared, it was one of the regularities of the work, the way specific things drew specific people.

"The books are moving slowly," Connie said. "They usually do. People think they want books until they're faced with the actual books."

"My mother read all of them," Marion said. "Most of them more than once."

Connie nodded with the particular nod of someone who has heard this said about many books in many houses. "Sometimes the library will take them. As a donation. Or a used book dealer." She paused. "We can arrange that at the end of day if you like. It simplifies things."

Marion said she would think about it.

She went back to the formal living room, which was considerably emptier now. The ceramic owls were gone. The commemorative plates were gone — she hadn't seen them go, had not been watching at the right moment. The books remained on their shelves, and Marion stood before them and felt, as she had not quite felt yet in the course of the day, the specific shape of her mother's life as defined by these particular objects, these volumes arranged in an order that was partly alphabetical and partly categorical and partly the order of a person's private reasoning that had never been explained to anyone and now never would be.

She found, on the third shelf, a novel she had not thought about in thirty years. She had read it here, in this house, the summer she was fourteen. She could remember with a precision that seemed unfair the feeling of reading it — the feeling of being inside it, of the world of the house and the town and the summer going transparent around her in the way the world went transparent when she was reading something that was working on her, and the particular late-summer evening when she'd finished it, sitting on the porch in the cooling air, and had felt that she was on the verge of understanding something important that she could not quite articulate. She had never been able to articulate it. She had, over the years, occasionally tried to re-read the book and found that the feeling did not return the way she wanted it to, that you could not reproduce the specific ignorance that made a first reading possible.

She took the book from the shelf and opened it and saw, in her mother's handwriting in the top right corner of the first page, her own name. Marion, 1980, her mother had written, and beneath that, in different ink as if added later: *She cried at the ending.* Marion did not remember crying, but she supposed she must have, or her mother would not have written it. Her mother had watched her cry at the ending of a book in 1980 and had written it down in the book itself, and had kept the book for forty-four years.

Marion put the book with the photograph on the passenger seat of the car.

The house was nearly empty by four o'clock. What remained were the things that had not sold — a set of mismatched bookshelves, several boxes of miscellaneous kitchen items, a standing lamp with a bent shade, the encyclopedias that had apparently returned, the young woman having changed her mind or been unable to fit them in her car. The toaster was still on the counter. Marion picked it up and removed the tag and put it in the box with the miscellaneous kitchen items, and then thought about this decision for a moment, and then took it back out and held it.

It was a perfectly ordinary toaster. Chrome, two slots, a crumb tray on the bottom that had probably never been cleaned. There was no reason to keep it. She had a toaster. She set it back on the counter.

She walked through the house. Through the rooms that were mostly empty now, the furniture gone or rearranged by absence, the floors showing where things had stood for years in shapes of slightly protected wood, slightly darker or lighter, the negative geometry of a life. In the formal living room the shelves were thinning but still present, the books still there, still arranged in her mother's private order. In the dining room the table's absence made the room look very large. In the bedroom the bed was still there — Dennis had said to leave it, that it was too big to move, that the new owners could deal with it — and Marion sat on the edge of it the way she had sat on the edge of it as a child when she came in on Saturday mornings to talk to her mother. The mattress sank with her weight. The room smelled like her mother's perfume and like something under that, something older and more basic, the smell of a house that had been inhabited for a very long time by one person.

She sat there for a while.

Connie knocked on the doorframe at a quarter past four and said that they were finishing up, that the remaining items would be collected by a donation service on Tuesday, that she would have the final accounting to Marion by end of week.

Marion said thank you. She said that Connie had been very efficient, which was true and which she meant as a compliment and which Connie received as one.

"It gets easier," Connie said, and then paused as if she had surprised herself. "I don't know if that's true, actually. It gets different."

Marion nodded.

She was the last one in the house. The Bulldog Moving boys had packed up their dolly and their orange t-shirts and driven away. Connie had locked the front door with the key Marion had given her and handed it back. The house was quiet in the way that a house is quiet when it understands something has changed, which was a thought Marion recognized as irrational and allowed herself anyway.

She went through the rooms one last time, not looking for anything, not doing inventory, just walking. The floors creaked in the places they had always creaked. The light through the kitchen window was the long light of the late afternoon now, lying in a pale rectangle across the linoleum. She stood in the kitchen and looked at the space where the radio had been and listened to the silence of its absence. She looked at the clock on the stove that had read 11:47 for as long as she could remember.

She saw that it read 4:22.

She stood there for a moment trying to understand this. At some point in the past several hours, in the commotion of the sale, someone had changed the clock. Perhaps one of the Bulldog boys, perhaps a browser, perhaps Connie herself, she would never know. Someone had simply adjusted the hands to the correct time, a small automatic act of order, the instinct to fix a thing that was wrong, performed by a person who had no idea it was wrong at all.

Marion looked at the clock reading the correct time and felt something move in her chest, something she couldn't name, and then she picked up the toaster from the counter and walked out through the back door into the late afternoon light, through the yard where the hostas were coming up and the forsythia was past its blooming, and around the side of the house to her car. She put the toaster in the back with the boxes. She sat in the driver's seat and put her hands on the wheel. On the passenger seat, the photograph of her parents, and the book with her name in it, and the notation in different ink, in her mother's hand, of something she had witnessed and found worth keeping.
