The ferry pulled away from the dock at half past ten, and Goran stood at the stern railing watching the lights of the terminal dissolve into a general smear of orange against the low cloud. The water in the harbour was the colour of pewter, indifferent and heavy, and the wake the vessel cut into it closed behind them with a patience that suggested the sea had been waiting for precisely this erasure. He stayed at the railing longer than was comfortable. The cold off the strait was a different kind of cold than what he had left behind in the city—wetter, more committed, the kind that found the gaps in clothing and worked at them methodically.

He had a cabin. A woman at the shipping company had told him this with a particular emphasis, as though she understood something about the nature of his errand and had decided a cabin was the least the company could provide. He did not know how she could have known. He had given nothing away on the telephone. He had said only that he needed a crossing on the overnight service and whether there were berths available. Perhaps she gave everyone that emphasis. Perhaps it was simply how she spoke, and he was attributing to her a sensitivity she did not possess because he needed, just then, to believe that the world contained people who perceived these things.

The cabin was on the lower passenger deck, and it smelled of the particular institutionalised cleanliness of vessels that carry strangers through the night—disinfectant and something sweet beneath it, a staleness that no amount of chemical treatment could fully suppress. There were two narrow berths, and he had been given the lower one. The upper was not made up. He set his bag on the berth and sat beside it and looked at the porthole, which was black and occasionally lit from below by the white fracturing of a wave. He had brought almost nothing with him. A change of clothes. His wallet and his passport. A paperback novel he had taken from the shelf by the door of his apartment without looking at the cover, and which turned out, when he examined it in the cabin's yellow light, to be a book he had already read years ago and had not much liked. He set it on the shelf above the berth. He thought he might not sleep, but he also thought that if he lay down he might discover that sleep was more available than it seemed.

He did not lie down. Instead he went back out into the corridor, which was narrow and slightly warm, and followed it forward until it opened into the passenger lounge. There were perhaps thirty people in the lounge, distributed among the padded bench seats in groupings that suggested prior acquaintance or a reluctance to appear lonely. A family with two small children who were asleep across their parents' laps. Several men in work clothes who had the sealed look of people making a trip they had made many times before and had long since stopped experiencing as travel. A younger woman reading from a tablet, her face lit faintly blue in the fluorescent overhead light.

Goran bought a coffee from the machine near the forward bulkhead and took it to a seat by the window, where the glass showed him his own reflection poorly superimposed over the darkness outside. He was fifty-one, and he looked, he felt, like the kind of man who had made decisions—not catastrophically, not with the drama that the word implied, but in the quiet incremental way that one makes decisions every day for decades and finds that they have accumulated into a life that can no longer be mistaken for provisional. He had a job that was not unpleasant. He had a flat that was adequately furnished. He had a woman he saw on weekends who did not ask him about the parts of himself he kept partitioned away, and he was grateful for this in a manner that he recognised, occasionally, as a form of cowardice.

The last time he had spoken to Luka was at their mother's funeral. That was six years ago. Before that, there had been a phone call during which Luka had asked him for money and Goran had said he would think about it, which was the thing he said when he meant no but could not yet say it plainly. Luka would have known what it meant. Luka had always been able to read him with a facility that Goran found, depending on his mood, touching or unbearable. After the funeral they had stood outside the crematorium in early March cold, the sky the same whitish grey as the gravel at their feet, and Luka had smoked a cigarette without offering one to Goran even though Goran had quit ten years before and Luka would have known this. The cigarette was something to do with his hands. He was thinner than Goran remembered and there was something skewed in his face, not dramatically but sufficiently that Goran thought: he is not well and has been not well for some time. They had talked about the arrangements, whether the ashes were to be collected or scattered, whether there was anything in their mother's flat that either of them needed. There was not much. She had lived frugally and her things were the things of someone who had expected to need them for longer than she had. When it was over—the conversation, and then the cigarette, and then a silence that was not comfortable but was at least honest—Luka had walked to a car that Goran did not recognise and driven away without the car and without another word, and that had been that.

The shipping company's contact had been a harbourmaster's office on the island. The woman there had been businesslike and kind in the way of people who have occasion to make calls of this nature with some regularity. She had explained that a body had been found on the rocks below the northern headland four days ago. There was no identification. The physical description she read to him was brief and not especially helpful, since it was the description of a white male in his middle forties, approximately five feet ten, brown hair going grey, and this was a description that might have covered any number of men. What had led them to Goran was a telephone number. The phone had been found in the rocks nearby, badly damaged by water, but the technician had recovered enough to produce a partial list of contacts, and Goran's number had been among them. There had been one other contact retrieved intact, a woman whose last name was the same as the man they had found and who had told them, when they called, that she did not know the man and that the last name was a coincidence.

Goran had asked the woman from the harbourmaster's office whether they believed it was his brother. She had said she couldn't tell him that. She had said that someone needed to come.

The coffee from the machine was thin and over-sweet and he drank it anyway because it was warm and because having something in his hands was useful. Around him the lounge settled into its late-night torpor. The children had been carried off to some berth or other. The men in work clothes had mostly fallen asleep with the practised ease of men who sleep wherever the job requires. The young woman with the tablet had closed it and was now looking at the window in the same way Goran was, which is to say not at anything specific but at the general fact of the darkness outside and the distance being consumed.

He tried to think about Luka in an orderly way, as though by arranging what he knew he might arrive at a probability. Luka had been living, as far as Goran knew, in a city some distance from the island in question. He had no reason to be on the island that Goran was aware of. He had, over the years, acquired reasons to be in various places that no one who knew him would have predicted, so this was not disqualifying. Luka had been, in the years of their closeness, a man who operated by impulse, who mistook impulse for instinct and instinct for wisdom, and who had sometimes been correct in this and sometimes spectacularly wrong in a way that he managed to survive and to find, retrospectively, instructive. Whether that impulse had carried him to a northern island and from there to the rocks below a headland in the dark was not something Goran could assess from the information he had.

He also tried to think about what it would mean if it was Luka and what it would mean if it was not. The latter seemed at first easier, since it would mean only a wasted crossing, a misidentification, a trip back on the return ferry with nothing changed except a day lost. But he found, when he tried to settle into this possibility, that it was not as neutral as it appeared. If it was not Luka, Luka was still somewhere. Luka was still a man who had his phone number in a damaged phone found on a beach near where a stranger had died, which meant Luka and this stranger had been in some proximity, and the nature of that proximity was not something Goran could evaluate from a ferry seat in the middle of a strait at one in the morning. Luka not being dead was not the same as Luka being well.

If it was Luka, then it was Luka and there would be things to do. Goran found that he could not locate, when he searched himself for it, the thing he thought should be present at this notion. He had spent enough time in the last two days deciding what he felt and finding the results inconclusive that he had mostly stopped the inquiry. He would feel what he felt when he had something to feel it about. This seemed practical, and he thought Luka would have called it cold, and he thought Luka would not necessarily have been wrong.

What he could locate, with no difficulty at all, was the summer they were seven and nine respectively, which was the summer their father had taken them to the coast for two weeks in a house that belonged to someone their father knew. The house had a garden that ran down to a low wall, and beyond the wall the land dropped steeply to a beach that was mostly stones but that had, at one end, a spread of coarse sand. Luka had been the one who discovered the sand, who came back up to the garden with his shoes off and his feet still wet, announcing it with the proprietary excitement of a very young man who has found something he intends to share but who wants, for just a moment, to have found it first. He remembered Luka's feet on the warm stone of the garden path. He remembered his own reluctance to be hurried, and then the immediate abandonment of that reluctance when they actually got down there, the smell of the beach and the noise of it, the way the sea had looked enormous and specific at once, like something that had opinions.

He pressed the paper cup between his palms and let it cool.

The island they were heading for was one of a loose chain that stretched across the strait, used historically for fishing and now for a combination of fishing and tourism and a small administrative function that Goran could not have named. He knew this from the map he had looked at on his phone before boarding. He knew the island was approximately eleven kilometres long and that its main settlement was on the southern shore and that the northern headland where the body had been found was accessible by a road that became a track about two kilometres from the end. He had looked at all of this as a way of feeling prepared, and then he had felt slightly ashamed of the impulse, as though preparation were inappropriate given the circumstances. As though he should be arriving in a state of open unknowing rather than having studied the topography.

The door from the outer deck opened and a man came in, younger than Goran by perhaps fifteen years, in a dark waterproof jacket with the hood still up. He pulled the hood back and his face was red from the cold and his hair was disordered by it. He looked around the lounge as though expecting to find someone there that he didn't find, and then his expression flattened out into something more ordinary and he went to the coffee machine and used it and took his cup to a seat two rows from Goran. After a moment he said, in the mild and testing way that people sometimes address strangers on late crossings, something about the cold.

Goran agreed that it was cold.

The man said he'd been out on the deck for nearly an hour, which he admitted was perhaps not sensible.

Goran said there were worse things to be than not sensible.

The man looked at him with a mild curiosity, as though trying to place this remark within a philosophy. He said he was going to the island for work—he worked for a company that installed monitoring equipment on the fishing boats, acoustic sensors for the catch reporting requirement. He did the island chain every six weeks or so. He said this with the equanimity of someone who has reconciled himself to a life of regular departure and return, who has found in the repetition something that sustains rather than depletes.

Goran said that he was going to identify a body.

He had not planned to say this. He said it and heard himself say it and was not sorry he had said it, because it was true and because there was something about the late hour and the crossing and the man's equanimity that made plain speech feel like the only reasonable mode.

The man said he was sorry.

Goran said they didn't know yet whether it was who they thought it might be.

The man considered this. He said that sounded like a difficult thing to hold, not knowing.

Goran said he supposed it was. He said his brother and he had not been close for some years, and the man nodded in a way that suggested he understood this as a complication rather than a simplification, which was exactly what it was.

They sat for a while without speaking further, which was comfortable in the way that silences between strangers can be when the strangers have been briefly but honestly with each other. Outside the window there was nothing visible now—no shore lights, no other vessels, only the darkness that was the open water of the strait and the sky which was only marginally lighter than the water. The ferry moved through all of this with a steadiness that felt almost conscientious.

Goran thought about a thing Luka had said to him once, during one of the periods when they were speaking regularly and their speaking was easy, before the distance between them had become structural. They had been in their mid-thirties, both of them, and they had been sitting in a bar in the city where their mother was then living, and Luka had said, after some conversation Goran could no longer reconstruct, that the problem with the two of them was that they were too similar in exactly the wrong ways and too different in exactly the wrong ways, and that this meant there would always be a specific kind of friction between them, not destructive friction, but the kind that wears things smooth over a long time.

Goran had disagreed at the time. Now, on the ferry, in the uncomfortable seat, he thought that Luka had probably been right, and that his disagreement had been one more piece of evidence for the very point Luka was making.

He had not cried in the two days since the phone call. He did not feel the need to cry now. He felt instead a kind of attentiveness, as though his body were tracking something at the edge of perception, some frequency just at the limit of what he could receive. He was aware of the engines of the ferry through the soles of his feet. He was aware of the quality of the light in the lounge and of the breathing of the sleeping men in the work clothes and of the smell of coffee from both his own cup and the cup of the man who had come in from the cold, and he was aware that all of these things were acutely themselves in a way that the ordinary occasions of life did not usually require him to notice.

He thought: whatever there is on that island, I will deal with it. He thought this without heroism, as a plain statement about capacity and intention. He thought he would sleep a few hours if the berth allowed it and he would eat something if there was food in the morning and he would take whatever transportation was available from the ferry terminal to the harbourmaster's office and he would speak to whoever needed to be spoken to and he would see whatever he needed to see. He had brought his passport. He had brought enough money. He had done what could be done in advance.

He thought of Luka's feet on the warm stone. He thought of Luka saying, years later, that Goran always made him feel as though he had to earn something, and Goran saying that wasn't fair, and Luka saying a lot of things weren't fair. He thought of the cigarette outside the crematorium and how he had watched Luka walk to the car and how he had thought, even then, he should call out something, ask him where he was going or when they would speak, but the moment had passed too quickly into the ordinary finality of partings, and the car had gone, and that was six years ago.

The man in the waterproof jacket had fallen asleep in his seat, his head tipped back at an angle that would mean a stiff neck in the morning. Goran finished his coffee. The ferry moved on through the strait with its cargo of sleeping strangers and its steady engines and the cold sea on all sides of it, indifferent and heavy, and Goran sat awake in the yellow light and waited for whatever the island would require him to know.

Around three in the morning the cloud must have thinned, because when he next looked at the window he could see, instead of his own pale reflection, the vague and scattered lights of the first island in the chain, small and far off but definite, yellow against the black water. He watched them for a long time. They neither approached nor receded, held in some trick of angle and distance, and he watched them as a man watches a thing he is not sure he is actually seeing, a thing that might resolve into something else or might simply, if he keeps watching, declare itself plainly for what it is.
