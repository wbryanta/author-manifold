The older one was already in the field when the younger came out and he stood watching his brother from the edge of the grass where the yard gave way to the first row of trees. In the early morning the light came level through the branches and made long shadows over the dry ground and the younger brother watched these shadows and then walked down through the trees to where the other knelt in the dirt.

What is it, he said.

Come look at this.

He crouched beside him. A length of black irrigation pipe had pulled free of its coupling and the ground around it had gone to gray dust and the trees in that row stood with their leaves slightly furled in the heat. The older brother pressed at the coupling with his thumb.

Whole thing's shot, the younger said.

I know it.

So what do you want to do.

What do you think I want to do. Fix it.

The younger brother stood up and looked down the row. Some of the trees were putting out small hard fruit and some were not putting out anything and the ones that were not had a look about them of exhaustion or resignation, some quality that was not quite either. He put his hands in his pockets.

We're going to need fittings, he said. Glue.

I know what we're going to need.

The older brother's name was Curtis and the younger was Robert and they had not spoken since the night before when the argument had finally gone quiet not because anything was resolved but because their mother had come to the kitchen door in her nightgown and stood there looking at them until they stopped. Now the sun was already coming hard off the caliche and the day had that quality of indifference particular to June in that country.

They drove into town in Curtis's truck. Robert sat with his elbow out the window and watched the passing scrub and the cedars dark in their drainages and the limestone cuts along the roadside where the county had come through years ago and left the rock face bare and pale. Curtis drove with both hands on the wheel.

You talk to Hennessey yet, Robert said. It wasn't quite a question.

No.

He's not going to wait forever.

Curtis didn't answer.

I'm just saying.

I heard you.

The feed store was cool inside and smelled of grain and the mineral smell of metal goods and the proprietor nodded at them from behind the counter and they walked back to the plumbing supplies. Curtis picked up fittings and turned them in his hands, reading the sizes. He set several down and picked them up again.

What size is the mainline, Robert said.

Three quarter.

Curtis found what he needed and took it to the counter and the proprietor bagged it without being asked and wrote the total on a slip. Curtis paid and they went out into the heat.

In the truck Robert said, Daddy put in that line in eighty-one.

I know when he put it in.

Be about what you'd expect then. Thirty some years.

Forty, Curtis said.

They drove back. The property came into view from the road as it always had, the white house behind its stand of pecans, the barn paint-faded to a color that was not quite red and not quite gray, and beyond the barn the orchard running up the gentle slope to where the cedar fence marked the north boundary. Robert had always thought the orchard beautiful in its order, in the deliberate geometry the old man had imposed upon that country. He wondered now if that beauty was real or if it was only what he had been told to see.

They parked and got out and walked back into the trees with their supplies. The ground was hard. Somewhere in the middle rows a mockingbird was working through its whole catalogue and then working through it again. Curtis knelt and cut the failed section of line with a knife and Robert handed him the primer and the glue and they fitted the new coupling and Curtis held it while it set.

That ought to do it, Robert said.

They'd need to check the whole line.

I know it. I said that ought to do it for now.

Curtis sat back on his heels. He was forty-three years old and had the look of a man who had been outside for most of those years, not bronzed or hardened in any dramatic way but simply worn to a kind of evenness, the way limestone weathers. He turned on the water at the head valve and they walked the line watching for wet spots in the dust.

Here, Robert said.

They stopped. A slow seep was darkening the ground at another coupling fifty feet down.

Curtis crouched and looked at it. Same deal, he said.

You got extra fittings?

Yeah.

They fixed that one and found two more before they reached the end of the row and by then the sun was directly overhead and both men were sweating through their shirts. They came out of the trees at the south fence and stood in the thin strip of shade the fence post made, which was no shade at all.

You want to eat something, Curtis said.

In a while.

Robert looked back at the orchard. Against the sky the fruit trees made a fine dark tracery and somewhere in that tracery the mockingbird was still at it.

Forty acres, Robert said.

Curtis didn't say anything.

Hennessey's offer is real money. You know it is.

It's real money, Curtis agreed.

Mama could live somewhere that doesn't need everything fixed all the time.

Curtis looked at him. She's not going to leave here.

I'm not talking about making her leave. I'm talking about giving her the choice.

She's had the choice. She doesn't want it.

Robert took off his hat and ran his hand through his hair and put the hat back. A red-tailed hawk was working thermals over the far ridge, tilting in long slow arcs against the white sky.

You can't run this place, Robert said. Not by yourself and she can't help you and I've got my own situation.

Curtis said nothing to this because it was true and they both knew it was true and there was no advantage in saying so out loud.

I'm not trying to take something from you, Robert said. I'm not trying to take it from her. I'm trying to say the thing is over and we ought to recognize that it's over.

He didn't expect his brother to answer right away and Curtis didn't. They stood there in the heat. Down at the barn the tin roof was throwing light like a signal mirror and across the yard the house sat quiet with its windows dark against the glare. Their mother was in there somewhere doing whatever she did in the mornings now, which was not a great deal. Robert thought about her at the window looking out at the trees the way she always had, in all weathers, in all the years of his life that he could remember, the trees budding in March and coming full in summer and the fruit hanging heavy in August and then the cold stripping them back until they stood spare and dark against the November sky and she would watch them through all of it as if they were telling her something she hadn't quite learned to read.

What would you do, Curtis said.

What do you mean.

After. What would you do.

Get out of debt for one thing. Robert paused. I don't know. Something that had a chance.

Curtis nodded slowly. He wasn't agreeing with anything. It was the nod of a man turning something over, examining it for what it was worth.

You really think it doesn't have a chance.

I think you know the answer to that.

I've been keeping it going.

I know you have. Robert looked at him. You've been keeping it going out of what you've got left and pretty soon you won't have anything left to keep it going with and then what.

Curtis pushed at the dirt with the toe of his boot. The dirt was very dry and it powdered away from his boot and settled.

He built this place from nothing, Curtis said.

I know it.

Got here with a mule and a plow and forty dollars in his pocket is what Mama says.

Robert nodded. And built something real. Something that worked. I know all of that.

So.

So things end, Robert said. That's the whole thing right there. Things end and it's not anybody's fault. It's not your fault it stopped raining right and it's not your fault the water table dropped and the market went the way it went. You can't make it not have happened by staying.

Curtis was quiet a long time. The hawk had moved off toward the east, still working those long tilted circles, smaller now against the distance. A wood dove called somewhere in the cedars beyond the fence.

Let me ask you something, Curtis said.

All right.

You remember that summer we put in the east section? What were you, eleven?

Twelve, I think.

Twelve. You remember how hot it was that summer.

Robert remembered it. They had worked alongside their father and there had been a hired man, a large silent man from somewhere in deep east Texas whose name Robert couldn't bring up now, and they had trenched the ground by hand and laid the pipe and covered it again and their father had surveyed the finished rows in the evening with his hands on his hips and the sun going down behind the ridge and something in the way he'd stood there had made Robert feel that he was witnessing something important, something that he was too young to understand but that he would understand eventually if he paid attention.

I remember it, he said.

You remember what he said when we got done.

Robert thought. He couldn't quite get at it.

He said the land would outlast all of us. Said it was the only true thing. Curtis looked off toward the orchard. Said we were just the people that had it for a while.

Robert considered this. That may be, he said. That may be exactly right. But having it for a while means there comes a time when somebody else has it. That's what having it for a while means.

Curtis didn't say anything.

Hennessey's going to put houses on it, Robert said. I know that's hard. I know what it looks like to you. But those trees are going to die anyway, Curtis. The water situation doesn't get better and they're going to die and then you've got forty acres of dead trees and you still can't pay the note.

I could drill deeper.

With what.

Curtis didn't answer.

The mockingbird had finally gone quiet and the air was very still. From somewhere far off came the sound of a truck on the highway, rising and then fading until there was nothing but the heat and the stillness and the small sound the new coupling made as the water moved through it into the dry ground.

They went back to the house for lunch and their mother had made sandwiches and she set them on the table and poured iced tea and they sat down. She was a small woman of seventy-one who moved carefully now but without hesitation, as if she had mapped out in advance every step she would take and needed only to execute the plan. She sat with them but didn't eat.

Robert told her they'd fixed several spots in the south line.

She nodded. Your father was always fixing that line, she said. Every summer.

You knew about the new breaks? Curtis said.

I saw them from the porch.

Why didn't you say something.

She looked at him with an expression of mild patience. I figured you'd find them, she said.

They ate. The kitchen was cool and smelled of the morning's coffee and something else, something older, the smell of the house itself, which was the smell of all the years they had spent in it.

Robert looked at his mother. Mama, he said.

She looked back at him.

Curtis and I've been talking, he said.

Curtis kept his eyes on his plate.

She looked at Robert and then she looked at Curtis and then she looked at Robert again and whatever she saw in their faces or in the arrangement of their silences told her what she needed to know because she nodded once in that careful way she had and stood up and took her glass to the sink.

You talked to the Hennessey man, she said. Her back was to them.

I talked to his people, Robert said.

She ran water in the glass and set it on the rack. She stood at the window. The orchard was visible from there and she looked at it in the way Robert had always known her to look at it.

Your daddy would understand, she said. She said it quietly and without any performance behind it.

Curtis looked up.

He would, she said. He was not a man that thought things lasted. She was still looking out the window. He was a man that thought you did what you could while you could and when you couldn't you didn't pretend otherwise. She paused. That was the thing about him.

Curtis pushed back from the table and went out the back door and they heard his boots on the steps and then on the dry ground going away toward the barn. Robert sat with his hands around his tea glass.

His mother turned from the window. He's not angry at you, she said.

I know.

He's just got to do something with it for a while.

I know it.

She came back to the table and sat down and looked at her hands which were the hands of someone who had worked outside for fifty years.

You'll make sure it goes to somebody that'll take care of it, she said. I don't mean the trees. I mean the land itself.

Robert said he would try.

It's not a question of trying, she said, not harshly.

No ma'am.

She nodded. She got up again and began to clear the plates. Robert sat there and through the window he watched his brother come out of the barn and stand in the yard looking up at the sky and then walk into the orchard and disappear among the trees, the old trees that their father had planted in the years when Robert was not yet born and Curtis was not yet born and the land itself had been nothing but cedar and rock and possibility, before any of them had arrived to put their temporary order upon it and before that order would pass away entirely leaving only what had been here at the beginning and would be here at the end, which was the land turning in its long turning and the sun coming and going and the water finding its own way down through the limestone to wherever water went when it was done with you.

Robert got up and put on his hat and went out to find his brother.

The trees closed around him as he walked in. He could not see Curtis but he could hear him somewhere ahead, the sound of boots on hard ground moving through the rows. The fruit that was there was small and hard and pale, the color of things that have not had enough of what they need. He walked on. The light came through the leaves in moving pieces and fell on the dry ground and he walked through that light and after a while he came to where Curtis stood at the far end of a row with his hand resting on the trunk of one of the older trees.

Robert stopped a few feet away. The tree was big enough that Curtis's hand looked small against it. Its bark was gray and deeply furrowed and in the furrows there was a darker gray that was almost black and the whole surface of it had the look of something that had been through weather, all the weather there was, and had come out the other side into a kind of permanence that was its own.

I know, Curtis said. He didn't look at his brother. I know you're right.

Robert didn't say anything. He stood there while a small wind came through and moved the leaves and then went away again.

Curtis took his hand off the tree. He looked at the row ahead of him and then at the row behind and then he looked at Robert.

I just needed to be out here for a minute, he said.

Robert nodded. Take what time you need.

They stood in the heat among the trees their father had planted and in a while they turned together and walked back toward the house.
