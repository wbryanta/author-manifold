The morning glories had come back. Ruthanne noticed them first thing, climbing the porch rail the way they always did in August, blue as a bruise and entirely indifferent to the FOR SALE sign nailed to the post just three feet away. Her mother had planted them from seed every spring, kneeling in the dirt with a butter knife to score each hull, whispering something Ruthanne had never been close enough to hear. Now here they were doing what seeds do, which is survive the planter.

She unlocked the front door at eight-fifteen. The estate sale people—a husband and wife from Zanesville who drove a white van with TRANSITIONS ESTATE SERVICES printed on the side in a font meant to suggest gentleness—had been there since seven, arranging and pricing and talking to each other in the low voices people use when they are being paid to handle grief. Ruthanne had let them in and then driven to the Sunoco for coffee, because she could not watch that part. The van said *transitions* and she understood the word was doing a great deal of labor it had not been hired for.

Inside, the air smelled like her mother and like strangers, which meant it smelled like dust and like unfamiliar soap, and the combination made something move sideways in her chest. The woman from Zanesville—Patrice, she had said, *call me Patrice*—came toward her with the practiced sympathy of someone who had walked through a thousand houses like this one, which meant she knew what she was doing and Ruthanne would not be offering her comfort in return.

"We've got everything tagged," Patrice said. "The hutch in the dining room is going to go fast. Just so you know."

Ruthanne said thank you. She went to stand in the kitchen doorway.

The hutch had been her grandmother's. Dark walnut, glass-fronted, with the small brass fittings that had needed polishing twice a year her whole childhood, polishing being one of those tasks that moved like weather through the house in certain seasons, requiring small rags and the white paste in the blue tin and the specific silence that her mother kept when she was working. The price tag on the hutch said eighty-five dollars. Ruthanne stared at it long enough that the numbers lost their meaning, became simply marks, the way any word does if you hold it in your mind past its natural life.

People started arriving at nine.

There was a system to it—Ruthanne had not expected a system, had imagined something more like scavenging, something shameful and exposed—but the estate sale people ran it like a modest commerce. Numbers given at the door. A line that formed in the driveway by eight-forty-five, which Ruthanne saw through the kitchen window and turned away from. She had not known there would be a line. She had not known there was this much wanting in a town of eleven thousand people, this many folks ready to stand in the August heat for the chance to own a dead woman's things.

She had driven up from Columbus the night before. Six weeks since the funeral, ten days since the lawyer's business was finished, and the house itself still needed to be emptied before the new owners—a young couple, the realtor had said *young couple* with such determination that Ruthanne understood something was being withheld about them, some ordinariness that the realtor feared she might resent—took possession on the thirty-first. So here she was. Fifty-four years old, standing in her mother's kitchen with a paper cup of gas station coffee, watching Patrice's husband Gary move a chair she had sat in every morning of her first eighteen years out through the back hallway toward the front of the house.

It was a ladder-back chair. Cane seat. Her mother had reseated it herself three times over the years, ordering the cane in a flat coil from a catalog, soaking it in the bathroom tub overnight so it would be supple enough to weave. Ruthanne had sat on the bathroom floor and watched her do this once, watched her thread the soaking strands through the holes in the frame with a patience that was different from the patience of someone who is simply willing to wait. It was the patience of someone who understood that the thing itself required time and that requiring time was not the same as being difficult.

The tag on the chair said twelve dollars.

A woman in a yellow blouse picked it up twenty minutes into the sale and turned it over, examined the seat, said to her companion, "Needs work." And set it back down. And left. And came back twenty minutes after that and bought it. Ruthanne watched this from the dining room archway, holding her coffee cup, which was empty now but still warm in her hands, or she was imagining warmth. The woman in the yellow blouse carried the chair out the front door tucked under one arm, the legs swinging. She had a silver braid down her back and moved like someone who had refinished furniture before. She would probably do a good job with it, Ruthanne thought. And then thought: that is the thing you tell yourself. That is the mercy story you build to live inside of.

Her brother Marcus had called that morning from Phoenix. He had said he was sorry he couldn't be there. He had said this about the funeral too and about the two weeks at the end when their mother was in the hospital and Ruthanne had slept on a cot in the hallway outside the ICU and drunk coffee from a machine that dispensed it into a cup the color of old teeth. Marcus had his reasons, as he had always had his reasons, the reasons being a life he had assembled three states away with some difficulty and could not afford to disassemble even briefly. Ruthanne did not judge this. She had done the same thing differently, which meant she had stayed close enough to be the one who showed up, and she had known when she made that choice that it was a choice about everything else too, the cot in the hallway, the estate sale, the morning glories. She had known and she had stayed anyway and she was not sentimental about this fact, or she was trying not to be, which amounted to the same work if not the same feeling.

The hutch sold at nine-forty-seven. She heard Patrice say "Sold" in the dining room in the low good voice and she did not go look at who had bought it. The buyers were a couple, she could tell that much from the voices—a low male murmur, a higher agreement—and they were working out how to carry it, the man saying he had rope in the truck, the woman saying be careful of the glass. They would be careful of the glass. The glass had not broken in sixty years. Ruthanne moved to the back porch and stood there until she heard the front door open and close twice.

When she came back in, the shape of the dining room had changed. That was the only way to think about it—not that something was missing but that the room itself had become a different room, the way a sentence means differently without one of its words. The wallpaper her mother had put up in 1987, cream with a thin stripe of dusty rose, showed a rectangle where the hutch had stood, the paper slightly more cream there, protected from the years. The room had a memory the room was now showing her.

There were, she estimated, thirty or forty people moving through the house at any given time. They came in waves, some lingering, some surgical in their efficiency, pulling open drawers and reading price tags and moving on. A man in his seventies spent a long time with a box of old tools in the garage, picking each one up and setting it down. A teenage girl followed her mother through every room, touching nothing, looking at everything with an expression Ruthanne recognized from the inside—that specific hunger of a young person in a house that has history, not knowing whose history it is, wanting it anyway.

An old woman—older than Ruthanne's mother would have been, eighty at least, a church hat in August, sensible shoes—came in and walked slowly through the front room and stopped at the bookshelf. Not the bookshelf itself, which had already been sold, but a small framed photograph that Patrice had propped against the wall below where the shelf had been, price tag five dollars, which seemed to Ruthanne either too little or too much for a photograph but she had not been in charge of the pricing, had specifically asked not to be.

The photograph was her parents' wedding picture. 1963. Her mother twenty-two, her father twenty-six, both of them dressed in their good clothes because they had married on a Wednesday at the county clerk's office and there had been no photographer. The picture was a portrait her grandmother had paid for afterward at the Woolworth's, which meant her mother was in her going-away suit and her father in his work jacket with a tie borrowed from his brother and they were both smiling the way people smile when they have just done a serious thing and are still feeling the weight of it, which is a particular kind of smile, more interior than exterior, barely touching the surface.

The old woman in the church hat stood looking at this photograph for a long time. Long enough that Ruthanne wanted to ask her something, or felt that she should, felt a pull toward speech that she did not follow. Then the woman reached into her pocketbook and took out her wallet and counted out five dollars in ones and left it on the little table Patrice had set up by the door for transactions.

She picked up the photograph.

She looked at it one more time.

She said, not to anyone in particular, to the air of the front room or to the photograph itself: "There you are."

And walked out.

Ruthanne stood very still. The phrase moved through her and she let it, she had learned that much at least in fifty-four years, that some things need to be let rather than stopped. *There you are.* As if her parents had been somewhere that woman knew about and had only just been found. As if the looking for them was a thing that had been going on. She did not know who that woman was. It was possible she had known her parents. It was possible she had been wandering through the rooms of her memory for years, carrying some image of two young people she had known once, and here was a photograph that said: *yes, them, that's them, they were real.*

Or she had said it to herself. People do that, come to themselves after a long looking. *There you are.*

She did not know. She went into the kitchen and ran the tap and stood with her hands under the water for no reason she could have named.

By noon the front room was mostly clear. The plaid sofa—forty dollars, she had seen the tag—was gone. The two armchairs. The coffee table her mother had bought at a garage sale in 1994 and refinished herself, painted antique white and then distressed with a key in that deliberate way her mother had of making things look naturally worn, as if age itself were an aesthetic she was achieving rather than enduring. Gone. The television cabinet. The floor lamp with the amber shade.

What remained was the arrangement of absences. The shapes of things in the carpet. The brighter squares and oblongs on the walls. The house was becoming a record of itself, which was not the same as itself, and this seemed to Ruthanne important in a way she was not yet equipped to think about directly.

She ate a sandwich she had brought from home—from Columbus, from the apartment that was hers, that she had made hers carefully and over many years, the way you make a thing yours when you have been a long time getting there. Turkey and mustard. She sat on the back porch steps where the afternoon shade had started to come in off the big maple her father had planted the year she was born, which was not going anywhere, which was not for sale, and ate the sandwich and listened to the sound of the sale happening inside the house and thought about her mother's hands.

Her mother's hands had been small and brown and capable of almost everything. Sewing, canning, engine work when the Buick needed it and there was no money for the mechanic, the cane-seating of chairs, the raising of children, the nursing of the man those same hands had held for forty-one years until the particular morning in October eleven years ago when they let go of him, as hands eventually must, and then had only each other. For eleven years her mother's hands had been folded in her lap or opened in prayer or gesturing when she talked or moving through her garden, and the garden had grown considerable in those years, not because of grief exactly, Ruthanne had thought, but because her mother was a person who had always had somewhere to put her energy and when one container became unavailable she simply found another.

She had not been a soft woman. She had been a warm one, which is different. The warmth was never without expectation, which some people mistook for hardness but was actually just the warmth of someone who believed in you specifically rather than in people generally. Ruthanne had not always wanted to be believed in specifically. Specifically meant there was a standard and a measuring. It meant your failures were legible. But she was fifty-four now and she knew the difference between being seen and being loved and she knew her mother had managed both, which is not common.

She finished the sandwich and went back in.

At two-fifteen a man bought the kitchen table.

He was perhaps forty, broad-shouldered, wearing a t-shirt with the name of a concrete company on it, and he had his daughter with him, a girl of maybe eight who had been quiet and well-behaved the whole time they moved through the house. The table was oval, maple, the same table Ruthanne had done her homework on, had sat at for every meal until she left for Ohio State at eighteen, had come home to sit at for holidays and ordinary visits for the thirty-six years after that. Her mother had been sitting at that table when Ruthanne called to tell her she had gotten the job at the library. Had been sitting at it when Marcus called to say he was not coming home for Christmas the first time, or the second, or after that. Had been sitting at it on the morning—Ruthanne knew this because she had been there, had driven up the night before on one of her ordinary visits—the morning the doctor called to tell her that what they had found was what they had feared. Her mother had sat at that table and held the phone and looked at nothing specific in the middle distance and said *all right* and then said *I understand* and then said *thank you* and hung up, and poured two cups of coffee, and handed one to Ruthanne across the table.

That table.

The man from the concrete company paid fifteen dollars and then stood looking at it with the particular expression of someone who has just bought something heavier than he expected, not in weight but in dimension. He looked at Ruthanne—she was standing in the doorway, she was always in doorways today, threshold being the only available position—and said, "Was this your mother's house?"

"Yes," she said.

He nodded. He looked at the table again. "My wife's been looking for something like this," he said. "For the kitchen. We've got kids." He glanced at the girl, who was examining a row of her mother's spice jars that hadn't sold yet, reading the labels with great concentration, moving her lips. "She wants something they can all sit around."

Ruthanne thought about this. She thought about it for longer than the pause in the conversation warranted, long enough that the man shifted his weight. "There's good years left in it," she finally said. Which was not what she had been thinking but was also true.

He nodded, not knowing he had been given something. He and his daughter carried the table out through the kitchen door, tilting it through the frame carefully, the girl holding the rear legs with the focused competence of a child who has helped move things before and knows her job. The table cleared the door. Ruthanne did not watch where it went after that.

The house emptied further through the afternoon. Books by the box. Her mother's sewing basket—the oval one, brown wicker, the lid worn smooth on the handle—which went to a woman with reading glasses on a beaded chain. Three boxes of Christmas ornaments, each one wrapped in tissue her mother had smoothed and folded back around it every January, went to a family who were visibly excited about this, the children lifting each tissue parcel with a reverence that meant they were the kind of family that still believed in ornaments, and Ruthanne stood in the hallway watching them and felt something she was reluctant to name, something that lived between loss and ordinary gladness, in that contested territory.

By four o'clock the house was not empty but it was changed in a way that emptiness is not the right word for. There were still things in it—some books, the kitchen things that hadn't moved, the curtain rods, the curtains themselves which Patrice had said were hard to sell—but the life had been redistributed. That was what had happened. Her mother's life had been redistributed out into the county, into kitchens and dining rooms and garages, into the homes of people who would use these things and not know what they were using, which is the way it goes, the way it has to go, the way the dead release into the living as water releases into ground.

Patrice came to find her at four-thirty to go over the accounting. She was kind about it, professional, moved through the numbers with care. The total from the day's sale was nine hundred and forty dollars. Ruthanne received this information and thanked her and they shook hands and Patrice and Gary loaded their display tables into the white van, leaving the door propped so the last few stragglers could finish up.

At five, Ruthanne locked the front door behind the last person, a man who had bought a box of extension cords for three dollars and seemed very pleased about this, the way practical men are pleased by practical things, and she stood in the front room alone.

The morning glories were still there, she could see them through the sidelight windows, closing now in the late afternoon the way morning glories do, drawing back into themselves for the night.

She had not cried all day. This was not fortitude. It was more that grief has its seasons and this had been a working season, a doing season, and the feeling was not absent but simply occupied elsewhere, the way a river doesn't stop being water because it is being used.

She walked through the rooms. She did this slowly, room to room, not searching for anything, not taking inventory. The house still smelled like her mother beneath the stranger-soap smell, beneath the dust disturbed by all that movement. It would go on smelling like her for a while. Then it would smell like paint, and then like the young couple, and the smell of her mother would be nowhere in the material world, would exist only in what Ruthanne carried, which would have to be enough because it would be all there was.

She stood in the kitchen doorway a last time. The white rectangle on the linoleum where the table had been. The window above the sink where her mother had stood washing dishes and watching the yard, watching the maple grow year by year with the patience of someone who had understood from the beginning that a tree is a long project and she had signed on for it knowing that.

She turned off the kitchen light.

She went out the back door and stood on the porch steps under the maple's broad shade and put her face in her hands, not to hide but simply to be in the dark of herself for a moment, where the table was still there and the hands were still small and brown and capable and the morning glories were climbing and the coffee was warm and everything was as it had been, briefly, briefly, which is all anything ever asks.
