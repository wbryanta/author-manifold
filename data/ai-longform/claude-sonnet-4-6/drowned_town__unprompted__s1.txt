The gauge at the spillway read four feet and falling when Maren drove the county truck down the access road, the trailer swaying behind her with the aluminum boat nested inside it like a tooth in a jaw. She had requested the assignment herself, filling out the paperwork twice because the first form had been lost, and when her supervisor Dale had looked at her over his reading glasses and said *you sure about this*, she had told him it was the most efficient use of her skills given the exposed terrain, which was true, and she had not told him the rest of it, which was also true. She had not been back since she was seven years old. That was thirty-one years. She was aware of both facts the way you are aware of a filling in a tooth—constantly, and without drama.

The reservoir had been dropping since March. Five years of below-average snowpack had drawn it down incrementally, and then the summer came on like something with intentions, and by September the water had retreated far enough that the county assessor's office had begun receiving calls. Old property owners. Heirs of old property owners. People who believed that the emergence of their grandmother's house from the water constituted a kind of resurrection with legal implications. Maren had listened to Dale explain this on speakerphone while she ate a sandwich at her desk. She had looked out the window at the parking lot and the beige mountains beyond it and she had thought about the street she had grown up on, which was called Sycamore, and whether the sycamores themselves had been taken down before the flooding or whether their root systems were still down there in the silt, patient and enormous and dark.

She backed the trailer down the boat launch, which was cracked concrete going to gravel going to the pale dried mud that the retreating waterline had left behind—a lunar surface, dimpled and fissured, smelling of minerals and old cold. The lake was still there, blue-gray in the early morning, occupying the deepest part of the original valley, but it had pulled back from what had once been its shores by nearly a quarter mile in places. What remained between the water's edge and the high-water mark was not quite land and not quite lake bed. It was something without a good name. She had been trying, on the drive up, to think of what you called the skin of a thing that had been turned inside out.

She unloaded the boat and left the truck and trailer in the parking area and walked down to the edge of what she was already, in her head, calling *the floor*. The silt was dry on the surface but gave slightly underfoot, and her boots left clean impressions that filled almost immediately with a thin seep of moisture, as if the ground were crying quietly behind her. She had her survey equipment in the pack on her back—the total station, the prism poles, the field notebook with its waterproof pages—and she carried the rods under one arm. The morning light came in low from the east and turned everything amber, and in that amber light she could see, maybe two hundred meters ahead of her, the shapes.

They emerged from the flat floor the way things emerge in dreams, which is to say without drama, without the theatrical timing you would expect, simply present in your vision when you attend to them—the low remains of walls, the regular geometry of foundations, the occasional upright that might have been a fence post or a door jamb, weathered to a gray so pale it was almost white. She stopped walking. She was aware that she had stopped but she could not immediately account for the decision. A great blue heron lifted from somewhere near the water's edge and crossed the sky above the ruins in a long unhurried arc, its neck folded back, its legs trailing, and she watched it until it was gone.

She started walking again.

The first structure she reached was not a house. It was a concrete pad with a rusted metal frame rising from it, and after a moment she recognized it as the foundation of the gas station that had stood at the edge of town, the one her mother had called the Shell station even though by the time Maren was old enough to remember it the signage had changed. She set down her rods and stood in the middle of the concrete and tried to locate herself. North was the direction she had come from. South was the water. East and west were the ranges, unchanged and enormous, indifferent witnesses. She oriented her compass, opened the field notebook, and began to write. *Survey Reference Point Alpha. Concrete slab, approximately 12m x 8m. Metal superstructure, corroded, height approximately 2.4m.* She wrote quickly, in the neat compressed hand she had developed over fifteen years of fieldwork, and when she had finished writing she stood still for a moment with the notebook open and did not write anything else.

Her mother had filled the car here, a green Subaru that Maren could picture with a precision that surprised her—the particular shade of green, the crack in the driver's-side mirror, the way the passenger window would only roll down three-quarters of the way and then stopped with a sound like a question. She could picture this car more clearly than she could picture her mother's face in those years, which was something she had noticed before and which she understood as one of the ways that grief and memory do not behave the way we think they will when we imagine losing someone. She was not grieving now. Her mother had died eleven years ago, from something unrelated to the town or the water, in a hospital in Reno, and Maren had been present and had held her hand and that part of the story was its own complete thing, separate. What she was doing now was something different. She was doing her job.

She walked south, into the grid.

The streets were not visible as streets, exactly. The asphalt had long ago been colonized by silt and then buried and then partially re-exposed, and what remained were faint linear depressions and occasional curb edges that caught the light at certain angles. But she had studied the original plat maps before driving up—had spent two evenings at the county records office with the rolled papers spread across the table, tracing the layout with her finger—and the geometry of the place was in her now, installed. She knew that she was walking east on what had been Oak Street. She knew that if she continued east for approximately three hundred meters she would reach the intersection with Manzanita. She knew that if she turned south on Manzanita and walked another hundred and fifty meters she would be standing in front of the house where she had been born.

She did not go there yet. She worked the eastern end of town first, methodically, the way she had been trained to work—establishing control points, taking measurements, building the framework into which the details would later be fitted. This was the discipline she valued most about her profession: you could not let your eye run ahead of your instrument. You could not trust your sense of where you were. The landscape would tell you things that were not true if you let it, would flatten distances and shift angles, would conspire with your own desires to put you somewhere you were not. The total station did not care where you wanted to be. It told you what was there.

The eastern section of town had been, she recalled from the plat maps and from whatever vague sedimented memory she carried, the commercial district. The hardware store. The pharmacy. The small grocery that had a barrel of peanuts by the door that you could scoop yourself. She was working near what she believed to be the footprint of the pharmacy when her boot struck something solid and she looked down. It was a glass bottle, intact, dark green, half-buried in silt with the neck angled upward as if someone had placed it there. She crouched and worked it free with both hands. There was no label. There was nothing inside except a residue of silt and what might have been, in another century, the ghost of whatever the bottle had held. She turned it in her hands for a moment. It was unremarkable and she had no way of knowing whose it was or where it had come from, and yet the fact of its intactness moved her in a way she could not immediately explain—the stubborn persistence of its form, the sixty years of cold pressure that had not broken it. She set it upright in the silt and went back to work.

By mid-morning she had established the eastern grid, and she walked to the boat, drank water, ate a granola bar, and looked out at the remaining lake. The sun was higher now and the water had lost its blue-gray cast and was simply bright. A pair of mergansers dove and surfaced near the center. She thought about what was under the water still—the deeper streets, the lower neighborhoods, the park with its bandstand where her father had taken her once on a summer evening to hear a band she couldn't remember, though she remembered the folding chairs and the moths around the lights and her father's hand warm and solid around her own small hand. That park was still underwater. The bandstand was still down there. She thought about the mergansers diving through the old air of it, their feet trailing, seeing nothing.

She had never blamed anyone for the flooding. This was something people sometimes assumed—that the residents of drowned towns carried their grievance like a stone—but she had questioned her mother about it once, when she was old enough to ask, and her mother had said only that there had been some money and they had been given time to move, which was more than a lot of people got for a lot of things. Her mother had been a practical woman with a talent for distinguishing between losses that could be argued with and losses that could not. The dam was a fact. The water was a fact. Maren had inherited this practicality, or she believed she had, and she had gone into a profession built on facts, on measurements, on the precise and repeatable description of what was there. She was aware that this might not be a coincidence.

She repacked her bag and walked back into the grid and turned south on Manzanita.

The street was identifiable here by a curb edge that ran for nearly fifty meters before disappearing into a drift of silt, the concrete still sharp-edged, pale as bone. She walked beside it. The houses on this block—she counted the foundations—had been modest, single-story, the kind of houses built in the 1930s and 1940s for working families, with small yards and covered porches and the kind of interior she remembered from the houses of her parents' friends: the particular smell of them, the low ceilings, the linoleum kitchens. She stopped at each foundation and noted its dimensions and condition. She was thorough and she was slow. She photographed the details. She noted the occasional artifact—a fragment of pipe, a section of wire fencing, what appeared to be the porcelain base of a light fixture—with the same care she would have noted a control point or a benchmark.

At the fourth foundation from the intersection, she stopped. She knew it was the fourth. She had counted. She stood outside its perimeter, on what had been the sidewalk, and looked at it from there.

The foundation was concrete block, standard for the period, thirty-seven feet across the front if the plat map was accurate—she would measure it herself in a few minutes. The front steps were still there, three of them, rising to the level where the porch had been, and the threshold, though the door and the doorframe and the walls and everything above the foundation were gone, replaced by sixty years of water and silt and the catastrophic patience of time. A volunteer tree—cottonwood, she thought, from the leaf litter—had established itself in what had been the front room and grown to perhaps twenty feet before the water reclaimed it, and it stood now as a pale dead thing, its bleached trunk leaning slightly east, its bare branches making a filigreed pattern against the morning sky. She looked at it for a long time.

She had been seven. The town had been emptied over the course of two years, and they had been among the last families to go, her father having argued with the county about the assessment until he couldn't anymore. She remembered the last time she had been in this house not because of the house itself but because of the drive away from it. She remembered looking back through the rear window of the green Subaru as her mother drove them up the access road—there had been no boat launch yet, it was just a gravel track—and she remembered that the house had looked exactly as it always looked, which was the thing that had troubled her most. She had expected it to look different. She had expected it to look like a thing being abandoned. Instead it had looked like a house that did not know what was about to happen to it, and this had seemed to her, even at seven, to be a kind of tragedy that went beyond the ordinary tragedy of losing things.

She climbed the front steps. Her feet knew how to do this. The first step was broad and the third was narrower than the others—she had known this without knowing she knew it, and her foot had adjusted before her mind had registered the adjustment. She stood on the threshold. The silt inside the foundation was higher than the silt outside by perhaps eight inches, settled into the shape of the old floor, and she could see the ghost of the interior in it—the faint ridge where the hall wall had stood, the low rectangular depression where the kitchen had been lower than the living room by a single step, the location of the chimney base still marked by a square of old mortar. She stood and looked at these things. The dead cottonwood creaked faintly in a breeze she couldn't feel.

She opened her field notebook. She did not write immediately. She held the pen over the waterproof page and looked at the silt floor of the house and she tried to feel whatever it was that she had come here expecting or not expecting to feel. She found something, but it was smaller and quieter than she had anticipated. It was not grief, exactly. It was closer to the feeling of finishing a proof—a sense of arrival that was also a sense of enclosure, of something that had been open now being gently closed. The house was not a wound. It was a site. It was a coordinate on a map she was making, as it had once been a coordinate on the map of her childhood, and the two maps now overlapped in her, layered like transparencies, and she could see through one to the other and back again.

She began to measure.

She measured the exterior dimensions and the interior dimensions. She photographed each wall of the foundation from multiple angles. She noted the construction materials, the condition of the mortar, the presence of embedded hardware—a bolt here, a section of rebar there—that had outlasted everything wooden and soft. She was precise. She was professional. She was also, she allowed herself to know, saying a kind of goodbye that was not a goodbye to the house but to the version of herself who had looked back through the rear window of the green Subaru and found that the house looked exactly the same, and who had been troubled by this for thirty-one years in a way she had never quite articulated, because what had troubled her was not the sameness of the house but the question it implied: if the thing being abandoned does not know it is being abandoned, where does the loss reside? It resides in the person leaving, she thought now, standing in the roofless foundation with her notebook open. It has always resided there. That is not a tragedy. That is simply where loss lives. In the people who remember. In no one else.

She closed the notebook.

She walked through the interior of the foundation toward the back, stepping carefully, and exited through what had been the back door—she could tell by the concrete lip of the sill—into the rectangle that had been the back yard. There had been a garden here. Her mother's garden, which had grown tomatoes and zucchini and a variety of squash whose name Maren could not recall that had produced enormous orange fruit her mother had roasted and served with butter and salt. The garden was indistinguishable from the surrounding silt. She stood in it anyway. A small wind moved through the dead cottonwood behind her, and the creaking of it was the only sound in the whole exposed valley, that and the distant lapping of the remaining lake, which was audible now that she was attending to it, a soft and persistent rhythm like something breathing.

She worked her way back through the residential blocks for another two hours, finishing the western section, completing the documentation that the county required. She was thorough. She made no exceptions for her own history. The house she had been born in received the same notation as its neighbors, the same measurements, the same photographs, the same clipped professional prose. When she was done she walked back to the boat launch and loaded her equipment and stood by the truck for a moment before getting in.

The sun was past its peak and the light had changed, flatter now, the amber gone out of it. The exposed floor of the former town was gray-white in this light, and the ruins in it were gray-white, and the remaining lake was a flat pewter disk at the center of the valley. She looked at it for a long time. She thought about the water, when it came back—and it would come back, the drought would end, it always ended—rising over all of this again, covering the foundations and the silt floor and the dead cottonwood and the green bottle she had found and set upright, covering the front steps and the threshold and the ghost of the kitchen with its single step down, rising until the valley was a lake again and there was nothing to see but a blue-gray surface in which the mountains were reflected, still and perfect and giving nothing away.

She thought: the water does not know what it covers. The water does not know what it uncovers. These are human categories, human wounds, human gifts. The water is just water. It moves toward the lowest point. It always has. It always will. She had spent her professional life measuring the distance between where things were and where things were recorded to be, and perhaps that was the only kind of return available to anyone—not the return to the place itself, which no longer existed in the form you remembered it, but the return to the act of paying attention, of looking carefully at what was there and writing it down, of saying *I was here, and I saw this, and I measured it as best I could*.

She got in the truck. She pulled the door shut. The survey forms on the passenger seat rustled in the change of air and settled. She had everything she needed. She had coordinates and measurements and photographs and notes. She had a complete and accurate record of the exposed streets and foundations of a town that had been submerged for sixty years, which was what the county had sent her to produce, and she had produced it, and her handwriting in the field notebook was as neat and regular on the last page as it had been on the first.

She drove up the access road without looking back. The gravel popped under the tires. The trailer swayed. In the rearview mirror the valley shrank as she climbed, and the lake at its center shrank with it, until it was just a bright chip of light between the ranges, not blue and not gray, the color of something that had no name yet, the color of water in a place that is still deciding what it is.
