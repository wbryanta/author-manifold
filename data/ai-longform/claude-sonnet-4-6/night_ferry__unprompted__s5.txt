The ferry left at eleven and he had been standing at the stern rail for twenty minutes before the lights of the mainland finally let go of the water. He watched them go. There was something almost voluntary in the way the darkness moved in to replace them, a kind of consent in the low clouds and the flat black surface of the strait, as if the sea had been waiting politely for the shore to finish its business and withdraw. He pulled the collar of his jacket higher. The wind off the water had a quality he associated with serious things — not violent, not dramatic, just unignorable, the way a doctor's voice changes when he has something to tell you.

His name was Calum. He was forty-four years old and he had not spoken to his brother in eleven years.

He went inside when the cold became something more than he wanted to negotiate with. The passenger lounge was fluorescent and institutional, rows of fixed seats in a shade of orange that had been new in another decade, a snack counter at one end presided over by a teenage girl reading from her phone, her thumb moving in small repeated arcs. Three or four other passengers were distributed through the space with the particular isolation of people who have each independently decided that this crossing is not a social occasion. An older man asleep with his mouth open. A woman with a baby that was not yet crying. Two young men in high-visibility vests sharing something from a thermos, speaking occasionally in low voices with the ease of people who have run out of things to say but do not feel threatened by this.

Calum took a seat near the window, where he could see nothing but the reflection of the lounge interior and, nested inside it, his own face. He looked at it for a moment and then looked away. He took out the piece of paper that the police officer had faxed to him — actually faxed, his office still had a machine — and read it again although he had already memorized it. Male, approximate age forty to fifty, found in the water near the north breakwater. Significant decomposition. No identification found with the body. Distinguishing characteristics and a list that he had only been able to read as far as the third item before folding the paper and walking to the window of his office and standing there until a colleague knocked and asked if he was free to look at something and he had said yes, yes, of course, come in.

The third item on the list was a scar on the left forearm, significant, approximately fifteen centimetres, consistent with previous trauma. He knew the scar. He had been there when it happened. He had been sixteen and his brother Donal had been thirteen and it had been a glass door and Donal's arm going through it in that particular suspended slow-motion way of accidents happening, the way time behaves strangely at moments that will matter later. Their mother had wrapped Donal's arm in a dish towel that turned quickly red and Calum had driven them to the hospital even though he had only had his provisional licence for two months, and he had done it calmly, he remembered, with a clarity that had surprised him at the time, talking to Donal steadily about nothing — about a film they had both seen, about the engine noise of their father's car, about anything — while Donal said very little and held his arm pressed against his chest and looked out the window.

Calum bought a coffee from the girl at the counter. She handed it to him without looking up and he stood for a moment with the cup in both hands before going back to his seat. The coffee was bad in the way ferry coffee always was, with a faint chemical aftertaste that suggested it had been made in the morning and held at temperature through the evening and into the night, distilling its own fatigue. He drank it anyway.

He had not told anyone where he was going. This was not unusual — he lived alone, had lived alone for three years since Miriam had moved to Edinburgh with the kind of careful patience that suggested she had been planning it for longer than she said, and his colleagues would simply assume he was at a site inspection or a client meeting. He was an architect. He designed houses for people who could afford to have an architect design their houses. He was good at it. He had learned to want the things he was good at, and for a long time, most of the time, this had felt like enough.

He thought about the last time he had seen Donal.

It had been at their mother's funeral, which put it eleven years ago. Their father had died when Calum was twenty-six and Donal was twenty-three, and by then they had already developed the habit of tolerating each other across distances — not hostility, nothing so definable as that, but a kind of managed absence, visits kept to family occasions, phone calls kept short by unspoken agreement. After their father died there had been a period of approximately six months during which they had talked more, called each other without the excuse of occasion, met twice in the city and walked and eaten and drunk enough to approach something like the closeness they'd had as children, a memory of which persisted in Calum like a splinter that had never been fully removed. But it hadn't lasted. He couldn't say now precisely why. He had been busy with work. Donal had been in one of his difficult periods. Something had been said at Christmas — not by Calum, or not primarily by Calum — and there had been a silence afterwards that neither of them had moved to fill, and then there had been the silence that came after that silence and by then it had become its own entity, the silence had, with its own inertia and its own kind of dignity that made it harder and harder to disturb.

At their mother's funeral they had been correct with each other, which was worse than hostility. They had sat beside each other in the front pew and stood beside each other at the grave and accepted food in their mother's house afterwards and Calum had watched his brother move through the rooms that had been their childhood rooms, picking things up and setting them down, and had thought: I don't know him. Thought it with something like grief and something like relief and something that was neither, that had no name he could locate.

After the funeral, he had tried. He had written an actual letter, handwritten, because email felt inadequate and a phone call felt too exposed, and he had written two drafts and sent the second one to an address he had for Donal in Inverness. The letter had not been returned. It had also not been answered. He had waited three months and written another, shorter letter, more careful, less of itself, and that one also had gone into silence. After that he had stopped.

He had thought about Donal, sporadically, with the particular guilt of the person who has given up. Not dramatically. Not obsessively. The way you think about something you've accepted you can't do anything about — with a kind of dull periodic acknowledgement, like a warning light you've learned to ignore because ignoring it has so far had no consequences.

Until the fax. The police officer on the island — a woman, her voice professionally kind in the way that made it worse — had explained that they had very few leads, that the body had been in the water for some time, that they were pursuing all avenues, and could he confirm that he was the nearest living relative of a Donal Innes MacAulay, formerly of an address in Inverness, most recently understood to be living somewhere on the northern coast. He had said yes. She had said that they might be asking him to come and look. He had said he understood. She had faxed the sheet with the distinguishing characteristics.

The ferry moved through the dark water at a pace that Calum found himself needing to resist. There was a part of him — he recognised it without wanting to — that wanted the ferry to slow. That wanted the crossing to extend, the destination to recede. Not from cowardice, or not only from cowardice, but from something more structural: the crossing had a shape to it, the movement across water between the mainland and whatever the island held was a shape with a certain integrity, and he was not sure he wanted the shape to end. On the ferry he was still in the passage. He was still the man crossing the water. The island would make him into something else, the man who had arrived, the man who had looked, and he would have to be that for the rest of his life regardless of what he found.

He had brought a book. He did not open it.

He thought about the scar and then he thought about trying not to think about the scar and then he accepted that he was going to think about it, the way it had looked years later, healed to a pale raised line that divided the inside of Donal's forearm into two different kinds of skin, the way Donal used to run his thumb along it when he was thinking, not with any particular drama, just a habit of touch, an automatic checking that it was still there. Calum had noticed it many times. It was the kind of thing you noticed about a person you grew up with, these small self-inventories they perform without knowing you're watching.

The baby had started crying. The mother rocked it with the contained competence of someone who has learned that the situation will pass, that it always passes, and the baby's cry rose and fell in the fluorescent air of the lounge and one of the men in the high-visibility vests glanced over and then glanced away.

Calum got up and walked to the bow-facing windows at the far end of the lounge. He could see nothing except the black water and, very faintly, a suggestion of where the sky was darker than the sea. He pressed his forehead against the glass. The vibration of the engines came through it.

Here is what he knew about the last eleven years of his brother's life, which was almost nothing. He knew that Donal had, at some point, left Inverness. He knew this because the second letter had been returned after all — not immediately, but about fourteen months after he sent it, forwarded to his address by someone unknown and stamped Not At This Address, Inverness becoming a way-station rather than a destination. He knew that Donal had, at some point, worked on fishing vessels — he had heard this second or third hand, through the residue of mutual acquaintance, the way information about estranged people arrives, unreliably and too late. He had heard that Donal was not well for a period. He had heard, more recently, from a cousin at a wedding — not heard so much as inferred from the cousin's careful shift of subject — that there had been some crisis, some hospitalisation perhaps, something of that order.

He had not pursued it. He had not asked the cousin to clarify. He had moved on to the buffet.

This was the thing he was going to have to carry. He understood that clearly, standing at the dark window of the overnight ferry. Whether the body was Donal's or not, whether he got off this boat and walked into an island police station and looked at something terrible and recognised it or did not recognise it — either way, the thing he was going to have to carry was the eleven years, and the cousin at the wedding, and the second letter that came back fourteen months late, and the way he had moved on to the buffet. The body was almost beside the point. The body was just the place where the eleven years had come to collect him.

He bought another coffee. The girl at the counter had put her phone away now and was wiping the surface with a cloth, working in steady circles, and she gave him the cup with a faint smile that asked nothing of him, that was just the normal human acknowledgement of another person's presence, and he was grateful for it in a way he could not have explained and did not try to.

He sat back down and thought: it might not be Donal.

He listed the reasons it might not be Donal. The scar was not unique — scars on the forearm were not uncommon, there were various ways a person could acquire a significant scar on the left forearm and not be Donal. The age range was approximate. The northern coast had a fishing industry, there were men of Donal's approximate age on the northern coast who were not Donal. The police officer had said they were pursuing all avenues, which meant there were other avenues, other relatives being faxed, other people on overnight ferries.

This was possible. He concentrated on its possibility with the methodical thoroughness of someone designing a load-bearing wall.

Then he thought: but it might be Donal.

He had not cried yet. He was aware of this as a fact about himself, not a judgement. He had read the fax in his office and he had been cold and still in a way that was not composure but its opposite — the way water goes still before it freezes. He had driven home and stood in his kitchen for a long time. He had packed a bag, which he had done well, practically, without forgetting anything. He had driven to the terminal and bought a ticket and parked the car in the long-stay car park and walked onto the ferry with his bag. He had been functional throughout. The function was its own form of shock, he suspected. He was an architect. He knew how to hold things up.

At some point, somewhere between midnight and the island, he must have slept, because he was aware at some point of the particular unreality of the fluorescent light and the fixed orange seats taking on a quality they didn't have before, a slight dislocation, a flatness that was the quality of surfaces just after waking. The baby had stopped crying. The men in high-visibility vests were both asleep, heads lolling in different directions, the thermos on the seat between them. The woman with the baby was also asleep, the baby against her chest, both of them sealed in whatever privacy sleep provides.

He looked at his reflection in the window again.

He thought about the drive to the hospital. Donal in the passenger seat. The dish towel, the blood coming through it. He had talked about the film — what was the film? Some action film they had both seen, a film their father had taken them to see as a special occasion, a rare concession to the kind of film their father did not enjoy but they had begged for. He couldn't remember what it was. He could remember that he had described it, scene by scene, going back over the parts Donal had particularly liked, and that Donal had made occasional sounds of confirmation, small acknowledgements that he was still there, still listening. He could remember the indicator light clicking as he turned at the hospital entrance. He could remember the particular yellow of the hospital car park's sodium lamps. He could remember walking around to the passenger side and opening the door and Donal turning to get out, and the look on Donal's face when he looked up at Calum — not fear exactly, not pain exactly, something younger than either of those things, something that looked like the pure relief of being with someone who knew what to do, and Calum had not known what to do, had simply done what presented itself, helping Donal up, saying come on, come on, and walking him through the glass doors into the light.

He had been a good brother once. He knew that. It was available to him as a fact.

The ferry's note changed — he heard it in the engines, felt it in the soles of his feet — and he looked up and out and there, very far ahead still but present now, definite, were the lights of the island. Not many lights. A cluster, the small bright grammar of a small harbour town, and above it the dark mass of whatever was behind it, hills or cliffs or simply the absence of habitation.

He watched the lights as the ferry worked toward them.

He thought about what he was going to say when he got there, what words a person used. He had been trying to prepare some words, somewhere in the night, in the half-sleep, and had not found any that were adequate. He supposed that was all right. He supposed there were no adequate words and that the people he was going to meet would know that. The police officer with the kind voice. Whoever else was there.

He thought: if it is Donal, what I will do is look at him. That is what I will do. I will look at him properly, in a way I didn't, in a way I should have, on the various occasions I had. I will look at him and I will know him, the scar and the rest of him, I will know him even if he is greatly changed, even if time and water and everything else have changed him, because he is my brother and I drove him to the hospital when I was sixteen years old and I talked about nothing to keep him calm and he looked up at me from the car with that look and I was competent and present and he knew I was there.

He didn't know if that would be enough. He suspected it would not. But it was what he had.

The lights came closer. The engines deepened. He could see the breakwater now, a dark arm extended into the water, and the lights of the harbour behind it, and beyond them the town's pale scattered arrangements. He gathered his bag from under the seat. He put on his jacket. He stood and went to the window and looked out at the approaching island and felt the cold coming through the glass against his face — the same cold as before, the unignorable cold, the cold that had something to tell him.

He was ready. He was not ready. The ferry came in.
