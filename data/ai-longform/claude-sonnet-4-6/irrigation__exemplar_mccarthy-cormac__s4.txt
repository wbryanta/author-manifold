The older one was already out by the pump house when the boy came across the yard in the early light. He could hear the starlings in the apple trees and the dog rose from the porch and stretched and lay back down. Dew on the grass and the mountains to the east pink and pale above the dark treeline. His brother looked up from the coupling he held in his hands and then looked back down at it.

You eat anything, Calvin said.

I'm all right.

There's biscuits in there from yesterday.

I said I'm all right.

Calvin set the coupling down on the wooden lid of the pump housing and wiped his hands on his jeans. He was forty-one years old and looked it and he stood looking at his younger brother and at the trees beyond him in the gray morning light.

You ready to work, he said.

I'm here ain't I.

The line had gone out somewhere between the second lateral and the head of the old Hendricks block. Fourteen trees in that block and they'd been dry three days already and the fruit showing it. They loaded the truck with couplings and gaskets and lengths of aluminum pipe and drove down the access road with the dog trotting behind until Calvin stopped and told it to go on back and it stood in the road watching them drive away.

They pulled over at the junction box and Calvin cut the engine. The ditch ran along beside the road and was dry and cracked and the weeds in it gray with dust. Thomas got out and stood looking at the trees. They were old trees, set by their father and their father's father before that, and the rows ran back into the morning shadow of the ridge and the grass between them was tall and going to seed.

You could start at the head and work down, Thomas said.

We find the break we fix the break, Calvin said. Dont matter where we start.

He pulled on his gloves and they walked the first row looking at the drip emitters and the lines that ran from emitter to emitter low along the ground. The soil here had gone pale and hard and in places it had cracked in long seams like something trying to open. A yellowjacket moved over a fallen apple, a small one, marble-sized, that would never have come to anything.

Here, Thomas said.

A coupling had sheared where the line made a turn around a tree and the ground around it was long dried. Calvin knelt and looked at it and dug with his fingers in the dry soil.

This didn't just happen, he said.

I know it.

This has been dry a week at least.

Thomas didn't say anything.

Calvin looked up at him. You knew about this.

I walked the block four days ago. I thought it was the emitters.

You thought it was the emitters.

I meant to come back out.

Calvin stood up. He took off his hat and held it in his hands and looked down the row of trees. Some of them had leaves beginning to curl at the edges, rolling lengthwise against the heat.

God almighty, Thomas, he said.

I know it.

This is sixty, seventy trees on this lateral.

I said I know it.

Calvin put his hat back on and went back to the truck for the fittings and the pipe saw. The mountains were brighter now and the sun beginning to clear the ridge and the light coming level and gold through the trees. Thomas went and got the hand tamper and the extra line and they worked without speaking for a while. Calvin cut the bad section out and cleaned the ends with a rag and fitted a new coupling and ran new line back to the junction and Thomas tamped the soil down around the emitters that had been exposed.

When they were done Calvin opened and bled the line and they watched the water come up through each emitter in its small perfect bead. Calvin walked the row and Thomas walked the other side and they watched the water and the trees stood over them in the morning light.

That'll hold, Calvin said.

It ought to.

You want to check the next lateral.

Might as well.

They drove to the next junction and parked and Thomas got out and leaned against the truck bed and lit a cigarette. Calvin pulled the fittings box out and set it on the tailgate.

How much you reckon they offered, Thomas said.

Calvin didn't answer for a moment. He was sorting through the fittings.

Enough, he said.

Enough meaning what.

Meaning enough to where a man ought to at least think about it.

Thomas smoked and looked at the trees. A scrub jay crossed between the rows and vanished.

I've thought about it, he said.

You've been against it.

I've been against selling, that's different from not thinking.

Calvin looked at him. You want to tell me what you've been thinking then.

Thomas dropped the cigarette and stepped on it. What I been thinking is that Daddy planted them trees in nineteen fifty-three. Planted some of them by hand. By his own hand in this ground. And his old man had ground here before that. You understand what I'm saying to you?

I understand it.

Do you.

Calvin pulled the fittings box closer and set both hands on the edge of the tailgate. He looked down the row of trees and there was nothing in his face that was easy to read.

What I understand, he said, is that Daddy also worked himself near to death on this place and didn't have a thing to show for it in the end and I watched that happen. I watched every bit of it happen. And what I understand about this water situation is that we're looking at three more years of this at a minimum and our allocation is already cut and it gets cut again next year and there ain't a thing we can do about that.

You don't know that.

I know it same as you do. Same as any man in this valley knows it.

Thomas was quiet. He pulled on his gloves and they walked the row. The line here was intact and the emitters running and the soil around each tree dark and good.

It ain't like we'd have nothing, Calvin said. We'd have what they pay us and that's real money.

To do what with.

I don't know. Something other than this.

Thomas stopped walking and Calvin went a few steps further and stopped too and turned around.

Something other than this, Thomas said.

That's what I said.

What does that mean.

It means options. It means not spending every June out here with your back bent over a line you can't keep running and your water going shorter every year and the price of everything going up and the price of apples going—you know where the price of apples is going. You know all of that same as I do.

Thomas looked at his brother. Calvin's face was weathered and serious and there was no meanness in it. He was a man who had thought about things at length and alone and what he said was what he believed.

I'll tell you what it means, Thomas said. It means some California outfit runs a subdivision out here and them trees get pulled and that ground gets covered over with concrete and that's the end of it. That's the end of what this family ever was or ever did on this earth.

Calvin looked off. A hawk was making a slow circle above the ridge.

Could be, he said.

No could be about it.

I ain't saying I want that.

Then what are you saying.

I'm saying we can't go on like this. I'm saying the math of this thing don't work and it ain't going to work and I been carrying the weight of that knowledge and I wanted you to know it too. I wanted us to talk about it like men.

Thomas walked on down the row and Calvin let him go and stood watching him. The morning was warming now and the shadow of the ridge had retreated up the hillside and the light was flat and broad across the orchard. Somewhere down in the third block a tractor started up on the neighboring property, somebody else's land, and ran a while and shut off.

Thomas came back down the row.

You talked to a lawyer, he said.

I talked to a lawyer.

Without telling me.

I was finding out what there was to find out. I wasn't signing anything.

Thomas took off his hat and ran his hand through his hair. He was lean in the way of men who have worked outdoors all their lives and there was a quality about him of something kept in close check.

What did the lawyer say.

He said the estate splits equal. He said we'd both have to agree.

I know that.

Well.

I'm telling you I know that, Calvin. I'm not an idiot.

I never said you were.

You're saying I'm sentimental.

I didn't say that either.

But that's what you mean.

Calvin leaned back against the truck. Look, he said. There's a thing about this place that I feel too. You ain't the only one that feels it. Daddy's still here to me when I walk out in those trees in the morning. I know what this ground is. I know what it means. But they're going to be feelings is all they are, Tom. Come the time when the line's gone for good and the allocation's nothing and we're selling fruit for less than it costs to grow it, they're just going to be feelings and they ain't going to be enough.

Thomas was quiet for a long time. A quail called from somewhere in the brush at the edge of the property and was quiet and called again.

How much, Thomas said.

Calvin told him.

Thomas looked at him.

Per acre, Calvin said.

Jesus.

It's real money.

I know it's real money.

They drove on to the next junction and pulled the line and found a crushed section where the tractor had crossed it years ago and patched it with a sleeve coupling and drove it down with the tamper. The sun was high now and they sweated through their shirts and drank water from the jug behind the seat.

At noon they sat in the shade of the truck on the downhill side and ate the sandwiches Thomas had brought and drank the last of the water. The valley spread below them and they could see the town and the glare off the river and the brown hills on the far side going up into mountains that were pale and distant and still capped in a little white.

You remember when Daddy used to flood this block, Thomas said.

Calvin smiled a little. Yeah I remember.

We'd come out here after school and dam the rows with dirt and the water would sheet out and we'd wade in it.

I remember.

You remember that old dog he had. That redbone.

Rex.

Rex. He'd run the rows and bark at the water.

Calvin ate his sandwich and looked at the valley.

I remember when this water was free, he said. I remember when you could run it any day you wanted for as long as you wanted. Everything else was hard but the water wasn't hard.

Thomas nodded.

He had eighty acres in apples at one point, Calvin said. Eighty acres. You couldn't do it now. No man could do it now by himself with the allocations the way they are and the cost of everything.

Thomas set down the rest of his sandwich. He looked at his hands. They were cracked along the knuckles and the nails were dark with soil.

I been having a dream, he said.

Calvin looked at him.

I been dreaming about this place. Not like it is but like it was. When we were kids. The grass is high in the dream and the water's running and Daddy's somewhere in the trees. I can't see him but I can hear him. Hear him working.

Calvin didn't say anything.

You ever dream about it, Thomas said.

Sometimes.

What do you dream.

I don't know. I wake up and it's gone.

Thomas picked up his sandwich again and looked at it and set it back down.

I know you're probably right, he said. About the math of it. About where it's headed.

Calvin waited.

I just don't know what we are if we ain't this, Thomas said. You know what I mean? Our whole lives. Our whole lives we've been the Cole boys from the Cole orchard out on Tule Road. What are we if we sell that? Who are we?

Calvin was quiet for a while. A hawk, or perhaps the same hawk, was working the thermals above the ridge again, tilting slightly as it turned.

We're ourselves, Calvin said. Same as we always were.

You believe that.

I believe you can be who you are somewhere other than this piece of ground.

Thomas looked at him. You really believe that.

I'm working on it, Calvin said.

Thomas almost smiled. That's honest, he said.

I'm trying to be.

They sat a while longer and the heat pressed down on the valley and the light off the distant river was white and hard. A truck passed on the main road below and they could see the dust of it a long time after.

They worked through the afternoon, moving through the blocks, checking laterals and flushing lines and replacing emitters that had clogged with algae or mineral scale. The work was meticulous and slow and they did it quietly for the most part, passing tools back and forth and grunting instructions and working around each other in the close spaces between the trees.

In the middle of the afternoon they found a length of line that had been chewed through, a woodrat or a squirrel, and Calvin cut it out and spliced it and Thomas held the sections while Calvin set the fittings.

Every year, Thomas said.

Yeah.

We lose more to that than anything.

Calvin snugged the coupling and turned the screwdriver and handed it back. I've thought about cats, he said.

A cat won't do it.

You'd need a bunch of cats.

You'd need an army of cats.

Calvin laughed, a short honest sound, and Thomas smiled and for a moment they were simply two men working and the larger questions were at some remove from them.

Toward evening they came to the last block, the original planting, the oldest trees on the property. These were big trees, unpruned to modern practice, high and spreading, their bark gray and deeply furrowed, and they stood in the long evening light with a quality of witness, as if they had observed the whole tenure of human effort on this ground and held it somewhere in their annual rings.

Thomas stopped walking and stood looking up into one of them. Calvin stood beside him.

Daddy grafted this one, Thomas said.

I know it.

You can still see the graft line.

He pointed and Calvin looked and you could see it, a slight swelling in the trunk five feet up where the tissues had knit decades ago into a seam that was invisible except that it was there.

He didn't know what he was doing when he did it, Thomas said. He'd never grafted before. Drove to town and bought a book.

He figured it out.

Eventually. Lost a few first.

Calvin reached up and touched the bark of the tree. It was rough and slightly warm from the day's sun and under his hand it felt like something real and particular and not replaceable by anything.

You know what I think sometimes, he said. I think about how every year there's a day when the blossoms open. Just one day when they're all open at once. And if you're here you see it and if you're not here you miss it. And I've seen it thirty years running and I never once told anybody about it. Never thought to. It was just a thing that happened and I was here and that was enough. I think about not seeing that.

Thomas looked at him.

You can see blossoms somewhere else, Thomas said. But he said it gently.

Not these blossoms.

No, Thomas said. Not these.

They stood under the old tree for a while and the light went gold and then amber and the shadow of the ridge came down across the orchard once more and the air was cooling and the smell of the grass rose around them.

They gathered the tools and loaded the truck in the failing light. The dog appeared from somewhere, padding up the road toward them, and Calvin opened the truck door and it jumped in and turned three times on the seat.

Move over, Calvin said.

They drove back up the access road with the windows down and the cool air coming in off the mountains. The pump house was dark against the sky and the house lights were on and through the kitchen window they could see the lamp burning on the table.

Thomas shut the truck off and they sat for a moment.

I ain't saying yes, Thomas said.

I know it.

I'm saying I'll think about it.

That's all I'm asking.

You should've told me before you talked to the lawyer.

I know I should've.

Calvin. This is your home as much as it's mine.

Calvin's hands were on the wheel. I know what it is, he said.

All right then.

All right.

Thomas got out and the dog jumped down after him and went under the porch. The stars were coming out over the mountains, a few at first and then more, and the night was very clear and still and somewhere across the valley a coyote yipped twice and was silent. Calvin sat in the truck a little longer and looked at the orchard in the darkness, the shapes of the trees against the sky, their particular crowns against the stars, and then he got out too and walked to the house and went in.
