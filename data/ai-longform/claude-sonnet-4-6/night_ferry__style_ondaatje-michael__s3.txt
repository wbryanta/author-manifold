The ferry leaves at eleven and he is already on it by nine-thirty, sitting in the forward lounge with a coffee that has gone cold, watching the dock lights through glass that is smeared with the breath of other crossings. Outside, a deckhand in an orange vest moves along the rail checking something he cannot see, bending at intervals like a man reading a very long sentence. The water is black and the dock is lit yellow and the deckhand moves through both colours alternately, orange, then briefly luminous, then orange again, and the man watches this until the deckhand disappears around the bow.

His name is Paul Ardenne. He is forty-three years old and he has not spoken to his brother in eleven years, not since the morning in their mother's kitchen when certain things were said that seemed, at the time, to belong to a temporary season of cruelty, the kind that passes. It did not pass. Instead it calcified, the way a small injury will sometimes calcify in the body, becoming not the original wound but something harder and more permanent, a new geography inside the chest.

The body was found on a gravel beach near a place called Nólsoy on the island of Eysturoy. That is what the Danish constable had told him on the telephone, the man's voice careful and considered, pausing between clauses as though aware that language, under certain pressures, must be handled differently. The body had been in the water perhaps four days. Perhaps five. There was a wallet but the wallet was empty except for a photograph of two boys standing in front of what appeared to be a spruce tree, one boy taller by a head, both of them squinting into a sun that came from somewhere off to the left of the frame.

Paul had kept that photograph for years. He could not remember losing it.

The ferry horn sounds once, low and prolonged, a sound that enters the body rather than the ears. He feels it in his sternum. Through the window he watches the dock begin to move, or rather the dock stays fixed and the ferry begins its slow withdrawal from certainty, the way sleep withdraws the room. He thinks of the photograph. He thinks of how his brother Luc had always squinted in that particular way, as though sunlight were a kind of argument he was not quite prepared to win. Even indoors, sometimes, he would do this, narrowing his eyes at something in the middle distance that nobody else could identify, some problem of light or interpretation that required this specific expression.

He has a bag with him, one change of clothes. The constable said it would not take long. There are procedures, forms, the identification itself, which would be quick, the man had said, not unkindly. Quick in the way that certain things must be quick, the way a verdict is read quickly, not because it is unimportant but because prolonging it would constitute a particular kind of cruelty.

—

He finds a seat near the stern where the engines are loudest and the vibration comes up through the metal floor and through the chair and into his spine. He prefers this. There are perhaps thirty other passengers on the night crossing, most of them sleeping under thin blankets in the reclining seats of the main cabin, their shoes removed, their faces slack and private. He walks past them and out onto the rear deck, where the wind is immediate and cold and smells of diesel and something older beneath it, something oceanic and without period, a smell that belongs to no particular century.

The wake spreads behind the ferry in a long white V, phosphorescent at the edges, and he watches it diminish into the dark. This is what recedes. The dock lights are already small. The city is already becoming a suggestion, an arrangement of brightness against a hill, and he thinks of how a life looks the same way from sufficient distance, a brightness that clarifies only once you have moved far enough away to lose the particulars, the specific failures and moments of grace becoming indistinguishable at last, becoming simply: light.

Luc had lived for a time in Bergen. This Paul had known through their aunt Margitte, who had continued to correspond with both brothers separately through the years of their silence, carrying news between them like a woman carrying water in her cupped hands, losing some of it with each step but arriving still with something. From Margitte he knew that Luc had worked on boats, that he had studied for a period the restoration of old woodwork, that he had for three years lived with a woman named Sigrid who kept bees and translated Norwegian poetry into French, that he had left Bergen eventually and moved further north. After that, the reports grew sporadic. The water spilled more easily. Then Margitte had died in the winter of the previous year and the last connection severed and he had thought: now I will never know where he is. He had thought this with what he believed was equanimity. He was not certain now that he had been right about that.

The cold intensifies. He should go inside. He stays.

There are things he remembers about his brother that have nothing to do with the argument, nothing to do with the kitchen and their mother's careful neutrality, her hands flat on the table as though she were trying to keep it from rising. What he remembers is: the sound of Luc practising scales on a guitar he played badly and without embarrassment for three years of their adolescence, the same scales, evening after evening, patient and slightly off. The way Luc could sleep anywhere, on floors, on ferries, in the back seat of cars, folding himself into sleep the way other people fold a map. How he had once, when Paul was seven and Luc was nine, carried him on his back for two kilometres through winter dark after Paul had twisted his ankle on the frozen path behind their grandmother's house, how Luc had not complained but had instead talked continuously as he walked, describing in great detail an imaginary city he was building in his mind, a city with particular bridges and a market that sold only blue objects, keeping Paul's attention fixed on something other than the pain or the cold or the immensity of the dark around them.

Paul has never told anyone about that walk.

He wonders who he is traveling toward. He wonders if the body on the beach near Nólsoy is his brother or is simply a dead man who was carrying a photograph that once belonged to his brother, which is a different kind of answer to a question he has been carrying so long he can no longer remember a time when he was not carrying it.

—

He goes inside eventually. He buys another coffee and sits with it at a small table near a window and takes out the notebook he carries in his jacket pocket, a small brown notebook in which he sometimes writes things down, not as a journal, not as a record, but simply to give language to what would otherwise remain shapeless, to impose on the flux of perception some temporary structure that makes it possible to move through the day. He opens it to a blank page and sits for a while without writing.

The man at the next table has fallen asleep with his head tipped back, his mouth slightly open, an unread paperback spread face-down on his lap. There is something tender about him. About all of them, really, these people sleeping on the night crossing, their defences lowered, their faces returned to something earlier than the faces they carry through the world. He thinks about this. He thinks about his brother's face, the actual face, which memory has continued to revise over eleven years, each revision slight but cumulative, so that he is no longer certain which version is the true one. There are photographs. Their mother's albums. But he has not looked at those.

He writes in the notebook: *The body in water for four days loses its name. It becomes the body. It is returned to the anonymous condition from which it emerged, which is also the condition to which all things are moving.*

He reads this back. He closes the notebook.

What he knows about death is what everyone knows, which is the formal version, the version with caskets and flowers and a specific quality of silence in rooms. His mother. His father before that. His father on a Tuesday afternoon in October, sitting in a chair on the porch of the house outside Rimouski, having been moving toward that chair for several years by then, and the sitting down was simply the last motion in a long series of motions that had all been moving in the same direction. His father had been a difficult man and Paul had loved him in the way you love difficult things, with a love that is partly resistance, partly exhaustion, partly the accumulated weight of everything that has ever passed between you. He had not cried at the funeral. He had cried instead three weeks later in a hardware store, standing in front of a display of paint samples, for reasons that remain opaque to him.

The ocean outside the window is invisible. There is only the window, smeared and dark, and beyond it, suggested rather than seen, the moving mass of northern water.

When the constable had called, Paul had been eating breakfast alone at his kitchen table. He had been eating toast and reading something on his phone and his coffee had been hot and the morning light had been coming through the east window at a low angle, lying across the table in a long stripe, and he had been, he realized later, content. Not happy in any dramatic sense, but content in the way of a body that is warm and fed and without immediate difficulty. And then the phone had rung and the careful Danish voice had begun its careful sentences and the morning light continued to lie across the table and he had thought: the light does not know this. The light is still performing its usual function, indifferent to what is being said in this kitchen, and there was something in that indifference that seemed, rather than cruel, simply accurate. A description of how things are.

He had written down the constable's name and a number and the name of the village, Nólsoy, which he had asked the man to spell twice.

—

Somewhere around two in the morning the ferry enters a period of rougher water and the hull begins to move differently, a slow rolling that sets the sleeping passengers shifting in their chairs, one woman reaching up in sleep to grip the armrest, her body knowing before her mind does that the conditions have changed. Paul is still awake. He has not slept on night crossings since he was young, since the crossings he and Luc used to take with their parents to the island in summer, both boys sprawled across the seats while their parents sat upright and quiet in the dark the way adults on those journeys are always upright and quiet, holding the weight of the arrival, the weight of the departure, all the things that need to be held.

He and Luc had played a game on those crossings. He cannot remember who invented it. The game was this: you named a thing, any thing, and the other person had to name the thing that thing became when it was no longer itself. A match became ash and light. A wave became the shore. A word became the silence after it. They played this for hours, elaborating the rules as they went, lying on their backs in the dark ferry with the engines vibrating beneath them, their voices low so as not to wake the adults, and he remembers the sense he had even then of something serious in the game, some genuine inquiry they were conducting through the available structures of play, some question about transformation and loss and whether the thing that remains after change is a continuation or an elegy.

What does a brother become? That is what he is thinking. He is thinking: there is the brother who is alive and estranged and who exists somewhere in the world, who eats and sleeps and looks at things with his particular squinting expression, who is known to you only as a possibility you carry inside yourself, a door you have not opened in eleven years but whose location you are always aware of. And then there is the other brother, the one on the gravel beach, the one who has been in the water, who has been returned to the anonymous condition. And these are two different brothers. Two different forms of loss. He has been living with one kind for eleven years. He does not yet know which kind he is traveling toward.

Perhaps both. Perhaps the living and the estranged are also a kind of body on a beach, a kind of loss that does not yet have a name because the identification has not been made, because you have not yet stood over it and said: yes, this was mine. This was once mine.

The ferry rolls. The woman releases the armrest in her sleep. Outside, the invisible sea is doing what it has always done, indifferent and enormous, and Paul Ardenne sits inside it, in a lighted room moving through the dark, with his cold coffee and his brown notebook and his eleven years of whatever you call what he has been carrying.

He thinks of Sigrid who kept bees and translated Norwegian poetry into French. He thinks of what it would mean to do that, to carry a thing across a language, to find in one set of sounds an equivalent for another set of sounds, knowing that something will be lost in the crossing and doing it anyway, not because equivalence is achievable but because the attempt is itself a kind of fidelity. He thinks of Luc sitting in a northern room with this woman, both of them working at their respective forms of translation, and he feels something he does not have an immediate word for, something that is neither grief nor envy but partakes of both, a feeling that has to do with the specific loneliness of imagining someone else's happiness in detail.

He should have called. He has known this for eleven years.

There is a particular quality of self-knowledge that arrives too late to be useful and can only be useful in a different way, as a form of testimony, as a record of the accurate dimensions of one's failure. He has been honest with himself about the argument. He knows that what was said in the kitchen was said by both of them, that cruelty in those situations is rarely entirely one-directional, that his own part in it was not small. He has known this for years. Knowledge of this kind, however precise, does not constitute action. This is the difference between understanding a thing and doing something with the understanding.

He picks up the pen. He opens the notebook again.

He writes his brother's name. Just the name, on an otherwise empty page. *Luc.* He looks at it. Four letters, a breath and a half, the name their father had chosen from a list their mother had kept in a small green notebook during the pregnancy, a name she had told him about once, how she had chosen his name and their father had chosen Luc, and he had asked her what else was on the list and she had laughed and told him names he had immediately forgotten, the other possible brothers and sisters who existed only as names in a notebook, who had been declined, who were perhaps living out their unlived lives somewhere in the space between intention and event.

*Luc.* He reads the name back as though it is a word in a language he is only beginning to understand. As though the word contains more information than he has yet been able to retrieve from it.

—

The ferry arrives at five-forty in a harbour that is still dark, the hills behind the town holding the last hour of night while the dock lights do their yellow work on the water. He is one of the first passengers off, descending the ramp with his small bag, his breath visible in the cold. There is a van waiting that will take a group of them to the town centre. He does not take the van. He walks instead, along the harbour road, past the coiled ropes and the sleeping boats, past a café that is not yet open, past a monument he does not stop to read.

The constable's name is Eidervik. He is to meet him at nine. He has three hours.

He walks to the edge of the harbour where the road ends and the stone begins, a long flat shelf of rock that extends into the water. He stands on it. The sky to the east is beginning its slow concession to light, the darkness diluting, becoming something that is not quite dark and not quite anything else, that period before dawn that belongs to no category, that is the pure duration of change. The water around the rock is very clear and he can see, in the growing light, the shapes of things beneath the surface, a darkness of kelp, the pale shifting of sand.

He does not know what he will find. He has known this since the telephone call and he has carried it across the water with him and he carries it still, this not-knowing, this condition that is also a kind of intimacy with the unknown thing, the closest he has been to his brother in eleven years.

He thinks of the game. He thinks: what does a brother become?

The light keeps coming. It does not ask permission. It enters the harbour the way it enters everywhere, without hesitation, finding the surfaces of things and declaring them, this boat, this rope, this man standing at the edge of the rock with a small bag at his feet, his face turned east toward the light and also toward the island beyond which, on a gravel beach, something is waiting to be identified or not identified, to be claimed or to remain unclaimed, to receive a name or to remain in the anonymous country where all unnamed things reside, patient and cold and attended only by the sound of water moving across stone, the same sound it has made for longer than there have been brothers, longer than there have been names, longer than the particular human grief of standing at the edge of a harbour in early light and not yet knowing, not yet being required to know.

He picks up his bag. He begins walking toward the town.
