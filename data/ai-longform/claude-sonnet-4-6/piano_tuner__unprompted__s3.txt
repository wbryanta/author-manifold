The truck knew the roads better than he did now. That was what Morris told himself on mornings when the fog sat low over the valley and he could not always trust his eyes to find the turnoffs, the gravel lanes that branched from the county road like smaller thoughts breaking from a larger worry. The truck—a 1994 Ford with a heater that worked on its own schedule and a passenger seat occupied permanently by his canvas bag of tools—leaned into the familiar curves with something that felt like memory, or at least like habit, which at his age Morris understood to be the same thing.

He had the route in his book, as he always had, though he had not needed the book in eleven years. Still he kept it on the dash, its cardboard cover soft as skin, the pages inside gone amber at the edges. The names were written in his own hand from a time when his hand was steadier, the addresses running down each page in neat columns: the families, the farmhouses, the one small inn that had closed and then reopened as something calling itself a wellness retreat, the church at the end of Route 9 where the road stopped pretending to go anywhere. He had crossed out names when people died or moved away or when the instruments themselves finally gave up, and there were many crossings-out now, many thin black lines drawn through what had once been stops on a living route. But he had never crossed out the church. He thought about that sometimes—why he had never crossed it out—and he did not pursue the thought too far.

It was October, the second week, and the hills were doing what they did this time of year, burning themselves down in oranges and reds with a kind of gorgeous indifference. Morris had stopped finding this beautiful in the way other people seemed to find it beautiful, people who came up from the city with their cameras and their ciders. To him the color meant the end of something, had always meant that, probably since childhood though he could not remember the origin of the feeling, only the feeling itself, reliable as the changing of the leaves that produced it.

His first stop was the Alderman place, six miles out on Cutter Road, where a Baldwin upright had been standing in the front parlor since 1962. He knew this because Eleanor Alderman had told him so the first time he came, twenty-three years ago, when she was already an old woman and the piano was already old in the way that things become old when they are loved and then forgotten, which is a different kind of old than simply aged. Eleanor had died in 2019 and her daughter Janet had kept the house and kept paying for the tuning, not because she played—she did not play—but because, she had told Morris with an embarrassed laugh the first time he came after her mother's death, she felt that stopping would be a kind of letting go she was not ready for. Morris had said he understood. He did understand.

Janet was not home, as she often wasn't. She left the door unlocked for him on tuning days, which occurred every six months as regular as the solstices. Morris let himself in, wiped his boots on the mat with the roosters on it, and went down the hall to the parlor where the Baldwin waited in the gray morning light coming through curtains Janet had not opened. He opened them himself, as he always did, because he needed the light and because he thought the piano deserved it.

He set his bag on the bench—there was nowhere else; the bench was not for sitting, had not been for sitting in some years—and unzipped it with the deliberate care he gave to all his movements now, understanding that care was the tax his body levied on speed. He took out his tuning lever, his mutes, the little flashlight he used to see into the action, and he laid them on the piano's lid in the order he would use them, which was the order he had always used them, which his own teacher had shown him in a shop in Montpelier forty-one years ago.

The piano, when he played a few test notes, was not badly out. Pianos that aren't played often hold their tuning longer than people expect, Morris knew, because it is use—the hammers striking, the soundboard flexing—that unsettles the strings. Silence is a kind of preservation. He had thought about this many times, what it meant, and he had reached no conclusions he could defend.

He worked through the bass first, then the tenor, then the treble, and the house was quiet around him except for the sound of a refrigerator in the kitchen and, once, a car going by on the road outside. The work itself had always felt to him like a conversation with the instrument, the kind of conversation you have with someone who can only answer in one register, in pitch rather than words, telling you not where it has been but what it needs. He listened. He adjusted. He listened again. He had been doing this so long that the listening had become something almost below conscious thought, the way breathing was below thought, and he was grateful for that, for the way the body eventually shoulders certain burdens so the mind can rest.

When he was finished he played an A-major scale, then a simple chord, then—he always did this at the end of a tuning, at every piano on every route, and he could not have explained why he had started—a few bars of something. Today it was the opening of a Schubert impromptu, the one in G-flat, which he had never played well but had always loved. The Baldwin's tone was round and a little dark, the way old Baldwins often were, like a voice that had settled into itself. He played eight bars and stopped. He gathered his tools. He closed the curtains as he had found them, which was not as he preferred them but which was not his house.

Outside, the air had the particular cold clarity of October mornings, and he stood beside the truck for a moment with his bag over his shoulder and looked at the hills. The maples along the Alderman fencerow were full red now. In a week they would be bare. He got in the truck.

The next stop was the Prentiss farm, where there was a Steinway parlor grand that had no business being in a farmhouse and that Morris loved unreasonably, the way you love things that have arrived somewhere unlikely and made it their home. The story of the Steinway, as he understood it from years of accumulated fragments, was that Calvin Prentiss's grandfather had brought it up from Boston on a flatbed wagon in 1921 for a wife who had died before it arrived, and that the grandfather had kept it and kept it tuned out of a grief that hardened over decades into something indistinguishable from principle. Calvin himself was eighty-one now and he played sometimes in the evenings, Morris knew, simple hymns and a few folk songs, though Calvin had never mentioned this and Morris had never asked. He knew because he could hear it sometimes in the way the keys had worn, in the areas of the keyboard that were played and the areas that were not, the way a path through grass tells you where people walk without needing any words.

Calvin met him at the door as he always did, with coffee that Morris accepted even though he had mostly given up coffee two years ago for reasons his doctor had explained and he had half-forgotten. They stood in the kitchen and Calvin talked about the weather and about a wall in the upper pasture that needed repointing and about his grandson who was studying something in Burlington that Calvin could not quite name but seemed proud of anyway. Morris listened and drank the coffee slowly, and this too felt like part of the work, the conversation that preceded the work, the way tuning a piano was never only tuning a piano but was also being present in the life of the house where the piano lived.

The Steinway was magnificent even now, even with the years on it. It sat in the room that must have been, once, a formal parlor, though the formality had long since given way to the practical incursions of farm life—there were seed catalogs on the side table, a pair of boots by the door, a child's drawing held to the window frame with tape. But the piano rose out of all of this with a kind of serene authority. Morris ran his hand along its lid before he opened it, as he did every time, a gesture that embarrassed him when he caught himself at it.

The Steinway was sharp throughout, the way instruments in dry heated houses went sharp in the winter and stayed there. Morris worked carefully, more carefully here than elsewhere, because the instrument asked for it—demanded it, almost—and because he felt that to do careless work on this piano would be a specific kind of failure. He worked for nearly two hours. Calvin brought him a second cup of coffee at some point, left it on the side table without speaking, and withdrew. Outside, a tractor started up somewhere beyond the barn and ran for a while and then stopped.

When he was finished he did not play. He never played the Steinway except in the most minimal way, a few chords to verify the tuning, and he would not have been able fully to explain this either, except that there was something about the instrument that made his playing feel like an imposition, like speaking too loudly in a place of genuine quiet.

He said goodbye to Calvin at the door and Calvin said he supposed he'd see him in the spring, and Morris said yes, in the spring, and they shook hands as they always did, and Morris noticed as he always noticed the strength still in Calvin's hand, the farm-strength that does not leave a man even when everything else begins to.

There was a stop at the inn—the wellness retreat—where a Yamaha console sat in a room called the Harmony Lounge, a name that pained Morris in a way he tried not to examine. The young woman at the desk did not know why they had a piano tuner coming or who had arranged it, and Morris told her he had an appointment and she accepted this because she was very young and because he was very old and because he said it with the authority of someone who has been doing a thing for a long time. The Yamaha was in tune, almost, and he made small adjustments and played his eight bars—this time something generic, a few measures of the Für Elise that he despised himself for playing but which came out of his hands before he could stop it—and left without interacting with anyone further.

Lunch was a sandwich he had made at five in the morning and wrapped in wax paper and put in the bag beside his tools, which was not hygienic, he knew, but which had been his system for thirty years and which he was not going to change. He ate in the truck on the side of the road outside the Beaumont property, which was the next stop, and he looked out through the windshield at a field where the grass had gone dry and bronze and where a single crow stood in the middle of it, doing nothing, going nowhere, present in the field the way objects are sometimes present, with a completeness that seems to ask nothing of the world.

The Beaumont house was vacant. This was new—it had been occupied as recently as his last visit in April, a young family who had moved up from somewhere and who had a daughter taking lessons with a teacher in town. He had known this because the piano, a modest Kawai upright, had been played, really played, the keys worn in the pattern of someone practicing scales and exercises with the dutiful repetition of a beginner. He had found that pattern moving in a way he could not account for, the evidence of effort, of someone learning to want something.

The Kawai sat in the empty living room, the family's absence written all around it in the pale rectangles on the walls where pictures had hung and the divots in the carpet where furniture had stood. Someone, probably the landlord, had left the electricity on, and the baseboard heat was running, and the piano had not suffered from the vacancy. He tuned it anyway. He always did what he had come to do.

He thought, tuning the empty house's piano, about a conversation he'd had with his son Daniel once, when Daniel was maybe nine or ten, about why pianos needed tuning at all. He had tried to explain about string tension, about temperature and humidity, about the physics of it, and Daniel had listened in the intent and slightly suspicious way he had of listening, as if information were something to be verified before accepted, and then he had said, But if nobody plays it, does it still go out of tune? And Morris had said yes, because the world acts on it regardless. Temperature changes, humidity changes, gravity acts on the strings. And Daniel had said, So there's no way to keep it perfect? And Morris had said, No, there isn't. You can only keep bringing it back.

He did not know why he thought of this conversation now rather than the other times he had thought of it in the past fifteen years. There were a handful of conversations like that one, things Daniel had said that Morris had not understood until later as the kind of thing you carry. He thought about Daniel every day, which he supposed was natural, though natural didn't make it easier and he had stopped expecting it to.

The afternoon light was dropping by the time he reached the last three stops, two of them quick—a spinet at the Hollowell place, strings so old they went false almost as fast as he corrected them, a losing battle he fought anyway, and a Mason & Hamlin at the Dressler house where an old woman named Ruth played every Sunday and whose piano was always nearly perfect, tuned by use and love and whatever mysterious attunement comes to instruments that are played by someone who has played them for sixty years. Ruth was not home either. Morris sat at her piano and played, when he was finished, not eight bars but a full page of something, the Brahms Intermezzo in A major, the op. 118 one, the one Daniel had played for his jury in the spring of his second year. He played it badly and did not care.

Then there was only the church.

He drove the last two miles on Route 9 in a light that had gone thin and silver, the sun somewhere behind clouds now, the day declining toward the cold. The church came up on the left as it always had, white clapboard against the hill, the steeple making its modest argument against the sky. The parking lot was empty. It was a Wednesday and services were Sunday mornings only now; Morris knew the pastor, a woman named Hendricks who had been kind to him in ways he didn't always know how to receive, and she left the side door unlatched on tuning days with the same unspoken faith that Janet Alderman left her front door unlocked—a faith that he would come, that he always came.

The side door opened into a short hallway that smelled of wood and candles and old paper, and then the hallway opened into the sanctuary, and the sanctuary was dim, lit only by the windows on the western wall where what remained of the afternoon came through in pale columns. The piano was a Bösendorfer, an Imperial, seventy-two years old, and it sat at the front of the church to the left of the altar, and it was the finest instrument on his route and possibly the finest instrument he had ever regularly maintained, and its presence here—in this small church, in this valley that the highway had bypassed in 1987 and that had been quietly declining ever since—was a fact he had long since stopped trying to explain and simply accepted as one accepts the presence of grace, which is to say with gratitude and without asking too many questions about how it got there.

He walked to it slowly, his bag over his shoulder, his footsteps quiet on the runner that went down the center aisle. He put his bag on the pew nearest the piano, the first pew, and he stood for a moment looking at the instrument the way he always stood for a moment looking at it. The lid was down, as he had left it in April. He lifted it.

He tuned the bass register first, and the church's acoustics opened everything up, turned the individual tones into something that filled the space, and Morris stood inside the sound as he worked, inside the sustained fifths and the gradual resolutions, feeling as he sometimes felt in this room that the tuning itself was the music, that this careful returning of things to where they ought to be was not preparation for something else but was itself the thing. He had been a religious man once, loosely, the way many people are religious loosely without examining it too carefully, and he was not that anymore, and what had replaced it was something harder to name, something he encountered most reliably in rooms like this one, alone with work he knew how to do.

He worked for an hour and a half. The church got darker. He brought the flashlight out and propped it to see into the action where he needed to see. The Bösendorfer had a very slight problem in the upper register, two notes that wanted to go sharp—they always did, these two, some idiosyncrasy of the soundboard in that register—and he coaxed them down with the patience that long acquaintance makes possible, the patience you can only have with things you have tended for a long time and understand not just technically but in the way you understand difficult people, people you love, people whose difficulties are familiar and therefore not frightening.

When he was finished he sat on the bench. He sat there for a while without playing, listening to the church, to the silence it made, which was a specific silence, denser than outdoor silence, accumulated and interior. The pale columns of light on the western wall had narrowed and gone gold.

Daniel had been twenty-two and a junior at the conservatory and the recital had been a homecoming recital, self-organized, done out of what Daniel had called at the time a desire to give something back, which Morris had known even then was partly true and partly a cover for something more private, some need to play in the room where he had first understood what music was, or what it could be, which for Daniel had happened at a Christmas service in this church when he was six years old. Morris remembered driving him home afterward in the snow and Daniel sitting in the back seat very quietly, not sleeping, not talking, and when Morris had asked what he was thinking Daniel had said, I want to do that. I want to make that. And Morris had said, What, music? And Daniel had said, Whatever that was.

The recital had been on a Saturday evening in March. Daniel had played Schumann and Schubert and, at the end, the Brahms op. 118, the intermezzo, which he had not told Morris he was going to play. Morris had heard it and understood, in the way you understand things that you know more fully than you know how to say, that his son was excellent, was genuinely excellent, in the particular and unrepeatable way that some people are excellent at some things, the way that calls for a special kind of grief when it is lost.

The spring had been hard and the summer harder and in the fall Daniel was gone, and he was gone in the ordinary way that young people are sometimes gone—a car, a road, a night in November, the call that comes at two in the morning—and Morris had kept the route because he did not know what else to keep, and he had ended each route at the church because he did not know what else to do with the ending.

He put his hands on the keys. He played the intermezzo slowly, more slowly than it is meant to be played, or perhaps at exactly the speed it is meant to be played when you are seventy-three and your son has been dead for fifteen years and the light in a small church in October is going gold and then going away. He played it through once without stopping, playing past the places where his fingers did not find what he intended, the way you speak past the places where your voice threatens not to hold. He came to the end and the last chord was there, quiet as something laid down.

Outside, wind moved through the maples along the church road, and he could hear it through the walls, the dry rush of leaves that were letting go. He sat at the piano for a long time after the sound had died, his hands in his lap, in the silence of the church which held the sound the way silence, after long enough, always holds the sounds that have been placed inside it. Then he covered the keys. He gathered his tools. He put on his coat and picked up his bag and walked down the aisle toward the side door, and his footsteps going out were the same as his footsteps coming in, careful and slow, the steps of a man with work to do, a man with more stops than this, a man who knew how to keep coming back.
