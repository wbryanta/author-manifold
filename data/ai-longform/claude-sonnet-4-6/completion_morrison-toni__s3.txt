He kept his pistol loaded and his eyes open for the signs men left to warn or invite or threaten, and he watched the tree line as he rode, noting the quality of silence, whether it was the silence of emptiness or the silence of waiting.

Regina picked her way along the old trail with the careful intelligence of a horse that has known bad ground before and does not forget it. The fog was thinning now. Through the canopy of oak and elm he could see the sky whitening toward noon, and the wetness on his coat had begun to dry at the shoulders though his boots were still sodden from the surf. He thought of the sloopmen, those two brothers from Providence who had agreed to carry him south for a price he found insulting and paid anyway, because there was no other passage to be had that week. They were cautious men, barely speaking, drinking their rum in a way that suggested they had learned to ration everything long ago. He wondered if they had made shore somewhere or were still drifting in that gold fog, following the sound of nothing, trusting the tide to keep them from the rocks.

His business here was not of his own choosing, exactly. He had been summoned, or something near enough to summoned that the distinction hardly mattered. A letter had come north by way of a tobacco factor he occasionally trusted, written in a hand that was educated but hurried, and it bore the name of a man called D'Ortega. The letter said that D'Ortega had a debt outstanding and would settle it not in currency, of which he claimed to have insufficient supply that season, but in kind. Jacob had read the letter twice, then a third time, translating the elaborate courtesy of its language into plain meaning. D'Ortega owed him for a consignment of iron goods—tools and implements ordered and received but not paid for. The debt was two years old and Jacob had been patient because patience in commerce was a kind of investment, but patience had a limit and he had reached it. He had written back saying he would come south and they would settle the matter between them like reasonable men. He had not said what he considered reasonable and he suspected D'Ortega had his own ideas.

The plantation revealed itself slowly, the way unpleasant things often do. First there were the cleared fields, still brown and bare, with the winter tobacco stubble showing through like a beard badly shaved. Then a smell, which was not the smell of the bay or the marsh grass but something older and closer, the smell of many bodies in confined space, of cook fires and privies and animals and worked earth. Then the fence, which was less a boundary than a statement, rough-hewn posts set close together and strung with wire that spoke of cost and effort and a particular philosophy regarding what lay inside and what lay without. Jacob slowed Regina to a walk and approached along the drive, if it could be called that—two ruts worn through the red clay, edged with weeds that had been cut back enough to suggest the appearance of order without quite achieving it.

The house was large by the standards of this country. He had seen grander in the English counties he had left behind, but here, set against the flat landscape and the grey sky and the particular desolation of post-winter Virginia, it had an ambition about it that was almost poignant. Brick on the lower story, wood above, with a porch that ran the length of the front and columns that were perhaps trying to be classical but had not quite managed it. Smoke from two chimneys. A dog that barked twice and was silenced. Three men watching him from the fence at the far edge of the yard, standing still with the particular stillness of people who have learned that movement calls attention.

A boy came for his horse. Jacob dismounted and handed over the reins with a coin, and the boy, who could not have been more than ten and whose bare feet on the cold clay made Jacob look briefly away, took the coin and the horse together and said nothing.

D'Ortega came to the door before Jacob could knock. He was a big man gone soft at the middle, with a face that had been handsome once and was now merely large, and hands that gripped Jacob's with the enthusiasm of a man who knows he is at a disadvantage and wishes to disguise it with warmth. He spoke in the accent of someone who had learned English from people who had learned it themselves, each word slightly too deliberate, as though he were translating in his private mind before releasing it.

Welcome, he said. You are very welcome. Come inside. The weather is foul.

Jacob agreed that the weather was foul and followed him inside, noting as he crossed the threshold the quality of the furnishings, which were good but not as good as they wanted to be, and the smell of the house, which was damp and overlaid with tobacco smoke and something sweet and slightly rotten that he could not identify and did not ask about.

They sat in a room that served as both parlor and office, with a desk at one end piled with papers and at the other end two chairs positioned before a fire that was doing its best. A woman brought wine and left quickly without looking at either of them. D'Ortega poured and spoke of the voyage south and whether it had been difficult in this weather and what Jacob's opinion was of the province generally, and Jacob answered each question briefly and without elaboration, sipping his wine, which was better than he expected, and waiting.

D'Ortega eventually arrived at the subject.

You understand, he said, that this past season was not a prosperous one.

Jacob said that he understood no such thing, only that two years had passed since the iron goods were delivered and that the agreed sum had not been paid.

D'Ortega nodded with great feeling, as though Jacob had said something profoundly true and moving. Yes, he said. Yes. And I intend to make it right. That is why I asked you here. To make it right between us.

Jacob set down his cup. He had carried north with him, in the pocket of his coat, the figure. He named it now. D'Ortega received it with a wince that he transformed, partway through, into a smile.

That is the figure as I remember it, D'Ortega said. Near enough. And I will pay it. But not in coin, as I wrote to you. In kind. As I explained.

In kind meaning what, Jacob said.

D'Ortega rose and went to the window that overlooked the yard. Jacob came to stand beside him and saw, in the grey light of the Virginia afternoon, the yard and beyond it the fence and beyond the fence the fields and at the edge of the fields a row of structures that were not quite buildings and not quite shelters but something in between, low and dark, from which smoke seeped rather than rose. Three figures moved between these structures with the slow deliberate movement of people who are conserving something. Even at this distance Jacob could see that two were women and one was a girl, and the girl moved differently from the women, with a quickness that she seemed to be suppressing, as though she had learned that quickness attracted notice.

There, D'Ortega said.

Jacob looked at him.

I have more than I need this season and I cannot feed them all through another winter at my present circumstances. There is one in particular. Useful. Strong. Young enough to be worth the investment of time and training. I would put her value at—and here he named a figure that was lower than Jacob's debt by a small and insulting amount.

Jacob was quiet for a moment. He looked back at the window but the girl had gone inside or around a corner and was not visible.

I am not a planter, he said.

You would not need to be. She can be hired out. Or used in the house. Your wife—

I know my wife's situation, Jacob said. And I know my own.

D'Ortega turned from the window with his large face arranged in an expression of patient suffering. Then what, he said. What would satisfy you, Mr. Vaark. Tell me what would satisfy you and I will try to accommodate it.

Jacob thought of the north. He thought of the land he had, more than he could properly use, and the house on it that was adequate but only that, and the cold that came down from the mountains in October and did not leave until May, and his wife Rebekka who managed everything with a competence that he relied on without fully acknowledging, and the graves near the fence line that they did not speak of directly. He thought of what it would mean to arrive home with a human being instead of coin, what that would mean about who he was and what he had decided to become. He had told himself certain things about himself over the years, careful things, distinctions he maintained with some effort, and he was aware that some decisions, once made, have a way of making themselves again and again, each time with less resistance.

He looked at D'Ortega and said, I will see what you have.

What D'Ortega had was misery organized into a system that had been in operation long enough to have developed its own customs and hierarchies and small private languages. Jacob walked through it behind his host, who narrated with the practiced ease of a man describing livestock, pointing out qualities and deficiencies with the same detached vocabulary, pausing at certain figures to note age or skill or temperament. The cold had settled fully now and the sky was the color of old pewter and the red clay sucked at Jacob's boots. He kept his face arranged and his hands behind his back and he looked at what he was shown, and what he was shown was this: men and women and children who had been taken from one life and installed in this one, and who had been in this one long enough that the other life existed only in whatever interior space they had managed to preserve, and that space grew smaller each year.

He saw the girl at the edge of a group near one of the low structures. She was perhaps ten, perhaps a little older, with a face that was watchful in a particular way—not frightened, which he had expected, but assessing, the way an animal is watchful that has survived by knowing precisely what is in front of it and deciding quickly what to do. When D'Ortega gestured toward her she did not lower her eyes the way the others did. She looked at Jacob directly and he found he could not look away as quickly as he would have liked. There was something in the looking that was not challenge and not appeal but something he could not immediately name, a kind of active attention that made him feel, momentarily, that he was the one being examined.

She is called Florens, D'Ortega said. Or that is what we call her here. The mother came to us some years ago from a factor in Barbados. The mother is still useful but this one is more so. She is quick, he said, and by quick he meant intelligent, and he said it the way men say such things about those they own, as though intelligence is a feature of the property rather than evidence of a person.

Jacob nodded. He did not speak to the girl.

On the way back to the house D'Ortega showed him the tobacco drying sheds, which were in better repair than the human quarters, and the stables, which were better still. Jacob noticed this and said nothing about it because there was nothing to say that D'Ortega would hear.

They returned to the parlor and the fire and the second cup of wine. D'Ortega laid out his terms again and Jacob listened and then said, quietly, that the figure named was insufficient and that he would require the difference in coin or in goods of equivalent value or he would take his debt to the county court, which was, he understood, not an outcome that would benefit either of them, but which he was entirely prepared to pursue if necessary. He said this without heat or volume, which he had found was more effective than anger, and he watched D'Ortega's large face move through several expressions before settling on resignation.

They negotiated for another hour. Jacob got most of what he wanted, which was the figure owed and slightly above it, settled partly in coin that D'Ortega produced from a locked box with theatrical reluctance, and the rest in a promissory note against the following season's crop, which Jacob did not trust but accepted because the alternative was to return home with nothing. Then D'Ortega raised again, almost as a postscript, the matter of the girl.

Jacob said he had not come to Virginia to acquire what D'Ortega was offering.

D'Ortega said that he understood, but that the offer stood, and that the girl's mother had in fact requested it. He paused here, letting that settle. The mother, he said, had asked that the girl be taken. Had asked it plainly, looking him in the face in the way they were not supposed to do, and asking it without words exactly but making the request unmistakable. He had not honored it because he could see no advantage in it until now.

Jacob looked at the fire. He thought of several things and allowed himself to think of them clearly, which was something he did not always do. He thought of the girl's face and the quality of her looking. He thought of the mother who had asked. He tried to imagine what it would mean to ask that, what calculation or desperation or love or knowledge would bring a mother to that particular door and make her knock on it. He could not fully imagine it. But he had seen enough of the world to know that when a person asks for the one thing they should not have to ask for, the asking itself tells you something essential about the conditions that produced it.

He said, finally, that he would take the girl in lieu of the remaining portion of the promissory note, which he was canceling, and that they would consider the debt settled between them and not revisit it.

D'Ortega agreed before Jacob had finished speaking.

That night Jacob slept in the house as D'Ortega's guest, in a room where the damp came through the walls and the fire in the small grate did not quite reach the corners. He lay on his back on the mattress, which was better than what he'd slept on in many places and worse than what he had at home, and he looked at the ceiling and did not sleep for some time. He thought about what he had agreed to and what it made him and what it would continue to make him each morning when he woke up in a house where another person was present by his arrangement and not by her own choosing. He thought about Rebekka and what she would say, which would be practical and not sentimental, because she was practical and not sentimental, and he loved her for that and sometimes it frightened him. He thought about the land and the house and what he wanted to build on that land and whether wanting it was a sin or simply an ambition, and whether there was as much difference between those two things as he had previously assumed.

He thought about the graves near the fence line and he let himself think about them, which he did not always do. Four of them. The last one only a year old. Rebekka had stood at each one with her face held in a certain way and had gone back to the house and continued the work of the house and had not spoken of it except once, late at night, when she had said, in a voice he had never heard from her before or since, that she did not understand what was required of her. He had not answered because he did not know.

In the morning there was a breakfast of cornmeal and salt pork and more of the wine. D'Ortega was expansive in the way of men who have gotten what they wanted, and Jacob ate and answered his questions about the north and the fur trade and the disposition of certain tribal groups above the river, and when breakfast was done he said he would take his leave.

The girl was brought to the yard with a bundle smaller than a child's bundle, smaller than what a person would need, and she stood with it in front of her in both hands and looked at Jacob. The mother was not visible. He had not seen her since the walk through the yard the day before and he did not see her now and he told himself that this was not his business to pursue, that D'Ortega would manage his own arrangements and Jacob had his.

He lifted the girl up to the saddle, which she received without expression, and mounted behind her, and D'Ortega raised his hand in farewell from the porch, and Jacob turned Regina back along the drive toward the old Lenape trail and the village where the sloopmen were waiting, or where he hoped they were still waiting.

For the first mile neither of them spoke. The girl sat in front of him with her bundle on her lap and held herself very straight and looked at the trail between the horse's ears. Jacob was aware of her weight and her warmth, which was the warmth of a small creature that generates considerable heat, and he was aware of her breathing, which was steady and controlled in the way that breathing is when a person is working to keep it that way.

He said, finally, Your name is Florens?

She said nothing for a moment. Then she said, That is what I am called here.

He thought about how to answer that. He said, What were you called before here.

She was quiet for another moment. Then she said a name that he could not quite catch, something with sounds in it that his tongue did not know how to make, and she said it quietly and without looking at him, looking still at the trail ahead, and she said it only once and did not repeat it when he made a sound of inquiry, and he understood that it was not for him, that name, that she had said it into the air for her own purposes, as a kind of marking, a record that she had said it at least this once before they arrived wherever they were going and she became, entirely and permanently, what she was called there.

The fog had cleared entirely by now. The sky was pale blue and enormous above the bare trees, and the trail was dry, and Regina moved with her usual confident intelligence, and the land on either side was the land of this troubled province with its history of burning and its present of uneasy arrangements and its future which no one could read. Jacob rode through it with the girl sitting straight in front of him and thought about what he was carrying north, what he was responsible for now, what that meant for his understanding of himself. He found he could not settle the question. He supposed he would carry it home and see what it looked like in the light of the place where he actually lived, in the ordinary days of work and weather and the faces of the people he knew.

The village appeared ahead through the trees. He could smell the water and hear it, and somewhere beyond the low rooftops the sloopmen were waiting with their careful silence and their rationed rum, and beyond them was the open Atlantic and then home, and the girl in his arms sat straight and breathed steadily and held her small bundle, and Jacob Vaark rode toward the water thinking that the world was a mess and that a man had to live in it anyway, with whatever decisions he had made, and that this was neither a comfort nor an excuse but simply the condition of things, the ground on which everything else had to be managed.
