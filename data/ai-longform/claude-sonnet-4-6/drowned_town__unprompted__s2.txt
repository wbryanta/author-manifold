The water had given back the church first.

Maren noticed it from the access road, a pale finger of masonry rising from the flats before she had even stopped the truck, and she sat with the engine idling longer than she needed to, watching it through the windshield with both hands still on the wheel. The steeple had broken off in the flooding or been taken down before—she didn't know which, nobody had ever told her exactly how the drowning happened, how methodical or how chaotic, whether the families had stood on the ridge watching or simply left one morning with their furniture in rented trucks and not looked back. The base of the bell tower was what remained, an eight-sided column perhaps twenty feet high, and at this distance, with the morning light quartering across it, it looked less like a ruin than like something that had been placed there deliberately, a monument to the fact of its own survival.

She cut the engine and got out.

The reservoir had been dropping since March. By June the county water authority had issued three separate advisories about summer restrictions, and by August the surface had receded far enough that the old survey markers began to reappear along what had been the eastern shore—small iron posts she had read about in the historical records but never seen, their orange paint gone to rust, their concrete footings cracked and heaved by sixty winters of submersion. Her supervisor had called her into his office on a Tuesday and slid the work order across the desk without preamble, and she had looked at it for a moment before picking it up, and he had said something about historic preservation protocols and updated flood-plain data and she had nodded and said yes, she could start that week. He knew where she was from. Everyone in the county offices knew where she was from. Nobody mentioned it.

The lakebed close to the access road was still soft, and her boots sank slightly with each step, leaving precise impressions she could see when she turned around. Further out the ground firmed into a pale mineral crust, cracked into irregular polygons like a map of somewhere else, the surface whitened with dried algae and the calcium deposits left by decades of slow evaporation. She had been told to expect the smell, and there was a smell—low and silty and faintly organic, the smell of things that had been sealed away from air for a long time—but it was not as bad as she had feared. It was more geological than biological. It smelled like the inside of an old well.

She set her equipment case down and took out the total station, extending the tripod legs and leveling the instrument with the same deliberateness she brought to every setup, the ritual of adjustment a way of being present to the work rather than to everything else. She had been a surveyor for eleven years. She was good at the work in the particular way that people are good at things that require patience alongside precision, that require you to hold a question exactly still while you look for its answer. Her colleagues said she had an unusual spatial memory, that she could walk a site once and then draw its relationships from recall. She had never thought of this as a gift exactly, more as a habit of attention that had calcified into aptitude.

The GPS coordinates she had logged the night before told her that she was standing approximately forty meters north of what had been Callum Street, the main commercial road through the old town center. She looked south. The church tower. Beyond it, the rough geometry of foundations beginning to resolve from the pale flats—low walls, rectangular shadows, the occasional upright remnant. From this distance it looked like an archaeological site, which she supposed it was, in the sense that archaeology was just the science of things that people had stopped living in.

She had been four years old when her family left.

This was the information she held the most loosely, the way you learn to carry something fragile across a long distance—not clutching it, not setting it down, but keeping it in a certain relationship to your body so that if you stumble the damage is limited. She had one clear memory from before: the kitchen of the house on Alderman Road, yellow linoleum, a window above the sink through which she could see the neighbors' apple tree. The apple tree had been famous in a small-town way, a particular variety that nobody else grew in the valley, and her mother had made preserves from it every September. After the move they had driven back several times for the preserves and then at some point they had stopped and she had not thought about why until she was an adult, until she understood that at some point the water had covered the tree too.

She began walking south.

The total station could be operated remotely from her data collector, but for the first pass she liked to walk the site, to build a physical understanding of the terrain before she began recording it. This was technically unnecessary—the instrument didn't care whether she had walked the ground—but she had always believed that the map was better when the mapper had been present to the landscape as a body, not just as a point of measurement. Her graduate advisor had called this mysticism and she had called it practice and they had argued about it pleasantly for two years.

The church tower was larger than it had appeared from the road. Standing at its base she could see that the mortar between the stones had eroded badly, that there were gaps wide enough to put her hand into, and that someone—some team of someone, before her—had installed steel rods through the upper courses to prevent collapse. Precautionary stabilization; it would have been done by the preservation office. She had read the stabilization report. She had read everything she could find about the town during the weeks between the work order and this morning, and the reading had been a strange experience, like receiving intelligence about a country you have only dreamed about. Population 847 in the 1950 census, declining thereafter. Three churches, two schools, a mill that had closed in 1948. The valley had been selected for reservoir construction in 1958 and the condemnation proceedings had run for three years while the residents argued and then accepted and then argued again and then finally, mostly, left. The dam had closed in 1963. The valley had filled over the following eighteen months.

She put her hand against the stone.

The surface was cold and slightly damp, granular under her fingers, and she felt the weight of the tower above her hand, the sixty years of water that had passed through these walls and the sixty winters and the particular quality of silence that old stone holds, a silence that is not the absence of sound but the presence of all the sounds it has absorbed. She was not a sentimental person, or she had trained herself not to be, and she was aware that the sensation of significance she was feeling was partly manufactured by knowledge, that if she had come here without knowing whose town this was she would have felt something but probably not this specific thing, this feeling like a key being turned in a lock she hadn't known was locked.

She took her hand away and walked on.

The street revealed itself gradually. First the curbing—granite blocks, surprisingly intact, still holding their alignment along what had been the sidewalk edge—and then the surface itself, or what remained of it: a gray subsurface of compacted gravel and clay, the asphalt long since dissolved or carried off, but the bed beneath unmistakable, its profile still slightly crowned the way roads were crowned in that era to shed rain. She walked down the center of it and something in her body recognized the scale of the thing, the width between curbs, the distance between intersections, in the way that bodies remember scales they have inhabited before even when the mind has no access to the specific memory.

At the first intersection she stopped and took out her field notebook and began a sketch, blocking in the corners, the angles of the cross streets, the positions of the remaining walls. This was also technically redundant; the total station would capture all of this more accurately than her pencil. She did it anyway. The sketch forced a different quality of looking, forced her to commit to decisions about what she was seeing, and the act of putting a line on paper made the line real in a way that a data point did not, or did not for her.

The building on the northeast corner was the most complete structure she had seen so far. Three walls still standing to shoulder height, the fourth collapsed inward, and within the footprint a debris field of tumbled masonry and something that might have been wooden shelving, reduced to gray fiber, barely distinguishable from the silted floor. She stepped over the threshold—there was no door, no doorframe, just a gap in the wall where the entrance had been—and stood inside.

The floor was silt. Smooth and pale and printed with the passage of small animals, probably muskrats, their trails converging toward a corner where there might be a gap in the foundation. Overhead, nothing but sky, and the sky was the flat white of a September morning before the heat had fully arrived, and she stood in the ruined room with the sky above her and tried to think practically about what she was looking at, what she could record that would be useful, what the preservation office would need in terms of structural data, wall heights, material documentation. This was her job. She was here to do her job.

The room smelled of the lakebed and of something else, something she identified slowly as wood—not rotting wood exactly but the mineral ghost of wood, the memory of timber left in the calcium-rich sediment the way a shell leaves an impression in limestone. She thought about the shelving. If this had been a commercial building—and on the main street it probably was—there would have been shelves. There would have been a counter. There would have been someone behind the counter. She thought about small towns in the 1950s, the particular architecture of commerce in places where everyone knew everyone, where the transaction was also always a social event, where you did not simply purchase something but exchanged it with a person you would see at church on Sunday and whose children went to school with your children.

Her mother had been born here. Her father too, though he had come from the eastern end of the valley where there had been farms rather than houses, and his relationship to the town had been more formal, the relationship of someone who comes in from outside for specific purposes. Her mother had grown up on a street whose name Maren now knew from the records: Birch Street, two blocks north of where she was standing. She had played here as a child and gone to school here and met Maren's father here and been married in—Maren looked up through the absent roof of the building—in the church whose tower stood forty meters to the north. None of this was information her mother had given her. Her mother had not talked about the town with any specificity. It was the one category of knowledge her mother had consistently, quietly declined to pass on, and Maren had understood the refusal as grief and had not pressed it, and now her mother was three years dead and the town was re-emerging from its sixty-year submersion and here was Maren, the instrument in her hands, the data collector in her vest pocket, doing the thing that felt most like asking.

She left the building and continued down Callum Street.

At the next intersection a fire hydrant stood intact. This startled her, though she could not have said why she expected it to have been removed—presumably the infrastructure had simply been left, the pipes and hydrants and whatever utility housings lay underground, all of it abandoned to the water. The hydrant was painted the dark red of old iron through which no color has been maintained, and it stood perhaps two feet high with its cap fittings still in place, and it was so recognizably itself, so insistently a hydrant in the way that fundamental objects insist on their own identity regardless of context, that she found herself stopping and looking at it for longer than she needed to.

She photographed it. This too was redundant. She photographed it anyway.

The residential streets opened off Callum to the north and south, and she turned north first, toward Birch Street, and told herself she was doing this in a logical sequence, north before south, which was the sequence she had planned, which was true. The foundations here were smaller—house-sized rather than commercial-building-sized—and more numerous, set closer together as they would have been in a town where land was not especially expensive but was also not being wasted, where the houses had modest yards and the yards had gardens and the gardens probably had the kind of vegetables and herbs that people grew in that era, before the word organic had acquired its commercial meaning, when things were simply grown because you grew them.

She counted foundations. The street had held perhaps eighteen houses on each side, and she could find the footprints of most of them, some reduced to barely a change in the surface of the silt—a slight darkness, a rectangular shadow visible only in certain angles of light—and some still showing courses of stone foundation rising a foot or two above the lakebed floor. She walked the full length of the street from intersection to intersection, noting on her sketch the positions and conditions of each foundation in turn, and when she reached the far end and turned to look back along the street's length, she had the sudden vertiginous impression of seeing two images superimposed: the pale empty geography of the lakebed as it was, and something else, something she was constructing from the records she had read and the facts she knew, a layering of image over reality that her brain performed without her asking it to.

Birch Street in, say, 1955. The houses. The yards. The particular light of a September morning—the same light that was falling now, the same angle of sun, the same quality of air that precedes heat. A child playing in a yard. A woman at a door calling the child in. The apple tree in the neighboring yard, heavy with fruit.

She stood with her notebook in her hand and the pen not moving and allowed herself to feel this without trying to stop it. She had learned this at some point, that allowing something to move through you was not the same as being overwhelmed by it, that there was a technique of grief as there was a technique of measurement, and the technique of grief was mostly just stillness and time. She stood in the dried bed of the reservoir that had drowned her mother's childhood and her own brief time in this valley, and she was still, and the light moved slightly as a cloud passed somewhere overhead, and then she was all right again and she put the pen to the notebook and wrote down what she saw.

She found the house in the middle of the block, second from the eastern end of the street. She found it by the numbers, by the survey records she had studied, by the lot dimensions that she had memorized from the 1958 assessment documents. The foundation was among the more intact ones—four courses of flat-laid fieldstone, each course corbeled slightly inward, forming a rectangle perhaps thirty by forty feet, and within the rectangle the silt was smooth except for a slight depression at what would have been the center of the structure, where the floor had perhaps settled, where the weight of the collapsed house had compressed the sediment.

She stepped over the foundation wall.

The floor plan was there if you knew how to look for it. Not visible, exactly—there was nothing visible but silt—but inferable from the foundation's proportions and from what she knew of the houses of that period in this region, the way a floor plan was a type of grammar that generated recognizable sentences. Here the front room, wide and shallow, facing the street. Behind it the stairs, probably, and beside the stairs the kitchen, and in the kitchen the window above the sink, and outside the window the apple tree, which would have stood in the yard to the east.

She walked to where the kitchen would have been. She stood there.

She was a surveyor. She believed in what could be measured. She had spent eleven years learning to translate the physical world into numerical data and the numerical data into maps and the maps into something that other people could use to make decisions about the physical world, and she believed in this translation, believed in its fidelity and its usefulness and its particular kind of beauty, which was the beauty of precision, of the thing held still and described without distortion. She knew that she was standing in a field of silt and mineral crust on the dry bed of a reservoir. She knew that the house was gone and had been gone for sixty years and that her mother was gone and the apple tree was gone and the woman who had stood at this window making preserves was gone, and that none of what she was feeling changed any of those facts or added information to the record.

But she had also been a child in this kitchen. She had stood at this window—she could not have told you how she knew this, except that she did, she had always known it without knowing that she knew it, the way you know the grammar of your first language without knowing that you are knowing it. She had been lifted up to see out of this window when she was too small to see over the sill, and someone's hands had been under her arms, her mother's hands probably, and through the window there had been the apple tree and the light of some season—autumn, because that was when the apples came—the light and the tree and the weight of the apples in the branches, and then she had been set down again on the yellow linoleum floor and she had gone on being four years old, not knowing that she was in the last year before the water.

She did not try to hold it or release it. She let it be the size it was.

Then she took out the data collector and began recording.

She worked steadily through the afternoon, the shadow of the equipment case moving across the pale ground as the sun arced west, the total station capturing points in its tireless binary way, each measurement a small certainty accumulating toward a larger one. She mapped Callum Street and Birch Street and the three cross streets between them. She mapped the church tower's footprint and the remains of what the records identified as the schoolhouse, a single room of it still largely intact, the pine ceiling collapsed inward but the walls holding, the chalkboard—miraculously, absurdly—still on the east wall, the chalk marks long since dissolved but the green surface remaining, stained and pitted, legible as a chalkboard. She stood in front of it for a moment, as if expecting something to be written there.

By four o'clock she had covered the town center and the northern residential area and was working south of Callum toward what had been the mill. The light was changing from white to gold and the shadows of the foundation walls were lengthening across the silt, making the geometry of the old streets suddenly more visible, more articulate, as if the town was using the angle of late sun to argue for its own legibility. She had been here for eight hours and she was tired and her boots were dusty to the shin and she had eaten her lunch on the steps of the church tower and not thought about anything in particular while she ate it, which was the best she had been able to do.

At five she began breaking down the equipment, packing the total station into its case, capping the prism pole. The data collector held the day's work—several hundred registered points, the skeleton of the old town translated into coordinates, into a language that could be carried away from this place and processed and rendered into a map that would go into the county archives and the state historical database and probably nowhere else that anyone would ever look. She thought about this as she loaded the truck, about who the map was for. The preservation office. The water authority. Possibly a historical society somewhere, a graduate student writing a thesis. Possibly no one, or almost no one, which was not so different from the way most records ended up, most inscriptions—carved into something and then slowly dissolved by the water that moved over everything eventually.

She sat in the truck without starting the engine and looked at the church tower one more time. The gold light was on it now, direct and full, and the stone was warm in that light, warm-colored, and it looked for a moment less like a remnant than like a building in the process of becoming itself, mid-construction rather than mid-ruin. She knew this was a trick of light and tiredness and the particular wish of the person looking. She knew it anyway.

She started the engine. She would come back tomorrow, and the day after, until the mapping was finished. The water would come back too, eventually—they were already talking about diverting the river upstream, about filling programs, about the reservoir returning to operational capacity within two to five years pending precipitation. The town would be re-submerged. The foundations would go under again, and the church tower, and the fire hydrant, and the chalkboard, and all the empty footprints of all the absent rooms. The map she was making would be what remained, the translation of a translation, the memory of the shape of a place that had itself been the memory of a place.

She turned the truck around on the access road and drove toward the ridge.

Behind her, in the long evening light, the reservoir floor held its pale geometry for a little while longer—streets and walls and the shadows of streets and walls doubling them, making a kind of brief abundance from the specific fact of absence—and then the sun dropped and the light flattened and the old town was just a field again, just the mineral remains of somewhere water had been, waiting with the patience of stone and silt for whatever came next.
