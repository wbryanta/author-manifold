The road out to Hester's farm had always been the worst of it, a dirt track that turned to paste in wet weather and to corrugation in dry, and Henry Engel drove it now in second gear with both hands on the wheel as though the truck itself required coaxing, as though it were old and uncertain the way he was old and uncertain. The tuning kit rode in the passenger seat. He had stopped belting it in years ago. There was something companionable about it there beside him, the black case worn soft at the corners, the felt strips and mutes rolled into their leather wallet, the electronic tuner he had bought in 1987 and still preferred to the app his daughter had shown him on her phone. She had demonstrated it at Christmas, holding the phone above the Steinway in her living room, and the phone had blushed green for each correct pitch, and Henry had said that was very clever. He had not told her that what the phone could not hear was everything else — the stiffness in a hammer shank, a unison string gone false not in pitch but in tone, the way a bass note in an old upright would bloom differently in November than in July because the whole instrument breathed with the house around it, contracting and expanding like a lung.

He pulled into Hester Markle's drive and sat for a moment with the engine idling. The farmhouse was the same as always — white paint that had been going gray since the eighties, a porch that listed slightly to the east, a wind chime made of horseshoes that rang in a key approximating B-flat. Hester came out before he had the engine off. She was eighty-three now, two years older than Henry himself, and she moved across the yard with the particular deliberate care of someone who has survived enough falls to regard the ground as an adversary.

He tuned her upright in the parlor while she made coffee he wouldn't drink but would accept because refusing it would hurt her. The piano was a Kimball from 1951, bought for her daughter Renata who had taken lessons for three years before deciding she preferred horses. Renata lived in Phoenix now. The piano had not been played in thirty years and possibly longer, though Hester sometimes touched the keys, Henry suspected, not to make music but to make sound, to confirm the room was still capable of producing something other than silence. Several keys had gone yellowish, the ivories shrinking from their edges, and the action on the upper register was stiff in the way of mechanisms that have been asked to do nothing for too long.

He worked methodically, as he always worked, beginning with the temperament octave in the middle of the keyboard and expanding outward, the tuning hammer turning in small increments while he struck each key and listened. There was a pleasure in this work that had never diminished. It was not the pleasure of performance, which he had never wanted — he had been a fair pianist as a young man, competent and conscientious, but he had understood early that competence and conscientiousness were not what audiences came to hear. What he had wanted was proximity to the instrument itself, the interior logic of it, the way a piano was essentially an argument about mathematics and wood and tension, and a good tuning was when all sides of the argument agreed. His father had been a tuner before him, and his father's father, and there was a village in the Rhineland his grandfather had described where the Engel men were known for this work the way other men were known for coopering or smithing, a tradition of the ear passed down like a characteristic nose.

Hester sat in the kitchen and called through the door occasionally. She asked about his hip. He said it was better. She asked about his daughter in Sacramento. He said she was fine, the children were fine. She asked if he was still driving all the way out to the Whitfield place, and he said he was, though he supposed it would be the last time.

That was the thing about today. He had not told Hester, had not told any of them, because there was nothing to say that wouldn't make it strange. He had simply decided, sometime in the fall, when the hip had been bad and the mornings had been very hard, that this would be the last round. He was eighty-one years old and had been tuning pianos in this county for fifty-three years, and it was time. He had no replacement, no apprentice, because no one in fifty-three years had expressed the slightest interest in the work, or not in the way that would have made a real apprentice. His daughter played the piano decently. His son had played beautifully. The county's pianos, after Henry, would go untuned, or they would find someone from the city who came out twice a year and charged accordingly, or they would be sold or given away or left in houses that were themselves eventually left. He found that he could think about this without grief, which surprised him. He had expected grief and found instead something closer to acceptance, which was either wisdom or the diminishment of feeling and he was no longer sure there was always a difference.

The Kimball came back slowly, the way old pianos did when they had drifted over years rather than months — there was a resilience to long-term detuning, a stubbornness in the strings, as though pitch were a habit the instrument had abandoned and now resisted resuming. He coaxed it. He was patient. By the time he reached the high treble the sound in the room had changed in a way that Hester would not consciously notice but that would change how she felt sitting in the parlor, the way correct proportions in a room affect you without your being able to name them.

She paid him with a check and a bag of apples from the tree at the back of the property. He took both. He put the apples on top of the tuning kit in the passenger seat and drove back out the corrugated track and turned east on the county road toward the Whitfield place.

The Whitfield place had been the Whitfields' for as long as anyone could remember and now was no one's in particular. The last Whitfield, old Dorcas, had died four years ago and left the house to her nephew in Columbus, who rented it to a series of tenants who always left after one winter because the heating was poor. Currently it was empty. The nephew had called Henry each spring regardless, and Henry had come each spring regardless, because there was a Bösendorfer in the front room that had been Dorcas Whitfield's grandmother's, brought overland from Cincinnati in 1889, and Henry felt an obligation to it that he would have had difficulty explaining. Dorcas had played it beautifully, a completely self-taught player who had somehow, through will and instinct, arrived at a genuine musicianship. She played Schubert mostly, and some Brahms, and occasionally she would call Henry and ask him to come out not for a tuning but simply to listen while she played the slow movement of something and then tell her what he thought. He had always said it was beautiful because it was always beautiful. After she died he had continued to tune the Bösendorfer because to stop felt like a form of abandonment he wasn't capable of.

The nephew didn't know about the tuning visits. Henry had not billed anyone for the Whitfield house in three years.

The house was cold inside, the heat turned down to whatever minimum kept the pipes from freezing, and Henry worked in his coat. The Bösendorfer was a full 97-key model, massive and dark in the light that came through windows that needed washing, and even deprived of heat and regular care it retained what Henry thought of as its character — a certain gravity in the bass, a warmth in the middle register, a treble that was clear without being harsh. He had read once that Bösendorfer used spruce from a forest in Austria that had been managed for instrument-making for three hundred years, and he believed it, because the wood in this piano seemed to have an intelligence the lesser instruments didn't have, a willingness to cooperate with the tuner that lesser instruments withheld.

He worked for two hours. The cold made his hands slow and he stopped twice to warm them in his coat pockets, standing in the middle of the room looking at nothing in particular while the blood came back. On the walls were photographs of the Whitfield family in chronological recession — color photographs near the door where it was easy to hang things, black and white photographs further in, and at the far end of the parlor a daguerreotype in an oval frame of a woman he had always assumed was the one who had brought the piano from Cincinnati. She had a direct and unembarrassed gaze that he admired. He wondered if she had played it well.

When the Bösendorfer was finished he played a single chord in the middle register, a simple major chord, D major, and let it die in the cold room. Then he closed the lid over the keys, which he always did at the Whitfield house because there was no one to play it between visits and because he felt the closed lid was a kind of courtesy to the instrument, a preservation of some interior warmth. He let himself out and locked the door with the key the nephew sent him each spring along with a check, and sat in the truck a moment before starting it.

There had been a dog at the Whitfield place for all the years he had been coming, a succession of dogs actually, all of them large and friendly, and for a moment he almost missed it — the expectation of being met. He had been met everywhere, once. Dorcas at the door. His wife's voice from the kitchen when he came home. His son Martin at the screen door of the house on Calloway Street, the summer Martin was nine or ten, pressing his face against the screen to make his features strange and funny. This was what eighty-one felt like, he had found: not so much loss as the accumulated recognition of loss, the understanding that the things you noticed the absence of had been present for years without your fully appreciating them, and now you were left with a kind of retroactive attention that was both gift and punishment.

He drove to the church.

The church was at the edge of town, which had never been much of a town — a grain elevator, a post office, a diner called Myra's that was now a tax preparation office, a hardware store that persisted against all probability. The church was Lutheran and white and sat on a small rise above the road, surrounded by oaks that Henry's father had helped plant in the 1940s as part of some civic project whose memory had entirely evaporated. The oaks were enormous now, holding the last of October's leaves in russets and yellows that caught the afternoon light, and in the truck Henry sat for a moment watching the light in those leaves before getting out.

The church's piano was a Baldwin Hamilton studio upright that had been there for as long as Henry had been tuning it, which was the full fifty-three years. It occupied the alcove to the left of the altar, slightly recessed, and it was used for Sunday services and for the occasional funeral and wedding and for the children's choir that practiced on Thursdays, though there were fewer children in the choir every year and Henry suspected that within a decade the choir would be something the congregation remembered rather than something it had.

Pastor Elaine met him at the door. She was sixty-something, the third pastor since old Reverend Thunemann, who had been the pastor when Henry had started tuning the church's piano and who had been the pastor for the recital. She was wearing a sweater with a hole at the elbow and reading glasses pushed up on her forehead. He liked her. She was unsentimental in her kindness, which was the best kind.

He told her this would be his last visit. He said it simply, the way he had not been able to say it to Hester. She looked at him for a moment and then she said she was sorry to hear it and she meant it, he could tell, and there was no performance of sadness in it, which was why he had been able to tell her.

She left him to the piano and went back to whatever she had been doing in the office down the corridor, and Henry set the tuning kit on the pew nearest the alcove and opened the Baldwin's lid and looked at the strings.

The Baldwin was in reasonable condition. The church kept the heat more consistent than a farmhouse or an unoccupied estate, and the piano was used regularly enough that it didn't sink into the detuning that came from long disuse. It needed what it always needed: a little settling in the bass, a few unisons in the middle register that had come apart, the high treble finicky the way high trebles always were. He began to work.

The light coming through the windows of the church was the stained-glass light of late afternoon, reds and blues thrown in oblongs across the floorboards and the face of the piano and his own hands. He had tuned in this light dozens of times. He knew how the acoustics of the church changed when the pews were full versus empty, how in summer the wood expanded and the action sat slightly heavier, how in winter the sound was drier and traveled differently. He knew this building the way you knew things you had returned to repeatedly over a long life — not with conscious knowledge but with the knowledge that lives in the body, in the hands, in the adjustment you made without deciding to make it.

The recital had been on a Saturday in April, which meant the windows would have been admitting that particular spring light, the pale yellowish light of an April morning before the sun was fully warm. Martin would have been twelve. Henry could see him now with the kind of clarity that older memories sometimes achieve, better than recent ones: Martin in a white shirt that was slightly too large because Henry's wife had bought it a size up for growing room, Martin's dark hair which had been cut that week and still showed the line of the cut at the back of his neck, the particular way Martin walked when he was nervous, a slight forward lean as though he were heading into a wind.

He had been six weeks out from the diagnosis when he gave the recital. They had known, by April, what they were dealing with, though they had not yet arrived at the particular geography of it, the stages and timelines, the vocabulary that medical language provides, which is both terrible and useful because it gives the mind something to do. Martin had wanted to play. The piano teacher, Mrs. Cavanaugh, had suggested perhaps the recital should be postponed, and Martin had said no with a firmness that surprised everyone including Henry, who knew his son as gentle and accommodating, a boy who resolved conflicts by finding the position where everyone could be comfortable. But about this he had been firm.

Henry remembered sitting in the fourth pew. He remembered the cold of his wife Dorothy's hand in his, though it was April and not cold, but that was how he remembered it. He remembered the other children who played before Martin — a girl with red braids who played a simplified Bach minuet very correctly, a heavy boy whose name he had forgotten who played something by Clementi with great energy and little accuracy — and he remembered the way the room felt when Martin sat down at the Baldwin.

There was a thing that happened sometimes with gifted players, even young ones, even ones playing in a small Lutheran church before an audience of forty people, which was that the room changed when they began. It was not a mystical thing. Henry had thought about it over the years and decided it was attention — the gifted player asked for your attention in a way that the competent player did not, and the room's quality shifted when that attention was given, the way a crowd shifts when something genuine is about to happen. Martin played the Schubert Impromptu in G-flat, the one from Opus 90, the one that begins with that rocking figuration in the left hand and opens into a melody of such simple rightness that it seems impossible anyone had to invent it. Mrs. Cavanaugh had said it was too difficult. Martin had said he wanted to play it. He had learned it in six weeks, which should not have been possible, and he played it from memory, and he played it — Henry had thought this at the time and had returned to the thought many times afterward — with an understanding of what the music was about that seemed to go beyond what a twelve-year-old could understand, unless what twelve-year-olds understood was different from what adults thought, unless there were things that age and experience obscured rather than revealed.

He played it without a mistake. He played it whole.

Henry worked his way up through the Baldwin's treble register now, in the reddish and blue afternoon light, and felt the church hold its silence around him, and did not try to do anything with what he felt. That was something that age had given him — the ability to feel things without the need to resolve them, to let grief and gratitude sit alongside each other without requiring that one defeat the other. Martin had died that August, seven months after the diagnosis, in the hospital in Millbridge, with Henry and Dorothy on either side of him. Dorothy had died eleven years ago. Henry still heard her voice sometimes, not as hallucination but as memory so vivid it had a kind of presence, and he had decided that was all right, that presence had different forms and you took the ones you were given.

What he had never been able to explain to anyone, including himself, was why he had continued to tune this particular piano. He told himself it was the church, the history, the fact that it was the last stop on the route and had always been. But he knew it was also this: that the Baldwin held something. Not literally. He was not a man given to metaphysical claims about objects. But there was a way in which the work of tuning this piano, of sitting in this alcove and listening to these strings and bringing them into their right relationships, was a form of return. A way of placing himself again in the fourth pew, Dorothy's cold hand in his, the afternoon light not stained glass but April, pale yellow, and Martin leaning slightly forward as though into a wind, taking his seat.

He finished the treble and played a scale slowly from one end of the keyboard to the other, and then the Schubert. Not all of it — he was not a performer and never had been — but the opening figuration in the left hand and then the melody, finding it in the right hand with the careful deliberateness of a man who has not played much in years. It came back to him. The piano rang in the empty church, the sound moving up into the vaulted ceiling and returning changed, fuller, the way sound in a good room gave back more than it received.

He played until the end of the first section and then stopped, his hands resting on the keys.

Outside, wind moved through the oaks, and he could hear it even here, a sound continuous and patient like breathing, like an organ note held so long it becomes part of the air. He had not cried at the recital. He had not cried at the hospital or the funeral or any of the occasions that people designate for grief. He had cried once, alone in this room, some years later, tuning the Baldwin on a winter morning when the light was flat and gray and the church had been cold and smell of old wood and candle wax. He had sat on the bench and cried in a way that was not dramatic and not particularly coherent and was not trying to be anything except what it was, and then he had stopped and gone back to work.

He closed the lid over the keys. He rolled the felt strips back into the leather wallet and closed the latches on the case and lifted it from the pew. He stood for a moment in the alcove, in the last of the colored light, which was going as the sun moved toward the tree line. Then he walked down the center aisle between the pews and through the vestibule and out the door into the October air, which was cold and clear and smelled of leaves breaking down in the way that October air always did, that sweet decomposition, and he stood on the steps for a moment and breathed it.

He put the tuning kit in the passenger seat. He put the apples from Hester Markle's tree beside it. He sat for a moment looking through the windshield at the oaks, which were losing their light, the last red and gold going gray.

He started the truck. The engine turned over with its familiar reluctance and then caught, and he sat a moment more with his hands on the wheel. He was eighty-one years old and it was the last day of his route and the oaks were the oaks his father had helped plant and the road home was a road he had driven for fifty-three years and he knew every curve of it, every place where the county had patched the asphalt and every place it hadn't, every farm he passed and the name of the family and whether there was a piano and whether anyone played it.

He put the truck in gear. He drove.
