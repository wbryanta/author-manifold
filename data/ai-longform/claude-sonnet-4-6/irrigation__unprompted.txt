The pipe had split along its seam sometime in the night, and by morning the upper grove was already showing the pale, desperate color of thirst—the leaves of the older trees turned slightly inward, as though each one were listening for something it couldn't hear. Martin found it just after six, walking the line the way their father had taught them both, following the flex hose from the pump house up through the apricots and into the first stretch of pears, looking for the wet place in the dry ground. He found it thirty yards in, the soil dark and spreading in a fan shape that would have been beautiful if it weren't wasteful, if there weren't a whole season riding on every gallon they pulled from the aquifer.

He stood over it for a moment, hands in his pockets, then went to get Owen.

Owen was at the kitchen table with a cup of coffee and the county assessor's letter that had been sitting on the counter for eleven days, the same letter Martin had been moving from the counter to the table and back again without opening, as though placement might change its contents. Owen had opened it. It lay flat between his forearms, and he was reading it as someone reads a thing they have already read several times, with a kind of stern and searching attention, looking for somewhere the language might give way.

"Pipe's split in the pear block," Martin said.

Owen looked up. He was thirty-four, two years younger than Martin, and he had their mother's eyes, which were gray and direct and which had the quality of mirrors turned slightly away from you—you could never quite see yourself in them but you were always aware that they were there. He looked at Martin for a moment and then down at the letter and then folded it carefully along its original creases and put it in the breast pocket of his shirt.

"How bad," he said.

"Thirty, forty feet of line, maybe. It's the old PVC. It was going to go."

"Everything was going to go," Owen said, but he stood up.

They loaded the truck from the barn—new pipe that Martin had bought in April on a credit account with the hardware store in Merced, primer, cement, the coupler fittings, the hacksaw and the utility knife and the chamfer tool. The morning was already warm in the way that June mornings in the valley are warm, which is not gently but with a kind of directness, the sun laying its hand flat against the back of your neck. They drove the truck up the service road as far as the road went and then carried everything the rest of the way on foot.

Neither of them spoke until they'd found the split and knelt over it together, and then it was only Owen saying hold on, let me see, and Martin moving aside.

Owen ran his thumb along the crack. It was a clean separation, the pipe having given at exactly the weakest point, which meant it would give again elsewhere, and they both understood this without saying so. The PVC was fifteen years old at least, some of it original to when their father had put in this block in the late nineties, and it was brittle now in the way that old plastic gets brittle, no longer willing to flex, wanting only to stay in exactly the position it had held for years and cracking along its length when the ground shifted beneath it or when the water pressure spiked in a dry season when you were pulling the pump harder than you should.

"We should cut back about two feet on each side," Martin said. "Make sure we're not splicing into something that's about to go on us."

"I know how to do it, Martin."

"I know you know."

Owen picked up the hacksaw and set the blade against the pipe four inches from the crack and began to cut. Martin held the pipe steady from the other side so it wouldn't torque. The saw made its small, repetitive complaint against the valley quiet—which was not truly quiet but layered, the pump running below them and a tractor somewhere on the Guadalupe ranch to the west and a red-tailed hawk making its sound above the tree line.

"You read it," Martin said. It was not a question.

"I read it."

"And?"

"And it says what the envelope says it says. Assessment. They've done the whole parcel. They'll have the final numbers by July fifteen."

Martin passed him the utility knife when he needed it for the burrs. "Those numbers aren't final."

"They're the county's numbers."

"The county's numbers are based on comparable sales, and the comparables they're using are all east-side properties that sold to the almond operations. That's not a fair comparison for this land."

"Maybe not," Owen said. "But it's the comparison they're making."

The cut section came free and Owen set it aside. He measured the replacement piece against it, marked it, cut it. His hands moved with the economy of someone who had done this work since boyhood, who had come up repairing things rather than replacing them, and there was something in that economy that made Martin want to say something about it, though he couldn't have said what.

They worked in silence for a time, dry-fitting the new section, checking the alignment. The sun moved. A woodpecker knocked at something in the far pears, three deliberate strokes and then silence.

"I talked to the Farber group again," Owen said.

Martin set down the primer. "When."

"Thursday. Kyle Farber called me. He called me, Martin, I didn't go to him."

"You should have told me."

"I'm telling you now."

"What did he say."

Owen sat back on his heels. He was squinting slightly in the direction of the trees, looking at them with an expression Martin recognized—it was the expression their father had worn when he was calculating something, when he was holding several futures in his mind simultaneously and feeling the weight of each. "He said the offer's still on the table. He said they're getting competitive interest in the area and they can't hold it past the first of August, but it's still there if we want it."

"We don't want it."

"You don't want it."

"Owen—"

"I'm allowed to want something different from what you want. I'm allowed to have a different assessment of the situation."

Martin pressed his palms to his thighs and looked up at the canopy. The pears above them were in decent shape—they'd had good fruit set in the spring, the thinning had gone well, and if the water held and the heat didn't spike in the wrong weeks they were looking at a fair crop, maybe even a good one. The apricots below were less certain. Apricots were always less certain. Their father had planted them because he loved the fruit, not because they were sensible, and you could say about the old man's farming in general that he had always made decisions with the heart of a man who planned to live forever and tended them with the hands of a man who understood that he might not.

"Tell me your assessment," Martin said. He meant it plainly, without sarcasm, and Owen seemed to hear it that way.

"My assessment is that we are in the third year of operating at a loss. My assessment is that the aquifer is down and the cost of pumping is going to keep going up and the price of pears has not moved in real terms since 2014. My assessment is that we have eighty-six thousand dollars of outstanding debt on this property and that the Farber offer—" Owen paused, then said it quietly, "—the Farber offer would settle everything and leave us each with something real. Something we could use."

"Use for what."

"For living, Martin. For living our actual lives."

Martin picked the primer back up and began brushing it onto the cut ends of the old pipe. The solvent smell rose sharp and petroleum-sweet in the morning heat.

"This is our actual life," he said.

"This is your actual life. I have—I've got Claire's situation with her mother, and we want to have a kid, we've been trying to have a kid for two years and there's a doctor in San Francisco who her OB wants her to see and it costs—" He stopped. "It costs what it costs, and right now I don't have it."

Martin hadn't known about the doctor. He turned to look at his brother and found Owen looking at the ground, his jaw set with the particular tightness that meant he had not meant to say as much as he had said. There was between them, as between all brothers who have worked the same land since childhood, a vast and complicated archive of the other's expressions—Martin could have identified that jaw-set in a photograph taken a hundred yards away.

"I didn't know that," Martin said.

"It's private."

"I understand that. I didn't know."

Owen shrugged, which was not dismissal but a kind of self-protection—the shrug meant the subject was still present but was not going to be examined any further in this particular light. "Hand me the cement," he said.

Martin handed it over and Owen applied it to the fitting, then to the pipe ends, and slid the coupling home and held it. They sat in the dry heat with the smell of the cement hardening around them, not talking. A ground squirrel appeared at the edge of the row and regarded them for a moment with the complete absence of affect that ground squirrels have, and then disappeared.

"Dad never should have planted the apricots," Martin said finally.

"No."

"He knew the margin was thin."

"He knew. He planted them anyway because he thought—I don't know what he thought."

"He thought he had more time."

Owen turned the fitting a quarter-turn to seat it. "He thought the land would take care of him the way he'd taken care of it. He thought the whole business was more reciprocal than it is."

"Maybe it was once," Martin said. "When water cost what it cost twenty years ago. When you could still get harvest labor at the rate you could get it."

"Maybe." Owen sat back and looked at what they'd done. "Give it a minute."

They gave it a minute. Above them the pear trees stood in their rows with the patience that trees have, which is absolute and which has nothing to do with hope.

"If we sold it," Martin said—and even beginning the sentence cost him something, he could feel it in his chest, a small structural failure—"what do you think happens to it."

"Farber converts to almonds almost certainly. Maybe pistachios if the water projection for this part of the basin keeps going the way it's going. They'd pull all of this out—" He made a gesture that took in the rows around them. "They'd pull it out and put in the wire and the drip system and the shell crackers and the whole operation."

"There wouldn't be a tree left."

"There wouldn't be a tree left," Owen confirmed.

Martin nodded. He had known this. He had known it as a kind of abstract fact and now it was less abstract, the way all facts become less abstract when you are sitting in the thing itself, in the actual June morning of it, with the actual trees around you and the actual hacksaw in your hand. He thought about the pear trees—the older ones, the ones his father had planted in the late eighties when Martin was a toddler, which meant they were close to forty years old now and still bearing, still reliable in a way that nothing else on the property was reliable, the oldest of them with bark as thick and furrowed as the hide of something prehistoric, their roots so deep in the valley floor that you could sometimes feel, standing next to them, a kind of gravity that was different from ordinary gravity, a pull not down but inward.

"Dad planted those d'Anjous the year I was born," Martin said.

"I know he did."

"I've never not known those trees."

Owen looked at him. The gray eyes were careful. "I know that," he said. "I know what that means. I grew up here too, Martin."

"I'm not saying you didn't."

"You're implying I don't feel it."

"I'm not—I'm just saying I don't know how you separate from it. I don't know where you even begin to do that."

"You begin," Owen said, "where the bank statement begins. You begin where the assessment begins. You begin where Claire's sitting in a doctor's office and I'm watching the number in the account go the wrong direction and I'm thinking about how many more years of this there are before there's nothing left to sell." His voice had not risen. It had actually gone quieter, which was more unsettling. "You begin where reality begins. That's where you begin."

Martin stood up. He walked a few paces down the row, past the fitting, past the wet dark soil that was already beginning to dry at its edges, toward the older pears. He put his hand on the bark of one of them—it was his habit, it had always been his habit, it was something he did without thought the way other men touched wood for luck or cupped their hands around a flame. The bark was warm from the sun and rough in a way that was specific and unrepeatable, that was different from the bark of the tree beside it, each tree having a texture as individual as a face.

He could hear Owen stand up behind him.

"I'm not asking you to not feel it," Owen said. "I'm asking you to think about what we have left to give it."

"We've got this season—"

"And if this season goes the way the last three went—"

"It won't. The fruit set is good, Owen, you saw it yourself. If we get a fair price at the shed and we keep the water on and we bring in—"

"And the pump fails. And the pipe splits. And the packing shed takes another twenty percent off the top because they can, because there's no one else to take your fruit to, because the cooperative dissolved and you and I and three other families are all that's left between here and the Guadalupe and the shed knows it."

"One fair season," Martin said.

"You said that last year."

"And last year the heat event in August took thirty percent of the crop. That doesn't happen every year."

"It's happening more often," Owen said. "That's the whole point. It's happening more often and it's going to keep happening more often and the water's going to keep costing more and the trees are going to keep getting older and at a certain point the thing you love stops being a farm and starts being an obligation you can't meet."

Martin turned around. His brother was standing at the end of the row with his arms at his sides, and behind him, through the break in the canopy, you could see the lower orchard and beyond it the flat of the valley floor going out toward the gray-green hills that were already cured to summer, and beyond those hills the suggestion of the mountains, white and enormous and indifferent. It was beautiful. It was the particular beauty of working land that is also failing land, which is not an ironic beauty but a genuine and mournful one, the kind that accumulates in a place over decades and cannot be relocated.

"Give me the summer," Martin said.

"What does that mean."

"It means give me the summer. August. Let's see what August looks like. If this crop comes in where I think it can come in, we talk again in September. If it doesn't—" He paused. He had not said this before, had not thought it in quite this shape, and saying it made it real in a way he hadn't anticipated. "If it doesn't come in, we call Farber."

Owen looked at him for a long time. The woodpecker knocked its three knocks again from somewhere in the canopy.

"You mean that," Owen said.

"I mean it."

"You'll actually call him."

"If August goes wrong. Yes."

Owen looked away, toward the valley, toward the hills. "Claire needs an answer by fall," he said. "About the doctor."

"I understand."

"If I come back to you in September and the numbers are what they're going to be, you won't—you won't make this into something it's not, Martin. You won't make me into the one who sold the farm."

The phrase arrived between them with its full weight, because they both knew it was a phrase their father would have recognized, would have used, had used—you don't sell the farm, it was the great and irrational commandment of the family, the commandment that had outlasted the man who'd given it, that lived now in the land itself, or in their idea of the land, which was possibly the same thing.

"No," Martin said. "I won't do that."

Owen nodded slowly, once, the way men nod when they are agreeing to something they know will cost them, that has already cost them in the anticipation of it.

They went back to the fitting and Martin turned on the line at the head valve, a quarter-turn at first, listening, then more, and they walked the replaced section together watching for seepage and there was none. The water moved through the new pipe and out through the emitters and into the root zones of the trees that had been thirsty since the night before, and the soil around each emitter darkened in its small circle, and the circles were at first discrete and then gradually widened toward each other and almost, though not quite, met.

They gathered their tools. Martin coiled the leftover pipe over his shoulder and Owen carried the bag with the fittings and the cement and they walked back down through the apricots, which were smaller trees and lower to the ground and which were carrying their fruit now—still green and hard, weeks from the color and sweetness that the old man had planted them for, that impractical sweetness, that sweetness that had nothing to do with margin. Martin brushed his hand through the leaves as he passed, not stopping, just trailing his fingers through, and a few of the fruits touched his palm and were warm from the sun and smooth in the way that apricots are smooth, which is unlike the smoothness of anything else.

"They look decent," Owen said, from behind him.

"They look decent," Martin agreed.

They loaded the truck in silence and Martin drove them back down the service road toward the house. At the bottom of the road where it joined the county lane there was a place where the valley opened up and you could see the full extent of the orchard from the outside, the rows of pears going up the gentle slope and the tighter rows of apricots below them, the whole fifty-three acres of it, and Martin stopped there for a moment as he always stopped, as their father had always stopped, the truck idling in the lane.

Owen sat beside him and did not say anything. The assessor's letter was in his breast pocket and between them on the seat was the hacksaw and the utility knife and the chamfer tool and the smell of PVC solvent and June heat and the particular exhausted hopefulness of men who have fixed a thing they know will break again, who have chosen to fix it anyway, who will fix it again when it does.

The trees stood in their rows.

Martin put the truck in gear.
