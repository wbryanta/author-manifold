Anil crouched at the edge of the grave and looked at the two men lying as they had fallen, or as they had been placed. There was always the question of placement. A body thrown or a body laid down carried different information, and that information was everything. She had learned to read the angles of bones the way a cartographer reads coastlines—with patience, with the understanding that the map is not the territory but is all you have to navigate by.

She was thirty-three years old and had spent the last seven years working in Guatemala, in Cambodia, in Sierra Leone. She had the face her mother had given her and the hands her work had made, and she kept the two selves that these implied as separate as she could manage. In her field notes she was precise, economical, a scientist's scientist. In the residue of herself that remained after field notes she was something more difficult to name. She ate little. She slept badly. She read novels during the hours before dawn when sleep became impossible, reading the same pages repeatedly without absorbing them, grateful simply for the fact of other voices.

She had come to Guatemala on a fellowship from the International Centre for Human Rights Investigations in Geneva, which was to say she had come as a kind of emissary, carrying the organization's mandate and its careful neutral language. She had come, more precisely, because a man named Claro Victorio had written her a letter explaining that his organization had been unable to identify the remains of eleven men recovered from a site in the western highlands and that they needed an outside specialist. The letter had been formal, bureaucratic, full of the language of protocols and mandates. At the bottom, in a different ink, as if added later or in a different state of mind, he had written: We have been looking at these men for four months and we cannot give them back to anyone.

She had arrived in March and it was now late May and the heat had grown into something physical, something you moved through rather than experienced. The site was in a shallow valley between two ridges, accessible only by a dirt road that became impassable in heavy rain, and on either side of the road the jungle asserted itself with a patience and totality that felt like argument. Every morning she drove the four-wheel drive from the town of Chimaltenango, a forty-minute journey that she had come to need the way she needed the first coffee of the day—as a preparatory ritual, a space between what she was and what the work required her to be.

There were four of them on the team. Besides Anil there was Claro himself, who was forty-five and had been trained as a physician and somewhere along the way had become something else, something for which there was no precise title. He carried the weight of the work differently than she did. He carried it visibly, in his shoulders, in the way he lowered himself to the ground at the end of each day, sitting with his back against the vehicle and not speaking for long minutes before he could make conversation. There was also a young woman named Laurana who was finishing a degree in forensic anthropology at a university in Guatemala City and who was meticulous and unreadable in the way of certain very intelligent young people, as if she was reserving judgment on everything until she had accumulated sufficient evidence. And there was a man named Hector who drove and managed the equipment and who had lost his own brother in a disappearance eleven years earlier and who never spoke of it and whose presence at these excavations Anil understood to be a form of penance or witness that she could not fully comprehend but deeply respected.

The two bodies in the grave had been designated Specimens J-7 and J-8 in the site log, the seventh and eighth of eleven recovered from this location. They were adult males. One had been shot once in the back of the skull. The other's bones showed perimortem fractures consistent with severe beating and the skeletal evidence suggested he had been alive, or near alive, when buried. This was the information Anil had spent four weeks assembling, and it was complete information of its kind, and it was almost nothing.

Identification required more. Identification required family. It required the woman who had just climbed out of the grave and was now standing a short distance away with her hands folded over her stomach, watching, and it required the specific biological data that would allow Anil to say: yes, this particular configuration of bone, this healed fracture on the left fibula, this dental structure, corresponds to the man you knew, the man who came in from the fields and ate the lunch you made and slept for an hour in the afternoon.

She had not yet been able to make that correspondence. The families of specimens J-7 and J-8 were unknown to her.

Claro came and stood beside her. He smelled of cigarettes and the particular soap they sold in the market in Chimaltenango, a yellow soap that smelled of something between lemons and machinery.

Let her stay if she wants, he said quietly.

She's already moved back.

I mean while we work. Some of them want to stay and I have been sending them to the shade and I don't think that's right.

Anil looked across at the woman, who had sat down on a large flat stone twenty yards from the grave and had taken something from her pocket, a small cloth or folded paper, and was looking at it.

No, Anil said. I don't think it's right either.

They worked through the morning in the way they had worked through all the mornings, carefully, with brushes and small picks, with measuring tape and with the camera that Laurana operated with a methodical patience, photographing each stage of the process from multiple angles. The sun rose above the ridge and the heat arrived. Hector unloaded the equipment from the vehicle and set up the shade structure and boiled water on the small gas stove. The woman on the stone did not move. Once Anil looked up and their eyes met across the distance of the excavation site and neither of them looked away for a moment, and Anil felt something she recognized as kinship of a kind that had nothing to do with similarity of experience and everything to do with the specific loneliness of waiting for information that may never come.

She had not always known how to be in this proximity to grief. In her early years she had managed the distance badly, overcorrecting in both directions—sometimes too clinical, armored against the human fact of what she was looking at, sometimes ambushed by it, sitting in her vehicle after the work day and not able to drive. A woman she had worked with in her first year, a South African named Tesseba van der Merwe who had spent fifteen years at these kinds of sites and who had the manner of someone who had been remade by difficult knowledge into a more useful form, had taken Anil for a walk one evening along the shore of a lake near the camp where they were staying and had said to her, simply: you have to find the part of you that can hold it and carry it without being consumed. Not a wall. A container. Those are different things.

Anil had spent several years not knowing how to accomplish this and she was not certain she had accomplished it now, but she had found, at least, that the work itself helped. The precision of it. The requirement to be exact, to keep the language clean and specific, to say what you could prove and not more than you could prove. There was something stabilizing in that discipline. It gave the days a structure inside which the other thing, the human thing, could exist without overwhelming the work or being overwhelmed by it.

By mid-afternoon they had removed and catalogued the remains of both men, and Anil was satisfied that she had the measurements and photographs and samples she needed to complete her analysis. She stood and stretched her back and looked up at the ridge and then across at the woman, who was still on the stone, who had not left.

Claro approached the woman and spoke to her in Spanish, too quietly for Anil to hear. The woman looked up at him and then looked past him at Anil and said something that Anil also couldn't hear. Claro came back.

She wants to know how long.

Before we know if it's her husband.

Yes.

Anil thought about the samples she had taken, the process she would need to run, the comparisons she would need to make. She thought about the database of missing persons that Claro's organization maintained, about the family members they had already interviewed and the biological reference data they had collected.

Two weeks, she said. Maybe ten days if everything goes well.

He went back and told her and the woman received this information without expression, which was itself an expression, and then she stood up and folded whatever she had been holding back into her pocket and walked past the grave without looking at it and continued down the road toward the valley until Anil could no longer see her.

That evening Anil drove back to Chimaltenango and ate at the small restaurant near the market where she ate most evenings, a plate of black beans and rice and plantain, and drank two cups of coffee and read fifteen pages of a novel she had been reading for three weeks without progress and then walked back to the house where she was staying, which belonged to a widow named Doña Florienda who rented two rooms to researchers and aid workers and who asked no questions and provided every morning a breakfast of fruit and tortillas and who had a small dog that slept at the foot of Anil's bed with the territorial confidence of an animal that has determined its rights are non-negotiable.

She lay on the bed and looked at the ceiling and thought about the woman's face, about the shoulder—that particular grammar of grief she'd described to herself, still looking for the right language. There are gestures the body makes that predate language, that carry more than language carries. She had seen it at many sites, in many countries. The way a person's body responds to the nearness of someone they have lost. Not the dramatic gestures of acute grief—those she had also seen, she had been present at moments of identification that she would carry with her always, the sounds people made, the way they fell or didn't fall—but these other gestures, the smaller ones, the ones that seemed involuntary, that seemed to come from a self deeper than the grieving self, from some layer of the person that remembered another grammar entirely, a physical grammar of proximity and recognition.

She thought about what it meant to identify someone. In her work the word had a technical meaning: to establish correspondence between biological evidence and a known individual. But it also meant something older. To identify. To say: I know this. To say: this is one of mine.

Sleep arrived eventually, as it always did, without announcement.

She stayed three more weeks in Guatemala, completing her analysis, filing her reports, attending the meetings with Claro and with the legal organizations that would use the forensic evidence in the processes—formal and informal, judicial and extrajudicial—by which the state was being asked to account for what had happened in these hills during the worst years. On the last day before she flew back to Geneva she went to the site one final time, alone, in the early morning before the heat arrived. The graves had been covered again, carefully, and around the perimeter someone had placed small stones in a line, a boundary or a marking. She did not know who had done it.

She stood there for a while. The jungle made its sounds around her.

She thought: this is what the work is. Not the science, though the science is essential. Not the reports, though the reports matter, though they will be read by lawyers and judges and historians. The work is this: the returning. The standing here. The refusal to look away.

Then she drove back to Chimaltenango and packed her bag and took a taxi to the airport in Guatemala City and flew to Miami and then to Geneva and did not sleep on either flight and arrived back at her apartment near the lake with her bag unpacked and her mind still on the other side of the world, in the western highlands, in the heat, in the particular quality of light at five-thirty in the morning when the team arrives and one or two family members are already waiting.

She had three weeks before the next assignment.

She spent most of them at her desk in the Centre's offices, writing up the Guatemala work, and in the evenings she walked along the lake and tried to remember what ordinary life felt like. She had been conducting this experiment, the experiment of ordinary life, since she had left Sri Lanka at the age of twenty, when she had gone first to London and then to an American university on a scholarship to study medicine, and she had not resolved it. She was not certain it was resolvable. The question of how to live in a world like this one, with the knowledge she carried, alongside people who did not carry it or carried it differently—she had not found a formula. She suspected there was no formula.

Her mother had died while she was in Cambodia. Her father had died earlier, during the worst years in Sri Lanka, and she had not gone back for his death either, which was a fact she lived with the way you live with a scar, aware of it constantly in certain weather. Her brother Sunil was in Colombo, working as an engineer, married with two daughters. They spoke on the phone occasionally. The calls were careful and affectionate and left her feeling more alone afterward, which she recognized was a failure of her own making and not a failure of his.

On the fourth day after her return from Guatemala, she received a phone call from the director of the Centre, a Norwegian named Bjorn Elstad who was precise and fair-minded and who had the gift of speaking difficult things plainly. He said that they had received a request from a human rights organization in Sri Lanka. He said the request was specific and unusual. He said the organization needed a forensic investigator with high-level expertise and also, if possible, someone who understood the context—the history, the culture, the particular languages of that particular place. He said her name had come up from multiple directions.

Anil stood at her window and looked at the lake.

She had not been back to Sri Lanka in eleven years.

She said: tell me about the request.

He described what the organization had asked for. It was, as he had said, specific and unusual. There was a skeleton, recently discovered at an archaeological site, in circumstances that suggested it was not ancient but recent—recent meaning within the last few years—and that therefore suggested it was not an archaeological artifact but evidence of something else, something that the organization believed needed outside examination precisely because any local investigation would be subject to pressures and complications that made independence impossible. The government was not cooperative. The political situation was complicated in ways that anyone familiar with Sri Lanka would understand immediately. The organization needed someone to come, to look, to document, and to report.

She stood at the window for a long time after he had finished speaking.

The lake outside was grey and still. A boat moved across its surface very slowly, pulling a long quiet wake.

What she thought about, standing there, was not her dead father or her distant brother or the eleven years she had been away. What she thought about was the woman sitting in the grave in Guatemala with her legs under her as if in formal prayer, looking down at the two men as if they were sleeping. What she thought about was the specific discipline she had spent her professional life developing: the ability to look at bones and read from them a story that the bones themselves could not tell without her. What she thought about was Tesseba's words: not a wall. A container.

I'll go, she said.

There was a brief silence on the line.

I want to be clear, Bjorn said, about the conditions. You would have authorization from the Human Rights Commission to be there, but that authorization is somewhat provisional. The government has agreed in principle but the agreement is fragile. You would be working within a structure that could change without warning.

She knew this. She had worked within such structures before.

Are there others on the team?

There will be a local specialist assigned to you by the Commission. A man named Sarath Diyasena. He works as an archaeologist. He knows the site. He has been, according to our information, cautious. I think that means he is afraid.

In her experience people who were cautious in those circumstances were either wise or afraid or both, and it was impossible to know which until you had worked beside them long enough.

And what is the site? she asked.

He described a place called Aranaduwa, in the north, near a rock temple that had been under excavation for several years. He described the circumstances of the skeleton's discovery: it had been found within a chamber that contained a series of ancient burials, the chambers sealed and apparently undisturbed for centuries until the recent excavations. But the skeleton in question did not belong with the others. The bones were not ancient. The analysis done so far suggested a male, adult, dead perhaps two or three years. The face, Bjorn said, with a slight hesitation that she noticed—the skull had been modified in a way that was deliberate and specific, altered in a way that she would need to see to fully understand.

She asked him to send her what documentation they had. He said he would.

She stood for a while longer at the window after the call ended. The lake had gone darker. The boat had disappeared. She thought about the bones of ancient men and the bones of recent men lying together in the same sealed chamber, and she thought about what it meant that someone had placed them there—had gone to the trouble, the specific deliberate trouble, of concealing what was recent inside what was ancient, as if the one might absorb the other, as if death of one kind could be hidden inside death of another kind.

She went to her desk and opened the file folder of the Guatemala work, the final reports, and she read through them again not because she needed to—she knew them by heart—but because she needed to be in that mode of thinking, that clean precise language, before she began to think about what was coming. She needed to be in her work self before she could begin the process of being in Sri Lanka.

Eleven years was not a long time if you measured it in the life of a country. Countries absorbed more than that without flinching. But eleven years was a significant portion of a life, and what she had been at twenty-two when she left was different enough from what she was now that she was not certain how to think about the relationship between the two people. The woman who had left—bright, ambitious, marked by losses she was not yet equipped to understand—and the woman who was returning, if returning was the right word, which she doubted.

She booked a flight for ten days later. She would go to London first to see an old friend from her university years, a woman named Georgia who was the closest thing she had to someone who had known her continuously, who had watched her change and who could testify to the changes if anyone asked. Georgia would feed her and argue with her and remind her that she was a person and not only a function, and this was something Anil needed, periodically, the way she needed sleep—not because she believed it would help, but because she knew from experience that it did.

Then she would fly to Colombo.

She would land at the airport she had left at twenty-two and drive into the city she had known as a child and young woman, the city that was still there in her the way first places are always there, present beneath all the subsequent geographies. She would be someone who had come with an authorization and a mandate and a specific technical expertise. She would look at bones and she would read from them what they were capable of saying. She would write a report.

She would try not to be anything other than what the work required.

She knew this would not entirely succeed. It never entirely succeeded. But the attempt was itself part of the discipline, and the discipline was what she had.

Outside the window the lake was almost dark now. The lights on the far shore had come on, reflected in the water in long shifting columns. She sat for a while looking at them and then she turned back to her desk and began to write.
