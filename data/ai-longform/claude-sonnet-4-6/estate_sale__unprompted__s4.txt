The tables had been priced the night before, Constance's work, done in the dark of the house with a flashlight and a roll of colored stickers she'd bought at the Dollar General on Route 9. Orange for furniture. Yellow for kitchen. Pink for linens and curtains and the various soft things her mother had accumulated the way some women accumulate opinions—quietly, over years, until the accumulation itself becomes a kind of argument. She'd gone room to room with the flashlight beam sliding across surfaces, and she had not cried, which she considered either a triumph or a warning sign, the way certain silences in a marriage can be either peace or the held breath before catastrophe.

Now it was nine in the morning on a Saturday in October and there were already eleven people in the front yard.

Constance stood on the porch and watched them. They had the particular posture of people who attended estate sales regularly—a forward lean, a readiness, eyes moving across surfaces the way a bird dog moves across a field, with purpose that looks casual until suddenly it isn't. An older man in a Carhartt jacket had pressed his face to the living room window and was cupping his hands around his eyes. A woman in her forties, heavy coat, sensible shoes, held a tote bag that said TRADER JOE'S and was already visibly calculating. A young couple, late twenties maybe, stood apart from the others with the self-conscious distance of people who want to seem like they've wandered here accidentally, like they're above the hunger of it, though they're here at nine on a Saturday morning same as everyone else.

Constance had told her sister Diane she could handle it alone.

This had been a lie that felt true when she said it, which was the most dangerous kind.

She unlocked the front door and the eleven people came through it, and then there were more people behind them who had arrived while she wasn't watching, and the house filled with the sound of strangers moving through the rooms of her childhood. Footsteps she didn't recognize. The slide of a drawer she knew by feel, the way it stuck and then released, interpreted now by hands that had never touched it before, coaxing it without the history of it, without knowing that you had to lift slightly as you pulled, that her mother had known this for thirty years and her mother's mother before that, though the chest had been bought at a Sears in 1962 and had no real history worth speaking of.

She set herself up at the card table near the front door. Cash box, receipt pad, the pricing notebook she'd put together with Diane over the phone two weeks ago, Diane calling out memories and Constance translating them into numbers. The dining room set, the one with the claw feet that their father had refinished in 1987, had been priced at four hundred dollars, which felt both obscene and insulting in alternating waves.

The man in the Carhartt jacket came to her table first. He was buying a cast iron skillet, a bread box, and a set of salt and pepper shakers shaped like roosters that Constance had not thought about in twenty years and now found she could think about nothing else. They had sat on the kitchen windowsill her entire childhood. Her mother had brought them from her own mother's kitchen when her grandmother died. They were ceramic, hand-painted, slightly chipped on the larger rooster's tail, and Constance had priced them at two dollars for the pair.

"Three fifty for all of it?" the man said. He meant the skillet and the bread box too.

Constance looked at the rooster salt shakers in his large, weathered hand. "The shakers are two dollars," she said. "The skillet is twelve and the bread box is eight."

"Twenty-two dollars," the man said, as if she hadn't done the math herself.

"Yes."

"I'll give you eighteen."

She looked at him. He had a kind face, she decided, creased and sun-worn, the face of a man who had worked outdoors for most of his life and had not much minded it. He didn't know he was holding her grandmother's roosters. There was no reason he should.

"Twenty," she said.

He paid and left, and she watched the roosters disappear through the front door in his jacket pocket and she thought: that's done, then. That's a thing that's over.

The morning moved.

The woman with the Trader Joe's bag bought four boxes of her mother's books, mostly paperback romances from the eighties, Jude Deveraux and Nora Roberts, though there were a few others in the mix, a biography of Eleanor Roosevelt, a falling-apart copy of *The Thorn Birds* that Constance had read herself at fourteen and which had given her complicated ideas about priests. The woman had pulled each book from the shelf with the efficiency of someone sorting recyclables, holding them spine-up to check for water damage, setting some aside, returning others. Constance watched from the doorway. Her mother had read those books. Had touched each cover. Had gone wherever each of them went and come back to Ohio.

"How much for the lot?" the woman said.

They settled on twelve dollars. Constance wrote the receipt and the woman carried her tote bag of her mother's inner life out to a white Subaru.

By ten thirty the dining room set was gone.

Two men she had not seen arrive had come with a truck and a furniture dolly, and they'd maneuvered the table and six chairs through the front door with a professional ease that required them to tilt the table on its side and shuffle it through the frame at an angle Constance would not have thought possible. She'd watched from the hallway. The man at the back had said, "Turn it, turn it, no, the other way, there," and then it was gone, the table where her family had eaten thirty years of dinners, where she had done homework, where her father had died, or rather where he had been sitting when he put down his fork and said I don't feel right and then had not gotten up from his chair but had slid sideways in a way that Constance, standing at the sink with her back to him, had heard as something falling and had turned around and seen.

He had been forty-nine years old. She was fifty-three now.

She went to the kitchen and drank a glass of water standing at the sink, looking out the window at the backyard where the oak tree her father had planted was enormous now, obscene in its health, blazing yellow in the October light.

A woman appeared beside her. She was perhaps seventy, small, wearing a fleece vest over a turtleneck, and she was holding a glass pie plate up to the kitchen window to examine it in the light.

"Is this Pyrex?" the woman asked.

Constance looked at it. "I think so."

"It's not marked."

"Then I don't know."

"Your price says three dollars." The woman turned it in the light again. "I'll give you two."

"The price is three dollars."

The woman looked at her. A long look, the kind that in other circumstances might be called measuring. "Two fifty."

Constance took a breath. "Three dollars, or you can leave it."

The woman paid three dollars and left with the pie plate and Constance stood alone in the kitchen for a moment. The kitchen was half-emptied now, the counters showing pale rectangles where appliances had stood. The coffee maker was gone, bought by the young couple who had been trying to seem accidental, who had turned out to be genuinely enthusiastic once they'd gotten inside, moving through the rooms with a kind of archaeological delight, touching things, holding them up to each other. They'd bought the coffee maker and a set of mixing bowls and a lamp from the living room that Constance had always found ugly but which they'd said, to each other, was mid-century, maybe, could be, look at the base. 

She'd heard them in the bedroom. Her mother's bedroom. She'd gone and stood in the doorway and watched them open the closet.

"Oh," the young woman had said, at the smell of it. The cedar and the particular chemical sweetness of her mother's brand of hand lotion, the small concentrated weather of a person who is gone. 

The young man had put his hand on the young woman's back, briefly, and they had both just stood there for a moment. They hadn't known they were doing it—bearing witness, Constance thought, involuntarily, the way you sometimes catch yourself having been kind before you decided to be.

"Sorry," the young woman said, turning to find Constance in the doorway. "We didn't realize you were—"

"It's fine," Constance said. "She kept everything in boxes. On the shelf there. Whatever you want."

She had not gone back into the bedroom since.

Now she returned to her card table by the door and managed the flow. People brought things to her, and she looked up prices in the notebook, and she made change from the cash box, and she said yes when people asked if things were negotiable and she said yes when they asked if they could take something out to their car before paying and she did not know why she kept saying yes when Diane had told her, twice on the phone, don't let things out the door before they've paid, you'll lose track, but she kept saying yes anyway. She trusted people too much. This was something her mother had said to her since she was young, not as a criticism exactly, more as an observation her mother couldn't help making repeatedly, the way you keep noting a crack in the ceiling even when there's nothing to be done about it.

She tried to remember if it was true. She thought of people she'd trusted who had not deserved it and people she hadn't trusted who had. David, her ex-husband, she had trusted absolutely and he had been, if not undeserving, then at least insufficient to the trust, which in the end amounted to the same thing. Her daughter Reyna she trusted completely and Reyna was in Columbus today—working, she'd said, and Constance had not asked what work, not wanting to make it into a thing. Her son Marcus had not come back for the funeral and she was still in the process of deciding what to do with this fact. She had been sliding it around inside her like a stone in a pocket for six weeks.

A man wanted to know about the cedar chest in the upstairs hallway.

She followed him up the stairs. The house had the creaking, specific voice it had always had; certain steps spoke and certain ones didn't, and she hit each one in the right place without thinking, the body's memory operating beneath consciousness. The man was already in the hallway, crouching before the cedar chest, which had been her mother's and before that her grandmother's, which held quilts and the kind of things you keep because you can't throw them away, the too-good-to-use, the too-sentimental-to-discard. She had priced it at sixty-five dollars, which she and Diane had agreed on while Diane cried quietly on the other end of the phone.

The man ran his hand along the top. He knocked on the wood, the way people knock on things with no real reason to. "Good condition," he said.

"It's solid cedar. Very old."

"Seventies?"

"Older." She paused. "My grandmother's."

The man looked up at her. He seemed to be deciding whether this was relevant. He decided it wasn't, or he decided to be polite about deciding it wasn't, which in practice looks the same. "Sixty-five?" he said.

"Yes."

"I'll take it."

They got two of his friends from downstairs and the three of them maneuvered the cedar chest down the staircase while Constance stood against the wall and watched. They were careful with it—that surprised her. They set it down twice on the stairs to adjust their grip, and none of them was careless. At the bottom, one of the men put it down and rolled his shoulder and said, whew, and then they went out the door with it, and Constance stood at the top of the stairs and looked down at the rectangle of daylight where the front door was open.

She could see people moving in and out. Strangers, all of them, carrying pieces of the house across the grass and loading them into cars and trucks. It had the quality of a dream in which familiar things are present but in wrong contexts. Her mother's armchair was in the yard, tagged but not yet sold, sitting on the brown October grass with a dignity it didn't have any right to have out there, but it had it anyway, the good upholstered posture of a chair that has been sat in correctly for years.

Constance sat down on the top step.

She was not going to cry. This was still true, she thought, as something moved in her chest. She would file it, this feeling, as she had learned to file things. It was a skill she'd developed in her forties, after David, the capacity to note what she was feeling and set it aside in an organized way, the way you note a thing you mean to address and put it on a list. She kept the list. She did eventually address things. She was not cold, she thought; she was sequential.

She sat on the top step and watched the light in the doorway for a moment.

A child appeared at the bottom of the stairs. A girl, seven or eight, wearing a purple coat, looking up at Constance with the frank curiosity of children who have not yet learned to make their curiosity polite. "What are you doing up there?" the girl said.

"Sitting."

"Why?"

"Resting."

The girl considered this. She was holding a small porcelain figurine that Constance recognized as one of a set that had been on the mantelpiece since before she could remember. A shepherd, she thought, or a boy with a lamb, some pastoral figure in blues and whites, the kind of figurine that accumulates in the houses of a certain generation the way receipts accumulate in drawers.

"My mom says can we have this one?"

Constance looked at the figurine. "How much is it marked?"

The girl turned it over and squinted. "Three dollars."

"Then it's three dollars."

"But can we have it," the girl said again, as if Constance had misunderstood the question.

"If you pay three dollars, yes."

The girl took this information and ran. Constance heard her across the floor below, across the room, then her mother's voice somewhere in the house, then she heard the child's feet return and a woman appeared at the bottom of the stairs—forties, tired eyes, the girl clutching her hand with one hand and holding the figurine with the other. The woman counted out three dollars in ones and handed them up to Constance, who was still sitting on the step.

"Thank you," the woman said.

"Sure."

The woman looked up at her for a moment with the tired eyes. There was something in the look that Constance could not categorize. Not pity. Not quite recognition. Something more like the momentary awareness one person has of another—the flash of knowing there is a whole life in there, inaccessible—before it passes and you go on.

They went. The figurine went with them.

Constance got up from the step.

She walked through the upstairs rooms, which were mostly emptied now. Her childhood bedroom held only the bed frame, which she had not been able to price. She had put a sign on it: NOT FOR SALE. She was sleeping here tonight and leaving in the morning and then the house would go to the realtor and that would be another thing that was over. She stood in the doorway and looked at the bare mattress and the naked windows and the walls where her old posters had hung, faint rectangles of slightly-less-faded paint. She had had a poster of a horse and a poster of a singer she could no longer remember the name of and a map of the world with a thumbtack in every country she planned to visit, which she had done at age twelve with great seriousness and had then forgotten about. She'd made it to seven of them.

Not bad, her mother would have said. Not bad at all.

Her mother would have been wrong about that in the way she was often wrong, with total confidence, in ways that were still somehow sustaining.

She went back downstairs.

By one o'clock the house was mostly emptied. People came less frequently now, the early fever broken, the good things gone. What remained were the things no one had wanted: a collection of decorative plates with state birds, two TV trays with bent legs, a box of VHS tapes including several from the eighties labeled in her mother's handwriting, *Recordings—TV*, and one that said *Constance, Christmas 1989*, which Constance put in her car without pricing it, without guilt.

A man with a beard bought the decorative plates for eight dollars, apologetically, the way some people spend money apologetically, as if it acknowledges some unfairness in the world.

The TV trays didn't sell.

At two thirty she closed the cash box and sat in her mother's armchair, which had not sold either, which still sat on the brown grass. She sat in it and looked at the house. The October light had gone amber and low, coming at the house from the side now, and the house looked the way things look in the late afternoon of their last day. She could not have said how she knew it was the last day; she only knew it.

The oak tree her father had planted moved in a wind she couldn't feel from where she was sitting.

She had an image of him suddenly, vivid in the particular way that the images of the dead are vivid, which is to say complete, frozen, lit differently than memory, more like a photograph than a memory—her father planting the tree, which she had not actually witnessed. He had planted it before she was born. But she had the image anyway, him young, younger than she was now, digging in the backyard in summer with a spade and a sapling no bigger than a walking stick, patient in the way he had been patient, not peaceful-patient but working-patient, the patience of a man who believed in the future of small things.

She had not thought about him enough today. She had been thinking about her mother because it was her mother's house and her mother's things, but her father had lived here too, had put his hands on the dining room table she'd sold this morning, had watched television from the chair she was sitting in now. She had sold his chair, was what she had done. She had put a price on it and sold it.

She sat in it a while longer.

A truck went past on the road, and then a car. The street was quiet the way streets in small Ohio towns are quiet on October afternoons, a quietness that doesn't feel empty but complete, like a sentence that has been said fully and ended properly. She had grown up on this street and she had not lived here for thirty years and those two facts coexisted without resolving. 

She would come back for the television, for a few last boxes, for the TV trays that had not sold. She would load her car in the morning and she would drive north to Cleveland, back to her apartment, back to her plants and her job and the life she had built from the materials available to her. It was a good life. She believed this. She believed it the way you believe things that are also somewhat beside the point.

She sat in the chair until the light had changed completely and the yard was in shadow and the house was just a house on a street, not hers, not anyone's for much longer, rectangular and patient, with its oak tree in the back going red now in the very last of the light, growing without intention in the direction of the sky.

She got up. She carried the chair inside. She was not leaving it out overnight, and she was not selling it, and she did not need a reason more complete than that.

She locked the door. She stood in the emptied living room in the late afternoon dark of it, her hands in her coat pockets, and she stood there for what felt like a long time, though it was probably only a minute, maybe two. The house held its breath around her. Everything removed, everything essential still somehow present, concentrated, the way fragrance lingers in a room after the flowers are gone, not absence exactly, but the specific gravity of absence, the shape that's left.

Then she went to the kitchen and she made herself a cup of tea with the electric kettle she'd set aside before the sale, that she'd kept back from the yellow stickers, and she sat at the little table—the kitchen table, which she had also not sold, that had also been NOT FOR SALE—and she drank her tea in the dark of her mother's kitchen while the October wind picked up outside and moved through the oak tree with a sound like the turning of many pages, and she did not cry, and she did not not cry, and the night came on.
