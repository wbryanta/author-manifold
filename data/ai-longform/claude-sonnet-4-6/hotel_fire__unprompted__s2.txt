The lobby smelled of new paint and something floral she couldn't name, and Vera stood in it for longer than was polite, longer than her coat warranted holding, longer than the young man at the front desk could maintain his practiced smile before it began to falter at the edges. She handed him her coat finally and accepted her key card and walked to the elevator with her small bag pulling at her shoulder and the whole architectural fact of the place pressing down on her like a hand on the back of her neck.

It was not the same building. She needed to remind herself of this continuously, in the way one might remind oneself of a new address, or a dead person's death. The original structure had been pulled to its foundations in 1987, the year after the fire, and what stood now was twenty-two years of someone else's decisions about ceilings and marble and the placement of sconces along the corridor walls. The Aldgate Grand, the signage still read, as though the name alone constituted a continuity. She supposed, in the way of hotels and families, it did.

Her room was on the fourth floor. She pressed the button in the elevator and watched the doors close and noted that her reflection in the polished steel was someone she recognized less readily than she once had. She was fifty-one years old. She had come for Daniel's wedding and she would stay two nights and she would eat the salmon or the chicken and she would dance if asked and she would leave on Sunday morning with her bag and her coat and the whole thing would be behind her. This was the plan. She believed in the plan with the fervor of someone who does not entirely trust it.

She had been twenty-three when she worked here. The hotel that existed then was an Edwardian structure with thick carpets the color of dried blood and light fixtures that were always threatening to loosen from their ceiling mounts and a system of service corridors behind the guest rooms that ran the length of each floor like a secondary skeleton. She had worked those corridors. She had known them better than she had known her own small flat in Barking, had known which doors stuck in July humidity and which trolley wheels shrieked against the flooring and which guests left their shoes outside for polishing and which left other things. She had been good at her work in the quiet, methodical way that work rewards when you ask nothing of it but the hours.

The fire started on the second floor, in a room that was being used for storage while the east wing underwent renovation. It started, the inquest eventually decided, because of a contractor's extension cord and a quantity of linen piled in a way that violated several principles both of physics and common sense. It started at eleven forty-three on a Thursday night in October, which was either very late or very early depending on your relationship to the hours in question. Vera had been finishing a shift that had run long because Mrs. Okafor, the head of housekeeping, had asked her to do a turndown service on three rooms that the junior girls had missed. She had been on the third floor. She had smelled it before she heard anything, had stood in the corridor with a stack of folded towels against her chest and thought, with a clarity that still embarrassed her in its practicality: that is a fire.

She did not think about this often. She had cultivated, over the years, a particular faculty for not thinking about things, which was different from forgetting them and different again from suppressing them, though she understood that to a therapist — and she had seen two, in the years immediately following, and found them both sincere and limited — these distinctions might seem self-serving. She preferred to think of it as spatial. There were rooms in her mind and some of them she did not enter. She was good at not entering rooms. She had been trained to service the interiors of strangers' lives with a minimum of intrusion, and she had applied this training inward with great thoroughness.

The fourth floor corridor of the new Aldgate Grand was carpeted in a deep teal that she found aggressive. The sconces were modern things, brushed nickel, their light warm and even. There was a large mirror at the end of the hall and she watched herself come toward it as she pulled her bag and thought that she looked like someone arriving somewhere reluctantly, which was accurate. Room 412. She found it and pressed her key card to the panel and went inside.

It was a nice room. Of course it was a nice room; Daniel's family had arranged everything and Daniel's family had money of the comfortable, undemonstrative sort that produces reliable outcomes. A window overlooking the small courtyard garden that had not existed before, a king bed with a headboard upholstered in something grey and muted, a desk, a chair, a minibar she would not touch. She sat on the edge of the bed and placed her hands flat on her thighs and looked at the window.

Daniel was her godson. His mother, Patricia, was the closest friend of Vera's life, the sort of friend who exists at the center of things rather than at their edges, the sort you measure other friendships against and find them wanting. They had met when Daniel was six months old and Vera had moved into the flat above Patricia's in Stoke Newington, two years after the fire, at a point when Vera was in the process of becoming someone different from who she had been, which is to say someone more guarded and more competent and more alone. Patricia had knocked on her door with a casserole and a baby on her hip and had said, with the directness that Vera would come to understand was just how she moved through the world, that she'd heard Vera was new and that she herself found new very difficult and that if Vera had any interest she was welcome to come down for a glass of wine after the baby was settled. Vera had said yes before she'd finished the sentence. She had thought about this moment many times, what would have happened if she had said no, which was what she generally said. She thought Patricia would have knocked again the following week. She thought Patricia was that kind of person.

She had been Daniel's godmother for twenty-five years and had watched him grow into someone earnest and slightly awkward and possessed of a quality she could only describe as sincere goodwill, which was rarer than it sounded, which was perhaps the rarest thing. He was marrying a woman named Chloe who worked in urban planning and had a laugh like something mechanical briefly failing, which Vera found enormously endearing. The wedding was tomorrow at three. There was a drinks reception tonight in the hotel's restored ballroom, which occupied a footprint that in the original building had contained the second-floor guest rooms and the storage room where the fire had started, and Vera was expected to attend at seven, and it was now five forty-five.

She unpacked slowly, hanging her dress for tomorrow, setting her toiletries in a line on the bathroom shelf with the particular care she brought to hotel bathrooms, which she always left exactly as she found them — a habit so deep now it had become invisible to her, like posture. She ran water over her hands and looked at herself in the mirror above the sink. The girl who had worked here had been small and dark-haired and had worn a uniform the color of pine needles with a white collar and had eaten her lunches in a basement room that smelled of industrial cleaning fluid and other people's food. That girl was available to her still, in the way of all previous selves, which is to say dimly and at a slant, the way you can see something clearly only when you stop looking directly at it.

She had pulled two guests from the corridor of the third floor. This was the part the newspapers had printed and that people occasionally brought up at the wrong moment, at dinner parties or on first dates or in the company of well-meaning strangers, the part that Vera found almost meaningless in isolation, stripped of everything before and after it. What the newspapers did not print, because it was not printable, was the specific texture of those minutes: the noise the fire made, which was more industrial than she had imagined, more like machinery than like something alive, though it also seemed in some sense alive. The weight of the woman she helped down the stairwell, who was large and terrified and kept saying a name that Vera did not recognize. The other guest, a man who had come to his door in response to her knocking and who had been so calm that she had thought he must work in emergency services or the military, but who was in fact, as she learned from a newspaper later, a retired schoolteacher from Derbyshire who gave no explanation for his composure because composure of that kind never has an explanation. She had not stayed in touch with either of them. She had sent a card to the woman, who had broken her arm on the stairs, and had received a reply that was very grateful and that mentioned God several times. She had not written back. She was not ungrateful for God; she simply had no correspondence with him.

Seven people had died. The number was its own room in her, one she had visited enough times to know its dimensions precisely. A contractor's assistant, two members of the hotel's night staff, and four guests. She had known one of the night staff by sight, a young Nigerian man named Emmanuel who worked the reception desk on the overnight and who made, she had always thought, too elaborate a ceremony of his sign-off at the end of his shift, as though he was performing cheerfulness for an audience that wasn't there. She had thought this uncharitably and had later hated herself for it and later still had arrived at something more neutral, which was simply that she had not known him and would not know him and that this was a fact about human lives that the fire had made vivid but not invented.

She dressed for the reception without looking at herself more than necessary, put on earrings, looked at them, took them off, put them back on. She took the elevator down and followed the signs through the lobby and along a corridor toward the sound of voices.

The ballroom was enormous and new and luminous, hung with lights that someone had spent real thought on, small warm points of it suspended at different heights above the room like a private sky. There were perhaps sixty people already there, clustered in the self-organizing way of wedding guests who know some people and not others, the clusters moving and regrouping like weather systems. She found Patricia within two minutes, which was always easy because Patricia was always moving, always in transit between one person and another, her body carrying a continuous implication of where it was going next.

Patricia embraced her and held on for a second longer than the occasion strictly required, which was Patricia's way of saying a number of things without saying them. She smelled of the perfume she'd worn for fifteen years, something cedar-ish, grounding.

"You made it," Patricia said.

"I made it."

"And the room?"

"The room is perfectly nice."

"You're doing the thing with your face."

"I'm not doing anything with my face."

"You are. It's the same face you made at my mother's funeral when you told me the catering was perfectly adequate."

Vera accepted a glass of wine from a passing tray and drank from it. "I'm fine," she said. "I'm very happy to be here. I'm very happy for Daniel."

"He's over there, somewhere," Patricia said, gesturing in a direction that contained several men in suits. "He's been an absolute wreck all day in the most wonderful way. He cried at lunch. We were just eating sandwiches and he suddenly—" She made a gesture with her free hand that indicated overflow, something beyond containing. "He's so happy, Vera. He's just so straightforwardly happy and I can't—" She stopped.

"I know," Vera said.

"Chloe's family is lovely. Her father keeps finding excuses to tell me things about drainage, and I genuinely don't mind. I find it very soothing."

Vera laughed and it came out of her cleanly, without effort, and she was grateful for this. She looked around the room and tried to let it be what it was: a room full of people who loved two people who loved each other. She tried to let the architecture be incidental.

But she found herself, as the evening moved forward and the wine moved through her and the noise of the room settled into a kind of ambient warmth, becoming aware of the floor. It was a specific awareness, not anxious exactly, but attentive, the way you become attentive to a sound you can't quite place. The new ballroom was built on a slab that was built on the foundations of the original structure, and there was, somewhere beneath the polished concrete and the underfloor heating and the layers of material and decision that constituted a modern floor, a stratum of the original building's matter. Ash, possibly, if ash persisted. Certainly the impressions of walls that no longer stood. She thought about the geometry of what this floor covered: the corridor she had walked with towels against her chest, the room that was 209 in the old numbering and that no longer existed in any form. She thought about Emmanuel at his desk, doing his elaborate sign-off for no one. She thought about the contractor's assistant, who had been seventeen years old and whose name she had carried for twenty-two years without knowing what to do with it: Kevin Marsh, printed in the paper alongside a photograph of a boy who looked younger than seventeen and who had been described by his family as devoted to his younger siblings, which was both insufficient and, she thought, precisely right.

Daniel found her near the edge of the room, by the tall windows that gave onto the courtyard garden, where a fountain she suspected was recent played against some stones. He had his father's height and his mother's face and he embraced her with the ease of a person who had been embraced by her since before he could remember, which is a specific and irreplaceable ease.

"Godmother," he said, which was what he'd called her since he was old enough to understand what it meant, not as a title but as a name, her name in his life.

"You look terrified," she said.

"I'm not terrified. I'm the opposite of terrified. There must be a word for the opposite of terrified."

"Ready."

He considered this. "Yes," he said. "Yes, that's exactly right." He looked at her with the directness his mother had given him, that quality of not looking away. "How is it? Being here. Is it—"

"It's fine," she said. "It's a very nice ballroom."

"Mum told me, when we were picking the venue, that maybe we shouldn't. That maybe we should find somewhere else. I said—" He paused, seemed to be choosing. "I said I thought you'd want to come. That you'd want to be here wherever it was, and that you'd—manage. Was I wrong to think that?"

She looked at him and thought about the twenty-five years she had held in relation to this person, had watched from the position of the person who is not the parent but is something specific and not-quite-nameable, had watched him move through all his configurations of self toward this one, this man standing here ready, and she thought: no. He was not wrong.

"No," she said. "You weren't wrong."

"I wanted you here. I wanted you here specifically."

"I know."

"And Chloe wanted to meet you. She's heard—I've told her things. About you." He smiled and it was helpless, the smile of someone who has been too happy for too long to have kept it properly contained. "Good things. Vera things."

She took his face briefly in her hands, a gesture she'd made since he was small, calibrated always to his current size so that it meant the same thing at every scale, and she said, "Go and find your woman. I'll be here."

After he'd moved back into the room, she turned to the window and looked at the courtyard garden, the fountain, the stones, the dark outside. She looked at her own reflection in the glass. Behind her reflection the warm suspended lights made their private sky and people moved beneath it in their clusters.

She had never told anyone about the specific quality of the thing she had taken from the fire, which was not trauma in the way trauma is usually described, not the retrospective terror that ambushes you, though she had that too in the early years. It was something else, something she had no adequate word for: a knowledge, bodily and permanent, about the nature of structures. That any structure — this floor, this wall, these lights — was also a future ruin. That what a building was, in addition to all its other attributes, was a set of decisions about how a failure would eventually propagate. She had come to find this, over the years, not terrifying but oddly clarifying, the way certain true things are. Not comforting. Not useful. Just true in a way that meant she could stand here in this rebuilt lobby of a rebuilt life, as a fifty-one-year-old woman with her wine and her earrings and her godson's happiness filling the room behind her, and feel the floor beneath her feet with an attention most people never brought to floors, an attention that was itself a kind of thanks. For the floor's persistence. For its present refusal to fail. For the fact that she was standing on it, among these particular people, in this room that was built on the place where she had learned what she knew.

She finished her wine. She turned back into the room. Patricia caught her eye across the crowd and raised her glass, and Vera raised hers, and somewhere in the middle of the room Daniel was laughing at something, his head tipped back, his whole body in it, and the lights above him held.
