The rebuilt hotel was called the Palomar now, although everyone in the valley still called it the Del Coronado, which had been its name when it burned. Francesca had not expected them to keep the name and they had not kept it, and this seemed to her neither surprising nor particularly significant, only one of those facts that accumulates around a thing until the thing itself is no longer visible beneath them. She had driven up from the city the night before the wedding and checked in under her married name, which was Sorel, and the girl at the desk had given her a key card and told her that the pool closed at ten and that the continental breakfast was served in the Mesquite Room beginning at six-thirty. Francesca had said thank you and taken the key card and ridden the elevator to the third floor and stood for a moment in the corridor before she could make herself walk to her room.

The corridor smelled of new carpet and something underneath the new carpet, something chemical and fixed, and Francesca thought of the way Dolores Reyes used to say that a hotel was only as clean as its baseboards. Dolores had trained her, if trained was the word for what Dolores had done, which was mostly to stand in doorways watching while Francesca made beds and then tell her what she had done wrong. You're leaving tracks in the pile, Dolores would say. You have to go back over it. And Francesca, who had been twenty-two and newly arrived from Modesto and willing to be corrected by anyone, would go back over it.

The room was fine. The room had a king bed and a view of the east garden and a television mounted on the wall above a credenza made of some pale composite wood. Francesca sat on the edge of the bed for a while without turning on any of the lights and looked out the window at the garden, which was lit by low amber fixtures along the paths, and thought about nothing in particular. This was a skill she had developed in the years since the fire, the ability to think about nothing in particular, and she was not unproud of it.

The wedding was at four o'clock the following afternoon, in the garden she could see from her window. Francesca had known the bride, whose name was Margaret Caswell, since Margaret was seven years old and Francesca had been briefly married to Margaret's father's law partner, a man named Tom Sorel whose name she had kept because changing it back seemed like a declaration of something she did not want to declare. Tom Sorel had died of a coronary event four years ago in the parking structure of a building in Century City. He had been a decent man and she had been fond of him and the marriage had lasted eleven years, which was longer than anyone had expected including herself. It was the Caswell family, rather than any lingering attachment to the Sorel name, that had brought her to the valley.

In the morning she went down to the Mesquite Room and drank coffee and ate half a pastry and watched three other early guests whom she did not recognize engage in the careful performance of people who do not know each other in a hotel breakfast room. They sat at separate tables and looked at their phones and occasionally looked up from their phones to look at the pastries and then looked at their phones again. One of the men, who was heavyset and wore a linen jacket already rumpled from the valley heat, refilled his coffee cup twice and each time looked around the room as if expecting someone to acknowledge the fact of his having done so. Francesca recognized in him the particular restlessness of a man who has come to an event like this alone and has not yet made peace with the fact.

She herself had made peace with the fact a long time ago, or if not peace then something that functioned similarly. What she had not made peace with, or what she was only now determining whether she had made peace with, was being inside this building. She had not been inside this building since the building had been a different building. She thought about this while she finished her coffee and folded her napkin and put it beside her plate.

The fire had happened in late October, which in the valley meant the dry winds and the particular orange quality of the afternoon light that she had disliked even before she had reason to associate it with anything. She had been on the fourth floor with her cart when the alarm went off, and for a moment she had not moved, because the alarm had gone off twice before that season for no reason and both times nothing had happened except that a man from management had come up from the office and reset something in the utility room and apologized to the guests. So she had stood in the corridor for perhaps fifteen seconds before she smelled it, and then she had moved. She had taken the stairwell at the east end of the building and not the stairwell at the west end of the building and this was the relevant fact, the only fact that mattered, since the fire had started in the laundry room on the subbasement level and run up through the west utility shaft like something with a purpose. Twelve people had not been as fortunate as Francesca in their choice of stairwell or their proximity to it. She had not known any of the twelve well, although she had known three of them by sight and one of them, a man named Bobby Restrepo who worked the breakfast shift and sometimes brought his radio to the service entrance and played it while he smoked, somewhat better than by sight.

What she had thought about in the years since was not the fire itself, about which she could remember very little in the way of sequence or detail, but the fifteen seconds in the corridor when she had stood still and not known yet that she needed to move. She had thought about those fifteen seconds with a consistency and thoroughness that she recognized as disproportionate and could not correct. The thought was simply this: that in those fifteen seconds she had not been afraid, because she had not known there was anything to be afraid of, and that this was the closest she had ever come to genuine peace, those fifteen seconds of not knowing.

She went back to her room and changed for the wedding.

The garden had been replanted entirely, which she had expected, and the ceremony was held in the far section of it beneath a structure of white fabric that moved in the warm air. Francesca found a seat in the third row on the groom's side, having been directed there by a young man with a boutonniere who seemed uncertain about the protocol for a guest who declined to specify. She sat next to a woman in her sixties named Harriet Morse who turned out to have been a college roommate of Margaret's mother and who had opinions about the fabric structure that she was willing to share at length. The fabric structure was not what Harriet Morse would have chosen. What Harriet Morse would have chosen was something more permanent-looking. Something that said commitment. Something with wood.

"I suppose it's the fashion," Harriet Morse said.

"I suppose so," Francesca agreed.

"Are you on the groom's side?"

"I'm a family friend. They put me here."

Harriet Morse nodded as if this confirmed something she had already suspected. The groom's name was Peter Avery and he was a cardiologist from San Diego and he had a son from a previous marriage who was serving as best man and who had his father's quality of contained, expensive calm, the quality Francesca associated with men who have learned very young that other people will extend them a great deal of latitude. She watched the son and thought about this.

Richard Caswell appeared and sat in the front row and did not turn around. He was seventy-one now and his hair had gone white in a way that looked distinguished rather than diminished, which was not what everyone could say about seventy-one. He had a second wife named Donna who sat beside him and occasionally put her hand on his arm and then removed it, a repeated gesture whose meaning Francesca could not quite determine. The first wife, Margaret's mother, had died of lung cancer eight years ago having never smoked a cigarette in her life, and Richard Caswell had said at the funeral in a tone of genuine bafflement that it simply did not make sense, as if sense were something the universe had contracted to provide.

Tom Sorel had stood next to Richard Caswell at that funeral and afterward they had all driven back to the Caswell house and Margaret, who had been twenty-three and finishing her MBA, had poured wine for everyone and said very little and looked out the large windows at the garden behind the house where her mother had grown things. Francesca had watched her and thought: she will be all right. She had been right about this, or Margaret had made it true through some effort Francesca had not been present to observe, which was perhaps the same thing.

The music changed and Margaret appeared.

She was wearing something ivory and simple and she was carrying gardenias, which her mother had grown in the garden behind the house. Francesca did not know whether this was intentional. She suspected it was. She thought of Margaret at seven, pouring water into a glass already full and watching the overflow with scientific attention while her father talked on the phone. The child had always wanted to see what happened when you pushed past a thing's capacity. Francesca had found this quality admirable then and found it admirable now, watching Margaret walk through the warm air toward the cardiologist who was waiting for her at the end of the aisle with the expression of a man who cannot quite believe his own luck, which was the right expression to have and which he wore without apparent self-consciousness.

"She looks beautiful," Harriet Morse whispered.

"She does," Francesca said.

She had not been back to this valley in the intervening years between the fire and now, or she had been back twice but not to this particular part of the valley, not to this hotel or its immediate surroundings, which amounted to the same thing since the hotel was what she meant when she thought about the valley. She had driven through the outskirts on the way north once and on the way south once and each time she had not looked at the road signs for the exit that would have brought her here. Whether this constituted avoidance she had considered at some length and concluded she did not know. It was possible that it was simply indifference, that she had not come back because there had been no reason to come back and not for any more complicated reason than that. It was also possible that this was not true.

The minister was talking about covenant. He used the word four times in the first two minutes and Harriet Morse shifted beside her in a way that suggested she was counting. Richard Caswell sat very still. The groom's son stood slightly behind his father and looked at his shoes and then looked up and looked at Margaret with an expression that Francesca could not read clearly from where she was sitting, something between admiration and a more complicated feeling that she chose not to attempt to identify.

She had been twenty-two and newly arrived from Modesto and she had taken the job at the hotel because the hotel was hiring and she had needed the work and she had not thought beyond this. This was what she wanted to be true and it was also what was true. She had not come to the valley for any reason. She had come because she had gotten on the highway and the highway had ended here, which was the way that most arrivals in the valley happened, to most people, at most times. You ran out of road. You stopped.

What she had not anticipated, arriving, was that the valley would be beautiful. She had expected something functional, something provisional, the landscape behind a landscape, and instead she had gotten the mornings, which were a specific quality of silver, and the light in the late afternoon before the hot winds came, which was amber and definitive. She had gotten the way the mountains looked when the air was clear enough to see them and the way they disappeared when it was not. She had gotten the oleander along the highway median, which bloomed pink and white and was poisonous, which she had not known until Dolores told her.

Everything was poisonous, Dolores said, if you ate enough of it.

This had struck Francesca as an important observation, though she had not been able at the time to articulate why. She thought she could articulate it now, standing in this garden, watching Margaret Caswell accept the groom's ring and slide it onto her own finger and look at her new husband with the clear unsentimental attention she had always given to things she was serious about. The observation seemed important because it was generous rather than cynical. It allowed for the possibility that the thing itself was not the problem. The thing itself, the oleander, the valley, the hotel, the fifteen seconds in the corridor, was what it was. What you did with your proximity to it was a separate question.

The minister pronounced them married and the cardiologist kissed his new wife and there was applause and the music changed again and Harriet Morse pressed her hand briefly and said wasn't it lovely and Francesca said that it was.

The reception was held in the hotel's east ballroom, which was a room Francesca had cleaned perhaps forty times and which she did not recognize in any particular. The walls were different and the floors were different and the light fixtures were different and the room was hung with garlands of something white and fragrant that she couldn't identify from across the room. She stood near the bar with a glass of wine and watched the room fill and tried to determine whether what she felt was anything she would be able to describe with precision if someone asked her to describe it. She decided it was not. It was too many things at once, which meant it was probably just the wine and the heat and the effort of the day.

Richard Caswell found her near the window.

"Francesca," he said. He had always said her name as if it were a word in a language he was not fluent in, carefully, with attention to the syllables.

"Richard." They embraced in the way they had embraced since Tom's funeral, with genuine warmth and a slight formality that acknowledged all the ways in which their connection had been altered by time and death and Donna. "She looked wonderful."

"She did." He was pleased in the uncomplicated way of fathers at the weddings of daughters they have been worried about. "I thought she was going to change her mind about the gardenias."

"Her mother's."

"Yes."

They stood looking at the room together for a moment. Donna was across the room talking to the groom's parents with the competent sociability of a woman who has been a second wife long enough to have developed a professional relationship with these kinds of events. Francesca watched her and felt the familiar mild guilt at liking her, which she had decided some time ago was not worth maintaining and maintained anyway.

"You took a room here," Richard said. It was not quite a question.

"Last night and tonight."

"I wasn't sure if you would."

"I wasn't sure either."

He looked at her with the expression she had always associated with the moments when Richard Caswell understood something he had not been told directly. He was a good lawyer because of this and she had always been grateful for it and occasionally had found it unwelcome. "How is it?" he said.

"Fine," she said. "It's a fine hotel."

"That isn't what I meant."

"I know it isn't." She drank some of her wine. At the far end of the room Margaret was laughing at something her new husband had said, and the laugh was the same laugh she had had at seven, sudden and unguarded and a little loud for the room. "I think I wanted to find out whether it had anything left to tell me."

"And?"

She looked at the room, which was full of flowers and light and the ordinary happiness of people at a wedding, the happiness that is partly genuine and partly a performance of the genuine and partly a recognition that the distinction does not matter as much as you once believed it did. She thought about the corridor and the fifteen seconds and the smell that had come after them, and she thought about Dolores Reyes and the baseboards and the pile of the carpet, and she thought about Bobby Restrepo and his radio at the service entrance, the radio playing something she had never been able to remember, only the fact of it playing.

"I think it's a different building," she said.

Richard Caswell nodded. He understood that this was not an architectural observation.

Across the room his daughter was dancing with her new husband, who held her with the careful attention of a man who has learned, or is learning, what it means to hold something he is afraid of losing. The fabric of Margaret's dress caught the light and the light in the room was warm and amber and not entirely unlike the light she remembered from this valley, the light that had seemed in the early mornings like a kind of provisional promise, as if the day were offering itself on condition.

Francesca finished her wine and set the glass on a passing tray and thought about going upstairs and thought about staying. She stayed.
