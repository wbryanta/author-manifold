The gauge at the spillway read four feet and falling when Maren drove the last stretch of county road to the reservoir's edge, her truck loaded with transit equipment and a week's worth of provisions she probably wouldn't need. She had requested the assignment herself, filling out a form she'd drafted specifically so that it could be filled out, checking boxes she had invented for the purpose, writing in the field marked *Justification* the words *Historical documentation required before further drawdown* in the neutral script she'd learned over seventeen years of municipal work—a handwriting that committed to nothing, promised nothing, bore no signature of the person who had made it.

The summer had been the kind that changes the names of things. Rivers became creek beds. Creek beds became archaeological sites. Lake Aldene, which was what the reservoir was called on current maps, though it had never been a lake, had pulled back from its banks through June and July until the marina sat stranded in shallow water the color of old jade, and the boat ramps ended in cracked mud, and by August the county extension office was warning about dust and advising residents to wet down their properties, as though any of them had water to spare. It was the worst drought in recorded history for this part of the state, though the phrase *recorded history* was doing considerable work there, since records in this county went back only ninety years, which meant they began just after the valley had been drowned.

Maren parked where the old access road terminated at a temporary fence the county had erected to discourage the curious. She sat for a moment with the engine off. Through the windshield she could see the reservoir's new shoreline, perhaps two hundred yards from where the old shoreline had been, and beyond it—she had to look directly, had to refuse the sideways glance her body kept offering her—the dark geometry of what the water had given back. Rooflines. She had known intellectually that some structures had survived intact enough to protrude, had read the hydrological reports and the historical commission's preliminary documentation, had seen photographs taken by a drone operator the county had sent out in late July. But the photographs had been taken from above, which made the drowned town look like a diagram of itself, clean and aerial and theoretical. What she saw now were actual rooflines, ragged with waterline and exposure, rising from the water at the distances she would have called, as a child, the distance between Mrs. Fournier's house and the school, the distance between the school and the gas station, which was called the gas station though it had not sold gas since before she was born.

She had been four years old when the valley was flooded. This was a fact she had always known the way she knew her own birthday—as information, as data, not as experience. Her mother had taken her in the car the week before the flooding, driven her through town in a loop that Maren could not remember as motion, only as a series of fixed images she was no longer sure she hadn't constructed from photographs: the white church, the yellow house with the screened porch, the stone wall along Main Street that someone had built in the 1890s and that stood, by her mother's account, with a solidity that made the whole flooding seem like a misunderstanding. She remembered her mother's hands on the steering wheel. She remembered that it was autumn and that the light was the particular amber of that season, the kind of light that makes everything look like it is already being remembered. She did not remember crying, but her mother had told her once that she cried for the whole drive, that she could not be consoled, that she had said over and over *I don't want it to go away*, and Maren had always found this story both plausible and completely foreign to her, because she had spent most of her adult life in the belief that things going away was simply the dominant condition of existence, and that the appropriate response was documentation.

She unlocked the fence with the key the county manager had given her and drove her truck down the access road and then, when the pavement ended, along the route she'd identified from topographical maps as the approximate path of the old county road. The ground was cracked and pale, silvered with mineral deposits left by the receding water, and it gave under her tires in a way that was not quite mud but was not quite solid either, a kind of provisional substance that hadn't decided yet what it was. She stopped where the road met what had been the edge of town—she could tell by the grade change in her topographical data, by the way the ground leveled into what would have been the commercial district's main approach—and she sat again for a moment before getting out.

The smell was the first thing. She had not anticipated the smell. It was layered and complicated in the way that only very old things are, a smell that contained within it categories of smell she did not have names for: the mineral smell of exposed stone, the organic smell of sixty years of sediment, something almost sweet beneath it that she thought might be wood, old waterlogged wood drying for the first time in decades, giving off whatever wood gives off when it remembers air. She stood beside her truck and breathed it and felt something happen in her chest that she diagnosed as professional interest and then corrected, standing alone in the dried bed of the reservoir with no one to perform competence for, to something more honest: a kind of structural grief, the way a building feels when a load-bearing wall is removed, everything still standing but the weight redistributed in ways that haven't manifested yet as damage.

She started where the maps told her to start, which was not where she wanted to start. This was the discipline she had learned and trusted. You began at a fixed point and you worked outward, systematically, because the alternative was to wander, and wandering in a professional context was a failure of method. She set up her total station at the northeastern corner of what her historical maps identified as the intersection of Aldene Road and Commerce Street, though those names existed now only in county records and in the memory of people who were old enough to have used them. She leveled the instrument. She confirmed her benchmarks. She began.

The foundations came up through the sediment like sentences interrupted mid-word. Some were complete—she could walk the entire perimeter of a structure, measure it, record it, enter the coordinates into her data logger with the same precision she brought to any other survey work. Others had collapsed inward, or had been partially demolished before the flooding, or had simply subsided in ways that suggested the sixty years under water had not been passive. She moved through the grid she had established, recording each structure, noting condition and materials and dimension. It was work she was good at. It produced clean data. She kept her face professional and her handwriting neutral and she did not allow herself to note, in any of her official records, the particular quality of feeling that accompanied her measurements of the foundation at the northeastern corner of what her historical maps called Second Street.

It was her grandmother's house. She had known it would be here. She had identified it on the historical maps weeks ago, had noted its coordinates, had assigned it a sequential number in her survey plan—Structure 14—and had placed it in the middle of her work schedule rather than the beginning or the end, which was itself a kind of acknowledgment she hadn't examined. She approached it as she had approached the others, instrument over her shoulder, data logger in her left hand. The foundation was limestone, which was why it had survived. The house above it was gone except for part of one wall, which still stood to a height of perhaps three feet, waterline clearly visible along its upper edge as a dark band of stain that she measured, by eye, at approximately fourteen feet above the current ground level. So the house had been under fourteen feet of water for most of its existence as a drowned thing.

She set up her instrument and began recording. Northwest corner: confirmed. She walked to the northeast corner, which had partially collapsed, and estimated its original position from the wall alignment. She entered the data. She walked to where the southeast corner should have been and found instead a shallow depression that she recorded as *probable corner, subsidence noted, structural integrity compromised.* She was, she understood, doing fine. She was doing the work correctly and efficiently and she was not stopping, which was the main thing, because she knew that if she stopped she would have to deal with the fact that she was standing in what had been her grandmother's kitchen.

She stopped.

The sediment in this corner of the foundation was a lighter color than elsewhere, and when she crouched to look at it she found this was because something had been here—tile, she thought, or some other flooring material that had leached minerals into the sediment before it disintegrated. She thought about the kitchen floor, which had been white and black in a checkerboard pattern that she remembered with a clarity that surprised her now, standing in the dried mud, the clarity of physical memory that bypasses whatever process converts experience to narrative. She had sat on that floor. She had sat on it repeatedly, at different ages, for reasons she could no longer reconstruct. Something about the coolness of the tile in summer. Something about being close to the ground.

Her grandmother had been a woman who cooked as though cooking were a form of argument. Not against anything in particular, or not against anything Maren could name, but with a forcefulness that suggested the process had stakes beyond nutrition. She had made biscuits by a method she described as *by feel*, which had frustrated Maren's mother and which Maren had understood even as a small child to be a description not of imprecision but of a different kind of knowledge—knowledge that lived in the hands and could not be written down without losing whatever made it true. Her grandmother had been seventy-one when the valley was flooded. She had moved into a house in the county seat that she described, for the remaining eleven years of her life, as *the other house*, and she had spent those eleven years completing the work of a life that was already mostly complete, gardening and baking and attending a church she found inferior to the one she'd left and occasionally, when Maren visited, telling her things about the valley that Maren had stored without knowing she was storing them.

She stood in the ruin of the kitchen and she thought about the biscuits. This seemed inadequate to the moment and she knew it was inadequate but she also knew that the biscuits were what she had, which was to say that grief does not arrive with the appropriate equipment, does not scale itself to the occasion, does not understand that when you are standing in the dried and mineral-stained remains of your grandmother's kitchen you should perhaps be feeling something more articulate than a sharp specific ache for food you last ate thirty years ago.

She took out her phone and photographed the lighter sediment. She recorded the location and labeled the file *Structure 14, interior, SE quadrant, probable flooring material* and she moved on.

By midday she had completed the commercial district, or what the historical records called the commercial district, which in practice had been perhaps eight buildings: the general store, the post office, the hardware store, a diner she had no memory of but whose foundation she found intact and which she measured with the same careful attention she gave everything else. There had been a small bank, but its foundation did not appear where her maps indicated it should, and she spent forty-five minutes searching the area and consulting her data before concluding that either her historical source was wrong about its location or the bank's foundation had been removed before the flooding, which was a thing that had sometimes been done, foundations cleared to prevent navigation hazards once the water rose. She recorded this uncertainty in her notes with language precise enough that whoever read the notes later would understand exactly what was known and what was inferred and what remained unknown.

She ate lunch sitting on the tailgate of her truck, looking out at the water, which was perhaps a quarter mile away now across the exposed lake bed. The reservoir's surface was flat and olive-colored in the midday light and it lay very still, the way bodies of water lie still when there is no wind and no life disturbing them, though she knew there was life in it, fish and algae and whatever else had adapted to a reservoir's artificial ecology in sixty years. The town had been drowned for longer than she had been alive. This seemed, as she sat eating her sandwich and looking at the water, like the most important fact, the one that reorganized the others around it. The town was not a thing she had lost. The town had continued, under the water, in a condition she had no language for because it was neither life nor death but something that happened to objects when they are removed from the use of the people they were built for—a kind of objecthood so complete it circled back around to something almost spiritual.

She had studied the history before coming. Not because she hadn't known it, but because she had wanted to know it again, with adult attention. The valley had been condemned in 1961, the dam constructed over two years, the filling begun in 1963 and completed, ahead of schedule, in 1964. The county had offered compensation to the landowners at rates that were described in contemporary accounts as fair and in subsequent accounts as a fraction of what the land was worth once the reservoir made the surrounding area desirable. Approximately three hundred families had been relocated. The cemetery had been exhumed and the bodies moved to a site on higher ground, which had been done carefully and respectfully according to all official accounts and about which her grandmother had said only once, in a tone that closed the subject permanently, *they didn't get everyone.* Maren had not asked what she meant. She had been perhaps twelve, old enough to understand that some questions were a form of intrusion.

She spent the afternoon on the residential streets west of Commerce, where the houses had been more modest and where, correspondingly, less had survived. Foundations here were often wood or rubble construction, and sixty years of submersion had not been kind to them. She found herself working in a landscape of suggestions rather than structures—a slight rise in the sediment that her instruments told her was a buried sill plate, a concentration of brick fragments that might be the remains of a chimney, a square depression that was the right size and shape to be a root cellar, or might be the right size and shape to be a number of other things. She recorded all of it. The data would go to the state historical commission and the county archive and eventually to a researcher somewhere who would correlate it with oral histories and deed records and whatever else survived, and from all of this something would be known that was not known before, which was what she had always believed about documentation: that it was a form of fidelity, that precision was a way of paying attention, that paying attention was the closest thing she had found to a practice of care.

Late afternoon, the light changed. It was the same amber light she associated with the drive her mother had taken her on, the week before the flooding, and she stood at what her instruments told her was the western edge of the survey area and she looked at the town and let herself see it the way the light suggested it, not as ruins but as a place that had existed, a place where people had woken in the mornings and complained about the weather and driven to work and disagreed with their neighbors and sat on kitchen floors for reasons they couldn't entirely explain. The stone wall that her mother had described as built with a solidity that made the flooding seem like a misunderstanding was still standing, or a portion of it was—she had found it near the church foundation early in the afternoon, and it had looked exactly like what it was, which was a stone wall that had been under fourteen feet of water for sixty years and had remained a stone wall anyway, through some stubbornness of construction that she could not account for with any of her instruments and so had recorded simply as *condition: intact* and moved on.

She walked back to it now, in the amber light, and she put her hand on it.

The stone was cold in the way that stone is always cold—not the cold of winter but the cold of depth, the cold of the earth's indifference to temperature, which is a different thing. She felt the mortar between the stones, still firm, damp at its core in a way that would dry out over the coming weeks as the exposure continued. She thought about the person who had laid this wall, whose name she did not know and whose name was probably not recorded anywhere in a way she could find. She thought about what it meant to build something well enough that it outlasted not just the context of its construction but the context of its reconstruction—the flooding, the decades, the drought, the return.

She had not cried yet and she did not cry now but she felt something shift in her chest in a way she recognized as a conclusion being reached by some faculty that was not quite conscious, some part of her that processed things at a rate slower than thought and delivered its findings, without announcement, in the form of a changed orientation to the world. What changed was this: she had come here thinking she was returning to something, and she understood now that this was wrong, not because the town wasn't here—it was here, more of it than she had expected, more solid and more real—but because she had never left it. She had been carrying it at four feet below the surface of herself for forty years, the same way the reservoir had been carrying it: under the surface, in the cold, in the dark, preserved imperfectly but preserved, and now the conditions had changed and there it was.

She would finish the survey. She would finish it correctly, with the care and precision the work required, and the data would be clean and the historical record would be augmented and the county would have what it needed before the rains came and the water rose again. She would do all of this. But she took a few more minutes with her hand on the stone wall in the amber light, long enough to let the cold of it move into her palm, long enough to let herself be present in the fact of it—the wall, the light, the dried bed of the reservoir with its mineral smell and its provisional ground, the rooflines of the other structures rising in the middle distance like a sentence she had been, all her life, waiting to finish reading.

Then she picked up her data logger and walked back toward Structure 14, because she had not yet measured the southwest corner, and the record she was building had to be complete.
