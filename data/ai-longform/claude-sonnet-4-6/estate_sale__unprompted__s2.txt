The first thing to go was the settee.

Margaret had not anticipated this—had anticipated many things, had lain awake in the Comfort Inn on Route 40 making her mental preparations, but not the settee specifically, not the image of two men in their thirties carrying it sidelong through the front door while she stood in the hallway holding a paper cup of coffee that had gone cold. The settee was celadon, or had been once, and her mother had called it by that name with a kind of reverence, celadon, as though the word itself were precious, and Margaret had grown up not knowing whether celadon was a color or a fabric or something she had misheard. She knew now. She had known for many years. It did not help.

"Ten dollars," said the woman who ran the estate sale company, a thick-armed woman named Patrice who wore her gray hair in a practical bun and moved through the house with the authority of someone accustomed to the condensed grief of others. "Someone got a steal." She said this without cruelty, only as fact, and made a notation on her clipboard.

Margaret watched the two men angle the settee past the door frame. One of them—the younger one, maybe twenty-five, with a beard that was more aspiration than achievement—looked up and caught her eye and said, "Beautiful piece." He meant it kindly.

"My mother called the color celadon," Margaret said.

The young man nodded as though this were a perfectly ordinary thing to say, which perhaps it was, and then he was gone, and the settee with him, out into the flat Ohio light of October.

The house was a two-story colonial on Birchwood Street, the kind of street that had once been genuinely aspirational and was now simply old, the trees having grown enormous over the decades so that the whole block existed in a state of magnificent, heavy shade even in autumn, when the leaves had gone and the branches made their complicated geometries against the pale sky. Margaret's mother had lived here for fifty-one years. Margaret had lived here for eighteen of those years and had then left, as children leave, first for Columbus and then for Philadelphia and then for the life she had assembled there: a husband, a practice—she was a therapist—two daughters who were now themselves in the process of leaving, a house in Chestnut Hill that had its own furniture, its own accumulated weight.

She had driven out alone. David had offered to come, had offered sincerely, and she had said no with equal sincerity, because she understood, without being able to fully explain it, that this was something that could not be witnessed by anyone who loved her. There were griefs that required an audience and griefs that required the opposite, and she had always been precise about which was which.

Her mother had died in August, at eighty-one, of a series of small strokes that had finally amassed into one definitive one. She had not suffered, the doctors said, or had not suffered in any way that registered on their instruments, which Margaret understood to be a different thing. She had driven out for the funeral, had driven out again in September to begin the sorting, and now this: the third drive, the final one, the liquidation. Her brother Terrence lived in Portland and had sent a check and his regrets in that order. She did not resent him for this, or resented him only in the mild, chronic way that had characterized their relationship for thirty years.

The dining room table was priced at forty-five dollars.

Margaret stood in the doorway of the dining room and watched a couple—perhaps sixty, perhaps younger, it was hard to tell—examine the table with the concentrated attention of people who were spending their own money carefully. The woman ran her palm along the surface, feeling for damage.

"There's a ring here," the woman said to her husband, or whoever he was.

"I can see it from here," the man said.

The ring was from a glass of iced tea that Margaret had set down in the summer of 1981, when she was seventeen, when she had come in from the backyard where she had been lying on the grass reading, because it was too hot to concentrate and she had wanted something cold. She had set the glass down and gone to look for something—she could not remember what—and had come back to find the ring already forming, the condensation having committed its slow damage, and had thought: Mother is going to be furious. Her mother had been furious. The argument that followed had been about the table but had been about other things too, as arguments in that house often were.

She almost told the couple this. She felt the words assembling themselves in some antechamber of her mind, ready to be spoken, and she chose not to speak them, because she understood that the information would be useless to these people, worse than useless, a weight they had not agreed to carry. The table would go into their dining room and they would put their own glasses on it and make their own rings and have their own arguments, and the ring from 1981 would be there underneath all of that, patient and invisible.

"We'll take it," the woman said.

Patrice made another notation.

Margaret moved through the house the way she had moved through dreams of the house, which were common dreams, the recurring kind—not nightmares, precisely, but dreams of a particular texture, slightly wrong in their proportions, the hallways a little longer than they should be, the light coming from the wrong direction. The actual house was smaller than the dream house, as actual places always are. She had read somewhere—had perhaps told patients this—that the brain expands the spaces of childhood in memory because a child's body is small and the world is correspondingly large, and what you remember is the ratio, not the measurement. Her patients found this interesting. She found it merely true.

In the kitchen, a woman was buying the percolator.

The percolator was chrome and very old and had been old when Margaret was a child, a survivor from some earlier decade of her mother's life, the early years of the marriage when her parents had been people Margaret could not quite imagine—young, unknowing, in the process of becoming the specific people they would be. The percolator had produced coffee every morning of Margaret's childhood, a thin, bitter coffee that her mother drank with two sugars and that Margaret had eventually learned to drink herself, and then unlearned in Philadelphia where the coffee was different, and then relearned each time she came back to visit, tasting something in the bitterness that was not quite flavor.

The woman buying the percolator was perhaps forty, with a child on her hip—a toddler, a girl, who was examining her own hand with the focused interest of toddlers. The woman was explaining to Patrice's assistant, a young man named Kyle, that she collected vintage kitchen items, that she had a whole shelf.

"It's beautiful," Kyle said, which was perhaps true. Margaret had not thought of it as beautiful but could see now that it might be.

The toddler looked at Margaret and Margaret looked back.

"Hi," Margaret said.

The toddler considered this for a long moment and then looked away, back to her hand.

Margaret went upstairs.

The upstairs was quieter. Patrice had told her that most buyers stayed on the ground floor, that the furniture up here was harder to move, that she should expect it to go slowly. Her mother's bedroom had been stripped already—the bed frame and mattress donated, the clothes sorted in September, the jewelry divided between Margaret and Terrence's wife in a transaction that had felt both necessary and obscene. What remained were a few pieces of furniture that Patrice had priced optimistically: a cedar chest, a vanity with a triptych mirror, a rocking chair that had sat in the corner for as long as Margaret could remember and that she associated with no specific memory but only with a quality of presence, the way certain objects achieve a kind of personhood through long residence.

She sat down on the cedar chest and looked at the vanity mirror.

The triptych showed her three versions of herself at slightly different angles—the slight leftward bias of her face that she had inherited from her father, the gray threading through the brown that had begun, she thought, earlier than was strictly fair, the fine lines around her eyes that her younger daughter Nora called distinguished and that Margaret sometimes believed and sometimes did not. She was fifty-three. This had seemed, in the abstract, like a reasonable age, and it still seemed so in the abstract, but here in this room, on this chest, it seemed like information about someone else.

She had sat at this vanity as a girl and watched her mother do her face—that was the phrase her mother used, do my face—with the focused attention of an artist working in a medium she had mastered. Her mother had been a beautiful woman in a particular way that took you a moment to register, not the immediate beauty of symmetry but something that emerged through observation, through the accumulation of small details that added to something greater than their sum. She had known she was beautiful and had treated this as a responsibility.

Margaret had inherited less of this than she would have liked and more of it than she sometimes admitted.

From downstairs came the sound of things being moved, voices negotiating, the particular percussion of an estate sale in progress. It was not an unpleasant sound. It had a kind of industry to it, a purposefulness, and she understood that this was what she had hired Patrice to provide: the conversion of grief into transaction, the making-useful of loss. This was, she reflected, not so different from what she did herself, in her office in Chestnut Hill, though she would never have described it that way to her patients, or to herself, usually.

The rocking chair was in the corner.

She had not sat in it as a child—it had seemed, even then, to belong to her mother in some proprietary way—and she did not sit in it now. Instead she looked at it. It was a simple chair, painted white, the paint worn to bare wood on the armrests where hands had rested over years and years. She did not know where it had come from. It was one of those objects whose origins had never been explained and which she had therefore always assumed had simply existed, in the way children assume the world existed before them in its current form, complete and coherent.

She should have asked. This was one of the specific shapes that grief took, she had learned—not only the loss of the person but the loss of every question you had not asked, the information that was gone now with finality, sealed away. She had asked her mother many things over the years, had been a more deliberate daughter than many, had known since she became a therapist that there were conversations worth having before it was too late. And yet there was always more. There was always one more question at the bottom of the pile.

Where did the rocking chair come from?

What did you think about, those mornings, at the vanity?

Were you happy? By which I mean: in the everyday sense, the Tuesday-afternoon sense, not on holidays or at peaks, but in the ordinary middle of things—were you happy, were you all right, were you okay?

Her mother would have answered briskly, or deflected with humor, or turned the question around in that maddening way she had of turning questions around. They had been close, in the way that women who are also stubborn can be close: warmly, combatively, with long stretches of ease interrupted by arguments that were sharp but not deep, that cut but did not wound permanently, that were over relatively quickly because neither of them could sustain a grudge without it becoming tedious. Margaret had cried at the funeral with a completeness that surprised her. She had not cried since, or had cried only in the practical, pressure-releasing way that she associated with fatigue. She did not know whether this meant she had processed her grief with professional efficiency or that she had not yet begun.

Downstairs, something was set down heavily. A voice said, "Sorry," to another voice.

She should go down. She was technically supposed to be present, to answer questions about provenance or history or whether items could be negotiated. Patrice had said she didn't need to hover but that it helped to have a family member available. She had been present and available all morning. She had stood in the living room as the sectional sofa was examined and purchased. She had watched her mother's collection of blue-and-white pottery go to a man who was clearly a reseller, who handled each piece with the quick, assessing touch of someone who was thinking about margin. She had answered questions: yes, that bookcase was solid oak; no, she didn't know exactly when the side tables were made, sometime in the seventies; yes, the lamps were included.

But she allowed herself a few more minutes in the room.

The light through the window was the particular light of Ohio in October, which she had spent decades away from and still recognized in her body before her mind caught up—a thin, clean light with something almost apologetic about it, the sun already beginning its retreat toward the low winter arc, the shadows longer than made sense for mid-afternoon. She had grown up in this light. She had gone to school in it and fallen in love in it and read her first adult novels and had her first significant heartbreak and learned to drive and failed her driving test and learned again, and all of it in this specific quality of light, and she had moved away and lived in other lights—Philadelphia had a different light, warmer, more amber, more sure of itself—and had not missed this one consciously. But it was hers. It had made her in some obscure photonic way.

There were footsteps on the stairs.

Patrice appeared in the doorway, her clipboard at her side, her expression the professionally compassionate one she likely kept available for these moments. "The cedar chest has some interest," she said. "Woman downstairs wants to know if there's anything left inside."

"I cleared everything out in September," Margaret said.

"Okay." Patrice made a note. "And the vanity—I've had two people ask. Sixty-five, I have it priced. One person offered fifty."

"Whatever you think is fair."

Patrice looked at her, not unkindly. "You don't have to stay up here," she said. "Some people find it easier if they go for a walk. Get some air. Come back at four when we're closing up."

"I know," Margaret said. "I'm all right."

Patrice nodded and went back downstairs, her footsteps measured and practical on the stairs.

Margaret stood and moved to the window. Below, on the street, a man was loading the settee—her mother's celadon settee—into the back of a pickup truck. He was alone now, the young bearded man, and he was managing it carefully, tipping it on its side to fit the angle, wrapping one end in a moving blanket. He worked with a quiet competence that she found, inexplicably, moving. He was taking care with it.

She watched until the truck pulled away.

Then she went to the vanity one last time and opened the small drawer on the right side—the only place she had not cleared in September, because she had opened it and found it empty and closed it again and moved on. But now she opened it and it was not entirely empty. There was a single item: a lipstick, uncapped, the color worn down to an oblique angle with use, the shade a coral-pink that her mother had worn for as long as Margaret could remember. The smell of it came up immediately—that particular waxy, floral smell that was the smell of her mother getting ready to go somewhere, the smell of Saturday evenings when her parents went out to dinner and Margaret and Terrence were left with a babysitter, the smell of church, of her mother bending to kiss her goodbye.

She held it for a long moment.

The practical thing to do was to throw it away. It was old, it was used, it was beyond any reasonable use. She put it in her jacket pocket.

Downstairs, the afternoon continued its work. She heard Patrice's voice and Kyle's voice and the voices of strangers assessing and deciding. She heard the front door open and close, open and close. She heard the accumulated sound of a household being dispersed, each object finding its new context, the settee already somewhere on Route 40 heading wherever it was heading, the dining room table soon to follow, the percolator beginning its second life on someone else's shelf. The house would be listed next week with a realtor named Susan Crabb whose sign she had seen all over town, whose face smiled from park benches. A young family would likely buy it, Patrice had said. The neighborhood was coming back. People wanted the old houses.

Margaret came downstairs.

In the living room, the sectional was gone and the room was enormous without it, the afternoon light falling across the hardwood in long rectangles that she had not seen in years. She stood in the center of the room and looked at the light on the floor and understood, with the particular certainty that comes without warning, that she would not stand in this room again. The next time she came to Birchwood Street—if she came—it would be to a different house, technically, a house that had been made other by the presence of other people's furniture and other people's choices. The house she was standing in now existed only in this moment, in these last hours before it was fully emptied and ceased, in any meaningful sense, to be her mother's. After today it would be a building.

She was not sure how she felt about this. She probed the feeling carefully, the way she would help a patient probe a feeling, asking it to be more specific, to articulate itself.

She felt—untethered was the word that came, but it wasn't quite right. She felt the loss of the anchor, which was different. You could feel the loss of an anchor and still be holding ground.

A woman came in from the dining room with a small figurine that had lived on the bookshelf for decades—a ceramic cat, white, with improbably blue eyes, the kind of object Margaret had looked at her whole childhood without seeing. "Is this included?" the woman asked. She was being careful, the kind of careful that comes from experience with estate sales, from knowing that sometimes items are off the table without being marked.

"Yes," Margaret said. "Take it."

The woman thanked her and moved on.

Margaret went to the kitchen and stood at the sink and looked out the window at the backyard, which was overgrown now, the garden her mother had kept until four or five years ago gone back to something wilder, the hydrangeas untended and enormous, the ornamental grass having seeded itself along the fence. She had not looked at the backyard today. She had kept herself inside. But now she looked, and it was beautiful in the way that abandoned things sometimes are, having found their own logic without the intervention of human intention.

She thought about her daughters—Nora in her first apartment in Brooklyn, twenty-four and figuring out the city; Claire in her last year of college in Ann Arbor, already angling toward London for graduate school. She thought about David in the house in Chestnut Hill, probably reading, probably texting her to see how she was doing and then deciding not to, knowing her. She thought about the drive back tomorrow, the long corridor of I-76, the way Ohio would give way to Pennsylvania slowly and then all at once.

She thought about her mother at thirty, standing at this same window looking at that same yard when it was ordered and tended and her children were small and everything was still ahead. She could not know what her mother had thought about, standing there. She had lost her chance to ask.

But she could stand here herself, now, at fifty-three, and look at the hydrangeas which were enormous and done with their blooming and still, in the flat October light, something worth looking at. She could put her hand in her pocket and feel the waxy cylinder of the lipstick against her fingers. She could hear, from the front of the house, the sound of strangers making their decisions.

She stayed at the window a long time, longer than was necessary, longer than served any purpose she could have named. Then she filled a glass from the tap—the water tasted the same as it always had, the same mineral plainness—and drank it standing up, the way her mother had always drunk water, and rinsed the glass, and set it on the counter for whoever would take it, and went back out to be useful.
