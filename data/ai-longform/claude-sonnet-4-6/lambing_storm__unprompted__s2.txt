The snow had started around three in the afternoon, those first few flakes that looked almost ornamental against the brown fields, and Nora had stood at the kitchen window watching them with the particular stillness of someone deciding something. By four it was coming sideways off the ridge. By five the power was gone.

She found the candles in the drawer under the microwave where she kept batteries and rubber bands and the good scissors that nobody was supposed to use for paper. She found the headlamps in the mudroom and charged them both, thinking, before she remembered they ran on batteries and the charging she was thinking of was last summer, before, when Martin had bought them off some website and shown her the base with the little blue light. She put them on the shelf anyway. She told herself they would be enough.

Danny was in the lambing shed when she came out. She could see the light through the gaps in the siding—they had been meaning to re-side that wall since October and now the snow was driving through those gaps and she tried not to think about what else they had been meaning to do. The path between the house and the shed was already shin-deep and she walked it with her arms out slightly, the way you do when you don't trust your footing, the way she had watched her own mother walk across icy church parking lots in good shoes and thought, I will never move like that, I will never be that old, and now here she was, forty-four, moving exactly like that through the dark.

She pushed the shed door with her shoulder and the warmth hit her like something physical, something she had not realized she needed until it arrived.

Danny was at the back pen. He was sixteen, nearly seventeen, and he had his father's height but not yet his father's solidity, so he stood the way tall thin young men stand, slightly apologetic about the space they're taking up. He had a ewe down in the straw and he was talking to her in a low voice and he looked up when his mother came in and said, "I've got two in the chute, she's having trouble, and there's another one down at the far end that I think is close."

"Okay," Nora said.

She set her bucket down and came and knelt beside him. The ewe was a Suffolk cross they had named Nothing because last year she had lambed without incident and given them nothing to worry about and they had been superstitious about renaming her. Nothing was lying on her side, her eyes showing more white than she liked to see, and she was breathing in that labored manner that meant the lambs were positioned wrong or she was simply exhausted or both.

"How long has she been like this?"

"Twenty minutes maybe. First one was coming and then it stopped."

"Did you check position?"

"I was waiting for you."

She pulled off her coat and her flannel overshirt and pushed the overshirt into the clean straw at the edge of the pen and rolled up the sleeve of her thermal undershirt to the shoulder. She lubricated her hand from the bottle on the shelf and spoke to Nothing while she worked, not words exactly, just sounds, the vowelly murmur that Martin had called her sheep voice, which he had said without any condescension, with what she now understood had been genuine admiration, had been love, one of the small particular forms love took after twenty years.

She found the first lamb's head and one leg and the other leg folded back along its body. She worked carefully. Danny had his hands on the ewe's haunches and was watching his mother's face to understand what was happening since he could not see her hands.

"Leg's back," she said. "Give me a second."

She maneuvered in the dark enclosed heat of it and brought the leg forward and repositioned the head and felt the ewe's next contraction work with her and the lamb came, a rush of fluid and then the small body in her hands, and she cleared the membrane from its face and set it in the straw and Danny was already there with the towel, rubbing vigorously, and the lamb startled and shook and Nora said, "Good. One more in there. Get the iodine ready."

The second lamb came easier, smaller, a little weak-looking but alive, and they worked the two lambs around to Nothing's head and watched her begin to nose them, that recognition that always seemed to Nora like something being confirmed rather than discovered, as if the ewe were saying yes, yes, these are mine, I thought as much.

"Good girl," Danny said, to the ewe or to his mother or to the room.

Nora sat back on her heels and looked at her arm. The cold when she rolled her sleeve down was a small bright shock.

The wind hit the shed in gusts that made the whole structure creak in a way she did not love. The candles in the lanterns they had hung from the central beam swayed in the drafts and threw unsteady light across the pens, and in this light the ewes and their new lambs looked ancient, looked like something painted on a wall in a place where people had needed to paint what mattered.

"How many tonight do you think?" Danny asked. He was at the lamb bar, mixing the colostrum supplement in case Nothing couldn't keep up with twins.

"I counted six showing sign this morning. We'll see."

"Dad would have known exactly."

She did not answer immediately. She was checking the lamb, the smaller one, pressing her finger to its gum to check color, lifting it slightly to gauge its weight. She set it back against its mother's belly.

"He kept better notes than me," she said. "That's true."

"I wasn't—I didn't mean it as a—"

"I know what you meant." She stood up, her knees complaining. "He would have known and he would have been right and we'll manage without knowing and that's fine. Go check on the one at the far end."

He went. She listened to his boots in the straw and the wind in the siding and the various vocalizations of forty sheep in a confined space, that layered sound that was somehow both chaotic and deeply rhythmic, like being inside a large and mediocre orchestra that was nonetheless playing something real.

She went down the pen row and checked on the three ewes that had lambed earlier in the week and their lambs, now strong-legged and imperious. She checked the ewe with the single lamb that had been slow to nurse and found her nursing now, good, and she checked the water buckets and broke the skin of ice that had formed on two of them. The pipes along the south wall were insulated but the insulation was old and she had been watching the temperature forecast for a week with the specific dread of someone who knows what a problem looks like before it fully arrives.

"Mom." Danny's voice from the far end. "She's going."

She walked quickly.

The ewe at the far end was a Corriedale they called Yolanda, a ridiculous name that Martin had assigned on a whim three years ago because she had, he said, a Yolanda energy, which they had never fully defined but had come to accept as self-evident. Yolanda was standing, which was a good sign, and she had already begun to lamb, a single lamb whose front hooves and nose Nora could see at the opening, everything where it should be.

"She's got it," Nora said. "Just watch. Don't crowd her."

They stood back and watched Yolanda deliver her lamb into the straw—one prolonged effort, the lamb collapsing out in a heap of dark wet, and the ewe turning immediately, already talking in that low chuckling sound that Nora thought of as the most purely animal thing she knew, more animal than any bellow or bleat, that low private voice for a new lamb, and the lamb lifting its head, stunned by the specific astonishment of being alive.

Danny laughed softly. He did this every year, laughed at the lamb lifting its head, and it occurred to Nora that she had been watching him laugh at this for as long as she could reasonably remember, for most of his life, and that this was one of the things that was still true.

They worked the next hour steadily. The storm did not let up. The temperature inside the shed held because of the animals but she could feel the cold trying the walls, pressing in at the gaps in the siding like something patient and determined. She sent Danny to the house twice, once for more towels and once for the emergency pellets they kept for exactly this, the late-storm laminitis risk, and each time she watched him push out into the dark and felt the particular tension of waiting for him to return, which was not quite fear, or was fear but very quiet, very leashed.

The third time the door opened it was Danny again and he brought cold air and also Martin's old oilskin, which she had not asked him to bring.

"I grabbed it off the hook," he said. "Yours isn't very warm."

She took it. She put it on. It was too big for her, it had always been too big for her even when Martin was wearing it because he had bought it one size too large for the layering, and the weight of it was familiar in a way she was not going to think about right now. She turned back to the pen she was working.

"The ewe with the spot," Danny said. "She's been circling."

"I see her. Let's watch a minute."

Spotted Face was one of their newer ewes, this her second lambing, last year unremarkable. She was pacing the perimeter of her pen in a way that was somewhere between distress and the concentrated inwardness of early labor. Nora watched her and the ewe watched her back, and Nora thought, as she often did at this precise moment, about the strange asymmetry of the knowledge between them—she knew what was coming and the ewe did not, or knew it differently, knew it in the body but not in the mind if those things could even be separated, and she felt the usual tenderness and also the usual frustration that she couldn't convey anything useful.

"She looks scared," Danny said.

"First lamb was hard. She might remember."

"Do they remember?"

"I think they remember more than we think they remember."

He was quiet. Then: "Do you think that about people too?"

She looked at him across the pen. In the candlelight he looked younger than he was, or a different kind of young, not a teenager's careless youth but something more considered and more fragile. He was asking something underneath the question he was asking.

"I think we remember more than we think we remember," she said. "And I think some of what we remember we can't get to directly. It's there but sideways."

"Like what?"

"Like muscle memory. Like how your hands know things."

He looked down at his hands.

"I've been dreaming about him," he said. "But in the dreams he's not dead, he's just somewhere I can't find. I keep looking in rooms."

"That's a common dream."

"It doesn't feel common. It feels very specific."

"I know," she said. "That's what I mean. It feels specific but it's also—there's a reason so many people have it. It's just what the mind does when it's trying to work something out that can't be worked out."

"Does it stop?"

She thought about the honest answer. She was committed to honesty with him in a way that was sometimes a form of respect and sometimes a form of her own need, her inability to perform certainty she didn't have.

"The dreams change," she said. "That's the best I can tell you."

Spotted Face went down in the corner and began pushing and they went to her.

It was a long one. The lamb was big and Spotted Face was small and the whole thing took forty minutes and required Nora's intervention twice—once for repositioning and once simply to hold the ewe still because she was fighting herself, fighting the process that was going to get her through it, and Nora held her and spoke to her and felt the ewe's panic moving through her own hands, something she had never fully gotten used to and hoped she wouldn't, because to get used to it would be to stop being present for it.

Danny held the lamp. His arm got tired and he switched arms. He did not complain.

The lamb came and it was enormous, proportionally startling, and for one bad second it was still and did not respond, and Nora rubbed it hard and blew in its nostrils and rubbed again and said something under her breath that was not quite a prayer but occupied the same space where a prayer would go, and the lamb gasped, and moved.

Danny made a sound she didn't have a word for.

The candles were burning low by midnight. She found the spares in the bucket she'd brought and replaced them, and the new light steadied the shed and she looked around at what they had: three sets of twins, two singles, all the lambs upright and nursing or having nursed, all the ewes with adequate color and respiration. Outside the wind was still driving the snow against the siding and the temperature had to be well below freezing but in here it was warm and full of sound and the smell of hay and lanolin and birth, which was, she had always thought, an honest smell, a smell that didn't try to be anything else.

She sat on the hay bale at the end of the row and Danny sat next to her. He had hay in his hair and there was something dark on his jacket she decided not to examine.

"When did you last eat?" she said.

"Lunch."

"That was twelve hours ago."

"I know."

"When this calms down I'll make eggs."

"It's almost one in the morning."

"Eggs work at one in the morning."

He leaned his head back against the siding and looked up at the ceiling where the candle shadows moved. She could hear him breathing. She could hear all of it, the breathing of the animals and her son and the storm and the particular creek and shift of the building in the wind.

"I thought about quitting," he said. "In January. I thought about—there's a program, like a school in Vermont, you board there, you do regular school but also outdoor education and sustainability stuff, and I thought about applying."

She looked at him. He was still looking at the ceiling.

"I didn't know that," she said.

"I didn't tell you. Because I knew what it would mean. If I went."

"What would it mean?"

"That you'd have to hire someone. Or sell. One or the other."

"Danny."

"I'm not going. I decided in February. I'm not going."

She was quiet for a moment. She was choosing words with care.

"You can't stay because of me. That's not—I don't want that to be the reason."

"It's not the only reason."

"What are the other reasons?"

He turned to look at her. In the low light his eyes were dark and serious and she saw in them something she recognized because she lived with it, some compound of grief and stubbornness and love and the refusal to abandon something just because it had become hard. She saw, in other words, herself.

"I don't want to go somewhere else right now," he said. "Maybe someday. But not right now."

"The program sounds good."

"It'll run again next year."

She nodded. She reached over without thinking and put her hand on the back of his neck, a brief pressure, and then took it away, and he didn't pull away, and neither of them said anything about it.

One of the ewes in the nearest pen began shifting in a way that suggested she might be starting, and Nora watched her for a moment, reading the signs, and decided she was just restless, just reacting to the storm.

"Your father would have checked that ewe twice," she said.

"I was going to check her."

"I know. He would have been right to check her but I'm telling you she's fine."

"How do you know?"

"Thirty years."

Danny stood and went and checked the ewe anyway, carefully and thoroughly, and came back.

"She's fine," he said.

"I know."

"You could have just—you could let me check things."

"I did let you check it."

"You told me she was fine first."

"And she was."

He sat back down on the hay bale and she could feel him deciding whether to stay annoyed or let it go, and she did not help him decide because that was not her job, that was his, and he let it go, and then they were just sitting there together in the lambing shed at one in the morning in the middle of a storm that was not yet over.

She thought about Martin. She thought about how grief, five months in, had stopped being the acute thing it had been and started being more environmental, more like weather or altitude, something that affected how everything felt rather than something that descended in discrete events. She thought about that and then she thought about whether that was better or worse, and then she stopped deciding between better and worse because that was a useless frame, because it just was, because this was the only year they had, this specific April with its late blizzard and its power outage and its lambs coming regardless.

She had told people, in the fall, the various people who had said things to her: she had told them that she would be fine, that they would be fine, meaning her and Danny. She had said it in the automatic way you say things when people need you to say them. And now, in the shed, in the warmth of all those animal bodies and all that new life and her son beside her with hay in his hair, she thought: I might have been right. Not fine in the way she'd meant it when she'd said it, not fine like recovered or healed or returned to baseline, but fine like still here, like upright, like continuing. Like the lamb gasping when she hadn't expected it to gasp. Like that.

Near two o'clock one of the earlier ewes showed signs of milk fever and they spent forty minutes on her, the calcium drip, the waiting, the gradual improvement, and by the time the ewe was standing again the storm had dropped to a steady moderate snowfall, still coming but without the violence, the wind gone off wherever wind went.

Danny stepped outside to assess and came back in with snow on his shoulders.

"I can see the house light," he said. "Power's back."

"Good."

"Still like a foot out there. Maybe more."

"Go make the eggs," she said. "Bring them back out here."

"Out here?"

"I want to watch them for another hour."

He started to say something about beds and school, which started at seven-forty, and then he looked at his mother and decided against it, all of this passing across his face in about a second and a half, and he said, "Scrambled or fried?"

"Scrambled," she said. "Lots of cheese."

He went out. She listened to the snow accept his footsteps and then heard nothing but the sheep.

She walked the row slowly, each pen, each lamb, checking again what she already knew. She stopped at Nothing's pen and watched the twins sleeping in the straw, the compressed boneless sleep of newborns, their ribs rising and falling in a rhythm she found, as always, quietly extraordinary—the fact of it, the simplicity of it, the two of them not knowing yet about any of it, just breathing, just warm.

When Danny came back he had scrambled eggs in a cast iron pan with a dish towel wrapped around the handle, and two forks stuck in the pan because he had forgotten plates, which she forgave him for instantly and completely.

They ate standing at the end of the pen row, passing the pan back and forth, and the eggs were good, too much salt but good, and the candles were still going in the lanterns, their light thinner now, almost ceremonial against the light coming in from outside, the storm-light off the snow, which was a cold blue that meant morning was closer than she thought.

"You got the cheese," she said.

"You asked for it."

"I wasn't sure if we had any."

"There was a lot, actually."

She handed the pan back. He finished what was left in four bites with the automatic appetite of someone his age, someone who needed that much fuel just to run, and she watched him eat and thought about the school in Vermont with something that was mostly relief and only a little sorrow, and she thought about Martin checking a ewe twice out of love for the animal and love for the work and love for the getting things right, and she thought about her own hands and what they knew, and then she stopped thinking for a little while and just stood in the shed with her son and the new lambs and the very old smell of everything continuing.
