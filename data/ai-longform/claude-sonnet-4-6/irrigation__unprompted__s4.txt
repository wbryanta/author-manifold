The pipe had split somewhere near the old walnut trees, and by the time Marcus found it the ground around the roots had turned to dark paste that sucked at his boots when he walked. He stood looking at it for a moment, at the way the water had been bleeding out unseen, probably for days, and felt something move through him that was not quite grief and not quite anger but some compound of the two that he had no name for. He called out to his brother.

Daniel was up near the pump house, wrestling with the pressure gauge that had been giving false readings since April. He heard Marcus call and didn't answer right away. He finished what he was doing, which was nothing, really—turning the gauge housing in his hands, looking at it as though it might explain itself—and then he set it down on the wooden shelf their father had built inside the pump house door and walked down through the rows.

The orchard in June could still be beautiful if you let yourself see it that way. The trees were old, most of them, planted by their grandfather in rows that weren't quite straight, rows that bent slightly as though the old man had been pulled by something as he worked, some distraction or intention that he'd never explained to anyone. The apples were on and small and hard and green, and the light came through the leaves in a particular way that Marcus associated with childhood so strongly that sometimes he couldn't look at it directly.

"Where," Daniel said when he reached him.

Marcus pointed. They both looked at the pipe. It was a section of the old clay line that their father had been meaning to replace for a decade, and it had finally let go along a seam, and the crack ran about eight inches and was leaking steadily, not fast but with a kind of patient persistence that suggested it would keep on indefinitely.

"We'll need to cut it out," Marcus said. "Put in a coupler. Maybe two feet of the PVC we have in the shed."

"I know what we need to do."

"I'm not saying you don't."

Daniel crouched down and pressed two fingers into the mud near the pipe, testing its depth. "The roots have gotten into the clay up here. You can see it. This whole section is probably compromised."

"Probably."

"So we fix this one and something else lets go next week."

"Maybe," Marcus said. "Probably. Yes."

Daniel stood up and wiped his hands on his jeans. He was forty-one, two years older than Marcus, and he had their father's way of holding his jaw when he was thinking through something he'd already decided. Their father had done it when he was about to tell you something you didn't want to hear, setting the jaw and then looking off at a middle distance, as though the words were located somewhere out there in the air and he was simply reading them.

"Go get the hacksaw," Marcus said. "I'll get the PVC from the shed."

"Marcus."

"What."

"We should talk about this."

"We've talked about it."

"We've talked around it. That's different."

Marcus looked at him. The light was coming hard now, nearly ten in the morning, and the heat was already establishing itself in the way it did out here, not the wet heat of town but a dry, absolute heat that pressed down from above and rose up from the ground simultaneously and left you feeling enclosed. "Get the hacksaw," he said. "We can talk while we work."

They worked. That was the thing about the two of them—they had always been able to work together regardless of what was happening between them, some physical competence they shared that existed beneath argument or resentment or love, a layer of the relationship that was purely practical and that neither of them had ever been able to damage no matter how hard they'd tried. Daniel held the pipe steady while Marcus cut, and Marcus cut cleanly and without wasting motion, and they fit the coupler together with the efficiency of men who had been repairing this same land in this same way for most of their lives.

"Hendricks made another offer," Daniel said. He was packing soil back around the pipe, pressing it in with both palms.

"I know."

"You talked to him?"

"He left a message. I didn't call back."

"He went up. From the first offer. He went up forty thousand."

Marcus sat back on his heels and looked at his brother. "When did he tell you this."

"Last week. I was going to tell you."

"You were going to tell me."

"I'm telling you now."

The sound of the water moving properly through the repaired line was audible for a moment, a low underground gurgling, and then it faded. Marcus picked up a clod of the dark soil and turned it in his hand. His grandfather had brought topsoil in by the truckload to build this ground up, had composted for thirty years, had treated the land as something to be constructed rather than merely used. The soil was extraordinary—you could see it in the color, in the weight of it. That was what Hendricks wanted. Not the trees, which were old and uneconomical, and not the house, which needed more work than anyone could reasonably justify, but the soil and the water rights and the three hundred acres that could be cleared and planted in something profitable. Grapes, probably. Everyone was planting grapes.

"What do you want me to say?" Marcus asked.

"I want you to say whether we're going to sell or not."

"I don't want to sell."

"You've said that. I've heard you say it. But I want you to tell me what we do instead."

"We do what we're doing. We fix the line."

"And next month?"

"We fix whatever breaks next month."

Daniel stood up and took his hat off and held it against his thigh. He had gone almost entirely gray in the past two years, which Marcus had noticed but not remarked on, gray at thirty-nine and forty and now forty-one, and it made him look like a different person sometimes, a person Marcus was still in the process of recognizing. "I have two kids," Daniel said. "Emma starts college in three years. Do you understand what I'm telling you?"

"I understand."

"I put thirty thousand into this place last year. My thirty thousand. My wife's thirty thousand, technically, which she has not mentioned once, not once, but which I think about constantly."

"I know."

"I don't think you do know, because if you knew you wouldn't be able to stand there and tell me we just fix whatever breaks."

Marcus set the clod down and stood up. He was taller than his brother by two inches and thinner by thirty pounds and he had always felt that the physical difference between them was a kind of statement about the difference in their lives, though he'd never been able to articulate what it meant. Daniel had stayed. Daniel had the house and the wife and the children and the weight of all that. Marcus had come back four years ago after the end of his marriage and his departure from the firm in Sacramento, had come back with nothing much but the quarter ownership of this land that his name had always been on, and he'd been living in the small house at the east end of the property that had once been the foreman's quarters and he'd been doing this, repairing things, trying to make the orchard viable again, and he knew that Daniel's patience with the arrangement had been long and genuine but that it was not infinite.

"I'm not going to apologize for loving this place," Marcus said.

"Nobody's asking you to apologize for it."

"You're asking me to give it up, which is the same thing."

"I'm asking you to think practically."

"I think practically every day. I think practically about what it would take to get the Gravensteins on the south section producing again. I think practically about what kind of drip system we could put in that wouldn't cost us the whole operating budget. I think practically about whether we bring in someone to do the pruning or do it ourselves. That's what I think about practically."

"That's not what I mean."

"I know it's not what you mean."

They stood facing each other in the row with the walnut trees behind them and the apple trees in their imperfect lines going away in both directions. A hawk was working the upper field, riding a thermal in long slow circles. Marcus watched it for a moment.

"Dad didn't want us to sell," he said.

"Dad also didn't want to die at sixty-seven, and yet."

"That's not—"

"I know what you're going to say and I'm telling you that what he wanted is not a trump card, Marcus. He's been gone for four years. We've been bleeding money for four years. The math doesn't change because of what he wanted."

"The math," Marcus said. The word felt wrong in the mouth, reductive, like calling something that had weight and history and color by a name that only described its edges.

"Yes. The math."

"There are other options."

"Name one."

"We could lease the grazing rights on the back forty to Portillo. He's been asking for two years."

"That's twelve thousand a year if Portillo's generous. That's nothing."

"It's not nothing."

"It's nothing relative to what we need."

Marcus started walking north along the row, toward the pump house, and Daniel followed him without being asked because they had more to check, there were always more things to check, the land had an endless capacity for requiring attention. At the pump house Marcus looked at the pressure gauge Daniel had left on the shelf. He picked it up and looked at it. The mechanism inside was gummed up with mineral deposit, the needle stuck somewhere in the middle of its range, optimistic and false.

"The Minettis sold," Daniel said. "You know what they got?"

"I don't want to hear what they got."

"They got two point three million for four hundred acres. They're—Carol Minetti is traveling. She went to Portugal. She went to Portugal, Marcus, and she sends pictures on her phone."

"Good for Carol Minetti."

"Frank Minetti is sixty-four years old and he's not worrying about irrigation lines."

"Frank Minetti's grandfather is turning in his grave."

"Frank Minetti doesn't care."

"No. He doesn't." Marcus set the pressure gauge back on the shelf. "That's the whole thing, isn't it. He doesn't care. His grandfather planted those trees and Frank sold them and now someone's going to pull them out and plant grapes and in ten years the land will have no memory of what it was. That's what happens."

"That's always what happens. That's what happening means."

"I don't accept that."

"You don't accept it," Daniel said, and the words came out flat, not contemptuous exactly but tired, carrying the tiredness of someone who had been patient for a long time. "Tell me what you accept, then. Tell me what alternative version of reality you're operating in where we can afford to keep doing this."

"I accept that this land made us. That we are—in some way that I don't know how to explain but that I believe—that we are continuous with this land, with the work that was done here before us, and that to sell it is to sever something that can't be repaired."

The silence between them was long.

"I believe that too," Daniel said finally. "I want you to know that I believe that. It's not that I don't."

"Then—"

"But I have to believe it alongside other things. You understand? I have to believe it alongside Emma and alongside Grace and alongside the fact that I'm forty-one years old and I've given everything I have to this place and it keeps needing more than I have to give. You get to believe it purely. That's the difference between us. Your belief in this place doesn't cost you anything except money you don't have, and my belief costs me things I can't afford to lose."

Marcus looked at his brother. Something in the speech had landed in him in a place that hurt, not because it was cruel but because it was accurate and because accuracy, in certain circumstances, was its own kind of cruelty.

"That's not fair," he said.

"I know it isn't."

"I lost things too. Coming back here was—it wasn't—" He stopped.

"I know," Daniel said. His voice was different now. "I know it wasn't nothing."

They stood in the pump house doorway with the heat coming off the metal roof above them and the smell of the dust and the particular mineral smell of the water system around them. Marcus thought about the morning he had arrived four years ago with two bags and a bitterness he'd been carrying since Sacramento and the way Daniel had met him in the driveway without saying anything, had simply taken one of the bags and carried it to the small house and helped him move in, and how that wordless help had been one of the more sustaining things anyone had ever done for him.

"I don't want to spend the rest of my life wishing I'd stayed," Marcus said.

"I know."

"If we sell and I spend the next twenty years thinking about what was here—"

"I know."

"Do you? Because it seems like you've already decided."

Daniel looked at him. "I haven't decided. If I'd decided I wouldn't be standing here arguing with you, would I. I'd have taken Hendricks's call and moved forward."

"But you want to."

"I want to not be afraid about money for the first time in ten years. Yes. That's what I want. Is that so incomprehensible?"

It wasn't incomprehensible. That was the difficulty. If Marcus could have found his brother's position incomprehensible he could have dismissed it, could have stood on his own belief and let the other thing fall away. But he understood every word of it and he understood the weight behind the words and he could not dismiss it.

They walked out from under the pump house roof and started back along the row. There was more to check—a section of drip line near the Fujis that Marcus suspected was kinked, and the junction box at the base of the south slope that had been throwing warnings. They walked and didn't speak for a while and the orchard moved around them in the heat, the leaves turning in whatever breath of air came through the gaps in the canopy.

"What did grandfather call the big Gravenstein," Marcus said. "The one at the corner of the east row."

"The senator," Daniel said. He almost smiled.

"Because it stood taller than the others."

"Because it stood taller and it never produced as much as it should have and he thought it was getting above itself."

Marcus looked toward the east rows. "I used to climb it. Before I knew better. Before dad told me not to."

"You fell out of it."

"I didn't fall out of it. I came down faster than I intended."

"You came down faster than you intended and landed on your arm and cried for an hour."

"I didn't cry for an hour."

"You cried for an hour. I was there."

The ease of it, the way the memory passed between them like something handed from one set of hands to another. That ease was also the land, Marcus thought. That was also what would be lost. The specific way two people remembered the same thing and argued about it affectionately and recognized each other in the argument—that was rooted here, in this place, in the particular configuration of these trees and this light and this ground, and it could not be replicated in some other location. He did not know how to say this to his brother without it sounding like sentiment, without it sounding like the kind of impractical love that Daniel had been gently and then less gently suggesting he couldn't afford.

They reached the Fujis and found the drip line kinked just where Marcus had thought it would be, caught under a root that had migrated. They worked the line free. It took fifteen minutes of careful pressure, of fingers working in the soil, and then the line ran straight and Marcus repressurized the section and they stood back and watched the drippers doing their small repetitive work against the base of each tree.

"What if we brought in someone," Marcus said. "A consultant. Someone who looks at operations like this and figures out—"

"We had someone. The guy from Davis. The extension program."

"He was looking at yield optimization. I mean someone who looks at the business. At whether there's a model that works."

"That costs money we don't have."

"I have some money."

Daniel looked at him.

"Not much," Marcus said. "But some. From the settlement. I've been holding it."

"You've been holding money."

"In case there was something that needed it."

"In case." Daniel shook his head, not in anger, in something more like wonder. "You've been sitting on money while I—" He stopped himself. "How much."

"About eighteen thousand."

"Marcus."

"I know."

"Why didn't you—"

"Because if I put it in I become more obligated to the outcome and I wasn't sure I could handle—" He stopped. "I wasn't sure I could stay if it failed. If I put everything in and it still failed I didn't think I could—"

Daniel looked at his brother for a long time. The hawk was still up, working its circuit, patient and purposeless looking from below. "You've been protecting yourself," he said. There was nothing accusatory in it. It was simply an observation, careful and not unkind.

"Yes."

"While asking me to commit."

"Yes."

The honesty of it sat between them. Marcus felt exposed in a way he hadn't in a long time, stripped of the story he'd been telling himself about his own motives, which was that he was here out of love and dedication, that he was the one fighting for what mattered. He was that. But he was also this other thing, a man who had kept something back, a man who had let his brother carry more than his share because he was afraid.

"It's not enough," Daniel said. "Eighteen thousand. Even if we put it in."

"I know."

"It doesn't change the math."

"Maybe not."

A long silence. One of the drippers near them was running slightly fast, a small consistent bead rather than the proper intermittent drop, and Marcus leaned down and adjusted the emitter and the dripper corrected itself and ran true.

"Don't answer Hendricks yet," Marcus said.

"Marcus—"

"Just. Give me sixty days. Let me find someone, a consultant, let me put together an actual case. Not what I want but what can actually work, or whether anything can actually work. Sixty days and I'll have real numbers and we can look at them together, and if the numbers say what you think they'll say then we talk about Hendricks seriously, we talk about it without me fighting you at every step. I'll come to the table."

Daniel looked at the trees, at the light coming through them, at all of it. Marcus watched his brother's face and saw in it the labor of a man holding multiple true things simultaneously, trying to find the arrangement in which they could coexist.

"Sixty days," Daniel said.

"Sixty days."

"And you put the eighteen thousand in."

"Yes."

"Into the operating account. Not held back."

"Yes."

Daniel picked up the hacksaw they'd been carrying back and forth all morning and looked at it. "The junction box on the south slope," he said. "We should do that before the heat gets any worse."

"All right."

They started down the row, moving south, moving through the imperfect lines of old trees that their grandfather had planted slightly wrong and their father had maintained with something close to devotion and that were still here in their obstinate and uneconomical beauty, bearing their small hard green apples in the June heat, drawing water through the mended pipes from the ground below, which was dark and extraordinary and which remembered, in whatever way ground remembers, everything that had ever been asked of it.
