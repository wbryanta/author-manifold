The smoke came up in the wrong shape.

That was the first thing Vera noticed, even before she understood what she was looking at—not the familiar dirty smudge of a campfire banked poorly, not the blue-gray pencil line of a woodstove working against the morning cold, but something columnar and pale at the base, darkening as it rose, bending in the upper air where the thermals caught it and pulled it northeast toward the ridge. She was eating oatmeal. She set the spoon down on the map table without looking at it, and when it rolled off the edge she didn't look at that either.

The Osborne fire finder occupied the center of the cab like an altar, its alidade rusted at one joint but still tracking true, and she had been using it since May to locate and report fires with a precision that felt, at this late stage, almost ceremonial. The Forest Service was decommissioning the station at the end of August. They had told her this in February in a letter that used the word *transition* four times. She was to be the last lookout at Hatchet Peak, a fact she had not shared with anyone, because sharing it would require explaining why she'd spent seven summers at elevation while the rest of her life gathered dust in a storage unit in Missoula, and she didn't have that conversation in her.

She put her eye to the finder and swung the alidade until the crosshair settled on the column. The bearing read two hundred forty-three degrees. She did not need to triangulate. She knew exactly where two hundred forty-three degrees led, because she had looked at it every morning for seven years with the specific avoidance of someone who has memorized the location of a wound.

The North Fork of Elk Creek drainage. Seven miles as the crow flew, maybe a mile and a half more to account for the basin's curve. There was one cabin down there that she knew of—she knew of it the way you know of a stone in your shoe that you've chosen not to remove because removing it would mean sitting down, and sitting down would mean stopping, and stopping would mean arriving somewhere you hadn't decided to go yet.

Ruth's cabin.

She picked up the radio handset and keyed it. Static. She tried the repeater frequency. Static with a slightly different texture, which was somehow worse. She had been having radio trouble since Thursday, when a storm had come through and deposited hail the size of chickpeas against the cab windows and she had stood in the middle of the little room watching them fracture against the glass, listening to the sound they made, which was the sound of something being said in a language she didn't speak. She had reported the radio issue in her log and waited for someone to drive up with a replacement unit. That had not happened. The Forest Service was in the process of not caring about Hatchet Peak. She had been watching them practice not caring all summer.

The phone line had been dead since June.

She looked at the smoke again. It was talking now, doing what fire does when it finds fuel—pulsing at the base, sending up those rhythmic exhalations that smokejumpers and the old lookouts called *breathing*, a word she had always found both accurate and too intimate, as though naming it that gave the fire a stake in things. The column was white-gray, which meant green fuel, living trees, brush still holding moisture. Not a campfire. Too large for a campfire. Not a structure fire yet—structure fires went black almost immediately as the synthetics caught. This was somewhere between, and the between was the dangerous part.

She needed to call it in and she had no way to call it in.

The road was eight miles of switchbacks, unpaved after the first two, subject to washouts, and had cost her a set of shocks last October when she'd driven down in November to close the station for winter and hit a rut that had been excavated by a week of rain. Eight miles. In the truck, maybe forty minutes if she pushed it. By the time she reached a radio signal, by the time dispatch got someone moving, by the time the tankers oriented themselves and the crew trucks navigated the access road to the Elk Creek trailhead—

She was already doing the math and the math was not cooperating.

She pulled on her boots. This was less a decision than something her body undertook while her mind was still cataloguing objections. She laced them with the tight, slightly furious efficiency of a person who has lived alone at high altitude long enough that every motion has become either purposeful or eliminated, there being no audience for the in-between kind. She took her radio—useless, she took it anyway—her pack, her water, the first aid kit, the weather notebook out of habit, and her truck keys.

Then she stopped at the door.

If she drove down the mountain to call it in, she would be doing the right thing and nothing else. Dispatch would handle it. There were protocols. She knew the protocols; she had followed them for seven summers and they were good protocols, built on the accumulated logic of many disasters. But the protocols assumed communication, and she had no communication, and in the absence of communication the protocol became a map to a country that didn't exist.

If she went toward the smoke instead, she would be doing something for which she had no training, no authorization, no support, and no guarantee that Ruth was even there—Ruth might have sold the cabin, Ruth might be in Spokane or Billings or somewhere with a proper address and a phone number that worked and all the trappings of a life that maintained connection with other lives.

Ruth might not have been to that cabin in years.

Vera stood in the doorway of the cab and looked at the smoke and then she looked down the road and then she looked at the smoke again, and what she thought was: *I don't know how to want the right thing anymore.* She had been up here so long, wanting had simplified into needing—she needed water, she needed food, she needed the radio to work and the weather to hold, she needed the woodpile restocked before October. Wanting was a more complex machinery, and she had let it go fallow, and now it was turning over uncertainly, reluctantly, like an engine left too long without use.

What she wanted, she realized, was for Ruth to be fine.

She wanted this with an intensity that surprised her, because she had spent a substantial portion of the last eleven years telling herself that Ruth was not her business, that the silence between them was a mutual construction, that she had stopped being responsible for her sister the night Ruth had said the thing she'd said and Vera had said the thing she'd said back and then their mother's kitchen had become a place that could not hold them both and one of them had to leave and Vera had left, and left, and kept leaving, going higher each time, the lookout towers an elegant solution to the problem of not wanting to be found.

She went back inside and wrote in the log: *0847. Smoke column observed at 243° bearing, approx. 7 mi, upper North Fork Elk Creek drainage. Estimated size: [2-3 acres and growing]. Fuel type: mixed conifer/brush. Column behavior: active. Radio nonfunctional. Departing station to report via vehicle. If I am not back by 1800, call dispatch. If no one reads this log until after that: I tried.*

She scratched out the last line. She wrote it back in. She left it.

The truck started on the third try, which was its usual number. She had named it nothing, on principle—naming things up here felt like a strategy for not leaving, and she was always technically leaving at the end of August, every August, until she came back in May. The unspoken conversation she'd had with herself about this particular loop was one she had become expert at not finishing.

She pulled away from the tower and then she stopped. She sat for a moment with the engine running and the smoke visible over the ridgeline to the southwest in her side mirror and she thought: *eight miles down to report it, and then however long for response, and then no one knows that drainage the way I do.*

This last thought was not entirely honest. Ruth knew that drainage. Ruth had gone there specifically because she knew it, had described it to Vera once in the overlap of a conversation that had happened before the kitchen incident, before the years of silence, described the way the creek ran clear over a particular shelf of granite and how the light came through the canopy in the late afternoon in long bars that looked almost solid, like things you could lean against. Ruth had a quality of attention that Vera had sometimes envied and sometimes found exhausting, and that quality of attention had led her to that cabin in that drainage and it had perhaps led her there this summer, this August, these particular weeks.

Or it hadn't.

Vera put the truck in gear and turned it not toward the road down but toward the two-track that angled southwest along the ridge. There was a track—barely, theoretically, in the sense that it had existed once and she had never seen evidence that it had been formally closed. It would get her to within two miles of the trailhead and save her forty-five minutes, and it would also possibly destroy the undercarriage of the truck on terrain that had not been graded in what looked like a geological era. She drove it anyway, and the truck said many things to her about this decision, and she did not disagree with any of them.

The track ended where she expected it to end, which was at a washout, a ten-foot gash where the hillside had simply revised its opinion of itself after last year's wet spring and taken the road with it. She parked and got out and smelled the air. Smoke, but faintly—she was upwind still, or mostly. She could hear nothing that sounded like fire from here, which meant either it was still small enough that the drainage walls were containing the sound, or the wind was wrong, or both.

She started down the trail at a pace she would regret later and did not adjust.

There is a particular kind of physical effort that clarifies rather than obscures thought, and Vera found it in the first mile—the steep rocky descent through lodgepole pine, the air cooling as she dropped into the drainage, the specific smell of a north-facing slope in August, damp and composted and a little fermented, like something good slowly becoming something else. She had walked thousands of miles in terrain like this. Her body knew how to do this, and she let it, and her mind moved around the question of Ruth in the way she'd been trained to move around a fire: trying to get above it, trying to see its shape.

The shape of the problem with Ruth was the shape of most problems between sisters, probably, which was not really about the kitchen incident at all. The kitchen incident was just where it had finally achieved the form of an event. Before that it had been years of a particular quality of atmosphere—the way Ruth's presence in a room made Vera feel simultaneously seen and misread, the way Ruth's judgments landed not like attacks but like diagnoses, which were worse because they implied that Ruth had studied her, had assessed her, had come to conclusions. Ruth, three years younger, had always had this tendency, and Vera had always had the complementary tendency to resist being known, and together these tendencies had created something that worked well enough until it didn't.

What Ruth had said in the kitchen was: *You want to be witnessed without being known. You want to stand on top of something and have everyone see you standing there, without anyone being able to read what's in your face.*

What Vera had said back was something she didn't repeat to herself. She had said it and she had watched Ruth's face do what Ruth's face did when something hit true, and then Vera had picked up her keys and she had driven north for two days and the summer after that she had applied for the lookout position at Hatchet Peak, which she had maintained, she was fairly certain, out of pure spite, and then out of habit, and then out of something else she was still identifying.

She smelled the smoke more strongly now. She was dropping toward the creek bottom and the creek was running, she could hear it, and through the trees she could see the gray-white drift of smoke moving along the drainage floor in the strange low-traveling way that smoke moved in the bottoms in the morning when the air was cool and still. It meant the fire was close but not above her. She came out of the trees and onto the creek bank and stood for a moment to orient herself.

The cabin was three hundred yards upstream, around a bend, and she knew this because she had looked at the map of this drainage many times, the way you look at the map of a place you're not going.

She went.

The fire was not in the cabin. This was the first thing she saw—the cabin was standing, its shake roof dark with morning damp, its small porch unharmed. The fire was in the woodshed adjacent, or had been, and had moved from there into a rectangle of dry grass between the woodshed and the outhouse and was now working its way up the slope into a stand of young fir at a speed that was not yet catastrophic but was committed. She could see how it had started, or thought she could—a stovepipe cap sitting wrong, or a spark from the flue landing in the tinder-dry stack. Summer had been dry in the drainage despite the spring wet. Everything was waiting to be the excuse.

She looked for Ruth.

Ruth was behind the cabin at the edge of the creek, filling a bucket, filling it in the stream and carrying it to the fire's advancing edge on the slope, which was not a sufficient strategy but was the only strategy available to someone alone with a bucket, and Vera recognized in this approach something essentially Ruth—the willingness to try the insufficient thing, to stand in the face of the disproportionate and respond anyway, to believe that a bucket of water was a real answer even when the arithmetic said otherwise.

She was older. Eleven years older than when Vera had last stood in the same space as her, which meant eleven years older than thirty-four, and her hair had gray in it at the temples and she moved with a purposeful efficiency that Vera did not remember but recognized, somehow, the way you recognize a voice on the phone when someone has grown into it.

Ruth had not seen her yet. She was carrying the bucket up the slope and pouring it at the base of the burning grass in a long arc, and then she was going back to the creek, and she had established a rhythm, a counterpoint to the fire's own rhythm, and she had the expression on her face that Vera had also not seen in eleven years but recognized: the expression of someone who has decided to solve the problem in front of them and will renegotiate the terms later.

Vera walked to the creek. She looked for another bucket. There was no other bucket. She took off her pack and found the collapsible water bag she used for spike camps and she unfolded it and filled it in the stream and she carried it up the slope and stood beside her sister and together they poured water on the burning ground.

Ruth did not say anything for a while. The fire said things, and the creek said things, and the trees at the uphill edge said the dry-static thing that trees say when they are nervous, and eventually the fire where they were working said less than it had been saying, and they moved up the slope to where it was saying more.

"There's a shovel in the cabin," Ruth said. "Behind the door."

Vera went and got it. When she came back Ruth had located a second container, a stockpot, and was using that. They worked with the tools they had, which were inadequate, and the techniques they knew, which were mostly intuited, and after another forty minutes the fire on the slope had been reduced to a smoking scar and the woodshed, minus the back wall and a winter's worth of birch, was simply a building that had been partly ruined rather than completely.

They stood at the uphill edge of the scar and Vera looked at it with the professional assessment she had spent seven summers developing, checking for smokes, checking for the deep-ground heat that could reignite from root systems, checking the wind against her face for any shift that would bring it back. The air said: *not today*. She believed it.

Ruth was breathing hard. Vera was breathing hard. The creek was doing what it had been doing before any of this, which was its own kind of commentary.

"How did you know," Ruth said. It was not quite a question.

"I was watching from the tower."

Ruth looked up the slope, as though she could see through miles of timber and ridge to Hatchet Peak. "I could see it up there sometimes," she said. "At night. The light."

Vera said nothing.

"I thought about driving up."

Vera thought about the many ways to answer this. She said, "The radio's out. I couldn't call anyone. I need to get down to a signal."

"My truck is here."

"I'll need to call dispatch. They'll want to document even if we've got it knocked down. They may want to send someone to check the woodshed for hotspots."

"I know," Ruth said. She had the bucket at her side, and she was looking at the smoking scar, and she looked tired in the way that people look tired when they have been tired for longer than this particular morning, tired in a way that had accumulated over time and was only visible now because they had stopped moving. "I know what to do with a fire report."

Vera looked at her sister's face. Ruth had their father's jaw and their mother's eyes and the specific expression she wore now she had developed herself, out of her own materials, and Vera had once known it well enough to read it and now could only guess at what it was carrying.

"I'm sorry I didn't—" Vera started.

"Don't," Ruth said. "Not yet."

Vera stopped.

"Help me bank the scar and then drive me down and we'll call it in and then—" Ruth paused. She looked at the bucket in her hand, then set it down carefully, as though it were full when it wasn't. "Then I'll buy you coffee in town if anything's open, and we can try."

It was the closest thing to a door that had been offered in eleven years. Vera recognized it not by its shape but by the way it felt to be standing in front of it: the specific compound of fear and wanting that is what it feels like to be almost somewhere, to have traveled a long distance and not yet know if you are going to be let in.

She picked up the shovel. She turned the earth at the fire's edge, cutting line, folding the black soil over on itself, and Ruth came with the bucket and wet it down, and they worked their way around the scar's perimeter together in silence, and the smoke that rose from the ground where they worked was thin and white and rose straight in the still air and was nothing like the column that had started all this, was barely smoke at all, was something that would disperse entirely by the time anyone else looked up.
