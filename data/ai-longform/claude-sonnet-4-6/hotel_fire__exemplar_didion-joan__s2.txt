The hotel had been rebuilt on the same footprint, which was the first thing Caroline noticed, and the second thing was that the footprint had always been wrong. She had not understood this in the years she worked there, carrying linens up the back stairs in the heat, but she understood it now, standing in the lobby with her wrap sliding off one shoulder and her invitation already creased from having been folded into her evening bag. The lobby was too narrow for its height. This had been true of the original building and was true of the rebuilt one, and whoever had made the decision to keep the proportions had done so deliberately, which meant they had looked at the plans and found in the height and the narrowness not an error but a feature, a kind of drama. Caroline supposed there was something to be said for that view of it. She took her wrap from her shoulder and held it.

"There you are," Mavis said from somewhere behind her. "I've been looking everywhere."

Mavis was the bride's aunt and had been in the bar for the past forty minutes, which was why she had been looking everywhere. Her dress was the color of old pewter and her hair had been done that afternoon at a place on Grant Avenue that Caroline had recommended without ever having been there. "Is it all right?" she had asked about her hair, and Caroline had said yes, it was very nice, because it was very nice, and also because there had been no other answer available. "I thought you'd gotten lost," Mavis said now. "I thought you'd taken a wrong turn somewhere and ended up in one of those dreadful rooms."

"I came straight from the car."

"Well, they've done something terrible with the bar. Or wonderful with it. I can't decide. It's all white. Everything's white in there." Mavis looked around the lobby with the satisfied proprietary air she brought to other people's occasions. "They've done a good job, haven't they. You'd never know."

"No," Caroline said. "You wouldn't."

What you would not know, if you had not been there, was the particular sound the fire had made. This was something Caroline had tried a number of times to describe and had always failed at. She had tried to describe it to the man she had been seeing for three years after the fire, a structural engineer named Peterson who would have had professional reasons to be interested in the acoustics of collapse, but Peterson had listened with a certain focused patience that she came to recognize as the patience he brought to things that made him uncomfortable, and so she had stopped trying. She had tried once to describe it to her daughter Nora, who had been eleven at the time of the fire and was now thirty-four and lived in Portland with a man who restored furniture and two dogs whose names Caroline could never keep straight. Nora had looked at her with the expression that meant she thought Caroline was being, in some way she could not specify, unfair. Caroline had not tried again after that.

The sound had been nothing like an explosion. That was the first thing she would have said, if she had found anyone to say it to. It was not percussive. It was more like a long exhalation, like a sound a building makes when it has been holding something back for a long time and then stops holding it. The roof of the east wing had gone first. She had been in the stairwell between the third and fourth floors with a cart full of dirty linens—the Thursday changeover, three rooms still to go—and she had felt the stairwell flex around her in a way that she had no word for except alive, which was not the right word but was the closest one. She had left the cart. She could not have explained afterward why leaving the cart had seemed like the essential act, why she had thought of it as leaving rather than abandoning, but she had thought of it that way then and thought of it that way still.

"Caroline." Mavis had taken her by the arm. "You're someplace else entirely."

"I'm sorry. The drive."

"Of course the drive." Mavis steered her toward the stairs that led up to the reception rooms. "Two hours from the city in that traffic, you must be exhausted. I've been here since three and I'm half dead. The flowers are extraordinary, you'll see. Renata found someone who does only white flowers, isn't that something. Only white, that's his whole thing. I think it's a little severe but Renata knows what she wants, she always has. Even as a child." She paused on the first landing and lowered her voice. "Tom is already drinking."

"It's a wedding."

"Tom is already drinking in the way I mean. You'll see it when you see it." She resumed climbing. "His people are all here. All the Demarests. There must be eight or ten of them, they all look alike, those big equine faces. I don't know what to do with people like that. I never have." She pushed open the door at the top of the stairs and the sound of the room came up around them, voices and the string quartet and the noise of glasses, the accumulated sound of sixty or seventy people in a space that was very slightly too small for them. "There's Renata. Don't say anything yet about Tom."

Renata was standing near the window with three women Caroline did not recognize, and she had the look that brides have in the first hours of an occasion they have planned for a long time, the look that is composed of both satisfaction and a kind of low ongoing vigilance, the sense that something still requires watching. She was twenty-nine. She had her mother's coloring and her father's way of standing, which was with her weight slightly back, as though she were perpetually prepared to leave. She kissed Caroline on both cheeks and said she looked wonderful and that she was so glad she'd made the drive and that she had to go speak to Tom's grandmother, who had arrived from Tucson and was ninety-one and could not be left alone for very long. "She wanders," Renata said. "Not mentally, I mean literally. She physically wanders away." She pressed Caroline's hand and moved off toward a small woman in lavender who was making her way with some determination toward the bar.

Caroline took a glass of champagne from a tray and stood near the window. Below was the courtyard where the original hotel had kept its roses, the thing it had been known for, and the rebuilt hotel had kept roses there too, or put roses back, it was hard to know which was the right way to say it. In August the roses would have been done but this was June and they were still flowering, or some of them were, the yellow ones and the pink ones and the small white ones that climbed the south wall. She had deadheaded those roses once a week when she worked here. It had not been in her job description but the head housekeeper, Mrs. Armendaris, had asked her to do it one summer when the regular groundskeeper was sick, and Caroline had done it through that summer and then kept doing it because no one told her to stop and because she had found the work, the specific work of finding the dead blooms and removing them cleanly, to be absorbing in a way that the rest of her work was not. It required attention of a particular kind. You had to look at each flower and decide, and the decision was usually obvious but not always, and the cases where it was not obvious were the ones that kept her interested.

She had been twenty-six. She had been in the process of leaving a marriage and had taken the job at the hotel because a job at the hotel was the job that had been available, and she had told herself at the time and for years afterward that it was temporary, that it was a thing she was doing while she worked out what to do next. She had worked there for four years. This seemed, now, like a long time to spend in the process of working out what to do next, but she had been, she thought, genuinely in the process. It was not as if she had stopped. It was not as if the four years had been nothing. She had finished her degree in those four years, taking classes two evenings a week at the state college, and she had made a decision about the marriage that she still, at sixty-one, considered the right one, and she had learned things about hotels and about the people who stayed in them that had turned out to be, in some way she had never fully articulated, useful. She did not know what exactly she had used them for. But she did not think they had been wasted.

"You're Caroline Sayers," said a man's voice beside her.

He was in his fifties, broad-shouldered, with the kind of gray hair that had clearly been very dark and that still carried the memory of that darkness in it. He had a drink that was not champagne.

"I am," she said.

"I'm Tom's uncle. Phillip Demarest." He said it without apparent expectation that the name would mean anything to her, which she appreciated. "I've seen your photograph. Tom has it, a picture from the fire, you probably know the one. He found it in a book about the hotel."

She did know it. It was a photograph taken by someone from the Examiner who had been driving past, and it showed the east wing coming down and in the foreground, small and slightly blurred, a woman in a gray uniform standing on the lawn looking up. The woman was looking up in a way that seemed, in the photograph, almost composed, almost like a deliberate posture, though Caroline had no memory of composure. She had no memory of looking up. She remembered looking at her hands.

"He's interested in the fire," she said.

"Interested in the hotel. The whole history of it. He found all these photographs, letters people wrote, newspaper pieces. I think it's displaced anxiety about the wedding." He said this without particular humor, as a hypothesis. "He showed me the picture and said do you know who that is, and I said no, and he said it's a woman who's coming to our wedding. Like a fact he'd been saving."

"I worked here for four years."

"He said." Phillip Demarest looked out at the courtyard. "It's a good job they did. Rebuilding it. They could have gone another direction."

"They kept the proportions," Caroline said. "The original proportions. The lobby is too narrow."

"I noticed that."

"It was like that before."

"I know. I stayed here once, before. I was here for a conference in—" He seemed to calculate. "Ninety-one. I remember thinking the lobby was wrong somehow and not being able to say why."

"Too narrow for its height."

"Yes." He looked at her with what she recognized as interest that had become, in the last thirty seconds, a different kind of interest. "You must find this strange. Being here."

It was a direct question of the kind that Caroline had not been asked and had not been able to ask herself for some time. She thought about the lobby and the courtyard and the proportions and the roses. She thought about the stairwell flexing. She thought about the cart.

"I find it—" She paused. "Accurate," she said finally.

He waited.

"I mean the place is accurate. The way it's been rebuilt. It's not a copy and it's not a pretense. It knows what it is."

Phillip Demarest considered this. He did not seem to find it strange as an answer. "Unlike most of the buildings that get rebuilt."

"Unlike most of them."

Across the room Tom Demarest had arrived at his own wedding reception in the way Mavis had predicted, with a forward momentum and a high color that had nothing to do with the occasion. He was handsome in the way his uncle was handsome, broadly and without apology, and he was moving through the room touching people's arms and laughing and Caroline could see Renata watching him with the vigilance she had seen in her face at the window, the vigilance dialed up now, quieter and more focused. She had seen that expression on women's faces all her life. She had worn it herself. She knew what it cost and she knew that Renata would not yet know what it cost, would not know for some time.

"That's your niece's husband," she said to Phillip Demarest.

"That's Tom." He had been watching him too. "He'll be all right."

She said nothing.

"I've known him since he was born. He'll be all right in the end."

"I hope so," she said, and meant it, and also knew that meaning it was not the same as believing it and that Phillip Demarest knew this too.

Mavis materialized at her elbow with a look of managed brightness that Caroline recognized as the look she put on when she had decided to stop worrying about something, which generally meant she had decided to start drinking instead. "Phillip Demarest, isn't it? I'm Renata's aunt. We met at the rehearsal dinner but only just barely. Isn't this beautiful what they've done. I was just saying to Caroline, you'd never know."

"It's quite something," Phillip Demarest said.

Caroline looked at her champagne glass and found it empty. A waiter moved past with a tray and she took another glass and thought about what she had said, accurate, and whether it was true. The fourth floor corridor had been where the fire started, an electrical fault in the room at the end of the hall, room 412, which had been checked out and unoccupied. There was something in that which had always bothered her without her being able to say why. The fact of the unoccupied room. The fact that no one had been in it. She had made up that room the morning of the fire, had stripped it and remade it and hung clean towels in the bathroom and drawn the curtain against the afternoon sun, had done all of this to a room that would not be occupied that night or any other night, and there was something in that which felt like, or which she had always found herself making into, a kind of lesson. Though about what she could not have said. About the relation between work and its occasion, perhaps. About the things you did when no one was watching, when there was no one there to see them done.

The quartet had shifted to something Caroline almost recognized, something that moved in and out of familiarity the way certain pieces of music do when you know them too well to hear them clearly. The roses were still visible in the courtyard below, small and distinct in the last of the evening light. Renata had moved toward Tom and was standing beside him with her hand in the crook of his arm and her face turned up toward his, and there was in the gesture both the specific gravity of the occasion and something else, something that predated the occasion, that was in the grain of the two of them together, and Caroline thought: well. There it is. The thing that is not a lesson and not a mistake and not a party, exactly, though it includes all the accouterments of parties, all the flowers and the string quartet and the champagne and the rose courtyard and the rebuilt lobby with its original proportions. The thing itself.

"She's beautiful," Mavis said, having followed her gaze. Her voice had gone soft and unguarded in a way that Caroline had not heard from her before, in all the years of knowing her. "Isn't she beautiful."

"Yes," Caroline said.

"I remember her at six years old. The things you remember." She pressed her lips together and then recovered. "Well. I need to find someone who can tell me whether dinner is actually going to happen or whether we're just going to drink until we fall down. That's always the question at these things." She moved off with her drink, restored.

Phillip Demarest had been patient through all of this in a way that was not passive, was instead a kind of attention. Caroline looked at him and he looked at her with the directness she had noticed before.

"You came back for the wedding," he said. "But also."

"But also," she agreed.

Below in the courtyard the last of the light was going. The yellow roses had turned, in that light, to something that was almost white, almost the color of the small white ones on the south wall, so that for a moment the courtyard was all one color, or appeared to be, before the light shifted again. She had not known, when she left the cart in the stairwell and got herself out of the building and stood on the lawn and looked up—or did not look up, she still did not know—she had not known then what she would do next. She had found out. It had taken time to find out, but she had found out. The hotel had been rebuilt on the same footprint with the same proportions and the roses were in the courtyard and the east wing stood where the east wing had fallen and the lobby was too narrow for its height and this was not, she thought—looking at the courtyard and at the roses and at the couple across the room with their convergent gravity, their specific and observable pull—this was not either a lesson or the opposite of a lesson. It was just a thing that was true. The building knew what it was. She had said that and she believed it and she was willing, standing at the window with her second glass of champagne and the long drive behind her and the sound of the room all around her, to find that sufficient.
