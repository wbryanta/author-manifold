The elder brother was already in the field when the younger came down from the house in the gray light before sunrise, his boots unlaced and his shirt only half tucked and carrying two cups of coffee of which he extended one without looking up. Harlan took it and stood drinking and looking out over the rows of trees. The apples had set poorly that year and even in that early light you could see the sparse clusters hanging in the branches like something the trees had said with reluctance.

We got to find where it's broke, Del said.

I know it.

Could be anywhere from the pump house down.

I know that too.

Del looked at his brother. He'd been up since four and his eyes showed it. You got a shovel in your truck?

I got two.

All right.

They walked down through the orchard in the thin light. The grass between the rows was tall and wet and darkened their boots to the knee. Somewhere up by the creek a bird was going on about something. The mountains to the east were just beginning to separate themselves from the dark of the sky, their outline coming up first like a rumor and then more certain. Del stopped and knelt in the grass and pressed his palm flat to the earth.

Dry, he said.

Down to here anyway.

He stood and they walked on.

The irrigation system had been put in by their father in the early sixties, a network of buried PVC running out from the pump house along the contour of the old creek bed, and it was old enough now that a failure anywhere along its length was not surprising only in the sense that surprise implies some prior expectation of things going well. Parts of it had been repaired and re-routed so many times that neither brother had in his head a complete picture of its course. Del had a hand-drawn map tacked to the wall in the pump house that his father had made and that had been amended and re-amended in different hands and different inks until it resembled less a schematic than some document of accumulated grief.

They found the break just below the second terrace where the ground had subsided slightly along an old gopher run and the pipe had cracked at a coupling. The grass there was bright and lush and the earth had the dark softness of a wound.

Well, Del said.

He looked along the line of it, squinting.

Might be worse.

Is worse, Harlan said. That's a four-inch main.

I know what it is.

They got the shovels from the truck and began to dig. The work was not hard in the softened earth but there was a lot of it to do before they'd have enough room to work and they dug for a long time without speaking. By the time they had it opened up it was full morning and the sun was coming into the valley and the dewy grass was starting to put off a faint mist. Harlan took off his shirt and hung it on a branch of the nearest tree and worked in his undershirt. He was forty-one years old and still lean, the cords of his forearms moving under the skin like ropes in water.

Del climbed down into the hole and looked at the fitting. It's been fixing to go for a while, he said. Look here.

Harlan looked.

You can see where it's been seeping. See how it's ate into the coupling?

Two and a half.

Del got two bottles of water from the cooler behind the seat and drank half of one standing in the sun.

How long you had that pipe?

Since last fall. Got it when I fixed the run on the east side.

Supposed to be a hundred and two by Saturday, Harlan said.

I heard.

Just say it, Harlan said.

I didn't say anything.

No but you're about to.

Two-four.

Yes.

He worked slowly and precisely, the way their father had worked, pausing to look at what he was doing, taking his time. Del stood at the edge of the hole above him.

That's money enough to be comfortable, Del said. That's money enough that you don't have to work like this.

Del didn't answer.

That's not what I want, he said.

This.

It suited daddy fine too.

He died pretty close to it.

He built something, Harlan said.

What did he build.

This place.
