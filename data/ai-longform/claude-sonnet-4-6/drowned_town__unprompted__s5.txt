The water had gone down so slowly that for weeks Maren thought she was imagining it. She would drive out on her lunch breaks and park at the end of the boat launch, which was no longer a boat launch but a cracked concrete tongue licking at mudflats, and she would watch the line on the far embankment where the darker, saturated rock gave way to pale oxidized limestone, and she would try to remember exactly where that line had been the week before. She was a surveyor. She understood measurement. But there was something about watching a thing emerge from water, something about the specific grammar of disappearance reversed, that made her instruments feel inadequate in ways she could not explain to her supervisor when she filed for the additional fieldwork days.

He approved them without question. The county needed updated maps regardless, and what was coming out of the reservoir was technically county property, technically had always been county property, dormant beneath the easement that the Harwick Municipal Water Authority held in perpetuity, or until the water was gone, which was the same thing, or had been. Now it wasn't.

She drove out on a Tuesday in October with her total station and her field notebook and a pair of rubber boots she'd bought new because her old ones had a split along the left heel that let the cold in. The morning was white and still. The reservoir spread before her like a pewter plate tilted slightly to the south, and from the boat launch she could see, extending maybe forty feet out from the southern shore, the beginning of what had once been Millhaven Road. She could see it because it was no longer underwater. A gray smear of gravel and cracked macadam, grasses already colonizing the softer shoulders, and at its far end, which disappeared again into the shallows, the suggestion of a right angle. A curb. A corner.

Maren stood at the water's edge for a long time before she put on her boots.

She had been seven when they left. She remembered her mother wrapping dishes in newspaper and her father standing in the backyard holding a can of beer and not drinking it, just holding it, looking at the garden as if he were trying to memorize something that was already behind his eyes. She remembered the smell of the house, which was the smell of all houses in summer but which she had catalogued without knowing she was cataloguing it — mildew behind the bathroom tiles, coffee in the walls, the particular sweetness of the wood in the staircase that she thought of even now when she walked into old buildings. She remembered less than she thought she should. She had compensated for this, she suspected, by becoming someone who recorded things.

The mud near the shore was soft and pulled at her boots with a sucking intimacy that felt personal. She stepped carefully, testing each foot before committing her weight, and after a dozen yards the ground firmed slightly beneath a crust of dried silt. The road appeared around her gradually, as if she were not walking toward it but conjuring it. She stepped onto what had been pavement and stood still.

Nothing announced itself. There was no trembling in her sternum, no visitation of childhood sensation. The road was a road the way bones are bones — the organizing logic visible, the animating presence withdrawn. She noted the grade, noted the crown, found a benchmark she had been told to look for, a concrete post with a brass disk that the original county survey had set in 1947 and that the Army Corps had documented before the flooding. She shot it with the total station and wrote the numbers in her book. The numbers were correct. The benchmark had not moved. Of course it had not moved. It had been underwater.

She walked the road south.

The right angle she had seen from shore was indeed a curb, and the curb met another at a corner, and in the corner's junction she found packed silt and a length of cast-iron pipe that had pulled free of whatever it once connected and lay at an angle, its open mouth directed at the sky. She tried to remember what had stood here. She had been given photographs by the county historical society, a manila envelope full of black-and-white images printed from old negatives, their surfaces carrying the silver fog of age. She had studied them at her kitchen table the night before, labeling what she could identify with the sharp geological logic she brought to topographic maps, and she had thought she understood the town's geometry. Now, standing in it, she found that the photographs had lied through accuracy — they had shown her the surface of things, the facades, the horizontals, and had hidden entirely what it would feel like to stand in the absence of what those facades had fronted. The pipe meant she was near what had been a gas station or a laundry or some other business that required significant plumbing. The pipe told her nothing she needed for the map.

She walked on.

The silt on the road was two inches deep in some places and absent entirely in others, where the current, in all those years, had scoured the surface clean. She found a fragment of brick, then another, and followed them to a foundation — a rectangle of limestone blocks, twenty-four feet by roughly thirty, she estimated, the interior filled with the same pale sediment that covered everything. In the photographs there had been a hardware store on this block. Bender's Hardware, which had been owned by a family named Carlson. She did not know why a hardware store would be named Bender's, whether Bender was a previous owner or a name someone had simply preferred. The historical society had not told her and she had not asked. She set a prism on the northeast corner of the foundation and shot it.

A heron landed forty feet away and stood watching her with the ancient, judicial calm that herons carry everywhere. She watched it back for a moment. It did not move. She wrote the coordinates in her book and moved south along what she was now fairly certain was the main commercial street, though the photographs had called it Dellwater Avenue and the name felt wrong to her, felt like a name given by someone who had already decided what the place should feel like rather than simply observing what it was. She had been told this about herself, that she named things too literally, that she had no talent for the poetic. Her ex-husband had said it gently, as if it were a minor allergy she would eventually outgrow.

She found two more foundations before she found the church.

She knew it was the church before she could have said why — before she had measured it or compared it to the photographs — some proportion in the ruins conveying authority, the limestone blocks larger here, more carefully fitted, and at the east end a raised platform that must have been the chancel, still elevated above the surrounding sediment because it had been built on poured concrete rather than the dry-stacked rubble most of the other foundations showed. She stood at what she supposed was the entrance and looked toward the chancel and felt, without wanting to feel it, a great deal.

Her parents had been married in this church. She knew this as information, had known it her whole life the way she knew her mother's maiden name and the make of her father's first car, but she had never been inside the building — it had been underwater since before she was born — and so the fact had always been purely abstract, a coordinate without a reference point. Now she was standing inside it. Now it had walls, or the memory of walls, the limestone blocks marking their course like a sentence in a language she could sound out without understanding. She thought of her parents as they had been in their wedding photograph, which hung in the hallway of her mother's house in the city she had grown up in after, the city that had been assembled from the insurance money and the settlement and the general good intentions of a county that had bought its water supply at the cost of drowning other people's kitchens. Her parents in the photograph were so young that she had always had trouble connecting them to herself. They existed in it at a remove, the way photographs always insisted on their own datedness, and she had looked at it her whole life and thought: strangers who became my parents. Now she thought something different. She thought: they stood here.

She had not expected this to matter more than the other things.

She spent twenty minutes shooting the foundation's corners and recording the chancel dimensions and pacing out what she believed was the original nave length, cross-referencing with a photograph she had folded into her field notebook. The numbers matched well enough, given the margin of error inherent in old photographic perspectives. She was being very thorough. She was also, she recognized, postponing.

The house where she had been born was three blocks south and one block east. She had the photograph. She had the address, though the address meant nothing now — the street was gone, the numbering was gone, the whole Cartesian logic of civic address had dissolved with the town in 1961 — but she had the coordinates of the lot as described in the original county plat, and she had mapped those coordinates to the survey grid before she left her truck, and she knew exactly where the house was. She had known before she drove out. She had known when she requested the additional fieldwork days. This was why she was here, and she was a person who believed in honesty with herself, who tried to be, and so she walked south and east across the silted bed of what had been her hometown with her total station on her shoulder and her field notebook in her hand, measuring things, the act of measurement her oldest and most reliable defense against whatever came next.

The street she crossed to get there — she could tell it had been a street by the relative smoothness of the mud, the way it lay slightly lower than the surrounding lots, the way it ran in a line — had no name she knew. It might have been one of the residential streets shown on the old plat map but the plat map's street names were faded and she had not been able to read them all. She walked across it and into the grid square that the plat recorded as lot fourteen of block seven of the original Millhaven townsite and she stood in what had been, once, the front yard of the house where she was born.

The foundation was there. Of course it was there. Foundations outlast everything except the things we build them beneath. The limestone blocks were lower to the ground than the church's had been, the gaps between them more pronounced, some of them shifted by decades of hydraulic pressure and the slow insistence of sediment. The interior of the foundation was filled with silt to a depth she estimated at eighteen inches, and in the silt, emerging from it at intervals like bad dreams half-remembered, were objects. A length of copper pipe. What appeared to be the rusted frame of a child's bicycle, though it might have washed in from anywhere, the town's objects had migrated under sixty years of moving water, there was no way to know. A ceramic doorknob, intact, pale blue against the gray, which she stared at for a long time without moving.

She did not touch it.

She set up the total station on what she was fairly confident had been the front walk — a line of stone pavers was just visible beneath a thin crust of silt — and began to shoot the foundation corners. Northeast corner: recorded. Northwest corner: recorded. The southeast corner was partially obscured by a collapse of the foundation wall, several blocks having fallen inward, and she had to estimate the line, which she noted in her field notebook with the appropriate caveat. She stood inside the foundation while she worked, stepping over and around the debris with the careful negotiating walk of someone who has learned to pay attention to where their feet go. The total station's small beeps in the silence were the only sounds except for the water, which was not far away, which was always nearby now, which had retreated but not left.

She was crouched near the southeast collapse, examining a block to determine whether it had been an exterior wall or a partition, when she understood with great sudden specificity which room she was in. She was in the kitchen. She knew this not from the photographs — the photographs had been exterior shots exclusively, she had not a single image of the house's interior — but from the fact of knowing, from some cellular archive that she had not known she was carrying. The kitchen had been in the southeast corner of the house. She knew the way she knew her own height, the kind of knowledge that had never required a moment of learning because it had always simply been in her. The linoleum had been yellow. There had been a window above the sink, which would have been in the east wall, which was now the fallen section. She had stood at that window — she had stood on a chair at that window, she had been small enough to need the chair — and looked out at the garden where her father would later stand with his beer, and beyond the garden at the hills, which were still there, which had always been there, which were the one thing that was the same.

She sat down on the edge of the foundation wall and let the total station rest against her knee.

She had been very small. She had been small enough to need a chair to see out a window and small enough that the leaving had not been grief exactly — had been more like a very long interruption, the sense that this was not finished, that when the interruption was over she would come back and resume. She had been wrong about that in every practical sense and in one sense she had apparently been right, in that she was here now, which was a way of having come back, which was a way of resuming something, though she couldn't have said what. She looked at the ceramic doorknob in the silt and thought about picking it up and thought about her mother wrapping dishes in newspaper and thought about whether it was hers, by any logic, whether possession survived submersion. The doorknob was pale blue. It was the exact blue of her mother's kitchen in the city, the kitchen of the house she had grown up in after, which she had always assumed her mother had painted from preference and which she understood now with a small sharp pain might have been painted from memory, an attempt to carry something forward through the medium of color, the way you might press a flower and keep the shape though the scent was gone.

She did not pick up the doorknob. It belonged where it was. It was part of what she was mapping.

She worked for another two hours in the direct, methodical way she always worked, moving through the grid squares nearest the house, recording foundations and road segments and the concrete pad that had been the elementary school and the irregular circle of stones that had been the park's fountain. She found a fire hydrant, painted red under the algae, still bolted to its base, which she thought was the most loyal thing she had ever seen. She found a storm drain with its grate intact, as if waiting for rain that could not now reach it from above. She found, in the mud south of the park, a marble — a cat's eye, the glass clouded with silt in its spiral — and this she did pick up, because it was small enough to have washed here from anywhere and small enough to fit in her breast pocket and because she thought that in some category of things the rules might be different.

By early afternoon the light had shifted and the reservoir's surface to the north had gone from pewter to a flat, hammered gold, and the heron had moved from wherever it had been and was now standing near the church foundation, which might have meant something in another kind of story. Maren ate the sandwich she had brought in her truck, sitting on the concrete pad of the school. She thought about the children who had sat here, which included a version of herself she had no access to — a version she thought of sometimes in the way you think of a foreign country you have been told exists, one whose language shares roots with yours but whose grammar you cannot follow. She thought that the country had been there and had been flooded and was now re-emerging, and that she did not know what to do with something that had been inaccessible so long it no longer quite matched the space that had been left for it. The space had changed too. It had changed as she changed. There was a gap.

She packed her sandwich wrapper into her bag and checked her total station's data log to confirm the day's records were properly saved. They were. She was meticulous and they always were. She looked at the numbers — coordinates, elevations, attribute codes — and thought about how a map of this place would look when she rendered it, the clean geometric abstraction, the contour lines indifferent to what they described, and she thought that the map would be accurate and would be true and would tell anyone who read it exactly where everything was, and would tell them nothing at all.

She walked back to her truck along the old main road, Dellwater Avenue, which looked different now that the angle of the light had changed. The afternoon was quiet the way rural places with bad news in them are quiet. She passed the hardware store foundation and the two unnamed foundations and the church, and at the church she stopped and looked for the heron, which was gone. She looked for a long time at the chancel platform, at its poured concrete still raised above the silt, at the way it caught the angled light and held it differently from the limestone around it, a brightness of a slightly different kind. She thought of the photograph in her mother's hallway. She took out her field notebook and found a blank page and wrote her parents' names, which she had never written in this notebook, which was a professional document, and then she stood with the pen over the page and thought about what else she wanted to write and found that she had already written it. She closed the notebook.

On the drive back to the county offices she passed three other cars, all of them heading toward the reservoir. She had noticed this in recent weeks, this slow pilgrimage of people coming to look, and she understood it in the way she understood her own presence here, understood that some losses people lived alongside for decades before they found a form in which to hold them, and sometimes the form was water filling a valley and sometimes it was water leaving, and in the leaving something returned that was not the thing itself but was what remained of the space the thing had occupied, which was a different kind of fact but was still a fact, still had coordinates, still could be recorded in a book.

She drove with the marble in her breast pocket. She could feel its small weight against her sternum when she breathed, which she thought was enough. Which was not nothing.
