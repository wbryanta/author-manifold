The ferry left at nine in the evening and would arrive, if the weather held, sometime around six in the morning, and Martin had been told this passage was beautiful in summer, that you could stand on deck and watch the islands slip past in the long northern light, seals on the rocks, eagles overhead, the whole catalogue of wildness arranged for your benefit, but it was October now and there was nothing to see beyond the salt-rimed glass except his own face floating in the dark, and he turned away from it and went to find somewhere to sit.

The passenger lounge smelled of diesel and vending machine coffee and the faint organic undertone of wet coats. A family with two small children occupied a cluster of seats near the window, the children already asleep across their parents' laps with the absolute boneless surrender only children achieve, and Martin felt something move through him at the sight of them, not envy exactly, more like recognition of a country he had once briefly visited and could no longer find on any map. There were a handful of other passengers. A pair of young women with matching rucksacks who spoke to each other in Norwegian or possibly Swedish, Martin couldn't tell the difference, not then. An old man in a canvas jacket reading a paperback with the methodical patience of someone who has made his peace with long crossings. A heavyset man sleeping upright in the corner with his arms folded and his chin on his chest, snoring softly, which struck Martin as either enviable or obscene, he wasn't sure which.

He found a seat near the heating unit and put his bag between his feet and tried to think about nothing.

The police contact—a constable named Burgess, whose voice on the phone had been carefully neutral in the way that suggested extensive training in how to deliver information to civilians without causing immediate collapse—had said the body was of a male, late thirties to mid-forties, found on the beach below the headland on the island's south shore. He had said there were identifying features that matched the description Martin had provided when he first called, four months ago, to report his brother as a missing person. He had said Martin's presence was required only if Martin was willing and able to make the journey, that there were other means of confirmation, but that in cases like this it was often better, the word had hung on the phone line like smoke, better, for a family member to come.

Martin had said he would come.

He had not spoken to his brother in seven years.

The ship's engines changed pitch slightly and Martin felt the hull adjusting to some minor variation in the sea state, a gentle lateral roll that came and went, and he gripped the armrests for a moment before releasing them. He was not afraid of the water. He was afraid of arriving, which was a different thing and harder to address. He was afraid of walking into a room, or perhaps a corridor, some institutional space with linoleum and fluorescent light, and being shown a face, and knowing. Or not knowing. Both possibilities had kept him awake for the past two nights with equal thoroughness.

His brother's name was Paul.

He had been born nineteen months after Martin, close enough that people had sometimes taken them for twins, which had pleased Paul and irritated Martin for reasons he had never satisfactorily explained, not even to himself. They had grown up in a house outside a town that was itself outside anywhere, their father working the refineries on a three-weeks-on, one-week-off rotation that meant his presence was always slightly ceremonial, slightly formal, like a visiting dignitary who expected gratitude for showing up and received it, and their mother coping with this arrangement through a combination of fierce competence and the occasional bottle of wine she thought nobody noticed. They had not been unhappy. That was the thing Martin returned to most often when he tried to construct the narrative of how they'd arrived at seven years of silence. They had not been unhappy. There had been no violence, no extraordinary cruelty, no rupture dramatic enough to justify the distance that had opened between them and kept opening until it became a kind of permanent condition, like a fault line that eventually produces no more earthquakes because there is nothing left to strain against, the plates having drifted too far apart to remember they had ever touched.

The proximate cause, if you were mapping it, was money. Paul had borrowed a significant amount from Martin to start a business that had failed with a comprehensiveness that bordered on artistry, and then had borrowed again from their mother without telling Martin, and when the second loan also disappeared Paul had disappeared with it, moving first to the north of the country and then, it seemed, further north still, finding work on fishing vessels and then losing it and finding it again, sending occasional texts that arrived like dispatches from a foreign correspondent—brief, dateless, slightly unreal—and Martin had replied to these at first and then less and then not at all, and Paul had eventually stopped sending them, and that was how it had ended, not with any confrontation or declaration but with the gradual cessation of signal, like a radio station going out of range.

The old man in the canvas jacket looked up from his book and caught Martin's eye and gave a small nod, the silent acknowledgment of fellow travelers, and Martin nodded back and looked away.

He had reported Paul missing because their mother had asked him to. She was in a care facility in the south now, her memory going room by room like a house being slowly cleared, and on one of her clearer days she had gripped Martin's wrist and said Paul's name three times with an urgency that made Martin understand she was not confused but afraid, that somewhere beneath the neurological weather she knew something was wrong, and he had promised her he would find out, and he had made the calls, and now he was on a ferry in October dark crossing a northern strait toward an island he'd never visited to look at a body that might be his brother.

He got up and went to the small café counter and bought a coffee he didn't want and stood drinking it, watching the woman behind the counter wipe down the espresso machine with the focused attention of someone determined to do one small thing perfectly, and he found this comforting in a way he couldn't articulate.

The crossing took nine hours and Martin slept for perhaps two of them, a shallow coastal sleep from which he surfaced repeatedly to find the lounge unchanged, the family with the children, the Norwegian or possibly Swedish women, the old man reading, the snoring man in the corner, everything preserved as if the whole vessel were a diorama of a certain kind of nocturnal transit. At some point the ferry passed through a stretch of rougher water and the children woke briefly and the mother held them and sang something low and repeated, and this happened, and then the water calmed, and the children slept again.

Martin thought about the last time he had seen Paul.

It had been Christmas, seven years ago, their mother's house, the last Christmas in that house before she moved to the flat and then eventually to the facility. Paul had arrived late and left early and in between had managed to eat their mother's food and drink their father's whisky and tell a story about a woman he'd met at a festival that summer, a story that had made their mother laugh and Martin feel obscurely angry, not at the story but at Paul's ease with it, his ability to inhabit the moment without apparent awareness of the unpaid debts, literal and otherwise, accumulating around him. They had not argued. They had exchanged perhaps forty words directly. When Paul left, hugging their mother at the door, she had watched him go down the path with an expression Martin recognized as the specific grief of loving someone you cannot help, someone who passes through your life like weather, leaving you intact but rearranged, and Martin had stood beside her and wanted to say something adequate to the moment and had said nothing, and she had closed the door against the cold, and that was the last time.

The ferry began its approach at around half past five, and people stirred and gathered bags and children, and the quality of the air in the lounge changed subtly as the collective holding-of-breath that accompanies arrival settled over everyone. Martin looked out the window and could see lights now, the cluster of the town at the head of the bay, the shapes of hills above it, very dark against a sky that was beginning to lighten at the eastern edge with a gray and tentative luminosity. He stood up and put on his coat and went out onto the rear deck despite the cold.

The air was savage with salt. The wake spread behind the ferry in a long white V that diminished into the darkness they'd come from, and the hills on either side of the approach were beginning to resolve themselves out of the dark into shapes with textures, heather, rock, the occasional pale scratch of a road. A seal surfaced briefly off the port side and was gone. Martin stood at the rail and breathed and tried to prepare himself for the next few hours by imagining them in sequence—the dock, the constable, the drive, the building, the corridor—but his mind kept stopping at the threshold of the room where the body was, unable to go further, like a horse refusing a jump.

He thought: if it is Paul, I will have to call our mother's facility and ask someone to tell her in the right way, at the right time, and I do not know what the right way or the right time is.

He thought: if it is not Paul, I will be back on a ferry by this afternoon and I still will not know where he is.

He thought: seven years is not so long. Seven years is almost nothing. People are estranged from their brothers for entire lifetimes without the estrangement ever resolving, without either party making any of the gestures toward reconciliation that both privately intend, and this is common, this is unremarkable, this is one of the ordinary griefs that everyone carries without mentioning because there is no emergency and therefore no occasion to mention it, until one October a policeman calls and suddenly you are standing on a ferry deck in the dark over cold northern water and every year of silence has acquired a weight it did not seem to have when you were inside it.

The ferry docked with a series of heavy mechanical sounds, the thud of contact, the groan of lines being secured, and Martin went back inside and joined the small stream of passengers moving toward the vehicle deck, though he had no vehicle, only his bag, and a deckhand directed him to a side stairway that deposited him on the dock in the early morning gray.

Constable Burgess was younger than his phone voice had suggested, a man in his early thirties with the weathered complexion of someone who spent a lot of time outdoors and a manner of careful, unhurried competence that Martin found immediately steadying. He shook Martin's hand and said he was sorry for the circumstances and asked if Martin wanted to get breakfast first or go directly, and Martin said directly, please, because he understood that if he sat down to eat he might not be able to get up again.

They drove through the sleeping town and out the other side along a coastal road where the sea appeared and disappeared between low hills and the light was coming up slowly, reluctantly, as if it had better things to do. Burgess drove in a silence that was not uncomfortable and had clearly been professionally calibrated to neither oppress nor demand, and Martin watched the landscape come into being around them and thought about Paul at twelve, Paul at seventeen, Paul at the Christmas dinner table passing the potatoes, Paul's face when he laughed, which involved his whole face, every component of it committed, which was something Martin had always envied about Paul and not told him.

The building was not the institutional space Martin had imagined. It was small, attached to what appeared to be the island's main medical practice, and the fluorescent corridor was brief and the whole atmosphere was of modest, scrubbed-clean municipal care rather than the gray bureaucratic labyrinth his imagination had constructed in the sleepless nights. Burgess spoke quietly to a woman at a desk and then turned to Martin and said they were ready when he was, and Martin said he was ready.

The room was cool and very quiet.

The body was covered to the neck and the face had been carefully arranged, Martin understood this, understood the preparation that had been made on his behalf, the small professional mercies involved in how a person is presented in these circumstances, and he stood in the doorway for a moment before he crossed the threshold and went to stand beside the table and look down at the face.

He stood there for a long time.

The face belonged to a man who was undeniably of the age range Burgess had described, the late thirties to mid-forties, and who had the broad forehead and the slightly cleft chin that ran in their father's side of the family, and whose hair was darker than Martin remembered and whose skin was weathered in ways it had not been weathered at Christmas seven years ago in their mother's warm house, and Martin looked at this face and tried to find in it the certainty he had assumed would arrive automatically, the flash of recognition that would make this either the thing he feared or the reprieve he barely allowed himself to hope for.

It did not arrive that way.

What arrived instead was something slower and stranger, a kind of layered looking, as if he were sifting through transparencies, holding each up to the light and comparing it to the one beneath. The nose: possible. The jaw: yes, consistent, but consistent also with a category of face rather than a specific one. The hands, which were visible at the edges of the covering: working hands, damaged by cold water and rope and the hundred physical demands of a life spent outdoors, and Paul had not had working hands when Martin knew him, Paul had had the soft hands of a man who spent his adult life in offices and coffee shops and borrowed money to start businesses, but that was seven years ago and seven years of fishing vessels would do what these hands showed had been done.

He became aware that Burgess had quietly withdrawn and that he was alone in the room.

He said, quietly, into the room: Paul.

Not as an identification. Not as a summons. More the way you say a word you're trying to remember, the word itself being used as a key to the room where its meaning is stored. Paul. The syllable hung in the cool air and dissolved.

There was a scar on the left eyebrow of the face, a small, pale interruption in the hair of the brow, and Martin looked at it for a long time. Paul had cut himself above the left eye when he was nine years old falling off a bicycle in their grandmother's driveway, and there had been a scar, Martin was almost certain there had been a scar, but almost certain was doing tremendous work in this room and he understood that he could not in good conscience say he was sure, because the scar he remembered was the scar of a nine-year-old child and this was the eyebrow of a middle-aged man who had been found on a beach and the distance between these two data points was too vast to be crossed by almost certainly.

He had not brought any photographs. This seemed, now, like an incomprehensible oversight, and yet when he tried to imagine the act of selecting a photograph of Paul and putting it in his bag for this specific purpose he could not imagine having done it. It was the kind of preparation that would have required accepting in advance something he had spent two days refusing to accept.

He took out his phone.

He had three photographs of Paul on his phone, all taken at the same Christmas dinner, seven years old, the light in them warm and slightly overexposed because their mother's living room had always been over-lit in the way of people who have spent time in dark places. In the first photograph Paul was looking at someone off-camera, mid-sentence, with the expression of a man building toward a punchline, and Martin looked from this image to the face before him and felt the ground shift under him in a way that was neither confirmation nor denial but something in between, a terrible opening. In the second photograph Paul was looking directly at the camera and laughing, the full-face laugh Martin had been thinking about on the ferry deck in the dark, and this was both more and less useful because a laughing face is a face doing something, transformed by the act, and the face before him was doing nothing, had been arranged into the neutrality that death and professional care produce, and the comparison could only be approximate, and Martin was aware that he was a man of no particular expertise in facial recognition looking at a dead face after nine hours of almost no sleep and seven years of distance from the living version of the face he was trying to match it to.

He put his phone away.

He stood in the room for what might have been ten minutes and might have been twenty and tried to simply be present with the question rather than forcing an answer from evidence that genuinely would not yield one. He thought about what it would mean to say yes and what it would mean to say no and understood that neither word, spoken by him in this room, would end the uncertainty, that there would need to be other confirmation regardless, that this was not a court and he was not a forensic expert and his yes or his no carried feeling but not proof.

What he felt, standing in that room, looking at that face, was that the question of whether this was Paul had become in some way less important than a question he had not known he was carrying until this moment, which was whether the version of Paul that existed in Martin's mind—the specific, living Paul of that warm over-lit kitchen, mid-sentence and building to a punchline—was a person Martin had adequately known. Whether, in the seven years of silence, he had taken his memory of Paul seriously enough, held it carefully enough, treated it as the record of a living person rather than allowing it to simplify into a symbol of something else, of failure, or obligation, or a particular kind of grief.

He thought: I have a brother. This is either him or it is not. Both possibilities require something of me.

He went to the door and opened it. Burgess was in the corridor, standing at a tactful distance, his hands loose at his sides.

Martin said: I can't be certain. I think it may be him. There are consistencies I can't discount, but I can't be certain.

Burgess nodded as if this were a common answer, which perhaps it was. He said there would be DNA testing, that he had expected this to be the case, that it was fine, that Martin had done what he could and that the testing would provide a definitive answer within a week or so, possibly sooner. He said Martin was free to remain on the island until then if he preferred not to make the return journey without knowing, or free to return to the mainland and receive the result by phone, whichever felt right.

Martin thought about his mother in the facility in the south, sitting in the good chair by the window that she preferred when she was in the sunlit part of herself, and he thought about what he would say to her when he knew, and what he would say to her if she asked before he knew, and he thought about a boy on a bicycle in a driveway falling and the particular small sound of a head hitting tarmac, which he had not thought of in thirty years and which arrived now with complete acoustic fidelity.

He said: I'll wait.

They went out of the building into the morning, which had arrived fully while Martin was inside, the sky pale and wide and washed with the kind of light that comes after overnight rain, everything slightly more itself than usual, the colors of the hills, the color of the water in the bay below the town, a boat heading out through the mouth of the bay toward open water, and Martin stood beside the constable's car and looked at all of this for a moment before getting in, the island making no particular claim on him, simply continuing to be what it was, indifferent and exact.
