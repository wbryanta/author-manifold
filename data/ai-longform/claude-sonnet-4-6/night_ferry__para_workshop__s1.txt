The ferry left at eleven and the man stood at the stern rail watching the lights of the terminal dissolve into the dark water. He had not eaten. He had the name of a detective in his jacket pocket, written on the back of a grocery receipt in ink that had already begun to bleed into the paper, the letters going soft at the edges. Lund, it said. Det. C. Lund. And a number.

His name was Mikkelsen, the man at the rail. He was fifty-three years old. He sold insurance in a medium-sized city and he had not spoken to his brother in eleven years. The lights were gone now and there was only the black water and the white churn below him and the cold coming off the strait in long slow waves that he could not see but could feel against his face.

He went inside.

The passenger lounge was long and low-ceilinged, smelling of diesel and the specific staleness of upholstered seats that had absorbed a decade of breath and damp coats. A row of fluorescent tubes ran down the center of the ceiling, one of them cycling through a slow, private emergency, flickering at intervals as though it were trying to communicate something in a code that no one had bothered to teach. A family sat near the vending machines—two children asleep across their parents' laps, the parents not speaking, looking at the floor with the particular exhaustion of people who have been awake too long in too public a place. An old man read a newspaper with the paper nearly touching his nose. A woman in her thirties sat with her knees together and a paper cup of coffee going cold in both hands, looking out the black window at nothing, or at her own reflection, or at whatever exists in the distance between those two things.

Mikkelsen found a seat along the wall and unzipped his coat but did not take it off. He thought about what the detective had told him on the phone. A man had been found on the rocks below the coast road, three kilometers north of the ferry landing on the island. The man had been there some time. His wallet was missing. His face was—the detective had paused here, had chosen a word with some care—compromised. There was a tattoo on the left forearm, three interlocking circles that meant something Mikkelsen had never been told. When they described it to him he had said, without deciding to say it, that sounds like Erik.

He had said it so quickly that it frightened him.

He put his head back against the seat and looked at the ceiling. The fluorescent tube continued its argument with darkness.

Erik was—or had been, he supposed he had to consider had been—five years younger than Mikkelsen. He had been, when they were boys, the kind of person onto whom light seemed to fall differently, as though the atmosphere around him had some optical quality that the atmosphere around other people lacked. This was not a matter of handsomeness, though he was handsome. It was something else, something that made people turn when he came into a room and keep looking and feel, when he left, that the room had given something up. Mikkelsen had studied this quality in his brother for years and had never been able to name it and had never, to be honest, entirely forgiven Erik for having it.

They had fought about money, in the end. Or money had been what the fight used, the way a fire uses whatever fuel is available. Their father's estate had been small and badly organized, and there had been a period of six months during which Mikkelsen had handled everything—the lawyers, the accountants, the sale of the house, the disposal of the furniture, the cancellation of a dozen small recurring payments that continued to arrive like loyal soldiers after the war had ended—while Erik had been unreachable, had been living, as he later explained, with a woman on the coast, in a village so small it had no post office and poor reception. Mikkelsen had managed the estate and when Erik returned, tanned and rested, to sign the papers and collect his half, Mikkelsen had said things he meant and Erik had said things he perhaps also meant and they had not spoken since.

He thought about this, sitting in the ferry lounge, and tried to work out whether the anger was still there. He pressed on it the way you press on a bruise, the same combination of curiosity and masochism. There was something. Not quite anger. Something dried-out and structural, the way a wasp's nest is still there in November, still the same shape, but empty.

He bought a coffee from the machine near the family and stood drinking it. It was bad coffee, thin and over-extracted, the kind of coffee that reminded him of other bad coffee in other insomniac hours. His father's hospital. The all-night garage near his office where he sometimes stopped when sleep wouldn't come and he needed, for reasons he couldn't articulate, to be somewhere that was open. He drank it all and dropped the cup and did not feel any more awake.

The crossing would take four hours. He had not booked a cabin—it had not occurred to him, and even if it had, he did not think he could sleep in one of those narrow rooms below the waterline, the ocean on the other side of a porthole, the body on the rocks waiting for him on the other side of the water.

He tried to think clinically. The tattoo. Erik had gotten it in his twenties, had called Mikkelsen from wherever he was and described it, clearly expecting enthusiasm Mikkelsen had not provided. What does it mean, Mikkelsen had asked. Erik had been quiet for a moment and then said something about the interconnectedness of things, about how in certain traditions three circles touching at their edges represented the meeting points of past and present and future, of the human and the natural and the spiritual. He had sounded, describing this, like a person reading from a brochure, but also like a person who was reading from a brochure about something they genuinely believed. That was Erik. The gap between the articulation and the belief was not insincerity. It was just that he had not yet found the words that were truly his, and so he borrowed other people's and wore them with a kind of transparent earnestness that made you unable to be entirely contemptuous of him, though you might try.

Three interlocking circles on the left forearm.

He sat down again. The family had managed to arrange themselves into sleep, all four of them, the children still draped across the parents, the parents' heads tilted back, their mouths slightly open. The old man had folded his newspaper and set it on the seat beside him and he too was asleep. The woman with the coffee cup was still looking at the window. She had not moved, as far as Mikkelsen could tell, since he sat down forty minutes ago.

He should call someone. He thought about who there was to call. His wife had died four years ago—a sudden thing, a hemorrhage, the kind of medical event that happens so fast there is no grammar for it, no before and after that feel like the same story. They had not had children. He had colleagues, friendly acquaintances, a neighbor with whom he discussed the maintenance of their shared fence. He had a woman he saw sometimes, a landscape architect named Sigrid who kept things between them at a temperature he recognized as deliberately managed and was grateful for. He could call Sigrid. But it was past midnight and what would he say. I am on a ferry to identify a body that may be my brother. My brother whom I have not spoken to in eleven years. My brother who may have been living and dying on an island across a strait while I sold insurance and argued about fence posts.

He did not call Sigrid.

He put his phone in his pocket and watched the water through the window. There was nothing to see. The lights of the island were not yet visible. There was only the black water with the ferry's own reflection running along its surface like a rumor.

He thought about the last time he had seen Erik. It had been in the parking lot outside the lawyer's office, in the rain. They had walked out together and stood by their separate cars and finished saying what needed to be said, most of it cruel, some of it precise, and then gotten in and driven away in opposite directions. He had looked in his rearview mirror as he turned onto the main road. Erik was still standing by his car in the rain, not getting in, just standing there, and then the turn had taken him out of the mirror's frame and Mikkelsen had never looked back again. Not in any of the eleven years since. Not once in a way that resulted in anything.

He tried to remember Erik's face. This troubled him—the fact that he had to try. He had photographs, of course. Their childhood was documented in the ordinary way. But the actual face, Erik's face in motion, the particular way it moved when he was telling a story he liked, the left eyebrow that lifted a half-second before the laugh arrived, the way he looked at you when he was listening, like he was building a scale model of you in his mind, detail by detail—Mikkelsen found he had to work to reconstruct this. The components were there but they took time to assemble. He did not know if this was a mercy or a damage.

If it was Erik. It might not be Erik. Men got tattoos. Three circles could mean many things to many people. The detective had said the man appeared to be in his late forties, which fit, but late forties was a wide country. The detective had said the man had been there some time, which he understood to mean at least several days, possibly more, and there had been weather, and the rocks, and—he pulled back from this. He could arrive and look and it would not be Erik. It happened. Families were put through this and the ordeal ended in relief, in the particular exhausted relief of an emergency that turns out not to have been yours, and they drove home and their brothers were alive somewhere, living in villages without post offices, tanned and careless and wonderfully, irritatingly alive.

He wanted this. He was startled by how much he wanted it. He hadn't known, until this moment, sitting in this chair in this buzzing fluorescent lounge in the middle of a cold strait, that he had been carrying, all these eleven years, a very quiet and persistent wish that his brother was somewhere in the world. Not a wish for reconciliation, not for anything as demanding as that. Just the wish that there was a place on the earth where Erik existed. That somewhere the light was falling differently on someone.

The ferry moved through the dark and Mikkelsen sat with this.

He had fallen into a kind of half-sleep when the lights of the island appeared, low on the horizon, the color of old amber. He straightened in his seat. The lights grew slowly. The engine changed its note. The woman with the coffee cup was gone—he hadn't seen her leave—and the old man was gathering his newspaper and his coat. The family was awake, the children confused and faintly resentful in the way of children woken before they are ready.

He stood up and went to the window. The island was coming out of the dark the way things come out of dark when your eyes have adjusted—not a sudden appearance but a gradual claiming of detail, the harbor lights, the outline of a hill, the white geometry of a fish-processing facility, the crane arm lifting against the sky. It looked ordinary. It looked like everywhere.

The ferry shuddered as it reduced speed. He could feel the change in his chest.

Mikkelsen had thought about Erik's life, across the years, in the way you think about things you have deliberately turned away from—in glimpses, in peripheral moments. He knew, from the occasional word relayed through mutual acquaintances, that Erik had lived in several places, had spent time abroad, had worked as a guide for walking tours through mountain terrain in summers and done other things in winters that no one had specified. He had been with various women, none apparently permanent. He had not had children. He had, from all accounts, continued to move through the world with a lightness that Mikkelsen had always found both admirable and exhausting, the lightness of someone who had never quite consented to be tethered.

The mooring lines were thrown and caught. The ferry settled against the dock with a heavy, final sound.

The harbor building was small and poorly lit at this hour, a few vending machines and a tourist board with information nobody was reading. Detective Lund was there, a compact woman in her fifties wearing a dark coat, holding a paper cup much like the one Mikkelsen had discarded. She looked at him with the professional, careful sympathy of someone who has done this before. She shook his hand.

She told him they would go in the morning, to the facility. There was no purpose in going tonight. She had arranged a room at a guesthouse, ten minutes away. He thanked her. She drove him there in a car that smelled of cigarettes with the window cracked. She said very little and he was grateful for the quality of her silence, which was not uncomfortable but was simply made available to him, like a spare room.

The guesthouse was run by a wide man named Thorvaldsen who gave him a key without curiosity and showed him to a room that was plain and clean and cold. There was a window. Outside the window was the harbor and the water and the dark, and across the water, invisible now, the mainland where he sold insurance and argued about fences.

He sat on the bed. He did not take off his coat for a long time.

He thought about a summer when he and Erik were teenagers, a week they had spent at their uncle's cabin at the edge of a lake. He could not remember why their parents hadn't been there. He remembered a canoe and a dock and evenings so long they seemed to exceed their own capacity, the light still present at ten o'clock, stretched thin and golden over the water. He remembered Erik standing in the shallows, waist-deep, holding a jar over his head for some reason Mikkelsen couldn't reconstruct, and laughing. Just laughing at something, at the light on the water, at being sixteen and alive in a lake in summer. And Mikkelsen, sitting on the dock with his feet in the water, had felt—what? He'd felt the specific happiness of being near someone who was unguardedly happy. It was one of the best things he knew how to remember.

He eventually took off his coat and lay on the bed without getting under the covers. He stared at the ceiling. The ferry had long since gone back across the water, carrying its sleeping family, its old man with his newspaper, its woman with her coffee cup and whatever she had been looking at in the black glass of the window.

He slept, finally, a shallow and unrestful sleep, and in the sleep he was in the parking lot in the rain again but it was different. He got to the turn in the road and he stopped. He looked in the rearview mirror and Erik was still there, standing in the rain, and in the dream Mikkelsen understood with a sadness so thorough it was almost peaceful that he had simply not turned back, had simply driven forward into the years, and that it had not been hatred or contempt or an irreconcilable wound that had kept him from turning back. It had been something smaller and more ordinary and in some ways harder to forgive. It had been stubbornness. It had been the way one moment of not turning becomes two becomes a habit becomes a life.

He woke to gray light at the window. The harbor was pale and quiet. A gull moved across the glass of the water.

Detective Lund picked him up at eight. They drove along the coast road, the ocean to their left, tremendous and indifferent, the color of pewter under the overcast. She asked him questions about Erik, routine questions—did he have any identifying marks other than the tattoo, did he have a doctor, had there been any history of—she paused—of difficulty. Mikkelsen said that as far as he knew there had not been. He said he had not been in contact with his brother for some time. She nodded and wrote nothing down, which he appreciated.

The facility was a low building set back from the road, functional, without character, the kind of building that asked nothing of you except that you complete your task and leave. A man in a white coat met them at the door. Everything was handled with a practiced quietness, a minimizing of friction, as though the people who worked here had understood that their job was partly architectural—to arrange the experience so that it took the least possible toll.

They walked down a corridor that smelled of nothing particular, which was its own kind of smell.

The room was small and cool. There was a table. There was a sheet.

The man in the white coat looked at him and Mikkelsen nodded and the sheet was folded back.

He looked.

He stood there looking for what felt like a very long time.

The face had been—the detective had used the right word. He could see the structure beneath, the bones, the shape of the jaw, the height of the forehead. The tattoo on the left forearm was clear, the three interlocking circles dark against the pale skin, slightly raised, ink that had gone in at some expense in some city on some afternoon.

He thought about what the detective had asked, about identifying marks.

When Erik was twelve he had fallen from a wall and broken his right collarbone. The break had not healed perfectly. There had always been a small deformity there, a slight asymmetry in the line of the shoulder that you would only notice if you knew to look.

He looked now. He looked at the right shoulder. He was aware of his own breathing.

The asymmetry was there. Small and unmistakable.

He said, yes. He said, this is my brother.

He heard himself say it and then he said nothing. He stood there. Detective Lund was behind him, not touching him, not speaking, making her silence available.

He looked at his brother's face. He looked at the tattoo, the three circles touching at their edges. He thought about what Erik had told him, on the phone, a long time ago. The meeting points of past and present and future. He had thought it was borrowed and soft and a little foolish. Standing here now he thought it was borrowed and soft and a little foolish and also that it didn't matter. People built what shelters they could from whatever materials were available. He had known this. He had simply not thought to extend it to his brother.

He stayed in the room longer than was necessary. Nobody asked him to leave.

When he came out into the corridor the fluorescent light was ordinary and unblinking, and he stood under it for a moment, and then he thanked the man in the white coat, and he walked back down the corridor with Detective Lund, and they came out through the door into the coast air, which was cold and salt and very clean, and the ocean was still there, pewter-colored and enormous, going all the way out to where the sky came down to meet it.

He would have to make arrangements. There was that. There were forms and there were decisions and there were phone calls to people who had not known Erik well and did not know Mikkelsen at all. He would do these things. He was organized and capable and he knew how to move through the procedural world that arrives in the wake of death, had moved through it before, and he would move through it again.

But he stood for a moment on the step, outside the door, looking at the water.

He thought about the lake, the summer evening, his brother standing in the shallows with a jar held over his head for no reason, laughing.

He thought about Erik alive for decades on an earth he had been on too, both of them breathing the same general air, both waking to mornings and walking through the hours of days and coming to evenings, separately, on opposite sides of a silence that neither of them had chosen exactly, that had simply accumulated, that had been easier on each successive day to leave in place.

He had not known. That was what he was trying to say to himself, standing on the step, looking at the water. He had not known, until a ferry crossing in the dark, until a room and a sheet and a shoulder with its old asymmetry, how much he had been counting on Erik to be somewhere. How much the world had needed, for Mikkelsen, to contain one person onto whom light fell differently.

Detective Lund stood beside him. Somewhere a gull called out and was answered.

He buttoned his coat against the cold. He took the folded grocery receipt from his pocket and looked at her name and number, bleeding at the edges.

He said: what happens now.

And she told him.
