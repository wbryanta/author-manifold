The sale had been advertised in the Coshocton Tribune and on three websites Ruth had never heard of, and by eight-thirty in the morning there were already eleven people standing in the damp grass of her mother's front yard, waiting. Ruth watched them from the kitchen window while she drank the last of the coffee she'd made in her mother's percolator, which was not for sale. She had marked it with a piece of blue painter's tape on which she'd written NOT FOR SALE in black marker, and she had done the same to four other things in the house: the percolator, a painting of a covered bridge that had hung above the stairs since before Ruth was born, the cedar chest at the foot of her mother's bed, and a ceramic rooster that sat on the windowsill above the kitchen sink and that Ruth could not remember a single morning of her childhood without. Everything else had a price tag.

She'd hired a woman named Debra Foss to run the sale. Debra was sixty, brisk, and had the particular confidence of someone who had spent decades presiding over the dispersal of other people's lives. She arrived at eight forty-five with a cash box and a young man named Tyler who was apparently her nephew, and she walked through the rooms with the efficient pleasure of an appraiser, straightening price tags, repositioning a lamp so that it caught the light from the front window better, moving a set of dishes from a low shelf to the kitchen table where people could see them without bending down. Ruth followed her for a moment and then stopped, understanding that she was in the way.

The people began coming in at nine o'clock. Ruth had expected to feel something operatic when the doors opened—some great sorrow descending—but what she felt instead was a low, continuous unease, the way you feel when you have forgotten something important and cannot remember what. She stationed herself near the dining room, where her mother's good china was laid out on the table with each piece priced individually, and she watched strangers move through the house with the careful, slightly predatory attention of people at a yard sale, which is to say with the attention of people who believe that somewhere in this accumulation of someone else's life there is something they need.

A man in his seventies picked up a gravy boat and turned it over to read the mark on the bottom. He set it back down. He picked it up again. Ruth watched him and thought about the Thanksgiving when she was nine years old and her mother had dropped that gravy boat while carrying it from the kitchen and the gravy had gone across the linoleum in a long brown arc and her mother had stood there looking at it for a moment and then laughed, really laughed, the kind of laugh that bent her forward at the waist, and Ruth had laughed too, and her father, who was still alive then, had come in from the living room and looked at the two of them and the gravy on the floor and had said, well, I guess we'll use the pan. The man set the gravy boat down a second time and moved on. Ruth did not know if she was relieved or not.

Debra materialized at her elbow. "The bedroom furniture is getting a lot of interest," she said. "I think the dresser will go in the first hour."

"All right," Ruth said.

"There's a couple looking at the sofa. They want to know if you'll take forty for it."

The sofa was priced at sixty-five. Ruth had priced it herself, going through the house the previous weekend with a roll of stickers and a guidebook she'd downloaded, feeling like a student who had not studied for the exam. She had no idea what things were worth. She had priced the sofa at sixty-five because it seemed like a reasonable number for a sofa and because she had needed to put a number on it and move on.

"Tell them fifty-five," she said.

Debra nodded and moved away, and Ruth turned to find a woman standing very close to her, examining the china cabinet that stood against the dining room wall. The cabinet was full of things Ruth's mother had kept behind glass for decades: crystal stemware that Ruth had never seen used, a set of silver plate serving pieces still in their velvet-lined box, several figurines that Ruth had always found slightly sinister—shepherdesses with painted smiles and lambs at their feet. The woman was perhaps forty, with dark hair pulled back, and she was looking at the figurines with an expression of genuine delight.

"These are wonderful," the woman said, not particularly to Ruth. "My grandmother had ones just like these."

Ruth said, "My mother never let anyone touch them."

The woman looked at her then, understanding something about who Ruth was, and her expression shifted into the particular softness people put on when they are near grief and don't know what to do with their hands. "I'm sorry," she said.

"She died in February," Ruth said, which was not exactly a response to the apology but seemed necessary to say.

"Are you selling all of them?"

"Yes," Ruth said. "All of them."

The woman bought two of the shepherdesses. Ruth watched Tyler wrap them in newspaper and put them in a paper bag, and she felt nothing, or something so close to nothing that she could not distinguish it from the absence of feeling. She had expected to feel possessive about things and she did not. What she felt possessive about was harder to name—some quality of the air in the house, the particular way the light came through the sheers her mother had hung in the living room and made everything golden and indistinct in the late afternoon. You could not price that. You could not wrap it in newspaper.

Her phone rang. It was her husband, David, calling from Columbus, where he had stayed because someone had to, because their daughter Nora had a soccer tournament and because there was no reason for both of them to come and stand in a house in Coshocton watching strangers carry furniture out the front door. Ruth stepped onto the back porch to take the call.

"How's it going?" he said.

"Fine," she said. "It's going fine."

"Are people coming?"

"There are a lot of people. Debra says the bedroom dresser will probably go in the first hour."

There was a pause, the kind David made when he was trying to decide whether to say something. They had been married for twenty-six years and she knew all his pauses. "Are you okay?" he said.

"I don't know," she said, which was more honest than she usually was, and which surprised her a little. "I don't think I know how I am yet."

"That makes sense," he said, and she believed that he meant it, and she was grateful for him in the abstract way she was often grateful for him—a gratitude that lived underneath everything else and was easy to forget until a moment like this when she felt it suddenly, like a hand on her back.

She went back inside. The house was full now, maybe thirty people moving through the rooms, and she could hear the floorboards in the upstairs hallway making the sound they had always made, a long complaint that she had known so long she had stopped hearing it until now. She went upstairs and stood in the doorway of her mother's bedroom and watched a couple, young, maybe twenty-five, examine the dresser. It was a good dresser, solid walnut, old, with a mirror attached that had gone slightly dark around the edges. Ruth had slept in this room the last two nights, her last two nights in this house, and she had looked in that mirror both mornings while she dressed and had seen herself looking slightly diminished in it, the way people look in other people's mirrors.

The couple were murmuring to each other with their heads close together in the way of people who share a private language. The young woman opened one of the drawers and slid it out and pushed it back in, testing it. The drawer had a particular resistance, a slight sticking at the end of the pull, that Ruth had known her whole life. She could feel it in her hand from across the room. The young woman seemed pleased. She turned to the young man and said something. He nodded.

Ruth went back downstairs.

She had brought a folding chair from her car and she set it up in the corner of the kitchen, out of the way of the traffic moving through. The kitchen had been picked over fairly well already—the dishes were going, the small appliances, a set of canisters her mother had used for flour and sugar and coffee and tea, each labeled in her mother's handwriting, which Ruth had not been able to bring herself to wash off before putting the stickers on. She sat in the folding chair and watched the ceramic rooster on the windowsill and thought about her mother.

Her mother had been a particular kind of person. Ruth was always looking for better ways to say this to people who asked about her, and she never found them. A particular kind of person. She had been careful and proud and occasionally cruel in the small, precise way of people who have been disappointed and have made a private accommodation with their disappointment. She had been funny in ways that surprised you. She had smelled of Jergens lotion and something else underneath it, something Ruth could not name and would never be able to name now. She had called Ruth every Sunday morning for thirty years, always at nine o'clock, always beginning the conversation with well, and then a pause, as if the rest of the sentence were waiting just out of reach. She had not told Ruth she loved her often, but when she did it was in situations that made no sense, which made Ruth think it was the only time she could manage it—once when Ruth was parallel parking outside a restaurant, once at the end of a phone call about nothing in particular, once when Ruth had dropped something and bent to pick it up. As if love required the cover of distraction.

She had died in February, in the hospital, with Ruth sitting next to the bed holding her hand, and she had not said anything at the end, nothing legible, and Ruth had wanted her to and had known she wouldn't and had sat there anyway, holding the hand, which was very light and very cold and still somehow entirely her mother's hand.

Debra came to find her. The dresser had sold. The young couple—could they back a truck up to the side of the house, was that all right? Yes, Ruth said. Tyler would help them carry it out. The sofa had sold, the man had taken the fifty-five. Several of the bedroom lamps. The kitchen table, which Ruth had not seen sell, which meant that at some point in the last hour she had not been watching and the kitchen table had found new owners and was already gone, or would be gone soon, the table at which she had eaten every meal of her childhood, at which she had done homework, at which she had told her mother about David before she'd brought him home to meet her, at which, two weeks after her father's funeral, she had sat with her mother at midnight while her mother drank a glass of whiskey and did not cry.

Ruth nodded at everything Debra told her, and Debra went away.

A woman about Ruth's age came into the kitchen and stood looking at the percolator with its strip of blue tape. "Is this really not for sale?" she said.

"Really not," Ruth said.

The woman looked at her with a sympathy that was somehow worse than indifference. "I have my mother's mixing bowls," she said. "I don't even use them. They're just on the shelf."

"Yes," Ruth said.

The woman bought the canisters with her mother's handwriting on them. Ruth let her. She watched the woman carry them toward the front of the house and she thought, she will see the handwriting eventually, after the stickers are off, she will see flour and sugar and coffee and tea in my mother's hand, and she will not know whose handwriting it is, and it will mean nothing to her, and that is all right, that is what happens. She tried to believe this.

She got up from the folding chair and walked through the house. The living room was strange now. The sofa was still there—they hadn't come to collect it yet—but two chairs were gone and the coffee table was gone and the room looked like a place that had already begun to forget itself. The painting of the covered bridge was still above the stairs, still marked with its blue tape, and she stood and looked at it for a moment. It was not a good painting. It was the kind of painting that was sold in the lobbies of hotels and in the back rooms of frame shops, vaguely competent, slightly sentimental. She had no idea where it had come from. She had never thought to ask and her mother was dead and she would never know. She was going to put it in her car and drive it to Columbus and hang it in her house and look at it for the rest of her life and never know.

She went out the front door and stood on the porch. The yard was busy with people carrying things to their cars—a floor lamp, a box of books, a small wooden end table she remembered from her parents' bedroom that she had not even registered as something she should feel anything about until she saw it in the arms of a stranger and felt the small, meaningless shock of it. The lawn had been pressed flat in places by the feet of people coming and going and the grass had the look of grass after rain, dark and close to the ground.

A man was walking toward the house carrying nothing, still coming in while others were leaving, and he was old, really old, perhaps eighty-five, moving carefully with a cane. He wore a canvas hat and his shirt was tucked in and he had the look of a man who had gotten dressed deliberately this morning, who had thought about it. He came up the porch steps one at a time and Ruth stepped to the side and he stopped and looked at her.

"Dolores's daughter?" he said.

"Yes," Ruth said.

He nodded, taking her in. "I'm Herb Castor," he said. "I used to play cards with your father. A long time ago. Before he was sick."

Ruth looked at him. She did not know this man. She had never heard his name. "I'm Ruth," she said.

"I know. Your dad talked about you." He looked past her into the house, then back at her. "I just wanted to see the inside of the place one more time, if that's all right. I was here a lot, years ago. I haven't been inside since your dad passed."

"Of course," she said.

She watched him go inside. She stood on the porch. She had not cried yet today, had not cried in the house, had cried twice in February and once in March and had thought by now she was past the place where crying happened without warning, but she stood on the porch of her mother's house on an October morning and felt the tears starting in the unannounced way, the way they came from some place below decision.

She let them come and she stood on the porch and watched a woman lift a cardboard box full of her mother's kitchen things into the back of a Subaru. She watched Tyler help the young couple maneuver the dresser out the front door, the legs catching for a moment on the doorframe in a way that made Ruth want to say be careful but she did not say it. The mirror caught the light as they turned it. For a moment the reflected sun moved across the porch ceiling like something alive. Then they had it through the door and they were carrying it down the steps and across the yard and that was that.

Herb Castor came back out after a while. Ruth had composed herself by then. He stood next to her on the porch and looked out at the yard.

"Your father was a good man," he said.

"He was," she said.

"Your mother too." He paused. "In her way."

Ruth almost laughed. In her way. It was the most accurate thing anyone had said to her about her mother in eight months. "Yes," she said. "In her way."

He went down the porch steps and down the front walk and he did not look back and she watched him go.

The afternoon came. The traffic through the house slowed and then slowed further, and by two o'clock there were only a few people still browsing, picking through what was left, which was the smaller and stranger and harder-to-want things—a box of miscellaneous hardware her mother had kept in the basement, some outdated cookbooks, a row of her father's paperbacks that no one had touched, a collection of decorative plates that Ruth had always hated, had hated so completely that she had forgotten to hate them and had almost priced them too high out of subconscious loyalty to her mother's taste. They had not sold. She lowered the price on them and a man bought three.

She went upstairs and stood in her mother's bedroom. The dresser was gone, leaving a rectangle of darker carpet where it had stood for thirty years. The bed was sold but had not yet been collected. The room was nearly empty. The window looked out on the back yard, the apple tree her father had planted when Ruth was four or five, which had never produced good apples but had produced apples every fall, small and greenish and sour, and which her mother had nonetheless gathered every year in a basket and made into a sauce that tasted like effort more than apples. The tree still had its leaves, most of them, going yellow. Ruth stood at the window and looked at it.

She tried to think of something that would be adequate to the moment. She had imagined this moment, or something like it, for the eight months since her mother died, had imagined standing in this empty room with some clear and organized emotion that would feel like a proper conclusion. But there was no conclusion. The room was not empty enough to be concluded. It still smelled like Jergens lotion and the particular old-house smell of plaster and wood and the accumulated weather of decades, and the carpet still held the shape of the dresser's feet, and the apple tree was still in the backyard doing exactly what it had always done, which was to go on being an apple tree whether anyone was watching or not.

She heard Debra on the stairs and turned from the window.

"We're about wrapped up," Debra said. "I've got the accounting here whenever you want it." She held a clipboard.

"How did we do?" Ruth said.

Debra named a number. It was a real number, an actual sum of money, and it was strange to hear it—to hear her mother's life in those rooms given a figure, to understand that the figure was fair, probably, given what things were worth in a small Ohio town, that the figure was what it was and would be deposited in the account Ruth had opened for the purpose and eventually used for purposes she had not yet determined. It was not a large number and it was not a small number. It was the number.

"Thank you," Ruth said. "You did a good job."

Debra said something modest and moved away.

Tyler brought the folding cash box to Ruth and she signed a form and gave him a check and he loaded the remnants of the sale—things that hadn't sold, which Debra's contract specified would be donated—into boxes and into his truck. Ruth watched him carry boxes through the front door. By three-thirty he was done and Debra shook Ruth's hand at the door and said she was sorry for her loss and Ruth said thank you and they were gone.

The house was very quiet. Ruth walked through the rooms. The kitchen had the counters and the appliances that had come with the house and the ceramic rooster on the windowsill and nothing else. The dining room had the china cabinet, empty now, its shelves bare, its glass doors reflecting the window light. The living room had the sofa still, waiting, the couple coming with a truck tomorrow morning. The covered bridge painting above the stairs. The upstairs rooms, their carpets marked with the shapes of absent things.

She took the ceramic rooster from the windowsill. It was lighter than she remembered. She had not picked it up since she was a child. She held it in both hands and stood at the kitchen window and looked at the front yard, which was empty of people now, the grass slowly lifting from where it had been pressed down. She thought about her mother standing at this window, every morning, for—how many years had her mother lived in this house? Thirty-five years after Ruth had left, thirty-five years of mornings at this window with the rooster on the sill and the percolator going behind her and whatever she thought about in those mornings, which Ruth did not know and would not know.

She put the rooster in the box she had packed for the car. She wrapped it in the sweater she had been wearing in the morning and put it carefully in the corner of the box. She looked around the kitchen once more, and then she picked up the percolator and put it in the box too, and she carried the box and the covered bridge painting out to her car, and she came back and locked the front door and stood on the porch with the key in her hand.

The afternoon had gone golden, the light coming in low through the trees at the edge of the yard, the kind of light that makes ordinary things look as though they are about to be remembered. Ruth stood in it for a moment. Then she went down the steps and got in her car and she drove away from her mother's house, through the small streets of Coshocton, past the places she knew and the places that had changed and the places that had been empty lots for as long as she could remember and were still empty lots, and out onto the highway toward Columbus, where David was, where Nora was, where the rest of her life was, and she drove for a while before she turned the radio on.
