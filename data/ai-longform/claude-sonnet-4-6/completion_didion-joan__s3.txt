But the Pacific, Jack Lovett said, the Pacific was another matter entirely.

He was telling her this in a hotel room in Kuala Lumpur in 1969 or possibly 1970, Inez could never afterward be entirely certain of the year, only that it was before certain things happened and after certain other things happened, before Harry was arrested in Seattle and after her father had his first mild coronary, the one the family pretended was fatigue, a question of overwork, nothing that a few weeks in Honolulu would not set right. The room had a ceiling fan that did not work and louvered shutters that opened onto an airshaft and Inez had ordered cold soup from room service and it had arrived warm, which was not what she wanted but was what she had, and she remembered thinking at the time that this was a reasonable model for most situations she had encountered.

She was thirty-four or thirty-five. Jack Lovett was older. Jack Lovett was always older, that was one of the ways she thought about him, he was always older and he always knew where he had been even when he would not say precisely and he always knew how to get somewhere else and he always had, as far as she could tell, enough local currency.

Listen, he said. Are you listening.

I'm listening, Inez said.

She was eating the warm soup.

What I'm telling you, he said, is that I could take you down there. We could go. You could see those islands.

They're contaminated, Inez said.

Some of them. Not all of them. Some of them are just the way they were, sand and water and the smell of flowers that aren't there. You could put your feet in the water.

She looked at him. He was standing at the window that looked onto the airshaft and he had his hands in his pockets and he was not looking at her, he was looking at whatever there was to see in the airshaft, which was not much, pipes and the back wall of the building and a cat that had been sitting on a ledge down there all afternoon. He was wearing the kind of shirt he always wore in those climates, cotton, pale blue or pale yellow, washed so many times the color was vestigial, just a suggestion of color. She had known Jack Lovett for almost twenty years by then. She had known him since she was sixteen years old and he used to come to the house on Manoa Road to see her father and drink her father's Scotch and talk to her father about matters she was not supposed to understand, and she had understood them perfectly, or had understood enough, had understood that the men who came to the house on Manoa Road to drink her father's Scotch and talk to her father about matters she was not supposed to understand were engaged in running various aspects of the world and that this was how the world got run, not by the people whose names appeared in newspapers, not by the people who stood up in rooms and spoke into microphones, but by men like Jack Lovett and men like her father, quietly, over Scotch, in houses on Manoa Road and in hotel rooms in Kuala Lumpur and in rooms she would never see in buildings she would never enter in cities where she had never been.

She had understood this and then she had married Paul Christian, who was not like her father and was not like Jack Lovett and did believe that the world got run by the people who stood up in rooms and spoke into microphones. She had married Paul Christian and moved to the mainland and had two children and Paul Christian had gone into his father-in-law's business and then out of his father-in-law's business and then into politics, first the California assembly and then, with her father's money and certain other money that came from places Inez preferred not to examine too closely, the United States Senate, where he served, if served was the right word, which Inez doubted, on the Armed Services Committee and the Foreign Relations Committee and on several subcommittees whose functions she could not have explained had she been required to do so. She was not required to do so. What she was required to do was appear, periodically, at events, wearing certain clothes, saying certain things or saying nothing, smiling. She was good at appearing. She was good at the clothes. The smiling she could do in her sleep and sometimes thought she did.

What I'm telling you, Jack Lovett said again, still looking at the airshaft and the cat, is that we could just go. Right now. Leave right now.

I know what you're telling me, Inez said.

Then what do you say.

I say Paul is in Jakarta. I say the children are in school in Virginia. I say my father is in Honolulu pretending to recover from something everyone is pretending was not a heart attack.

Those are facts, Jack Lovett said. I didn't ask about facts.

This was one of the ways Jack Lovett operated. He had always been this way. He was not interested in facts as obstacles, only as information, data points, things to be routed around or used. He had a way of looking at a situation that stripped it of its emotional content and left only the operational question, which was always the same question: what do you do next. Inez had found this quality by turns attractive and frightening and she had never been able to decide which it was more of, the attraction or the fear, and she had come to believe that this indecision was itself the answer, that you could not have one without the other, that the thing that drew her to Jack Lovett was precisely the thing that should have made her stay in the house in McLean and supervise the homework and attend the dinners and smile at the people she was supposed to smile at.

She finished the soup.

Tell me more about the pink, she said.

He turned from the window and looked at her.

The pink sky, she said. You never finished telling me about the pink sky.

He sat down on the edge of the bed and was quiet for a moment. The ceiling fan ticked. It had been ticking all afternoon, some mechanical tic, some wire loose in the motor, tick tick tick, and Inez had been waiting for it to stop or to start moving and it had done neither.

You'd have to see it, he said finally.

You said that about the gardenias too.

You'd have to smell that too.

I've smelled gardenias.

Not like that. Not in that air. Not at four in the morning with the generators running and the technical guys on the radio and the whole thing about to go. It's not a smell you can approximate any other way, it's specific to the situation. Like the light. That pink is specific to that situation. I've been in the Pacific at dawn a hundred times since and I never saw that pink again, you'd see red and you'd see the regular colors but never that particular pink. It required the event.

He was quiet again.

I've thought about that, he said. About whether that makes it beautiful or whether it just makes it the thing it was. Whether you can call something beautiful when it requires that to make it happen.

Inez looked at him. This was unusual. This was not how Jack Lovett generally talked, he did not generally raise this kind of question, he had not in her experience spent much time wondering whether things were beautiful, he had spent his time knowing where they were and how to get to them and what they would cost and what they were worth in terms of other things. This was, she understood, why he was useful to the people he was useful to. This was why men called him from the kinds of telephones that were not listed and met him in the lobbies of certain hotels and why packages came to him and went from him and why he was always moving, Honolulu to Manila to Bangkok to Kuala Lumpur to Singapore and back again, the same circuit, year after year, fraying slightly at the edges the way the pale shirts frayed, the color going out of things gradually, the cotton thinning at the collar, but the essential structure remaining.

She wondered sometimes what he thought about Paul. He never said. She had asked him once, years before, what he thought of Paul Christian, and he had said, Paul's a good-looking fellow, and she had known that was all she was going to get on the subject of Paul Christian from Jack Lovett.

Paul was not a good-looking fellow, actually. Paul was tall and had his teeth fixed regularly and knew how to wear a suit and those things together could read as good-looking in certain lights and at certain distances, the distances at which most people encountered United States senators, which was not close. Up close Paul had a quality she had never found the right word for, an effortfulness, as if each expression had to be consciously assembled from available parts, here is the concerned look, here is the warm look, here is the look of a man who has listened carefully to what you said and found it interesting. It was not dishonesty exactly. She believed that Paul believed he was concerned, warm, interested. It was more that nothing in him happened automatically, everything required this small assembly, and she could see the assembly, had always been able to see it, and suspected that this was why Paul did not entirely like her, had never entirely liked her even in the period when he believed he was in love with her, because she could see the assembly and he knew it.

Jack Lovett looked at her.

Come to the islands with me, he said.

You don't have time, Inez said. You have something in Singapore on Thursday.

He didn't ask how she knew about Singapore. She always knew these things. She had always paid attention to where Jack Lovett was going and when, she had done this since she was sixteen years old, had kept some internal calendar of his movements the way you would track weather if you lived somewhere the weather could turn.

I can make time, he said.

You always say that.

I always mean it.

You always mean it and then you go to Singapore.

He smiled at her. He had a particular smile, not warm, not the assembled warmth of Paul's smile, something more like recognition, as if he kept seeing the same thing in her that he had first seen when she was sixteen years old and was unsurprised to find it still there.

Your father is going to be all right, he said. You know that.

I know that.

The kids are fine.

I know that too.

Paul will be in Jakarta until the fourteenth and then he's going to San Francisco for the fundraiser and then back to Washington.

She looked at him. You know his schedule better than I do, she said.

I know everybody's schedule, he said. That's what I do.

This was true. It was, as far as she had ever been able to determine, the core of what Jack Lovett did, the thing that lay beneath all the other things, the pale shirts and the local currency and the phone calls and the packages: he knew where people were and where they were going to be. He had built his entire life on this knowledge, had made himself valuable to certain interests by maintaining this knowledge, had traveled the same circuits year after year not because the circuits were interesting but because they were the circuits along which information moved, and information about where people were and where they were going was the only information that finally mattered, because everything else, all the other information, the tonnage and the coordinates and the dates and the money, all of it was only useful if you knew where the people were.

She had thought about this, over the years. She had thought about what it meant to have organized your life around the question of location, where everyone was at every moment, and she had thought that it must make certain things very clear and certain other things very difficult, you would be very clear on logistics and very unclear on everything else, you would know the what and the when and the where and you would be largely uninterested in the why, which was why Jack Lovett could sit on the edge of a bed in Kuala Lumpur and say come to the islands with me and not be asking her to make any kind of declaration, not asking her to say anything about Paul or about the house in McLean or about the life that was conducted in that house and those like it, he was asking her only the operational question, would she go or wouldn't she, and the rest of it was her problem, or possibly nobody's problem, possibly just the way things were.

She got up and went to the window where he had been standing and looked at the airshaft. The cat was still there. It was a thin cat with an air of patient entitlement that reminded her of certain people she knew in Washington.

Tell me something, she said.

He waited.

Those islands, she said. The contaminated ones. The ones where the shots were.

What about them.

Are they empty. Is there anything living there.

He was quiet for a moment. Some things, he said. You get some vegetation coming back. Some bird populations. Frigate birds, some species like that. The fish are back in most of the lagoons.

But no people.

No. No people.

She watched the cat.

Sometimes I think about that, she said. Places without people. Whether that's better or worse.

Better or worse than what, Jack Lovett said.

Just better or worse.

That's not a question I can answer, he said. That's not the kind of question I answer.

I know that.

It's not the kind of question anybody can answer.

Somebody ought to, Inez said.

He didn't say anything. She could hear him behind her, not moving, the ceiling fan ticking, the distant sound of the street below, motorbikes, someone selling something from a cart, a sound she could not identify, musical, two notes repeated.

She thought about her daughter Jessie, who was fourteen and had opinions about everything and who had lately developed a habit of looking at Inez with an expression that suggested Inez was evidence of something, proof of some thesis Jessie was developing, though what the thesis was Inez could not determine and was not sure she wanted to. She thought about her son Harry, who was seventeen and who had already begun, she could see it happening, to have the quality of a person who moves through spaces without quite occupying them, the quality Paul had but in Harry it was different, in Paul it was an absence of natural expression and in Harry it was more like a principled withdrawal, as if Harry had looked at the available options for occupying a space and found them all inadequate and was holding out for something else. She did not know what would happen to Harry. She thought about this more than she thought about most things.

She turned from the window.

All right, she said.

Jack Lovett looked at her without surprise. All right what, he said, though he knew.

All right the islands. If you can get there and back before the fourteenth. If there's a way to do it.

There's always a way, he said. You know that.

And this was also true. There was always a way. She had been watching Jack Lovett find ways since she was sixteen years old and she had never seen him fail to find one, never seen him come up against a situation where there was no available route, no contact, no transport, no document, no arrangement that could not be made. It was possible this was because he only attempted things that could be arranged, that he had a very accurate prior sense of what was possible and never extended himself beyond it, but she had chosen over the years to believe that it was something else, that it was a genuine capacity, a way of seeing the material world as fundamentally permeable, full of doors that looked like walls until you knew where to push.

She thought about the pink sky. She had been hearing about it for twenty years and she had formed an idea of it that was probably nothing like the thing itself, she had this pink in her mind that was the pink of certain clouds she had seen from the lanai on Manoa Road when she was a girl, before her mother left, before certain things happened, and she knew that was wrong, that what Jack Lovett was describing was nothing like that pink, required the event, he had said, required the generators and the radio and the technical guys and then the shot, and she knew she would probably go to those islands and see the sky at dawn and it would not be what she was imagining and would be something else entirely, something she had no image for yet, and she thought that was probably why she was going, that was probably the whole of it, to see the thing she had no image for.

Outside in the street the vendor or whoever it was repeated the two notes, musical, patient, and the cat in the airshaft shifted on its ledge and regarded some private matter with the calm of something that has been waiting a long time and expects to wait longer still.

She began to think about what she would need to take and what she could leave, which was almost everything, she had learned over the years to travel light, not because Jack Lovett had taught her this but because she had observed that the things you brought were rarely the things you needed and the things you needed were rarely things you could bring, and so the calculus of packing had come to seem to her a kind of lesson in the limits of anticipation, you packed for the version of the trip that existed in your imagination and the actual trip was always somewhere else, always in a place the imagination had not quite reached.

Jack Lovett was on the telephone, she realized. He had picked up the telephone at some point while she was thinking and was talking to someone in a language she didn't speak, arranging the way, and she stood at the window and listened to the two musical notes from the street and watched the cat and thought about her daughter Jessie's expression and about her son Harry's principled withdrawal and about her father lying in a bed in Honolulu while her mother, who had been gone for more than fifteen years, sent a telegram that Inez had read three times and still could not parse, and she thought that the difficulty with pink skies was that they were always in the past tense, always something someone was telling you about, you never got to them in time, you always arrived after the pink had already gone and what was left was just the regular sky, red and then blue, the available colors, nothing specific to the situation.

But you went anyway. You went to the islands anyway, you put your feet in the water, you got there at four in the morning and you watched for the light. That was what she had decided, standing at the window in Kuala Lumpur in 1969 or possibly 1970, listening to Jack Lovett arrange the way in a language she didn't speak. You went anyway. You paid attention anyway. You kept the internal calendar of someone else's movements and you traveled light and you ate the warm soup when the cold soup didn't come and you looked at the sky and called it beautiful or didn't call it anything and either way the pink was specific to the situation and you were there for it or you weren't and she intended to be there for it.

Jack Lovett hung up the telephone.

Tomorrow, he said. Six A.M. There's a flight to Manila and then we go south. I've got a contact at Clark who can put us on something from there.

She nodded.

You should sleep, he said.

I know.

Are you going to sleep.

Probably not, she said.

He looked at her for a moment with the recognition that was not warmth but was something more durable than warmth, something that had survived twenty years and two continents and Paul Christian and certain arrangements she did not ask about and certain things that had happened in the world during those twenty years that they never discussed, never directly discussed, though the world and its arrangements were always present in the room with them the way the ceiling fan was present, ticking, not working, the wire loose in the motor.

Get some sleep, he said. We have to be at the airport at five.

She lay down on the bed with her clothes on. The ceiling fan ticked. In the street below the vendor repeated the two notes one more time and then was gone, and the street settled into its other sounds, and Inez lay on the bed in Kuala Lumpur and thought about atolls, sand spits, the matting rolled down like a bathmat, two Quonsets, the smell of flowers that weren't there, and somewhere in thinking about this she fell asleep, and the last thing she was aware of was Jack Lovett at the window looking into the airshaft, looking at whatever there was to see.
