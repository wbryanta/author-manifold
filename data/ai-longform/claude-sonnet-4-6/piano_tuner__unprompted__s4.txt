The morning he drove out to begin the last round, there was frost on the windshield that he scraped away with a credit card he no longer used, and the fields on either side of the highway were pale and stiff as sailcloth. Walter Foss had been driving this route for thirty-one years, longer than some of the farms he visited had been in their current families' hands. He knew the sequence of mailboxes, the way the road dipped before the Hendersons' place and rose sharply after the Gruber property, the particular gravel-crunch of the Oleander Church driveway that announced arrival before the building itself came into view. He knew all of it the way you know a piece you have played so many times the fingers move without instruction from the mind, the way the body becomes a kind of memory.

He had not told anyone it would be the last round. He had told his daughter-in-law, Miriam, only that he was taking a few days to finish some business upstate, which was not untrue, just not complete. She had offered to drive him. He had refused in the particular way he had of refusing things, which was to change the subject entirely and ask about the grandchildren until the offer was forgotten. He was seventy-four years old. His hands still worked. The arthritis was in his left hip now, not his hands, and he had decided some time ago that he would stop when his hands stopped, not before.

In the back of the Subaru, wrapped in old moving blankets, were his tools. The tuning lever, which he had used for forty years and which fit his palm like something that had grown there. The mutes—rubber wedges and felt strips—which he organized by size in a wooden box his wife had given him in 1987, a birthday present, stained walnut, with a small brass clasp that still clicked cleanly. The pitch pipe he rarely used anymore, having long since internalized the reference A, which lived in his inner ear like a tone that never stopped sounding. He had sometimes wondered, in the years since Ruth died, whether that internal A was the last clean thing in him, the one frequency that remained accurate while everything else drifted.

The first stop was the Brennan farm, twelve miles out, where there was a 1920s Steinway upright that had not been played regularly since the grandmother died. The current Brennans were Caitlin and her husband Doug, who raised heritage turkeys and homeschooled their three children. Caitlin always offered him coffee. He always accepted. The children watched him work from the doorway of the parlor, which was actually the converted front room of the farmhouse where the piano sat on a braided rug, legs slightly uneven on the old floor so that a folded matchbook was required under the left front foot to keep it from rocking. He had put that matchbook there himself, years ago.

Caitlin met him at the door before he knocked. She did this every time—appeared as if she'd been watching from the window, which she probably had.

"Walter," she said. "I've got the coffee on."

"You always do," he said.

He followed her inside. The house smelled of wood smoke and something baking, and the piano was where it always was, waiting in the dimness of the front room with its lid closed and a folded quilt draped over the bench. He set down his bag and opened the lid and pressed middle C before he even took off his coat. It was flat by about thirty cents. Humidity in the summer had swelled the soundboard, and the winter's dry air had contracted it again, and the strings had followed along like everything else that has no choice but to respond to the conditions in which it finds itself.

The children appeared in the doorway. Two girls and a boy, the oldest perhaps twelve. They never came into the room. They just watched. He had noticed over the years that children either found tuning deeply boring or were transfixed by it, and these three had always been the latter. He did not perform for them but he was aware of them, in the way he was aware of the quality of light in a room.

He began with the temperament octave, spanning from F3 to F4, setting the intervals in sequence, the fourths and fifths beating against each other in the precise ratios that equal temperament required. This was the part of the work that most people didn't understand—that a piano in tune was not a piano in pure mathematical harmony, that equal temperament was actually a system of calibrated compromises, every interval slightly off from its acoustic ideal, the errors distributed evenly across all twelve keys so that no single key suffered more than any other. He had tried to explain this once to Doug Brennan, who had stared at him and then said, so you mean it's always a little bit wrong? And Walter had thought about this and said yes, exactly, that's exactly right, it's always a little bit wrong in a way that makes it work.

He moved through the octaves. The treble strings were single and bright; the bass strings were wound copper, thicker and slower in their response. He worked by listening to the beats—the wavering oscillations that occurred when two strings were slightly misaligned in frequency—and eliminating them, or in some cases, in the upper treble where pure beatlessness sounded actually sharp to the human ear, leaving a controlled imprecision. The room filled with the sound of single notes struck and ringing, then pairs, then the occasional chord to check the overall color of the tuning. The children did not move.

When he was finished, he played a few bars of something. He did not decide to do this—his hands simply moved. They played the opening of a Chopin nocturne, Op. 9, No. 2, which was not technically demanding and which he had known since he was a boy. The room sounded different now. Caitlin appeared in the doorway.

"Beautiful," she said.

He closed the lid. She paid him in cash, which he accepted, and pressed upon him a small jar of honey from her hives, which he also accepted, and they stood for a moment in the way of people who have known each other for a long time without ever becoming close, which is its own kind of relationship, defined by the precise nature of its limits.

Outside, he sat in the car for a moment before starting it. The fields beyond the Brennan fence were the color of old paper. He put the honey on the passenger seat and drove.

The next stop was nine miles further, the Vickers place, where there was a younger piano, a 1970s Yamaha console that had been purchased optimistically when their daughter expressed interest in lessons. The daughter was now forty and lived in Portland. The piano was used occasionally as a surface upon which to store mail and decorative gourds and one year a taxidermied fox that had since migrated to the mantelpiece. Harold Vickers was a widower now and answered the door in the same green cardigan he had been wearing every October for as long as Walter could remember. He was eighty-one. He moved slowly. He had the patience of someone for whom there was no longer any reason to hurry.

Walter worked on the Yamaha while Harold sat in the kitchen and read the newspaper. They did not talk much. This had always been their arrangement. Harold had no real feeling for the piano and called it the instrument, as in the instrument needs tuning again, said with a slight bewilderment, as if the piano's demands on him were mildly unreasonable but he had learned to accommodate them. Walter appreciated this. He did not require that people love what he loved. He only required that they take it seriously enough to have it maintained.

The Yamaha was in reasonable condition. The strings were slightly bright in the middle register, a common problem in instruments that sat in heated rooms through dry winters. He adjusted and checked, adjusted and checked. Through the window he could see Harold's fields, a surviving stand of corn left unharvested, the stalks brown and rattling in the light wind. He did not know why Harold had not harvested it. He did not ask.

When he finished, he knocked on the kitchen doorframe and Harold looked up from his paper.

"All done?" Harold said.

"All done," Walter said.

Harold paid him from a white envelope he had apparently prepared in advance and left on the counter. This, too, was always their arrangement. As Walter was putting on his coat, Harold said, without looking up from the newspaper he had already returned to, I heard you're retiring.

Walter paused. He had not told anyone.

"Word gets around," he said.

"It does," said Harold.

There was a silence that was not uncomfortable. Harold turned a page.

"I'll miss you coming," Harold said. "Not the piano. Just you coming."

Walter stood for a moment. "I'll miss coming," he said.

He drove north.

The afternoon brought him to the Oleander settlement, which was not exactly a town—it had no gas station, no store, nothing that would make it appear on most maps—but was rather a loose congregation of farms and a few old houses and the church, all gathered around a crossroads as if for warmth. He had three more calls before the church. A young family named Kowalski who had moved into the old Schafer place and had two piano-playing children, which was a relief to him whenever he thought about the alternative futures of this route. An elderly woman named Doris Pflaum who played hymns by ear and whose piano, a 1950s Baldwin spinet, had a sticky D-sharp in the fourth octave that always returned no matter how many times he fixed it, as if the key were committed to its dysfunction. And finally, the Merchant house, where there was an old Mason and Hamlin grand that was the finest piano on the route and possibly the finest piano Walter had worked on outside a professional concert setting, kept in a room that was used for nothing else, owned by a man named George Merchant who did not play and whose wife, who had played beautifully, had been dead for fifteen years.

George Merchant was the one who had told Walter about the route in the first place. They had been young men together, working at a piano shop in the city, and when the old tuner who serviced this rural district retired, George had said, Walter, you should take the route, it's not much but it'll be steady, and Walter had taken it and it had been steady, and then George had married a woman named Annette who played Schubert with a quality of attention that Walter had found almost unbearable to be in the presence of, and then Annette had died, and George had kept the Mason and Hamlin exactly as she had left it, and asked Walter to come every year to tune it, which Walter did.

George met him in the driveway, wearing a barn coat, white-haired now, thinner than he had been.

"Last time," George said.

"Last time," Walter agreed.

They went inside. The room with the piano was dim and cool, a room that had been maintained as a kind of stasis, as if the air inside it had been preserved from some earlier moment. The Mason and Hamlin was in excellent condition. George kept a humidity control system installed in the case and the room temperature was consistent, and the result was that the piano aged slowly, the way things age when they are very carefully loved. It was also, Walter thought, one of the loneliest rooms he had ever been in.

He worked. The piano needed very little—some adjustment in the upper registers, a couple of bass strings that had drifted, a general refinement of the temperament. It was already close. It was always already close.

George stood in the doorway. He had never sat down during the tuning, in all the years Walter had come. He always stood in the doorway, arms crossed, watching, the way people watch something they cannot participate in but cannot look away from either.

When Walter finished, he played. He had not always done this at the Merchant house—he had started doing it perhaps ten years ago, without deciding to, without being asked. He played whatever came. Tonight his hands found a Schubert impromptu, the G-flat major one, which he played imperfectly but with the kind of feeling that forgives a great deal of imprecision. He did not look at George. He heard George's breathing change.

When he finished, the sound died in the room, absorbed by the curtains and the carpet and the walls.

"Thank you," George said. His voice was level.

"The piano is beautiful," Walter said. "She kept it beautifully."

"She did," George said.

They stood for a moment in the accumulated weight of all the things they had known about each other for forty years. Then Walter packed his tools and put on his coat and George walked him to the car.

"You'll be all right?" George said.

It was an odd question, or perhaps not. "I expect so," Walter said.

"Come back and visit," George said. "You don't have to tune anything."

Walter said he would. He did not know if this was true or not. He drove down the gravel lane toward the crossroads, and in the rearview mirror George stood by his mailbox watching until the road bent and the house was no longer visible.

The church was a half-mile from the crossroads, set back from the road on a slight rise. It was white clapboard with a small bell tower that had no bell, the bell having been removed for safety reasons some years ago when the steeple began to show structural concerns that were repaired but after which the bell was never returned. A cemetery sat to the east side, modest headstones, the oldest ones tilted by decades of frost. The parking lot was empty. Pastor Eileen Garber had left the side door unlocked for him, as arranged. He had her phone number in case of questions. He doubted there would be questions.

He sat in the car for a while.

The piano inside was an old Baldwin console, similar to Doris Pflaum's but better maintained, a modest instrument appropriate to a modest congregation. It sat to the right of the pulpit, on a low platform. The church was used for services on Sunday mornings and Wednesday evenings, for the occasional wedding or funeral, for the Christmas pageant. It was used, which was more than could be said for most of the instruments he had tuned that day.

His son Peter had been nine years old when he gave his recital here. That would have been thirty-nine years ago, in a spring. The church had agreed to loan the space—they had had a better piano then, a full upright Kawai that had eventually been replaced—and Ruth had arranged folding chairs, and the families of Peter's classmates from his piano teacher Mrs. Holt's studio had come, perhaps thirty people, a small audience, the kind suited to a nine-year-old's first public performance.

Walter had not been nervous. He remembered this clearly—that he had not been nervous, because he had thought he knew how it would go. He knew his son's playing. He knew the pieces. He had heard Peter work through them for weeks, had sat in the kitchen pretending to read the paper while the sound came through the wall. Peter was not exceptional but he was earnest and consistent, and he had the quality that Walter believed was actually more important than talent, which was that he genuinely heard what he was doing. He was not playing notes. He was listening to them.

Peter had played three pieces. A Bach minuet, a simplified Clementi sonatina, and a piece by a composer Walter could no longer recall, something modern, slightly jazzy, which had been Mrs. Holt's idea of keeping things fresh. He had played all three without stopping, without significant error, with the careful gravity of a nine-year-old who understands that something is being witnessed. Walter had watched his son's back—the piano faced away from the audience, as it did now—the narrow shoulders, the way his head inclined slightly to the left when he was concentrating. Ruth had been beside Walter, and at some point her hand had found his without either of them planning it.

The applause when Peter finished had been warm and genuine and the sound of it in the church acoustics had been full, had been more than thirty people could normally produce, the ceiling and walls giving it back doubled, and Peter had stood and turned and bowed with a formality that had made people laugh, gently, affectionately, and his face in that moment had been—

Walter could not finish the thought. He got out of the car.

Inside, the church smelled of old wood and candle wax and hymnals. The rows of folding chairs had been replaced long ago with plain wooden pews. The Baldwin sat on its platform. Someone had left a piece of sheet music on the stand, a hymn, pages slightly curled. He left it where it was.

He set down his bag and took off his coat and hung it on a hook by the door and then he stood for a moment, not in front of the piano but in the middle of the room, in the aisle between the pews. The light was going. It came through the west windows at a long angle, the color of the honey jar on his passenger seat, and lit the dust that moved slowly in the air, and the room was very quiet except for the sound of his own breathing.

Peter had not played again after that recital. Not publicly. He had continued lessons for another year and then stopped—Mrs. Holt had moved away, and they had not found another teacher, and gradually the piano in their house had become a different kind of furniture. Walter had not pushed. He was not certain now, this many years later, whether not pushing had been the right decision, whether some other version of himself might have found a way to keep that door open. Peter had become an engineer. He had married Miriam and had two children and lived forty minutes away. He called on Sundays. He was a good man. He did not play the piano.

What Walter had never told Peter, had never told anyone, was that the recital had been the finest musical experience of his life. Not because of the playing, which had been modest, appropriate to its performer. But because of the quality of attention Peter had brought to it—that listening quality, the inclined head, the sense of a person really hearing what was happening in the room. Walter had spent forty years working on instruments that other people played, had spent his life in the service of music without being its source, and he had made his peace with this, and it had been a good life, but what he had seen in his son that afternoon, the capacity for that quality of attention, had seemed to him the essential thing, the thing at the center of it all. And Peter had had it. Whatever had happened later, he had had it then.

He went to the piano.

He opened the lid and pressed middle C. It was flat, noticeably so—the church was not climate-controlled and the instrument had suffered for it, had been drifting since probably the last time he had come, twelve months ago. He opened his bag and took out his tools, the tuning lever, the mutes, the felt strips. He sat down on the bench.

He worked. The light through the windows moved as he worked, the long afternoon becoming the early evening, and he worked by feel in the dimming room, his hands certain of what they were doing, the sounds of the individual strings and then the pairs and then the chords filling the church and returning to him, the room taking what he gave it and giving it back changed. He moved through the octaves. He set the temperament. He refined. The piano was worse than he had expected—there was a cracked bridge pin in the bass that he noted, would leave in a note for Pastor Garber—but the essential structure was sound, and what could be tuned was tuned, and the room filled gradually with the corrected sound, the sound of something being brought back into its proper relationship with itself.

When he was finished, he sat for a long time without playing.

He thought about Ruth's hand finding his in the dark. He thought about Peter's shoulders, the left-inclining head. He thought about the applause that had been larger than the room should have produced, that had seemed to come from the walls themselves, as if the church had been moved by something and was responding.

He put his hands on the keys.

He did not play the Chopin or the Schubert. He played the Bach minuet that Peter had played thirty-nine years ago, which Walter had heard through the kitchen wall so many times that fall that he still knew it by heart. He played it slowly, at a child's tempo, not performing it but following it, the way you follow a path you walked once with someone you loved. His left hip ached from the drive, from the hours of sitting and standing, and the bench was hard, and his eyes were wet in a way he did not bother to address. He played it through once without stopping. He made no errors.

The last note rang in the church and he lifted his hands from the keys and let it go.

The room was dark now. He could see through the west windows that the last light had left the sky and the stars were beginning, and the fields beyond the church were colorless, and the gravestones to the east were shapes only, forms his mind identified without requiring detail. He sat at the piano in the good dark, in the church that gave back what you brought to it, and he listened to the silence that followed the note, which was not the same silence that had preceded it, which was the silence of something completed, of an interval resolved at last to its proper tone.

He sat there a little while longer. Then he packed his tools.
