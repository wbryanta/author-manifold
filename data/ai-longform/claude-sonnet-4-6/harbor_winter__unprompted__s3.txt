The water told different stories depending on where you put your eyes. That was the first thing he said to her, standing at the end of the municipal dock in the gray of early January, their breath making small clouds that the wind took apart before they could amount to anything. Marisol had her hands inside the front pocket of her sweatshirt and she was watching him instead of the water, watching the way his jaw worked when he was thinking, the way he had of squinting that made him look angry when she knew by now he was only concentrating.

Read the water, he said. Not me.

So she looked. It was the color of pewter that morning, and it moved in long low swells that seemed to come from everywhere and nowhere, and she couldn't tell what she was supposed to see. She told him that.

He said: You're looking at it like it's a picture. Look at it like it's talking.

She was eleven years old and she had been in Paulsen Bay for three months, since her mother had driven her up from Sacramento in the rusted Civic and left her with a bag and a box and a kiss on the forehead that lasted maybe two seconds and a promise that she'd be back in the spring, which Marisol had begun to suspect was the kind of promise that existed mainly to make the person making it feel better about leaving. She understood this without quite being able to say it. She was old enough to understand certain things without language and young enough that this felt like a deficiency rather than a kind of wisdom.

Her grandfather's name was Einar Voss. People in town called him Captain, which he corrected every time and which never once changed what they called him. He was seventy-three and moved like a man who had spent decades learning how to balance on surfaces that would not hold still, a slight roll in his walk even on land, as though his body still expected the ground to shift. He had piloted vessels in and out of this harbor for thirty-one years before his eyes started going, and before the eyes there had been the hands, a tremor that came and went and then mostly came, and before the tremor there had been his wife Ingrid dying in the night the way some people do, just deciding, her body making a private decision without consulting anyone. That had been four years ago. Marisol had not known her grandmother. She knew about her mostly from the photographs on the mantle and from the way her grandfather went quiet in certain rooms of the house.

The cannery behind them was still running that winter, barely. You could hear it from the dock some mornings, the industrial hum and clatter of it, and you could smell it always, that deep salt-and-iron smell that had soaked into the wood of the whole town, into the weatherboarding of the houses, into the coats that people wore. The Halverson Cannery had been the reason for Paulsen Bay in any practical sense. It had been the reason people stayed, the reason people came, the reason the school had stayed open as long as it had and the reason there was still a bar and a hardware store and a woman named Doris who ran a flower shop out of her garage that also sold lottery tickets and cigarettes. The announcement had come in November, three weeks after Marisol arrived, and the town had received it the way people receive news they have been expecting for a long time: with a kind of exhausted acknowledgment, like watching a thing you've been bracing for finally fall.

Her grandfather had not seemed surprised. He had read about it in the weekly paper, folded it, and put it on the kitchen table with a kind of deliberate care.

Well, he'd said. There it is.

She had asked him what would happen and he had said he supposed people would leave, some of them, the ones who could. And the others? The others will stay, he said, and turned back to the stove where a pot of something was making the windows fog.

She understood she was one of the others.

The reading of the water was his idea. She had mentioned, on her second week there, that she was bored in the way that eleven-year-olds are bored, comprehensively and without apology, and he had taken her out to the dock the next morning without explaining why. She had expected something more structured, a lesson with components, the kind of thing school organized into packets and worksheets. Instead he just stood there and looked at the water and occasionally said things that were more like noticing aloud than teaching.

Ripple pattern there, he might say, pointing. Current pushing against something below. A shelf, maybe. Or a wreck.

Or: Color change. You see it? Right along that line. Depth change. The water knows where the bottom is even when you don't.

He didn't check whether she was following. He just said the things, and she found that she began to listen in a different way because of this, the way you listen to a conversation you're not supposed to hear, carefully and without the performance of attention.

By January she was following him voluntarily. She'd started getting up before school and going down to the dock in the dark, and sometimes he was already there and sometimes he came down twenty minutes later with two cups of coffee, one of which was mostly milk, and gave her the lesser one without comment. The dock smelled of old wood and diesel and the brine that had worked its way into everything. The pylons were draped with weed at their bases and sometimes there were sea lions on the floating dock to the east, slumped against each other with profound indifference to the cold.

That January morning she was looking at the water and trying to let it talk.

What she could see: the way the surface wasn't uniform, the way there were textures within the gray, some places that looked almost smooth and other places that were finely ridged, like the skin of something large and breathing. She could see where the current ran because the water there had a different quality of movement, a purpose in it, and she could see where the wind was hitting the surface because the ripples there were smaller and more chaotic, crosshatched. There was a dark line about two hundred meters out that could have been a change in depth or could have been the shadow of a cloud.

She described these things to him.

He was quiet for a moment. Then he said: What else.

She looked harder. There was a disturbance near the north end of the channel, a slight boiling-up that came and went, that wasn't consistent with the surface movement around it.

She pointed. What's that?

Upwelling, he said. Cold water from below. There's a spring down there, fresh water, comes up through a crack in the rock. Been there my whole life. My father showed it to me.

She thought about that. How the water remembered the crack in the rock and came up through it year after year, cold and fresh in all that salt.

In the afternoons after school she sometimes walked through town, which took about as long as it sounds. The main street was four blocks and most of it had the look of a place waiting to see what it would become next. Several storefronts had been empty long enough that people had stopped noticing them. The barbershop was still open, run by a man named Pete Orvald who was also the town's unofficial historian and who would tell you the history of any building you pointed at, in detail and with an enthusiasm that the buildings themselves seemed unable to justify. The school had forty-one students in grades kindergarten through eight. Her class had six. There was a girl named Ragna whose family ran the hardware store and who had accepted Marisol's presence with a pragmatic warmth that Marisol was grateful for without knowing quite how to express it.

They walked home together sometimes, Ragna and Marisol, taking the long way along the water. Ragna had grown up here and knew everyone and had the particular unselfconsciousness of children who have not yet been somewhere else and measured home against it. She talked about the cannery the way she talked about the weather, as something large and external that shaped what was possible.

My dad says we'll stay, Ragna told her once. We stayed through the last bad time and we'll stay through this one.

What was the last bad time? Marisol asked.

Ragna thought about it. I don't know exactly, she said. Before us.

Marisol told her grandfather about this conversation. They were in the kitchen, a Saturday, and he was making the fish stew he made every Saturday, working slowly at the counter, his hands moving through the familiar steps.

Halvorsens built that cannery in 1931, he said. Right in the middle of everything being terrible. His voice had that particular flatness that wasn't indifference, she had learned, but rather the opposite of indifference, a deliberate holding.

Why then?

He turned to look at her. Because when everything is already bad, he said, you're not scared of it going wrong.

She thought about her mother. She thought about the drive up, the long miles of interstate and then the smaller roads and finally the gravel turnoff, her mother getting quieter the closer they got, as though the distance from Sacramento was a kind of decompression and at the end of it she had run out of words entirely. She had stood in her father's kitchen looking at the walls and Marisol had watched her do this and understood without being told that her mother was trying to see something that wasn't there anymore, some version of herself that had lived here once and been young and had not yet made most of her choices.

Her mother called on Sundays. The calls lasted somewhere between eight and twenty minutes and covered school and the weather and what Marisol had been eating and then arrived at a silence that neither of them could quite fill and they said goodbye and Marisol sat with the phone for a moment after before putting it down. Her grandfather never asked about the calls. He was in the other room when they happened and he stayed there, which she appreciated.

One Sunday in late January, after the call, she went out to the dock alone. It was late afternoon and the light was going in that particular way it went in winter up here, not dramatically but steadily, the way water drains, and the harbor had gone from pewter to a deep blue-gray that was almost purple at the edges. She stood at the end of the dock and she looked at the water.

She could see the current now without thinking about it, could see the upwelling to the north that she had been watching long enough to know its rhythm, could see the way the incoming tide was pushing against the outflow of the river two miles north and creating a confusion of small competing waves at a particular spot that her grandfather had told her was called a rip, though not a dangerous one, just the water arguing with itself. She could see a vessel coming in around the head, a seiner by its silhouette, running lights just beginning to show in the fading afternoon.

She watched it come and she tracked its line automatically, the way she'd seen her grandfather track it, noting where it was relative to the channel markers, the slight adjustment it made approaching the first buoy, the reduction in speed she could infer from the change in its wake. It was a good line. Whoever was driving knew the harbor.

That would be Solvieg Andersen's boat, her grandfather said from behind her. She hadn't heard him come.

She said: How'd you know I was here?

Where else, he said.

He stood beside her and they watched the seiner come in. The lights of the cannery were on across the harbor, the building's big windows throwing yellow rectangles onto the water. There were only two seasons' worth of fish left to process under the Halverson contract, maybe less if the fleet kept coming in light the way it had been coming in light. People talked about this in the way they talked about the weather, with a mixture of complaint and fatalism. Some of the fishermen had already started making calls to Kodiak, to Dutch Harbor, to wherever the work might be.

How come you stayed? Marisol asked.

He was quiet for long enough that she thought he might not answer. Then he said: After your grandmother died, people asked me that. They meant, why didn't I move somewhere warmer, somewhere closer to—he paused—to other things. He picked up the thread again. I told them I didn't know another place. This harbor. I know it. I know where the bottom is without looking. I know where the water comes up cold by the north dock and I know which way the current runs in January and I know that there's a submerged piling about sixty meters off the end of this dock that's been there since before I was born and I know how the morning light hits the water in August and I know what a swell looks like when it's wrapping around the head and bringing weather behind it.

He stopped. She waited.

You can't put that down somewhere and pick it up again somewhere else, he said. It's not portable.

She thought about what it would mean to know a place that way, all the way down to the bottom of it, to have it in your body the way he had it in his, in the roll of his walk and the automatic tracking of a boat's line. She thought about Sacramento, which she knew mostly as a series of routes between places: the apartment, the school, the bus stop, the corner store where her mother bought cigarettes even though she was supposed to have quit. She knew it as a logistical problem rather than a place. She didn't know what was under it.

The seiner came alongside the cannery dock and she could hear, distantly, the voices carrying over the water. The cannery lights made a long broken reflection in the chop.

She asked him: What will the water look like when the cannery's gone?

He looked at her sideways. The same, he said. It'll look the same.

She wasn't sure that was right but she didn't argue.

February came in with its full weight and the days were short enough that she was walking to school in the dark and coming home in the near-dark and the harbor was a dark thing at the center of the town's life, felt more than seen on most days, heard always—the water against the dock, the water against the breakwater, the water in the pipes of the building when the wind came off it and pushed its cold through every crack. She had stopped minding the cold in the specific way she had minded it at first. It had become a condition of things rather than a problem with things, which was a change she noticed as a kind of relief.

She was learning to read clouds too now, which he said was part of the same thing. The water and the sky were in conversation, he said, and you had to learn both sides of it before you could follow the argument. He showed her the different architectures of clouds and what they meant for weather, the mares' tails that meant wind coming, the low gray ceiling that meant the front was already on top of them. He showed her how to read the color of the horizon and what the behavior of birds could tell you, the way the gulls got low and scrappy before a storm, the way certain ducks appeared in the harbor only when something had pushed them from somewhere else.

Ragna came over one Saturday and found them out at the dock and stood listening for a while with her arms crossed against the cold.

What are you guys doing? she said.

Marisol said: Reading the water.

Ragna looked at the water. It looks cold, she said.

It is cold, said Marisol. Also there's a rip forming out by the second buoy, see it? That's the tide coming in against the river current.

Ragna looked at her and then looked at the water and then looked at her again. Her expression had something new in it, some small recalibration, the look of someone who has seen a familiar person do an unfamiliar thing and has found it unexpectedly interesting.

After that Ragna came to the dock sometimes on her own mornings, and the three of them stood there with their breath making clouds, and her grandfather said the things he noticed and the two girls listened. He did not modify the lessons for two. He just said what he saw, and they heard it or they didn't.

In March the cannery ran its last processing line. Marisol knew about it because the town knew about it, the way news moves in a small town, not announced exactly but arrived at simultaneously, people knowing before they could say how they knew. She walked by the building on the last day and watched the workers coming out at the end of the shift. Some of them she recognized from around town, the faces she'd catalogued over the months. There was a woman who always wore a bright yellow slicker that had become for Marisol a kind of landmark, a fixed point of reference, and she came out of the cannery that last day and stood in the parking lot and looked at the building for a moment before getting in her truck. It was not a dramatic moment. It was a person looking at a building. But Marisol stood and watched until the truck was gone.

That night at dinner her grandfather talked about the winter of 1971 when a container ship had come in with a cracked hull, taking on water faster than the pumps could handle it, and he had to bring it in through a fog so thick the instruments were the only reality. He had done it by feel and by knowledge, by the picture of the harbor he held inside him like a map that existed in three dimensions and in time, that included not just the shapes of the bottom but the behavior of the water in different conditions, the eddies and the currents and the places where the channel narrowed. He had been twenty-four years old. He had not told anyone how frightened he was.

She asked: Not even grandmother?

He smiled, which was not something he did frequently. Especially not her, he said.

Why?

She would have worried, he said. She was a champion worrier. He said this with a tenderness that made it clear that he had loved her for exactly this.

April came slowly, the light returning in increments so gradual that each day's addition was invisible and then suddenly one afternoon it was perceptibly spring, the quality of the light different, softer and more golden, lasting longer into the evening. The water changed too. It had a different surface in spring, a slight chop where there had been winter swells, the gray giving way to something that, while still cold, had a different relationship with the light, took it in rather than refusing it.

She had not heard from her mother about coming in the spring. The Sunday calls continued and the spring was not mentioned, and Marisol stopped waiting for it to be mentioned and found that this caused less pain than she'd expected, as though the expectation had been the thing that hurt rather than the fact itself. She was making her own local geography. She knew the upwelling and the rip and the submerged piling and the behavior of the gulls before weather and she knew the crack in Ragna's voice when she laughed hard enough and she knew the smell of her grandfather's kitchen at every hour of the day and she knew the sound the dock made underfoot, its particular vocabulary of creaks that changed with the tide.

On a morning in late April she woke before him for the first time, dressed in the dark, and went down to the dock alone. The harbor was very still that morning. The cannery building was dark across the water, its windows empty, and in the stillness it looked like something geological, a formation rather than a structure, something that had always been there and would remain now in a new way, unmanned and quiet, a different kind of presence.

She looked at the water.

The tide was coming in and she could read it in the water's edge where it touched the dock, the slow purposeful rise. She could see the upwelling to the north, faithful as ever. She could see the morning light beginning to differentiate the surface, picking out the current lines, the small eddies that formed and dissolved around the pylons, the long silver path that the early sun made on the eastern reach of the harbor. Somewhere out past the head a boat was moving, its wake visible before its hull came into view.

She put her hands on the railing and she stood there and let the harbor come toward her, which is what it was doing, the tide pressing gently at everything, filling in, the water telling its story in its own unhurried way, and she listened in the manner she had been taught, not looking for a picture but for a conversation, not standing outside it but leaning into it, learning the difference between what was always true and what was only true this morning and what was true only now, at this exact moment, with the light coming this way and the tide at this particular height and the air smelling of brine and diesel and something else underneath it that she had finally learned to name: the raw cold smell of open water, the smell of a place that goes on, that has always gone on, that will continue going on beyond any human arrangement of it, beyond canneries and contracts and the particular decisions of particular people, past all of it, the water reading itself forward into whatever comes next.

She heard his step on the dock behind her. He came and stood beside her and he did not say anything for a while.

Then he said: What do you see?

She took a breath.

Everything, she said. And then she began to tell him.
