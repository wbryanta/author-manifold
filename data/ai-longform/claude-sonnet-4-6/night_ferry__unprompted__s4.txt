The ferry left at eleven, which meant standing in the car queue for two hours beforehand, engine idling, watching the dock workers move in and out of the sodium light with the particular purposefulness of men who have somewhere specific to be. Einar had brought nothing to read. He'd thrown a bag together in twenty minutes—a change of clothes, his razor, the photograph his mother had given him before she died, which he carried everywhere now not out of sentiment but because he didn't know what else to do with it—and driven the four hours north without stopping, eating a gas station sandwich over the wheel somewhere past Røros where the trees thinned and the roadside snow acquired a blue quality in the headlights, a depth that made it look cold even from inside a heated car. He knew it was cold. He knew it was cold the way he knew many things that had stopped mattering to him.

A woman in an orange vest waved him onto the ferry with a flashlight. He drove into the hold, which smelled of diesel and bilge and something sweetly chemical he couldn't name, and he sat in his car for a moment after cutting the engine, listening to the metal tick as it cooled. Around him other drivers were already climbing out, stretching, reaching back for bags and children. A man two cars over lit a cigarette immediately upon stepping out, drawing on it with a concentration that looked like relief. Einar watched him. Then he got out too, because there was nothing to do in a car on a ferry except think, and he had been thinking for four hours and he needed to stop.

He climbed the interior stairs to the passenger deck, which was warm and smelled of coffee and the particular synthetic fog of institutional carpeting. The lounge had the suspended quality of all such spaces—the airport gates and hospital waiting rooms and train station cafes that exist outside ordinary time, holding their populations in a kind of provisional suspension. People arranged themselves in the plastic chairs with their coats still on, most of them looking at phones, a few couples talking in low voices. An older man slept upright with his mouth open, his chin on his chest, entirely committed to unconsciousness in a way Einar found almost admirable.

He bought a coffee from the machine and carried it to the forward windows. The dock was still visible, the town behind it lit in patches, and then the engines changed their note and the water between the ferry and the dock widened and the town began to move away, or the ferry did—the distinction, as always, felt philosophical. He pressed his forehead lightly against the cold glass and watched the lights diminish.

His brother's name was Mats. Was or had been. That was the question, or part of it.

Mats was four years younger and had spent most of his adult life making himself difficult to locate. Not dramatically so—not in the way of people who disappear into addiction or crime, though there had been years when those were real possibilities. More in the way of a man who simply preferred the difficulty of being found to the difficulty of being known. He had worked, at various times, on fishing boats in the north, in a copper mine outside Kiruna, as a cook in a kitchen somewhere, as something vague and possibly illegal involving boats and the Norwegian coast that Einar had not asked about because Mats had mentioned it once, briefly, and then changed the subject with such deliberate casualness that the change itself was a kind of warning. He sent postcards occasionally. He had a phone he answered perhaps a third of the time. He had, as far as Einar knew, no fixed address, no relationship of any permanence, no position in the world that could be described with a noun.

Their mother had died in March. Einar had called Mats when it happened. He'd called six times over two days before Mats picked up. There had been a silence on the line before Mats said anything, a silence long enough that Einar had thought they'd been disconnected, and then Mats said, okay, and Einar said, okay what, and Mats said, okay I heard you, which had not been an adequate response to their mother's death but was, Einar had come to understand, the only response Mats was capable of making—not because he was callous but because feeling things had always been, for Mats, a physical problem, something that happened in the body at a level below language, a pressure he couldn't articulate and therefore couldn't share. He had not come to the funeral. He had sent flowers, which arrived two days late, and Einar had stood in the emptied living room of his mother's house looking at them in their cellophane and had felt an anger so total it had nowhere to go.

That had been eight months ago. They had not spoken since.

The call had come from a police officer named Halvorsen, or perhaps Halvorsen was the name on the form—he hadn't been paying attention. A man matching a general description, found in a fishing village on the island. Tall, lean, possibly in his late thirties or early forties, a scar on the left forearm from what appeared to be an old burn. Some identification had been found but it was incomplete and of uncertain provenance, which was a bureaucratic way of saying that whatever Mats was carrying had been insufficient to establish who he was or had been. The officer had said, we understand this may be distressing, and Einar had said, yes, and had then sat very still in his kitchen for a long time with the phone still in his hand.

The scar on the left forearm. Mats had gotten it when they were children. Their father had been a man with a temper that arrived without announcement and required careful management, and one afternoon in the kitchen—Einar was twelve, Mats would have been eight—something had escalated in the usual way, a dropped plate, a raised voice, the geography of the kitchen rearranging itself into something dangerous, and Mats had caught the edge of a pot of boiling water knocked from the stove, the water catching his left arm from elbow to wrist. The scar that resulted was not dramatic, not the kind that strangers noticed, but it had always been there, a slightly shiny irregularity running the inside of his forearm, and their father had felt terrible about it for approximately two weeks before returning to his ordinary self.

Their father was also dead. He had died in the way of men who have spent their lives in a state of low hostility toward their own health, abruptly and without apparent remorse. Einar had gone to that funeral too. Mats had not.

He found a seat near the rear of the lounge, away from the windows, and drank his coffee. It was bad coffee, the kind that tasted primarily of its own container, but it was hot and he held the cup in both hands and let the warmth move into his palms. Outside the windows the sea was invisible—the same black as the sky, no horizon, just darkness and the occasional white fragment of a wave caught in the ship's lights. He had crossed this strait once before, years ago, with a woman he'd been seeing at the time, on their way to a summer rental that had turned out to be much smaller and much damper than the photographs had suggested. He had a clear memory of standing on the deck in July sunshine with her, her hair moving in the wind, both of them slightly sunburned, slightly drunk on beer from the onboard bar, and of feeling—not happiness exactly, but proximity to it, the sense of being in the right place in a life that was generally going well. That woman was now married to someone in Stavanger and had two children he knew about from social media with the guilty unreality of knowing things about people you've lost. The summer rental had leaked. They'd found it funny at the time.

He thought he might sleep. He tilted his head back against the hard upholstered seat and closed his eyes and the darkness behind them was productive of nothing—not sleep, not rest, just the engine hum transmitted through the hull into the seat into his back, a vibration that felt like the ferry's equivalent of breathing. He thought about the photograph in his bag. It was a photograph of the four of them, the last one, taken by someone he couldn't remember at a Christmas he remembered only partially. His father's eyes were doing the thing his father's eyes sometimes did in photographs, a slight unfocusing, as if he were looking not at the camera but at something behind or beyond it, some point of concern in the middle distance. His mother was smiling. Mats, who would have been maybe twenty in this photograph, was looking at something off to the side—someone had caught his attention, or he'd thought of something, or he was doing what he'd always done in photographs which was decline to fully inhabit them. Einar was looking directly at the camera with an expression he found, when he looked at it now, slightly painful, not because it was sad but because the person looking at the camera seemed to believe in things, seemed to be proceeding on the assumption that a general continuity applied—that the four people in the photograph would continue to exist in their relations to each other, that nothing was being concluded, that this was an occasion and not, as it turned out, a document.

He did sleep eventually, or something adjacent to sleep. He became aware of the lounge differently, of the quality of light through his eyelids, of the altered sounds of breathing and shifting around him, the lounge's nighttime register. A child cried somewhere and was consoled. The old man across the space continued his oceanic sleep. Einar drifted in and out of a gray awareness, not dreaming but thinking in images—Mats's forearm, the blistered white of that kitchen afternoon, his mother's hands sorting through papers in the weeks before she died, looking for something she could never specify, just looking, running her fingers along the edges of things.

He woke when the lights came up incrementally in the way of ferry lights preparing their passengers for landfall, a slow increase in voltage like an unwilling dawn. Through the windows the island was visible now—dark hills above a dark water, and then the harbor lights of the village, orange and white, and the ferry began its turn toward the dock with the ponderous precision of large vessels, the whole world rotating slowly around the fixed point of the bow.

The police station was a low yellow building near the harbor, separated from the water by a road and a thin strip of car park where a few vehicles were parked at angles suggesting a casual relationship with the marked spaces. Einar found it without difficulty—the village was small, the harbor was obvious, there was nowhere else the thing could be. It was just past seven in the morning and the light was coming in low and white from the east, hitting the water at an angle that made it look like hammered tin. He sat in his car for a few minutes in the car park, engine running. There were fishing boats at the dock. A gull sat on a bollard and attended to something in its beak with methodical precision. The cold had a mineral quality, the kind that lives inside metal and stone.

Inside, a young officer who was not the one he'd spoken to on the phone took his name and asked him to wait, gesturing toward a row of chairs that were the same model as the chairs on the ferry—the same plastic, the same slight wrongness of angle, as if the human body's preference for comfort had been studied and then deliberately set aside. He sat. He looked at a bulletin board with missing persons flyers and community notices and a laminated card with emergency numbers. After a few minutes a second officer came out, older, with a graying beard and the slightly compressed quality of a man who has spent years delivering news.

He led Einar down a corridor and through a door and there were certain procedural things, certain preliminaries, a clipboard, a form, a low-voiced explanation of what he would see and what he should do if he needed a moment. Einar said he understood. The officer—his name was, in fact, Halvorsen—looked at him with a professional sympathy that had some genuine quality underneath it, a residue of actual concern. Einar was grateful for this in an abstract way, the way you are grateful for weather that matches your mood without being grateful to anyone in particular.

The room was cold and white and there was a table with a covered shape on it that had the dimensions of a man.

Halvorsen looked at him once and Einar nodded.

He had been preparing for this moment for the entire drive north and the entire crossing and the night on the ferry and all of it had been, he now understood, inadequate preparation, not because the moment was worse than he'd imagined but because imagining it had been a performance he'd been giving for himself, a way of being seen to be prepared. The actual moment had a different texture—not dramatic, not the thing you'd see in a film or feel in a dream. Just a man on a table.

The face was not Mats's face.

He stood looking at it for what felt like a respectful interval. The man was about the right age, and had the right general proportions, lean and angular, the kind of frame that belonged to a man who'd worked outdoors in cold places. But the face—the specific, irreducible particularity of a face—was not his brother's. He could see what had caused the confusion, or could almost see it, the way you can understand a misreading once you've seen the word correctly. Some quality of bone, perhaps. Some posture of the features in death. He looked at the left forearm and there was, indeed, a scar, and for a moment—a bad moment, a moment that seemed to confirm something about the limits of certainty—he thought, this could be it, this could be the one, but the scar ran along the outside of the arm, not the inside, and it was wider, darker, differently shaped, a working scar from a blade or a line rather than the specific pale ribbon he'd been carrying in his memory for thirty years.

He said to Halvorsen, this isn't him.

Halvorsen wrote something. There was a form. There were more forms. There was, as there always is, a great deal of paper that the living must complete for the dead.

He drove to the only cafe in the village, which was attached to the post office in a combination that seemed to make a statement about the nature of small islands, and he sat at a table near the window and ordered coffee and a roll and sat with them without eating or drinking for a while, looking out at the harbor where the fishing boats were going about their business. The water was gray and choppy and the light had moved since morning, become less oblique, though it was still winter light, provisional and thin. On the pier a man was coiling rope with the easy automation of a man who has coiled rope many thousands of times, his hands doing it without instruction while he looked at something else entirely.

It was not Mats. He didn't know where Mats was. It was possible that Mats was fine, was on a boat somewhere, was in a kitchen somewhere, was on a stretch of coastal road eating a gas station sandwich with the radio on. It was also possible—it had always been possible—that Mats was not fine, that some years ago or recently or at this moment something had happened or was happening to Mats that could not be remedied. The not-knowing was not new. The not-knowing had been the condition of having Mats as a brother for as long as Einar could remember, the permanent weather of it, and he had learned to live inside it the way you learn to live in a climate that doesn't suit you, with adjustments, with layers, with a practiced management of expectation that could look, from the outside, like indifference and was not.

He took the photograph out of his bag and set it on the table.

Four people at Christmas. His father's eyes unfocused. His mother smiling. Mats attending to something off to the left. Himself, looking at the camera with his believing expression, proceeding on the assumption of continuity.

He thought about calling Mats. He had been thinking about calling Mats since March. He had thought about it in the car and on the ferry and in the cold white room and at this table. He thought about the silence on the line that preceded okay, that particular Mats silence that was not emptiness but something more like pressure, like a room before a storm. He thought about the flowers that had arrived two days late in their cellophane. He thought about a kitchen in winter, water moving across a floor, a boy with his left arm pressed against his side, and himself twelve years old and absolutely unable to do anything, standing very still in the way that had been his primary adaptation to their father, making himself still and small and invisible, which had always been the wrong adaptation, which had always been the adaptation that left Mats alone.

He picked up his phone. He put it down. He drank some of the coffee, which was better than the coffee on the ferry, though not by much. Outside, the man with the rope had finished coiling it and was now doing something with a net, the same automatic competence, hands ahead of intention.

He picked up the phone again. He called the number he had for Mats, which was the same number he'd called in March, the same number that answered approximately a third of the time.

It rang four times. Five. He was preparing himself, locating the particular muscle required for a voicemail he wasn't sure he could leave, when the line connected.

The silence lasted long enough that he thought again of disconnection. Then he heard breathing.

He said, Mats.

He said it not as a question, though it was a question, though it was several questions, all of them the same question, none of them about location or plans or the practical details of a life. Just the name. Just his brother's name in a cafe on a small island in a northern strait, the gray water outside the window moving in its gray way, the photograph on the table, the coffee going cold.

The breathing on the line continued.

Then his brother said, ja.

And Einar sat for a while with that.
