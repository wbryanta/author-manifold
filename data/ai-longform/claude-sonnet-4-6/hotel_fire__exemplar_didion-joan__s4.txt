The hotel had been rebuilt in the Spanish Colonial style, which was not the style of the original hotel, and this seemed to Nora, standing at the entrance with her wrap over her arm and the invitation still in her purse, to be the kind of detail that was meant to settle something. A white facade now where the brick had been. Red tile where there had been tar paper and, for a period she did not like to remember, nothing at all. The doorman was a young man she had never seen, which was of course inevitable, and he held the door with the particular solicitude reserved for women arriving at formal occasions without escorts. She went in.

The lobby smelled of tuberose and something underneath the tuberose, a cleaning agent probably, the kind of industrial citrus that she associated not with cleanliness but with the effort to suggest it. The ceiling had been raised. There were columns now, pale marble or marble-effect, she could not have said which, and this too seemed intentional, the way that certain changes are made not to improve a thing but to make it unrecognizable to people who might have known it before. She was not supposed to be able to walk in here and remember anything. Someone had understood that much.

"Mrs. Hale," a woman in hotel livery said. "The McCready reception is in the Camellia Ballroom, just through here."

"I know where the ballroom is," Nora said.

The woman looked at her.

"It's through there," Nora said. "I know."

She did not know where the ballroom was, as it happened. The whole east wing was new, poured concrete under the stucco, and the ballroom had not existed in any form in any version of this building she had any reason to remember. She found this reassuring in a way she could not have explained to anyone at the reception, and so she did not try. She followed the woman in livery and looked at the flower arrangements in the corridor and thought about nothing.

---

The Fabers were there, Helen and Gene, standing near the bar with their oldest daughter, who had grown, or had been growing for some years without Nora's attention, into a young woman with her mother's collarbone and her father's unease. Helen kissed Nora on the cheek and held on to her upper arm for a moment and asked about the drive down.

"It was fine," Nora said.

"You came alone."

"I came alone."

Helen looked at her with the expression Nora recognized as concern overlaid with the social decision not to express concern, which is a different thing from not feeling it. Gene touched her shoulder and said something about finding her a drink.

"I can find my own drink," Nora said, and then, because this was a wedding, because Deborah McCready was twenty-six and deserved better than the conversation Nora was apparently going to have at her reception, she added: "That would be wonderful, actually. Whatever you're having."

The ballroom was already crowded. There were people she recognized from the years she had lived in the valley—Hartwell Spence in his summer suit, the Vásquez sisters from Reedley, a man she could not place who kept nodding at her from across the room with the confidence of people who have been told they are easy to remember. The McCreadys had placed gardenias at every table and strung lights behind the drapery, and the effect was of a room that was trying very hard to be beautiful, which Nora found more touching than beauty. Paul McCready came toward her from the direction of the head table with his hand already extended, a man who had turned sixty-five without quite accepting it.

"Nora," he said. "Nora, you came."

"Of course I came."

"You look wonderful. Doesn't she look wonderful." He was saying this to a woman beside him, his second wife, Claire, whom Nora had met once at a dinner in Fresno three years ago and whose last name Nora could not now recall, though she was fairly certain it was not McCready.

"You look wonderful," Claire said.

"You're very kind," Nora said.

Paul put his hand on her back and leaned in, the way men of his age and type did when they wanted to be heard without being overheard. "Deborah wanted you here. She was insistent. I want you to know that."

"I wanted to be here," Nora said.

"After everything." He said it quietly.

"It was a long time ago, Paul."

He straightened and looked at her for a moment with something she could not name, something that was not quite gratitude and not quite guilt and was probably the combination of both that people feel when they have been the employer of someone who suffered in their employment. "Twenty-two years," he said.

"Twenty-three," Nora said.

---

She had been nineteen when she came to work at the hotel. This was a fact she had told almost no one in the past fifteen years, in the way that certain facts about one's life become less available over time not because they are forgotten but because the person who lived them seems increasingly to belong to someone else. The girl who had taken the job—who had taken it from a woman named Roberta Vilas, a friend of her aunt's who worked housekeeping and had put in a word—was someone Nora could examine only with effort, the way you look directly at something bright.

She had been given a section of twelve rooms on the third floor. She had learned the protocol: strip, spray, wipe, replace, fold, align. The toilet paper folded into a point. The complimentary soap unwrapped and positioned precisely so. She had learned to move through a room without looking at the things people left in it, which was harder than it sounds. She had learned to knock twice and say housekeeping and wait, and she had learned what it meant when people did not answer and what it meant when they answered right away and the difference between the two. She had been good at the job. Roberta had said so, and she had believed it, in the way that you believe the first real thing anyone says about you that you have not heard before.

The fire had started on a Thursday in February. This was also a fact. She kept the facts in a particular order: Thursday. February. The third floor of the east wing, which had been the newer wing, built in 1961, the one that did not meet current code because current code had not existed in 1961 and the variance had been extended twice. The call box at the end of the corridor. The smell, which she had identified before the alarm, the way she understood later that you always identify the smell before the alarm, because the alarm is for people who need to be told a thing and the smell is the thing itself.

She had gone down the interior stairwell with two women from the second floor and a man who was on his way to a sales conference in Bakersfield and who kept saying that he had left his briefcase, that he needed to go back for his briefcase. She had not gone back. She had not let him go back. On this point she had been, over the years, occasionally proud of herself, and occasionally she woke at four in the morning wondering whether she had been right.

Four people had died. This was also a fact, also in the sequence. The number had been higher for the first two hours, six, and then the revision had come and she had felt something shameful and immediate, relief followed immediately by the recognition that four was not a number that warranted relief. She had never spoken about the relief to anyone.

---

The band was playing something she didn't recognize, jazz standards rearranged for sentiment. She found her table and sat and looked at the centerpiece—gardenias, as she'd noted, massed around a pillar candle—and thought about whether she wanted wine. She decided she wanted wine. A young man materialized and she asked for whatever white was open and he said of course with the expression of someone for whom of course was a new acquisition.

The woman to her right was named Sandra Kessler. She introduced herself with the confidence of someone who expected to be recognized and when she was not she identified herself as a Prentiss, the Selma Prentisses, and when this too produced nothing she said that her aunt had been at school with Grace McCready, Deborah's grandmother, which explained her presence and seemed to satisfy her.

"And you?" Sandra Kessler said.

"I used to work here," Nora said. She said it before she had decided to say it. She picked up her wine and looked at Sandra Kessler over the rim of the glass.

"At the hotel."

"A long time ago."

"How interesting," Sandra Kessler said, in the tone that means the opposite. "Paul McCready must think very highly of you."

"I don't know what Paul McCready thinks," Nora said, "but Deborah was nine years old when the hotel burned down and she used to bring me apples from the tree in the back. She brought them in a paper bag. It was an unusual thing to do."

Sandra Kessler looked at her and then looked toward the dance floor and then looked back. "The fire," she said.

"Yes."

"I remember reading about that." A pause. "You were there?"

"I was there."

"How terrible for you."

"For the four people who died," Nora said. "Yes."

---

Deborah McCready was now Deborah Ainsworth, or would be by the time the evening concluded its formal business. She was small and dark-haired and moved through the room with the self-possession of someone who has been happy enough for long enough that it no longer surprises her. Her husband—her husband as of two hours ago, Nora corrected herself—was a tall man named Jeff who laughed too quickly at things that were not quite funny and who had the look of someone who understood, on some level, the precise nature of his good fortune.

Deborah found her before the first course.

"You're here," Deborah said. "I wasn't sure you'd come."

"I said I was coming."

"You say things," Deborah said, which was fair.

Nora stood and they embraced, and Deborah was wearing something that smelled like gardenias or like the gardenia candles or possibly just like the room, and Nora thought about the paper bag and the apples and the particular quality of Deborah's attention at nine, the way she had asked questions and then waited, actually waited, for the answers.

"It's strange," Deborah said. "Being here. It must be strange for you."

"It's strange for everyone. The Spanish Colonial is a very particular choice."

Deborah laughed, sudden and real. "Mother picked it. She said it was elegant."

"It's elegant," Nora agreed.

"Nora." Deborah took her hand and held it for a moment, looking at her with the directness she had apparently had since childhood and never seen fit to correct. "I wanted you here because I wanted someone here who knew. Do you understand what I mean?"

"I think so."

"Daddy doesn't talk about it. He's never talked about it. Mother—" She glanced across the room toward Claire, who was now talking to the Vásquez sisters. "Claire doesn't know, really. Jeff knows the outline. But I wanted someone who knew."

The band started something slower. On the dance floor, elderly couples began to move in the deliberate way of people who have been dancing together for decades and have stopped needing to think about it.

"Tell me about the apples," Nora said.

Deborah looked at her for a moment. "The apples."

"You used to bring them. Every other week or so. I don't know if you remember."

"I remember," Deborah said. "My grandmother planted that tree. The Gravensteins, they were. I don't know why I brought them to you particularly." She paused. "Maybe I did know. You seemed—I don't know what the word is. Reliable. You seemed like someone things would not shake."

Nora thought about the four in the morning waking. The revised number. The man and the briefcase. "That was a misreading," she said.

"Maybe," Deborah said. "But maybe not." She squeezed Nora's hand and let it go. "Jeff's looking for me. He has the face he gets." She glanced toward her husband and the look she gave him, private and accustomed, was the look of someone already four years into a marriage, not two hours. Nora envied her this in a way that did not feel like envy. "Come find me later," Deborah said. "After the cake."

---

She had not been in this building, in any version of this building, since the morning of the fire. This was perhaps obvious. This was perhaps a thing she should have mentioned to someone, to her therapist in the years when she had a therapist, or to David Hale in the years when she was married to David Hale and he was the kind of man who asked. She had not mentioned it. She had moved the fact around in her mind the way you move furniture in a room you don't want to change—adjusting the angles, finding new positions, but always returning eventually to the same walls, the same floor, the same view from the window that you have told yourself you don't mind.

The third floor of the east wing did not exist in the same form. She knew this. The east wing was new construction, new concrete, new plumbing behind new walls. The corridor she had walked every morning for fourteen months was not a corridor that existed anywhere except in the way that places continue to exist after they have been demolished, which is to say in the minds of the people who walked them. She understood all of this. She ordered another glass of wine.

Hartwell Spence sat down across from her and said that Paul McCready had done a remarkable job with the rebuild, considering.

"Considering what?" Nora said.

"Well," Hartwell Spence said. "Considering everything." He was a man who had owned an almond operation in the valley for forty years and had opinions about water rights that he expressed to anyone who remained stationary long enough to hear them, and normally Nora would have found a reason to move. She stayed.

"I worked here," she said. "Before."

"I know," Hartwell said. "Paul told me. Years ago." He looked at her directly, which she had not expected from him. "You got people out."

"I got two women and a man with a briefcase out."

"That's people."

"Four others didn't get out."

"I know," he said.

They sat with this for a moment. The band was playing something she recognized now, Gershwin, "Someone to Watch Over Me" in a slow arrangement that the band seemed to find very moving. Through the tall windows at the far end of the ballroom she could see the hotel's courtyard, the new fountain, the walkway lit with embedded lights that had not been there before and gave the stone the appearance of floating.

"Do you think about them," Hartwell said. Not a question exactly.

"Not the way I used to," she said. "It used to be constant. Now it's more like—" She looked at the window. "You know how sometimes you'll be driving and you realize you've driven through a town and you know you came through it, you know the route, but you don't have any image of it. It's like that. I know it happened because I know it happened. Sometimes it's something specific. The smell, usually."

"The citrus," Hartwell said.

"I beg your pardon."

"The cleaning product they use. I noticed it when I came in." He looked at her without apology. "I wondered if that would be difficult."

She found that she had nothing to say to this. She looked at Hartwell Spence, whom she had understood for twenty years to be primarily a man with opinions about water rights, and she revised this understanding slightly. "It wasn't pleasant," she said finally.

"No," he said. "I imagine not."

---

The cake was four tiers and the ceremony of the cutting was performed with the slightly self-conscious pleasure of two people who have agreed to enjoy the rituals they are performing. Jeff's hand over Deborah's hand, the knife, the flash of phones. Someone made the joke about feeding each other, and Jeff and Deborah navigated it with the ease of people who had discussed ahead of time how they wanted to navigate it. Nora stood near the back of the gathering and watched.

Paul McCready was beside her. She had not heard him approach.

"Twenty-three years," he said.

"Yes."

"I should have—" He stopped.

"You paid the medical," Nora said. "And the counseling, when I was seeing someone. You didn't have to do that."

"I know I didn't have to do it."

"It helped," she said.

He was quiet for a moment. On the small dance floor near the cake table, Deborah and Jeff were beginning their first dance, and the room rearranged itself around them, that natural movement of people at weddings toward the couple, the slight lean, the held breath. "She asked me once," Paul said, "after she was older, what I remembered about that night. I told her I remembered calling the fire marshal's office and I remembered calling the families and I remembered sitting in the parking lot at six in the morning waiting for the investigators. She said, what do you remember about the people. And I didn't—" He cleared his throat. "I didn't have a good answer."

"No," Nora said.

"You had them out before the fire marshal could even confirm the floor of origin. That corridor."

She watched Deborah and Jeff turn slowly in the cleared space, Deborah with her head against his shoulder, Jeff with his eyes open, watching the room. "I knew that corridor very well," she said.

"I know you did."

"The call box was at the south end. I'd walked past it six days a week for over a year. It's a thing you notice and then stop noticing, except that you've noticed it. So when it mattered I knew." She paused. "That's not heroism. That's just a corridor."

"Maybe," Paul said. "Maybe that's what heroism usually is."

She thought about this and decided not to argue it. The band had modulated into something else, the first dance becoming a general invitation, and couples were moving onto the floor. Helen and Gene Faber, swaying in the way of old habits. The Vásquez sisters partnering each other without embarrassment. Sandra Kessler with a man in a blue suit who might have been a Prentiss or might have been no one she knew.

"Dance with me," Paul said.

She looked at him.

"My wife will understand," he said, and there was something in the way he said it, the plain acknowledgment of what he was asking and why, that she found she could not refuse. She took his hand and they moved onto the floor, and he danced the way she would have expected, correctly and without distinction, and she danced the way she had always danced, which was adequately and without particular feeling for it.

They turned past the tall windows. The courtyard outside was empty now, the floating stone, the new fountain. Somewhere above them and to the east was the floor that was not the floor, the corridor that was not the corridor, the call box at the south end that was not there. She knew all of this. She turned with Paul McCready in the rebuilt room, in the Spanish Colonial hotel that had been built on the address of another hotel the way that everything is eventually built on the address of something else, and outside in the dark the fountain made its sound, and the lights in the stone made their approximation of floating, and from here, if you did not know what you were looking at, it was simply a courtyard, a fountain, a night in the valley in late summer, which is to say it was almost beautiful and almost enough.
