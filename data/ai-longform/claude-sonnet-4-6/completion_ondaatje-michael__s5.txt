Anil crouched at the edge of the pit and began again with her brush, working in careful arcs across the surface of the exposed tibia, reading the bone the way she had been taught to read bone, which was the way you read anything that could not speak for itself. The morning light came low across the western highlands, throwing shallow shadows into every groove and cavity, and she used this, tilting her head to catch the angle, her eyes moving from the bone to the light and back. The two bodies had been placed in a deliberate position. She had noted this in her field journal but had not yet written what she believed it meant, because believing things too early was the beginning of error, was the place where the story you told became the story you wanted rather than the story that had occurred.

The others settled in around her. Guzmán laid his tools in the ordered row he always made, equidistant from each other, and Chávez began photographing from the northern end. The woman was some twenty feet away, sitting under a tree now, watching. Anil worked. The particular silence of a grave site was something she had grown used to in the years since Colombo, since the lab at Guy's Hospital, since the fellowship in Oklahoma where she had first learned that the dead carry a specific grammar, that the arrangement of remains constitutes a kind of sentence that, if you are patient enough and skilled enough, you can parse. She had not grown used to the silence in the way one grows used to noise, by no longer hearing it. She had grown used to it the way you grow used to a scar, by incorporating it, by letting it become part of the body's knowledge.

By midmorning they had fully exposed the upper skeleton of the first body and she could see what she had suspected from the initial survey. The wrists had been bound. The tissue was long gone but the positioning of the bones was unambiguous. She photographed it herself, from four angles, and then sat back on her heels and looked at Guzmán.

He said, We knew.

She said, I know we knew.

It was something they did, saying aloud the things already established, not out of redundancy but out of respect, the way you state a name at a grave even though everyone present knows whose grave it is. The naming mattered.

She spent another hour on the second body and found that both femora had been fractured before death. The pattern of the breaks, the absence of remodeling, the way the bone had attempted no healing whatsoever. She held this in her mind for a moment, held the fact of it. A man lying in a field in the western highlands of Guatemala with his legs broken in the hours before he died and beside him a man who had been bound and the woman sitting under the tree had lost both of them in a single abduction and now she watched the scientists reconstructing the last hours of their lives from what their bodies had preserved. This was the work. This was always the work. You tried to let the work be larger than what it cost you.

At noon the woman brought food wrapped in cloth, tortillas and black beans and sliced avocado, and set it at the edge of the excavation without speaking. Anil washed her hands in the water from her bottle and sat beside Guzmán and ate. The shade had moved and the woman had moved with it, sitting with her back against the same tree, her eyes still on the pit.

Chávez said, She's been here every day?

Every day, Anil said.

He was new to the team, from Mexico City, trained in physical anthropology but still learning the other kind. The kind that was not in any training. He watched the woman for a moment and then looked down at his food.

After lunch Anil took her field journal and walked to where the woman sat. She was perhaps fifty, perhaps forty-five—grief aged people, grief and labor, the sun—and she wore her hair braided with a red cord and her hands in her lap were the hands of someone who worked with them constantly, the knuckles large, the skin thickened at the palm. Anil sat down a few feet away and opened her journal to a blank page and said in her careful Spanish, I have some questions I would like to ask, if you are willing.

The woman looked at her. Her eyes were very dark and entirely composed. She had moved past the place where composure was an effort.

They spoke for forty minutes. Anil wrote down what the woman told her about the day of the abduction, the hour, the direction from which the vehicles had come, the number of men, whether they had been in uniform, what kind of uniform, whether there had been any exchange of words before the men were taken. The woman answered everything directly and without apparent emotion. She had told this story before. She knew it was necessary to go on telling it, that each telling was a form of the vigil she kept at the pit, a way of ensuring that the evidence would not be lost again.

When they finished Anil closed her journal and they sat for a moment in the diminished silence.

The woman said, in Spanish, You are not from here.

Anil said, No. I am from Sri Lanka.

The woman looked at her without expression and Anil understood that Sri Lanka was not a place that carried meaning for her, that it was simply a name, a syllable, the way Guatemala was simply a syllable to most people in the world.

Anil said, It is an island. In the Indian Ocean.

The woman nodded slowly.

Anil said, We have the same work there. The same kind of work.

The woman looked at her again. And you came here instead, she said.

Anil thought about how to answer this and then said, I came here first. I will go back.

She had said this to herself in various forms for three years. She had said it in the lab in London and in the apartment in Oklahoma and in letters to her colleague Leaf who was still in Colombo and who wrote to her about the continuing situation in the way you write about weather, obliquely, without stating the worst of it outright. She would go back. She had not yet identified the exact nature of what she was preparing for by not going back, whether it was skill or courage or simply a sufficient accumulation of distance that would make it possible to see clearly what she had spent her childhood inside of and could not therefore see at all.

That afternoon they found a third body beneath the other two.

This was not unusual. In Guatemala you often found the excavation had more in it than the initial survey indicated. But this one had not been placed with any deliberateness. The body had been dropped, or thrown, and had come to rest in an attitude of absolute abandonment, the arms flung out, the head turned hard to one side, and from the bone density and the structure of the pelvis Anil judged it to be a young woman, perhaps seventeen or eighteen years old. She worked on this body until the light failed and the insects became too loud and Guzmán said they needed to stop.

She could not stop.

Guzmán crouched beside her and said her name. Anil.

She brushed a centimeter of soil from the clavicle.

Anil.

She sat back.

He said, Tomorrow.

She said, I know.

They covered the site with the plastic sheeting and weighted it with stones and walked back to the truck. Behind her she heard the woman settling in to wait through the night, the small adjustments of a body preparing for long sitting.

In the hotel that night—hotel was generous, it was a building with beds and a generator and cold water—she lay on top of the sheets and looked at the ceiling and tried to do what she always tried to do in the hours after the work, which was to stop. She was not successful at stopping. Her mind went on working in the dark the way a bone goes on existing after the body that held it has dissolved. She thought about the third body. She thought about the angle of the arms. She thought about the woman in the tree's shade and the clean factual way she had described the vehicles, the number of men, the direction, and she thought about the shoulder she had watched in the Guatemalan light, the grief of love in the shoulder, which was a different grief from all other grief, which was the grief of the particular, the grief that could only exist if this specific person had existed and had been loved by this specific person. It was the thing forensic science could never restore: not the fact of the death but the specific weight of the loss. You could establish identity. You could establish cause. You could establish time and manner. What you could not establish was what it meant to this woman that these two particular men were gone from the world and would not come in from the fields and eat the food she had made and sleep for an hour on the mat in the afternoon. That was not in the bone. That was in the living, in the ones left behind.

She had been thirteen when she first understood that her country was in a certain kind of trouble. She had grown up in Colombo in a household of doctors and lawyers, a household that discussed things at meals, that kept newspapers and maintained opinions about what was in the newspapers, that believed in the power of law to adjudicate the competing claims of a complicated world. And then slowly, across the years of her adolescence, the world her household believed in had been revealed as something more provisional than it had appeared. The men found on roadsides. The disappeared. The way people began to lower their voices in certain conversations even indoors even within families. The way her father would sometimes come home from the hospital and sit at the table for a long time before he was able to eat. She had not known then what it was she was seeing but she had felt it the way a child feels the structure of the adult world shifting beneath the surface, the plates moving.

She had left for London at twenty-three with a scholarship in forensic science and a set of letters from professors at the University of Colombo that described her as exceptional, which she was, which she had worked to be. She had sat in the library at Guy's Hospital with bones laid out before her and textbooks open and she had understood within the first weeks that this was precisely the thing she had been moving toward without knowing it, that this work of reading the dead, of restoring to the anonymous their names and their stories and the truth of what had been done to them, was the work her life had been organized around in advance of her knowing it. She had come to it with the speed of recognition.

And yet she had not gone back. Leaf wrote and she wrote back and the years accumulated and she told herself she was building something, accumulating the skills and the credentials and the international standing that would make her work more useful when she returned than it would have been if she had returned immediately. This was true. It was also not the whole truth. The other part of the truth was that to go back was to go back inside the thing she had left and she was not certain she could maintain from inside it the clear vision she had developed from outside.

She turned on her side and looked at the wall.

Tomorrow they would finish with the third body. Then there would be a week of laboratory work and then she would present the findings to the commission and then there was another site, in the highlands, in a different province, where the preliminary survey had identified remains that might number in the dozens. She would go to that site. She was very good at this work. The team moved with the efficiency of people who have worked together long enough that the work itself has become a form of communication, each person knowing what the others need before they ask for it.

She thought of the woman settling into her vigil. The cold of the Guatemalan night, which surprised people who expected only heat, the altitude making the nights sharp and clear and very cold. She hoped someone had brought the woman a blanket. She thought she should have thought of this before she left.

She slept without meaning to and dreamed, as she sometimes did, of the archaeology she had loved before she found forensics, the Bronze Age excavations she had read about as a child, the way objects emerged from the ground that had been placed there two thousand years before with love or ceremony or simple practical necessity, a pot, a clasp, a child's toy of fired clay. The violence of ancient history was distant enough to be purely interesting. She had loved the idea of objects persisting. She had believed, briefly and at a young age, that permanence was possible, that the things we made and loved would outlast the forces that sought to destroy them.

She woke before dawn and lay still and listened to the sounds of the building, the wind, a dog somewhere, and thought about what she knew.

What she knew: the two men had been bound, one of them beaten severely before death. The third body, the young woman, had been shot—she had found the entry wound in the posterior cranium. All three had been placed or dropped into the pit within a similar timeframe, the decomposition rates consistent with a shared burial date. The soil had been disturbed only once. There had been no subsequent interference with the remains.

What she did not know: who they were. This was still a question. The commission had preliminary identifications from the family's testimony but the laboratory work would confirm or challenge this. Until the work was done she held the identities as provisional, the way she held all interpretations as provisional, because the moment you became certain you had stopped reading and started writing, you had moved from evidence to story, and story in a context like this one was not neutral, story in a context like this one had weight, had consequences, could shatter the provisional reconstructions that allowed people to continue living.

She dressed in the dark and went outside and stood in the hotel's courtyard looking up at the stars that were very bright at this altitude and thought about Sri Lanka. She did it rarely and with discipline, the way you might allow yourself a controlled amount of a substance you knew could undo you. She thought about the coast and she thought about her family and she thought about Leaf, who was twelve years older than her and had been her mentor and her friend and who continued to do in Colombo what she was doing here, continued to do it in the country itself without the protection of distance or institutional affiliation, just a man with training and a belief in the necessity of the work. His letters were careful. He told her about specific cases in the oblique way, told her about the lab, about his students, and occasionally he would write something that was simply itself, unguarded, a sentence that arrived in her hands like something thrown across a great distance. The last letter had said: I found a skeleton last month that had been moved after burial. Someone disturbed the original site and reinterred the remains elsewhere, and for a moment I thought I understood why, and then I thought perhaps I understood nothing and had never understood anything, and then I had lunch and went back to work.

She understood this exactly.

The sky was beginning to lighten in the east, a very faint alteration in the quality of the dark. She could hear the truck coming up the road, Guzmán arriving with the equipment, and she thought about what the day would require of her and whether she was capable of it, and she thought yes. She was capable. She had been made capable by years of practice and by the particular education that only the work itself provides. She walked back through the courtyard to collect her bag and her field journal.

When she reached the site the woman was there, as she always was, crouched at the edge of the uncovered pit, her arms around her knees, looking down at the plastic sheeting that covered what was left of the men she had lost. She had slept, if she had slept, in this same position or close to it. The cold had whitened her breath in the early air and she was entirely still, entirely focused, as if she were reading something in the covered shapes beneath the plastic.

Anil stopped a few feet away.

The woman did not turn. After a moment she said, in Spanish, in a very quiet voice, I want to know if they were together at the end.

Anil stood in the early morning with her bag over her shoulder and her field journal in her hand and she thought about what she had seen, what the arrangement of the three bodies told her, what could be honestly said and what could not. She thought about the difference between what was established and what was believed, the line between them, how much depended on maintaining that line, how much was lost when you crossed it.

She sat down beside the woman.

She said, I cannot tell you everything yet. We still have work to do and the work will tell us more than I know now.

The woman nodded, her eyes still on the plastic sheeting.

Anil said, What I can tell you is that the position they were in, the two men, the way they were placed. Someone arranged them with care. Someone who buried them wanted them to be together.

She said this because it was true and because it was something she could give, because in this work there were things you found that could be given and things that had to be withheld for reasons of evidence or protocol or simple mercy, and this one could be given. The woman received it without visible change in her expression but she was still for a longer moment than she had been still before, and then she unfolded herself and rose to her feet and began to remove the plastic sheeting from the pit.

Guzmán arrived with the equipment. Chávez was behind him. The sun was coming up across the highland and the light was moving over the surface of the ground and Anil stood and opened her field journal to a new page, wrote the date, the time, the temperature, the weather, and crouched at the edge of the pit to begin.
