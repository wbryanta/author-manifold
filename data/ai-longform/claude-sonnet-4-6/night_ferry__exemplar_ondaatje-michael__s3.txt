He had taken the night ferry before, years ago, with their mother, when she was still alive and capable of wonder at large things — the black water moving under the hull, the lights of the opposite shore appearing and then disappearing in fog as though a city were deciding whether to exist. That had been in summer. Now it was March and the strait was the colour of old pewter and the ferry groaned in its slip as Marcus carried his single bag up the metal gangway. He had packed without thinking. A shirt. His shaving kit. The letter from the constabulary in Sorrel Harbour that used phrases like *in the event of positive identification* and *next of kin* and which he had read four times on the train and folded into the breast pocket of his coat where it sat now like something that had punctured him.

The crossing would take nine hours. He had not planned for nine hours.

Below deck the passenger lounge smelled of diesel and the particular human warmth of people who had resigned themselves to transit. A family occupied a row of orange seats near the porthole, a father with two children who had already fallen asleep against him like small collapsed things. An older woman in a grey coat read from a paperback she held close to her face. Marcus found a seat away from the others and set his bag on the floor and sat with his coat still buttoned. He was forty-one years old. He had not spoken to Daniel in seven years, which was not quite the same as seven years of silence — Daniel's presence had continued to arrive and depart in his life like weather. A postcard from a place called Tofino showing grey-green ocean and a single tree bent permanently sideways by wind. A phone call at three in the morning two Christmases ago in which Daniel had said his name, just Marcus, twice, and then breathed for a moment and hung up. The card from Tofino Marcus had kept in the drawer of his bedside table and sometimes would take it out and hold it, not reading it, just feeling the slight flex of the cardboard.

He understood that he could not yet ask himself whether he hoped it was Daniel or hoped it was not.

The ferry shuddered and moved.

Through the porthole he watched the harbour recede, the lights along the wharf becoming first distinct and then abstract, a smear of amber on the black water. He thought about the word *identify*. What it assumed. That you would know. That seven years and whatever had happened to Daniel in those years would not have changed him past the point of recognition, would not have worn him down into something generic, the face of any man who had lived close to water and wind and whatever else Daniel had been living close to. Their mother had always said Daniel had their father's face. Marcus had never seen it himself, their father having died when both of them were very young — a farm accident, a thing that happened fast and without witness. But he remembered his mother touching Daniel's jaw sometimes with her fingers in a way that seemed to be testing for something, a memory preserved in bone.

He went to the bar and bought a whisky and brought it back to his seat. He did not drink it immediately. He let it sit on the armrest in its plastic cup.

There was a woman sitting two rows ahead of him now who had not been there before. She had dark hair pinned up and she was writing in a notebook with concentrated attention, pausing only to look out at the darkness as though the darkness were a text she was cross-referencing. He watched her for a while. Not with desire exactly, or not only that. More the way you watch anyone who seems to be occupied by something real. She wore a green wool sweater, the sleeves pushed to the elbows, and when she turned her head he could see that she was young, perhaps thirty, and that her expression when she looked out at the water had in it something private and not entirely comfortable, as though the ferry crossing were solving a problem she had not chosen to think about.

He drank the whisky. It was poor whisky, thin and hot, but it accomplished something.

When their mother died Marcus had been the one to call Daniel, who was living in Halifax at the time, or had been last known to be living there. The number rang and rang. He called the woman Daniel had been with and she said she didn't know, she hadn't known for three months, there might be someone in Cape Breton, that was all she could say. Marcus drove to Halifax himself and put a notice in the paper and walked up and down Barrington Street for two days as though Daniel might simply appear. He organized the funeral alone. He chose the hymns, which had felt absurd and also necessary, as though the choosing of the right words for the dead could compensate for the wrong words said to the living. Daniel had arrived the morning of the funeral in the same coat he always wore, the black wool one with two missing buttons, and they had stood together at the graveside and Marcus had waited through the service for Daniel to say something and afterward at the house Daniel had drunk too much and said things that could not be unsaid, things that were not wrong exactly but that were precise and accurate in the way only certain cruelties manage to be. He had left before dark. Marcus had washed the cups and glasses until he could not stand to wash anything more and then sat in his mother's kitchen alone and listened to the house decide what it was without her in it.

Outside the porthole there was nothing now. The far shore still too far, the near shore gone. Just the black strait and the occasional whitecap catching light.

He must have slept because when he opened his eyes the woman with the notebook was sitting beside him, not close, but in the adjacent seat, and she was eating something wrapped in foil and looking at him with an expression of mild interest.

— Sorry, he said, though he didn't know for what.

— You were talking, she said.

— What did I say.

She considered this. — Something about a coat.

He sat up and rubbed his face. The lounge was quieter now, the family gone, the older woman asleep. The bar was shut. Through the porthole he could see that the fog had come in and the darkness was no longer absolute but had taken on a soft grey quality, diffuse, the fog reflecting back the ship's own lights.

— Are you all right, she said, and the question was simple, without the performance of sympathy.

— I'm going to identify a body.

He had not said this to anyone. He had called his supervisor and said there was a family matter. He had told his neighbour he would be gone three or four days. He had said nothing about the letter, about the phrase *distinctive scar tissue along the left forearm*, about what he had thought when he read that phrase, which was: yes, the wood stove, he was eleven, I was fourteen, I pulled him back.

The woman finished whatever she was eating and folded the foil carefully. — Who, she asked.

— My brother. Maybe. They're not certain it's him.

She nodded. She had the manner of someone who understood that some information needed to be received without reaction, simply taken in. He found he was grateful for this. He looked at her notebook on her knee, the pen laid across it.

— Are you a writer, he said.

— I'm trying to remember something. I find if I write around it I can sometimes get at it from the side.

— What are you trying to remember.

— The way my grandmother's hands looked. She died six months ago and I can remember everything about her except her hands, which seems wrong.

He understood this, the specific grief of the missing detail. When he tried to remember Daniel's voice he could recall its register, its tendency to flatten at the end of sentences in a way that had sometimes sounded like boredom but was more likely its opposite, a kind of withheld intensity. But the voice itself, the actual sound, had faded. He could not make it play anymore. This was what he had been most afraid of and it had happened anyway, the way things do.

— My brother has a scar on his arm. Left forearm. He burned it on a wood stove when he was a boy.

— And the body has that.

— Yes.

She turned and looked out the porthole at the fog. He could see her face in partial reflection in the glass, superimposed over the grey nothing outside. — Then you probably already know, she said.

He had known since the second reading of the letter. He had conducted the subsequent readings as a kind of argument against himself, finding points of uncertainty, the age estimate off by two years, the description of the hair inconsistent, but the scar — *approximately eight centimetres, irregular, on the volar surface of the left forearm* — that was specific in a way that resisted argument. And yet. He was on a ferry in the middle of a northern strait in March and he had not told anyone and he was going to see for himself. Because the seeing was different from the knowing. Because until he saw he could still imagine Daniel somewhere in a city, in a rented room, sitting at a table in whatever coat he wore now, having replaced the black wool one with two missing buttons, sitting there alive and difficult and making the wrong choices in the way Daniel always made the wrong choices, which was wholeheartedly, with a kind of furious sincerity that Marcus had never been able to sustain.

When they were boys, ten and twelve or eleven and thirteen, they had found a dead seal on the shore below their grandmother's house on the mainland. It was summer, the light lasting past nine in the evening. The seal was on its side in the wrack at the high-tide line, its eyes already clouded. Daniel had crouched beside it for a long time. He had taken off his sneaker and used it to gently turn the seal's flipper, examining the underside. Marcus had waited up the beach. He didn't know what he had been afraid of — not the seal, something about Daniel's proximity to it, his willingness to be that close. Later that night Daniel had described it to their grandmother at dinner, the size of it, the texture of its coat, the way its whiskers were still perfectly intact and stiff. Their grandmother had listened and said nothing, only passed him more bread, and Marcus had watched this exchange with something that might have been envy or might have been something with a longer name.

— I'm sorry, the woman said.

— He wasn't easy.

— No.

— We hadn't spoken in seven years. I don't know if that changes how much — He stopped. The fog pressed at the porthole.

She said: — My father and I didn't speak for three years once. When he had a heart attack I drove eight hours to the hospital and sat in the waiting room and didn't go in.

— Why not.

— I don't know. I think I was waiting to find out if it mattered enough to go in. She paused. — It did. Eventually.

He thought about the nine hours of this crossing, how the constabulary had offered to send someone to meet him at the ferry terminal, a grief counsellor or a victim support person, and he had said no, he would find the address himself, he had a map. He wanted the time between the ferry and what came after. He wanted to walk to it.

— What's his name, the woman said.

— Daniel. He went by Dan sometimes but he didn't like it.

He saw the woman press her pen once against the page of the notebook. Not writing, just touching it. He looked at his coat folded on the seat beside him and thought of the letter inside it, *next of kin*, which had always seemed to him one of the stranger phrases in the language, suggesting proximity measured not in space or affection but in blood, in accident, in the fact of having grown in the same house and shared the one bathroom and known each other before either of them was entirely formed and capable of choosing what they would become to each other.

He had a photograph of Daniel at twenty-two in his wallet, the same wallet he'd had for fifteen years, and the photograph had gone soft at the corners from handling. In it Daniel was standing in front of a truck in a laneway somewhere, squinting into the sun, grinning at something off to the left of the frame. He had one hand raised in a gesture that might have been hello or might have been an instruction to wait. Marcus had carried this photograph so long he had stopped seeing it, the way you stop seeing a thing that is always present. But earlier on the train, in the moment after he had read the letter the first time and folded it into his pocket, he had taken out his wallet and looked at the photograph and tried to age it forward, to extrapolate from that twenty-two-year-old face squinting into the sun the face that would now be forty years old or that might no longer be any age at all.

The woman's name was Saoirse. She told him this when he asked, explaining the spelling. She was going to Sorrel Harbour to see a friend who had just had a child, the first of their group, she said, as though births too required a form of witness. She had been born in Galway and had lived in Canada since she was twelve and still felt, she said, the way a word feels when you say it in the wrong language — right meaning, wrong shape in the mouth.

They talked through the rest of the crossing, or not talked exactly — it was something more intermittent than that, with long silences that were not uncomfortable, the way silence can be between people who have arrived at something real without the usual preparations. She told him about her grandmother, the visits to the farmhouse outside Galway, the smell of the house in rain, and he listened and the listening was easy. He told her about the summer their mother had taken them across this same strait, the lights on the far shore. He did not say: I don't know how to do what I am about to do. But he thought it, and perhaps she knew it, because at some point she said — You'll just be present. That's enough. You won't have to do anything other than that.

By the time the announcement came over the intercom — the shore lights of Sorrel Harbour resolving out of the fog, the ferry beginning its slow adjustment toward the slip — the lounge had filled again with people who had been sleeping in the berths below and now moved through the space with the particular purposeful shuffle of imminent arrival, gathering bags and waking children. The family with the sleeping children reappeared. The older woman folded her paperback into her coat pocket.

Marcus put on his coat and picked up his bag. The ferry shuddered as it adjusted its course, a long low vibration he felt through the soles of his shoes.

He looked at Saoirse. She was putting on her own coat, a grey-green colour, and she had let her hair down from its pin and it fell across her shoulder and she was looking at something past him, at the lights coming in from the dark water, her expression open now in a way that was different from the expression he had first seen on her face — that private, solving look. The lights of the harbour moved across the porthole glass, real and sequential, a place declaring its own existence one lamp at a time.

He thought of Daniel on the beach beside the seal, crouching there in the long summer light with his sneaker in his hand. He thought of Daniel's voice flattening at the ends of sentences. He thought of the black coat with the two missing buttons. He thought: whatever I see in that room I will have been his brother. This did not seem like much and it seemed like the only thing.

The ferry groaned and was still.

Saoirse turned to him and put her hand briefly on his arm — just a moment, the warmth of it through the coat — and then she lifted her bag and walked with the others toward the gangway and he stood a moment longer in the emptying lounge as the engines went quiet and the harbour came fully into view, its lights cold and clear and absolute in the grey morning air.
