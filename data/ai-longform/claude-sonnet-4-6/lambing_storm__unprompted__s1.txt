The snow had started before dawn, which meant it had started without warning, the way all the worst things did. Maren heard it first as silence—the absence of the wind that had been blowing since Tuesday, replaced by something denser, a held breath, and then she was at the window watching the flakes come down in the dark, not drifting but falling with intention, straight down through the still air, each one landing exactly where it meant to.

She dressed without turning on the lamp. She knew the room well enough by grief, which had made her familiar with every obstacle in the dark—the corner of the cedar chest, the doorframe two inches closer than you'd think, the spot on the stairs that sang out underfoot. She had been navigating this house without Thomas for seven months now, which was not enough time to stop reaching for him and just barely enough time to know she was going to keep doing it regardless.

Outside, the thermometer on the porch post read eighteen degrees. Late April, and eighteen degrees. The lambing shed was lit from inside, a warm yellow seam under the door she could see from fifty yards, and she stood a moment in the new snow looking at it, the flakes landing on her face and not melting the way they would have in March, the way they did when the cold was merely seasonal. This cold was different. This was the cold that had a grudge.

Eli had beaten her out by an hour. She found him kneeling in the far corner of the shed with his arms around a ewe she recognized as Number 22, a Suffolk cross they'd had since Eli was nine, a ewe that had always lambed easily and now appeared to be having difficulty, her sides heaving in a rhythm that was too fast and too shallow. Eli looked up when Maren came in. His face was smeared with something she didn't examine too closely, and his eyes had that particular quality she'd come to think of as his winter face—flattened affect, watchfulness, the emotional circuitry routed somewhere internal where she couldn't follow.

"How long?" Maren said.

"She was already going when I got here. Maybe ninety minutes. I can feel a nose but the legs are back."

Maren shed her coat on a hay bale and knelt beside him. She'd delivered enough lambs that her hands knew what to do before she did, the muscle memory running ahead of thought, and she worked her fingers in alongside the ewe's straining to find the problem, which was exactly what Eli had said—a nose presenting, two front legs folded back instead of extended, a lamb trying to enter the world chin-first. She worked methodically, speaking to the ewe in a low continuous murmur that was less language than sound, something more fundamental, the kind of noise that meant *I am here, I mean no harm, this will hurt but I am here.*

The lamb came after another twenty minutes. It was alive. Maren cleared its face and set it near the ewe's head, and Number 22 turned to it with that complete and sudden absorption that always caught Maren somewhere unguarded—the way an animal could move instantly from agony into love, without any apparent need to recover first.

"Good," Eli said. He stood and went to check the water.

This was the other thing she had noticed about him since Thomas died: he had stopped lingering over the moments. He used to watch the newborns with an attention that was almost embarrassing in its openness, the way children watch things before they learn that watching too hard is a form of wanting, and wanting is a form of risk. Now he moved through the shed with efficiency. He checked things and noted them and moved to the next thing. She recognized the behavior. She was doing it herself.

They had forty-three ewes and twenty-one had lambed already, the lambs in various stages of newness ranging from a set of triplets born two days ago and already bouncing with an energy that seemed to mock the seriousness of the world, to a single ram lamb born this morning whose legs still had the uncertain quality of new furniture, not yet certain of its purpose. The remaining twenty-two ewes were in various stages of readiness, and on a normal late-April night Maren would have expected maybe three or four more before morning. But the storm changed the calculus. Cold and pressure and the particular stress of weather moved through a flock like a current, and ewes that had been comfortably days away from lambing sometimes felt the barometric drop and decided that now, in fact, was the time, as though the body read the weather as urgency, the way humans do.

The lights went out at two-fifteen.

There was a moment of perfect dark, absolute, the kind that erases distance and makes the walls uncertain, and then Eli's headlamp came on. He'd put it on his head before coming out, she realized. He'd known, or guessed, or simply prepared for the worst the way he'd been preparing for the worst since October, which is what happened when the worst had already arrived once and you'd been unprepared for it.

"Generator," he said.

"I'll go."

"I can—"

"Stay with them. I'll go."

She found her coat by feel and went back out into the snow, which had been accumulating while they worked and was now mid-shin, which was not alarming but was also not nothing. The generator lived in a lean-to on the east side of the barn, a relic Thomas had bought from an estate sale in Roundup six years ago, a machine he had maintained with the focused tenderness some men give machines because machines are easier to understand than people and never leave you for reasons you can't explain. She had watched him service it twice a year, once in fall and once in spring, draining and replacing the oil, checking the fuel line, treating the carburetor with something that came in a small red can. She had watched but she had not asked questions because Thomas was there and that seemed like enough of a reason not to ask.

He wasn't here.

She found the generator in the dark by memory, found the pull cord, pulled it four times before it caught, and stood for a moment in the lean-to listening to it run with a sound like something being worked out of a system, a cough becoming a rhythm becoming something almost steady. The lights in the shed would be back on. She had done it. She stood in the dark with her face hot and the snow coming sideways now in the wind that had returned, and she thought: *Thomas, I started the generator. I know you think I should have already known how but I'm telling you anyway.* She did this sometimes, these small reports. It was not prayer. It was not even comfort exactly. It was closer to the habit of living with someone who paid attention, and the difficulty of breaking that habit just because the someone was gone.

Back in the shed, lights on, Eli was already attending to a second ewe in early labor—Number 7, a big Rambouillet that Thomas had named Gertrude because he said she had the face of someone who had opinions about your decisions, and she did, she watched you with an expression of measured skepticism that Thomas had found hilarious and that Maren had always secretly found comforting. Gertrude was circling, pawing, lying down and getting up with the restless inability to settle that meant she was three or four hours out but committed.

"She's been like this twenty minutes," Eli said.

"She'll take her time. She always takes her time."

"Dad used to say she was doing it on purpose."

"He said she was doing everything on purpose."

Eli almost smiled. It crossed his face the way weather crosses a field—you could see it coming from a distance and then it was there and then it was gone and the field looked the same but wasn't.

They worked. The snow hit the roof with a sound like something impatient, and the wind now was active, pushing at the walls, finding the places where the boards had gaps and sending thin cold fingers through. Maren had insulated the shed in February, her and Eli over three weekends while the ground was still frozen, rolling in fiberglass batts and stapling plastic sheeting while Eli complained about the itch and she thought about Thomas, who had always said he was going to re-insulate this shed and never had, which was not a criticism, it was just the truth of how things worked, how intention lived comfortably beside inaction until inaction became permanent. She had done it in February. She had gotten it done.

At three the triplet mother needed her udder stripped; one of the three lambs wasn't nursing effectively and Maren crouched with it in her lap, getting colostrum into it from a bottle, feeling its weak pull strengthen over twenty minutes into something more convinced, more present. Eli came and looked at the lamb and then at her.

"It's going to be okay," she said.

"I know," he said, without conviction.

"You don't know that."

He looked at her. He was fifteen. He had his father's mouth, the same slight asymmetry that had made Thomas look perpetually amused by something private, and he had her eyes, dark and direct, uncomfortable with abstraction. He was large for fifteen—Thomas had been large, a physical man, a man who fixed things and moved things and was present in a room in a way that was partly just his size, and Eli had inherited this without quite having grown into it, so that he was sometimes startling, a largeness that hadn't yet become itself.

"I don't know anything," he said. It wasn't self-pity. It was forensic.

"Nobody does," Maren said.

"You do. You know how to do all this."

"I learned it."

"You learned it from Dad."

"I learned some of it from Dad. I learned some of it from my father and some of it from books and some of it from doing it wrong."

He was quiet for a moment. The lamb in her lap had gone to sleep, which was fine, which was what she wanted—a sleeping lamb was a lamb whose body was working on something, conserving, building. "Dad knew everything about this place," Eli said. Not accusatory. Factual.

"He knew a lot."

"What does he know that you don't? That I don't? What's going to happen that we don't know how to do?" He paused. "I think about it."

"What specifically."

"The fence along the north section. He always said there was a way the posts were sinking and he knew how to fix it but he never told me."

"We'll figure out the fence."

"The combine."

"We'll figure out the combine."

"The—" He stopped. "It's not really about the fence."

"I know."

He sat down on a hay bale. The lamb between them breathed with the shallow rapid rhythm of the newborn, the breath rate that always seemed too fast to be sustainable and somehow always was. "It snowed on the day of the funeral," Eli said.

"It did."

"Not like this. Just a little."

"Just flurries."

"I remember standing outside with Uncle Rob and he kept trying to put his arm around me and I kept moving away. I don't know why. I just didn't want him to."

Maren watched him. She had learned to do this without offering the thing you offered when you couldn't stand the silence—the reassurance, the interpretation, the conversion of his experience into something more manageable. Thomas had been good at that silence too, the real kind, the kind that didn't press.

"I felt bad about it later," Eli said.

"Rob didn't mind."

"How do you know?"

"Because he's been doing that his whole life. Trying to help and having people move away. He's used to it."

Eli made a sound that wasn't quite a laugh. "That's kind of mean."

"It's kind about the right things."

He considered this. Then he stood, because Gertrude had lowered herself to the straw in a way that meant business, and Eli moved to her with that competence that was new, the competence that had arrived in October along with everything else that arrived in October—the responsibility, the early mornings, the willingness to do hard things without being asked. She watched him kneel beside the big ewe, put his hand on her side, speak to her in a low voice, her husband's son being her husband in the ways that mattered and being entirely himself in all the others.

By four-thirty they had three more lambs. One was born without assistance—a small Rambouillet ewe who had crept to the corner and handled the whole thing herself with an air of preferring privacy, the lamb already on its feet and nursing by the time Maren checked on her, which made Maren feel something close to awe, the small fierce sufficiency of it. The second was Gertrude's, a single large ram lamb with a black face and a kind of outraged dignity in his first moments, as if the delivery had been an indignity he planned to remember. The third was complicated.

Number 31 was a first-time mother, a two-year-old ewe they'd bought at auction in September, and she had been quiet in her corner all evening in a way that Maren had been watching but not acting on—the watchfulness of experience, knowing when to approach and when to leave alone. At quarter to five she was in active labor but the lamb was positioned badly, not just legs back but turned sideways, and Maren called Eli over and they worked together, her hands and his, she directing and he following and then after a moment she following him because his hands were smaller and could find the angles hers couldn't.

This was new. This was something that had not been true before this winter, before he'd grown, before some increment of ability had arrived in his hands without announcement. She felt it clearly: the moment when you are no longer teaching and you are both just doing the thing that needs doing, the hierarchy dissolved by necessity into something more honest.

The lamb came. It was a ewe lamb, small and white, and she was not breathing.

Eli took her immediately, by instinct or by memory—Thomas had shown him, years ago, Maren now recalled, had shown him with some other struggling lamb, hold her by the hind legs and swing her gently in an arc, the centrifugal force clearing the fluid from the lungs, it sounds barbaric and works the way a lot of things work, by sounding wrong but being right. Eli swung the lamb and set her down and rubbed her vigorously with a towel, his whole body in the motion, his large hands more capable than his face, which was doing something complicated and private.

The lamb shook her head. Sneezed. Breathed.

"There," Eli said. Just the word.

Number 31 turned to her lamb with that same complete absorption Maren had seen earlier, the instant transition. The ewe was young and this was her first and for a moment she wasn't sure what to do with the lamb, circling her uncertainly, sniffing, and Maren thought she might need help, might need Maren to rub the lamb with hay and bring the scent to the mother's nose to convince her—but then something in Number 31 resolved, some ancient recognition arrived, and she began to clean her lamb with the single-mindedness of new love.

Eli sat back on the straw. His hands were in his lap and he was looking at the ewe and her lamb and he was not moving away from it this time, not converting it into a task completed and moving on. He sat with it. The wind pushed against the shed and the generator hummed outside and the lambs rustled in the straw and the snow kept coming and he sat with it.

Maren sat beside him. Not close enough to require anything of him, just near. This was something she'd learned in seven months—the specific proximity of grief, how it had its own geometry, how sometimes near meant across the room and sometimes it meant shoulder to shoulder in a cold lambing shed at five in the morning with the snow coming down outside and nothing resolved and everything continuing.

"Dad would have liked that," Eli said. "The way she came back."

"He would have said something ridiculous about it."

"He would have named her Lazarus."

"He would have named her Lazarus and written it on the ear tag and explained to everyone why it was funny."

"And then been surprised that they didn't think it was funny."

"He was always surprised by that."

They sat with this a moment—not the grief of it, exactly, but the shape of him, how he fit into a conversation even now, how he kept arriving uninvited and welcome.

"Do you think it gets easier?" Eli asked.

"I think it gets different."

"Is different easier?"

She thought about this honestly, the way the question deserved. "Different is easier in some ways. The sharp part—" She paused. "The sharp part doesn't go away but it comes less often, I think. Like how a fracture heals. It's not the same bone. But it holds."

He nodded slowly. He was fifteen and he was processing this with the deliberateness of someone who understood that this was important information about the future, information he needed to store carefully.

"I don't want to forget him," Eli said.

"You won't."

"How do you know?"

"Because I haven't forgotten my father, and it's been twenty-two years."

"Do you still miss him?"

She thought about the generator. About standing in the lean-to in the dark with the pull cord in her hand, running through what she'd seen Thomas do, what she'd only half-watched, making it work anyway. "Yes," she said. "But it's mine now. The missing. It belongs to me instead of just happening to me. That's the different part."

He turned this over. Outside the wind was doing something structural, pressing against the east wall with a weight that shifted the shadows, and Maren made a note to check the lean-to roof in the morning, the morning which was coming now, gray and gradual, the quality of the dark changing from absolute to merely deep. The snow had not stopped. But the barometric feeling had changed; she could feel it the way she could feel the lambing pressure in the flock, something known through the body rather than the mind.

She got up and walked the shed, checking each of the pens, counting the lambs she'd counted twice already, running her eyes along the remaining ewes for signs—the wax on the udder, the sinking of the loin, the particular inward turn of an animal approaching its own threshold. Three or four more before morning, she thought. Maybe five. The storm had pushed them along, as she'd expected. She would be here all day. Eli had school on Monday—this was Sunday, or had been Sunday when it started, was now the quiet hours that belong to neither Sunday nor Monday but to the work itself.

When she came back Eli was dozing against the hay bale, his chin to his chest, one of the barn cats—a gray tom they called Majority because he was always in the majority of any space he occupied—settled in his lap with the pragmatic comfort of animals, who understand the exchange of warmth as a complete and sufficient conversation.

She let him sleep. She moved through the shed in the gray half-light, quiet, checking the water and the hay and the lambs, doing the small calibrations of care, the kind that don't add up to anything dramatic but add up to everything sustainable. Thomas would have been here. Thomas would have been here in his red barn coat with the torn pocket he always meant to fix, bringing coffee out in the thermos that said WORLD'S OKAYEST FISHERMAN because it had been a joke from Eli at Christmas three years ago and Thomas had used it unironically ever since. He would have been here. He was not here. She was here, which was a fact and also, she was beginning to understand, something more than a fact—a choice that became a fact through repetition, through the accumulated weight of all the mornings she had gotten up and come out and done the thing.

Number 18 lay down in the corner pen and Maren went to her and knelt in the straw, and the lamb came fast, as some of them did, with a sudden efficiency that felt like relief. It was alive and vocal, a strong lamb, and the ewe turned and began to clean it and Maren sat on her heels in the straw and watched the light come through the gap at the roof's peak, pale and cold, the specific light of a storm that has not finished but has paused to catch its breath.

She heard Eli behind her, the shift and rustle of him waking.

"Another one?" he said.

"Another one."

He came and stood beside her. They watched the ewe work over her lamb. Outside the snow kept falling, purposeful and indifferent. Inside the shed it was warm enough, the collective heat of animals, the animals keeping each other, which was the only way any of them were going to get through this.
