He came up out of sleep clawing at the sheets and she held him by the wrists and spoke his name until the shaking eased and he sat there in the dark listening to his own breath go in and out.

Damn, he said.

She had turned on her side and watched him with her hands beneath her cheek. The window showed a pale rectangle against which the lace curtain moved without wind. He could not account for any movement.

You were hollerin again, she said.

I know it.

You want to tell it?

He put his feet on the floor. The boards were cold. No, he said.

She watched him go to the window and stand there with his hands at his sides looking out at whatever there was to see in the dark of that hour which was not much. The town below lay quiet. A dog called from somewhere south along the river. He turned and she could see his shape against the glass and not much else.

Go on back to sleep, he said.

I was asleep.

I know. Go on back.

He pulled on his trousers and went out. She lay listening to his footsteps on the stairs and after a while the sound of him below in the kitchen and she put her hand on her belly and lay staring at the ceiling in the dark.

HE SAT AT the kitchen table with the lamp turned low and drank cold coffee from a tin cup and looked at the wall. The house made its sounds around him. He'd built it himself out of river lumber and it moved in the weather like a thing still living, popping and settling, and he'd grown accustomed to it over the years so that the sound of it had become a kind of company in the night hours. He sat with the cup in both hands and thought about the dream which he did not want to think about and thought about it anyway.

It was the third time in a fortnight he'd had something like it. Not always the same dream but the same quality of dream, the same dark valence, as if they were chapters in some book being written in a language he couldn't read but whose import was nonetheless clear enough. He was not a man given to omens. He had been raised by people who believed in them absolutely and perhaps it was that which had cured him of the tendency, the way a man who has been made sick on a certain food will not thereafter touch it. But these dreams were different from ordinary sleeping imaginings and he knew it and sat in the kitchen and tried to say what the difference was.

The baby moved and he heard her cry out softly from above. He waited. Silence returned and he drank the cold coffee and set the cup down.

He'd come to this country eight years ago following a dispute in the town where he was born which had left a man lame and himself with no prospects. He'd driven cattle up from Texas and saved his wages and built the house and found her, or she'd found him, or they'd found each other in the manner of people who have been damaged in similar ways and recognize in the other a corresponding wound. Her name was Ruth. She was twenty-three years old and carrying his child and he loved her in the way that men who are not able to speak of love nonetheless demonstrate it through an attention to the physical world around the beloved, the splitting of wood, the mending of fences, the laying in of stores against winter. He looked after her in other words. He was good at that. It was nearly everything else he was uncertain about.

Outside something moved in the yard and he went still and listened. The dog had gone quiet. He rose from the table and took the rifle from where it leaned by the door and stepped out onto the porch.

The sky was enormous and clogged with stars and gave light enough to see by after a moment's adjustment. The yard was empty. He stood on the porch boards and looked out at the dark tree line beyond the fence and at the river glinting below the bluff and at the shapes of the outbuildings standing in their arrangement and he didn't see anything. He stood there a long time. The air smelled of the river and of something else he couldn't name, something vaguely organic and wrong, like a thing turned in the ground. He went back inside.

He did not sleep again that night.

IN THE MORNING he rode into town for supplies and tied his horse at the rail before Greer's store and went in. The storekeeper was behind the counter talking to a man he didn't recognize, a small man in a black coat who turned when the door opened and looked at him steadily. He nodded at them both and took up a basket and went about collecting what he needed from the shelves. He could hear them talking low behind him. He brought his goods to the counter and set them down.

Mornin, he said.

Greer counted the items and wrote in his ledger. Mornin, John. How's Ruth keepin?

Well enough.

Getting close now I'd imagine.

Closer every day.

The man in the black coat was still watching him. He was perhaps fifty years old with a face that was very pale and smooth and eyes of a color that was difficult to fix, something between grey and green, and they were eyes that held a careful stillness in them the way water is still in a very deep place.

This here is Mister Dowd, Greer said. He's come up from the south.

The man extended his hand and John took it. The grip was dry and firm.

Dowd, John said.

He's a surveyor, Greer said. For the railroad.

John looked at him and then at the man. That so, he said.

That's so, Dowd said. His voice was soft and had no particular regional character, as if it had been worn down to something without origin. They're running a line through this country. Coming up along the river.

John packed his goods into a flour sack. I hadn't heard that, he said.

It's recent. Dowd smiled. Things move fast when there's money involved.

They do, John agreed.

You own property out this way?

I do.

Then you'll want to be talking with me at some point. The railroad will want easement or outright purchase depending on where the line runs. I'll be out your direction in the next week or two.

John picked up his sack. Well, he said. You'll find me there.

He went out. He stood on the boardwalk a moment in the morning sun and looked up and down the street at whatever there was. A woman he knew by sight but not by name came out of the dressmaker's shop and walked away from him. Three horses stood at the rail further down. A boy was rolling a hoop along the far side of the street and calling to it as if it were an animal. John untied his horse and lashed the flour sack behind the saddle and mounted and rode back up toward the river.

He was thinking about the railroad. He'd known it would come eventually, had heard for years that it was coming, the way a man hears reports of weather approaching from the west, and he'd done what he could to establish his claim and get his fences right and keep his records in order. But knowing a thing is coming and having it arrive before you in the form of a pale man with peculiar eyes are different orders of knowing and he rode home with something sitting in him that he couldn't quite work loose.

THAT EVENING AT supper she asked him about town and he told her about Dowd and the railroad and she listened without interrupting in the manner that was particular to her, one hand flat on the table, looking at the space between them.

What did you make of him, she said when he'd finished.

Hard to say. He seemed like a capable man. Professional.

But?

He lifted his coffee. I don't know, he said. Something a little cold in him.

She nodded slowly. What will it mean for the land?

It depends where they run the line. Could be they'll want the bottomland along the river.

That's the best of what we have.

I know it.

She was quiet. The lamp between them threw its orange light and outside the evening was going down in layers of red and grey above the tree line. He could hear the river from where they sat, or thought he could, that constant low murmur that was maybe only the blood moving in his own ears.

What about the other thing, she said.

He looked at her.

The dreaming.

He didn't answer right away. He turned his cup in a slow circle on the table. It's nothing, he said.

She looked at him with the level quality of attention she had that was neither accusing nor particularly forgiving but simply present, as if she had decided to see him clearly whatever that meant for either of them. It doesn't sound like nothing, she said.

Dreams are dreams.

You've never hollered in your sleep before. Not in three years.

He didn't have an answer for that. He got up and went to the window and stood looking out at the dark. I'll pass, he said.

She didn't argue. That was another thing about her. She knew which hills to die on.

THE NEXT SEVERAL days were fair and he worked the fence line along the north pasture and cut wood and in the evenings sat with her on the porch while the light went off the river below and the first bats came out working the insects above the grass. They talked or didn't talk and it was comfortable either way. He thought about the land and what it had taken from him to get it and hold it and he thought about the railroad and what it might mean and he watched her in the evenings when she didn't know he was watching and her face in that light was something he could not have described to anyone who asked and so he didn't try.

He dreamed again on the fourth night.

He was in a country he didn't know, a wide plain under a white sky, and he was walking toward something he could see at the edge of his vision but which moved away as he moved toward it so that he was always at the same remove. The ground under his feet was dry and cracked and gave a hollow sound when he walked on it as if there were spaces beneath. He walked for a very long time. There was no sun but the light was there, even and sourceless, and it cast no shadows. After a while he noticed there were others walking with him, not beside him but behind him and to the sides, and he could hear their feet on the hollow ground, that light crackling sound, and he did not look at them because something told him not to. They walked a long time in that white country and then he heard them stop. He stopped too. He turned.

He woke up before he could see what was behind him and lay in the dark with his heart going and Ruth's breathing slow and even beside him, her back to him, one arm curled under her head. The window showed black. He lay still for a long time and did not sleep again.

In the morning he was out early and rode the south fence down to the river and back and the country looked the same as it always looked in the early light, the dew on the grass burning off, the hawks already working the updrafts above the bluff, the river going along below in its brown unhurried way. He sat his horse on the bluff and looked down at the water. A piece of wood was turning slowly in the current, a plank or part of a plank, and he watched it until it went around the bend and was gone.

He had not told her about the second or third or now fourth dreams. He was not sure what there was to tell. He was a healthy man of thirty-one who had never been much troubled by sleep and something had come into his sleeping that felt like a communication from a source he didn't believe in, which was the particular hell of it. He did not believe in signs and omens and the visited sleep of troubled men and yet here he was.

He rode back to the house.

DOWD ARRIVED ON a Thursday, riding a good black horse and trailing a mule loaded with equipment, long wooden stakes bundled together and an instrument case in brass that caught the sun. He came up the lane from the road and John came out to meet him. They shook hands in the yard.

I appreciate you receiving me, Dowd said.

Come inside.

Dowd looked around the yard and at the house and at the outbuildings and then at the land beyond, the long sweep of it down toward the river, the timber on the far side, the bluff to the north. He looked at it all with the careful measuring attention of a man whose profession had trained him to see land as a set of problems to be solved.

Nice property, he said.

I've worked it some years.

It shows.

They went inside and Ruth brought coffee and Dowd thanked her with a slight inclination of his head and she went back to whatever she'd been doing in the other room. He unrolled his maps on the table and John stood looking at them. The lines on the maps were very fine and precise and the land they represented was shown without sentiment, without any of the textures that made it the thing it was, reduced to its geometry.

Dowd put his finger on the map. The line comes up here from the south. It follows the river valley because that's the path of least resistance through the hills. He traced it with his finger. Here it needs to cross to the north bank and that puts it right along through here.

Through the bottomland, John said.

Through the bottomland, yes. About a hundred and sixty acres of what I believe is your property.

John looked at the map. The best hundred and sixty acres, he said.

I couldn't speak to the relative quality. I can speak to the location.

He showed him the proposed easement and the proposed purchase price per acre and John looked at the numbers and didn't say anything for a moment. Then he said: That's not much.

It's fair market.

By whose measure.

Dowd looked at him with those still grey-green eyes. By the railroad's measure, he said. Which is currently the only measure that matters in this transaction.

John straightened up from the table. He looked at Dowd and Dowd looked back at him in that even way he had that was not unkind but was entirely without give.

I'll need to think on it, John said.

Of course. Dowd rolled the map carefully and replaced the rubber band around it. I'll be in town until the end of the month. He finished his coffee and stood. Thank you for the hospitality.

John walked him to the door and watched him go across the yard to his horse. Dowd mounted in one smooth motion and settled himself in the saddle and looked back once with a kind of professional detachment as if committing the property to some mental catalogue and then rode down the lane without hurrying.

Ruth came and stood beside him in the doorway.

Well, she said.

They want the bottomland.

She was quiet.

The pasture, the timber along the creek, and the lowground corn. About everything we'd need to put a real crop in next season.

What did he offer?

He told her.

She stood there. The afternoon light came across the yard and lit the dust that Dowd's horse had raised still hanging in the air. She put her hand on her belly. Can we fight it? she said.

Men have tried. I don't know of one that's won.

They stood in the doorway a while longer. The dust settled. The yard returned to its ordinary stillness and the light on it was the same light it had always been, or appeared to be. He put his arm around her and she leaned against him and they stood there while the afternoon went.

THAT NIGHT HE woke before the dream came, or woke out of its earliest passages before it had gathered itself into whatever it meant to be, and lay in the dark listening. There was a sound. He held still and worked to locate it and after a moment understood it was coming from outside, from the yard or near it, a low rhythmic sound that was not animal and not wind and not any ordinary night sound of that country.

He rose and took the rifle and went to the window. The moon was up, three-quarters, and lay its light over the yard and the ground beyond and he could see clearly. There was nothing in the yard. The sound continued. He watched. After a time he saw, along the far fence line, three figures moving. They moved in single file and slowly. They were moving east along the fence toward the river and they did not look toward the house. He watched them until they passed through the far gate and went over the bluff and down toward the water, their shapes against the moonlit sky and then going under it, and then there was nothing but the fence and the grass and the moon on it.

He stood at the window for a long time.

When he went back to bed Ruth was awake.

What was it, she said.

Men, he said. Passing through.

Whose men?

He lay down and pulled the blanket up. I don't know, he said.

She was quiet. Then she said: John.

He looked at the ceiling.

What is it that's coming? she said.

He didn't answer because he didn't know and because even if he'd known he would not have been able to say it in any way that would have made it smaller.

She moved close to him in the dark and he could feel the warmth of her and the weight of the child between them and outside the land lay in the moonlight in its accustomed silence and the river went on below the bluff the way it always had gone on, pulling everything south and away in its brown patient current, the way it would go on pulling long after whatever was coming had come and gone and long after the names of those who had stood above it watching were lost to any memory or record. He put his hand over hers where it rested on the blanket. She was already asleep. He lay watching the ceiling slowly lighten toward a grey that was not yet morning and was not any longer the full dark of night and thought about the railroad and the dream and the three men moving in the moonlight along his fence line and could not make of them any single coherent thing. They were only the pieces that had arrived so far. He understood without being able to say how he understood it that there were more pieces coming and that eventually they would make a shape he would recognize and that he was not going to like what he saw when they did.

The grey at the window deepened without brightening. A mockingbird started up somewhere in the chinaberry tree at the corner of the house, running through its whole inherited repertoire in the dark before dawn, singing everything it knew.
