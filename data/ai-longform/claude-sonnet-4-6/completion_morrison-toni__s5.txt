He had a pistol, primed and loaded, but chose to carry it in the satchel rather than his belt, thinking a weaponed stranger might invite the very trouble he hoped to avoid. Better to appear a simple traveler, a man of accounts and paper, which in most respects he was.

The trail narrowed as it entered a stand of pine and he ducked branches that swept at his hat. Regina moved with a steady patience he appreciated, her hooves finding the soft places in the earth without his instruction, as though she had walked this ground before and remembered it. He wondered briefly about her history, what hands had managed her, what landscape had made her calm. A good horse was its own argument for providence, he thought, though he was not a man much given to theological conclusions.

The letter in his satchel, pressed between two boards and wrapped in oilcloth, had come to him three weeks prior by way of a Dutch trader who owed him a minor favor. The letter was from D'Ortega, a tobacco man operating a plantation south of the James, a man Jacob had met twice before at a merchant's table in Maryland and found to be the variety of person whose company was instructive in the way that bad weather is instructive. You learned from it what you hoped to avoid. D'Ortega was the sort who required an audience for his opinions and punishments both, who spoke of his workers the way another man might speak of troublesome weather or failed crops, with a combined irritation and pride that suggested ownership itself was the true yield of his labors, not tobacco at all. Jacob had done moderate business with him before and found the accounting honest enough even if the man himself was not. The letter proposed a settlement of a debt that D'Ortega owed him, not in coin, which he claimed was scarce, but in kind.

Jacob had turned this over many times during the journey south. In kind. The phrase could mean stores of tobacco, which had its uses, or tools, or land. It could mean a person.

He was not opposed to slaves in the way that the Friends were opposed, those serious-faced people who gathered in their plain meetinghouses and spoke of men's souls as though the soul were a document requiring only the right signatures to become legal. He had attended one such meeting in his early years in the colony, more from curiosity than conviction, and found their certainty tedious even if their reasoning was not entirely without merit. He himself worked his land with indentured labor when he could get it, which was less and less often since the rebellion had made landowners skittish about contracts and freedoms alike. He had Lina, who was not indentured and not enslaved in any arrangement that he had made, though the arrangement had been made. He had Sorrow, whose situation was stranger still and which he tried not to examine too closely because the examination led him somewhere he did not wish to go. He had Willard and Scully, who were bound for another three years and spent much of those years in a low, performative misery that he found more exhausting than their actual labor.

What he wanted, when he permitted himself to want it clearly, was land enough to build something that would stand. Not the mean farmhouse they occupied now, its timbers soft with age and the cellar pooling with each hard rain, but something with permanence in it. Stone, if he could manage it. Two full stories with glass windows that caught the morning. A staircase. He had seen such houses in England, in the country north of London where his uncle had kept modest prosperity for a generation, and the image of them had settled in him with the tenacity of old grief, not painful exactly but always present, a residue of longing that flavored everything he built and everything he failed to build.

He had a wife. Rebekka. Dutch by birth, practical by nature, given to direct speech and a cleanliness that operated as a form of philosophy. She had come to him arranged, which both of them understood, and what had grown between them since was not exactly love in the way the word was used in the songs that traveled through the colonies in the mouths of young people who did not yet know what winters were, but it was a kind of reliable regard, a daily choosing that had its own dignity. She had buried three children and one who was not quite yet a child and had not let the burying break her, though Jacob knew it had done something more subtle than breaking. Made her more interior. Made the back of her eyes, when she thought no one was looking, very quiet and very far away.

He came out of the pines onto higher ground and the morning began its proper work. The fog was dissolving in pieces, the bay visible now in long grey-silver strips to his left, and ahead of him the road widened into something that had been, at some point, intentional. Two ruts worn deep by wagon wheels and between them a strip of weeds and grass that had long stopped expecting to be anything else. He could smell, before he could see, the plantation. Smoke first, then something animal and ripe, then the sweet rot of cut tobacco mixing with river mud.

He slowed Regina to a walk.

D'Ortega's property announced itself the way ambitious poverty often did—a large gatehouse whose boards were freshly whitewashed but whose foundations were sinking, an avenue of young oaks that had not yet grown into the grandeur they were intended to represent, a fence line well-maintained where it faced the road and collapsing where it turned from view. Jacob had seen enough of this country's aspirant class to read such a property like a ledger. The debt was real. D'Ortega needed him more than the letter suggested.

A boy of perhaps ten ran from nowhere to take Regina's bridle, and Jacob dismounted with the stiffness of a man who had been riding since before good sense, which was since before dawn. He handed over the reins and looked around. Three men moved slowly in a far field, their backs bent with a rhythm that was not quite human in its steadiness, as though they had long since left the decision-making to their bodies. A woman crossed from one outbuilding to another without looking up. Two hounds lifted their heads from the shade of a porch and then, deciding he was not interesting, lowered them again.

D'Ortega himself appeared in the doorway of the main house, a man broad through the chest who had the look of someone who had once been stronger and still had not accepted the revision. He wore a dark coat despite the heat and his face had the high, sustained color of either drink or sun or both, a redness that sat on his cheeks and neck like an accusation.

Come, come, man, he said. You've made good time.

Jacob said he had the weather to thank for it and D'Ortega said something about the bay being manageable this season which Jacob agreed to without interest and they moved inside where the air was somewhat cooler and darker and smelled of pipe smoke and something undercooked.

They sat across from each other at a table that was too large for the room. A woman, not quite girl and not quite woman, brought in two cups and a pitcher and set them down without raising her eyes and left again with the particular efficiency of someone who has learned that invisibility is a form of safety. D'Ortega poured without asking what Jacob preferred.

You've come a long way, he said, which was true but was also the beginning of a negotiation.

I have, Jacob said. And I have business north of here before the month ends. So let us not spend the length of my stay in approach.

D'Ortega smiled. He had good teeth, which surprised Jacob slightly. He said: You always were a man who disliked ceremony.

I dislike waste, Jacob said. They are not always the same thing.

The drink was rough and Jacob set it aside after the first taste and D'Ortega watched him do this with an expression that registered the slight and filed it. They talked then. D'Ortega's tobacco had been poor for two seasons, not ruinous but poor, and a shipment that was meant to clear his debts at the London end had been lost, he claimed, though he offered no documentation and Jacob did not ask for it. The specifics of the story were less important than the fact of it, which was that D'Ortega owed him forty pounds and had no forty pounds and was not going to have forty pounds in any arrangement that relied on tobacco.

What he had, he said, was people.

Jacob listened.

He had more than he currently needed for his operation, D'Ortega said, because he had made certain acquisitions in the previous year that had proved—he paused here and chose his word—redundant. He could part with two or three at a value to be determined. He had a woman who was skilled in the growing, who had come from a coastal people south and knew the land in a way that was useful. He had a young man who was strong and gave little trouble. He had a girl, scarcely more than a child, who had been given to him in payment of a separate debt and whose skills were various.

Jacob looked at the table. The wood was good oak but had not been properly finished and was beginning to lift at the joints. Whoever had built it had not built it to last.

I have workers, he said.

D'Ortega said everyone had workers. The question was whether you had enough of the right kind.

Jacob thought of the farm. The soft timbers. The pooling cellar. The stone house that lived only in the image he carried of it, pressed between two boards and wrapped in oilcloth the same way he carried D'Ortega's letter, as something that required protection from the weather of ordinary days.

He thought of Rebekka's quiet eyes in the moments when she thought no one was looking.

He thought of Lina, who knew things about this land that he had never asked her directly to teach him, who moved through the farm with the authority of someone operating according to a system he could see the effects of but not the logic. She had her own grief and her own reason and a capacity for work that shamed him sometimes in the way that any genuine competence shames a person who is aware of their own limits. He relied on her more than the arrangement between them formally required, and he knew it, and he thought she knew it, and neither of them spoke of it because speaking of it would require them both to recognize something about the nature of his authority that was more complicated than he preferred.

He thought of Sorrow and whatever it was she carried, the strangeness of her that sat in the farmhouse like weather, the way she moved through days as though half of her was still at sea, wherever it was they had found her, pulled from some wreck of a boat or the boat's wreck itself, he was never entirely clear, and even those who had told him the story had told it badly, as though the story itself resisted coherence.

Tell me about the girl, he said.

D'Ortega leaned back. She came to me from a Barbados man, he said, as payment of a debt. His failing, not mine. She is not large but she is not weak. She learns quickly. She is—he considered the ceiling for a moment—not troublesome in the ways some are troublesome.

Her age, Jacob said.

Difficult to say with certainty. Young. Eleven, twelve years, somewhere near.

Jacob picked up his cup and set it down again without drinking.

What was her work, he said.

D'Ortega moved his hand as though dispersing smoke. Various. The Barbados man used her in the house. There was a child she cared for, I believe. Cooking, cleaning. The usual domestic education.

Jacob said: Has she other languages.

Portuguese, D'Ortega said, and seemed to find this slightly amusing. And something else. Something from before. It doesn't interfere.

They were quiet for a moment. Outside one of the hounds had found reason to voice an opinion about something and then lost interest in it.

I am not a man who trades in this, Jacob said. He said it clearly and without apology but also without the heat that would have made it a conviction rather than a preference.

D'Ortega looked at him with an expression of perfect patience, which was more unsettling than argument would have been. He said: You are a man who is owed forty pounds.

Jacob allowed this.

He said: I will take her in settlement of fifteen. I will require documentation of the transfer.

D'Ortega said twenty-five.

They arrived at twenty, which was where Jacob had expected they would arrive, which meant D'Ortega had expected the same, which meant the negotiation had been, from the beginning, a ceremony after all.

The documentation was produced with a speed that suggested it had been prepared in advance. Jacob signed where he was shown to sign. The ink was poor quality and spread at the edges of his name, making his signature look uncertain, which irritated him.

He spent one night. He was given a room that was clean enough with a window that faced east and let in the morning before he was ready for it. He lay in the dark and listened to the sounds of the plantation resolving itself for sleep, the distant voices, the closing of doors, the particular silence that fell over places like this as the night deepened, a silence that was not the absence of sound but the sound of many people deciding to become quiet for the same reason.

He did not sleep well. He was not sure this was guilt, exactly. He turned the question over as he turned in the bed, examining it from different angles as he might examine an account that did not balance, looking for the entry that explained the discrepancy. He thought of the Friends and their meetings. He thought of D'Ortega's face when he said the word redundant, the satisfaction in it, the pleasure of being the one who had more than he needed. He thought of the laws passed after the rebellion, the way they had taken something that was already wrong and organized it, given it the dignity of language and the force of the courts, as though naming a thing carefully enough could make it something other than what it was.

None of this was unfamiliar. He had lived in this world long enough to know its arrangements. He simply had not, until now, stepped fully into them.

She was brought to him in the morning before he had finished the breakfast that was set for him, a girl in a grey dress that was too large at the shoulders, as though it had been chosen to minimize her. She was small, as D'Ortega had said, and very dark, and she stood in the doorway of the room with the satchel he had told them to give her and looked at him with eyes that were assessing in a way that he found, unexpectedly, almost amusing, because they were his own eyes, the eyes he used when he entered a room and needed to know quickly what it contained.

He said good morning in English.

She looked at him.

He said it again, more slowly, which he recognized as the slow speech of a man who has never had to learn another language and whose solution to incomprehension is volume and patience.

She said: Good morning, sir. In English that was accented but clear.

He nodded. Good, he said, which meant nothing or meant that he was relieved or meant that they could proceed.

They rode north that day, Regina carrying him and the girl sitting before him in the saddle in the manner of a child or a package, holding the pommel with both hands and leaning neither toward him nor away, maintaining the balance of someone who has learned that proximity to strangers requires its own discipline. He could not see her face. He could see the top of her head and the set of her shoulders and the way she adjusted her weight when the ground shifted beneath Regina's feet, an adjustment that was automatic and precise and spoke of some experience with horses or with being carried.

He did not speak to her for a long time. The morning was clear and the trail was easier going north than it had been coming south, or perhaps he simply knew it better. The bay appeared and disappeared to his left. Birds he could not name made noises in the trees that he could not interpret. The heat was building in its patient way.

After some time he said: What do they call you.

She was quiet for a moment. Then she said a name, or something that might have been a name, that he could not reproduce in his own mouth. It had sounds in it that his tongue refused.

He said: I will call you—and stopped. He was not a man who named things carelessly. His children he and Rebekka had named from scripture or from family, the names carrying freight that the children were meant to grow into or at least grow alongside. He looked at the top of her head and the thin line of her neck and thought of nothing from scripture that seemed to fit and nothing from family that he wanted to put on her.

He said: We will find something.

She made no response, which he took as consent or as its absence. He was not sure, in this particular arrangement, that the difference was meaningful.

They traveled. The sun moved. Regina was patient. The girl held the pommel and balanced herself and watched the country moving past them with those assessing eyes that Jacob could not see but could feel, or imagined he could feel, turned outward at the trees and the trail and the bright water beyond, cataloging what she found there, filing it away in whatever system she used for such things, preparing herself for what was next.

He thought: she has done this before. She has been transported before, has sat in exactly this posture of careful readiness before, watching a new landscape replace an old one and doing the internal work of survival, which is the work of learning quickly what is required and what is forbidden and who holds which kind of power and what they will do with it.

He thought: she is not so different from Regina, and then he thought: no. That is not right. That is the way D'Ortega thinks and I will not think that way.

He thought instead: she is not so different from me. Which was also not right, or not entirely right, but was closer to something he could live with. He had been transported too, in his fashion, though by his own will and with the document of his own name in his pocket. He had arrived in a fog and found his way by stumbling and learned the country by being in it and was still learning it and would perhaps die still learning it, which seemed to him, in the clear morning light with the bay going silver to his left and the horse moving steadily north, neither a tragedy nor a consolation, but simply the way things were for anyone who had the misfortune and the fortune of surviving long enough to require a new place.

He thought: I will build the house. Not this season, but soon. Stone, if he could manage it. Two full stories and glass in the windows to catch the morning. And there would be enough hands to work the land properly if he chose wisely and managed fairly and did not let himself become D'Ortega, did not let the having of things become the purpose of things.

He thought this and he knew, somewhere beneath the thinking, that the thought itself was a kind of story he was telling himself, and that the story was not precisely a lie but was not precisely the truth either, and that this was how men in his position moved through their days, by telling themselves stories that were generous enough to be bearable.

The girl shifted her weight as Regina stepped over a root.

Jacob shifted his weight too, adjusting for her.

They rode on into the remainder of the morning.
