The ferry left at eleven and Marcus stood at the stern rail watching the lights of the town organize themselves into something legible — the grain elevator, the motel sign, the orange wash above the highway — and then the darkness came up between him and the shore and those arrangements dissolved. He had not eaten. In his coat pocket was a photograph taken in 1987 at the mouth of a river in the interior where his brother had worked a summer cutting brush for a mining survey. Daniel was twenty in the photograph, his arm around a man Marcus did not recognize, both of them squinting against light that was coming from somewhere off to the left. Marcus had taken the photograph from the drawer in his mother's kitchen three days ago when the call came from the constable in Anse-au-Mort. He did not know why he had taken it. Perhaps because it showed Daniel in the act of squinting, which seemed important — some residual life in the expression, something unresolved, the face still negotiating its relationship with the world.

The strait was rough. He could feel the hull working under him, the long slow lean and then the recovery. He went inside.

The passenger lounge had the quality of a hospital waiting room at four in the morning — a fluorescent pallor over everything, a few travellers strewn across the fixed seats in the attitudes of the defeated. A woman with two small children was trying to manage sleep, the children sprawled against her like warm stones. An old man in a good suit read a newspaper with the focused attention of someone who had trained himself, over years, not to look up. Marcus found a seat near the window and put his bag between his feet and watched the black water moving alongside the hull.

He thought about the last time he had seen Daniel. Eight years ago in a bar in Halifax, a Tuesday afternoon, rain on the windows and Daniel already drunk when Marcus arrived, his jacket dark at the shoulders. They had argued about money, or about what money was a way of saying. Daniel had a way of becoming very still when he was angry, a stillness that wasn't calm but was instead a kind of withdrawal, the way water drops away from a shore before a wave comes. He had looked at Marcus with that stillness for a long time and then he said — you have always thought that I owed you something for being what I am. He picked up his glass and finished it. Marcus had sat with his own drink untouched while the rain thickened on the window. When he finally looked over, the chair was empty.

There was a cafeteria at the far end of the lounge. He went and got a coffee and carried it back and sat with it between his hands, not drinking. Through the window there was nothing but darkness and the intermittent white of water.

The constable's name was Arsenault and he had been brief on the phone, the way people are brief when they have said this kind of thing many times and have learned that brevity is a form of mercy. A body recovered from the shore below the barachois. No identification. The physical description — height, approximate age, a scar along the left forearm — had come through the national registry and matched what Marcus's mother had filed years ago after Daniel vanished from the address he had given her in Baie-Comeau. Marcus had not known his mother had filed that report. She had done it quietly, alone, and said nothing to him about it. He learned this only when Arsenault told him the body had been flagged to next of kin.

The scar he remembered. Daniel at sixteen, drunk for the first time or one of the first times, trying to cut the top from a can with a jackknife. The blood had been extraordinary, the way blood from a forearm is always extraordinary, dark and fast and frightening. Their mother had wrapped a dishcloth around it and held it there for a long time, talking calmly about something else entirely, the way she talked when she was frightened.

He dozed briefly and woke to find the lounge thinned out, the woman and her children gone somewhere, the old man still reading. Outside, a grey light was beginning at the waterline. The ferry would dock in roughly two hours.

He went out again onto the deck. The air was cold and tasted of distance, of the other shore coming closer now though he couldn't see it. The sky in the east had the particular quality of northern dawns — not a brightening so much as a slow retraction of darkness, the way a tide goes out and leaves things behind. He gripped the rail and looked at the water. The ferry's wake unspooled behind them, white and purposeful, and was then lost in the grey.

He had tried to find Daniel twice in the years after Halifax. Once through a mutual friend who thought he had heard Daniel was in Yellowknife working in camp kitchens. Once through a woman named Sylvie who had been involved with Daniel for several years in their twenties and who met Marcus for lunch in Montreal and told him, carefully, as though she were setting down something fragile, that Daniel had written to her from somewhere in Labrador two years before, that the letter had been — she searched for the word — oceanic. He asked her what she meant. She said that it went on and on, that it kept opening into something further. She said she had read it three times and could not have told you afterward what it was about. I kept it for a while, she said. I'm sorry, I don't have it anymore.

He had looked at her across the restaurant table, her hands around her wine glass, the afternoon light behind her, and thought about his brother writing a letter that kept opening into something further and then sending it to this woman who could not say what it was about. He had felt something then that was close to envy and could not explain that to himself.

The constable had said the body was in the facility in Anse-au-Mort and that someone could bring Marcus out in the morning. There was no hotel in Anse-au-Mort but there was a woman who took in travelers and Arsenault had called ahead. Marcus had thanked him and sat for a long time with the phone still in his hand after the call ended.

His mother had said, when he told her — I can't go, Marcus. You go. She was seventy-two and had a hip that was waiting on a surgery and she did not drive, and the logistics were genuinely difficult, but he knew it was not the logistics. She had been waiting for a call like this one for eight years, possibly for longer than that, and now that it had come she needed to sit in her kitchen in New Glasgow and hold very still with it, the way she had held the dishcloth against Daniel's arm, talking about something else.

It might not be him. This was what Marcus had been carrying without looking at directly, the way you carry something in peripheral vision, aware of its shape without being able to name it. The registry had flagged the description as a possible match. Possible. A height, an approximate age, a scar. Scars were common. Men who worked on coasts had scars and were of a certain height and had lived to approximately a certain age before something on a shore ended them. It might be anyone. He might step into a room in Anse-au-Mort and find a stranger on a table, someone who had nothing to do with him or his brother or the bar in Halifax or the mouth of that river in 1987, and he would come back across the strait carrying only the knowledge that Daniel was still somewhere, still writing letters that kept opening into further rooms, still squinting at a light that was coming from somewhere off to the left.

He did not know which possibility frightened him more.

The sky was lighter now. He could see the line of the shore ahead, dark and low, a few lights at the water's edge. The ferry was slowing almost imperceptibly, the engines changing pitch. He stayed at the rail as the shore grew specific — a breakwater, a light on a pole, the rooflines of a small town coming out of the dark. The smell of the water here was different, cold and fecund, the smell of an enclosed coast, a strait rather than an ocean, something that moved back and forth between shores.

He thought of Daniel on a night like this, coming across on a ferry or some smaller boat, watching this same coast materialize out of darkness. Whether he had been coming to this place or leaving it. Whether he had stood at a rail like this one and thought about Marcus or their mother or Sylvie or the letter he had sent that kept opening. Or whether he had stood there not thinking of any of them, having become someone for whom the other shore was simply the other shore, neither arrival nor departure, just the next place the water put you down.

This was something Marcus had never understood about his brother — the way Daniel moved through the world without apparent accumulation, as though each place and each person were complete in themselves and did not have to carry forward. Marcus carried everything. He had boxes of photographs in his apartment, letters he had received over thirty years, the menus from restaurants where important things had happened, a child's drawing his daughter had made when she was four that he still had even though his daughter was now twenty-three and lived in Edinburgh and called him on his birthday. He accumulated. It was a form of argument he had never been able to put down.

Daniel had traveled light. He left no forwarding address. He sent one oceanic letter and then silence.

The ferry was docking. The crew moved along the deck with the purposeful unhurry of people doing something they had done hundreds of times. Lines were thrown. The hull settled against the pilings with a sound like a word in a language Marcus didn't know.

He picked up his bag and joined the small queue moving toward the gangway.

The woman who took in travelers was named Mrs. Cormier and she was awake when the cab dropped him at her door, the kitchen light on, the smell of coffee coming through the screen. She showed him to a room that looked out over a small garden and a fence and beyond the fence the rooftop of another house with a satellite dish tilted toward something in the south. The bed was made with the kind of care that suggested she had not slept. He thanked her. She looked at him for a moment with the frank and unguarded assessment of a person who had seen many travelers arrive at her door before light. — Arsenault told me, she said. He nodded. She left him.

He did not sleep. He lay on the bed with his coat off and looked at the ceiling while the light in the room changed, the grey becoming something warmer, the garden outside the window making itself visible by degrees. He heard birds. He heard a truck go past on the highway behind the house.

He took the photograph out of his pocket and looked at it. Daniel squinting. The man beside him whose name Marcus had never known. The mouth of a river that he could not name either. It struck him now that the photograph was full of things he didn't know — the name of the man, the name of the river, what they were laughing about, where Daniel went afterward, what the summer had been for him, whether he thought about Marcus in whatever tent or bunkhouse he slept in that summer in the interior, whether he thought about their father who had been dead three years by then. The photograph was a record of a moment but also a record of the limits of what Marcus could know about the person in it. He had been carrying it as though it were evidence of something. He wasn't sure now what it was evidence of.

At eight-thirty he heard Mrs. Cormier moving in the kitchen and he got up and washed his face and went downstairs. She put toast and eggs in front of him without asking and he ate, surprised by his own hunger. She sat across from him with her coffee and looked out the window at the same garden he had watched from above.

— Did you know him here, he asked. Did Daniel — was he here.

She looked at him carefully. — I only know what Arsenault told me. That someone has to come and look.

He nodded. She poured more coffee into his cup.

— There was a man here for a while, she said. Not this past year but the year before. He worked on the boats out of Anse-au-Mort, short stints, the way men do. She looked at her hands. — I don't know if it was your brother. I don't know what your brother looks like.

He put the photograph on the table. She looked at it for a long time without touching it.

— He would be older, Marcus said. He would be forty-four now.

She looked at the photograph. — This man I knew had a — she raised her hand toward her chin. A beard.

He nodded.

She slid the photograph back to him without speaking. He did not know what that meant and he did not ask.

Arsenault came at nine in his truck and drove Marcus out to the facility, which was not a hospital but was attached to one, a low beige building that smelled of cold and cleaning solution. A man named Thibodeau who was not a doctor but filled a doctor's function here met them in a hallway and shook Marcus's hand and looked at him in the way that Arsenault had also looked at him, with a kind of practiced readiness, prepared to proceed at whatever pace Marcus required.

They went down a short hall. Thibodeau paused at a door.

Marcus thought about what his mother was doing at this moment in her kitchen in New Glasgow. Whether she was sitting with her tea or standing at the window that looked out over the harbor. Whether she was talking to herself, as she sometimes did when she was alone and did not know anyone could hear — a soft continuous murmur, not words so much as the sound of her mind keeping company with itself.

He thought about the letter that kept opening into further rooms. He thought about Daniel standing at a rail watching a shore come forward out of darkness, whether or not that shore had been this one.

— I'm ready, he said.

Thibodeau opened the door.

Afterward he stood outside the building in the cold morning air for a long time. Arsenault gave him his space, standing a few feet away, looking at the water that was visible at the end of the road, the strait grey and flat now in the morning calm, the far shore just visible, the shore Marcus had come from.

He did not know yet what he was going to tell his mother. He did not know what language there was for this particular thing, which was neither the thing you feared nor the thing you had not let yourself hope for but was instead something else, a third thing without a name, the kind of knowledge that takes a long time to understand what it is.

He put his hand in his pocket and felt the photograph and did not take it out.

The water moved in the strait between the two shores. A gull crossed from left to right and was gone. Somewhere behind him he could hear Mrs. Cormier's satellite dish making its fine adjustments, searching for the signal, turning slowly in the direction of whatever was sending something down through all that cold and distant air.
