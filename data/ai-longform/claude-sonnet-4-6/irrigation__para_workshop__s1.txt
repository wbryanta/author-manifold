The first leak they found by walking the line, watching for the dark seam of wet earth that would appear sooner or later in the dry grass. Denny found it, his boot sinking an inch into ground that should have been hard as brick in June, and he crouched and pressed his palm flat against the soil and felt the cool water wicking up between his fingers.

"Here," he called back.

Ray was twenty yards behind him, carrying the pipe cement and the collar fittings in a canvas bag over one shoulder. He walked up and looked at Denny's hand buried in the dirt and didn't say anything. He set the bag down and pulled out the folding shovel.

They took turns digging. The soil was cracked on the surface but soft and reddish an inch down where the water had been spreading through it, and it came up easily. Ray dug longer than his turns required. Denny stood back and looked down the row of Braeburns, which were coming in small this year — hard green knobs that should have been sizing up by now, should have been swelling with the deep watering they weren't getting because the pressure in this lateral was half what it needed to be.

"You call Petersen back," Denny said. It wasn't a question.

Ray kept digging. "I got his number."

"That's not what I asked."

Ray stopped and rested his forearms on the handle and looked up at his brother. He was three years older and it showed now, in his face, the skin creasing at the corners of his eyes in a way that happened to men who worked in the sun. "I'll call him when I have something to say."

"You have something to say. You just don't want to say it yet."

"That's probably right."

Denny crouched and reached into the hole and found the line, a two-inch black poly pipe, and ran his fingers along it until he felt the split. It wasn't a pinhole. It was a four-inch fracture that had opened along the seam of an old coupler, one of the original fittings their father had put in, and it gaped open slightly when he pressed it, like a mouth.

"Cut it out or sleeve it," Denny said.

"Cut it out. Do it right."

Denny reached back and Ray handed him the pipe cutter without looking. That was something they could still do — move around each other in work the way they'd learned to when they were boys, each one knowing what the other needed before the words formed. It was one of the few remaining fluencies between them.

Denny made the cuts, two clean perpendicular cuts bracketing the failed coupler, and lifted the section of pipe out and set it in the dirt. Brown water ran from both cut ends and soaked into the ground. Ray was already measuring the replacement section with his eyes.

"Foot and a half," Denny said.

Ray cut it. He was meticulous with the cutter, rotating it slowly, letting the blade do the work instead of forcing it. Their father had taught them that. Forcing the cutter crushes the pipe, won't seat right, you'll be back next week doing it again. Most of what they knew about keeping this place alive had come from their father that way — not in speeches but in the doing of small specific things, repeated until they were reflex.

They primed both ends of the repair section and both stubs of the mainline with the purple primer, the sharp chemical smell rising in the heat, and then Ray applied the cement while Denny steadied the couplers, and they twisted each fitting into place and held it while the bond set.

"Twenty seconds," Ray said.

They held it.

There was no wind. Down the row a scrub jay was shouting about something in the upper branches of the nearest tree and then stopped. The silence the jay left behind was very large. Denny became aware of the sound of the irrigation pump running somewhere back at the pump house, a low mechanical pulse he'd stopped hearing years ago the way you stop hearing a refrigerator, and he listened to it now and thought about what it cost to run it every day, what it cost against what the fruit would bring.

"Petersen's offer is real," Denny said.

"I know it's real."

"Six-forty an acre. You're not going to see that number again. Not on this ground."

"I know what the number is."

"Then help me understand what we're waiting for."

Ray let go of the fitting and it held. He sat back on his heels and wiped his hands on the thighs of his jeans and looked at the repair for a long moment, checking the alignment. "We're waiting for me to be ready," he said.

"Ready." Denny stood. His knees had started to ache from crouching, a new thing this year. He was forty-one. "How long does ready take?"

Ray looked up at him. "Don't."

"I'm asking a sincere question."

"You're asking it the way you ask things when you already think I'm wrong. You got that from Mom. That tone."

"Well." Denny looked off down the row. "She wasn't wrong very often."

Ray stood too. He was shorter than Denny by two inches but built broader, the kind of build that read as menacing in strangers and just read as Ray to Denny, though sometimes Denny forgot which was which. He picked up the shovel and began filling the hole back in. The dirt fell over the repaired pipe and covered it.

"There's a second leak," Denny said. "Probably more, with the pressure reading I got this morning. We should find them all before we turn the system back up."

"I know."

They walked on down the row. The orchard smelled of warming earth and of the pear trees that ran parallel in the next block, a yeasty sweetness that was strongest in the morning but lingered through midday. Their father had planted the pears to extend the season — Braeburns off in August, Bartletts and Boscs coming in September and October — and it had worked well for twenty years. Less well since.

The blight had started in the pears four years ago, a fire blight that moved fast in the wet spring they'd had, blackening the branch tips until they curled downward like burned fingers. They'd cut out the infected wood and burned it and thought they'd gotten ahead of it, and then the next spring it came back harder. Now there was a full acre of the pear block that was effectively dead — the trees alive in a technical sense, still leafing out, but producing almost nothing, and the infection moved outward from that acre every season no matter what they sprayed.

Petersen was a developer out of Sacramento, but his agent had been straightforward: the acreage would be divided into five-acre parcels, each with a house, the whole thing becoming the kind of rural-residential community that had been eating up the valley for thirty years. There was no moral high ground from which to object to this. Denny had tried to find it anyway and couldn't.

"He'll break it up," Ray said, without preamble, as though he'd been continuing an argument in his head. Probably he had.

"Yes."

"Twenty years from now it's a neighborhood."

"Probably."

"Dad planted eleven of those Braeburn trees himself. The ones along the east edge. I watched him do it. I was nine years old."

Denny waited.

"He made me carry the rootstock," Ray said. "Each one, out of the truck, down the row. He said carry it like it matters. Because it does matter. That's what he said."

"I know," Denny said. "I heard him say it to you."

Ray looked over at him.

"I was standing at the end of the row," Denny said. "You didn't see me. I was seven, I came out to watch and you two didn't notice me. I watched the whole thing."

Ray looked back at the trees. He moved his jaw the way he did when he was working something over. "You never said that."

"It never came up."

They walked. The ground between the rows was mowed in April but already tall again, dry seed heads brushing their jeans below the knee, and Denny watched his feet and watched the ground alongside the pipe route for the sign of the next leak.

"You remember what he got for Braeburns in '98," Ray said.

"You don't have to—"

"A dollar forty a pound at the farmgate. Bianchi took twelve tons off this block alone. A dollar forty." Ray shook his head slowly. "I was in high school. I thought we were rich. I thought this was what rich looked like."

Denny remembered it differently — he'd been fourteen that year, and he remembered his father coming inside after Bianchi's truck left and sitting at the kitchen table and drinking a cup of coffee and not saying anything for a long time, and his mother had finally sat down across from him and said Frank, and his father had said I know, I know it's good, and his mother had said so let it be good, and his father had picked up his cup and looked at it and said it doesn't feel like enough. It never does. His mother had said that's not the money talking, that's you. And his father had looked at her and after a while had smiled in the compressed, reluctant way he smiled, as though smiling was an admission of something.

Denny didn't tell Ray this. It felt like it might be used as evidence for something, though he couldn't have said what.

"Ninety cents last year," Denny said instead. "Eighty-two the year before."

"I know the numbers."

"We lost money two of the last four years. Actually lost money."

"I know."

"On the trees Dad planted."

Ray stopped walking. He turned and looked at his brother and for a moment his face was open in a way it rarely was, something undefended moving across it. Then it closed again. "That's a shitty thing to say."

"I'm not saying it to be shitty. I'm saying we can honor what he built by admitting that it's over. We can say: it was real, it mattered, and now it's over. That's allowed."

"It's allowed," Ray repeated. "Who's allowing it?"

"I'm not looking for a fight."

"You've been looking for this fight since the funeral. Since before the funeral. You'd already done the math before we put him in the ground."

The accusation landed. Denny let it land. It was not entirely untrue — he had, sitting in the second pew of St. Catherine's while the priest spoke, found himself calculating in the margins of his own grief, tallying what the trees would bring against what they owed, and he had felt the shame of that calculation and then had kept calculating because the shame didn't change the numbers.

"I have a daughter who starts college in two years," he said.

"I know you do."

"My half of a sale at Petersen's number pays for that and pays off the line of credit and leaves something over."

"I know the math."

"Then do the math on what another five years of this looks like. Two bad seasons out of four, Ray. The blight isn't going anywhere. The water costs aren't going down. We've put forty thousand dollars of repairs into equipment in the last three years that probably isn't worth forty thousand dollars."

Ray started walking again. Denny followed.

"You could buy something else," Denny said to his back. "Your half, you could find a going operation somewhere. Something that's actually making money. The Estrada place over on 88 is—"

"Don't."

"It's fifteen acres of productive—"

"I don't want the Estrada place." Ray stopped again, but didn't turn around this time. He was looking at the trees. "I want this place. I have always wanted this place. Since I was old enough to know what wanting something meant."

Denny stopped beside him and looked at the trees with him.

This was the middle of the Braeburn block, the oldest trees, planted in the 1970s by their grandfather. They were big trees, bigger than modern orchardists planted, because the thinking in those days was different — you let a tree reach its full size, grow into its own character, and you worked around it. The trunks were thick as a man's thigh and the bark was dark and furrowed. In early spring when they blossomed the whole block went white, five hundred small white fires burning at once, and the bees came in such numbers you could hear the whole orchard from the house.

Denny had not thought about the blossoming until this moment, standing in the June heat in the dry grass with the smell of pipe cement still on his hands. He thought about it now.

"You know what it costs to keep it," he said.

"Yes."

"Knowing what it costs, what is your actual plan?"

Ray was quiet for a moment. A real quietness, not the one that meant he was refusing to answer. "I've talked to the water district," he said. "About the drip conversion. Converting the whole orchard. The district covers forty percent of the cost for efficiency projects, and there's a federal program on top of that. I could get to drip for somewhere around sixty thousand out of pocket, and I'd cut water costs by—"

"When were you going to tell me this."

"I'm telling you now."

"We're co-owners, Ray. We've been co-owners for eight months. When were you going to bring me into a conversation about sixty thousand dollars?"

"I'm bringing you in now."

"After you already have a plan."

"After I have something to bring." Ray's voice was patient in the way that was also infuriating, the older-brother patience that had always made Denny feel managed. "I don't come to you with a half-formed idea, you know that. I come when I have something solid."

Denny looked down at the ground. He kicked at a clump of dry grass and it broke apart. "Sixty thousand gets you drip conversion. What does it get you on the blight?"

Ray didn't answer immediately.

"Because the blight is why we're losing the pears," Denny said, "and the pears are a third of the operation, and drip conversion doesn't touch it."

"No," Ray said. "It doesn't."

"So you'd still have a third of the orchard that's dying."

"I'd have two-thirds that's more viable. I'd have water costs down significantly enough that—"

"That you could lose money more slowly."

"That I could stay." Ray turned and looked at him, and his voice lost the patience in it, the management fell out of it, and what was underneath was just the plain fact of what he was saying. "I could stay here. On this land. For another twenty years. Maybe more."

Denny heard it. He heard all of it. He looked at his brother's face, the sun-worn creases, the jaw set with a stubbornness that was their father's exact stubbornness, the thing that had made their father impossible to reason with about money and insurance and planning for the future, and the same thing that had made their father keep faith with this ground for forty years in the face of every reason not to.

Denny thought about his daughter. He thought about his apartment in Stockton, on the third floor of a building he'd lived in for six years without once thinking of it as a place, merely as a location where he kept his things and slept. He thought about his ex-wife, who was in Tucson now with her partner, and about the way his daughter sometimes called him on Sunday mornings and they would talk for an hour about nothing in particular, books she was reading, a movie, a friend's drama, and how those calls were the clearest thing in his week, the sharpest, the thing that felt most like being a person.

He didn't know how to put any of that on the same scale as Ray, looking at these trees.

"I want you to hear me," Denny said. "I'm not trying to take something from you."

"I know that."

"I genuinely don't know what the right thing is."

Ray looked at him. "That might be the most honest thing you've said today."

"I have a lot of honest things to say. They mostly come out as arguments."

Something in Ray's face shifted. He looked down and when he looked up there was the beginning, just the beginning, of something that in another setting might become a laugh. "Yeah," he said. "You do."

They were quiet. The jay came back, somewhere to their left, and said its piece, and left again.

"If I bought you out," Ray said, and stopped. He started again. "If I bought you out — not at Petersen's number. I couldn't do Petersen's number. But something fair. Something I could do."

"I'm not — Ray, I'm not trying to force your hand."

"I know. But say I did. Say I bought your half at a number we could both live with. You could walk away, you'd have something, and I'd have the place. I'd carry the debt, I'd carry the drip conversion, I'd carry whatever happens with the pears. You'd be out of it."

Denny thought about it. He thought about it the way he'd been thinking about the orchard for eight months, feeling along the walls of it in the dark for the shape of what was fair. "What's the number you're thinking."

"I don't know yet. I'd need to get a proper appraisal done, not the Petersen number, an honest ag valuation. And you could get your own appraisal and we'd find something in between."

"That could take a while to put together."

"Yes."

"And in the meantime the operation needs—"

"I know what the operation needs."

Denny looked down the row. There was a second leak — they hadn't found it yet, and the water was running, and the pressure was wrong, and the small hard fruit hung in the trees above them going nowhere. He began to walk again and Ray fell in beside him.

"I don't want to sell to Petersen either," Denny said.

Ray looked over.

"I don't want that. I want to say that clearly. I want the money, I need the money, but I don't want this to become a neighborhood. I don't want someone to take out these trees."

Ray was quiet.

"I watched him plant the ones he planted," Denny said, "and I watched you plant the ones you planted, and I watched him watch you, and I don't want someone to come in here with a chipper and turn all of it into mulch for someone's new lawn." He paused. "I don't know what to do with that feeling, since the feeling doesn't pay anyone's bills, but I wanted to say it."

Ray said: "Okay."

"Okay."

They walked. The ground was flat and the rows ran straight and even, the way their grandfather had laid them out with a transit level fifty years ago, and you could stand at one end and see clean to the other, the diminishing perspective of trunks and canopy. In the distance the Coast Range made a low blue wall against the sky and above it the sky was the white-hot blue of a June noon.

Denny saw the dark ground before Ray did. A ten-foot spread of it, wet and deep-colored, the earth gone soft around a split in the mainline that must have been running for days. He stopped and they both looked at it.

"That's a bad one," Ray said.

"Yeah."

"Coupler or the pipe itself?"

Denny crouched and found the line and felt it. The pipe had failed mid-section, not at a fitting, a stress fracture maybe two inches long. It would require a longer replacement section, two new couplers, more cement. They had everything they needed in the bag.

Ray handed him the shovel.

Denny started to dig. The wet soil came up dark and clean, piling beside the hole, and the water ran from the pipe end in a thin bright thread and soaked immediately into the ground. He dug steadily and didn't hurry and Ray crouched beside him and held the edge of the hole to keep it from collapsing and neither of them spoke.

The sun was directly overhead and their shadows were small and directly below them, and the irrigation pump kept its low pulse in the middle distance, and a hawk moved in a long slow circle above the pear block without landing. The trees stood as they had always stood. The fruit hung in them, hard and green and waiting for August, and the season was still going, and there was still time, and neither of them knew what would happen, and they kept working.
