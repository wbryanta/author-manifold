He came awake cursing and she had him by the shoulder.

Sull, she said. You'll wake the whole house.

He sat in the dark with his chest heaving and looked at his hands in his lap as though they might tell him something. The room was cold. Through the single window above the headboard the stars burnt hard and indifferent and he could hear the river somewhere in the dark below the bluff, running as it had always run and would run past any accounting of him or his kind.

What is it, she said.

Nothing. Go back to sleep.

She lay back and he could feel her watching him. The child moved in her and she put her hand to her belly and he watched her do it, that unconscious benediction, and he thought about the dream and the crowd seething in that absolute dark and the voices raised against him and how he had known even in the dream that they were right to raise them.

Sull.

I said go back to sleep.

I will if you'll lie down.

He lay down. The corn shuck mattress crackled under him and he stared at the ceiling where the plank shadows played in some faint residual light from outside. After a while her breathing evened and deepened and the child was still again. He did not sleep.

He rose before light and dressed in the cold and went out through the front room where his brother lay on his blanket before the dead fire. He stepped over him. Ches did not stir. Outside the door the morning was coming grey and chill off the river and he stood on the porch and looked down through the bare black trees to where the water lay flat and pewter-colored between the banks. A heron stood motionless at the near edge. It could have been carved from the same grey light that held everything.

He went out to the barn and fed the mule and the two shoats that were all that remained of a greater ambition and he pumped water at the trough and watched it fall bright and cold into the mossy dark. In the east a thin seam of light had opened and through it came a color like old blood. He pumped until the trough was full and then stood watching that cold sun haul itself over the treeline.

His name was Hollis Sullivan and he was twenty-eight years old and he had a brother and a wife with child and a piece of ground that he had worn down to nearly nothing in three years of trying. The land was thin and the summers were dry and what the drought left the grasshoppers took and what the grasshoppers left was barely worth putting by. He had borrowed against next year and the year before that and there was a man in town, the same man as always in these stories, who held the notes and smiled pleasantly when he came in and slid the papers across the desk without looking at them.

He went back inside and built up the fire from the coals and put the coffee on and stood at the window. Ches was awake, sitting up on his blanket with his hair wild and his eyes still clotted with sleep, watching him.

You look bad, Ches said.

Sullivan poured the coffee without answering.

Bad dream?

Sullivan set the cup in front of him and stood with his own. The fire was catching now, the new wood beginning to pop and speak.

I heard them men was back, Ches said.

Which men.

Them three. Suttle and the other two.

Sullivan looked at him.

Over to Prewitt's yesterday. Harlan seen em.

What are they doing over to Prewitt's.

Ches shrugged and drank his coffee. Just drinking I reckon. Same as they do.

Sullivan went back to the window. Down through the trees the heron had gone and the river ran alone in its grey light. He thought about the three men he had seen before, once, on a road south of town, moving with that loose deliberate purposefulness that some men carry like a weapon. He had not spoken to them. He had pulled his wagon to the verge and waited for them to pass and they had looked at him as they went by in the way that a man looks at something he has already decided the value of.

Who sent for them, he said.

Ches looked at the fire. Well. That's the question, ain't it.

You know who.

I know what Harlan said. That's not the same thing.

Sullivan drank his coffee. It was thin and bitter and he had been drinking thin bitter coffee all his life and thought he probably would continue to until the end of it. He heard Mae moving in the back room and the soft grunt she gave when she sat up and the creak of the floor under her feet. He thought about the child in her and felt something that was partly tenderness and partly a cold and formless dread.

She came out pulling her shawl and looked at the two of them standing there with their cups.

Morning, she said.

Morning.

She went to the fire and held her hands to it and then poured herself coffee and stood with them. She looked better than she had in weeks, the color in her cheeks from the cold and her hair loose down her back. She looked like a girl. He watched her and thought how it was possible to love someone so much that the feeling sat in you like a sickness.

Ches says them men are back, he told her.

She looked at Ches and then at Sullivan. What men.

Suttle.

She was quiet a moment. She turned back to the fire. Well, she said.

Well, Sullivan said.

Are you going to town.

I was planning to.

She didn't say anything else. She drank her coffee and stood looking at the fire and he watched her profile, the clean line of jaw and brow, the belly pulling her shawl tight. Outside a crow called once from the bare trees and then was still.

He hitched the mule and drove the wagon down the rutted track to the road that ran along the ridge and then south toward town. The morning had gone clear and cold and the sky above the bare trees was the blue of old pottery. The fields he passed were empty and dead-looking, the stalks broken and frost-blackened, the furrows hard as iron. A dog came out from a farmyard and barked at the mule and then gave it up. He passed the Greer place where the house stood empty now, the windows dark, the porch beginning to cave on one side. The Greers had gone west two years ago. Half a dozen families had gone west. He had stood at each departure with his hat in his hands and nodded and said well, good luck to you, and watched them go with their wagons loaded and their faces forward and something in their eyes that was neither hope nor the absence of it but some third thing he didn't have a name for.

The town was called Regard and it sat in a bend of the river where a creek came in from the east and the flat ground had allowed for a degree of permanence. There was a mill and a dry goods and a livery and two saloons and a church of the kind that is more attended in difficult times. He tied the mule in front of the dry goods and went in and the bell above the door rang in its tinny way and Pettus looked up from behind the counter.

Sullivan, he said.

Pettus.

He set his list on the counter. Pettus read it over and went about filling it without comment, moving among his shelves with the practiced economy of a man who has done one thing for a long time. Sullivan looked around the store. There was a boy sitting on a barrel in the corner eating a pickle and watching him.

You hear them men are back, Pettus said.

I heard.

Pettus set a tin of lard on the counter. You know what they're about?

I expect.

Pettus looked at him. Dolan sent for em is what I think.

Sullivan didn't answer.

He's been patient, Sullivan. I'll give him that. He's been more patient than most would be.

Patient, Sullivan said.

A man can't carry paper forever. It ain't personal.

No, Sullivan said. I don't expect it is.

Pettus wrapped the dry goods in paper and stacked them on the counter and read the prices off and Sullivan counted the coins out and they were not enough and he counted again as though that would change them.

I can put the rest down, Pettus said.

Sullivan looked at the small pile of coins. He was aware of the boy watching him from the corner.

Put it down, he said.

He carried the goods out to the wagon and stood in the thin cold sunlight and looked up the street toward the saloon. The horses tied out front were not horses he recognized. He stood looking at them for a while. Then he got up on the wagon and turned the mule and drove back the way he had come.

Riding back up the ridge road he saw the smoke from a fire down along the river, back in the trees a ways, and he stopped the mule and sat looking at it. It was a small fire, the smoke thin and white against the dark tangle of bare trees. He sat there for a long time. The mule stamped and blew. Then he slapped the reins and drove on.

That night after supper Ches cleaned his rifle at the table by the lamp while Sullivan sat with his hands around his coffee cup and Mae sat mending the tear in the sleeve of his coat. The fire was good and the room was warm and from outside came the sound of the wind in the eaves that was the sound of all the nights of his life.

You could go see Harlan, Ches said without looking up from the rifle.

What would Harlan do.

He could talk to some of the others. Marsh. Dobbs.

Sullivan shook his head.

They'd come if it was put to them right.

They got their own troubles.

Ches ran the rag down the barrel. Everyone's got their own troubles, he said. That don't mean they won't stand.

Sullivan looked at the fire. Mae's needle went in and out of the dark cloth and he watched her hands, the small sure movements.

It's not that kind of thing, Sullivan said.

What kind of thing is it then.

He didn't answer. Ches set the rifle on the table and looked at him and then looked at Mae and then looked back at Sullivan.

They're just men, Ches said.

Yes, Sullivan said.

Well.

He pushed back from the table and went to the window. The night outside was very dark and he could see nothing but his own reflection hanging there, a pale face in the glass, and behind him the warm room, the lamp, his wife bent over her mending, his brother at the table. He looked at all of it. He thought about the dream, the sun going out, the cold spreading through that absolute black, and the voices coming up around him in the dark.

When did you stop being afraid of men? Sullivan said.

Ches was quiet for a moment. I didn't say I wasn't afraid, he said. I said they're just men.

Sullivan looked at his reflection. It looked back at him with his own eyes and told him nothing he didn't already know.

In the night Mae woke him again but this time it was not for dreams. He found the lamp and lit it with hands that were steadier than he felt and by its light her face was white and wet and she held to his arm with both hands.

Is it time, he said.

Get Ches.

He shook Ches awake and Ches came up fast and clearheaded the way he always did and looked at Sullivan's face and said I'll go for the woman and was pulling on his boots before Sullivan was back through the door.

He sat with her through the long hours. The lamp burned and he fed it and held her hand and spoke to her and she suffered and was silent except when she was not silent and then the sounds she made were sounds he had not known a person could make, sounds that seemed to come from some place deeper than the body, from the place where the body meets the ancient dark. He held her hand and she crushed it in hers and he would have given anything in that moment, all the notes, all the thin land, all the coming years of it, to have moved that suffering out of her and into himself.

The woman Ches brought was Dora Mears who had done this work for thirty years and she came in and took charge of things with the calm of long experience and sent Sullivan out of the room. He sat in the front room and put wood on the fire and sat with his elbows on his knees and stared into the flames. Ches sat across from him.

After a while the sounds from the back room stopped and Sullivan sat very still and then Dora Mears opened the door and looked at him and her face told him nothing and then it told him everything.

It's a girl, she said. And Mae is all right.

He sat there a moment longer. Then he stood and went to the door and Dora Mears stepped aside and he went in.

Mae was sitting up against the pillows with the infant wrapped in cloth against her chest. She was grey with exhaustion and her hair was wet and plastered to her temples and she was the most beautiful thing he had ever seen. She looked up at him and her eyes were bright.

Come look, she said.

He went to the bed and sat on the edge of it and looked at the small sleeping face tucked in the cloth. The face was red and ancient and new and it breathed with a concentrated industry that seemed to say this is the most serious work there is and I am wholly given to it.

She's all right, Mae said. She's fine.

He put out one finger and touched the infant's cheek. The skin was impossibly soft. The child stirred slightly and was still.

He sat there for a long time. Outside the window the dark had begun to pale and the birds were starting up in the trees along the river, their voices coming in one by one like instruments tuning.

Toward full light he went out on the porch. The morning was very cold and very clear. The frost lay on everything and caught the early light so that the yard and the bare trees and the distant fields all glittered with a cold fire. The river below the bluff was smoke-colored and still and the tree-line on the far bank stood black against a sky that was going from grey to a pale clean blue.

He stood and looked at all of it. He thought about the man in town, Dolan, and the paper with his name on it and the three men camped down along the river in the trees. He thought about the dream, the prophet, the crowd, the long cold dark and the stars of another season. He thought about his daughter sleeping against his wife's chest, her small face, the seriousness of her breathing.

Ches came out and stood beside him.

We need to talk, Sullivan said.

All right.

Not now. Later.

Ches nodded. He looked out at the frosted morning, the glittering fields. He didn't say anything.

I've been thinking wrong about this, Sullivan said.

How's that.

Like it was something happening to me. Like I was just in the path of it.

Ches waited.

It ain't that, Sullivan said. It's something else.

What is it then.

He watched the last of the frost fire die as the sun came up full and the light went ordinary. Down in the trees something moved along the river bank, a deer or a dog or a man, briefly visible between the bare trees and then gone.

I don't know yet, he said. But I aim to find out.

They stood on the porch in the cold light and the river ran below them and the day came on. In the house behind them the woman and the child slept. The world was what it was and had been what it was for a long time and would continue to be what it was and none of that was in his power to change. But there were smaller things. There were things a man could put his hands to. He thought about Harlan and Marsh and Dobbs and the others on their pieces of thin ground with their own notes and their own seasons of loss. He thought about Ches cleaning the rifle by lamplight without being asked. He thought about what it meant to stand in a place and call it yours not because of any paper but because you had bled into the ground and put seed in it and pulled life out of it by main force and buried your failures in it and gotten up the next morning and gone back to it again.

The sun was up full now and beginning to carry a little warmth and the frost was running off the eaves in a thin bright trickle. From inside he heard the child cry, one sharp sound of outrage at the cold world, and then Mae's voice low and wordless and then silence.

He went inside.
