The first thing to go was the fainting couch.

Marion had not expected that. She'd assumed the fainting couch—a ridiculous piece of furniture, puce velvet worn to bare wood at both armrests, one leg repaired long ago with a wooden dowel that didn't quite match—would sit in the back bedroom until the estate sale company packed everything remaining into boxes for the Goodwill truck. It had seemed to her the kind of object no one would want. A period piece without sufficient period. Damaged goods. But a woman in a barn jacket arrived at eight forty-five, fifteen minutes before the official opening, and stood on the porch with her face pressed to the sidelight window until Marion's cousin Deb unlocked the door, and the woman walked directly to the back bedroom as though she'd been given directions, and she paid sixty dollars for it, and two men who must have been waiting in her truck materialized and carried it out while Marion stood in the kitchen holding a paper cup of coffee that had already gone cold.

She watched through the kitchen window as the men loaded it into the truck bed.

"That was your grandmother's," she said to no one.

Deb came into the kitchen and refilled her own cup from the carafe they'd set up on the counter, the carafe sitting on a piece of notebook paper that read COFFEE $1 DONATIONS WELCOME. No one had donated. "You want me to handle the back rooms?" Deb said. She was a large woman with short gray hair and reading glasses perpetually pushed up on her forehead, and she had driven four hours from Columbus for this, which Marion knew she should feel grateful for and did feel grateful for, in a buried way, beneath everything else.

"I'm fine," Marion said.

"You're not fine, but that's fine," Deb said. "It's allowed."

People were coming through the front door in a steady stream now. The estate sale company—a husband and wife named Gerry and Pat Hollenbeck, who operated out of Findlay and had done this kind of thing for twenty-two years, they'd mentioned twice—had priced everything over the past three days, while Marion was not present. She had made a deliberate choice not to be present for the pricing. The Hollenbecks had sent her photographs on her phone, little numbered tags affixed to each item, and she had looked at some of them and not looked at others. Her mother's sewing table: $45. The set of Lenox china, service for twelve, never used in Marion's memory, kept in the tall cabinet in the dining room in its original boxes: $180 for the set. A ceramic lamp shaped like a heron that had stood in the corner of the living room for as long as Marion could remember: $12.

The lamp had sold in the first ten minutes. She'd seen it go out the door under a woman's arm and had felt something loosen in her chest, not grief exactly, more like the giving way of something that had been under pressure.

She needed to move around. Standing in the kitchen was making it worse.

The living room was crowded with people holding items up to examine them, turning price tags over, setting things back down. The carpet had been shampooed by the Hollenbecks' crew, and the room had that particular institutional cleanliness of spaces that have been rendered temporarily neutral, the smell of her mother—lavender powder, something slightly medicinal, the wool of her cardigans—replaced by cleaning solution and the various outdoor smells the strangers brought in on their clothes. Marion moved through carefully, sideways between a man studying the collection of decorative plates and a teenage boy who seemed to be with him and who was looking at his phone with the patience of someone serving a sentence.

Her mother had collected the plates over forty years. They came from everywhere—not from travel, her mother had not traveled, but from the Franklin Mint catalogs that arrived in the mail, from the gift shops of places her friends visited and brought things back from, from church sales and the antique mall off Route 12. Cardinals on snow-covered branches. A covered bridge in autumn. The Last Supper rendered in cobalt blue and gold. Marion had grown up with these plates arranged on a high shelf around the perimeter of the living room, so familiar they had become invisible, and now she saw them through the eyes of the man examining them and they looked like what they were: mass-produced kitsch, cheerful and impermanent, worth whatever someone was willing to pay.

The man bought six of them for eight dollars total.

Pat Hollenbeck was working the register—an actual cash register they'd brought and set up on the dining room table—with the focused efficiency of someone who had long ago stopped registering the human content of what passed through her hands. She was a small woman with dyed auburn hair cut in a practical style, and she greeted each customer with the same smile, and she wrapped things in newspaper before she put them in bags. Marion watched her for a moment and felt a strange admiration, the way she might admire a surgeon or a long-haul trucker, someone whose work required a useful form of detachment.

She went upstairs.

The upstairs rooms were quieter. A few people moved through the bedrooms, looking at the furniture with the critical assessment of people who have to imagine things in their own homes, measuring with their eyes. In her mother's bedroom, a younger woman—thirty-something, wearing the particular combination of vintage flannel shirt and expensive sneakers that Marion associated with a certain kind of urban resettlement, people who had come back to small Ohio towns from Columbus or Cleveland and brought aesthetics with them—was sitting on the edge of her mother's bed, bouncing slightly, testing the mattress.

Marion stopped in the doorway.

The bed was iron, painted white, with a headboard her mother had bought secondhand in 1971 and repainted herself every few years, so that if you looked closely at the curves of the iron you could see the accumulated layers, white over white over white, little chips and cracks revealing the history. Marion knew this because she had looked closely, many times, lying in that bed during childhood illnesses, during thunderstorms, during the years after her father left when she had climbed into her mother's bed in the middle of the night not because she was afraid but because she didn't know what else to do.

The young woman was looking at her phone, probably measuring something.

Marion moved on.

In what had been Marion's own childhood bedroom—empty of furniture now, the Hollenbecks having consolidated everything into the main rooms downstairs, a strategic presentation of goods—she stood at the window and looked at the backyard. The garden was overgrown. Her mother had maintained it fiercely until she couldn't, and the last two years it had gone to seed, and now it was the particular overgrown beauty of a garden that was returning to something, grasses tall and seeding, the rose bushes sprawling over the fence, some late-season wildflowers she couldn't name coming up through what had been the vegetable beds. A bird feeder hung from the maple tree, empty, spinning slightly in a wind Marion couldn't feel from inside the house.

She had grown up in this room. She had looked out this window for eighteen years at that backyard and that maple tree and that fence, and then she had gone away to college and come back for holidays and eventually come back less and less, and her mother had lived in this house for another thirty years after Marion left, and Marion was aware that her mother's life in this house had been longer and more complete than Marion's own time in it, and yet it was Marion's version of the house that felt real to her, which was a kind of selfishness she recognized and could not correct.

She heard voices downstairs, a brief negotiation over price, Deb's laugh.

She should go back down.

She stayed at the window for another minute, watching a cat she didn't recognize pick its way along the top of the back fence, moving with that precise, slow confidence of cats who own nothing and are at home everywhere.

---

By noon, the dining room table was sold, the chairs going with it for a package deal Marion had not been consulted on but which she understood was the Hollenbecks' prerogative, that was the arrangement. She ate half a sandwich Deb had brought in a cooler from Columbus—turkey and Swiss on good bread, Deb always brought good food, it was a form of care she offered consistently—and stood in the kitchen and watched people carry her mother's things out the front door.

A man in his seventies bought the rollaway desk from the study. He examined it for a long time, opening and closing the tambour cover several times with what Marion could only describe as tenderness. He ran his fingers along the pigeonholes inside. He looked at the price tag, which said $95, and he looked at Pat Hollenbeck, and Pat said, "Best I can do is eighty-five," in a tone suggesting this had not been a negotiation he'd initiated but one she was offering as a courtesy. He paid in cash, counting it out from a billfold. He drove a Silverado with a camper shell, and he loaded it himself, slowly, with great care, using a moving blanket he'd brought in from his truck without being asked.

Marion found herself hoping he had grandchildren. She found herself constructing a life for him in which the desk would go into a study where grandchildren would do homework, where he would sit and pay bills and write the occasional letter. This was not something she could know and was probably not even something she should want—her mother had sat at that desk and written letters and paid bills and the man had no obligation to continue any particular story—but she constructed it anyway, and it helped.

An older woman, heavyset, moving carefully with the help of a cane, spent forty-five minutes in the living room. She looked at everything. She picked things up and put them down and picked them up again. She didn't seem to be shopping so much as visiting. At some point Marion realized the woman had not bought anything and was simply standing in the middle of the room with her cane and looking around with an expression Marion couldn't quite read—not sadness, not nostalgia, something more like orientation, the look of someone trying to place themselves.

Marion went to her. "Did you know my mother?"

The woman looked at her. Her eyes were very clear, a pale gray. "We were in the same circle for thirty years," she said. "Thursday lunches. The garden club before it fell apart. I'm Norma Schilling."

Marion said her own name.

"I know who you are," Norma said, without any particular edge. "Barbara talked about you."

Marion did not know what to do with this. "Did she," she said, which was not a question.

"She was proud of you," Norma said. "The job in Seattle. Your daughter." A pause. "I saw your daughter, last Christmas, when she brought the baby."

Marion had not been there for Christmas. She had sent gifts and called on Christmas morning, and her daughter Sylvie had driven from Cleveland with the baby to spend three days with Barbara, and Marion had not come because she had told herself the flight was too expensive in December and she had a work obligation the day after Christmas that she could not move, and both of these things were true, and she had not come.

"She was glad to have them," Norma said. Simply. Not as accusation.

Marion looked at the worn carpet where the living room rug had been—sold first thing this morning, a young couple who'd rolled it up themselves and carried it out between them, a good wool rug her mother had saved for and bought in 1988, hundred-and-twenty dollars this morning—and she said, "I know."

Norma bought a small lamp from the side table and a set of embroidered pillowcases, which Marion had not known were in the sale and which she looked at in Norma's hands and felt certain she remembered from her childhood, from the linen closet at the top of the stairs that smelled of cedar. Norma paid nine dollars for these items and moved toward the door and then stopped and turned back to Marion.

"The house'll sell fine," she said. "Someone will fix it up. There's a young couple—" she waved her hand vaguely, the gesture encompassing the town, the real estate market, the continual turnover of places. "It'll be fine."

Marion thanked her.

She went back to the kitchen and stood at the sink and ran cold water over her wrists for a moment, a thing she did when she was overwhelmed, had done since she was a child, and she was aware this was a thing her mother had told her to do—run cold water on your wrists, it'll settle you—and that her mother had probably told her this in this kitchen, possibly standing at this sink, and she turned the water off and dried her hands on a paper towel because the dish towels were in the sale.

---

The afternoon came on with its particular late September light, the quality of Ohio light at that time of year that Marion had forgotten about, or not forgotten but stopped expecting, the way it came in low and golden and made everything look provisional, lit from the side. The crowds had thinned. The Hollenbecks were moving through the remaining rooms with markers, reducing prices on items that hadn't moved. Half off. Half off. A red dot.

The iron bed had not sold. Marion had checked twice, and it was still upstairs with a reduced price tag now, $75, down from $120. The young woman in the flannel shirt had not come back.

Deb found her in the study, which was almost empty now, just the desk chair that hadn't gone with the rollaway—a different model, a simple oak chair with a woven seat—and a box of miscellaneous items the Hollenbecks had priced collectively, a felt-tipped sign on the box reading MAKE AN OFFER. Marion was going through the box. She didn't know why. She knew what was in it; she'd helped carry it up from the basement two days ago when they were preparing. Candle holders. A broken clock radio. A tin of sewing notions. Old greeting cards bundled with rubber bands. A snow globe from Niagara Falls.

She picked up the snow globe. It was heavy, the glass thick, and inside it was the Falls rendered in miniature, a curved white cliff, the blue-green water at the base, and when she turned it over and righted it the snow fell and she watched it fall and settle and thought about her mother at Niagara Falls, which she could not actually picture because she was not sure her mother had ever been to Niagara Falls. Someone had given her this thing, or she'd bought it somewhere without going. It was possible to own a snow globe of a place you'd never been. Marion turned it over again and watched the snow rise and fall.

"You're going to drive yourself crazy with the box," Deb said.

"I know."

"Put the snow globe in the keep pile if you want the snow globe."

The keep pile was a laundry basket in the corner of the upstairs hallway, which Marion had filled over the past two days with things she couldn't leave: her mother's address book, worn to softness, the handwriting inside in various inks going back to the seventies. A photograph she'd found in the kitchen drawer of Marion at approximately three years old, sitting on the front porch steps in a yellow dress, face turned up toward whoever was holding the camera, laughing at something. A small soapstone carving of a bear that had sat on the windowsill in her mother's bedroom for so long Marion could not remember a time before it. The keep pile was modest, contained, and she'd made herself keep it that way, understanding that the process of keeping had its own gravity and she needed to resist it, because she lived in a two-bedroom apartment in Seattle and she did not have room for a houseful of things and more importantly—this was the thing she kept having to return to, to press on like a bruise—more importantly, the things themselves were not her mother. The keeping would be a substitution, and she was too old to fall for substitutions.

She put the snow globe in the keep basket.

Deb said nothing.

By three o'clock, the Hollenbecks were telling the last few stragglers that they'd be wrapping up in the hour. Gerry Hollenbeck, who had spent most of the day doing the physical work—carrying larger items, loading dollies, organizing the back of the growing charity truck—came into the kitchen where Marion was making notes in a small notebook about the items she needed to tell the real estate agent about, repairs mostly, the back window that didn't latch, the gutters on the north side, and he poured himself a cup of the cold coffee and drank it and said, "Your mother had good things."

Marion looked up.

"I mean that as a compliment," he said. "Sometimes we go into these sales and everything is—" he paused, choosing, "—not worth anybody's time. Your mother had an eye. Not fashionable, but an eye. People responded."

Marion thought about her mother, who had grown up with nothing, in a different small Ohio town forty miles east of this one, who had learned the word for things slowly and carefully over a long life, who had acquired a kind of domestic beauty by attention and patience rather than money, who had known what she liked and liked it steadily, without apology, for sixty years.

"She did," Marion said.

Gerry Hollenbeck nodded and left the kitchen with his coffee.

At four fifteen, a young man—Marion guessed early twenties, in a Carhartt jacket, with the combination of deliberate beard and underlying boyishness of someone recently past adolescence—came up to her where she was standing in the living room looking at the walls, which were marked with the shadows and pale squares where things had hung. He was holding the iron bed's price tag, which he had apparently removed from upstairs.

"The bed," he said. "Is this still available?"

"As far as I know."

"Would you take fifty?"

Marion looked at him. He was nervous. She couldn't tell if he was nervous because of the negotiation or because of something else, some awareness of the situation, of what it was.

"It's not up to me," she said. "But I'll ask."

She found Pat Hollenbeck and Pat said fifty was fine, they'd factor it in. The young man went upstairs with a friend Marion hadn't noticed waiting near the door, another young man in a similar jacket, and they came down carrying the bed in pieces—the headboard, the footboard, the frame—and they loaded it into a truck that was, Marion realized, the same vintage and general state as the truck her own first boyfriend had driven when she was seventeen, twenty-something years ago in a different small Ohio town, and she watched them from the porch, the afternoon light doing what it did at this hour and this time of year, and she thought about the bed going into an apartment or a rental house, being assembled again, being slept in.

It was a good bed. It had held weight.

Deb came and stood beside her on the porch. They watched the truck pull away down the street, past the neighbor's maples which had just begun to turn, a color that was not yet red but was moving toward it.

"Okay," Marion said.

This was not an answer to anything Deb had said, but Deb understood it and put her hand briefly on Marion's shoulder and they stood there in the cooling light while inside the house the Hollenbecks began the final work of clearing and accounting, the tallying up of what had been and what remained, the patient arithmetic of other people's lives.

Marion stayed on the porch a while longer. The street was quiet in the way of streets in small Ohio towns on autumn afternoons, a quiet that was not emptiness but accumulation, all the years of such afternoons laid over each other until they became a texture, a weather, a particular quality of late light over old maples and houses that had been standing longer than anyone walking past them could remember. She had grown up in this light. She had forgotten it the way you forget a language you no longer use and then suddenly understand again, hearing it spoken by someone else, in a different context, years later, when you are old enough to know what it costs to forget and not quite young enough to believe you can get it back.

The Hollenbeck truck idled at the curb. Inside, Deb was helping with the last boxes.

Marion put her hand on the porch railing, the wood soft and slightly giving under her palm, the paint peeling in long curls she had not let herself notice until now, and she stood there until the light changed, and then she went inside.
