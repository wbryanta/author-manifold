The price tags were her sister Carol's idea—little orange stickers with numbers written in Carol's careful schoolteacher hand—and Ruth had agreed to them because agreeing to Carol's ideas was the path of least resistance through this particular day. Carol had driven up from Columbus the night before, taken the guest room, and spent two hours after dinner with her yellow legal pad making a list of what should go and what Ruth might want to keep, and Ruth had sat at the kitchen table drinking wine and saying yes, yes, that sounds right, sure. The wine was from a bottle she'd found in the back of the pantry behind the spice rack. It was probably fifteen years old and tasted like vinegar and she drank three glasses of it anyway.

Now it was nine-fifty in the morning and Carol had a conflict—Carol always had a conflict—and so Ruth stood alone in the front doorway of the house on Beaumont Street, watching a man she had never seen before carry her mother's china cabinet down the porch steps.

He was doing it wrong. She could see that plainly. He had it tilted forward at an angle that put all the stress on the glass in the doors, and she considered saying something, and then she considered that it didn't matter, and then she considered that it did matter, because if the glass broke there would be a problem about the price, and then she considered that it still didn't matter because the price was twelve dollars—Carol had written it—and even if the glass held together, what was twelve dollars, what was any of it.

"You need help with that?" she said.

"I got it," the man said. He was perhaps forty, wearing a Buckeyes sweatshirt with a hole near the collar. He didn't look at her. He made it to the bottom of the steps and then shifted the cabinet against his hip and worked it toward a pickup truck parked at the curb. Ruth watched him load it into the truck bed, watched him throw a furniture pad over it and cinch a strap around it, and the whole operation was competent enough. He knew what he was doing. She was the one who didn't know what she was doing.

The estate sale had been advertised in the Dennison Courier and on three Facebook pages she'd never heard of, and by ten o'clock there were eleven people moving through the house. Ruth had hired a woman named Diane to run it—Diane had a business doing this, Diane's card said *Graceful Transitions* in lavender script, which Ruth had found unbearable and Carol had found practical—and Diane stood in the living room in a red cardigan with a zippered money pouch on a lanyard around her neck, answering questions, making change, keeping a tally in a spiral notebook. Diane had done this for years. Diane knew how to be in a dead person's house.

Ruth drifted from room to room, not quite an owner and not quite a customer, touching things.

In the kitchen, a young woman with a baby on her hip was going through the drawer where her mother had kept the kitchen tools. She was pulling things out one at a time, holding them up, putting them back. She found the potato ricer and held it up and looked at it. She turned it over in her free hand. The baby grabbed at it and she moved it away, said something low and soft to the baby, the way you do.

Ruth's mother had used that potato ricer every Thanksgiving of Ruth's life. She had stood at the counter in this kitchen and pressed cooked potatoes through the fine mesh and the potatoes came out in long soft threads and they were the best mashed potatoes Ruth had ever eaten, better than any restaurant, better than anything she'd made herself in forty years of cooking in her own kitchen in Pittsburgh. She had never asked how her mother did it, had never thought to ask, and now she stood in the doorway and watched the young woman put the ricer in the cardboard box she had going on the floor, and the price Carol had written on it was a dollar fifty, and that was exactly right, that was its objective value, and Ruth felt something move through her chest that was not grief exactly but was grief's country cousin, grief's practical friend who drove you to the airport.

She went upstairs.

The bedroom was mostly cleared out. Diane had recommended selling the furniture and Carol had agreed and Ruth had said yes, yes, that sounds right. The bed frame had already gone—a couple had taken it first thing, they'd clearly come early and come for it specifically—and the mattress leaned against the wall with a SOLD tag on it, waiting for the man in the Buckeyes sweatshirt to make a second trip. The dresser was still there, an oak piece with a mirror that Ruth's mother had gotten from her own mother, and someone had left the drawers open in the process of emptying them, and Ruth went around the room closing them. She closed the top drawer. She closed the middle drawer. She pulled the bottom drawer and it stuck, as it always had, as it had stuck for as long as Ruth could remember, and she had to jiggle it the way you do and then pull again. The drawer came out.

Inside was a single thing that had been overlooked: a folded piece of pale blue paper.

She picked it up and opened it and it was a letter, written in her mother's handwriting, and it was addressed to no one. It began: *I don't know how to say this so I will just say it.*

Ruth stood in the empty bedroom and read the letter.

It was not a letter about anything dramatic. It was a letter about a trip her mother had taken when she was twenty-three, before she met Ruth's father, a trip to Portland, Oregon, that Ruth had never known about, a trip her mother had apparently taken by herself on a Greyhound bus and spent two weeks, and the letter described the city, the cold gray water of the Columbia, a park where she had sat and read a novel she loved, and a man she had met whose name was David and who had taken her to hear jazz in a small club and had kissed her in the street outside afterward. The letter said: *I thought about staying. I almost stayed. I had my bag with me and I thought about staying.* It said: *He was kind in a way I hadn't known people could be kind. He was kind the way weather is kind when it finally breaks.* And then it said: *But I came home. You know how it is. You get afraid. I got afraid and I came home and I never told anyone and now I'm telling this piece of paper in case I forget.*

The letter was not dated. The handwriting was her mother's handwriting but younger, looser, and Ruth tried to calculate and couldn't—her mother had been born in 1939 and so twenty-three would have been 1962, and her parents married in 1964, and there was room in there for a bus trip, there was room in there for David, there was room in there for her mother to have stood in a city she'd never been to and thought about staying and gotten afraid and come home and kept it the way you keep a coin in a pocket until the pocket wears through.

Ruth folded the letter along its original creases and stood at the window that looked out over the backyard.

The backyard was brown with November. The apple tree her father had planted was still there. She had fallen out of that tree when she was seven and broken her wrist and had cried, not because of the pain, but because her mother's voice when she came running across the yard was a sound Ruth had never heard before and it had frightened her. Her mother had knelt in the dead leaves and taken Ruth's face in both hands and Ruth could remember—she could still remember—how her mother's palms felt against her cheeks, the slight roughness, the heat. And then her mother had said, let me see, and looked at the wrist, and said, okay, okay, we're going to the hospital, okay.

Okay.

She put the letter in her jacket pocket.

Downstairs, more people had come in. Diane was managing them with quiet efficiency, pointing people toward the dining room, toward the basement where the tools were. Ruth stood on the bottom step and watched a man examine her father's recliner. He sat down in it and worked the handle that brought up the footrest and the footrest came up and he settled back with his arms on the armrests and looked at the ceiling. He was testing it the way you test a recliner, but he was also—Ruth saw this clearly—he was also just sitting in it, just resting for a moment in a dead woman's house, in a dead man's chair, having a moment of something. He was probably sixty. He was wearing work boots with dried mud on them. He looked comfortable. He looked like a man who needed to sit down.

She went to the kitchen and got a glass of water and drank it standing at the sink, looking out at the driveway. A station wagon she didn't recognize was parked behind her rental car. The sky was the low white sky of Ohio in November, the sky she had grown up under and moved away from, the sky she associated with the particular melancholy of late Sunday afternoons when she was a teenager and the weekend was ending and school was coming and the light was going and her mother would start dinner and the sounds and smells of it would come to Ruth in her bedroom where she was reading or listening to records or simply lying on her bed looking at the ceiling with her arms at her sides in the particular dramatic suffering of adolescence.

She had been so desperate to leave. It was important to remember that. She had wanted to leave with a physical urgency, had wanted it the way you want water when you're thirsty, had thought about college applications and then the acceptance letter from Pitt and then the August morning she loaded her father's car with her things as a kind of extended drink of cold water. And she had left and she had built a life in Pittsburgh and the life was real and good and hers, she wasn't going to pretend otherwise. She had a job she was good at. She had friends. She had been married once, for eleven years, to a man named Richard who was not unkind and with whom she had shared many ordinary happinesses before they became strangers and agreed about it civilly and separated. She had an apartment with good light and a cat named after a jazz musician and a Saturday routine she liked. The life was good. She was not one of those people who believed that leaving made you a traitor, that staying made you virtuous. She had known people who stayed and people who went and the outcomes seemed more or less random, seemed to have less to do with where you lived than people generally admitted.

But.

She put the glass in the sink.

She went to the living room where a teenager was sitting cross-legged on the floor going through a box of record albums. This was her mother's records, the ones that had been in the console stereo that had already sold. The kid was going through them one by one, holding them up, reading the backs. He was maybe sixteen, with the kind of long awkward neck that sixteen-year-olds have, and he was concentrating with his whole face.

"Find anything good?" Ruth said.

He looked up. "This one," he said, and held it up. It was a Chet Baker album. *Chet Baker Sings.* The cover was a close-up photograph of Chet Baker looking young and doomed in the way he always looked.

"I know that record," Ruth said.

"Yeah?"

"My mother liked it. She used to play it on Sunday mornings."

The kid looked at the cover. "It's really good," he said. "I didn't know she—I mean. Was it your—"

"My mother's house, yes."

"Sorry," he said.

"Don't be. She would have been glad you wanted it."

This was probably not true exactly but it was in the neighborhood of true. Her mother had opinions about music and would not have considered a sixteen-year-old boy to be an appropriate appreciator of Chet Baker, but she also had a fundamental generosity that would have overridden the opinions. She had believed that things should go to people who wanted them. She had believed that fiercely, about things and about people and, Ruth thought now, standing in her childhood living room with a glass she'd forgotten she wasn't holding anymore, about herself.

She had wanted David to have her and she had gotten afraid and come home.

You get afraid. I got afraid.

The kid bought the album for two dollars and tucked it under his arm and left, and Ruth watched him go out the front door and down the porch steps and into a car where someone—a parent, probably—was waiting.

By twelve-thirty, much of the furniture was gone. The rooms had that startled, enlarged quality that furniture-less rooms have. Ruth could see places on the walls where pictures had hung—lighter rectangles against the faded paint—and she stood in the living room looking at them. Her mother had a kind of system, a gallery of family photographs going back generations, and Ruth knew what had been in each rectangle by the ghost it left. Her grandparents in front of the house in 1949. Ruth and Carol as children, squinting into summer sun somewhere. Her father in his Navy uniform, which he had worn briefly and had nothing much to say about. Her parents' wedding portrait.

She had that photograph. She had packed it two nights ago into the bag she was taking back to Pittsburgh, the small pile of things she had decided she wanted to keep. The photograph, a quilt her mother had made, a wooden box her grandfather had carved, the letters they'd found in the secretary desk, a coffee cup with a crack in it that she had made in ceramics class in eighth grade and which her mother had used every day as a pencil holder on her desk for the subsequent four decades.

She had not thought to look in the bottom drawer of the dresser.

She thought about that, about what she hadn't thought of. About all the bottom drawers of all the dressers. You couldn't know. You could do your best and you still couldn't know because people put things in places that didn't make sense to anyone but themselves, and the sense died with them.

Diane appeared beside her. "How are you holding up?"

"Fine," Ruth said, which was her answer to that question, which would always be her answer to that question.

"That's good," Diane said, meaning it, somehow. "It's a hard day."

"Have there been many—do a lot of people have days like this?"

"Honey," Diane said, and then didn't say anything else, and the word was enough.

A woman came in around two o'clock, older than Ruth, maybe seventy, and she moved through the house slowly, not buying anything, just looking. Ruth watched her from the hallway. She picked things up and set them down. She stood for a long time in the kitchen doorway. Then she came out and found Ruth and said, "Did you know Marian Scholl? This was her house."

"She was my mother," Ruth said.

The woman looked at her. "You're the one who went to Pittsburgh."

"I am."

"I'm Anne Gerber. I lived on this street for thirty years. We weren't close, exactly, but we knew each other the way you know people." She paused. "She was a good woman. She had a good sense of humor. I didn't know her well enough and I've thought about that."

Ruth said thank you. It was the thing you said.

"She used to bring extra tomatoes from the garden," Anne Gerber said. "She'd just leave them on the porch. No note or anything. You'd just find them."

Ruth nodded. This was true. This was her mother completely, the leaving of tomatoes without a note, the specific way she had of giving that didn't want to be witnessed.

Anne Gerber bought a pair of candlesticks for four dollars and left.

By three the traffic had slowed and by four Diane was boxing up what remained for donation, moving with practiced efficiency, and Ruth sat on the porch steps and looked at the street. A few leaves still clung to the oak at the curb—her mother had loved that tree, had once told a utility company that she would need to speak to their supervisor's supervisor's supervisor before she would allow anyone to trim it, had made three phone calls over two days and gotten what she wanted. The tree stood there. It had outlasted her. This was what trees generally did.

Ruth took the letter from her pocket and read it again.

*He was kind the way weather is kind when it finally breaks.*

She sat with it. She tried to imagine her mother at twenty-three, on a bus across the country, which was itself hard to imagine—her mother had been in Ohio when Ruth was born and was in Ohio when she died and Ruth had thought of her as essentially Ohio, as woven into its particular topography—and she tried to imagine the bus stopping in Portland and her mother stepping off into whatever Portland was in 1962 and finding David, who was kind, and being kissed in the street, and standing with her bag thinking about staying.

What would have happened if she'd stayed? The question had no useful answer but she asked it anyway, the way you do. Ruth would not have been born, presumably, or would have been someone else, would have been some other combination, a Portland Ruth, West Coast, different in ways she couldn't imagine. This was the vertigo of genealogy, the way you could trace yourself back through a series of accidents and choices and moments of fear and find that your existence hung on a bus schedule, a gray morning, a man's particular kindness in a city your mother passed through once and never saw again.

She put the letter in her pocket.

She went inside.

Diane was nearly finished. Most of the boxes were stacked by the front door for the donation pickup tomorrow morning, and the house was very quiet and very empty and smelled like old wood and something faintly floral that Ruth could not identify, probably a soap or a cleaner her mother had used, something Ruth had been smelling her whole life without knowing she was smelling it. She walked through the rooms one more time.

The kitchen. The living room, which had one armchair left in it—not sold, not wanted—and the ghost-rectangles on the walls. The small room her parents had called the study, which had her father's desk in it, which had sold, and her mother's secretary desk, which had sold, and the empty built-in shelves where her mother's books had been. Ruth had shipped the books to Pittsburgh, two boxes of them, novels mostly, some history. She hadn't known her mother read so much history. She had thought her mother read novels. She was learning things.

She stood in the study for a long time. Her father had sat at that desk every evening of her childhood, paying bills, writing checks, doing whatever he did. He had been a quiet man who died young and she did not have enough of him—not enough memories, not enough of his particular quality of silence, which had been a comfortable silence, the silence of a man who was simply present and did not need to fill space. She had looked for that quality in men ever since and had not consistently found it.

She turned off the light.

Diane handed her an envelope with the day's earnings—nine hundred and forty dollars—and they shook hands and Diane said the usual things and Ruth said yes, thank you, you were a tremendous help, and she meant it, and Diane went out to her car and drove away.

Ruth stood in the empty house.

The chairs were gone, so she sat on the floor in the living room, her back against the wall under one of the ghost-rectangles. The floorboards were the original oak, her mother had been proud of them, had refinished them twice in Ruth's lifetime. They were good boards. Whoever bought this house—it was already under contract, a young couple from Dennison, Carol had managed all of that—whoever bought this house would have good floors.

She took out her phone and then put it back.

She sat in the quiet. It was not uncomfortable. The house ticked and settled around her, doing what old houses do in the cold, and she sat with it and let it tick.

She thought about her mother on a bus. She thought about her mother kneeling in the dead leaves of the backyard, saying okay, okay, we're going to the hospital. She thought about tomatoes on a neighbor's porch, left without a note. She thought about a potato ricer in a young woman's cardboard box, bought for a dollar fifty, which was right, which was exactly right. She thought about her mother at her desk using a cracked coffee cup to hold pencils for forty years. She thought about a record playing on a Sunday morning, a young man's voice, doomed and beautiful, filling these rooms that were empty now.

She was afraid of a lot of things. She had been afraid of a lot of things for a long time and had managed anyway, which she supposed was the whole of it, which she supposed was what her mother's letter was about, even though her mother had framed it as a story about staying, because the truth was it was a story about going home, about knowing what you were going back to, about choosing the life you had out of love for it even when another life was briefly possible. She had come home, Ruth's mother. She had married Ruth's father and raised Ruth and Carol in this house with its good floors and its apple tree and its Chet Baker records and its extra tomatoes for the neighbors, and she had kept one secret about one kindness in one city she saw once, and now Ruth had it, now Ruth was keeping it too.

She got up off the floor.

She got her bag from the guest room. She checked the windows on the ground floor—locked, locked, locked—and the back door. She turned off the lights room by room, kitchen, study, living room. She stood in the front doorway for a moment, looking in, and the house looked back at her the way houses do, the way they hold the shapes of all the living you've done in them, indifferent and faithful at once.

She pulled the door closed behind her and locked it with the key Carol would come for tomorrow, and she walked down the porch steps and along the front path and she didn't look back, not because it would have hurt to look, but because she already knew what was there.
