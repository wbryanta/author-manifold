He kept his pistol loose in its holster and his eyes moving between the canopy and the trail ahead, noting the bent grass, the broken branch, the silence where birds ought to be. Regina, for her part, seemed unbothered, her ears swiveling with professional interest rather than alarm, and he trusted the horse more than he trusted himself in this country. He had been in Virginia only twice before and both times briefly, passing through on business that required him to see men he would rather not have seen and drink rum he would rather not have drunk and sleep in beds whose previous occupants had left behind company too small to see with the naked eye.

This time the business was different and the man he needed to see was D'Ortega.

He did not relish the meeting. D'Ortega was Portuguese by birth, English by ambition and Christian by convenience. He was also in debt to Jacob Vaark for a sum of some consequence, a loan made three years prior through an intermediary in Rotterdam who had since died of fever, making the arrangement both more important and more complicated to enforce. The intermediary's records—a ledger in poor condition, water stained and smelling of bilge—showed the amount clearly enough. What it did not show was an agreed schedule of repayment, because Vaark had expected to deal with the intermediary, not the man himself, and had allowed the paperwork to remain as loose as a gentleman's agreement. He was no longer in a mood for gentlemen's agreements. The farm needed a new well. The farm needed timber. The farm needed, if he was honest with himself riding through this gold fog toward a man's plantation, a future of some kind, a reason to keep building what the land so stubbornly resisted.

He had come south in autumn partly because the seas were calmer and partly because he had told himself he would not spend another winter in that cold house pretending the cold was only weather.

Rebekka would manage. Rebekka always managed. He thought of her standing at the door as he left, one hand on the frame, not waving, watching him go with those pale eyes that he had first thought were cold and later understood were simply far-seeing. She had come to him a stranger off a ship packed with women who were not quite criminals and not quite charity cases and were in fact both. He had chosen her from a list the way you choose a field you have not walked but whose dimensions have been read to you by a man who seems reliable. She had exceeded the dimensions. She had exceeded everything he had thought to want from a wife, which meant she had surprised him, which meant he was, after eleven years, still a little in awe of her and unwilling to say so, because to say it would be to admit how modest his original wanting had been.

He pushed Regina up a rise and at the top paused to look out over the river. The fog had thinned here and he could see the brown water moving with purpose, barges on it hauling what looked like casks, men on the bank watching. The smell was extraordinary—plant rot and mineral earth and underneath it something almost sweet, like overripe fruit left in the sun. Everything here grew with an urgency he found both impressive and faintly threatening. In his own country things grew with reluctance and you were grateful for each inch. Here the country seemed not to care whether you were grateful. It would grow regardless.

He came down the other side of the rise and the trail widened into a road, and the road was lined with young trees that someone had planted at equal intervals with the deliberate symmetry of a man making a statement. Beyond the trees were fields, and in the fields people moved with the slow steady pace of those who know the day will not end. He watched them without slowing Regina's trot. A man straightening to look at him. A woman who did not straighten. Children carrying something in baskets. He kept his eyes forward because to look too long was to invite complication and he had complication enough waiting at the end of this road.

The house was larger than he had expected. Three stories and brick, with a wide porch and windows that caught the returning sun and threw it back. A house that said: I have arrived. A house that said: I was not always this, but I intend to remain it. He knew the type. He had seen it in Maryland and in New York among the Dutch merchants who had rebuilt after the English came through. The house was a declaration and declarations annoyed him, perhaps because he could not quite afford one himself.

A boy took Regina and he was shown inside by a woman who spoke no English or pretended not to, and he was made to wait in a room that contained more furniture than was sensible—three chairs where one would do, a table that could seat twelve though the family was small, a portrait of a man he did not recognize done in the style of the previous century, all earnest eyes and unfortunate collar. He stood rather than sat and looked at the portrait until he heard a step behind him.

D'Ortega was smaller than his house. He was a compact man in his fifties with the kind of face that had once been handsome and had not yet entirely forgiven itself for ceasing to be. He wore good wool badly, his collar loose, his boots carrying red clay from the fields that he had either not noticed or not bothered to remove. He smiled when he saw Vaark and the smile was warm enough to be either genuine or practised, and Vaark could not yet tell which.

—Mr. Vaark, he said. I had word you were coming. I confess I did not expect you so soon.

—I wrote in April.

—So you did. The post is a rumor in this part of the country. Come in further. Sit. You will take something.

It was not a question and Vaark sat, and the woman who spoke no English brought rum that was better than the rum in the village and they sat opposite each other across the enormous table like two men negotiating a treaty, which in a sense they were.

D'Ortega spoke of the season and the crop. He spoke of the new laws and their inconvenience. He spoke of an acquaintance in Barbados whose sugar operation had made him a rich man, and his voice when he said it carried something—envy or admiration or both—that told Vaark a great deal about the direction of his thinking. He listened and drank and waited for the moment when D'Ortega would acknowledge why he was here. That moment arrived in the form of a pause, a settling back in the chair, a small adjustment of the collar.

—You have come about the matter of the loan, D'Ortega said.

—I have.

—I thought as much. And I will not insult you by pretending the sum has escaped my recollection.

—That is a relief.

—However. D'Ortega set down his cup. The word sat between them like a stone in a stream. However. The seasons have not been cooperative. You will have heard this from other men in similar positions and you may believe it or not as you choose, but it is true in my case. We had blight two seasons running. We have had trouble with the labor. A man of your intelligence will understand what I mean when I say trouble.

—I understand you to mean that men you own have declined to be owned as conveniently as you would like.

D'Ortega looked at him steadily. He was not offended or not obviously so. He seemed instead to be taking a measurement.

—You are a plain-spoken man, Mr. Vaark.

—It saves time.

—It does. He nodded slowly. I respect it. My own father was plain-spoken to the point of cruelty, but I respected him for it too. He paused. What I am proposing—what I would ask you to consider—is an arrangement in lieu of immediate payment. Something that might satisfy your investment more broadly than coin.

Vaark waited.

—I have three I could part with, D'Ortega said. Good workers. One of them, the girl, is exceptional. Strong and quick. She learns, which is either a virtue or a danger depending on how you manage it. I believe you would find her an asset.

The room was very still. Outside, a bird was making a sound that was new to Vaark—a high liquid call that dropped at the end as though asking a question. He thought about the farm. He thought about Rebekka and the work that did not diminish but only changed its shape from season to season. He thought about the women Rebekka kept losing, the ones who came and went or came and stayed but then were gone, the ones whose names he sometimes confused because he did not pay close enough attention to their faces, and he felt the small familiar shame of that.

—I did not come south to traffic in flesh, he said.

—No, said D'Ortega, and the word was not unkind. But you came south for payment and I am offering payment of a kind. The girl alone is worth—

—I know what she is worth. In your accounting, I know exactly.

D'Ortega spread his hands. He had the hands of a man who had once worked and no longer did. —I am not your enemy, Mr. Vaark.

—You are my debtor.

—I am both. He smiled again and this time it seemed more genuine, or perhaps more tired. I am asking for your patience and offering you something real in exchange for it. Take the girl. Take one other if you like. Apply it against the principal. Come back for the rest when the crop recovers.

Vaark stood and went to the window. The fields were still visible, the light lower now and golden again, not the fog-gold of the morning but a clear settling gold of late afternoon. The figures in the fields moved in it like figures in a painting, slowness made beautiful by distance. He knew better than to let distance do that work on him. He had been young once and allowed distance to do that work and it had cost him things he did not name.

He thought about the girl. He had not seen her and already he was thinking about her with a specificity that troubled him. He turned back to D'Ortega.

—Let me see who you are proposing to offer, he said.

D'Ortega rose with the ease of a man for whom this had gone better than expected.

They walked out through the back of the house into a yard where the work of the plantation made itself more visible and more audible—the sound of an axe, the smell of something boiling, a dog that appeared and was ignored by both men. D'Ortega spoke to an overseer in low tones and the overseer went off and they waited. Vaark watched the dog investigate the hem of his coat with professional interest.

Three people came across the yard. A man of middle age who walked with a slight hesitation, not quite a limp, something that suggested an old injury accommodated rather than healed. A boy of perhaps twelve or thirteen, tall for his age, with the watchful stillness of someone who had learned that stillness was a form of safety. And a girl. A girl of perhaps eight or nine, small even for that age, wearing a cloth that was too large and had been gathered at the waist with a piece of rope. She was not looking at either man. She was looking at the dog.

Vaark looked at her looking at the dog and felt something move in him that he did not have a clean name for.

D'Ortega was speaking about the man's experience, his skill with timber and livestock, his strength. Vaark listened with one part of his mind and with another part watched the girl, who had now crouched down slightly, not approaching the dog but inviting it, one hand held out at her side as though the gesture were casual, as though she were not watching from the corner of her eye to see whether it would come. The dog went to her. Of course the dog went to her.

—The girl, Vaark said.

D'Ortega stopped speaking.

—How old?

—Eight. Perhaps nine. She was brought from the coast. Her mother is here still—a good worker, the mother, reliable—

—Is the mother among the three you proposed to part with?

—No. D'Ortega's voice had shifted slightly, become more careful. No, the mother is—I have other plans for the mother.

—And the girl is of an age to be separated from her.

—As I said, she is eight. Perhaps nine.

Vaark looked at the girl, who had now allowed the dog to press its nose against her palm and was receiving this with a gravity that struck him as remarkable in a child. She was not smiling. She was giving the dog her full attention as though it were a serious matter, which perhaps to both of them it was.

He thought about Rebekka. He thought about the work of the farm. He thought about the note he had signed for the horse and the well that needed digging and the timber and the future that resisted his efforts to build it. He was not a man who traded in people. He had told himself this many times and it was true. He was not that kind of man. But there were kinds of man you were and kinds of man you became and the distance between the two was sometimes only a single afternoon.

—Apply her against the interest, he said. The first year's interest only. I will return in spring for the remainder of what you owe. In coin.

D'Ortega nodded. —Done. And done fairly, I think.

—I did not ask for your opinion of the fairness.

—No. D'Ortega extended his hand and Vaark shook it because not to shake it would be theater and he had no patience for theater. The hand was dry and firm and gave away nothing.

He went back to the room with the portrait to collect his satchel and stood for a moment alone before the painting of the unknown man with the earnest eyes, wondering who had thought it worth preserving this face, and for whom. The bird outside the window was still asking its question. He still had no answer for it.

When he came back into the yard the three were still standing where they had been and the boy was now watching him with an expression he could not read, though he tried. The man with the old injury was looking at the ground. The girl had released the dog and the dog had gone on with its business and the girl was standing very straight, which was the kind of straightness that costs something.

He did not speak to them. He did not know what to say that would not be worse than silence. He went to where the boy had taken Regina and found her fed and watered and more content than he deserved, and he mounted her and turned her toward the tree-lined road and the setting sun, which was doing something violent and gorgeous to the sky above the river.

He would need to find lodging for the night. The child could not travel far today and neither could he. There was a house he had been told of, a widow who kept rooms, back in the direction of the village. He would go there and in the morning he would make arrangements for the child to be brought to him and they would begin the journey north. He worked this out in his mind as Regina moved beneath him with her good reliable stride and he did not look back at the house or the yard or the three figures who were no longer three.

He thought about what Rebekka would say. He thought about Lina, who was nobody's fool and would understand immediately. He thought about the child's face, which he was already losing in the way you lose a face you have not looked at long enough. He tried to bring it back and could not recover the exact arrangement of the features, only the quality of the attention she had given the dog, the straightness that cost something, the rope gathering the cloth at her waist.

The road darkened under the trees and he slowed Regina without meaning to. Above him the live oaks made a sound that was almost nothing, a breath, a rumor of wind in the last leaves that autumn had not yet taken. He had been in this country only a day and already it felt like longer. Everything here asked something of you. The soil asked, the heat asked, the enormous impossible growth asked. What it asked was whether you intended to stay and whether you could bear what staying required.

He did not yet know the answer. He urged Regina forward and the trees closed behind him and the house with its statement-making brick and its portrait of a forgotten man disappeared behind a bend and he was alone on the Lenape trail again with the pistol loose at his hip and the sky above the tree line going from violent to quiet in the way of autumn skies, the colors draining out of them slowly, as though reluctant to give up the day.

He would tell himself, later, that he had done the best available thing within a bad set of choices. He had told himself this before about other decisions and sometimes it turned out to be true. The farm did need help. Rebekka did need company of a kind that was not the company of women who came and went and left nothing behind but their absence. The child would be fed and clothed and not worked past her strength, or he intended that she would not be. He intended many things. He was a man of intentions.

The village lights appeared between the trees and he smelled cook fires and heard dogs and a child somewhere crying with the specific exhaustion of the end of a day. He followed his nose to the widow's house and paid for the room and paid extra for something cold to drink and sat in the small room under the low ceiling and looked at the note he had signed for the horse and thought: this is what a life is. You sign notes. You ride through fog. You make decisions in the yards of men you do not respect, looking at children who are watching dogs, and you tell yourself you are not the kind of man you are becoming, and you ride on, and the sky goes quiet, and somewhere behind you a bird asks its question into the dark and does not wait for an answer.

In the morning he would go back for her.

He set the note on the table and lay down on the narrow bed in his clothes and listened to the sounds of the village settling into night and thought about the farm, the cold house, the well that needed digging, Rebekka's far-seeing eyes. He thought about the girl's straightness and wondered whether she still had it or whether the night had taken it from her. He thought that she was eight. Perhaps nine. He thought that her mother was there still, reliable, and that D'Ortega had other plans for the mother. He thought about what other plans meant in the mouth of such a man. He thought about the mother and then he stopped thinking about the mother because there was nowhere to go with it that didn't lead somewhere he couldn't afford to be and still sleep.

He slept. Outside, the Atlantic fog was gathering itself again in the bay, preparing for another morning of gold blindness, and the sloopmen were anchored or not, and the river moved the way rivers do, indifferent and continuous and carrying everything down to the sea.
