He woke to her hand on his chest and lay there listening to his own breathing slow in the dark. The fire had burned to coals and their cold orange light moved in the draft from the window across the low ceiling like something alive and fugitive. He sat with his legs over the side of the bed and his hands on his knees.

You was hollerin again, she said.

I know it.

She did not say anything more. He could hear her breathing behind him and then the small shift of weight as she moved in the bed. Through the window the sky held a thin and grudging light at its lowest edge and he thought he could make out the shapes of the treeline but perhaps he was only remembering them from the night before. From some farther dark a dog was barking in that hollow persistent way that meant nothing and would not stop.

He rose and dressed in the cold and went out.

The yard was packed dirt that held the nightchill and he crossed it to the woodpile and stood there a moment. The sky to the east was lighter now and he could see his breath and he could see the mule in the pen watching him with its enormous and patient eyes. He split kindling and carried it inside and built the fire up and stood over it with his hands out.

She came in from the other room wrapped in the old quilt she'd had since before they were married and she put the coffee on and stood beside him.

How long till the baby, he said.

She said she did not know exactly. Some weeks.

He nodded. He was watching the fire.

Are you going to tell me what it was about, she said.

He shook his head.

You never do.

No.

She went to the window and looked out. The light was coming up gray and cold over the fields and the fence posts stood in the mist at intervals like something recently abandoned.

Harmon Fields came by yesterday, she said.

He looked at her back.

What did he want.

He said they'd found the Greer boy.

He stood very still. Where, he said.

Down in the Blackwater. Snagged up on a deadfall below the mill.

He did not say anything.

She turned from the window and looked at him. He was a man of middle years, not tall, with the kind of face that takes whatever weather comes to it and keeps a record. His eyes were the pale gray of creek water over slate.

That's the third one, she said.

I know how many it is.

Well.

He took his coffee and went and sat at the table. She stood with her back to the window and her hands folded over her belly under the quilt and watched him.

Are you going to go out there, she said.

Somebody has to.

She said nothing.

He drank the coffee and then he rose and took down his coat from the peg by the door.

Don't, she said.

He looked at her.

Don't tell me not to worry, she said. I know you were going to say it.

He put the coat on. I wasn't going to say anything, he said.

He walked out into the morning.

THE FIELDS WERE gray and spare and the frost still lay in the low places unthawed. He walked the road along the creek with his hands in his coat pockets and the dog from somewhere came and fell in beside him without invitation. It was a yellow dog of no particular kind and it wanted nothing from him but the company. He let it come.

The mill was a mile down the creek and by the time he reached it the sun had cleared the ridge to the east but gave no warmth and sat small and pale in a sky the color of old tin. Harmon Fields was there already with two other men he knew by sight but not well. They stood on the bank above where the Greer boy was caught in the tangle of a fallen sycamore and they were not talking. When he came up Harmon looked at him and back at the water.

Morning, Harmon said.

He stood beside them and looked down. The boy was face down in the shallow water with his arms out from his sides and the current moved his hair gently as if arranging it. He was perhaps fourteen years of age. He had the look of something that had come a long way from wherever it had started.

His daddy know? he said.

Harmon nodded. Sent word this morning.

He looked at the other two men and they looked at the ground and at the water and at the mill and at nothing in particular.

We ought to get him out, he said.

Nobody moved. Then one of the other men, the one called Puckett, stepped down the bank and waded in to the knee and got hold of the boy's collar and they pulled him up onto the bank and laid him on the frost-killed grass. They stood around him. His face was white and smooth and had the terrible neutrality of the very young dead. Someone had put marks on him but he did not look at those long.

He looked up the creek and down it. The water ran black and cold between its banks and the sycamores stood white along it and the whole of the world in that moment had a quality of suspension as if it were waiting for something to be said or done that no one there knew how to say or do.

Same as the others, Puckett said.

He did not answer.

He crouched down and looked at the boy's hands. Then he stood and looked at Harmon.

Who found him, he said.

Harmon nodded at the other man whose name he did not know. This one. Early this morning.

The man looked at him with the flat and slightly aggrieved expression of someone who has already told this story more times than he found interesting.

I was running my lines, the man said. Just at first light. Seen something in the deadfall and come over to look.

You didn't see anything else, he said.

The man shook his head.

Didn't hear anything in the night.

No.

He looked at the water again. He thought about the three boys and what they had in common beside the dying. They were all of an age. They all came from the east side of the creek. He did not know what else yet but he believed there was more and that it would come to him or be brought to him in time, in the way these things came.

Harmon said, You think it's somebody we know.

It generally is, he said.

Harmon looked away.

I want to look around some, he said. Up the creek.

You want company, Harmon said.

He shook his head.

He left them there with the boy and walked up the creek in the cold morning. The yellow dog went with him. They walked for some time through the pale sycamore wood with the creek talking to itself on their left. He was not looking for anything specific but was letting himself be available to whatever there was to be found, a practice he had fallen into years ago and which he could not have described to anyone's satisfaction, not even his own. His wife said it was like watching a man sleepwalk. He thought that was not entirely wrong.

He came to a place where the bank had been broken down and the grass pushed flat and he stopped and looked at it for a long time. The dog sniffed along the perimeter of the broken ground and then sat and looked up at him. There had been several people here and they had stood around a fire the remains of which were cold and gray in the center of the scuffed earth. The ashes had not been scattered, which was a kind of arrogance, he thought, or perhaps only indifference. He crouched over them and held his hand above and felt no heat. He sifted through the edge of them with a stick and found a piece of bone that was not large and might have been animal but he put it in his coat pocket.

He walked on.

There was a place further up where someone had been recently. An old flatboat landing that had not been used in some years, the planking rotted and the mooring post standing up out of the water with nothing tied to it. There were tracks in the mud at the edge of the water and he crouched over them. Three sets, or perhaps four. Men's boots of various sizes. They went down to the water and some of them came back and some of them did not and he could not tell the order in which these things had happened.

He stood and looked across the creek. The other bank was identical in its appearance. Gray trees, gray water, gray sky. Whatever happened here happened in the dark, he thought. In the absolute blank of it.

He turned and walked back downstream.

BY MIDMORNING he had made his report to the county man who drove out from the seat in a black automobile that sat too low for the road and had to be pushed out of a washout twice. The county man was named Odom and he was not a bad man but he had a politician's instinct for the containable and he looked at everything the way a man looks at a bill he is deciding whether to pay.

You say there's no witnesses, Odom said.

Not that I've found.

Odom stood with his hands in his overcoat pockets and looked at the spot on the bank where the Greer boy had lain. They'd taken the body away by then and what remained was only a flattening in the frost-killed grass and a darkness in the soil.

These boys, Odom said. They were not boys that were careful about where they went and who with.

He looked at Odom.

I don't know that that's a distinction that serves us here, he said.

Odom looked at him sideways. I'm not making a distinction, he said. I'm talking about method. I'm saying there's somebody who knew them, or knew how to find them.

He said nothing.

Odom turned up his collar against the cold. The county man was perhaps fifty, with the permanently wind-burned look of a man who had spent years outdoors doing things he had not enjoyed. He had an honesty that appeared sometimes without warning through the politician's surface and you could not quite trust it because you did not know how long it would stay.

What do you know about the boy's people, Odom said.

The father is Clarence Greer. He's got the sawmill lease on the Canby tract. The mother died some years back.

Brothers? Sisters?

Two brothers, both older. One's gone to Memphis, I believe. The other I don't know.

He had a friend, he said. Boy named Lester Motes. Ran with him some.

Odom wrote in a small notebook. His handwriting was large and uneven like a schoolboy's.

I'll want to talk to this Motes, he said.

The boy's not right in the head, he said. I don't mean simple. I mean he's not easy to talk to. He's scared of something most of the time and you can never get at what it is.

Odom closed the notebook. Well, he said. I need to talk to him anyway.

They walked back up the bank to where the car sat on the road. Odom's driver was leaning against it smoking and looking at the creek without interest.

You'll keep on this, Odom said. It was not quite a question.

Yes.

Odom nodded and got in the car. The driver flicked his cigarette into the road and got in after him. The car started with some difficulty and backed slowly up the road.

He watched it go.

THE MOTES BOY lived with an uncle in a house at the end of a dirt road that had not been graveled in any living memory and wound back through second-growth timber for nearly a mile before it reached the house. The house was not much. It had been built without any architectural intention beyond the purely functional and even in that regard had fallen somewhat short of its ambitions. Two rooms and a lean-to kitchen. A porch that leaned away from the house as if considering separation. In the yard was the machinery of several abandoned projects, things rusted and returning slowly to the ground.

He knocked.

The uncle came to the door. He was a man of perhaps sixty with a drinker's ruined face and the deliberate movements of someone who has made a long peace with his own diminishment. He looked at him without expression.

I need to speak with Lester, he said.

The uncle turned and went back into the house. He stood on the porch and waited. After some time the Motes boy appeared in the doorway. He was seventeen and slight with colorless hair and eyes that moved constantly and rested nowhere long. He looked at him and looked away and looked at him again.

You heard about Calvin Greer, he said.

The boy said nothing but something happened in his face.

He was found this morning. In the creek below the mill.

The boy put his hand on the door frame. His knuckles were white.

You two ran together, he said.

Some, the boy said. His voice was thin and without resonance, the voice of someone speaking from a long way off inside himself.

I need you to tell me about the last time you saw him.

The boy looked at the yard, at the rusted machinery. The trees stood at the edge of the yard and the wind went through them.

We was up the creek, the boy said. Few nights back.

How many nights.

The boy thought. Three. Three nights back.

What were you doing up there.

The boy was quiet.

Lester.

We was supposed to meet somebody, the boy said.

He waited.

Calvin knew some men. I don't know where he met them. He said they had money and they were looking for boys to help them move some things.

What things.

I don't know what things. Calvin didn't say. He said it was easy money.

He looked at the boy steadily. How many men.

The boy said he thought three. He had only seen two of them himself.

Tell me about the two you saw.

The boy described them badly and in fragments. One was large and wore a beard and had the hands of someone who worked with them. The other was younger and had something wrong with one eye. He could not say what exactly. Just that it was wrong.

Did you know either of them.

No.

Did Calvin know them from around here.

The boy shook his head. He said they come through. He said they were going west.

He thought about that.

What happened when you met them, he said.

The boy's hand tightened on the door frame.

I didn't go, he said.

Why not.

The boy looked at him for the first time directly and in the boy's eyes was something clear and certain among all the fear, a sharp particular knowledge.

Because I was scared, he said.

Scared of what.

The boy looked away again. I don't know exactly, he said. Something about the big one. The bearded one. He looked at me and I thought something I don't know how to say.

Try.

The boy was quiet for a long time. The wind moved in the trees.

I thought, he said. I thought that whatever it was I would be doing for him it wouldn't be what he told me it was. I thought it would be something else and at the end of it I wouldn't be going home.

He stood there on the leaning porch and looked at the Motes boy and the Motes boy looked at the trees.

You were right, he said.

The boy's jaw moved. He nodded once, a single fractional movement, as if confirming something he had both known and refused to know since the morning.

Do you know which way they went, he said. After.

The boy shook his head. Up the creek, he thought. That was all he knew. Calvin had gone up the creek to meet them and that was the last he'd seen of him and he had not said anything to anyone for three days because he did not know how to explain the being scared and the going home and the Calvin not going home, as if the fear were an accusation, as if he had purchased his own continuation at a price he had not agreed to but accepted anyway.

He thanked the boy and went back down the long dirt road. The yellow dog had waited for him at the end of it and fell in beside him again on the county road as if it had never left.

IN THE AFTERNOON he drove his truck out along the river road to where the old Canby ferry had been, fifteen years defunct, the cable still strung across the water and the barge sitting in the mud on the far side with the river growing around it. He parked and walked down to the bank. The river here was wide and flat and brown and moved with a slow and total authority as if it had decided something long ago and saw no reason to revisit it.

He thought about the three boys. He thought about the camp above the mill and the tracks at the landing. He thought about three men moving west, which was what men with something to put behind them did. He thought about the Motes boy's eyes and the clear thing in them.

Something about the big one.

He sat on a rock at the water's edge and took the piece of bone from his pocket and held it in his palm. Small. Smooth. He could not have said with any certainty that it was human. He put it back.

The river moved.

His wife would be at home. She would be sitting at the window in the last of the afternoon light with her hands in her lap and the weight of the child riding in her and she would be looking out at the fields going colorless in the dusk and she would be thinking about him out here and what he was doing and whether it would end the way the last three had ended, with something found in the water, with a thing that had been a person now needing to be carried home.

He had been at this work for eleven years. He had done it in this county and the two counties adjacent and in one case in a state across the river and he had never lost the ability to be brought up short by what one person would do to another. He was not sure if this was a virtue or a deficiency. He suspected it was both.

He rose from the rock.

He would go home and eat and he would sit with his wife in the evening and he would not talk about the Greer boy or the other two or the bearded man with the hands of a worker or the road that ran west along the river. He would sit with her and look at the fire and they would speak of ordinary things, the baby, the state of the road, the price of corn, all the small ordinary beautiful things that constituted a life and that seemed to require, for their continuation, that someone go out in the cold mornings and look at what was in the water.

He walked back to the truck. The dog got in when he opened the door and sat on the seat and looked out the windshield at the road. He sat beside it and started the engine and they sat there for a moment while it warmed. The light was nearly gone now. Along the river the trees stood black against a sky still holding the last of the color, a thin burning at the horizon that neither promised nor threatened anything but simply was, the day's final notation in a ledger that was kept somewhere in terms he could not fully read but felt compelled nonetheless to contribute to.

He put the truck in gear and turned for home.

Three men moving west along the river.

He thought: they would stop again. They always stopped again. The river was long and the country on either side of it was vast and gave no accounting of itself and there were boys in it everywhere who were poor and uncareful and who would follow a man that had money and a destination, follow him right up to the moment that revealed itself as a last moment, and often not even then. Often even then they would be unable to believe in the nature of the thing they had come to. He had seen this. He had seen what that looked like.

He drove through the dark with the dog beside him.

His wife had a lamp burning in the window.
