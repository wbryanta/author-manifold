Anil crouched at the edge of the grave and began to document what remained. She was thirty-three years old, had spent the last six years working for human rights organizations in various parts of the world, and she had learned that the body keeps its own chronology, separate from the stories the living tell about it. The bones do not lie in the way that governments lie, in the way that army communiqués lie, in the way that the silence of frightened neighbors lies. The bones simply are. They record what happened to them without interpretation, without the self-protective silence of witnesses.

Her notebook was open across her knee. She made small careful drawings, annotated with measurements, the kind of work that a distant courtroom might one day require. The sun had moved to the center of the sky and the light was harsh and without shadow and in such light everything looked equally ordinary, equally without meaning, a collection of calcium shapes in the red earth. That was something you learned too. That the terrible thing, once exposed, once brought into the flat light of documentation, began to look ordinary. This was both a mercy and a horror and Anil had not yet decided which quality was stronger.

She had come to Guatemala two years earlier, newly hired by a Geneva-based organization that sent forensic teams to conflict zones and post-conflict zones and zones where the question of whether the conflict had ended remained genuinely unanswered. She had trained in London and then in the United States, learning the careful grammar of bones, their syntax of fracture and healing and final dissolution. She had a gift for it, her teachers said. A gift for the dead. She was not sure this was meant as a compliment. But she accepted it as a description.

The team worked through the afternoon. There were five of them. Chiti, the Guatemalan anthropologist who led the local excavations, worked with a small brush near the skulls. Two younger assistants moved the sieved earth in careful loads. A photographer named Martín circled them constantly, kneeling, rising, his camera the only restless thing at the site. And Anil, who had her particular specialty: she could read the narrative of a skeleton the way a good reader reads a paragraph, moving through it quickly at first and then returning to the difficult passages with renewed attention.

Chiti looked up at her.

I think this one is younger than we said, he told her.

She moved across to look. He had exposed more of the second skeleton during the afternoon. The pelvis, now visible, confirmed a younger age than the preliminary assessment. Perhaps eighteen. Perhaps less.

They worked for another hour without speaking. The woman who had sat in the grave had gone. Anil looked up once and the land beyond the site was empty, a long flat scrub broken by occasional trees and in the far distance the smudge of a village. The sky was beginning to lose its hardness at the edges.

She thought about the woman's shoulder. The specific angle of it. She had trained herself over years to observe rather than to feel, to translate immediate emotional response into technical attention, but there were moments when something arrived through all that careful training and moved directly into her. The shoulder had been one of those moments. There was a quality to it that she associated with exhaustion so deep it had passed beyond grief into something more architectural. As though the woman's body had been permanently altered by what had happened to her, had taken on a new structure around the loss, the way a tree grows around a wound.

That evening Chiti cooked in the small house they were using as a base. He made a fish stew with plantain and they ate it outside because the evening had cooled and there were no insects for once. Martín showed them photographs on his laptop, the day's documentation, and they looked at the images together in the blue light of the screen, pointing at particular details, noting what needed further work in the morning. This was the professional part of the evening. After Martín closed the laptop they sat for a while and drank coffee and sometimes someone spoke and sometimes they didn't.

She thought about going home. She thought about this more often now than she had in the earlier years, and she was uncertain what the thought meant, whether it was a sign of something wearing down in her or simply a consequence of being abroad for so long. Home was Sri Lanka. She had left it at eighteen for university in England and had returned only for visits, for the holidays and the obligation-visits that she had gradually reduced as the years passed and as the country deepened into its own violence. Her parents were dead. Her brother Nirantha was still there, in Colombo, working in some capacity that changed with the political weather. She was not sure exactly what he did now. Their communication had become the kind of communication that exists between people who have remained fond of one another while growing entirely apart.

She had been offered a position with a Geneva organization that was sending a team to Sri Lanka. It was a significant position, the kind of work she had been moving toward for years. She would be returning to her own country as a professional foreigner, using the credentials of international forensic science to examine what the government preferred to leave unexamined. It was the kind of work that made governments nervous and made colleagues respectful and made the people who had lost their children grateful in the complicated way that people are grateful when someone is willing to look directly at the worst thing.

She had not yet decided whether to accept the position. She had been not-deciding for three weeks, which was itself a kind of decision.

Chiti said something to Martín in Spanish and they both laughed, a private reference to some earlier conversation. Anil smiled without knowing what she was smiling at. The stars above the scrubland were very bright and very numerous in the way they only were in places far from cities, in places that had been abandoned by the ordinary flow of commerce and habitation, the dark places of the world that were dark for political reasons, that had been emptied by violence and by the slow hemorrhage of people moving away from violence toward anywhere that might be safer.

She went inside and wrote in her notebook for an hour. Not the professional documentation but the other notebook, the one she kept in parallel. She wrote about the woman's shoulder. She wrote about the petal she had found that morning in the mud at the site's edge, a small yellow petal, already decomposing, that had fallen from a flower she could not identify. She wrote about the photograph Martín had shown them of the second skeleton, the young one, the one that was perhaps eighteen, perhaps younger, and the way its hands had been positioned behind where the back had been, and what that positioning indicated about what had happened in the final moments.

The notebook was not for any court. It was not documentation. She had kept such notebooks since she was a student and she had come to think of them as the other record, the one that held the things that did not fit into the professional record, the texture and weight of the work, the specific quality of light at a particular excavation in a particular country at a particular hour. If the professional notebooks were about what could be proved, the private notebooks were about what could only be witnessed.

She closed the notebook and lay on her cot and listened to the night sounds. Somewhere in the darkness outside an animal moved through dry grass. She thought about Sri Lanka.

What she knew about the situation there she had assembled from a distance, from news reports and from the accounts of people she had encountered at conferences and at the margins of other investigations. The shape of it was familiar in its broad strokes—a government, an insurgency in the south, a separatist movement in the north, and around all three the civilian population, which was the population that always suffered most precisely because it was caught between parties each of whom demanded its complete allegiance. The government demanded loyalty and punished those it suspected of sympathy with either the insurgents or the separatists. The insurgents demanded support and punished collaboration with the government. The separatists in the north had their own particular history and their own particular grievances, the long accumulation of cultural and political dispossession that had finally curdled into armed struggle. Between them they had created a country in which a person could be killed for almost anything or nothing.

She had grown up in a Colombo that had been, at least in her memory, a Colombo at peace. Or at a kind of peace. The violence had been present but not yet fully arrived, the way a storm is present in the pressure of the air before it breaks. She had left before it broke. She was aware that this made her both an insider and an outsider, possessed of the emotional claim of origin while lacking the experiential claim of having stayed.

She thought this might be, in part, why she had not yet decided about the position.

In the morning she called the Geneva office from the satellite phone and told them she would accept.

---

She left Guatemala three weeks later, after the team had completed the current phase of the excavation. Chiti drove her to the airport in the four-wheel drive that had carried them across the rough roads of the highlands every morning for months. They drove mostly in silence, which was comfortable, the silence of people who had worked closely together and had arrived at an understanding that did not require maintenance. At the airport he embraced her and said something she didn't quite catch, something in Spanish that she thought was about safe return but might have been about something else entirely.

The flight to Geneva took many hours with a stop in Miami. She sat by the window and watched the Gulf below her and thought about documentation. She thought about what it meant to bring evidence from a place where evidence was dangerous to a place where evidence could be presented safely, to remove the record of what had happened from the jurisdiction of those who had done it to the jurisdiction of those who might judge it. There was something in this movement, this physical transport, that seemed to her essential. The bones themselves could not always be moved. But the knowledge of the bones could be moved. This was the work. She was, in a way, a courier. She carried the testimony of the dead across borders.

In Geneva she spent two weeks in briefings. She was given files on the Sri Lankan situation, thick with reports and photographs and the kind of careful, qualified language that international organizations used to describe atrocities, always careful to appear balanced, always careful to attribute to multiple parties, always careful not to be the kind of document that could be thrown back at the organization in a diplomatic complaint. She read the files carefully and annotated them in her small precise handwriting. She met the other members of the team. There was a pathologist from South Africa named Blum who had done work in Rwanda and Bosnia. There was a young British archaeologist, recently certified, on her first major field assignment. There was a documentation specialist named Perera who was himself Sri Lankan, who had left the country fifteen years earlier and who spoke about it with the careful neutrality of someone who has learned that strong feeling is a professional liability.

She and Perera ate lunch together on their second day. He was a slight man, perhaps fifty, with a precise quality to his movements, everything economical, nothing wasted. He had been born in Jaffna, in the north, and had left as a young man, before the worst of it. He did not speak about his family there in any detail. She did not ask. They spoke instead about the work, about what they expected and what they had been told to expect, about the specific legal framework under which the team would be operating, about the government ministers who had authorized the investigation with what the briefing notes described as reluctant cooperation.

The reluctant cooperation of governments was something she had become expert in reading. She had seen it in several countries now, the specific combination of official welcome and unofficial obstruction that characterized a government that had agreed to an investigation while retaining every intention of limiting what the investigation could find. The agreement itself was a kind of performance, aimed at international audiences, at the bodies and governments that provided financial support and diplomatic recognition. The obstruction was the reality beneath the performance. The work of the forensic team was to move through the performance toward the reality with enough professional precision that the findings could not easily be challenged. It was delicate work. It required a particular temperament, the ability to remain formally polite while being internally relentless.

Anil thought she had that temperament. She was not certain it was a quality to be proud of.

---

She flew to Colombo on a Tuesday morning, arriving in the late afternoon. The descent through clouds revealed the island below her, greener than she remembered or perhaps she had simply forgotten, the green of a tropical coastline after rain, the Indian Ocean visible beyond it as a flat brightness. She felt something that she did not immediately identify and then identified as a kind of physical recognition, as though her body knew the place before her mind had finished processing what she was looking at.

The airport had been renovated since her last visit. There were new signs, new shops, a general brightness that was the brightness of recent construction, surfaces that had not yet been weathered into their permanent state. The immigration officer looked at her British travel document and then at her Sri Lankan identification and then at her without particular expression and stamped both and said nothing.

Outside the terminal the heat arrived immediately, the particular quality of Colombo heat, damp and total, the kind of heat that is inseparable from smell, from the smell of diesel and flowers and the particular sweetness of something decaying in warm air. She stood on the pavement with her two cases and breathed it in.

A man from the Geneva organization's local liaison office was waiting for her. His name was Gamini. He was younger than she expected, perhaps twenty-eight, and he drove with the aggressive confidence of someone who had grown up navigating Colombo traffic, which was the traffic of a city that had been planned for a much smaller number of vehicles and had improvised everything since. He spoke good English, formal in the way that Sri Lankan English was often formal, with an occasional phrase that was entirely contemporary and that sat oddly inside the formality like a modern object in an old room.

He told her about the current situation as they drove. There had been a bombing in the east the previous week. Seven dead, the identity of the perpetrators disputed. The government had responded with a sweep in two coastal villages, detentions, the usual sequence. In the south the insurgent organization had been weakened significantly in the past year but had not been destroyed, and there were people within the government who believed, or claimed to believe, that its remnants were regrouping. This belief, or claimed belief, was the justification for continued operations that those not in the government described differently.

She listened and watched the city pass. The streets were full. There were soldiers at certain intersections, young men with weapons standing in the particular posture of soldiers who are standing guard at a location where nothing is currently happening but where something could. She had seen that posture in many countries. It was the posture of enforced alertness, of being required to maintain a state of readiness that the body and the mind resist because they are designed for intervals of rest.

They passed a temple where flowers had been placed along the walls. They passed a school where children in white uniforms were leaving through a gate. They passed a billboard advertising a mobile phone service, a family smiling against a bright blue sky. Ordinary life, moving around and beneath and alongside the other thing, the violence, in the way that water moves around stones without ceasing to be water.

The team would be working under the oversight of a government archaeologist named Sarath Diyasena, Gamini told her. Dr. Diyasena was a respected figure, had done significant work at several historical sites in the country, was known to be professional and careful. He was also, Gamini said, choosing his words with a precision that she noted, a person with complicated relationships with various aspects of the current government.

She asked what that meant.

Gamini was quiet for a moment. He said that Dr. Diyasena had brothers in positions of some importance. That this created a situation.

She understood. She had been in situations created by the existence of brothers in positions of importance. She had learned that such situations required patience and a certain strategic blindness, the willingness to work carefully within the visible framework while remaining acutely aware of the invisible one.

The guesthouse where they had arranged for her to stay was in a neighborhood she half-recognized. She had not grown up in this part of the city but she had been here often as a child, she thought, she had a memory of the particular configuration of the street and then was not sure whether she was remembering or constructing, doing the thing the mind does when it wants to belong to a place. She got out of the car and stood in front of the guesthouse and looked at the street and breathed again. The air was cooler now, the day descending toward evening. From somewhere nearby she heard the sound of crows, the specific quarrelsome crows of Sri Lankan cities, loud and unimpressed.

Her room was on the second floor, small and clean, with a window that looked out over a garden where a frangipani tree was flowering. She unpacked her two cases with the efficiency of someone who had been unpacking in small rooms in various countries for years, putting each thing in its place with the certainty of habit. She put her professional notebooks on the desk and her private notebook beside them. She put the files from Geneva in the order in which she intended to work through them. She put her toothbrush in the bathroom where the mirror was slightly mottled at one corner, an old mirror, the silver beginning to separate from the glass.

She looked at her face in the mottled mirror. She was thirty-three years old and had spent six years working with the dead in the most difficult places available and she was back in the country where she had been born and she did not know yet what that would mean, what version of herself it would require, what new or old self would have to be located and employed in order to do the work.

She thought about the woman sitting in the grave in Guatemala. The shoulder. The specific angle of grief that had no word for it in any language she knew.

She thought about what Chiti had told her once, late in an evening, about why he did the work. He had said that someone had to be willing to be the last witness, the final one, the one who looked at what remained and said: this happened. This was a person. This person was killed in this way and the record of it will not be allowed to disappear into the silence that those who did it are depending on. He had said this quietly and without drama, in the matter-of-fact way he said most things, and it had seemed to her then and still seemed to her now to be the most complete description of the work she had heard.

She went back to the window and looked at the frangipani tree in the failing light. The flowers were white with a yellow center, the kind of flower that is found around temples and at funerals, that is threaded into garlands for the dead, that is placed on the bare earth above a grave. Its scent came through the window, heavy and sweet, the kind of scent that was everywhere on this island and that she had apparently carried in some part of her memory for all the years she had been away, because smelling it now she felt, for a moment, entirely located. Not at home—she was not sure what home meant and was not sure it meant anything precise—but located. Present in a specific place on the earth, with a specific history behind her and a specific task ahead, and the evening coming down over the city, and the crows still quarreling somewhere out of sight, and somewhere across the island the bones waiting in the earth with their particular and irrefutable testimony.

She turned from the window and opened the first file.
