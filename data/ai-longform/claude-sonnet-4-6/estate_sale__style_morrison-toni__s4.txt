The morning glory had come back without permission, the way it always did, threading itself through the porch rail and up along the gutter where her mother had never thought to stop it. Cora stood at the end of the driveway and looked at the house and the house looked back at her, and neither one of them said anything for a long time. September, and the light already going the color of old brass, and strangers' cars parked along Birch Street where cars had never been parked before, and inside, a woman named Deborah Settle from the estate sale company was arranging price stickers with the calm efficiency of someone who had never known any of these objects to be anything other than objects.

Cora had driven up from Columbus the night before and slept at the Holiday Inn on Route 30 rather than in the house, which was a decision she did not examine too carefully. She was fifty-three years old and her mother had been dead for six weeks and the grief was not the kind that announced itself cleanly. It was the kind that lived inside practical tasks. That hid itself in the question of what to do with a 1974 Kenmore dryer, still functional. That folded itself small and tight inside the matter of the china cabinet her mother had carried piece by piece from a store in Zanesville thirty years ago, wrapping each glass door panel in newspaper during the move, talking to each one.

The sale opened at nine.

By nine-fifteen a man in a Cardinals cap was carrying the wingback chair through the front door.

Cora watched him from where she stood near the fireplace, holding a cup of coffee that had gone cold, watching him angle the chair's wide back through the frame of the doorway with the practiced ease of a man who had taken many things out of many houses. She had sat in that chair. She had sat in it sick and she had sat in it reading and she had sat in it waiting for her mother to come home from the second shift at the mill before the mill closed and after the mill closed she had sat in it watching her mother figure out what to do. The chair was priced at forty dollars. The man in the Cardinals cap had not hesitated.

She should go outside. Deborah had suggested gently, with the professional kindness of someone trained to handle the living as well as the dead, that some families found it easier to not be present during the sale. She had framed it carefully, the way you might explain to a child why they shouldn't watch a certain thing. Cora had said, I'll be fine. And she had meant it when she said it, which told her something about the distance she still had to travel to understand what was happening to her.

Her mother's name was Vivienne. Vivienne Grace Peoples, née Carter, and she had lived in this house for thirty-one years and she had died in it, in the bedroom at the back where the window looked out at the neighbor's pine tree, and Cora had been with her when she died and that was something Cora kept separate from everything else, kept it away from the estate sale and the cold coffee and the man with the Cardinals cap, kept it in a pocket of herself she did not open in front of strangers.

A woman was examining the contents of the hall closet. Taking things out one at a time and holding them up and putting them back. Cora watched her pick up the wooden box her mother had kept on the top shelf. It was a small box, cedar, with a brass clasp, and it held nothing that Cora knew of. Her mother had never said what was in it. Cora had asked as a child and been told it was private, which had satisfied her in the way that clear boundaries sometimes do satisfy children, giving the shape of a thing even if not its content. She had taken the box down after her mother died and opened it and found three things: a man's ring with no stone in the setting, a photograph of two women she did not recognize standing in front of a car she did not recognize, and a folded piece of paper that said only the word *careful* in her mother's handwriting. Cora had put all three things back and put the box in the closet and priced it at five dollars.

She did not know why she had priced it at five dollars. She did not know why she had not taken it with her to the Holiday Inn. She understood that she was doing things whose reasons she would only find out later, if at all, and that this was part of grief's particular instruction.

The woman with the cedar box turned it over in her hands. She was perhaps sixty, white-haired, wearing earrings that caught the light, and her face had the look of a person who appreciated the craft of small things. She opened the clasp. She looked inside. She looked up and found Cora's eyes across the room with the directness of someone who understood that the woman standing by the fireplace was the woman the house had belonged to, or belonged to someone who had belonged to the woman whose house this was, and she closed the box and set it back on the shelf and moved on to other things, and Cora did not know if she was grateful or not.

Outside a screen door banged. Her mother's screen door, which had a particular voice, a particular complaint that was not like any other screen door's complaint in the world, and Cora felt it in her teeth.

By ten o'clock the dining room table was gone.

She had not seen it go. She had been in the kitchen when it went, standing at the window that looked onto the backyard where the garden had gone ragged and tall, the tomato stakes leaning at angles, the last tomatoes going soft on the vine because nobody had picked them. The table had been in the dining room since before Cora was born. Her mother had told the story of buying it from a widow woman in Steubenville who was moving to be near her daughter in Phoenix, and how she had borrowed her brother Leonard's truck and driven it two hours each way and how she had wept a little, just a little, driving home with the table in the truck bed, not because she was sad but because she had wanted a dining room table since she was a girl and now she had one. That had been in 1968. Cora had been two. She had no memory of the table arriving, only a memory of it always having been there, the way you remember certain people—as though they had always existed and could not have not existed.

She went back into the dining room and it was empty except for the marks on the floor where the table legs had stood, four pale squares in the hardwood, the wood there slightly different in color from the wood around it, protected all these decades from light and air and the slow bleaching that everything undergoes when it is exposed to time. Those four squares were the shape of her mother's wanting, and Cora stood over them and felt something she did not have a word for.

Deborah came and stood near her. She was a small woman with efficient hands and a face that held professional sympathy the way a cup holds water.

We've done well this morning, she said.

Good, Cora said.

The bedroom furniture went at noon. Cora sat on the porch steps and watched the headboard go past—dark walnut, carved along the top with a simple repeated curve that her mother had always called her wave pattern, as though the wood had been made for her—carried by two young men who moved it with ease and without ceremony, talking to each other about something else, their conversation trailing behind them down the porch steps and across the grass to the truck at the curb. The mattress went after, zippered into a plastic bag by Deborah's assistant, a tall young woman named Sadie who worked silently and thoroughly and who Cora suspected, in a distant way, of being the kind of person who would have things organized and in order if everything fell apart.

Cora's daughter Nadine had called twice that morning and Cora had let it go to voicemail both times. Nadine was thirty-one and lived in Portland now and felt things about the house in the way of someone who had visited it but had not grown up in it, who had known Vivienne as a grandmother and not as the woman who stood in the kitchen at six in the morning in January making oatmeal and saying nothing, just making the oatmeal, the sound of the spoon against the pot a kind of language. Nadine's grief was real and Cora did not dispute it. But it was a different country from Cora's grief and she did not have the translation for it today. She would call her back tonight. She would call her back from the Holiday Inn where the ice machine down the hall made a noise every forty minutes through the wall and where she had lain awake until three o'clock going through the list of things left to do, the utilities, the lawyer, the question of the house itself, which was another list nested inside the first list, which was itself nested inside something she was not ready to name.

She was eating a sandwich she had bought at the gas station on Route 30—turkey, in a triangular plastic container, the bread already going soft—when the woman with the white hair and the earrings came and sat on the steps beside her without asking. Cora looked at her. The woman looked at the street where a man was loading things into a Subaru.

I used to know a woman on this street, the woman said. Dorothy Fitch. You know her?

I don't, Cora said.

She moved away. Or died. I can't remember now which. She had a beautiful hydrangea along the side of her house.

My mother had roses, Cora said. In the back.

The woman nodded as though this confirmed something.

Are you the daughter?

I'm a daughter, Cora said. There's one more. She lives in Atlanta.

That's your mother's house, the woman said. It wasn't a question.

Cora said yes.

They sat for a moment with that.

Then the woman said: I bought a lamp. Little blue one, from the bedroom. I hope that's all right.

Cora thought about the lamp. She could see it if she looked at it from the inside, the way you can see certain things you've known your whole life when you close your eyes—a blue ceramic lamp with a cream shade, sitting on the nightstand on the right side of her mother's bed, which was the side her mother had slept on for thirty-one years because the left side was the side that had been her husband's side before he left, before Cora was eight years old and her sister Marlene was five. The lamp had been there so long it had made a ring on the wood of the nightstand, a faint circle of wear. Cora had not thought about who would buy the lamp. She had priced it and not thought about where it would go.

I'm glad it's going to a home, Cora said, and she meant it, and she also felt the strangeness of meaning it, the way you can mean something and still have it cost you.

The woman nodded again. She had the quality of someone who had been through something herself, some equivalent passage, and who had come out on the other side of it still willing to sit down next to a stranger on a porch step, which was not nothing.

She went away without ceremony and Cora finished the sandwich and went back inside.

The living room was half empty now. The wingback chair gone, the side table gone, the bookshelf gone with all its books—Deborah had sold them by the box, two dollars a box, and Cora had agreed to this without thinking it through and was now thinking it through. Her mother had read. She had read with the dedication of someone who had been told as a girl that she was not the kind of person who read, and who had spent her adult life proving the contrary. She had read history and she had read novels and she had read books about birds, which she fed from a feeder outside the kitchen window with the attention of a person conducting ongoing research. Cora stood in the half-empty living room and wondered where the books were going and felt something move through her that was neither anger nor sadness exactly but was related to both, was downstream from both.

There was a girl in the corner looking at the last things on the card table near the door. The girl was maybe twelve, maybe thirteen, with the particular self-containment of a child who has learned to be quiet in adult spaces. She was holding a small figurine—a blue bird, Cora saw, a ceramic bluebird her mother had kept on the windowsill above the kitchen sink for as long as memory held. Cora had washed dishes next to that bird. Her mother had washed dishes next to that bird. It had no price sticker.

That one isn't for sale, Cora said.

The girl looked up. She wasn't startled. She just looked.

I know, the girl said. I was just looking at it.

And she set it back on the table with a care that seemed deliberate, seemed chosen, and walked away toward where her mother was negotiating over something in the back of the house, and Cora picked up the bluebird and held it in her palm. It was lighter than she expected. It was always lighter than she expected—she had picked it up a hundred times in her life and it was always that small surprise of lightness, as though it might just lift, as though the bird inside the ceramic was still considering flight.

Her mother had bought it at a church sale in 1979 or 1980. Cora had been with her. She remembered the gymnasium of the AME church on Madison Street, the tables covered with donated things, the smell of something being cooked in the back, and her mother picking up the bluebird and saying I'll take this home. Not asking the price. Just saying it, already decided. Cora had been twelve or thirteen, the age of the girl in the corner, and she had not understood then what it meant to see something and know with absolute clarity that it was yours, that it had been waiting for you to find it. She thought she understood it better now.

She put the bluebird in her coat pocket.

By two o'clock the house was mostly done, mostly empty, the rooms going bare in ways that changed their voices—sounds moved differently now, footsteps reporting back with more authority, and the light moved differently too, falling on walls where furniture had been and showing the shapes of absence, the brighter rectangles where pictures had hung. Her mother had hung pictures from the first year she lived in this house. Cora's school pictures in a sequence along the hallway, Marlene's beside them, and other pictures—a print of something blue and abstract that Cora had never asked about, a photograph of their grandmother in a hat. All sold or taken down. The hooks still in the walls like small questions.

Deborah found her in the hallway looking at the hooks.

We're about wrapped up, she said. There are a few larger items that didn't move, the dryer I mentioned, the workbench in the basement. We can arrange pickup—

Yes, Cora said. That's fine.

You did very well, Deborah said. A good estate.

Cora looked at her. She knew that Deborah meant this practically—meant that the sale had gone well, that people had come and bought and that the proceeds would be respectable. She knew this and she received it as it was meant.

But she thought: a good estate. Her mother's life, assembled and then disassembled in a morning, and the word for it was *good*.

She thought: yes. It was. It was a good estate. Her mother had made something from what she had, and what she had was not everything she deserved and not nothing either, and she had made of it something real—these rooms, these objects that had now gone out into the world to do the work of being useful or beautiful or both in other hands, other houses. This was not nothing. This was, in its way, a kind of continuation.

She did not know if she believed this. She was trying it out.

After Deborah and Sadie and the last of the shoppers had gone, Cora walked through the empty rooms. She did this slowly. She did it with her hands in her pockets and the bluebird a small weight against her hip. She did it the way you read something a second time after you know the ending, when the whole thing means differently. The kitchen with its linoleum the color of old cream and its window where the bird feeder still hung empty and the ring on the sill from the ceramic lamp—no, that ring was in the bedroom, she was confused, she was putting things in the wrong rooms. The kitchen sill had its own marks. Its own history of small objects, small weathers.

The bedroom. She had not been in the bedroom since the morning after her mother died, when she had come and stood in it and looked at the made bed with the understanding that it would not be unmade again from inside. Now it was empty, and the window was still there, and through it the neighbor's pine tree, going blue-dark in the afternoon light, the same tree that had been her mother's last long view of the outside world.

Cora sat down on the floor. The hardwood was cold through her jeans. She sat cross-legged in the center of the room the way she had sat as a child, the way children sit when the floor is the appropriate place for the size of the feeling, and she let herself feel what she had been carrying all day in practical tasks and cold coffee and the prices of things.

It took a while to arrive. Grief is not impatient. It will wait.

When it came she let it come, and it had the quality of something that had been waiting a long time for permission—not the sharp particular pain of the first weeks but something older and wider, something that had to do not just with her mother's death but with her mother's life, with the fact of a woman who had wanted things and gotten some of them and not others, who had worked a second shift and raised two daughters in a house with a porch rail threaded through with morning glory and a cedar box with the word *careful* written in it, who had sat in a wingback chair and read books she had been told she had no business reading, who had bought a bluebird from a church sale table and taken it home and kept it for thirty-five years on the sill above the kitchen sink, who had died in this room watching a pine tree go through its colors, who had been known and unknown, as everyone is, as everyone is.

The house was quiet.

The light on the empty walls was the color it had been when she was a child and came home to a quiet house, before her mother was home from work, and she would walk through the rooms in the particular silence of a child alone in the family space, everything familiar and strange in the early evening light. She had not known then that she was memorizing it. She had not known she was building something she would need later, an interior record of these rooms, their proportions, the way the light fell, the sound of the screen door and the voice of the floors. She had not known it was possible to need something you had not yet lost.

She sat until the room went dark.

Then she stood and brushed herself off and took the bluebird from her pocket and set it on the windowsill above where the bed had been, in front of the window that looked out at the pine tree. She stood there looking at it for a moment. The small ceramic bird in the last gray light.

She left it there.

She walked through the empty house and out the front door and pulled it shut behind her. The morning glory moved in the September air. Down the street a child was calling to another child, the pure long vowels of children's calling, and the sound went out into the ordinary evening and disappeared into it the way sounds do, the way everything does, eventually, and Cora walked to her car and got in and sat for a moment with her hands on the wheel before she started the engine and pulled out from the curb onto Birch Street and drove away from her mother's house in the direction of the highway, toward the life that continued.
