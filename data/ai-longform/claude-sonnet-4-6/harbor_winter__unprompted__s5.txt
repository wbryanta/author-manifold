The light came off the water wrong in February—too flat, too silver, like the underside of a flounder—and Marta Voss had spent enough years watching her grandfather study it to know that wrong light meant something, even if she couldn't yet say what. She was eleven, home from school with a cough that had turned to nothing by Tuesday but which her mother had decided warranted the full week, and so she had been delivered to the harbor house where her grandfather lived alone since Grandma Helene's passing, three rooms stacked above a boat shed that smelled permanently of diesel and salt and the particular sweetness of wood that has been wet ten thousand times and dried nine thousand nine hundred and ninety-nine.

Her grandfather's name was Einar Voss. He was seventy-three and moved the way retired pilots always moved, she would learn later, as if the ground were mildly suspect, as if solid earth were a temporary arrangement he had agreed to provisionally. He had guided ships into Karlvik harbor for thirty-one years: tankers, container vessels, the occasional cruise ship that wandered north in summer and seemed embarrassed about it, and the fishing fleet that had once been sixty boats and was now nine. He had known the channel the way other men knew the insides of their own hands, known it in darkness and in the white blindness of snow squalls, known it as it changed from decade to decade, the sandbar that migrated north by sixty meters in the 1980s, the rock ledge that winter ice had sheared away so that the depth readings of his youth no longer matched and had to be carried always with that adjustment in mind.

He sat at the kitchen table when she arrived, drinking coffee and looking at the harbor. This was most of what he did now, her mother said, as if it were something to worry about. But Marta had stayed here enough times to understand that looking at the harbor was not the same as doing nothing, the way that being a doctor and listening was not the same as being idle.

She dropped her bag by the door. He didn't turn.

"Come," he said. "Sit. Tell me what you see."

She sat across from him at the window. The harbor was laid out below: the fish docks with their rust-stained pilings, the cannery building that ran the length of the south quay, long and low and painted the red-brown that had faded to something more like old blood, and beyond the breakwater the open water of the fjord, and beyond that the islands, and beyond those, though she could not see it, the sea.

"Water," she said.

"More."

"Gray water. Some boats. The cannery."

"Look at the water. Not at the things on it."

She looked. It was harder than it sounded. The fishing boats were more interesting than the water, the way objects were always more interesting than the spaces between them. But she tried, and after a while she saw that the water was not simply gray. There was a band of darker color running from the breakwater mouth eastward toward the cannery dock, almost purple-black, distinct from the choppier silver-gray on either side.

"There's a darker part," she said. "Like a stripe."

He looked at her. He was a big man who had become a thinner version of a big man, with a face that the weather had treated as raw material and worked on for decades, and his eyes when he turned them on her were still sharp and very pale, the pale of sea ice. "Good," he said. "What does it mean?"

"I don't know."

"Guess."

"Deeper water?"

"Current," he said. "Moving water looks different. It holds the light differently, darker or lighter depending on the angle of the sun and where you're standing. That stripe you see is the main channel current, the same water that's been running through that gap since before the town was built. You can see it from here every morning in winter when the light is low and coming from the south. In summer you lose it. The high sun flattens everything." He drank his coffee. "Your father drove a truck for twelve years and never once looked at the water."

"Papa looks at the water."

"He looks toward it. That's different."

She didn't argue because she suspected he was right. Her father was a kind man who had grown up here and moved away and come back and still seemed to regard the harbor as backdrop rather than subject, the way people in famous cities walked past their famous buildings without seeing them. It wasn't a flaw so much as a form of belonging that had calcified into blindness.

The cannery horn went at eight, which meant the night boat had come in with its load from the trawl grounds and they were starting the line. You could hear it on still mornings across the whole harbor basin, that flat agricultural bellow. It was the sound that had organized this town's time for sixty years. Her grandfather listened to it without expression.

"Is it true they're closing?" she asked.

"End of March."

"What happens then?"

He looked back at the water. "Nothing. And then something else, but slowly, and not good." He seemed to hear himself. "Maybe something good eventually. Hard to say. Towns are long."

She watched the cannery. Even from here she could see the steam beginning to rise from the processing vents on the roof, white vapor against the gray sky, salmon-smell on certain winds. Two of the nine boats were tied at the cannery dock, the *Ingrid B* and the Sørensens' boat whose name she could never remember, and a truck was backed to the loading ramp with its rear doors open. She had been inside the cannery twice on school trips and remembered it as a place of tremendous noise and cold and the hypnotic rhythm of women's hands moving over the conveyor belts with a speed that seemed impossible until it became ordinary.

"Grandpa," she said. "What's wrong with the light this morning?"

He went very still for a moment, the way an animal goes still that has heard something far off and cannot yet identify it. "What do you mean, wrong?"

"I don't know. When I was coming in the boat from the house I thought it looked—off. Too flat."

"You came in the boat?"

"Mama let me take the Lund."

"By yourself?"

"Mama let me," she said again, and he accepted this with a small movement of his head that was not quite a nod.

He looked at the water a long time. "Ice above," he said finally. "Above the fjord, far inland. When there's heavy ice on the upper fjord the light off the water changes because the surface is a different color from the reflections—the sky sees itself differently in open water than in ice, and so the open water looks strange too. Strange back. Like—" He paused. "You know how when someone is sick, even before they look sick, there's something? Your grandmother used to say she could always tell. Something in the eyes before the symptoms. It's like that. The water tells you what the upper fjord looks like before you can see it." He stood, slowly, his knees announcing their objection as they always did. "I'll show you tonight."

They spent the morning at the kitchen table, which was where he did everything that didn't require the outdoors. He had charts—paper charts, not the electronic plotter she had seen on the newer boats—rolled in a wooden rack her grandmother had made from curtain rods, and he unrolled one now, the inner harbor chart, and held it flat with a jar of pens and a stone he'd carried back from somewhere on the coast of Norway forty years ago. She had seen this chart before but never studied it. He moved his finger over the soundings, the small numbers printed in pale blue that indicated depth.

"These numbers," he said, "were surveyed in 1974. Every year they're a little wrong, and you have to know how and why. Not the deep channel—that doesn't change much, granite bottom, nothing moves it. But here—" his finger found the eastern shallows "—this is sand. River-fed. Every spring flood changes it. I kept my own notes for thirty years, every passage, and you build up a picture that the charts can't give you. The charts are someone's measurement of one moment. Your notes are the story."

"Where are your notes?"

He gestured at a shelf above the radiator, and she saw the row of composition notebooks, two dozen or more, the same black-and-white marbled covers they sold at the school supply counter, fat with use.

"Can I read them?"

"When you're ready." He said it without condescension, as a simple statement of sequence, and she understood that ready didn't mean older, necessarily, but more something she didn't have yet which she would have to work toward. "First learn to see it. Then what's written will mean something."

She asked if she could hold the sounding stone, and he passed it to her without comment. It was attached to a length of braided cord, old and dark with use, the stone itself roughly shaped and heavy, the size of her fist. She had thought it was only a paperweight.

"You still use this?"

"I used to keep it on the bridge. Habit. Modern ships have electronic depth finders, of course, but I always liked knowing I could drop a weight on a string if everything else failed." He poured more coffee. "The old pilots used to cast the lead every quarter mile through the whole channel approach. Feel it come up, and sometimes you could tell the bottom type by what stuck to the tallow they packed in the hollow—sand, or mud, or shell—and that told you more than the number alone. Mud bottom in a place that should be sand meant something was different. Could be dangerous, could be nothing, but you had to know."

"Do you miss it? Piloting?"

"Some mornings. When I see a ship come in and it sets wrong in the water." He saw her expression. "A ship sits different depending on its load and how that load is distributed. An even load, she rides steady. Uneven, she lists. And a pilot sees it from the water, coming alongside in the pilot boat, and you're already adjusting before you've climbed the ladder. You're reading before you're even aboard."

It occurred to her, not for the first time and not yet fully formed, that her grandfather had spent his entire life reading one text, and that the text was the water and everything in relationship with it, and that unlike the texts she was required to read at school, this one was never the same twice and never completely knowable and did not offer itself to the patient or the passive. It demanded a particular kind of attention that was neither relaxed nor anxious, that held focus without gripping, the way you were supposed to hold a small bird.

In the afternoon the weather came in from the northwest, soft and dark, not a violent sky but an enclosing one, the clouds lowering until the ridge on the far shore disappeared and the fjord seemed to shrink. She watched her grandfather watch it. He made no particular note of it, which she took to mean it was ordinary, which turned out to be correct—a few inches of snow by evening, nothing remarkable. But watching him not-react to it taught her something about calibration, about the difference between weather worth attention and weather that was simply weather.

They walked down to the docks at three, before the light failed entirely. He put on his wool coat and she put on her school jacket, which was insufficient, and he lent her a canvas jacket from behind the door without comment. The dock boards were salted against ice and the salt had made them look pale and old, and the smell coming off the water was the winter smell, cleaner than summer, the organic processing slowed by cold. The *Ingrid B* was still at the cannery dock. The Sørensens' boat had gone back out.

Old Stig was mending net at the end of the main dock, working with the automatic patience of a man who has done a thing so many times the hands have their own understanding and the mind is free to wander. He was seventy or close to it, had fished these waters for fifty years. He looked up when they came and gave a nod that encompassed both of them.

"Einar," he said.

"Stig." Her grandfather sat on a crate without being invited, which was a form of intimacy. "How were the grounds?"

"Thin." He worked the net. "Been thin since November. Little better east of the Holm, but not worth the fuel over a long time." He glanced up again. "The Lindqvists came in this morning with maybe sixty percent of what they'd have wanted. Knutsen broke even, maybe." He shrugged, which was a large movement on a man as broad as Stig, almost a full-body motion. "End of March, what does it matter."

"What will you do?"

"My brother-in-law has a salmon operation up the coast, pen fish, I won't pretend I like it but it's work." He nodded at Marta. "You teaching her?"

"Starting to."

"About time. Her father can't tell the tide from a traffic light, God love him."

Marta looked at the net. Up close the mesh was intricate and each mend was a small architectural decision, which hole to close, which passage to leave, the whole thing a kind of writing in cord. She thought about her grandfather's notebooks above the radiator.

"Does it get easier?" she asked Stig. "Reading the water."

He thought about this with genuine seriousness. "You read it less with your eyes after a while. It comes in through—" he touched his chest, approximately where his sternum was, "—here. Like music. You stop hearing the notes separate." He went back to the net. "But you have to learn the notes first and they're a bastard."

Walking back from the dock she asked her grandfather about the channel approach in fog, which she'd heard him speak of before but only in fragments. He talked for most of the walk, and she listened with both the conscious part of her attention and whatever else she had that was not quite conscious but absorbed information through proximity and repetition and the quality of the voice in which it was delivered. He described the particular anxiety of the channel approach in zero visibility, the way instruments gave you data but not understanding, the way he had learned to use engine sound reflected off the breakwater as a rough guide to position, sound-navigation that no one had taught him but that he'd worked out over years of paying attention to what the world sent back. He described two near-misses, both in fog, both in different decades, described them with the flat specificity of someone who has processed the terror into usable information, who has metabolized the fear into knowledge. She thought: this is what experience is. Not the accumulation of time but the conversion of alarm into literacy.

By evening the snow was coming, light and dry, and they ate soup at the kitchen table and watched the harbor disappear under it. The cannery lights were still on, burning yellow-white in the snowfall, and she thought about the women at the conveyor belts working the evening shift, the last winter of it, their hands still moving at that impossible speed over fish that were getting thinner, catch that was getting thinner, the whole thing thinning out and somehow the hands still moving because the hands knew how and the knowing how was the last thing to go.

"Will you miss the cannery?" she asked.

He considered it. "I'll miss what it meant. That there was enough." He watched the lights in the snow. "When I was young and starting on the water, the harbor was full by seven every morning. You could walk the dock and there was work everywhere, the smell of it, noise. The pilot station had four of us, always something coming in or going out. The cannery ran two shifts and sometimes three in season. It felt—" he paused, finding the word carefully, the way he found depth soundings, lowering something into the dark and reading what came back, "—sufficient. That it was enough to live this way and the sea would allow it."

"And now it doesn't allow it."

"The sea allows whatever it allows. We took too much for too long and now there is less. The sea is indifferent." He said it without bitterness, which impressed her. "A pilot learns that. The channel doesn't care if you know it or not. It will put you aground whether you deserve it or not. The water isn't cruel and isn't kind. That's not pessimism. It's just accurate." He looked at her. "The accurate thing is what you want. Not the comforting thing."

After dinner he took one of the composition notebooks from the shelf and set it in front of her. It was from 1987. She opened it and found dense columns of dates, times, vessel names, tonnage, weather conditions, tide state, visibility, and then beside each passage a column of notes he'd written in a small tight hand that ran toward abbreviations she couldn't decode but that gave the impression of a whole texture of event compressed into symbols. And here and there a longer note, written in the same hand but with more space around it: *northwest swell 2m, standing wave at channel mouth, new behavior, investigate. Unusual phosphorescence, east bar, night passage. Sandbar has moved—confirmed with lead, now 3m at low water where 5m shown on chart.*

She read without understanding most of it and understood that she was reading the edge of something large, a whole archive of attention that had been paid to one narrow stretch of water over three decades, and that this was what expertise looked like from the inside—not confident pronouncements but question after question held patiently in a notebook, the accumulation of questions eventually becoming something like an answer.

The snow fell. The cannery lights burned. Her grandfather washed the soup bowls and then sat again with his coffee and his view, which was a habit so settled in him it had become almost architectural, part of how the room was organized.

"Grandpa," she said, "if there are no more ships to pilot, and the cannery closes, and the fish are thin—what is the harbor for?"

He was quiet for a long time. Outside the snow came down with that particular winter silence, not true silence but a kind of acoustic absorption, the world softening its own edges.

"When I was learning piloting, my teacher—old Haraldsen, he'd done it forty years by then—he told me that the harbor is for ships, yes, and for fish, yes. But under all of that it's for the reading of it. That people needed to live close to something they couldn't fully understand and had to keep trying. That the effort was the point." He set down his cup. "I thought he was being romantic. I was twenty-four and I thought romance was a failure of precision." His pale eyes moved across the harbor, patient, reading. "I think he was right, though. The effort is the point."

She looked at the notebook and then out at the harbor, and she tried to hold in mind at the same time the depth of what she couldn't see—the channel bed, the migrated sandbars, the granite shelves and the mud and the shell—and the surface of what she could, the flat silver water going dark now in the evening, the snow touching it and vanishing. Her grandfather sat beside her reading the same thing she was, or rather the same text differently, with thirty more years of vocabulary, a whole grammar she was only now learning had rules, and she felt the particular feeling that is not inadequacy but its more useful cousin, which is the recognition that there is a great deal to know and that you have been shown where to stand and how to look, and that is enough to begin.

The cannery horn sounded at nine, the end of the evening shift, and they both turned toward the sound without speaking, attending to it the way you attend to something that may not sound much longer, holding it for an extra beat before letting it go out into the dark and the snow and the cold water that received it without acknowledgment, as it received everything, as it would receive the silence that came after.
