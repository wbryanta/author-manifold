He came out of it gasping, his hands raised before him in the dark.

She had her hand on his chest and he could feel his own heart knocking at it like something trying to get out. Her hair was against his face and she smelled of sleep and of the cheap soap she used.

Settle down, she said. You're all right.

He put his hands down. He sat there breathing. The room assembled itself slowly around him in the dark, the mean dresser against the wall with its spotted glass and the window giving onto the alley and the cold coming under the door from the hall. He could hear rain somewhere. He could hear the soft anonymous breathing of the building around him.

What was it this time, she said.

Nothing. Go back to sleep.

She lay back beside him and he could feel her watching him in the dark. He swung his legs over and sat on the edge of the bed and put his face in his hands. Through the window the alley showed a slick black pavement and above it a narrow strip of sky in which no star was visible. Somewhere below a cat moved among ashcans. He sat there for a time and then he rose and went to the window and stood looking out.

His name was Ballard. John Wesley Ballard. He had come to this city from a place he did not speak of, had taken up a series of occupations none of which suited him, had come finally to live in this rooming house on a street of rooming houses in a part of the city where men went anonymously about their lives with great success. He worked for a man named Glover who ran a warehouse on the river and he moved boxes from one place to another in that warehouse and he was paid for this and he spent some of what he was paid on drink and some on the woman in the bed behind him whose name was Dorothy but whom he called by no name at all if he could help it.

She was asleep again. He could tell by her breathing.

He went to the small table and found the bottle there and poured what remained of it into a glass and stood holding the glass. Rain had begun on the window now, a light and intermittent thing, and the streetlamp below sent up a cone of misted light in which the drops were briefly visible and then were not. He drank the whiskey and set the glass down and went back to the bed and lay down on top of the covers with his boots still on and stared at the ceiling.

He was not a man who put much stock in dreams.

IN THE MORNING he walked the twelve blocks to the warehouse through a drizzle that could not commit to being rain. The streets were wet and the storefronts showed their goods behind fogged glass and men came and went in overcoats with their collars up. He walked with his hands in his pockets and his hat pulled forward and nodded to no one and no one nodded to him.

The warehouse sat at the foot of a street that ran down to the river and all day the river smell was on everything, that smell of mud and old water and things rotting in slow perpetuity, and the sound of it too was always there, the deep patience of it, the brown and moving weight of it going about its blind and tireless business regardless.

Harmon was already there when he arrived, sitting on a crate near the loading dock smoking. Harmon was a tall thin man with no color in his face and he watched Ballard approach with eyes that were neither hostile nor welcoming, that were in fact nothing at all.

You look rough, Harmon said.

I'm fine.

You look like you been rode hard.

Ballard took off his hat and shook the water from it and put it back on. Where's Glover, he said.

Office. He wants to see you.

He went through the warehouse floor, past the stacked crates and the smell of sawdust and machine oil, past two men who were loading a hand truck and who glanced up at him without interest, and knocked on the office door.

Come on.

Glover was behind his desk, a heavy man going to fat, with his sleeves rolled and his suspenders showing and a face that seemed to conduct all his calculations visibly. He had before him a ledger and a cup of coffee and he did not look up immediately.

Close it, he said.

Ballard closed the door and stood before the desk.

Glover looked up. You know a man named Peers, he said.

No.

He knows you.

I don't know him.

Glover turned a page in the ledger and turned it back. He looked up again at Ballard with those calculating eyes. He came here two days ago asking about you, he said. Big man, red in the face. Said he was from Caulfield County.

Ballard said nothing.

Said he had business with you.

I don't know him, Ballard said again.

Glover watched him for a moment. All right, he said. He picked up his coffee and drank from it. There's a freight came in last night needs unloading. See Harmon about it.

Ballard turned to go.

Ballard.

He turned back.

You know how I feel about trouble, Glover said. He said it looking down at the ledger, the way a man might say a thing he has said so many times it requires no more emphasis than that.

Ballard went out and found Harmon and they spent the morning unloading the freight and stacking it according to Harmon's system which was known only to Harmon and to which Harmon adhered with a devotion that was perhaps the nearest thing to belief in his life. They worked without much conversation. Around midday the rain quit and a pale light came through the high windows and they sat on crates and ate what they had brought.

Who's Peers, Ballard said.

Harmon looked at him. Don't know the name, he said.

Ballard chewed. Don't matter, he said.

Harmon studied the far wall of the warehouse for a time. There's a man been around, he said. I seen him twice now outside. Stands across the street.

What's he look like.

Big. Red faced. Wears a canvas coat.

Ballard ate and did not say anything and after a while they went back to work.

HE DID NOT go directly home that evening. He walked instead toward the river and stood at the railing above the levee and looked out at the water. It was moving fast from the recent rains, the color of old pewter in the failing light, carrying on its surface branches and debris and the occasional unnameable thing. Lights were coming on across the far bank. A towboat moved upriver with a string of barges and its lights were reflected in long wobbling lines on the water and then it was gone around the bend and the sound of its engines faded and the river resumed its ancient indifference.

He had not thought about Caulfield County in some time. He had cultivated not thinking about it as other men cultivate a skill, with practice and with discipline, and he had arrived at something close to mastery of it in his better stretches. But mastery was not permanence. Mastery was only proficiency. The thing waited beneath the surface of his days with the same patience as the river, moving in darkness, moving always, indifferent to what passed above.

He turned from the river and walked up into the city.

The woman was in the kitchen when he came in, standing at the stove in her housedress with her back to him. The room smelled of whatever she was cooking and of the cigarette burning in the ashtray on the table. He hung his hat and coat and sat down.

Somebody come by here today, she said. She said it without turning.

Who.

Big man. Said he was a cousin of yours.

He has no cousins.

That's what I thought. She turned then and looked at him. She was not bad to look at, Dorothy, though the life had not been gentle with her and you could see that too. She had brown eyes that were direct and that missed very little. I told him I didn't know where you were, she said.

What'd he want.

Said he just wanted to talk to you. Said it was family business.

He has no family, Ballard said.

She turned back to the stove. He picked up her cigarette and drew on it and set it back in the ashtray. The linoleum on the floor was cracked and he looked at it and looked away.

What was he like, he said. This man.

Quiet. Polite enough. Big like I said. She paused in her stirring. He had something about him I didn't care for.

What kind of something.

She thought about it. Like when you're around something that could go bad, she said. You know how that is. It's all right and then all at once it isn't.

He was quiet a moment. Ballard, she said. Tell me what this is.

It's nothing.

Don't say it's nothing.

Go ahead and eat, he said. I'm not hungry.

She brought two plates to the table and sat down across from him and he sat watching her eat and she ate without speaking, looking at her plate, and the kitchen was quiet except for the sounds of it and somewhere in the building a radio playing.

After a while he said: I got to take a trip.

She looked up.

Few days, he said. Maybe a week.

She put her fork down. She was looking at him carefully. All right, she said.

Is it.

She picked her fork back up. No, she said. But it will be.

HE LEFT ON the early bus, sitting in the back with his coat across his knees and watching the city recede through the rain-streaked window and then the outskirts and then open country. The bus smelled of wet wool and cigarette smoke and the faint residue of some industrial solvent he could not identify. He slept some and woke and slept again, and in his sleep there was the dream but truncated, cut short before the crowd turned on him, and he was left instead standing in that darkness with the prophet's words fading in his ear. Yes, I think perhaps you will be cured.

He rode the bus to Malone and then hired a truck to take him up into the hill country, a rusted Ford driven by an old man who did not speak and who smelled of tobacco and had about him the total incuriosity of a man whose world has contracted to a precise and sufficient geography. They went up through country that Ballard had not seen in four years and that showed him no change of any kind. The same cedared hills, the same clay banks cut by the road's passage showing their red strata, the same tenant farms set back from the road on their mud tracks with the same lean cattle standing in the same bare fields. The sky was low and colorless. The old man drove with one wrist over the wheel, looking out at all of it as a man might look at something he owns outright but no longer has much use for.

He got out at the crossroads below Caulfield and stood in the road while the truck went on and the sound of it diminished and was gone. Then he was alone in a silence that was not silence at all but full of the sounds of the country, a crow calling somewhere back in the pines, the wind in the dry grass of the ditch, far off the sound of a dog.

He set out down the road toward the town.

Caulfield was not much. It had been not much four years ago and it remained not much. A courthouse with a yard of bare dirt and a Confederate monument going green with lichen. A hardware store, a general merchandise, a drugstore with a soda counter barely visible through the fogged glass of its window. Trucks parked at angles before these establishments. Men on the wooden sidewalk who looked at him with the frank and measuring appraisal of men who know their territory and know who belongs to it.

He went into the drugstore and sat at the counter.

A girl came and looked at him.

Coffee, he said.

She brought it and he sat with his hands around the cup and looked at himself in the mirror behind the counter. He looked like what he was and he did not look at himself long.

A man came in and sat two stools down. Ballard did not look at him but he knew who it was before the man spoke, knew by some principle of physical recognition older than thought.

John Wesley, the man said.

Eugene, Ballard said.

The man called Eugene was younger than Ballard by some years, thickly built, with the kind of face that has never quite lost its look of boyhood regardless of what the years put on it. He had large clean hands that he set flat on the counter and he looked at Ballard in the mirror.

You got my messages, he said.

I got them.

The girl came back and Eugene ordered coffee and waited until she had gone again. It's about the old place, he said. About what your daddy left.

He didn't leave anything.

Well that's the question, Eugene said. That right there is the whole question in a nutshell. He wrapped his hands around his cup. Man named Tanner says he has papers on it. Says your daddy signed it over before he died.

Ballard looked at the mirror. He turned the coffee cup in its saucer.

That's a lie, he said.

Maybe so. Eugene drank. But he's got something that looks enough like truth to where the county's taking notice. There's talk of a sale. He glanced sideways at Ballard. I thought you'd want to know.

Who is Tanner.

He's been around three, four years now. Came up from somewhere south. He has interests in a few things around the county. Land mostly. He paused. He has friends.

What kind of friends.

The kind that matter to the county.

Ballard was quiet. He looked at his hands on the counter, the cracked knuckles, the scar along the base of the left thumb he had carried since he was eleven years old. He was looking at them and then he was looking through them at some distance no surface could account for.

I don't want the place, he said.

Eugene looked at him. Well, he said. That may be. But there's what you want and there's what's yours by right and those are two separate questions.

Is that why you sent a man to my work? Ballard said. Is that why you sent him to where I live?

Eugene set his cup down. I didn't send anybody anywhere, he said.

Ballard looked at him in the mirror.

I wrote you letters, Eugene said. Two letters. That's all I did. He met Ballard's eyes in the glass. Whoever come to see you, it wasn't from me.

They sat for a moment in the close warmth of the drugstore with the sounds of the town muffled around them and the girl somewhere in the back and the smell of coffee and the candy jars along the back counter and the dusty bottles of this and that.

Then who, Ballard said.

Eugene looked down at his hands. Tanner, maybe, he said. If he knows you're the heir. If he knows you're the last of them that could make a claim.

Ballard put money on the counter and stood and put on his coat.

Where are you going, Eugene said.

He went out into the cold air and stood on the sidewalk. Men passed. A truck went by with a load of fence posts. Across the square the Confederate soldier stood in his permanent contemplation of some middle distance where the battle was always about to begin, where it had not yet been lost, where the order had not yet come down and the field was still open and the morning still possible. Ballard looked at him for a moment. Then he turned and walked up the street toward the edge of town.

THE HOUSE sat on forty acres of second growth timber above a branch of Caulfield Creek and it was in worse repair than he had been prepared for. The porch had given way on the left side and the roof above it sagged correspondingly and two of the windows on the upper floor were gone, the openings covered with cardboard that had itself gone dark and soft in the weather. The yard was grown up. The barn behind it had lost its doors and inside he could see the skeleton of it, the bare crossbeams, the packed earth floor, a rusted piece of machinery so long present it seemed to have grown there. He stood in the yard for a time. The cold came through his coat. A hawk turned overhead in a sky the color of pewter.

He did not go in.

He walked the perimeter of the property instead, along the fence line that marked it, much of the fence down or failing, the posts rotted off at the ground and the wire rusted and collapsed and in places consumed by the briars that had grown up through it. His father had built this fence. He could remember being a boy and handing up the staples and the old man driving them with the fencing hammer and the sun on the back of his neck and the smell of the woods in late summer. He could remember the old man's hands, the sureness and economy of them, the absence of waste in them. He could not remember the old man's face with any clarity. He did not try.

At the back of the property where the trees thickened he found where someone had been. A fire ring. A bottle. The dark impression in the leaf mold where a bedroll had lain. Recent enough that the fire ring still showed ash that the rains had not dissolved entirely.

He stood looking at it.

Then he heard something move in the trees behind him and he turned.

The man who came out of the trees was big and red faced as Dorothy had described him and he wore a canvas coat and he had his hands in his pockets and he came on slowly, watching Ballard, until he stopped some twenty feet away and they stood looking at each other in the thin winter light.

I figured you'd come here first, the man said.

Who are you, Ballard said.

The man looked around the clearing, at the trees, at the cold sky above them. He seemed to be taking his time, or rather seemed a man for whom time was a different proposition than it was for other men.

I work for Mister Tanner, he said. My name's Roper.

What do you want.

Mister Tanner would like to speak with you. He said it simply, as if the simplicity of it were itself an argument.

Ballard said nothing.

He's a reasonable man, Roper said. He'd like to make you a fair offer on the property and be done with it. He looked at Ballard with an expression that was not unfriendly and not its opposite either. He looked at him the way a man looks at a problem he has examined from all sides and has arrived at a clear view of. He said: You don't want to let this get complicated.
