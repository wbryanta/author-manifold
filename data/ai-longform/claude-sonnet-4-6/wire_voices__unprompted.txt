The switchboard filled the wall like an organ in a cathedral, all those holes waiting to be occupied, all those lights going dark and bright with the small urgencies of the living. Eleanor had worked the long-distance board for eleven years, since her husband came back from Korea with a quietness she couldn't reach through, and she had learned in those years that a voice stripped of its body becomes something else entirely, something more concentrated, the way a reduction sauce is truer to the animal than the roast itself. You heard the thing beneath the words. You heard whether someone was calling because they missed a person or because they missed a version of themselves they'd been when that person knew them. Eleanor could hear all of it in the two or three seconds before she broke through with the operator's formula, before she said the words that were not her words, the words that belonged to the company, to the uniform blouse she wore Monday through Friday, to the linoleum and the fluorescent lights and the paper cups of coffee she drank without tasting.

The calls came in Tuesday evenings, between seven and eight o'clock, which was Eleanor's favorite shift because the board thinned out and the supervisor, Mr. Gaddis, retreated to his office to work on whatever Mr. Gaddis worked on in the evenings, and the girls on the long-distance board — five of them, six when Paulette wasn't sick — had a kind of latitude they didn't have in the afternoons when the board was fully lit and Mr. Gaddis stood at the glass partition watching them the way a man watches the weather when he has somewhere he needs to get to.

The first time she connected them was in March, still cold, Eleanor wearing the cardigan she kept on the back of her chair. A man's voice, calling from Davenport, Iowa, wanting to reach a number in Savannah, Georgia. She placed the call and there was the usual delay, the underwater sound of connection being made across the miles of wire strung between poles that stood in fields she would never see, and then a woman's voice came on, a Southern voice but not a thick one, and the man said, simply, Margaret, and the woman said, I've been waiting by the phone, and Eleanor should have dropped out of the line then, should have left them to it, but the board was quiet and something in those first two words — the particular way the man said the name Margaret, as if the word had weight, as if he had been carrying it for days — made her stay on half a second longer than protocol allowed. She heard the woman laugh, a soft releasing laugh, and then she withdrew.

She thought about them for the rest of her shift, placing other calls between strangers and their families, between businessmen and their secretaries, between daughters who had moved away and mothers who had stayed behind. She thought about the specific gravity of that name, the way it fell from the man's mouth.

The following Tuesday he called again, and Eleanor recognized him before he gave the number. The voice was not unusual in any technical sense — a middle register, flat Midwestern vowels, careful in his enunciation the way men of a certain age and education were careful, as if the language were something borrowed that had to be returned intact. But she recognized the feeling in it, which was the particular feeling of a person approaching something they have been looking forward to all week. She had felt that feeling once herself, a long time ago, before Korea.

She placed the call. The woman answered on the second ring this time, as if she had been closer to the phone, and he said, Margaret, again, just the name, and the woman said, Victor, I was starting to think you weren't going to call, and he said, I always call, and she said, I know, and Eleanor withdrew more quickly this time, but not before she heard him begin — because he began quickly, the way people begin who have been composing a sentence all week — to tell her about a thing that had happened at the grain elevator where he worked, something involving a delivery and a misrouted shipment, and the pleasure in his voice was not in the story itself but in the telling of it to her, this specific her, this Margaret who was waiting in Savannah in a house Eleanor could not picture.

By April Eleanor had a clearer picture of them, assembled from the fragments she absorbed in those two or three seconds before and after connection, the small hearings she allowed herself, and she knew she was wrong to do it, she knew it was a violation of something, of their privacy and also perhaps of something in herself, but she had come to think of it as a form of care, the way a librarian might know which books a patron loved without the patron ever having said so. She knew from something he'd said one Tuesday that his name was Victor Marsh and that he was fifty-four years old and that he had a daughter in Ames who was expecting, and she knew from something Margaret had said another Tuesday that she lived in a house on a street with old oaks, that she had a garden she was putting in, that she had been widowed three years ago and that she and Victor had known each other a long time ago, how long ago Eleanor couldn't say, but long enough that the past was always present in their conversations, a country they both held citizenship in.

She did not know what they were to each other, exactly. They were not quite lovers, or if they were lovers they were lovers at a distance, which meant they were lovers mostly in the mind, which Eleanor thought might be the truest kind, or at least the most durable. They spoke with the ease of people who have already survived the hardest part of knowing each other. There was no performance in it. She had listened to enough calls to know the difference between people performing their relationship for each other and people simply being inside it, and these two were simply inside it, inside each other in the way that people become when they have known each other across decades and changes and loss.

In May he told her about the spring, about the fields coming green, about driving out along the river on a Sunday and stopping to watch the water, and his voice when he described the river had in it something she recognized as longing, the particular longing of a person who is somewhere beautiful but cannot share it, and she heard him want to put Margaret in the truck beside him, to say, look at this, and she thought about all the years she had spent next to a man who had come back from somewhere and left something there that she could not retrieve for him, and she felt for Victor Marsh, whom she had never met and would never meet, a fondness so deep it embarrassed her.

In June the calls shifted slightly. Margaret's voice held something new, a tension or a restraint, the way a voice sounds when the speaker is choosing words more carefully than usual, and Eleanor tried to identify the change without knowing the context, the way a doctor listens to a heart without knowing the patient's history. One Tuesday Margaret told Victor that she had been having tests done and was waiting to hear, and Victor's voice went quiet in a particular way, not the silence of not knowing what to say but the silence of a man who has already begun preparing himself for something, who is a step ahead of the conversation and bracing for it. Eleanor withdrew quickly that evening and sat for a moment with her hands in her lap before the next call came through.

The tests must have come back, because in July — it was hot, the switchboard room was always overheated in summer, and Eleanor's cardigan was folded in her bag — Margaret's voice had changed again, had a different quality, lighter in one way and heavier in another, the way a person sounds when they have moved from uncertainty to knowledge and knowledge, even hard knowledge, has its own strange relief. She heard Victor say, I want to come down, and Margaret say, not yet, and he said, Margaret, and she said, there's time, Victor, and Eleanor did not know what Margaret had been told or what it meant but she knew from the grain of those two voices, from the way they held each other at the frequency she couldn't name but always recognized, that something had changed in the larger architecture of their situation.

August was when he started calling twice a week, Tuesdays and sometimes Thursdays, and Eleanor began to look forward to both nights with an attentiveness she recognized as something other than professional. On a Thursday in August she was not on the board and she found herself, that evening, in her apartment with the television on, wondering if he had called, whether another operator had connected them, whether the call had gone through smoothly. She felt foolish for thinking it. She felt something else too, something she couldn't put a clean name to, something adjacent to jealousy but not jealousy, more like the feeling of missing something that has happened in your absence, some small event that you could not have prevented missing but mourn anyway.

In September his voice had begun to carry a visible effort. The ease was still there, the fundamental ease of a man talking to a woman he loves, but beneath it was a tiredness, the kind that settles in the shoulders first and eventually reaches the voice. He told her about his daughter's baby, a girl born in late August, and Eleanor heard Margaret say, tell me everything, and she heard him smile, she could hear the smile, she had been doing this long enough to hear a smile, and he told her about the baby's hands, how they had gripped his finger, and Margaret said, oh, Victor, with such tenderness that Eleanor had to take a breath.

The Tuesday calls in October were harder to sit with. Margaret's voice was thinner, something in the breath behind the words that wasn't right, and Eleanor had worked enough night shifts over enough years to have heard enough voices in diminishment to recognize what was happening to Margaret's voice without wanting to name it. Victor was calling on Thursdays now almost without exception, which meant someone else was connecting them on Thursdays, and Eleanor on those Tuesday evenings would connect him and hear Margaret's thinned voice and know that on Thursday some other operator would hear what Eleanor heard, would notice or not notice, would understand or not understand what they were hearing. This bothered her more than it should have. She wanted to be the one who connected them. She wanted to be there. She didn't know what there meant when she thought it, except that it meant something about witnessing, about a kind of presence she had been maintaining without their knowledge that felt, to her, necessary. As if by listening she was holding something steady.

She mentioned them to no one. Not to Paulette, who sat two stations down and who sometimes made remarks about the calls she connected, not unkind remarks but the remarks of someone who needed to make the day manageable by reducing it to commentary. Not to the woman she sometimes ate lunch with, Vera, from accounting, who had been divorced twice and held strong opinions about men. Not to her sister in Cleveland who called on Sundays. Eleanor held Victor Marsh and Margaret — she still didn't know Margaret's last name, had never heard it said — in a privacy inside her privacy, a room within the room of herself.

November came. There was talk of the election in the break room, everyone with an opinion about Nixon and about the mess, the terrible ongoing mess of the war, and Eleanor thought about Korea without meaning to, about her husband, about the long year of his silence when he came back and the shorter year of his silence after he left, and she thought about the ways a war takes a person without killing them and goes on taking from everyone arranged around that person in expanding rings. She thought about this while she worked the board on a Tuesday evening, the first Tuesday of November, election night, the board busier than usual because everyone was calling everyone, and she connected Victor in Davenport to Margaret in Savannah just after seven, and Margaret's voice was very thin now, and there was the sound of effort in her breathing, and Eleanor heard Victor say, how are you, and she heard Margaret say, I'm here, and she heard what that meant.

The second Tuesday of November he called at his usual time and Margaret answered and Eleanor could hear, from the quality of the connection, that Margaret was not at the same phone she usually used, the acoustics were different, more institutional, and she thought, hospital, and she withdrew.

The third Tuesday of November he called at his usual time. Eleanor plugged in the connection and let it ring through and there was no answer. She let it ring eight times, which was four more than she should have, and then she disconnected and said to him, I'm sorry, sir, there's no answer at that number. And there was a pause on his end, not a surprised pause but a preparing pause, and he said, yes, thank you, and his voice did not break, but she heard what was happening to the voice, beneath the surface of it, the thing a voice does when the body that belongs to it is absorbing something and has not yet decided what to do with it. She said, shall I try again in a few minutes, and he said, no, thank you, and then he was gone.

Eleanor sat for a moment with her hands on the board. The light at her station went dark and she waited for the next one to come up. Around her the other operators worked their boards, Paulette telling someone the call had gone through, another girl repeating a number back to a caller, the ordinary machinery of connection and disconnection that had been Eleanor's world for eleven years. The light came up again and she plugged in and said the words: Long distance, how may I direct your call.

The following Tuesday he did not call.

She had expected him not to call. She had told herself, on the bus on the way to work, on the walk from the bus stop to the building, that he would not call this week, that the call to the number in Savannah that he had been making every week for eight months would not come, because there was no longer anyone at that number waiting beside the phone. She had prepared herself with this knowledge, she thought she had, and yet when seven o'clock passed and then seven-thirty and eight without his voice coming through her board, she felt the absence of it as a physical thing, a pressure in the chest, not grief exactly, because she had no right to grief, but something that lived in the same neighborhood.

She thought about him driving along the river on Sundays and stopping to watch the water, alone now in the truck, with no one to want beside him. She thought about the grain elevator, the misrouted shipment that had somehow become one of her private memories of something she had never been present for. She thought about his daughter's baby, the small hands gripping the finger, and Margaret saying, tell me everything, tell me everything about those hands.

Vera asked her in the break room if she was feeling all right and Eleanor said she had a headache, which was easier than the truth, and Vera gave her two aspirin from her purse and said something sympathetic about the fluorescent lights, how they gave everyone headaches. Eleanor took the aspirin with water from the paper cup and nodded and listened to Vera's opinion about the lights, and she felt, sitting there in the break room with the aspirin dissolving in her and the fluorescent lights humming above them, a gratitude for Vera that surprised her, gratitude for this particular human proximity, this inconsequential conversation that was asking nothing of her, that required no witnessing, that was simply the sound of another person filling a room.

She went back to the board at half past eight and worked until ten and connected a woman in Milwaukee to her son in San Francisco, connected a businessman in Dallas to someone in New York, connected a girl who sounded very young to a boy who sounded very young to a number in a town in New Jersey she didn't know, and the girl said his name into the phone with the relief of someone coming up for air, and the boy said, I know, I know, as if she had said something he had been waiting to hear, and Eleanor withdrew from the line and sat with her hands on the board and listened to the room around her, all those voices passing through on their way to each other, all those miles of wire strung between poles in fields she would never see, and she thought that this was what she had been doing for eleven years, not connecting calls but witnessing, being present at the edge of the real life of people, which was the life they were always trying to get to, the life waiting on the other end of the line, the voice they needed to hear when the week had been long and the silence had been large and the distance had become, for a few minutes, intolerable.

She did not know if Victor Marsh would call again. She thought perhaps he might, months from now, when the loss had settled into something he could move around inside, when Tuesdays had become ordinary again rather than the specific shape of something he had lost. She thought perhaps he would call the number anyway, once more, as people sometimes dial a number after a death, not expecting an answer, needing only to make the gesture, to send the signal into the wire the way you put flowers on a grave knowing the dead cannot smell them. She had connected those calls too. She knew what they sounded like.

She sat at her board and the lights went on and dark and she plugged in and said the words, long distance, how may I direct your call, and between calls she thought about a woman in Savannah in a house on a street with old oaks, a garden going into its last October, a woman who had been loved at a distance and had waited beside a phone every Tuesday evening for eight months, which was in its way a form of waiting that took courage, to sit beside the telephone knowing it would ring, knowing his voice would come through it from a distance too large for anything but a voice to cross, and to find in that voice sufficient nearness. She thought that Margaret had understood something about the available forms of closeness and had taken the available form without wasting herself in mourning for the forms that weren't available. She thought that this was a kind of wisdom she recognized without being able to claim it for herself.

At ten o'clock she straightened the cord at her station and collected her cardigan from the back of her chair and put on her coat and walked out through the lobby and through the glass doors into the November air, which was cold now, properly cold, the first cold that meant it. She stood for a moment on the sidewalk before walking to the bus stop. The city was going about its business around her, the ordinary business of a Tuesday night, people moving along the sidewalk with their particular urgencies. She thought about all the calls still in progress right now, crossing the country on the wires, all those voices traveling in the dark toward the people who needed to hear them. She thought about Victor Marsh in Davenport, Iowa, tonight, what he was doing, whether he was sitting in his truck beside the river, whether the cold had come there too. She thought she hoped someone had called him this week, his daughter perhaps, with some news about the baby, something small and good and new, some reason to say, tell me everything.

The bus came and she got on.
