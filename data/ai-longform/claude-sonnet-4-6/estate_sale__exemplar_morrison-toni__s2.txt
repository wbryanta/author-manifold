The tables went first, which she had expected. Folding tables always went first, the auctioneers told her, because people were practical before they were sentimental, and practicality required a surface on which to put things. Rita watched the man with the gray beard and the green suspenders lift her mother's kitchen table—the one with the cigarette burn shaped like a crescent moon near the left leg, the one where she had done every homework assignment from second grade through high school, the one where her mother had sat in the blue hours before dawn with a cup of coffee and whatever private thing she carried into the day with her—watched him lift it and carry it through the front door without a word of what it was.

Seven dollars. She had priced it at seven dollars because the auctioneer woman said anything above ten for a kitchen table this old would make people put it down and walk to the next item and keep walking. Price it to move, he had said, meaning the table, meaning the chairs, meaning the sideboard with the broken hinge, meaning all of it. Price everything in this house to move, as though the furniture were a problem the house was tired of having.

And it was moving. That was the thing. The house was emptying out by two in the afternoon and the people were coming through in a steady stream, not urgent, not rude, just steady and purposeful the way weather is purposeful, and Rita stood near the door to the back bedroom with a little steel lockbox for the money and watched them come. A woman in a barn coat took the reading lamp. A young couple, young enough that they had probably never owned anything that wasn't chosen from a catalog, came and went twice, the second time taking the wingback chair that had lived in the corner of the living room for as long as Rita could remember, the chair her mother called simply the good chair in a tone that made clear no other chair would aspire to that name.

Twelve dollars for the good chair.

The couple carried it out together, laughing at the awkwardness of it, their arms around the thing like it was a drunk friend, and she thought: they don't know. They couldn't know. Not that knowing would have stopped them or should have. A chair was a chair. That was the point of this whole day—the whole merciful and brutal point.

Her mother had been dead eleven weeks. The death was not a surprise. The dying had been long and visible and they had all watched it arrive the way you watch a slow train from a distance, its light growing, knowing exactly what was coming and being unable to look away anyway. Rita's sister Delvina had come up from Columbus for the funeral and stayed three days and then gone back to her life with the kind of decisiveness that was either a coping mechanism or a character flaw and probably both, and it had been left to Rita to manage the house because Rita had never gone anywhere. This was not a resentment. It was a fact with some resentment in it, the way a creek has some mud.

A man bought the blender for a dollar fifty. He held it up and turned it and asked if it worked and Rita said she believed it did and he paid and left. Simple as that. That was the thing about estate sales. Everybody knew what they were there for and nobody was lying about it and for a day, just one day, all the circling and the softness that people wrapped around their needs fell away and they came in and said I want that and paid for it and took it with them. There was something almost restful about it.

She had tried to feel more. She had tried to feel what Delvina cried about on the phone from Columbus, that weeping that sounded like water being wrung from cloth, genuine and also somehow performed for an audience that included Rita. Rita had not cried like that. She had cried once at the funeral home when the man in the blue tie had shown her the caskets and she had stood looking at them and thought, with absolute unwilled clarity, that her mother would have found this funny. The prices. The upsell. The way the man in the blue tie said the word dignity as though it were a product with a warranty. And it was that, the laughter inside the grief, that had made her cry, briefly, before she composed herself and pointed to the mid-range model and said that one and meant it.

She went now into the kitchen because she needed a glass of water and because a woman and her teenage daughter were looking at the contents of the living room with that considering look people got, the look that weighed the object not against its price but against its fate in a new house, a new life, and Rita did not want to watch it. The kitchen was nearly empty now, the table gone, the chairs gone, just the appliances that didn't sell at these things and the curtains she had decided at the last minute she could not put a price on and so had taken down and folded and put in the back of her car that morning before the doors opened. Her mother had made those curtains. Not sewn—her mother was no seamstress—but had made them in the sense that she had found the fabric on a clearance table at the fabric store on Route 9 and brought it home and looked at it for two weeks before taking it to Mrs. Aurelio down the street who charged fifteen dollars to hem anything. Fifteen dollars per panel, four panels, sixty dollars, which was a lot for curtains in a house where money was always the question underneath the other questions.

Rita drank her water standing at the sink and looked out the window at the backyard. The yard was the same. Whatever happened inside the house did not change the yard. The yew hedge her father had planted thirty years ago and maintained with a religious attention that was the only religion she had ever seen him practice, the birdbath that no bird had used in living memory but which her mother kept full of clean water as a matter of principle, the rusted gate that led to the alley, everything still in the October light, still and unremarkable and hers, still hers, though the house would sell in thirty days to whoever the realtor found and then it would be theirs, and after that the yard would become what they made of it.

She was thinking about her mother's hands.

She had not meant to think about them but the sink was there and the water was there and she had watched her mother at this sink so many times—washing, wringing, holding her hands under the hot water in winter because the cold got into her knuckles and would not leave—that the sink was just a shape her mother's hands had made. Brown and capable hands, thickened at the knuckles, the left one with a scar on the thumb from the mandoline slicer she had used exactly once. Her mother had not been a woman who dwelled on injury but she had kept that scar a long time. Rita herself had it in a photograph she hadn't looked at in years, a Christmas photograph, her mother handing someone a dish, the scar catching the light.

She rinsed her glass and put it on the counter and went back to the living room.

The woman in the barn coat was back and was now looking at the last bookshelf, the tall narrow one that had been in the hallway but which they had moved to the living room for the sale. She had her head tilted to read the spines of the books they hadn't sold individually. Rita had priced the books at fifty cents each and most had gone in the first hour to a small hunched man with wire-rimmed glasses who had filled two paper grocery bags without apparent selection, as though he were harvesting. Now there were perhaps thirty books left, miscellaneous and slightly defeated looking, and the woman was pulling one out.

"Would you take a dollar for all of them?" she said, not looking up.

Rita thought about this. The books were priced at fifty cents each and there were thirty of them and the math said fifteen dollars, but the day was getting later and she would have to box up whatever didn't sell and carry it somewhere and a dollar for a problem solved was not the worst transaction.

"Sure," she said.

The woman nodded, still reading the back of whatever she'd pulled out. Her daughter came over and immediately began pulling books too, reading nothing, just pulling and looking at covers the way teenage girls looked at everything, looking for the one thing that meant something to them personally and rejecting the rest without apology.

"This one's good," the daughter said, holding up a copy of something with a blue cover.

"How do you know, you haven't read it," said the woman.

"The cover," said the daughter, as if this were obvious.

Rita took their dollar. She watched the daughter tuck the book with the blue cover under her arm like it belonged to her, which it did now, and she thought: that book sat on my mother's shelf for thirty years and my mother never opened it and now this girl is going to take it home to—where, somewhere—and maybe read it and maybe not, but the book will be in a different house by nightfall, in a different life, and what my mother's house never gave it, some other house might. Or might not. You couldn't know. That was the mercy of objects, that they outlasted knowing.

By four o'clock the house had the feeling of a place that had been ransacked politely. The floors were clear and you could see where the furniture had been by the slight impressions in the carpet, the clean rectangles and circles where things had stood so long they had changed the pile. These were the last records of the arrangement her mother had chosen, and they would last maybe a week before the carpet forgot. Rita walked the rooms. She did this slowly, not performing grief but not fleeing it either, just walking through the empty rooms the way you read a document before you sign it. Kitchen. Hallway. Living room. The room that had been her mother's bedroom, in which the bed had sold for forty dollars to the gray-bearded man who had also taken the kitchen table, so that he had essentially taken the whole architecture of her mother's daily life, the place she ate and the place she slept, and carried it away in a truck that was probably already down the highway.

The bedroom had a closet. She had not opened the closet during the sale because the closet held her mother's clothes and she had told herself those weren't for sale, those were for the church donation, and this was true but it was also true that she had not been able to open the closet door all morning. She stood in front of it now.

Her mother had worn a size sixteen for thirty years, then a fourteen at the end when the dying thinned her, and the clothes in this closet would be a record of both. House dresses. The good coat, dark green, bought for her brother Marcus's wedding in 1987. Two church suits in navy and gray. A housecoat printed with small yellow flowers that Rita associated with every sick day of her childhood—every fever broken in that housecoat, every nightmare talked back by it, every skinned knee and disappointed birthday made smaller by that yellow pattern. She knew it was in there. She could almost smell it. Ivory soap and something else, something that was her mother's own, that had no name but a place in the body where memory sits beneath language.

She opened the closet.

The housecoat was on the third hanger.

She stood and looked at it. She did not take it down. She stood with one hand on the door and looked at it for a long time and the light in the room changed slightly as the sun moved behind the tree outside the window and then moved again, and she thought about her mother putting this on, the specific motion of it, the way her mother's arms went into sleeves, the way she'd belt a housecoat with a kind of authority, as though the house were something worth being dressed for. Her mother had believed in being presentable. Not for other people—or not only—but as a matter of self-regard so deep it had no need to explain itself. You kept yourself. You kept your house. Not because the world was watching but because you were watching, because you were the one who had to live inside the life you made.

Rita had not thought she was her mother's daughter in this. She had spent considerable portions of her adult life being specifically not her mother, which was one kind of love. But she kept a clean kitchen. She could not sleep in an unmade bed. She had her own version of the yellow housecoat, which was a particular green cardigan she wore at home and nowhere else, and she had never connected these things until right now in this empty bedroom with her hand on the door.

She took the housecoat off the hanger.

She didn't fold it. She held it, and this was not theater, this was not the weeping her sister did on the phone, this was just a woman holding a piece of cloth in a house that was still, for twenty-eight more days, hers to stand in. The cloth smelled like the back of the closet, like cedar and time, and beneath that, faint as a rumor, something else.

She heard the front door.

She had told the auctioneer woman the sale ended at four and it was four and there should be no one coming in, but there was the sound of the door and then footsteps on the wood floor of the hallway, tentative footsteps, the kind of footsteps that knew they were in someone else's house.

Rita draped the housecoat over her arm and went to the hallway.

It was an old man, easily eighty, a man she didn't know by name but knew by face, the face of a certain kind of Ohio man from a certain generation, weathered and organized around a fundamental seriousness of purpose. He had on a good hat and a jacket that was too light for October. He was looking at the walls, which were still hung with a few pictures she hadn't priced—she had not been able to price the pictures either—and he seemed uncertain now, now that he was in the door, about why he had come.

"We're closed," she said, not unkindly.

He turned toward her. "I know it," he said. "I'm sorry for your loss."

"Thank you."

"I knew your mother," he said. "From before, I mean. From when she first came here." He said this with a precision that suggested he meant before as a specific place in time with a border. Her mother had come to this town in 1961, eighteen years old, from a small city in Tennessee that she almost never talked about. Rita knew almost nothing about her mother before 1961 except what she could infer, which was not the same as knowing.

"She was—" the man started. He stopped. He looked at the pictures on the wall as though they might help him. "She was something," he said, and this was inadequate and he knew it and his face showed that he knew it. "When she first came here people weren't always—" He stopped again.

"I know," Rita said.

He nodded. The inadequacy of the conversation seemed to cost him something. He had come here with something to say about her mother and the thing he'd come to say was still in him, undeliverable, the way certain things stayed permanently inside the people who carried them, not for lack of wanting to let them out but for lack of the exact right opening.

"She told me once," he said, working at it, "that she didn't come here to be liked. She said she came here to stay." He let that settle. "I thought that was about the truest thing I ever heard a person say."

Rita looked at him.

She had not heard this. In fifty-some years of being her mother's daughter she had not heard this particular piece of her mother and here it was in an old man in a too-light jacket in an empty hallway, offered to her without price or condition. She came here to stay. Yes, she thought. Yes, that was exactly right. That was the whole thing. Not courage, not defiance, not even stubbornness in any simple sense but the pure deep decision to remain, and the remaining done so completely that it looked like soil, like something that had always been there.

"Thank you," she said, and she meant it the way she had not meant much that day, with the whole surface of herself.

He nodded once and looked at his hat in his hands and put it back on his head and let himself out the front door, and Rita stood in the hallway holding her mother's housecoat and listening to the house.

This was what it sounded like now. This was its voice without furniture to absorb it, without the presence of people moving and buying and carrying things away. It was the voice of a house that had done its work and gone quiet, the voice of floors and walls that had held a life so long they were shaped by it the way water shapes stone, and the shaping stayed even after the water moved on.

She would not cry. She had cried once, at the funeral home, and that had been enough, or it had been what it was. She felt something larger than crying, some condition of the chest that was not about release. She came here to stay. And stayed forty years and raised three children who moved away and died in a house that smelled like cedar and time and something that had no name. Paid sixty dollars for curtains and bought a blender that worked.

Rita folded the housecoat over her arm and walked through each room one last time, turning off the lights. They had not needed the lights at noon; they needed them now, in the late October afternoon when the shadows organized themselves against the windows. She turned them off one by one and each room that went dark behind her gave back the last of the day's light through its windows, gray-gold and level, the light of a season that had decided.

At the front door she paused. She stood in the doorway with the housecoat and the lockbox and looked back at the empty living room where the carpet still held the shapes of what had been there, and she thought about the couple with the wingback chair, the way they had laughed carrying it, the way it was a good chair and would be a good chair in their house too, in their unimaginable life. She thought about the hunched man with his grocery bags of books. She thought about the man with the green suspenders somewhere with her mother's kitchen table in his house, setting things on it, wiping it down, maybe running his thumb along the crescent burn without knowing what it was, without needing to know. The objects were dispersed now into lives she would never see and this was not a loss she could grieve, or not only. It was also just the world, doing what it did, which was taking things apart and distributing them and turning nothing into nothing but doing it slowly, doing it across many hands, leaving traces everywhere and in everyone of what had passed through.

She locked the door behind her.

The key was still warm from her hand when she held it, stood on her mother's porch in her mother's October, and then she put it in her coat pocket and went down the steps to her car.
