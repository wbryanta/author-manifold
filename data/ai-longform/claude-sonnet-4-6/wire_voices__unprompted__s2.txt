The voices came to her like weather. That was the only way Miriam could explain it to herself, lying awake in the apartment above the hardware store on Clement Street, listening to the foghorns drag their low notes through the dark. You did not choose weather. Weather arrived and moved through you and you were changed in ways too small to measure until one morning you caught yourself standing at the window and the light was different than it had ever been before.

She had been connecting calls for Pacific Bell since 1961, which meant that by 1968 she had heard approximately everything. She had connected a man weeping so hard he could not speak his own name while she waited with the patience of someone who has learned that silence can be a kind of room, a place to stand inside. She had connected a woman in Sacramento who called her mother in Honolulu every Sunday at precisely eight o'clock and said, for seven years running, nothing that Miriam had not heard before: the weather, the children, the small betrayals of the body. She had connected men calling their offices from motel rooms whose voices carried the particular flat quality of people trying very hard not to feel. She had connected soldiers, before the war had grown large enough to be the war, calling from Fort Ord, their voices young and smelling, somehow, of cut grass and borrowed confidence. She had connected a man who said I love you to a number in Portland that rang twelve times and went unanswered and she had wanted to say, to no one, he loves someone in Portland, and this seemed to her important, whether or not Portland answered.

The voices did not usually stay with her. They moved through the switchboard and out into the country like radio waves, touching everything lightly and being absorbed. But some voices found a frequency in her that resonated. She was not sentimental about this. It was simply a fact of the nervous system, the way certain music opened something in the chest that other music left closed.

The two voices had been coming to her board for eleven weeks. She knew this because she had begun, without fully deciding to, making small pencil marks in the margin of her crossword book, one mark each Thursday evening when she connected them, the calls always coming between seven and seven-thirty, always long-distance from the 415 to a number in the 504, which was New Orleans, and she had never been to New Orleans but she knew from the voices that it was warm there, that the air was different, that a person could be a certain kind of person in New Orleans that San Francisco would not allow.

The first voice — the calling voice, the San Francisco voice — belonged to a man she thought of as being perhaps fifty, perhaps a difficult fifty, a fifty that had been built out of interesting failures. His voice had what she could only call mass. It occupied the line the way certain people occupied rooms. He always identified himself to the operator with a slight formality that she found touching, as though he wanted to ensure that the machinery of connection understood it was dealing with a person and not merely a transaction. He said, I'd like to place a call to New Orleans, Louisiana. Not I need to call, not I want to make a call. I'd like. The conditional politeness of someone for whom the world had not always been accommodating.

She had learned, over years of connecting, to understand the architecture of a voice. The San Francisco voice was a voice that had been broken and set badly and broken again and set better the second time, and you could hear both the damage and the repair in it. It was a voice that knew about 3 a.m. It was a voice that drank, she thought, not desperately but steadily, the way a man waters a garden he is not sure he deserves. When he said his number — the number in the 504, which she could have recited in her sleep by the sixth week — he said it with a care that verged on tenderness, as though the digits themselves were something he did not want to damage.

The second voice was a woman's. The New Orleans voice. She answered on the second ring, always, which Miriam had come to understand was not coincidence but a kind of declaration. You are expected. The New Orleans voice was lower than you might expect from a woman, lower and slower, moving with the patience of a river that has decided not to hurry. There was humor in it, deeply embedded, the way gold is embedded in rock, requiring some labor to reach but unmistakably there once you arrived. When she said hello it sounded like she was saying it for the first time, like the word had not yet been worn smooth by use, and Miriam would connect the call and hold the line for just a moment — only a moment, only what was necessary — and she would hear the man's voice change when the woman said hello. It opened. That was the only word. A door opened in his voice.

Miriam was required to disconnect once the parties were connected. She was not required to listen. She did not listen. She was rigorous about this, because she was rigorous by nature, because she had grown up in a family where privacy was treated as a form of dignity, where closing a door meant something, and she believed in closing doors. But she held the line for the moment of connection, which was her job, which was necessary, and in that moment she heard the shape of what passed between them and the shape was enough to understand.

She had been married for four years to a man named Dennis who sold insurance and was kind and ultimately insufficient, not because of any particular failing but because of a general quality in him of existing at a slight remove from his own life, as though he were watching himself from a distance and finding the spectacle moderately interesting. They had separated without drama in 1964, which had seemed to Miriam like a mercy and had felt, in the years since, like a different kind of wound, one that came from the absence of drama, from the discovery that a marriage could end as quietly as a conversation that simply runs out of things to say. She was thirty-four now. She lived above the hardware store. She did her crosswords and she connected her calls and she walked to the park on weekends and she read novels by women who had died too young, which she found consoling for reasons she could not have fully articulated.

She was not a woman given to fantasy. She had no fantasy about the San Francisco voice. She did not wish herself into New Orleans, into the role of the woman who answered on the second ring and said hello like a first word. She understood, or thought she understood, the precise nature of her attachment: it was the attachment of a person who has been trusted, however unknowingly, with something true. Most of what passed through her board was dressed up in one way or another. People called each other and performed themselves. But these two, on Thursday evenings — they had apparently ceased performing. She did not know their names. She did not know their histories. She knew only what leaked from the quality of their voices in the moment of connection, which was enough to constitute, she believed, a kind of knowing.

By the eighth week she had begun to dread Thursdays with the particular sweet dread of anticipation, had begun arriving at her shift slightly early, had begun thinking during the earlier hours of the shift — the hours crowded with business calls and family calls and calls that were purely logistical, the necessary traffic of people maintaining their claims on one another across distance — she had begun thinking of the Thursday-evening call as a fixed point in her week around which other things arranged themselves. She knew this was disproportionate. She was not troubled by it. She was curious about herself, the way she was sometimes curious about other things — the way fog moved, the way light changed color as it traveled through old glass — but she was not troubled.

On the ninth week the call came at seven-twelve and the San Francisco voice, when he identified himself, sounded tired in a new way, not the habitual tiredness she had come to recognize but a tiredness that sat closer to the surface, a tiredness that had not been there the week before, and when the New Orleans voice answered and said hello, the door that usually opened in his voice opened only partway, and she disconnected and sat with this for the remainder of the evening in the way that you sit with a change in the weather, noting it, uncertain of what it portends.

On the tenth week everything was as it had always been and she was relieved, which told her something about herself that she filed away.

On the eleventh week the New Orleans voice answered and said hello and the door opened completely and she disconnected and made her small pencil mark and went home and slept easily.

On the twelfth week, which was a Thursday in late October, which was an evening when the fog had come in early and the streetlights on Geary had halos, she sat at her board and worked through the early hours with her usual competence and waited, without appearing to wait, for seven o'clock. The calls came through her: a man in the financial district connecting to his broker in New York; a woman calling a hospital in Sacramento; a teenager, she thought, calling somewhere in the Valley, the voice soft with embarrassment or longing. The clock moved. Seven o'clock came and passed.

At seven-seventeen, the San Francisco voice came on the line. She recognized it before he finished his first word. She had a system of recognition that bypassed the conscious mind entirely.

I'd like to place a call to New Orleans, Louisiana.

Of course, she said. She always said of course. She realized, hearing herself say it, that she said it to him with a slightly different inflection than she used for other callers, a slightly warmer inflection, and she wondered if he had ever noticed, and she knew he had not, because she was not part of what he experienced when he made this call. She was the mechanism. She was the weather through which he traveled to reach somewhere else.

She dialed the 504 number and it rang once and it rang twice and she waited for the second ring to resolve into the pickup, into the hello that always sounded like a first word, and the phone rang a third time, and a fourth, and a fifth, and she held, because sometimes there were delays, sometimes lines were crossed or connections were slow, and the phone rang a sixth time and a seventh, and she heard the San Francisco voice breathing on the line, heard the quality of his breathing change as the rings accumulated, the way a person's breathing changes when they begin to understand something they are not ready to understand. At the tenth ring she said, I'm sorry, sir, there doesn't appear to be an answer.

He said nothing for a moment. She had the distinct impression of a man standing very still, the way animals stand still when something in the air has changed.

Could you try again, he said. Please.

The please. She would think about that please for a long time after, the way it sat on the end of the sentence without weight, without urgency, the way it was simply there, small and necessary, the please of a person who has learned that he cannot compel what he most needs and who asks anyway, from habit or from hope or from the inability to stop.

She redialed. The number rang ten times. Twelve. She counted. She always counted, though there was no professional reason to, but she needed to be able to tell him something definite, something with a number in it, because numbers were a kind of company.

I'm sorry, she said. No answer.

He said, Thank you, and disconnected.

She sat for a moment with the dead line.

The calls that followed — there were four more hours of shift remaining — she handled with her usual competence but she was not entirely present. Some part of her was following the San Francisco voice out into the fog on Geary, watching him walk, if he walked, or drive, if he drove, watching him carry the unanswered phone back into whatever room he had called from, watching him set it down, watching him decide what to do with the silence that had answered him. She was aware that she was doing this and she did not try to stop herself.

She had no information. This was the fact she kept returning to, working at it the way you work at a knot that cannot be loosened but must be worked at anyway for the sake of having done something. She did not know what the silence meant. She did not know what the woman in New Orleans was to the man in San Francisco or what absence could mean in the context of what she understood their connection to be. She knew only the quality of his voice when the door opened, which was the quality of a man who has found, perhaps late, perhaps after much searching and much not-finding, a place that received him without conditions. She knew the quality of the New Orleans voice saying hello, which was the quality of someone who has, against the odds and perhaps against their own previous experience, allowed themselves to expect.

There were many explanations. The most common explanations for unanswered phones were banal and innocent: a trip, an errand, an evening out, the simple failure of proximity, the body being required in some other place at some other task. She told herself this. She was a practical woman and she told herself practical things.

But there was another knowledge, one that lived below the practical, and she had learned, in thirty-four years, to distinguish between these two levels of knowing. The practical knowledge spoke in clear sentences. The other knowledge spoke in the quality of a voice breathing on a line while an unanswered phone rings for the twelfth time.

She finished her shift and walked home through the fog. The hardware store below her apartment had a light on in the back, which meant Mr. Abruzzio was doing his inventory, which he did every Thursday evening, and she found this comforting in the way that the habits of others are comforting, the evidence that the world continues to perform its necessary maintenance. She climbed the stairs and sat at her kitchen table and did not do her crossword. She sat with her hands around a cup of tea that had gone cold and she thought about a word she had been trying to find for some time, for something she knew from her work, some specific experience of being adjacent to a connection between people, of being the means by which two people find each other across distance, and she supposed the word was just that: adjacent. She had been adjacent to something. She had not been part of it. She had been the weather through which it moved.

She was not sad, exactly. Sadness had a specific shape, a specific weight. What she felt was something without clean edges, something more like the feeling she got from novels about women who had died young, which was not sadness for those women but something more complex, a recognition of the cost of things, the way that what is most alive in us is also most susceptible, the way that the door that opens is also the door that can be closed, and sometimes the closing is not a choice but simply what happens, the way weather happens, the way fog moves in from the ocean and takes the city and the city does not resist.

She thought she might call her sister in the morning. She had not called her sister in some time, for no particular reason, for the accumulating reason of days. Her sister lived in Fresno and had three children and a husband who was a high school principal and was genuinely kind, and her sister was perhaps not the most interesting person Miriam had ever known but she was the person who knew Miriam from before Miriam was any of the things she had become, which was its own form of knowledge, irreplaceable, the kind of knowledge that only time could make. She would call in the morning. She would be the San Francisco voice. Her sister would answer on the second ring or the third and would say hello, and the hello would open something, and this was available to her, this was a door she could walk through, and she had not been walking through it, had been living instead in an adjacency she had told herself was enough.

She poured the cold tea down the sink. The foghorn sounded over the water, that long low note that had kept her company for years, that she had stopped hearing as sound and heard instead as presence, the way you stop hearing the particular sounds of a place you live and they become simply the texture of being there. She heard it tonight as sound. It moved through the apartment and she let it.

She tried to imagine what the San Francisco voice was doing now, in whatever room he had placed his call from, and she tried to imagine the room but she had nothing to build it from except the quality of his voice, which gave her: something orderly but not cold, something that had been arranged by a person who understood the weight of objects, who chose what to keep. She tried to imagine him and she could not fully imagine him, which was correct, which was as it should be, because he was not hers to imagine. He had never been hers. She had been adjacent. She had been the means. She had connected the call.

But she had also been, she thought, something else, something she did not have the right word for, something that the word witness came close to but did not quite reach. She had been present at the edge of something true. And if the line went unanswered now, if New Orleans did not pick up, if whatever had been between them was altered or gone — she did not know this, she reminded herself, she did not know any of this — but if it was, then she had been present at the edge of something that had been true, which was not nothing. Which was, she thought, quite a lot. Which was perhaps what was available to a person who lived as she lived, who did the work she did, who had learned that meaning did not require ownership, that a thing could matter to you without belonging to you, that a door opening was a real event whether you walked through it or not.

She turned off the kitchen light. In the dark she stood for a moment with her hand still on the switch, listening to the foghorn, listening to the building settle around her, listening, below everything, for some frequency she could not name, some transmission still moving through the dark air of the country, two voices finding each other across the enormous indifferent distance, or not finding each other, the line ringing out into a room where no one stood to answer. Either way, the signal had been sent. Either way, she had been the one to send it.

She went to bed. The fog moved through the city. In the morning, she decided, she would call Fresno.
