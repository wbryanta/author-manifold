He had not slept the night before the crossing and so he was already half inside some other world when he drove the car onto the ferry at dusk, the deckhand waving him forward with that bored authority of someone who has guided the living and their vehicles into the hold of a ship ten thousand times and felt nothing particular about any of it. The lower deck smelled of diesel and cold saltwater and the exhaust of the car ahead, a green sedan with a cracked rear light that bled a thin red seam into the dark. Martin switched off the engine. He sat for a moment listening to it tick and cool. Then he got out and pulled his jacket from the back seat and walked toward the stairwell, his footsteps loud on the steel grating, the whole structure shuddering faintly as more cars entered behind him.

The crossing would take nine hours. He had made it once before, twenty years ago with their father, going the other direction, going south, which had felt at the time like going toward everything the world was holding in reserve for them. He and Joel had stood at the stern rail watching the island recede and their father had put one hand on each of their shoulders and said something that was immediately taken by the wind. Martin had not asked him to repeat it. He had been seventeen and already in the habit of pretending to understand things he had not heard.

He went up to the passenger deck and found a seat by the window. Outside, the dock crews were pulling the lines and a woman in an orange vest stood on the pier watching the gap of grey water widen between the hull and the dock's edge with the look of someone who does this every evening and has still not decided what to feel about departure. Martin watched her until she was too small to distinguish from the pier itself. Then the strait opened up ahead and the ferry's lights came on and the windows turned to dark mirrors and he saw his own face looking back at him, a little older than he expected, with the expression of a man who has not yet decided what story he is in.

A family settled at the table across from him. A boy of perhaps nine arranged and rearranged a collection of small cars on the tabletop with the fierce concentration of someone solving a proof. His mother looked out the window. His father slept already, his head against the glass, his mouth slightly open in a way that suggested complete trust in the world, or complete exhaustion, which were perhaps the same thing.

Martin thought about the phone call. It had come four days ago on a Tuesday morning while he was standing in his kitchen in the act of pouring coffee, the kind of ordinary moment that afterward you remember with strange precision because it is the last moment before the thing that changed everything. A woman from the provincial coroner's office. Methodical, not unkind. There had been a body found on the shore of the north island, she said, and in the jacket there was a wallet and the wallet contained a card with a name and a phone number and the name was his and the number was his and so she was calling. She explained what she knew, which was not much, and she used the word *remains* which was the word they used, he understood, because *body* was somehow too complete, too much like a person who had only just stopped, and *remains* was more honest about what time and cold water did.

He had stood there with the coffee growing cold in the cup and asked her the questions you asked. She had answered them in the way they answered, which was careful and equivocal, and at the end she had said that a formal identification would be required, from someone who knew the deceased. That word too. The *deceased*. As if naming the category was a kind of comfort.

He had not seen Joel in eleven years.

Before that the years themselves had grown irregular, stretched thin by distance and intention, visits becoming occasional and then rarer and finally assembled mostly by obligation — their father's illness, their father's death, the estate which had taken three months and most of what goodwill remained between them to settle. The last time Martin had seen his brother was at a gas station outside Sudbury on a January afternoon, both of them passing through in opposite directions on the Trans-Canada in a coincidence so improbable that when Joel's truck had pulled in across the pumps Martin had thought for a moment he was imagining it. They had shaken hands. They had stood in the wind and cold and spoken for perhaps twelve minutes about the road conditions and about nothing. Then Joel had gotten back into his truck and pulled out onto the highway, heading north.

Martin had watched the truck until it disappeared and then stood for a moment longer looking at the empty place in the grey distance where it had been, the way you stand after a magician's trick trying to locate the mechanism of the illusion.

He did not know, sitting on the ferry now, whether the body was Joel's. The coroner's office had told him only that the man found was roughly the right age and that the face was not, due to the time the body had been in the water, easily recognizable from photographs. They had a photograph. They had a name from the wallet. But photographs and wallets were not, the woman had said carefully, conclusive.

He thought about the word *conclusive*. The way it implied that somewhere there was a conclusion waiting, some final coherent sentence the world was trying to form, if only the evidence could be properly assembled.

The boy across from him had stopped arranging his cars and fallen asleep slumped against his mother's arm. She was still looking out the window at the dark water. She had the stillness of a person who has learned to exist in transit, in the interstitial hours, the hours that don't quite count. Martin found himself grateful for her presence without knowing why. Perhaps only that she was looking in the same direction he would have been looking if there were anything to see.

He got up and went to the small cafeteria at the end of the lounge and bought a coffee from a machine and stood drinking it by the window looking out at the running lights of a vessel heading the opposite way, south, toward where he had come from. Two ships passing was a phrase people used so often it had become almost transparent with use, and yet there was still something true in the image that the phrase managed to survive its own overuse — the brief proximity of two journeys that would never intersect again, each carrying its cargo of the particular and irreducible. He thought that Joel would have found this observation unbearably pretentious. Joel had been funny about sentimentality, which was a word he used to describe anything that tried to make meaning from coincidence or proximity. *You're always trying to make it mean something*, he had said once, and Martin had said, *Isn't that what people do*, and Joel had said, *No. That's what you do. Most people just go on.*

This had been at a kitchen table somewhere. Their father's house, maybe, or Joel's apartment in the north end of the city he had lived in for a few years in his thirties. Martin could not remember which. The memory had the character of many memories he had of Joel, a kind of fluorescent brightness in some details and a complete blankness in others, as if certain parts had been exposed too long and others not at all.

What he remembered most clearly was the particular way his brother had of laughing at something without wanting to let on he was laughing — a slight tightening of the left side of his face, a compression of the lips, the look of a man swallowing something. It was a private laugh, not unfriendly, but interior. A laugh that said: I have seen this before from the inside.

He carried the coffee back to his seat. The family were all sleeping now, the father still with his mouth slightly open, the boy in a complicated sprawl across two seats with his arms flung out in that lavish unconscious surrender children had in sleep. Martin thought about his daughter, who was nine and who slept the same way, who he had called the night before the crossing and who had spoken to him for four minutes about a project at school involving the migration of birds before asking if he was okay and when he said yes had accepted this without question or elaboration, which was both the thing he loved most about her and the thing that sometimes frightened him a little.

He opened the bag he had brought and took out a folder. Inside were the things the coroner's office had sent, scanned documents, a photograph. He looked at the photograph for a long time. It had been taken from a distance, before they moved the body, and showed the shoreline and the grey rocks and a figure lying at the water's edge in a dark jacket with one arm extended toward the water as if reaching for something or having just released something. The figure's face was not visible. Martin kept looking at the photograph as though it might eventually resolve into something more legible, the way an eye chart comes gradually into focus, but it did not. What he saw was a shoreline and a figure and a dark jacket and the absolute indifference of the rocks and the cold northern light coming in low from the left. He put the photograph back in the folder and put the folder in the bag.

He had not told anyone at work where he was going. He had told his daughter's mother only that he had to take a few days, a family matter, which was a phrase capacious enough to hold most things. She had looked at him in the way she sometimes looked at him, that old measuring look, assessing whether whatever he was not saying was something she needed to know. Then she had nodded and said she would keep Lily for the week. He had thanked her. There had been a moment at the door when neither of them spoke, a small suspension, and then she had closed the door and he had walked down to his car in the early morning dark.

The strait was rougher now and the ferry rolled with a slow, heavy persistence. A few people around him had the expressions of people managing mild nausea with enormous dignity. The boy was still asleep. Martin put his head back against the seat and looked at the ceiling and thought about the last thing Joel had said to him, the last communicable thing, which was not the parting at the gas station outside Sudbury — that had involved no real words — but a phone call two years before that, in the evening, Joel calling from somewhere with a lot of ambient sound, a bar or a diner, saying without much preamble that he had been thinking about the summer they spent at their uncle's place on the shield when Martin was twelve and Joel was fifteen, the summer their parents had been having the particular difficulty that neither of them had ever been given a name for. *You remember that*, Joel had said. *You remember that dock.* And Martin had said yes, the dock, yes of course. And Joel had said, *I've been thinking about that a lot lately*, and Martin had waited for what came next but what came next had been only a brief silence and then Joel saying he had to go, someone needed the phone, and then the click and the long tone.

He had thought about that conversation many times in the years since without being able to determine what Joel had meant him to understand from it. The dock had been grey and warped and extended perhaps thirty feet out over a lake that was cold even in August. They had fished from it in the mornings and swum from it in the afternoons and at night sometimes sat out on it in the dark listening to the sound the water made against the pilings. He did not know what had been meant by returning to it. Perhaps nothing had been meant. Perhaps Joel had only wanted to say: I was somewhere in the past this evening and you were there too.

Outside the window the darkness was complete now, the horizon gone, only the ferry's own lights reflecting back from the black water. Martin found he could not sleep despite his exhaustion or perhaps because of it. There was a particular wakefulness that came from being too tired, a hyper-clarity where everything was slightly too present — the plastic smell of the seat, the vibration of the engines in the floor beneath his feet, the family across from him with their faces slack and private in sleep, the sound of the water and the hull working against each other in low industrial complaint.

He tried to think practically. Tomorrow morning the ferry would dock. A car would take him, or he would take a taxi, or he would walk if the distances were what they appeared on the map, to the building where the coroner's office kept its particular archive of the unclaimed and unresolved. He would go inside and he would speak to someone and they would take him to a room. He had been trying for four days to imagine what would happen in that room but his imagination kept stopping at the door, kept refusing the next step, which told him something about the nature of the question he was traveling toward.

Because there were two versions of the answer and he had been living, these four days, at the exact midpoint between them, equidistant from both outcomes, and in this midpoint there was a kind of suspension that he recognized was not available to him much longer.

If it was Joel then there would be the grief, which was a word too small for the thing, or perhaps just a word that had never been properly inhabited, had always been kept at a certain theoretical distance. If it was not Joel then there would be the other thing, the other version of grief, which had no proper name at all — the continued not-knowing, the continued fact of a brother who was alive somewhere in the north in the direction of which he had been heading for eleven years, alive and not in contact, living whatever life had claimed him. Both of these required a different kind of continuation. He did not know which one he was prepared for. He was not sure prepared was the right word.

He thought about what Joel had looked like at fifteen, sitting on the end of that dock at night, his feet over the edge, the darkness below them and the darkness above them equally absolute, stars overhead in the numbers that were only visible that far north, that far from the competing light of ordinary life. Joel had said something that summer, many things probably, but one that had stayed — lying on the dock boards looking up, he had said that the light from some of those stars had been traveling since before there were people to see it, that it had been going for so long through nothing and then arrived here, at this dock, at exactly the moment when there happened to be someone lying on their back looking up. He had seemed to find this funny in his private internal way, that slight compression of the lips. And then he had said, *Think about everything that didn't work out for that to work out*, and Martin had said, *That's not how probability works*, and Joel had said, *I know, I know*, still smiling at the stars.

The ferry rolled and settled. The engines changed note beneath him, lower, more resolved, and he realized they must be approaching the northern shore, that the nine hours had gone in the way time went when you were not quite present for it. Around him people were beginning to wake, to gather bags, to find their shoes. The mother across from him was touching her son's shoulder and the boy was coming up slowly out of sleep with the reluctant, aggrieved expression of someone returning from somewhere better.

Martin sat still a moment longer. Through the window the darkness had begun to separate from itself in the way darkness did before dawn, not becoming light but becoming different textures of dark, and he could make out a shoreline, a distant arrangement of lights low against the water, the island coming forward to meet them. He picked up his bag. He checked that the folder was inside. He stood and put on his jacket and stood looking out the window at the approaching shore with the feeling — not quite dread, not quite hope, but something that contained the pressure of both — of a man who has traveled a long way toward a question he has not yet learned how to ask. The ferry's horn sounded once, deep and brief, and the sound traveled out over the black water and the island received it in silence, the way it received everything, the way the north received everything, without answer, without judgment, with only its own inscrutable and enduring dark.
