The gauge at the spillway read four feet and falling when Maren drove the truck down the service road, the morning still holding its darkness like a cupped hand. She had the windows down despite the cold because she wanted to smell it first—the reservoir, the mud, the particular mineral rot of a lakebed giving itself back to air. She had done this before with other sites. Silted harbors, drained canals, the alkaline flat of a Nevada lake where a wagon wheel had stood for forty years in shallow water before the water finally left it. She knew how to read what the land remembered. What she had not done before was this.

The truck lights found the gate and she got out to unlock it, her boots sinking an inch into the clay path. August had been the worst month in the county's recorded history—not a quarter inch of rain, the kind of heat that made the air above the blacktop shimmer like something trying to escape—and September had continued the work. She had watched the news coverage from her apartment in the city with a feeling she could not name and did not try to. When the state office called and said they needed current survey data on the exposed formation, she had told them she was familiar with the area. She had not said how.

She drove the truck through the gate and left it at the end of the paved section where the old county road simply stopped, its terminus submerged for six decades before the drought, now standing in the air again, the pavement ending in a crumbled lip eight feet above the mudflat. She sat for a moment with the engine off. Light was beginning in the east, a thin seam of copper along the ridgeline. Below her, what had been the floor of Ellison Reservoir spread out in the early gray like a country seen from altitude—the shapes of things becoming visible as her eyes adjusted, long shadows thrown by what she finally understood were walls.

She had her equipment in the truck bed. The total station, the rod, the laptop in its padded case, the aerial photographs the office had pulled from the nineteen-fifty survey, three years before the valley was flooded. She did not need the photographs to know the town. She knew the town the way she knew the bones of her own hands.

Maren was forty-one years old. She had been four when they left, and she had carried the place inside her for thirty-seven years in the way that early memory works—not as narrative but as sensation, as the body's knowledge, the way a sound or a smell could return her to a kitchen she had not seen since she was too small to reach the countertop. Her mother had not come back, not for the first reunion the displaced families held at the old church camp on the ridge, not for the second. Her mother had a philosophy about the past that Maren had spent her career gently and professionally contradicting. The past was not past. It was under the water. It was waiting for the water to leave.

She climbed down the embankment using the survey rod as a walking stick, testing the mud before she committed her weight. The lakebed surface had dried to a cracked crust in the shallower areas near the old town center—she could see the pattern from above, the polygonal cracking of desiccated clay that looked from a distance like a map of somewhere else entirely. Closer, the cracks ran along what she began to understand were the lines of old streets, the clay above the former roadbeds behaving differently than the clay above the former yards and foundations, the different fill materials producing different shrinkage patterns. The town was writing itself back into legibility.

Her boots broke through the surface crust with each step, sinking two inches into the wet clay beneath. She stopped and looked east along what the aerial photograph told her was Millard Street, the main commercial thoroughfare, and what her body told her was the street she had walked holding her father's hand on Saturday mornings when he collected the mail and bought the paper and sometimes a bag of butterscotch candies that he kept in the glove box of the truck and doled out on the drive home, one per mile. The street was fifty feet wide from building face to building face, which seemed impossibly wide now, an avenue, though she remembered it as a canyon. Buildings that had been two stories showed their foundations and the first two or three courses of their masonry above the mud, the upper portions long since dissolved or relocated by six decades of water and silt. The facades were gone. What remained was structure—the bones, the grid, the logic of how people had organized themselves here before the state decided the valley was worth more as a reservoir than as a place where people lived.

She set up the total station on its tripod at what the photograph identified as the intersection of Millard and Church, the geometric center of the original townsite, and began the systematic work of recording. This was what she was good at. This was what she had made herself good at, the patient accumulation of precise data, the conversion of physical reality into numbers that other people would use to make decisions about flood planning and shoreline management and recreational access. She was good at the patience. She was good at the conversion.

But the numbers kept reaching for something else.

The township had been called Dunmore. Population eight hundred and twelve at the time of condemnation, according to the state archives—she had looked this up in the years before the drought, looked it up the way you look something up when you are not ready to admit why. Eight hundred and twelve people who had received their notices and their appraisals and their relocation payments, who had held estate sales in their yards and packed their furniture into trucks and driven up out of the valley for the last time, who had come from somewhere and made a place and then been unmade from it. She had been one of the eight hundred and twelve, though she had been too young to know it as loss. She had learned it as loss later, slowly, in the way a scar teaches you about a wound you cannot remember receiving.

The total station hummed through its initialization routine and she began shooting points, calling them into the recorder with the abbreviations she had used for twenty years—BF for building foundation, CL for centerline, EP for edge of pavement. The old street grid was regular, laid out in 1887 by a surveyor whose name she had looked up too—a man named Trout, H.W. Trout, whose meticulous plat maps were held in the county archives in a folder that smelled of cedar and old paper. She had not been able to look at those maps for very long when she found them. Something about the handwriting.

Millard Street ran east-west. Church Street ran north-south. The town had four blocks in each direction, which made it smaller than she had remembered but also more coherent—she could see it now as a complete thing, a finished sentence, whereas in memory it had been limitless, the way all childhood landscapes are limitless because the child cannot conceive of their own smallness in relation to the world. She was small in relation to it now too, a single figure in the gray light of early morning on a vast expanse of mud, the ridgeline forest dark above her on three sides, the dam wall visible to the northeast where the water still stood high and silver, sixty feet of it still backed up behind the concrete, the drought having taken forty feet and still working.

She moved north on Church Street, shooting points, and came to what the photograph confirmed was the elementary school. The foundation was more substantial here, the walls standing chest-high where the building had been built into the slope of the hill, and she stopped and put her hand on the stone. The stone was cold and wet and furred with green-gray algae. She tried to remember what this building had felt like from the inside but she had only been four when they left and she was not sure she had ever been inside the school, not as a student, only perhaps passing through a doorway once on some errand with an adult. What she remembered, or thought she remembered, was windows—high windows through which the light came at an angle that made rectangles on a wooden floor. She could not say with confidence that this memory belonged to this building and not to some other, some later building she had attended in the city to which her parents moved. Memory was not reliable in this way. This was something she had thought about for a long time.

She photographed the foundation from four angles and moved on.

The residential streets began at the north end of town where Church gave way to First and Second and the named streets that ran parallel to Millard—Oak, Elm, the aspirational American naming convention that had nothing to do with the actual trees of this particular valley, which ran to cottonwood and locust and a scrubby mountain ash that produced hard orange berries in October. She had seen photographs of these streets in the county historical society, the kind of photographs that the historical society had gathered when the flooding was announced—residents sending in their shoebox pictures, smiling in front of houses and yards with the studied normalcy of people who did not yet know how to perform loss or perhaps already knew too well. She had studied these photographs on a screen at one in the morning, enlarging faces, looking for her parents' faces and finding them once, twice, in group photographs, her father a slim dark-haired man and her mother beside him with the look she always had in photographs, the look of someone who was not quite sure she was supposed to be there.

The streets here were narrower. Fifty-foot lots. The foundations were lower, the walls barely showing above the mud, and she had to read the land more carefully now, the slight ridges where concrete footings ran, the changed texture of the soil where a basement had been. She was shooting GPS coordinates and elevations and building a point cloud that would eventually become a digital model of what was here, and she was also doing something else, something that was not in the scope of work, not billable, not technical. She was looking for a specific foundation.

The house had been at the corner of Second and Oak. She knew this from the plat map she had memorized and from the census record she had found that listed her grandparents' names and address and from a single photograph she had been given when her grandmother died, a photograph of a white clapboard house with a covered porch and a garden visible at the left edge of the frame. Her parents had moved in with her grandparents when her father was laid off from the mill—the mill that sat at the south end of town, whose own remains she had passed on the way in, the concrete floor of the main building large and flat and cracked, the remnant iron of some machinery rusted into shapes that no longer declared their function. They had been in that house from Maren's birth until the condemnation notice. She had been born in a room of that house, her mother in the habit of telling this story whenever it came up—not the hospital, in the house, there was no time, which had always seemed to Maren to be a particular kind of origin story, to arrive in the world in the house that would later be drowned.

The corner of Second and Oak was marked by a fire hydrant. She had not expected this—the hydrant standing in the mud like a civic sentinel, orange-red with rust, its bonnet and nozzle caps intact, the paint long gone but the form perfectly preserved. She stood beside it for a moment. In the archive photographs she had seen a tree at this corner, an elm she thought, though the photographs were black and white and she could not be certain of the species. The tree was not there. She looked for a stump, for a root mass extruded from the mud, and found nothing. Six decades of water had undone the tree completely, returned it to whatever a tree returns to.

The foundation was there.

It was lower than she had hoped, the walls standing only a foot above the mud surface on the street sides, higher on the interior where the basement had been. She could see the basement clearly—the water had drained from it or the mud had filled it partially, she could not tell, but what remained was a depression maybe four feet deep, roughly rectangular, twenty feet by twelve, with the concrete floor visible beneath a few inches of standing water that reflected the sky. She stood at the edge and looked down at the reflected sky.

This was where her parents had slept. Or her grandparents. She was not certain which room had been which, the house having had, according to the photograph, two stories and at least four or five rooms on the ground floor. The basement had been her grandfather's workshop—this she knew from her father, who had mentioned it once when they were repairing something together, her father saying his father had built things in the basement, had a workbench there, and she had filed this information away without knowing she was filing it.

She set up the total station again and began shooting the foundation, doing the work of recording it with the same precision she brought to the other foundations. The building footprint was thirty feet by thirty-six, she estimated, larger than the photograph had made it seem, but the photograph had been taken from across the street and compressed everything. She shot eighteen points to define the foundation perimeter and then moved inside it, stepping carefully over the wall, her boot sending a small avalanche of clay down into the basement depression. The floor inside the foundation was a different texture—she crouched and pressed her palm to the surface and felt the grit of what might have been plaster, incorporated into the mud. Or it might have been anything. She could not know. She was not a chemist or an archaeologist. She was a surveyor.

What a surveyor does is measure. What a surveyor does is convert the physical world into a system of numbers and coordinates that can be transmitted, recorded, reproduced. What a surveyor does is resist the impulse to interpret, to project, to assign meaning to a datum. The datum is the datum. The elevation is the elevation. The point is where the point is.

She measured for two more hours, moving methodically from foundation to foundation, filling the recorder with coordinates, occasionally taking notes about materials and conditions in the field book. The sun came up properly and dried the top surface of the mud so that her boots no longer broke through, and she was grateful for this, though the mud had already given her boots and shins a gray coating that made her look partially dissolved into the landscape. She photographed the mill floor. She photographed the concrete pad of what had been the filling station at the south end of Millard, the island where the pumps had stood still visible as two circular stains in the concrete. She photographed the stone wall of the old town hall, three courses still standing, the lintel stone still in place over what had been the front door, the door and the wall above it and everything above gone, only the suggestion of an entrance remaining.

She ate the sandwich she had packed sitting on the remnant of a stone wall she could not identify—a garden wall perhaps, or the low foundation of a shed—and looked at the town around her. She had been working for four hours and the light was good now, mid-morning light, and she could see clearly what the town was and what it was not. It was not what she had imagined, which was to say it was both more and less. Less in that the structures were so reduced, so comprehensively unmade by their decades underwater, that nothing rose higher than her shoulder, nothing gave the impression of enclosure or shelter, nothing was recognizable as a room. More in that the grid was so clear, the logic of the town so legible in the exposed foundations, the way the streets and lots and buildings organized themselves into a comprehensible plan. From the right angle, standing at an intersection and looking down a street, you could see the town as a town—not the town as it had been, but the grammar of the town, the underlying structure by which people had organized themselves in relation to each other.

She thought about her mother, who had not come back to the reunions, who had the philosophy about the past. Her mother was seventy-three now and lived in a apartment four blocks from Maren's own apartment and sometimes they walked to the farmer's market together on Sundays. Her mother never mentioned Dunmore by name. She referred to it, when she referred to it, as the valley, as in when we were in the valley, and she did not say this often. Maren had stopped asking. There were ways of surviving loss that required not speaking of the lost thing, and she had come to understand this in her mother even when she could not share it. Her own way was the opposite: her own way was to learn the topography of every place, to make the physical world submit to measurement, to insist that things that had existed continued to exist in some form that could be known.

She finished her sandwich and walked back down Church Street to the intersection with Millard and stood in the center of the crossing where she had set up the total station that morning. She turned in a slow circle. The butterscotch candy in the glove box. The high windows making rectangles of light on a wooden floor. Her grandfather's tools on a pegboard above a workbench she had never seen. The name H.W. Trout in brown ink on a plat map, the careful notation of dimensions, the surveyor who had come before her and made the town legible in a different way, measuring it into existence where she was now measuring it back out.

There was a boy, she suddenly remembered. A boy who had lived two houses from her grandparents' house, whose name she had never known, with whom she had played at some point during the period of the move—she had a memory of a game involving chalk, or a stick used in place of chalk, drawing lines on pavement, some game with rules she could not reconstruct. She had no other memory of this boy. She did not know if he had been five years old or eight, whether he had been sad about leaving or indifferent. She did not know where he had gone. He would be in his mid-forties now, if he was anywhere. He might be anywhere. He might have become any kind of person. He was somewhere above the old waterline, that much was certain, because everyone who had been here had been displaced above the waterline, scattered into the surrounding country and beyond. Eight hundred and twelve people finding new addresses, new routines, new streets to commit to their bodies' memories. The town they had left continued to exist in eight hundred and twelve different interiors, in differing states of preservation, degrading at different rates, occasionally surfacing in response to some sensory trigger—the smell of mud and cold stone, say, or the particular quality of light in a valley in the morning, or the sight of a rust-red fire hydrant standing in a flat expanse of gray.

She broke down the total station and carried it back to the truck. Two trips. She scraped the worst of the mud from her boots on the crumbled edge of the pavement and got into the truck and sat with her hands on the wheel for a while. The recorder held several thousand points. The field book had twenty pages of notes. The photographs numbered well over two hundred. She had everything she had come for, everything the office had asked her to collect, a complete technical record of the formation. When she delivered it, the data would be used to make maps, and the maps would be used to make decisions, and the decisions would have to do with the management of a water resource in a county that was running out of water, and none of that would have anything to do with what she had done here this morning, which had also been a kind of survey.

She looked back through the windshield at the exposed town. From up here it was small. She could see the whole of it—the intersecting ridges of the street grid, the darker rectangles of the foundation plots, the one standing wall of the town hall, the fire hydrant catching the morning light. A surveyor knows better than to trust scale. A surveyor knows that the instrument of measurement changes what is being measured, that to look at a thing carefully is already to do something to it. She had thought, coming here, that she wanted to see what was left. She understood now that was not quite it. She had wanted to confirm that something was left. That the water had not been the final word.

She started the truck and sat another moment. Then she pulled out her phone and called her mother.

Her mother answered on the second ring. There was the sound of a television being lowered. Her mother said her name as a question, as she always did, as though each call were a mild surprise.

Maren said: I'm at the reservoir. I'm sitting up at the top of the hill. Mom, you can see all of it from up here. You can see the whole town.

Her mother was quiet for a moment that lasted long enough for Maren to consider the possibility she would hang up, or change the subject, or invoke the philosophy.

Then her mother said: Can you see the school.

Yes, Maren said. I can see it. The walls are still there.

Another silence. The sound of her mother setting something down, the clink of a cup or a glass. Then her mother said, very quietly, as though testing the weight of words she had not used in a very long time: That school had a bell. A real bell in a cupola. Every morning at eight o'clock.

I know, Maren said, though she had not known. Or perhaps she had known and forgotten. Or perhaps she had never been told and simply knew anyway, the way you know certain things about the place that made you, even when no one was left to tell you.

I know, she said again.
