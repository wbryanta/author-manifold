The pipe had cracked sometime in March, probably during that last hard freeze, and the water had been bleeding out underground ever since, which was why the third row of Galas looked the way they looked—stunted, yellowing, the fruit the size of a child's fist and probably not worth the picking. Dean had found the wet patch two days ago when he walked the rows in the early morning, the ground spongy and dark around the base of a Baldwin that had been struggling for three seasons now. He'd called Robbie from the kitchen phone, standing in his socks on the linoleum their mother had picked out in 1987, and Robbie had said he'd come Saturday, and now it was Saturday and Dean was standing at the top of the row watching his brother's truck come up the long gravel drive trailing a small white cloud of dust.

Robbie stepped out in clean jeans and work boots that still had laces. He was forty-three years old and had the kind of tan you got from a deck rather than a field. He shaded his eyes and looked at the orchard the way someone looks at a painting they've decided they don't like but are trying to give a fair chance.

"The Galas look rough," he said.

"I told you on the phone."

"I know you did. I'm saying it looking at them."

Dean had already pulled the equipment from the barn—a pipe cutter, couplings, the PVC cement and primer, the hand trencher that their father had bought at an auction sometime in the early nineties and that still worked because their father had believed in keeping things that worked. The summer heat was already settling onto the valley with a kind of deliberate weight, the sky white at the edges and blue only directly overhead, and the forecast had the temperature reaching ninety-four by early afternoon.

They carried the equipment down the row. The grass between the trees had been mowed two weeks ago but was already knee-high in places, thick and bleached at the top where the drought had gone after it. Dean walked ahead. He could feel Robbie's eyes on the trees on either side, doing arithmetic.

"How far you think the crack goes?" Robbie asked.

"I don't know yet. That's why we're digging."

"I just want to know what we're dealing with."

"We're dealing with a cracked pipe," Dean said. "Same as I told you."

They found the wet patch easily—the ground was dark for a stretch of maybe six feet, the grass greener there, almost obscene in its greenness against the dry soil on either side. Dean knelt and pressed his palm flat against the earth and felt the moisture seep up between his fingers. Below that the water had been collecting in a lens, spreading probably, working its way down toward the clay layer. He'd seen it before. Sometimes it didn't do much damage and sometimes it rotted roots for forty feet in either direction and you didn't know which kind it was until you saw what came up in the spring, or what didn't.

"We should use the hand trencher first," Dean said. "Get down to the line and see the extent."

"You want me to do it?"

"I'll do it."

"I can do it, Dean."

He handed Robbie the trencher and stood back. Robbie worked it into the earth with his foot and began rocking it, and for a few minutes neither of them spoke. A crow called from somewhere beyond the tree line at the south end of the property, two short syllables, something officious about it. The Baldwins on either side of them threw thin shade in the early morning light. Dean noticed that the one to the east had a split in its main scaffold branch—not new, it had been there since the late frost three years ago that had taken probably a quarter of the state's apple crop, but he'd neglected to make a proper repair and now it had the dark puckered look of a wound that was healing wrong.

"Forty feet," Robbie said, not looking up from the trench.

"What about it."

"That's how far the wet patch runs. I paced it coming down the row. I was just thinking about it."

"The break itself won't be more than a foot of pipe, maybe two."

"I know, but the saturation. If the roots took water—"

"The roots didn't take water, Robbie. The roots need water. That's what the line is for."

"Too much water after a drought. You know that as well as I do."

Dean said nothing. He got down on one knee and began to loosen the soil at the edge of the trench with a hand trowel, working around what he judged to be the location of the line. Robbie kept working the trencher. They could hear each other breathing.

The pipe was about fourteen inches down, which was correct—their father had laid the original system in 1978 and had done it to depth, and the sections that Dean had replaced over the years he'd done to the same depth because it seemed wrong to do it differently. When the metal of the trencher scraped against the PVC, Dean told Robbie to stop and they both crouched and used their hands, clearing the soil from around the pipe until a foot-long section was exposed. The crack ran lengthwise, maybe three inches, its edges curled slightly outward where the ice had pushed through.

"Clean," Dean said.

"Yeah. One coupling should do it."

"Probably."

They dug out another eighteen inches on either side to give themselves room to work. Dean got the pipe cutter and lined it up and began to turn it around the pipe, and Robbie sat back on his heels and looked at the trees over Dean's shoulder. A hot wind came through from the west and moved the high grass between the rows and moved the leaves of the Baldwins and was gone.

"I talked to Harlan Foss last week," Robbie said.

Dean kept turning the cutter. "Did you."

"He called me. I didn't call him."

"All right."

"He's still interested. He said to say hello."

"You can tell him I said hello back." The cutter bit through and Dean shifted it down the pipe and began again. "Then you can tell him we're not selling."

Robbie picked up a clod of soil and turned it in his hands and then broke it apart. The soil was pale brown and dry even down here except right at the wet patch, which told Dean something about the water table he didn't particularly want to know. "He said he'd go to seven-fifty," Robbie said. "For the whole parcel."

"For eighty acres of failing orchard and a farmhouse that needs a new roof."

"For eighty acres in a valley where they're putting in a bypass. The value isn't the apples, Dean."

"I know what the value is."

"I'm not sure you do."

Dean finished the first cut and moved the cutter to his second mark and began to turn it. "Hand me the primer when I'm through this."

Robbie picked it up but didn't hand it over yet. "I'm serious. Seven-fifty is above what I thought we'd see. Before the bypass announcement I would have said we'd be lucky to get five, maybe five-twenty-five."

"We would have been lucky to get five. We don't need luck from Harlan Foss."

"It's not luck, it's the market. It's just where things are." Robbie set the primer down between them. "You remember what Dad paid for this place?"

"You know I know."

"Sixty-eight thousand dollars. In 1976. And he thought he'd overpaid. He told me once he thought he'd overpaid."

"He didn't tell me that."

"He told me." Robbie paused. "I'm not saying that means we should sell. I'm just saying, if you want to think about what he'd want, I don't know that you can use the purchase price as evidence of anything."

Dean finished the second cut and lifted the damaged section of pipe out of the trench. He held it up and looked at the crack the way you'd look at a thing to understand it. Three inches of failure in forty-four years of pipe. He set it aside and reached for the primer. "You don't know what he'd want," Dean said.

"Neither do you."

"He worked this land for thirty years."

"He worked it and lost money on it for thirty years. Or close to it."

"Close to it isn't the same as losing money."

"Some years it is."

The primer went on the pipe ends and the coupling, the smell of it sharp and industrial against the smell of the grass and the warm soil. Dean waited the correct amount of time. He knew Robbie was watching him but he kept his attention on the work. A meadowlark called from somewhere in the tall grass at the fence line—two descending notes and then a phrase that sounded like something it kept meaning to finish.

"What would you do with seven-fifty?" Dean asked.

"What do you mean."

"I mean where does the money go. What happens to it."

Robbie was quiet for a moment. "Half to you, half to me. That's always been the understanding."

"I know that's always been the understanding. I mean what do you do with three hundred and seventy-five thousand dollars. Do you have a use for it."

"I could invest it. Pay off the house." He paused. "Sarah wants to look at a place up near Hood River. There's some land up there."

"Agricultural land?"

"Some of it."

"So you'd take your half of the sale of this orchard and buy into another orchard."

"Different situation. Managed differently. There's an operation up there, it's—they do things differently, Dean. Agri-tourism, the u-pick model, a processing shed. It's not the same as what this is."

Dean pressed the coupling onto the prepared pipe end and held it, counting, the way his father had taught him. When he'd been twelve or thirteen his father had let him help replace a section of the north line and had stood over him and said, you have to hold it long enough or the joint won't set, and you'll be back here in a year doing it again. He'd said it without any particular weight but Dean had understood that it was important, that the holding was not incidental but essential, that the time you spent waiting was the same as work.

"What this is," Dean said, "is what Dad built."

"I know what Dad built." There was something careful in Robbie's voice now, some adjustment. "I'm not dismissing what he built."

"You're describing it as a failed enterprise. That's a kind of dismissal."

"It's a financial description."

"Things are more than their financials."

"Things are also their financials, Dean. You can't just—" Robbie stopped and started again. "You can't farm sentiment. I respect what he did. I worked here too, you know. I was here same as you were."

"You left."

"I left to go to college. You didn't have to stay."

"I didn't feel like I had to. I wanted to."

"I know you did. I'm saying that's a choice you made and I made a different choice and neither of those choices should determine what we do with the property now that it belongs to both of us equally."

Dean fitted the second end of the coupling and held it and counted. A hawk moved overhead, riding something invisible, its shadow passing briefly over the trench. The smell of the primer and cement was strong in the warm air.

"There are seven varieties on this land," Dean said. "The Galas, the Baldwins, the Honeycrisps in the east block, the Cortlands, the Pippins along the south fence, the two rows of Gravensteins that were here before Dad bought the place, and the Empires he put in the last year he farmed it. Seven varieties. Some of those trees are older than I am."

"I know that."

"The Gravensteins especially. There are maybe four orchards in the whole state that still have Gravensteins of that age."

"I know, Dean."

"They make a different apple than anything you'll find in a store. You know that because you grew up eating them. There's a flavor in a fifty-year-old Gravenstein that you cannot replicate with a younger tree and you cannot replicate with any other variety and once those trees are gone they are gone. Whatever Harlan Foss puts on this land, whether it's a subdivision or a logistics park or a storage facility, those trees are gone."

Robbie said nothing for a moment. He was looking at the coupling, watching Dean's hands holding it.

"I know," he said again, more quietly.

"I don't think you do, or you wouldn't keep bringing up Harlan Foss."

"I bring up Harlan Foss because I have a daughter starting college in two years and a house with a second mortgage and because the orchard has not turned a profit in four of the last six years and I'm not sure it ever will again and I don't know how to make that square with the Gravensteins, Dean. I love the Gravensteins. I also need to provide for my family and I think—" He stopped. When he continued his voice had changed in some way that Dean recognized from childhood, a register that meant Robbie was getting at something he'd been carrying for a while. "I think sometimes that you confuse loving a place with being obligated to ruin yourself for it. That those are the same thing to you."

Dean released the coupling. He sat back and looked at it and it looked correct. He would pressure-test the line before he turned the water back on, the way his father had taught him, and then he would watch the coupling for a few days to make sure it held. That was all you could do. You did the repair correctly and then you watched to see if the repair held.

"Mom left me the operations," Dean said. "When she drew up the estate after Dad died. You got the equity split equal, but she left me the operations."

"I know what the estate says."

"Which means if I decide not to sell, we don't sell."

"Legally, yes. I know that."

"I'm not saying it to threaten you."

"I know you're not."

"I'm saying it because I want you to understand that I'm not an obstacle between you and three hundred and seventy-five thousand dollars. I'm the person who decides what happens to this land and I've decided."

Robbie picked up the section of cracked pipe from where Dean had set it aside and turned it in his hands. The crack was obvious, a white stress fracture along its length. He ran his thumb along it.

"And if you can't make it work?" Robbie said. "If the Galas come in at half value again and the Honeycrisps get the scab like they did last year and the equipment keeps breaking down. What then?"

"Then I deal with it."

"But it's my land too. Half of it. And watching you deal with it, watching you sink into it—" He set the pipe down. "It's not nothing to me. You understand that. It's not nothing to watch you work yourself into the ground on something that may not be workable."

Dean looked at him. They had the same jaw, the same way of holding their shoulders when they were in discomfort, a slight elevation, a bracing. Their mother used to say they were the same man born twice with different ideas of what to do about it.

"I'm going to need your help backfilling," Dean said.

Robbie looked at the coupling and then at his brother and something moved through his face. "All right," he said.

They worked for a while without speaking. The sun had moved and the shade had moved with it and now they were working in full sun, the soil at the surface already hot. Dean shoveled the excavated earth back into the trench and Robbie tamped it down with his boot heel. When the trench was filled they raked the disturbed soil flat and Dean gathered the tools and looked at the row.

"You want some water?" Dean asked.

"Yeah."

They walked back up the row toward the barn. The orchard stretched on either side—eighty acres was not large by any commercial standard but on foot, in the heat, walking through it, it could feel vast. The trees in the east block had come on well this year, the Honeycrisps heavy and the foliage dense and dark, and Dean felt the familiar complicated feeling he had whenever part of the orchard looked right—a satisfaction that was also a pressure, the knowledge that what looked good today could look different in two weeks, in three weeks, that you were always managing conditions that were only partly within your control.

He got two bottles of water from the refrigerator in the barn and handed one to Robbie and they stood in the shade of the overhang on the barn's east side. A ground squirrel appeared from under the equipment shed, regarded them with a look of ancient concern, and vanished again.

"Ellen thinks we should sell," Robbie said.

"I know."

"She told you?"

"She didn't have to." Dean drank from his water. "She's practical."

"She is."

"So is your wanting to sell. I know that. I'm not calling you impractical or calling Ellen impractical. I'm saying that practical is not the only consideration here."

"What's the other consideration. I want you to name it."

Dean thought about this for a moment in a genuine way, not the performance of thought but actual thought. He looked at the orchard, at the rows going back from where they stood, the trees in their irregular ages and sizes, some of them tall and open-centered the way their father had trained them and some of them newer and more compact. The Gravensteins were at the south end and you couldn't see them from here but he knew the shape of them, the way the oldest ones had that particular lean from the prevailing west wind, the way in August they would be the most fragrant trees on the property, the way the apples when you bit into them had a tart brightness that Dean associated with everything the word August meant to him.

"When I was nine," Dean said, "there was a year the crop came in so good that Dad let us each pick a bag to take to school for the other kids. You remember that?"

"I remember."

"He let us pick whatever we wanted, any variety, and you picked Galas because they were the sweetest. And I picked Gravensteins because I thought they were the best. And we both took them to school and I gave mine out and some of the kids didn't like them because they were too tart, and I remember being surprised by that. Being surprised that someone could taste the same thing I was tasting and not love it."

Robbie was quiet.

"I'm not selling because the Gravensteins taste a certain way," Dean continued. "I know that's not a financial argument. But I've been trying to think about what it would mean to be the person who sold those trees. Not the person who lost them in a bad year or lost them to a disease or a late frost. The person who signed the paper that sent the bulldozer in. And I don't know how to be that person."

"You might have to learn," Robbie said. He said it without any hardness in it.

"Maybe. But not this year."

Robbie turned his water bottle in his hands. "Foss won't wait indefinitely. The bypass changes the market but it doesn't change it forever."

"I know that."

"And if you change your mind—"

"I'll call you before I call Foss. You'll be the first call I make."

Robbie nodded. He looked out at the east block, the heavy Honeycrisps, and then further south toward where the Gravensteins were, though you couldn't see them. He had a look that Dean recognized as longing, though what Robbie longed for Dean couldn't entirely say—whether it was money or relief or the orchard itself, some version of the orchard that existed only in the past, when it was neither of theirs to worry about but simply the place where they lived.

"I need to look at that scaffold split on the Baldwin in the third row," Dean said. "Before we leave."

"We can do that."

"And the irrigation controller needs a new battery. Fifteen minutes at most."

"Whatever you need."

They walked back into the orchard. The heat was full now, the earlier white at the sky's edges spread inward so the whole sky was a hot pale blue, the sun without a position, its light seeming to come from everywhere at once. The grass between the rows caught at their ankles. Somewhere in the deep tangle of a tree near the end of the row a bee was working, and then two bees, and then the sound of them rose briefly into something larger before subsiding into the individual bees again, which was the way of it with bees.

Dean found the split on the Baldwin and put his fingers against it and felt the wood, which was still firm. He went back to the barn for a pruning saw and some grafting wax and came back and cut the damaged limb back to where the wood was clean, the saw moving through the branch with a sound like something whispered, and then he sealed the wound with the wax, pressing it into the cut with his thumb, working it into the edges the way his father had worked it into cuts when Dean had watched him as a child, the wax dark and warm in the heat, sealing what had been opened to keep the good wood good.

Robbie held the branch while Dean worked. His clean jeans had soil on the knees now and a dark half-moon of sweat had appeared on the back of his shirt. He held the branch steady and did not need to be told what to do, because he had learned the same things Dean had learned, in the same orchard, from the same man, and that was not nothing, even if it was not everything.

The cut was clean. The wax set in the heat. Dean stepped back and looked at it and Robbie let the branch go and they both looked at the tree, which would probably be fine.
