The hotel had been rebuilt in the image of what everyone agreed it should have looked like all along. This was how the architect described it to the newspaper, three years after the fire and two years before the ribbon cutting, and Marisol had read the article at her kitchen table in Tucson where she was then living with a man she did not love, both of them eating cereal in the grey morning light. *The image of what it should have looked like all along.* She had torn out the article and kept it in the drawer with the takeout menus and the loose batteries, and she did not know why she kept it, whether it was evidence of something or merely a record, the way you save ticket stubs from concerts you didn't particularly enjoy.

The wedding was her cousin Delia's, who had chosen the hotel because it was beautiful and historic and because she did not know, or if she knew had decided not to care, that Marisol had spent eight months of her twenty-second year pushing a cart down its corridors, stripping beds, collecting the miniature bottles that guests left behind in configurations that told you more than you wanted to know about them. Three mostly empty vodka bottles and an untouched Bible. A room where someone had rearranged the furniture in the night so that all the chairs faced the window. The particular smell of a room where someone had been crying, which Marisol had never been able to fully articulate but which she recognized the way you recognize a song from childhood, in the body before the mind.

She drove from Tucson alone. She and the man, whose name was Gerald and who worked in insurance, had separated four months earlier, and the separation had been so quiet it was almost theoretical, more like a decision to stop watering a plant than anything with shouting or tears. She drove through the desert with the windows down and the radio finding and losing stations across the emptiness and she thought about what it meant to return to a place. Not what it meant in the abstract but what it meant in the specific and muscular sense, the way the body held geography differently than the mind did. She had not been back to Yuma since the fire.

The hotel stood at the edge of the river, which was not much of a river anymore, more a suggestion of what a river had once intended. The original building had been a railroad hotel from 1910, three stories of pink stucco with wrought iron balconies and a courtyard where bougainvillea grew through the railings in a way that made it impossible to say where the plant ended and the architecture began. That was gone. The new building was five stories, the stucco a brighter pink, the balconies wider, the courtyard landscaped with the kind of deliberateness that resembled natural beauty the way a wig resembles hair. It was, Marisol thought, standing in the parking lot with her garment bag over one arm and her overnight case on the asphalt, more beautiful than the original. This seemed important to note, that the thing which had replaced a ruined thing could be genuinely better, though she couldn't decide whether this was reassuring or troubling or neither.

The lobby smelled of fresh cut flowers and something underneath the flowers, something clean and slightly chemical the way all new buildings smelled, as if they were still in the process of becoming themselves. She checked in at the desk and the young woman behind it, who wore her hair in a bun so severe it gave her face a stretched look, handed her a key card in a paper sleeve.

"Room 412," the young woman said.

Marisol took the key card and looked at it.

"Is there a problem with that room?" she asked, and the young woman looked up with the careful blankness of hospitality training.

"Is there something you'd prefer? We do have some courtyard-facing rooms available."

"No," Marisol said. "412 is fine."

There had been no room 412 in the old building. There had been no fourth floor. She knew this and still, carrying her bags to the elevator, she felt something in her chest she would have called apprehension if she were being precise about it, though it sat below apprehension somehow, in a register she associated with the period just after waking when you can still feel the shape of a dream without being able to see it.

The elevator opened onto a corridor that was wide and well lit, the carpet patterned in terracotta and cream, wall sconces made to look like gas lamps. Marisol walked to her room and opened it and stood in the doorway for a moment before going in. It was a good room. A king bed with a white duvet. A window looking south toward the river. A desk and a chair and a bathroom with a soaking tub that she would not use. On the nightstand, a card welcoming her to the hotel and wishing her a pleasant stay, signed with the printed name of the general manager, a name she did not recognize.

She hung her dress in the closet and set her toiletries on the bathroom counter and sat on the edge of the bed and looked at the window. The light was coming in at a low afternoon angle and the river was visible as a silver line in the distance, beyond the scrub and the highway. She had a thought, not for the first time, that the river did not know it had almost disappeared, that it went on being a river in the way it had always known, which was perhaps the only honest response to diminishment.

She had told no one about the fire. This was not precisely true—she had told Gerald, once, early in their relationship when they were still in the stage of exchanging formative disasters. She had told him the bare facts: the fire, the evacuation, the two guests who did not make it out. He had listened and said that must have been terrifying and she had said yes and they had moved on to other things. What she had not told him, what she had not told anyone, was what she had done in the seven minutes between the alarm sounding and her own evacuation.

She had been on the second floor, at the far east end of the corridor, when the alarm went off. She had a cart with her—it was a Tuesday morning in November, checkout day, and she had been working the east wing—and her first instinct, which she was not proud of and had spent years trying to understand in terms that were neither too harsh nor too forgiving, was to finish the room she was in. Not because she didn't believe the alarm. She believed it. She could already smell smoke, distantly, the way you smell rain before it arrives. She finished the room because rooms had to be finished, because the motion of finishing was embedded in her hands and her body in a way that the alarm, however urgent, had not immediately overridden. She tucked the corners of the duvet. She aligned the toiletries on the bathroom counter. She wheeled the cart into the corridor.

Then she had gone door to door, knocking and calling out, all the way down the east wing to the stairwell, and she had found three rooms occupied, all three guests successfully alarmed and directed toward the stairs. An elderly couple who moved with excruciating slowness but moved. A young man from Phoenix who had slept through the alarm and came to the door with the confused innocence of someone whose body had not yet understood what was happening. She had walked them all to the stairs. She had been the last one from the east wing.

The two guests who died were on the third floor, west wing, which was not her floor and not her wing, and she had learned this later and had carried it with her nonetheless, the arithmetic of it, the directional specificity of west when she had gone east.

She changed out of her driving clothes and put on the dress she had brought for the rehearsal dinner, which was that evening in the courtyard. The dress was blue linen that she had bought three weeks ago in a shop in Tucson, standing in the dressing room under fluorescent light and thinking that she looked like a person who was trying to look fine. She had bought it anyway. She had worn worse things for worse reasons.

The courtyard was strung with lights that hadn't been turned on yet, and the afternoon heat was beginning to lose its edge the way desert heat did, suddenly, as if someone had made a decision. Tables were being arranged by young men and women in black aprons, and Marisol stood at the edge of the courtyard holding a glass of water and watching them work with the specific attention she had never been able to entirely stop applying to service workers. The way the young woman nearest her moved the centerpiece two inches to the left and then stepped back and tilted her head and moved it two inches back. The way one of the young men near the bar carried himself with the careful efficiency of someone who knew exactly how much energy the night would require and was managing his reserves. She recognized this. She had been twenty-two and doing the same mathematics.

Delia arrived in a white sundress with her fiancé Martin, who was tall and fond of expressing opinions about wine in ways that made Marisol feel tired. Delia was Marisol's mother's sister's daughter, which meant they had grown up in proximity without ever being fully in each other's lives, orbiting through holidays and quinceañeras and the occasional summer visit. Delia was thirty-one now and in love in the visible and slightly embarrassing way of people who have recently discovered what they had been missing. Marisol was glad for her. This gladness was genuine and sat alongside something lonelier that she didn't examine directly.

"You came all the way from Tucson," Delia said, embracing her.

"It's four hours," Marisol said.

"You look wonderful." Delia held her at arm's length and studied her face with the frank assessment of family. "You look thin."

"I've been running," Marisol said, which was true. She had taken up running after Gerald, going out before sunrise when the streets were still cool and empty, and she ran not with any particular destination in mind but with the simple animal pleasure of the body doing what it was built to do, moving through space, covering ground.

Martin brought her a glass of champagne and said something about the bubbles that she smiled at and forgot immediately. More family arrived, aunts she recognized and cousins she didn't, and the courtyard filled with the noise of family which was a specific kind of noise, at once more comfortable and more complicated than the noise of strangers. Her aunt Ceci found her and held both her hands and asked about Gerald and Marisol said they had separated and her aunt said *mija* in the tone that meant she was deciding whether to be sympathetic or practical, and settled on a brief additional hand squeeze before being absorbed by someone else.

Marisol ate from the plates of appetizers that circulated and drank one glass of champagne and then switched to sparkling water and stood and talked and laughed at the appropriate moments and at some point found herself alone for a moment near the bougainvillea that had been planted along the far wall of the courtyard, which was not the old bougainvillea, could not be—the old had burned with everything else—but was bougainvillea nonetheless, and was growing through the new wrought iron railing in a way that made it impossible to say, already, where the plant ended and the architecture began.

She put her hand on one of the iron rails and felt the warmth it had stored from the day's sun. The metal released heat slowly, as metal did, giving back what the light had given it.

There was a woman standing nearby, also apart from the larger noise of the party, also holding a glass of sparkling water. She was in her late sixties, Marisol guessed, with white hair cut short and a face that had been shaped by considerable weather, not unhappily. She wore a green dress and earrings that were small silver birds.

"Do you know the couple?" the woman asked, not looking at Marisol but at the party.

"The bride is my cousin. You?"

"Martin's aunt. Eleanor." She looked at Marisol then. "You were watching the staff."

Marisol felt something shift in her chest. "Was I."

"I notice it because I do the same thing," Eleanor said. "I worked in a hotel kitchen for eleven years. You never stop watching how people carry themselves in a job."

"I was a chambermaid," Marisol said. "Here, actually. In the old building."

Eleanor was quiet for a moment. "The fire," she said.

"Yes."

"I read about it. I've thought about it, since Martin chose this venue." She looked at Marisol again, more carefully this time. "Is it strange, being here?"

Marisol considered this. She was a person who tried to be precise about what she actually felt rather than what she thought she should feel, and she had found that this precision was often impossible in real time, that the most honest answer was frequently some version of *I don't know yet*.

"The building is different," she said finally. "So in some ways it's not here. It's just a building."

"And in other ways?"

"In other ways it's very much here."

Eleanor nodded as if this was what she had expected. They stood together for a while without speaking, watching the party, and Marisol found this easy in the way she sometimes found silence with strangers easier than silence with people she knew.

The rehearsal dinner moved inside for the dinner portion and Marisol was seated beside one of Delia's college friends who had strong opinions about fermenting foods and Marisol listened and asked questions and ate the salmon and drank a second glass of champagne. The dining room was on the ground floor, south-facing, and through the tall windows she could see the courtyard lights coming on as the sky darkened, the bougainvillea lit from below in a way that made it look like something from a different world.

She excused herself once during dinner and walked through the lobby to the exterior and stood on the front steps in the dark. The night air had the desert's specific cooling, a relief that happened all at once and felt like a decision. She could see, above the roofline of the new building, the stars that the old building had also stood under, the same stars, unchanged and indifferent in the way she found more comforting than troubling, that the universe was not paying attention, that the stars over Yuma on the night of the fire and the stars over Yuma now and the stars that would be over whatever stood here in a hundred years were all the same stars, unmoved.

She thought about the room she had finished. The tucked corners of the duvet. The aligned toiletries. She thought about this often, the gesture itself, the way her hands had known what to do when her mind was still translating the alarm's meaning, and she thought about it not with shame exactly but with the particular interest she brought to her own behavior in extreme conditions, the way the body's habits operated below the level of conscious choice. She had read about this, years later, in a psychology book she'd come across in a used bookstore in Albuquerque—she'd lived in Albuquerque for two years before Tucson—the way trained behavior could persist through crisis as a form of the mind's protection, routing action through the familiar while the unfamiliar was being processed. This explanation had helped and also had not helped, the way explanations of things you have done do not change the fact of having done them.

The three guests she had found and directed to the stairs. She held this too. The young man from Phoenix who came to the door confused and whom she had taken by the arm and said *Come on, we have to go now, come on* in a voice she didn't recognize as her own, the voice of someone who knew exactly what was needed.

She went back inside and ate dessert and helped Delia laugh at a toast Martin's best man gave that was longer than it needed to be. She told her aunt Ceci that she was doing well, that Tucson was fine, that the running was something she had come to love. She danced one dance with a cousin she barely knew, a boy of about fifteen who was tall and mortified by the slow song that came on and she told him he was doing great, which made him more mortified, and she thought this was fine, that being mortified at fifteen by a slow song at your cousin's rehearsal dinner was the correct experience to be having, that it meant everything was as it should be.

In her room later she turned off the lights and sat in the chair by the window and looked at the river in the dark. You couldn't see it exactly, more its absence, the flat darkness in the distance where the lights of the far bank dropped away. She had stayed in rooms in hotels her whole adult life and she had never stopped noticing the things she noticed: the alignment of the furniture, the placement of the trash can relative to the desk, the way the housekeeping staff had mitered or not mitered the corners of the duvet, the small evidence of their passage. She noticed it not as judgment but as recognition, the way you recognize a language you grew up speaking even in countries where you don't expect to hear it.

She thought about what she had told Eleanor: *the building is different, so in some ways it's not here.* She believed this and she also believed the opposite, which was that the ground was the ground, that the site of a thing retained the thing in some way that was not mystical but simply material, particles of ash in soil, the persistence of structural fact. The river had been here and was still here even diminished. The hotel had been here and was still here even transformed. She had been here and was still here even changed in ways she could identify and ways she could not.

She took out her phone and looked at it without turning it on. She thought about calling Gerald, not because she missed him exactly but because she wanted to tell someone where she was and why it mattered, and she understood that this impulse was precisely why they hadn't worked, because what she needed was not someone to tell but someone who already knew, who didn't need it explained, and she and Gerald had never arrived at that place despite two years of trying.

She put the phone on the nightstand and lay down on top of the duvet without getting under it. The room was cool and she was tired in the way that was more emotional than physical, the tiredness of being present in a place that asked something of you. She lay and looked at the ceiling and thought of the chambermaid she had been, twenty-two, pushing the cart down the corridor on a Tuesday morning in November, and she tried to locate some feeling about that woman that was not judgment and not pity and arrived at something closer to simple recognition, as if across the distance of years she could make out the other woman's features and say only: yes, that is a person I know. She did what her hands knew. She went back for the ones she could reach. She got out.

In the morning, the wedding. Delia in a dress that was the color of cream, moving down the aisle of the courtyard with the bougainvillea lit now by actual sun, and Martin receiving her with an expression that Marisol would remember for a long time, not because it was dramatic but because it was so thoroughly unguarded, the expression of someone who had briefly forgotten that other people could see him. The minister said the words and Delia and Martin said the words and Marisol stood with the assembled family and felt the gladness again, the genuine gladness, and beside it the loneliness, and she let them both be there because she had learned, slowly, that feelings did not require resolution, only acknowledgment, the way you acknowledge a room you are leaving, a brief pause in the doorway before you move on.

At the reception she sat with Eleanor again, briefly, at the bar while the band changed songs.

"How are you?" Eleanor asked.

"Better than I expected," Marisol said, and meant it.

She danced. She ate wedding cake. She stood at the edge of the courtyard in the afternoon heat and watched Delia laugh at something her mother had said, the full body laugh, and she thought about the drive back to Tucson tomorrow, the four hours through the desert, the radio finding and losing stations, the windows down. She would stop somewhere for coffee and sit for a while before the last stretch. She would get home and go for a run in the evening when the heat had decided to quit. The apartment would be quiet and she would shower and eat something simple and sit on the porch and watch the neighborhood put itself to bed.

She would be all right. She had been all right before all right had seemed possible, which was the thing she knew now that she had not known at twenty-two, not in any useful way. You came through what you came through. You finished the room your hands already knew how to finish. You went back for who you could reach. Then you went down the stairs, and you came out into whatever the air was outside, and you stood in it.

The river in the distance did what rivers did. The bougainvillea grew on.
