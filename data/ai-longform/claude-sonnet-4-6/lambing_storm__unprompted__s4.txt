The snow had been falling since noon but the real trouble started after dark, when the wind found its voice and the lights in the lambing shed stuttered twice before going out altogether. Ruth stood in the sudden black with her hand still on the heat lamp she'd been adjusting, and she counted to three the way she used to count before speaking her mind at town meetings, before doing anything that required her to be steady. She found the flashlight on the shelf where it always lived, clicked it on, and swept the beam across the shed: seventeen ewes in various states of readiness, the smell of wet wool and blood and hay, and her son Daniel kneeling in the far corner with his arms elbow-deep in a ewe they'd been watching all afternoon.

"Power's out," she said.

"I know," he said, without turning.

She hung the flashlight from a nail on a crossbeam so it threw a cone of white light across the middle of the shed and left the corners in shadow. Then she went to the generator they kept for exactly this, and she pulled the cord once, twice, a third time that sent pain up her shoulder, and the thing coughed and sputtered and caught. The heat lamps glowed back to life. The red warmers over the two jugging pens came on. Outside, the wind pressed against the aluminum siding of the shed and the whole structure groaned like something trying to decide whether to stay.

"She's not dilated," Daniel said. He pulled his arms free and sat back on his heels. He'd turned seventeen in February and he had his father's hands, wide across the knuckle, though he was still growing into them. "Been six hours at least."

"Let me see."

He moved aside and Ruth knelt where he'd been kneeling. The ewe—one of their older crossbreeds, a big raw-boned animal they'd named Duchess years ago as a joke because of how she held her head—was lying on her side with her legs extended and her breathing coming in long, measured efforts that were not yet panic but were close to it. Ruth worked her fingers gently along the cervix, feeling the hard knob of a head that was presenting slightly wrong, tilted, the way you'd hold a ball if you were trying to keep someone from taking it from you.

"Presentation's off," Ruth said. "She needs help."

"I know."

She could feel him watching her. This was what she'd dreaded about the season, not the physical work, which she knew she could do, but the weight of being the one who knew. Marcus had been the one who knew. He'd been farming these hills for twenty years before he met her and she'd been a quick learner, a good partner, but there is a difference between knowing and being the one who knows, and that difference had never been so clear to her as it was right now in the leaking light with the generator running its diesel-stink beneath the smell of everything else, her son's face watching her from the shadows.

She lubricated her hands from the bottle on the shelf and went back in, working her fingers carefully around the lamb's head, feeling the architecture of the thing, the neck, the folded ears, the way the skull had torqued just enough to lodge itself wrong. The ewe shuddered but held still. Ruth had always thought that animals in labor had a particular kind of courage that humans rarely managed, a willingness to endure without editorial.

"Talk to her," Ruth said.

Daniel moved to the ewe's head and put his hand flat against her jaw. He started talking in a low murmur that Ruth couldn't quite make out, not words really, just sound shaped like reassurance, and the ewe's ear swiveled toward him.

Ruth worked the lamb's head slowly, not forcing, applying the pressure in a rhythm, waiting for the pauses between contractions to make her adjustments. Outside, something metallic—the gate to the near paddock, she thought—had come loose in the wind and was banging in a steady arrhythmic pattern against its post. She'd deal with that after. She was categorizing the night's problems into after and now, and there were too many afters accumulating.

The lamb came in a rush of fluid when it finally turned right, sliding out into Ruth's hands with the particular boneless urgency of new life, and Ruth held it up and cleared its nose and mouth with her fingers and swung it once the way Marcus had showed her, that motion that looked brutal but was mercy, and the lamb shook its head and sneezed and began to breathe.

"Boy or girl?" Daniel asked.

Ruth checked. "Boy."

She laid him on the straw in front of his mother's face and watched Duchess do what ewes do, that immediate, unsentimental work of recognition, the long tongue moving over the lamb's face and neck, the lamb already trying to organize his unsteady legs beneath him. It happened every time and it still did something to Ruth's chest that she had no adequate word for.

She stood up slowly. Her knees had started hurting in the cold this winter in a way they hadn't before, and she was forty-six years old and filing that fact away.

"Check the waterers," she said. "Make sure they're not freezing."

While Daniel moved down the line of pens, Ruth washed her hands in the bucket and looked at the rest of the shed. Three ewes in early labor, two of them pacing, one lying down with the glazed interior look of an animal that has gone somewhere else to manage what's happening to its body. Two more she'd expected to go tonight who were still standing and looking offended by the general state of affairs. The lambs already born—eleven of them, plus the boy from Duchess—were in the jugging pens with their mothers, most sleeping in the coiled way lambs sleep, like small scattered stones.

Marcus had kept a lambing book. A composition notebook with a blue cover that lived on the shelf by the first aid kit, and in it he'd recorded every birth with date and time, mother and offspring, weights if he had them, any complications. His handwriting was small and cramped and very neat, the handwriting of someone who'd gone to school in a time when you were corrected for poor penmanship. Ruth had started a new notebook this season—she'd bought a composition notebook like his, same brand, without quite admitting to herself why—and she found it now and recorded Duchess's lamb, and she allowed herself to look, just for a moment, at last year's entries in Marcus's hand. April 22nd. April 23rd. The dates moved down the page. She closed the book.

The wind hit the shed broadside and the generator hiccupped and the lights dimmed and held.

"Waterers are fine," Daniel called. "But there's something going on with Maren."

Maren was the youngest ewe they'd bred this season, a first-timer, and first-timers were their own particular problem. Ruth went to where Maren stood in her pen making a low continuous sound that was not quite a bleat, the sound of an animal in the grip of something it has no framework for understanding. Her abdomen was tight and high. Ruth watched her for a moment without touching, reading.

"She's been making that sound for twenty minutes," Daniel said. He was leaning on the pen rail with his arms folded on top of it, watching the way he watched things, quietly, storing it.

"I know. I heard it."

"I thought you were busy with Duchess."

"I was. I can hear two things at once."

He didn't say anything for a moment. Then: "I know you can."

She looked at him. He was looking at the ewe.

There were conversations she and Daniel had been not-having for eight months, since Marcus died in late August from the thing in his brain that had given them four months of warning and then taken him anyway, four months that Ruth had spent oscillating between gratitude for the warning and a furious suspicion that no warning was better, that it only extended the duration of the wound. Daniel had not cried at the funeral. He'd cried twice that she knew of, once in July when they'd understood that the harvest was going to be the last one, and once in September in the barn late at night when he'd thought she was in the house, and she had been for a while, but she'd come back for something she'd forgotten, and she'd heard him through the barn wall and stood in the dark on the wrong side of the boards and waited until he was finished.

They did not discuss this. They discussed fencing, feed costs, the state of the road in mud season, whether the Hendersons' ram was worth hiring for next breeding season. They discussed practical things. Ruth did not know if this was health or damage and suspected it was both.

Maren had gone to her knees. Ruth unlatched the pen gate and went to her and ran a hand down her flank, feeling the hard worked muscles of the abdomen, the lamb moving in its water, very close.

"Is she okay?" Daniel asked.

"She will be. First-timers are always scared. It's not unlike."

She didn't finish the sentence. Daniel was quiet. Maren heaved herself onto her side with a grunt of surrender and the work began in earnest, and Ruth talked to the young ewe in the same low register that Daniel had used with Duchess, not words, not quite, just the sound of a voice, here, here, here, this will pass.

The lamb came in the proper orientation and the proper time and it was a girl, small and dark-faced, and she was alive. Maren turned to look at her with the expression of an animal confronting an unexpected version of itself. Ruth stepped back and let it happen.

By eleven o'clock the temperature inside the shed had dropped to where she could see her breath, the generator working hard but the cold working harder through the gaps in the old siding, through the places where they'd meant to weatherstrip and hadn't gotten to. Three more lambs had been born, all of them clean births, and one of the standing ewes had finally settled into labor and was making reasonable progress. Ruth was tying the navel cord on the last newborn when Daniel came in from outside, snow on his shoulders, his cheeks the color of brickwork.

"How bad?" she said.

"I can't see the far fence." He stomped his boots on the mat. "Road's gone completely. And that gate has torn loose—the lower paddock one. I got it latched but it's not going to hold."

"Did you check the water tanks outside?"

"Heaters are working. Older ewes are all in the three-shed."

She looked at him. Snow was melting out of his hair and his expression had the particular flatness of exhaustion pretending to be fine, and she recognized it because it was her expression too, she supposed, all the time now.

"Come eat something," she said.

"We can't leave."

"I'm not saying leave. I brought food in the bucket." She gestured toward the far end of the shed where she'd left it hours ago, a canvas bag with a thermos and sandwiches she'd made in the late afternoon when the snow was still only a threat. It felt like something from another era, that making of sandwiches, that preparatory domesticity.

They sat on an overturned crate and a hay bale and ate, watching the shed. The ewes that were not in labor made their low sounds, the shuffling restlessness of animals that know weather has gone wrong but are safely inside and willing to tolerate this. The lambs slept. The generator ran.

Daniel drank the coffee she poured for him and didn't speak for a while, and Ruth let him not speak because she was tired too and the silence was not the bad kind. There were kinds of silence she'd been learning to categorize, since August, the silence in the bedroom in the mornings which was one kind, the silence at dinner which was another, the silence on Sunday when Marcus had always driven into town for the paper and now no one did. This silence was all right. It was the silence of two people who knew where they were and what they were doing and what still needed to be done.

"He would have liked this storm," Daniel said.

She looked at him.

"He would have hated the generator going." Daniel turned the cup in his hands. "But he would have liked the storm part. You know how he was about weather."

She did know. Marcus had a particular appreciation for weather that went wrong in spectacular ways, not because he was a man who enjoyed difficulty, but because he believed that difficulty clarified things, that it sorted what mattered from what only seemed to. He'd stood outside during a hailstorm once with his arms spread and his face up while Ruth watched from the kitchen window and felt the particular exasperated love she'd carried for him for twenty-two years, that love which was also sometimes bewilderment.

"Yes," she said. "He would have."

"We're doing all right, aren't we?" Daniel said. He was looking at the pens. "Tonight, I mean. We're handling it."

"We are."

"I didn't know about the presentation thing. I didn't know you could fix that."

"It took me a while to learn. Your father was patient about teaching."

"He wasn't patient about everything."

"No," she said. "Not everything."

This was as close as they came sometimes, the edge of the actual conversation, and then one of the laboring ewes made a sound that required attention and Ruth stood up and the moment passed and she was grateful for the ewe and also sorry. She was learning that grief was not a thing you moved through but a thing you moved alongside, that it walked at your shoulder and you could turn to look at it or not turn, and both were survivable.

The big ewe in the end pen, a Corriedale they'd had for eight years and called Peg for no reason anymore anyone could remember, was in the final stages of labor and in trouble. Ruth saw it immediately, the angle of the back legs, the way the ewe had gone flat and was breathing in the short panting rhythm of an animal spending everything it had.

"Twins," Daniel said from behind her. "She went down fast."

Ruth was already through the gate. The first lamb was partially born, a front foot visible, but only one, and the head was not there, tucked back in the way that meant the lamb could not be born as presented, and the second lamb was pressing behind the first, both of them now urgent, the pressure enormous.

"Hold her head," Ruth said.

Daniel went to Peg's head and held her, one arm firm around the ewe's neck, and talked to her in the voice that was not quite words, the same voice, and Ruth worked her hands carefully into the birth canal and found the lamb's tucked head and brought it gently forward, feeling the neck, the resistance of the tucked chin, and she worked against the resistance, steady pressure and then relief, the head coming forward into position, and she guided the lamb out and it came and it was alive, a girl, small but moving.

"Good," Daniel said. "Good, come on."

Ruth cleared the lamb's face and set her down and went back for the second one, who was coming now on his own, the pressure behind him releasing with the first one's passage, and he came sliding out with an expression—insofar as a lamb could be said to have an expression—of tremendous impatience, as though he'd been made to wait unnecessarily for something he'd wanted for quite some time.

Two girls, it turned out; she'd been wrong about the second. She told Daniel and he smiled, the first real smile she'd seen from him all night, and it caught her off guard the way it sometimes did, how much he looked like Marcus when he smiled and also how much he didn't, how he was his own person becoming more himself every year, and that was right, that was how it should be, and it was still strange.

They worked on. The storm made its noise against the shed and the generator kept running and the hours passed in the way hours pass when the body is occupied and the mind is only partly present, the rest of it somewhere else, doing the accounting that the mind does when the body is working. Ruth thought about the east fence that needed new posts before May. She thought about the feed bill and the prices she'd get for the wool and whether they were realistic. She thought about calling Nora, her friend in Burlington, who called every week and who Ruth could not quite manage to call back, not yet. She thought about a morning in early April two years ago when Marcus had come in from the barn with a newborn lamb inside his coat, the lamb's head poking out from the unzipped front, and he'd come into the kitchen looking like a man who'd grown a second head and said, her mother won't take her, in the plaintive tone of someone who knows what comes next and is resigned to it, and they'd raised that lamb in the kitchen for two weeks and called her Button and she was in the near field now, three years old, and sometimes she still came to the fence when she heard Ruth's voice.

By two in the morning, twenty-three lambs had been born. Four more ewes were still working. Daniel had fallen asleep sitting against the wall between two pens, his chin on his chest and his hands open in his lap like a man waiting to receive something, and Ruth had let him sleep for an hour and then woken him because she needed him and because sleeping on a cold floor in a lambing shed was not sleeping, not really, and he'd woken without complaint and gotten up and gone to wash his hands.

The snow was still coming. She could hear it in the way the wind had changed, gone lower and more constant, the long settling sound of a storm that has found its pace and intends to maintain it. Come morning the road would be closed and they would be, as they had been before, an island, a small lit thing in a lot of white, and that was all right. She had twelve days of feed. She had diesel for the generator. She had her son.

One of the remaining ewes delivered without assistance sometime around three, Ruth turning from the opposite end of the shed to find the ewe already at work, already recognizing her lamb, and she thought: they don't need us for most of it. They manage most of it themselves. We are only here for the part that goes wrong.

Which was maybe enough, she thought. To be there for the part that goes wrong.

She wrote in the lambing book by the light of a headlamp she'd strapped on, moving down the column, the dates, the times, her handwriting nothing like Marcus's, big and slightly slanted as it had always been. She did not look at last year's entries. She recorded what the night had produced: twenty-six lambs by four in the morning, all living, and then she sat on the hay bale with the book open on her knees and listened to the shed, the various sounds of it, the lambs sleeping and the ewes settled and the generator going and outside the storm doing what it was doing, which was the same thing it had been doing all night and which it would do until it stopped.

Daniel brought her a cup of coffee from the thermos, the last of it, lukewarm now.

"Three more to go," he said, looking down the row.

"They'll wait," she said. "They'll be morning lambs."

"Is that a real thing or are you making it up?"

"I'm making it up," she said. "But I believe it."

He sat down beside her on the hay bale, his shoulder against her shoulder, and they looked at the shed together, at the lit pens and the sleeping lambs and the ewes standing in the red heat-lamp glow with the patient composure of creatures that have been doing this forever and will do it again. The wind moved along the outside of the building. The generator held. Somewhere in the near paddock, a new ewe who was not new anymore stood learning her own lamb's smell in the dark, committing it to whatever register of self it is that animals use for love, so she would know it the next morning and the morning after and not mistake it for any other thing in the world.

Ruth drank her lukewarm coffee. Her son was warm against her side. The snow kept coming down.
