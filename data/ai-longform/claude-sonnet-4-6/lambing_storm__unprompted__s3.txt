The first ewe started at dusk, before the snow had committed to anything serious, just a few exploratory flakes catching in the yard light and dissolving against the mud. Ruth had seen the signs before supper—the hollow flanks, the restless circling, the way the animal pressed her nose against the corner post as though looking for a door—and had told Callum to eat quickly. He'd pushed his food around the plate with the particular teenage economy of protest that said he understood this was not a night for argument, which meant he was already afraid.

Now it was past nine and the shed was warm with animal heat, the bare bulb throwing its yellow cone over the pen where the first lamb lay in the straw, shakily discovering its legs while its mother worked at it with her tongue. Ruth watched the lamb's ears, the twitch and flatten, the small adjustments of an animal arriving into its body. Callum was on the other side of the pen boards, forearms resting on the top rail, watching too. The snow was serious now. She could hear it against the shed's tin roof, not the soft hiss of light snowfall but something denser, with ambition.

"You should have brought your coat in," she said.

"I'm not cold."

She looked at him briefly. He was wearing two flannels and the expression of someone who was, in fact, cold, but had calculated that admitting it would cost more than tolerating it.

"Coat's on the hook," she said, and left it there.

They'd had fifty-one ewes put to the ram in November, and thirty-eight had held. Of those thirty-eight, Ruth's count based on body condition and the dates she'd recorded in the green ledger suggested twenty-six were still to lamb, not counting this one tonight. She had never run the numbers by herself before. Her husband had been the one who kept the ledger, who circled dates and drew small symbols in the margins—a star for twins, a cross when they lost one—and when she'd found it in January she'd sat at the kitchen table and read it like a letter. Three years of his handwriting. His notation was more optimistic than hers; where she wrote numbers he had written observations. *Ewe 14 anxious, watching.* *Cold night, all well.* She had studied those parenthetical phrases the way you study a foreign language you once knew.

The second ewe was showing. Ruth moved through the pen, checking the position, and felt the animal's back contract under her hand like something being wrung out.

"Get me the iodine and the towels from the shelf," she said. "Second shelf, the blue tub."

Callum went without the sound of his boots changing pace, which meant he was alert. She was grateful for small shifts like this, the way his body could signal cooperation even when his face had gone somewhere else. He was sixteen and had his father's jaw and the beginning of his father's tendency to solve things by going quiet—a trait she had once found reassuring in a husband and now found destabilizing in a son, because she had learned to read her husband's silences and Callum's were still uncharted.

The second lamb came easily, a single, and Ruth had it cleared and the cord done while Callum held the light closer—he'd picked up the flashlight from the shelf on his own, understanding she needed it before she'd asked. The lamb was vigorous, shaking its head, full of objection. Ruth laughed, a real one, sudden enough to surprise her.

"What?" Callum said.

"Nothing. She's a complainer. Look at her."

He watched the lamb nosing along its mother's flank. The ewe was attentive, thorough, already committed. "How do you know it's a she?"

"I checked."

"How can you tell already?"

She showed him, and he accepted this information with the neutrality of a person cataloguing something for later use. He was like that—received things and filed them. At ten he had been still and watchful in a way that made teachers say *he's so mature* and mean something that was only partly a compliment. Now at sixteen the stillness had compacted into something denser and less readable. Since January, since the funeral, since the whole architecture of his father's presence had been removed from their days, she'd noticed him watching her the way you watch a load-bearing wall after a nearby collapse.

She washed her hands in the bucket, dried them on her jeans. The wind had changed pitch outside. It was, she could tell, no longer a single-night nuisance. She'd seen the radar before supper and seen the mass of white over the mountains to the northwest and thought: *maybe it'll split* the way she thought every time, and every time half the storms split and half the storms didn't, and this was one that hadn't.

She went to the shed door and opened it three inches and looked out. The yard light showed her a wall of horizontal snow, the fence posts becoming suggestions. It was past the fence at the road. It was the kind of snow that resculpted things. She closed the door and dropped the bar across it.

"Bad?" Callum said.

"It's fine."

"You don't have to—"

"It's bad," she said. "It's a late-April blizzard and it's going to go all night. We're fine in here, everything's fine, but we're in here until it's done." She said it cleanly, without softening. She had made a decision in January, after the third week of finding herself editing information for him, shaping it, tilting it toward better—she'd decided to stop. He was sixteen. He'd seen his father lifted into an ambulance on a Tuesday morning and by Thursday understood that he would not return. He did not need her to renovate the weather.

He nodded. "Okay."

"I need you to check the water heater in the back corner. Make sure it hasn't tripped. And count what we have in the way of colostrum, there should be a box on the third shelf."

He went. She watched the ewes.

There were eleven in the lambing pens, in various states of progress. Six had already delivered—four singles, one set of twins where the second twin had needed help, a brief alarming crisis that had passed without requiring more than her hands and calm—and five were waiting, pacing, pressing against the boards. The shed smelled of straw and lanolin and the particular iron smell of birth, which she had never found unpleasant. Her husband had. He'd had a sensitive stomach, unlikely in a farmer, and had compensated with a very particular brand of self-discipline that she had admired and occasionally mocked and now thought about with the precision of someone who has lost an object they handled daily. She could describe its weight and shape perfectly but not its absence.

"Heater's on," Callum said, coming back. "Three bags of colostrum."

"Good. That's enough."

"Is it?"

"For tonight." She considered. "Probably."

The lights went out.

The darkness was total for three seconds and then Callum switched on the flashlight and swept it across the pens, catching the reflective green of the ewes' eyes, the pale shapes of lambs against their mothers. Ruth already had her headlamp from the pocket of her barn jacket—she'd put it there in November and hadn't moved it—and clicked it on. Her husband had bought her the headlamp three Christmases ago, a good one, after she'd complained about holding the flashlight in her armpit during difficult deliveries. She'd made fun of how enthusiastically he'd presented it, as though he'd found the solution to a problem that had plagued him personally. She had thanked him and set it on the shelf and that first season used it every single night.

"Should we check the generator?" Callum said.

"It'll be covered by now. We can get it in the morning." She moved the headlamp beam across the hanging thermometer near the door. "It's thirty-eight in here. With the animals it'll hold. We need the lantern from the tack room."

The lantern was a kerosene one, and she'd bought it at the end of last season and it had lived in the tack room ever since, one of those purchases you make in acknowledgment of a specific future disaster. She'd made a dozen such purchases since January. The chainsaw chain, sharpened and hung. The antifreeze in the pump house. The spare water pump still in its box. She had understood, moving through last autumn like someone examining a map for the first time, that there was an entire architecture of preparedness she had not been building, because someone else had been building it. And that person was gone, and the architecture was now hers to understand.

Callum went for the lantern. She moved to the ewe in the third pen who was circling rapidly, side-pressing, and knelt in the straw beside her. The light from her headlamp caught the beginning of the water sac, the gleam of it. *All right,* she said to the ewe, meaning it genuinely, as a form of greeting and acknowledgment—*I see you, I'm here, I know*—the way you speak to someone when there is nothing yet to report except your presence.

By eleven the tally had grown. Four more lambs, two of them twins from a ewe that Ruth had been watching all week, a big ewe, experienced, the kind of animal you trusted to manage, and she had managed—Ruth had arrived to the second twin already two-thirds out and had simply received it, guided it down, cleared its face. The mother had turned before Ruth had finished the cord and started in on the first twin with such single-minded urgency that Ruth had laughed again, the same surprised laugh, and this time Callum had seen her face.

"You do that," he said. "You laugh when they're really—" He searched for the word.

"Alive," she offered.

"Yeah." He paused. "Dad used to do that too."

She didn't answer right away. She finished what she was doing, which required her whole attention, and then she said, "Did he?"

"Sometimes. I only came out a few times."

"You were young."

"I'm not young now."

"No," she agreed. "You're not."

She'd brought him once when he was about seven, a milder night, more of a demonstration than a working night, and her husband had let him name the lamb. He'd chosen the name *Captain,* with complete conviction, and her husband had written it in the margin of the ledger without apparent irony, as though this were a perfectly functional name for a sheep. She had looked at that entry in January: *April 9, single ram lamb, Captain.* She had not expected to feel it as much as she did.

The wind intensified around midnight. The shed boards flexed and the lantern put a soft motion into all the shadows. Callum had dragged two bales from the stack along the back wall and arranged them in the aisle and was sitting on them, back against the post, monitoring. She'd given him a specific job: to watch for any ewe that went past forty minutes on active labor without progress, to come and get her. He was doing this with the same compacted attention he brought to everything—very still, not missing things.

They had lost a lamb at two in the morning. A twin, the second of a pair, born small and with a misaligned jaw, a rare thing, the kind of bad news that sometimes comes at the end of a sequence of good nights, as though the balance sheet requires an entry. Ruth had worked on the lamb for eleven minutes and then had held it while it did what small things did when the structure was wrong, and then she had set it in the corner on fresh straw and covered it with a towel and not looked at it again. She had done this many times before. The ceremony of covering was something her husband had taught her—*cover it so the others don't get bothered, and so you can collect yourself.*

She didn't think she'd said anything. But Callum had been watching her from the bales and when she stood up and went to the next pen he was beside her a minute later, not touching her, not speaking, but beside her with the flashlight, pointing it where she needed it.

She looked at him over the beam.

"You okay?" he said.

"Yes." She paused. "Are you?"

He thought about it with visible effort, which was something she respected in him—he did not answer reflexively. "I think so," he said. "It's harder to watch you work on one and not—" He stopped. "I kept thinking if I kept looking it would be okay."

"That feeling doesn't go away," she said. "You still think that at forty-three."

He accepted this.

"It's part of being here," she said. "It means you're paying attention."

She had no way of knowing if this was wisdom or justification. She'd been saying things like this since January—small formulations, compact sentences about grief or work or the relationship between them—and she was never sure if she was telling him something she knew or working something out aloud. Her husband had been the one who said things with certainty. He had held opinions about things—weather patterns, machinery, human nature—with a steadiness that she had sometimes contested and more often borrowed, stood near like you stand near a fire, grateful for the warmth without asking about the fuel. Now there was no fire. She was working from her own heat and it was sufficient, she thought, it was going to be sufficient, but it was not the same experience.

At two-thirty the snow eased slightly, not stopped but changed in character, dropping from horizontal to something more vertical, which she interpreted as the wind shifting rather than weakening. She could tell this because she went to the door again and opened it three inches and looked out, and the yard light had given up—it was on a circuit with the house—but she had the headlamp and she shone it into the yard and the snow fell differently than before.

Callum came and stood behind her and looked over her shoulder.

"The road'll be gone," he said.

"Completely."

"When do you think—"

"Morning, probably. Could be afternoon." She closed the door. "It doesn't matter. We have everything we need."

"The coffee's gone."

"We have water."

"It's not the same."

"No," she said. "It's not."

There were three ewes still to go by her count. One of them, number fourteen in the ledger—her husband had noted, years running, *ewe 14 anxious, watching*—was showing the first signs of labor, the same nose-against-the-boards she'd seen in the first one at dusk. Ruth watched her and felt something she could not name precisely, a recognition that was more than practical. She'd had this ewe for four years. She knew her the way you know a difficult colleague—with a specific mixture of exasperation and respect, a knowledge of the patterns of her difficulty.

"This one's going to be trouble," she told Callum.

"How do you know?"

"She always is."

"Can't you tell from the—" He made a gesture indicating position, the general interior landscape.

"You can tell some things by feel. Some things you just know from watching something over years."

He considered this. "Is that what you do with people?"

The question caught her. It was a genuinely open question, not pointed, not rhetorical—he was actually asking. She thought about it while she watched the ewe.

"I think so," she said. "I think you learn people the same way. You learn their patterns and you learn which patterns mean something serious and which ones are just how they are."

"Did you know that about Dad?"

"Yes."

"Could you always tell when something was serious?"

She was quiet for a moment. The ewe was circling. "Not always," she said. "I missed things sometimes. I got used to certain patterns and I thought they were just how he was and some of them were." She felt the accuracy of what she was saying as she said it, the way you feel a knife's edge. "Some of them were something else."

She did not say what she meant by this because she didn't need to and because it wasn't the place. But it was there between them, the question that had lived in the house since January, the question that had no useful answer but that occasionally needed to be acknowledged, like a difficult animal you could not put down and could not fully manage—only learn to work around, give it space, not turn your back on it.

"I miss him," Callum said.

"I know."

"I know you do too."

"Every day," she said. "Not always the same way. It changes. What you miss changes." She was looking at the ewe, at the anxious body circling. "Some days I miss the way he moved around the kitchen. Some days I miss arguing with him about things. Some days I miss knowing someone else's count was different from mine and that we could check each other."

"Is that what tonight is?"

She looked at him. The lantern light caught his face at an angle that was entirely his own and also entirely his father's, that unnerving doubleness that had been with her since January, the sense of looking at a continuing sentence.

"Tonight is what it always was," she said. "It's the same work. It was always this work."

She meant something by this that was neither consolation nor its opposite. She meant that the work was not a stand-in for something else, not a therapy or a metaphor—it was the thing itself, the specific weight of it, the cold and the smell and the animals and the hours, and her husband had stood in it the same way she stood in it, and now she stood in it alone and with her son, and those were different configurations of the same thing. She was not sure she was saying this well enough to be understood. She was not sure she was saying it at all.

The ewe fourteen labored badly, as predicted. The lamb was big and positioned at a difficult angle and Ruth was up to her elbow for longer than she liked, working carefully, the ewe bellowing, Callum keeping the headlamp steady with both hands and not flinching. She got the lamb's legs aligned. She worked it through the correction by feel alone, the total dark of that interior space, moving purely by what she knew—the shapes of bones, the geography of effort, the particular language of an animal in extremity. She had learned this over fifteen seasons and it was her knowledge, hers specifically, and there was something in that which she had not expected to feel: not pride exactly, not ownership, but a specific recognition of capacity. She was able to do this. She had always been able to do this.

The lamb came out at three forty-one in the morning, very large, somewhat stunned, and Ruth cleared it and held it upside down briefly to clear the airway and it coughed and began the urgent business of being alive. The ewe turned to it immediately, relieved of the long effort, single-minded with attention.

"There," Ruth said.

Callum let out a breath he had apparently been holding for some time.

"She's all right?"

"She's all right."

He set the flashlight down in the straw so it lit the pen sideways and then sat back on his heels and rubbed his face with both hands, a gesture so adult and so exhausted that she felt it in her chest—the accumulated weight of the night and the season and the months before it, the whole year of reckoning made visible in that simple motion.

"You should sleep," she said. "There are two more to go but they're not close. Sleep on the bales and I'll wake you if I need you."

He didn't argue. He rearranged the bales he'd stacked and lay down on them with his flannel jacket pulled over his shoulders and was asleep in three minutes. She watched him briefly. She watched his chest rise and fall.

Outside the snow was still coming but differently—she could hear it changing again, the sound on the tin roof losing its aggressive edge, becoming something quieter. By morning there would be three feet in the yard and the road would be gone until the county ran the plow, and there were two more ewes to go before she could sleep, and then everything after that: the long work of spring, the counting and recording, the lambs growing into themselves, the fields coming back. She had not run a farm alone before. She had not understood, until this year, that she had been running half of one for a very long time.

She went to the next ewe and knelt in the straw and put her hand to the animal's warm side and felt the great muscular patient work of it, the body doing what bodies did, making the thing that would inherit the field. The lantern burned. The lambs slept in clusters against their mothers. On the bales her son slept with his face turned sideways, toward the light.

She worked, and the night moved around her toward morning, and she was enough.
