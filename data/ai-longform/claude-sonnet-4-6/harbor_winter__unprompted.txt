The cold came early that year, before the nets were even hauled in from the last run, and Maren watched her grandfather stand at the kitchen window each morning with his coffee cooling in his hand, looking out at the harbor the way a man looks at something he believes he invented. She was eleven that winter, old enough to understand that the cannery closing meant something adult and irreversible was happening to the town, but still young enough to think that understanding it was enough, that comprehension alone could hold a thing in place.

Her mother had gone to Portland in October to look at apartments, a reconnaissance mission she had explained carefully to Maren, as though the word itself carried official weight, as though saying reconnaissance instead of looking for somewhere to leave would make leaving feel more measured, more earned. She had been gone six weeks. The calls came on Sundays, her mother's voice thin and compressed through the phone like something being shipped in inadequate packaging, and Maren would listen and answer in the monosyllables that constituted her emotional vocabulary that autumn, while her grandfather sat at the table pretending to read the newspaper he had already read.

His name was Einar Voss, and he had piloted ships through the harbor mouth for thirty-one years, guiding freighters and tankers through the channel where the rocks showed their black teeth at low tide, where the currents ran sideways and deceptive under a surface that gave away nothing except to those who knew how to ask. He was seventy-three years old and his hands still looked like they were made for gripping something larger than a coffee cup. His face was a record of weather. His wife, Maren's grandmother, had died the previous spring of a cancer that moved fast and without sentiment, and the house still held her shape the way a coat holds the shape of whoever last wore it—her reading glasses on the nightstand, a jar of buttons on the windowsill that no one could bring themselves to put away.

Maren had been sent to stay with him while her mother conducted her reconnaissance, and what was supposed to be two weeks had become six, and when her grandfather called her mother on a Tuesday evening—Maren listening from the hallway—she heard him say, simply, let her stay through winter, Ruth, she's fine, she's good company and she's good with boats, all of which was partly true. Maren was fine. She was, in the provisional way of children absorbing catastrophe, completely fine. And she had been in a boat exactly twice in her life, both times in summer, both times with life preservers cinched so tight she could barely breathe.

He began taking her down to the harbor on the mornings when the fog lay thick and flat across the water, which was most mornings that November. The docks were quieter than they had been in her mother's childhood stories—her grandfather's truck the only one in the lot most days, or occasionally one of the Halvorsen brothers' pickups, the men themselves visible only as shapes moving on the dock, as voices absorbed by the fog before they carried any distance. The cannery sat at the south end of the harbor, and even when it was running it was running less than before, and you could hear the difference, the machinery intermittent where it had once been constant, a pulse grown arrhythmic, the town's body failing in the way bodies fail, not all at once but in the withdrawal of one system after another.

Her grandfather always stopped before he stepped onto the dock. He would stand at the edge where the concrete met the weathered planks and look at the water, and Maren began stopping beside him, not knowing at first what she was supposed to be looking at.

What are we doing, she asked one morning.

Reading it, he said.

Reading what.

The water. What it says.

She looked. The harbor was gray-green under a white sky, opaque, giving nothing back. It looked like it had nothing to say.

It doesn't look like it's saying anything, she said.

He glanced at her sideways, something almost amused crossing his face. He had a way of being almost amused that was as close as he came, that autumn, to joy. Everything says something, he said. Water especially. It just doesn't use words.

He pointed to a stretch near the middle of the channel. You see where the surface is rougher there, he said. More texture. She looked and saw it, or thought she did, a slight differentiation, a place where the gray was darker and more agitated. That's where the tide is running against the wind, he said. They don't agree. When they don't agree they make it visible. When they're going the same direction it all smooths out. Looks like there's nothing to it.

So it argues with itself, Maren said.

He considered this. In a manner of speaking, he said. Yes.

This became the lesson. Every morning he would name something: the upwellings that brought cold water to the surface and marked themselves with a strange oily flatness, as though the depth below had pressed its palm against the underside; the lines of current that drew themselves in floating debris, in foam, in the way a gull on the water traveled without swimming; the rips near the harbor mouth where the channel narrowed and the water stood up in confused ridges that had wrecked three boats in his working lifetime. He was precise and patient in the way of men who have spent their lives managing risk by the refinement of attention, and Maren found that she was genuinely learning, which surprised her. She had not thought of herself as someone who learned things easily, partly because school had never provided her with information that felt necessary to know.

But this felt necessary. Or at least it felt connected to something necessary—to the harbor, to her grandfather's life in it, to the fact that this was all going away and someone should know what he knew, even if they were eleven.

In December the cannery had its last run. Word went through the town in that way word traveled in small places, not announced so much as simply known, a fact that materialized in conversations at Ringstad's hardware store and at the coffee counter of the Dock & Diner as though it had been growing there for years, which it had. Maren was with her grandfather at the diner on the morning the final haul came in, and through the window she watched the boats come into the harbor, five of them, the Halvorsen brothers and the Nilsens and old Costa in his green-hulled seiner, the boats low and burdened with the last catch, moving across the flat gray water with a kind of dignity that may have been projected, she understood later, by everyone who watched them. The whole diner watched. The woman behind the counter, whose name was Gloria and who had worked there as long as anyone could remember, stopped moving. The coffee cups sat where they were. Everyone watched the boats come in, and then Gloria turned and began filling cups and the moment was done, absorbed into the business of the day, the way moments are absorbed, the way everything is eventually absorbed.

Her grandfather's hand was wrapped around his cup and he was watching too, and she saw his jaw work slightly, the way it did when he was keeping something back.

Were you ever on those boats, she asked. When you weren't piloting.

Before. When I was young. My uncle had a dragger out of this harbor.

Did you like it.

He thought about this honestly, which she appreciated. Parts of it, he said. Being on the water. I always liked that. The work was brutal, though. Cold and brutal and the margins were too thin for how hard you worked. He paused. But the water. He stopped there, as though the sentence were complete.

She understood it was complete.

He had a name for the thing she was learning. He called it reading the water but he also sometimes called it, in the old Norwegian his parents had spoken, å kjenne sjøen, which he translated roughly as knowing the sea or, more accurately he said, feeling acquainted with the sea, the way you might be acquainted with a difficult person you'd spent a long time around. You didn't trust them exactly but you knew their habits. You knew when they were building toward something.

He told her about the ships he had guided through the channel over thirty-one years. He told her about a supertanker, immense and stupid in the water, two hundred thousand tons of crude drawing twenty meters of draft, and how guiding that into a harbor built for fishing boats was like asking a cathedral to walk through a doorway, you could do it if you were extremely precise and extremely patient and if the conditions gave you exactly what you needed, and mostly they had. He told her about a night in February, his third year as a pilot, when a freighter's engine failed as he was bringing her through the cut and there was nothing to do but use the remaining way on the ship, the inertia of all that mass still moving, and steer her into a controlled grounding on the shingle to the south of the main channel, and how he had stood on the bridge in the dark and spoken to the water the way his grandfather had taught him, telling it what he needed, which he understood was superstition but was also something more than superstition, was the act of focusing the mind completely on what the water was doing so that the mind could respond before it knew it had.

Maren listened to these stories carefully. She was building a picture of him that was different from the grandfather she had known in summers, the one who grilled fish and took her to the tide pools and drove her to the ice cream place north of town in his old truck with the radio that got only one station. This person was older than that grandfather and also younger, as though the stories held a version of him that predated her experience of him. She found she was glad to be knowing this version. She found she was glad her mother had gone to Portland and stayed.

The calls still came on Sundays. Her mother had found an apartment near a school that was supposed to be good. There was a room for Maren. The school had an arts program and a music program and after-school activities that her mother named with a kind of salesmanship that landed badly over the phone, the salesmanship of someone trying to make the future more appealing than the present, which Maren understood was her mother's job and also her mother's right, but which did not make the present less appealing. The present was her grandfather in his kitchen at six in the morning with his coffee and his window and his harbor. The present was the thing she was learning to read.

In late December a storm came in from the northwest, a real one, not the gray drizzle and chop that passed for weather most of that winter but an actual storm with wind that made the house speak and rain that came sideways and drove itself under the eaves and against the glass. They lost power for eight hours. Her grandfather lit the oil lamps his grandmother had brought from Norway seventy years ago and they ate the soup he had made the day before, cold from the pot because the stove was electric, and he told her about his grandmother, who had been afraid of very little and had crossed the Atlantic at nineteen in a ship with seventeen other women from her village, arriving in a harbor she had only heard described, stepping onto a dock in a country whose language she owned almost none of, and making of that almost-nothing a life.

Her name was also Maren, her grandfather said.

She knew this. Her mother had told her. But she had not known what to do with the information before, and sitting in the lamplight with the storm throwing itself against the house she found she could feel the connection between herself and that other Maren in a way that was almost physical, a thread running back through the dark.

She must have been brave, Maren said.

Her grandfather tilted his lamp's light against his coffee cup, watching the light move in the liquid. She didn't have any particular choice, he said. That's one kind of brave.

Is there another kind.

He looked at her. When you do have a choice, he said. And you do it anyway.

After the storm he took her out in the small aluminum skiff he kept on the dock, the one with the ten-horsepower outboard that he nursed along with the tenderness he reserved for old things that still worked. The harbor had been rearranged by the storm, or at least the surface of it had—there was debris everywhere, a carpet of kelp and broken wood and plastic that moved sluggishly in the tidal run. Two of the moored boats had taken damage and the dock had lost a section of railing that had gone somewhere, the sea not returning what it borrowed. The air was cold and clean in the particular way that follows real weather, scraped clean, beginning again.

He idled out past the boats and then cut the engine and let them drift, and for a while neither of them said anything. She could feel the water working under the skiff, the small rhythmic movement of residual swell still unsettled from the night's disturbance, and she understood she was feeling what he had taught her to see, that the information was coming through the aluminum and through the seat and into her body, and her body was processing it the way it processed balance, below thought, automatically.

See how the rip's moved, he said.

She looked toward the harbor mouth. The rip was in a different place than it usually was, the choppy standing water that marked the confluence of the channel current with the tidal pull, it had migrated south, toward the cannery side.

Why, she said.

The storm moved sand in the cut, he said. Changed the bottom. Changes the rip. He paused. It changes every winter, a little. You have to keep reading it. It's never finished.

She sat with this. The skiff turned slowly on some invisible axis, describing a lazy circle in the still water between the dock and the channel, and she watched the harbor mouth where the rocks showed with the tide low, the black teeth her grandfather had navigated around for thirty-one years, and she thought about all the ships that had come through there under his guidance, all the mass and inertia and loaded holds, all the possibility of catastrophe that his attentiveness had made into uneventful passage, the ships arriving at their berths without incident, the incident having been forestalled so completely that no one thought to name it.

This seemed to her a strange kind of heroism. The heroism of the thing not happening.

They drifted a while longer and then he started the engine and brought them back to the dock, tying off with the cleat hitch he had shown her, which she now did without being reminded.

On New Year's Eve the cannery workers held a party at the American Legion hall, which was the building in town used for everything that required a hall, and her grandfather took her, and she wore the dress her mother had sent for Christmas and sat at a table with her grandfather and the Halvorsens and drank ginger ale while the adults drank what they drank and the music played from a speaker system someone had hauled in, Norwegian folk songs and country music and once, inexplicably, a Frank Sinatra record that cleared the floor of everyone over sixty only to fill it with the same people sixty seconds later. She watched her grandfather dance with Brita Halvorsen whose husband had died three years ago of his heart, and she thought that her grandfather danced the way he did most things, carefully and with full attention, as though this particular dance were a channel to be navigated, as though he were reading the water of the music and of the person across from him and adjusting, adjusting, always adjusting.

She thought about the things she would take with her to Portland. She had not said yes to Portland yet, not explicitly, but she understood it was coming the way you understand weather is coming, by reading the signs. The apartment her mother described, the good school, the arts program: these were the dark texture on the smooth water, the flag of current below. Her mother was lonely. That, beneath all the salesmanship, was what was being said.

She could feel the moment coming the way her grandfather had taught her to feel a ship moving through her feet when it was still too far away to see.

The cannery was closed by February. On the day they locked it she and her grandfather drove past it without stopping, the parking lot empty, the loading dock bare, the smell of fish and brine and cold metal that had clung to it for eighty years already beginning to disperse, or maybe she only imagined it dispersing, maybe she was reading into the scene a transition it had not yet made. The building was still there. The smell probably still clung to everything inside. But something was gone from it, some animating principle, the way a house feels different when the people have left even if all their furniture remains.

In March her mother drove up from Portland to collect her. She arrived on a Saturday with the car already packed with boxes of things she had retrieved from storage, her new life assembled and ready. She embraced Maren's grandfather in the kitchen for a long time, her face against his chest, and he held her with his large weathered hands spread across her back. Then she looked around the kitchen the way you look at a place you are deciding how to carry.

Your grandmother's buttons, she said, looking at the jar on the windowsill.

Leave them, her grandfather said. They're fine where they are.

Maren helped load the car in the late morning, the harbor visible from the driveway, the water doing what it did, moving and reflecting and giving nothing away except to those who knew how to ask. She made one last trip to the dock before they left, alone, while her grandfather and mother were inside managing the things that needed to be managed. She stood at the edge where the concrete met the planks and looked at the water the way she had been taught.

The tide was dropping. She could see it in the way the current ran against the dock pilings, the water pulling seaward, and in the exposure of the rocks at the harbor mouth, their upper edges dry and dark. The rip was where her grandfather said it had moved after the storm, still there, still marking the changed bottom beneath. Near the north wall a thermal upwelling lay flat and oily on the surface, pressing up from below. On the far side of the harbor a line of foam described the edge of the channel current, a sentence laid down on the water, moving and reforming and moving again, never the same sentence twice.

She read it. She stood there in the cold March air with her jacket buttoned wrong and her hair pulled by the wind and she read it, all of it, the depth beneath the surface and the conflict at the edges and the slow pull of the tide toward open water. She was not illiterate anymore in this particular language. She was not fluent either, not nearly, not after one winter—her grandfather had spent his whole life learning and had said that he was still learning, that it was never finished, and she believed him.

But she could read it. Imperfectly, provisionally, the way you read in a language you have only begun to learn: catching some of it, knowing there is more you're missing, understanding that the missing is not failure but invitation. Come back. Keep reading. There is always more.

She heard her grandfather's voice from the direction of the house, calling her name, not urgently, the way you call someone when it's time. She took one more look at the water. The rip moved where the bottom had changed. The current wrote its foam along the channel edge. The tide continued its withdrawal, the sea keeping faith with some rhythm too large and old to be affected by canneries or Portland or any of the things that came and went on its surface.

She turned and walked back up the dock toward the house, where her grandfather was standing in the doorway with his coffee, watching her, and she thought that this was something she would carry—not the harbor itself, which could not be carried, but the way of looking at it, the quality of attention he had given her, the understanding that every surface is a text and that every text has a depth beneath it and that the depth does not reveal itself to people who are not paying attention, but to those who are, who stand at the edge and read and keep reading, it gives up something. Not everything. Never everything.

But something. Something true and cold and moving, something that does not use words.
