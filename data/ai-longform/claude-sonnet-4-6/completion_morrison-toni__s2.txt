He kept his eyes moving left and right along the tree line, watching for the particular stillness that meant watching. Regina, to her credit, was a calm animal, unbothered by the dense undergrowth pressing close on either side of the trail, unbothered by the sudden percussion of a woodpecker somewhere above, or the long shriek of something dying in the brush. Jacob loosened his coat despite the heat, wanting his hands free. The satchel across his chest contained papers enough to make his purpose clear to any official who stopped him, but officials were not the only men riding trails in this country.

The errand itself was simple enough, or should have been. Reinholt D'Ortega owed him money, had owed it for three seasons, and correspondence had proven useless. D'Ortega wrote back with great flourishes of language, elaborate courtesies and apologies and promises, and then nothing. No coin, no note honored, no arrangement kept. Jacob had decided that a man who would not pay through ink and paper might be shamed into paying through the pressure of another man's eyes. It was the way things were settled, finally. You rode to the place and you stood in front of the man and you made visible what had been abstract. Debt was always more comfortable as an abstraction.

He had heard things about D'Ortega. A planter of some ambition, Virginia born but with Lisbon connections, a Catholic who had survived the various upheavals of allegiance by the simple expedient of being rich enough to be forgiven his theology. He kept a large operation on the river, tobacco mainly, with some mixed crops, and he was said to be extending. A man extending always had money problems. A man with money problems was a man to watch.

The trail widened gradually as it neared higher ground and Jacob allowed Regina a little more pace. The fog had entirely burned off now and the light was the particular flat white of late morning in the tidewater, pressing down on everything with a kind of indifference, as though the sun had no preference for what it illuminated. Swamp gave way to stands of pine, and then to cleared land that smelled of turned earth and something sweeter underneath it that Jacob had learned to associate with this latitude, some combination of rot and flower that the north did not have, a perfume both attractive and faintly threatening, like the smile of a man who wants something from you.

He passed a crew of men working a field to his left and he did not slow. They did not look up. Their movements had the particular rhythm of people who have ceased to think about their movements, a kind of bodily forgetting that allowed the work to continue without the weight of its meaning. He had seen it before, that quality of motion, and he preferred not to think too carefully about what it meant, about what it required of a person to move that way.

It was another hour before the D'Ortega plantation came into view. It announced itself first as a smell, woodsmoke and something animal, and then as sound, the distant percussion of cooperage work, hammering in multiples, and then finally as a sight: the great house rising white and assertive at the end of an avenue of young oaks not yet large enough to provide the shade their planting had intended. Everything about the place spoke of ambition slightly outrunning its execution. The trees were too small. The house was large but its paint was already showing the climate's opinion of pretension. The outbuildings were numerous but not yet organized into any coherent arrangement, sprawling toward the river with an improvised quality, as though each one had been built in response to an emergency.

A boy came to take Regina and Jacob swung down, straightening himself, feeling the road in his hips. He was not yet forty but horses reminded him that he was closer to it than he had been. He gave the boy a coin out of habit and the boy looked at it with an expression that suggested coins were not the usual reward, which meant D'Ortega was either stingy or chaotic in his household management. Either quality was a bad sign for Jacob's purposes.

He was kept waiting on the porch for some time. A woman brought water and then disappeared. He could hear voices inside the house, the particular cadenced quiet of people regulating themselves in preparation for a conversation they were not sure they wanted to have. He drank the water and looked at the river, which was wide here and moving slowly, carrying on its brown surface the reflections of clouds and the occasional debris of the upstream country, a branch, a gourd, once a child's shoe floating upright with a dreadful persistence.

D'Ortega, when he came, was smaller than Jacob had imagined from his letters. The letters had the proportions of a large man, wide gestures of language, a kind of spatial generosity that belonged to someone who had always had enough room. The actual D'Ortega was compact, dark complexioned, dressed well but not lavishly, with eyes that moved quickly and a handshake that was firm in the particular way of men who have been taught that firmness is required but do not naturally incline toward it.

They spoke pleasantries of the kind that cost nothing. The journey, the weather, the condition of the trails, the news from the north, which was always of interest to men in the south because it arrived late enough to have already resolved itself and could therefore be discussed without anxiety. Jacob watched D'Ortega's face during this interval and saw what he expected to see: a man counting, adding up the cost of what was coming, rehearsing positions.

They moved inside to a room that was cooler but not cool, furnished with more taste than the exterior of the house had promised. Someone had an eye. There were objects on the shelves that had come from somewhere else, Portugal probably, a few pieces of delft, a painted chest, a mirror in a carved frame. The mirror showed Jacob his own face unexpectedly and he was briefly surprised, as always, by how much older he looked than he felt himself to be.

Wine was brought by a woman whose face he did not look at directly, a habit he had developed not from indifference but from something more complicated that he preferred not to examine.

D'Ortega opened by being gracious about the debt. He acknowledged it fully, framed it in the language of temporary difficulty and impending resolution, spoke of a cargo delayed, a partner in Barbados who had not performed as promised, the general unreliability of the Atlantic and its commerce. Jacob listened with patience because patience at this stage was not weakness but investment. Let the man finish his architecture. It was always better to hear it entire before you began to address its weak points.

When D'Ortega paused, Jacob said, I have notes in my satchel.

Of course, said D'Ortega. The specifics are not in question.

The amount is not in question, Jacob agreed. The timing has become a problem for me. You understand that I have my own obligations. Men I have committed to on the assumption that your commitment to me would be honored.

D'Ortega moved his wine glass one inch to the left and then one inch back. A small displacement with no purpose other than to give his hands something to do. Jacob watched it.

There is something I could offer, D'Ortega said. By way of partial settlement. Pending the resolution of the other matters.

Jacob waited.

D'Ortega stood and went to the window. Beyond it, across a bare yard, some of the outbuildings were visible. He spoke toward the glass rather than toward Jacob. I have more than I can manage at present, he said. In the expansion. The new ground has required more hands than I anticipated and I have overextended in that direction. He paused. I could transfer the note on two of them. Three, even. Against the debt.

Jacob understood him immediately and felt the understanding settle in his chest like something swallowed wrong. He looked at his wine.

That is not my trade, he said.

D'Ortega turned from the window. It is everyone's trade here, he said. He said it without aggression, as a simple statement of regional fact, which was more troubling than aggression would have been.

I am in different trade, Jacob said. Rum, mostly. Some dry goods. Notes and currency when the occasion requires.

D'Ortega nodded slowly, the nod of a man who has heard a position he does not believe but will not directly challenge. Then he sat back down and they negotiated, and by the time the afternoon light had shifted to a lower angle through the window's watery glass, they had arrived at an arrangement involving partial payment in coin, a transfer of goods through a factor in the port, and a promissory structure that Jacob did not fully trust but that was better than nothing, which was what he had arrived with.

He agreed to stay the night. It would have been impractical to refuse, and practicality was the god he served in such situations.

Supper was a significant production, the table set for more people than Jacob had expected. There were neighbors, a minister of some kind whose denomination was not clear, a young man who appeared to be D'Ortega's son or nephew and who had the particular vacancy of expression that belongs to people who have never needed to be intelligent, and a woman who was introduced as D'Ortega's wife and who was everything her husband was not: tall, fair, given to a kind of careful stillness that suggested either composure or suppressed grief, he could not tell which.

She spoke to Jacob at supper with directness and some intelligence, asking about the country he came from, the north, the farms up there, the winters, with the genuine curiosity of someone who has lived in one place long enough that other places have become imaginatively vivid to her. He told her about the farm, the cold that was a different order of thing than what she knew, the way snow transformed the land into something that had no equivalent in any other season, a country that appeared briefly between November and March that did not exist at other times.

She said, I should like to see snow.

Her husband said, You would not, and the table laughed in the way that tables laugh at things husbands say.

Jacob ate the food, which was good, and drank the wine, which was also good, and tried not to think too carefully about the arrangement he had made, whether the factor could be trusted, whether D'Ortega's Barbados partner was real or invented, whether the coin he had been given that afternoon was sufficiently reliable to build anything on. These were the thoughts that lived in the back of a man's mind during supper and could not be invited into the conversation.

After supper there was a period of sitting that went on too long and Jacob excused himself earlier than was polite. He was shown to a room by the same woman whose face he had not looked at directly, and in the light of the candle she carried he looked at her now, unable not to, because the light was doing something particular to the angles of her face, making it temporary, the way candlelight made everything temporary, as though the darkness pressing at every edge was simply waiting.

She was young. She was, he judged, somewhere between fifteen and twenty, though in this country and in her circumstances age had a different relationship to appearance than it did in the north, the climate and the work conspiring to hurry the body's accounting. She set the candle on the table beside the bed and turned to go and he said thank you without thinking and she stopped and looked at him directly, which was not the response he had expected, and then she looked away and went.

He lay on the bed in his clothes because the sheets had a quality he did not trust and he stared at the ceiling and listened to the house settle and the insects outside maintaining their relentless theological argument. He thought about the farm. He thought about Rebekka and whether the letter he had sent ahead of him had arrived, and whether she was managing the difficulty with the west field, and whether Lina's assessment of the situation he had left behind was correct. Lina's assessments usually were. She had a faculty for seeing the structure beneath the surface of events, for reading the forces that shaped a situation long before the situation announced itself openly. He valued this in her more than he valued almost any other quality in the people around him, though he had never said so because saying so would have changed the nature of what he valued, made it into something more formal and therefore more fragile.

He thought about D'Ortega's offer and felt again the particular quality of his discomfort with it, which was not simple. He was not a man who trafficked in that commerce, and he had a clear enough sense of why, though his reasons were not the reasons that certain ministers would have offered and that Jacob would not have claimed without feeling the dishonesty of the claiming. His reasons were more modest. He did not like it. It was a plain and insufficient reason that he had never managed to improve upon, and he had stopped trying. The world was full of things he did not like and that he nevertheless moved around and through with whatever grace he could manage, but this particular thing had an edge to it that others did not, a quality that made him feel, when he was adjacent to it, that he was in danger of becoming unrecognizable to himself, and that was a practical matter as much as a moral one. A man who is unrecognizable to himself cannot navigate.

The night passed with the slowness that strange beds imposed. He heard rain begin at some point and then stop. He heard something that might have been a dog and might not. He thought at one point that he heard a woman's voice raised in something that was not quite crying and not quite singing, and then he was not sure whether he had heard it or been briefly asleep. In the morning when he woke the air was washed and the light was better and the plantation had already organized itself into its daytime operations before he had finished dressing.

D'Ortega was at breakfast and was easier in manner than he had been the previous day, the way men sometimes were once the difficult conversation was behind them, as though difficulty were weather and it had passed. He spoke about his expansion plans with more detail than Jacob needed or wanted, the acreage under new cultivation, the tobacco market and its movements, the various calculations he was making about the coming decade. Jacob ate his eggs and listened with the portion of his attention that business required and let the rest of his mind move to the problem of the return journey.

He would not go back the same way. The Lenape trail made more sense southward when you were arriving, following the river, but returning he could take the coastal route and pick up the road that came in from the ferry crossing, which added time but reduced the isolation. The fog this morning was light, nothing like the previous day's obscurement, and if it continued to lift he could make reasonable time.

He said his good-byes with the appropriate ceremonies and D'Ortega walked him to the stables himself, which was either a gesture of respect or of relief. Regina had been well kept, which raised Jacob's opinion of the household slightly. The boy who brought her out looked for another coin and got one, and Jacob mounted and turned her head toward the avenue of young oaks and the road beyond.

He paused at the end of the avenue for a reason he could not have fully explained, looking back at the house in its white ambition, the way it stood up against the sky with a confidence that the peeling paint undermined but did not destroy. There were people moving in the yard between the house and the outbuildings, figures crossing from one task to another with the same quality of bodily forgetting he had noticed in the field workers the previous day. For a moment his eye was drawn to one figure near the far outbuilding, a small figure moving very slowly, with a carefulness that was different from the forgetting of the others, a carefulness that was more like intention, like someone moving through a space they were memorizing in case they needed to find it again in the dark. He watched this figure for a moment and then Regina shifted under him and he turned her and rode.

The coastal route was longer but easier on his nerves. He kept a good pace along the packed sand where the tide had been, the Atlantic to his right making its permanent indifferent argument against the shore, flattening and rebuilding and flattening again. The fog was gone by mid-morning and the light was sharper than it had been the previous day, showing him everything without the gold-filtered uncertainty of arrival. He felt the difference that direction made, outbound and returning, the way the same landscape read differently depending on whether you were moving toward your obligation or away from it.

He passed through a small settlement of freed men working garden plots, who watched him without hostility but without welcome, which was their right, and he tipped his hat and kept his pace. He passed a church standing alone in a clearing with no sign of the congregation it served. He passed a stretch of burned ground that had been something—structures of some kind—and was now only the bones of posts and the geometry of what had stood, legible to anyone who took the time to read it, another account of the pitched battles and their aftermath that still marked this landscape with the record of its choices.

Midday he stopped at a crossroads where there was a water trough and a post with directions crudely carved. He let Regina drink and ate what he had brought in the satchel, bread that had gone stiff but was not unpleasant, dried meat that required attention and rewarded it. He thought about nothing for a while, which was a skill he had cultivated over many years of solitary traveling, the capacity to let the mind go empty the way a room goes empty, furniture and all, not vacant but cleared. It helped with the problem of deciding, which was the problem of knowing which of your thoughts were actually thoughts and which were only sounds the mind made in the company of its own anxiety.

He sat on a fallen log near the trough and looked at the sky through the canopy of a live oak that was ancient and enormous and entirely unconcerned with his affairs. A woodpecker somewhere was still hammering or had begun again. He thought of Rebekka and felt the particular longing that distance made, which was not quite the same as the feeling he had for her when he was present, but was its cousin, its traveling version, shaped differently by being carried rather than held.

He had been a lucky man in her. He knew it and tried to know it consistently rather than only in moments of distance or gratitude. She had come to him across the water with no guarantee and only approximate information about what she was coming to and she had made of the improbable situation something that had coherence and warmth, something that felt, in the good seasons, like the right shape for two people to inhabit. The bad seasons had been real. They both knew the inventory. But they had not let the bad seasons stand for everything, and that was a kind of accomplishment he did not underestimate.

He mounted again and rode on, and the afternoon stretched out before him in its flat white sufficiency. He tried not to think about what D'Ortega had offered and found he could not entirely stop thinking about it. The image that had no right to be there: the figure near the outbuilding, moving with that careful intentional slowness, memorizing the space. He did not know why that image persisted when so many others from the two days had already faded. He did not know what the figure had been doing, or who it was, or what the carefulness meant in that specific life and not some abstract version of it.

He reached the ferry crossing in the late afternoon. The ferryman was an old man with no interest in conversation, which suited Jacob, and the crossing was uneventful, the river wide and slow at this point, carrying its usual cargo of debris and reflection. On the opposite bank he paid and remounted and turned north, toward the country he knew, toward the cold that was coming in its season, toward the farm and its difficulties and its particular shape of life.

The road north was better than the coastal routes and he made better time. As the light softened toward evening he began to feel the familiar adjustment of a man approaching home, the way the body recalibrated, anticipating rest. He passed through a village where he stopped long enough to confirm the road and to buy oats for Regina and a small jug of something dark that the man selling it called cider and that Jacob recognized as something stronger, which was fine. He drank a small portion of it while still in the village and corked it and stowed it and rode on.

The evening birds were beginning their business in the trees along the road and the light was doing the thing it did in this season, this latitude, turning everything briefly and without warning into gold, not the thick gold of the morning fog but something thinner and more temporary, a glaze laid over the world by a hand that was already moving to take it back. He rode through it and past it and into the gray that followed, and continued north.

Behind him, if he could have seen it, the plantation stood in its white ambition against the last of the light, and in the yard the figure he had watched moved again with its careful memorizing slowness, and the river carried its clouds and its debris, and the young oaks stood too small for the shade they had been planted to provide, and the evening settled over everything with the same indifference with which the morning had lifted.
