The ferry leaves at eleven and he is the last to board, coming through the metal gate with his bag already wet from the rain that has been falling since afternoon. The deck officer doesn't look at him. No one looks at him. That is the gift of this hour, this particular darkness that belongs to coastal towns after the restaurants close and before the fishing boats go out, a darkness that permits a man to be only mass and movement, neither known nor required to know himself.

He finds a seat in the passenger lounge near a window opaque with condensation and sets his bag between his feet and does not remove his jacket. Around him people sleep in the particular collapsed way of ferry passengers, mouths soft, arms folded across their chests as if conserving heat against some cold they have learned to anticipate. A woman near the bow has arranged her coat over a child. An older man holds a paper coffee cup with both hands though the coffee has long gone cold and his eyes are shut. Marcus watches them without meaning to, the way you watch anything when what you are travelling toward is too specific to think about directly, when the mind requires objects, surfaces, the small adjustments bodies make in sleep.

He had gotten the call that morning. Not morning. Four in the morning, which is its own country.

The voice on the phone belonged to a constable named Elspeth Vore, and she had spoken with a care that suggested she had done this before and had learned that care was what was required, not efficiency, though efficiency was perhaps what she wanted. There is a body, she said. We believe we know who it is but we would like someone to come. Marcus had written down what she told him on the back of an envelope because there was nothing else near the phone and then he had stood in his kitchen holding the envelope and looking at his own handwriting as if the words were in a language he was beginning to learn.

She had been the one to say the name. Davy. He had not been able to say it, not that morning, not during the six hours of driving to the coast.

Fourteen years. Fourteen years since the last time he had seen his brother's face as a present-tense fact and not as something receding, something already becoming the past even as it happened, Davy standing in the doorway of their mother's house on the November morning after her funeral, his coat already on, his bag at his feet, his face wearing the expression it always wore when he had decided something, which was no expression at all, the face emptied out to allow the decision to occupy it completely. Marcus had said something. He cannot now remember what. Something inadequate, or else something that had seemed adequate but arrived too late, after the moment had already closed. Davy had picked up the bag. The door was a brown door, he remembers that, brown with a brass knocker in the shape of a thistle, a knocker no one had ever used because everyone who came to that house knew to simply try the handle. He had watched his brother walk down the path to the road and had not followed and the next morning the house was emptier by one person and then emptier again when Marcus himself left, and then it was sold and there was no more path.

He does not know what he expects to find in the morning. He knows what the constable told him and he knows what fourteen years of not-knowing feels like, which is less like not-knowing than like knowing something that cannot be verified, a knowledge that lives in the body rather than the mind, in the particular way you answer when someone asks do you have a brother, the slight hesitation before have, the way the tense itself becomes uncertain. He has never said I had a brother. He has said I have. Even in the years when cards went unanswered, when addresses stopped working, when the single mutual friend they shared, a man named Colin who had been at school with both of them and who Marcus now saw perhaps once a year at Christmas, shrugged and said he hadn't heard. Even then Marcus has said have. The present tense as a kind of refusal.

Outside the window there is nothing. The strait in the dark is just the dark. He can hear the engines somewhere below him, the low determined sound of something very large moving through resistance, and occasionally the ferry shudders and he puts his hand flat on the seat beside him without thinking about it, the way you brace yourself when a vehicle goes over rough ground.

The constable had said they found him on the rocks below the headland at Stornaglass. She had said they believed it was exposure, possibly, or perhaps he had gone into the water somewhere further north and been brought in by the current. She had been careful not to say too much. She had said there was identification on the body, a wallet, and cards in the wallet with a name, but she wanted someone to come.

Marcus is fifty-one years old. Davy would be forty-eight. Marcus is the kind of man who has carried a sensible life carefully, the way you carry something full, always aware of the weight and the possibility of spilling. He has a house in a small city, not the city where they grew up, a different one. He has a job that involves reading documents and responding to them. He has a woman he has loved for nine years who has her own house four streets from his and who stood with him in his kitchen this morning at five o'clock and put her hand on his back while he drank the coffee she had made and did not say anything for a long time and then said do you want me to come and he had said no and had meant it and she had understood and that understanding was one of the things he loved in her. Her name is Moira. He would have liked to say her name aloud on the ferry, just to have something familiar in his mouth.

He falls asleep without meaning to and wakes when the ferry shifts and for a moment he does not know where he is and the not-knowing is briefly a relief, blank and clean, before the knowledge returns and settles on him like a coat put on in the wrong order, sleeves first, the weight distributed incorrectly.

There had been a time before the not-knowing. A time that memory serves up in pieces, the way memory always serves things up, not as narrative but as images that carry too much feeling for their actual content. Davy at seven, or perhaps nine, standing in a burn in his school clothes catching something in cupped hands, something small enough to be invisible from where Marcus stood on the bank, showing it to Marcus with the particular intensity children bring to small things, a water beetle or a caddis fly, his face so concentrated it looked almost angry. The two of them in the back of their father's car driving somewhere in winter, both of them asleep, or Marcus asleep and Davy pretending, Davy who had never been able to sleep in cars, who would lie back with his eyes closed and his breathing slowed and his body positioned carefully to look like sleep while staying completely awake, a habit he maintained into adulthood, this ability to perform rest while remaining somewhere else entirely. What it meant Marcus had never been certain. A need for the privacy of wakefulness, perhaps. A wish to be invisible without being absent.

Their father was a man who loved both his sons in the undifferentiated way of fathers who are not cruel but who are also not present in any way that the sons could locate and therefore use. He worked for the council doing something with water infrastructure, roads, the maintenance of things. He smelled of outdoors and coffee and something synthetic that was perhaps his jacket. He died when Marcus was twenty-three and Davy was twenty, and Marcus has sometimes thought that the dying was the first thing their father did that fully required them to feel something about him, and that this was perhaps unfair to everyone involved but was also just the truth.

Their mother was a different matter. Their mother was present in all the ways their father was not, was so present that her presence filled the house and both of them had grown up in the atmosphere of her, the way you grow up in a climate, unable to imagine air that doesn't contain that particular humidity, those particular pressures. She had opinions about everything, she remembered everything, she loved them with an attentiveness that could become, as they got older, a kind of weight. When she died—a stroke, quick, in the kitchen—Marcus had felt both grief and something else he was not proud of, a feeling like a window opening, cold air coming in but also light, also space. He suspected Davy had felt the same and that neither of them had ever said this to each other, and that this unspoken thing was the ground in which everything subsequent had grown.

He is not, he thinks, the kind of man who understands his own family. He understands documents. He understands the logic of procedure, of sequence, of one thing following from another in a way that can be traced and demonstrated. The logic of Davy has never been available to him in this form. Davy moved according to principles Marcus could not read, made decisions that seemed to arrive from regions of himself that Marcus had no access to. He had left three jobs that Marcus knew of, possibly more. He had lived in six or seven places, possibly more. He had been in relationships, marriages perhaps—there had been a card once, years ago, with a return address in a town on the west coast and a woman's name beside Davy's on the envelope, joined not by and but simply by the comma that served the same function—and Marcus had put the envelope in a drawer and had meant to write back and had not written back and then the drawer had been sorted and the envelope was gone.

He should have written back.

The ferry is moving through what must be the deepest part of the strait now, where the water is coldest and the distance to either shore is greatest, and though he can see nothing through the window he is aware of the depth below him, the specific cold depth of northern water, and he thinks of what the constable did not say, which was what the body looked like, what the sea does to a body, and he stops himself from thinking it because it is not yet time to think it. There will be time tomorrow. There will be a specific room and a specific task and he will need to be functional when he performs it. Until then he will remain on the surface.

He remembers, instead, a trip they took together. He was perhaps twenty-five, Davy was twenty-two, and their mother was still alive. They had borrowed Colin's car and driven north for a weekend, one of the north coasts, and they had stayed in a caravan park that was mostly deserted in early September and had cooked on a small stove inside because of the wind and had walked on the beach and talked in the way that brothers sometimes talk, not about anything in particular but with a directness that came from the removal of ordinary life. They had talked about their father in the past tense, which was new and strange. They had talked about what they thought they wanted, the way young men talk about wanting, with more certainty than they have any right to but with a sincerity that the later certainty is trying to replicate. Davy had said he wanted to be somewhere where he could see a long way. Not to get away from anything, he said. Not that. Just to have the distance available. Marcus had understood this as a wish for landscape and had said something about the north, about how you could have that here, and Davy had not disagreed but had seemed to mean something else, something the word distance only approximately pointed toward.

He thought about that trip many times in the subsequent years. He thought about whether it had been a kind of telling, Davy saying what he was going to do long before he did it, and Marcus hearing the surface of the words and missing what was underneath. He has catalogued his own failures of attention toward his brother the way accountants catalogue figures, each one small in itself, each one adding to a total that is larger than you expected when you are finally forced to compute it. The unanswered card. The phone call after the funeral that should have been longer. The year when they had been in the same city—Marcus passing through for six months, working on a project—and had met twice, twice, and both times in the company of others and both times brief, and Marcus had thought there was time, there was always more time, time was the one thing in adequate supply.

This is the story he has been telling himself for fourteen years. Not in these words, not consciously. But present, always present, the way the bodies of old structures are present beneath newer ground, in the way things give slightly underfoot, in the way certain areas remain cold.

What he does not know—and this is the piece that the constable's careful words could not resolve—is whether the body is Davy's. She had said the cards in the wallet. She had said they believed. But she had wanted someone to come, which meant it was not certain, which meant there was a remaining possibility, which Marcus is aware he is holding at a distance because it is dangerous. To let himself hope for the wrong thing. To arrange himself around the hope that it is Davy and then to arrive and find that it is Davy and to carry the confirmation back across this water. Or: to arrive and find that it is not, and to return to the long practice of not-knowing, which he has mastered, which he can continue. Both of these outcomes are available. He cannot determine in advance which is more bearable or which he wants, and the not-being-able-to-determine feels important, feels like information about himself that he has been trying to arrive at for a long time.

Outside the window something has changed. Not light exactly but the first diminishment of darkness, the dark becoming granular, losing its completeness. He can see the water now, or begin to see it, a surface that receives and returns the sky's first grey. The waves are modest. There are low clouds but in the east, the direction the ferry is moving toward, there is a line that is slightly brighter than the rest, the sky preparing.

The other passengers are beginning to stir. The woman near the bow lifts her head and pushes her hair back and looks around with the bewildered deliberateness of someone reinhabiting themselves. The old man with the cold cup opens his eyes and looks at his hands. Marcus has not slept again since the first brief time, has spent the hours since in the unproductive wakefulness that is not rest but is its own state, mind moving without direction over the same ground, the same few images, the same inventory.

He takes out his phone. He looks at Moira's number for a moment and then puts it back in his pocket. It is too early. She will be asleep, or she will be lying awake waiting for him to call, and either way the call will require things of him that he cannot yet provide. He will call her after. After the room, after the task, after whatever knowledge is going to be given to him today has been given.

What he will do with the knowledge is not something he can see from here.

If it is Davy, there will be arrangements. There will be the specific bureaucracy of death, the forms and decisions that provide the mind with a structure during the weeks when structure is necessary. There will be a funeral of some kind, he supposes, and there will be people to contact, though he cannot think who they would be. Colin. Perhaps the woman from the envelope, if she can be found, if she is still—he stops. He will handle what is given to him. He has always been able to do that. What he cannot handle is the space before the giving, the transit, the water.

He stands and goes to the window at the forward end of the lounge and looks out toward the coast. He can see it now, a darker mass against the sky's incrementally brightening grey, the headlands and the hills, the line where sea and land negotiate their permanent agreement. There are lights. Not many, but there are lights, which means people who are awake at this hour, who have had their own sleepless night or who have risen early for some ordinary purpose, feeding something, beginning something. He watches the lights and the land comes slowly closer and the engine note changes and somewhere on the deck above a door opens and he hears footsteps and the distant voice of someone on a radio, the ordinary sounds of arrival.

He picks up his bag. He puts on his jacket properly. He checks his pocket for the constable's number, the piece of paper with the address of the building where he is supposed to go, the address written in his own handwriting at four in the morning which means it is barely legible but it is there. He is, he thinks, as prepared as he is able to be.

There was a photograph on the mantelpiece in his mother's house for as long as he can remember. Both of them in it, himself at perhaps eleven and Davy at eight, standing at the edge of something—a field, a cliff, a shoreline, the edge of the photograph's context makes it impossible to tell. Both of them facing the same direction, away from the camera, looking at whatever it was they were looking at. You could not see their faces. You could not know what they were seeing. This was the photograph his mother had chosen to display above all others and he had never understood it, the preference for their backs over their faces, but he has thought about it often and he thinks about it now as the ferry slows and the water whitens at the stern and the dark shape of the dock resolves itself from the darkness of the coast and a gull appears from nowhere, white and absolute in the grey morning, flying alongside the boat for a moment with precise, mechanical wingbeats, as if it knows exactly where it is going, as if direction, for it, is not a question.
