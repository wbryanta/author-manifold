The price tags had been Marlene's idea, the little orange stickers that said $4 and $12 and $35, and Patricia had agreed to them over the phone from Cincinnati as though she were agreeing to some minor procedural matter, which was how she had agreed to everything for the past four months: the funeral home, the cremation, the obituary that described her mother as a devoted homemaker and community volunteer, which was accurate in the way that a photograph of a mountain is accurate. It shows you the shape but not the cold.

She arrived at eleven in the morning, an hour before Marlene said the people would come, and she sat in her car in the driveway for longer than she intended, looking at the house. It was a Cape Cod built in 1952, white with green shutters that her father had repainted every few years until he stopped, and then they had faded to the color of old limes, and her mother had never repainted them because she said the color had grown on her, which Patricia had always suspected was a lie told to avoid the cost of paint. The hydrangeas along the foundation were enormous now, the way things get when no one is cutting them back, and they leaned against the siding with a kind of slow insistence, the way the natural world always leans against the things people build.

She had her coffee in a paper cup from a gas station on 42, and she drank it sitting in the car because she did not yet want to go inside. The house was not yet a place where strangers came to buy things. It was still, for a few minutes more, her mother's house, and inside it were the objects of her entire childhood arranged more or less as they had always been arranged, because her mother had been a woman who did not believe in rearranging things once they found their place. The couch against the south wall. The television at an angle to the window. The dining table her parents had received as a wedding gift still in the dining room where it had sat for sixty-one years, a solid maple table that was now priced at $85, which Marlene said was fair.

Patricia finished her coffee and got out of the car.

The key was the same key she had carried until she was eighteen, copied onto a new blank sometime in the nineties but cut to the same pattern, and it turned in the lock the same way it always had, with a slight resistance and then a giving, like a small argument resolved. The smell hit her immediately. Not the smell of her mother exactly, or not only that, but the compound smell of the house itself: the particular mustiness of a closed Cape Cod in October, the ghost of forty years of cooking, of the cedar sachet her mother kept in the coat closet, of the carpet that had been there since 1988 and would probably outlast several more human lives, being that it was orange-brown and indestructible and not the kind of carpet anyone would deliberately replace. Patricia stood in the entryway and breathed it in and told herself this was fine.

Marlene arrived at five past eleven, a small energetic woman in her sixties who ran estate sales out of a home office in Marysville and who had the manner of someone who had learned to be professionally compassionate in the way that contractors learn to be professionally apologetic about delays. She had set everything up over two previous days, the pricing and the arranging, and now she moved through the rooms with a clipboard, adjusting tags, muttering to herself in a way that was almost musical. She had brought her daughter to help, a young woman named Brittany who was perhaps twenty and who wore her hair in a ponytail and who regarded Patricia with the particular sympathy of someone who cannot imagine being old enough to have a dead mother.

"The furniture always goes first," Marlene told Patricia, not for the first time. "People come for the furniture. They see it in the listing. So expect the dining set to move early, and the bedroom furniture. I priced the headboard separately from the frame because I've found you get more that way."

Patricia said that was fine.

"The books are a dollar each," Marlene said, "unless they're hardcovers, in which case it's two. But honestly most people don't buy the books. They look at them and they don't buy them. I should warn you."

Patricia said she understood about the books.

There were perhaps four hundred books in the house, distributed between the built-in shelves in the living room and the smaller shelves in the bedrooms, and Patricia had been meaning for weeks to go through them before the sale to pull out anything she wanted to keep, and she had not done this, partly because she lived two hours away and worked four days a week and had her own house and her own life, and partly because going through the books would have meant touching each one and thinking about it, which was a project she was not sure she had the interior resources to complete. She had told herself she would do it today. She would walk through the house before the people came and take what she wanted to take.

She had one hour.

She started in the kitchen because the kitchen was practical. She took the cast-iron skillet that had been her grandmother's and that her mother had used for cornbread every Thanksgiving, seasoned to a black smoothness that took decades to achieve. She took the wooden spoon that had a crack in the handle wrapped with electrical tape, which she could not have explained to anyone. She stood in front of the open cabinet and looked at the Depression glass, the pink and green pieces her mother had collected at flea markets over thirty years, each piece tagged at three or four dollars, and she thought: I cannot take all of it, I don't have room, I don't have a house where Depression glass makes sense. She took one green pitcher and put it in the tote bag she'd brought and closed the cabinet.

At eleven forty-five the first customers arrived. They were a couple, late forties, wearing matching fleece vests in different colors, and they walked through the front door with the purposeful cheerfulness of people who considered estate sales a hobby. They went straight to the living room and began examining things with the focused neutrality of appraisers, touching the lamp bases, opening the drawers of the side tables. Patricia watched them from the kitchen doorway. The man picked up a small ceramic owl that had sat on the bookshelf for as long as Patricia could remember and turned it over to look at the bottom. He put it back. He picked up a different owl. Her mother had collected owls, ceramic and glass and cast-iron owls of every size and expression, and they were priced at one and two dollars each, arranged on a card table near the window, and the couple stood before this arrangement like ornithologists before a discovery.

By noon there were twelve people in the house.

Patricia went upstairs.

The second floor was two bedrooms and a bathroom, and she stood in the hallway between them and felt the specific vertigo of returning to a place that was simultaneously completely known and completely strange. Her old bedroom had been converted to a sewing room sometime in the mid-nineties, and now it held her mother's sewing machine and the folding table and the plastic bins of fabric sorted by color, and Patricia had not slept in this room in thirty years but still felt its original use like a phantom limb. The wallpaper was still the wallpaper of her childhood: tiny blue flowers on a cream background. Her mother had refused to change it even when the room changed its function, saying it was good wallpaper and had done nothing wrong.

She sat on the edge of the small daybed that had replaced her childhood bed and looked at the room. Through the floor she could hear movement, voices, the dull thud of a drawer being opened and closed. Marlene's voice carrying on some transaction. She heard Brittany say thank you, have a good one, the casual blessing of commerce.

On the small shelf above the sewing machine there were three paperbacks, mass-market romances with creased spines that her mother had read in the last years of her life when she couldn't sleep, and Patricia touched them without taking them. Beside the shelf there was a framed photograph she had not remembered was there: Patricia herself at sixteen or seventeen, standing in front of what appeared to be a lake, squinting against the sun, wearing a yellow t-shirt. She had no memory of the photograph being taken. She did not know what lake it was. She looked happy in a way that seemed foreign to her now, not because she was unhappy but because the happiness in the photograph was so unguarded, so unselfconscious, the happiness of a person who does not yet know what happiness costs to maintain.

She took the photograph off the wall.

Her mother's bedroom was at the end of the hall. The door was open, which meant that Marlene had opened it, which meant that strangers could walk in, which Patricia had known and agreed to and which she had not, until this moment, fully understood what it would mean. A woman she had never seen was standing at her mother's dresser, opening the top drawer. The woman was perhaps seventy, small and deliberate, wearing a rain jacket the color of a school bus. She opened the drawer and looked inside and then closed it with satisfaction, as though confirming a suspicion.

"Excuse me," Patricia said, and then felt stupid because this was what the sale was for, this was the entire point of Marlene and the orange tags and the four months of phone calls, and the woman looked up at her with no embarrassment whatsoever.

"Is this real mahogany?" the woman said, putting her hand flat on the top of the dresser.

Patricia said she thought so. Her parents had bought the bedroom set the year they were married, 1963, and it was solid and heavy and had the amber warmth of furniture made when furniture was made to last, and it was priced at $175 for the dresser and $120 for the matching wardrobe and $65 for the mirror that hung above the dresser, and Patricia had seen these numbers in Marlene's email and had not objected to them. She watched the woman examine the dresser with both hands now, testing the drawer slides, looking at the joins.

"I'll take it," the woman said.

"You'll want to speak with Marlene," Patricia said. "She's downstairs."

The woman went downstairs. Patricia stood alone in her mother's bedroom.

The bed was still made. Marlene had left it made, had even straightened the coverlet, which was the blue and white one Patricia's mother had used for fifteen years, washed so many times it had softened to the texture of something almost alive. The bed was priced at $200 for the frame and headboard combined, in the end, because Marlene had reconsidered. On the nightstand there was still a glass of water that was not water anymore, that had evaporated down to a faint mineral residue at the bottom, and a library book that was four months overdue, which Patricia had been meaning to return. The library book was called something about gardens in winter, a title that now seemed unbearably specific.

She picked up the library book and put it in her tote bag.

Downstairs someone laughed at something, a bright social laugh, the laugh of a person enjoying themselves at a sale on a Saturday afternoon, and Patricia thought: this is the appropriate response. This is what you do with the furniture of the dead. You price it and you open the doors and the people come and they laugh and they buy the owls and they take the mahogany dresser and they carry it to their cars and they drive it to their houses where it will live in some other bedroom among other people's lives and that is a kind of continuation, she understood this, she had made her peace with it or had told herself she had made her peace with it, which is a different thing.

She sat on the edge of her mother's bed. She did not lie down because lying down felt like a boundary she would not recover from crossing. But she sat, and she put her hand on the coverlet, and the texture of it was exactly as she remembered, the slight roughness at the hem where the embroidery was, the worn smoothness of the middle, and for a moment she could feel the whole geography of being a child sick with something mild, a winter cold, lying in her parents' bed because it was the warmest place, her mother somewhere in the house making tea or soup, the sounds of the house moving around her like water around a still point. She had been safe in a way she had never been able to fully reconstruct since, not through her marriage or her career or her friendships or the very real and sustaining pleasures of her adult life, but a particular animal safety that was about being inside a specific smell in a specific room on a winter afternoon with someone who loved you moving quietly through the other rooms of the house.

Her mother had been difficult. Patricia needed to be honest about this, even now, even sitting on the edge of the bed. She had been a woman of fixed opinions and intermittent warmth and a perfectionism that had made childhood feel at times like a long audition for a role that was never clearly described. She had been critical of Patricia's choices in ways that were sometimes useful and often not. She had loved Patricia in a way that Patricia had not always been able to feel as love, a love expressed primarily through worry and instruction and the preparation of food. But she had also sat at the edge of the bed on winter afternoons and put her hand on Patricia's forehead, and the hand had been cool and dry and absolutely sure of itself, and Patricia had felt known in some preverbal and complete way that she could not have articulated at the time and could not fully articulate even now, sitting in the same room fifty years later, listening to strangers move through the house below.

She heard the front door open and close. Open and close. A rhythm of taking.

She went to the closet and opened it. The closet smelled more strongly of her mother than anywhere else in the house, the particular mixture of her soap and her powder and the dry wool smell of her winter coats, which still hung there, five or six of them, priced at ten and fifteen dollars each, and Patricia pushed her face into the nearest one, a gray wool coat with a velvet collar, and she stood there for a while. No one came in. She could hear Marlene's voice downstairs, conducting something, and Brittany's younger voice answering, and the various sounds of objects being moved and examined and decided upon. She stood with her face in her mother's coat.

When she came out she took the coat off the hanger and folded it over her arm. She did not look at the price tag.

She spent the next two hours moving through the house on the periphery of the sale, occasionally directing someone to Marlene for payment, occasionally answering a question about the age of something or whether there were more pieces to a set, slipping into the role of expert on her own childhood without meaning to, becoming the person who knew where things came from. The dining table went at twelve thirty to a young family with three children, two boys and a girl, and the father and a friend they had brought for the purpose carried it out the front door in sections, the leaves stacked against the legs, and Patricia watched from the kitchen and felt something she could not name precisely, something that was not exactly grief because grief felt larger than this, felt less like watching and more like falling. This was more like the feeling of watching a ship leave a harbor, a completed departure, nothing to be done about it now.

The young mother, carrying a chair, paused near Patricia and said, "This is a beautiful table. We've been looking for something like this for so long."

Patricia said she was glad. She meant it. She was glad in some part of herself and in another part of herself she was standing in 1979 doing homework at this table while her mother read the newspaper in the chair by the window, and both of these things were true simultaneously, the way two transparencies laid over each other are both true but make a picture that is neither one alone.

By two o'clock the house had begun to thin. The owl collection was mostly gone, the card table holding only four or five survivors. The bookshelf looked ransacked, which surprised her, given what Marlene had said about books. Someone had bought most of the paperbacks and a fair number of the hardcovers, and the gaps in the shelves had the look of missing teeth. The Depression glass was almost entirely gone. The cast-iron doorstop shaped like a cat, which had held the back door open since approximately forever, was gone. The set of encyclopedias from 1974 was still there, dark green and untouched, the kind of thing that would go to the donation pile because nobody, not even people who bought books at estate sales, wanted a 1974 encyclopedia.

Patricia took volume M from the shelf.

She had used this encyclopedia for every school report from third grade through seventh, and M was the one she had used most, for mammals and mountains and maps and Mexico, and it fell open in her hands to a page about Mars, the photographs grainy and orange, the text describing a planet we had not yet visited but were already imagining, and she thought about the child who had read this page in 1977 or 1978, lying on her stomach on the living room floor, her chin in her hands, reading about a planet nobody had been to yet, in a house that was then the entire world.

She put M in her tote bag.

At three thirty Marlene found her in the kitchen and gave her the accounting: $1,847 in total sales, which was better than average for a house this size, which Marlene attributed to the quality of the furniture and the good turnout. She handed Patricia an envelope with the cash less her percentage, which they had agreed on, and Patricia took the envelope and put it in her purse without opening it and thanked Marlene, who pressed her hand with the practiced warmth of someone who has pressed many hands in many kitchens.

"Do you want a few minutes?" Marlene asked.

Patricia said she was all right.

Marlene and Brittany began the work of cataloguing what remained for the donation truck, and Patricia walked through the rooms one last time. The house was depleted now, the shelves and surfaces bearing the pale squares and rectangles where things had been, the carpet showing the impressions of furniture legs, the walls with their hook holes and their faded rectangles where pictures had hung. The structure remained completely intact: the walls, the floors, the windows with their afternoon light, the ceiling of the living room with its single water stain in the shape of nothing in particular that had been there since the mid-eighties when the roof had leaked and been repaired. The house was like a sentence from which most of the nouns had been removed. She could still read the grammar of it. She could still see where everything had been.

She stood at the living room window, the same window she had stood at as a child watching for her father's car. The street was ordinary and unhurried in the October afternoon, leaves moving in a small wind, a neighbor's dog crossing the yard across the street with the focused intention of dogs going somewhere. The hydrangeas against the glass were brown now at their edges, the big flower heads drying on the stem, and they moved against the window with a sound like paper.

She had her tote bag: the cast-iron skillet, the wooden spoon with the cracked handle, the green glass pitcher, the photograph from the sewing room, the library book about gardens in winter, her mother's gray wool coat, volume M of the 1974 World Book Encyclopedia. She thought about whether there was anything else and decided there was not, or rather that there was everything else and she could not carry everything else and this was the nature of the situation, this was what it meant to be a person moving forward in time, that you choose what fits in the bag you brought and you carry it out the door.

She called goodbye to Marlene, who called back from upstairs.

She stood for a moment in the entryway with her hand on the doorframe. The paint was white with a slight texture where it had been painted over the wood grain, and she knew the texture of it in her palm the way she knew very few things, with a knowledge that lived entirely in the body and had no language attached to it. She pressed her palm flat against it. Then she opened the door and went out into the afternoon, where her car was still in the driveway, in the same place it had been that morning, solid and present and pointed toward the road.
