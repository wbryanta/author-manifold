The hotel had been rebuilt in a style the local papers called Mediterranean, which meant stucco the color of old teeth and a fountain in the lobby that did not work. Marian had read about the reopening in the Sacramento Bee, three paragraphs below the fold, and had torn it out and put it in her kitchen drawer with the coupons and the Christmas cards she kept meaning to answer. That had been in March. In June her nephew called to say he was getting married at the Palomar and would she come, and Marian had stood at the kitchen counter holding the phone and looking out the window at the oleanders her husband had planted before he left and thought: well.

The drive from Modesto took an hour and forty minutes. She had agreed to stay two nights, which seemed to her now, as she turned off the freeway, an error in judgment of a particular kind she recognized, the kind she made when she was trying to be generous.

The new lobby was larger than she remembered. This was not surprising; the fire had taken the east wing, and what they had rebuilt was bigger by a third than what had burned, the architect apparently having reasoned that improvement was a natural consequence of disaster. There were palms in terracotta pots. There was the fountain, dry, with a brass plate explaining that it was seasonal. Behind the reception desk a young woman with a name tag that read BRITTANY was on the phone, and Marian stood with her overnight bag and waited, looking at the ceiling, which was new, and the floor, which was travertine and also new, and the wall where the corridor to the east wing used to be, which was now a wall with a painting of a vineyard on it.

"Mrs. Doyle," Brittany said, and smiled as though Marian's arrival were a matter she had been anticipating with pleasure.

"Corrales. My name is Marian Corrales." She spelled it.

"Of course." Brittany's fingers moved across her keyboard. "We have you in room two-fourteen."

Marian accepted the key card and did not say that she had once had a passkey that opened every room in this building or what had been this building. It seemed unnecessary. She picked up her overnight bag, declined the offer of assistance from a young man in a vest, and found the elevator.

The room was adequate. There was a view of the parking structure and, beyond it, a slice of the valley that turned golden in the late afternoon light in the way the valley did, the way it always had, indifferently, whether you were watching it or not. She sat on the edge of the bed and took off her shoes and thought about her nephew Daniel, whom she had last seen at Christmas and who was twenty-seven years old and worked for a company in Sacramento that did something involving water rights. She tried to remember the name of the woman he was marrying. Kelsey, she thought. Or possibly Chelsea.

She had been nineteen when she took the job at the Palomar. This was a fact she generally did not think about. It belonged to a category of facts she held at a certain distance, not suppressed exactly but not examined either, the way you did not examine certain rooms in your own house because the light was bad and nothing in them had changed. She had needed money and her cousin Inez had worked there the previous summer and said it was fine, the housekeeper Mrs. Gable was strict but not unfair, and the other girls were mostly from the valley and spoke Spanish when the guests weren't around. Marian had taken the bus from Fresno on a Monday in June with a reference from her high school principal and been hired the same afternoon.

What she remembered now was not the fire. What she remembered was the smell of the cleaning carts, the weight of the wet sheets, the way the service corridor ran behind the rooms like a secret kept from the people sleeping in them. She remembered that the guests' shoes, left outside the doors at night for the concierge to collect, looked abandoned in the half-light, helpless in a way the guests themselves never looked. She remembered that the Palomar in those years had a ballroom with red carpeting that was maintained by a separate crew and that she had stood in the doorway of the ballroom once, on a Tuesday when there was no event, and looked at the chairs stacked along the walls and the chandelier hanging dark and thought that she had never been in a room so clearly intended for people unlike herself.

She remembered Mrs. Gable's hands, which were large and red and moved quickly, and she remembered a girl named Patrice who was from Merced and who did an impression of Mrs. Gable that made the other girls laugh in the service corridor during the lunch break, and she remembered that she had laughed too, though she could no longer hear the sound of it.

The fire had started on a Thursday night in August. The cause was later determined to be electrical, a phrase that seemed to Marian to promise more explanation than it delivered. She had been in room 118, which was in the east wing, which was the wing that burned. She had gone back for the cart.

What happened next was something she had told twice: once to the investigator and once to a woman at a party in Stockton twenty years later who had pushed her in the way that people at parties sometimes did, following one question with another until you were somewhere you had not intended to go. Both times she had told it the same way, briefly, without the parts that did not make sense in the telling. The part that she left out both times was that when she came out of the stairwell into the parking lot she had stood there for a moment before she moved toward the others, stood there with the heat behind her and the cold air of the August night on her face and looked up at the sky, which was very clear, and felt nothing in particular, which she understood even then was the wrong thing to feel and which she had never satisfactorily explained to herself.

Three people had died. She had not known any of them.

The rehearsal dinner was in a room the hotel called the Vineyard Room, which was where the old ballroom had been. There was no red carpeting now. The carpet was ivory, and along one wall there were windows that looked out on a terrace with lights strung in the trees, and the other walls were painted the color of old straw and hung with more paintings of vineyards. Marian arrived seven minutes late and found a seat near the back. Daniel came over and kissed her cheek and introduced her to a woman named Patricia, who turned out to be the mother of the bride, and then was pulled away by someone.

The bride was called Kelsey. Marian had been right. She was a small, dark-haired girl with the kind of confidence that presented itself as warmth, and she moved around the room in a white dress that was not quite formal, stopping at each table, touching people's arms. Marian watched her and thought that she was the type of girl who had always known what rooms she was intended for.

"You're Daniel's aunt," said the woman next to her. She was perhaps sixty-five, with silver earrings and a glass of white wine. "I'm Connie Marsh. I was at Davis with Daniel's mother."

"Marian Corrales."

"Are you from Sacramento?"

"Modesto."

"Oh, Modesto." Connie Marsh said this in the way that people said it, with a small adjustment of the face that was not quite condescension, something more like the brief recognition of a category. "Have you been to this hotel before? It's just beautiful, I think. They did such a lovely job rebuilding it."

"I have," Marian said. "Yes."

Connie Marsh waited, and when Marian did not continue she said, "They kept the bones of the original, you know. The west wing is all original. I was reading about it. Very thoughtful restoration."

"The east wing is new," Marian said.

"Is it? I wouldn't know the difference, honestly."

"No," Marian said. "I don't suppose you would."

She regretted this almost immediately, or not regretted it exactly but recognized it as the kind of remark that required management, a conversation to be carried for the rest of the evening in a particular direction. But Connie Marsh only nodded and looked toward the front of the room where Daniel's father was beginning to speak, and Marian thought perhaps the remark had not registered as she had feared.

Daniel's father was named Jim Doyle, and he had been married to Marian's sister Rosaria for twenty-two years and divorced from her for six. He was a large, fair, affable man who had sold agricultural equipment for most of his adult life and who had a way of speaking in public that sounded like prepared remarks even when it wasn't. He said that Daniel was the best thing he'd ever done. He said that Kelsey was a remarkable young woman and that her parents were fine people, and he nodded at Patricia and at a man who must have been Kelsey's father, a compact, tan man at a corner table who nodded back with the expression of someone who had been agreeing to things all evening and intended to continue. Jim Doyle said that marriage was not to be entered into lightly, and Marian looked at her wine glass.

After dinner she walked out onto the terrace. The lights in the trees moved in the warm air and someone had put on music inside, something orchestral and undemanding. She could hear voices from the tables behind the windows. She walked to the railing and stood looking out at the parking structure and the valley beyond it.

She was fifty-four years old. Her husband had left eight years ago and this was no longer, most of the time, a fact that required management; it had become simply a condition, like the oleanders, which she kept watering out of an inertia she recognized but saw no particular reason to interrupt. She had worked for twenty-one years as an office manager for an orthodontist named Dr. Reyes, who was careful and humorless and whose patients, who were children, were sometimes frightened of him, and she had retired in February and was not yet accustomed to the mornings, the specific quality of time that had no structure to receive it.

"Miss Corrales." She turned. It was Kelsey's father, the compact, tan man. Up close he was older than she had thought, his face creased in a way that the terrace light was not quite forgiving about. He had his hands in his pockets. "I don't think we've met properly. Bob Hearne."

"Marian."

"Beautiful evening." He came to stand beside her at the railing. "You're Daniel's family."

"His aunt."

"His aunt." He said it as though filing it somewhere. "He's a good kid. We like him."

"Yes," she said. "He is."

"You grow up around here?"

"Fresno."

"I grew up in Bakersfield. Valley girl."

"Valley girl," she agreed.

Bob Hearne looked out at the lights of the parking structure. "Hell of a thing, what happened here," he said. "The fire. You know about that?"

"I know about it."

"Kelsey wanted this hotel specifically, I want you to know that. I said to her, you sure, and she said Dad it's a beautiful hotel and it was rebuilt, it's not like anything is the same. She has her own way of looking at things." He paused. "Her mother looked it up. Three people died."

"Yes."

"You said you'd been here before." He glanced at her. She had not said this to him; he had been listening from inside, or Connie Marsh had said something, or simply the remark had traveled in the way that remarks did at these events. "During the fire?"

"I worked here," Marian said.

He looked at her steadily. He had the manner of a man who had sat through a number of difficult conversations and had learned to wait.

"I was a chambermaid. I was nineteen." She heard herself say this in the same tone she would have used to say she had once driven through on her way somewhere else, and was obscurely pleased by this.

"Jesus," Bob Hearne said.

"I came out through the stairwell. I was fine."

"Jesus," he said again. He appeared to be deciding something. "Does Daniel know that? That you were here?"

"My sister knows." She thought about this. "She may have told Jim. It's not something I generally discuss."

"But you came."

"He's my nephew," Marian said. "He asked me."

Bob Hearne nodded slowly. He reached into his jacket and took out a cigar, looked at it, put it back. "I appreciate that," he said, though it was not clear to Marian what he was appreciating, whether it was her presence or her explanation of it or the fact that she had told him, which she had not particularly intended to do and could not now account for.

"Your daughter is lovely," Marian said.

"She is." His voice shifted slightly on this. "She's the best thing. Like Jim said about Daniel, she's the best thing, that's not just something you say." He stopped. "They'll be good together, I think. They're both—" He moved his hand in a gesture that seemed to be searching for the word. "Capable," he said finally. "They're both capable people."

Marian thought about this. It seemed to her a serious and underrated thing to say about two people getting married, more honest in its way than remarkable or wonderful or any of the other words that had been offered at the dinner table, and she found that she was grateful to Bob Hearne for it in a way she had not expected to be grateful to anyone this weekend.

Inside, the music had changed. Someone was laughing, a high clear sound that came through the glass and dissipated in the warm air.

"My wife wants to redo the whole east wing," Bob Hearne said. "Different flowers, different table arrangements, something. I told her the hotel has a decorator." He smiled briefly. "She's very organized, Patricia."

"My mother was organized," Marian said. This surprised her; she had not meant to say it. But Bob Hearne only nodded again, receiving it as though it were a reasonable thing to have said, which perhaps it was.

She looked at the wall of the east wing, white stucco in the terrace light. From where she stood she could see a row of second-floor windows, lit from inside, and she tried to remember where room 118 would have been, but the geography was wrong now, the wing extended further than it had, the windows were in different places. The room was somewhere under the new building or was itself new, she couldn't tell, and it occurred to her that this was one of those questions that were less interesting than they seemed, that had the shape of significance without the substance of it.

"Well," Bob Hearne said. He pushed back from the railing. "I should go find Patricia." He extended his hand. "I'm glad you're here, Marian. I mean that."

She shook his hand. He went inside, and through the glass she watched him make his way to the table where Patricia sat with her silver earrings and her organized expression, and watched him bend and say something in her ear, and watched Patricia look up and find Marian through the window with a look that was brief and unreadable and then was gone.

Marian stayed on the terrace. The music continued. In the valley the lights were coming on in their scattered way, farmhouses and the highway and the distant orange smear of some city she couldn't name from here, everything at a distance that made it look considered, intentional. She thought about Patrice from Merced, whose last name she had never known or had forgotten, and about Mrs. Gable's red hands, and about the shoes in the corridor. She thought about the particular quality of a service corridor, the way it ran behind everything, parallel to the public world but unlit and unpainted, the infrastructure of comfort that comfort depended on not seeing.

She thought that she was fifty-four years old and had driven an hour and forty minutes and would stay two nights and on Saturday would watch her nephew marry a capable young woman in a hotel built on the ground where something had burned, and this seemed to her neither poignant nor ironic but simply the case, the way most things were simply the case when you were old enough to have stopped expecting them to mean something beyond themselves.

The terrace door opened and Daniel came out, loosening his tie, and looked at her with the uncomplicated affection of someone who has known you long enough not to need anything from you.

"There you are," he said. "I've been looking."

"Here I am," Marian said.

He came and stood beside her. He smelled of wine and of the slightly anxious hopefulness particular to grooms. He was twenty-seven years old and his whole life was arranged in front of him in a way that it would not always be, and he did not know this yet, and she was glad he didn't.

"What are you looking at?" he said.

"The valley," she said. "Just the valley."

He put his arm around her shoulder and she let him, and they stood there for a moment in the warm night while inside the lights and the music continued and the fountain that did not work sat in the lobby in its reasonable silence, and Marian thought: well. And then thought nothing in particular, which was, she had learned, not the wrong thing to feel, not wrong at all, but simply what was left when you had carried something long enough and set it down in a place that was not the same place but would do.
