But the Pacific, Jack Lovett said, the Pacific was another thing entirely.

Inez listened. Inez was good at listening, had been good at it her whole life, it was one of the things people said about Inez Victor, one of the things they had always said about her, that she listened. Her father said it. Her father Harry Christian said it. Harry Christian who had built his particular life on the proposition that other people's attention was the only currency worth collecting, Harry Christian who had so thoroughly arranged the facts of his own history that no original version remained, Harry Christian said Inez was a good listener the way he said the weather was fine or the fish weren't running, as simple observation, without attachment to any particular meaning. Her husband said it too, said it at dinners and at receptions and on those occasions when the cameras were there and some journalist would ask and he would say Inez is extraordinary actually, she just listens, which was of course the way he meant it, as a quality useful to him, as a portable item in the inventory of their life together. Paul Christian said it as well. Inez's brother Paul. Paul who was in Honolulu. Paul who was always either in Honolulu or on his way to Honolulu or just lately returned from some place that was not Honolulu and which had not worked out for him, Paul who had their father's ease with spending money and none of their father's instinct for acquiring it. Inez's sister Janet had strong opinions about Paul's situation and expressed them at length whenever the situation arose, which was frequently. Janet was like that. Janet had always been like that. There was something about Janet's way of having opinions that made Inez tired, had always made Inez tired, made her want to lie down in a dark room with a wet cloth on her forehead, which was something their mother had done, had done frequently, their mother Carol Christian née Doyle who was originally from Boca Raton.

This was before.

This was all before.

The year before Jack Lovett said what he said to Inez Victor on that particular morning when the air in Honolulu smelled like gardenias and the mangoes were ripe on the trees at the Manoa place and both of them knew without saying so that certain things were going to happen, certain arrangements were going to be set in motion that could not be reversed, certain doors were going to open and then close and then not open again.

The year before, the things that happened were these.

Harry Christian fell down the stairs at the house on Kahala and broke his hip and told no one for eleven days, conducting his regular business from the floor of the front hallway, calling his broker and his lawyer and at least two women whose names appeared in a small address book he kept separate from his regular address book and which Janet found and then lost, or said she lost, after the ambulance came. The ambulance came because the housekeeper, Estelita, who had been with Harry Christian for sixteen years and who had very strong feelings about the appropriate distance between her professional responsibilities and the private arrangements of the Christian family, finally called not Janet and not Inez but Jack Lovett, whose number she had in the kitchen on a card that Harry Christian himself had tacked up above the telephone at some point during the preceding years, the card saying only Jack Lovett and the number, no explanation, which was itself a kind of explanation.

So Jack Lovett had come. Of course Jack Lovett had come. That was the kind of thing Jack Lovett did, arrived at the necessary moment with the minimum of observable effort, arranged whatever needed arranging, and then was gone again, and the arrangement remained. Inez had understood this about Jack Lovett for a long time. She had understood it in the way you understand something you have decided not to examine, the way you understand that the mechanism behind certain things working out is a mechanism you would prefer not to know about in any detail.

Harry Christian was in the hospital for three weeks and then he was not in the hospital and then the whole business of the stairs and the hip was a fact that existed in the family the way certain other facts existed in the family, fully present and entirely unaddressed. The family was good at this. The Christians were accomplished practitioners of the complete avoidance of the subject at hand, which Inez had for many years mistaken for discretion and which she now understood was something else. She was not entirely sure what to call it. She had discussed this with a woman she knew in Washington, a woman named Frances Landau who was a psychiatrist and whose husband worked at State, and Frances Landau had used the word dissociation, which Inez had written in the small notebook she kept then, writing things down being something she had started doing the year she turned forty and which she had continued as a hedge against a feeling she had that things were happening too quickly to be retained in any reliable way.

The dissociation of the Christian family. She had written that down.

Then she had lost the notebook.

This was, as noted, the year before.

The year before, her husband was running for something again. This was not in itself unusual, her husband was always running for something, the Senator was perpetually in motion between one thing he had run for and another thing he intended to run for, covering the distance between them in the peculiar suspended state of men who have decided that their destiny is a matter of public record and who behave accordingly. Inez had watched him do this for twenty years. Longer. She had watched him do this since before they were married, since the summer in Honolulu when she was twenty-two and visiting her father and Jack Lovett was there, Jack Lovett was always there, and Billy Dillon had brought Dick Victor to a party at the Outrigger Club and Dick Victor had looked at Inez across a table of people she had known all her life and said something that she no longer remembered and she had said something back that she no longer remembered and three months later they were married.

Billy Dillon said it was one of the great courtships in his personal experience.

Billy Dillon was Dick Victor's man, had been Dick Victor's man since before Dick Victor was the Senator, since before he was anything in particular, since the time in California when he was a law student at Boalt and Billy Dillon was working for a congressman whose name nobody remembered anymore and who had retired under circumstances that Billy Dillon preferred not to discuss. Billy Dillon was from San Francisco and he had the San Francisco Democrat's particular combination of sentimentality about lost causes and ruthlessness about how things actually worked. He was short and fat and he had a genius for the operational, for the logistics of moving men and events from one place to another, and he loved Dick Victor in the way that certain men of Billy Dillon's type love the person onto whom they have projected their most serious ambitions. This was not a small love. It was not an uncomplicated love. It required a great deal of Inez, though this was not a thing Billy Dillon would have said, would have recognized as a formulation even if Inez had offered it to him, which she would not have done.

The thing that happened during the year before, the thing that had to do with Dick Victor's running for something, was the thing with the photographs.

Inez had known about the photographs for some time before they became public, had known about them in the way she knew about certain other things, which is to say she had filed the knowledge in the place where she filed knowledge of that type and had not taken it out and examined it. The photographs had been taken in Los Angeles, at a house in Bel Air that belonged to a man named Stills, or Stillings, who was in production of some kind and who had been introduced to Dick Victor at a fundraiser by someone whose name Billy Dillon also preferred not to discuss. The woman in the photographs was not famous. This was remarked upon later as if it were relevant information, as if it indicated something about Dick Victor's judgment or his appetites or his state of mind at the time, when in Inez's view it indicated nothing except that Dick Victor had been in Los Angeles and it had been a long fundraising trip and things happened.

Things happened. Inez had been saying this to herself for years and it had served her adequately and she saw no reason to stop.

The photographs were obtained by a man in Washington who sold them to a man in New York who sold the relevant ones, three of the twelve, to a publication whose name Inez knew and whose offices she had passed many times on her way to other places in the city. Billy Dillon found out about the three photographs six days before they were published and he called Inez at the house in Georgetown on a Tuesday morning in October. It was raining. She remembered that it was raining because she had been looking at the garden when the phone rang, looking at the way the rain came down through the oak tree and made the leaves, which had just started to turn, shine in a particular way that she found she wanted to keep looking at.

She did not keep looking at it. She stopped looking at it and listened to Billy Dillon.

Billy Dillon told her what the photographs showed and she listened and said nothing and Billy Dillon continued talking, outlining the operational situation as he saw it, the responses available, the timing of each response and its probable consequences. He was thorough. He was very thorough. When he finished she said she needed to think and he said he needed an answer by that evening and she said she understood that and they said goodbye and she stood by the window for a while and then she called Jack Lovett.

She did not call Dick Victor. She called Jack Lovett.

Jack Lovett was in Hong Kong or in Singapore or in one of those places that Jack Lovett was always in, that Jack Lovett moved between so fluidly that they seemed to Inez to constitute not different countries but different rooms in the same large house, a house whose floor plan she had never been shown. She left a message. Jack Lovett called back in two hours, which given the time difference meant that it was the middle of the night wherever he was. She told him about the photographs and he said what he said, which was not very much and which she did not record in the notebook she no longer had, and then he said he would be in Washington in four days. She said four days was after Billy Dillon's deadline. Jack Lovett said he understood that. He said: handle Billy Dillon.

She had handled Billy Dillon. She had called him back that evening as agreed and told him what to say and when to say it and in what order and to whom, and Billy Dillon had been silent for a moment in a way that indicated that he was reconsidering something he had previously thought about Inez, revising some estimate upward, and then he said he thought that would work. It worked. The three photographs appeared in the publication on a Thursday and by the following Monday the story was about something else, which was about certain financial arrangements involving a PAC that supported Dick Victor's opponent and a corporation whose subsidiary had interests in three defense contracts currently under congressional review, and this story had the advantage of being true, or true enough, true in the way that stories are true when they are put together by people who know which details to leave in and which to omit.

She had handled it. She handled things.

Jack Lovett arrived on a Wednesday. He came to the house in Georgetown and they sat in the kitchen and drank the coffee she made and he said what he said about the photograph business, which was not congratulations exactly but was acknowledgment, which from Jack Lovett was the same thing. She said she needed to ask him something and he said go ahead and she said why he had put his number on the card in her father's kitchen, the card above the telephone in Kahala. He looked at her for a moment. He said it wasn't anything. He said Harry Christian had his number, had always had his number, it was just a convenience.

She said she understood that.

He said: you understand that.

She said: I understand that.

They looked at each other across the kitchen table and outside in the Georgetown street someone was moving a car and the rain, which had continued all week, came down on the skylight over the back of the house and made a sound that Inez associated with being somewhere else, being somewhere she had been a long time ago, being fourteen or fifteen in Honolulu with the rain on the roof of the lanai.

She said: were you there because of him or because of me.

He said: what do you think.

She didn't say what she thought. She didn't say it then and she didn't say it later and there came a point at which the question itself dissolved, became part of the general atmosphere of their particular arrangement, present in every conversation and never the subject of any. This was, Inez understood, part of what they were to each other. Not the whole of it. Not the part that mattered most, or not the only part that mattered most. But present.

The year before, Jessie was in trouble.

Jessie was Inez's daughter, Inez and Dick Victor's daughter, their younger child. The older one was named Adlai, after the candidate, because Dick Victor had been in love with Adlai Stevenson in the way that men of his generation and type were in love with Adlai Stevenson, meaning they had transferred onto him all the idealism they found professionally useful to display but personally inconvenient to act on, and when Adlai died in London in sixty-five Dick Victor had been in San Francisco at a fundraiser and had cried, which was one of the things about Dick Victor that Inez found she could not entirely explain but also could not entirely dismiss, the fact that he had cried for Adlai Stevenson in San Francisco at a fundraiser while she was in Washington with a colicky infant and a fever of a hundred and two.

Adlai Victor was twenty-two. Adlai was in school, ostensibly, was at Berkeley, ostensibly, was working toward a degree in something that changed with each telling but which seemed to circle around film and around history and around a general critique of institutions that Adlai held with great conviction and applied with great selectivity, exempting for example the institution of his father's position in those institutions and the money that his father's position provided. Inez had noted this and had said nothing about it. Inez was good at saying nothing.

Jessie was nineteen. Jessie was not in school. Jessie had been in school at three different schools over the preceding four years and had left each one under circumstances that differed in their details and were identical in their structure, which was to say there was always someone Jessie had gotten involved with and there was always something Jessie had taken or had been given to take and there was always a point at which the taking of the something and the involvement with the someone reached a kind of critical mass that caused the school in question to communicate with Dick Victor's office, never with Inez directly, always with Dick Victor's office, and then Billy Dillon would manage it and Jessie would come home for a while and she would be very thin and very still and then she would not be home anymore, she would be at the next school.

The year before, Jessie was not at any school. Jessie was in Honolulu, staying with Harry Christian, which had seemed at the time like an arrangement that had a certain logic to it, Harry Christian being someone who, whatever his deficiencies, understood the uses of appearances and would keep Jessie in a situation where appearances were maintained, where she was visible and accounted for. This had seemed logical. It had not worked out to be logical. It had worked out to be the kind of thing that, after the fact, seemed to contain its own obvious refutation, seemed to be the sort of idea that anyone who had thought about it carefully would have known was not going to work out, and Inez had thought about it carefully, had thought she had thought about it carefully, and had missed the obvious refutation.

What happened with Jessie in Honolulu during the year before is not the whole story and is not the beginning of the story but it is the part of the story that Inez thought about most on that particular morning when she and Jack Lovett were together and the air smelled like gardenias and they both knew that certain things were going to happen, certain doors were going to close.

Jessie in Honolulu. Harry Christian's house in Kahala where the mango trees dropped their fruit on the driveway and left stains on the concrete that Estelita could not get out. The house where Inez had grown up, or had spent part of growing up, the other parts having been spent in various other houses in various other places that Harry Christian had passed through during those years, houses in Bel Air and in Montecito and one year a house in Tangier that none of them, not even Harry, had been willing to explain. The Kahala house was the one that lasted. The Kahala house was where Carol Christian had come back to after the Tangier year, had come back to and then not left, or not left except for the times she left with the wet cloth and the Nembutal and the private room at Queen's Medical, and then in the end she had left for good, which was 1966, which was the year Inez was twenty-six and already married to Dick Victor and already in Washington and already in the middle of the life she was going to be in the middle of for a long time.

Jessie liked Harry Christian. This was one of the things about Jessie that Inez found difficult, the fact that Jessie liked Harry Christian in a clear and uncomplicated way that Inez herself had not managed for some time. Harry Christian was charming to Jessie. He told her things. He took her to dinner at restaurants where the maître d' knew him and where Harry Christian was the kind of man the maître d' knew, expansive and easy and interested in a way that seemed genuine even to Inez, who knew it was not so much genuine as it was practiced past the point where the distinction held. He told Jessie about his life, or about the version of his life that he told, and Jessie listened in the way that Jessie listened when she was listening, which was completely, which was with her whole thin focused self.

And then Jessie had found other things to do in Honolulu. Jessie had found the other things that there were to find in Honolulu if you were nineteen and thin and the granddaughter of Harry Christian and living in Kahala with not very much to structure your time. Inez understood this. She did not excuse it but she understood it, and the understanding and the not-excusing existed in her simultaneously without apparent contradiction, which was another thing she had written in the notebook she had lost, that she seemed able to hold two contradictory recognitions at once without the friction between them generating anything useful in the way of action.

Jack Lovett had found Jessie.

That was the other thing that happened in the year before.

Jack Lovett had found Jessie in Honolulu in a way that he did not explain to Inez and that Inez did not ask him to explain, understanding that the explanation would include information about the network of things he knew and people he paid and arrangements he maintained, the whole subterranean geography of Jack Lovett's actual work, which existed below the official description of his work the way the actual terrain of an atoll exists below the chart of it. He had found her and he had managed the situation and Jessie had come home, and by come home Inez meant Washington, Dick Victor's house, which was also Inez's house and which did not feel like home to Inez in any operative sense but was where she lived.

Dick Victor did not know that it was Jack Lovett who had found Jessie. Dick Victor knew that Jessie had been in a difficult situation in Honolulu and that the situation had been resolved, and he knew this the way he knew most things about his family, which was as a set of facts that had been processed by Billy Dillon and delivered to him in a form that required nothing further from him. Dick Victor was not a bad man. Inez had believed this for a long time and continued to believe it and believed it on that morning when the air smelled like gardenias and she and Jack Lovett were together knowing what they knew, believing it even then, or choosing to believe it, or understanding that the distinction between believing and choosing to believe was not one she was prepared to investigate.

The morning she was remembering, the morning of the gardenias, was in March.

By March, certain things were already in motion that could not be stopped, or that could theoretically be stopped but that neither she nor Jack Lovett was going to stop, which amounted to the same thing. She knew this and he knew this and the knowing of it was present between them in the kitchen of the Manoa house where she had sent Jessie to stay with an old friend of Carol Christian's named Lorraine Moana who was careful and kind and who did not ask questions.

He was telling her about the light at those Pacific tests, the pink sky, the air like gardenias. She was listening. She was listening the way she had always listened and outside the window the mango trees were heavy with fruit and the rain from the night before was still on everything, still on the leaves and on the grass and on the road, and somewhere in the house Jessie was sleeping in a white room with louvered windows and the sound of a fan.

Jack Lovett said: it was the most beautiful thing. The light before. Not the shot itself, the shot was the shot, you either had yield or you didn't and you went home or you stayed, but the light before the shot, first light over the water, the pink sky and the air like flowers, that was the beautiful thing, that was the thing that stayed.

He looked at her.

He said: the thing that stays.

She said: yes.
