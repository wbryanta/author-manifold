The ferry leaves at eleven and he is on the upper deck watching the lights of the town diminish, become particular — the orange sodium of the harbour road, the blinking red of a crane someone forgot to power down, the scattered domestic yellow of the hill houses where people are doing what people do in the hours before midnight. He has a paper cup of coffee that has already gone cold. He doesn't throw it away. There is something about the holding of it, the slight damp give of the rim where his thumb presses.

His name is Paul. He is fifty-three years old. His brother's name, the name of the man who may or may not be lying in a refrigerated drawer in the hospital at Stornoway, is Declan. These are the facts he has been given. The rest is a kind of weather he is moving through.

He had not spoken to Declan in eleven years. He is trying not to make this into more than it is, the way you try not to make a scar into a mythology. It was not dramatic, the estrangement. That's the word people use — estrangement — as if there had been some purposeful geographic removal, some voyage. There was only the slow accumulation of silences, like sediment, and then one Christmas Eve a thing was said and then not taken back, and after that the silences were of a different quality. Purposeful. Architectural.

The ferry moves and he can feel it in the soles of his feet, that deep mechanical shudder, a vibration that belongs to the category of things that are both comforting and slightly ominous. He thinks of his daughter, who is seven, who calls ferries the big boats with teeth, meaning the metal ramps that lower at the bow. She had wanted to come. He had said no and she had accepted this with the seriousness she brought to all denials, turning her face away with a dignity that sometimes frightened him. Her name is Mira. He had called her mother from the car park in Ullapool, standing in the wind with his phone pressed against his ear, the call lasting forty seconds, forty-five. Not enough time to say what he wanted to say, which was: I don't know what I'm doing. He had said only that he'd be back by tomorrow evening, he'd call when he arrived. Her mother had said fine. He had said fine. The word fine doing its usual impossible work.

—

Below deck, in the lounge, people are arranged in the attitudes of long journeys. A family with a sleeping child spread across three seats like water finding its level. Two young men with a guitar case, neither of them speaking, watching something on a shared phone, their faces lit blue-white in the dimness. An old woman reading a paperback whose cover he can't see, her lips moving very slightly, not in prayer but in the way some people read, as if the words need to be tasted.

He doesn't stay. He buys another coffee, drinks it while it's still hot this time, standing at the counter where a teenage girl in a high-visibility vest is mopping the floor with a practised and faintly contemptuous efficiency. The floor is already clean. She mops it anyway. He understands this.

Back on the upper deck the mainland is gone. There is only the black water and the black sky and the lights of the ferry strung along the railing like a pretense of festivity. The wind is considerable. It takes his collar and turns it up against his face. He thinks about what the body will look like. He has never identified a body. He knows this is something people do — it is done every day, in every country, the bureaucracy of death requiring these final acknowledgements — but he cannot make it feel ordinary in the way that he suspects it might actually be. He tries to imagine a room. A fluorescent light. A drawer pulled open. A face.

When they were children, Declan had a habit of holding his breath underwater. Not in a frightening way, or rather — it was frightening, but Declan found this funny, would surface with his hair dark and streaming and his face open in a grin at whatever panic he'd produced. He was four years older than Paul, and this seems relevant somehow, the way that four years can mean everything when you are seven and eleven, can mean a kind of lordship, a natural authority that never quite dissolves even when both of you are grown.

What Paul remembers: the particular weight of Declan's hand on the back of his neck when they were at the swimming pool in Inverness, a Wednesday, chlorine and the shouts of other children and the tiles the colour of old teeth. Declan's thumb pressing at the knob of his cervical spine, not threateningly, not at all — it was affectionate, some expression of male tenderness that they hadn't learned the language for. Then Declan let go and cannonballed into the shallow end and the lifeguard blew his whistle and Declan came up laughing.

Paul has not thought about this for years. He is thinking about it now because the cold and the dark and the movement of water have put him into a different relation with himself. He is further from the shore than he has been in a long time. In all senses, he thinks, and then hates himself for thinking it that way, too neatly, as though the situation were a poem rather than a thing that was happening to him.

—

The police had called in the afternoon. A detective constable, her voice careful and practiced at this kind of carefulness. She had said: we have reason to believe. She had said: the body of a man. She had said: identification marks including a tattoo — and here she had paused in a way that made him think she was reading from a report — a tattoo on the left forearm, depicting what appears to be a compass rose.

Paul had sat down. He was in his kitchen, which has a window that looks out over a garden that needs attention. He had sat down and looked at the garden and the detective constable had said: are you still there.

Yes, he said. I'm here.

He knew about the tattoo. It was Declan's, had been Declan's since he was nineteen, done at a place in Aberdeen by someone whose name Declan had thought was funny, though Paul couldn't remember now why it was funny or what the name had been. A compass rose. Because I like knowing which way is north, Declan had said. This was the kind of thing Declan said. It contained a kind of philosophy that Paul had always found simultaneously attractive and exhausting.

The body had been found on the shore of Loch Erisort, on the eastern side of Lewis. How it had come to be there the detective constable did not speculate. The man had been carrying no wallet, no phone. His clothes — dark jeans, a heavy wool sweater, walking boots — told them a kind of story but not a complete one. Someone had reported seeing a figure on the shore road two days before, in the early morning, walking east. This was all.

Declan had been living, as far as Paul knew, in Inverness. As far as Paul knew was doing considerable work here. As far as Paul knew was an ocean of unknowing.

—

He finds a spot on the lee side of the deck where he's partially sheltered. He leans on the railing. The water is very black and very close in the way that water always is from the deck of a ship at night, closer than you'd think, closer than seems right. He watches it for a while. Then he thinks about what the detective constable had said before she'd hung up, the last thing: there's no certainty yet. She said it gently, but also as a kind of professional offering, the way a doctor might offer an ambiguous test result as a form of temporary mercy. There's no certainty yet. This could mean his brother was not dead. This could mean his brother was dead and there were simply the necessary formalities still to perform. Both of these things were true simultaneously, in the way that some things are, and he was sailing toward the place where one of them would become untrue.

He thinks about the last time he saw Declan. Not Christmas Eve — that was on the phone, the awful phone call with its aftermath of silence — but the actual last time, in person, which was the summer before that, at their mother's house in Nairn after she had her hip done. Declan had driven down from Inverness and Paul had driven up from Edinburgh and they had eaten sandwiches in the conservatory while their mother slept. They had not fought. This is important to remember. They had talked about a documentary they'd both seen, on different nights in different houses, about deep-sea creatures that made their own light. They had talked about their mother's garden. Declan had asked about Mira and Paul had said she was good, she was brilliant, he worried about her the way you worry about things that matter too much. Declan had nodded. He understood this. Then their mother had woken and called from the bedroom and they had both stood up at the same moment and looked at each other, and there was something in that — some residual fluency — that had made Paul almost say something. What he might have said he doesn't know. The moment closed.

Declan left before dinner. He had a thing, he said. He had that energy he sometimes had, the edges of him slightly blurred, which Paul knew as the sign of a drink taken earlier but said nothing about. They hugged briefly on the front step. Declan's arms were thin. Paul had noticed this and then not let himself think about what it meant.

—

Inside the ship there is warmth and the smell of diesel and cooking fat and carpet that has absorbed years of damp coats and boots. He buys a scotch at the bar that opens at midnight, even though he doesn't particularly want it. There is a television mounted on the wall showing football highlights with the sound off. He finds a table near the window where there is nothing to see but his own reflection and beyond it the darkness.

He had a marriage before Mira's mother. This is not relevant to what is happening but he finds himself thinking about it, the way the mind refuses direction during vigils. Her name was Cath and they were twenty-four and twenty-six and deeply unsuited in ways that took them four years to accept. What he remembers about Cath is the particular patience of her unhappiness, how she was unhappy without drama or demand, which was somehow worse than if she had been demanding. She had liked Declan. Declan had made her laugh in a way that Paul sometimes didn't. He had never been jealous of this. He'd been grateful.

He wonders if Declan ever married. He doesn't know. This is the kind of thing you lose, in eleven years.

The scotch is mediocre and he drinks it slowly and the television shows a manager being interviewed on a touchline, the manager's face performing a controlled expressiveness, saying things that can't be heard. There is an art, Paul thinks, to saying nothing with conviction. He has become reasonably accomplished at this. His work requires it — he is an architect, which people always find more interesting than it is. He designs buildings that are meant to be inhabited rather than admired. He is good at this. He is less good at other things.

Declan had wanted to be a painter. He had been, for a while — had studied in Glasgow, had shown at a small gallery in Leith, had produced canvases that Paul found difficult to describe because they were not quite abstract and not quite not-abstract. Large. That was the word. They were large paintings, and when you stood in front of them you felt something happening in your peripheral vision, some suggestion of movement or depth. Paul still has one. It is in the hallway of his flat in Edinburgh, leaning against the wall because he has never gotten round to having it properly hung. Four feet by six, oil on canvas, the colour of it like the inside of a wave. Declan had given it to him without comment. This was fifteen years ago. Paul does not know if he is keeping it because he values it or because getting rid of it would require a decision.

—

After midnight the ferry is quieter. The family has migrated to the overnight cabins and the two young men with the guitar case are asleep, the guitar propped between them. The old woman is still reading. Paul watches her for a while, the slight movement of her lips, and feels something he can't name — not envy, not quite, but something adjacent. The quality of her absorption. The way she is entirely somewhere else.

He thinks about what he will say when he arrives. He has no idea what you say. He thinks you say nothing, probably. He thinks the speaking must be done by others — by the detective, by whoever is in charge of these procedures — and that your role is to receive. He is not good at receiving. He is better at the building of things, the consideration of how a space might be used, what light is required and from what angle, how a person will feel moving through a corridor from this room to this other room. He is good at anticipating need. He is less good at the thing that comes after need, which is the allowing of help.

He tries to imagine Declan's face. Not as it would be in the drawer — he will not let himself imagine that — but as it was. The specific Declan-ness of his face, the jaw that was their father's jaw, the way his eyes changed colour slightly depending on the weather or his mood, grey in certain lights and in others almost green. The way he ate — quickly, with attention, as if food were interesting. The sound of him clearing his throat before he said something he meant. There were signs. Paul had learned them and then, in the years of silence, he had been in no position to read them, and now he was on a ferry crossing the Minch in the dark and there was a drawer somewhere ahead of him that was either full of his brother or full of a stranger, and both of these possibilities were, in their different ways, requiring things of him that he did not know how to give.

—

Near two in the morning he goes back to the upper deck. He can't sleep. He's tried — has stretched across a seat in a quiet corner of the lounge — but sleep is somewhere else tonight, occupying another country. The cold is denser now, more deliberate. He pulls his jacket tighter. The water around the ferry has changed — he can see it in the lights from the bow, a different texture, something in it that suggests the proximity of land, the subtle pressure of the seabed rising. The crossing is nearly done.

He thinks about the last thing Declan said to him on the phone. Christmas Eve, eleven years ago. He thinks about it carefully and then decides not to. Some material is too tender for the middle of the night on open water. He has learned this about himself, the hard way — that there are thoughts that are safe to think in daylight, in the presence of structure and activity, and thoughts that should not be approached in the dark, at sea, alone. He is not stable enough right now. He is permeable. The water and the cold and the motion have made him permeable.

What he allows himself, instead, is this: Declan had been beautiful. Not handsome exactly — the word handsome suggests too much deliberateness — but beautiful in the way that some people are, without awareness of it, without exploitation of it. He had moved through the world with a carelessness that was not indifference but rather a kind of genuine unrelatedness to the idea of his own appearance. He had worn the same jacket for ten years. He had had a dog once, in Glasgow, a lurcher named after a footballer Paul had never heard of. He had been capable of real and sudden generosity, the kind that leaves the recipient not knowing where to put the feeling. He had also been capable of a coldness that was almost geological in its patience. He had been difficult to know and difficult not to know.

He had been Paul's brother. He is Paul's brother. The tense undetermined, waiting.

—

Stornoway comes first as a glow on the water, a diffuse and brownish light at the horizon that could almost be dawn if it were not still the deep of the night. Then shapes, incrementally. The headland, the castle on its rise above the harbour, the rows of houses with their pale gable-ends, a fishing boat at anchor, its mast light swaying. The ferry slows perceptibly, the engine changing its note, deepening into the work of arrival. He can smell it — the seaweed and diesel of the harbour, something underneath that is just the smell of a place that is itself, particular and unrepeatable.

The other passengers begin to appear. They come up from below or uncurl from their seats in the lounge, gathering their things with the slightly dazed efficiency of people who have been elsewhere and are now returning to the body, to locality, to the requirement of forward motion. Paul has nothing to gather. He has a bag below but he leaves it. He stands at the railing and watches the town arrange itself, become legible.

He will go to the hospital. He has the address on his phone. He will go and someone will take him through a corridor and he will stand in a room with a particular quality of light and then he will know. One possibility will become true and the other will cease. He understands that whichever it is, something is required of him afterward — some action, some phone call, some form to be signed — and that he will do it. He will do it because doing is what remains when everything else has been taken away. He knows this about himself. He is his own architecture.

The ramp goes down with a sound like weather.

He picks up his bag from the seat where he left it and he moves with the others through the corridor and down the metal steps and out onto the pier where the wind is coming off the moor to the west and the streetlights are making halos in the dampness and a car is waiting — not for him, for someone else — and he walks past it and keeps walking, into the town, toward what the morning is going to ask of him.

Behind him the ferry sits in the harbour and breathes, ticking with the residual heat of the crossing, going nowhere. In its lounge the old woman is still reading, her lips still moving. Around her the cleaners begin their work. The guitar case stands against a wall, abandoned in the rush — the two young men have forgotten it, or left it on purpose, it is impossible to tell which. Somewhere in the mechanism of the boat there is a sound like water against metal, rhythmic, persistent. Like someone patiently trying a door.
