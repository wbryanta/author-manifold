The morning already smelled like heat. Marcus pulled the truck off the gravel path and killed the engine and sat listening to the ticking of the cooling metal, looking out at the rows of apple trees whose leaves had gone that particular dull green that meant not enough water or too much sun or some combination of troubles the land had been accumulating for years. Daniel was already out of the passenger seat and walking toward the tree line with the shovel over his shoulder before Marcus had even opened his door.

They found the break where they'd expected to find it, roughly forty feet in from the main line where a gopher had worked its way through the black plastic tubing at some point in the week past. The soil around it had formed a small hardened crust from the brief weeping of water and then gone dry again. Marcus knelt and pressed his thumb into the crust and it crumbled.

"Here," he said, though Daniel was standing right beside him and could see it.

Daniel set the shovel in the ground a few inches from the break and turned a clean cut of earth. He was forty-one, four years older than Marcus, and he moved with the practiced economy of someone who had done this kind of work since childhood and did not need to think about it. Marcus noticed, not for the first time, the gray that had moved into his brother's temples and the back of his neck where the hair was cut short. Their father's gray had come in exactly the same way. Their father had been dead three years.

"We need to trench back another two feet at least," Marcus said.

"I know how to fix a line, Marcus."

"I'm just saying the fitting won't seat right unless we give ourselves room to work."

Daniel didn't answer. He moved the shovel and cut again.

They worked in silence for a while. The sun came up over the ridge to the east and began its business with the back of Marcus's neck. He had forgotten his hat in the truck and considered going back for it but didn't. He worked the soil with the hand trowel and Daniel worked with the shovel and after twenty minutes they had exposed a section of line about six feet long, the gopher's damage visible at its center like a small catastrophe, the plastic tubing gnawed through in two places.

Marcus went to the truck for the repair kit and when he came back Daniel had sat down on the upturned earth and was drinking from his water bottle, looking out at the trees.

"What's the yield looking like to you?" Marcus said.

"You can see what it looks like."

"I mean the numbers. Did you talk to Harmon?"

"I talked to Harmon."

Marcus waited. He crouched beside the exposed line and opened the kit and began cutting out the damaged section. His hands knew this task and did it without much supervision from his brain, which left his brain free to sit in the particular discomfort of waiting for his brother to say the next thing.

"He thinks maybe sixty percent of last year," Daniel said. "Which was already sixty percent of the year before."

"And the year before that was already bad."

"Yes."

Marcus fitted the first coupling and pressed it onto the cut end of tubing. "Okay," he said.

"Okay," Daniel repeated, with a flatness that meant it was not okay and they both understood that.

The coupling seated with a soft click and Marcus moved to the other end. Behind them and above, a red-tailed hawk was making long slow circles over the east block, the block their father had planted with Honeycrisps in what turned out to be one of his last optimistic acts, 2009, when the variety was still commanding a premium and their father had believed the premium would hold. The Honeycrisps were among the worst-looking trees on the property now, underfed and stressed, dropping fruit before it was ready.

"I had a conversation," Daniel said.

Marcus kept his eyes on the fitting. "With who."

"Peregrine Land."

Marcus stopped. He looked at his brother. Daniel was still looking at the trees, the water bottle held loosely in both hands between his knees.

"When," Marcus said.

"Three weeks ago. And then again last week."

"Three weeks ago." Marcus set the coupling down on the dirt. "You talked to a land company three weeks ago and you're telling me now."

"I'm telling you now because I wanted to know what they were actually offering before I brought it to you. So we could have a real conversation instead of you just—" Daniel paused.

"Instead of me just what."

"Instead of you just shutting it down before we'd even talked about it."

The hawk dropped suddenly, folded its wings and fell toward the far end of the row, and then at the last moment opened them again and rose without landing, having seen something move or having changed its mind. Marcus watched it and then looked back at the dirt.

"What are they offering," he said.

"Three-point-two."

"That's per acre?"

"Total."

Marcus did the arithmetic. The orchard was one hundred and twelve acres, including the twelve acres of mixed-use their father had always meant to develop and never had. Three-point-two million for one hundred and twelve acres in a valley where the water table was dropping and the summers were getting longer and hotter and the premium apple market was something that had happened to other people's farms for the past several years. He understood the number was not unreasonable. He understood this was the problem.

"What do they want it for," Marcus said.

"Development. They said mixed residential."

"So they'd take out the trees."

Daniel looked at him directly for the first time that morning. "Marcus."

"I'm asking."

"What do you think they'd do with it. Yes. They'd take out the trees."

Marcus picked up the coupling and pressed it onto the line and it seated correctly. He fitted the splice piece between the two couplings and heard them both click. He turned the water on at the valve twenty yards back and walked the line looking for seepage and there wasn't any. The repair had taken less than fifteen minutes once they'd gotten the trench right, which was how it always was, how all these repairs were. The damage was always disproportionate to the fixing of it. The damage was the thing that took time.

He turned the water back off and began refilling the trench by hand, moving the soil back over the line.

"I don't want to sell it," he said.

"I know you don't."

"Then why are we having this conversation."

"Because wanting things and being able to afford things aren't the same situation," Daniel said. He stood up and took the shovel and began tamping the returned earth. "Because I'm forty-one years old and I have forty-six thousand dollars in a retirement account and a truck with a hundred and forty thousand miles on it and a daughter who's going to want to go to college in four years. Because Mom is living on Social Security and what she makes at the nursery and the small piece of rental income from the Hendersons, and the Hendersons are moving in September. Because the orchard has not made money in six years."

"It made money in—"

"In 2019 it broke even. Breaking even is not making money."

Marcus didn't say anything. He moved soil with his hands, pressing it back into place.

"Three-point-two," Daniel said. "Split appropriately, given that I've been carrying more of the operating—"

"I know what you've been carrying."

"Then you understand that three-point-two split between us plus what we'd free up from the operating costs and the debt service would—"

"I know what it would do," Marcus said. "I know how to do that math."

A mockingbird had started up somewhere in the row to their left, running through its catalog with the slightly deranged energy of mockingbirds in June. Marcus thought about his father, who had loved mockingbirds and who had one summer identified and catalogued the seventeen distinct songs of the bird that nested in the big valley oak at the property's edge, writing them in his cramped hand in one of the small notebooks he kept in his shirt pocket. His father had kept those notebooks his entire adult life and there were dozens of them in the house, in a box in the closet of the room that had been Marcus and Daniel's room when they were boys. Their mother had not been able to go through the box. Marcus had gone through it once and then not been able to go through it again.

"He would have sold it," Daniel said.

"Don't."

"I mean it. If he'd been in this situation. If he'd seen clearly what was happening and had a young enough mind to—" Daniel stopped. This was close to something they had only approached before and not gone into, the subject of their father's mind in his last years, the slow leakage that everyone around him had been slow to name.

"He planted those Honeycrisps in 2009," Marcus said. "He thought they were going to save the place."

"He was already—"

"I know he was already. I know that. But he planted them because he believed in this land. He believed that if you—" Marcus stopped and pushed the heel of his hand against the returned earth. "He believed that if you just kept at it, kept taking care of it, the land would eventually—"

"The land doesn't care about us," Daniel said. Not unkindly. "That's one of the things you learn when you've been doing this as long as we have. The land doesn't reward faith. It rewards water and good soil temperature and market conditions and a dozen other things that we do not have enough of right now and are going to have less of as time goes on. That's not sentiment. That's just what's true."

Marcus stood up. His knees were dirty. He looked down the long row of trees, past the apples into the block of pears their grandfather had planted, the oldest trees on the property, gnarled and enormous, their trunks gray-brown and fissured like the bark of something older than it actually was, the pears always smaller than the commercial standard but dense and sweet, the kind of pears nobody sold anymore because they didn't ship well and the grocery chains wanted uniformity. Every year a significant portion of the pear crop fell before harvest and the rest they sold at the farmstand and to two restaurants in town that still cared about that sort of thing.

"The pear block," Marcus said.

"What about it."

"It's part of the deal. All one hundred and twelve acres."

"Yes."

"Granddad planted those trees."

Daniel leaned on the shovel. He looked at Marcus with an expression that was not without sympathy but was tired of the conversation, tired in the way you could only be tired of a conversation you'd been having internally for months before you'd had it out loud. "I know he did," Daniel said.

"In 1954. He was twenty-three years old."

"Marcus."

"I'm not making a sentimental argument. I'm just—" But he was making a sentimental argument. He was aware of this. He pressed his lips together and looked at the pear trees and breathed through his nose.

The heat was fully established now. It was eleven in the morning and the temperature was already somewhere in the nineties and the sky above the ridge was white rather than blue, that bleached summer sky that meant the heat had beaten the color out of the day. They would need to check two more sections of line in the back block and Marcus already knew from the pressure readings that at least one of them was compromised. This was the nature of the work now. Finding breaks. Making repairs. Walking the rows and finding the next thing that had gone wrong.

"Tell me about Peregrine," Marcus said. "Tell me who they are."

"Portland-based. They do a lot of work in the valley. They did the Sutherland development off Route 9, and the—"

"I know which one that is."

"Then you know they do decent work. It's not going to be a warehouse or a—"

"It's going to be houses," Marcus said. "It's going to be forty houses in rows where these trees are."

"Probably closer to sixty."

"Jesus."

"People need places to live, Marcus."

"I know people need places to live." He picked up the trowel and the repair kit and walked to the truck and set them in the bed. He stood at the tailgate. Down the slope below the orchard he could see the farmhouse roof, the gray shingles their father had replaced the summer Marcus turned sixteen, Marcus and Daniel both up there passing bundles of shingles while their father worked the nail gun, and how at noon their mother had brought lemonade in a metal pitcher and they had sat on the warm pitched roof and drunk it and looked out over the valley, which had seemed to Marcus at sixteen like the most permanent and complete thing in the world, a thing that would always be as it was then.

Daniel came to the truck and set the shovel in the bed and took another drink of his water. They stood on either side of the tailgate in the way of men with something between them.

"There are things we could try," Marcus said.

Daniel waited.

"We could diversify. Get off the commodity track entirely. There's a market for—"

"We've had this conversation."

"Heritage varieties. Direct sales. Agritourism. People pay for that now."

"People pay for it when they have margin, Marcus. We don't have margin. We don't have cash on hand to invest in a new direction and we don't have the yield to sustain what we're doing now while we pivot toward something else. And we don't—" Daniel stopped. He seemed to be deciding something. "We don't have the labor. I can't work this place the way it needs to be worked and run my own business and be present for Sarah and Cora the way I need to be. I've been killing myself for three years. Since Dad died I have been— I have been holding this together with both hands and I am tired, Marcus. I am genuinely tired."

Marcus looked at him. He had known his brother for thirty-seven years. He had been present in the world for thirty-seven years and Daniel had been present in all of them, had preceded him into the world and would, if the odds held, outlast him, and what Marcus felt looking at him now in the hot flat light of June was something that did not have a clean name but was composed of love and guilt and the particular grief of watching someone you know as well as you know yourself become a stranger to his own energy, hollowed out in a way you had contributed to.

"I know," Marcus said. "I know you have been."

"I'm not saying this to reproach you."

"I know you're not."

Marcus had been living in Medford for six years, working as an estimator for a construction firm, driving out on weekends when he could manage it. He had a life there, a small house, a woman he'd been seeing for two years named Adrienne who was patient about the weekends and patient about what she called his unfinished business with the orchard and who had said once, with great gentleness, that she thought he kept himself in debt to something that didn't need his debt. He had known she was right and had resented her for it and had not been able to let it change anything.

"If we sold," Marcus said, "what happens to Mom."

"She keeps the house. That's not part of the sale. The property line on the house parcel is clearly—"

"I know where the property line is. I mean what happens to her. She'd be looking at sixty houses."

Daniel was quiet for a moment. "She'd have income. We'd make sure she was—"

"She grew up a half mile from here. She moved to this house when she married Dad and she has been here for forty-five years and she would be looking at sixty houses."

"We'd have to talk to her."

"We'd have to talk to her," Marcus said. "Yes. We would."

They drove the truck along the edge of the property to the back block without talking. The radio had been broken since March and Marcus had not fixed it because he found he didn't mind the silence. Or had not minded it before today. Today the silence in the cab felt pressurized.

He found the second break quickly. Third year in a row they'd had problems in this block, the clay content of the soil higher here, expansion and contraction with the temperature swings working on the line. He crouched beside it and looked at it and thought about his father crouching in this same spot, in this same posture, looking at a different break in a different line, and how many times in forty years his father had made this exact trip to this exact corner of the property and found something that needed fixing and fixed it. It occurred to Marcus that he had always thought of the orchard as the thing his father loved, the object of the love, but perhaps the orchard had always been only the occasion for the love, which was actually the fixing and the tending and the being present in the rows. You could not inherit an occasion. You could only find your own.

"Talk to Peregrine again," Marcus said.

Daniel looked at him.

"Don't agree to anything. But talk to them again and ask them—" Marcus paused. He was working something out. "The pear block. It's what, nine acres?"

"Eight and a half."

"Ask them if they'd consider excluding it. If the deal could be structured to leave that parcel."

"They want the whole—"

"I know what they want. Ask them anyway. It's a negotiation." Marcus stood up. "And I want to talk to Mom before anything else happens. Not to get her permission. Just to—she should know where things are."

Daniel studied him. "What are you thinking about the pear block."

"I'm thinking that I don't know yet. I'm thinking eight and a half acres is something I might be able to manage on weekends without destroying you. Or that there might be someone who'd lease it." Marcus looked at the pears. "I'm thinking they're Granddad's trees and they're going to die in the next fifteen years regardless of what we do or don't do and that's just what happens to trees, but I'm not ready to take them out on purpose."

Daniel was quiet. He picked up a piece of dried soil and turned it in his fingers and let it crumble.

"That might be a hill to climb with them," he said. "Carving out a parcel."

"Maybe."

"They might say no."

"They might. Ask them anyway."

They dug out the second break and repaired it. They ate the sandwiches they'd brought in the relative shade of the truck and drank warm water and didn't talk much. The heat peaked sometime around two and then began its long reluctant withdrawal and the color came back into the sky by degrees. A different hawk, or the same one, was working the thermals above the ridge.

When they were packing up to leave, Daniel said: "What would you do. With the pear block. If they agreed and you kept it."

"I don't know exactly," Marcus said. "Keep it going. Sell to the restaurants, to whoever wants them. Not make any money at it."

"That's not a business."

"No," Marcus agreed. "It's not a business."

He thought about his father's notebooks. He thought about the mockingbird with its seventeen songs. He thought about Adrienne, who was right about most things and who was in Medford right now in the Saturday afternoon of her own life, waiting to see what he would do with his unfinished business, patient in a way that had limits, as patience always did.

"Tell me what Mom says," Daniel said.

"We'll go together."

"You want to drive out there after—"

"Tonight. Before it gets dark."

Daniel put his shovel in the truck bed. He rubbed the back of his neck where the sweat had dried and left a pale residue of salt. He looked at his brother.

"Okay," he said. This time it meant something different.

The drive back to the house was five minutes. The pear trees were on their left as they went and the apples on their right and between the rows the dry grass moved in whatever small breeze was available. Marcus drove slowly. He was not ready to be done looking. He understood that this was one of the last times or one of the times leading up to the last time, which was a different thing from the last time itself but was its own kind of knowledge, its own weight to carry through the day.

He stopped the truck and got out and walked into the pear rows. Daniel watched from the cab without saying anything. Marcus put his hand on one of the old trunks, on the gray fissured bark, and stood there for a moment feeling the tree's indifference to being touched and its willingness to be touched anyway, which was perhaps not so different from anything else that endured. Then he got back in the truck and they drove down to the house.
