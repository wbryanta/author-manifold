She had told herself it would not be the furniture that broke her. Not the furniture. The rest of it, maybe—the smell of the house when she first opened the door that morning, the particular way September light came through the kitchen window and made the linoleum look almost gold, the finding of small things in drawers. Those she had prepared for. But not the furniture. The furniture was just wood. Just stuffing and fabric and legs that had been bought and paid for at Lowell's on Route 9 sometime before she was born and would be bought and paid for again this afternoon by people she did not know. She had told herself this.

By two o'clock there were strangers in every room.

Adelle had set the prices herself, taped small stickers to the undersides of things the way the estate company suggested, white stickers with red numbers, and when she walked through the house that morning before anyone arrived the stickers on her mother's belongings looked like wounds, like something had bitten each piece and left a mark. She had walked through quickly and not stopped walking.

Now a man in a John Deere cap was carrying out the dining room chairs two at a time, one under each arm, carrying them the way you'd carry children. His wife—or some woman with him—was trying to negotiate the price of the buffet. A mahogany buffet that Adelle's mother had referred to as the sideboard, always the sideboard, and had polished every third Sunday after church with a rag she kept specifically for that purpose, a yellow rag that lived in the second drawer of the sideboard itself, which meant that now the yellow rag was also thirty-five dollars.

"Would you take twenty for the buffet?" the woman asked.

Adelle looked at her. A round woman in a pink sweatshirt with a rhinestone cat on it. Not a bad face. A face that had seen things and was not asking to be congratulated for having seen them.

"Sixty-five is the price," Adelle said.

"I understand, honey. I just thought—"

"The price is sixty-five." She said it without meanness and the woman heard that and didn't push. Good. Adelle didn't want meanness. She wanted sixty-five dollars and she wanted the woman to take the sideboard out of the house so that Adelle would not have to look at it anymore.

She went to stand on the porch.

From the porch she could see the lawn and the street and three cars parked at angles against the curb, the way people parked in this town where there was no pressure of space, no one behind you waiting. She had forgotten that. Fifteen years in Columbus and she had forgotten about parking without consequence.

The town was called Cressfield. Population forty-two hundred or so, depending on whether you counted the parts that wanted to be counted. Her mother had lived here for fifty-one years, since marrying Roy Pickett in 1963 and moving into this house on Garner Street, and had never once in those fifty-one years referred to leaving. Not longingly, not bitterly. She would mention other places the way you mention the weather in a city you've never been to—as information, not desire. Adelle's brother Marcus had moved to Portland and her mother said Portland and nodded like she was confirming something she'd already known. Adelle moved to Columbus and her mother said Columbus and nodded the same way. The house on Garner Street was the weather she lived in. It was just the weather.

A boy of maybe eight came up the porch steps dragging something behind him—a lamp, the brass floor lamp from the living room, which Adelle had priced at twelve dollars though it was probably worth more and probably worth less and either way was a lamp and lamps didn't matter. The boy was dragging it by its cord.

"Hey," she said. Not unkindly.

He stopped and looked at her.

"You going to buy that?"

"My mom is."

"Your mom know you're dragging it like that?"

He looked at the cord in his hand, then up at her, and Adelle saw in his face the brief calculation of the very young: whether the adult meant this as a real question or as a the beginning of trouble. He let go the cord. The lamp stood there on the porch between them, sixty watts of history, and Adelle thought about how her mother used to read under that lamp. Sat in the wingback chair with the lamp pulled close and read those paperback romances she pretended not to read, slipping them under the cushion when someone came in, as if she needed protecting from being known.

"You can carry it," Adelle said. "Just not by the cord."

The boy picked it up by the base and went carefully down the steps.

She watched him go.

What she had not told Marcus—who had sent money and regrets from Portland, flowers for the funeral and a check for the estate company and his absence, which was its own kind of check—was that she had already taken the things that mattered before the sale was organized. Weeks ago, the day after the funeral, she had come to the house alone and walked through it at four in the morning after driving up from Columbus in that dead-hour dark, and she had stood in each room long enough to find what was real. Real meaning not monetary. Real meaning the things that had her mother in them in a way that couldn't be laundered out.

The yellow rag. She had taken the yellow rag.

A coffee cup with a chip on the rim that her mother drank from every morning because she said throwing away a cup that was still a cup was wasteful. A photograph not in a frame, just loose in the junk drawer, of her mother at maybe twenty-five, twenty-six, standing in front of a car Adelle didn't recognize in a place she couldn't name, squinting into the sun and laughing at something outside the frame. Not posing. Just caught. Adelle had never seen her mother look like that. She must have been someone, before all this. Before Roy and the house and the children and the sideboard. She must have been someone standing in front of a car laughing, and whoever took the picture knew something about her that Adelle didn't.

She had taken the photograph. Folded it carefully and put it in her coat pocket and driven back to Columbus with it pressed against her chest by the seat belt.

Everything else was thirty-five dollars or sixty-five or twelve.

A woman with short gray hair and librarian's glasses came up the porch steps now and excused herself past Adelle with a small, respectful nod. This was the kind of town where people did that. Where they knew, or suspected, or could read on your face that the house they were picking over was your house, your mother's house, which amounted to the same thing, and they treated you with a particular courtesy that was almost worse than if they had not treated you that way at all. The courtesy said: we know. And because they knew and were being kind about it, you were obligated to receive their kindness without collapsing, which was exhausting, which was maybe the whole problem with grief in a small town. Nobody let you be ugly in it.

Her mother would not have wanted ugly anyway.

Her mother had been a woman of considerable discipline about her own interior life. She showed you what she decided to show you and the rest was hers and it stayed hers until she died, and dying didn't release it either—it just transferred it to that locked room that every child knows is in their parent and can never enter. Adelle had accepted this. Mostly. Except for the photograph, which she had not accepted, which she had taken and was keeping and would not be showing Marcus because Marcus had sent a check and that meant he got the version of the estate that came with a receipt.

She went back inside.

The living room was nearly bare. The wingback chair was gone and two women she hadn't noticed before were maneuvering the couch through the front door with the practiced difficulty of people accustomed to acquiring things too large for easy living. The lampstand table was still there with its sticker: fifteen dollars. Her mother's mother's Bible was on the lampstand table and was not for sale—Adelle had forgotten to move it, and someone had left it there, which meant someone had seen it and made a decision not to buy a Bible from a dead woman's house. Good. There were still certain manners.

She picked up the Bible and held it.

It was heavy the way Bibles are always heavy, heavier than the pages alone could account for, as if the years of handling added weight, as if the hands of her grandmother and her mother had left something in the binding that didn't go away. The cover was black and worn smooth at the corners and there was a small water stain on the back that had been there as long as Adelle could remember. She opened it to the family page, the one in the front where generations had written their names and dates in a variety of hands, and she read her own name there: Adelle Ruth Pickett, born March 3, 1961. Written in her mother's hand. Her mother had written it the day she was born or shortly after, had taken this Bible and written her daughter into it, and what that meant was something that Adelle did not have language for and was not going to find language for standing in this empty living room while two strangers took the couch.

She closed the Bible and held it against her chest with both arms the same way the boy had held the lamp.

In the kitchen a man was asking about the refrigerator.

"It's not for sale," Adelle said, coming in. "I told them to mark it not for sale."

"There's a sticker on it," he said, not accusatory, just reporting.

She looked and there was a sticker. Seventy-five dollars. She stared at it. She had marked the refrigerator. She had walked through this house at six in the morning with her box of stickers and marked the refrigerator because a refrigerator was an appliance and she would buy a new one for the house in Columbus and her mother's refrigerator with its particular hum and its one broken shelf that her mother had propped up with a folded paper towel for as long as Adelle could remember—

"I'll give you sixty-five," the man said.

"It's not for sale," she said.

He looked at the sticker.

"The sticker says seventy-five."

"I know what the sticker says."

He was quiet for a moment. Not a bad man. A man who wanted a refrigerator and had driven here from wherever he lived thinking he might get one for a reasonable price and now a woman with a Bible pressed to her chest was telling him the refrigerator with its own price tag was not available. He did the thing that was right, which was to nod and thank her and walk out.

Adelle stood in the kitchen.

She had not eaten since morning—since the drive up, she'd stopped at a McDonald's outside Chillicothe and eaten an Egg McMuffin in the parking lot watching the light come up over the highway, thinking about nothing, making her mind flat and serviceable the way she needed it to be for today. But now she was hungry and everything in the kitchen had already been sold or would be sold or was the refrigerator, which she appeared to not be selling.

She opened the refrigerator. This was absurd. She knew this was absurd. But she opened it and stood in the cold breath of it and looked at what her mother had left: half a jar of sweet pickles, a bottle of French dressing, two apples, a Tupperware container of something that had gone bad and that Adelle had meant to throw away and hadn't. Also a stick of butter. Also a carton of milk that had been sour for weeks.

The sweet pickles had been there forever. Her mother bought sweet pickles once every several years and ate them in small quantities over time, a single pickle with lunch, one alongside a sandwich, and the jar lasted so long that it became a fixture, a permanent low-level presence in the refrigerator that Adelle had never thought about except that she was thinking about it now, and what she was thinking was: who finishes the pickles. The jar was three-quarters full and her mother was dead and who was going to eat those pickles. She certainly wasn't going to eat them. She didn't like sweet pickles. Marcus didn't like sweet pickles either and anyway Marcus was in Portland. Nobody in this family liked sweet pickles except her mother, who was seventy-four years old when she died and had eaten them in careful moderation for her entire adult life, and now there was three-quarters of a jar.

She closed the refrigerator.

She went back out to the porch and sat in the one chair she'd kept out there—the old aluminum lawn chair, green and white weave, which she hadn't priced and no one had asked about because it was the kind of chair people bring to football games and leave at football games and everyone already owns and no one needs. She sat in it and held the Bible in her lap and looked at the street.

Two men from earlier were loading the dining room table into the back of a pickup. They wrestled it over the tailgate and it went in with a sound like a body dropped. The John Deere man was already gone with his chairs. The pink sweatshirt woman was coming down the front walk with the sideboard's yellow rag in her hand, separate from the sideboard, just the rag, which meant she had found it in the drawer and taken it as part of the purchase and why wouldn't she, it was in the drawer, it was the rag, she didn't know what it was.

Adelle watched her go.

The yellow rag in the woman's hand was not her mother. She knew that. Her mother was not in the yellow rag the way she was not in the furniture or in the sweet pickles or in the water-stained Bible or in the loose photograph in Adelle's coat pocket, and also her mother was in all of these things in a way that couldn't be explained to anyone, not to Marcus, not to the estate company, not to the grief counselor she had seen twice after the funeral and had stopped seeing because the grief counselor kept trying to give her a process and what she had was not a process. What she had was more like weather. What she had was her mother's voice saying Columbus and nodding, saying Portland and nodding, as if the whole world was information that confirmed what she already knew, which was that the house on Garner Street was where she lived, and she was going to keep on living there until she didn't, and that was all there was to it.

She had lived there until she didn't.

Now it was four-thirty and the last few people were coming out of the house with lamps and boxes and a set of dishes and a small table that had been in the hallway, and Adelle sat in the aluminum chair and let them come and go. She said thank you when they said thank you. She confirmed prices when there were questions. She watched the light do what September light does in Ohio, which is different from what it does anywhere else, which is go gold and level and very serious about itself, throwing long shadows off the trees and making everything it touches look like it means something.

Her mother had looked at this light for fifty-one years.

She had looked at it from the kitchen window and from the porch and from the car and from the yard and probably from the sidewalk walking back from somewhere, and what she made of it, whether she found it beautiful or oppressive or simply the light she lived under, Adelle didn't know and now would never know. That was the thing about parents. They took their inner weather with them. You thought you knew the terrain and then they were gone and you realized you had only been seeing the edges of a place you were never shown inside. All your life you had been standing at the border and mistaking the view for the whole country.

The woman with librarian glasses came back out with a box of kitchen things—measuring cups, a colander, small implements—and smiled at Adelle on the way to her car, a kind, appropriate smile, not lingering.

Inside the house was quiet.

Adelle sat a moment longer, then went in.

The rooms had been emptied the way rooms are emptied when the emptying is done by people who want the things and not the room—thoroughly, without ceremony, the negative space left clean. In the living room only the lampstand table remained, the one with the fifteen-dollar sticker, which she peeled off now and put in her pocket. In the dining room only the marks on the carpet where the table legs had been. In the bedroom the curtains her mother had chosen were still on the windows—white curtains with a blue stripe at the hem, not for sale, never for sale—and the light coming through them was the serious gold September light, and the room smelled like her mother in a way that the rest of the house did not quite anymore.

Adelle stood in it.

She stood in the smell of it the way you stand in a river when you are a child, aware of the current but not afraid, aware that it will move you if you let it, aware that eventually you will have to come out.

She stood in it until she was done standing in it.

Then she put the Bible under her arm and went through each room one more time, turning off lights that weren't on, closing windows that weren't open, doing the small automatic things that people do when they leave a house as if the house needed attending, as if the house didn't know. In the kitchen she stood at the window and looked at the yard—the garden patch that had been her mother's project every year, done for the season now, the soil turned and ready for nothing—and she thought about the woman in the photograph, the one standing laughing in front of the car, twenty-five and squinting into the sun.

She put her hand flat on the window glass.

The glass was warm from the light.

Then she took her hand back, and picked up her keys from the kitchen counter where she had set them that morning, and walked out the front door, and pulled it shut behind her, and went down the porch steps, and did not look back at the house because looking back was not a thing that would help her now, and got into her car, and sat for a moment in the particular silence of a car that is about to move, and then she moved.
