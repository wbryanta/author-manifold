The water had moods the way people had moods, and Vera had been reading them for sixty years, which meant she had stopped being surprised by either. She stood at the end of the breakwater with her granddaughter, who was eleven and had her mother's way of holding her shoulders like she was bracing for something that hadn't arrived yet, and she pointed at a dark patch of surface two hundred yards out where the chop was running contrary to the prevailing swell.

"What do you see?" Vera asked.

Maisie squinted. The girl had Vera's eyes, which was to say gray-green and slightly too close together, a face built for skepticism. "Water," she said.

"That's a start."

The cold came off the harbor in layers. It was February, and the season had been brutal in the way that only coastal February could be brutal, a wet penetrating cold that had nothing theatrical about it, no drama of snowfall, just a gray persistence that wore at things. The cannery at the south end of the harbor had its lights on, which was something, the yellow glow of it visible even in the afternoon when the sky was bright enough to compete. But everyone in Dunmore knew it wouldn't last past April. The Delvecchio family had sent their eldest son down from Portland in November, a man Vera had met twice at town meetings, careful and soft-spoken in the way of people who had been trained to deliver bad news gently, and he had laid it out in the community hall with charts and graphs that meant nothing to anyone who hadn't already understood for years what was happening. The fish weren't coming back. The boats that remained were working three times as hard for a third of the yield they'd had twenty years ago. The economics were the economics.

Vera had been a harbor pilot for thirty-one years. She had guided freighters and tankers and the occasional research vessel through the channel, had read the water in fog so thick you navigated by sound and instinct, had known this harbor the way she supposed some people knew music, which was not as something you studied but as something you heard in your body. She had retired at sixty-eight after her knees made the ladder work genuinely dangerous, and in the four years since she had come down to the water every morning because she didn't know what else to do with herself, and because she owed the harbor that, the way you owed someone who had kept you for most of your life at least the courtesy of your attention.

Maisie was here because her mother, Vera's daughter Pauline, was going through something that Pauline called a reorganization and that Vera called a breakdown with more compassion than the word suggested. Pauline had called in January and asked if Maisie could stay for a month, maybe six weeks, until she got some things sorted out. Vera had said yes without asking the questions she wanted to ask, which was something she had learned to do in her sixties, not asking the questions, accepting that people would tell you what they could when they could. Maisie had arrived on a Tuesday with a duffel bag and a tablet computer and the quality of careful neutrality that children develop when they've decided the adults around them cannot be trusted to hold themselves together.

"Look at the color difference," Vera said now. "See where it's darker? That's deeper water, or there's a temperature change, or there's something moving underneath that's disturbing the surface from below. The water tells you everything. You just have to know what language it's speaking."

Maisie looked. She had been resistant the first week, more interested in the tablet, the group chats with friends back in Portland, the maintenance of whatever social infrastructure eleven-year-olds maintained with such urgency. But Vera had not pushed. She had simply continued going to the harbor each morning and had invited Maisie each time without insisting, and on the fourth day Maisie had appeared in the kitchen at seven wearing her coat already, which Vera understood to be a form of surrender disguised as initiative.

"Is it colder?" Maisie said. "The dark part."

"Probably. Cold water sinks but it also moves differently. See how the surface there has a kind of grain to it, like brushed metal? That's where two different bodies of water are meeting. They don't want to mix. They have different densities."

"They're arguing," Maisie said.

Vera looked at her. "Yes," she said. "That's exactly right."

The girl had taken to it faster than Vera expected, which maybe shouldn't have surprised her. Intelligence ran in the family the way stubbornness ran in the family, the way the inability to ask for help ran in the family, and Vera recognized in Maisie's manner of observation—the stillness of it, the patience—something she recognized from her own early years on the water, before she knew what she was looking at, when looking was its own sufficient act.

They walked back along the breakwater toward the harbor. The working boats were in, most of them, four or five vessels that represented what remained of Dunmore's fleet. There had been forty boats when Vera was young. The reduction hadn't happened all at once. It happened the way most losses happened, gradually and then completely, one boat a year or two boats a year, the Hendersons selling up after Jim Henderson's back gave out, the Kowalski brothers moving to Alaska where there was still something to fish for, the Nguyen family's trawler sitting at the dock for a season and then simply not being there anymore, and no one held a funeral for a boat but perhaps they should have.

Maisie stopped to look at a harbor seal that had hauled itself onto the dock float, a fat and apparently self-satisfied animal that regarded them with the mild contempt of something that had solved all its problems.

"Does it live here?" Maisie said.

"Few of them do. They come in when the water's rough outside. Smarter than people."

"Mom says you always say things like that."

"Things like what?"

"That animals are smarter than people."

Vera considered this. "I probably mean something more specific. I mean they don't make things more complicated than they are."

Maisie crouched to look at the seal, who declined to move. "She talks about you a lot," Maisie said, in the careful tone of someone relaying information they've assessed for safety.

"Does she."

"She says you're hard to know."

"That's fair," Vera said.

"She doesn't say it mean."

"I know she doesn't."

They stood with the seal for a while. The light was doing what it did in February, beginning its early withdrawal, the harbor going from silver to pewter. From the south end came the sounds of the cannery, a low mechanical pulse that Vera had heard for so long it had become part of the harbor's ambient sound the way the bell buoy was part of it, the way the cry of the herring gulls was part of it. After April there would be a different silence. She had been thinking about this the way you thought about an operation you were scheduled for, with a kind of dread that was also, embarrassingly, a kind of preparation, the body getting ready to survive something.

"Can I come on the boat tomorrow?" Maisie said.

"It'll be cold."

"I know it's cold."

"Colder on the water than it is here."

"Grandma."

"Yes," Vera said. "You can come."

---

She had borrowed the use of Pete Saavedra's lobster boat for the winter, or Pete had offered it when he saw her walking the docks in early December with the look she supposed she had, the look of someone who needed to be on the water or would begin to ruin. Pete was sixty-three and still working, one of the ones who had stayed, and he used the boat five days a week but left her tied up on Sundays and Mondays, and he had handed Vera the key without ceremony and said take her out when you want and Vera had said I'll put fuel in and Pete had said don't worry about it and they had stood there for a moment in the particular silence of people who understood each other well enough not to need to finish every transaction.

The boat was called the Marie Claire, after Pete's mother, and it smelled of bait and salt and diesel in proportions that Vera had always found more comforting than anything that was supposed to be comforting. She had Maisie put on the life jacket before they untied, which Maisie did without arguing, and she stood at the wheel and took them out through the harbor mouth slow and careful in the gray morning with Maisie standing beside her, both of them watching the water.

Outside the harbor mouth the sea had some motion to it, a long groundswell coming from the southwest, remnants of weather that had passed through three days ago. Vera cut the engine back and let the boat rise and fall with it.

"Put your hand on the gunwale," she said.

Maisie did.

"Don't think about it. Just feel it."

The boat lifted and dropped. Lifted and dropped. The rhythm was long and slow and very old, geological almost, the movement of water that had been making that movement for longer than there had been anyone to feel it.

"Okay," Maisie said.

"That's the primary swell. Everything else is on top of that. The chop, the local wind waves, the wake from other boats—those are all secondary. When you're reading water you always find the primary rhythm first. If you can't find it you're going to misread everything."

Maisie nodded. Her eyes were on the horizon, which was the right instinct, and Vera said so.

"Why the horizon?"

"Because that's where the water is coming from. Whatever you're feeling under the boat, you can see it approaching from a distance if you watch the right place. The horizon shows you the future. The surface around the boat shows you the present."

Maisie thought about this in the way she thought about things, which was with her whole face, an expression of concentration that made her look briefly much older. "Is that true for other things too?" she said.

"Probably," Vera said. She turned them north, running with the swell at a shallow angle, and the motion changed slightly, the rise and fall becoming a rise and roll and fall that was more complex and required small constant corrections at the wheel. She let Maisie watch her hands. "I'll tell you when you can try," she said.

They ran north along the coast for half an hour. The shore was dark rock and sparse winter spruce and occasional houses, most of them summer places that stood empty and shuttered. Vera pointed out the landmarks she'd learned to use before GPS, before LORAN, back when navigation was still an argument you made with the land and the water and the stars, a conversation that required you to pay attention to everything simultaneously. The notch in the ridge above Seal Point. The white water that showed above the Sisters reef at anything less than half tide. The way the color of the water shifted from deep blue to the particular blue-green that meant you were over the shelf, the bottom coming up beneath you.

"How do you know all of this?" Maisie asked.

"I was out here for thirty years."

"But how do you remember it all?"

Vera thought about how to say it. "It's not exactly remembering. When you've been somewhere long enough it becomes part of how you see. You don't remember that your kitchen table is in the middle of the room, you just know where not to walk. It's like that."

"But you're retired," Maisie said. "Don't you forget?"

"Not this kind of knowing."

The girl was quiet for a while, and Vera knew she was thinking about something else, working toward it. She gave her time. They went through a patch of rougher water where a current was running against the swell, the surface steep and confused, and Vera made the adjustments without thinking about making them and felt the boat's behavior change and then stabilize.

"Is Mom going to be okay?" Maisie said.

The directness of it was startling. Not the question, which Vera had expected eventually, but the directness, the clean way the girl had of asking what she meant to ask when she was ready to ask it.

"Yes," Vera said.

"How do you know?"

"Because I know your mother."

"She's been like this before," Maisie said. "She gets bad and then she gets better and then she gets bad."

"I know."

"Does it bother you?"

Vera thought about lying and decided against it. "Yes. It's hard to watch someone you love struggle."

Maisie seemed to accept this. She put both hands on the gunwale and looked out at the water, and Vera understood that the girl had been carrying this for some time, the careful neutrality she'd arrived with was the face of someone who had learned to manage their own anxiety about an unreliable situation, and bringing it into the open, even partially, had shifted something. The shoulders came down a fraction.

"She says you and she are the same," Maisie said.

"We're very different."

"She says that's why."

Vera turned this over. It was possible that Pauline was right. Vera had her own history, her own seasons of difficulty that she'd navigated largely without asking for help, and the question of whether that was admirable or simply lonely was one she had stopped trying to settle. What she had instead of an answer was the water, thirty-one years of mornings that had required her full attention and in requiring it had provided her with something she couldn't quite name, a structure for living maybe, a reason to be awake and paying attention that was larger than her own internal weather.

"Come here," she said. "Take the wheel."

Maisie looked at her.

"It's all right. I'm right here."

The girl took the wheel with both hands, and Vera put her own hands lightly over Maisie's, not gripping, just present.

"Feel what the boat is doing?"

"It's moving around a lot."

"It's moving around a little. But yes. Feel where the pressure is? When the swell comes under the stern she wants to turn. You have to anticipate it. You don't wait for her to move and then correct—you're already correcting before she moves, because you've felt the swell coming."

"How?"

"Your feet. Feel it through your feet."

Maisie was quiet, concentrating. The boat lifted to the swell and began its tendency to yaw, and before Vera could do anything the girl had made a small correction, not enough but in the right direction and at the right moment.

"Like that?" Maisie said.

"Like that," Vera said.

They ran another twenty minutes north with Maisie at the wheel, Vera's hands growing lighter and then lifting away entirely for stretches at a time while she watched and was ready. The girl made mistakes, overcorrected, let the bow wander before catching it, but she was learning the boat's language, that particular conversation between water and hull that was different for every vessel, and Vera could see it happening in real time, the incremental shift from resistance to dialogue.

When they turned for home the swell was behind them, which was easier in some ways and harder in others, and Vera took the wheel back for that and explained why, explained what it meant to have following seas, how the danger in it was the comfort of it, how a following sea could make you feel like everything was going your way when in fact you were in a situation that required more care than the ones that felt difficult.

Maisie listened with her whole face the way she always did.

---

In the evenings they ate dinner and Vera did not turn the television on, which she'd stopped doing years ago, the evenings too loud with it, and instead they sat at the kitchen table where the oil lamp was burning because she preferred it to the overhead light, and Maisie did homework that she was supposed to submit online and Vera read or worked on the charts she'd been annotating for her own satisfaction, not for any practical purpose but because the work of it was satisfying, the way any record is satisfying, making sure something is written down.

One night, the third week, Maisie looked up from her screen and said, "What happens to a place when it stops being what it was?"

Vera looked up. "What do you mean?"

"The cannery. If it closes. What happens to here?"

"It changes. It's already changed. It keeps changing."

"But does it stop being itself?"

It was the kind of question that made Vera aware of the specific quality of the girl's mind, the way it moved straight to the center of things. She put her pencil down.

"No," she said. "I don't think so. The harbor was here before the cannery. It'll be here after. The town will be different. Smaller probably, or just different in composition—different people, different reasons to be here. But the water doesn't know about the cannery."

"That sounds lonely," Maisie said.

"Which part?"

"The water not knowing."

Vera thought about it. "I think it's the opposite of lonely," she said finally. "The water keeps doing what water does regardless of what we build on the shore. I find that comforting, actually. It's one of the reasons I come down every morning. Whatever is happening, the harbor is having its own life. It doesn't need me to witness it but I like to."

Maisie looked at her tablet screen for a moment. "Mom says you came to the harbor every day even when Dad left."

Vera said nothing.

"She said you would just come down and look at the water for a long time and then go back home."

"That sounds about right."

"Did it help?"

"Yes," Vera said. She considered elaborating and decided the elaboration wasn't necessary. Yes was enough and it was true, and it was true in a way that resisted explanation, the way certain things were true, the way grief was true and solace was true and the fact of the harbor in the morning was true, the light on the water, the particular cold, the smell, the bell buoy, the seals, the remaining boats with their names that she knew the way she knew the names of everyone she'd loved.

She thought about Pauline in Portland, in whatever difficulty she was in, and she thought about how she had spent thirty years guiding ships through a channel that was unforgiving of inattention, and she thought about how little of that had translated to guiding a daughter through the particular channel of her life, how the skills were not the same skills, how knowing water was not knowing people, and how this was the central failure she had lived with, the one she could not navigate around.

And then she looked at Maisie, who had gone back to her homework, who was sitting under the oil lamp with the same posture as her mother and the same eyes as Vera and who had, three weeks into February, learned to read a following sea and to find the primary swell beneath the secondary chop and to keep her eyes on the horizon to see the future coming, and Vera thought that perhaps this was not nothing. Perhaps this was a kind of transmission. The skills were not the same skills, and the water was not a person, and knowing what she knew would not save the cannery or fix Pauline or make the fish come back, but it was something she had, something real and hard-won, and she was giving it to the girl, and the girl was taking it.

Maisie looked up again. "Can we go out on Monday too?"

"The water will be rough," Vera said. "There's a front coming through Sunday night."

"Good," Maisie said.

Vera looked at her.

"I want to see it rough," Maisie said.

Outside the kitchen window the harbor was dark and the cannery lights were on at the south end, yellow and steady, the low pulse of it carrying through the walls of the house, and Vera thought: a few more months. A few more months of that sound that she had stopped hearing twenty years ago and would miss the moment it was gone. She thought about all the things you don't hear until they're about to stop, and she thought that this was perhaps the only honest argument for paying attention, that it let you hear things while they were still there to hear.

"All right," she said. "Rough it is."
