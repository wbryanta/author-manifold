The first flake came down sideways, which was how Margaret knew it meant business. She was standing at the kitchen window with her coffee going cold in her hand when she saw it cross the glass like something thrown, horizontal and purposeful, and she thought, well. The ewes had been restless all morning. That was how it went sometimes, some animal telegraph that moved through them before the barometer had even flinched, and she'd watched them from the hill gate at first light, the heaviest ones pressing together near the shed door as though they'd already read the forecast and found it worse than she had.

She called up the stairs to Daniel twice before she heard his feet hit the floor.

By the time they'd eaten and pulled on their layers the snow was coming in earnest, a fine dry stuff that hissed against the window glass and didn't so much fall as migrate, traveling in long low skeins across the yard. The temperature had been sitting just below freezing for three days. The ground was iron. Margaret had checked the ewes at midnight and again at four and found two in early labor, both experienced mothers who would likely manage without her, and she'd gone back to bed with the monitor crackling on the nightstand beside her. Now at half past seven with the light still thin and uncertain and the snow building against the fence posts she walked out to the lambing shed with Daniel behind her carrying a bucket of warm water and a coil of rope halter over one shoulder. He was fifteen and tall for it, though he didn't yet know what to do with the height, moved as though apologizing for taking up space.

Inside the shed the air was close and lanolin-thick, the smell she'd known since she was Daniel's age and her own father had walked her through the same motions, the same patient explanations. Seventeen ewes in various stages. She walked the pen slowly, reading each one. The two who'd been laboring at four had both delivered successfully—twins in the first pen, a single in the second, all three lambs under the heat lamp and wobbling toward the teat with that tottering urgency that never stopped moving something in her.

She said their names quietly as she went, a habit Robert had teased her about gently and only once.

You can't name the sheep, he'd said.

They already have names, she'd told him. I'm just using them.

He'd accepted this with the expression he'd had for most of her convictions, fond and slightly baffled, as though she were a weather system too complex to predict but beautiful enough to watch.

Number six pen was showing early contractions. She asked Daniel to check the water bucket, top it up, and watched him go without comment. He'd been quieter since February, since the ground had been too hard to dig and they'd had to wait for the burial with the body at the funeral home in town, three weeks between his father's death and the burial and something in Daniel had gone into that interval and not entirely come back out. She didn't press. She recognized the territory. She'd grown up in it herself, raised by a man of few words in a place that discouraged them, and she'd married a man who talked freely and easily and sometimes she'd found that frightening in its own way, the exposure of it, all that interior made exterior, and now she would have given a great deal to hear him talk about anything at all.

The power went just before nine.

It happened without drama, the way power always went—the small electric hum that underlies a building, that you don't notice until it stops, and then the particular quality of silence that follows. The heat lamps cut. The radio in the corner cut. Daniel looked up from where he was kneeling beside a ewe and said, there it goes, and Margaret was already moving toward the supply corner where they kept the battery lanterns and the propane heater. She got both lanterns lit and set one at either end of the shed, their light warm and insufficient, casting long soft shadows from the sheep along the walls. She lit the propane heater and positioned it away from the bedding. It would be enough for the lambs if she could get them close enough to it. It would have to be.

The wind was something else by now. She could hear it working at the shed roof, looking for purchase, and once in a while the whole structure shuddered in a gust the way a large animal shivers to rid itself of flies. Snow was finding the gaps around the door and the small high window above the water trough and sifting in along the floor, delicate and indifferent. Outside, if she opened the door, she could see nothing past six feet. The house had become theoretical.

She said to Daniel, stay inside now. Don't go out for anything.

He said, I know, Mum.

She said it again anyway.

He looked at her with a patience that made him look briefly older than she was, and she understood that he was giving her something, some allowance, and she took it without acknowledgment because that was how they both preferred to operate.

The morning went by in the way that crisis mornings go by, which is to say both very slowly and not at all—time stopped meaning what it usually meant and the shed became its own complete world with its own logic, governed by the rhythms of the laboring ewes and the wind and the careful management of warmth. Pen by pen she moved. A ewe she'd named Hawthorn, a big reliable Cheviot who'd thrown good lambs three years running, went into hard labor at half nine and Margaret knelt beside her in the straw and talked to her in a low continuing voice while the ewe worked. Daniel held the lantern close. The lamb came without intervention, a good-sized single, and Margaret cleared its face and rubbed it down briskly with a dry cloth while Hawthorn turned to investigate her offspring with that combination of exhaustion and wonder that Margaret had decided long ago was the same in every creature.

Good girl, she told Hawthorn. Good girl.

She felt Daniel watching her.

She said, without looking up, your father used to say she was the most reliable animal on the farm.

Daniel said, I know. He used to say she had more sense than most people.

That made her smile, because it was exactly the kind of thing Robert would say, that exact register of warmth and deflection. She settled the lamb under the propane heater and stood up and her knees ached with the cold and she was glad of it in a way she would not have been able to explain.

What she had not allowed herself to think about, in the three months since Robert died, was whether she would keep the farm. She had not allowed herself to think about it because it was not a question. The farm was not property in any way that made selling it possible. It was the place where she had become the person she was, through the specific education of cold ground and sick animals and seasons that did not respect planning, through the failures and recoveries and the arithmetic of keeping something alive year after year. She'd been a farmer's daughter who married a farmer and become a farmer herself, and she did not know how to extract that from whatever else she might be, did not know if there was anything else underneath or whether the stripping away of it would leave only weather and open air. But none of this was what kept her from considering it.

What kept her from considering it was the boy.

She watched him working beside her and thought about the education happening in him right now, in this shed, on this morning, whether he wanted it or not. The way he'd learned without being formally taught to move quietly among the ewes, to read the signs, to put his hands somewhere useful. The way he was learning it again now without his father here, learning it differently, learning it as something that existed before and after one man's particular expression of it. She wanted that for him more than she wanted comfort for either of them. She believed—and this was not a pleasant belief, she held it the way you hold something necessary and sharp—that the farm would save him if she could keep it. Save him from something she'd seen in town boys, in boys raised without the specific instruction of difficulty, a softness that was not kindness but only the absence of having been tested.

Robert would have argued with her about this. He argued tenderly and she missed it.

At eleven she found a ewe in trouble.

It was one she'd been watching, a younger animal on her second lambing, and the labor had been going on too long. She asked Daniel to wash his hands in the bucket of warm water and when he came back she talked him through what she was doing as she examined the ewe—a lamb presenting badly, one leg folded back, a situation that could resolve or could not depending on the next few minutes. She spoke in the even factual voice she used for things that required steadiness. She corrected the presentation and the ewe labored and the lamb came, a small one, a ewe lamb, limp.

Margaret worked on her for two minutes. Cleared the airway, rubbed hard, held her up by the hind legs. Breathed once into her nose. Rubbed again.

Daniel said nothing. She could feel him behind her, his held breath.

The lamb sneezed, a tremendous full-body convulsion for something that size, and gasped, and sneezed again.

Margaret said, there you are, and set her down in the straw.

She heard Daniel exhale.

She did not turn around. She gave him the moment privately, which was the only way to give it. She focused on the lamb, rubbing her dry, while behind her she heard him make a sound that might have been a laugh or something else entirely, brief and swallowed quickly, and she kept her back to him a moment longer than she needed to.

The storm peaked around noon, though they had no way of knowing it was peaking, no way of knowing anything from inside the shed except the pitch of the wind and the snow coming under the door. She'd blocked the gap with a rolled feed sack and it helped. She kept the propane heater fed. She did a count of the lambs that had come and the ewes still to go and calculated propane and water and feed and found the numbers adequate, which was the best you could say for numbers in a storm—not good, adequate, meaning the margin between what you had and what you needed had not yet gone negative.

She thought about Robert in the small intervals between tasks, which was the only time she'd found to think about him, in the breaks between urgency when the mind briefly unfocused and he was there, not dramatically, not with the weight of grief the grief counselor in town had described as a wave—she'd gone once to the grief counselor and found the language unhelpful, oceanic and passive, the grief as a thing that happened to you—but simply present, the way a known landscape is present. She'd turn to say something and he wouldn't be there. She'd make a decision and have the brief involuntary impulse to run it past him, not because she needed his approval but because they'd thought together for twenty-two years and the habit of it outlasted the person. She'd find herself on his side of the argument before she remembered there was no argument, just her single position, uncontested.

What she'd found in the three months was that she was angrier than the language allowed for. The grief counselor had not mentioned anger except as a stage to pass through. Margaret had found it was not a stage but a climate, variable but persistent, and she did not think it would pass so much as weather into something else over enough years, wear into the landscape of her the way weather wears stone, and the stone remains, changed but present, harder in some places and smoother in others. She was angry at Robert for leaving before the lambing, which was irrational, he had not chosen the timing of his aneurysm, no one chose the timing of their aneurysm. She was angry anyway. She was angry at herself for being angry. She found this, at least, clarifying.

Around one in the afternoon Daniel said, I'm hungry, and she felt a lurch of guilt at having not thought of it herself—they'd had nothing since seven—and then she remembered the emergency supplies on the shelf above the medicine cabinet, the lambing shed's own small provisions. She found two cereal bars and a tin of shortbread she'd put there in February and forgotten. She gave Daniel the cereal bars and the larger portion of the shortbread and ate a few pieces herself standing up, looking over the pen at Hawthorn's lamb, who was sleeping in the straw with the self-sufficient heaviness of the newly living.

She said, do you remember the lambing when you were about seven?

He was eating with the focused efficiency of the genuinely hungry. He nodded.

The one where we had that lamb in the kitchen, she said.

He said, the one that slept on Dad's feet.

That's right. You two watched television with it all weekend.

He kept eating. After a moment he said, he let it sleep on him because he thought I'd be sad if it went in the box.

She hadn't known that. She looked at her son and thought, there it is, that's how it works, the small stories that surface after and change the shape of the person retroactively, the slow revelation of who someone was. She would be finding them for years. She would be finding them and passing them to Daniel and he would be finding his own, the ones she hadn't been there for.

She said, that sounds like him.

It does, doesn't it, Daniel said, and his voice was careful and factual, not performing anything, and she thought: he's going to be all right. She thought it without sentimentality, as a farmer's assessment, reading the animal for soundness.

A ewe called Broom went into labor at two and delivered without assistance, twins, both up quickly. A small ewe Margaret wasn't sure about turned out to be fine, labored steadily, produced a robust single who was nursing within twenty minutes. The wind began to ease around three, not stopping but losing its sustained fury, coming in gusts and silences instead of the constant grinding pressure of the morning. Snow still fell but vertically now, or nearly, and she opened the shed door once and looked out at the white yard and the fence posts and the vague shapes of the hill beyond, everything simplified to black and white and grey, all the minor visual information of the world deleted, and she found it, as she always found the snow, both frightening and deeply restful.

She thought: in the spring we'll fix the east fence where the posts are going. She thought: I need to call the bank on Monday. She thought: we'll need a new heater for next lambing, this one is getting on.

She thought: next lambing.

It was involuntary, a small declaration from some practical portion of herself that hadn't consulted her grief. Next lambing, already assumed. She held the thought for a moment. The yard white and quiet before her. The shed warm and animal-smelling at her back. She thought: all right, then. All right.

By five o'clock the storm had become ordinary snowfall and the yard had returned from white abstraction to specific white yard, the barn visible, the hill visible, the long stone wall that ran from the barn to the upper gate appearing first as a grey line and then as itself. Power was still out. She sent Daniel to the house for more propane and a proper meal—she listed what to make with the patience of someone managing not to express worry—and watched from the doorway as he walked across the yard in the falling dark, his silhouette confident in the snow, and came back twenty minutes later with a thermos of soup and the propane and his phone because the signal was back and he wanted to check in with his friend Joe, who he texted while eating. She ate the soup standing and watched the last two ewes. By six thirty both had lambed. She did her final count: twenty-one lambs, all living. Two on supplemental feeding, which she'd manage through the night. Everyone else eating, standing, pressing their new-laundered-looking faces against the pen slats.

She turned off one of the lanterns to save the battery. The shed went half golden, half shadow, and the ewes moved in the straw with their familiar bulk and patience.

She said to Daniel, you did well today.

He looked up from his phone and she could see him calibrating the compliment, deciding whether to deflect or accept it. He said, I didn't do that much.

She said, you did exactly as much as needed doing.

He seemed to find this acceptable. He went back to his phone. After a moment he said, Joe's mum wants to know if we need anything.

She said, that's kind. Tell her we're all right.

He texted it. She watched him. She thought about what she wanted to say and the distance between what she wanted to say and what the moment could hold. She thought: not yet. She thought: there'll be time.

What she said was, the east fence is going to need doing. I'd like you to help me with it come May.

He said, all right.

She said, and I think we should take on another twenty ewes for next year. If you're willing to do more of the feeding.

He looked up again. He was not fooled. He understood that she was asking him something that went beyond ewes and feeding and she could see him considering the size of it, considering it honestly, which was all she'd ever asked of him or of herself.

He said, yeah. Yeah, that sounds right.

She turned back to the nearest pen where Hawthorn's lamb was awake and investigating its mother with the single-minded intellectual seriousness that lambs brought to everything, pressing its nose against her flank as though trying to read something written there. Margaret watched it. The propane heater ticked. The snow fell in the small square of window above the water trough. She could hear Daniel behind her clicking through his phone and then a sound—something brief, something that might have been a voice, a video clip, the fragment of a show they'd both watched, and then him laughing quietly at whatever it was. She did not turn around. She let the sound of it go into the shed the way heat goes into cold air, mixing until you cannot find where one begins and the other ends.

She said good night to each of the ewes. She left one lantern burning on low and positioned the heater where it would do the most good. She made a note in the lambing book—the date, the conditions, the count, twenty-one, all well—and she wrote it in the space below Robert's handwriting from the previous year, different from her own but not entirely, the pen pressing in the same places, the same abbreviations, the same unspoken language of a farm recorded in fragments over decades.

Daniel held the door for her when they went out. The snow had stopped. The sky above the hills had cleared to the color of iron and the first star was hard and certain above the east ridge, and the yard lay between them and the house and it was nothing, fifty yards, a small crossing, they'd both made it a thousand times, and she walked it with her son beside her and did not say any of the things she was thinking, and did not need to, and the house was warm when they got inside because Daniel had thought to leave the Aga door open before he'd come back to the shed, and she noticed this, and was glad of it, and said nothing, and put the kettle on.
