He had not slept on a train or a boat in eleven years and so when the ferry's engines shuddered alive beneath him he lay on the narrow bunk and felt the vibration move through the mattress and into his spine the way certain memories do, arriving not in the mind but in the body first. The strait in October. He had bought a ticket at the window and the woman behind the glass had not looked at him, only slid the card through the slot, and he had understood this as a kind of mercy. Outside the harbor the water went grey-green and then simply grey and the last lights of the town were absorbed into mist.

His name was Calum. He was forty-three years old and had not seen his brother Donal in seven years and had not spoken to him in four. The difference between those numbers was a thing he carried the way you carry a stone in your shoe, aware of it only when you stop walking.

He left the bunk and went up to the deck. A young woman was sitting against the iron rail with her knees drawn up, smoking. She had an enormous canvas bag beside her and from it came, incongruously, the neck of a cello. The cold was serious. She looked at him with the calm assessment of someone who has learned to read strangers quickly and often, and he nodded and stood at the rail. The mainland had gone. Ahead there was only the dark water pleated by wind and the ferry's running lights making their small, red and green irrelevance against the immensity.

He thought of the phone call. A woman from the provincial coroner's office whose name he had already lost, speaking to him in the particular voice that institutions use when they are being careful — measured, not unkind, stripped of everything unnecessary. We have a deceased male. There are documents suggesting you might be a next of kin. He had written on the back of an envelope the address of the facility in Cartwright. He had written it slowly and carefully as if the act of writing might make it mean something other than what it meant. When he hung up he stood in the kitchen of his apartment in the city and looked at the objects on his counter — a coffee cup, a jar of mustard, a book splayed open and face down — and felt the gap between those ordinary objects and the call he had just received, the absolute failure of the world to acknowledge that something had shifted.

He had driven to the ferry terminal that evening through a storm that moved in off the Atlantic in the late afternoon, the sky going the colour of a bruise over the headlands, rain so heavy on the highway that he could see only the blurred suggestion of the car in front of him. He drove with both hands on the wheel and the radio off.

Now the woman with the cello was looking out at the water too. She had finished her cigarette and dropped it over the rail.

— Is it far, he asked. He meant the crossing.

— Six hours, she said. Maybe more if the weather holds like this.

She seemed to consider whether to say more. There was the quality of someone choosing words carefully, not from shyness but from the habit of precision.

— I come over every few months, she said. My teacher is in Goose Bay.

— You study there.

— He won't travel. She shrugged, not bitterly. He is very old and very good and those two things mean he does not have to go anywhere.

Calum looked at the bag, the cello's neck emerging from it with what seemed like a kind of patience. He tried to remember the last time he had spoken to anyone on a boat or a train, had allowed the temporary intimacy that travel permits, those conversations that exist in their own parenthesis and leave no residue. He had become a man who made himself unavailable for such things. He was not certain when this had happened.

— You have family up there, she said. It was not quite a question.

He paused. The water below them was black and moved in long swells that lifted the ferry with a slowness that was almost tender.

— I'm not sure, he said.

She looked at him then. He expected her to pursue it but she did not. She pulled the strap of the canvas bag closer and said goodnight and went below. He stood at the rail for another hour in the cold.

Donal had been the kind of person who moved through the world at an angle to it. Calum had spent thirty years watching him step sideways from one life into another, leaving behind each time some accumulation of people and objects and obligations, unmoored from all of it with what had seemed, when they were young, like freedom. Their father had used a different word. He had stood in the kitchen of the house they'd grown up in and used the word selfish and Calum had watched Donal absorb this without expression, and had wondered even then, at seventeen, whether his brother simply did not feel such words or whether he felt them completely and had learned to show nothing. He had never determined which.

After their father died Donal had appeared at the door of the house in the way a tide brings things back, briefly and without explanation. They had gone through the boxes in the spare room together. Most of it was their father's fishing equipment, hooks still wrapped in wax paper, a rod he'd bought and never used, catalogues from suppliers going back fifteen years. There was a photograph of the four of them, their parents and the two boys, standing in front of a boat at the wharf. In the photograph Donal was looking not at the camera but slightly past it, towards something outside the frame, and Calum had held the photograph and felt the old impatience rise in him. Always past the frame. Always toward whatever was not there. Donal had taken a coffee cup from the cupboard and stood at the sink drinking and looking out the window at the harbour while Calum went through the boxes alone, and neither of them had said anything about this division of labour.

The last time they'd spoken Donal had called from somewhere in the Labrador interior, a mining camp or a surveying job, Calum couldn't remember and had not wanted to ask because asking would have meant listening to the answer which would have been elaborate and interesting in the way Donal's answers always were, full of the names of places and people, geographies traversed, all of it offered with a kind of enthusiasm that Calum had long since stopped being able to receive without anger. He had been brief on the phone. He had said I have to go and Donal had said yes okay well and there had been the silence of two people who have run out of the language that would let them say what they actually mean.

He went below and lay on the bunk and listened to the engines. The motion of the ferry in the open water was a long rolling heave, rhythmic, almost solemn. He thought about identification. The word in its bureaucratic sense. What it required. He had seen photographs of Donal in his mind all evening as he drove and as he waited at the terminal, cycling through them in no particular order — Donal at eight in a red coat, Donal at twenty sitting on a truck's running board somewhere out west where he'd gone for a season, Donal at their mother's funeral standing with his hands in his coat pockets, his face carrying its particular combination of grief and absence, as if grief itself was not sufficient to make him fully present.

He slept without meaning to and woke when the engines changed their tone. There was grey light around the edges of the porthole. He lay still for a moment listening to the sounds of the vessel, the groaning of the hull, and then he stood and put on his jacket and went up to the deck.

The land appeared out of the dark water slowly, as land does from a boat, asserting itself incrementally — a darkness that was darker than the water, then the suggestion of relief, hills, then the particular darkness of hills covered in boreal forest, spruce and fir, the trees so dense and uniform they seemed from this distance like a texture rather than a collection of individual things. The sky was the colour of pewter and the air smelled of cold water and fish and the particular bleached smell of northern mornings.

The woman with the cello was beside him again. She must have been there already, watching the approach.

— There it is, she said.

— Yes.

She was looking at the land with an expression he recognized — not happiness exactly but a kind of returning, the face you make at something that requires you. He thought of what she had said about her teacher. He is very old and very good and those two things mean he does not have to go anywhere.

— What do you study, he asked. With him. What specifically.

She thought about this. — He makes me play the same passage until the technique disappears. Until you can't hear the effort. He says technique that can be heard is a failure of the imagination.

— And can you do it.

— Sometimes. She paused. Less often than I need to.

The ferry was slowing. The lights of Cartwright were coming into definition — a few clusters along the waterfront, a dock, the shapes of buildings. Behind them the dark hills. It was not a large town. Calum had never been here and had not thought about it before last night and now it was the only place in the world.

He went below and got his bag. There was a heaviness in his body that was not simply the sleepless night. He recognized it as an old companion. It was the heaviness of being summoned toward something you cannot prepare for because preparation requires imagining and imagining requires a kind of permission you have not given yourself.

The coroner's facility was not on the waterfront. He had the address and a name, a Dr. Arsenault, and he walked from the dock through streets that were beginning their morning — a man hosing down the walk in front of a store, a truck moving slowly with the amber patience of early purpose, a dog crossing an intersection with the particular authority of dogs in small towns. He walked and the cold moved through his jacket and he did not try to warm himself against it. He thought about Donal's hands, how they had been large and rough from whatever work he'd been doing and how in the photograph from the wharf he had one of them raised, shielding his eyes from the sun, not looking at the camera, looking out over the water.

Dr. Arsenault was a woman in her fifties with short grey hair and glasses on a chain around her neck. She shook his hand and did not make the careful institutional voice. She spoke plainly.

— You've come a long way.

— I came on the overnight ferry.

— Can I give you coffee.

He said yes and she poured it and they sat in her office, which was small and had on its walls a topographic map of the region and a child's drawing of a house. She held her coffee in both hands.

— Before we go in, she said, I want to tell you a few things. He was found four days ago on a logging road twenty kilometers from here. There was a vehicle, a pickup, registered to him. He had been there, we think, for about a week. There is significant — she paused, choosing — the identification will not be simple visually. There were documents. A provincial health card, a letter in the jacket. The letter has your name on it.

Calum set down his cup.

— You didn't tell me that on the phone.

— I know. I thought it was better to tell you here.

He looked at the child's drawing. A house with a door and four windows and a sun in the corner, the sun with lines radiating from it like a child's idea of warmth.

— Whose is that, he asked.

— My daughter's. She paused. She's twelve now and she finds it embarrassing that I still have it up. But I like to look at it. She looked at it herself for a moment. It reminds me that a house needs a door.

He didn't know what she meant and didn't ask. He finished the coffee.

They walked down a corridor that smelled of cold and antiseptic and something beneath those smells, something ancient and specific. She gave him nothing to hold, no clipboard, no forms. That would come later. She pushed open a door and they entered a room with light that was even and white and he felt it on his face like a held breath.

There was a table. Beneath a sheet the shape of a person.

He stood a few feet back. He was aware of his own hands at his sides and of his breathing and of the sound the ventilation system made, a low continuous tone that was below the threshold of hearing but registered in the body, in the sternum.

— Are you ready, she said.

He did not answer. He thought of Donal at eight in the red coat. He thought of Donal sitting on the truck's running board with his face tipped up toward whatever sun was there that day. He thought of the photograph and the raised hand and the gaze that went past the frame of things toward whatever lay beyond the edge of what could be recorded.

He thought about what the musician had said on the deck in the early morning. Technique that can be heard is a failure of the imagination. He did not know why this came to him now except that he was standing in a room with grief and grief also required something beyond technique, required some ability to sustain the passage until the effort disappeared, until what remained was only the thing itself and not the apparatus of enduring it.

— I'm ready, he said.

She drew back the sheet from the face.

He looked for a long time. The room held its white light and the ventilation made its low constant presence. Outside somewhere in the town a vehicle was backing up, the alarm of it muffled and intermittent. The world going about its morning with its customary indifference to everything that occurred within rooms like this one.

He knew the face and he did not know it. He knew its structure — the broad forehead and the set of the jaw, the way the nose had been broken once and healed not quite straight. But knowing a structure and knowing a face are different things. A face requires animation, requires the particular way a person moves within their own features, the way their thought surfaces and disappears and surfaces again. Without that movement he was looking at a topography. He was looking at the map on the office wall.

— It's him, Calum said.

He said it and then stood with the truth of having said it. Dr. Arsenault replaced the sheet carefully, with a deliberateness that he recognized as its own kind of practice, its own learned skill, and he was grateful for the care of it.

She brought him the letter in the anteroom. His name on the envelope in Donal's handwriting, which he recognized immediately, the tall looping cursive their mother had somehow given him, the same writing on birthday cards sent sporadically through the years, on the back of a postcard from Inuvik, from Churchill, from a town on the British Columbia coast whose name Calum could no longer remember. His name. Just that.

He did not open it there. He put it in the inside pocket of his jacket and he could feel it against his sternum as he walked back through the corridor and out into the morning.

The light was stronger now. The harbour lay below the street and the water was the colour of tin and a few boats were moving. He stood and breathed the cold air and looked at the water and the dark hills beyond it. The hills went back and back into the interior, ridge after ridge, spruce and fir, the labrador country, enormous and indifferent and ancient beyond any human claim on it. Donal had gone into that country and had not come out of it in the way that he had gone in, which was alive and with his face looking forward, looking always at whatever lay beyond the next thing.

He thought of the ferry and the crossing and the woman with the cello going to her old teacher who would not travel. He thought of her playing the same passage until the technique disappeared. He thought of what it would mean to do such a thing, to empty a gesture of everything but itself.

The letter in his pocket. The weight of it specific and separate from everything else he carried.

He walked toward the harbour and when he reached the dock he sat on a bollard and looked at the water. The light changed as it does on northern mornings, slowly and with authority. He would sit here for a while. Then he would find somewhere to have breakfast. Then he would open the letter and read what his brother had written for him, words composed in some solitude out there in the interior, in whatever cabin or camp or truck cab, composed in the particular kind of quiet that comes before something, and he would read them here in the quiet that comes after.

The boats moved across the tin-coloured water. Somewhere in the town behind him a school bell rang. The hills held their dark patience on the far side of the strait and the water between them was the water his brother had not crossed and he had, and this was the only difference between them now, that and the letter, that and whatever the letter held — its words already written, already finished, waiting in his pocket with the stillness of all completed things.
