The ferry left at eleven, and by midnight the mainland had dissolved completely into a darkness that seemed less like distance than erasure. Callum stood at the stern railing and watched the wake unfurl beneath him, that white perpetual road going nowhere, and he tried to think about something other than what waited at the end of this crossing. He had been on this boat perhaps a dozen times as a child, though the crossings ran together now into a single composite memory: his mother's hand in his, the smell of engine oil and institutional coffee, his brother Euan pressing his face flat against the glass of the observation deck until their father told him to stop. The ferry had been refitted since those years. The carpets were newer and the cafeteria had been reorganized so that the serving counter ran along the wrong wall, and this small wrongness bothered Callum more than he could account for, as if the ship had betrayed something it had no obligation to preserve.

He went inside when the wind became too insistent. The lounge was sparse at this hour—a young woman sleeping across two seats with her shoes tucked beneath her, a man in a high-visibility vest reading a newspaper that looked several days old. A teenage boy sat alone at a table near the window playing a game on his phone, the screen casting a pale wash of light across his face, and for a moment—just a fraction of a second, the kind of misperception the exhausted mind permits itself—Callum thought the boy was Euan. Not Euan now, Euan at fourteen or fifteen, at that particular age of sullen remove. The resemblance, once he looked properly, was nonexistent. He bought a coffee from the machine and sat down.

The phone call had come at seven in the morning, four days ago. A woman from something called the Island Liaison Office, her voice professionally gentle in the way of people who make these calls regularly. A body had been found on the foreshore of the northern settlement, she said—she used that word, foreshore, as if choosing the most neutral possible vocabulary for what had happened. The body had been in the water. There were no identification documents. They had found, in the lining of a waterproof jacket, a business card. The business card was Callum's.

He had pressed the phone against his ear and looked out his kitchen window at the garden, at the apple tree he kept meaning to cut back. He said, is it my brother? And the woman said she wasn't able to confirm, but that they had reason to believe the deceased had connections to the mainland, and given the card—and Callum said, yes, all right, I understand. He had stood there for a long time after she'd rung off, still looking at the apple tree, and then he'd called the surgery to cancel his afternoon appointments and gone upstairs to pack a bag.

His wife had offered to come. He'd said no without thinking, too quickly, and she had received that quickness with a look he recognized—the compressed understanding she had developed over years of his reticences. She had stood in the kitchen doorway while he put his jacket on and said, Callum, you don't have to do this alone, and he had said, I know that, which was not the same as accepting the offer, and they both understood the distinction. She had kissed him on the cheek and he had driven to the terminal with the radio on, not listening to it.

The business card was a puzzle he had been turning over for four days. He hadn't spoken to Euan in three years. The last time had been a phone call, brief and formal as a diplomatic communiqué, in which Euan had asked to borrow money. Callum had said he would have to think about it. He had not, in fact, thought about it—or rather he had thought about it constantly, had thought of little else for several weeks, and the thinking had led him in circles that always returned to the same terminus: their father's house, the smell of that kitchen, the particular configuration of damage that childhood had arranged in both of them. He had not called Euan back. He had told himself he was waiting until he knew what to say.

Three years. And yet Euan had been carrying his card.

There were possibilities, of course. Euan had probably possessed that card for years, since Callum had joined the practice, when he'd handed them out to family with the automatic hopefulness of the newly established. Or someone else had found the card somewhere, had picked it up from a table or a street, had slipped it for whatever reason into a jacket lining. The body might not be Euan's at all. The woman on the phone had been careful not to say that it was. There had been something in her carefulness that he'd been unable to read—whether it was the professional neutrality of someone withholding confirmation, or the professional neutrality of someone genuinely uncertain, he couldn't tell, and the ambiguity had accompanied him for four days like weather.

He drank the coffee, which was bad in the reliable way of ferry coffee, and watched the boy with the phone. The boy's thumbs moved with the quick assurance of someone operating in a medium native to them. Callum thought of the last time he and Euan had been on this ferry together, which would have been thirty years ago, maybe more. They'd been going to visit their aunt on the island, a woman who smelled of lavender and kept cats that Euan was allergic to, and Euan had spent the crossing sneezing into a succession of tissues that their mother produced from her bag with the inexhaustible conjuring of mothers. He had been perhaps eight, Euan six. They'd stood together at the railing—at the bow, not the stern, facing toward the island rather than away from it—and their father had crouched behind them and pointed out a light on the horizon that he said was the lighthouse, though Callum had always wondered since whether this was true or whether their father had simply known it would satisfy them to be given something to look at in all that darkness.

Their father had died nine years ago. The funeral had been the last time Callum had seen Euan in person, though that was not quite accurate—he had seen him, and they had spoken in the mechanical way of people sharing a grief neither can afford to examine, but the version of Euan he'd sat with in the funeral parlor had felt like a rough approximation, a sketch. By then there had already been the years of absence, the dwindling contact, the long intervals between calls, during each of which Callum had noticed with relief and shame that his life had continued perfectly adequately. He was a man with a practice and a wife and a house that needed work and an apple tree he kept meaning to cut back, and he had managed, with some effort, to misplace a brother.

The ferry moved through the strait with a slow implacable authority. He could feel its weight beneath him—all that mass in motion, indifferent to his presence on it. He had sometimes thought that he and Euan had been failed by the metaphors available to them: the language of blood, of family, of duty and return, all the inherited vocabularies that were supposed to organize a relationship but which had in practice only added a moral charge to what was ultimately a simple problem of two people who caused each other pain. He did not know when it had become a problem. Childhood had seemed ordinary enough from the inside, the usual frictions, the usual adjustments. It was only later that the damages had become legible, the way a figure emerges from a painting when you move to the correct distance.

Euan had been the one who moved away first. At twenty-two he'd gone north, nominally for work, in practice running the kind of determined flight that was too purposeful to call drifting but too unplanned to call ambition. He'd worked boats for a while, then something in the fishing industry, then nothing in particular. Their mother had tracked him through intermittent calls that grew further apart as her memory declined, and Callum had been the one who listened to her worry, who absorbed it and transformed it through the alchemy of reassurance into something manageable, and in doing so had become, he supposed, the good son—a role he had not sought but had accepted, because the alternative was to be the other kind, and that kind had already been taken.

He finished the coffee and threw away the cup. The woman was still sleeping. The man with the newspaper had folded it away and was looking out at the dark water with the expression of someone thinking about something he doesn't particularly want to think about. The boy was still playing his game. Callum went back to the observation deck and sat in one of the fixed seats that faced the bow, and he watched the darkness ahead of them for a light that might or might not be there.

He had tried, over the years, to construct an honest account of his own culpability. This was the physician in him, the habit of rigorous assessment. He had not been unkind to Euan, exactly. He had been worse than unkind: he had been absent in the way of someone who is technically present, had offered the words appropriate to a brother while withholding whatever it was that made those words mean something. When Euan called in trouble—and Euan called in trouble with a regularity that Callum had learned to brace himself against—he had listened and suggested and occasionally sent money and always felt, beneath his concern, a hard kernel of resentment that he'd never been able to fully dissolve, because its origins were too old and too entangled with things neither of them could name. He had loved Euan in the way you love something that has cost you a great deal. Which is to say, imperfectly. Which is to say, with complications.

Now possibly Euan was dead. Or possibly someone else was dead and Callum was on a ferry in the middle of a northern strait at two in the morning for reasons that would turn out to have nothing to do with him. He found, when he tried to assess his own feelings, that they were not what he had expected. He had expected grief, or something in the neighborhood of grief. What he found instead was something quieter and more difficult, less an emotion than a quality of attention—the way he felt before a diagnosis, before the results resolved themselves into a picture with a name. He was waiting. He had been waiting for a long time, he thought, for something about Euan to resolve.

The boat shifted slightly beneath him, adjusting to some feature of the current or the tide, and the lights of the lounge caught the window at a new angle and turned it briefly into a mirror. He saw himself sitting there: a man in his late forties, winter jacket, grey beginning in his hair. He looked like someone waiting for a train, he thought. Someone who had accepted that the train would be late but had not yet accepted how late. He looked away.

He thought of the foreshore. He had looked up the settlement on a map—small, northern, the sort of place that registers on maps primarily as evidence that the cartographer was being thorough. A harbor. A handful of roads. He had tried to imagine how Euan had come to be in such a place, or how anyone had, the particular sequence of choices and accidents that deposits a person on a foreshore at the northernmost edge of things. But this was pointless speculation, the mind filling space it had no business filling. He did not know that it was Euan. He would not know until morning.

A steward moved through the lounge collecting cups. The teenage boy put away his phone and curled sideways in his seat with his jacket over him, and was asleep within minutes in the way of the young, dropping into unconsciousness like a stone into still water. Callum envied this. He had not slept properly in four days, or rather he had slept in the fitful, unreliable way of a man whose thoughts reconvene the moment his attention lapses. He would close his eyes and there would be a brief interval of blankness, and then he would find himself already thinking, as if his mind had simply paused to let his body catch up.

He put his head back against the seat and closed his eyes and tried to go somewhere specific and organized, somewhere interior where he could manage what he found. He thought about the practice. He thought about Mrs. Alderson's blood pressure and the follow-up he'd scheduled, and the young man with the persistent cough he was fairly certain was anxiety but couldn't yet say so, and the hundred small specific concerns that accumulated daily into the thing he called his work. These were useful thoughts—bounded, actionable, pointing toward something he could do. He thought about the apple tree and how he would cut it back in spring, how he would take the afternoon and do it properly this time. He thought about his wife reading in bed, her particular concentrated stillness when she was absorbed in something, the lamp on her side making its small warm jurisdication. He held these things in mind and felt them slowly settling him, the way sediment settles in water when the disturbance stops.

He woke to the changed note of the engines. They were slowing. The quality of darkness outside the window had shifted, not lighter yet but somehow less absolute, and on the horizon there was the faint phosphorescence of a settlement, a small gathering of lights against the black hills that could have been any human habitation but was not. He straightened in the seat and pressed his hands against his thighs and sat for a moment in the precise attention of someone preparing.

Euan had once, years ago, described to Callum a morning he had spent on a boat coming in from the open sea toward a harbor, and he had said there was a feeling as the land appeared of such sheer gratitude that it was almost unbearable—gratitude not for anything specific, not for being safe exactly, but for the mere fact of continuation, for the ordinary ongoing of things. He had been trying to describe a mystical experience, Callum thought, or something adjacent to one, and Callum had listened and said, yes, I can imagine that, and the conversation had moved on. He had not thought about this in years. He thought about it now, watching the lights approaching from the sea.

The terminal was small and fluorescent, and the woman from the Liaison Office was waiting in the car park. She was younger than he had imagined from her voice, mid-thirties, with the particular composure of someone who has learned to be calm in the presence of other people's worst moments. She said his name and shook his hand and led him to a car, and during the drive through the island's dark interior—the roads narrow and glistening from recent rain, the hills visible only as a slightly darker texture against the sky—she explained the procedure simply and quietly, and he listened and said yes at the appropriate points.

The mortuary was in the hospital, which was small and which at four in the morning felt abandoned in its own particular way, the night-staff having reduced the building to its essential functions. There was a smell of antiseptic and something beneath it that he recognized and did not think about. He had been in mortuaries before, in his training and since; he understood them as working spaces rather than as the solemn theatres they became in imagination. The attendant was a heavy-set man of sixty with a gentle manner. He spoke quietly, and asked Callum if he was ready, and Callum said yes.

The drawer opened with a mechanical ease that still, even after everything, seemed wrong—too smooth, too routine.

He looked at the face for a long time.

He had prepared himself for both outcomes, he had thought. He had spent four days carefully constructing the psychological infrastructure for a definitive answer—yes or no, confirmed or not, grief or relief, something to work with. He had not prepared for the third thing, which was this: the face was Euan's, and simultaneously it was a face from which Euan had entirely departed, a face that had been Euan's and was now something returned to some category that preceded naming, and what Callum felt standing there was not grief and not relief but something that had the texture of both and was neither, an emotion for which he did not have a word, which may have been the truest and most accurate response to the situation.

He said yes, and signed what needed to be signed, and thanked the attendant, who received his thanks with a small nod.

Outside, the sky was beginning its preliminary lightening, the darkness conceding by degrees. He sat on a low wall beside the hospital entrance and held his phone in his hands and thought about who to call. His wife's number was there, her name on the screen—just her name, which seemed suddenly both completely insufficient and exactly right. He pressed call and she answered on the third ring, not groggy, fully present, which meant she had not slept either.

He said: it was him.

She said his name, just his name, and said nothing more for a moment, and in that moment, in the silence she had the wisdom to let exist between them, he felt something in him release, some long-held muscular effort at the center of things.

He said: I don't know what to do next.

She said she would come. She said she would find the next ferry and come to him, and he said all right, and they stayed on the line for a while without speaking—not the silence of people who have run out of things to say but the other kind, the silence that has its own weight and purpose, that does its own work. He could hear her breathing. Outside the hospital the sky was lightening further, the hills resolving from darkness into their actual shapes, the ordinary colors of things beginning to reassert themselves against the night. He sat on the wall and looked at the hills and thought about Euan pressing his face flat against the glass, and about a harbor approached from the sea, and about all the years that had been what they had been, and the morning came on around him, indifferent and generous, the way mornings are.
