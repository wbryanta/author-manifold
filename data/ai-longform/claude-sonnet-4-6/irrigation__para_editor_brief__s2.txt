The pipe had cracked sometime in March, or so Owen guessed from the orange stain that had leached down through the soil and dried into a kind of rust fresco along the irrigation trench. He was kneeling over it now, pressing his thumb into the fracture as though taking its pulse, while his brother Dale stood at the edge of the trench with his arms crossed and his shadow falling over Owen's work.

"It goes further than that," Dale said. "Look at the staining. It goes another six, eight inches at least."

"I can see that."

"So you'll need a longer section."

"I know what I'll need, Dale."

Dale uncrossed his arms and then crossed them again. He was wearing a gray t-shirt that had gone nearly white at the shoulders from too many washings, and his work boots were unlaced at the top two eyelets because his ankles had started swelling in the heat. He was forty-three. Owen was forty. These were facts that felt different to each of them in ways neither had ever tried to articulate. Dale looked at the back of his brother's neck, at the dark line of sweat along the hairline, and then looked out at the orchard.

The trees ran in rows up the gentle slope to the east, maybe four hundred of them, Honeycrisp and Fuji and a section of old Gravensteins at the top of the hill that their father had planted in 1974 and that produced abundantly and beautifully for about fifteen years and had been, for the past two decades, producing less and less, as though slowly losing interest. The Honeycrisps along the south border had a fungal problem that had cost them eight thousand dollars to treat the previous summer and that was, apparently, coming back. Owen had shown Dale the lesions on the leaves three weeks ago when Dale drove up from Sacramento, and Dale had stood among the trees looking at the damage with an expression Owen recognized as the expression their father had worn whenever something broke and he was calculating whether it was worth the cost of fixing or whether the thing had already outlived its usefulness.

Owen cut the damaged section of pipe with a hacksaw, working carefully in the narrow trench. The soil down here was damp and cool and smelled of iron. He could feel the temperature differential on his forearms, cooler below, the sun a flat pressure on his back and shoulders. He'd been up since five. He'd had coffee and a heel of bread and driven the tractor out to the east line to check the emitters, finding four clogged and two completely missing, the sockets empty, probably coyotes pawing at them or just time. He had a method for working alone that had developed over the six years since their father died and their mother moved to her sister's place in Medford, a method that was essentially the conversion of anxiety into sequence. Do this, then this, then this. Don't look at the whole. Look at the three feet of ground in front of you.

"I've been talking to someone," Dale said.

Owen kept sawing. "I know you have."

"A broker. Not a developer. He works primarily with agricultural properties."

"I know what a land broker is."

"His name is Rutherford. He's got a good reputation."

The hacksaw completed its work and Owen lifted out the damaged section and set it on the ground beside him and looked at it. It was a clean break, or rather the break was cleaner than it had looked. The pipe had simply given way at a joint, the fitting gone brittle and cracked through on one side. He could see where water had been seeping in a fine spray for months, maybe longer. The soil around it had a different texture, almost sandy, the minerals leached away.

"How good of a reputation," Owen said.

"He sold the Pemberton property in Winters. The whole two hundred and forty acres. Sold it to a land trust, which was what the family wanted. They kept a right of return clause."

"The Pembertons didn't have debt."

"We don't have insurmountable debt."

Owen stood up and climbed out of the trench and looked at Dale. "Sixty thousand dollars is insurmountable to me."

"It's not sixty, it's fifty-three, and it's only insurmountable if the orchard can't service it, which—"

"Which it can't."

"Which it might not be able to in its current form." Dale said this carefully, the way he said things he had rehearsed. "That's different from can't."

"It's the same."

"It's not the same, Owen."

A ground squirrel moved at the far edge of the row and both brothers watched it without meaning to, that animal instinct to track motion, and then it was gone under the grass and they were looking at where it had been.

Owen picked up the new pipe section he'd cut that morning. He'd cut it six inches longer than the damaged section, enough for the couplings to seat properly on both ends. He'd learned to cut long from his father, who said you could always shorten but you could never add. His father had said this about pipe and about words and, once, strangely, about remorse, though Owen had been sixteen at the time and hadn't understood how remorse could be cut short.

"If we sell to a land trust," Dale said, following Owen back into the trench, "we could potentially negotiate a lease. There are structures where the seller retains operational control for—"

"Don't." Owen was fitting the coupling over the existing pipe, pressing it by hand to check the alignment. "Don't give me the brochure version."

"It's not a brochure. I've done actual research."

"I know you've done research. That's what you do. You research."

"What does that mean?"

"It means you're good at information. You're very good at it. You know everything about the Pemberton property and about Rutherford the broker and about lease-back arrangements. You know all of it from a distance of four hundred miles."

Dale was quiet. A scrub jay landed on a fence post twenty yards off and regarded them with its shrewd, commissarial eye, and then flew.

"That's not fair," Dale said finally.

"I'm not trying to be fair. I'm trying to fix this line."

"It's not fair because I was here. I was here for two years after Dad died. I ran this place alongside you."

"You ran the books alongside me."

"The books matter."

"I know the books matter. I'm the one who looks at them every month."

"Then you know," Dale said. His voice had changed, gone softer, which was more dangerous. "You know what they say."

Owen was applying primer to the pipe, the sharp chemical smell rising in the warm air. He worked precisely, coating the pipe and the inside of the coupling with even strokes.

"The Gravensteins up top," Dale said, "they produced eleven hundred pounds last year. In 1995 they produced nine thousand."

"The variety's in decline everywhere."

"I know that. But we're not just experiencing what everyone else is experiencing. We're below the regional average on yield per acre by almost twenty percent. The Honeycrisp disease is going to cost us again this year if we treat it and cost us the trees if we don't. The well pump is going to need replacing, you said so yourself in February."

"I said it might need replacing."

"The email said, and I'm quoting you, 'I think the pump is dying.'"

"A pump dying is different from a pump dead."

"Owen."

Owen applied the cement and held the coupling in place, counting in his head. He counted to thirty the way their father had taught him. You hold it for thirty even if it feels set at fifteen. What feels set isn't set.

"I know what the books say," Owen said, when he'd reached thirty. "I've always known what the books say. The books have said more or less the same thing for six years. A little worse each year. I know that."

"Then what's your plan?"

"My plan."

"Yes. Your plan. If not selling, then what? What's the plan for the Honeycrisp disease, for the pump, for the debt service? What's the plan for next year when you're another twenty thousand down and the year after when you're forty?"

Owen began work on the other end of the new section, the upstream coupling. His hands were steady. He had always had steady hands, which he considered not a personal virtue but simply a physical fact, the way his eyes were brown and his second toe was longer than his first.

"The plan is to get through this year," he said.

"That's not a plan. That's deferral."

"All farming is deferral."

"That's a beautiful sentiment and it will not satisfy a creditor."

"I'm not trying to satisfy a creditor right now. I'm trying to satisfy this irrigation line."

Dale sat down on the edge of the trench, his feet hanging in. His boots were good boots, Owen noticed, relatively new, the kind of boots you bought when you lived in Sacramento and occasionally drove up to walk around an orchard. His own boots had been resoled twice. This was not a resentment so much as an observation, a piece of data about the texture of their separate lives, which had been separating for twenty years and were now so different that when they stood in the same trench they were doing so from a great distance.

"I don't want to sell it either," Dale said.

"I know you don't."

"I want you to believe that."

"I do believe it."

"But wanting to keep it and being able to keep it are different things."

"Everything you're saying is true," Owen said. He was cementing the second coupling now, holding it. Counting. "Every number you have is right. The yield is down. The debt is real. The pump is probably dying. The fungus is coming back. All of that is true."

"So."

"So."

"So what do you want me to say?" Dale said. "You want me to say, yes, let's keep losing money until the bank takes it? Until there's no equity left to even negotiate with? At least if we sell now, if we find the right buyer—"

"It's June," Owen said.

"What?"

"It's June. The fruit's on the trees." He finished counting and released the coupling and straightened up and looked at his brother. "I'm not going to make a decision about selling in June, when the fruit's on the trees. I'm not able to do that."

Dale looked at him for a moment, and then looked at the trees. The sun was past its apex and falling westward, and the light was coming through the rows at an angle now, making corridors of brightness and shadow that ran the length of the slope. In this light the orchard looked the way orchards looked in paintings, or in memories, idealized by atmosphere. Dale knew this. He knew that he was susceptible to it, that the beauty of the place in certain lights was a kind of argument that had nothing to do with solvency.

"October," he said.

"Maybe October. After harvest."

"You said maybe October last October."

"I know I did."

"And the October before that you said after the new year."

"I know."

"So I'm wondering what October will be different."

Owen climbed out of the trench and began backfilling, using the flat of the shovel to push the soil in carefully around the new pipe, not compacting too hard, leaving some give. "I don't know what October will be different. Maybe nothing."

"That's honest."

"I'm trying to be."

"But you're still not going to call Rutherford."

"No."

"Even with everything you just admitted."

"Even with everything I just admitted." Owen worked the soil steadily. "When Dad was sick the last time, the real last time, not the false last time in 2012 but the actual last time in 2015, he asked me to come out here with him. It was October, actually. Mid-October. We walked up to the Gravensteins. He'd been on oxygen but he left the tank in the truck because he said he wasn't going to walk through his orchard dragging a tank. So he was moving slowly and stopping every few rows to catch his breath. And when we got up to the top he just stood there for a while and he put his hand on one of the trunks. Just his hand flat on the bark. He didn't say anything about it and I didn't ask."

Dale was quiet.

"He wasn't a sentimental man," Owen said. "You know that better than I do, probably. He was not a man who spoke about what he loved. But his hand was on that tree for a long time."

"I know."

"I'm not telling you that as an argument. I'm telling you because you asked what October would be different and I don't know what to tell you except I think about that a lot."

"You can think about it and still make a rational decision."

"I'm sure you can. I haven't managed it yet."

He finished the backfill and tamped it gently and then walked to the head of the line to turn the valve and check the repair. Dale walked with him. They moved through the rows without speaking, their feet finding the familiar path, the slight downward grade of the terrace that their grandfather had cut with a rented dozer in 1962, a thing that had seemed like an extravagance and had turned out to be the single best investment the property ever saw, those flat terraces holding the winter moisture and keeping the roots from drowning in a wet year.

Owen turned the valve slowly, the standard quarter turn, and they heard the line pressurize, the soft sound of water moving through the system. They walked back to the repair and crouched over it and watched. The soil above the new coupling was dry and stayed dry. No seep, no vapor, no stain beginning. Owen pushed his fingers into the soil around the fitting to feel for any moisture and found none.

"Good," Dale said.

"Yeah."

They stood up. Owen's knees ached in the straightening, a new development of the past year or so, not debilitating but present, a small announcement. He was forty. Their father had been sixty-eight when he died, which meant Owen had, potentially, twenty-eight more years here. He was aware that this calculation was both reasonable and delusional. He made it regularly.

They walked back toward the truck. The shadows were long now, the late afternoon committed, and the air had the quality of June evenings in the valley, dry heat giving way to something lighter and more forgiving, the grasses along the fence lines silver in the angled light. A dozen crows moved in loose formation over the top of the ridge and descended into the oaks beyond the property line, their calls carrying back.

"Rutherford thinks we could get fourteen hundred an acre," Dale said. "Maybe more. That's a hundred and twelve thousand for the eighty."

"I know."

"Clear the debt. Have money left over."

"I know the math."

"What would you do with it? With your half?"

"I don't want to talk about my half."

"But if you did."

"I don't know." Owen put the shovel in the truck bed and the repair kit and the hacksaw. He stood with his hands on the bed rail. "I honestly don't know. I've never thought about it in a way that felt real."

"You could buy something smaller. Something manageable. A few acres."

"It wouldn't be this."

"No," Dale said. "It wouldn't."

They got in the truck, Owen driving. They drove along the farm road that ran the lower border of the property, past the old equipment shed where their father's first tractor still sat, a 1969 Ford 3000 that hadn't run since 2008 but that no one had moved or sold, some variety of monument, though to what exactly they'd never agreed. Past the stand of eucalyptus their mother had planted as a windbreak in the eighties and that had grown into massive silver columns, the bark hanging in long strips, the smell of them pungent and medicinal.

At the house Owen stopped the truck but didn't get out. Dale didn't either. The house needed painting. It had needed it for two years. Owen looked at the peeling eaves and felt the particular quality of shame that attached to visible neglect, the way it was both evidence and accusation.

"I'll come back in August," Dale said. "Help with the fungus treatment."

"You don't have to."

"I want to."

"Okay."

"And we'll talk in October."

"We'll talk."

"Really talk. I'm not asking you to decide anything. But I want to actually have the conversation, with the broker's information in front of us, with actual numbers."

"Okay."

Dale nodded. He opened the door and got out and stood for a moment in the yard. He looked at the orchard, at the upper slope, at the Gravensteins up at the top where the hill crested and the trees were oldest and their crowns were wide and full in the way of trees that have been growing for fifty years. Their father's trees. Their grandfather's land.

"It's a beautiful place," Dale said.

"Yes," Owen said.

"That's the problem, isn't it."

"That's part of it."

Dale got his bag from the back seat and walked to his car, a silver Civic that looked startlingly ordinary in the farmyard, a car designed for parking structures and drive-throughs, and Owen sat in the truck and watched him go. At the car Dale turned and raised one hand, not a wave exactly, something less formal and more final, and Owen raised his in return.

He sat for a while after the car was gone. The evening birds were starting, the towhees in the hedgerow, a meadowlark somewhere farther off. He thought about the irrigation line, about the new coupling seated and holding, about the four clogged emitters he still needed to clear in the morning and the two missing ones he'd have to order. He thought about the Honeycrisp trees and the copper fungicide he'd priced out last week, trying to determine whether to treat the whole south block or only the visibly affected trees, whether the containment strategy was already too late. He thought about the pump in the well, the slight change in sound he'd noticed in April, a hesitation in the pressure that came and went.

He got out of the truck and walked to the edge of the yard where the orchard began, to the first row of trees. The Fujis here were heavy with fruit, the apples still green and hard, months from anything, but dense on the branches, a good set this year, better than last. He walked into the row and put his hand on the nearest tree. The bark was warm from the day, rough-textured, slightly damp where a sucker had been removed earlier in the spring. He stood with his hand there for a moment in the ordinary way of a man performing no gesture that anyone would need to interpret.

The meadowlark called again from somewhere to the south, out beyond the fence, one of the birds that nested in the field the Hendersons leased for hay, and Owen listened to it without moving. The sound carried in the cooling air with that particular June clarity, each note distinct, the phrasing familiar and strange in the same instant, the way certain things were, the way a language you were born to could still, at moments, arrive as though for the first time.

He would call his mother in the morning. He had been meaning to for two weeks. He would clear the emitters and order the replacements and call his mother and price the copper fungicide again, and at some point in the coming days he would drive up to the Gravensteins and check their fruit set and look at the crowns for any sign of the fire blight that had appeared in one tree at the northeast corner three years ago and that he had cut out and burned and watched for every season since, not yet returned.

The light was going fast now, the sky above the ridge moving through its changes, and the orchard was settling into the blue and shapeless hour between day and dark. Owen stayed a while longer in the row, his hand on the tree, the fruit all around him in the darkening green, the ground cool at his feet, his father's land, his grandfather's land, his.
