The switchboard had a smell to it, something between warm copper and the inside of a clock, and Maren had decided early in her career that this was the smell of other people's urgency. She had worked the evening shift at the Cincinnati exchange for eleven years, and in that time she had developed the peculiar telephonist's talent for near-invisibility, for existing in the seam between two strangers without disturbing the fabric of what passed between them. She plugged and unplugged, she said *please hold* and *go ahead, caller*, and she was gone from the line before they remembered she'd ever been there. This, her supervisor Mrs. Aultman had told her on her first day, was the whole art of it.

The calls from the man in Dayton had begun in January, a Wednesday evening somewhere after nine o'clock when the board had thinned and the other girls were trading sections of the evening paper. He'd asked for a Louisville number in a voice that was careful in the way that voices are careful when they expect to be told no. Not young, Maren thought, though she hadn't yet developed the instinct for aging voices that would come to her later. Not old either. Somewhere in the country between those two territories, where a person was still trying to figure out which direction he was walking.

She connected him, and then, as was procedure, she held for the pickup confirmation before releasing the line, and in those three seconds she heard the other voice answer. A woman. Unmistakably a woman, and unmistakably glad.

*Harold,* the woman said, and the word had the quality of something set down after being carried a long way.

Maren released the line.

She didn't think much of it that first time. She connected two hundred calls on a busy evening, and she had learned not to carry them home with her, not to wonder what became of the insurance claims and the birthday wishes and the arguments about money that passed through her board each night like birds through a particular piece of sky. But the following Wednesday the man called again, same time, same Louisville number, and his voice had the same quality of careful anticipation, and when the woman answered she said his name again in the same way, and Maren felt something catch in her, some small mechanism engaging.

By February she knew their voices well enough that she recognized them before she'd finished taking down the number. Harold's voice had a slight flatness to the vowels that she associated with men who'd grown up without money, men who'd learned to keep their words economical because waste of any kind had once been something they couldn't afford. The woman in Louisville—Maren thought of her simply as *the Louisville woman* for a long time, until she gave her the name Grace, which she did in the privacy of her own mind around the third week of March—Grace's voice was something else entirely. It was a voice that had been educated, Maren thought, but educated in a way that hadn't made it stiff. It moved easily, that voice. It had warm places in it.

What she learned about them came in pieces, the way you understand a landscape from a moving train: a glimpse of one thing, then another, and the mind assembles the picture in between. They were not married to each other. This she understood fairly quickly, not from anything said directly but from the shape of their conversations, the way each call began with a period of adjustment, both voices settling in, losing the tension of the week between them. Married people didn't do that. Married people picked up in the middle. These two were always, in some sense, arriving.

Harold worked for a company that made parts for something—agricultural equipment, Maren thought, though she was never certain. He had a supervisor named Briggs who was unreasonable, and there was some matter with a pension that caused him periodic distress. Grace seemed to teach something, or had taught, and she lived with a sister whose name Maren never caught, only the pronoun, *she said* and *she thinks* and *she wouldn't understand*, the sister an ambient presence, a weather system that affected Grace's life without ever quite arriving in the conversation.

They never said they loved each other, not in the year and some months that Maren listened to the beginnings of their calls. This was interesting to her. She had listened to people who said it constantly, people who deployed the phrase like punctuation, draining it of specific gravity. Harold and Grace didn't say it, and yet the whole architecture of each call was made of it, the way a house is made of lumber without being made of trees.

She knew it was wrong to listen. She knew this clearly, without guilt working at her about it, the way you can know that a particular food isn't good for you and still understand it as a fact of your life. She listened for ten, sometimes fifteen seconds before releasing the line, just long enough to confirm the connection had been properly made, or this was what she told herself. The other girls did it too, though no one said so. It was the occupational condition of the work, the way surgeons grew accustomed to opened bodies, the way librarians knew things about their patrons' inner lives that the patrons would have been startled to imagine.

What she permitted herself was the beginnings of things. The first thirty seconds, the first minute at most, and then she pulled the plug and they were gone, and she sat with whatever small piece of them she'd collected, and she assembled her version of their lives. She thought about this sometimes on the bus home, the strange double existence of these two people: the real Harold and Grace, who continued their conversation for however long they continued, who knew things about each other that no one else knew, and then the Harold and Grace inside Maren's imagination, who had become something richer and more constructed, who had the advantage of being considered by someone who found them genuinely interesting.

She lived alone on Vine Street in an apartment that had good light in the morning and almost none by afternoon. She had been engaged once, to a man named Paul Wertz, who had eventually married someone else and now lived in Columbus and sold insurance. She had been not-quite-engaged twice more, with men who were decent and unexciting and who had drifted toward other lives in a way that Maren recognized as probably her fault without being able to identify exactly what she'd done. She had a cat named Copernicus and a library book always on the nightstand and a sister in Indianapolis who called every Sunday and wanted to know if Maren was eating properly. She was thirty-seven years old in the autumn of 1968 and she was not unhappy in any way she could put her finger on.

October came and with it the particular desolation of early darkness, the city pulling in on itself, and the calls from Dayton came every Wednesday as reliably as anything in Maren's life. She had developed a small ritual around them. On Wednesday evenings she managed, through the informal negotiations of shift scheduling, to be at position three, which was where she'd first taken his call, and which she felt, superstitiously, gave her some proprietary connection to the line. She ate her dinner break a little early so she'd be back at the board by nine. She was, she understood, waiting for him.

The call in the second week of October was different from the first moment. She knew it before she could have said why—some quality in his silence when she answered, some alteration in the air pressure of him.

*Operator,* she said, and he said the Louisville number, and she said *one moment please* and began to connect him, and in the three or four seconds of connection she heard him breathing on his side of the line in a way that made her think of someone standing at the edge of something, deciding.

Grace answered and said his name and he said *hello, Evvie*, and Maren understood three things simultaneously: that Grace's name was not Grace, that she had given her the wrong name for seven months, and that something tonight was different enough that she did not release the line.

She should have. The moment for release passed and she held, her hand near the plug, telling herself she was confirming the connection, confirming the connection, and she listened.

*I've been sitting with this all week,* Harold said. *I've been trying to find a way to say it that isn't what it is.*

There was a pause from Louisville, from Evvie, from the woman whose voice Maren had been studying since January with the attention she had once given to things she loved.

*Then just say what it is,* Evvie said, and her voice was steady in the way that voices are steady when steadiness is costing something.

*My wife,* Harold said. And stopped. And then: *Eleanor is sick. It's in her liver and they've found it in her spine and the doctor says spring at the outside and probably sooner.*

The silence that followed was long enough that Maren's training said she should check the connection, say *go ahead, caller*, but she did not move.

*Oh Harold,* Evvie said.

*I know,* he said.

*Oh, you poor man.*

*I'm not the one who's sick.*

*I know you're not.* And then: *I know what you're going to say.*

*Yes,* he said. *I think you do.*

Maren pulled the plug. She sat for a moment with her hands in her lap, which was an odd posture at the board, hands in the lap meaning nothing to do, and the other girls were busy and no one looked at her, and she sat there for perhaps thirty seconds that felt architecturally significant, the way certain silences feel like rooms you have wandered into.

She thought about Eleanor. She had never thought about Eleanor before because Eleanor had not existed before, or had existed only as an absence, as the negative space around Harold's Wednesday evenings, the reason he called from Dayton instead of from whatever life he shared with his wife. Eleanor who was sick. Eleanor whose name she now knew without ever having heard her voice.

She thought about Evvie saying *I know what you're going to say*, the precision of it, the intimacy that made prediction possible. She thought about Harold saying *yes, I think you do*, the weight of the yes, the way the *I think* was tender rather than uncertain, the way it gave Evvie some small room.

The board lit up and she plugged in, and the night continued.

In the weeks that followed she listened more carefully to things she'd been hearing without fully attending to, the long-distance calls from husbands whose voices had the careful formality of men trying to say something true inside a wire, the calls from daughters to mothers, the calls from people who sounded frightened. The country was full of frightened voices that autumn. She had always known this, but she had not always felt it.

November came. Harold called the second Wednesday at nine-fifteen and she was at position three and she connected him and held the line for what she would have admitted, under examination, was nearly a minute.

They talked about practical things, or rather Harold talked about them and Evvie listened with the sort of attention that changes the quality of what's being said, that makes a man's accounting of his ordinary difficulties into something more bearable for the accounting. He had taken Eleanor to a specialist in Cleveland. There would be treatments that might not help but wouldn't hurt. The house needed weatherizing before winter and he'd been managing it on evenings and weekends. His son—there was a son, Maren noted, filing this—was coming for Thanksgiving from somewhere far away, Texas maybe, she thought she heard Texas.

*I keep thinking,* Harold said, *that I should feel more than I feel. Or differently than I feel.*

*What do you feel?* Evvie asked.

*Tired,* he said. *Organized. Like I'm managing a project.*

*That's not nothing,* Evvie said. *That's a form of loving someone.*

There was a pause.

*Is it?*

*Yes,* she said, and her certainty was a kindness. *It is.*

Maren released the line.

She walked home that night, though it was cold and she had the bus pass and there was no sensible reason to walk. She walked down Vine Street and the wind came along the street toward her with the smell of the river in it, that deep mineral coldness that had always been one of the smells she associated with Cincinnati, with being in a specific place rather than anywhere. She walked and thought about Harold managing his project, weatherizing the house, driving to Cleveland, coming home to Eleanor. She thought about what it cost him to also be the man who called Louisville on Wednesdays and said *hello, Evvie* in that voice. She thought that people contained more than they showed and that this was generally considered a problem but might not always be one.

She thought: I am thirty-seven years old and I am attached to two people I have never met and will never meet, and the shape of this is not entirely different from the shape of certain other things in my life.

She thought: that is perhaps not a charitable thing to think about oneself.

She thought: but perhaps it is accurate.

The December call was brief, his voice thinned and tired, and they didn't talk much before he said he had to go and she said of course and said something that Maren didn't catch before he said goodbye, and his goodbye was the goodbye of a man standing in a hallway between rooms. She released the line and sat with it.

January again. The anniversary of his first call, though he wouldn't know it was, wouldn't know someone was marking it. The January call lasted longer than the December one had, and there was something in both their voices that had shifted, some new country they'd arrived at, the conversation of people who've understood something between them without saying it. Eleanor had been moved, he said. To his sister's house in Kettering. It was easier, he said, and Evvie said *yes* and didn't say anything else, and the yes was enough.

*I think about you on Tuesdays,* he said. *All day on Tuesdays I think about calling.*

*What do you think about?* Evvie asked.

*I think about what I'll say. I think about how you'll sound.*

*And how do I sound?*

*Like yourself,* he said. *Which is what I need.*

Maren released the line. She pressed her fingers briefly against her eyes beneath her headset, a thing she had never done before at the board, and she returned to work.

February. His call came on a Wednesday and she put it through and held, and Evvie answered and said Harold, and he said he couldn't talk long, just wanted to hear her voice, and Evvie said then tell me something, tell me something small, tell me something from this week, and he laughed—Maren heard the laugh, a small surprised thing, like finding money in a coat pocket—and he said *I saw a fox. On the way home last Thursday. It was just standing in the road looking at my headlights.* And Evvie said *what did you do?* And he said *I waited. It eventually decided I wasn't interesting enough* and he laughed again, slightly, and Evvie laughed with him, and for that moment their laughter was in Maren's ears and she felt the specific grief of being the third person in a two-person moment, felt it precisely and without sentiment, and released the line.

She thought about the fox on the way home. The fox standing in the road deciding whether Harold was interesting enough. She thought it was the kind of detail that revealed a person, the kind of thing you noticed and held onto when the rest of your life was made of harder material.

March. Eleanor died on a Sunday, which Maren didn't know but inferred from the call that Wednesday, from the quality of silence that preceded his words, from the way he said *it's over* without explaining what *it* meant, because Evvie knew. There was crying, his, which Maren had not heard from him before, a sound like a man who has been holding something heavy for a long time finally permitted to set it down, which doesn't mean the relief is simple. There was Evvie saying his name in a low voice, saying it several times, his name as a kind of steadying, the way you put a hand on someone's shoulder in the dark.

Maren held the line for two minutes. Perhaps more. Long past any professional justification, held it and listened and was with them in the only way she was capable of being with them, which was this adjacent and invisible way, this being-in-the-seam. She released the line and she sat quietly and she thought that some portion of what she felt was grief, real grief, for a woman whose voice she had never heard, and that this was strange and possibly excessive and was also simply what she felt.

The April call was different in register—still tender, still careful, but with some future in it now, some cautious looking-forward that they were both trying and both slightly afraid of. She could hear the fear in the way they talked around certain things, the way the conversation would approach something and then ease sideways. She held the line a little, released it.

The Wednesday of the third week of April she was at position three and nine o'clock came and nine-fifteen came and nine-thirty came and the board lit up with other calls and she took them, one after another, voices asking for numbers, voices with their urgencies and their practical needs, and she plugged and connected and released. The clock above the board moved in its usual indifferent manner and she watched it in the way you watch something when you're pretending to do something else, and by ten o'clock she understood that Harold was not going to call.

She thought about this on the bus home. She held her purse in her lap and looked out at the wet black streets and she thought about all the reasons a person might not call on a given Wednesday. An illness, her own or another's. A late meeting, a family obligation, car trouble, any of the ordinary interruptions that aggregate into a life. She thought: he simply didn't call this week. She thought: there will be another Wednesday.

She thought about Evvie in Louisville waiting.

This was the part that stayed with her, accompanied her up the stairs to her apartment, sat with her while Copernicus arranged himself on the chair opposite and regarded her with his particular yellow-eyed judgment. Evvie in Louisville, who would have been waiting in the same way Maren had been waiting, who would have sat by the telephone—she imagined a particular kind of telephone, black, on a small table in a particular kind of hallway—and waited for it to ring in the way you wait for something you've been given no reason to doubt will come, and it had not come, and she would not know why.

Maren thought about what it was to wait for a voice and not receive it. She thought she knew something about this. She sat in her apartment on Vine Street with the light from the lamp making the room smaller and warmer than it was by day, and she thought about how all she had ever had of these two people was the first moments of their voices, the threshold of what they were to each other, and how she had built from those thresholds something she had not expected to value as much as she apparently did.

She was not, she thought, in love with Harold. She was not in love with Evvie. What she felt was something without a precise name, some form of attention that had become over fourteen months a form of care, and what she recognized now, with the strange calm of recognizing something true, was that she had let herself be altered by people who would never know she existed. This seemed to her, in the lamp-lit quiet of her apartment, both foolish and entirely reasonable, both a small and a significant thing. She had spent eleven years perfecting her near-invisibility, her presence in the seam, and somewhere in the weekly accumulation of Harold's careful voice and Evvie's warm one, she had failed at this professionally necessary vanishing and become, privately and without consequence, a person who was present.

Next Wednesday, she thought. He'll call next Wednesday.

Copernicus dropped from the chair and padded across the floor and butted his head against her ankle, which was his manner of expressing either affection or interest in his dinner, and she reached down and put her hand on his warm small skull and she sat there in the ordinary apartment with the ordinary darkness outside the window and she waited, as people do, for the following week to arrive.
