Anil crouched down at the edge of the grave and opened her field notebook. The two men lay side by side, their bones darkened by soil and time, and she began the slow work of recording what she found — the orientation of the bodies, the condition of the remains, the position of hands and feet. She noted the absence of personal effects. No coins, no belt buckle, no religious medal. They had been stripped before burial or before death, and this itself was information, this itself told a story she would write down in the language of evidence.

She was thirty-three years old and had been doing this work for six years, first in England, then the training period in the United States, and then the assignments that took her to places whose names she had not known as a child in Colombo. She had worked in Guatemala for eight months. She would be in Sri Lanka for three months, perhaps four. She had a green card, a British education, a Sri Lankan passport that was, as her colleague Leaf had once observed, the most heavily stamped document he had ever seen. She had been gone from the island for fifteen years.

She pressed the tip of her pencil against the page and did not write anything for a moment.

The woman had walked back to the shade of a mango tree at the edge of the field and stood there watching. Anil could feel the watching the way you feel weather, the way you feel a change in the light. She made herself concentrate on the bones, on the work. This was the discipline the work demanded and that she believed in: that the body itself was the record, that if you were careful and patient and resistant to the stories you were already inclined to believe, the body would tell you what had happened to it. The body did not lie in the way that witnesses lied, in the way that governments lied, in the way that she herself had sometimes lied, in small ways, to protect herself or to protect others. The skeleton was pure information.

She was not sure she still believed this. She had believed it in England, learning the principles of forensic anthropology in a cold building at the edge of a university campus, identifying pathological conditions in bones pulled from a teaching collection. She had believed it more or less in the American Southwest, where she'd done her field training on pre-Columbian remains and where the political conditions, though not uncomplicated, were at least historical, at least resolved. She had believed it somewhat less in Guatemala, where the political conditions were neither historical nor resolved, and where the families waiting at the edge of the sites had faces that complicated the clean epistemology of forensic science, that made the distance between evidence and truth feel very long.

Now she was here.

Leaf came and knelt beside her. He was a skeletal biologist from London who had worked in seven countries and who had a gift for remaining cheerful in circumstances that Anil found oppressive. He had thin reddish hair and long arms and a habit of humming very quietly while he worked, not any identifiable song, just a low continuous tone that he seemed unaware of. She had worked with him in Guatemala for two months and had come to trust him in the way that you trusted certain people in the field — not with everything, but with the things that mattered for the work.

He looked at the two skeletons and said nothing for a moment.

There's something wrong with this one, he said finally, gesturing toward the skeleton on the left.

She looked at it again. She had not been able to identify what was bothering her about the configuration of the remains, but something had been troubling her since they had pulled back the sheeting that morning.

The legs, she said.

Yes.

The legs had been positioned after death, she was fairly certain. The femurs were together, the bones of the lower legs crossed at the ankle. It was an arrangement that suggested deliberateness, that suggested someone had taken care.

They photographed everything before touching it. Leaf set up the camera and Anil held the scale bar and they worked in silence, moving methodically around the grave. The light was coming in low from the east and threw long shadows across the site, which made the photography difficult but which Anil preferred to the flat obliterating light of midday. In this light the contours of the bones were visible, the small depressions and irregularities of the surface that contained information about cause of death, about age, about the events that had attended the body before and after burial.

The other members of the team arrived by seven. There were four of them, plus a young Sri Lankan man named Gunesena who had been assigned to them by the organization that had brought them here — the Human Rights Commission, which was nominally independent but which operated within constraints that Anil was still trying to understand. Gunesena was polite and apparently well-intentioned and spoke excellent English and said very little. He took notes. He never participated in the actual excavation. Anil was not sure whether he was present to assist them or to observe them, and she suspected the answer was both.

By nine o'clock the heat was serious. One of the team members, a young American woman named Diane who had come directly from graduate school and who was on her first overseas assignment, kept pausing in her work to wipe her face with the hem of her shirt. Anil remembered doing that in Guatemala in her first weeks. You stopped doing it eventually, not because you stopped sweating but because the gesture stopped providing any relief and you found ways of working within the discomfort rather than repeatedly acknowledging it.

The woman under the mango tree had been joined by two other women. They were not speaking to each other. They sat or stood in attitudes of waiting that were specific and that Anil recognized from other sites in other countries — a particular stillness that was not peace, that was the opposite of peace, that was the stillness of people who had been waiting for a very long time and had organized their bodies around the waiting. She thought of the woman who had sat within the grave that morning. She thought of the shoulder.

She found herself thinking about the work she had done in England, the paper she had written on perimortem trauma in skeletal remains, the clean careful language of that paper, its apparatus of citations and methodological caveats. She had been proud of it. Her supervisor had been proud of it. It had been published in a journal that was read by several hundred people in the world, most of whom were specialists in forensic anthropology or physical anthropology or related fields. She had been twenty-six years old and she had written the paper and she had gone to a conference in Edinburgh and presented it, and someone in the audience had asked her a question about her statistical methods, and she had answered it, and afterwards several people had come up to speak with her and she had felt, standing in that conference room with its green carpet and its institutional lighting, that she was doing something that mattered.

She thought about this now, kneeling in the red dirt of this field, and she felt something that was not quite shame and not quite anger, but that was in the vicinity of both.

By noon they had fully exposed both skeletons and had taken all the photographs and measurements that could be taken in situ. They had recovered a small number of artifacts — buttons, a fragment of woven cloth that had survived in the anaerobic soil near the pelvic region of one of the bodies, what appeared to be a sandal strap. These were bagged and labeled. Gunesena photographed each bag before it went into the evidence case. He did this with great precision and said nothing.

They ate lunch under a temporary shade structure that the team had erected on the first day of the excavation. It was made from poles and a tarpaulin and it provided imperfect shelter but it was better than nothing. Diane had brought mangoes and distributed them. The juice ran down Anil's wrists and she let it, too tired to be careful.

She was thinking about why she had come back.

It was not a simple question and she had not given herself a simple answer when she had accepted the assignment. There were official reasons: she had expertise in skeletal trauma analysis that was specifically relevant to this investigation, she spoke Sinhala, she knew the country. These were the reasons she had given to people who asked. They were true. They were not the whole truth.

The whole truth was harder to locate. She had left Sri Lanka at eighteen, had gone to England on a scholarship, had not come back except for two brief visits in fifteen years, had built a life and a career in places that were not Sri Lanka. She had colleagues who asked her occasionally whether she missed it, and she gave answers that were sufficiently personal to seem honest and sufficiently vague to foreclose further inquiry. The truth was that she had not known how to miss it, had not known what missing it would mean, had not allowed herself the examination. And then she had been offered this assignment, and she had said yes, and now she was here, and the question of why she had come back was present with her every morning when she woke in the small room she had taken in Colombo, and she had still not found the answer.

She noticed that the women under the mango tree were eating as well. Someone had brought food. One of them had a child on her lap that Anil had not seen before, a small boy of perhaps three who was eating something with both hands and who looked up periodically at the site with an expression of blank curiosity.

She finished her mango and washed her hands from the water bottle and went back to work.

The afternoon was spent in the laboratory tent, which had been set up fifty meters from the excavation site — a large green tent with folding tables and portable light sources and the equipment that she and Leaf had brought in their cases. She laid out both skeletons on the examination tables and began the systematic analysis. Age, sex, stature, pathological conditions, perimortem trauma. She worked slowly and with full attention, the way she had been trained. This was what she was good at. This was the thing she knew how to do.

The first skeleton — she was calling them SK-1 and SK-2 in her notes — was a male, she was fairly confident. The brow ridges, the mastoid processes, the general robusticity of the bones. Age at death difficult to assess with precision, but she would estimate between thirty-five and fifty. There was evidence of healed trauma to the left forearm, a well-remodeled fracture that had probably occurred years before death. There was arthritis in the lumbar vertebrae, moderate. And there was a wound to the posterior of the skull, to the occipital bone, a perimortem wound — meaning a wound that had occurred at or around the time of death — that was consistent with a heavy blow from behind.

She wrote this down in her notebook with care, with precision.

SK-2 was also male, younger, probably between twenty and thirty-five. The pelvis had remodeled from what appeared to be a significant trauma, perhaps a vehicle accident, perhaps a fall from a height. He had walked with this injury for some time before his death. The wound on his skull was similar to the wound on SK-1 — occipital, perimortem, consistent with a blow from behind.

Both men had been struck from behind. Both had been buried together. The arrangement of the legs of SK-2, the deliberate crossing of the ankles, suggested that whoever had buried them had felt something for at least one of them. An obligation, perhaps. Or grief.

She sat back and looked at the two skeletons laid out on the tables and thought about what she was seeing. Two men, killed by blows to the head, buried together in a field in western Sri Lanka. The field was in an area that had been, during the late 1980s, the site of significant insurgent activity and also of significant government counterinsurgency activity. The bodies had been reported to the Human Rights Commission by a person whose name was not in the file she had been given. She did not know when the bodies had been buried, though the degree of decomposition was consistent with a burial period of several years.

Leaf came to the table and looked at her notes.

Execution-style, he said.

Yes.

The question is by whom.

She said nothing.

She understood the implication of the question. Both the insurgents and the government had killed people in this region during those years. Both sides had produced bodies that ended up in unmarked graves in fields like this one. The forensic evidence could tell her how these men had died. It could not always tell her who had killed them, and that distinction — that gap between how and who — was where the politics lived, where the danger lived, where the truth that mattered most to the families waiting under the mango tree could be found or not found.

Gunesena appeared at the entrance to the tent. He looked at the skeletons on the tables with an expression she could not read.

The families are asking, he said, whether you have been able to determine anything.

She looked at him. She was aware that what she said next would travel immediately to the families outside and also, eventually, to whoever had assigned Gunesena to accompany this team.

We're still in the early stages of analysis, she said. We'll need to complete our examination before we can draw any conclusions.

He nodded and withdrew.

She looked at Leaf. He was humming very quietly.

She went to the entrance of the tent and looked out at the field in the late afternoon light. The women were still under the mango tree. The child was asleep now, stretched out on one of the women's laps. The excavation site was covered again with the plastic sheeting, weighted with stones. A bird she didn't recognize was calling from somewhere in the trees.

She had come back. She was here. These were the facts.

She thought about her uncle, who had died in Colombo in 1987, during the years in question, whose death had been attributed officially to a heart attack and about which her mother had, on the single occasion Anil had asked about it directly, said only that these things happened and that it was better not to speak about it. She had been twenty years old and she had accepted this, had filed it with the other things she was accepting in those years, had gone back to England and continued her studies. She was not sure she would have done anything differently if she had known then what she suspected now about how these things happened. She was not sure what she would do with the knowledge now, here, given the specific constraints under which she was working.

She went back to the table and picked up her pencil.

The cloth fragment recovered from the pelvic region of SK-2. She had bagged it and labeled it. She took it out now and examined it under the magnifying glass. It was a small piece of what appeared to have been a shirt or sarong, pale blue, with a narrow stripe. The weave was tight, the thread finer than ordinary working cloth. This was not a poor man's clothing, or not necessarily. It was the kind of cloth that could be bought in any market in Colombo, that was worn by shopkeepers and teachers and government clerks and insurgents and people with no politics at all, by anyone in a country where this particular weave and this particular pale blue were simply common.

She bagged it again and wrote a note about it.

Outside, the light was going gold and the shadows were long and the women under the mango tree were talking quietly to each other now. The child was awake and walking between the women in small circles, stopping to look at things on the ground. Anil watched him from the entrance of the tent for a moment. He crouched down and picked something up — a stone or a seed — and held it up to examine it, held it up to the light.

She would be in Sri Lanka for three months, perhaps four. She had a room in Colombo and a field site two hours from the city and a case file and a notebook and the bodies of two men on a table. She had the discipline of the work, which was the thing she trusted most, the thing she returned to. She had been trained to let the body tell its story, to resist her own inclinations, to remain in the evidence. She had been trained well and she believed in the training.

But she was also here, in this specific place, in this light, watching a child hold a stone up to the last of the afternoon sun, and she was aware — had been aware all day, had been aware every day since she landed in Colombo and drove through streets that remembered her body even when her mind could not locate them precisely — that being here was itself a kind of evidence, that her presence in this country at this time in this field was not neutral, was not the same as being in Guatemala or anywhere else, was something she would have to reckon with in ways she did not yet fully understand.

The child set the stone down carefully, with great deliberateness, and stood up and looked around with the look of a person who has completed something.

Anil turned back to the tent and her work and the two men laid out on the tables, their bones clean now in the bright light of the portable lamps, patient as they had always been, holding the record of what had happened to them, waiting for her to read it correctly.
