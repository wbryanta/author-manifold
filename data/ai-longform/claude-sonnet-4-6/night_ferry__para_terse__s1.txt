The ferry left at eleven and he had not eaten since morning, but there was something that felt necessary about the hunger, a kind of fasting he hadn't consciously decided on but didn't want to break. He bought a coffee from the machine near the car deck entrance and carried it to the upper lounge, where the fluorescent light had the blue cast of an operating theatre and a family of four had claimed the long padded bench along the stern window, all of them already sleeping in a heap of jackets and backpacks that made them look like they'd washed up there. He found a seat by the window on the port side and watched the lights of the town slide away, first in clusters and then in singles, and then there was just the black water and the black sky distinguishing themselves from each other with difficulty.

His name was David Calder and he was forty-one years old and he had not spoken to his brother in six years, though he had thought about him with the particular frequency of the unresolved, which was to say often and usually at wrong times: while standing at the sink, while sitting in traffic on the Queensway, once in the middle of a meeting about fourth-quarter projections when something about the way his colleague tapped a pen against his teeth had recalled, for no reason David could trace, a summer when he and Joel were boys and they had caught a pike in the shallows behind their uncle's cottage and Joel had held it, this long muscular fish, against his chest and made a face of such theatrical rapture that their mother had laughed until she bent over.

The police officer he'd spoken to was named Constable Reimer and she had been careful on the phone. She'd said they had a body, male, late thirties to mid-forties, found in a rental cabin outside a town called Toften on the northern coast. She'd said there was no identification but there was a wallet with no cards and two hundred dollars in cash and a photograph that appeared to show two boys on a dock. She'd said she understood this was difficult, but given that David Calder was listed as next of kin on a tenancy agreement in the deceased's effects, would he be able to come. She'd said deceased with a practiced neutrality, and it was this word that had gotten under his skin the most, the way it turned whoever this was—Joel or not Joel—into a category, a genus of person rather than a person.

He thought: it might not be him. He'd thought this with some regularity during the four-hour drive to the ferry terminal, saying it to himself like a thing he was trying to believe. It might not be him. He knew almost nothing about Joel's life now. Joel might be anywhere. Joel might be in Mexico or in Vancouver or in one of the western provinces where a man could work quietly on a drilling crew and send no signal home and no one would be surprised. Joel had done this before, the going quiet, the long silences that David used to call offices when they were younger, as in Joel's gone to his office again, which had been funny when they were young and then less funny as they got older and the offices got longer.

The last time they'd spoken it had been a phone call that David had made, standing in his kitchen in the early afternoon of a Tuesday in October, with leaves coming down outside the window in the particular orange of a hard frost. He'd called to tell Joel that their mother had been diagnosed with the thing they'd been afraid she'd be diagnosed with, and Joel had said nothing for a long time, and then he'd said, she has people, and David had said, yes, she has people, she has me, and Joel had said, then she's fine, and David had said something he was not going to say on a ferry on the way to identify his brother's possible body, had said it badly and with force and with the full weight of six years of being the one who showed up, and Joel had gone quiet the way he did, not hanging up but going silent so completely that the line seemed to hum with a new quality, a loaded kind of silence, and David had finally said, fine, and Joel had said, fine, and David had ended the call.

Their mother had died eleven months later. David had called Joel's number eight times in the week before she died and twice in the week after. Joel had not answered. David had left two messages, the second of which was nineteen seconds long and contained only his name and the date and the time of the service and then a pause in which he had nearly said something else before not saying it.

He pressed his forehead against the cold glass of the porthole window. The water out there was invisible in the darkness, but he knew it was rough because the ferry moved in a specific way, a slow pendular roll punctuated by deeper surges, and the coffee in his cup tilted in one direction and then the other with the absolute democracy of liquids. He tried to remember Joel's face from the last time he'd actually seen him, which had been Christmas of the year before the phone call. They'd met at a hotel bar near the airport because neither of them wanted to deal with the apartment, with their mother's apartment and all its careful arrangements of things. Joel had come directly off a flight from somewhere, he had luggage with him, he looked thin in a way that had a story behind it that he didn't tell. They had drinks. They talked about hockey, about the Senators, about whether the Senators would ever stop being an embarrassment and both of them had thought they might, which in retrospect had the quality of a shared delusion. They did not talk about the things they should have talked about, which was a habit so old between them that it had the comfort of a habit. Joel had laughed twice in the genuine way, the way that was his real laugh and not his social laugh, the real one was high and sudden and a little undignified, and he'd gotten it both times from David, which David had carried home with him as a small gift or maybe a small achievement.

He was aware that he was preparing himself for something and that the preparation was taking the form of memory, which is what the mind does when it suspects it may need to restructure itself around an absence. He caught himself at this and put his hands flat on the table and looked at them, the familiar geography of his own hands. He thought: wait. He thought: you don't know. He thought: it might be no one you know, some poor man in a rental cabin with a wallet and a photograph, whose life ended in that particular longitude and latitude, with no more connection to your own than the administrative accident of a phone number.

A man sat down across the aisle from him, also alone, with a beer from the vending machine and a paperback he didn't open. He was fiftyish, heavyset, wearing a canvas jacket with a collar that he'd turned up though it wasn't cold in here. He put the book on the table and looked at it. He put the beer beside the book and looked at it. He seemed like a man for whom the evening had not been conclusive. David felt some solidarity with this but did not act on it. They sat in their respective silences while the ferry worked through the water.

He thought about what the body would look like, and then deliberately didn't think about it, and then thought about it anyway. He'd never seen a body in this context. His mother he'd seen in the hospital and then at the funeral home, and both times she'd looked like a version of herself that had been edited for content, the extraneous parts removed, the parts that were really her—the specific way she moved her hands when she told a story, the way she held a cigarette after she'd quit smoking just for the ceremony of it, holding a pen or a pencil with the same two fingers—all of that was just gone and what remained was only a shape. He thought that if it was Joel the same would be true, and that this was something he would have to be ready for: not Joel, but a shape that had been Joel, and the knowledge that Joel himself was elsewhere now, in some condition he didn't have language for.

He thought: elsewhere. He thought: what a word. He used it to avoid the words he didn't want to use, the religious words he didn't believe and the clinical words that didn't satisfy. Joel would have had a position on this. Joel had positions on everything except the things that mattered most to him, about which he said almost nothing. He was like that: opinionated about cinema and politics and the correct way to make coffee and completely silent on the subject of what he wanted from his life, who he was, what he was sorry for, what he hoped for. David had spent large portions of his adult life being frustrated by this and larger portions, if he was honest, being quietly grateful for it because it meant that Joel was always to some extent unknown, which meant that whatever version of him you kept in your head was more or less intact, unspoiled by disclosure. You could still think of him as okay without him having provided evidence to the contrary.

He got up and walked to the stern and looked through the family's window over their sleeping bodies. The wake was a long white road on dark water and it was being reclaimed steadily by the sea. He thought about the photograph: two boys on a dock. He knew which one. He knew without any doubt. It was a photograph from their uncle's cottage, summer, he thought he was eleven which would make Joel eight, and in the photograph he is sitting on the dock with his feet in the water and Joel is standing behind him with his arms held out like he is about to push David in, though he never did, it was always a pretense, the threat was the point, and their uncle had caught it at the peak of the gesture. David's face in the photo is already turned back over his shoulder, already reading the situation, already deciding whether to laugh or brace, and Joel's face is pure delight at the power of the pretense, the leverage of the nearly-done thing.

He went back to his seat. He thought: why would a man carry that photograph without any other identification. And then he thought about who Joel was, who Joel had become in the absence of David's knowledge, and he thought it was not impossible. He thought it was, in fact, quite possible for a man like Joel to have arrived somewhere north and cold and to have a wallet with cash and no cards, to live in a way that didn't need cards, and to carry one photograph of two boys on a dock as if that were the necessary thing, the portable proof of something that couldn't be documented any other way. He thought: I'm doing it again. Making him into the story I want. Deciding what he would do.

He slept for a while and dreamed nothing he could remember, woke with his neck at a wrong angle and a grey quality to the light coming through the porthole that meant dawn was happening somewhere above the cloud cover. The man with the paperback was gone. The family was waking in stages, children first, then parents, the parents with that blasted-open expression that young children produce in the early morning of an overnight journey. David stretched his neck left and right and felt the click that had been there for years and drank the cold remainder of his coffee, which was a mistake.

The announcement came at six-fifteen. They would be docking at Toften Harbour at seven. He went to the bathroom and looked at himself in the mirror the way you do before something, the assessing look, the look that tries to determine whether you are capable. He looked fine. He looked like a man in his early forties who had slept in a chair and not eaten since yesterday morning and was about to do a thing he didn't want to do. He wet his hands and pressed them against his face and that was as good as it was going to get.

Constable Reimer met him at the dock. She was younger than he'd expected from her voice, mid-thirties, with the wind-abraded complexion of someone who spent time outside in this climate and had made peace with it. She shook his hand and thanked him for coming and said she was sorry for the circumstances. She drove him in an RCMP truck through a town that was mostly closed at this hour, a main street with a pharmacy and a hardware store and two restaurants and what appeared to be a small library, all of them with their lights off. The town had the particular quality of northern towns where the practical has long since outlasted the ornamental, where nothing is there except for a reason.

They drove to what she called the facility, which was a hospital annex. There was a man there named Dr. Prochuk who had a beard and good shoes and who also thanked him for coming and who was kind in the efficient way of people who do this regularly. Dr. Prochuk explained that the deceased had been found three days prior by the cabin's owner, who had gone to collect the rent. He explained the cause of death with careful language, cardiac, explained that it would have been fast, explained that there had been no evidence of anything else, which David understood to mean: nothing suspicious, nothing deliberate, just the body stopping. He said there were no distinguishing documents but that fingerprints were being processed. He asked if David was ready.

David said yes, which was not accurate but was the only available answer.

It was his brother. He knew before Dr. Prochuk had the sheet fully down, knew from the jaw, from the particular architecture of the forehead, which was their father's forehead, which was his own forehead. He knew and he stood with that knowledge for what felt like a long time, looking at the shape that had been Joel, looking for his brother in there the way you look for the original landscape in a painting that's been varnished over. There were lines in Joel's face that hadn't been there at Christmas seven years ago. His hair was shorter. He looked, in the terrible language of the observation, like he had been tired.

He said, yes, this is my brother, Joel William Calder. He heard himself say it and it sounded like a thing someone in a film would say, but there was no other way to say it, those were the words for the situation.

Dr. Prochuk said he was sorry. Constable Reimer said she was sorry. David said thank you, which was also a film thing to say, but again.

They gave him coffee that was better than the ferry's, and they gave him a room with a chair and a window that looked out at a parking lot where a crow was investigating something near a dumpster with the committed focus of an academic, and they gave him time, which he sat in and didn't know what to do with. He thought about calling someone but couldn't think who. He had friends, he had a colleague he was close to, he had a woman he'd been seeing for eight months who knew about Joel in the way you know about someone's life without knowing the texture of it. He thought about calling her and hearing her voice say something careful and warm and right, and he thought that this was what he wanted and that for some reason he couldn't do it yet, that doing it would move something from one category to another and he wasn't ready for the move.

He thought about Joel in the cabin. He tried to think about it not cinematically but actually: what does a man do in a cabin in the north in the late autumn. He cooks things, probably simple things. He reads, Joel had always read, fiction mostly. He walks. He looks at the water. He wakes early because you wake early in those places and the mornings are a particular quality of cold and blue. He drinks coffee, Joel always had his position on coffee, and he sits and he thinks whatever he thinks. He is not, in this version, miserable. He is not in this version the wreckage that David's anger had sometimes needed him to be. He is just a man in a cabin who chose a particular longitude and latitude and whose heart stopped.

David took the wallet and the effects from Constable Reimer in a sealed bag. He signed for them. The photograph was there, he could see it through the plastic, the two boys on the dock, exactly as he'd known. He didn't take it out. He held the bag on his lap and looked at the crow, which had found what it was looking for and was now departing with it.

He thought: six years. He thought: eight phone calls. He thought about the nineteen-second message and what had been in the pause, what he had almost said and had not said and would not say now, ever, not into any phone, not to any person who would carry it back to Joel, not in any church or ceremony or gathering of people saying the things you say. He had almost said: I miss you. He had almost said: I don't know what happened to us but I remember who we were. He had almost said: come to the service, just come, just be there, I won't say anything, we don't have to talk about any of it, just be in the room.

He had not said it. Joel had not come. Their mother had died. Here they were.

He became aware that he was crying, which surprised him because he was not, in general, a person who cried easily, and the crying did not feel like grief exactly, not yet, or not only grief. It felt like a door he'd been standing at for a long time that was now simply and indisputably shut, which was in one way worse than grief and in another way a kind of relief so unwelcome that he hated himself for feeling it, the relief of resolution, of knowing, of the case being in some sense closed. He hated that his nervous system was capable of producing relief in this moment. And then he thought: Joel would understand this. Joel, whatever else he was, had a particular understanding of the mind's disloyalties, the way it wants what it wants in spite of what you tell it.

He wiped his face with the back of his hand. The crow was gone. He sat until he wasn't crying and then a little longer, until he felt he had a serviceable version of himself again, which would have to do.

Constable Reimer drove him back to the ferry terminal and told him she'd be in touch about the arrangements, and she said it again, that she was sorry, and this time it landed differently, it landed as what it was, a person saying the true and insufficient thing that gets said because there is no other thing and because the saying of it is still, somehow, necessary. He thanked her and meant it.

He sat on the ferry going back the same way, same lounge, same blue light, no family this time, just other people in early-morning attitudes of privacy, and he looked out at the strait in the daylight, which was grey and choppy, and could see the water now, the dark green of it, the cold specific colour of northern water, and he thought about Joel seeing this water from the cabin, every morning, that particular view, and choosing it, or not choosing it but arriving at it the way you arrive at places and then staying long enough that you've chosen it. He thought that it was possible Joel had been happy there. He thought that it was also possible Joel had been lonely there. He thought both could be true, and that he would never know, and that not knowing was the condition he had been living in for six years and would continue living in, just with a different quality now, a finality that changed the texture without changing the essential shape.

He opened the sealed bag and took out the photograph and looked at it for a long time. Joel's arms out. David's face turned back, already reading the situation. The water of the lake behind them in the high afternoon light of that summer, that specific summer, before everything that came after. He thought about the pike. He thought about Joel holding the pike against his chest, the fish strong and silver and alive, and Joel's face full of that theatrical joy, and their mother bent double with laughter on the dock.

He put the photograph in his breast pocket, against his chest. He pressed his palm flat against it. He looked at the water going past and the water going past and the water going past.
