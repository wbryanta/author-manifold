The ferry left the harbour at eleven and by midnight there was nothing beyond the windows but dark water and the suggestion of the man's own face in the glass. He had a paper cup of coffee gone cold beside him on the seat. He did not drink it. He had not brought a book. These were the kinds of decisions he would examine later, in the particular way he had of accounting for himself — the coffee, the book, the single change of clothes in the green bag on the rack above him. As if the objects he carried or failed to carry made a true record of who he was when he crossed into something he hadn't chosen.

His name was Calum. He was forty-three years old. He had last seen his brother eleven years ago in the parking lot of a grocery store in a city neither of them lived in anymore, and that encounter — if it could be called that — had lasted perhaps four minutes, the two of them standing in the specific cold of November, not touching, not quite arguing, saying the kinds of things that are meant to close doors, which is to say the kinds of things that are never enough to close them.

The ferry was called the *Tern* or the *Heron* — he had not noted which, the gangway was slick, he had been watching his feet. There were perhaps a dozen other passengers in the lounge, most of them sleeping under their coats or staring at phones, the screens making small cold altars of their faces. A woman near the front had a child asleep across her lap and she moved her hand in slow circles on the child's back, not thinking about it, the way you do a thing you have done so many times it becomes the movement itself rather than an act. Calum watched this and felt something shift in his chest that he would not name.

He had spoken to a Sergeant McLaren by telephone that afternoon. McLaren's voice had been cautious and careful in the way of someone delivering information in a container he hoped would hold. There was a man, McLaren said. Found on the rocks below the coastal road north of Strathport, three days ago. No identification on him. They had a description. Calum had listened and said the description could match his brother — height, colouring, age — and McLaren had said yes, that was why they were calling, and Calum had understood he was being asked to take the ferry north and stand in a room and look at a face.

He had not asked how the man died. He would learn later, from the same McLaren in a different room, that there had been no signs of struggle, no obvious cause, that the body had been there perhaps two nights before the fisherman found it, and that this ambiguity — this refusal of the facts to arrange themselves into a clear shape — was entirely consistent with how his brother had moved through the world for the last twenty years. Leaving things unresolved. Not from cruelty, or not only from cruelty, but from some deep constitutional inability to let anything be finished.

His brother's name was Seamus, though he had called himself other things at other times. James. Shay. A period, in his thirties, when he'd gone by their father's middle name, which was Iain, a borrowing that Calum had found obscene though he could not entirely explain why. Their father had been dead by then. Perhaps that was it. You don't take a dead man's name and walk around in it.

Outside the window the water was very dark and then occasionally it was not dark — a whiteness moving through it, a disturbance, some physics of pressure and cold Calum didn't know the name for, and then dark again. He thought about how much of the world he had never learned the names for. He was an engineer. He understood load and stress and the behaviour of materials under duress. He had built, or helped to build, bridges over rivers in three countries and an overpass outside Lyon that he still thought was the most honest thing he'd ever done. But the sea at night, what made it white in places — he did not know. He suspected Seamus would have known.

This was one of the specific injuries of having a brother. That some knowledge lived only in him and you couldn't get to it without getting to him first.

They had grown up on the island. Their mother kept a garden on the north-facing slope behind the house where almost nothing would grow and she tended it anyway with the particular devotion people give to difficult things. Seamus had been eight years older than Calum and this gap had made him less a companion than a weather system, something you watched from a distance and tried to read. By the time Calum was ten Seamus was eighteen and already leaving, already turned toward whatever it was he spent his life turning toward, though Calum had never been able to identify it precisely and had a feeling now, on this dark water, that possibly Seamus hadn't been able to either.

Their father was a careful, unhappy man who worked on the boats and came home smelling of diesel and something else Calum associated with deep water, as if the depths had their own particular chemistry that clung to you after. He was not unkind. He was simply elsewhere. You could speak to him and watch him hear you from a distance. Their mother managed this with a kind of cheerful determination that only revealed its cost in the garden, in those hours she spent on her knees in the cold turning the soil of something that would not yield.

Seamus had first left when Calum was ten. He had come back several times over the years in the way that people return to places — not because of what was there but because of what it was easier to be there than to be away. He was brilliant in the irregular, uncertified way of people who educate themselves out of need rather than structure. He had read everything and retained it all and deployed it sometimes gently and sometimes not. He had, at various points, worked on fishing vessels, translated technical documents from Finnish, done something with computers in the nineties that he could never satisfactorily explain, managed a small hotel outside Inverness for two years, painted houses. He had married once, a woman named Petra whom Calum had met only at the wedding and who had separated from Seamus within eighteen months and moved to Canada, where Calum understood she was still living and had made a life.

What broke them — Calum and Seamus, the two of them — was not a single thing. He knew this now though for years he had tried to make it a single thing because a single thing is easier to carry than a distributed weight. There had been money, yes. There had been the matter of their mother's last years, who came and who didn't, who answered the phone at ten at night and who didn't, who drove up on weekends and sat in the hospital room and who didn't. But underneath this, deeper than this, something older. A way Seamus had of looking at him sometimes that suggested Calum was a version of something, a copy, a dilution — not of Seamus specifically but of some shared original they had both fallen short of and Calum had fallen shorter. He knew this was possibly his own invention. He knew it was possibly true.

In the parking lot in November Seamus had said: *You have always needed to be the one who does the right thing.* He had said it with what might have been contempt and might have been grief and in eleven years Calum had not managed to determine which. He had said: *It must be very comfortable, that.* And then he had gotten into a car that wasn't his — a friend's car, a woman's car, Calum never knew — and driven away, and that had been that.

The ferry moved. The engines made a sound beneath everything that you stopped hearing after a while, the way you stopped hearing your own heartbeat. A man across the aisle was eating something from a bag, deliberately, each piece placed and chewed. An older man with a face that had been somewhere and come back changed. Calum watched him and thought about the different kinds of witness. Being in a room and looking at a face and confirming or not confirming. The sergeant's careful voice. *If you could just tell us.* As if this were a small thing, this looking.

He had loved his brother. He wanted to be careful about this, accurate, not to perform the love now that Seamus might be dead, not to inflate it into something it hadn't been. But yes, before whatever calcified between them, before the long years of distance and before the parking lot, he had loved him with the helpless comprehensiveness of a child who worships someone whose attention is never fully available. He had learned things by watching him. How to hold a fishing line. How to read weather coming from the west. A way of moving through a difficult room that took up no more space than necessary. He had learned later, in his twenties, that these lessons had a cost he hadn't been told about upfront, that the things Seamus taught were inseparable from the way Seamus saw, and that the way Seamus saw was not always trustworthy. But the learning had happened. It was part of him. He could not unlearn how to read weather from the west.

He got up and walked to the rear of the ferry. There was a door to an external deck and he pushed through it into the cold. The wake spread behind them, white and phosphorescent in a way he now thought might be biological — organisms in the water disturbed by the passage of the hull, the way living things respond to displacement with light. He stood and watched it. The island was behind him somewhere in all that dark, behind them by three hours now. Somewhere ahead the mainland and its coast road and a town he had never been to and a building where his brother might or might not be lying on a table waiting to be identified.

He thought about what it would mean if it was Seamus. He thought about what it would mean if it wasn't.

If it was Seamus then certain things would close. There would be arrangements, logistics, the specific bureaucracy of the dead that he had moved through once before with his mother and that he knew the shape of. There would be a ceremony of some kind, at some point. He would stand in it. Petra in Canada would receive a call. He would be the one to make that call — there was no one else — and he would hear her absorb the information and he would have to be present for that absorption without being able to help it.

If it was not Seamus then Seamus was somewhere. On a vessel, in a room, in a car belonging to a woman, in one of the countries where he had occasionally gone quiet for months at a time — Finland, once Denmark, a period of inexplicable silence that had resolved into a postcard from Lisbon, a postcard Calum still had somewhere in a drawer, the handwriting very clean and the message only: *there are excellent pastries here and the light is different than they say.* No question. No return address. Just the statement of pastries and light, offered as if it were enough, and perhaps for Seamus it had been, or perhaps Seamus had understood it would not be enough for Calum and had sent it anyway.

He held the rail. The cold moved through his jacket. He thought about a summer, the summer he was twelve, when Seamus had come back for three weeks and taken him fishing every morning for a week, the two of them out before light in a borrowed boat, and Seamus had been different those mornings — present in some way he usually wasn't, stripped of whatever performance he generally maintained, just a man in a boat in the dark with his younger brother, pointing at things. The patterns of birds. How to tell the depth by colour. He had said very little and what he said was not memorable in itself but there was something in the quality of his attention those mornings that Calum had never fully left. As if he were being shown that his brother was real, that underneath everything he was made of the same materials as the world, that this was both less and more than Calum had imagined.

One morning a grey seal had surfaced beside the boat and regarded them. It had stayed for a long time, moving slightly with the water, its face turned toward them with what looked like curiosity but was probably something else, some animal calculation they weren't equipped to read. Seamus had gone very still. He had looked at the seal for a long time and then he had looked at Calum, and the look was not something Calum had ever been able to translate into words. It was not tender exactly, or not only. It was something that acknowledged the fact of them both being there, in the boat, watched by an animal, in the early light that was coming cold off the water. It was perhaps the least guarded Calum had ever seen him.

The seal eventually went. The morning continued. They caught nothing that day and rowed back in.

He had been an uncle. Calum had a daughter, Niamh, who was nine now and who had never met Seamus. He had told her she had an uncle. He had shown her a photograph — one of the few he had, Seamus in his late twenties outside a pub somewhere, squinting into the sun, the expression between a smile and something more private. She had looked at the photograph with the serious attention she gave to things she was trying to fully understand and said: *does he look like you?* And Calum had said he supposed there were ways they looked alike, and she had studied the photograph again and said: *around the eyes, maybe.* Around the eyes. He had not been able to speak for a moment. He had said yes. He thought so too.

He would have to call his wife when he arrived. Sara would be awake — she did not sleep well, she never had, she moved through the early mornings of their house like something that couldn't quite land. She knew where he was going and why. She had held his face in her hands when he left and said she was sorry, and he had understood that she was sorry not for what was definitely true but for the whole accumulated territory — the ferry he was taking, the room he might stand in, the eleven years, the parking lot, all of it. She was capable of this kind of sorrow, wide and containing. It was one of the ways she was better at being human than he was.

The wake spread and spread and was absorbed by the dark.

He thought: if it is him, I will stand beside him and I will not perform anything. I will not grieve for the grief of those eleven years and make it retroactively tender. I will just look at him. I will look at whatever his face has become and I will say yes or no and I will carry it back across the water.

He thought: if it is not him, I will stand in that room anyway. I will stand in it and feel what it is to have stood in a room prepared to lose someone and been refused — which is not relief, or not simply, but something more ambivalent, some doubling of the loss and the continuation of it, because if Seamus is alive then Seamus is still not speaking and the parking lot is still the last thing and there is still no way across.

There was a name for the strait they were crossing. He hadn't looked at a map; he knew it vaguely from childhood, a name in Gaelic that had been anglicized and then half-forgotten. His father had crossed it regularly, had known it in weather and in calm, had once described to Calum — one of the few long things his father had ever said — being caught in the strait in a force nine when he was nineteen years old, the water coming in from the northwest in walls, the boat a thirty-foot creel boat not designed for open crossing, and how in the middle of it he had stopped being frightened and become simply a body doing what the moment required, and that this was the most alive he had ever felt. He had said this quietly, as if it surprised him to say it, as if he hadn't known it until the saying. Calum had been perhaps fourteen. He had not fully known what to do with it. He filed it somewhere and returned to it sometimes, in the way you return to a thing you know you understand less than you should.

His father died on land. A quiet failure of the heart in the kitchen, a Wednesday afternoon, nothing around him requiring his particular skill with wind and water. Seamus had been gone by then, somewhere in his years of going. He had come back for the funeral and had stood at the grave looking not at it but at the sea.

The engines changed pitch slightly and Calum felt the ferry adjust. There might be a current here, some underwater argument between bodies of water meeting at different temperatures, different densities — he thought he remembered something about that, a place in the strait where the conditions changed, where you could feel it in the hull. His father had known these things. Seamus had known these things. He knew load-bearing and material fatigue and the mathematics of span. These were different knowledges and he had made his peace with having his rather than the other, or thought he had, though standing on the deck in the cold he was less certain of this peace, less certain it had been fully made, or made by him rather than simply accepted as the geography of a life he'd stopped questioning.

He would arrive in two hours. He would find somewhere to sit until morning, until a reasonable hour, and then he would call McLaren and McLaren would tell him where to go and he would go there. He had done harder things. He had stood at the edge of a bridge under construction in a crosswind and looked down at the river and felt the structure move beneath him in the particular permitted flex that was designed into it — the bridge was not rigid, rigidity would break it, the flex was the thing that held — and he had trusted the mathematics of it, had trusted that the numbers he and others had worked and reworked were equal to what the wind was doing. He would carry this into the room. The knowledge that you could be designed to hold.

He stayed on the deck until the cold was past discomfort and into something clarifying. The wake continued to spread. Some bird — he didn't know what kind, some seabird moving in the dark for reasons of its own — passed low over the water and was gone.

He thought about Seamus reading something in a room, somewhere, in this same moment. He let himself think it. The specific image of his brother with a book — the way he held them, close, as if they might escape — in some rented room, the window behind him full of water or light, the morning or the night. He let this exist for a while. He did not require it to be true. He only required that it was possible, that the world was large enough to contain it as a possibility, this man alive somewhere reading in a room, and that Calum could carry this alongside the other thing — the body on the rocks, the sergeant's voice, the room he was moving toward across dark water — without choosing between them yet. Both present. Both held.

The ferry moved north. He held the rail. Around the eyes, maybe.
