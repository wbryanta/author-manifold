The price tags had been Clara's idea, or rather the idea of the woman Clara had hired to do this, a competent stranger named Donna who had arrived at seven in the morning with a cashbox and a roll of orange stickers and had moved through the house like someone who had done this so many times the rooms no longer registered as rooms. Donna had stickered the lamps and the end tables and the good china that had never been used and the bad china that had been used every day, and she had done it with a kind of professional tenderness that Clara found both comforting and obscene. The good china was priced at forty dollars for the set. The everyday china at twelve. Clara had not argued. She had stood in the kitchen doorway watching and had thought about how her mother had washed those plates every night, how the pattern around the rim—a thin blue line interrupted by small flowers that might have been cornflowers or might have been chicory, nobody had ever been sure—had faded on the left side of each plate from years of being stacked the same way in the same cabinet.

It was now two in the afternoon and the sale had been open since nine and the house was already strange.

People moved through it with the unhurried confidence of those who have been given permission. An older man in a green Carhartt jacket stood in the living room holding one of the candlesticks from the mantel, turning it in his hands the way you turn a thing when you are deciding whether you want it or whether you just want to want it. A young woman with a sleeping baby strapped to her chest was crouched in front of the bookcase, reading spines. Two teenage boys had discovered the garage and were presumably cataloguing its contents with the greedy attention teenagers give to anything that might be mechanical or dangerous or free.

Clara stood on the front porch with a cup of coffee that had gone cold and watched an elderly couple carry her mother's wingback chair down the porch steps. The chair was the color of old mustard. It had been in the corner of the living room for as long as Clara could remember, and her mother had sat in it every evening after dinner to watch the news and then, as she got older, to watch whatever was on because the news had become too much for her, the particulars of the world too insistent, too unkind. The chair still held, Clara thought, something of the shape of her mother's sitting—a slight depression in the left cushion, an asymmetry in the back where her mother had always leaned to one side. The elderly couple maneuvered it carefully down the steps, and the man said something to the woman and the woman laughed, and Clara watched them load it into the back of a pickup truck and she felt nothing, or felt something she didn't have a name for yet, something that lived below feeling.

She had driven up from Columbus that morning, the two-hour drive through the flat center of the state, through towns that existed primarily to interrupt the fields. Millersport, Granville, the long straight roads with their mailboxes and their grain elevators and their occasional white churches standing alone in the middle of nothing in particular, as if God had made a note to himself and then forgotten what the note was for. She had listened to the radio and then turned it off and driven in silence and had found herself, somewhere around Pataskala, thinking about the summer she was eleven years old and her mother had taught her to drive a lawnmower. The memory had arrived without announcement, without apparent cause—just the sudden vivid fact of her mother's hands over her hands on the steering bar, her mother's voice close to her ear, the smell of cut grass and something floral that her mother wore in those years, a perfume whose name Clara couldn't remember and whose bottle she had not found in the bathroom when she had cleaned it out last month. The bottle had already been gone. Her mother had stopped wearing it at some point, years ago, and Clara had never noticed.

She went back inside.

Donna was stationed at a card table near the front door with the cashbox and a calculator and a patience that seemed genuinely inexhaustible. A man was buying a set of tools and Donna was tallying them with the calm efficiency of someone for whom value was a settled question. Clara moved past them and into the kitchen, where a woman she didn't recognize was holding up two mixing bowls and comparing them as though the choice were significant.

The kitchen had been her mother's best room. This was where she had existed most completely, where her authority had been clearest—over the stove and the cutting board and the particular geography of the cabinets, whose contents she had known the way a person knows their own body, reaching without looking. Clara had spent a great deal of time in this room. She had sat at this table to do homework and to be sick with childhood fevers and to have the conversations that mattered—or most of them; some of the conversations that mattered had happened in the car, because her mother had known, intuitively, that certain things could be said more easily in motion, without eye contact. When Clara was sixteen and failing algebra and also failing at the more serious project of being sixteen, it had been in this kitchen that her mother had sat across from her with a cup of tea and said, with a directness that Clara had not understood as a kindness until much later, that some things are hard and that being bad at them is not the same as being bad. Clara had wanted to dismiss this as the generic comfort of a parent, and she had tried to, and had been unable to.

The woman chose the larger of the two mixing bowls and carried it toward the front. The sticker said two dollars. The bowl had made a thousand batters.

Clara opened the refrigerator. She had cleaned it out two weeks ago, leaving it empty with the door slightly ajar the way you're supposed to. The inside was white and blank and smelled faintly of cold. She stood there looking at it for a moment, aware that she was doing something strange, and then closed it.

Her brother had called last night. Philip, who lived in Portland and had not been able to come, had called to tell her that he was thinking about her and that he was sorry he couldn't be there and that he thought she was handling everything incredibly well. Clara had said thank you and had meant to leave it there but had instead said, quite without planning to, that she didn't feel like she was handling anything, that handling implied control over something and she didn't feel in control of anything, she felt like she was standing very still while things moved past her. Philip had been quiet for a moment and then had said that maybe that was the same thing. Maybe it is, Clara had said. She wasn't sure that it was.

Their mother had died in February, six weeks after her diagnosis. Pancreatic, which everyone said was the worst way, as if there were a hierarchy, as if the other ways had been ranked and graded and this one had placed last. Clara didn't know if it was the worst way. It had been fast. There had been pain and then less pain and then her mother, in the hospital room, had looked at Clara with an expression that Clara could only describe as considering—the look of someone working out a complicated problem—and then had closed her eyes, and that had been that. The problem, apparently, resolved.

Clara had been the one here. She had always been the one here, not because of any sacrifice or resentment but simply because Columbus was close and Philip was not, and because she and her mother had been the kind of close that doesn't announce itself, that doesn't require acknowledgment. They had talked on the phone every Sunday. They had not always had things to say. They had said them anyway.

Now she walked through the dining room, where the table had already been sold but the buyer wasn't coming until tomorrow, and into the small room at the back of the house that her mother had called the sitting room, though she had rarely sat in it—it was more of an aspirational room, a room that held the idea of leisure without quite achieving it. Here were the things that hadn't sold yet or wouldn't sell: a stack of old magazines, a broken clock, a plastic plant that had been there since 1987, a framed print of a lighthouse that Clara had always found aggressively generic. Against the wall was the cedar chest.

She had been avoiding the cedar chest.

Donna had put no sticker on the cedar chest because Clara had told her not to. It was not for sale. Clara had said this firmly and Donna had accepted it and moved on, and Clara had felt, in that moment, that she had preserved something—some final inviolable claim on the house, some territory that the orange stickers could not colonize. The chest had been in this room for as long as she could remember. Before that it had been in her parents' bedroom, but after her father left, when Clara was nine and Philip was six, her mother had moved it here, and Clara had understood even then that this relocation meant something, that the chest had been demoted from the private geography of the marriage into the more general space of the house.

She sat down on the floor in front of it. The floorboards were the original ones, wide pine boards that creaked in the specific places she had always known them to creak. She put her hand on the lid of the chest.

The cedar smell when she lifted the lid was so complete, so undiminished by time, that she had to stop. There it was: cedar, and something faint beneath the cedar that was the smell of the chest itself, of whatever was stored in it, of years of careful closing and opening. Her mother had kept blankets in here, the good ones, the ones too nice for regular use. There was the quilt that Clara's grandmother had made, in colors that had once been bright and were now muted to something more beautiful. There was a wool blanket, dark green, that had come from somewhere—Scotland, her mother had said, though Clara didn't know if this was true, and now there was no one to ask. There was a small stack of Christmas tablecloths, folded precisely. There were, at the bottom, some things wrapped in tissue paper, and Clara carefully lifted these out.

She unwrapped the first one. It was a small glass bird, blue, with a tail that fanned out in an exaggerated way that was either artistic or simply inaccurate. She held it up to the light from the window. She had never seen this before. She had no memory of this bird, and it was difficult to understand how she had no memory of this bird, because she had grown up in this house and had presumably seen, over the course of eighteen years, everything that the house contained. But here was this bird, a complete surprise, small and blue and apparently hers to know now. She set it on the floor beside her.

There were two more packages. The second was a set of letters, bound with a rubber band that had become brittle and broke when she touched it. She looked at the first envelope. The handwriting was her mother's, and the letter was addressed to someone named Gerald, which was not her father's name. She looked at the second letter. Also Gerald. She looked at the postmarks: 1971. Her mother would have been—Clara did quick arithmetic—twenty-two. Unmarried. Before everything that had followed. She set the letters down beside the bird without opening them.

The third package contained nothing she could immediately identify—just a folded piece of paper with a shape cut out of it, a rough oval, and inside the fold, wrapped in the tissue, a pressed flower. The flower was too far gone to identify, just a dark stain in the shape of something that had once been alive. The paper had pencil writing on it, but the pencil had faded almost to nothing—she could make out what might have been a date, 1968, and below it something that ended in the word *always*, and that was all.

Clara sat with these three things arranged before her on the floorboards—the glass bird, the letters, the pressed flower—and felt the vertigo of encountering a stranger. This was the problem with grief, she thought, not for the first time but with a new precision: the dead don't stay still. You think you know who you are mourning and then you open a cedar chest and there is someone else, someone younger, someone who sent letters to a Gerald and pressed flowers in 1968 and kept a small blue bird for reasons that died with her. You mourn one person and discover you are also mourning all the others—all the versions of a person that preceded the person you knew, all the selves they inhabited before you arrived to witness them.

Her mother had been born in 1949. She had been born in a farmhouse outside Findlay, Ohio, the second of three children. She had graduated from high school in 1967 and had not gone to college, which she had not, as far as Clara could tell, resented, or had resented in a way she'd resolved long enough ago that it no longer showed. She had met Clara's father at a dance in 1972, had married him in 1974, had had Clara in 1971—

Clara stopped.

She looked at the letters again. 1971.

She picked up the first letter and held it without opening it. The envelope had never been sent. It was still sealed. She turned it over, looking at her mother's handwriting—the neat round hand that Clara had known all her life—and thought about the envelope as an object, thought about what it meant to address a letter and seal it and then not send it, to put it in a cedar chest and keep it for fifty years. You would have to mean to keep it. You couldn't not mean to keep it. It was a kind of preservation, a kind of insistence, addressed to a person who maybe never knew he'd been written to, preserved by a woman who had then married someone else and had children and lived a life in a small Ohio town and eventually died in February with an expression like she was working out a problem.

Clara set the letter down.

From the front of the house came voices, the sound of Donna making change, the door opening and closing. Somewhere outside a car engine started. The afternoon was going. The house was emptying. She could feel it in the way you feel a tide going out—not by watching the water recede but by the altered quality of the air, some reduction in pressure, something departing that had always been present.

She rewrapped the bird and the letters and the pressed flower in their tissue paper and placed them carefully at the bottom of the chest, beneath the grandmother's quilt, beneath the green wool blanket from maybe Scotland. She closed the lid. She sat for a moment with her hand on top of it, which felt foolish and also necessary. Then she got up.

The floorboards spoke in their old language as she walked back through the house. The sitting room, the dining room, the kitchen with its two-dollar bowl now gone. She paused at the kitchen table and put both hands flat on its surface. This table was the last thing she had any real difficulty about; it was the one thing she had considered, briefly, keeping, having shipped to Columbus, fitting somehow into her apartment and her different life. But Columbus was an apartment and the table required a house, required the specific conditions that had made it meaningful—the chair against the window where her mother had always sat, the window itself looking out onto the backyard where the elm tree had been before it had to be taken down. A table without its history was just a surface. She had told herself this and mostly believed it. She had let Donna sticker it at forty-five dollars and it had sold in the first hour.

She went out to the front porch. The afternoon light had moved, was lying longer and lower across the lawn. The wingback chair was gone. In the driveway, an SUV was being loaded with a floor lamp and several boxes of books, and Clara watched the man loading it—young, maybe thirty, in a flannel shirt, handling her mother's books with a casualness that she found she didn't mind. He didn't know whose they were. That was fine. That was actually fine. The books would go somewhere else and be read or not read and sit on someone else's shelves and it didn't diminish them and it didn't diminish her mother and this was something she was going to need to keep telling herself and eventually, she thought, she might believe it the way you believe things that are true.

Donna appeared at the door behind her. "We're winding down," she said, in the specific tone of someone managing another person's emotion from a professional distance, which was to say: kindly. "I'd say another hour. There's some furniture left that didn't move. I have a few contacts if you want to donate, or there's an auction house in Newark."

"That's fine," Clara said. "Whatever's easiest."

"The garage mostly cleared out. The boys bought the power tools."

"Good."

Donna went back inside. Clara stood at the top of the porch steps and looked at the yard, which needed mowing—she would have to arrange for that, or perhaps not, perhaps that would be the new owners' problem, the house was already under contract—and at the elm tree's absence, which she still noticed even though the tree had been gone for eleven years, a gap in the sightline that her eye still tried to fill. She had told her mother, at Thanksgiving the year before last, that she still saw the tree. I know, her mother had said. I see it too. It's all right to see things that aren't there anymore.

The man with the books was in his car now, backing out of the driveway. From inside came the sound of someone buying something, a brief negotiation, the snap of the cashbox.

Clara stood in the October light and thought about Gerald, who had received no letters, who may have been wondering about her mother for fifty years or may have forgotten her entirely, who was alive somewhere or was not. She thought about the glass bird, its improbable tail. She thought about her mother at twenty-two, alone with a sealed envelope, deciding not to send it, tucking it away with the same careful hands that would later wash plates with a blue-line pattern, that would later hold Clara's hands on the steering bar of a lawnmower, that would later fold the good blankets and lay them in the cedar chest and close the lid.

It occurred to her, standing there, that this was what she had actually come to do today. Not the sale, not the orange stickers, not the decisions about the auction house in Newark. She had come because the house required a witness. Someone who had lived here needed to stand in the doorway while the furniture left, needed to hold the space between what it had been and what it was becoming, needed to stay until the staying was done.

She would stay until the staying was done.

Inside, Donna was counting the cashbox. The light came low and flat across the lawn where the elm tree was not. A car slowed on the street, a late arrival reading the sign, and then pulled up to the curb, and a woman got out and looked at the house with the considering expression of someone who is about to enter a stranger's history, who is about to pay a few dollars to take a piece of it home, and Clara watched her coming up the walk and moved aside to let her in.
