The tide had gone out far enough that the mud showed its back like something ashamed, and Maren stood at the end of the dock with her hands in her coat pockets watching her grandfather read the surface of the harbor the way she had seen him read nothing else—not a newspaper, not a menu, not the faces of people who spoke to him. He was seventy-one and his hands had the look of things long worked in salt. His name was Einar but she had never once called him that.

"Come here," he said, without turning.

She was fourteen and had been sent for the winter by her mother, who needed, as she put it, a different kind of quiet than the kind Maren made. There had been a scene. There had been several scenes. The details are not important except to say that Maren had learned, in the course of them, that her mother was not as steady as she had always appeared, which was a knowledge that had weight to it, like a stone carried in the chest.

She walked to where he stood. The dock planks were dark with moisture and between them she could see the water moving, pewter-colored, slow.

"What do you see?" he said.

"Water."

"What kind?"

She looked. It was December and the harbor was cupped between two points of land that had been logged a hundred years ago and grown back in a different way, the new trees thinner and crowded and without the authority of the old ones. The town of Haskell sat at the head of the harbor and from here she could see the cannery on the eastern shore, its corrugated roof, its pipe that no longer smoked, its parking lot with three trucks in it where there had once been, according to everyone who mentioned it, so many more.

"Gray water," she said. "Cold water."

"It's telling you something."

"It's telling me it's cold."

He almost smiled. He had a face that had been handsome once and was now something better than handsome, eroded into a kind of clean usefulness, like a tool that has been handled so long it has taken the shape of a specific hand. "The chop," he said. "Look at the chop."

She looked. There was a small irregular roughness on the surface, not waves exactly, more like the water was thinking about something and couldn't settle.

"Wind's turning," he said. "It's been coming out of the northwest all day but in an hour it'll come from the south. The chop is confused because the water remembers the old direction and the air is already moving in the new one. The surface is the argument between them."

Maren looked at the water again. She had not thought of it as an argument. She had thought of it as nothing, as background, as the thing that the town sat beside and smelled of and was defined by but that you did not actually look at unless you were going to be sick into it. She had grown up three hours inland and visited here summers but summers were a different education.

"What do you do with that?" she asked. "Knowing about the wind."

"You plan. You know what's coming before it comes. You've got an hour."

"And then what?"

"Then you're no longer surprised." He turned from the water finally and looked at her. "That's the whole business. Learning not to be surprised."

She thought about her mother and said nothing.

---

She had been enrolled in the local school but it was the last week before the winter break and the teacher, a young woman named Ms. Ferretti who wore large earrings and seemed slightly bewildered by the permanence of her posting here, had given up on anything resembling curriculum and was showing films. Maren sat in the back and watched the other students, twelve of them across three grades combined in the one secondary classroom, and tried to locate herself in relation to them. They were not unfriendly. They were simply accustomed to a texture of life that was not her texture, and she was not yet sure whether to admire or pity this, which meant she probably admired it.

The boy who sat nearest her was named Dex and he had the particular composure of someone who had never had to perform for a larger audience. He told her on the second day that his father worked at the cannery, quality control, and that after Christmas there would not be a cannery anymore.

"What will he do?" she asked.

Dex shrugged in a way that was not indifferent but was practiced against indifference. "Something else," he said. "There's always something else." He said this like it was a thing he'd heard enough times that he'd begun to believe it, which was not the same as it being true.

That evening she asked her grandfather about the cannery.

He was at the kitchen table with his charts. He kept the nautical charts even though he had been retired for six years, had stopped piloting the deep-draft ships through the channel when his eyesight in the left eye began to go, and the charts were both professional artifact and private comfort, she understood, the way some people keep photographs of places they no longer live. He was marking something with a pencil, very lightly, the way you'd mark something you weren't sure of.

"Sixty years," he said. "Opened the year my father came here."

"Did he work there?"

"He did everything here. There wasn't enough of any one thing to do only that." He set down the pencil. "The fish changed. That's the beginning of it. But it was going before the fish changed. The market changed. The way people eat. It's a hundred reasons that are really one reason."

"What's the one reason?"

He thought about this. "That things have a length," he said finally. "That nothing is permanent just because it was here before you were."

Maren looked at the chart on the table. It showed the harbor and the channel and the approach from the sea, with its depths marked in fathoms, its hazards annotated in a small precise script that she recognized now as her grandfather's. He had written on his own charts. He had made them more specific with his own knowledge.

"Can I go out with you?" she asked. "On the water."

"It's December."

"I know what month it is."

He looked at her for a long moment. "Tomorrow," he said. "Early."

---

The boat was a eighteen-foot aluminum skiff that he kept at the town float and it smelled of diesel and old brine and something underneath those smells that was just the smell of water itself, the mineral cold of it, which Maren found she did not mind. He had given her a pair of rubber bibs that were too large and a wool sweater that had been her father's, or so she assumed from the way he'd taken it from the chest in the spare room without saying whose it was.

Her father had left when she was four and she had a child's memory of him, which is to say she had images without narrative, a hand, a particular green jacket, the sound of a door. Her mother did not discuss him in a way that suggested there was much to discuss, only that discussion would not help.

They went out on a gray morning, the harbor flat and pewter as it had been, the sky the same shade as the water so that the horizon was more assumed than seen. Her grandfather ran the motor and she sat in the bow and watched the town recede. The cannery slid past on the eastern shore, close enough that she could see the yellow forklifts through the open bay doors, moving slowly, purposelessly it seemed, as if they too were uncertain about what came next.

Past the harbor mouth the water changed. It had texture now, a long low swell from the southwest, and the skiff moved with it in a way that was not unpleasant, a lifting and settling, lifting and settling. Her grandfather cut the motor.

In the silence she became aware of the water differently. The sounds it made against the hull. The way the swell had a period to it, a rhythm that was its own kind of sentence.

"The swell," he said. "What does it tell you?"

She was getting used to this, the way he taught by question, which she had at first found evasive and now understood was the only honest method, since the answers were not his to give.

"It's coming from far away," she said. This was a guess.

"How do you know?"

She thought. "Because it's long. The distance between the peaks. If it was from somewhere close it would be—shorter? Choppier?"

"Yes." He sounded neither pleased nor displeased, just attentive, which was its own encouragement. "A long swell means a long fetch. It's traveled hundreds of miles. There's a storm out there." He pointed to the southwest, to the place where water and sky merged without distinction. "Maybe two days out. Maybe a day and a half."

She looked at the unremarkable gray horizon. "You can see that from this?"

"The water remembers. Everything that happened to it, it carries." He sat on the thwart with his hands loose between his knees. "The surface is always the history of the surface."

She turned this over. A herring gull passed low overhead and screamed at them and was gone, and the silence that followed felt larger than the silence before.

"There's color, too," he said. "In summer it's easier. Right now—" he pointed, "—you see the line there, maybe a quarter mile out, where it's a little darker?"

She looked. She might have seen it. She chose to believe she saw it.

"That's where the cold water comes up from below. Upwelling. The bottom here is about forty fathoms, but there's a shelf further out, and when the current runs over the edge of the shelf it pulls cold water up. Cold water is dark. Warmer water is lighter, more green. The fish know this." He paused. "The fish know everything about the water. We only know what we can read from the outside."

Maren sat with this. The idea of the fish down there below her, in their complete literacy. "Is that how you piloted?" she asked. "Reading it?"

"Reading it, yes. But also knowing. It's different. Reading is what you do when you're learning. Knowing is what you have after long enough that you've stopped reading and started feeling."

"How long does that take?"

He looked at the water. A muscle moved in his jaw. "Too long for me to have done anything else," he said.

She understood that this was not self-pity but simply accounting, the accurate ledger of a life, and she did not try to argue with it.

They drifted for a while. The swell lifted them and set them down and lifted them again. Twice she thought she saw the shadow of something large below them and once she was sure of it, a darkness that moved with intent, changed direction, was gone.

"Grandfather."

"Seal," he said, without looking. "Maybe two of them. They've been following us since we left the harbor mouth."

She looked astern and saw nothing. "How do you know?"

"Because they always do."

She laughed, and she could not have said why exactly the laugh came then. He looked at her with something that required a name she didn't have for it and then looked away.

---

Christmas came and the school closed and the three weeks of break opened around her like a room she had not expected. Her grandfather rose each morning at five and she began to rise with him, which surprised her in retrospect more than it seemed to at the time. They ate oatmeal with canned milk. They walked to the dock before the light. They went out when the weather allowed, which was not every day.

On the days they could not go out she learned the charts. He spread them on the kitchen table and she learned to read the depth soundings, the contour lines of the seafloor, which she came to think of as a landscape seen from above but from the wrong above, from below. She learned the marks for ledges, for rocks that showed at low tide and hid at high, for the wrecks that were on the charts as cautionary notations in that small precise hand.

"This one," she said, pointing to a notation in the channel.

"Steam freighter," he said. "1931. Coal delivery. The pilot misjudged the set of the current on the ebb. She went over the ledge and broke up on the bottom."

"People died?"

"Three. The rest got off."

She looked at the small annotation. Two letters and a depth sounding and a year. "And you knew this when you were piloting through here?"

"You know everything that's happened in a channel when you're piloting it. The history is part of the navigation. You're not just moving through space, you're moving through time."

She thought she understood this. She wrote it in the notebook she had begun keeping, without exactly deciding to begin keeping it, one evening after she found her grandfather's old logbooks in the spare room. They were not dramatic documents, those logs. They were recording: dates, vessels, conditions, remarks. She was struck by the absence of anything personal in them, and then gradually came to understand that the professional precision was a form of intimacy she didn't have a name for yet.

Dex came by one afternoon between Christmas and New Year. He appeared at the door without calling first, which seemed to be the custom here, and her grandfather let him in and did not remark on the visit one way or another, only went back to the table with his charts.

They sat in the kitchen and Dex drank coffee, which she could not yet manage, and told her his father had gotten a job offer in Aberdeen, working a fish processing plant there, and that they would probably take it.

"Aberdeen," she said.

"It's still fish," he said, in the tone of someone who has decided not to be sad about a thing.

"Is that okay?"

He looked out the window at the harbor. A trawler was working its way in, low in the water, and he tracked it without turning his head, the way her grandfather tracked things, with a peripheral attention that was not distraction but its opposite, a kind of broadened awareness that she was beginning to understand was specific to people who lived near water. "My dad says the hard thing isn't leaving a place," he said. "It's that the place keeps existing after you leave. That's the part you can't get used to."

Maren thought about her mother's apartment, the way it continued to exist with or without her in it, continuing its own life, the coffee maker and the particular light through the kitchen window and the sound of the street. She thought about how the apartment did not miss her.

"He's right," she said.

Dex finished his coffee. He looked at the charts on the table where her grandfather sat and said, "He teaching you the harbor?"

"He's teaching me to read the water," she said, and was surprised to hear herself say it exactly that way.

Dex nodded as though this was a known and sufficient thing. "My grandfather tried to teach me," he said. "I didn't have the patience for it then. I was like ten." He said this without regret, or with regret that had been lived with long enough to become something else. "You should learn it while you can."

After he left her grandfather looked up from the chart. "Good family," he said. Which was the entirety of what he said about it.

---

The last week of January, the cannery closed. Not with announcement or ceremony but with a subtraction: the trucks that had been in the parking lot were gone, the bay doors shut, the forklifts presumably sold or removed in the night like they had never been. The pipe on the roof had not smoked all winter but its not-smoking was different now, was definitive.

She heard about it from Ms. Ferretti, who said it with a careful neutrality that made clear she had been thinking about how to say it. Several of the kids whose parents had worked there were absent that day. Dex was present and sat in the same composed way, looking at his desk, and Maren did not try to say anything to him because she didn't have anything that was better than silence.

That afternoon she went to find her grandfather and he was not on the dock where she expected him. She found him on the breakwater, sitting on the stone with his back to the town, looking at the harbor. The cold was thorough and the sky was the particular white that precedes snow.

She sat beside him.

After a time he said: "Your great-grandfather worked thirty years in that building. My father. He came here from Norway without anything and he smelled like fish for thirty years and he considered that a good life."

"Did you?"

"I consider his life was his to consider."

She thought about this.

"Did you ever want to leave?" she asked. "When you were young. Did you want to go somewhere else?"

He looked at the harbor for a long time. The water was very still, the stillness before weather, and even the gulls had gone somewhere, leaving the air above the water empty and waiting. "I wanted what everyone wants," he said. "Which is to be elsewhere and here at the same time. To have left and stayed. I don't know anyone who's solved that problem."

Snow came that evening, light and purposeful, and lay on the dock boards and on the gunwales of the boats in the float and on the rooftop of the cannery which was dark now for the first time in sixty years. Maren stood at the living room window and watched it. Behind her her grandfather was on the phone, a short conversation in a low voice, and when he hung up he said, "Your mother."

She turned.

"She wants to know how you are."

"What did you tell her?"

He considered. "I told her you were learning something."

Maren looked back at the snow. Her mother was three hours away and the snow was falling there too probably, or different snow, or the same cold front expressing itself differently over different ground. Her mother had her own weather. Everyone did. The question was whether you could read it.

"Will you teach me the rest of it?" Maren asked. "The channel. The whole harbor."

"That takes years."

"I know."

"You won't be here years."

She turned from the window. Her grandfather was standing with his hands at his sides and his face had the look it got when he was reading something difficult, attending to it carefully and without assumption.

"I can come back," she said.

He did not point out the contingencies of this, all the reasonable objections, the way life arranges itself to prevent the things we mean to do. He only looked at her and she understood that the looking was a kind of belief, and belief was as much as anyone could offer.

They went out the next morning in the snow, which had stopped overnight but left a white quiet over everything, the town, the harbor, the thin ice at the edges of the float. The water was very dark against all that white and the swell from the south was long and slow, the storm having passed or turned, its memory moving through the water in that patient diminishing way.

Her grandfather cut the engine and they drifted.

"Look," he said.

She looked. The water was dark and cold and moving in its complex slow grammar and underneath it the seals were somewhere, probably, and the fish in their cold literacy, and the old freighter on the bottom in forty fathoms with its three names on the chart, and the channels that generations of pilots had memorized and the shelf where the cold water came up and the line where dark met light. She looked at all of it, the surface of it, which was an argument and a memory and a record and a live and present thing simultaneously.

"What does it tell you?" he asked.

She understood that this was not a test. It was an offering. He was giving her the question the way you give someone a tool they might not need for years but should have in hand regardless, should know the weight of, should learn the particular grip.

"That it's always moving," she said. "Even when it looks still."

He didn't say yes or no. He looked at the water with her.

The snow on the dock behind them was already losing its edges in the morning light, the white becoming gray becoming the color of the planks beneath, and the cannery on the eastern shore stood in its new and permanent quiet, and the harbor held the reflection of the sky the way it had always held it, faithfully and without preference, taking in whatever light was given and returning it slightly changed.
