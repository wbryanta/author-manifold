The price tags were Loretta's idea. Small squares of masking tape with numbers written in black marker, and Celestine had let her do it, had stood at the kitchen doorway watching Loretta move through the house with her roll of tape and her Sharpie like she was labeling specimens in a laboratory, like the pressed-glass candy dish and the cedar chest and the two end tables with their matching lamps were things that needed identifying, needed to be told what they were worth before anyone could agree. Fifty dollars on the cedar chest. Ten on each lamp. Two-fifty on the dining room table that Celestine's mother had refinished herself three times in forty years, going down to the wood each time with steel wool and patience and something that smelled of lemons and mineral spirits, and Celestine had not argued about the price or about anything. She had nodded and said, yes, that sounds right, that sounds about right, and driven out here alone this morning while the fog was still sitting in the low places along Route 9.

Now it was afternoon and strangers were in the house.

A man in a Cardinals cap was turning over the blue mixing bowl, reading whatever he thought he'd find on the bottom. A woman had been standing in front of the linen closet for ten minutes, running her fingers across the folded edges of towels the way you'd read Braille, and Celestine watched her from across the hall and thought, those were her good towels, she only took those out for company, she would not have wanted you touching them that way, but she said nothing because the woman had a right to touch them, that was what a sale meant, that was what she had agreed to when she'd agreed to the masking tape and the Sharpie and the signs that Loretta's son had taped to telephone poles all the way up Main Street.

Estate Sale, the signs said. Everything Must Go.

Everything must go. She had read those words on the sign hammered into her mother's front yard without registering them as language for a moment, the way you stop understanding a word if you say it too many times, and then they had landed. Everything. The word like a stone dropped into deep water, going down and down and still going.

Her mother had been dead eleven weeks. The house had been sitting here all eleven weeks, doing what houses do when no one comes. Settling. Going a little colder in the rooms no one enters. The refrigerator still ran. Celestine had found that out the second week, had let herself in with the key she'd had since she was nineteen and heard the refrigerator humming away in the kitchen, faithful as a dog, keeping cold a stick of butter and two cans of ginger ale and a Tupperware container of something she did not open. She had stood in the kitchen that day for a long time, listening to the refrigerator and to the particular quality of silence that collects in a house that is waiting.

Now the silence was gone. There were voices in every room.

A teenage boy had been carrying things to the front porch to show his mother. First the brass magazine rack, then the small footstool with the needlepoint cover that Celestine's mother had made herself over the course of a winter, a rooster and some stylized wheat, in colors that had faded from what they were. The boy held it up to show his mother and the mother shook her head, and the boy brought it back inside and set it down somewhere, not where it had been, and moved on. Celestine watched this. She thought she should probably position herself near the door, the way Loretta had suggested, make herself available to take money, to make change, to answer questions. She had a metal box with a handle on it, the kind of box she associated with church fundraisers and school carnivals, and she was carrying it with both hands in front of her like an offering.

She had come into the dining room and stopped.

The table was still there. A couple, middle-aged, were standing on either side of it with their palms flat on the surface, pressing down, testing the sturdiness of the thing, and Celestine thought of her mother's hands on that same surface, pressing the same way, but not testing, just resting, the way her mother would stand at the table sometimes at the end of a long day and rest her hands on it and look out the window at the backyard, at the ash tree and the bird feeder and whatever was out there. Standing between the table and the window and resting. And Celestine had come in sometimes, as a girl, as a teenager, as a grown woman home for a visit, and found her there, and it had never seemed to require comment, and so she had never commented on it, and now there was no one to comment to, and the couple on either side of the table were murmuring to each other about whether it would fit.

It will fit, Celestine thought. It's not as big as it looks.

The woman said this to her husband, or whoever he was, and they nodded at each other, and then they looked at Celestine with the expression people got at estate sales, a carefully assembled expression that was trying to be several things at once — sympathetic without being sentimental, interested without being greedy, casual without being dismissive. She had seen this expression all day. It was the expression of people who understood they were in a dead woman's house and wanted to acknowledge that fact without dwelling on it, because dwelling on it would ruin the sale for everyone, would make the whole afternoon into something other than what they needed it to be.

How much for the table, the woman asked.

Two-fifty, Celestine said. It includes the chairs.

They looked at each other again. They were going to buy it. She could see that they were going to buy it and she felt something she could not have named exactly — not grief, she had been doing grief for eleven weeks and knew what that felt like, this was something else, something with a different texture, something that ran alongside grief the way a creek runs alongside a road, close and parallel and occasionally crossing.

We'll take it, the woman said.

All right, Celestine said.

She opened her money box and made change for three hundred dollars, and the man tucked the receipt into his shirt pocket and they went off to find out how to arrange for pickup, and Celestine stood at the table alone for a moment with her hands on it. The surface was smooth where her mother had refinished it and slightly rough at the edges where she hadn't reached, where the old varnish was cracked and lifting. She could feel both kinds of surface at once, the smooth and the rough, and she stood there with her hands on her mother's table until she heard someone asking about the lamps.

There had been a moment, two months ago, when Celestine had thought she might keep the house. This was not a practical thought and she had known it was not a practical thought even at the time. She lived in Columbus, had lived there twenty-some years, had her work there, her apartment, the particular routines of her life, the Kroger where she shopped and the park where she walked on Sunday mornings and the two or three friends who knew her well enough to call without a reason. She had no cause to come back to Dennisville, had not had cause in years, had come back for Christmas and for the particular family occasions that required presence, and even those had been difficult in the way that home is difficult when it no longer fits you the way it once did, when you've grown in directions the house did not account for.

But she had thought about keeping it. She had stood in the kitchen that second week, listening to the refrigerator, and thought: I could come back here. She had thought this the way you think about the lives you are not living, with a mixture of longing and relief, understanding that the longing is for the life and the relief is for not having to live it. She could come back here and refinish the table herself and keep the pressed-glass dish on the kitchen counter where her mother had always kept it and fill the bird feeder that had been empty since October, and there was a version of herself in which this was not a fantasy but a possibility, a genuine fork in the road still available to her.

But the house needed a new roof. The inspector had told her this. The inspector had told her this and other things in the flat, careful voice of someone who has learned to deliver bad news in a way that sounds like information, and Celestine had listened and taken notes on the back of the grocery receipt she'd had in her pocket, and driven back to Columbus that night and called the realtor the next morning.

The roof and the furnace and the windows. The windows on the south side had lost their seal, the inspector said, you can see the fogging between the panes, and Celestine had looked at those windows and seen for the first time what she had been looking at without seeing her entire life, the faint milkiness in the double glass, the way the light came through slightly diffused, slightly softened. She thought about her mother looking out through those windows for years. Looking out at the ash tree and the bird feeder through that softened, fogged light, seeing everything slightly blurred, slightly gentle, and whether her mother had known the windows were failing or whether she'd simply seen the world that way because that was how the world presented itself to her — Celestine couldn't know. Couldn't ask. The elevator, as Loretta liked to say, no longer goes to that floor.

Loretta had been saying things like that for years. Loretta was her mother's younger sister and had been saying practical things in a kind way for as long as Celestine could remember, had always been the one who could take something terrible and hold it at the right angle so it looked manageable. It was Loretta who had called Celestine the morning her mother fell the first time. Celestine, she'd said, your mother had a little tumble. I'm at the hospital with her now, she's all right, we just want to make sure everything's where it should be. A little tumble, as though her mother had gone off a step and collected herself and was already a little embarrassed about the whole thing. And then the next year, Loretta again: Celestine, your mother had another little tumble, and this time we're going to talk about some things together. Together, Loretta had said, because Loretta knew how to make it so no one had to be alone in the deciding.

But Celestine had felt alone anyway. Had felt alone in the way you feel alone in the presence of a fact that belongs specifically to you, that has your name on it and no one else's, that says: you are the one she named on all the forms, you are the only child, this is the thing you came from and now you are watching it go. There was a man in the living room now who was looking at her mother's television as though it might give him trouble, lifting the cords and examining the back of the set, and Celestine thought: she watched her programs on that. She watched her programs every night in that chair and she liked the shows about people living together in houses and cooking things and you found that sentimental and you said so once and she never mentioned it again. She never said, come watch with me, she never said, sit down, she just watched her programs alone in the evenings and now a man is going to put your mother's television in his truck and take it somewhere else and it will show other programs in another room and that is simply what is going to happen.

Twenty dollars, she told him. For the television.

He seemed surprised. He had expected to negotiate.

It works fine, she said. She watched it every night.

He paid and she made change and he went to find his son to help him carry it, and Celestine went to the kitchen and stood there looking at the pressed-glass candy dish on the counter. Ten dollars on the masking tape. She peeled the tape off slowly, the adhesive pulling against the glass and releasing with a faint, soft sound. She put the tape in her pocket. She set the dish back on the counter.

She was going to keep the dish.

She stood at the kitchen window and looked at the backyard. The ash tree had the grayish, stripped look of November, its branches laid bare and patient against the white sky. The bird feeder was a wooden thing her father had made thirty years ago, shaped like a little house, with a pointed roof and a small platform and a hole for the birds to put their heads in, and it was empty and had been empty a long time, and the wood had weathered past the point of paint, and still it hung there from the ash tree on its wire, still shaped like a house.

Her father had been gone sixteen years. She had done this before, in another version, in another house, at another point in her life when she had thought she understood loss and had not. You think each time that you understand it and each time there is more of it than you thought, more texture and more rooms, and you go through those rooms for a while and then you come out the other side and you live your life and you think: yes, all right, yes, and then there is more.

She heard a sound from the hallway, something being set down too hard, a flat impact against the wooden floor, and she went to look.

A small child, three or four, had dropped a glass figurine. The child looked at the figurine and then looked at Celestine with enormous eyes, preparing for the various possibilities of what might come next. The figurine was a horse, pale green glass, about four inches tall. It was intact. It had been on the windowsill in the hallway for Celestine's entire life, or nearly, had come from somewhere she no longer knew, had simply always been there in the way that certain things are simply always there, so constant they precede memory, so familiar they have become part of the architecture of your knowing.

The child began to cry. Not loudly but steadily, the way small children cry when they've frightened themselves, when the thing that happened is less important than the feeling of it happening.

Celestine knelt down, which was a thing her knees registered. She picked up the horse and held it out to the child.

Look, she said. It's fine. It didn't break.

The child looked at the horse. The child's mother appeared from the living room with the particular velocity of a parent who has heard their child go quiet in a way that means something, and she looked at the figurine and then at Celestine with the assembled expression, the careful expression, and said, I'm so sorry, he got away from me, did he —

It's fine, Celestine said. Nothing broke.

She held the horse for a moment after the mother took the child away. The glass was cool and smooth, slightly thick the way old glass is, the green deep in it rather than on it, a color that came from inside the material. Her mother had dusted it. Had picked it up carefully every week or whenever she cleaned and held it the way Celestine was holding it now and set it back in its place. Had done this for years, for decades, had held this small cool thing in her hands and put it back in the light, and it was ten dollars on the masking tape and Celestine was going to keep this too.

She went back to the kitchen with the horse and set it next to the candy dish and looked at them together on the counter, the pressed green glass of the dish and the pale green glass of the horse, and they looked like they had always belonged together, which perhaps they had and she had never seen it, had never stood in this kitchen and looked at these two things at the same time. You don't look, when you live inside a life. You inhabit it, which is different. You move through it the way you move through your own house in the dark, by memory and by feel, and you don't look until the lights come on and then there is too much to look at.

Out front a man and his son were trying to get the cedar chest through the front door and it was not going easily. Celestine could hear them, could hear the grunting effort of it and then the instruction and then the turning of the thing at an angle, and she stood in the kitchen listening to her mother's cedar chest being negotiated through her mother's front door and she breathed. The chest had been in her mother's bedroom. Had held things, had held the wedding tablecloth her mother had received from her own mother and the christening gown that had been used twice, for Celestine and for a cousin's child twenty years later, and the photographs that her mother had not put in albums but had kept loose in a shoebox inside the chest, layered there like sediment. Those things were gone now, boxed and in Celestine's car, and the chest was empty and was going out the front door and she breathed.

She had gone through the shoebox last month. Had sat on her mother's bedroom floor with the chest open in front of her and the shoebox in her lap and gone through the photographs in the order they came, which was no order at all, which was the order of accumulation, the order of being kept rather than arranged. Her mother at twenty, her mother at fifty, her parents on their wedding day and then again somewhere with snow, her father laughing at something outside the frame, her as a child she barely recognized, her as a teenager she recognized too well, her mother's sisters, her mother's mother, her mother's mother's face looking up at her from the floor of her mother's bedroom with the eyes Celestine saw in her own mirror every morning, the same set to the jaw, the same way of looking at something directly that could seem like severity if you didn't know it was just attention.

She had sat there a long time on the floor.

The cedar chest had made it through the door. She heard the sound of it change, the acoustic shift from inside to outside, and then the voices of the men receding down the front walk.

A woman appeared in the kitchen doorway. She was about Celestine's age, maybe a few years younger, and she had the look of someone who had been to many of these sales, who knew how they worked, who understood the protocol, and she held up a small ceramic pot with a lid, cream-colored with a blue rim.

How much for this? she asked.

Celestine looked at the pot. There was no price on it. Loretta had missed it, or it had been somewhere else and had migrated, or it was simply a thing that had not been priced.

I don't know, Celestine said. What do you think it's worth?

The woman looked at it. Studied it in the way people study things when they're trying to be honest.

Five dollars, the woman said. Maybe a little more.

Then give me five, Celestine said.

The woman opened her purse. She had a kind face, the kind of face that had been pretty when young and was now something better, more formed, something that had taken on the shape of what its owner had lived through. She held out a five and then she hesitated.

Was it your mother's house? she asked.

Yes, Celestine said.

The woman looked at the ceramic pot and then at Celestine and then at the counter where the candy dish and the green glass horse sat together.

I lost mine last year, the woman said.

The simplicity of this. Lost mine. As though they were objects that had been set down somewhere and not yet recovered. As though there were a room somewhere full of mothers, waiting to be found.

I know, Celestine said. I know.

The woman nodded and took her pot and went. Celestine stood in the kitchen. Through the window the ash tree was still, and the bird feeder still hung from its wire, and the white sky behind it held its color without apology. By three o'clock most of the furniture would be gone and she would walk through these rooms that were her mother's rooms and that would no longer be her mother's rooms, and she would carry out two green glass objects and a shoebox of photographs and a tablecloth folded into a square, and she would drive back to Columbus in the dark, and the house would be sold, and the ash tree would be someone else's ash tree, and she would go on, which is what you do, which is the only thing, which is what her mother had done and her mother's mother before her, going on through the rooms of the world with whatever you could carry, moving through the dark by memory, by feel, by the particular knowledge of where things are even when you cannot see them.

She stood at the window. The metal box was heavy in her hands.

Outside, two men carried her mother's dining room table down the front walk, one at each end, careful with it on the steps, taking their time.
