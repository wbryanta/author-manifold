The pipe had burst sometime in the night, and by the time Cass found it the alfalfa at the west edge of the property had already gone yellow at the tips, the way alfalfa does when it's been holding its breath too long. He stood in the furrow looking at the break—a clean split along the seam, the kind of thing that happened when the soil shifted, when January frost crept deeper than it should have and the ground remembered it all summer long by heaving imperceptibly, grinding on whatever you'd buried. He walked back to the equipment shed and started pulling fittings from the coffee can on the shelf, not counting them, just letting his hands do the sorting the way they always had.

He heard Marcus's truck before he saw it. The particular complaint of that engine—a 2003 Silverado with a cracked manifold his brother had been meaning to fix for four years—was as familiar to Cass as any voice. He didn't go out to meet him. He heard the door and then the footsteps on the gravel and then Marcus was standing in the shed doorway with his coffee still in his hand, wearing the kind of clean jeans that told you he'd driven from somewhere with a closet and a mirror.

"I saw the alfalfa," Marcus said.

"Pipe broke."

"Yeah." Marcus looked at the fittings in Cass's hand. "Where?"

"West line. Maybe forty feet past the standpipe."

Marcus set his coffee on the workbench and looked around the shed in that way he had, the cataloguing look, like he was pricing things out. He picked up a coupling and turned it in his fingers. Cass noticed that his hands had softened. Not gone soft entirely—Marcus had spent enough years on this ground that the structure was still there, the width of the palm, the particular thickness of the thumb—but the callus had thinned. The skin had a kind of indoor quality to it now.

"You want to get the trencher or go by hand?" Marcus asked.

"Trencher's down. Belt broke in April. I've been meaning—"

"By hand then."

They took the two good spades from against the wall and the pipe cement and the fittings and loaded everything into the wheelbarrow. Cass added the pipe cutter and the level and a length of half-inch flex he'd been saving for exactly this kind of repair, the length he'd bought three summers ago when he'd fixed the east line and had enough left over to coil on a nail. He pushed the wheelbarrow and Marcus walked beside him and neither of them spoke for a while, which was the shape their silences had always taken—not hostile, not comfortable exactly, just the silence of two people who shared a particular history and were in no hurry to start talking across it.

The morning was already hot. By seven-thirty the light had that flat white quality that meant by afternoon it would be brutal, the kind of June day the valley manufactured casually and in quantity, as though heat were a crop that never failed. The trees on either side of them stood in their rows with their small unripe fruit, still green and hard, weeks away from anything useful. Cass could read the trees the way other people read faces. That one with the slightly grayed leaves, the one Marcus wouldn't notice—he'd need to check the roots for crown rot. The one at the end of row seven had been leaning incrementally into the row for two seasons; he'd need to stake it by August or lose it to its own weight in the first winter storm.

He knew every one of them. He knew them the way you knew people you'd watched age.

"How long are you here?" Cass asked.

"Through tomorrow. I need to be back Thursday."

"Teresa?"

"Teresa's fine. The kids are good. Jacob lost a tooth."

"That's something."

"He was very proud of it."

They reached the break and Cass set the wheelbarrow down and stepped into the furrow to show Marcus where the split ran. The pipe was old, PVC gone brittle at the edges, the split about eight inches long and puckered open like a mouth. Water had been bleeding into the soil at the base and there was a soft dark stain in the earth all around it, surprisingly lush, a little ecosystem of dampness the rest of the orchard didn't know about.

"Clean break at least," Marcus said.

"It's the whole line," Cass said. "This is just where it finally went. I've been watching it since March."

"Then it'll break again."

"Yes."

"So we patch it now and replace the whole line in the fall?"

Cass didn't answer. The fall was where he and his brother diverged. The fall was the whole argument.

Marcus picked up a spade and started breaking ground six inches to the left of the break, moving with the automatic efficiency of someone who had done this particular thing enough times that his body kept the memory even if his daily life had moved on from it. Cass started from the other side and they worked toward each other in a slow rhythm, trading shovel-loads out of the furrow, the smell of the opened earth rising up between them—that dense mineral smell, the iron in it, the residue of twenty years of water and root and patient decomposition. Their father had always said this valley had the best topsoil in the state and it might have been true, though Cass had come to understand that best topsoil and viable operation were not the same thing, were in fact different categories that overlapped only under certain conditions, and those conditions had been leaving for a long time.

"I had another call from Delacroix," Marcus said. He said it carefully, with the controlled flatness of someone trying to give information rather than an argument.

"I know. He left a message here too."

"You didn't call him back."

"No."

Marcus turned a shovelful. "He's offering one-eight now. That's up from one-six in February."

"I know what he's offering."

"It's a good offer, Cass."

"I know it's a good offer."

"Then why—"

"I didn't say I didn't know it was a good offer. I said I know what he's offering."

Marcus stopped digging and stood up straight and looked at his brother across the partial trench. He had their father's face, or a version of it—same horizontal brow, same tendency in the jaw—but he'd grown a beard in the last few years that Cass didn't understand, that seemed to be an artifact of Marcus's other life, his Reno life, and it sat on his face like an accessory. Their father would never have grown a beard. Their father had shaved every morning of his life until the last two weeks of it, when he'd been too weak to hold the razor.

"Dad didn't want this place to drain us," Marcus said.

"That's not what he said."

"It's what he meant."

"I know what he meant. I was there."

"I was there too, Cass."

"You were there at the end. I was here for the whole thing."

Marcus went back to digging. That last part had been a cruelty and Cass knew it and didn't retract it. The accusation had been available to him for six years and he'd used it now as he sometimes did, as a way of drawing the map of things—who had stayed, who had gone, who had carried what—even though he was aware that the map was unfair, that Marcus had built a life at some real cost to himself, that leaving had not been simple or entirely voluntary, that their father in his own inarticulate way had pushed Marcus toward the door because he'd understood that two sons and one failing orchard was a tragedy of a specific and avoidable kind.

None of that changed the fact that Cass had been here. It didn't change the weight of it.

They worked in silence until the pipe was exposed on both sides, a gray length of it lying in its bed of turned earth like something skeletal. Cass crouched down and marked his cut lines with a grease pencil, measuring twice with the kind of precision that was also stubbornness—the insistence on doing the thing right even when the thing might not matter for long. He fitted the cutter and began to score the pipe in a slow rotation.

"It's not just the money," Marcus said from above him. "It's—Cass, you're working yourself to nothing. I can see it. You look ten years older than you did at Christmas."

"I'm fine."

"You lost weight."

"It's summer."

"You lost weight at Christmas too."

Cass turned the cutter. The plastic gave with a soft squeak. "You should see me in a bathing suit," he said, and Marcus made a sound that was almost a laugh, the laugh they'd had as boys, the one that came out before the argument could catch it.

"Mom is worried," Marcus said.

"Mom worries. That's what she does."

"She says you don't come in on Sundays anymore."

"I'll come in."

"She's seventy-three."

"I know how old she is." The first cut finished. Cass measured and marked the second. "She was here this morning, actually. Brought biscuits."

"Yeah?"

"She sat on the porch. We talked."

"About the offer?"

"About the apricots. About whether the Valencia variety is doing better than the Tiltons this year. About a blue heron she saw on the irrigation pond last week. About your father."

Marcus was quiet for a moment. "What about him?"

"She said she dreamed he was in the orchard. Walking the rows at night with a flashlight like he used to do when he was worried about frost. She said in the dream she wasn't afraid. She said she watched him from the window and he was just—doing what he did."

The second cut went through and Cass lifted the damaged section of pipe out and set it on the pile of turned earth. He dry-fitted the coupling first, checking the alignment, then began to apply the primer with the purple brush, the sharp chemical smell of it rising up in the heat and mingling with the earth smell in a way that seemed to Cass like a condensed version of his whole working life—chemistry and biology, the manufactured and the grown, the constant effort to marry them.

"He would tell you to sell," Marcus said.

"You don't know that."

"He told me. In the fall before he died. When you were in the south field and I drove out and sat with him in the afternoon."

Cass applied the cement without looking up. He fitted the coupling and held it with the patient steady pressure the directions had always indicated—thirty seconds, count them, don't let it slip.

"He said the land was going to take everything from whoever held it," Marcus said. "He said he'd known that for a long time and had held it anyway because he didn't know another way to be, but that he didn't want that for you. Or for me. He said it specifically."

"He said other things too."

"What things?"

"He said a man who didn't know what he was without a piece of ground was in some kind of trouble." Cass kept his hands on the coupling. Twenty seconds. Twenty-five. "He said that to me. Also in the fall."

"That's not an argument against selling."

"I know it's not an argument. I'm just telling you he said different things to different people on different days. He was a man who held contradictions. He was dying. People say what the person in front of them needs to hear when they're dying."

"Or they finally tell the truth."

Thirty seconds. Cass released the coupling and let it set. He stood up and his knees ached in the way they'd been aching for two years, the right one worse than the left, the cartilage doing its slow and irreversible work. He was thirty-eight years old. He'd been thirty-eight for three months and he felt the age in his body in a way he hadn't felt thirty-five or thirty-six, as though thirty-eight were a threshold the body acknowledged even if the mind preferred not to.

"Move up a foot," he said. "We're going to flush the line before we close it up."

They worked the repair through the morning, the conversation dropping and returning the way it had dropped and returned for six years, since the week after the funeral when Marcus had said the first careful thing about the property and Cass had said no with a thoroughness that closed the subject for another year. It was not a new argument. It had the quality of an argument that had been had so many times that both men knew all the lines, knew which concessions they were willing to make and which were cosmetic, knew where the real divergence was and where it couldn't be crossed.

What Cass couldn't explain, and had never managed to explain—not to Marcus, not to his mother, not to the agricultural extension officer who'd come out in March and given him a very direct talk about operating margins—was what exactly he thought he was preserving. He understood the economics. He'd understood them for years. He kept books with the same precision he cut pipe, and the books told him the same thing every year with minor variations, told him the story of a business losing ground, inputs rising, yield unpredictable, water rights contested, market prices for tree fruit behaving with the casual hostility of things that don't care about your particular situation. He knew the number Delacroix was offering and he knew it was real money, money that would settle his mother, give Marcus what he was owed from the estate, leave Cass himself with enough to do almost anything else.

It was the almost anything else that stopped him.

Not because he couldn't imagine other lives—he could imagine them; he was not a man without imagination—but because when he tried to locate himself in them, tried to put the weight of himself into the imagined life, he found a kind of emptiness that he suspected was not the emptiness of the unknown but the emptiness of the genuinely unsuitable. Some people were made for mobility and some people were made to stay and the wisdom of the age was that mobility was health and staying was pathology but Cass was not sure that was true, had never been sure of it, and was less sure of it every year he spent in this particular place knowing these particular trees and this particular light and the way the heat fell off the ridge at seven in the evening and moved across the orchard like a tide going out.

They ate lunch at the tailgate of Marcus's truck, the sandwiches their mother had sent with Marcus—she'd known he was coming—wrapped in white paper and held together with toothpicks the way she'd made sandwiches since they were boys. Ham and mustard. There was also a plastic container of cut melon, already warm from the heat.

"Tell me what the plan is," Marcus said. He said it without edge, genuinely. "Not to keep the land. That's not a plan. Tell me what happens in five years."

Cass looked at the orchard while he ate. "I'm replanting the south forty with almonds. I've been in contact with a water broker about rights on the secondary channel. If I can get that water and convert the south forty, the operation changes pretty significantly."

"How much does that cost?"

"The replanting or the water?"

"Either. Both."

"Together—" Cass paused. "A lot. I'd need to refinance."

"Cass."

"I know."

"You're talking about going further in."

"I'm talking about transforming the operation. There's a difference."

"In a favorable outcome there's a difference. In the mean outcome you're talking about losing the land and the equity both."

"You sound like an investor."

"I am an investor. I have a stake in this property."

"I know you do."

"And I'm telling you—"

"I know what you're telling me." Cass ate his melon. A red-tailed hawk was working the thermal above the south ridge, the same ridge that had been there every afternoon of his life, the hawk tilting in that particular lazy circle that was really a kind of attention, the way stillness could be a kind of watching. "I know every version of what you're telling me. I've run every number. I know what the numbers say."

"Then why—"

"Because the numbers have been saying it for twenty years and Dad was still here, growing apricots, and the trees were still standing and every June there were still apricots on them." He said it without anger. It came out flat and declarative, a statement of fact. "Delacroix wants to build two hundred and forty houses. I've seen the plans—he filed them with the county. Two hundred and forty houses on a hundred and sixty acres. He'll put in a HOA and a retention pond where the irrigation pond is and they'll name a street after the orchard. Orchard Lane. Orchard Court. Something like that."

Marcus didn't say anything.

"That's what we're talking about," Cass said. "When we talk about the offer. That's the specific thing."

"I know."

"I just want to make sure we're saying it out loud."

"We've said it out loud before."

"Yes."

They finished eating. They went back to work. By early afternoon they'd run the pressure test and the line was holding and Cass set the irrigation timer to run the west field at six, two hours before sunset, enough time for the trees to drink before the air cooled and the foliage sat wet through the night. He watched the coupling for a while after Marcus had moved on to filling the trench, watching the joint for any seep, any translucent bead of pressure finding its way through, and there was nothing, just the gray dry pipe and the dry earth.

Marcus was filling the trench with the shoveled soil, tamping it lightly with his foot. Cass watched his brother working, watched the familiar shape of him moving in the familiar light of this place, and felt the thing he usually tried not to feel, which was the grief of a particular kind—not grief for his father, not exactly, though that was in it too, but grief for the shared boyhood this land held, for the permanence of that record, for the fact that everything that had happened here was somehow still happening here in the way that the past happened in physical places, was present in them the way water was present in dry soil, invisible until you broke the surface.

"You could come back," Cass said.

Marcus stopped tamping. He looked at his brother.

"I'm not saying you would. I'm not asking. I'm just saying that it's a thing that exists. It would change the math."

"Teresa has work. The kids have school."

"I know."

"I can't—Cass. I have a life."

"I know you do."

"I'm not going to tell you it was easy to build."

"I know that too."

Marcus looked off toward the ridge. The hawk was gone. The afternoon heat had reached its full white amplitude and the trees stood in it with the stoic adaptation of things that had no choice but to endure, their leaves slightly curled at the edges in the way of drought stress that was not yet crisis. Cass would run a longer irrigation cycle this week. He'd check the soil moisture Saturday morning.

"What if we tried the almonds," Marcus said, not quite a question, not quite a statement.

Cass waited.

"What if I put in what I'd get from the sale. My portion. Half into the water rights and half into the replanting. What does that look like."

"That looks like—" Cass stopped. He'd run this number before, in a slightly different form, on a night in February when he couldn't sleep and had sat at the kitchen table with the laptop and the bills. He knew what it looked like. "That looks like it could work. In a favorable outcome."

"And if it doesn't work?"

"Then we sell what's left at whatever the market is in five or six years."

"And if the market's worse by then."

"It might be. Probably isn't. Land doesn't get cheaper around here."

Marcus picked up his shovel and finished the last few inches of fill. He tamped it. He stepped back and looked at the seam in the earth, the neat line of their afternoon's work. "Let me talk to Teresa," he said.

"Okay."

"I'm not promising."

"I'm not asking for a promise."

They loaded the wheelbarrow and Cass pushed it back toward the shed and Marcus walked beside him through the rows of trees in their rows, through the late afternoon light that had gone from white to a thin gold, the trees casting their first real shadows of the day in the long eastward direction, everything that had been compressed by noon light now extending, recovering its dimension. The ground was warm under their feet and there were bees in the cover crop at the edges of the rows and a barn swallow was doing its delirious work over the far hedgerow, impossible quick arcs that served some purpose even if you couldn't follow the logic.

Cass stopped at the tree he'd been watching all morning, the one with the grayed leaves. He crouched and scraped the soil at the base with his hand and looked at what the soil showed him. It was not as bad as he'd feared. Or it was bad but manageable, which in his experience was the same thing as not bad, which in his experience was most of what this life consisted of—the bad thing that turned out to be manageable, the problem that turned out to have a repair, the line that burst in the night and waited patiently in the morning light to be found and cut back and fitted with something new and sealed.

"Crown rot?" Marcus asked.

"Maybe. Not sure yet."

"You'll deal with it."

"Yes."

They went on toward the shed, the wheelbarrow making its known sound on the known ground, the orchard holding them in the way it always had—not tenderly, not cruelly, but with the profound indifference of something that existed on a different scale of time, that had been here before them and expected, in the way that land always expected, to be here after.
