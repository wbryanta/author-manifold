The ferry left at eleven and he had booked no cabin. He told himself this was economy but standing at the rail watching the terminal lights diminish and flatten into a single smear of orange he understood it was punishment, or something adjacent to punishment, a word he couldn't locate. Penance required belief in an accounting system and he wasn't sure he had that. What he had was the cold, which was genuine, and the black water sliding forty feet below him, and seven hours before Torshavn.

His name was Carl. He was fifty-one years old and he worked in insurance, the assessment of claims, which people found either ironic or apt when he mentioned it at parties, though he had stopped mentioning it at parties some years ago, and had largely stopped attending them. He had a daughter in Edinburgh who called him most Sundays and a flat in Aberdeen whose lease came up in March. These were the coordinates of his life and he held them in mind now as a kind of inventory, the way you might check your pockets before a long journey to confirm you still possessed the essential things.

The terminal had vanished. There was only the water and the dark and the vibration of the engines coming up through the deck plates into his feet.

He went inside.

The passenger lounge was institutional in the way of all overnight ferries, which is to say it had the feel of a place designed to process rather than accommodate human beings. Rows of reclining seats in a durable blue fabric, a cafeteria counter shuttered now with a metal grille, fluorescent lighting that divided everyone beneath it into the healthy and the unwell. Most passengers had already arranged themselves for sleep, or the attempt at it. Carl walked the length of the lounge twice and bought coffee from a machine near the toilets and found a seat beside a window that showed him nothing but his own reflection suspended in the dark.

He looked older than he expected. He always looked older than he expected.

The coffee was very bad and very hot and he was grateful for both qualities.

His phone showed a voicemail from the number he'd called back — a Sergeant Magnusson or Magnusdottir, he hadn't caught it properly, the connection poor, her English precise in the way of someone who has learned it carefully and resents having to use it on people who cannot reciprocate in kind. She had called at half past seven that evening while he was eating a sandwich over his kitchen sink and the message she left was administrative, mostly — the address of the facility, the name of the person to ask for, a reference number he wrote on the back of his hand because he could not find paper — but near the end of it she said, and I am sorry for your situation, and it was the specificity of this, your situation rather than your loss or your trouble, that had lodged in him. She didn't know it was a loss. Neither did he. That was precisely the situation.

He played it again with the phone pressed to his ear.

The man had been found four days ago on a beach on the eastern side of the island. No identification. Approximate age fifty-five to sixty-five, which was a ten-year window and told you how the sea had dealt with him. Dark hair, or had been dark. A tattoo on the left forearm — this was what had triggered the database search, what had eventually produced Carl's name from a decade-old police report filed in Inverness that he had entirely forgotten making, or had tried to. The tattoo was a bird of some kind. In the photograph they'd emailed him it was too degraded to identify precisely, more a suggestion of wings than wings themselves, the ink dispersed under the skin like something trying to escape.

His brother's name was Dennis. He was, if living, fifty-eight.

Carl had not seen him in eleven years. Before that there had been a period of three years with no contact, and before that two years, and before that various shorter intervals that were not clean breaks so much as silence accruing until it had weight and structure and became indistinguishable from a decision. The last time Carl had seen him, Dennis was living in a caravan outside Dingwall with a woman Carl had never met and a dog that barked without ceasing for the duration of his visit. They had drunk whisky in the afternoon and Dennis had told a long story about a fishing trip that Carl couldn't follow and at some point Carl had looked at his brother's hands wrapped around his glass and seen that they were their father's hands exactly, the same thick knuckle, the same way of holding a thing as though it might be taken, and something in him had closed over this observation like water over a stone, quiet and complete.

He had driven back to Inverness that evening. He had not called.

There was no clean explanation for this. He had tried to construct one over the years — their childhood, the drinking, a specific argument about money that had escalated into something neither of them was equipped to survive cleanly — but the honest version was simpler and worse. He had looked at his brother and seen the life he himself had narrowly missed and he had been afraid, not of Dennis, but of proximity. As though misfortune were communicable. As though the caravan outside Dingwall were a diagnosis he might carry home.

This was the understanding he lived with. He did not share it with anyone.

He finished the coffee and tried to sleep and could not sleep.

Around two in the morning the sea began to make itself known. The ferry climbed and fell in slow long movements, patient and indifferent, and the few overhead lights swayed slightly on their fittings. Most passengers had taken pills or had the sailor's gift and slept through it. Carl sat with his feet braced against the seat in front and watched the lights move and thought about the North Atlantic, its depth and acreage, what lay in it. He had read somewhere that the average depth of the ocean was three thousand seven hundred metres. He had no way to make this figure felt. He thought instead of his brother at twenty-two, in the shallow end of the public baths in Inverness, trying to teach Carl to float. Carl had been fifteen and could not surrender the muscular panic that sank him. Dennis had put one hand flat under Carl's shoulder blades and said just stop helping, which was the most useful thing anyone had ever said to him and which he had spent thirty-six years failing to apply.

He got up and went back out on deck.

The cold was serious now, the kind that found the gaps in clothing and worked through them with intent. The ship's wake stretched behind them, disturbed and luminous, the foam catching whatever light existed in the overcast sky, and Carl stood and watched it and understood for the first time since the phone call, or perhaps since he had been a fifteen-year-old boy sinking in a public pool, that he was frightened.

Not of grief, or not only of grief, but of its absence. Of arriving in the morning and looking at whatever there was to look at and feeling nothing, or feeling the administrative feelings — arrangement, procedure, the filling in of forms — and returning to Aberdeen and to the Sunday calls from Edinburgh and to the claims that required assessment, a life that had been, he saw now, so thoroughly insulated against disruption that a death, an actual death, might pass through it and leave no detectable mark.

He was frightened he had become someone that could happen to.

The ship climbed a long swell and descended. Spray came over the bow and reached him as a fine mist, salt on his lips, and he tasted it and stayed.

There had been good years, he made himself remember them. A holiday in Ullapool when they were children, their mother still alive, the four of them in a rented cottage and Dennis, who was seven, who was always in motion, Dennis catching a crab in a rockpool and carrying it ceremonially to their father as a gift, the crab making its slow articulate protest in his cupped hands. Their father's face doing something Carl rarely saw it do. Their mother laughing. The green hills going down to the sea in the long northern evening. He had been three years old. He should not have been able to remember it but he did, or he had a photograph of it, and he could no longer say with certainty where one ended and the other began. This was what memory did. This was what you were left with.

He went back inside at three and this time he did sleep, badly, waking every forty minutes or so to the changed sound of the engines or the shifting of the swell, each waking its own small surprised return to where he was and why.

At six-fifteen the light began.

It came not as sunrise but as a general grey relenting, the dark becoming less than dark and then gradually distinguishing itself into sky and water. Carl was awake and at the window when the islands appeared, low and brown at first, a thickening of the horizon that resolved itself slowly into shape and definition. Hillsides. A lighthouse blinking. The first visible land in seven hours and he pressed his forehead against the cold glass and looked at it with an attention he could not have explained.

The cafeteria opened and he ate eggs he didn't want and more bad coffee and watched through the window as the ferry moved between the islands, the water narrowing, becoming a sound, becoming a harbour. The town accumulated from its waterfront upward, coloured houses against a hillside, a church, warehouses, the infrastructure of a working port. It was small and solid and had the look of a place that endured things.

He put on his coat and joined the other foot passengers at the vehicle deck door. Around him people were composed, purposeful, doing the small organising things of arrival. He had a bag with a change of clothes and his toiletries and the charger for his phone. He had a reference number on the back of his hand, faded now with sweat and the night.

The door opened and he stepped out into the Faroese morning.

It was clear and very cold, the sky an enormous pale blue above the hills, the air having a quality he noticed immediately, a clarity, a cleanliness, as though the night's crossing had moved him to some place where the ordinary atmosphere had been refined. A gull stood on a bollard and regarded him without apparent interest and then opened its wings and fell into the air.

He found the taxi rank and gave the driver the address.

They drove in silence through the town and out along the water's edge, the driver a compact and unhurried man who offered nothing conversationally and evidently required nothing. Carl sat in the back and watched the harbour give way to a road that ran along a fjord, the water on one side hammered bright by the sun, the hillside on the other rising steeply, terraced with the occasional house. It was extraordinary country, he thought. The kind of country that made you feel the brevity of human arrangements. The hills had been here before the houses and would be here after them and were not interested in the distinction.

The facility was a low modern building set back from the road, functional and not unkind, the sort of architecture that tries to impose as little as possible on events that are already an imposition. There was a car park with three cars in it. A flagpole with no flag. He paid the driver and stood in the car park for a moment and looked at the building.

Inside, a woman at a desk looked up at him and he gave her the reference number from the back of his hand and she made a small note and picked up a phone. He stood and waited. There was a watercolour on the wall of a coastal scene, waves and cliffs, presumably local. There were plastic chairs in a row. He did not sit in them.

The woman who came to meet him was perhaps forty, with close-cut grey hair and the kind of face that had decided long ago to be direct. She shook his hand and said she was the Sergeant he had spoken to — her name was Sigrid Magnusdottir — and she said she was sorry for his situation and it sounded exactly as it had on the voicemail, careful and precise, and he found he was glad of it, glad she didn't reach for easier words.

She led him down a corridor that was the same as all such corridors, linoleum and fluorescent light and the faint chemical neutrality of a building where difficult things were regularly handled, and she asked him, without inflection, whether he had done this before. He said no. She nodded and said it was not always conclusive from looking, particularly after time in the water, but that they had photographs of details if he needed them, the tattoo and other identifying marks, and she said this plainly, not to prepare him but because it was what was true. He appreciated the plainness.

She stopped at a door and looked at him. He nodded.

The room was cold and white and smelled of nothing, or of the chemicals that were the smell of nothing. There was a table. There was a sheet.

He stood beside the table and looked at the shape beneath the sheet and the shape was undeniably a person and he breathed.

Sigrid Magnusdottir stood to one side and said, I will give you a moment, and folded her hands and looked at the wall. This was her gift to him, or one of them. He stood and breathed.

Then he said, all right, and she lifted the edge of the sheet and folded it back.

He looked.

He looked for a long time, and the looking was work, the kind that required everything he had and reached into places he did not know he had, extracting things he had stored without meaning to. The holiday in Ullapool. The public baths. The hands holding the glass. The caravan outside Dingwall and the dog that would not stop barking. All of it came and he let it come and he looked at the face on the table.

The sea had been at it. There was no question about that. It had taken certain things and softened others and there were aspects of the face that were simply gone, translated into damage. But the bones beneath it, the particular architecture of the forehead and the jaw, the way the brow descended to the nose — these were legible, in the way that essential things persisted through transformation, and he read them with the specific literacy of someone who had grown up alongside a face and could not, even now, even after everything, have failed to know it.

He stood there a long time.

Then he said, yes. He said, that's Dennis. He said it without surprise, which surprised him. He had known, he thought, or some part of him had known, not from the email with its degraded photograph of wings, not from the reference number in a database, but from the moment Sigrid Magnusdottir's voice had come crackling across three hundred miles of ocean to say I am sorry for your situation. You didn't say that when you weren't sure.

She asked him some questions and he answered them. He gave his brother's full name and date of birth and the last address he had for him, which was the caravan and which was eleven years old, and he said I don't know if he was still there, he probably wasn't, and she wrote it down without comment. She asked if there was other family and he said no, just him, which was true if you didn't count the daughter in Edinburgh, and he corrected himself and described his daughter, and she wrote that down too. She asked if he knew of any medical history and he said he knew very little, he knew Dennis had broken his collarbone as a child falling out of an apple tree, and he stopped, and said that wasn't what she meant, and she said it was all useful. He didn't know if this was policy or kindness. He suspected it was both.

She brought him tea in a room with chairs in it and sat with him while he drank it. He looked out the window at the hillside rising above the road, the grass a particular gold-green in the autumn light, and at the water of the fjord lying still between the hills.

He asked her where exactly he had been found.

She said the name of the place, which he couldn't hold, but she said it was a small beach on the eastern coast, facing toward Norway. He thought about this. He thought about the distances, the movements of the water, where a person might enter it to end up there. She did not know yet, she said, whether it was accident or deliberate. The post-mortem was not complete. He said he understood.

He asked if Dennis had been in the water long. She said several weeks, perhaps longer. He said he understood again. He sat with this. Several weeks. He had been eating sandwiches over his sink, assessing claims, failing to call his daughter back one Sunday because he was watching a film, and his brother had been in the North Atlantic for several weeks. He did not say this aloud. There was nowhere to put it except inside himself, and he put it there.

She asked, gently, whether he would need help with arrangements, and he said no, he would manage, and then he thought about what that actually meant, what managed meant in this context, a body requiring transport, a funeral requiring planning when there was no address and no community of which he was aware and no one, probably, to attend it except a younger brother who had last visited a caravan outside Dingwall eleven years ago and driven home the same evening. He said actually, yes, that would be useful, if she could tell him where to begin.

She gave him a card with numbers on it and names and he put it in his coat pocket and took it out again and looked at it.

Outside the window a cloud had moved and the water of the fjord had changed colour, deepened, gone from silver to something closer to slate, and he watched the change move across it like a thought, slow and complete, end to end.

He had one more night here before the return ferry. He hadn't planned what to do with it. He thought he would find somewhere to eat and perhaps walk, if the weather held, and look at the country his brother had ended up in, this extraordinary country at the edge of things. He would call his daughter, probably tonight, though he would have to think about what to tell her. She had never known Dennis, or had known of him the way you know of a piece of country you've never visited — named on a map, vaguely oriented, the territory itself unresolved. He would have to find a way to speak about this. He was aware, dimly, as he sat in the chair with the tea going cold, that finding a way to speak about this was going to require him to speak about other things, earlier things, the decisions that had made Dennis into a name on a map, and that these conversations would be difficult and perhaps necessary and perhaps, in some way he could not yet see the shape of, the point.

He was aware also that he was not yet feeling what he would eventually feel. He was in the anteroom of it. The door was there and he could see it and he was not afraid, quite, but he was not ready to open it either, and so he sat, in this clean cold building on an island in the North Atlantic, and he breathed, and he let the sea-light move across the water of the fjord, and he waited.
