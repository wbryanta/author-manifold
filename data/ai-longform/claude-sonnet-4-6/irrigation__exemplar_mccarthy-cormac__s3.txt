The elder brother came down through the orchard in the early light and his shadow went long before him across the dry grass and the trees stood gray and still as the bones of something old. Burl walked with his hands loose at his sides and he looked at the trees as he went among them and his face showed nothing of what he thought about them. He found his brother kneeling at the pump shed with tools spread around him on an oilcloth and the dog lying in the dust nearby watching the work with that expression of careful desolation particular to dogs.

What's it doing, Burl said.

Clete looked up. It aint doing much of anything. That's the problem.

He stood and wiped his hands on his pants and they both looked at the pump housing. A hairline crack ran up through the fiberglass casing like a dry creek on a map. The pipe coupling at the elbow was wrapped in calcium where the water had seeped and dried and seeped again over years.

There's your trouble right there, Burl said.

I know where the trouble is.

Well.

I need to get in under here. Clete knelt again and worked at a fitting with a pipe wrench. The dog watched him. He got the fitting loose and set it aside and reached into the housing.

Burl looked out across the orchard. The trees were heavy with June and the apples small and hard and green among the leaves and the leaves themselves a dusty color from the lack of water. He could see where the irrigation tape had pulled loose from the second row and lay flat on the ground like something dead. Some of the trees at the far end he knew were already gone. He'd known it since March.

I'm going to get the tape from the truck, he said.

Clete said nothing.

Burl walked back up the slope to where the truck sat in the lane and he pulled a roll of irrigation tape from the bed and a handful of fittings in a paper bag and he came back down through the trees and started at the head of the first row splicing the line where it had split. The sun was coming up proper now and already he could feel it on his neck. He worked methodically down the row and at the third tree he found where a gopher had tunneled under and cut through the tape in two places and he sat back on his heels and looked at the damage.

Son of a bitch, he said quietly.

He worked the fittings on and crimped them and moved to the next break. Below him he could hear Clete with the wrench, the clank of it against the pipe, a muffled curse. The dog had come up through the trees and stood watching Burl with its ears forward.

Go on, Burl said.

The dog sat down.

He finished the first row and started on the second and the sun was well up when Clete called out to him.

Try it now.

Burl walked back to the shed and Clete had reconnected the coupling and replaced the housing as best he could and stood with his hand on the switch.

Go ahead, Burl said.

Clete threw the switch and the pump shuddered and a groan came up through the pipes and then a ticking sound that slowed and stopped. They waited. Then water began moving through the main line, a faint pressure you could feel if you put your hand on the pipe, and out in the rows the tape lay briefly still and then swelled.

There it is, Clete said.

Burl watched the tape in the nearest row begin to bead and sweat along the drip holes. He watched the water darken the dust beneath the nearest tree and spread and be taken into the earth.

How long you think that coupling holds, he said.

Clete shrugged. I can get another one in Madera. This'll hold till then.

Burl nodded. He looked off toward the bad end of the orchard.

You want to walk down and look at those trees, he said.

Not especially.

Well.

Clete picked up his tools from the oilcloth and folded it and set it on a shelf in the shed. He came out and latched the shed door and they walked together down through the rows without talking. Their boots were wet from the irrigation and the smell of water on dry ground was around them. The dog ranged out ahead and came back and ranged again.

It was nearly nine when they reached the far end and stood among the trees there. The trees were stripped of leaves on the south side and the bark had a gray papery look like the skin of something diseased and the apples that hung there were small and few and already beginning to mottle. One tree had lost a major limb in the spring winds and the wound had gone black at the edges. Burl put his hand on the trunk of that one.

This one's dead already, he said.

Clete didn't answer right away. He looked at the adjacent trees.

Maybe not, he said.

Burl took his hand away. He looked at the row. Five trees, he said. These five.

I don't know.

I know.

Clete walked to the edge tree and reached up and took a small apple between his fingers and turned it on the branch. The skin of it was yellowed underneath the mottling and soft in a way that June apples have no business being.

Even if we get water to them now.

I know it.

We might get some production off the others. The middle block looks decent.

It looks all right.

Well.

Burl looked back up the slope to where the other trees stood in their rows under the hard morning light. He could see where the land rose to the north and the fence line that marked where their land ended. Beyond the fence the Aldredge property had been scraped and graded and the frames of a housing development stood half-built on the hillside, the lumber yellow and new, the raw earth between the frames the color of a wound.

You talked to Estes, Burl said.

It wasn't a question.

Clete looked at him. He called me.

And.

He said what he said before.

Burl turned back to the dead tree and looked at it. A woodpecker had been at the trunk and the small neat holes ran up the bark in an irregular line.

What's he saying now.

Clete named a figure.

Burl was quiet a moment. He looked at his brother. That's what he's paying for this.

Clete nodded.

Burl pulled at a loose strip of bark on the dead tree and it came away in his hand and he dropped it. Underneath the wood was pale and dry as chalk.

I told him we'd talk, Clete said.

You told him that.

I did.

Burl walked to the next tree and put his hand on it too, lower down near the base, as if taking the temperature of something.

This one might come back, he said.

Might.

If we get water to it. If we get the tape laid right and keep it watered from here out.

Clete was quiet.

Burl, Clete said.

Don't.

I'm just saying.

I know what you're saying. I know what you've been saying.

They walked back up through the trees together. The irrigation tape was working now in all the rows and the ground beneath the trees was darkening with moisture and the smell of it was everywhere, wet earth and something green underneath, some cellular exhalation of the roots taking up water. The dog trotted ahead of them up the slope and into the shade of the equipment shed.

They'd brought a cooler in the truck and they ate their lunch on the tailgate. Sandwiches and cold sodas and Clete had boiled eggs wrapped in wax paper and they ate without talking much and watched a red-tailed hawk working the updraft over the near ridge.

Daddy planted those trees in sixty-one, Burl said.

I know it.

You know how old I was.

I know how old you were.

I carried the hose for him all through that first summer. Every evening after school. He said the roots needed to believe in water before you could trust them on their own.

Clete peeled an egg and salted it.

I remember that, he said.

Burl drank from his soda. He said if you could get the roots to believe it then you had a tree that would work for you. If you didn't you had a tree that was always waiting to fail.

That sounds like him.

I think about that sometimes. Whether I got the roots right.

Meaning what.

Meaning whatever you want it to mean.

Clete ate the egg and wiped his hands and looked out at the orchard. You can't keep the water in the ground by believing in it, he said. You can't irrigate with sentiment.

That's a cold thing to say.

Maybe. But it's dry up here, Burl. It's been dry. The aquifer's down and it's going to keep going down and the Aldredge water share sold off with the property and we don't have the allocation we had before. That's not sentiment. That's just a fact like any other fact.

Burl looked at him.

And the money, Clete said. I don't have to tell you about the money.

No.

Then I don't know what we're doing. Hauling water and tape and sitting on this hillside watching it.

It's ours.

It's an asset.

It's not an asset. It's not something that you—Burl stopped. He wrapped what was left of his sandwich in the paper and set it on the cooler. It was our mother's yard, he said. She spent thirty years walking those rows.

I know what she spent.

She knew every tree.

Burl.

She named them. You remember that. She named every tree in the middle block.

I remember.

You'd walk with her in the evening and she'd say, here's this one, here's Thomas, here's this old woman I call Ruth.

Clete smiled a little in spite of himself.

Burl looked at his brother and the smile held a moment and left.

I can't sell those trees, Burl said.

We may not have a choice.

A person always has a choice.

Clete said nothing to that. He crumpled the wax paper from his egg and put it in the bag they'd brought the lunch in and finished his soda and set the can in the bed of the truck. He looked out at the orchard and then he looked at the development on the hillside beyond the fence.

You know what's going to be there, Burl said.

Houses.

You know what kind.

Clete looked at them. The big kind.

Four thousand square feet. Five. Little pieces of grass front and back.

People want what they want.

They'll take the water. Whatever's left in the table up here they'll take it. You know that. Wells and swimming pools and all of it.

That may be.

So we sell and they build more of those or we keep it orchard and at least the water stays in the trees.

That's not how property works.

I know how property works.

Then you know that if we don't sell to Estes someone else buys this from the bank in about two years. That's what you're looking at. That's the choice.

Burl was quiet a long time. A car went down the county road below and its sound faded into the heat of the day. The hawk had moved on. The dog slept in the shade of the truck with its legs stretched out, twitching now and again at something in its sleep.

You remember Manuel, Burl said.

The pruning.

All those years. Him and his brothers.

His son came up last spring about the spraying.

I know it. Burl looked at his hands. His boy must be what, thirty now.

Something like that.

He grew up on these trees same as us. You think about that.

Clete didn't answer.

I think about that, Burl said.

Clete climbed down from the tailgate and stretched and looked at the sun. We ought to check the coupling on that pump again before it gets too hot, he said.

Burl nodded and didn't move.

Clete put on his hat and started down through the trees. After a while Burl climbed down too and followed him and they went down through the rows in the heat with the drip tape sweating along the ground and the small apples hanging in the green shade of the leaves. They stopped at the tree with the split limb and Clete looked at it again.

You could cut this back and let it put on wood, Clete said.

Through the wound?

Dress it up and paint it. It might.

Maybe.

Or it might just die from that spot down. You can't always tell.

No, Burl said. You can't always tell.

They reached the pump shed and Clete went inside and checked the coupling and came back out.

It's holding, he said.

Good.

For now.

They stood in the thin shade of the shed wall and looked out at the orchard. All down the rows the irrigation tape was working, drawing up from the aquifer whatever the aquifer still held, delivering it to the fine roots that reached for it in the dark beneath the trees. The light came hard and flat now through the leaves. In the near row a tree stood full and the apples were thick on it and the grass under it was wet and green as a thing recently emerged from the earth.

I need to think about it, Burl said.

All right.

I'm not saying no. I'm saying I need to think.

Clete nodded. You know the timeline.

I know the timeline.

He'll want an answer before the end of the month.

I know it.

Burl stepped out of the shade and looked up at the sky. Flat blue and depthless. Not a cloud from one end of it to the other.

Think it'll rain, Clete said.

Burl looked at the sky a while. No, he said.

Me neither.

They went back up through the orchard to the truck and loaded the cooler and the remaining fittings and the roll of tape and Clete put his tools in the box behind the cab. Burl stood at the passenger door and looked back down the slope one more time. He could see the whole of it from here, the ordered rows and the trees of different ages and the dead ones at the bottom showing gray among the green, and the tape running its length in the dust, and the shed with its crooked door standing open, and beyond it all the far hills going pale in the midday light.

His mother had been dead eleven years. He tried to remember what she looked like walking in the evening among the trees and he could bring up only a shape, a quality of movement, the sound of her voice saying the names she'd given things. There had been a time when that was enough. He wasn't sure what time it was now.

He got in the truck.

Clete started the engine and they sat a moment with it running. The dog jumped in the bed.

She wouldn't want you to go broke over it, Clete said.

I know that.

She was practical.

She loved these trees.

A person can love something and still know when it's done.

Burl looked out the window at the nearest row, the drip holes darkening the ground.

You don't think she's here somehow, he said. In this. In the trees she walked.

Clete put the truck in gear.

I think she's in us, he said. Wherever we go.

Burl said nothing. They drove up the lane and through the gate and out onto the county road and the orchard fell behind them and then it was only a shape in the mirror and then the road curved and there was only the hills and the hard sky and the rangeland running off to the east.
