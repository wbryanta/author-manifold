The morning came up gray and the boy—he was not a boy anymore but the older one still thought of him that way—was already in the bed of the truck when Calvin got outside, sitting there with his legs dangling over the tailgate and a cup of coffee going cold in his hands. The orchard stretched behind him in the pearl light and the trees stood in their rows without apology, the unpicked fruit from the previous fall gone now to rot and the new growth just coming, small and green and not yet committed to anything.

You ready, Denton said.

Calvin looked at him and looked at the trees. I been ready, he said.

They drove the access road that ran the southern edge of the property, the truck lurching in the ruts, and neither of them spoke. The irrigation line had split somewhere in the lower section, the third time in as many years, and the grass there had gone yellow in a long diagonal stripe that you could read from the house if you knew what you were looking for. Their father had known. He'd stood at the kitchen window the last summer and traced it with his finger against the glass without saying anything and now he was two years in the ground and the line was split again.

Denton stopped the truck at the head of the lower block and they got out and walked the rows. The trees here were Gravensteins, old ones, their bark gone gray and corrugated like the skin of something ancient and their limbs reaching in the particular way of old apple trees as if they were always in the middle of asking a question. The grass between the rows was thick with dew and it soaked Calvin's boots through to the sock before he'd gone twenty feet.

Here, he said.

Denton came and looked at where the line had blown. It was a two-inch poly pipe and it had split along a seam for maybe fourteen inches and the ground around it was mud still, dark and tamped-down where the water pressure had beaten it before the line lost enough head to quit. There were small smooth craters in the mud like the water had made divots falling.

We'll need the coupler and the clamps, Calvin said.

I brought them.

And the heat gun.

Brought that too.

Calvin looked at him. Denton had his hands in his back pockets and was looking at the split in the line the way you look at something you've already decided about. He was thirty-one years old and he looked like their father and did not know it or did not acknowledge it, which might have been the same thing.

They walked back to the truck and got the tools and brought them up in a canvas bag and Calvin knelt in the mud beside the line and began to work. He cut out the damaged section with a utility knife, making his cuts clean and square, and Denton held the pipe sections while he fitted the coupler. The clamps were stainless and they tightened down with a quarter-inch driver and Calvin worked each one in stages, alternating, not torquing one side down before the other had drawn even.

You know what I want to talk about, Denton said.

Calvin kept working. I expect I do.

We can't keep patching it. We can't keep patching all of it.

I know that.

The whole lower block needs new main. Probably six hundred feet of it. And that's before you even start on the secondaries.

Calvin sat back on his heels and looked at the coupling. He'd done it right. He always did it right, which was not always the same as it being enough. He picked up the heat gun and began to shrink the sleeve over the joint.

I'm not talking about selling, he said.

I am.

A crow crossed the end of the row and disappeared into the next block, its call trailing behind it like something dropped. The heat gun made its steady tone and Calvin moved it in slow passes along the sleeve and the material contracted and gripped.

There's money to be made still, he said.

Calvin.

We had a decent year two years ago.

We had a year that didn't put us further back. That's not the same thing.

Calvin set the heat gun down and looked up at his brother. Denton was standing with his arms crossed and the morning light was strengthening now and it came through the trees and fell on him in pieces, light and shadow both, and Calvin thought that this was how he'd always seemed to him, his brother, partial like that. Not hiding exactly but never fully in the light either.

You talked to somebody, Calvin said.

Denton didn't answer right away. A developer, he said. Reston Properties. They've done three other parcels on the bench.

When.

Two months ago. Maybe three.

Calvin stood up. He was a big man and he stood up slowly the way big men do, unfolding. He picked the heat gun up by its cord and put it in the bag. Three months, he said.

I didn't know how to start the conversation.

You started it now.

Denton looked away down the row. The dew was burning off the grass and the air smelled of it, of green things warming. What do you want me to say, he said.

I want you to say you didn't already tell them yes.

I didn't tell them yes.

Calvin looked at him.

I didn't, Cal. I'm telling you.

Calvin picked up the bag and walked back toward the truck and Denton followed. They put the tools in the bed and stood there a moment, each of them on his own side of the truck, the orchard behind Denton and the access road behind Calvin, the yellow hills in the distance beyond the fence line.

He said we could get four-two for the parcel, Denton said. Maybe four-five if we cleaned up the titles.

The titles are clean.

I know they're clean, I'm just saying what he said.

Four-point-two million, Calvin said.

Four-point-two. Yes.

Calvin put his hands on the hood of the truck. The metal was warming from the sun. He pressed his palms flat against it. The orchard had been in the family since their grandfather's time, planted in the years after the war by a man who'd come back from the Pacific with shrapnel in his hip and a need to do something with his hands that would stay done. He'd bought the bench land when it was cheap and rocky and he'd amended the soil with compost and manure and he'd planted a hundred trees the first year and lost sixty of them to a late frost and planted again the year after that. Their father had grown up here and had brought their mother here and she had died here, the both of them had, and the trees were still going though going slower now and the money was not what it had been when the processing contracts were solid and the pickers still came up reliably from the valley.

You think that's what he would have wanted, Calvin said.

Don't do that.

I'm asking.

You know I hate when you ask it that way. Like you already know his answer and you're just checking to see if I do.

Maybe I am.

Denton came around to Calvin's side of the truck. He was not as big as his brother but he was not small and there was something in the way he moved that suggested he'd thought about this, that he had assembled himself against the conversation over the months of not having it.

Dad was practical, he said. He was sentimental too but he was practical first.

He planted sixty trees that froze out and planted again.

Because he had no other options. Because this was all there was. We have options. We have a chance to walk away with something real.

Walk away, Calvin said.

Walk away, Denton said. Yes. And don't say it like that. You know what I mean.

The sun was up properly now and the shadows in the orchard had shortened and gone crisp and the trees stood in them as if planted twice, once in the ground and once in the light. Calvin looked at them. He had worked this ground since he was old enough to carry a bucket. He knew which trees were finicky and which were generous and which needed their leaders cut back hard and which you left alone because touching them did more harm than good. He knew where the soil went heavy and wet in the northwest corner where the hardpan was close to the surface and where it opened up and drained clean along the south-facing slope. He knew all of this the way he knew his own weight on a ladder, not as information but as the body's knowledge, accumulated and stored somewhere below thought.

What do you owe it, Denton said. Seriously. What do you owe a piece of ground.

Calvin said nothing.

You've put fifteen years of your life in here since Dad went. I'm not saying it wasn't worth it. I'm saying it's been hard and it's going to get harder and you've got nothing to show for it except more work waiting on you tomorrow.

There's the land, Calvin said.

Which Reston will pay four million dollars for.

It won't be an orchard anymore if they buy it.

No. It won't.

You know what they'll do with it.

Same thing they did with the Hartley place and the Medina place. Houses. Maybe a hundred of them, hundred and fifty.

Calvin looked out at the hills. The grass on them was already going gold even this early in the summer and there were oaks along the ridgeline and the sky above them was a hard mineral blue. He tried to think about what his father's voice had sounded like. He could remember its quality, its register, but not the actual sound of it anymore and this troubled him and had troubled him for a year or more, the way memory edited things, kept the shape and lost the substance.

I need to turn the water back on, he said. We need to check the joint.

They walked back down the row. Calvin opened the valve at the head of the block and they stood watching the line pressurize. The fitting held.

Good, Denton said.

Calvin crouched and put his hand on it anyway, feeling for seep, for the faint cold of water finding its way through.

It's good, he said.

He stood and they walked back. In the next row over a robin was working the duff under one of the old Gravensteins, pulling worms up methodically, carrying each one off. They watched it for a moment.

You got a family, Denton said. Lena and the kids. This would set them up. Set them up properly.

My family's not your argument to make.

I know that. I'm making it anyway.

Calvin said nothing. He was thinking about his son, who was nine, and his daughter, who was six, and how neither of them had any particular feeling for the orchard, or if they did it was the feeling children have for a place they've grown up in, unexamined, not yet chosen, not yet owned. He wondered if that was how he himself had come to it, whether any of what he felt about this ground was something he'd truly chosen or whether it was just what had been poured into him young and had hardened there like concrete poured in a form.

I'm not saying sign the papers tomorrow, Denton said. I'm saying consider it. Really consider it. Not for me. For yourself.

You'd take your half and do what.

Buy into the Waverly Ranch operation. Jim Waverly's been after me about it. A share in the cattle and the hay ground.

That's more work not less.

It's different work. It's work that isn't already losing.

Calvin picked up a windfall that had come down early from one of the trees, soft and spotted, and turned it in his hand. It was from last year's growth, overlooked in the harvest, a Gravenstein, flushed yellow and red. It smelled good still. He threw it into the grass between the rows.

Granddad didn't buy easy ground, he said. He bought hard ground and he made it produce. You could make an argument that's worth something as its own thing, separate from the money.

You can make that argument, Denton said. I'm not sure it pays the irrigation bill.

The irrigation bill.

The irrigation bill. The equipment maintenance. The labor if we ever go back to running a real crew. The processing contracts we lost when Mendenhall shut down. You want me to keep going.

No.

Because I can.

I said no.

They reached the truck and Calvin dropped the tailgate and sat on it. After a moment Denton sat beside him. They were close enough that their arms nearly touched. They sat like that for a while without speaking and the morning went on around them, the light moving through the trees, the birds working. Somewhere in the upper block a woodpecker was going at something with absolute commitment, the sound of it carrying clean in the still air.

What if we replanted the lower block, Calvin said. New trees. Took the insurance money from the Mayer hailstorm, put it toward new stock. Different varietals. The market's there for heritage strains now. Honeycrisps. Pink Pearls. You could get a premium price at the farmers markets, direct to consumer, cut out the middleman entirely.

That insurance money's already spent, Denton said. Or mostly.

On what.

On keeping us going since February.

Calvin looked at his hands. His hands were like their father's hands, broad and rough, the skin across the knuckles permanently cracked in the winter and slow to heal. He turned them over and looked at the palms.

How much do you actually owe, he said.

Denton was quiet for a moment. To who.

To anyone.

Denton breathed out through his nose. About forty, he said.

Thousand.

Yes.

Calvin looked at him. Denton was looking at the ground.

Why didn't you say.

I'm saying now.

Why didn't you say before now.

Because I was embarrassed. Because I didn't want to have the conversation. I've been carrying it and I'm tired of carrying it and I'm telling you now.

The woodpecker stopped. The silence it left was large.

Some of it is back taxes, Denton said. On my portion of the parcel. I got behind and I didn't catch up and I'm further back now than I was.

Calvin sat with that for a while. He looked out at the orchard. The trees were beautiful in the way that old things that have survived difficulty are beautiful, not despite the evidence of it but because of it. The codling moth damage on the upper limbs. The fire blight scars on two of the Gravensteins in the third row. The way the oldest tree in the block, the one their grandfather had planted and called by no particular name, had split years back in a January ice storm and had healed itself improbably, the wound closed over with new wood, the scar raised and pale.

You should have told me, Calvin said.

I know it.

We could have worked something.

I know it. But I didn't and here we are.

Calvin got off the tailgate and stood in the road. He looked north up the access road to where it bent and you lost sight of the house but could see the roof of the old sorting barn, the weathervane on it that their mother had liked, a copper horse gone green.

If I say yes, he said. And I'm not saying yes. But if I say yes, you hear what goes with that.

Denton looked at him.

It's gone, Calvin said. It's not like selling a car. You sell this, this is gone. These trees get pushed and the ground gets graded and in five years there's a cul-de-sac where this row is and some family's looking out their kitchen window at a lawn where the irrigation line used to run. You understand that.

I understand it.

And you can live with it.

Denton looked away. I don't know if I can live with any of it, he said. I don't know if I can live with it one way or the other. I just know I can't keep pretending.

Calvin looked at his brother for a long time. Denton didn't look back at him. He was looking at the trees.

We'll finish the checks, Calvin said. The rest of the main line. We'll finish the checks and then I want to see the numbers. All of them. Everything you haven't shown me.

Okay.

The back taxes and the equipment notes and whatever else.

Okay. Yes.

And then we'll talk.

Denton nodded. He got off the tailgate and they got in the truck and drove further down the access road to where the line ran under the berm and they checked the secondaries and found another soft spot at a union that hadn't been glued right, years ago, just weeping slightly, not a crisis yet but a thing to note. Calvin cut a short sleeve of tape and wrapped it and they marked the location with a piece of orange flagging tied to the nearest stake.

They drove the full perimeter of the lower block and the middle block and stopped at the edge of the upper block where the road petered out at the old stone wall that marked the property line. Beyond the wall was a neighbor's hayfield, cut already, the windrows gray-green and drying. They got out and stood at the wall.

This is where he used to bring us, Calvin said. When we were small.

I remember.

He'd put us up on this wall and tell us all of it was ours. Everything we could see.

He thought it was a good thing to tell a child.

Calvin put his hand on the stone. The stones were old, laid by hand, fitted without mortar, and they had survived a long time through the intelligence of their fitting. He thought about the man who had laid them. Some hired man or neighbor or perhaps his grandfather himself, much younger than he ever was in any photograph Calvin had ever seen of him.

Maybe it was, he said.

Maybe it was what.

A good thing to tell a child.

Denton looked out over the hayfield. A red-tailed hawk was working the far edge, hanging over the stubble, and they watched it.

I'm not trying to take anything from you, Denton said.

I know that.

I know it feels that way.

I know you're not, Calvin said.

The hawk dropped into the stubble and rose again and was gone over the hill.

You know I love this place, Denton said. You know I do.

Calvin looked at him. Yes, he said. I know you do.

They stood there a while more and then got in the truck and drove back up the road toward the house. The orchard fell away on either side of them and the light was full now, morning completed, the day properly begun, and the trees in their rows received the light the way they always had, generously, without condition, converting it silently into the green work of their living. Calvin drove slow and watched them. He had watched them all his life. He thought that this was what loss felt like even before it happened, that the watching itself became a kind of mourning, the eye recording what the hands might not get to keep.

At the house he stopped the truck and they got out. Lena was in the garden with the kids and she raised a hand to them and he raised a hand back. Denton stood by the truck and looked at the house and at the garden and at the fields and orchards beyond.

Come inside, Calvin said. Show me the numbers.

Denton looked at him. The morning light was strong on his face and he looked for a moment like the child he had been, caught between the knowledge of something and the fear of its consequences.

Alright, he said.

They went inside.
