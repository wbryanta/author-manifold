The morning came in gray and without ceremony over the eastern ridge and the two brothers stood at the edge of the orchard in the early light and said nothing to one another. The trees were old pears mostly, a few apple along the south row, and they stood in their columns like men who had forgotten what they were waiting for. The grass between them had gone to seed and the irrigation line ran along the ground half-buried in places and cracked open in others where the clay soil had heaved and contracted through the dry years and the water had seeped out into the dirt and gone nowhere useful.

Harlan was the older by four years and he carried the pipe fittings in a canvas bag on his shoulder and he walked out into the first row without looking to see if his brother followed. His boots were the same boots he'd worn to their father's funeral eleven months prior and they were cracked at the toes and the left sole had begun to separate and slapped against the ground with each step in a way that seemed almost deliberate. Almost like commentary.

Joel came behind him with the shovel and the pipe cutter and the can of primer. He was thinner than Harlan and his face had the quality of someone who slept poorly and thought about it. He had driven up from the city the night before and had not slept well in the house either, lying in the room that had been his room and staring at the ceiling where a water stain had spread and dried and spread again over the years into a shape he had once decided looked like the state of Nevada and still did.

They walked to where the first break was. The pipe lay open, a clean split along the top, and the dirt around it had the dark color of old moisture though it was dry now and had been dry for some time.

Harlan set down the bag. He crouched over the pipe and ran his thumb along the split and looked at the sky with the expression of a man consulting something.

Cut here and here, he said.

Joel placed the cutter and bore down and the pipe gave a sound like a small complaint.

You check the whole line? Joel said.

Harlan said he had.

How many breaks?

Seven that I found.

Joel paused in his cutting. Seven.

Could be more once we get water in it.

Joel finished the cut and moved the cutter down and Harlan held the pipe still. The sun had come above the ridge now and the light fell flat and white through the trees. A mockingbird started up somewhere back toward the barn and ran through its repertoire with an almost aggressive thoroughness.

You talk to anybody yet, Harlan said.

Joel glanced up. About what.

You know about what.

I talked to a man.

What kind of man.

A realtor. Joel said it plainly and looked back at the pipe.

Harlan was quiet for a moment. He took the cut section out and set it to the side and reached in the canvas bag for a coupling.

These trees are forty years old some of them, he said.

I know how old they are.

Daddy planted the back row himself. You and me were there.

Joel said nothing.

You remember that. You remember helping him.

I remember, Joel said. I remember a lot of things.

Harlan primed the pipe end and the coupling and the smell of the solvent rose up in the still morning air sharp and chemical and wrong-seeming in that place. He worked the coupling on and held it.

What'd the man say, he said.

Joel set the cutter on the ground and picked up a rag and wiped his hands. The land and what's on it, he said. Maybe eight hundred an acre if we move it right. Maybe less the way things are.

How many acres.

You know how many acres.

I want to hear you say it.

Joel looked at him. Sixty-two.

Harlan did the arithmetic in the way of a man who has already done it many times and is only confirming the result. He nodded slowly at nothing in particular.

That's not enough to do anything with, he said.

It's enough to get clear, Joel said. It's enough to not have this hanging.

What hanging. What is hanging.

The debt, Harlan. The note on the equipment. The back taxes. You know what's hanging.

Harlan stood and shouldered the canvas bag and moved down the row to the next break without responding. Joel picked up the shovel and followed. The trees on either side stood indifferent and the fruit on them was small and June-hard and there was less of it than there should have been. They had not been sprayed properly and some of the leaves showed the pale stippling of mite damage and others had gone yellow in a way that had nothing to do with the season.

At the second break Harlan crouched again and examined the pipe. This one had not split but had come apart at a fitting, the glue failed, and the dirt below was still faintly damp. He looked at it for a long time.

I can get the taxes current, he said.

With what.

I've been putting money aside.

Joel leaned on the shovel. He looked at the back of his brother's head, the gray coming in along the sides and at the crown, the neck burned dark from working outdoors. He thought about what he wanted to say and decided to say something else.

Harlan, he said. The market for this fruit is not what it was. You know that. You've known it.

The market's been off.

It's been off for six years.

It'll come back.

Joel didn't answer. He watched a ground squirrel cross the end of the row and disappear under the root of the nearest tree.

This land was in the family eighty years, Harlan said. He was working the old fitting loose as he spoke. Grandaddy bought it off a man who was leaving for the coast. Paid for it with money he made pouring concrete for the county. You know all this.

I know it.

Then you know it's not just a number. It's not acreage times some figure a realtor gives you.

I know what it is, Joel said quietly. That's not what I'm saying it is.

What are you saying it is.

I'm saying it's also that. I'm saying it can't only be one thing.

Harlan fit the new coupling and primed it and pushed it on and held it. He held it longer than was necessary.

You going to help me or just stand there philosophizing, he said.

Joel set down the shovel and knelt beside him and they worked together in silence for a while, the way they had worked as boys, one holding and one fitting, and the morning got warmer and the white light softened into yellow and the mockingbird had gone quiet and now there was only the sound of their work and the occasional stir of a breeze through the pear leaves which made a dry whispering like pages turning in a book no one was reading.

By midmorning they had fixed four of the breaks and had walked the line twice and found two more. They sat at the edge of the row and drank water from a jug Joel had carried from the house and ate the sandwiches he had made that morning while Harlan was already outside. It was warm now and the smell of the warming grass and the dry soil was the particular smell of this place that Joel associated with summers of childhood so strongly that it was almost painful to breathe. Like breathing something that no longer existed.

What would you do, Joel said. If you kept it.

Harlan looked out at the trees.

I'd fix the line, he said. Get the water right. Spray the back rows.

I mean after that. I mean next year and the year after.

Maybe try some different varieties. There's some new rootstock that's disease resistant. I read about it.

Joel looked at him. With what money.

I told you I've been putting money aside.

How much money.

Harlan was quiet.

How much, Harlan.

Not enough, Harlan said. Not by itself. He said it the way a man says something that has cost him considerable effort to admit.

Joel finished his water and set the jug down and looked at his hands. They were not the hands of a man who worked outdoors and they looked wrong to him in this place, soft and office-pale, and he felt the familiar guilt of having left, which was always underneath things when he came back.

There's a developer, he said. The realtor mentioned him. He's buying parcels all through this valley. Putting in a subdivision. The Hendersons already sold.

I heard, Harlan said.

Thirty, forty houses where those apricots were.

Harlan said nothing.

I'm not saying that's good, Joel said. I'm not saying I like it. But the Hendersons had their own situation and they made their own decision and I don't think we have the right to judge them for it.

I never said anything about the Hendersons.

You think it though.

Harlan picked up a clod of dirt and crumbled it in his fingers and let the pieces fall. The soil was good soil, well-aggregated, dark with organic matter from decades of mulching and care. It fell in fine crumbles and was wasted on the air.

Mama's buried here, he said.

Joel went still.

She is. Up on the corner by the old oak, Harlan said. Same as Daddy now.

You could still visit. It would still be there. It would be somebody's yard.

Harlan looked at him.

Somebody's yard, he said.

Joel met his eyes and held them.

You think Daddy worked this ground thirty-five years so it could be somebody's yard.

He's not here anymore, Joel said. I wish he was. He's not. The land doesn't know it belonged to him. The land doesn't know anything.

Harlan stood up. He lifted the canvas bag and walked to the fifth break without speaking and Joel sat a moment longer looking at the trees and then he stood and took up the shovel and followed.

The fifth break was a bad one, the pipe having been run over at some point by the tractor and crushed nearly flat and then cracked along the crush line and the damage extended a foot and a half and required cutting out a section. Harlan measured and marked and cut and Joel held the pieces as they came free. The work required concentration and they gave it their concentration and for a time there was nothing between them but the work itself, which was clean in the way that physical work is clean, uncomplicated by the various corruptions of intent and feeling.

When they were fitting the new section Harlan spoke without looking at him.

You need the money, he said.

It's not about what I need.

Joel. I know things have been hard for you. Since the divorce and all.

That's not what this is.

I'm not saying it is. I'm just saying I know.

Joel fit his end of the pipe and they glued and coupled and held and the solvent smell rose up again.

It is partly that, Joel said. After a moment. I'm not going to lie to you about it.

Harlan nodded.

But it's not only that. Even if I had plenty of money I'd be saying the same thing. I've been watching you out here for three years trying to make this work and it's not working. You're killing yourself over it. You look like—he stopped.

I look like what.

You look tired, Harlan.

I am tired.

I know you are.

Being tired's not a reason to quit.

I know that too. But there's other reasons.

A hawk circled high above the orchard in the hot sky and they both watched it for a moment without being aware they were watching it.

What would you do, Joel said. Without the place.

Harlan thought about this. The hawk caught a thermal and rose without effort, a thing of extraordinary ease.

I don't know, he said. I genuinely don't know.

That worries me more than anything, Joel said. That you don't know.

I know how to work, Harlan said. I know that much.

I know you do. But working and working this particular ground are not the same thing.

No. Harlan looked down at the joined pipe, the new white coupling among the old gray sections. No, they're not the same thing.

They moved down to the sixth break and then the seventh. The seventh was the farthest point on the line, at the back of the southernmost row, and they had to walk past the small rise where the two graves were. Harlan did not look at them as he passed but Joel did. The stones were simple and they stood in the shade of the old valley oak and the grass around them had been kept cleared and there were no flowers, the brothers not being men who brought flowers, but the ground was well kept and tended and spoke of someone's regular attention.

They fixed the last break and tested the fittings on the ones before it and Harlan walked back to the pump house and threw the valve and they stood at the far end of the line and waited. After a while the water came through, moving the dirt, feeling its way. It seeped out from two fittings they had not made tight enough and they tightened them and then the water ran as it was supposed to, along the base of the trees, and the dry soil received it.

They watched the water run.

You know what Daddy said, Harlan said, that last summer. You know what he said to me about the place.

I know what he said to me, Joel said.

What did he say to you.

He said do what's right by each other. He said that was all he asked.

Harlan looked at him for a long time.

That's what he said to me too, he said.

The water ran along the row and darkened the soil where it went and the roots of the old trees pulled at it though the trees could not know yet that it was there. Could not know and yet reached for it with all they had, the way living things do in dry country, without intelligence, without anything like hope, only the deep dumb imperative of making it another season.

I'm not going to vote to sell, Harlan said. I want you to know that. Whatever happens. I'm not going to.

I know, Joel said.

But if you— Harlan stopped. He took a breath and looked at the water running. If you push it, he said. If you pushed it legally I mean. Since we both have to agree and you pushed it. I don't know what I'd do.

I'm not going to push it, Joel said.

Harlan looked at him.

I came up here to talk about it. I've said what I think. But I'm not going to push it.

Why not.

Joel thought about it. He watched the water reach the end of the row and start to puddle around the last tree and sink in.

Because it would be like winning something that I don't actually want to win, he said.

Harlan nodded. He picked up the canvas bag. They started back through the rows toward the house, the long way, along the south side, past the apple trees that their father had grafted himself in the early years, two and three varieties on a single trunk so that you could stand at one tree and pick a yellow apple and a red one, which had seemed to them as boys like something from a story, like a thing not quite of this world.

Harlan put his hand on one of the trunks as they passed. Just briefly, just the flat of his hand against the bark, and then he took it away and walked on.

Joel looked at the trunk where his brother's hand had been. The bark was gray and furrowed and old and had the quality of something that had been here before them and would in some form be here after. He kept walking.

Back at the house Harlan stood at the spigot and ran water over his hands and forearms and the back of his neck and stood a moment with his head down and the water running off him. Joel set the shovel against the barn wall and put the pipe cutter and the primer on the shelf inside where they kept such things and he stood in the barn's cool shadow a moment looking at the old equipment, the tractor with its faded paint and the sprayer and the bins stacked to the rafters, and he felt the particular quality of sadness that is not grief but is adjacent to it, that comes from contemplating things that have been loved and used and are wearing out.

He walked to the house and Harlan was already in the kitchen making coffee, moving in the kitchen the way a man moves in a place that is completely his, without thought for where things are because where things are is not a question but a fact, continuous and settled and old.

Joel sat down at the table. It was the same table, the same four chairs, the same window with the same view of the near orchard rows. A calendar on the wall, the current year. Otherwise it could have been any year of his life.

I'll stay tonight, he said. Leave in the morning.

Harlan poured water into the machine and did not look up.

I know you will, he said.

Joel looked at his hands again in the way he had been looking at them all day. Pale, soft, not belonging. Then he looked up at his brother's back, the broad shoulders, the gray-shot hair, the way he stood, which was exactly how their father had stood in that kitchen in every memory Joel had of him, upright and forward-leaning, like a man always about to go back out.

The coffee maker began its work and the smell of it filled the kitchen and through the window the orchard rows stretched away in the afternoon light, the trees standing in their long columns, the water moving slow and sure along the ground beneath them, doing what water does in dry country, finding the low places, filling them, moving on.
