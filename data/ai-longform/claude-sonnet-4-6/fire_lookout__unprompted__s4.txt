The smoke came up from Cutter Drainage at quarter past two in the afternoon, and Nora watched it the way she had learned to watch everything from up here—without moving, first, without even letting herself form a thought around it, just letting the eyes gather what was real before the mind started making stories. It was a single column, pale gray shading to white at the top where it bloomed and spread, and it rose from a place she knew by heart even though she had not been there in four years. She knew the line of ridgeline spruce that notched the view from the east-facing window. She knew the exact bend in the drainage where the timber thinned and the cabin sat in a brushy clearing that her sister had called, without irony, a meadow. The word for what Nora felt when she understood where the smoke was coming from had no clean name. It passed through her the way weather passes through a building that has no walls anymore.

She crossed to the radio first, because that was what you did. She keyed the handset and said her station number and waited. Static, and not even the good kind of static that meant the atmosphere was doing something complicated but honest—just the flat gray sound of nothing connecting to nothing. She had been reporting the dead radio for eleven days. Supply drops came every two weeks in summer, and she was three days from the next one, and the district office had written in their last message, the one that arrived with the first-aid restock and four pounds of rice and a paperback novel she hadn't opened, that a technician would come with the next drop. Three days. The smoke column rose in the windless afternoon and spread at the top like a hand opening.

She went to the Osborne firefinder, though she didn't need it. She knew exactly what azimuth she was looking at. She set the crosshairs on the base of the column where it entered the tree canopy and read the number anyway: 214 degrees. She recorded it in the log, noted the time, noted the weather—clear, humidity somewhere in the twenties, the worst kind of afternoon for what smoke looked like in a drainage full of lodgepole and dry grass. She wrote the radio status in the log margin: still inoperative, day eleven. She wrote it the way she had been writing it, as a record, as a document of something that would matter to someone later, perhaps to an inquiry, perhaps to no one.

She had been up here since May, her sixth and final season, because the tower was being decommissioned at the end of August. The Forest Service had sent a memo in the spring—she still had it, folded under the corner of the mattress—explaining that aerial detection and satellite imaging had made the ground-based stations redundant. The memo used the word redundant three times. Nora had read it at her kitchen table in Missoula, in February, when the world outside was still white and she had a glass of wine she hadn't drunk yet and the sound of the upstairs neighbor's television bleeding through the ceiling. She had felt, reading it, less like someone being laid off than like a language being retired, a whole way of seeing things declared unnecessary by people who had never looked out at forty miles of timber from a height of seven thousand feet and understood what it meant to be the instrument through which that landscape knew itself.

Now the smoke was real and it was in Cutter Drainage and her radio was dead and her sister was possibly burning.

She stood at the east window for three more minutes, which was all she allowed herself, timing it against the clock on the wall. The column had not changed character. It wasn't blossoming the way a crown fire would, wasn't blackening, wasn't throwing embers you could see from this distance. It had the quality of something just started, something still deciding what it wanted to be. In her experience that could mean a campfire got loose, a trash pile, a lightning snag that had been smoldering for days and finally found enough oxygen at the base. It could also mean a structure. Structures made a different kind of smoke, thicker and more chemical, with a brownish undertone if there were synthetic materials involved—insulation, carpet, furniture from the last four decades of manufacturing. Margaret's cabin had been their parents' cabin originally, and their parents had furnished it in the nineteen-seventies with wool and wood and a cast-iron stove and almost nothing that had come from a factory after 1985. What Nora could see from two miles of air was pale gray and white, which might have meant anything or nothing.

She put on her boots.

This is where the mind does its worst work, she thought. This is where the mind tries to make the thing simple. She had left the cabin the last time in September four years ago after a conversation that had lasted four hours and covered thirty years of grievance and love and misunderstanding and the specific cruelty that is available only between sisters, that intimacy as weapon, and she had driven her truck down the eight miles of switchbacks and out the forest road and all the way back to Missoula without stopping and she had not gone back. Margaret had not come to her, either. They had exchanged three emails in four years, each one formally polite and incomplete, the way you write to someone you are still trying to remember how to love.

The road was eight miles, and it was steep for the first three, and her truck was at the bottom. She had a pack frame. She had done eight miles in mountains in under two hours when she was younger and in different circumstances, and she was forty-four now and it had been four years and the circumstances were that there might be a fire in the drainage where her sister kept a cabin and slept in a bed she had had since she was a girl.

She put water in the pack, all she had, which was two liters. She put in the hand radio, which was also dead but which she carried on the theory that dead things sometimes revive if you take them somewhere with better luck. She put in the laminated map she had never once needed because she knew this ground the way she knew her own body—imperfectly, but by feel—and she put it in anyway because she was trying to make the right decisions in the right order instead of just running. She wrote in the log: 14:23. Smoke column, Cutter Drainage, 214 degrees, uncontrolled origin unknown. Radio inoperative. Departing tower on foot to investigate. This was not protocol. Protocol was to continue monitoring and attempt radio contact and wait for the next scheduled drop. Protocol assumed a working radio and a fire that was not, specifically and with terrible coincidence, in the place where your estranged sister lived. She wrote a second line: In the event this log is found without attendant personnel, see attached record of radio outage, days 1 through 11. She didn't know why she wrote that. She was not planning on anything happening to her on an eight-mile walk down a forest road. She was writing to the future the way she always wrote in the log, as if it were a person who would arrive eventually and need to understand what had happened here.

She locked the tower door behind her even though there was nothing left in the tower worth stealing, just her few boxes of personal belongings and the Osborne and the logbook and the iron cot and the paperback she hadn't opened. She locked it because it was her place, and the lock was a way of saying so, and she would not be made careless by urgency. She put the key in her breast pocket and buttoned it.

The first half mile of the trail down was through a burn from three summers ago—her first summer up here, though the fire had started at the end of August, just days before she arrived for the season, so she had come to a landscape that was already changed, the black snags still standing, the fireweed coming up in bright columns between them. She had learned the mountain through that burn, had learned to read the topography by the way the fire had moved through it, which draws it had run up and which ridges it had crowned and where it had laid down in the night when the humidity came up and waited to start again at dawn. The fireweed was gone now, replaced by grasses and young lodgepole too small to cast shade, and walking through it was like walking through a time she could measure but not fully comprehend, the way you feel your own age in photographs.

She walked fast. She had the smoke at her back and she didn't turn to check it, which required more discipline than it sounds like. When you have spent six summers learning to see smoke as the central event of your world, the thing that gave your presence at altitude meaning, not turning to watch a smoke column requires a deliberate redirection of a very deep instinct. She made herself think about her feet, the placement of each step on the rutted road, the way the dust was fine and powdery and rose a little around her boots and fell back. She made herself think about the road as itself, as the road she had driven up at the beginning of May in her truck with the windows down and the aspens still bare and the smell of the mountain that she had missed all winter coming through the cab like a word she'd been trying to remember.

She had not thought, driving up in May, about Margaret's cabin. She had thought about it in February, reading the decommissioning memo, because the memo had mentioned Cutter Drainage explicitly—the cabin there was one of three private inholdings in the district, and the tower's coverage area included it, and the memo had noted that the district was in the process of acquiring the inholdings or negotiating new easements. She had read that sentence twice. She had thought: someone should tell Margaret. She had not contacted Margaret. She had driven to the mountain in May thinking about other things, about the last of the rice she'd left in her Missoula apartment for the friend who was watching her plants, about whether the truck's alignment had been fixed properly, about a book she was listening to on the drive, a novel about people in a city she had never been to making choices she did not understand.

Now she thought about the memo sentence. The district is in the process of acquiring the inholdings. She thought about Margaret receiving a letter like that, a Forest Service letter, formal and uninflected, explaining that the land her parents had left her was wanted by the government and here was what that process looked like. Margaret would not have called Nora about it. Margaret would not have called anyone. Margaret handled things by making them smaller inside herself until they were the size of something she could carry without anyone noticing the weight. That was what their mother had said once, a long time ago, not critically, just observationally, as their mother described her daughters with the accuracy of someone who had nothing invested in the description being flattering. Nora lets things get bigger, their mother had said. Margaret makes them smaller. Neither is better. Both are going to cost you.

At three miles the road flattened and entered a section of older timber, ponderosa and larch, trees big enough to make the light in there amber and cool, and she stopped to drink half a liter of water and actually listen to her body, which was reporting a fine tremor in her right hand that she recognized as being made of something other than exertion. The smoke she couldn't see from in here. She could smell something on the air that might have been smoke or might have been just the afternoon heating up the resin in the trees around her. She stood with her hand around the water bottle and tried to decide what she knew for certain, which was almost nothing, versus what she was afraid of, which was a great deal.

She was afraid that the cabin was burning.

She was afraid that Margaret was in it.

She was afraid that Margaret was in it and that they had spent four years not speaking and that this meant something about who Nora was, about the particular way she let things get bigger instead of finding a way to put them down.

She was also afraid, and this felt like the lowest and most honest fear, underneath all the others, that Margaret was fine—that the smoke was a campfire or a snag or a pile of brush Margaret had decided to burn on a bad burn day because Margaret had never paid much attention to burn advisories—and that Nora would arrive down the eight miles of road and across the creek and up the drainage trail and find her sister standing in the clearing, alive and fine and surprised, and that all of the arriving would have meant something Nora wasn't ready for it to mean.

She put the water away and kept walking.

The road crossed the creek at four miles on a plank bridge that the district put in every June and pulled out every October, and this year the bridge was already in, already weathered a little, and she crossed it and heard the water below which was still running good this late in summer, the drainage holding its snowpack longer than the south-facing slopes. She stopped in the middle of the bridge for a moment because the creek was saying something in the particular voice of water over shaped stone and she had always been unable to pass over water without stopping, which Margaret had made fun of when they were girls, calling her River Nora, saying she was going to float away one day. River Nora. She had not thought of that in years. She stood on the bridge and the creek went under her and ahead the road bent into the timber and beyond that, still a mile, the junction for the Cutter Drainage trail.

She could see the smoke again from the bridge. It had changed. The column was thicker now, more active at the base, and she thought she could see a brownish undertone, though the light was different down here than it was from the tower and she could not trust her reading of it the same way. Structures, she thought again. That color means structures. She got off the bridge and started walking faster than was wise on a rutted road and stopped caring about being wise.

The trail junction was marked with a brown post she had always found vaguely apologetic in its modesty, a two-inch square of wood where there should have been something more significant, given what the trail led to. A half mile in, the trail dropped into the drainage properly, following the creek, the trees coming close on both sides and then opening suddenly into the clearing, which was bigger than she always remembered, maybe two acres, the grass brown from summer dryness, and the cabin at the far end of it, which was—

Intact. Standing. Not burning.

The smoke was coming from the fire ring twenty feet from the cabin's east wall, a stone ring she had helped Margaret build twelve years ago, both of them moving rocks from the creek in wheelbarrow loads and arguing about the ring's diameter and which stones were flat enough and whether fire rings really needed mortar. The fire in it now was large for a fire ring—too large, Nora thought, for a day like this, humidity in the twenties, no wind to make it immediately dangerous but no buffer either. Margaret was standing beside it with a long stick, moving things in the fire, and she did not look up when Nora came out of the trees.

There was a pile of things beside the fire ring. Nora could see papers, a box of files, what looked like an accordion folder, some fabric. Margaret was burning documents. She was burning, Nora realized, the contents of the cabin.

She walked across the clearing and Margaret heard her and looked up, and they looked at each other across the smoke and the fire and twelve years and four years and all the other numbers that accumulate between people who have known each other their whole lives.

Margaret was thinner than Nora remembered. She had the same face, of course, the same face their mother had had but differently arranged, or the same arrangement and differently lived-in. Her hair was shorter. She was wearing a canvas coat Nora had never seen, olive green, too warm for the afternoon. She held the stick in both hands.

Neither of them said anything for a moment that lasted long enough to have its own weather.

"I could see this from the tower," Nora said finally.

"I know." Margaret looked back at the fire. "I thought about that."

"The radio's been out for eleven days."

"I know that too. The district sent a letter."

Nora stopped a few feet from the fire ring. The heat came off it in waves that weren't unpleasant in the cooling afternoon, and she could smell now what was burning—paper, the char smell of paper, and something else beneath it, something she couldn't name, the smell of a specific life making itself into smoke. "They're taking the cabin," she said.

"August first. I have until August first to clear it." Margaret pushed something deeper into the fire. A photograph, Nora thought, though she couldn't see it clearly enough to be sure. "I'm not leaving anything for them to move."

"There's a fire advisory today."

"I know."

"Humidity's twenty-two."

"Nora." Margaret said her name the way she had always said it when she wanted her to stop saying what she was currently saying, the word a complete sentence with a period already in it.

Nora sat down on the ground beside the fire ring, on the side that kept the smoke out of her face. She did this without deciding to. Her legs had walked eight miles and they wanted to stop, and some part of her that was finished with the eight miles of urgency and fear understood that there was nothing here that needed urgency, that what was here needed something else entirely, something slower.

Margaret kept moving things into the fire.

"Mom's letters?" Nora asked, because she thought she saw the shape of an envelope in the pile.

"Some of them."

"You should keep them."

"I've read them."

"That's not what I mean."

"I know what you mean." Margaret's voice was not unkind. It was the voice of someone who had been making a decision for a long time and had finished making it. "I've been up here for two months getting this done. I don't need to take all of it with me."

"Where are you going?"

Margaret looked at her for the first time since the first look, the real look, the look across all the distance. "I was going to ask you the same thing. Isn't the tower closing?"

"End of August."

"So we're both done with this mountain."

Nora didn't answer, which was an answer. She looked at the fire, which was doing what fire does, which is to consume patiently and without preference, treating letters and memories and the accumulated evidence of a life with exactly the same process it treats a lodgepole snag: something to move through until there is nothing left to move through. She thought about the tower, about the logbook with her handwriting in it, about the Osborne with its 214-degree azimuth. She had locked the door. She had locked the door and buttoned the key into her breast pocket and the key was there now, a small hard rectangle against her sternum, and when she put her hand there she could feel it.

She reached into the pile beside the fire ring without asking. She found the envelope she thought she'd seen and she held it. Her mother's handwriting on the outside, Margaret's name in blue ink. She didn't open it. She held it until it was warm from the fire's nearness and then she put it back in the pile, which was Margaret's decision, not hers. That was something that had taken her a long time to learn. She had made things bigger all her life, had let them expand until they filled the rooms she was in, and she was forty-four years old on the ground beside a fire ring in a mountain drainage and she was maybe just now beginning to understand the size of the things that were actually hers to carry.

"I'll help you with the rest of it," she said.

Margaret was quiet for a moment. Then: "You walked eight miles."

"Yes."

"There's food in the cabin. There's probably an hour of daylight left worth working in."

"Okay."

"And then—" Margaret stopped. And then was a large country. They were both standing at its border.

"And then we can figure out the rest," Nora said.

Margaret looked at her again with the face that was their mother's face but differently arranged and had been a girl's face and was a woman's face now, and something in it that had been held was, very slightly, less held. She nodded once. She handed Nora the stick.

Nora took it and stood with it beside the fire and began to do what needed doing, which was to move things toward the center where the heat was, and to tend the edge so nothing got loose, and to watch the smoke rise into the afternoon that was cooling now around them, the mountain starting its long exhalation toward evening, the column of smoke rising pale and straight in the still air and spreading at the top, visible for miles, visible perhaps from the locked tower on the ridge where the logbook lay open to her handwriting, and she was down here now, below the long view, in the close work of the fire.
