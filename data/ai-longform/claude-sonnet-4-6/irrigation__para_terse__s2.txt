The morning came in low and white, the kind of overcast that didn't promise rain but only withheld sun, and Darryl was already in the truck when Marcus came out of the house pulling on his second glove. He could see his brother through the windshield, one arm draped over the steering wheel, looking at nothing in particular, or looking at the orchard the way you look at something you've stopped seeing. Marcus climbed in and the door complained on its hinge and neither of them said anything for the quarter mile of dirt track to the equipment shed.

They loaded the truck with what they needed: sections of half-inch poly pipe, the coupling kit in its plastic case, a flat-head and a Phillips, the pipe cutter that their father had kept on the second shelf to the right of the door, always, for thirty years. Marcus took it down and put it in the bed and felt the specific weight of it, the orange handle worn pale where the hand goes. He didn't say anything about that either.

The break was somewhere in the north block, in the Gravensteins. Their mother had called Darryl about it the week before, said she'd noticed the ground was dry in a way it shouldn't be, that the fruit was starting to show it. She'd walked the block herself trying to find where the water was going and when she couldn't find it she'd called Darryl in Sacramento and he'd driven down the night before. Marcus had been here. Had been here all spring, and all the spring before that.

They drove the track along the windbreak and Darryl cracked his window and the air came in smelling of grass and something faintly sour, the beginning of the apples' early drop scattered at the margins of the rows. The trees went on in their lines, the grass between them waist-high and going yellow, and Marcus looked at them the way he always looked at them, which was close, which was the way you look at things that require something from you.

Darryl said, "It's been dry."

"May was short," Marcus said. "And before that."

"I know before that."

"Then you know."

Darryl pulled up where the pressure gauge was zip-tied to the riser at the head of the block and they both got out. Marcus crouched and read it. He stood and walked into the first row and Darryl followed him. They walked the drip tape in the first row and didn't find anything and walked the second and Marcus stopped in the third, just past the fifth tree, where the ground had a depression in it and the tape was pulled up on one side where something, a squirrel or a vole, had worked at it.

"Here's one," Marcus said.

"One," Darryl said.

"There'll be more."

There were two more in the next row and one in the row after that, each a small puncture or a full break, the tape skinned open or just pinholed by teeth. At each site the ground was dust for a foot in every direction and then you didn't know, with ground that thirsty. They walked the block for an hour and found seven breaks in the drip tape and one in the half-inch supply line where a coupling had failed, probably in the winter's one frost, the cold having gotten into the water left standing in the pipe and split the housing of the coupler clean along a seam you could trace with your thumbnail.

"That's your pressure problem," Marcus said.

"I figured something like that."

They went back to the truck and Marcus got the bag of coupling repair kits and the drip tape repair fittings and they split the rows between them. There was no conversation in this, just the old division, the way they had learned the orchard in halves, and for a time they worked apart and Marcus could hear Darryl somewhere in the rows, the sounds of exertion small and then swallowed. He worked on his knees mostly, pressing the barbed repair fittings into the cut ends of the tape, the tape stiff in his hands, resistant. He cleaned the dirt from around each fitting before he put it in and he firmed the ground back down after, which was not strictly necessary and which he did anyway.

When he got to the supply line he called to Darryl and Darryl came over and they looked at the coupler together. Marcus got the pipe cutter from where he'd set it on the truck's bumper and cut an inch back from either side of the failed coupling and Darryl held the new coupling while Marcus worked the pipe ends in, pressing hard against the resistance of the poly, bracing the pipe against his thigh. He could feel his brother's stillness beside him, which was not a helpful stillness but more like the stillness of a man with something ready to say.

"This is the second one of these this season," Marcus said.

"I know."

"If it's starting to go in the joints it'll go in more of them. It's old line. Pop put it in ninety-four, ninety-five."

"I know when he put it in."

"I'm saying we'll be back out here."

"I know what you're saying."

Marcus fit the second end of the coupling and let go and it held. He picked up the failed piece from the ground and turned it in his fingers. "I want to clear the grass," he said. "Get the mower in this week before it really dries and there's a fire risk. And I want to check the pressure over in the Galas because if we've got this issue here I'd bet we've got it in that block too."

Darryl had moved a few feet away and was standing with his hands in his back pockets, looking down the row at the trees. After a moment he said, "Marcus."

"I'm going to turn the system back on," Marcus said. He got up and walked to the riser.

"Marcus."

He opened the valve and waited and they both listened to the line fill, the soft percussion of air working out of the fittings, and then the low hiss of pressure settling and the quiet after.

"I don't want to do this here," Marcus said.

"Then where."

"We can talk tonight. At the house."

"We talked tonight at the house last night and you went to bed."

That was true. Marcus looked at the trees. The morning's overcast was beginning to lift and the light in the row was changing, the apple leaves going from gray-green to a color that was more properly their own. He had spent twenty years learning to read this light, or not learning it so much as accumulating it, the way you accumulate any knowledge that nobody teaches you, in pieces, without knowing you're doing it, until one day you look at a row of trees in a certain light and you know things you couldn't say you know.

"Forty acres," Darryl said. "You're working forty acres by yourself."

"Mom helps."

"Mom is sixty-eight."

"She knows that. She's there every morning."

"That's not the point I'm making."

"I know your point."

Darryl pulled his hands from his pockets and crossed his arms and looked up through the canopy, through the pale undersides of the leaves to the white sky. He had their father's way of doing this, of looking up when he was trying to find something, as if the thinking happened better with height. Marcus had always looked down, at the ground, at what was in his hands. Their father had said once that this was the difference between the two of them and he'd said it without apparent judgment, as a man reports a fact, though Marcus had spent some years not being sure it was without judgment.

"Petrosyan offered again," Darryl said.

"I know."

"More than last time."

"I know that too."

"He's not going to keep offering."

"Armenians are patient," Marcus said. "He'll keep offering."

"That's a thing you're telling yourself." Darryl turned to face him. He looked like their father and Marcus supposed he did too, though it was harder to see in yourself. Same broadness through the shoulders, same length to the face. Darryl kept his hair shorter now than he had when they were young and there was gray in it, at the temples especially. "You know what he's going to do with it," Darryl said.

"I know."

"Just—you know. It won't be an orchard."

"I know it won't be an orchard."

"You'd be all right with that."

"I didn't say I'd be all right with it. I said I know."

Darryl looked at him. "You're not all right with it."

"Nobody's asking me to be all right with it. You want to sell, you have the same share I do, you can start that conversation. That's all I'm saying. You want to have that conversation, have it."

"I'm having it."

"With Mom," Marcus said. "That's who you need to have it with, not me."

Darryl was quiet. He looked down the row again and then back. "She asked me to talk to you first."

That was something Marcus hadn't known and he received it the way you receive a thing you should have anticipated, with a feeling that was less surprise than a kind of shame at having missed it. He thought of his mother that morning at the kitchen table with her coffee, already up when he came down, the way she was always already up, her hands around the mug in the way she had of holding things as if they might be taken. She hadn't said anything about Darryl coming. She'd asked him whether he'd slept and he'd said he had.

"She's tired," Darryl said, more quietly.

"I know she's tired."

"She doesn't show it."

"That's how you know she's tired. When she stops showing it."

Darryl moved down the row and stopped at the nearest tree. He put his hand on the bark the way you'd put your hand on the shoulder of someone you weren't sure you had the right to touch. The tree was old, older than both of them, the trunk thick and gray-brown and furrowed, the kind of bark that looks like it has survived things. "Pop would've sold," Darryl said.

"No."

"You don't know that."

"I know that."

"He was a practical man."

"He was," Marcus said. "He also planted a hundred Gravensteins in 1987 when everyone he knew said not to because the market was gone and Gravensteins were done. He did that practically. He thought it through practically and he did it."

"And now we're keeping sixty-year-old trees going with poly pipe and—"

"Ninety-four, I said. They're not sixty."

"—with poly pipe and prayer and the prayer isn't working—"

"The pipe is working fine, I just fixed it."

"Marcus." Darryl took his hand from the tree. There was a tiredness in the way he said the name, and Marcus heard it and didn't want to hear it. "I have the boys to think about. Caleb starts school in the fall. Private school, Dana's got her heart set, and I'm not fighting that fight, I'm not. And the house—we're into the house for what we're into it for, and—"

"I'm not asking you to carry anything," Marcus said. "I'm not asking you to put any money in. You haven't put money in in four years."

"And I feel that."

"Do you."

"Yes. I feel it." He said it straight, without defensiveness, and that was harder to answer than defensiveness would have been. "I do. But feeling it doesn't fix what this place costs. Mom's not making the mortgage from the harvest, Marcus. You know what last year looked like."

"I know what last year looked like."

"And this year isn't better."

Marcus walked to the end of the row and stood at the edge of the block where the trees stopped and the unplanted headland began, the grass long there, unkempt, going to seed. Beyond it was the fence line and beyond the fence was the neighbor's pasture, dry and amber, and beyond that the hills, brown already, the summer having gone into them early. He thought about his father walking this same headland, which his father had done most evenings when he was trying to think. He himself had never walked it for that purpose. He had walked it to check the fence, to pick up the drops, to look at the outside trees for disease or damage. He had not walked it to think because the orchard itself was where he thought, in the doing of things, in the rows.

He heard Darryl come up behind him.

"You've given this place twelve years," Darryl said.

"Fourteen."

"Fourteen. Since you came back from—after Lisa."

"We don't need to bring that in."

"I'm not bringing it in as anything. I'm saying—you've given it that. Nobody can say you didn't give it that. Dad knew you gave it that and he knew what it cost you."

"It didn't cost me anything."

"Marcus."

"It didn't. I want to be here. That's the whole point. I want to be here. This is not a sacrifice I'm performing."

"But if it went away."

"It won't go away."

"But if."

Marcus looked at the hills. A red-tailed hawk was working a thermal over the far end of the pasture, and he watched it for a moment, the way it moved without moving, the stillness of its wings that was not stillness but control. "I'd still be here," he said. "I'd be here either way. The land would still be here. Petrosyan would build his houses on it and I'd still be in the county and the land would be underneath the houses and the trees would be gone but the land would be there."

"That's a bleak way to be comforted."

"I'm not looking for comfort."

"What are you looking for."

He didn't answer right away. In the row behind them one of the drip emitters opened and they heard it, the soft release of pressure, the minute sound of water finding ground. "I'm looking for another year," Marcus said. "I'm always looking for another year. You think I don't know it every time? I know it. I knew it when I signed off on the repair bill for the irrigation pump in February. I knew it when we replanted the three trees we lost in November. I know it every morning."

"Then why."

"Because another year is something. Another harvest is something. Even a bad harvest. Even last year." He turned to look at his brother. "The Galas were good last year. You didn't see them. Third week of August, before the heat came, they were really good. The color was—I'm telling you, Darryl, the color was something. I had Reyes and his boys picking and I walked behind them and I kept stopping. I kept just stopping in the row."

Darryl looked at him with something that was not pity exactly but was in that neighborhood, and Marcus looked back and held it.

"We'd split it even," Darryl said. "I checked what he paid for the Hollis piece, hundred and twelve acres, and if he's paying comparable—"

"I know what we'd get."

"Mom would be fine. She could—there's a place near Dana's parents, a good place, she'd have people around—"

"She'd never go there. She'd never leave the valley."

"She might."

"She wouldn't. Darryl. She was born in the house."

Darryl rubbed his face with both hands and looked up again at the sky, which was fully bright now, the overcast gone, the day arriving at what it actually was. The light fell hard on the tops of the trees and on the dry grass of the headland and on the two of them standing there. Marcus could feel it on the back of his neck.

"What do you need," Darryl said. "Concretely. What is the actual thing you need to make this work for another, for a few years."

"I need the pump replaced, not repaired. And I need the north block re-piped. The whole block. You saw today, it's going to be one break and then another all season, it's already compromised, the whole run of it."

"What does that come to."

"For the pump, good pump, installed, eighteen, twenty. For the north block—material and labor, I can do some of the labor—maybe thirty-five, maybe more."

"So fifty, sixty."

"It's an estimate."

"And after that."

"After that it runs. I keep it running. There's always something but that's the—the pump and the north block are the structural things. After that it's maintenance."

"You think."

"I think."

Darryl was doing the arithmetic that Marcus had done so many times he had worn a groove in it. He was staring at the hills and doing it and Marcus watched him do it. The hawk had moved off. The pasture was still.

"I can do thirty," Darryl said. "I can put in thirty. Dana will ask me things I don't want to be asked but I can do it."

"I'm not asking."

"I know you're not. I'm saying."

"I'd want to pay you back out of—I'd want it to be a loan."

"I know you would. Let's not fight about that now."

"I'm not fighting."

"I know. I'm saying let's not fight about it now." Darryl put his hands in his pockets and looked at the Gravensteins behind them, the rows going back in their dim and ordered way, the fruit still weeks from any color, hard and pale, the size of large marbles, hanging in the leaves. "They did look good last fall," he said. "I'm not disputing that. I remember when Pop—that year we had that run in August, the week before school, and he wouldn't hire extra help, just the three of us, and Mom, and Reyes's father, and we barely got them in."

"He said he didn't like strangers in those rows."

"He did say that."

"Which was insane. Which was not a sustainable philosophy."

"No." Darryl smiled briefly. It passed. "It was not." He started walking back toward the truck and Marcus walked alongside him and their footsteps were soft in the dry grass. "You should come for the fourth," Darryl said. "Caleb asks about you. Both of them."

"I'll try. If the mowing is done I'll try."

"Not try. Come."

"All right."

"Dana makes a thing. She does a whole—it's a production. You'd like it."

"I don't doubt it."

They had reached the truck. Marcus put the coupling kit and the leftover fittings in the bed and picked up the failed coupler from where he'd left it on the bumper, the thing split along its seam, and held it for a moment and then put it in the bed with the rest. He'd throw it away later or he wouldn't and it would accumulate in the shed the way things accumulated in the shed, everything broken kept against some possible future that kept not arriving.

Darryl got in the driver's side and Marcus went around and got in and they sat for a moment before Darryl started the truck, both of them looking out through the windshield at the rows. The light now was full and direct and made the green of the canopy very bright, almost garish, the trees too present, too insistently alive. Marcus thought of the water moving under the ground in the poly pipe, the repaired fittings holding, the emitters opening and closing. He thought of the roots in the dark taking what they were given. It was not a sentimental thought but an operational one, the thought of a man who has chosen maintenance over everything else, who has organized his life around the prevention of loss, which is never finally preventable, which is the whole of it.

Darryl started the truck and turned it around in the headland and they drove back along the windbreak. The orchard moved past Marcus's window and he watched it without turning his head, the rows striding past in sequence, each one a moment and gone, and the grass between the trees going gold where the sun fell and green where it didn't, the light not even, the light as it always was out here, various, local, tending to fall on some things and leave others in shade. His brother drove and said nothing and Marcus was grateful for the nothing, which held more than most of what they'd said. The house came into view at the end of the track, the white of it gone to cream in the morning light, and behind it the hills, and behind the hills the sky going intensely and uselessly blue, the way the sky in the valley goes blue in June, as if it were trying to tell you something you already know about the length and cost of summer, about the nature of what you're in the middle of, which does not stop being beautiful because it is hard, or because it is ending, or because what you love about it is the part that cannot hold.
