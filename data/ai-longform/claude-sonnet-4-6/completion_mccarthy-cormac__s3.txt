He came awake again to her hand on his arm and the darkness of the room and lay there breathing.

Lord, he said.

She had turned away from him and lay on her side with the great swell of her belly toward the wall. Outside the window the sky held a thin pewter light that was not yet dawn and a dog somewhere in the town was barking at something or at nothing. He lay listening to it.

Go back to sleep, she said.

He put his feet on the floor and sat with his elbows on his knees and his face in his hands. The cold came up through the boards. When he looked up he could see the window as a pale rectangle against the dark and beyond the glass the last few stars of the season hung cold and indifferent and he watched them until the dog quit its barking and everything fell quiet again.

He dressed in the dark and went out into the hall and down the stairs and into the kitchen where he built up the fire and put the coffeepot on. He stood at the window looking out at the yard. The outhouse, the woodpile. The old wagon sitting where he'd left it three months gone and where it would sit until he got around to fixing the wheel or till it fell to pieces entire, whichever came first. He thought it would be the latter.

When the coffee was done he poured a cup and took it to the table and sat down. The fire had begun to throw some heat and he let it work on him and drank his coffee and by the time the cup was empty the sky outside had gone from pewter to a thin gray and he could hear birds beginning somewhere off in the brush beyond the yard.

His name was Sylder. John Wesley Sylder. He'd been in the valley his whole life except for two years with the army in a place he did not discuss and seldom thought about directly, the way you don't look directly at the sun, though it came at him sideways all the same in dreams and in certain qualities of light and in the specific smell of mud mixed with something sweeter that he'd catch sometimes in spring when the fields were being turned. He was thirty-four years old and his wife was twenty-two and they had been married three years and this would be their first child if it lived.

He poured a second cup and took it back to the table.

The child had been a long time coming. She'd lost two before this one and they didn't speak of it. The doctor in town had told her things he hadn't shared with Sylder and Sylder hadn't asked because he knew that the asking would require the answering and he didn't want to carry whatever the doctor had to say around inside him on top of everything else he already carried.

She called from upstairs.

He went to the foot of the stairs. Yeah, he called back.

Is there coffee.

I'll bring it up.

He fixed a cup the way she liked it and carried it up the stairs and into the room. She had propped herself against the headboard and sat with her hair down around her shoulders and her hands folded on her belly. Even in the bad light she looked very young to him. She always did. He handed her the cup and she took it in both hands.

Thank you, she said.

He sat on the edge of the bed. She drank the coffee and he watched the light coming up outside the window. The sky was going pink over the eastern ridge and the shapes of the trees there stood up black against it in a kind of stark ceremony.

How long were you up? she said.

Not long.

Was it the same one?

He looked at his hands. Near enough, he said.

She didn't say anything to that. She'd learned early on not to push at it. She drank her coffee and he sat there and they listened to the morning come on, the birds getting louder now, a rooster somewhere down the hollow starting up.

I need to go over to Gifford's today, he said.

What for.

He owes me for the timber.

She shifted her weight and made a small sound that had nothing to do with what he'd said. He looked at her.

I'm all right, she said. Go on.

You sure.

I'm not made of glass.

I know it.

Well then.

He stood and went to the window and looked out. The light was coming fast now, filling up the yard and the field beyond. The wagon sat red with rust in the growing light. He'd have to get someone to help him move it come spring.

Gifford, she said. I don't like you going over there.

It's just business.

It's never just business with him.

Sylder didn't answer. He picked up his jacket from the chair and shrugged into it.

John Wesley, she said.

It'll be fine.

You said that about the Henderson business.

He turned to look at her. That's different, he said.

She watched him with those steady gray eyes that had looked at him the same way since she was sixteen years old and he'd first seen her at her father's place up on the ridge. Eyes that didn't believe in much they couldn't see and touch but that looked at whatever was in front of them without flinching.

I'll be back before noon, he said.

She looked down at her coffee. All right, she said.

He went down and ate a piece of cold bread standing at the counter and then went out to saddle the horse.

THE ROAD TO Gifford's ran south along the river for three miles and then cut up into the hills on a switchback trace that had been a game trail before it was a wagon road and before that probably something older still, some ancient transit whose originators were long since gone from the world and unmourned and unremembered. The horse picked its way along the frozen ruts and Sylder rode with his collar up and his hat pulled down and his breath coming white in the cold morning air.

He'd known Gifford twenty years. Known him and done business with him and stayed as clear of him as a man could while still doing the one and the other. Gifford was one of those men who seemed to have been born into the world already knowing certain things about it that other men took years to learn or never learned at all, and what he'd learned had not made him a better man but had made him a more capable one, which is a different thing altogether. He ran cattle and sold timber and had a hand in other things that were less easily named and the county gave him a wide berth the way you give a wide berth to something you're not sure is dead.

Sylder had sold him a stand of walnut off the east forty in October and had yet to see a dollar of it.

The road climbed and the river fell away below him and through the bare trees he could see it moving, gray and cold and indifferent, carrying whatever it carried south toward wherever it was going. He watched it until the trees closed again and then he watched the road.

There were three horses at the rail when he came into Gifford's yard. He didn't recognize two of them but the third was the big roan that belonged to Cecil Noakes and he sat his horse a moment looking at it before he dismounted and tied up and went to the door.

He knocked and waited.

The door was opened by a man he didn't know, young and wide through the shoulders with pale eyes and a week's beard coming in patchy and uneven. He looked at Sylder without expression.

I'm here to see Gifford, Sylder said.

The man held the door and Sylder went in.

The front room was low-ceilinged and close with a big stone fireplace in which a fire was going hard despite the lateness of the morning. Cecil Noakes was sitting in a chair by the fire with his legs crossed and a cup of something in his hand and he looked up when Sylder came in and then looked back at the fire. He was a small man, narrow, with the look of a person who had been dried out from the inside. He and Sylder had grown up in the same valley and did not like each other and had not for a long time.

Gifford himself came in from the back of the house. He was a big man, going gray, with large rough hands that looked made for entirely different purposes than whatever they were currently engaged in and a face that the years had not so much aged as organized, all of it settling into a final arrangement that said something Sylder didn't like to read.

Sylder, he said.

Gifford.

He gestured at a chair but Sylder didn't sit. Gifford studied him a moment with something like amusement and then went and poured himself a cup from the pot on the fire.

You're here about the timber, Gifford said.

I am.

Gifford drank from his cup and looked into the fire. Noakes hadn't moved or spoken. The pale-eyed young man had gone back to wherever he'd come from and there was one other in the room, another man Sylder didn't know, older, sitting at the table in the corner doing nothing that Sylder could see, just sitting there in the way that certain men sit who have learned how to wait without it costing them anything.

What are you getting for walnut up at the mill now? Gifford said.

We agreed on a price, Sylder said.

Gifford nodded slowly. We did, he said. We did agree on a price.

Sylder waited.

The thing is, Gifford said. He paused and drank from his cup. The thing is the market's moved.

The market moved after we agreed.

Gifford looked at him pleasantly. That's right, he said.

Sylder looked at him. He felt the thing in his chest that he'd felt a hundred times before in his life at moments like this, that pressure behind the sternum, and he breathed through it.

We had an agreement, Sylder said.

Gifford set his cup down on the mantel and turned to face him and the pleasantness didn't leave his face but something else came up behind it.

You've got a wife about to drop a child, Gifford said. You've got a wagon that's been sitting broke in your yard since September. You've got a note at the bank in town with Deaver's name on it that comes due in the spring. He paused. I know what you've got, he said. And I know what you need. And I know what the market'll bear.

Sylder stood there.

I'll give you two thirds of what we said, Gifford said. You can take it or you can try to get your timber back out of my yard, which I don't think you'll do.

The fire crackled. Noakes recrossed his legs and drank from his cup. The man at the table continued to sit the way he sat.

Sylder looked at Gifford for a long time. Then he nodded once.

Two thirds, he said.

Two thirds. Gifford turned back to the fire. I'll have someone bring it out to you tomorrow.

Sylder went out. He stood in the cold yard for a moment and then untied his horse and mounted and rode back down the hill the way he'd come.

He rode slowly. The river was below him again and he watched it. A hawk was working the far bank, hanging in the cold air above the bare sycamores, and he watched the hawk until the road curved and it was gone.

He'd known it would go that way. He'd known it the night before when he'd decided to go and he'd known it the week before when he'd first started thinking about going and some part of him had known it since the day he'd taken Gifford's word in October without a piece of paper between them. You could call it foolishness or you could call it something else, some need to believe that a man's word still meant what it once had meant, or was supposed to have meant, in some prior world that may or may not have ever actually existed. His father had been the same way and it hadn't served him either.

He thought about the child. He thought about what two thirds would get them and what it wouldn't.

The road came back down to the river and he rode along it with the water running dark and close beside him.

HE DIDN'T GO straight home. He went instead to the town, which was three miles the other direction, and he tied the horse in front of the hardware store and went in and bought what he needed for the wagon wheel and then stood in front of the store for a while watching the street.

The town was not much. It had been more once and was less now and the direction of travel was not encouraging. The bank was still there, Deaver's bank, the old limestone front of it looking like a face that has given up expecting good news. The hotel was still open, technically. The barbershop. Two of the three saloons that had operated when Sylder was young were gone, replaced by a dry goods and an empty building whose purpose he'd forgotten. He stood with the paper package under his arm and watched a wagon go by with two men on the seat who didn't look at him.

He went to the post office and there was a letter waiting for him from his brother in Memphis, the first in eight months. He stood in the street and read it. The letter said that his brother was well, that he had found work of an unspecified nature, that Memphis was Memphis. The letter did not say anything that it meant and it meant several things it didn't say. Sylder folded it and put it in his pocket.

He rode home.

She was up when he got back, moving around the kitchen with a deliberateness that he'd come to recognize as her body asserting itself against the limits she was trying to refuse to acknowledge. He put the package on the table and took off his coat and hat.

Well? she said.

He told her.

She was quiet for a moment. She had her back to him, stirring something on the stove.

Two thirds, she said.

Yes.

And you just took it.

What else was I going to do.

She turned and looked at him. He saw the thing in her face that she didn't say and he didn't want her to say it and she knew that and looked back at the stove.

It'll be enough, he said. If the child comes all right and we get through the winter we'll be all right.

You keep saying all right like it means something.

He sat down at the table. Outside the window the sky had closed over and gone the color of old iron and there was weather coming down off the ridge to the north. He could smell it.

I went to the post office, he said. There was a letter from Calvin.

What did he want.

Just writing, near as I could tell.

She made a sound.

He's still in Memphis, Sylder said.

She turned and set a bowl in front of him and then sat down across from him with her hands flat on the table. The firelight moved on her face. She looked tired in a way that was deeper than sleep would fix.

John Wesley, she said.

He looked at her.

She was quiet for a moment. Then: I had a dream last night while you were having yours.

What did you dream.

She looked at her hands. I dreamed the baby came, she said. And it was fine. It was a boy and it was fine.

He watched her.

That's a good dream, he said.

Yes, she said. She looked up at him. But I was so frightened in it. Even though everything was fine I was so frightened I could hardly stand it and I didn't know what I was frightened of because everything was all right. The baby was there and it was fine and you were there and I was so frightened.

He reached across the table and put his hand over hers.

It was just a dream, he said.

I know it.

They sat there. The wind was coming up outside and it moved against the windows and in the chimney it found the flue and made a long low sound.

You eat, she said. She stood and went back to the stove.

He ate. After a while she sat down again and ate too and they didn't talk much but it was all right, the silence between them, it had the quality of a thing long worn smooth and shaped to use. After supper he sat by the fire and she sat across from him with her mending and they listened to the wind work itself up outside.

He thought about Gifford. He thought about Noakes sitting there by the fire like he'd been placed there, like furniture arranged to make a point. He thought about the pale-eyed young man at the door and the man at the table in the corner and how none of it had been accidental.

After a while he said: I need to go out and check the animals.

She nodded without looking up from her mending.

He put on his coat and went out into the wind and crossed the yard to the barn. Inside the animals moved in the dark and he could hear their breathing and smell the good warm smell of them. He stood in the doorway of the barn and looked back at the house where the light from the windows lay out in yellow rectangles on the frozen ground. The wind came across the yard and it had ice in it. He could feel the cold working at the edge of the light.

He thought: two thirds.

He stood there long enough that the cold got all the way into him and then he went back inside.

She had fallen asleep in the chair. He stood looking at her for a moment, the firelight on her face, the mending fallen into her lap, and the great swell of her belly under her hands. Something moved in him that had no name he knew and he stood there with it a while and then carefully, so as not to wake her, he picked up her mending and set it aside and put another piece of wood on the fire and went to bed.

He lay in the dark listening to the wind. After a while he heard her come up the stairs and undress and get into the bed beside him.

You should have woke me, she said.

You needed the sleep.

She was quiet. Outside the wind was steady and purposeful, going somewhere, just using this place to pass through.

John Wesley.

Yeah.

It'll be all right, she said.

He didn't say anything.

I mean it, she said. 

He lay there listening to the wind and to her breathing and after a while her breathing changed and he knew she was asleep. He lay awake a long time after, looking up at the dark ceiling, while the house shifted and settled around him in the cold and the wind went on about its business out in the world where all things were being carried whether they would or no toward whatever it was that waited for them at the end of their carrying.
