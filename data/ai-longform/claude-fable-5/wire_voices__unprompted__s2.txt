The board I worked was the third from the window, position fourteen, and on Sunday nights in winter the glass behind me went black by five so that the lamps of the multiple floated doubled in it, a second board of lights hanging out over Pine Street where no one could reach them. I had been with Southwestern Bell nine years by then. I knew the weight of a cord the way a seamstress knows thread, knew before I touched a key whether a line was live by some sense I could not have named, and I could tell you, though I was never asked, that no two people in this country said the word *hello* the same way. Some said it like a question they were afraid of. Some said it like a door swinging open. Mr. Hale said it like a man taking off his hat.

He called the first Sunday of November, 1967, at nine o'clock, from a coin telephone that I came to understand stood in the hallway of a rooming house somewhere on the north side, because I could hear behind his voice the noises of such a hallway — a radio upstairs, a door, once a child running and being hushed. *Long distance*, I said, the way I said it two hundred times a night, and he said, Good evening, ma'am. I'd like to place a call to Drew, Mississippi. Person to person, please, to Miss Opal Yancey. And then he gave me the number slow and careful, the way people do who have written a number down years ago on something they have folded and unfolded many times.

I wrote the ticket, stamped it, and built the call south through Memphis the way you did then, operator to operator, my voice going down the country like a stone skipped across water — Memphis, then Greenwood, then the little board at Drew where a woman with a sleepy voice rang the line — and when Miss Opal Yancey answered and said that yes, this was she, and I said, Go ahead, please, your party is on the line, Mr. Hale said, Opal, and she said, Moses, and I withdrew, because that is the discipline and the law of the work. You hear the connection made and you come out of the circuit. You are a bridge, not a room.

But it was a coin call, and on a coin call the operator is never wholly gone. At the end of the first three minutes I had to come back on and say, Signal when through, please, or, Please deposit one dollar eighty-five for the next three minutes, and he would say, Yes, ma'am, hold on, and I would hear the quarters go down — the coin telephone rang a little gong for a quarter, a double chime for a dime, a single for a nickel, so that paying for a call was a kind of carillon, and Mr. Hale paid in quarters, seven gongs and two chimes, and I would say, Thank you, and step out again. He bought time in three-minute parcels, four of them, sometimes five, never six. Twelve or fifteen minutes a week, fifty-one weeks a year if a man kept it up, and a man who fed quarters into a hallway telephone did not have money to keep it up idly. That was the first thing I knew about them, before I knew anything: that the call cost him something, and he paid it gladly, every Sunday, at nine.

I want to say plainly that I listened, because there is no way to tell this without saying it. There is a rule and I broke it, not all at once but the way you wade into cold water, an inch at a Sunday. In the seconds when I came on to ask for the deposit I heard them, and the seconds got longer, because I learned to come on a breath early and leave a breath late, and then, some nights, when position thirteen was dark and Donna was on her relief and the supervisor, Mrs. Tillman, was down at the far end with her clipboard, I would sit with my key half-thrown and the two of them in my ear like a radio program made for one listener in all the world, and the lights of my board burning patiently around me, and I would tell myself I was monitoring for the timing, which was true the way a coat of paint is true about a house.

What did they talk about. Weather, first — always weather, his and hers, traded like coins themselves. It had snowed in St. Louis; in Drew it had rained and the road past her place was mud to the ankle. Her hip, which was bad in the cold. His cough, which she did not like the sound of and said so every week, and every week he laughed it off, and his laugh turned into the cough, and she would say, You hear that? and he would say, That's just St. Louis air, that's just the foundry in me, and she would let it go because what else could she do from seven hundred miles. He worked, I gathered, or had worked, at a place that made castings; his hands were bad; he was sixty-some. She kept chickens and a garden and there was a pecan tree she spoke of the way you speak of a difficult relative. There was a June — her daughter, I decided eventually, grown and gone to Chicago — and a man named Tillis who plowed Opal's garden patch every spring for three dollars and a dinner, and whose price and appetite were both, by her account, rising.

And under all of it, under the weather and the hip and the pecans, there was a long thing, an old thing, that they touched only at the edges, the way you touch a scar through cloth. I pieced it together over months and I will not swear to all of it. He had left Sunflower County in 1936, on a train, with a cardboard suitcase — that much he said once, plainly, the suitcase, the year — and she had not gone. Whether she would not or could not, whether there was a father who forbade it or a sick mother who needed her or simply the fear that kept so many people in so many places, I never learned. He went north the way half the South went north, and he married up here, and she married down there, and his wife had died — Celeste, he said her name once, gently, the way you set down something breakable — and Opal's husband had died, Ernest Yancey, of whom she spoke without bitterness and without much else. And then, sometime in the fall of 1967, one of them had written the other a letter. I never knew which. And then the calls.

You will say I made a story of them because I had none of my own, and I will not argue very hard. I was thirty-four that winter. I lived in two rooms on Wyoming Street with a cat I had not exactly chosen and a hi-fi I had bought on time and mostly didn't play. There had been a man, Eugene, who managed a shoe department and wanted things tidier than I turned out to be, and after Eugene there had been the board, which wanted nothing from me but my hands and my voice and gave me, in exchange, the whole murmuring country. People think the work is mechanical. It is, the way playing the organ is mechanical. All night the lamps flare and you reach and connect and release, and through your headset goes everything — daughters calling mothers, men calling bookies, a woman in Kirkwood every Friday calling a number in Denver that never, in two years, answered, and she always said, Thank you anyway, like a person leaving flowers at a gate. You learn that the country at night is a single house with the lights on in every room, and you are standing in the hallway, and you can hear it breathing.

So yes. I waited for them. By February I had arranged my reliefs around them; I took my lunch at seven-thirty so that nine o'clock would find me at position fourteen with my ticket pad squared. Donna noticed — Donna noticed everything, it was her gift and her affliction — and she said, Frannie's got a fella on the line, and I said, Donna, I have got a coin call with overtime deposits, which shut her up because nobody envies a coin call. The other girls hated them, the interruptions, the arithmetic. I loved that call the way you love a window in a wall.

That winter the news came through the board before it came through anywhere else; it always did. The night of the Tet business in February the boards lit up like a pinball machine, everybody calling everybody, mothers trying to raise army posts in California, and we worked it flat-out till two in the morning, and Mrs. Tillman brought us coffee herself, which happened maybe twice a year. And that Sunday, Mr. Hale and Miss Yancey talked about June's boy — June had a boy, it turned out, nineteen, and there was a draft notice in it somewhere, or the fear of one — and Mr. Hale said something I wrote down on a scrap of ticket paper and kept in my purse until the ink wore off, which was: They have always had somewhere for our boys to go and it has never once been home. And Opal said, Moses, and nothing after it, and the line carried their silence, and I sat in the silence with them, uninvited, three people listening to seven hundred miles of wire hum.

In April, the Thursday, Memphis. I was on the board when it happened and I will not describe that night except to say that for an hour you could not have routed a call through Memphis if the President himself had picked up the phone, and that some of the calls I did connect, I heard things in the first seconds, before I withdrew, that I hear still. The Sunday after, I did not know if he would call. The city was under curfew north of Delmar; there had been fires. But at nine o'clock my lamp lit and there was the hallway radio and there was his voice, lower than I had ever heard it, and he placed the call, and I built it south, and when she came on neither of them said hello. She said, Moses, are you safe, and he said, I'm safe, are you, and she said, Nobody's safe, and then for the better part of his first three minutes — minutes he was paying for, minutes that cost him standing in a hallway in a city under curfew — they said almost nothing at all. I came on at the three-minute mark and asked for the deposit as quietly as the work can be done, and the quarters went down, seven gongs, two chimes, and he said, Thank you, ma'am, even then, even that night, and I went out of the circuit and put my hand over my mouthpiece and sat with my eyes shut.

After that the calls changed. Or the season changed and the calls with it. The garden went in; Tillis got his three dollars and ate, she reported, like a man with a tapeworm and a grudge. Mr. Hale's cough loosened with the warm weather, or he said it did. And the long thing under the conversation rose nearer the surface, because he began to talk about coming down. In the fall, he said. October, when it's cooled off. I've got money put by. There's a man here sells me a bus ticket and I am sixty-eight hours later standing in your road, Opal, mud or no mud. And she would laugh — she had a laugh like a screen door, quick and bright and a little rusty — and say, You'll stand in my road, all right, and then we'll see, and he'd say, We will see, and the way he said *see*, you understood that for him the word held thirty-one years of not seeing. One Sunday in May he said, I should never have got on that train without you, and she said, You should never have got on that train without me, and neither voice accused the other; it was a thing they had finally agreed on, like a price, and having agreed they could afford it. I sat at position fourteen with cords in both hands and tears dropping straight off my chin onto the ticket, and Donna, three positions down, mouthing at me, *Coin call?*, and me nodding, coin call, overtime deposits, terrible nuisance.

The first Sunday of June, the country was three days into mourning again — the senator shot in that hotel kitchen in Los Angeles on the Wednesday, dead Thursday, buried Saturday, the train down from New York with the people standing along the tracks all those hours, which I did not see but heard about all week through the board, person after person describing it to person after person, so that I feel to this day that I stood on an embankment in New Jersey in the heat. That Sunday at nine my lamp lit and it was Mr. Hale, and the call was short, three minutes and three more, and tired, and at the end of it he said, Opal, I'm going to move that trip up. I don't want to wait for October. Nobody is promised October. And she said, Then come on, and he said, I'll see about the ticket Tuesday, and she said, Moses Hale, and he said, Miss Opal Yancey, and they laughed, both of them, the screen door and his low rumble under it, and he signaled through, and I took down the connection and wrote the overtime on the ticket: nine-oh-one to nine-oh-seven, six minutes, and I remember thinking, six minutes, that's all they needed this week, because now there was going to be a road and a man standing in it.

The second Sunday of June he did not call.

I want to be careful here and say only what happened, which was: nothing. Nine o'clock came over the board like any minute. I had the pad squared. Nine-fifteen. The board does not pause for what doesn't happen; the lamps came up and I worked them, Tulsa, Chicago, a collect call from Fort Leonard Wood that the party in Florissant accepted before I finished asking, a woman crying to a man in Phoenix who kept saying, I know it, I know it, I know it. Nine-forty. I told myself: the bus. He's bought his ticket and he's on the bus this minute, riding down through the dark of Arkansas, and why would a man telephone a woman he is going to stand in front of by Tuesday. That was a good story and I lived in it all week the way you live in a house you know is sold.

The third Sunday at ten minutes past eight, fifty minutes before their hour, a lamp lit on my board and the inward operator at Greenwood said, St. Louis, I have a call for you, and put her through, and the voice said, Yes, ma'am, I want to place a call to St. Louis, Missouri, and I knew the voice the way you know a bell in your own town. She gave the number of the rooming house. Person to person, she said, to Mr. Moses Hale. And then she said — because some part of her had heard some part of me, I believe that, I believed it then — she said, You the one always on of a Sunday? And the discipline of the work is that you are no one, you are the company, you are a bridge and not a room, and I said, Yes, ma'am, I'm the one. One moment, please.

I rang the rooming house. It rang four times, five, and a woman answered, out of breath, the landlady, I suppose, and I said what we say: I have a long-distance call, person to person, for Mr. Moses Hale. And the woman on the St. Louis end was quiet for a moment, and in that quiet I knew, and I believe the woman in Drew knew, because a held line carries everything, it carries breath, it carries the shape of what a person is about to say. And the landlady said, Oh, honey. Mr. Hale passed. Tuesday morning. They found him — he was going out, he had his case packed, he was sitting on the bottom stair with his hat on. His heart. Is this his people?

The rules are clear and they are not cruel, they are only rules: the called party is not available, the calling party is not charged, the operator says, I'm sorry, your party is unavailable, and takes down the connection. I did not take down the connection. I came into the circuit, which I had no right to do, and I said, Miss Yancey, and she said, after a while, in a voice gone all the way down to the floor of itself, He had his case packed. And the landlady, who was kind, said, Packed and sitting by the door, and his hat on, and a bus ticket in his coat — they showed me — to Greenwood, Mississippi, and I couldn't think who he knew down there. And Opal Yancey said, He knew me. And nobody said anything, the three of us on the line, and the wire hummed its seven hundred miles, and somewhere on the route a carrier tone rose and fell like wind over a chimney, and finally Miss Yancey said, Thank you, ma'am, and I never knew, I have never known, which of us she was thanking, and the connection came down.

There was no charge to write. A call that doesn't complete is no call at all; the ticket gets a line through it and the word *unavailable*, and that is its whole record on this earth — that, and me.

She called once more, in July, on a Sunday, at nine. The lamp lit and I answered, Long distance, and there was the hallway hum of the Drew board behind her, and she said, I don't want to place a call. I want to ask you something, if they let you. And I looked down the room — Mrs. Tillman at the far end, Donna with three cords up — and I said, Ask. And Miss Opal Yancey said, You heard us. All those Sundays. I'm not saying you did wrong. I'm asking did you hear us. And I said, Yes, ma'am. I heard you. And she said, Then somebody did. That's all. He and I were a thing that happened, and somebody knows it happened. They don't write our kind down much. And I said — and they could have walked me out of the building for it, and I would have set my headset on the board and gone — I said, Miss Yancey, he said thank you every time the quarters went down. Every single time, for nine months. And he always said good evening before he said anything else, and the last Sunday, when he said he'd see about the ticket Tuesday, his voice had a thing in it I only ever heard in voices when the news is good. I have heard this whole country talk, ma'am, nine years of it, and I never heard a man gladder than your man was, the last time I heard him.

She was quiet so long I thought the line had failed. Then she said, October would have been fine, too. I'd have taken October. And then: Good night, operator, and the lamp went dark.

It is November again now. The window behind me is black by five and the lamps hang doubled in it, out over Pine Street. I work position fourteen, Sundays. At nine o'clock something in me still squares itself, the way a dog stands up at a certain hour whether anyone is coming home or not. The board lights and I reach and connect and release, daughters and mothers, men and bookies, the woman in Kirkwood still trying Denver — and last Friday, I will tell you, Denver answered, after two years it answered, and I heard her say, Oh, before I withdrew, just *Oh*, like a person whose flowers were finally taken in off the gate. The country at night is a house with the lights on in every room, and I stand in the hallway of it, and I have learned what the hallway knows: that nearly all of it is ordinary, weather and hips and the price of plowing, and that the ordinary is what people will stand at a coin box in a curfew to say to each other, seven gongs and two chimes at a time. Somebody has to hear it. Somebody has to write the ticket. *Long distance*, I say, and then I wait, in the second before the voice, for the breath — because the breath comes first, always, and it tells you everything, and it goes nowhere on the bill.
