The sale had been going since nine, but it was almost one before Ellen let herself stand still in the front room and watch a stranger carry her mother's reading lamp out the door. The lamp had a fluted brass stem and a shade the color of weak tea, and for thirty years it had stood at the left arm of the wingback chair, and the man carrying it held it upside down by the stem, the cord swinging, the way you'd carry a dead goose. He'd paid four dollars. Ellen had priced it herself, two weeks ago, sitting on the floor of this room with a roll of orange stickers and a glass of wine, and four dollars had seemed honest then, generous even, and now it seemed like a thing she had done to somebody in her sleep.

Through the window she watched him cross the lawn — the grass already pressed flat in lanes where people had been walking all morning — and set the lamp in the bed of a pickup beside a wicker hamper and somebody's plastic sled. October light, thin and yellow as broth. Across the street the Kowalskis' maple had gone over entirely to red, the way it did every year in the same week, and her mother used to call her in Columbus to tell her about it. *The tree's turned*, Dot would say, as if reporting a death or an engagement. *You should see it.*

"Ellen." Phyllis Garber was at her elbow with the cash box, which was really a tackle box, which was really Ellen's father's tackle box, which had a sticker on it too, three dollars, because everything in the house had a sticker on it, and at the end of the day she supposed somebody could buy the cash box and they'd have to dump the money into a grocery bag. "Man in the kitchen wants to know if you'll take twenty for the Hoosier cabinet."

"It's marked ninety."

"I know what it's marked. I'm just the messenger." Phyllis had lived two doors down from Dot for forty-one years and had appointed herself to this day the way she'd appointed herself to the funeral lunch, to the thank-you cards, to driving Dot to Marion for the eye appointments at the end. She was seventy-eight and wore a sweatshirt with a cardinal on it and her reading glasses on a chain, and she had been selling Dot's possessions all morning with the cheerful ruthlessness of a woman who had buried two husbands and knew exactly how much of the material world was worth keeping.

"Tell him no," Ellen said. "Tell him sixty."

"He'll say forty."

"Then I'll say fifty and we'll both feel like we won something."

Phyllis went back toward the kitchen, and Ellen stayed where she was, in the rectangle of floor where the wingback chair had been until ten-fifteen. You could see the shape of it in the carpet, a darker green inside the green, the pile crushed down by decades. The whole room was like that now — ghosts of furniture printed on the floor, pale squares on the walls where pictures had hung. The wallpaper had faded everywhere except behind the frames, so the walls were covered with bright rectangles of the pattern as it had looked in 1974, cream and gold, little medallions, and the rest of it gone dim around them. Her mother had never once mentioned wanting to repaper. Toward the end Dot didn't see the walls much anyway. Macular degeneration had taken the centers out of things; she saw the edges of the world, she said, and had to guess at the rest, which Ellen had thought at the time was as good a description of her mother's whole life as anybody would ever produce.

A woman in a Carhartt jacket was working through the box of doilies on the card table by the stairs, lifting each one, holding it up to the window light like an X-ray. The doilies were Dot's mother's, which made them Ellen's grandmother's, which made them a hundred years old or close to it, crocheted in a farmhouse outside Bucyrus by a woman Ellen knew only from two photographs and the family habit of saying *waste not* in a particular dry tone. Fifty cents apiece, the sticker said. The woman bought eleven of them and counted out exact change, and as Phyllis made the entry in the spiral notebook, the woman said, "These are beautiful work. Somebody knew what they were doing," and Ellen said thank you as though she had crocheted them herself, as though the compliment could be received and forwarded somewhere.

It went on like that. That was the thing nobody had told her about an estate sale, that it would just go on, hour after hour, neither terrible nor bearable, a procession. People came up the porch steps in twos and threes, wiped their feet or didn't, said *morning* or said nothing, and moved through the rooms of her childhood with their heads tilted, appraising, and there was no insult in it and that was almost the worst part. They weren't ghouls. They were retired couples and young marrieds and a dealer or two from Mansfield with that flat, fast-scanning look, and a man who collected fishing lures who spent forty minutes in the garage and came out with her father's tackle — not the box, the lures, the Pflueger reels — eighty dollars cash, and shook her hand and said her dad must have known the Sandusky River pretty well, judging by what he'd kept, and Ellen, who had not thought about her father standing thigh-deep in the Sandusky in probably ten years, had to go into the bathroom and run the water for a minute.

The bathroom was the one room with nothing for sale in it. She'd left the towels up so people would have something. She sat on the edge of the tub, the same tub, the porcelain worn gray in a comet-shaped streak below the faucet, and looked at the floor tiles, white hexagons with black diamonds, and thought, somebody is going to buy this whole house in November and stand in this room and decide to gut it. They would be right to. The tiles were cracked along the wall and the window was painted shut and there was a water stain on the ceiling shaped like Florida that had been there since the Carter administration. They would be right to gut it and she hoped she never drove past and saw the dumpster in the yard.

When she came out, the man in the kitchen had taken the Hoosier cabinet for fifty, just as predicted, and was unscrewing the flour bin to carry it in pieces, and Phyllis caught her eye over his bent back and gave a little nod, *told you*, and made the entry. The notebook was Phyllis's system. Item, price, time of day. *Lamp brs 4.00. Doilies x11 5.50. Hoosier cab 50.00.* At the funeral lunch in the church basement, Phyllis had pressed Ellen's hand between her two dry ones and said, *Honey, the dying is the easy part, it's the dishes that'll kill you*, and Ellen had thought it was just one of those things old women said, and now, watching the spiral notebook fill, she understood it had been practical advice.

Her phone buzzed against her hip. Roger, from Scottsdale. She stepped out the back door to take it, onto the little concrete stoop where the milk box had sat until — she checked herself — until eleven-forty this morning, when a woman bought it for seven dollars to put gardening tools in.

"How's it going?" Roger said. There was golf in his voice, or a patio, somewhere bright.

"It's going. We've made about nine hundred dollars."

"That's it?"

"That's a lot, actually. It's mostly a one-dollar-at-a-time kind of operation."

"What about the piano?"

"Nobody wants the piano, Rog. Nobody in America wants a piano. I've had three people offer to take it if we pay them."

A pause she could read like a road sign. "Mom paid two thousand dollars for that piano."

"In 1968."

"I'm just saying."

"You could have flown out," Ellen said, and heard her own voice, and made it stop. They'd had this fight, or rather they'd had the long-distance bloodless version of it, in March when Dot died and again in June over the realtor and she did not want to have it on the back stoop with strangers carrying out the dining chairs twenty feet away. Roger was not a bad man. Roger had paid for things without being asked, the roof in 2019, half the assisted-living deposit they'd never ended up needing because Dot had refused, flatly, serenely, to go. Roger sent money the way some people send casseroles, and it was a real thing he sent, it was not nothing, and he had not seen the inside of this house in six years and was not going to see it again, and both of those facts lived in Ellen's chest in adjoining rooms.

"You're right," he said, surprising her. "I should have. Listen — is the clock still there? Dad's mantel clock?"

"It's in my car. I told you. It's been in my car since Wednesday."

"Okay. Good. That's the only — okay. Good." He cleared his throat in the Arizona sunshine. "Don't let anybody steamroll you on prices. People at these things are vultures."

"They're mostly just people, Rog."

"Same thing," he said, but mildly, and they hung up the way they always did, with the unsaid things folded neatly and put away like linens.

The afternoon found its rhythm. Two o'clock brought the after-church-and-lunch crowd, women in good slacks moving through the rooms in pairs, narrating to each other. *Oh, my mother had this exact pattern. Currier and Ives, the blue. We ate off it every Sunday.* The dishes went in lots — Phyllis had bundled them with rubber bands and priced the bundles — and Ellen stood by the front door taking money and making change out of the tackle box, and learned that you could chart the whole century off what people picked up. The young ones wanted the heavy stuff, the oak and the cast iron and the Pyrex in colors; they wanted weight, things with evidence in them. The old ones touched everything and bought almost nothing, because they were here for the touching. One woman, eighty if she was a day, stood at the kitchen counter holding Dot's sifter, just holding it, working the crank slowly so the blades whispered inside the tin, and her eyes were somewhere else entirely, and Ellen did not interrupt her, and the woman set it down at last and left without buying anything and that was fine. That was the other commerce of the day and Ellen had stopped resenting it around noon.

Around two-thirty a girl came in — fifteen, sixteen, alone, in an army jacket that had been somebody's father's — and went straight up the stairs as if she knew the house, which spooked Ellen for a second until she remembered the sign at the curb said WHOLE HOUSE, UPSTAIRS TOO. She followed anyway, on the pretext of straightening the bedrooms, and found the girl in Ellen's old room, which had been the sewing room for thirty years, standing in front of the shelf of Reader's Digest Condensed Books like someone at a museum.

"Are these a dollar each or a dollar for all of them?" the girl asked. "The sticker's on the shelf, I can't tell."

"Which were you hoping?"

"All of them for a dollar," the girl said, without shame, and Ellen laughed for the first time that day.

"What do you want them for? They're abridged. They cut all the good parts out."

"I make things out of them. Like, art things. The covers." The girl pulled one out — the spines were that fake leather, marbled cream and rust and avocado, the colors of 1971 — and showed her the endpapers, the looping pattern inside. "People throw these away. There's like a million of them in the world and everybody's grandma had them and nobody wants them, which is the whole — that's kind of the point of the thing I make. I don't know." She shrugged the shrug of a sixteen-year-old who has said too much about her art to an adult.

"Dollar for all of them," Ellen said. "But you have to carry them yourself."

It took the girl four trips down the stairs, the books stacked to her chin, and on the last trip Ellen stopped her in the hall and gave her the sewing tin too, the big Danish butter cookie tin full of buttons that every grandmother in the western hemisphere had owned, because the girl had looked at it twice, and because there are certain transmissions that can only happen at estate sales, the dead handing things to the young through a stranger in the middle.

"For free?" the girl said.

"It's buttons," Ellen said. "It's a hundred years of cutting the buttons off shirts before you rag them. Waste not." The girl looked at her like she'd spoken Latin, and took the tin, and said thanks, and her car needed a muffler, you could hear it the whole way down Sycamore Street.

After that the bedroom. She'd been putting off the bedroom all day, leaving Phyllis to handle it, but Phyllis was working the cash box now and there was a question about the cedar chest, so Ellen went in. Two women were at the open closet. Dot's clothes hung there still — Ellen had taken the good wool coat and the funeral dress and a cardigan she couldn't explain wanting, and the rest she'd priced to move, fifty cents, a dollar, because Goodwill was the alternative and at least this way the clothes stayed in the county. One of the women, fortyish, ruddy, had Dot's church coat on, the dusty-rose one with the big buttons, and was looking at herself in the mirror on the closet door, turning to see the side of it, and for one second — the mirror, the rose wool, the angle — Ellen's whole body misfired. It wasn't even that the woman looked like Dot. It was the coat performing its old function, holding a shape, being on a person in this room, in this mirror.

The woman caught Ellen's eye in the glass and read something in her face — they always could, all day people had been reading her face and then becoming gentle, it was a small town and half of them knew exactly whose daughter she was — and the woman said, "Was this your mom's?"

"It was."

"It's a beautiful coat. They don't make wool like this now." She smoothed the front of it. "I don't want to — I mean, if it's hard to see somebody —"

"She'd be tickled," Ellen said, and was surprised to find it true as she said it. "She bought that coat at Uhlman's downtown, before it closed. She'd be tickled to death it's getting worn instead of eaten by moths in my closet in Columbus." The woman bought the coat for a dollar and wore it out the front door rather than carry it, and through the window Ellen watched the dusty rose move down the front walk and turn left at the sidewalk, the direction of the church, as it happened, and she let herself have ten seconds of watching it and then went to deal with the cedar chest.

The cedar chest she would not sell and had to keep telling people so. The sticker on it said NFS in Phyllis's block letters, and still every twenty minutes somebody lifted the lid — empty now, she'd cleared it Wednesday, the quilts and the christening gown and the manila envelope of report cards and the lock of somebody's baby hair in waxed paper, all of it boxed in her trunk next to the mantel clock — and somebody would call across the room, *How much for the chest?*, and she would say not for sale, and they would say, *Everything's for sale, honey,* in a joking way, and twice she'd had to say it a second time with her voice flattened out before they believed her.

A man offered her two hundred dollars for it at three o'clock. He was a dealer; she'd watched him work the house in eleven minutes flat, and the chest was the only thing he'd come back to. Two hundred was more than everything else in the house would bring combined, probably, and he counted the bills out of a money clip onto the closed lid like a man laying down a winning hand, and Ellen looked at the money lying on the cedar — her mother's hope chest, 1949, hauled from the farm to this house in the bed of a borrowed truck, the thing in which Dot had carried her whole dowry of futures, the embroidered pillowcases for a marriage that hadn't happened yet, the tablecloth for a table she didn't own — and Ellen said, "It's not for sale," and the man said, "Two-fifty," and she said, "There's no number," and watched him try to compute that, a thing without a number, and fail, and clip his money back up, and leave. Phyllis, who had seen the whole exchange from the doorway, said nothing, but later, passing her in the hall, squeezed Ellen's arm above the elbow, once, hard, the way you'd squeeze the arm of someone who had passed a test.

By four the light was changing, going amber and long, and the crowd thinned to stragglers. A man bought the lawnmower and pushed it home, just pushed it down the sidewalk, four blocks, he said, no sense loading it. A young couple — very young, the girl pregnant, the boy in a Cedar Point hoodie — circled the kitchen table three times. It was Formica, gray with the boomerang pattern, chrome legs, the table at which Ellen had eaten approximately six thousand suppers and done her homework and been told her father was dead, in 1981, on a Tuesday, her mother standing at the stove with her back to the room because she could not say it facing front. Forty dollars, the sticker said. The boy had thirty-two. Ellen watched him count it twice, his ears going red, and the girl put her hand flat on the table the way pregnant women put hands flat on things, testing the steadiness of the world, and said, "It just needs to not wobble. Everything at the apartment wobbles."

"It doesn't wobble," Ellen said. "It hasn't wobbled since Eisenhower. Thirty-two is fine."

They carried it out together, the boy walking backward down the porch steps, the girl calling *left, left, your other left*, both of them laughing, and the chrome legs flashed once in the low sun as they turned it sideways through the door, and Ellen stood in the kitchen where the table had been, in the bare place, and looked at the wall where the years of chair-backs had worn two pale scuffs into the paint, and thought that of everything that had left the house today this was the only departure that felt like a launch instead of a loss. That table was going to a wobbling apartment to be the steady thing. Somebody would do homework on it. Somebody, God help them, would someday be told something at it with a mother's back turned, because that was what kitchen tables were for, they were the anvils on which families were hammered, and it was better — she felt this suddenly and completely — it was so much better than the table going to a vintage shop in Columbus to be sold to someone ironic.

At five Phyllis counted the box. One thousand two hundred and six dollars, and change. They sat on the porch steps to do it, because there were no chairs left inside to sit on, and the cold came up through the concrete the way it always had, and across the street the Kowalski maple lit up like a struck match in the last of the sun. The leavings were small now: the piano, three boxes of mismatched everything marked FREE at the curb, a dehumidifier, the bed in the back room that the Goodwill truck would take Tuesday. Inside, the house had gone strange. Empty rooms are loud; every footstep had a flat echo in it now, and the furnace kicking on sounded like weather.

"You did good," Phyllis said, snapping the rubber band around the bills. "Dot would've haggled harder, but you did good."

"She would have stood at the door telling everybody what they were doing wrong with their lives."

"She would have sold the doilies for two dollars and made them feel lucky." Phyllis looked at her sideways. "She talked about you all the time, you know. Toward the end. Couldn't see the television anymore, so we just talked. Mostly about you."

"Complaining?"

"Bragging," Phyllis said. "Same thing, with her." She got up by stages, knees first, and patted Ellen's shoulder, and said she had a casserole in the freezer with Ellen's name on it, literally, on masking tape, for the drive home tomorrow, and went off down the sidewalk into the blue evening, two doors, a porch light coming on ahead of her like it knew.

Ellen sat a while longer. A car slowed at the curb, idled at the FREE boxes; a door opened and a kid jumped out and took the box with the Christmas decorations in it, the plastic Santa with the light inside, and the car pulled away, and that was fine too. The streetlight at the corner buzzed on. Somewhere behind her, in the dark of the empty living room, the piano stood against the wall with its sticker still on it, FREE TO GOOD HOME, and tomorrow she would call the church and the school and somebody would want it or nobody would, and either way it had held its one note of furniture-silence all day like the last guest who doesn't know the party's over.

She went in to lock up. Her footsteps went ahead of her through the rooms, loud, and the bright rectangles on the walls were going gray now with the rest of the wallpaper, all of it evening-colored, evened out at last. In the kitchen she turned off the light and then, in the dark, by old instinct, put her hand out and touched the wall where the table had been, the worn place, the smooth scuff at exactly the height of a chair, and stood there a minute with her palm against it, the way the old woman had held the sifter, the way the pregnant girl had pressed the tabletop, testing the steadiness of the world. Then she let herself out the front door and locked it, and the key turned with the same stiff click it had made her whole life, and she put the key in her pocket, where it weighed nothing at all, and walked down the steps to the car where the clock and the quilts and the cedar chest were waiting, and the maple across the street was just a darker dark now, but she knew it was red, she didn't have to see it, she knew.
