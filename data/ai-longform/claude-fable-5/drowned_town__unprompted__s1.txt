The water had been going for two years before anyone said the word drought out loud, and by the time Ruth Colvard drove up the access road with the county truck and her father's old tripod rattling in the bed, the reservoir had drawn down past every mark anyone living could remember. The hills wore a pale band where the lake used to be, sixty feet of bleached stone and gray silt the color of old newsprint, and below that band, in the broad flat where the Toller River had once made its slow turn west, the town of Harmon was coming back into the light.

She had seen the photographs already. Everyone had. A man with a drone had put them on the internet in March—the grid of foundations rising out of the mudflats like the floor plan of something God had abandoned mid-thought—and for a week the boat ramp parking lot had filled with people from three counties who wanted to walk out and stand in the streets of a drowned town. Then the water authority closed the gate, citing liability, citing the mud, citing the simple decency of not letting strangers carry off doorknobs in their pockets, and now the only people allowed below the old shoreline were authority staff and, as of this morning, the county surveyor, who had a work order in her clipboard and a reason no one at the courthouse knew about folded up underneath it.

Dale Pruitt met her at the gate. He was maybe thirty-five, with the water authority's logo on his cap and an air of apology that seemed permanent, as if he personally had misplaced the lake.

"Miz Colvard," he said. "They told me you'd be coming. Said you're mapping it for the historical record."

"For the record," she said. "Foundations, street alignments, anything structural. The county never had good plats of the town. Everything went to the state when they condemned it, and half of that burned in fifty-nine."

"Mud's bad past the old depot," Dale said. "It'll look dry and it isn't. Fella from the paper went in to his hips last month. We had to pull him out with a strap."

"I'll stay on the streets," she said. "The streets were graveled. They'll bear weight."

He looked at her a moment, and she could see him doing the arithmetic—her age, the date on the dam, the way she'd said the streets were graveled like a fact she owned rather than one she'd read. But he was too polite or too incurious to finish the sum, and he swung the gate and let her through.

She parked above the waterline, what was left of it, and stood for a while at the tailgate getting her gear together, which was also a way of getting herself together. Sixty-eight years old, eight months from a retirement she had filed for and then quietly withdrawn when the lake began to fall. The GPS rover and its pole. The field book, a new one, its pages stiff. And the tripod, which had no business on this job at all—the work was satellite work, she didn't need a transit, she had not needed a transit in twenty years—but it was her father's, the wooden legs worn smooth at the clamps from his hands, and she had taken it down from the garage wall this morning the way other people take flowers to a grave.

The descent was longer than she expected. That was the first strange thing, the sheer vertical distance of it, walking down and down through the band of dead ground, past the successive shorelines the falling lake had printed on the hillside like the growth rings of a tree run backward. Driftwood lay in windrows at each old water line, silvered and light as bone. The silt underfoot was cracked into plates that tilted and chimed faintly when she stepped on them. Halfway down she passed a fence post, just one, standing upright with two strands of wire still stapled to it, the wire running six feet and stopping in the air, and she stood looking at it longer than a fence post deserved, because she knew whose it was. Hartsell's upper pasture. She had walked that fence line as a girl, carrying a syrup bucket of blackberries, the wire humming when the wind came down the valley. The post was furred all over with dried algae, a soft gray-green pelt, and when she touched it the pelt crumbled like ash.

Below the pasture the grade gentled and the foundations began.

She had told herself, driving up, that it would look like ruins, and that she had seen ruins, and that a foundation is only geometry, and geometry had been her whole working life and held no terror for her. What she had not accounted for was that she would know the geometry. That was the thing that stopped her at the first one—a rectangle of fieldstone, silt-filled, a set of three concrete steps rising at its eastern edge to a door that was not there—and she stood with the rover pole planted like a pilgrim's staff and her heart going hard, because the steps faced east and the chimney fall lay at the north end and that meant this was the Andresses' place, where she had taken piano lessons in a parlor that smelled of camphor and where Mrs. Andress had kept a glass dish of butterscotch she never offered. Forty-one years that woman's parlor had been at the bottom of a lake, and Ruth could have walked to the piano blindfolded.

She got out the field book and made herself work.

The work steadied her, as it always had. She shot the corners of the Andress foundation, logged them, sketched the footprint with the steps and the chimney noted, and wrote in the remarks column, in her small draftsman's hand, residence, stone fndn, identity known: Andress, then moved on down what the mud could not disguise was Cherry Street, because the streets were there. That was the second strange thing. The houses had been razed or had rotted and collapsed and been swallowed, but the streets had kept their shape under all that water, compacted gravel armored with sixty years of fine sediment, and they ran exactly where they had always run, and her feet knew them. She had measured many things in her career—section corners lost since the 1880s, fence disputes, the centerlines of highways—and she had never measured anything that measured her back.

She had been seven the last summer. She would tell people eight when they asked, because eight sounded like an age from which a person could legitimately remember things, but she had been seven, and the things she remembered came not as a film but as a box of unsorted slides: the way the federal men's sedans looked parked in front of the post office, dark and identical; her mother standing at the clothesline with a wet sheet half-pinned, looking at the river bottom and not moving for so long that Ruth got frightened; the church bell coming down out of the steeple on a block and tackle, swinging slow against the white clapboard, and Reverend Ott standing below it with his hat in both hands like a man at a funeral, which she understood now was exactly what he had been. They had taken the bell to the new consolidated church in Tolerton, where it still hung, and where it had always sounded wrong to her, a captive sound.

And she remembered her father with his transit—this transit, these wooden legs—set up at the corner of Main and Depot Street on a January morning, running the line of takings for the state, because Emmett Colvard had been the only licensed surveyor within forty miles and when the project came he had been hired, and he had taken the work. He had staked the high-water line through his neighbors' farms. He had set the brass monuments that said, in figures, here and no farther, everything below this elevation belongs to the future, and the future is water. Some men stopped speaking to him. Old Hartsell spat once at his feet, in front of the feed store, and her father had looked down at the spit on his boot and said nothing, and walked on, and Ruth, holding his hand, had felt through his hand the tremor he did not let into his face.

Somebody has to hold the chain, he told her mother that night, through the wall, in the low voice that meant the children were supposed to be asleep. If I don't do it true, they'll send somebody who'll do it fast. And her mother had said something Ruth couldn't hear, and then there had been a long quiet, the kind that fills a small house entirely.

He had done it true. That was the thing she carried about her father, the heavy true thing. He had measured his own town's death certificate to the hundredth of a foot.

Cherry Street brought her to Main, and Main lay open before her in the gray morning light, broad and straight, running northwest toward where the depot had been and southeast toward the church, and along both sides the foundations stood up out of the silt in their old order: Pember's grocery with its long footprint and the iron pipe columns still standing where the awning had been, snapped off at shoulder height and bearded with dried zebra mussels; the bank, which had been brick and was now a knee-high rampart of brick rubble with its vault, absurdly, intact—a squat concrete room standing alone and doorless in the flat, like a thing built by an earlier civilization for an unguessable rite. She shot it all. She walked the centerline of Main Street shooting points every fifty feet and the line came out straight on her screen, dead straight, and she thought, he laid this out, or his predecessor did, some man with a transit and a chain in 1888, and the lake has lain on it for sixty years like a thumb trying to smudge it, and it would not smudge.

A great blue heron stood in the intersection of Main and Church Street, motionless, regarding her. Pools of trapped lakewater lay here and there in the cellars, and the heron had been fishing them, picking off the stranded shad. When she came near it unfolded itself with the reluctance of a property owner and flew low down Main Street, between the foundations, beneath where the second stories had been, and something about the flight—a heron flying down the middle of Main Street at the altitude of a man on horseback—undid her a little, and she stood and let it.

The church steps were whole. Seven of them, limestone, rising to the absent doors. The building itself had gone wherever buildings go; the foundation behind the steps was filled with silt and one season's brave volunteer weeds, cockleburs and pigweed gone brittle, the first plants to grow in Harmon since Kennedy was president. But the steps stood, and at the base of them, set into the lowest one at the south corner, exactly where she knew to look because she had watched him set it, was the brass disk.

She knelt and brushed the silt away with her glove. The brass had gone dark and lakish, green-black, but the stamping was legible. STATE WATER COMM. B.M. 12. 1961. And below the official stamping, small, unauthorized, struck with letter punches by a man who must have done it alone, after hours, because it would never have been permitted: E.C.

She sat down on the church steps next to her father's initials and did not work for a while.

Benchmark twelve. Elevation 847.31 feet, she still knew, above sea level, datum of 1929. The full pool of the reservoir was 905. Everything in Harmon lay between fifty and seventy feet down when the lake stood where the engineers intended, and the lake this morning stood at 851 and falling, which meant the church steps had been in open air since sometime in February, taking rain and sun for the first time in her adult life, and the benchmark her father set was once again doing the only thing a benchmark knows how to do, which is to hold still and mean something.

She set up the tripod over it. There was no professional reason. The rover told her her position to a centimeter without ceremony, and the transit's optics were probably fogged with age. But she spread the wooden legs and pressed the points into the gravel of Church Street and leveled the head by eye, and the motions came back to her hands from a great distance, from summer evenings when she was ten and eleven and they were in the new house in Tolerton and her father, who had grown quiet by then in a way that never afterward lifted, would let her set up the instrument in the backyard and sight the water tower and the school cupola and the standpipe on Greer's Knob, and turn the angles, and he would check her figures at the kitchen table without a word, and once—once—he had looked at her closed-out traverse, the error of closure four seconds of arc, and said, well, and that was all, and it had been enough to set the course of her entire life.

He had never come back. That was the part she had turned over for fifty years without wearing it smooth. When the gates closed in sixty-two and the pool began to fill, men drove up on Sundays for two whole summers to watch it rise—it rose slowly, a few feet a month, a patient drowning—and they stood on the overlooks pointing out their own rooflines to their children until the rooflines went under. Her mother went twice. Her father never went once. He maintained the benchmarks above the waterline for the state until 1970, and he must have stood a hundred times within sight of the lake, and Ruth did not believe he had ever once looked at it. He looked at his level rod, his angles, his figures. He died in 1981 with the transit in the garage and a drawer full of field books written in a hand so disciplined it looked typeset, and in none of those books, she had checked, in not one remark column across thirty years, did the word Harmon appear after the year the water came.

The cemetery was the next thing, and she had been circling it in her mind all morning the way the heron circled the cellar pools.

The state had moved the graves. That was the official record: 314 burials disinterred from the Harmon cemetery and re-established at Memorial Gardens in Tolerton, each with a flat granite marker, paid for by the project, in rows of a machine regularity the old hillside cemetery had never had. Ruth's people were there—grandparents, a great-aunt, the rows of Colvards and Renners. But the record, she knew from forty years inside records, was a description of intent, and the relocation had been done in eleven weeks by a contractor from out of state, in winter, in ground that had been receiving its dead since before the county kept books. Her mother had said it once, only once, near the end of her own life, in the unguarded hour after supper: They never found all of them. Your father knew. He had the old plat in his head and he counted, and they came up short, and the state man told him the survey would reflect three hundred fourteen, and your father wrote three hundred fourteen.

The cemetery had been on the rise east of Church Street, which meant it had been the last part of Harmon to go under and was the first to come back. She walked up out of the grid of streets onto ground that lifted maybe twelve feet—nothing, a swell, but in this drowned flatness it read as a hill—and the silt thinned as she climbed until she was walking on the original clay, and in the clay, in the slanting light, she could see them. Depressions. Rectangular, subsided, in rows, oriented east to west as Christian burial had always been oriented, so that the dead could rise facing the dawn. Most of them were the ghosts of graves, the prints of coffins long since lifted out and carried to Tolerton, the disturbed ground settling differently for sixty years even under water.

But near the eastern edge, where the old fence had been, in the section her memory and the burned plat both called the old part, she found four depressions that were not empty prints. The difference was legible to her the way a misclosure is legible: the subsidence was wrong, centered, gradual, the settling of ground over something rather than into the absence of something. And lying beside the second one, heaved up by frost or freed by the retreating water, was a footstone. Small, limestone, the kind they used for children, scoured nearly blank. She knelt and tilted it to the light and ran her thumb along it and raised two letters out of the stone the way her father had raised his initials out of the brass: I. R.

Infant Renner, she thought. Or Isaac, or Ida. Her mother had been a Renner. There had been a sister of her grandmother's, dead at two of diphtheria, in the winter of 1899, and Ruth had heard her named at exactly one Thanksgiving in her life, in the way families name such children, briefly, with a small silence on either side of the name, and the name had been—she stood very still and went down into herself after it—the name had been Ila.

Ila Renner. Aged two years. Left behind by the count, left under the lake, sleeping facing east these sixty years toward a dawn that came down through fifty feet of green water, if it came at all.

Ruth took off her glove. The clay was cold. She laid her bare hand flat on the ground above the small wrong-settled depression and kept it there while the wind came across the flats smelling of mud and dead shells, and she said nothing, because there was nothing that was hers to say, and then she did the only thing she had ever known how to do for anyone. She shot the point. She shot all four points, the four unemptied graves, to the centimeter, and entered them in the field book with their depressions sketched and the footstone described and the letters recorded, and in the remarks column she wrote: burials, apparently not relocated, recommend notification of state historic preservation officer prior to refill, and then, below that, in smaller letters, because the record was a description of intent and her intent should be plain to whoever read it after her: descendant of record available. R. Colvard, Co. Surveyor.

Three hundred fourteen, her father had written, knowing. She wondered, sitting back on her heels on the cold hill, whether this was why she had really come—not the house, not the streets, but this, the figure he had been made to falsify, the one error in thirty years of field books, carried like a stone in the chest pocket all the way to the grave. If I don't do it true, they'll send somebody who'll do it fast. He had done it true everywhere they let him. She closed the field book and thought: there, then. Closed out. Error of closure, sixty years.

The light was going long when she finally walked to the house.

She had saved it the way you save the thing you are afraid of, and she came at it down Walnut Street from the north, past the foundations she could name without trying—Beckers, Otts, the Frenches with their odd double cellar—and then there it was, third from the corner, the low rectangle of poured concrete her grandfather had been proud of because everyone else on the street had fieldstone, and the front steps, two of them, and set into the top step, where a seven-year-old had been allowed to press them into the wet concrete in the spring of 1959 while her father held her wrists to keep the spacing even, a line of marbles. Glass marbles, set like rivets. The lake had taken the shine off them. Five remained of what she remembered as nine, clouded little planets in the gray step, and one of them, the big shooter with the blue spiral, was still there at the end of the row, and she stood above it in the rubber boots of an old county official and was seven, absolutely and without transition seven, smelling concrete and April and her father's pipe.

The house itself was gone to its bones. In the silt of what had been the kitchen she found the iron foot of the stove. In the back, where the garden had been, where her mother had stood with the wet sheet half-pinned, there was nothing at all, only the flat gray flood-laid mud, level as a table, which was its own kind of accuracy.

She set the rover pole on the top step, beside the marbles, and let it average its position for a long time, far longer than needed, the instrument counting satellites in the cold sky while the sun went down behind the western rim where the water authority's gate was, and Dale Pruitt was no doubt checking his watch. The figures settled. She wrote them in the book: the threshold of the house she was born in, fixed now in the same datum as the highways and the section corners and the dam itself, a coordinate that would outlast the page, and in the remarks column she wrote residence, conc. fndn, and then sat on the step a minute with the pencil still in her hand and added: Colvard. And then, because the book was the public record and the public record had been short with her family long enough: born here, 1955.

They said the rains would come back. They always did; the lake had been low before, though never this low, and the engineers gave it three years, maybe five, before the pool stood at 905 again and Main Street went back under and the heron lost its fishing grounds and the streets resumed their long patient work of holding their shape in the dark. She found she did not mind it the way she had expected to mind it. The town had been measured now. It was in the book.

She broke down the tripod last, folding the wooden legs and strapping them, and shouldered it, and the weight of it on her shoulder was her father's weight, his Saturday weight, going out to hold the chain. The first stars were coming on above the rim. She walked up Main Street toward the hill in the blue dusk, up out of Harmon the long way, past the bank vault and the awning posts and the church steps with their brass disk holding still in the dark, holding its elevation, meaning what it meant, and she did not look back, not because she couldn't, but because she didn't need to anymore. She had the figures. They closed.
