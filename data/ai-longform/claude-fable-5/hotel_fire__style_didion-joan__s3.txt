The Mirasol burned on the night of October 3, 1972, between the hours of one and four in the morning, and eleven people died there, and twenty-six years later I drove up from Ventura to watch Cissy Lockhart get married on its south lawn. I want to be exact about this because exactness is the only thing I have ever been able to bring to the subject. The fire marshal's report ran to forty-one pages and fixed the point of origin in a linen storage closet on the third floor and fixed the cause as a carelessly discarded cigarette, and I have a copy of that report, I have had it since the spring of 1973, when I wrote to the county and paid two dollars and fifty cents for it, and I could recite certain of its sentences to you now, in the dark, without error. *The fire load in the closet of origin consisted primarily of terry toweling and cotton bed linen.* *Smoldering combustion may persist in such materials for a period of hours before transition to open flame.* For a period of hours. I was nineteen years old in October of 1972 and I cleaned rooms at the Mirasol, fourteen rooms a day at a dollar eighty-five an hour, and the third floor was my floor, and the linen closet on the third floor was where the girls went to smoke.

I tell you this at the outset so that you will understand the spirit in which I accepted the invitation. The invitation arrived in February, engraved on stock so heavy that the envelope had required extra postage, and it requested the pleasure of my company at the marriage of Cecilia Anne Lockhart to a man named Witt Garland on the twentieth of June at the Hotel Mirasol, Punta Blanca, California. I stood at my kitchen counter with the envelope in my hand for some time. Outside, my neighbor was running a leaf blower. I remember the leaf blower because the sound of it seemed for a moment to be coming from very far away, the way sound comes to you underwater, or in smoke.

Diana Lockhart called me that same evening, which meant she had timed the call to the mail, which was like her. She said Cissy had her heart set on it. She said the new owners had done a beautiful job. She said, and I am quoting her, that Cissy felt the place was part of her story, and that having the wedding there would close the circle. She used the word closure. Later, at the rehearsal dinner, Cissy herself would use the word healing, and the word journey, and I would sit with my glass of wine and listen to a twenty-five-year-old describe as her journey a night that occurred seven weeks before she was born, and I would think: this is what the living do. They take the fire and they make it a story, and they make the story about themselves, and who am I to say they are wrong. I no longer believe that getting the story right keeps anyone out of the fire. I only believe in the forty-one pages, and in the smell, which the report does not mention, and which I will not describe except to say that for some years afterward I could not walk past a dry cleaner's.

In June I drove up the 101 in the late morning, past Gaviota, past the place where the highway lets go of the ocean and takes it up again, and I took the Punta Blanca exit and went west two miles through the eucalyptus, and the eucalyptus was the first thing. Eucalyptus oils itself; in heat it gives off a smell like cough drops and creosote, and the trees on the Mirasol road were the same trees, or the children of the same trees, and the smell came through the vents of the car before I ever saw the gates. The gates were new. The sign was new, MIRASOL in bronze letters of a type someone in a design office had decided said 1928, said Old California, said nothing whatever about 1972. A boy in a polo shirt took my keys and called me ma'am and I stood on the gravel and looked up at the building.

They had rebuilt it three stories, as it had been, with the same long Mediterranean roofline and the same arcaded loggia along the front, and they had done it well; it took me a moment to find the seams. The west wing was eleven feet longer than it had been, to accommodate, I learned later, an interior fire stair. The windows on the third floor were operable casements where the originals had been fixed above the transom line, a detail that figured in the litigation and that I noticed the way you notice a scar on a face you used to know. In the lobby the terra-cotta tile had been replaced with limestone and the front desk had been moved from the east wall to the west, and set into the lobby ceiling at intervals of roughly twelve feet were sprinkler heads, recessed, fitted with little white escutcheons so that they would not trouble the eye. I counted them. There were fourteen in the lobby. There had been none, anywhere, in 1972. There had been a standpipe system that the report described as dry and inoperable, and there had been fire doors at the ends of the corridors that had been chained because guests were using them to bring in girls, and there had been an alarm, of a sort, a manual pull station behind the front desk, and the night man, Eddie Salazar, had pulled it, and it had rung in the lobby and nowhere else, and Eddie Salazar had then gone up the main stair with the cash drawer keys still on his belt and had not come down.

I had not planned to think about Eddie Salazar in the lobby. I had planned, insofar as I had planned anything, to think about him later, privately, in my room, the way you plan to take a pill at a certain hour. I gave my name at the desk. The girl found my reservation and said, Welcome back to the Mirasol, which was the script, she said it to everyone, and I signed the card and took the key, which was not a key but a plastic card, and I rode the elevator to the second floor and lay down on the bed in room 217 with my shoes on and looked at the sprinkler head above me until it was time to dress.

Here is what I was doing at the Mirasol in 1972. I was making beds. I had come down from Salinas the summer after high school because a cousin knew the head housekeeper, Mrs. Echeverría, and I had stayed because the annex room was free and the tips in season were good and because I had a vague idea, common to nineteen-year-olds on that coast in those years, that something was about to happen to me. I pushed a cart. The cart held forty sets of sheets and the passkey rang against my hip on its long chain and I knocked twice and said housekeeping and waited and knocked again. I knew things about the guests that the guests did not know I knew. I knew who drank and who cried and who slept apart, I knew that 308 wanted extra towels every day and used none of them, I knew that the man in 224 kept a photograph of a woman in his shaving kit and that the woman was not the woman he was registered with. None of this seemed like knowledge at the time. It seemed like laundry. And in the afternoons, when the floor was done, we went into the linen closet at the end of the third-floor corridor, between 312 and the service stair, Teresa Ávalos and Alma and I, and we sat on the folded blankets and smoked and talked about the guests, and we put the butts out in a coffee can that Teresa kept on the shelf behind the pool towels, and the can was usually there and sometimes it was not, and when it was not we were careful, or we believed we were careful, and I have spent twenty-six years inside the distance between those two phrases.

I smoked in that closet on the afternoon of October 3. I have never told anyone this, and I am not, strictly speaking, telling you now; I am telling you that the report says a carelessly discarded cigarette, smoldering combustion, a period of hours, and that the afternoon ended at four-thirty and the fire was discovered at ten past one, and that eight and a half hours is at the outer edge of what cotton batting will do but it is not past the edge. Twice in my life I have found myself seated at dinner beside men who investigated fires for a living, once in Sacramento and once at a wedding in La Jolla, and both times I turned the conversation, idly, the way you idle a car, to the question of how long a cigarette can lie in toweling before it opens into flame, and both times the man said it depends, and both times I asked depends on what, and the answers did not release me. Teresa smoked in that closet too, that afternoon. Alma did not smoke. There were also two painters working on three that week, and the report notes their solvents and their rags and clears them, more or less, on the strength of where the char was deepest. The char was deepest under the towel shelf. I have read the diagram so many times that I could draw it freehand: the door, the mop sink, the shelving, the little x where it began.

Teresa Ávalos died in the fire. She was twenty-three. She had taken the turndown shift that week because the regular girl was in Fresno for a funeral, and at one in the morning she was somewhere on three with her basket of chocolates and her stack of cards that said GOOD NIGHT FROM THE MIRASOL, and the corridor on three went, the report says, from tenable to untenable in under four minutes once the closet door failed. The others were guests. A retired pharmacist and his wife from Fresno, in 310. A honeymoon couple from Bakersfield. A salesman of irrigation equipment whose company sent flowers to the hotel, to the hotel, for years afterward, as though the building were the widow. A woman in 318, Mrs. Reillyn, seventy-nine, who had come to the Mirasol every October since 1951 and who was found by the window with the window unopenable above the transom line. Eddie Salazar on the main stair. Eleven. I could go on. I will not go on, except to say that I knew the number eleven before I knew the names, because the radio said eleven while we were still standing on the lawn in the dark, all of us counting and counting again, and the number has never since been a number to me. When Cissy Lockhart told me at the rehearsal dinner that eleven was her lucky number, that she and Witt had met on the eleventh of November, I said, Isn't that something, and drank my wine.

What I myself did on the night of October 3 takes less time to tell than the versions of it I have heard told. I woke in the annex to shouting, not to any alarm. I pulled on jeans under my nightgown and ran across the service yard and in through the kitchen because that was the door I had a key to, and I went up the service stair because that was the stair I used, the way a horse runs into a burning barn because the barn is what it knows. On two the smoke was at the ceiling, lying there like a second ceiling, gray and moving. I had my passkey. I went down the corridor banging on doors with the heel of my fist and opening the ones that did not answer, and most of the rooms were empty already or emptying, and at the elevator alcove I found a woman standing quite still in a white nightgown, barefoot, with her hands crossed over her stomach, watching the brass arrow above the elevator door as though the arrow were going to move. The elevator was dead. The woman was Diana Lockhart and she was seven months pregnant and her husband, Howard, was downstairs in the bar, or had been; the bar closed at midnight and Howard Lockhart's whereabouts between midnight and the lawn have never been firmly established and are no longer anyone's business. I took Diana Lockhart by the wrist. I did not carry her. She outweighed me by thirty pounds before you counted Cissy. I took her by the wrist and said the stairs, the stairs, and we went down the service stair through smoke that was by then at our shoulders, and at the kitchen door she stopped and turned around as though she had forgotten something, and I put my hand flat on her back and pushed, and we were out, and that was all of it. Ninety seconds, perhaps. On the lawn she held my hand so hard that her rings cut me, and we stood there with the others and watched the third floor light up window by window from the inside, like a procession, and the windows on three were fixed above the transom line.

In the story as Cissy tells it, I carried her mother down three flights through the flames. In the story as Cissy tells it there is sometimes a wet towel over our faces and sometimes there is not, depending on the audience, and I went back in afterward for an old woman, which I did not do, which no one could have done. I have given up correcting the story. The first few times, years ago, when Diana would bring Cissy down to Ventura for lunch on the anniversary, which Diana did until Cissy was ten or eleven, I would say, it wasn't like that, I only walked her down the stairs, and Diana would look at me across the table with a look I eventually understood. The look said: let the child have it. The look said: the child has to have come from somewhere other than a coffee can behind the pool towels, an arrow that would not move, a man unaccounted for between midnight and the lawn. The look said: she is going to be told a story by someone, and it might as well have a girl with a passkey in it. So Cissy was raised on the fire the way other children are raised on a grandfather's war. The settlement, and there was a settlement, the alarm and the chains and the dry standpipes saw to that, paid for the house in Pasadena and, I have always assumed, for the heavy stock the invitation was engraved on. I do not say this bitterly. I do title searches for an escrow company in Ventura; I know better than most how the money from a catastrophe moves quietly down through the years and comes out the other end as a lawn, a wedding, a band playing Cole Porter under strings of lights.

The ceremony was at six, on the south lawn, with the ocean doing what the ocean does. Cissy was beautiful in the way that brides are reported to be beautiful and in fact frequently are. The minister, who was not a minister but a college friend ordained by mail, said that Cissy and Witt had chosen this place because out of ashes comes love, and a number of people turned, not quite discreetly, to look at me, because by six o'clock everyone at that wedding knew who I was. I had been introduced eleven times, and yes, I counted, as the woman who saved Diana, the woman who carried Diana, the chambermaid, the heroine, this is Frankie, Frankie was there. A young man in a blazer asked me what it had been like and held his drink in a way that told me he wanted ninety seconds of it, no more, something he could carry to the bar. I gave him the ninety seconds. The stairs, the smoke, the wrist. I have it down by now. It is a clean little story and nobody dies in it.

At dinner I was seated at table two, between Diana's brother and a Garland aunt, and after the toasts Cissy came around the tables in her dress and crouched down beside my chair with her hand on my arm and said, Frankie, will you tell it, and the table went attentive in the way tables do, and I looked at her. She has Diana's coloring and somebody's jaw, Howard's I suppose, and she was glowing, and she was asking me to tell, at her wedding, at the rebuilt scene of it, the story of the worst night of my life, and the terrible thing, the thing I want you to understand, is that there was nothing cruel in it anywhere. She loves the story. The story is how she knows she was meant to be here, in the dress, under the lights. We tell ourselves these things and then we book the venue. So I told it. I told it the way they wanted it told, and I gave them the towel over the face, and the Garland aunt put her hand to her throat, and when I finished Cissy kissed my cheek and said, You're the reason for all of this, and gestured, with her white arm, at the lawn and the lights and the band, and I said thank you, which was not the right thing to say but there is no right thing.

Later, when the dancing had started, Diana found me on the terrace. She is sixty-one now and has been married to a cardiologist for nineteen years and she looked, in the light off the lawn, very fine. We stood at the balustrade without talking for a while. Then she said, I come back, you know. Every October. I take a room and I have dinner and I leave in the morning. She said it the way you confess a small vice. I asked her why and she considered the question for longer than I expected and said, Because if I don't, it's just something that happened to me, and I said nothing, because I understood her exactly and because the thing she was warding off was the only thing I have ever had. She put her hand over my hand on the stone. Her rings were different rings. You kept saying the stairs, the stairs, she said. I'd have stood at that elevator till the ceiling came down, watching that arrow. Then she went back to the lights, because the band was starting the song she had requested, and I went inside and got in the elevator and pressed three.

The third floor of the new Mirasol is carpeted in a sea-grass weave and the room numbers run 300 to 322 just as they did, although nothing behind the numbers corresponds; the corridor is wider by the width of the litigation. At the end of the corridor, between 312 and the door marked STAIR, there is an alcove now, with an ice machine in it, humming, and a shelf of folded pool towels, and I stood in front of the ice machine for a while in my wedding clothes. A chambermaid came around the corner with a turndown basket, a girl of maybe twenty, and she hesitated when she saw me, the way you hesitate at a guest standing where no guest stands, and she said, Can I help you find your room? and I said no, thank you, I used to work here, a long time ago, and she smiled the smile you give to that sentence and went on to knock at 310, two knocks, housekeeping, wait, two knocks. I listened to her go down the corridor doing it correctly, door after door, and then I put my hand flat on the pool towels, just for a moment, the way you would touch the shoulder of someone sleeping, and took the stairs down.

I left before the cake. On the road out the eucalyptus came through the vents again and at the junction with the 101 there is a vista point, and I pulled over there, which I had not planned to do, and stood at the rail. Below and to the north the Mirasol lay along its bluff with every window lit, and the strings of lights on the lawn, and at that distance it looked exactly as it had looked on other nights in 1972, the good nights, when I would walk out to the highway on my evening off and look back at it and think that something was about to happen to me. A truck went by and shook the air and was gone. I have not had a cigarette since the third of October, 1972. When people ask, and people ask, because I have the manner of a smoker, I hold my glass a certain way, I say I quit for my health, and that is true, in its fashion. Eleven people died at the Mirasol. The fire marshal's report says a cigarette, carelessly discarded, smoldering for a period of hours in cotton and terry, and I have read it more times than I have read anything else I own, and I have never said otherwise, and I drove south with the windows shut and the ocean on my right, invisible, going about its business in the dark.
