The leak announced itself the way most things did on the Calloway place, which is to say it had been announcing itself for weeks before anyone listened. A green stripe in the dry grass below the headgate, a softness in the ground that Dean had walked over twice without thinking, and then on the first Tuesday of June a hole opened beside the mainline like a mouth, and the pressure in the upper block dropped so low the emitters under the Galas only spat and sighed, and Dean called his brother and said, you better come out, I can't dig it alone with my back.

Marcus drove up from Sacramento before the heat got serious, which in June meant before nine. He parked his sedan in the dust beside the shop where their father's flatbed still sat on tires gone square from sitting, and he stood for a minute looking at the orchard the way he always did when he first arrived, like a man checking a wound he hoped had healed in his absence. It hadn't. The Galas were okay, holding their leaves, but the old block of Red Delicious along the east fence had the gray, exhausted look of trees that had been asked too many times to make do, and three of them at the corner were simply dead, brown leaves rattling, kept standing only because nobody had found the day to take a chainsaw to them.

Dean came out of the shop carrying two shovels and a digging bar, and the brothers nodded at each other the way they did, without embracing, and walked up the slope to where the wet ground was.

"How long's it been losing?" Marcus asked.

"Pressure's been soft maybe two weeks. I thought it was the filter."

"Two weeks."

"I cleaned the filter twice. Then yesterday I saw the grass."

Marcus didn't say anything to that, which was its own kind of saying, and Dean heard it and let it go, and they set their shovels and started to dig. The ground on top was dry as flour, and then four inches down it turned dark, and a foot down it was saturated, heavy, and it came up on the shovel blades in slabs that smelled of iron and rot. The mainline was three feet deep here, six-inch PVC their father had buried in 1988, the summer Marcus turned nine, and both of them remembered that trench, the rented Ditch Witch, their father walking behind it in the noise and the dust with his hat pushed back, certain, the way he was certain about everything, that he was building something that would outlast him.

It had, technically. He had been dead four years. The pipe was thirty-six years old and still mostly in the ground doing its work, and that, Dean would have said if he were the kind of man who said such things, was about as much as anybody could ask of anything.

They dug for forty minutes, taking turns in the hole as it deepened, the one above hauling the slop away in a bucket because the water kept seeping back in, and finally Dean's shovel rang on the pipe and they got down on their knees and worked with their hands, scooping mud away from the white curve of it until they found the break. It wasn't a crack. It was a hole, round and clean, the size of a half dollar, and the pipe around it was scored and pale.

"Gopher?" Marcus said.

"Gophers don't chew through six-inch PVC."

"Then what."

Dean ran his thumb over the scoring. "Rock, probably. Settled against it all these years, and the pipe flexing against it every time the pump kicks on and off. Wore through like a stone in a shoe." He sat back on his heels in the mud. "Dad never bedded this line in sand. Said it was a waste of money. Just laid it on whatever the trench bottom was."

"Sounds right."

"He wasn't wrong about everything."

"I didn't say he was wrong about everything. I said it sounds right. That he'd skip the sand."

They looked at the hole. Water was still rising slowly around the pipe, the line draining back from the high block, and Dean said they'd have to cut out a section and put in a repair coupling, and that he had a coupling in the shop, he was almost sure, left over from the break in '21, and Marcus said you should check before we cut, and Dean said he would, but first they had to widen the hole enough to get a saw in, and Marcus took off his shirt and hung it on a tree limb and got back in the trench.

He was forty-five and Dean was forty-nine. Marcus had a desk job, something with the state water board that Dean had stopped trying to fully understand, modeling and allocations, and he had a desk body now, soft at the middle, though the work came back to him fast, the old motions, knees and hips and the short stroke of the shovel in a confined hole. Dean was leaner but bent, his back ruined in increments since he was twenty by exactly this kind of morning multiplied across thirty years, and he worked slower, in a careful economy of movement that looked lazy to anyone who hadn't earned it.

"I want to talk about the Hollis offer," Marcus said, not looking up from the trench.

"I figured you did."

"It expires end of the month. June thirtieth. After that he says he's looking at the Pedersen ground instead."

"He's not looking at the Pedersen ground. The Pedersen ground doesn't have our water rights. He's saying that to make us jump."

"Maybe. Probably. It doesn't change that the offer expires."

"Offers come back."

"Not at that number they don't. Dean. Not at that number." Marcus stopped and leaned on the shovel handle and looked at his brother above him at the trench lip, a dark shape against the bright sky. "Two point one million dollars. For ground that cleared, what, last year. What did it clear last year."

Dean didn't answer.

"What did it clear."

"It lost eleven thousand," Dean said. "You know that. You did the taxes."

"I did the taxes. Eleven thousand down, and the year before that it lost four, and the year before that it made nine, which doesn't count what your labor's worth, which means it lost thirty. The trees are old, the variety's worth nothing, the packers don't want Red Delicious, they barely want Galas, the water assessment's going up again, and you're out here at six in the morning digging holes with a back you can't straighten."

"You're digging the hole."

"Today I'm digging the hole. I'm here, what, six days a year? You're here three hundred and sixty-five."

Dean lowered the bucket on its rope and Marcus filled it without being asked and Dean hauled it up and dumped it and lowered it again. The sun cleared the line of eucalyptus along the county road and the light came across the orchard floor in long stripes and the mud in the spoil pile started to dry at its edges, lightening, and somewhere down the rows a mockingbird was running through its whole repertoire like it was being paid by the song.

"Half of two point one is yours," Dean said. "I know that. I'm not trying to keep your money from you."

"That is not what this is about and you know it."

"Then what's it about. Say what it's about."

"It's about you dying out here," Marcus said, and then, hearing himself, softer: "It's about watching you turn into him."

Dean hauled the bucket up and set it down and stood there a moment with the rope in his hand. Then he said, "I'm going to go see about that coupling," and walked down the row toward the shop, and Marcus stood in the trench up to his thighs in mud and watched him go and said nothing, because there was nothing in the sentence worth taking back, and that was the trouble with it.

The shop was their father in miniature, which is to say it was their father exactly: every bolt and fitting and length of pipe saved against a future need, sorted into coffee cans and bins labeled in his square carpenter's pencil hand, 3/4 GALV, ORCHARD STAPLES, MISC BRASS, the labels themselves now forty years old and soft at the edges. Dean stood in the cool dark of it and breathed in the smell, gear oil and dust and old wood, and looked along the shelf where the PVC fittings lived, and there it was, the six-inch repair coupling, still in its plastic sleeve, exactly where it would be, because of course it was, because the old man's whole life had been the building of a place where everything was where it would be. Dean held it a minute. Then he found the can of primer and the can of glue and shook them and heard the rattle and the slosh, gathered the hacksaw and a rag and a square, and carried it all back up the slope.

Marcus had the trench widened and was bailing with the bucket, methodical, his back and shoulders already going pink. Dean set the supplies down and dropped Marcus's shirt onto his head without comment, and Marcus put it on, and they worked the next stretch in silence except for the necessary words, hold this, cut square, measure it again. They cut out a fourteen-inch section of pipe with the hole in its center, and the hacksaw was slow going at the bottom of the cut where they couldn't get a full stroke, and they took turns, and the cut pipe when it finally came free was heavy with the wet and Dean turned it over and found the rock still seated in the mud beneath, a smooth river cobble the size of a fist, and he held it up.

"There's your culprit."

"Thirty-six years to do it."

"Patient son of a gun." Dean set the rock on the spoil pile like an exhibit. "I'll bed the new section in sand. There's half a yard of it behind the shop from the patio that never happened."

Marcus laughed, a single breath through the nose. "Mom's patio."

"Mom's patio."

"She asked for that patio for, what."

"I remember her asking when I was in high school. So twenty years she asked, and then he buys the sand the spring before she got sick, and then." Dean shrugged. "And then it sat. And now it'll go under a pipe, which would make him feel vindicated, probably. Knew that sand would come in handy."

"He would say that. He would say those exact words."

"Knew it'd come in handy. Everything comes in handy if you wait long enough and never do the thing you bought it for."

It was almost noon by then and the heat had arrived in earnest, that flat valley heat that didn't press down so much as remove things, shade, moisture, the will to argue, and they got the coupling dry-fitted and marked their depths with a pencil and then sat down in the strip of shade under the nearest Gala to let the inside of the pipe dry before gluing, because the glue wouldn't take on wet PVC and there was no rushing it. Dean had brought a jug of water and a sleeve of saltines and some cheese in a plastic bag, and they ate without talking for a while, and the mockingbird kept on, and a truck went by out on the county road trailing a long sigh of tires.

"I'm not turning into him," Dean said finally.

"Okay."

"He never sat down. You notice that? Thirty years I worked beside him and I never once saw him sit under a tree in the middle of a job and eat a cracker. I sit down. I take Sundays, mostly. I went to Reno in March."

"You went to Reno for a drip-irrigation trade show."

"There was a buffet."

Marcus laughed for real this time, and it loosened something, and they sat there in the shade with their backs against the trunk and the wet trench in front of them gleaming, and Marcus said, gently, the way you'd reach toward a dog that had been kicked, "Tell me what the plan is, then. Not the defense of the place. The plan. Five years from now, what does this look like."

Dean was quiet long enough that the question started to answer itself, and he knew it, and he talked anyway. "Pull the Red Delicious this winter. All eleven acres. Burn pile or the grinder, depending what the air board allows. Replant in something with a market. Honeycrisp wants more chill than we get now, so probably not, but there's club varieties, Cosmic Crisp money's gone but there's others, or you go a whole different direction, almonds are oversupplied, pistachios want better drainage than we've got on the east side, but cherries, maybe, early cherries, we're early ground, we always picked before the foothill guys."

"And replanting costs what."

"Eight, ten thousand an acre by the time you're done. More for cherries with the trellising."

"So a hundred thousand, hundred twenty, that you don't have, to plant trees that don't bear for four years, on water that the state, and I'm telling you this as the state, is going to keep getting more expensive and less reliable for the rest of your life. The new groundwater rules alone, when the sustainability plan kicks all the way in, this basin's overdrafted, Dean, I've seen the numbers, I make the numbers, the allocations are going down, not flat, down."

"We've got surface rights. Pre-fourteen."

"You've got surface rights to a river that didn't make it past the diversion two of the last five Augusts. Rights to water that isn't there is paper."

"It's good paper. Hollis thinks so. It's why he's offering two point one for ground that, what'd you say, can't make money."

That landed, and Marcus took it, nodding slowly, looking out at the trees. "Yeah," he said. "It's the water he wants. He'll pull every tree on the place within a year and put the rights to work on his ground down by the river, or bank them, or sell them south. The orchard part of it's worth nothing to him. You know that. I know it. So let's not either of us pretend the question is who'll take better care of the trees."

"Then what is the question."

"The question is whether you get to spend the next twenty years of your one life being the last man holding a thing nobody else wants, because Dad arranged it so somebody would have to, and you drew the short straw by being good."

The shade had moved while they sat and the sun was on Dean's boots now and he watched it there. "You think I drew a straw."

"I think you were seventeen and I was thirteen when he started saying Dean's the farmer in front of company, like it was a fact of your body, like your hair color. I think you got told who you were before you got a vote, and I got told who I wasn't, which, I'll be honest with you, took me years to understand was the better deal."

"You wanted to leave."

"I wanted to leave because there wasn't room to stay. There was one job here, son who farms, and it was filled."

"That's a nice story," Dean said, and there was an edge in it now, the first all day. "It makes everything his fault, which is convenient, because he's dead and can't argue, which is the only condition under which anybody ever won an argument with him. But I'm going to tell you something. I like it. I have liked it. Not every day. Not the years Mom was sick and he got mean, not the hail in '09, not standing in the packing shed watching them dump fruit because the price didn't cover the haul. But I have stood in this orchard in February with everything bare and pruned and the fog coming up off the ground at first light and felt like, this is it, this is what a person is for, and I don't know that you've ever felt that about a spreadsheet, and if you haven't, then I'm not sure you're the one who got the better deal, and I'm not sure you get to tell me what my life has been."

The mockingbird had stopped. Marcus sat with his forearms on his knees and turned a dirt clod over in his fingers, breaking it down small, and when he spoke his voice had gone careful. "Okay," he said. "That's fair. I don't get to tell you what it's been." He pitched the dirt away. "But I'm allowed to be scared about what it's going to be. You're forty-nine. Your back's gone. There's no wife, no kid, no hired man, no money for one. When you can't dig anymore, who digs? When you fall off a ladder thinning fruit, who finds you? I'm two hours away and getting older too, and I lie awake about it, Dean, that's the truth, I lie in my bed in Sacramento and think about you face down in a row and nobody coming. That's not me being the state water board. That's not me wanting a check. That's me being your brother, and being scared, and the only door I can see out of being scared is Hollis's stupid offer, so I keep pushing on it. Maybe it's the wrong door. But show me another one, because I can't keep standing in the hallway."

Dean didn't answer for a while. He got up, with the slow ratcheting motion his back required, and went and crouched by the trench and put his hand inside the cut end of the pipe, checking it for dry, and it was dry, or close enough, and the doing of that small task seemed to be how he was going to manage whatever came next, so Marcus got up too and they primed the pipe ends and the coupling, the purple primer staining their fingers, and then the glue, working fast because the glue gave you seconds, not minutes, and they drove the coupling home over the gap and across both ends, quarter-turning it to spread the glue the way their father had taught them with the same five words he always used, turn it, don't just push, and they held it under pressure for a count of sixty, both of them, four hands on the pipe in the bottom of the hole, and neither of them said anything during the count, and it was, although they would not have called it this, the closest thing to prayer either had done in years.

Then Dean sat back against the trench wall, mud and all, and said, "Sandra Vieira's kid. Anthony. You remember the Vieiras, off Road 19."

"Sure."

"He's twenty-six. Did two years at the JC in ag business, worked for the big Wonderful outfit down south and hated it, came home. He's been coming around since spring. Asking things. Good questions, mostly. He wants ground and can't touch ground at what ground costs now, nobody his age can, it's all Hollis and the pension funds." Dean picked at the dried purple on his thumbnail. "There's a thing the extension office was talking about. Lease-to-own, or a long lease with him farming it and me stepping back by degrees. He plants the new trees, his back, his years, and I carry him on the land cost where a bank wouldn't. I'd come off it slow. I'd come out with a lot less than two point one. A lot less. But the place stays a place. The water stays on this ground. And somebody finds me if I fall off the ladder, because somebody's here."

Marcus looked at him. "How long have you been sitting on this?"

"Since April. I didn't say anything because I didn't have it worked out, and you'd have come at it with forty questions I couldn't answer, and it would've died in the crib."

"I would not have."

"You're the state water board, Marcus. You'd have modeled it."

Marcus laughed, and then he didn't, and he looked down the rows, the light coming through lower now, late afternoon, gilding the dust they'd raised, and the dead trees at the east corner standing in it like the argument's witnesses. "It probably doesn't pencil," he said. "You know that. Kid that age, no equity, old trees, this basin."

"Probably not."

"Hollis pencils."

"Hollis pencils beautiful."

"And you're going to do it anyway."

"I'm going to try it anyway. If it falls apart in two years, Hollis or somebody like him will still be there. Money doesn't get tired of waiting. That's the one thing money's good at." Dean got himself upright again. "But I wanted, one time in my life, to do the thing on purpose. Not because I drew a straw. You want to know the difference between me and Dad, that's it, that's the whole difference, and it's enough. He never chose this. He inherited it and resented it and made everybody pay the resentment like a tax. I'm choosing it. Today. Out loud. In front of a witness." He looked at his brother. "You're the witness."

Marcus stood there a long moment, and whatever was moving through his face moved through it, fear and relief and the old grief and something that might have been envy, and what he finally said was, "Backfill first, or pressure test first?"

"Test first. Backfill over a leak and you get to dig it twice."

"Dad's voice. You hear it? That was his voice coming out of you."

"He wasn't wrong about everything," Dean said, and they walked down the slope together in the long light to the pump, and Dean threw the switch, and the motor wound up and the line filled, and they walked back up beside it listening to the water knock and rush through the buried dark beneath their feet, and stood at the edge of the trench watching the coupling, the white of it beaded with mud water, and it held, and it held, and up the row under the Galas the emitters began, one after another, to drip.
