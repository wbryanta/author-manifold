They came before noon, the way she knew they would, the way they always do — early birds with folded bills in their breast pockets and the patience of buzzards, parking their trucks along Cato Street with two wheels up on the grass her mother had cut herself until she was eighty-one. Vesta watched them through the front window, through glass her mother had washed every Good Friday with vinegar and newspaper, and she thought: come on, then. Come on in and carry it all away.

The sale was advertised for noon. By eleven-fifteen there were nine of them on the porch, and one woman had her face pressed to the screen door like a child looking into a candy store, calling Hello? Hello? in a voice meant to sound friendly and sounding instead like ownership rehearsing itself. Vesta let them stand there another four minutes — long enough to finish her coffee, long enough to walk the rooms one last time with everything still in its place, the chifforobe still holding its corner, the piano still leaning its dark weight against the east wall, the kitchen table still wearing its scorch mark like a birthmark, like a brand, like the proof of a night nobody alive but her remembered. Then she opened the door.

"Everything's priced," she said. "Yellow stickers. What ain't stickered ain't for sale."

They flowed past her with their soft excuse-me's, fanning out into Odessa Bonner's house the way water finds the low places, and Vesta stood holding the door and felt the cold come in around their bodies even though it was June, even though the maple out front was in full leaf and the heat was already pooling in the street. Some cold comes with people. Her mother had taught her that. Some folks bring winter in their pockets, Odessa used to say, and don't even know they carrying it. Shut the door behind them quick.

She had been in the house eleven days. Eleven days of sorting, of kneeling in closets, of opening drawers that exhaled her mother's smell — Cashmere Bouquet and camphor and underneath it something older, something that was just Odessa, the smell of her neck when Vesta was small and being carried up the stairs half asleep, pretending to be all asleep, because a child carried is a child loved beyond argument. Eleven days and her knees had quit complaining and started just refusing, and her sister Geneva had called twice from Detroit to say she couldn't get away, the second time not even bothering to dress the excuse in anything, just laying it down naked: I can't be in that house, Vesta. I can't do it. You always could do things. Like it was a talent. Like it was a gift God passed out at the river and Vesta got a double portion: the doing of things. The staying. The being the one.

Fifty-six years old and still the one.

A man in a Cincinnati Reds cap was running his hand over the chifforobe. He had the look — she'd learned the look in one morning of this — the dealer look, the eyes that didn't see furniture but margins, that undressed a piece down to what it would fetch in a shop in German Village with a hand-lettered tag calling it primitive or farmhouse or, God help us, authentic. He opened the door of it and the door swung clean and silent because Odessa had kept it oiled for sixty years, had wiped it down with lemon oil every spring whether it asked for it or not, and inside hung nothing now but four wooden hangers and the ghost of a navy blue dress.

The funeral dress. Her mother had kept it pressed and ready since 1979, the year Walter died, because a woman who had buried a husband knew that death didn't send a card first, and she would not — would not — be caught by it unprepared, would not have her daughters scrambling through her closet while she lay at Simmons Funeral Home in something hasty. The dress had done its work, finally, three weeks ago. Odessa had worn it into the ground at Mount Calvary with the little pearl buttons done all the way to the throat, and Vesta had stood at the graveside thinking, absurdly, you finally got to wear it, Mama, and then had laughed, one short broken sound, and Geneva had gripped her arm thinking she was crying. Maybe she was. The two things had stopped being different somewhere along the way.

"How firm are you on this?" the Reds cap man said.

"Firm as the sticker."

"It's got some wear on the —"

"It's got sixty-three years on it," Vesta said. "It came up from Alabama in the back of a truck in 1946 with everything else my mother owned in the world inside it, which was one quilt, one Bible, one cast-iron skillet, and a photograph of her people. It crossed the Ohio River wrapped in that quilt. The price is the price."

He looked at her then, actually looked, the way they did when the furniture suddenly talked back, when the estate turned out to still have a body and the body had a mouth. Most of them, after the look, would set the thing down gently — gentler than they'd picked it up — and drift toward the kitchen, toward the Pyrex and the jelly glasses, the safe things, the things without testimony. But this one nodded slowly and peeled five twenties off a fold and said, "I'll take it. You want me to know anything else about it?"

And that — that small unexpected courtesy, the asking — undid something in her chest she'd kept buckled for eleven days, and she had to turn toward the window and pretend to be checking on the porch.

"No," she said. "It told you what it had to tell."

She didn't watch them carry it out. There are things a person can watch and things a person can hear instead, and she stood in the kitchen with her back to the hall and heard the men grunt and shuffle, heard the screen door bang its old two-note bang — slap, then the little kiss of the latch — heard the chifforobe pass over the threshold her mother had crossed ten thousand times, twenty thousand, coming home from Murchison's laundry with her hands cracked from the steam, coming home from Mount Calvary with her hat still on, coming home the last time from St. Joseph's hospital in 1981 carrying Geneva's first baby, who was now a grown woman in Atlanta who hadn't come to the funeral either. The truck door slammed outside. An engine turned over. Sixty-three years drove away down Cato Street doing about fifteen miles an hour, careful over the speed bump, and Vesta laid both hands flat on the kitchen table and breathed.

The table. Lord, the table.

It was oak, claw-footed, heavy as sin, and it had a black scorch shaped like a comma on its near corner where Odessa had set down a hot iron the night Walter came home from the foundry in November of 1958 with his left hand wrapped in another man's shirt and blood coming through the cotton in blooms. He'd caught it in the rollers. Three fingers gone at the second knuckle, just gone, left behind in the mill the way the mill took pieces of all of them — fingers, hearing, lung, years — taking its tithe of Black men's bodies and paying out in return just enough to keep a mortgage breathing on Cato Street, just enough to be called good money by people who had been offered so much worse. Odessa had been ironing Sunday shirts. She set the iron down flat on the wood — didn't stand it up, didn't think — and crossed the kitchen to her husband, and by the time anybody remembered the iron it had eaten its mark deep into the oak, and the kitchen smelled like scorch and blood and the loud terrible calm of her mother's voice saying sit down, Walter, sit down, baby, let me see it, I got you, sit down.

She never sanded the mark out. Vesta asked her once, years later, why, and Odessa had run her thumb along it the way you'd touch a child's scar and said, "Because we came through that night. A table ought to remember what a family remembers."

Now a young couple was circling it. Young — early thirties, the woman with her hair in twists and a baby riding her hip, the man with careful eyes, both of them doing that married arithmetic, that wordless conference of eyebrows. Vesta watched the woman put her palm down on the wood, right next to the scorch mark, and leave it there a second longer than shopping required.

"This is a beautiful piece," the woman said. "We just bought over on Tremont. First house." She said first house the way you'd say firstborn. "But I think it's out of our —"

"What can you do?" Vesta said.

The man named a number that was less than half the sticker, and named it ashamed, looking at his shoes. The baby grabbed a fistful of his mother's twists and pulled, and the woman untangled the little hand without looking, automatic, the way mothers do, the way Odessa used to reach back blind from the front seat of the Buick and find exactly the child that needed finding.

"That mark there," Vesta said, "don't come out. Don't try to make it come out. You hear me?"

The woman looked down at the scorch, then up. Something passed across her face — not understanding, exactly, because she couldn't understand, but the willingness to. The leaning toward it. That was all Vesta needed. That was more than most of the day had offered.

"Take it for what he said," Vesta told them. "And feed people at it. It don't sit right empty."

She helped them carry it, which she had no business doing with her knees, but some things you put your hands to whether you've got business or not. The four of them — counting the baby, who supervised from a blanket on the grass — walked the table down the porch steps into the long light, and Vesta thought about all the suppers. Thought about Sonny doing his homework on it with his tongue between his teeth before Vietnam got him, not killed — Vietnam didn't kill Sonny, it just kept him, sent back a man wearing Sonny's face who sat at that table moving food around his plate for two years and then went to California and called every Christmas until he didn't. Thought about Geneva's wedding cake sitting on it under a dish towel. Thought about her mother and Miss Pearline from next door shelling peas at it for forty summers, the radio going, the two of them laughing low about things they hushed when children came in, that whole library of woman-talk, that whole testament, gone now into the ground twice over — Pearline in '04, Odessa three weeks back — and nobody ever wrote a word of it down because it wasn't the kind of thing the world had decided was worth ink.

The afternoon wore on the way afternoons do at the end of things: in pieces. The Pyrex went, the jelly glasses, the percolator. A man bought Walter's tools, the whole pegboard's worth, and Vesta made herself not mind, because tools want hands and Walter's hands were thirty years gone and hers had never learned them. The bedroom set went to a woman who ran a shelter over in Elyria and got it for almost nothing because of where it was going, because Odessa would have skinned Vesta alive for taking full price off a woman housing women. Church hats went, the ones Geneva hadn't claimed — went to a girl who tried one on in the hall mirror and laughed at herself with such pure delight that Vesta gave her a second one free, the lavender, and the girl wore them both out the door, stacked, like a queen of someplace merciful.

The piano did not go.

People played at it — strangers' fingers on the yellowed keys, picking out chopsticks, picking out the first four bars of things they half knew — and it was out of tune and Vesta let it stay out of tune all day like a deliberate lie, because the truth was she didn't want it to sing for them. The piano was bought in 1961 on layaway from Hubbell's downtown, fourteen dollars a month for two years and a half, because Geneva at nine had a gift, everybody said so, the choir director said so, and Odessa had decided her daughter's gift would not die for lack of fourteen dollars a month even if it meant taking in extra wash, which it did, which it meant her mother's hands cracked deeper every winter so that Geneva could play Clair de Lune at the spring recital in a dress made from a Simplicity pattern. And Geneva played beautifully for six years and then turned sixteen and discovered boys and Motown and the wide world, and the piano fell silent in 1967 and stayed silent, and Odessa never said one word of reproach about it, not one, not in forty years, just kept it dusted, kept it tuned for the first decade and then quietly let even that go — but kept it dusted, kept the wood gleaming, the way you keep a grave.

A dealer offered her three hundred for it at four o'clock. Vesta heard her own mouth say, "It's not for sale," and was surprised, and then wasn't. There was no yellow sticker on it. She must have known all along. She'd pay the movers whatever foolishness it cost to haul it to Columbus, to her own small house where nobody played either, and it would sit against her east wall being dusted and silent and hers, and someday her granddaughter or somebody's granddaughter would put her hands on it, and that would be Odessa's fourteen dollars a month coming home at last, forty, fifty, sixty years late, because that was how it worked, her mother's kind of investment — it paid out down generations or not at all, and you put the money in anyway. You put the money in anyway. That was the whole of the religion underneath the religion.

Around five the house began to empty of strangers and fill back up with light. June light, low and gold, coming through curtainless windows — the curtains had sold, somebody was going to hang her mother's curtains in some other town — and the light lay down in long lanes across the bare floors, and the rooms without their furniture turned out to be small. That was the shock of it. All her childhood the house had been enormous, the hallway a road, the stairs a mountain, the front room a country with borders and laws — don't run in here, don't touch the figurines, company sits there — and now, undressed, it was just a little frame house, four rooms down and two up, eleven hundred square feet of a woman's whole long stand against the world. Her mother had held this much ground. This much, for sixty-three years, against banks and winters and grief and the steady cold wish of the country around her that she be less, have less, want less. Eleven hundred square feet, held. It looked small the way a fist looks small.

It was while she was peeling the shelf paper out of the pantry — she didn't have to, the buyers were gutting the kitchen anyway, but her hands wanted the task — that she found the envelope. Under the third shelf's paper, flat against the wood, taped at the corners with tape gone amber and brittle. No writing on the outside. Inside: a photograph, small, scalloped-edged, of a baby in a christening gown, propped on a chair that Vesta did not recognize in a room she had never seen, a Southern room, you could feel the heat in it even through black and white. And folded around the photograph, a curl of dark hair tied with thread. And on the back of the photograph, in her mother's young hand, before her hand had become the hand Vesta knew, two words and a number: Julius. 1944.

Vesta sat down on the pantry floor. Just sat, the way you do when a floor offers itself.

Nineteen forty-four. Two years before the truck, the river, Ohio. Two years before Walter, before any of the story Vesta had ever been told, which began — had always begun — your mother came up from Alabama in 1946, like the curtain rising on act one, like there was nothing behind the curtain but the orderly dark. Julius. Her mother had had a son. Her mother had had a son and buried him or lost him in some Alabama that she folded up and taped under shelf paper and stood in front of, cooking, for sixty-three years. Every biscuit rolled out on that counter was rolled out eighteen inches above this baby's face. Every grace said in that kitchen was said over him. And she never told. Not Walter, maybe — or maybe Walter, maybe that was a thing a husband got told in the dark of 1947 and carried to his own grave like a second wedding ring. But not the children. Not the daughters. Not Vesta, who had sat with her at the end, the very end, holding the hand with the cracks worn smooth at last, listening to her mother's breathing get curious and slow, thinking she knew the woman entire.

You always could do things, Geneva said. But Odessa was the one who could do things. Could carry a dead son across the Ohio River inside her chest and set up housekeeping on top of him and raise three more children in the warmth coming off that buried fire and let every one of them believe her life began the day theirs became possible. Vesta held the photograph in the pantry light and looked at the baby's face for her mother's face and found it — the brow, the deep eyes, Sonny's eyes, Lord, Sonny had this child's eyes — and she said out loud, to the empty house, "Okay, Mama." Not a question. Not forgiveness, because there was nothing to forgive; a woman is entitled to her own marrow. Just: okay. I'll carry him now. There's room.

By seven the last of them were gone. The shelter woman's nephew came back for the bed frame; the young couple's truck made its second trip for the table's leaves, which Vesta had found in the basement at the final hour; a teenage boy bought the lawnmower for ten dollars and pushed it home down the middle of Cato Street like a man with a future. Then nothing. Then the street noise of a small Ohio town at suppertime — a dog, a screen door three houses down, somebody's television talking to itself through an open window — and Vesta alone in the front room with the piano and a cookie tin and an envelope.

The tin was the one thing she'd stickered NOT FOR SALE in her own hand, first thing, eleven days ago, before she'd decided anything else: a Royal Dansk butter cookie tin that had not held a butter cookie since the Johnson administration. Buttons. Her mother's button tin, hundreds of them, bone and brass and shell and Bakelite, cut off of every garment that had ever died in this house, because Odessa Bonner did not throw away a button any more than she threw away a soul — everything was going to be useful again, everything was coming back, that was the whole of the religion underneath the religion underneath the religion. Vesta had played in that tin as a child, run her fingers through it like a pirate through doubloons, sorted them by color on the rug while her mother hummed at the ironing board — hummed, always, no words, some tune from before, from behind the curtain, and Vesta understood now, only now, on the bare floor in the gold light, that the hum had probably had words once, in Alabama, in 1944, sung low over a chair with a baby propped in it, and that her mother had brought the tune across the river and left the words on the other side because the words could not be carried. The tune could. The tune got through.

She sat down on the floor where the table used to be, fifty-six years old, knees be damned, and put the tin in her lap and the envelope on top of the tin. Through the window the maple was going black against a sky the color of peach flesh. Tomorrow the lawyer, the buyers, the signing; tomorrow the highway south to Columbus with a piano following her like a hearse. Tonight, just this. She ran her thumb through the buttons and they made their small river sound, and she found she was humming. Not deciding to. Just humming, low, the no-words tune, the one that got through, and the empty house took it up the way empty houses do, holding the note a half-second after she let it go, as if someone in the next room were learning it. As if someone in the next room had known it all along, and had only been waiting, all these years, through all these suppers, for somebody to finally sing it in the open — and was joining in now, quiet, gone, unburdened, hers.
