They came up through the orchard in the dark before dawn with their shovels over their shoulders like soldiers of some forgotten campaign and the trees stood in their rows on either side of them black and twisted and silent. The older brother carried a lantern though there was no need of it for the sky in the east had already begun to gray and he carried it unlit, swinging from his fist, the glass chattering faintly in its frame. Their boots in the dry grass. Somewhere down by the creek a bird had begun to call and quit and call again as if it could not make up its mind about the day.

The break was in the lower forty where the line crossed under the old lane and they knew it was there because the ground above it had gone to mud in a country where nothing had gone to mud in six weeks. The water rose up out of the earth dark and welling like something the earth had decided to give back and it lay in a long seep down the ruts of the lane and the younger brother stood looking at it while the light came up around them.

How long you reckon it's been runnin like this.

I dont know. Since Tuesday maybe. I shut the head-gate Tuesday night.

That's a lot of water.

It is.

That's water you paid for.

The older brother set the lantern in the grass and unshouldered his shovel and pushed the blade into the mud with his boot and turned it. I know whose water it is, he said.

They dug. The sun cleared the bench to the east and came down through the trees in long bars and the mud they threw up out of the trench shone in it and dried gray at the edges almost as they watched. The trees here were Elbertas the old man had put in the year the younger brother was born and they were past their best now, black-knotted, gummed at the crotches, the bark coming away in plates on the oldest of them, but they had set fruit, hard green fists of fruit clustered too thick along the limbs because no one had thinned them. The older brother had meant to thin them. There was a great deal he had meant to do.

By eight oclock they had the pipe exposed for a length of ten feet and they stood in the trench up to their knees in water the color of coffee and looked at it. It was the old concrete pipe the old man had laid in nineteen and fifty-eight, hauled up from Delta on a borrowed truck section by section, and the joint had let go where a root from a cottonwood long since cut down had found it and pried at it patiently for fifty years until it gave. The root itself was still there, dead, silvered, thick as a man's wrist, curled about the pipe like something feeding.

Lord, said the younger brother. Look at that.

Yeah.

That's been waitin all this time.

Everthing's been waitin all this time, the older brother said. That's all waitin is. Time.

They cut the root out with the mattock and bailed the trench with a bucket, passing it up between them hand to hand, and when the joint stood clear they could see where the old man had mended it once before, a collar of mortar troweled rough about the bell of the pipe and printed still with the marks of his fingers. The older brother knelt in the mud and put his own hand to it. He didnt say anything. The younger brother stood above him on the lip of the trench with the bucket hanging from his hand and watched him do it and then looked away down the rows where the light moved among the leaves.

We could be in Grand Junction by noon, he said. Sign the papers, eat us a steak dinner, be home by dark.

The older brother stood up out of his kneeling and took up the shovel again.

I'm just sayin.

I heard what you said.

You aint goin to answer me.

I answered you in March. I answered you at Christmas. I'm runnin out of new ways to say it.

The younger brother set the bucket down. He took off his cap and wiped his forehead with the back of his wrist and put the cap on again. He was forty-one years old and he had a wife in Montrose and two boys and a mortgage and a quarter share in an orchard that had not cleared money in four years, and his brother was forty-six and had the other three quarters and a house with one light burning in it of an evening and no one to leave any of it to, and they had been having this conversation in one form or another for two years, in kitchens and in trucks and at gravesides, and it had worn a channel in both of them the way water wears a channel, by never being anything other than what it was.

Hendricks called the house again, he said. Tuesday.

The older brother went on shoveling.

He's up to nine hundred a unit. You know what that comes to. The whole place. You know what that comes to.

I can do figures.

A million one. Better than. You could put your share in the bank and never lift a shovel again in your life.

And do what.

Whatever you wanted.

This is what I want.

The younger brother laughed, not meanly, a short tired sound. He looked up through the limbs at the sky, which was a deep cloudless blue going white at the edges with the heat already coming on, and he shook his head. Standin in a ditch, he said. Bailin out the old man's pipe with a five-gallon bucket. That's what you want.

It aint the old man's pipe. It's mine.

It's ours.

The older brother stopped and stood resting his forearm on the haunch of the shovel and looked at him. All right, he said. It's ours. You want to get down in here and help me fix what's ours or you want to stand up there sellin it.

The younger brother got down in the trench.

They worked through the morning. They chipped the old mortar from the joint and chiseled the bell clean and the older brother walked back down to the barn for the sack of cement and a coil of oakum and a length of rubber sleeve he'd had off a man in Eckert who salvaged such things, and while he was gone the younger brother sat on the lip of the trench with his boots hanging in the shade of it and ate an apple from the lunch pail and looked at the place. The house with its swayback ridgeline. The barn that had been red and was now no color that had a name, the color of weather itself. The rows running away to the south in their long perspective and beyond them the rim of the mesa burnt pale and the desert beyond that which no one had ever wanted until now and which men in pressed shirts now drove out from Grand Junction to look at with instruments. He could see the survey stakes on the Pruitt place across the creek, little flags of pink plastic hanging dead in the still air. The Pruitts had sold in the fall. There were house lots staked across ground where he had picked peaches as a boy at three cents the pound and he sat eating his apple and looking at the little pink flags and he could not have said entirely what he felt, whether it was grief or relief or only the strange weightlessness of watching a thing end that you had thought was the permanent furniture of the world.

His brother came back up the lane with the wheelbarrow and they mixed the mortar in it with ditchwater and the older brother packed the oakum into the joint with a caulking iron and a hand sledge, tapping it home in short ringing strokes, and then he buttered the mortar on with his bare hands the way the old man had done because the old man had held that a trowel couldnt feel anything and hands could. The younger brother held the pipe steady and watched him work.

You remember when he put this in, he said.

You wasnt even born.

I know it. I'm askin if you remember.

Some of it. I was little. I remember the truck. I remember him and Albert Mahan settin the sections with a chain off the loader and Mahan smashed his thumb and stood out here cussin in the rain and the old man told him to mind his language in front of the boy. Meanin me. And Mahan looked at me and looked at his thumb and he said, son, they's words for this and I aint allowed to say em.

The younger brother smiled. He held the pipe.

He thought this pipe would see him out, the older brother said. He was right about that much.

He thought a lot of things.

He did.

He thought one of us'd have sense enough to let it go when the time come.

The older brother went on smoothing the mortar with the flat of his thumb, around and around the collar of the joint, his face down close to the work. He never said that, he said.

He said it to me.

When.

In the hospital. The last week.

The older brother's hands stopped. The water in the bottom of the trench moved a little around their boots, settling, and a dragonfly came down the line of the trench in the heat as if patrolling it and tilted and rose away over the trees. The older brother knelt there with his hands shining gray with the mortar and he did not look up.

He never said no such thing to me, he said.

He didnt have to say it to you. He knew you. He said it to me because I'd carry it. He said, dont let your brother nail hisself to this place. He said it aint a sin to sell a thing. He said the sin's in lettin a thing kill you that dont even know your name.

That sounds like you. That dont sound like him.

I aint lyin to you, Wes.

I didnt say you was lyin. I said it sounds like you.

He took up smoothing the joint again though the joint was smooth. The younger brother watched him. Up the slope a tractor started somewhere on the Carmichael place, far off, dim, a sound like a fly in a bottle, and then it faded as it went away over the hill and the only sound was the small wet sounds of the work and the heat ticking in the grass.

He was eighty-six years old, the older brother said at last. He was full of morphine and he didnt know which of us was which by the end. You want to take a dyin man's talk and sell forty acres of bearin trees on it.

They aint bearin. That's the whole thing of it. Look at em. You aint thinned, you aint sprayed but twice, half the lower block's got borer in it, the packin shed roof is gone at the north end and the co-op wont take softs no more on account of the new rules and the water's nine years junior on this ditch and gettin cut ever year earlier. They aint bearin, Wes. They're dyin. They been dyin for ten years and you been standin out here holdin their hands.

The older brother rose up out of the trench. He climbed out stiffly, a big man going to bone, and he rinsed his hands in the bucket and dried them on his thighs and walked off a few steps into the row and stood with his back turned. The trees stood about him. The light came down through them and broke on his shoulders and on the ruined grass and the small hard peaches hung everywhere in their clusters, thousands of them, green and dusty and secret, drawing what they drew up out of the dark of the ground by a way no man had made or could make, and he stood among them a long time.

You done? he said.

I aint hardly started.

Well. Whyfore dont you hold the rest of it till the mortar's set. I dont want it shook.

They ate their dinner in the shade of the big Bartlett at the head of the lane, the one tree on the place older than the old man's tenure, planted by whatever vanished family had proved up the ground before them, and it threw a shade like a building. They ate the sandwiches their mother would once have made and which the older brother had made instead, white bread and ham and too much mustard, and they drank water out of the same scoured thermos they had carried to this work as boys, and for a while neither of them said anything and the day stood around them enormous and bright and indifferent.

You recall the hail in eighty-eight, the older brother said.

The younger brother lay back in the grass with his cap over his eyes. I recall it.

Tenth of June. Greener'n this. Crop set heavy as I ever seen it and it come up out of the west about four in the afternoon and the light turned that color. That green-yeller. And him standin on the porch watchin it come.

I recall it. I was there.

Beat ever peach off this place in eleven minutes. I timed it. Eleven minutes. Leaves and all. Looked like winter out here in the middle of June, ice layin in the rows four inches deep and steamin. The whole year's work and the year's money laid out on the ground meltin. And you know what he done.

He went to bed.

He went to bed. Four thirty in the afternoon. Never said a word, took off his boots on the porch and went in and went to bed. And the next mornin he was out here at five with the flatbed and a scoop shovel cleanin ice out of the ditches so it wouldnt dam, and he never spoke of it again in his life that I heard. Not once. Not, that was a bad year. Nothin.

The younger brother lay with his cap over his eyes. And what do you take from that, he said.

I dont take nothin from it. I'm tellin you what he was.

I know what he was. I'm tellin you what it cost him. You think that was strength, him not speakin of it. I think it ate him from the inside out like that root ate the pipe and it taken fifty years instead of eleven minutes, that's all. And I watched it eat Mama too and I'm watchin it start in on you and I am askin you, I am askin you, Wesley, to let the goddamned thing go.

The older brother sat with his forearms on his knees and his hands hanging and he looked out down the rows. A meadowlark was singing somewhere out in the dead pasture beyond the lower block, the long bright falling phrase of it over and over, the only extravagance in all that burnt country.

I cant, he said.

Cant aint a reason.

It's the only one I got.

Then give me somethin. Make me understand it. Because from where I sit it looks like pride, and pride at nine hundred a unit is a luxury, brother, and I cant afford my quarter of it.

The older brother was quiet a long time. When he spoke it was slowly, like a man feeling in the dark for things set down long ago and not moved since.

When I was nineteen, he said, I had a place at Fort Lewis. You didnt know that. Nobody knowed it but Mama. Had it in a letter and I carried that letter in my shirt pocket for two weeks tryin to figure how to tell him, and it come on harvest and we was short-handed like always and one evenin in the shed he put his hand on my shoulder, he never done that, you know he never done that, and he said, I dont know how I'd run this place without you. That's all he said. And I went up to my room that night and I burnt the letter in a coffee can and I never spoke of it and here I am. So dont tell me about cost. I have done the figures on cost my whole life. And what I know is this. If I sell this ground, then that letter burnt for nothin. Then every year of it was for nothin. A man cant live with that. Maybe a smarter man could. I cant.

The younger brother had taken his cap from his face and sat up and he was looking at him. That aint a reason to keep it, he said quietly. That's a reason you cant let it go. Them's different things.

Maybe.

They are. One of em's about the land. Other one's about you.

I dont know that there's a difference no more. I have put forty years of myself in this dirt. Sweat and worse. Tell me where the dirt leaves off and I begin and I'll cut along the line.

The mortar set through the heat of the afternoon and they went and saw to other things, the two of them moving about the place in the old wordless choreography of men who have worked together since childhood, mending the screen on the headgate, swapping out two risers in the upper block, the younger brother going along the rows with a paintbrush and a can of borer paste daubing the gummed wounds at the base of the trees the way their mother used to do it, kneeling at each tree like a man administering some rite. Toward five oclock a wind came up out of the southwest and moved in the orchard and the leaves all turned their pale undersides and the whole place shimmered and whispered like a thing dreaming, and then the wind passed on and it was still again.

At six they walked up to the headgate together. The ditch ran along the upper line of the property carrying other men's water to other men's ground and the older brother knelt and unchained the wheel and looked back down the slope at the orchard standing in the long light.

She wont blow out.

The gate rose grating in its frame and the water came through the throat of it and folded down into the dry company ditch with a sound like cloth tearing and went away from them down the slope, sucking at the dust, running brown and quick, and they walked along beside it the way they had walked beside it as boys, the way all boys have walked beside first water in a dry ditch since the world's beginning, not racing it exactly but not letting it get away from them either. It ran the length of the upper block and dropped into the standpipe and they heard it go down the buried line beneath their feet, a deep hollow swallowing in the earth, and they walked down the lane to the place where they had dug and stood over the filled trench and waited. The ground there stayed dry. The water passed beneath it and went on and a minute later they heard it rise in the lower risers one after another down the slope, each one coughing and then running clean, and the furrows of the lower forty began to darken one by one in the evening light like the slow opening of something.

It held, the younger brother said.

It held.
