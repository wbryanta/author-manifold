The tide turned at 6:47 that morning, and Einar Sorensen was on the breakwater to watch it happen, the way other men his age watched the news. He could feel it before he could see it — a slackening in the air itself, as if the whole strait had paused to take a breath — and then the kelp heads at the harbor mouth began to lean the other way, slow as the hands of a clock, and the water that had been pouring out of the bay all night began to pour back in. Sixty-one years he had watched this, and it had never once bored him. He thought that was probably the secret of his life, if his life had a secret: he had found the one thing that could not bore him, and he had stood next to it until he got old.

His granddaughter came down the breakwater at seven with her hood up and her hands jammed in her pockets, walking the way twelve-year-olds walk when they have been made to get up in the dark, which is to say like prisoners of an unjust regime. Her name was Dagny, after his wife, though everyone called her Dee, which his wife would have hated. She stopped beside him and looked out at the gray water and the grayer sky and the cannery on its pilings across the bay, lit up yellow at the windows because the herring boats had come in at four and the line was running.

"Mom says I have to come back for lunch," she said. "She's on till six."

"We'll be back before lunch."

"What are we even looking at."

"The tide turning."

She looked. He watched her look. She had her mother's dark hair and her grandmother's way of frowning when she concentrated, two vertical lines between the eyebrows, and she was looking at the water the way people look at water who have lived beside it all their lives and never once seen it — as a backdrop, a gray sheet, the thing that was there when you looked past the thing you were looking at.

"It looks the same," she said.

"Look at the kelp. There, off the end of the rocks."

She found it. The amber heads of the bull kelp lay on the surface like seals, and the long stipes beneath them streamed shoreward now, all of them, combed in one direction by the flooding current.

"It's pointing in," she said.

"This morning it points in until almost one o'clock. Then it stands straight up for about twenty minutes, like it can't make up its mind. Then it points out. If you can't remember anything else in your life, remember that the kelp doesn't lie. The tide book lies, sometimes, when there's a big wind stacking water in the strait. Your eyes lie. Kelp doesn't lie."

"Why would my eyes lie?"

"Because they belong to you," he said, "and you want things."

She gave him a sideways look, deciding whether this was wisdom or just the way old men talked, and he let her decide on her own time, because that was a lesson too. Then they went down the rocks to where the skiff was tied, and he held it steady while she got in, and he did not help her more than that, and she did not ask him to.

This was November. The announcement had come in October, a single sheet of paper taped to the cannery door and another in the window of the grocery: Anchor Bay Packing thanked the community for eighty-four years and would cease operations at the end of the winter season. The parent company, which was in Tokyo, regretted. The men who had nailed up the paper were already gone by the time anyone read it. Einar's daughter Marit had worked the sliming line and then the office for nineteen years, and she came home that night and sat at the kitchen table and said, "Well," and then said nothing else for a long time, and Einar had understood that the word contained everything — the house payment, the girl's school, the question of whether they would stay or go inland to Yakima where Marit's sister kept saying there was work. He had not asked her which it would be. He had a coward's instinct about certain questions, which was to stand very still near them, the way you stood still near a rip you didn't want to be set into.

But the next morning he had gone to the school and waited by the fence at three o'clock, and when Dee came out he had said, "I'm going to teach you to read the water," and she had said, "Why," and he had said, "Because nobody else is going to," which was not the real reason but was at least true.

The real reason was harder. He had spent forty years as a pilot, climbing rope ladders up the steel sides of ships in the dark, in weather that would frighten God, taking the wheel — taking the conn, rather, because the wheel was the helmsman's, the pilot only spoke — of vessels seven hundred feet long that drew thirty feet of water in a channel that gave them thirty-four, and he had brought every one of them in and brought every one of them out, and the way he had done it was that he could read the water the way other men read a page. He knew what the strait was doing by the color of it, the texture, the set of the foam lines, the way the chop stood up here and lay down there. He knew where the bottom shoaled by the shape of the swell over it. He knew, rounding the point in fog so thick the bow disappeared, where he was by the sound the whistle made coming back off the cliffs, and how long it took, and what that meant. All of this was in him, a whole literacy, a dead language with one living speaker, and the ships didn't come anymore — the big ones went north to the deepwater port at Calloway now, had for fifteen years — and the fleet was down to eleven boats from a hundred and forty, and after this winter the cannery lights would go out and there would be no reason for anyone ever again to know what he knew. He could not save the town. He had understood for some time that the town was a tide going out, and he had spent his life learning that you do not argue with tides; you learn their hours and you use them. But it seemed to him intolerable, in some way he could not have said out loud at the Sons of Norway hall without embarrassing everyone, that the knowing itself should die in him like a fish in a bucket. So: the girl.

They went out three mornings a week, weather permitting, and some mornings when it didn't. He taught her the way he had been taught, which was almost without teaching. He put her on the oars and made her hold the skiff in one place against the current, so that she could feel in her shoulders what the water was doing, because the body learned faster than the head and forgot slower. He made her close her eyes at the harbor mouth and tell him which way they were being set, and at first she couldn't, and then one morning in December she said, "Left — no — toward the spit," with her eyes still shut, and opened them and saw she was right, and looked at her own hands like they had been swapped for someone else's.

"How did I know that?"

"You felt the boat. The boat always knows. People spend a lot of money on instruments to find out what the boat is trying to tell them for free."

He taught her colors. Green-brown over the eelgrass flats, and don't trust your depth there at half tide. The hard dark green of deep water, almost black under cloud. The pale milky green over the sandbar off Larsen's point, which would show you exactly where the bar was at any tide if you knew to look, and which a man named Roy Hodge had not known to look for in 1971 and had put a fifty-foot seiner on at full cruise, and you could still find pieces of her in the sand at low water springs. He taught her foam lines: where two currents met they laid down a seam on the surface, a line of foam and feathers and cedar bark and whatever else the water carried, straight as a ruled margin, and on one side of it the water might be moving north at two knots and on the other side south at one, and a boat crossing it would feel a hand take her bow and turn it. He taught her that smooth water was not always kind and rough water was not always cruel; that the worst rip on the whole stretch, where the ebb out of the bay met the west swell off the point, looked from a distance like nothing, a slightly different texture, a patch of water that seemed to be simmering — and that you learned to see that simmer from a mile away or you learned about it some other way, once.

"What other way," she said.

"There's a bench at the church with four names on it from one family. Ask me another question."

She asked questions constantly, which pleased him more than he showed. Why does the wind against the tide make the waves stand up? Because the current shortens the wave and the wind keeps feeding it; the wave has to put the energy somewhere, so it goes up. Why is the slack at the point twenty minutes later than the slack at the harbor? Because the tide is not a bathtub filling; it is a wave a hundred miles long, and the crest of it arrives at different doors at different times, like a mailman. Why did the herring used to come and now they don't? That one he took his time with, rowing two full strokes before he answered, because the easy answers were lies and the true answer was long.

"Lots of reasons. We took too many, for too long. The water's warmer than it was. The eelgrass beds where they spawned — half of them got dredged out for the marina expansion in '79, and herring are like anybody, they want to come home to spawn, and if home's gone they don't just pick somewhere else, they mill around and the seals eat them. Everybody did a little of it and nobody did all of it, and now your mother's packing the last of them."

"Mr. Vance at school says they might come back. The herring. If everyone stops fishing them."

"They might. Fish forgive faster than people do." He feathered his oar, looked at the cannery, its rust streaks bleeding down the white siding, the steam standing off the stack into the cold. "But the cannery won't be there to meet them. That's the thing nobody at your school will say out loud. The fish can come back. The buyers, the boats, the people who know how — that's harder. You can't spawn a town."

She was quiet awhile. Then she said, "Are we going to move to Yakima?"

So she had been standing near the same question he had. "I don't know," he said. "That's your mother's water, not mine. I don't read it as well."

In January he started her on charts, evenings at the kitchen table while Marit worked late doing the inventory of a place that was inventorying itself out of existence. He spread chart 18465 under the hanging lamp, soft as cloth at the folds from forty years of his hands, and showed her how the whole language of the morning lessons was written down here in another alphabet — the fathom curves crowding together where the bottom shelved steeply, which meant the swell would jack up there; the tide-rip symbols, those little agitated squiggles, printed exactly where she had felt the boat shudder off the point; the abbreviations for the bottom itself, S and M and Rky, sand and mud and rock, so that you knew before you ever dropped an anchor whether it would hold. He showed her how to walk dividers along a course like a man pacing off a field. He showed her the ranges: how the church steeple lined up over the red roof of the net shed gave you a line that led straight up the channel, safe water the whole way, and how pilots before lighthouses, before charts, had carried hundreds of these in their heads, steeple over roof, big fir over white rock, a whole town pressed into service as instruments.

"So the town is part of it," she said. "The reading."

He looked at her. The two lines were between her eyebrows. "Say that again."

"Like — the water by itself isn't enough. You need the steeple and the net shed. If they tear down the net shed, the range is gone. The town and the water read each other."

He sat back. Dagny — his Dagny, the first one — used to say the girl had an old soul, and he had always considered this the kind of thing women said, and he saw now that he had been wrong, or that old souls were simply people who paid attention early. "Yes," he said. "That's exactly it. Nobody ever said it to me that way in sixty years."

The cannery's last day was the fourth of March. Everyone had known the date for weeks, and the town handled it the way the town handled everything, which was by pretending it was an ordinary day until about noon and then giving up. The final tote of fish went down the line at 11:40 in the morning. Einar was there — half the town was there, standing around in the cold parking lot like it was a fire they'd been evacuated for — and at the end the foreman, Petey Galanos, whose father and grandfather had both been foremen in that building, blew the shift whistle and just held it, held it for a count of twenty, the sound rolling flat across the water and coming back off the hills, and then it stopped and the steam stopped and the building, which had hummed for eighty-four years, eighty-four years of compressors and retorts and conveyors, fell silent, and the silence was a physical thing, like a pressure change, like the slack of a tide. Gulls wheeled over the outfall pipe for an hour out of habit. Marit came out with a cardboard box and her good coat over her arm, and she was not crying, and Einar put his hand on her shoulder and she put her hand on top of his and they stood like that, and Dee stood on her other side, and nobody said well.

That night Marit said, "Carol says the orchard office in Yakima needs a bookkeeper starting in May. She can get me the interview."

"All right," Einar said.

"I haven't said yes."

"I know it."

"I don't know what I'd do with you in Yakima," she said, and her voice did something on the last word, and he understood that she was asking him a question that was too big to be asked straight, the same way you couldn't look straight at certain stars.

"You wouldn't do anything with me in Yakima," he said gently. "There's no water in Yakima. A pilot in Yakima is just an old man who talks funny." He turned his coffee cup a quarter turn on the table. "This house is paid for. I'll be all right. You go where the work is. That's not betrayal, that's seamanship. You don't anchor in a harbor that's gone dry just because your father's fond of the view."

"Dad."

"I mean it. I had the whole life of this place. The good of it. The fish, the fleet, the works. You got the long ebb of it, and that's not your fault, and the girl shouldn't get the mudflat." He heard his own voice stay level and was proud of it the way he'd been proud, climbing pilot ladders in a six-foot chop, of hands that didn't shake until afterward. "But I'll ask one thing. Leave her the rest of the school year. Through June. I'm not done."

Marit looked at him for a long moment, and she had her mother's eyes, and she knew exactly what he meant by done. "Through June," she said.

So the spring lessons had a deadline now, and he taught with a kind of urgency he disguised as patience. Fog: how sound bends and lies in it, how to time an echo, how to throttle down until you could hear the difference between surf on sand and surf on rock — sand hisses, rock booms — and steer by your ears. Weather: the high mare's tails that meant wind in twenty-four hours, the hard line of a front like a lid sliding over the sky, the williwaw gusts that fell down off the bluffs without warning so you never, never came close under them carrying too much sail or too much trust. He told her things he had never told anyone, not because they were secrets but because no one had asked: that the strait had a smell before a southeaster, iodine and something like cut grass; that the water in April carried a green the rest of the year never had, from the plankton bloom, and that the gray whales knew it and came in close on the young flood, and you could set your calendar by them more reliably than by anything the school taught.

In the last week of June, two days before the moving truck, he took her out on the morning ebb and ran them west to the point, past the dead cannery with its windows already going blind, and at the turn of the tide he shipped his oars and traded seats with her, the skiff rocking, his hand on her shoulder for balance and not only for balance.

"Take us home," he said.

"By the channel?"

"By whatever the water gives you. It's blowing southeast fifteen and building, the flood's just started, and there's fog coming up the strait behind us — look. You tell me."

She looked. He watched her look, and it was nothing like November. Her eyes went where his would have gone, in nearly the order his would have gone: the kelp at the point, streaming shoreward, flood confirmed; the seam of the rip off the bar, simmering, and she gave it a wide and respectful berth without being told; the texture of the water under the bluffs, dark cat's-paws falling vertically, and she edged offshore away from the williwaws; and then, coming up the channel with the fog swallowing the point behind them, she found the steeple and laid it over the red roof of the net shed and held the range, glancing back at it every few strokes the way he had taught her, correcting for the set of the current with a touch on the starboard oar she probably did not even know she was making, because her shoulders knew it, because the boat had told her and she had listened.

"The net shed's coming down in the fall," she said, not breaking stroke. "Mr. Galanos told Mom. They're selling the lumber."

"I heard."

"So the range will be gone." Stroke. Stroke. "But the steeple over the notch in the hill — the low place, see it? — that's almost the same line. It's good to about half tide. I checked it on the chart."

He sat in the stern of his own skiff and looked at her, this child rowing him home through the first fingers of the fog, who had found a new range to replace a dying one, who had checked it on the chart, and something moved through him that was too large to be called pride, the way the tide is too large to be called a current. He thought: it will be no use to her. There will be no ships, no fleet, no cannery whistle; she will live in Yakima among the orchards and then somewhere else, and somewhere else after that, the way the young moved now, like weather. And he thought, in nearly the same breath: it is not for use. It never was, entirely. It was a way of being in the world with your eyes open. Wherever she went there would be water of some kind — a river, a crowd, a marriage, a hard year — and it would have its seams and its rips and its slack, its kelp that didn't lie, and she would be a person who knew that the surface was legible, that the world was written in a hand you could learn, and that the learning was a kind of love.

The fog took the breakwater, then the masts of the eleven boats, then the steeple itself, and she brought them in on sound and feel the last hundred yards, the surf hissing on the sand to port, sand not rock, and laid the skiff alongside the float so softly the fenders barely sighed.

"How was that," she said, and her voice tried for casual and missed it by a mile.

"That was a piloted vessel," he said, and made fast the bow line, and his hands did not shake until afterward, in the kitchen, with his coffee, when nobody was watching, and even then it was not grief exactly, or not only. The tide book on the windowsill was open to June. Somewhere out past the fog the flood was still making, water pouring into the bay the way it had before the town and the way it would after, and in two days she would be gone east over the mountains, carrying it all in her shoulders and her eyes, the whole drowned language, one living speaker, young.
