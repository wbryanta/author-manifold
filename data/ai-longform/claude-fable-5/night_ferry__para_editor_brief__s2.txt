The ferry left Aberdeen at seven in the evening, and by half past, the city was already a smear of sodium light along the water, the granite gone grey-gold and then simply grey, and then nothing at all. Colin stood at the stern rail longer than he needed to. Other passengers had drifted inside as soon as the gangway lifted—families, men in work fleeces with company logos, a school party in matching red jackets—but he stayed until the wake stopped pointing back at anything, until it was only a pale rope unravelling into the dark, and then he went down to find his cabin.

It was an inside cabin, no window, two berths though he'd booked for one. He sat on the lower bunk with his bag between his feet and listened to the ship. There was a deep, continuous shudder in everything, in the bedframe, in the small steel sink, in his own sternum, the engines turning somewhere far beneath him, and beneath that the slower lift and settle of the swell. Twelve hours to Lerwick. He had taken the boat instead of the plane because the plane felt obscene, an hour in the air and then standing in a room before he had understood that he was going anywhere. The police officer on the phone, a woman with a careful voice, had said there was no urgency. She had used the word *gentleman*. A gentleman had been recovered from the water near Scalloway on Tuesday morning, she said, and among his effects was a notebook, water-damaged but legible in places, and in the notebook was Colin's name and an address Colin had not lived at for nineteen years. The age was approximately right. The description was approximately right. She said *approximately* twice and Colin had appreciated that, in the way you appreciate a doctor who does not smile too much.

He had not seen Ewan in twenty-two years. He had said this to the officer because it seemed dishonest not to, and she had paused and said that was all right, that families would often—and then she had stopped and started again, and said it would still be a great help if he could come.

He unpacked nothing. He took off his shoes and lay on the bunk in his clothes with the light on and his hands folded on his chest like a man practising, and after twenty minutes of that he put his shoes back on and went up to the bar.

The bar occupied the forward end of the passenger deck, low-ceilinged, carpeted in a pattern designed to hide everything, with windows that showed only the room's own reflection laid over blackness. A television above the till played football with the sound off. The oil men had colonised the tables near the food counter; the school party had been put somewhere else, mercifully; a few solitary travellers were spaced along the window seats with the precise, defended distances of people who do not want to talk. Colin bought a whisky he didn't especially want and took a stool at the bar itself, which was the one place no one else had chosen.

The barman was young, with a Shetland accent so soft it was almost Scandinavian. "First crossing?"

"No," Colin said, and then, because it was true in every way that mattered, "Yes. First in a long time."

"She'll roll a bit after Fair Isle. Nothing serious. Forecast's kind."

Colin nodded and the barman went back to his glasses, and Colin sat with the whisky and looked at his own face in the black window, hung out there over the sea like a lantern.

The last crossing had been with Ewan. Nineteen seventy-nine, the summer Colin was fourteen and Ewan sixteen, and their father had taken them north for the herring, or what was left of the herring, on his cousin's boat out of Whalsay, and they had gone up on the old ferry, the *St Clair*, sleeping in reclining chairs because berths were for tourists. Ewan had been sick for nine hours. Colin had not been sick at all, and it was the only voyage of their lives in which Colin had been the better sailor, the only time the order of them had reversed, and he had been quietly, shamefully delighted, fetching his brother water, holding the paper bag, magnanimous as a prince. Ewan, grey-faced between bouts, had looked at him with flat, knowing eyes. *Enjoy it*, he'd said. *It'll no happen again.* And it hadn't. By the end of that fortnight Ewan could gut fish in a four-foot chop without looking at his hands, and Colin had been left ashore at Symbister with their great-aunt, helping her with the hens, reading, watching the boats come in. They had both understood something that summer, without a word being said, about which of them belonged to the water and which of them belonged to the land, and everything afterwards had only been the working out of it.

He turned the whisky on the bar top without drinking it. A woman two stools down was doing a crossword in a folded newspaper, a woman of about his own age, late fifties, with reading glasses pushed up into grey hair and a fisherman's jumper that had actually seen weather. She caught him looking and he looked away, and a minute later she said, without looking up, "Seven letters. Crosses over. Begins with P."

"Passage," Colin said, before he could decide not to.

She counted it on the squares with the tip of her pen. "Passage," she agreed. "Thank you." And that was all; she went back to the puzzle and he went back to the window, and the small exchange sat in him strangely warm, the way such things did now that he lived alone. Helen would have started a whole conversation. Helen would have known the woman's name and grandchildren by Fair Isle. Three years gone and he still measured every room by what she would have done in it.

It was Helen who had made him keep the postcards. There had been four of them, in the first years after Ewan left—left finally, that is, after their mother's funeral, after the night in the kitchen of the house in Gourdon that Colin had spent twenty-two years not thinking about with such discipline that it had become a kind of thinking, constant, low, like the engines under the bar. The postcards had come from Peterhead, from Lerwick, from Lerwick again, and the last from Hammerfest in Norway, of all places, a picture of midnight sun on an oil terminal. None of them said anything. *Working here the now. E.* That was the longest. No return address ever, which Colin had taken at the time as a door closed and which Helen, years later, sorting a drawer, had held up to the light and read differently. *He's no asking you to write*, she'd said. *He's just letting you ken he's alive.* She had wanted Colin to do something with that—what, neither of them could say; you cannot answer a man who gives no address—and when he did nothing she had put the postcards in the shortbread tin with the photographs, where they still were, where he had gone looking two nights ago after the phone call, kneeling on the floor of the spare room with the tin open, looking at his brother's handwriting, which was their father's handwriting, which was, he had realised with a sensation like missing a stair, his own.

The notebook, the officer had said. Water-damaged but legible in places. He had wanted to ask what else it said, what was on the other pages, and had not asked, because the question felt like reading a man's mail while he stood in the room.

He finished the whisky after all and went out on deck.

The wind up there was clean and enormous, funnelling along the boat deck, thrumming in the rails. The ship carried her own small weather of light—floodlamps on the funnel, the glow of windows—and beyond it the dark was total, sea and sky one substance, except far off to port where a fishing boat showed as a single working light, tilting. He stood in the lee of a lifeboat davit and let the cold do its work. Somewhere below and behind him Scotland was paying itself out like a line; somewhere ahead, still seven or eight hours ahead, was a town he'd seen once at fourteen, and a hospital he'd looked up on his phone, a low building near the water, and a room in that building, and in that room a man who was approximately right.

There was a version of tomorrow in which it was not Ewan. He made himself walk through it. The sheet drawn back, or more likely a photograph first, that was how they did it now, a photograph in a quiet room so that the shock could happen with a wall between you and the fact of the body—and the face would be a stranger's face, some other man of sixty with the sea's general weathering on him, some other family's silence, and Colin would say no, I'm sorry, that's not him, and there would be apologies all round, and he would walk out into the Lerwick morning with his brother restored to him, alive again by elimination, alive somewhere, Hammerfest or Aberdeen or the next street over from Colin's own house for all anyone knew, alive and not writing, alive and unfound, and nothing whatever would have changed except that Colin would know, finally and without anaesthetic, that he had spent twenty-two years waiting for permission to grieve, and that the permission was never coming, and that he had crossed a strait at night to be told so.

And there was the other version.

The wind shifted a point and brought him spray, or rain, he couldn't tell which. A fulmar hung off the rail for a moment, stiff-winged, riding the ship's updraught, and slid away into the dark.

The night in Gourdon. All right. He was sixty years old on a boat deck in the North Sea; he could stand to look at it. Their mother three days buried. The house already half-emptied, because their mother had been a woman who prepared, who had labelled the backs of picture frames with the names of who should have them, and Ewan home for the first time in four years, off a boat at Peterhead with his gear still in a kitbag in the hall, drinking their father's whisky that had stood unopened in the press since their father's own death because their mother would neither drink it nor pour it away. Ewan drinking it like a job of work. And the argument had not even been about anything—that was what Colin had never been able to explain to Helen, or to himself—it had been about the creels. Forty rotten lobster creels in the shed that had been their father's, that Ewan said he'd take, and Colin had said take them where, you've no place to keep a pair of boots, and it had gone from there the way fires go, finding everything dry, finding twenty years of it dry and stacked and waiting. Who had stayed. Who had gone. Who had sat with her through the last of it, the hospital runs, the nights—Colin had said that, had itemised the nights like a tradesman presenting a bill—and who had sent postcards. Ewan standing at the sink with the glass in his hand, getting quieter as Colin got louder, which was their father in him exactly, that lowering quiet, and then Ewan had said the thing about Colin and the land, about Colin being born with both feet in a graveyard, content among the kept things, and Colin had said—

He gripped the rail. The deck rose, paused, settled. They'd be off Fair Isle in a few hours; the barman was right, she was starting to roll.

He had said: *She watched that door for you for twenty years. You did that to her. Whatever it was killed her, you were in it.*

And Ewan had set the glass down on the draining board with great care, as if it were the glass that might break, and had gone up the stair, and in the morning the kitbag was gone from the hall, and the creels stayed in the shed until Colin sold the house, and the man who bought it burned them.

You could not post a letter to that. Helen had never understood, not fully, because Helen came from a family that shouted and wept and was reconciled by teatime, and she could not be made to feel the specific gravity of silence in a family like Colin's, where silence was the element itself, where the men went under into it and swam there all their lives. Ewan had not refused to forgive him. Ewan had done something worse and simpler: he had taken Colin at his word. He had accepted the sentence and served it. Four postcards in twenty-two years was not a sulk. It was a man checking, at long intervals, that the door was still shut—or, Helen's reading, that the house still stood. Both. Neither. You could turn it forever, like a stone in the pocket, and never wear it smooth.

He went down at midnight and lay in the throbbing dark of the cabin, and the swell got into his sleep and made it strange, and at some point he dreamed, not of Ewan, but of the hens at Symbister, the summer he was fourteen, the warm dusty smell of the henhouse and his great-aunt's voice telling him that eggs must be lifted gently or the hens learn fear, and he woke at five to the tannoy testing itself with a soft chime, and could not have said whether the dream had been a kindness or not.

He shaved at the steel sink with the floor tilting, dressed in the better of his two shirts, and went up. The bar was serving breakfast; the windows had turned a deep slate blue, and then, as he drank his tea, a paler blue, and then he could see the horizon, a hard line, and land on it. Sumburgh Head first, far off, then the long low backs of the islands coming up out of the sea one behind another, treeless, sleeked with first light, exactly as he remembered them and not at all, the way a word repeated becomes strange. The crossword woman was at a window seat with her bag already packed at her feet. She nodded to him. "Grand morning for it," she said, and he said it was, and the ship came up the sound between Bressay and the town with the gulls finding her, and Lerwick assembled itself along the water, grey roofs, the gable ends of the old lodberries standing with their feet in the sea, and the lighthouse on the point flashing its last few times against the daylight as if reluctant to stop.

The officer met him at the terminal. She was younger than her phone voice, in plain clothes, with a folder she carried like something that might spill, and she shook his hand and said his name and her own—Sergeant Tait—and asked about the crossing the way people do, and drove him through the waking town at a gentle speed, past the harbour where two big trawlers lay alongside, past a bakery with its lights on, and he watched it all with the particular attention of a man postponing something, and she let him.

At the hospital she took him not to a basement, as he had imagined, but to a small room off a corridor on the ground floor, a room with a window and four chairs and a low table, and a box of tissues placed on the table with such tact that it was the loudest thing in the room. She sat across from him and opened the folder partway.

"What we'd usually do first," she said, "is show you a photograph. Of his face. And I have to be straightforward with you, Mr Strachan—the gentleman was in the water some days. There are changes. It can make things hard. People expect to know at once, and sometimes they do, but often they don't, and that's no failing in anybody. If you're not sure, you say you're not sure. We've other ways and means these days. There's no weight on you this morning beyond what you can carry. All right?"

"All right," Colin said.

"Take whatever time."

She turned the photograph and slid it across the table, and Colin looked at it.

The first thing was that it was no one. That was the truthful first thing: a face that the sea had been at, swollen and smoothed, the particularities sanded down, a face like a face remembered badly. Grey-white hair, longish. A beard, mostly white, which Ewan had never had, but twenty-two years, what did that mean, nothing. The brow could have been their father's brow. The brow could have been a thousand men's. He sat looking at it and felt nothing arrive, no recognition and no certainty of its absence, only a kind of vertigo, the ground of the past refusing to hold, and he understood with a slow horror that he might not know. That estrangement was not only the punishment he'd thought it was. It had been quietly working all these years at this exact task: making his brother unidentifiable to him.

"I don't—" he said, and stopped. Sergeant Tait waited.

"I last saw him at thirty-eight," Colin said. "I'm trying to make this face out of that one and I can't tell if I'm finding him or building him."

"That's honestly said," she said. "And it's common as rain. Is there anything else might help you? Marks, old injuries? Sometimes folk know hands better than faces, especially family."

Colin sat very still.

"His left hand," he said. "The ring finger. It was broken when he was sixteen and it set crooked. At the middle knuckle. It pointed away—outward. He couldn't straighten it."

She made a small note and stood. "Give me a minute."

The minute was eleven by the clock on the wall. He spent it at the window, which looked onto a strip of grass and then the sound, the water bright and ruffled now, a small ferry crossing to Bressay, and he thought about the byre door at Gourdon, the heavy sliding door with the iron runner, and the argument that day—he could not even bring up what it had been about, that was the obscenity of it, two boys shoving at a door from opposite sides for reasons neither would remember within the year—and the runner jumping its track, and the door coming over, and Ewan's hand. Their father splinting the finger himself with a spale of kindling because a doctor cost money and Strachans didn't break, and the finger knitting crooked, and Ewan never once, not once in all the years after, saying that Colin had done it, though Colin had, Colin's shove had jumped the track; Ewan had told their father a sheep had startled him, and had held that line through their father's contempt for his clumsiness, and Colin, fourteen and afraid, had let him hold it. It was the first debt. He had spent a lifetime not looking at the ledger.

The door opened. Sergeant Tait came in and sat down before she spoke, which told him.

"The left hand," she said. "Ring finger. There's an old fracture at the proximal interphalangeal joint, healed with angulation. Crooked at the middle knuckle, pointing outward, just as you said. The doctor says it would have been decades old. Likely never properly set."

The room did something. Settled, or tilted, he couldn't have said which. He heard himself ask if he could see him. Not the photograph.

She didn't try to talk him out of it. There were a few minutes of corridor and waiting, and then a small clean room with a window of obscured glass and a trolley, and an attendant who drew back the cover from the face and folded it with a neatness that was its own kind of speech, and stepped to the wall. Colin stood beside his brother. The face was still nobody's, still the sea's rough draft. But the left hand lay outside the cover, arranged so, and the finger angled away from its fellows exactly as it had angled away across forty-six years, across the breakfast table, around a gutting knife, around the glass set down so carefully on the draining board, and Colin took the cold hand in both of his and held it, the way you lift an egg, gently, so that nothing learns fear, and said, "Aye. That's Ewan. That's my brother."

Sergeant Tait gave him a while before the forms.

Outside, afterwards, the morning had gone on without him and was nearly noon. He walked down to the harbour because there was nowhere he had to be until the boat sailed south that evening, and he stood by the old lodberries where the water came right to the stone, and gulls bickered over something on the slip, and a man on one of the trawlers was coiling rope with the unhurried economy of a lifetime, and Colin watched him for a long time. Then he found a bench in the sun, out of the wind, and took from his jacket the thing Sergeant Tait had given him in a clear sealed bag with the rest to follow: the notebook, swollen and salt-stiffened, its pages clubbed together. Legible in places. He did not open it yet. He sat with it on his knee, his hand resting on it, and looked out at the sound, at the water moving the way it always had and always would, and let the town go about its business around him, a man with both feet on the land, keeping a thing that had been given him to keep.
