The ferry left Aberdeen at seven, and by half past the city was a smear of sodium light off the stern, the breakwater lamps going green, red, green behind us until the swell of the open firth put them under. I stood at the rail longer than I needed to. It was June, so the dark would never properly arrive, only a long grey loosening of the light, twelve hours of it between me and Lerwick, and at the end of those twelve hours a room with a steel table in it.

The flights had been grounded two days running. Fog on Sumburgh Head, the woman at the airline desk said, as if reporting weather on another planet, haar lying on the runway thick as fleece while the rest of the country sweated through a heatwave. I could have waited it out in an airport hotel. I told myself I took the boat because waiting was worse. The truth is I was glad of the fog. Twelve hours is what I felt I owed, though I could not have said to whom.

The constable had phoned on Tuesday morning while I was standing in my kitchen in Stonehaven watching the kettle. Police Scotland, Lerwick. A young voice, careful, the way they must train them to be careful. A man's body had been recovered from the water below the Knab on Sunday. No identification on him. He had been in the sea some days. A man answering the description had not been seen at the small boat harbour since the previous weekend, a man who lived aboard a boat there, and that man's name, the constable said, going slower now, was believed to be Magnus Tulloch, and was I — he had got my number from the council, from old papers to do with a house — was I a relative of a Magnus Tulloch, of Shetland.

I am his brother, I said. The kettle clicked off behind me and the kitchen went very quiet.

The constable said believed to be twice more in the course of the call. He was scrupulous about it. Nothing was certain. The post-mortem would tell them things but it would not tell them a name. A name needed a face that knew it, and the nearest face that knew Magnus Tulloch's, it turned out, after they had asked around the harbour and the bars and come up with shrugs and second-hand stories, was mine, two hundred miles of water away, belonging to a man who had not seen him in eleven years.

I found the bar on deck seven, up a staircase carpeted in a pattern like disturbed water. The boat was maybe half full. Oil men in branded fleeces, a school orchestra going home with their instruments stacked in the corner like a small black city, two lorry drivers at the bar already settled in for the night with the particular patience of men who do this crossing the way other men take a bus. I bought a whisky I didn't much want and took it to a table by the window, where there was nothing to see but our own lights on the water sliding past.

I had taken this boat at eighteen going the other way, the old St Clair, sitting up all night in the reclining lounge with a duffel bag between my feet and my whole chest hurting with how much I wanted to be gone. Magnus came down to the pier to see me off. He was sixteen, in our father's old yellow oilskin jacket with the sleeves turned back, and he didn't wave, just stood with his hands in his pockets watching the boat warp off, and the last I saw of him that time he was turning away up the pier before we'd even cleared the harbour mouth, which I took then for indifference and have since had a long time to reconsider.

He stayed. That was the whole of it, really, the seed everything else grew from. I went and he stayed. Our mother was four years dead by then and our father was a man who spoke perhaps forty words a day, most of them about weather or rope, and Magnus stayed in the house above the voe and went to the fishing the way you'd step from one room into another, no decision visible in it. I sent postcards for a while. Aberdeen, then a survey company, then years of moving where the work moved, the North Sea, a spell in Rotterdam, and the postcards became Christmas cards became a phone call every few months in which our father's silence seemed to have got into the line itself, both of us holding the receiver and listening to the other one not speak.

When the stroke took half our father's body and most of his words, it was Magnus who moved back into his old room and learned to shave another man's face. Five years of that. I sent money, which was accepted without comment, and I came up twice, both times in summer, both times for under a week, and I remember being startled — that is the shameful, exact word — startled at the sight of my brother at forty, thickened, weathered, lifting our father out of a chair with a tenderness that had nowhere else in his life to go. I remember thinking, on the plane south, that I would come back soon and stay longer, and I remember the precise quality of the relief when the seatbelt sign went off, like surfacing.

One of the lorry drivers came over while I was on the second whisky. The bar had thinned out, the orchestra children herded off to their cabins, and he had the look of a man who needed an ear more than a drink. Big, sixty-odd, a Shetland voice worn smooth at the edges by years on mainland roads.

You're never a Tulloch, he said. It wasn't quite a question. You've the look o' them.

I said I was. He sat down without asking, the way men of his kind do, which is not rudeness but a sort of trust.

Whalsay Tullochs or the Vidlin anes?

Vidlin, I said. The house above the voe at Laxo.

Then you'll be Mansie's brother, he said, and something moved in my chest like a fish under ice. The ane that went sooth. He said it without judgement, just placing me, a man putting a tool back on its hook. I did a winter wi' Mansie on the Alison Kay, he said. Whitefish, oot o' Scalloway, this'd be eight, nine year ago. He laughed, remembering something. Best hand wi' a net needle I ever saw. He'd mend a cod-end in the dark wi' his gloves on and be tellin' you some yarn the whole time. And he could make you laugh when there were nothing to laugh at, which on yon boat in January is worth more than wages.

I sat very still and let him talk, and he talked, and a man I did not know assembled in front of me out of a stranger's memories: my brother singing, badly, on purpose, to annoy a skipper; my brother off the drink for two years and then not; my brother living aboard a Shetland model with a stove in her, the Solan, down at the small boat harbour, after — and here the driver's voice did change, dropped a register — after the business wi' the hoose. He looked at me when he said that, not unkindly, and I understood that he knew exactly what the business with the house had been and who had done it, and that he had decided, in the way of the isles, to say no more about it to my face.

I'll tell you, he said, standing, gathering his glass, when you see him, tell him Lowrie Anderson's still waitin' on the twenty pound from the Cup final, he'll mind. And he laughed and went back to his stool, and I sat with my mouth full of the truth and did not say it, could not, watched him settle and order another half pint, and felt the words I should have said go down into me like stones into deep water.

The business with the house. Our father died in March, eleven years ago, and at the funeral tea in the Vidlin hall, with the plates of fancies still going round, Magnus told me he meant to stay on at Laxo, do the place up, maybe get a few sheep on it again, and I, the elder son, the executor, the man with a mortgage in Aberdeenshire and a marriage already coming apart at the seams in ways that needed money, said the house would have to be sold. I said it in a low voice, reasonably, with the will in my jacket pocket like a stone, and Magnus looked at me for a long moment in front of our aunts and our father's crew-mates and then walked out of the hall, and the next time we spoke it was through a solicitor in Lerwick, and the time after that was never.

I told myself for years that it was business, that half of nothing helps nobody, that he could have bought me out and didn't, couldn't, that the drink was in it by then and the money would have run through him anyway. All of which was true and none of which was the truth. The truth was that as long as the house stood with a Tulloch in it I was answerable to it, and to him, and I sold my half of an island to be free of being from it. The new folk put in dormer windows and a decking. I know because I looked at it once on a property site, late at night, the way you'd press a bruise.

He phoned me once after that. Two years ago, near enough; the third of November, the phone tells me, 11:47 at night. I was asleep and missed it, and in the morning there was a voicemail twenty seconds long. Aye, he says. It's Mansie. Then a long pause with the sound of wind in it, an outdoor pause, the sea somewhere in it, halyards going against a mast. Then: I was up by the hoose the day. The new folk hae — and he stops, and there's another breath, and he says, Right. Never mind, and the line goes dead. That is the entire message. Every twenty-eight days the network threatens to delete it and every twenty-eight days I press nine to save it, and I have never once, in two years, pressed the digit that would have called him back. I have constructed many explanations for this and they are all of them lies, and the truth is small and squalid: I did not call because I would have had to begin with sorry, and I could not find a sorry of the right size, and so I waited for one to arrive, and now the steel table.

I gave up on the reclining chair around two and went out on deck. The light was doing what it does in those latitudes in June, the sky a luminous wet grey to the north as if the sun were just behind a sheet hung out to dry, the sea black-violet and long-swelled, and the wake going out behind us pale as a scar. Fulmars hung off the stern on stiff wings, keeping pace without effort, the way they will for hours, and I thought of Magnus at ten on the pier at Vidlin telling me fulmars were the souls of drowned men, which he had from our grandmother, and that you must never strike one, and me at twelve telling him that was rubbish, and the way he shrugged, untroubled, owning a world I had already started pricing.

I should set down the scar now, because everything afterwards turns on it. I was fourteen and he was twelve and we were gutting sillocks on the pier on a flat blue evening, and I was showing off with the knife — there had been a girl that summer, Marie Williamson, sitting on a bollard watching — flipping it and catching it, and Magnus said something, I don't even remember what, something true probably, about me and Marie, and I flicked the blade at him meaning only to make him flinch, and he put his arm up and the knife went along the inside of his left forearm like a match being struck. Twelve stitches at the Gilbert Bain. He told the doctor, told our father, told everyone, that he'd slipped climbing the pier ladder with the knife in his hand. He never once told on me, not even when our father, who suspected, took me out to the byre and stood looking at me in silence for a length of time I still measure other silences against. The scar healed white and slightly proud, three inches, with the stitch-dots either side like the bones off a fish's spine, and when he was a young man and the boats made him broad, it sat there on his forearm among the rope burns and the anchor he had done in biro and gone over in Aberdeen with real ink, my mark, the first wound I gave him, the only one that ever closed.

I must have slept an hour or two in the chair after that, because I woke to the boat's note changing, the engines easing, and through the salt-greased window there was land: the long whale-back of Bressay off the starboard side with the fog lying on its crown like a pelt, and then the town opening up, the way Lerwick does, suddenly, grey roofs stepped up from the grey water, gulls coming off the fish sheds to meet us, and everything smaller than memory and more itself. The fog the planes couldn't land in was only on the high ground. At sea level the morning was clear and cold-bright and indecently lovely, and I stood at the rail in it while we came alongside and felt nothing I have a word for.

The constable met me at the terminal. Younger even than his voice, fair, with the raw shaved cheeks of a boy, and he shook my hand and said he was sorry for the circumstances and called me Mr Tulloch, and in the car — three minutes, you could walk it, but they will always drive you — he explained what would happen in sentences he had clearly said before, though not, I thought, many times. The viewing room at the Gilbert Bain. A window, or I could be in the room itself, my choice. Take all the time I needed. I should be prepared, he said, slowing, choosing, for the fact that the sea — and he left it there, decently, and I said I understood, and we both looked out at the morning traffic on the Esplanade, a delivery van, a woman with a pushchair, the world going on with its groceries.

The hospital smelled of every hospital. A woman in lilac scrubs took us down a corridor with handrails and through a door that said no more than it had to, into a small room with two chairs and a box of tissues and a watercolour of Mousa Broch on the wall, and through an inner door was the other room, white, cool, with the table in it, and on the table a form under a sheet of such a particular blue that I will never see that blue again without my stomach dropping.

I'll be in the room, I said. Not the window.

The woman in lilac folded the sheet back from the face with two hands, the way you'd turn down a bed, and stepped aside.

The constable had been right to leave his sentence unfinished, because there is no finishing it. The sea is not gentle with what it keeps. I will not set down here what nine days of water does to a face, except to say that what was on the table was both unmistakably a man and almost not a particular one, the features blurred and swollen as if seen through bottle glass, the hair — and here something in me lurched, because the hair was right, grey-streaked rust, our mother's colour, still somehow combed by the water into the lie of it I knew. But hair is not a name. The constable was watching me with his careful young face and I heard myself say, in a voice that came out level, I can't be sure. From the face. I'm sorry. I can't be sure.

Take your time, the woman said. There's no clock in here, she said, which was true, and kind, and the kind of thing you only learn to say by doing this many times.

His arm, I said. The left arm. The inside of the forearm.

She looked at the constable and the constable nodded, and she went round the table and drew the blue sheet back along the left side, carefully, only so far, and lifted the arm a little, turning it.

The skin there had not fared as the face had. And there it was. Three inches, white, slightly proud of the skin even now, even after everything, with the stitch-dots either side like the bones off a fish's spine. The mark I made on the pier at Vidlin forty-five years ago, the wound he carried for me and lied for me about, riding above the wreck of him like the one thing the sea had agreed to leave alone.

Aye, I said. That's him. That's Magnus. That's my brother.

I had thought, on the boat, in the long grey hours, about what I might feel at this moment, had auditioned griefs the way you do, shamefully, in advance. What arrived was not on the list. What arrived was the pier, the blue evening, Marie Williamson on the bollard, the knife turning in the air with the light on it, and my brother's face in the half-second before the blade touched him — not afraid, that was the thing, not even then, just surprised that I would, and then deciding, in the time it took the blood to come, that he would cover for me. He was twelve. He had already worked out what I was and decided to love me anyway, and he kept that decision for thirty-four years and then sat with it on a boat with a stove in her, and one night near the dark of the year he stood in the wind and rang my number and got my voice telling him to leave a message.

Could I have a minute, I said.

They went out, both of them, and the door sighed shut, and I stood in the white room with my brother. I did not say anything. There was nothing in the room to say it to; I understood that finally and completely, standing there, that the time for saying had been the third of November two years ago at 11:47 at night, and every day since, and that this — the table, the blue sheet, the cold coming up off him faintly like the cold off a winter window — this was only the sentence with the full stop on it. I put my hand on his hair, because it was the only part of him the sea had returned intact, and it felt like his hair. That is all. It felt like my brother's hair, and I am sixty-one years old and I stood in a room in Lerwick with my hand on it for a long time.

Afterwards there were forms, and tea I didn't drink, and the constable, gentle as a nurse, brought through a clear plastic bag of effects and asked me to confirm them, sign for them. A tobacco tin, the lid worn to bare metal at the corners. Two keys on a loop of tarred twine — the boat, he said, the Solan, she's still at her mooring, you'll want to see about her. Four pound coins and some smaller. A folding knife with a fid. And a square of paper, folded and refolded so many times it had gone soft as cloth, furred along the creases, and when the constable smoothed it open with one finger I saw that it was a page off a notepad from a marine chandler's in Scalloway, and on it, in my brother's careful, terrible, fishing-boat handwriting, was an address in Stonehaven and a mobile number. My address. My number. The address was my current one. I have lived there four years. He had kept track of me. He had carried me in his jacket pocket, soft from the carrying, through I don't know how many seasons, the way you carry a thing you mean to use and keep not using, and one night he had taken it out in the wind and dialled, and I had been asleep.

Is that significant to you, the constable asked, because I had been quiet too long.

It's my address, I said. He had my address.

The boy nodded and wrote something down, and I signed where he showed me, and outside the morning was still going on, gulls, the smell of the sea and diesel and somebody's bakery, the fog burning off Bressay in slow white rags. I told the constable I'd see about the funeral, that I'd take him home, that there was a kirkyard above the voe at Laxo where our folk were, and I heard myself say it as if I had always intended it, as if I were a man who did such things. Then I walked down past the lodberries to the small boat harbour and found her without having to ask, a Shetland model with green topsides and a stovepipe through her foredeck, Solan in white on her bow, riding to her mooring as neat as a duck, every line of her coiled and cheesed down, because whatever else had come apart in my brother's life, he could not leave a rope untidy, it was not in him.

I sat down on the harbour wall beside her and took out my phone. There was the usual freight of a day's messages. And there, near the top, the prompt that comes every twenty-eight days, that had come, I saw, while I was on the boat in the night, somewhere out on the black water between his life and this: one saved message. Listen, save, or delete.

I pressed the key and held the phone to my ear, and the wind of two Novembers ago blew through it, and the halyards rang against their masts, and my brother said, Aye. It's Mansie, and breathed, and didn't manage it, and said, Right. Never mind.

To save this message, the voice said, press nine.

I pressed nine. I will press it every twenty-eight days for the rest of my life, and it will never once be enough, and I will do it anyway, because it is the only number of his I can still call, and because somewhere in a pocket gone soft with folding, he kept mine.
