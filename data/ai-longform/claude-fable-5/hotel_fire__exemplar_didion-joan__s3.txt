The invitation was engraved, and the engraving was correct, and at the bottom, in Patsy Forrest's looping convent hand, was written *You of all people have to come, Howard would want you there*, which was one of those things people had taken to saying to me since Howard died and which had, like most of them, no meaning that I could determine. I do not know what Howard would have wanted. I had stopped pretending to know what Howard would have wanted around the time I stopped pretending that I knew why I did the things I did, and so when the invitation came I looked at the name of the hotel for a while and then I wrote on the little card that I would be delighted, and I sealed the envelope, and I sat in the kitchen in Pasadena until it got dark.

The hotel was the Del Rey, at Punta Arena, three miles south of the town on the coast highway, and Cecily Forrest was to be married there in June because her grandfather had owned it and her mother had been married there and the family regarded the Del Rey, Patsy told everyone, as practically an ancestral home. "We *are* the Del Rey," Patsy liked to say, "in a funny way." The Forrests had sold the hotel to a Honolulu syndicate in 1968 but the syndicate let them have the lawn and the main dining room for the wedding at what Patsy called a family rate, and Patsy had a scrapbook, which she brought to lunch in town six weeks before the wedding, of clippings about the hotel: the opening in 1929, the Christmas dances, the tennis weeks, her own wedding in 1951, Cecily in a white dress on the seawall, photographed for the announcement. "Three generations," Patsy said, turning the pages with one finger as if they were leaves of a Bible. "Can you stand it."

I said that I could stand it.

The Del Rey burned on the night of September the second, 1954. Eleven people died, nine of them on the fourth floor, which was where the women on the housekeeping staff slept, under the mansard roof, two to a room, behind a door that was locked at night from the corridor side. I have seen it written that seven died, and I have seen it written that the fire began with a cigarette, and I have heard it said, twice, at dinner parties, by people who had no reason to know, that everyone got out. Eleven died. I was nineteen years old that summer and I was a chambermaid at the Del Rey, and I got out because I was sitting on the iron landing of the outside stair at a quarter past two in the morning, smoking a Chesterfield, which was against the rules, when the windows of the linen room went bright behind me. I never told Howard. In nineteen years of marriage it did not come up, which will tell you something about the kind of marriage it was, or the kind of wife I was, or possibly only the kind of thing it was. Howard believed that I had never been north of Santa Barbara before 1964. I let him believe it. It was not a lie so much as a tidiness.

I drove up alone on the Friday. The fog had come in early and lay along the artichoke fields, and the eucalyptus windbreaks went by in the fog like things remembered rather than seen, and at the place where the highway curved down toward the point I could see the hotel, white and long against the gray water, and the flag on the roof, and I noticed that my hands on the wheel were entirely steady, which interested me in a clinical way. The hotel they built in 1956 is not the hotel that burned. They will tell you it is. The brochure in the room said *faithfully restored*, and the syndicate's manager said at the rehearsal dinner that the Del Rey was "the same grand lady she always was," and got his laugh, but the new building is concrete block faced with stucco, and the roof is tile rather than shingle, and there is no fourth floor at all. Where the staff wing stood there is now a rose garden, with a sundial, and a plaque on the sundial that says it was given by the Punta Arena Garden Club, and does not say in memory of what.

"You made it," Patsy said, in the lobby, kissing the air beside my face. "You look marvelous, you look rested, doesn't she look rested, Deedee, this is Nora Lessard, Howard Lessard's — Nora, you know what I mean." She took my arm and walked me toward the desk and lowered her voice to the register she used for things that mattered. "It's the fog I can't stand. They swear it burns off by eleven but I've been watching it all day and it sits right there on the water like a *judgment*. If it doesn't lift we move the whole thing into the Cypress Room and the Cypress Room only seats one-eighty and we are two-twenty without the Honolulu people."

"The bride is generally considered attraction enough," I said.

"You sound exactly like my mother." Patsy laughed and let go of my arm. "Cocktails at six. Don't dress, it's only family tonight, family and whoever Ward Junior's brought, he's brought some person from Palm Beach. Six o'clock."

The girl at the desk gave me a key to a room on the second floor, ocean side, and a boy in a green jacket carried my bag, and in the elevator he asked if I had stayed at the Del Rey before. He was perhaps twenty, with the sunburned look they all had, and he asked it the way they are trained to ask it, without wanting an answer, and I gave him the answer he wanted, which was no. In 1954 there was no elevator that the staff could use. We used the back stair, which was concrete to the third floor and wood from the third floor up, and at night the wooden flight held the day's heat and smelled of resin, and you could put your hand flat on the wall going up and feel the warmth come off it like something breathing. I tipped the boy a dollar. The room had a balcony and a basket of fruit with a card from Patsy and a view of the seawall, and if you leaned on the rail and looked north you could see the rose garden, and I leaned on the rail and looked north for a while, and then I drew the curtain and slept for two hours, without dreaming, which also interested me.

At dinner that night I was put between Ward Forrest Junior, the bride's uncle, and an old man whose name I did not get when it was said, and the talk was about the hotel. The talk all weekend was about the hotel. The Forrests talked about the Del Rey the way certain families talk about a ranch that has been sold, with a grief that improved their idea of themselves, and the syndicate's wine was poured, and the fog sat on the water, and Ward Junior told the table about the time Bing Crosby had been turned away from the dining room for wearing espadrilles. "That was the old place, of course," Ward Junior said. "Before the fire. The fire was the end of an era, in a way."

"What fire?" the person from Palm Beach said.

"The old hotel burned. Fifty-four, fifty-five. Burned to the sills. Dad rebuilt it bigger and better, but it broke his heart, I think, the old place. Hand-cut shingles. You couldn't get that work done today."

"Was anyone hurt?" the person from Palm Beach said.

"There were some deaths," Ward Junior said. "Staff, mostly. It started up in the staff quarters, you see, that was the tragedy of it, careless smoking up there, and those poor girls — well." He turned his glass by the stem. "Mostly staff, thank God. I mean thank God it wasn't worse. It could have been so much worse."

I have never been able to hear the phrase "the staff, mostly" evenly. There are certain phrases I cannot hear evenly, and most of them are phrases that have the word "mostly" in them somewhere, visible or not, and when I hear them something happens behind my teeth, and what I do at such moments is look at my hands and count something. That night I counted the names. Donna Jean Pruitt. Alma Reyes. Carolina Reyes, who was Alma's cousin and had been at the Del Rey eleven days. Patricia Mott. Yvonne Kessler. June Schmidt. A girl called Babe whose Christian name I never learned and which did not appear correctly in any newspaper. Opal Trask, the second-floor supervisor, who was fifty-one. Erlinda Vasquez. Wesley Coombs, the night dishwasher, who went up the back stair with a wet apron over his face and did not come down. And Mrs. Drummond, a guest, in 318, who would not leave without her dog. Eleven. I counted them in order while Ward Junior talked about shingles, and when I had finished I asked the old man on my other side whether he had come far, and he said from Hillsborough, and we discussed the artichoke crop until the plates were taken away.

It was at the reception itself, the next afternoon, that the man came over to me on the lawn. The fog had burned off by noon, just as the syndicate had sworn it would, and the lawn was very green and very level, running to the seawall, and the bridesmaids were in apricot, and a band under a striped canopy played the kind of music that bands play at weddings on lawns, and I was standing alone with a glass of champagne I did not want when I heard a man say my name. He said it carefully, with a small pause in the middle, the way you say a name you have been rehearsing.

"It is Mrs. Lessard, isn't it."

He was about seventy, very thin, in a blazer with a club button, and his face meant nothing to me for a moment and then it meant everything, because faces do that, they come to you not as faces but as situations, and the situation this face came to me as was a small office off the kitchen corridor with a frosted-glass door, and a green metal desk, and a pay envelope on the desk, and a typed page, and a fountain pen lying on the typed page at a helpful angle.

"It was," I said.

"Arthur Soames." He put out his hand and I shook it because it was there. "I managed the property for Ward Senior, oh, donkey's years. I doubt you remember."

"I remember," I said.

"I rather thought you might." He looked out at the water as if the water had been mentioned. "You were a Quirk then. Nora Quirk. Marysville."

"Yreka," I said. "Marysville was where the bus went."

"Yreka, of course." He smiled the smile of a man conceding a small score in a game he believed he was winning. "I have a good memory for the old days, it's the new days I lose. You've done well. I read about Mr. Lessard, the firm, all of it. I was glad. I want you to know I was glad, I used to wonder what became of you."

"Did you," I said.

"You were a sensible girl. I always said so. There was a great deal of hysteria afterward, you'll recall, girls saying all manner of things, and you were sensible." Down the lawn the photographer was arranging Cecily and her grandfather against the seawall, the old man's hand trembling on his cane, the veil taking the wind. "Terrible time. Terrible. I don't suppose a week goes by I don't think of it."

"The alarm never rang," I said.

He went on looking at the water. The band finished the song it was playing and began another one very like it.

"There was an inquest, Mrs. Lessard," he said. "The inquest heard all the testimony and the inquest made its finding. The system was tested afterward and found in working order. It's all in the record."

"The system was electric and the fire was in the wiring. I stood on that lawn, right about where the cake table is, for two hours, and it never rang. The corridor door on four was locked and the key was on Mrs. Eustace's ring and Mrs. Eustace slept in the cottage. None of that is in the record. What's in the record is a cigarette."

"You testified yourself, as I recall, that you'd been smoking on the landing." He said it gently, almost kindly, the way you remind someone of a debt. "That very night. Against the rules."

I looked at him for a moment. The interesting thing, I came to think later, was that there was no malice in him at all, that there had probably never been any malice in him, that malice was not required. He had been a man with a green metal desk and a sheaf of releases and a list of next of kin, most of whom did not read English, and he had done the work in front of him, and the work in front of him had been to make eleven deaths cost five hundred dollars apiece, and he had done it well, and he had spent twenty-two years thinking of it not a week going by, and he wanted me to absolve him, or to incriminate myself, and I am not sure he could have told the two wishes apart.

"Five hundred dollars," I said. "And the ticket to Marysville."

"You signed of your own free will."

"I was nineteen years old and my roommate was in the funeral home in Salinas and you had the envelope on the desk before I sat down."

"Donna Jean," he said. "Pruitt." He said the name the way he had said mine, carefully, with the pause, and for a moment the rehearsed quality of it sickened me more than anything else had, more than the rose garden, more than the sundial, because I understood that he had a list too, that he had been counting his own names for twenty-two years, and that his list and my list were the same list and were not the same list at all. "She was underage, you know. We found that out after. Seventeen. She'd given a false date."

"She gave a false date because her father drank up everything that came into that house and the Del Rey paid thirty dollars a week with room and board," I said. "She kept her money in a sock in the bureau, the second drawer, under the slips. Forty-one dollars. Nobody ever sent it to anyone, did they."

He did not answer that. The photographer called for the bride's side, and people began moving past us toward the seawall, laughing, holding their glasses high and away from each other, and Mr. Soames touched his club button with two fingers as if confirming it was still sewn on.

"It does no good, Mrs. Lessard," he said finally. "Whatever you think you'd be doing, it does no good. Ward Senior is eighty-six. The girl getting her picture made over there had nothing to do with any of it. The company that owned the negligence, if you want to call it that, was dissolved in 1957. There is no one left to pay. There's only people left to hurt."

"I haven't said a word in twenty-two years," I said. "You came over to me."

He looked at me then, and whatever he had rehearsed, that was not in it, and he made a small motion with his head, not quite a nod, and walked away across the grass toward the photographer, and I noticed he had the old hotel walk still, the walk of a man trained never to seem to hurry in front of guests.

I want to be accurate about the rest of the afternoon, because the rest of the afternoon was in most respects a pleasant afternoon. I ate the lunch. I talked with the old man from Hillsborough about artichokes again, and with a young Honolulu wife about the difficulty of getting plumeria to set bud on the mainland, and when the toasts came I raised my glass with everyone else. Ward Senior was helped up and said that this hotel had been good to his family for fifty years, in good times and bad, and that seeing his granddaughter married on this lawn closed a circle for him, and there was a sound from the tables that was partly applause and partly something tenderer, and Patsy cried, and the fog stayed off the water, and the cake was cut with a sword that a friend of the groom's had carried in the Navy. At my table a bridesmaid was telling the younger ones that the hotel was haunted, that everybody at Punta Arena knew it, that a chambermaid in an old-fashioned uniform walked at night where the rose garden was, and one of the girls shivered pleasurably and said she hoped she'd see her, and I sat there with my coffee and thought that the dead are made into ghost stories by people who would not have let them in the front door, and that this is called keeping their memory alive.

When the dancing started I walked up across the lawn alone. No one stops a middle-aged woman walking alone at a wedding; it is one of the freedoms of the condition. I went past the cake table and past the canopy and along the gravel path to the rose garden, and the music came up the lawn behind me thinned by the air, and I stood at the sundial. The roses were Peace and Tropicana, well kept, deadheaded that week. The staff wing had stood, as nearly as I could fix it, with its outside stair where the bed of Tropicana now was, and its windows facing not the ocean, which was for guests, but the kitchen yard, and on the iron landing of that stair, at a quarter past two in the morning, twenty-two years before, I had been sitting in my nightgown with a cardigan over it, smoking, because the wooden floors held the heat and I could not sleep, and the linen room windows below me had gone bright all at once, with no sound at all, that is the thing no one will believe, with no sound at all, like a lamp switched on, and I had gone down the iron stair and then I had tried to go back up, and the wooden flight above the third floor was already not a stair, and I stood in the kitchen yard and threw gravel at our window, and threw gravel, and the window stayed dark, and then it was not dark, and a man I never identified took my arm and walked me to the lawn, and someone put a tablecloth around me, and the alarm did not ring, that whole night the alarm did not ring, and at dawn the fog came in over what was left and the fog horn off the point sounded every thirty seconds the way it always had, and that was the only bell anyone got.

I picked one rose, a Tropicana, which was against the rules, and I put it in the lapel of my suit, and I walked back down to the wedding.

Cecily found me at the edge of the dance floor an hour later, flushed, her veil off, her hem grass-stained, lovely in the way that brides are lovely, which has nothing to do with the face. "Aunt Nora," she said, which I was not. "You came all this way. Mummy says you knew this place even before she did, is that true?"

I looked at her. Down the lawn Arthur Soames was sitting alone at an emptied table with his hands folded, watching the dancing, and the band was playing something slow, and the light was going long and gold across the water the way it does on that coast in June, in the hour before the fog decides.

"Once," I said. "Before you were born."

"Isn't it heaven," she said. "Daddy says they don't build them like this anymore."

"No," I said. "They don't."

She was pulled away to dance, and I stood a while longer, and then I went into the lobby to leave before the going-away, because I have never seen the point of watching people leave. By the door, on a stand, the guest book lay open, white leather, a ribbon marker, a pen on a chain, and the page was full of the afternoon's names, the Hillsborough names and the Honolulu names, with their towns written after them. I took the pen. I wrote *Nora Quirk*, and after it, because the line asked for one, *fourth floor*, and I capped the pen, and laid it in the gutter of the book, and walked out to my car through the smell of eucalyptus and salt, and drove south with the windows down until the fog took the road behind me.
