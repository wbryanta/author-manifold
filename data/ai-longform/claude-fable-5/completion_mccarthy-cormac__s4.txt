He sat for a long time in the dark with his heart going and the sweat cooling on him. Beside him she lay propped on one elbow, a paler shape in the paleness of the quilts, watching him with what he could not see was watchfulness but knew anyway.

You was hollerin somethin awful, she said.

I know it.

What was it you was dreamin?

Nothin. Go on back to sleep.

It must of been somethin.

It wasn't nothin. He lay back down with his face to the wall where the chinking had fallen from between the logs and through which he could see one star burning in the slot of mud and night like an eye set there to watch him. He could hear her breathing and beneath that or within it he fancied he could hear the other breathing too, the thing she carried, the two of them respiring in the dark like some conspiracy of lungs. He closed his eyes. After a while she said his name but he did not answer and after a while longer she lay back down and he listened to her settle, the cornshuck ticking rasping under her, her breath going long and even at last. He did not sleep. He lay watching the star until it paled and went and the chinks in the wall went from black to gray and a rooster called somewhere far off across the river bottom like a thing crying out of another world altogether, and then he rose.

He built up the fire from the banked coals and set water on and stood at the one window looking out at the morning coming up out of the trees. Mist lay along the creek in the low ground and the trees stood up out of it black and dripping and a crow crossed against the colorless sky going somewhere with slow strokes of its wings as if it bore news. The cabin sat in a clearing that was no clearing but only a place where the woods had been beaten back a little and were waiting. There was a shed of poles and rived boards and a small lot grown with dock and jimson and beyond that the woods again, dark even at noon, and through them the path that went down to the spring. He had been here three months. Before that other places. He did not think much on the places before that.

She came up behind him with the quilt about her shoulders and her hair down and her belly going before her huge and taut under the thin shift so that he looked away.

It's right pretty of a mornin, she said.

He didn't say anything. He took up the bucket from beside the door.

You want I should fix ye somethin?

They ain't nothin to fix, he said. Less you can fix meal and water two ways.

I could fry it.

Fry it then. He went out and down the path through the wet weeds to the spring and knelt and dipped the bucket and watched the spider trout flare away over the sand like shadows fleeing some judgment, and he raised the bucket and drank from the lip of it, the water so cold it ached in his teeth, and he looked at his face wobbling in the dark water left in the bucket's bottom and then he poured it out and filled it again and went back up the path with the birds beginning in the trees all about him like small reproaches.

They ate the fried mush in silence at the table he had cobbled from shipping boards, she eating with a great and shameless hunger that he could not watch, eating for the two of them, and he chewed and looked at the door. When he was done he rose and took the axe from where it stood against the wall.

You goin somewheres?

Cut wood.

We got wood.

We ain't got wood for what's comin. Cold weather.

It's June, she said.

He stood with the axe. She had turned in the chair to look at him and one hand had gone to the great curve of her belly the way it did, unthinking, like a thing tending itself, and he looked at the hand there and then at the door.

You reckon it'll come soon? she said.

It'll come when it comes.

Granny Lambert said she'd come up if I was to send for her. Said she'd not even ast nothin for it, not from kin nor stranger neither. Said all I had to do was send.

We don't need her.

She's the granny-woman. She's borned half this county.

We don't need her, he said. We don't need nobody up here.

She looked at him for a long time with her pale eyes that were like his own eyes, that were his own eyes given back to him, and that was the thing he could not abide, and he turned and went out the door and pulled it to behind him.

He cut wood through the morning, working in the dappled shade of the blackoaks above the creek, the axe ringing and the chips spalling off white and clean out of the gray dead wood, and he worked until his shirt was wet through and his hands burned and then he sat on a down log and looked at nothing. A jay screamed at him from a hickory and screamed again and went off through the woods still screaming. He thought he might just walk. Take the axe over his shoulder and walk down the creek to the river and down the river to wherever the river went and never look back at the cabin or the clearing or her in it. Men did such. He'd known of them, men gone off and never heard from, and nobody mourned them long. But he knew he would not, not while it was still in her, because as long as it was in her it was hid, and once it was out it was in the world, and a thing in the world wants tending or it wants hiding, one, and either way somebody had to be there for the doing of it. He spat and took up the axe again.

In the afternoon he heard the bell. A small cracked chiming coming up the road through the trees, and then the squeal of an ungreased hub, and he came around the corner of the cabin and stood in the yard and watched the tinker come up out of the woods with his cart. A small man humped under years of his own walking, drawing the cart by its shafts like a beast indentured to it, the cart hung all over with pans and dippers and trinkets and bolts of dusty cloth and stopped bottles of cures, all of it swaying and chiming faintly, and over the top of the load a parasol of stained orange paper such as no one in that country had ever bought or would. The tinker saw him and stopped and let down the shafts and straightened himself by sections and looked up at the cabin with his small eyes going over everything there was to go over, which wasn't much.

Howdy, he said.

Howdy.

The tinker took off his hat and ran his hand through the gray scruff of his hair and put the hat back. Hot, ain't it, he said.

It's summer.

It is that. The tinker came a few steps closer. I got needles, he said. Thread. Pins. Salts and liniments. I got a tonic here that's knowed to cure the rheumatism, the neuralgy, the complaints of women...

We don't need nothin.

The tinker stopped. He looked at the cabin again, and at the window of it, and the man turned and looked too and she was standing in the window looking out at them with her hands at her sides and her belly plain against the light inside, and he turned back and the tinker was watching him.

I see they's a young'un comin, the tinker said. I got cloth here, soft cloth, good for swaddlin. Got a little dutch oven a woman could warm milk in. I'd let it go cheap.

We don't need nothin, he said again. We got no money noway.

The tinker nodded slowly the way a man nods who is counting something other than what is being said. Well, he said. I come back through here ever month or two. Up this road and down the other'n. He smiled a smile with not much in it. Was they a child here, he said, and was it not wanted, they's folks would pay for a child. Childless folks. I've knowed it done. I could carry word.

The man took one step toward him. Git, he said.

I never meant nothin by it.

You git on down that road and don't come up it again.

The tinker backed to his cart and took up the shafts. A man hadn't ought to be so proud, he said. Proud and poor is a hard team to drive. He hauled the cart around in the ruts and went off down the road with the bell chinking and the hub crying and the orange parasol nodding above the load until the woods took him, and for a long time after they could still hear him, fainter and fainter, going away into the country below like the sound of something that would not altogether go.

That night she asked him who it had been and he said it was nobody, a peddler, and she said she'd have admired to see the things in the cart and he said there wasn't nothing in the cart but trash and dear at any price, and they didn't speak again. He lay awake a long time. The star was back in the chink. Somewhere a dog bayed and was answered and the two of them bayed together for a while at whatever it was and then quit.

In a gray dawn forty miles to the east the three came down out of a wood and stood looking at a farmhouse in the bottom where smoke was just beginning at the chimney. They stood in the fog with the dew on their shoulders like a kind of mail and they did not speak. After a while the bearded one started down through the dead corn and the other two came behind him in his very steps like figures in a procession the meaning of which was lost and only the form remembered. A hound came off the porch at them and stood the bearded one and then did not stand him, going back under the porch with its hackles still up and a low sound in its throat that it could not seem to stop making. The man who came to the door was old and he held the door with one hand. They wanted bread, they said, or one of them said. They were paid laborers going to a job of work down the river. The old man looked at them a long time and at the country behind them out of which they had come and there was nothing in that country to vouch for them or to vouch for anything, and perhaps he knew it, but he was old and had been raised in an older way and he stood back from the door and bid them in. By full light they were on the road again, going west, fed, one of them wearing boots he had not worn before, and behind them the smoke from the chimney had stopped and after a while started again much blacker, standing on the still air of the bottom like a mark made there for someone to read, though there was no one in all that country to read it, or none that would say what it said.

Her time came two nights after, in the dark before the moon rose. She woke him with a sound he had never heard her make and he came up out of sleep with his fists balled and she was sitting on the side of the bed bent over herself.

It's a-comin, she said.

He lit the lamp. In the light her face was wet and strange, gone away from him already into the country where she was going, where he could not follow and would not if he could.

You got to go fetch Granny Lambert, she said.

No.

It hurts. It hurts somethin awful. She ain't but five mile. You could be back fore day.

I ain't fetchin nobody, he said. You think I'd have that old woman up here? You think I'd have her lookin and seein and goin back down tellin?

Tellin what? she cried, and then she bent again with the pain and held to the bedframe and made the sound again, and he stood holding the lamp and watching her, and the shadows of the two of them stood on the wall behind, huge and bowed, like the shadows of older and greater sufferers come to attend them.

Women has babies, she said when she could talk. Hit's the most natural thing they is. They ain't nothin to tell.

You know what they is to tell.

She looked up at him then with her hair stuck to her face and her eyes burning and she said: It's your'n too.

Hush.

It is. It's your'n too and you know it and God knows it.

You hush, he said. Don't you call on God. Don't you never call on God in this house.

The pains came all night. He kept the fire and kept water hot at it for no reason but that he had heard that water was wanted at such times, and he sat by the hearth with his back to her and listened to her labor in the bed, her breathing and her crying out and her praying, for she did pray, she prayed all night in a small voice between the pains, not to be spared, but for the child, that it be whole, that it live, and he sat and stared into the coals and his own lips moved once or twice but whatever they shaped it was not prayer or it was prayer to no god that has a name. Toward the gray end of the night her crying changed and he knew without knowing how he knew that the time was on her, and he made himself rise and go to her, and what he did then he did with his jaw set and his eyes half shut like a man handling fire, and the child came into his hands in a rush of heat and blood, very small, very slick, trailing its cord, and it hung there in his two hands in the lamplight and twisted and opened its mouth and cried.

It was a chap, a boy. He cut the cord with the beltknife and tied it with thread she had saved from the hem of a dress and he wrapped the child in a piece of the quilt and all this while she was saying let me see him, let me hold him, in a voice gone down to nearly nothing, and he laid the bundle in the crook of her arm and she looked at it and a look came on her face that he had to turn away from, that he could no more watch than a man can watch the sun.

He's awful little, she said.

He'll not live, the man said.

What?

Look at him. Little as he is. They don't never live, ones little as that.

He's a-breathin. Look at him breathe. He's breathin good.

The man didn't look. He went and sat by the fire again. Behind him he could hear her talking to it, low foolish loving talk, the way women talk to such things, and the small mewling of it, and outside the first birds were beginning and the window was going gray. He sat there a long time. He was tired in a way he had never been tired, as if the night had taken something out of him by hand. Toward midmorning she slept, worn down into sleep with the child in the bed beside her, and he rose and stood over them. The child slept with its fists beside its head and its face crumpled and ancient, like a thing that had already been a long time in the world and was only now passing back through. He stood looking at it. Then he took it up.

It did not wake or it half woke and made a small sound and quieted against his chest, and that was the worst of it, that it quieted, that it took him for what it took him for, and he went out the door with it into the heat of the day and across the clearing and into the woods.

He walked a long way. He had not planned a way to walk but his feet seemed to know one, down along the creek and through the laurel where the light came green and broken, and the child rode quiet in the crook of his arm and once he caught himself shielding its face from a branch and he cursed and went on. He crossed the creek on the stones and went up the far slope through old growth where the ground was bare and brown and the trees went up like columns into a darkness overhead, and he came out in a glade he had not known was there, a small bald of grass and moss with the woods standing all about it and a light in it that came from no particular place. He stood at the edge of it breathing. He told himself things. He told himself it would die anyway. He told himself it was no person yet, that small, that new, that a thing must be in the world some time before it is of the world. He told himself God would not count it. And all the while his feet were carrying him to the middle of the glade, and he knelt there and laid the bundle down in the moss and folded the quilting back from its face so that it could breathe, and that too he could not have said why he did.

It opened its eyes. They say they cannot see, the newborn, that the light is only light to them, without shape or name. Its eyes were dark and they moved and seemed to find him where he knelt and to fix there, and he stayed kneeling longer than he meant, looking at it looking at him, and then he rose and walked away. He did not run. He walked with his shoulders up around his ears as a man walks in rain, and behind him after a few steps it began to cry, the sound very thin in that big silence, rising and falling, and he kept on, and the sound followed him down through the trees, growing fainter, until he crossed the creek again, and either it stopped then or the water covered it, and he stood on the near bank a long time listening and could not tell which, and that not telling he would carry with him all his days.

When he came back to the cabin the sun was past noon. She was awake. She was sitting up in the bloody bed and her arms were over the place where it had lain, and when the door opened she looked at his face and then at his empty hands and a sound came out of her that was no word.

It died, he said. It went in its sleep. I never wanted to wake ye.

Where is it, she said. Where's he at.

I buried him. Up on the hill yonder. I done it proper.

You never.

I done it proper, I said. They wasn't no use you seein it. It'd of just gone harder with ye.

She was getting up. He had not thought she could get up, weak as she was, but she got up out of the bed with the shift bloody to her knees and she came across the floor at him and he caught her wrists and held her and she fought him with what little there was in her to fight with and then hung in his hands sobbing.

Show me where, she said. Show me where at you buried him.

In time, he said. When you're able. They's no headboard yet nor nothin. I'll show ye in time.

He half carried her back to the bed and she lay there with her face to the wall and would not eat what he fixed nor speak, and the day went down outside, and he sat in the door with his back against the jamb and watched the light go off the trees and the swallows come out over the clearing, cutting and turning in the last of it, and dark came up out of the woods the way it does in that country, not falling but rising, up out of the hollows and the laurel and the creek, until it stood all about the cabin and there was nothing anywhere but the dark and the sound of the creek going and her breathing in the bed behind him, and far off, so far it might have been the blood in his own ears, a small cracked bell, chiming and chiming, somewhere out there in the night, going along some road he couldn't name, toward what he didn't know and never would until it was done and past all mending. He sat there a long time. Then he got up and shut the door.
