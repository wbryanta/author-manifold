The Hotel Miramonte burned on the second of October, 1964, during the third night of a Santa Ana wind, and six people died there, and on the fourteenth of October, 1978, I drove down the coast highway to attend a wedding in its Garden Room. I want to be exact about this, because exactness is the only thing I have ever been able to offer the Miramonte. The fire began at approximately 3:40 in the morning. It began, according to the county fire investigators, in a sofa in the north lounge, a cigarette left burning in the cushions, although among the staff it was always said to have been the wiring, and I have noticed over the years that people believe the version that suits the story they are already telling. The hotel had ninety-six rooms. I cleaned twenty-two of them, the third floor, north wing, and I was paid a dollar ten an hour, and I folded the towels in thirds because Mrs. Aguirre, the head housekeeper, believed that a towel folded in halves looked like a towel in a motel.

I was nineteen years old. I mention my age not to excuse anything but because nineteen is a fact, like the wind, like the wiring, like the dollar ten.

The invitation came in August, engraved, heavy stock, tissue interleaf. Mr. and Mrs. Howell Bryce request the honour of your presence at the marriage of their daughter Charlotte Anne to Mr. Steven R. Whitfield, Saturday, the fourteenth of October, at half after four o'clock, the Hotel Miramonte, Capistrano Beach, California. I read the invitation standing at the kitchen counter and I read it again sitting down, and what I noticed the second time was the deliberateness of it, the way the name of the hotel sat there on the card as though it were any hotel, as though it were a hotel chosen for the view. The Bryces had not chosen it for the view. Every December since 1964 I had received from the Bryces a Christmas card with a photograph of Charlotte in it, Charlotte at six on a pony, Charlotte at ten with her front teeth missing, Charlotte at seventeen in a pale blue dress before some dance, and every card was signed the same way, with love and eternal gratitude, the Bryces, and I had understood for some time that the Bryces were keeping the story alive the way other families keep a christening gown, folded in paper, brought out on occasions. Now there was an occasion. Where it almost ended, Howell Bryce would say that afternoon, his glass raised, his voice carrying out over the lawn toward the water, we begin.

The dead at the Miramonte were these. Margaret Sheehan, seventy-one, of Pasadena. Ofelia Vargas, twenty-six, of Santa Ana, and that the town and the wind should share a name is the kind of fact that means nothing at all and that I have never once been able to put down. Walter and Doris Kemp of Fresno, married three days. Lloyd Echols, the night engineer, who went back in for the boilers, or for the elevator machinery, or for reasons no one ever established. And a man registered as J. Carroll of Phoenix, whose name turned out not to be Carroll and whose city turned out not to be Phoenix, and whose actual name I have never learned, and about whom no one ever came forward, which seemed to me then, and seems to me still, the loneliest of all the facts in the coroner's report.

I drove down alone. Richard was in Houston, or said he was in Houston; we had by then arrived at an arrangement in which neither of us examined the other's geography too closely. The day was hot and dry and very clear, a Santa Ana day as it happened, the air scoured and glittering, the mountains pulled up close behind the freeway the way they are only when the wind comes from the desert, and I drove with the windows down past San Juan Capistrano and through the eucalyptus and down to where the highway runs along the bluff, and there it was. The new Miramonte. It had been rebuilt in 1968, by old Mr. Staley, who had owned it since the thirties, and who died in 1971, after which it was sold to a chain, and the chain had kept the name and the bougainvillea and very little else. The old hotel had been a wedding cake of white frame and shingle. The new hotel was low and wide and made of concrete, and the first thing I noticed, before the porte cochere, before the valets in their green vests, before the wedding guests already drifting across the lawn with drinks in their hands, was the fire stairs, exterior, steel, painted to match the stucco but unmistakable, switchbacking down the seaward face of the building. I noticed the fire stairs first. I will always notice the fire stairs first. This is not a sensitivity I cultivate. It is simply the order in which my eye now takes in a building, the way a pilot's eye takes in the horizon, the way a mother's eye takes in the gate latch and the depth of the pool.

The ceremony was held on the lawn at the south end, where the rose garden used to be, white chairs in rows, a trellis, the Pacific flat and shining behind the minister so that everyone watching the vows was also watching the sea. Charlotte Bryce was eighteen years old and she came down the grass aisle on her father's arm in a dress with a high lace collar and she was, everyone said so and everyone was right, a beautiful bride. I sat in the fourth row on the bride's side, between a woman in yellow silk who told me she had known the Bryces from the club and a man who managed money for Howell Bryce and who asked me twice, in the pleasant unlistening way of such men, how I knew the family. An old friend, I said. From before Charlotte was born, almost, I said, and the man nodded and looked back at the trellis, satisfied, and I sat there in the wind off the desert, which at four in the afternoon was just beginning to lose its argument with the wind off the water, and I counted the sprinkler heads in the overhang of the terrace roof. There were nine that I could see. The reception afterward was in the Garden Room, which is a long glass pavilion on the north end of the property, and the Garden Room stands, although I am quite sure no one at the wedding knew this and I am quite sure I will never be the one to tell them, on the footprint of the old laundry, where the mangle was, and the soaking sinks, and the smell of chlorine and scorched cotton that I can call up now, this minute, more easily than I can call up the smell of my mother's kitchen.

I had been at the Miramonte fourteen months when it burned. I lived in the staff annex, a two-story frame building behind the parking lot, two girls to a room, and on the night of the fire I was in the annex and not in the hotel because Ofelia Vargas had taken my turndown shift. She had taken it because there was a boy in Dana Point I wanted to see. I had driven up to Dana Point at seven and I had learned, in the parking lot of a drive-in restaurant, in the space of about four minutes, that the boy no longer wanted to see me, a development he had apparently arrived at some days earlier and not troubled to mention, and I was back in the annex by eleven, lying on my bed with my shoes on, doing the kind of crying that is mostly anger, and Ofelia was in the hotel turning down beds and leaving chocolates on pillows because I had wanted to be told in person what could as easily have been told to me on the telephone. I offer no moral here. There is no moral. There is only the schedule, who was where, and the schedule was made by a boy in Dana Point who I am told sells boats now in Newport Beach and who has no idea, none, that he ever decided anything at all.

The sirens woke me at a quarter to four. I want to say that I knew at once, but I did not know at once; what I knew was that sirens on the coast highway at that hour were unusual, and then I smelled it, and the smell was wrong, not brush, not the dry October hills, but paint and varnish and horsehair, an indoor smell out of doors, and I was across the parking lot in my nightgown and a cardigan before I had made any decision I can remember making. I had my passkey. The passkeys were brass, stamped with the name of the hotel and a number, mine was number nine, and the rule was that you signed your key out from Mrs. Aguirre at six in the morning and signed it back at four in the afternoon, and I had for some months been violating this rule by keeping mine in my cardigan pocket, out of laziness, out of nineteen-year-old carelessness, out of nothing that deserves to be called foresight. I have thought about this often. The thing that made me of any use at all that night was a habit for which I would have been docked a day's pay.

The north wing was already gone, or going. What I could do, what anyone could do, was on the south side, and I went up the south service stairs because I knew where they were, and the guests did not know where they were, because guests never know where the service stairs are; that is the point of service stairs. The corridor on three was filling from the north end, the smoke moving along the ceiling first, the way it does, a thing I did not know then and know now, and I went down the corridor banging on doors and opening them with the key, and people came out, and I sent them behind me to the stairs, and they went down, and that part of it, I will say, was almost orderly, was almost nothing, was a fire drill with worse air. Eleven people went down the south service stairs ahead of the smoke. The Register put the number at eleven and put my photograph on the second page, in my nightgown and cardigan on the lawn at dawn, and the headline said MAID'S COOL HEAD SAVES ELEVEN, and Mr. Staley gave me a check for a hundred dollars and his wife embraced me and wept, and for years afterward Richard would tell the story at dinner parties, the eleven, the passkey, the cool head, until he understood that I would leave the table, and after that he told it only when I was in the other room, and I would stand in the other room and listen to the shape of it through the wall, the pauses where the pauses always went, the laugh of relief at the end.

I have never counted the eleven. I count the one, and I count Ofelia, who is not mine to count and whom I count anyway.

Room 34 was the Bryces' room. The Bryces themselves were not in it; they were downstairs in the south lounge, where the bar stayed open late for a private party, an anniversary as it happened, their own, their sixth, and Charlotte was in the room asleep, four years old, and when I opened the door with the key she was standing up in the bed in the dark with her hands at her sides, not crying, perfectly silent, which frightened me more than crying would have. I took a towel from the rack in the bathroom and ran it under the tap and wrapped it around the lower half of her face, and I remember registering, in the way the mind will file an absurdity in the middle of an emergency, that the towel was folded in thirds, that someone, Ofelia, had folded that towel in thirds two hours before, and then I picked the child up and she put her arms around my neck and held on, and I went out into the corridor, and the corridor had changed in the ninety seconds I had been in the room. It had changed the way weather changes at sea. And the next door was 36.

Mrs. Sheehan was in 36. I had cleaned her room for two weeks; she was booked for a month, a widow from Pasadena, and I knew her room the way you know rooms when cleaning them is your trade, which is to say better than the people living in them know them. I knew the photograph of Mr. Sheehan in its silver frame on the dresser, a man in a hat squinting at some earlier sun. I knew the rosary on the nightstand and the box of saltines in the second drawer and I knew the hearing aid, which she wore on a cord like a pendant during the day and which she took off at night and set beside the rosary, and I knew, banging on the door of 36 with the flat of my hand, that she would not hear me, and she did not hear me, and I used the key, and the door opened two inches and stopped. She had put up the chain. She had never once put up the chain in the two weeks I had cleaned that room, the chain hung against the jamb every morning like something vestigial, and on that night of all nights she had put up the chain, and through the gap I could see the foot of the bed and one of her feet at the end of it, and I shouted into the gap, and the foot moved, I will always believe the foot moved, and Charlotte was coughing into the wet towel against my neck, a small steady machine of coughing, and the smoke was coming down the corridor at the level of my head, and I did the arithmetic.

I have done the arithmetic many times since. It is simple arithmetic and it always comes out the same way and it has never once settled anything. A four-year-old in my arms, breathing smoke. A chained door that would need to be broken, by a nineteen-year-old who weighed a hundred and eight pounds, in a corridor with perhaps a minute of air left in it, perhaps less. A woman who could not hear me and might already be past hearing anything. You take the child down. Anyone would tell you that you take the child down. Everyone, in fact, has told me that you take the child down, the fire captain told me, the coroner's man told me, Mrs. Aguirre told me with her hands gripping my hands, and they were all of them right, and I took the child down, and Margaret Sheehan, seventy-one, of Pasadena, died in room 36 of smoke inhalation, in her bed, possibly without waking, the report said, possibly. Ofelia Vargas died in the third-floor linen room, where she had gone, the investigators believed, because the linen room had no windows and a heavy fire door, and she may have believed the door would hold. The door did hold. The smoke did not require the door.

At the reception there were toasts. The best man's, the maid of honor's, and then Howell Bryce stood up at the head table with the glass walls of the Garden Room going gold behind him in the late light, and he told it. He told it well; he had been telling it for fourteen years, and the story had been worn smooth in the telling, worn into a shape that the hand of a wedding could hold. In his version I was on the fourth floor. In his version I wrapped Charlotte in my own coat. In his version I never let go, that was the phrase, she never let go, and the room turned, two hundred faces turning in the gold light, and the people near my table looked at me and smiled, and the woman in yellow silk put her hand to her throat, and I sat there and was looked at. I do not say his version was false. The fourth floor, the coat, these are not lies, they are polish, they are what happens to a stone in a river, and who am I to say that a story should keep its edges. The Bryces had made of the fire a thing with a beginning and an end, a thing in which everything was saved and nothing was lost, and they had built a wedding on it the way you build on landfill, and the proof that it held was standing right there in white lace, alive, married, laughing at her father's tears. What was not in his version was the door of room 36, because Howell Bryce has never known about the door of room 36. No one has ever known about the door of room 36 except me and the fire investigators, who logged the chain as engaged and moved on, chains being engaged in hotel fires as often as not, and there was no reason ever to connect that chain to the child, no reason except the arithmetic, and the arithmetic was mine.

Charlotte came to my table during the dancing. Up close she smelled of lilies and cigarettes, and she crouched beside my chair in the enormous dress and took both my hands, and her eyes were shining with champagne and the day, and she said, You're my good-luck charm, you know. You're why there's any of this. And she swept one hand at the room, the band, the cake, the boy in the rented tails watching her from across the floor, the whole gold evening, and I said what one says. I said she was a beautiful bride. I said her mother must be very happy. She pressed my hands and was pulled away by bridesmaids, and I sat for a while longer, and then I took my evening bag and went to find the third floor.

I had carried the key for fourteen years. I am not able to tell you why. It had lived in a drawer in three different houses, among the spare buttons and the foreign coins, brass, stamped MIRAMONTE and the numeral 9, and when I dressed for the wedding I had put it in the evening bag with my lipstick and my reading glasses, and I had not examined the impulse then and I will not pretend to examine it now. The elevator was playing a string arrangement of something. The third-floor corridor of the new Miramonte is wide and carpeted in green and lit with brass sconces, and the numbers begin at 300, and there is no room 36, there is no 36 anywhere in the building, the new numbering has swallowed it, and I walked the length of the corridor to roughly where the north wing used to begin, where the building now simply stops at a window with a sea view, and I stood in front of a door, 318, chosen for no reason, and I took out the key and tried it in the lock. The lock was a different kind of lock. The key did not fit. It did not begin to fit; it would not even enter the channel. I had known it would not fit. I had needed, apparently, to establish it.

A chambermaid came around the corner with her cart. She was young, younger than I had been, and the cart was stacked with linen, and she asked me, in the careful voice the staff of good hotels use with wedding guests found wandering the upper floors, whether I was lost, whether she could help me find anything. I told her no. I told her I used to work here, a long time ago, and she smiled the polite, unbelieving smile that the young give to such announcements, the smile that files you under champagne, and she went on down the green corridor with the cart, and I watched her go. The towels on the cart were folded in halves. I noticed this and I had nothing to attach to it. Mrs. Aguirre is dead. The chain on those new doors, I had noticed at 318, is the swing-bar kind now, not the old slide chain, and it is supposed to be stronger, and perhaps it is.

Downstairs they were calling the unmarried women to the floor; Charlotte was going to throw the bouquet from the staircase, a staircase that did not exist in 1964, that stands in air that in 1964 was three feet inside the burning north lounge. I did not go down for the bouquet. I stood at the window at the end of the corridor, and outside the wind had finished changing, it was coming off the water now, onshore, the kind of wind that turns a fire back on what it has already taken, and I stood there until the noise of the catch and the cheering came up through the floor, and then I put the key back in my bag. I have it still. The play in an engaged door chain, if you are wondering, is about two inches. I have measured it since, in other hotels, on other doors, more often than I would tell anyone who asked. Two inches, a little more, a little less. The chains are all the same.
