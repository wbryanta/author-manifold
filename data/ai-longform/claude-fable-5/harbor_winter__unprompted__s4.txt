The cannery whistle blew at seven that morning, the way it had for eighty-one years, and Einar Sorensen stood at his kitchen window with his coffee and listened to it the way a man listens to the heartbeat of someone he knows is dying. The sound came across the harbor flattened by the cold, a long B-flat moan that lifted gulls off the breakwater in a gray scatter, and when it stopped, the silence afterward seemed to have a shape, like a mold the sound had been poured from. In April the company would shut the doors. Everyone knew it now. The notice had gone up on the bulletin board outside the office in November, typed on company letterhead, thumbtacked at all four corners as if anyone might be tempted to steal it.

His granddaughter came down the stairs in two sweaters and her mother's old wool hat, eleven years old, with her bootlaces dragging. Her name was Lena and she had been living with him since September, when her mother took the night shift on the slime line because the night shift paid forty cents more an hour and forty cents an hour mattered now in a way it was hard to explain to a child, though Lena understood more than anyone gave her credit for. Her father was in Anchorage, working on a road crew, sending money that came less often than his phone calls, which also came less often.

"Tide's at nine-forty," Einar said. "Eat something."

She ate standing up, the way her mother did, a piece of toast held flat on her palm like a small tray, looking out the window at the harbor. The boats that were left lay against the floats in their winter colors, rust-streaked, blue tarps lashed over their drums. Half the slips were empty. When Einar was her age there had been so many seiners rafted out from the dock that you could walk a quarter mile across their decks and never wet your feet, and men would shout at you in Norwegian and Croatian and Tlingit to mind their gear, and you minded it.

"Grandpa," Lena said. "Is it true the water remembers things?"

"Who told you that?"

"Mrs. Vukovich. At school. She said the water remembers everything that ever happened in it."

Einar rinsed his cup and set it upside down in the rack. He had been a harbor pilot for thirty-one years, had brought four hundred and some ships across the bar and up the channel to the cannery dock, freighters and tenders and once a Navy survey vessel whose captain had not wanted a pilot at all and had nearly put her on Halibut Rock to prove he didn't need one. In all that time he had never known the water to remember anything. The water forgot you the moment you passed through it. That was the whole truth of it, and also the mercy of it, though he did not think a schoolteacher would put it that way to children.

"The water doesn't remember," he said. "You do the remembering. The water just tells you what it's doing right now, if you know how to listen. Get your float coat."

The skiff was an eighteen-foot open boat with a forty-horse outboard and a wooden tiller extension Einar had carved himself out of an oar handle, smooth as bone from his hand. He had Lena untie the bow line and coil it, and he watched her coil it wrong, over-and-over instead of with the lay, and he made her do it again, and she sighed the way her mother used to sigh, with her whole rib cage, and did it right.

They ran out past the breakwater into a morning the color of an oyster shell. The mountains came down to the water on both sides, black timber and snow, and the channel ran between them like a road that had been there before any other road. Einar throttled back where the harbor mouth opened up and let the skiff settle off plane.

"All right," he said. "Tell me what you see."

"Water."

"Lena."

"I see water, Grandpa. It's all just water."

"That's what a tourist sees." He pointed with his chin, a habit from years of keeping both hands occupied. "Look at the surface off the point. Then look at the surface out in the middle. Tell me the difference."

She looked. He gave her time. That was the first lesson and maybe the only one, though it took a winter to teach: you gave the water time. You didn't glance at it the way you glanced at a clock. You watched it long enough to see what it was doing instead of what you assumed it was doing.

"The part by the point is all wrinkly," she said at last. "Little wrinkles. The middle part is smoother. And there's like — a line. Between them."

"That line is the tide," Einar said. "Ebb's still running out in the middle of the channel. By the point there's a back eddy — the water there is actually going the other way, upstream, see, because the point splits the current and curls it around behind itself. If you were rowing, which side would you want to be on?"

"The wrinkly side. The eddy."

"If you were going upstream, yes. The water would carry you the way you wanted to go while the whole rest of the channel was against you. Fish know it. Old men in rowboats knew it. Everybody with a big engine forgot it." He pushed the tiller over and ran them slowly along the seam where the eddy met the main current, and the skiff wobbled and yawed as it crossed the line, and Lena grabbed the gunwale and then, when she saw he was watching, let go of it.

"You felt that," he said.

"The boat went sideways."

"The boat didn't do anything. The water under one half of the boat was going one direction and the water under the other half was going the other direction. The boat just told you about it. A boat is honest that way. It can't lie about what the water's doing. Men lie about it all the time, but boats can't."

All that winter the Saturdays held more or less to that shape. If it was blowing too hard they stayed at the kitchen table and he taught her the tide book, how to read the corrections for their harbor off the Sitka reference station, how the moon dragged the whole ocean around like a dog on a long leash, springs and neaps, why the biggest tides came when the moon was either full or new and not, as she'd assumed, only when it was full. He taught her to draw the channel from memory and then walked her down to the chart on his wall, the old paper chart with his pencil notes still on it from 1971, and showed her where her drawing was wrong, and where it was right in ways the chart wasn't, because the chart had been surveyed before the river changed its mind about where it wanted to put its sandbar.

"Charts are a letter from the past," he told her. "Useful letter. But the man who wrote it is dead and the water he wrote about is gone to Japan by now. You read the chart, then you read the water, and when they disagree, you believe the water."

If the weather allowed, they went out. He taught her wind first, because wind was the easiest to see and the quickest to kill you. He showed her cat's-paws, the dark hurrying patches that came down the channel ahead of a gust like the shadow of a hand, and he made her call them out before they arrived — gust coming, ten seconds, port side — until she could feel the skiff heel before it heeled. He showed her how the williwaws fell off the mountain on a northerly, how you could look at the snow smoking off the ridge two thousand feet up and know that in four minutes the harbor would be white and shrieking though right now it lay flat as poured lead. He showed her the difference between wind against tide and wind with tide, took her to the bar mouth on a day when the ebb was running into a modest southwest swell and let her see how a three-foot sea became a six-foot sea with a curling top, steep-faced and close together and mean, simply because the water under it was moving out while the wind pushed in.

"Same wind," he said, raising his voice over it, holding the skiff well back of the break. "Same swell as an hour ago. The only thing that changed is the tide turned. The bar doesn't get angry. It just does arithmetic. Wind plus ebb. You do the arithmetic before the bar does it for you."

She was pale, watching the seas stand up and topple on the shoal, the spray torn sideways off their crests. "Did you go through that? In the big ships?"

"In the big ships that was nothing. Forty feet of steel under you, you don't feel a six-foot sea. In this skiff it would be the last thing you ever did." He let that sit. He did not believe in frightening children for sport, but he believed in frightening them accurately. "Your great-uncle Magnus crossed this bar on an ebb in 1958 because he had a load of fish going soft and a cannery tally man he didn't trust. They found the boat. Most of it."

"Mom never told me that."

"Your mom's generation decided not to tell each other things. I never understood what they thought they were saving up for."

The town shrank around them as the winter went on. It had been shrinking for years, but the closure notice gave the shrinking a deadline, and people who had been leaving slowly began to leave fast. The Pedersens went in January, the whole family, the house left with curtains still in the windows. The hardware store cut back to four days a week, then three. At the school there was talk that next year the seventh and eighth grades would be folded together, and the year after that, who knew, maybe the kids would ride a ferry to the consolidated school forty minutes up the strait, weather permitting, which in this country was a phrase that decided more lives than any government did.

The cannery itself worked on through it all, running winter cod and the last of the brined summer salmon out of the warehouse freezers, and at night when Einar couldn't sleep he could see its lights across the harbor, the long low buildings on their forest of creosote pilings, steam rising from the retort room into the cold, and he could hear, if the wind was right, the clatter of the seamer and the conveyor lines, the sound of his whole life going about its business as if nothing were wrong. His father had worked there. His wife had worked there thirty summers in the egg house and could grade roe by lamplight faster than the Japanese technicians who came to check her work. She had been dead nine years and there were still days he came in from the woodpile and opened his mouth to tell her something.

Lena's mother came on Sundays, her one day off, and slept until noon, and Einar kept the girl quiet downstairs, and they worked on knots, bowline, clove hitch, rolling hitch, sheet bend, until Lena could tie them behind her back, which Einar insisted on, because the day you needed a bowline most was the day you couldn't see your hands. When her mother woke they ate together, the three of them, and her mother's hands at the table were nicked and swollen from the cold and the knives, and once Lena reached over without saying anything and turned her mother's hand palm up and looked at it for a long moment and turned it back, and nobody said anything about that either.

In February he taught her fog. They went out into it deliberately, on a windless morning when it lay on the channel like wool, after he had made her sit at the table and work the problem first: course out, speed, time, course back, what the current would do to them in each direction, written in pencil in a small waterproof notebook he gave her and told her was now hers.

"This is dead reckoning," he said. "Deduced reckoning, some say, but the dead is more honest. You reckon or you're dead." Out in the whiteness, with the world gone and the skiff's bow fading at five feet, he shut the engine off and made her listen. At first she said she heard nothing. Then, given time — always, given time — she began to pick it apart: the gulls on the breakwater, faint and behind them; the bell buoy off the point, off the starboard bow; a softer sound under everything, a long exhale.

"That's the bar," he said. "You can hear it on a quiet day from three miles. Now — where are we?"

She looked at her notebook, at her watch, at the gray nothing all around, and he watched her fight down the panic that fog put in everyone the first time, the feeling that the world had been erased and you with it, and then he watched her think. "The bell should be there," she said, pointing. "And it is there. And the breakwater birds are behind us, so... we're about here." She put her finger on her hand-drawn chart, on a spot that was wrong by maybe three hundred yards, which for a first reckoning in fog was close to miraculous.

"You're here," he said, moving her finger. "You forgot the flood set you sideways while you were busy being scared. The current doesn't stop working just because you can't see. Nothing out here stops working because you can't see. That's the whole of fog. The water keeps telling the truth; you just have to listen with something other than your eyes."

He taught her birds — how a tight, working flock of gulls meant feed pushed to the surface, which meant fish, which in the old days had meant money and now meant mostly memory; how murres flying low and fast in one direction in the evening were going somewhere worth knowing about. He taught her kelp: that a kelp stalk lying flat on the surface, streaming, was a current meter God put there for free, and the direction it streamed and how hard it pulled told you the set and the strength, and that kelp also meant rock beneath, always, no exceptions, kelp was the sea's way of marking its own teeth. He taught her color, the hardest lesson, the one that took the longest: that deep water on an overcast day had a particular weight to its gray, and that a shoal announced itself first as a paleness, a kind of held breath in the surface, before any depth sounder ever beeped at you; that the river's outflow wrote itself across the salt water in a long tea-colored tongue with a visible edge, and on one side of that edge the water was thirty-nine degrees and on the other side it was thirty-four, and a man overboard had about fifteen minutes' difference in his life depending on which side he went in.

"How do you know all this?" she asked him once, not flattering him, genuinely wanting the mechanism, the way she wanted to know how the seamer at the cannery crimped the can lids.

"Old men knew it and told me when I was your size and a pain in the neck, same as you. And then thirty years of standing on bridge wings at four in the morning with a captain who didn't speak English, betting both our lives that I knew what the water was doing better than his instruments did." He throttled down to take a tug's wake on the quarter. "A pilot doesn't carry anything aboard with him, you know. No tools. Just himself. The ship has everything — radar, charts, engines, crew. What the pilot brings is local knowledge. That's the whole cargo. Everything I'm telling you, that was my cargo, my entire working life. It weighed nothing and it was worth more than the ship."

"And now you're giving it to me."

"Now I'm giving it to you."

She was quiet for a while, watching the wake fan out behind them and break against the pilings of the cannery dock, where her mother was at that moment standing on a wet concrete floor in rubber gloves under fluorescent light.

"What if I leave, though," Lena said. "Everybody leaves. Mom says after April we might go to Anchorage. What good is it knowing this water if I'm not here?"

He had thought about that question for longer than she'd been alive, and he gave her the only answer he had ever come to. "The particulars stay here," he said. "Where the bar shoals, how the eddy sets off the point — that's this water's business and it'll keep it whether anyone knows it or not. But the way of it travels. Anywhere there's water, it's telling the truth about itself to whoever takes the time. You learn to read one water properly, all the way down, and you can learn any water. It's like — " he searched, a man not given to figures of speech — "you learned to read on some particular book. Cat sat on a mat, whatever it was. You're not stuck with that book forever. But you needed a first one."

The closure came faster at the end than anyone expected, the way the ebb always ran hardest in its third and fourth hours. The company moved the date up to the last week of March; there was a meeting in the school gym where a man in a soft jacket used the words consolidation and untenable and thanked the town for its years of partnership, and old Hans Berg stood up and asked the man whether he had ever in his life smelled a cannery in August, and the man said he hadn't had that pleasure, and Hans said no, he supposed not, and sat down, and that was somehow the end of the meeting.

On the last day they ran the lines until two in the afternoon. Einar took Lena down to the harbor to watch, because he believed in watching things, even this. The whole town was there along the dock in the cold sunshine, and at two o'clock the lines stopped, and the workers came out onto the dock still in their aprons, and the manager — a local man, Tollefson's boy, who had taken the job thinking he was securing his future — climbed the ladder to the whistle house and blew the whistle one last time and held it, held it far past the regulation blast, held it until the steam pressure itself began to die and the B-flat sagged and wavered and went down through tones the whistle had never made before, a long descending cry, and the gulls rose off the whole waterfront at once, and then it was quiet. Lena's mother stood among the others with her gloves still on. Einar saw her find Lena's face in the crowd and saw something pass between them that he was not part of and was not meant to be, and he looked away, out at the water, which was at slack just then, the channel lying still and silver, between intentions.

The next morning, Sunday, the harbor was as quiet as he had ever heard it. He woke Lena early. The forecast was fair and the tide book said high slack at 8:52, and a fair morning at high slack was the gentlest face the bar ever wore, and he wanted her to cross it.

They ran down the channel past the silent cannery, past the empty slips, out toward the mouth where the swell came in long and low from a storm a thousand miles away, and at the bar the seas were smooth-backed and orderly, just lifting the skiff and setting it down, and Einar pulled the throttle back and took his hand off the tiller.

"Take her across."

Lena looked at him. "Me?"

"You did the arithmetic. Slack water, fair wind, long-period swell. Tell me where the deep water is."

She looked, and gave it time, and he watched her eyes move the way he had taught them to move, reading the paleness over the shoal to the south, the kelp streaming faintly off the rocks to the north, the lane between where the swells passed through unbroken, only lifting, the water there a heavier gray, deep and sure of itself.

"There," she said, and put her hand on the tiller, and took them through.

On the outside, beyond the bar, the ocean opened up the way it always did, the land falling away behind, the swell longer, the light different, and Lena laughed out loud, once, a sound startled out of her, and Einar sat in the bow of his own skiff being driven by his granddaughter and felt the particular weightless feeling he used to get stepping off a ship's ladder onto the pilot boat after a good transit, the job done, the cargo delivered.

"Where do you want to go?" she shouted over the engine.

He looked at the water, all of it, the whole gray breathing reach of it, telling the truth about itself in every direction to anyone with the time, and he thought that whatever happened in April, in Anchorage, in all the years he would not see, she would be on some shore of it somewhere, reading.

"Your call," he said. "You're the pilot."
