All night the ferry moved north through the channels with its lights dimmed, and Elias Maki could not sleep. He had taken one of the reclining chairs in the forward lounge, among strangers wrapped in coats and sleeping bags, a child somewhere crying briefly and then stopping as if a hand had been laid on it. Around two in the morning he gave up and went out onto the deck, into the wet black air, and stood at the rail with the wind coming off the water smelling of cedar and diesel and the open sea somewhere to the west. The engines hummed up through the steel into his hands. An A-flat, very slightly sharp. He had spent thirty years tuning pianos in the houses of Vancouver, kneeling at the open hearts of instruments while their owners hovered in doorways, and he could not stop himself from naming the pitch of things. Refrigerators, furnaces, the drone of a ship carrying him toward his brother's body. Or toward a body that might be his brother's. The corporal on the telephone had been careful about this, twice, with the practised gentleness of a man who had made such calls before.

– We can't be certain, Mr. Maki. The water does things. We're hoping you can help us.

The call had come on a Tuesday, mid-afternoon, while he was working on a Heintzman upright in a house on Blenheim Street, the owner out, the key under a flowerpot, and he had stood in the stranger's living room with the phone against his ear and his other hand still resting inside the piano, on the cold speaking length of the strings. A man had been found near the mouth of the Skeena, among the pilings of one of the old canneries. In the water some weeks. No wallet. But there had been a name, the corporal said, sewn into the collar of the wool jersey in the old way, the way fishermen's wives had once done it so the drowned could be sent home. K. Maki. Faded thread, hand-stitched.

His mother had done that. He remembered her doing it, at the kitchen table under the kerosene lamp before the power came to that end of the island, her needle moving through the dark blue wool while outside the strait turned over in its sleep. She had sewn their father's name into his sweaters too, and it had made no difference; the sea had kept Toivo Maki and given back only the boat, the *Lempi*, found turning in slow circles off Malcolm Point with the net half out and the coffee still warm in the thermos. Elias was eleven. Karl was fifteen. After that Karl had become a kind of weather in the house, unpredictable, necessary, the one who brought the fish money in, and Elias had become the quiet one, the one with the ear, sitting beside Mrs. Salo at the harmonium in the Sointula church learning the distances between notes the way other boys learned the distances between reefs.

Sointula. Place of harmony. The Finns had come there at the turn of the century to build a perfect world on an island of rain, and the perfect world had burned down and gone bankrupt and the dreamers had drifted off, but his people had stayed and fished. He had grown up in a town of saunas and net lofts and the Co-op store, where old men argued socialism in two languages over coffee and the women wove rag rugs the colours of the sea in winter. He had not been back in fourteen years. On the drive up-island the previous day he had passed the turnoff to the Sointula ferry without slowing, but standing now at the stern rail he believed he could see, far back in the darkness, the small patient pulse of the Pulteney Point light, the light their bedroom window had faced, the light he and Karl had fallen asleep counting, and he watched it until the ship took a long slow bend in the channel and the land closed over it like water over a stone.

There is an hour out there when the boat seems to be the only made thing left in the world. The forest comes down black on both sides to the black water and there are no other lights, not a troller, not a cabin, and the wake unrolls behind you, pale, persistent, the only sentence being written anywhere. A man can stand inside that hour and feel his whole life become approximate. Elias stood inside it. He thought of his brother's hands.

It was the hands he had told the corporal he could know him by. Not the face, he had said before the corporal could begin the careful sentence about the face. The hands. Karl's hands had been a chart of his work. The left forearm carried a scar six inches long, slightly curved, a pale raised seam like the lateral line on a fish, and Elias had put it there. He was fourteen, dressing coho on the float in October with cold-stupid fingers, and the knife had skidded off a backbone and opened his brother's arm where it reached past him for the next fish. The blood had come up astonishingly, more colour than either of them had seen in weeks of grey water and grey sky, and Karl had clamped his right hand over it and laughed.

– Now you've signed me, he said. Now everybody can see whose brother I am.

He told their mother he had done it on the gunwale. He held that lie all his life as far as Elias knew, held it casually, the way he held everything, a cigarette, a wheel, a grudge. The scar had been their secret and then it had been simply a fact of his body and then, after the funeral, after the silence began, it had become for Elias a kind of proof he carried around. Somewhere in the world there is a man wearing my signature. We cannot be entirely strangers.

They had not spoken in fourteen years. He could date the silence exactly because it began on the day they buried their mother, in the cemetery above the breakwater where the wind never stops, August of that year, the alders moving, Karl in a borrowed suit standing apart from everyone with his fists in his pockets. Afterwards in the kitchen of the house, with the casseroles of the neighbour women cooling on every surface, Karl had asked him for eleven thousand dollars. The bank was taking the *Lempi*. He said it flatly, looking out the window at the strait, and Elias — who had the money, who had a house in Vancouver and a wife named Anna and a business with his name on a small brass plate — Elias had hesitated. That was all. A breath, two breaths, the length of a rest in a bar of music, while he thought about Karl's drinking and the two seasons of bad fish and the engine that always needed something, and in that rest Karl heard everything, the whole verdict, the entire history of the younger brother's leaving rendered as a silence in a kitchen. He nodded once. He picked his cigarettes off the table.

– Forget it, he said. Forget I asked.

And had walked out through the screen door past the casseroles and the cousins, and the bank took the boat in the spring, their father's boat with their mother's name on the bow, and Karl went north to crew on other men's boats, seiners, packers, the herring in March, anywhere, and the silence sealed over like water behind a stern. Elias wrote twice. The letters were not returned and not answered. Once, years into it, the phone rang at three in the morning and he picked it up and heard nothing, or not nothing — wind, a gull, the particular hollow roar of a phone booth by a working harbour — and he said his brother's name into it, Karl, twice, and the line went dead, and he sat on the edge of the bed in the dark with Anna's hand on his back, certain and unable to say why.

In the cafeteria, near three, a few figures sat far apart under the fluorescent lights with the steam trays empty and shining. He bought coffee from a machine. A man at the next table was reading a paperback western with his finger moving under the lines, a heavy man in a halibut jacket, sixty maybe, and when Elias set his cup down the man glanced up and then looked again.

– You'd be a Maki, the man said.

Elias stood still with his hand around the cup.

– It's the face, the man said, not apologizing. I crewed with Karl Maki out of Rupert. The *Ocean Dawn*, then the *Western Girl*. Herring, mostly. You're the brother. The piano man.

– The piano man, Elias said.

– He talked about you. The man turned his book face down on the table as if settling in. Not often. When he was drinking he'd talk about the island, the sauna, the old man's boat. He used to say his brother could hear a wrong note through a wall. He said it like bragging.

The room hummed. Somewhere below them seven hundred feet of steel pushed water aside in the dark.

– I'm going up to see him, Elias said, and heard how it sounded, and did not correct it.

– He still up there? The man frowned at the table. I lost track of him, must be three, four years. He went on the beach for a while. Then somebody said he was running a packer for the shore plant, but somebody else said that was a different Maki, there was a Maki from Finn Slough too, no relation. Hard to keep track of a man who doesn't want keeping track of.

He told Elias a story then, unasked, the way men on night boats will. Rivers Inlet, a fouled propeller, the skipper hollering, and Karl stripping to his shorts in the rain and going over the side with a knife in his teeth like a pirate in a comic book, forty-nine-degree water, down into the dark green under the hull, and the whole crew leaning over the side watching the pale shape of him working below them, and how when he came up he wouldn't take the blanket at first, stood streaming on the deck and laughing, holding up the cut webbing like a scalp. He'd lost the top of a finger that summer or the next, the man said. Ring finger, he thought. The winch. Or maybe that was the other Maki. The man's eyes drifted; the years moved around in him like ballast.

– They were hard on themselves, those Sointula men, he said finally. Your father's people. Like it was a competition with the water.

Elias carried his cold coffee out on deck and poured it over the rail and watched the pale rope of it whipped away. Ring finger. He had heard the finger story too, years ago, thirdhand, from a cousin at a wedding — Karl lost a finger up north, did you hear — and he had thought at the time, with a bitterness that shamed him now: the sea keeps editing him, and I am not even allowed to read the drafts. A man's body goes on without you. It gathers scars and losses in rooms you will never enter, and then one day you are asked to stand over it and say whether you know it, as if knowledge were a thing that kept.

Toward five the sky began to exist again, a grey weight lifting off a grey weight, and the channel widened and there were islands like the backs of whales and then real whales, two of them, their breath standing in the air long after they had gone down. Rain came on slowly, without drama, the way it does up there, less an event than a climate of mind. Elias shaved in the steel mirror of the men's washroom among fishermen and tree planters and a young man with a guitar case, and looked at his face, which the man in the cafeteria had read so easily, the Maki face, their father's heavy brows and their mother's mouth, and thought: this is the face they could not show me. This face, worn by water.

Prince Rupert came up out of the rain in pieces — the grain elevator, the container cranes, the hillsides of small wooden houses with lit kitchen windows at eight in the morning. The corporal was waiting at the foot of the ramp, younger than his telephone voice, holding an umbrella he did not open.

– Mr. Maki. Thank you for coming up. I know it's a hard thing.

– It's a long way around by road, Elias said, which was not an answer to anything, and the corporal nodded as if it were.

They drove through the town in an unmarked car, the wipers ticking, and the corporal told him what they knew, in order, quietly, his eyes on the road. A tug crew had seen it first, off the old Inverness cannery, where the pilings stand out of the river mouth in rows like a drowned orchard. Male, between fifty and sixty-five. In the water three weeks, perhaps four; the river was cold, which was, he said, and paused over the word, a kindness, in terms of what remained. The jersey with the name. Work boots, one missing. No vessel reported overdue that fit, but men went out alone in skiffs up there all the time, off floats, off beaches, and there was a Karl Maki on an old crew list at the shore plant, address a hotel that had since closed. They had pulled dental records from a Vancouver clinic under that name but the records were forty years old and the mouth, the corporal said, had had a hard life since.

– There's a healed break in the right wrist. There's a partial amputation, left hand, fourth finger, old, well healed. And there's the forearm. You mentioned the forearm on the phone.

– I mentioned it, Elias said.

The hospital sat on its hill in the rain. They went down, not up, into corridors of painted cement where the air changed and became chemical and patient, and a woman in surgical greens met them at a steel door and spoke to Elias in a low voice about what he would and would not see, the sheet, the screen, the option of photographs instead, and he heard himself decline the photographs, and then they were inside, and the room was small and very clean and cold, and the body lay on its table under green cloth like a hill under a field, and he understood that all the hours of the boat, all the black water he had crossed, had been only the approach to this one square room, the way a long Sointula winter is only the approach to a single morning when the herring arrive and the whole sea turns silver and the gulls go insane and a year is decided in a week.

– The face won't help you, the woman said gently. I'd rather you didn't ask for the face.

– The left arm, Elias said. And the hands.

She folded the cloth back from the left side the way his mother had folded linen, in halves, deliberate, and there was the arm. It did not look like an arm at first. It looked like something made of the river, the skin gone the colour of certain stones, loosened, remote, and Elias stood with his hands at his sides and made himself read it as he would read the inside of a ruined piano, part by part, without grief, grief being a thing that interferes with the ear. The hand. Big through the palm. The fingers thickened at the joints, the nails of a man who worked, and the fourth finger ending bluntly at the second knuckle in a smooth old scar like a sealed envelope. He looked at that for a long time. Then he let his eyes go up the forearm, along the inner side where the soft skin had been, where a boy's knife had skidded forty-six years ago on a cold float with the gulls screaming over the offal, and there, under all the water's revision, faint as a tide line on sand, was a raised pale seam, six inches, slightly curved.

A scar is not a name. He knew that. Skin tears along its own grain; the coast was full of men with old wounds in old places; the river had had a month to write whatever it pleased on this body, and the man in the cafeteria himself could not keep his Makis apart. Elias knew all of this, standing in the cold room, and held his knowledge the way you hold a struggling fish, firmly, away from your body. He asked, and the woman allowed it, and he took the dead left hand in both of his.

He had spent his life knowing things through his fingertips that he could not have seen or said. The give of a hammer's felt, the eighth of a turn that brings a string from grief into accord. He held the hand and felt the heavy palm, the calluses set like rivets along the base of the fingers where net twine runs and burns, the third knuckle enlarged where — he had forgotten this until the moment his thumb crossed it — where Karl had broken it at sixteen on the jaw of a boy from Alert Bay who had said something about Finns, and had laughed about that too, flexing it for weeks like a trophy. The knuckle was there. It was there under his thumb like a note he had tuned a thousand times, and he stood holding his brother's hand in a basement in the rain at the end of the continent, and the fourteen years did not fall away, nothing so merciful, they simply stood in the room with him, all of them at once, present and finished, like a chord held down after the hands have lifted.

– Mr. Maki, the corporal said from somewhere.

– Yes, Elias said.

– Is it your brother?

Outside, much later it seemed, though it was barely noon, the rain had eased and the harbour lay flat and pewter-coloured below the town, and he stood on the hospital steps with the carbon copy of a form folded in his inside pocket, his own signature still wet in him, *Elias Toivo Maki, brother of the deceased*. The corporal had offered lunch, a ride, the name of a man about arrangements, and Elias had taken the name and refused the rest, and now he walked downhill toward the water because all his life that was the direction his feet had argued for.

He was thinking of a night in August when he was ten and Karl fourteen and the strait was full of phosphorescence, the whole bay loaded with cold green light, and they had slipped out of the house past midnight and swum off the breakwater into it, two boys igniting the sea with every stroke, their bodies sheathed in fire, and Karl had floated on his back far out, farther than Elias dared, only his face above the burning water, and called back across the dark in a voice that carried the way voices carry over water and over years, with everything in it and nothing explained.

– Elias. Elias. This is where we're from.

He had not answered then, treading water closer to shore, saving his breath. He walked down through the wet town to the harbour and stood at the rail above the fish boats, the trollers and seiners rafted three deep, their names patient under the rain — *Silver Isle*, *Aino II*, *Western Girl* — and somewhere below him a halyard tapped its mast, a high irregular note, B, B, B, like a man knocking softly at a door he is no longer sure is his, and Elias Maki stood listening to it for a long time, and then, quietly, in the manner of his mother's people, who answered late or not at all but carried every question with them to the end, he answered it.
