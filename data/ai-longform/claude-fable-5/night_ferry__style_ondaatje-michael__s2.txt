The ship hums all night through the bones of anyone who lies down in it. Ellis Wade has not lain down. He sits in the forward lounge with his coat still on, a manila envelope on his knee, and feels the engines four decks below him the way you feel a chord through the floor of a church. Around him people sleep in the reclining chairs with their mouths open, their arms flung out, abandoned to the crossing, and they look to him the way sleepers always look on boats at night — like the rescued, or like the drowned laid out in rows, waiting for names.

He has not opened the envelope since Sydney. He knows what is in it. A photocopy of a form, a constable's card, and a photograph, face down even inside the envelope, of a brass tuning fork found in the pocket of a dead man's coat on the shore below Cape Ray.

The strait they are crossing is not wide as oceans go. Sixty miles of black water between one island and another, between the last light of Cape Breton and the first light of Newfoundland. In winter the ice comes down through it from the north in great rafted pans, and there were years, his father told him, when caribou crossed on the ice, when men walked out from the Cape Breton side to hunt seals and the wind shifted and carried them away standing upright on their floes, dignified, diminishing, until they were only a flaw in the whiteness. The strait keeps what it takes. In October of 1942 a German submarine waited in it for the ferry *Caribou*, this same run, this same darkness, and put a torpedo into her side at twenty to four in the morning, and one hundred and thirty-seven people went into the water, nursing sisters and sailors and children and a stoker who had survived the sinking of two other ships and must have thought, going under for the third time in his life, that the sea had been writing to him for years and he had never once answered.

Ellis thinks about the stoker because it is easier than thinking about his brother.

His brother. Garnet. He makes himself say the name silently, mouthing it, the way as a boy he mouthed the names of notes before he trusted himself to play them. Garnet Wade. Two years older. Last seen by Ellis on a June afternoon twenty-three years ago in the yard of their father's house in Ingonish, among the stacked lobster traps with their laths silvered like driftwood, blood on Garnet's lip and Garnet's left hand held against his chest like a small animal he was protecting, the ring finger already swelling where Ellis had bent it back until it gave. He can still hear the sound it made. A green branch. A piano wire let go too fast.

You don't choose what you keep. He has forgotten whole years of his marriage, the names of towns he has worked in, the faces of women he believed he loved. He has kept the sound of his brother's finger breaking with perfect fidelity for twenty-three years, the way a fork holds its pitch in a drawer, in the dark, saying nothing until struck.

The constable's voice on the telephone had been young and careful. A man found on the rocks west of Cape Ray, no wallet, no rings. In the water some time, the constable said, in the tone of a man choosing each word out of a small box of approved words. But the coat had stayed on him, and in the inside pocket, zipped, there was a tuning fork. Brass. Old. Initials filed into the flat of the handle. *E.R.W.*

Evelyn Rose Wade. Their mother. She had been the organist at St. Peter's for thirty-one years, and the fork was the one she carried in her handbag the way other women carried rosaries, an A given to her by her own teacher in Antigonish before the war. She tuned the choir off it, struck it on her knee and held it to the bone behind her ear, eyes closed, and the boys watching her believed she was listening to something on the other side of a wall only she could find. When she died she left the piano to no one, the house to their father, and the fork, by a line in her own hand, *to Ellis, who hears it.*

It went missing the week Garnet left for the boats. Ellis had searched the parlour for it on his knees, had accused no one out loud, which is its own kind of accusation, the worst kind, the kind that lives in a house like damp.

So. A fork with his mother's initials in a dead man's coat on the far shore of the strait. There were other possibilities. A theft, a pawnshop, a stranger with a liking for old brass. He had listed the possibilities to the constable and the constable had listened politely and then said: There's also this, sir. The man who found him knew him a little, from the wharf at Isle aux Morts. Knew him as Wade. Didn't know a first name. We were hoping you'd come over.

We were hoping you'd come over. As if it were a christening. As if there would be tea.

He gets up and walks. It is what he does in the night now, on land too, in his apartment in Halifax above the music shop, walking the hallway while the radiators tick. On the ship walking is better because the ship gives you somewhere to arrive: the cafeteria with its chained-off dimness, the corridor of cabins where shoes wait outside no doors because no one does that anymore, the heavy steel door to the after deck that he leans on with his shoulder until the wind accepts him.

Outside, the noise of the engines becomes the noise of the wake. The wake is the only pale thing in the world, a long road of churned light unspooling behind them toward Cape Breton, toward everything he is leaving at fourteen knots. Above him the radar bar turns and turns with the patience of the blind. There is no moon. The strait and the sky have agreed on a single blackness and the ship carries its own small civilization of light through it, lounge windows, masthead, the cigarette of a trucker at the far rail who lifts a hand to Ellis and then respects his silence, two men keeping watch over nothing together.

Garnet was the swimmer. That is the thing Ellis cannot put anywhere tonight. Of the two of them, Garnet was the one the water loved. At ten, at twelve, he would go off the government wharf in May when the harbour was still half winter, and come up hooting, and the men mending twine would shake their heads with that particular Maritime mixture of contempt and pride. He could hold his breath the length of a verse and a chorus. He dove for coins the tourists threw and came up with them in his teeth like a retriever, grinning around the Queen's face. Their mother said he had been born in the wrong order of things, that he should have been a seal, and Garnet took it as the compliment it was.

Ellis was the one who listened. He understood early, the way children understand their assignments without being told, that the family had divided its inheritance between them in the womb: the water to Garnet, the church organ and the swollen-stringed parlour piano to Ellis, the boat to both of them and to neither. Their father, Hollis Wade, fished lobster in spring and groundfish after, out of a Cape Islander called the *Evelyn Rose*, and he wanted no part of either music or diving; he wanted hands. He would take whichever son was nearest and least defended on a given morning, four o'clock, the kitchen light a yellow wound in the dark, tea you drank standing up.

On the fourteenth of June in the year Ellis was fifteen, the morning was Ellis's. It had been agreed at supper the night before. And Ellis woke at half three with a throat like a rasp and a fever he might, in honesty — and he has had thirty-six years to interrogate his honesty, lying awake in apartments above music shops — a fever he might have worked through. He lay in the dark and heard his father moving below, and he did the arithmetic of a boy: the recital in Sydney in nine days, the Chopin he had nearly tamed, his hands, his throat. He let his father's footsteps come up the stairs and stood at the door and said he was sick. He watched his father's face do nothing at all, which was how his father's face did everything, and then he heard, through the wall, Garnet being woken in his place, the creak, the soft swearing, the wonderful unbothered ease with which Garnet rose into any morning.

The fog came on by ten. By noon the wind behind it. The *Evelyn Rose* did not come in with the others, and the others went back out, and the women came down to the wharf in twos, and Mrs. Dunphy stood beside Ellis's mother without touching her, which was how the women there held each other up.

A dragger out of Neil's Harbour found Garnet at dusk, three miles off, holding a red gas can to his chest the way a child holds a toy it has been told to give up. He had been in the water four hours, June water, water that takes a man's reason in one and his life in two, and the doctor in Sydney could not explain him except to say that some bodies refuse. They never found Hollis Wade. The boat came ashore in pieces over the next month, a plank here, the wheelhouse door at Smelt Brook, the family receiving their father back in fragments the way relics travel, a splinter at a time, each one knocked at the front door by some apologetic neighbour holding wood.

Garnet did not speak of it. Not at the funeral, where there was no body and the church felt to Ellis like a piano with its strings cut, all that shaped air and nothing to sound it. Not after. He was seventeen and he had floated four hours in the strait's cold annex and watched, or not watched — Ellis never learned which, that was the whole hollow center of everything after — watched or not watched their father go down, and he carried it the way he carried everything, lightly, terribly, with a grin that stopped at his eyes. And Ellis, who had been warm in bed, who had been protecting his hands, began to hate him for surviving it so casually, which was easier than the other hatred, the one with his own name on it.

It took eight years for the words to come out of him, and they chose, as such words do, the worst available door: the night after their mother's funeral, the rum, the kitchen, Garnet saying something idle about selling the house. *You came back and he didn't.* Five words. Ellis has tuned perhaps eleven thousand pianos since, and he could tune eleven thousand more and never get the sourness of those five words out of the air. Garnet hit him, once, fair, a fisherman's short hook, and they went through the screen door into the yard among the traps, and Ellis got his brother's left hand and bent the ring finger back, deliberately, looking at him, until it broke.

Then Garnet stood up, and held the hand to his chest, and looked at Ellis with an expression Ellis has spent twenty-three years failing to read — it was not anger; he would give a great deal for it to have been anger — and walked out of the yard, and that was the end of the brothers Wade.

A postcard from Argentia, year two, no message, just his name and the address in handwriting like trawl gear. A phone call, year nine, two in the morning, breathing and harbour noise and Ellis saying Garnet, Garnet, until the line clicked. Word, through cousins, of work on the coastal boats, then on the Labrador run, then nothing, then Isle aux Morts. Island of the dead, named for its wrecks. You could not, Ellis thinks at the rail, put that in a novel. The strait has no shame about its symbols.

Toward three he goes back inside because the cold has reached the place where the cold matters. In the lounge a girl of nineteen or twenty has arranged herself across three chairs under a man's flannel shirt, and her boots stand together on the floor below her like patient dogs. An old man works a crossword by the light of his phone. The vending machine hums its single note — B flat, slightly sharp, Ellis cannot help it, his ear files everything, the engines a low F, the wind at the door a chord that refuses to resolve. His mother used to say that the world was tuned once, in the beginning, and has been going out ever since, and that her work and his were the same work as the priest's, smaller corrections against a general falling.

He sleeps without meaning to, upright, the envelope under his crossed arms, and dreams not of Garnet but of Wreckhouse, the stretch of land below the Table Mountains on the far side, where the wind pours off the plateau hard enough to lift boxcars off the rails — it did, more than once, the railway has photographs — and where the company in his grandfather's time hired a farmer named Lauchie MacDougall who could smell the wind coming. That was his whole employment. He lived in the gap with his wife and his children and his nose, and when the gusts were building he wired up the line and the trains waited, sometimes days, on the word of one man's body. They called him the human wind gauge. He died in 1965 and the wind, presumably, has gone unsmelled since. In the dream Ellis is on the stopped train and MacDougall walks the length of it in the dark outside, touching each car with the flat of his hand, the way you check a horse, the way a doctor checks a chest, and Ellis wants to lower the window and ask him — what — *will it hold*, perhaps, or *how do you carry a gift like that in a place like this* — and wakes with the question still shaped in his mouth as the ship's note changes, deepens, the engines coming down from F, and through the window the lights of Channel-Port aux Basques stand in the last of the night like a handful of embers thrown against a hill.

The town in early light is the colour of work. Houses painted the bold colours people use against eight months of grey — teal, ox-blood, a yellow that would be a joke anywhere south — clamped onto rock that rises straight out of the harbour, with the Table Mountains behind, flat-topped, biblical, holding up a sky that is doing its weak November best. The cars come off the ferry in a long clattering exodus and disperse, and within twenty minutes the town has swallowed them all and returned to itself, and Ellis stands on Water Street with his one bag, too early for everything.

The funeral home does not open until nine. The constable — Pittman, younger even than his voice — meets him at half eight in a coffee shop and lays things out with a decency that Ellis will remember later as the one mercy of the day. The man was found eleven days ago. The water and the rocks have done what they do. There will be a sheet, folded back only so far. If Ellis cannot say, then he cannot say, and there is dental, there is DNA, these things take weeks but they exist; no one is asking him to be certain on behalf of science. They are asking him to be a brother for five minutes, if a brother is what he turns out to be.

The room at the back of the funeral home is small and very clean and smells of nothing, which is its own smell. The table is steel. The sheet is white and the shape under it is the shape that every human being makes lying down, no more specific than that, and Ellis understands, standing in the doorway, that he has been preparing all night for a face and that the face will be the wrong place to look. Eleven days in the strait. He knew that in his hands before the constable said it.

The attendant folds the sheet back to the collarbones.

He makes himself look, because not looking would be the bed in Ingonish all over again, the warm boy protecting his hands. And it is as he feared and as he was promised: the sea has taken the face the way it took the *Evelyn Rose*, in instalments, and what is left is an approximation, a forgery of a man, the rough work an apprentice does before the master comes in to put the likeness on. The hair could be Garnet's hair gone grey. The brow could be their father's brow; but their father's brow is on half the men in every harbour from Canso to Nain. He stands there and feels nothing he expected to feel, no recognition and no relief at the absence of recognition, only a kind of roaring quiet, the sustain pedal held down on an empty room.

Then he says, and his voice comes out steadier than he has any right to: The hands. Can I see the left hand.

The attendant glances at Pittman, and Pittman nods, and the sheet comes back along one side, and there is the arm of a man who worked, the forearm still ropey even now, and the hand.

The sea has been at the hands too. But hands are what Ellis knows. He has spent his life inside the bodies of pianos, reading the histories of strain in wood and wire, and before that he spent a childhood watching his brother's hands bait trawl and palm coins out of green water, and on a June night in a yard full of traps he held this hand — if it is this hand — and did the unforgivable thing to it. He leans down. He does not touch it; he has been told he must not touch it; his own hands are clasped behind his back like a schoolboy's at a museum.

The ring finger of the left hand is crooked. Not broken by rocks, not the random violence of eleven days of surf — he can see those injuries too, and they are different, fresh, white, without grief in them. This is an old deformity, healed wrong decades ago, the middle joint set forever at its small unnatural angle, a sentence in bone, and Ellis wrote it.

He stands up straight. Outside, a truck goes by on Water Street, and somewhere in the building a kettle is coming to the boil, the long rising note of it, and he thinks, absurdly, helplessly, that it will reach its pitch and someone will take it off and the note will never be finished.

It's him, he says. That's my brother. That's Garnet Wade.

There are forms. Of course there are forms; the dead generate paper the way wrecks generate driftwood, and a neighbour brings each sheet to your door. He signs his own name three times and then comes to the line that asks for the name of the deceased in block capitals and discovers that he has not written his brother's name in twenty-three years — has said it, has mouthed it on a night ferry, but not written it — and his hand, his tuner's hand, his protected hand, shakes on the G and then steadies, and he prints the rest of it the way you finish anything, one letter at a time.

Pittman gives him the personal effects in a clear bag. A jackknife. Forty-one dollars. A pencil stub. The tuning fork.

On the afternoon boat back across the strait Ellis stands at the after rail with the fork in his fist, warming the brass, the Table Mountains going slowly to blue behind him, Wreckhouse off there to the north with its dead wind-smeller and its waiting trains. He has been thinking, all day, about what Garnet was doing with it. Twenty-three years, boats, bunkhouses, the Labrador run, every chance to lose or pawn or pitch it over a hundred rails into a hundred harbours, and instead a zipped inside pocket, against the heart, their mother's initials going soft under his thumb. A man who could not write a message on a postcard, carrying a perfect A through every weather. It was the one thing in the family that never went out of tune, and Garnet took it, and Ellis understands now, far too late, in the way we are permitted to understand only what we can no longer use, that the theft was not a theft. It was Garnet asking to be the one who hears. It was Garnet keeping the only voice in the house that had never accused him.

The wake unrolls its pale road back toward Newfoundland. Somewhere under all this water the *Caribou* lies with her hundred and thirty-seven, and the stoker who never answered his letters, and the ice will come down in February over all of them, and the caribou no longer cross because the world was tuned once and has been going out ever since.

Ellis strikes the fork against the rail.

He cannot hear it in the wind — no one could — but that was never how his mother listened. He sets the humming brass against the bone behind his ear, the way she did, eyes closed, and the note comes up through his skull from the inside, pure, patient, unaccusing, the same A it was in Antigonish before the war, the same A it was in a coat pocket under eleven days of the strait, and he stands holding his brother to his head in the cold and lets it ring all the way down.
