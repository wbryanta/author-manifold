He was up before light and he stood on the porch with his coffee and watched the east pale over the orchard where the trees stood in their long rows like figures in some patient assembly awaiting a thing that had been promised them and was now long in coming. The dead ones you could tell even in that gray light. They held their limbs differently. He drank the coffee and threw the dregs into the yard and went down the steps to the truck.

He drove out along the headland row with the lights off because he knew the road and because the day coming up was light enough. Doves called from the brush along the dry creek. Where the line crossed the creek he stopped and got out and walked down into the cut and stood looking at the washout. The May rain had taken the bank out from under the pipe for a length of twenty feet and the pipe had sagged and broken at the old coupling where his father had married the plastic to the galvanized iron thirty years ago and the water had run out under the ground for nobody knew how long before the pressure fell off at the far risers and told them. The ground all about was still dark with it. He squatted and looked at the broken collar and turned a piece of it in his hand. The iron pipe where it came out of the ground was scaled with rust the color of dried blood and the threads were near gone.

Charlie's truck came up the county road a little after seven with the sun on the windshield so that it looked to be carrying fire. He pulled in at the gap and got out and stood looking at the orchard and then he came down the row to where Tom was unloading the shovels.

You been here long?

Little while.

Charlie looked down into the cut at the broken line. Well, he said. There she is.

There she is.

How long you think it's been runnin out?

Week maybe. Maybe more. I didnt notice the pressure till Tuesday.

Week of water.

Yeah.

Charlie spat and looked off down the creek. You got coffee in that thermos?

I might.

They drank the coffee standing at the tailgate and the sun came up over the trees and the shadows of the rows lay long and blue down the orchard floor and then shortened and the light came yellow and ordinary and the day's heat was already in it. Cicadas had started in the liveoaks along the fenceline. Charlie set his cup on the tailgate and took off his hat and resettled it.

You want the shovel or the mattock?

I dont care.

Take the shovel then.

They went down into the cut and began to dig out the line. The ground was caliche under the dark wash of soil and it came up in pale clods and chunks and they pried at it and shoveled it out and the morning went on that way. They didnt talk much. They had dug together before, fence postholes and stocktanks and a grave for a dog when they were boys and another for their father's horse that he wouldnt let the renderer have, and there is a rhythm two men find who have dug together and they found it and kept it and the trench opened along the pipe foot by foot. By nine o'clock their shirts were soaked through and they climbed out and sat on the bank and drank water out of the jug and looked at what they'd done.

That iron pipe's done for, Charlie said.

I know it.

You cant thread it. It'd crumble in the dies.

I got a dresser coupling. We'll cut back to good iron and clamp it.

If there is good iron.

There's good iron. Daddy laid that pipe in fifty-seven. They made pipe then.

They made everthing then to hear you tell it.

Tom drank from the jug and capped it and set it in the shade of the bank. A hawk had set its wings over the creek and hung there riding the first thermal of the day and they watched it till it slid off down the air and was gone beyond the trees.

You talk to Mama Sunday? Charlie said.

I did.

How'd she seem.

She seemed all right. She asked about the Lorings.

What'd you tell her.

I told her they set a good crop.

Did they?

Some of em.

Charlie nodded slowly. He picked up a clod of caliche and crumbled it in his fist and let it sift away. She asked me was the water runnin, he said. Last time I was up there. First thing she asked me. Not how's Janie, not how's the kids. Was the water runnin.

It's what she'd ask.

I know it's what she'd ask. That's what I'm sayin.

They went back to the digging. By late morning they had the break opened out and the pipe lying exposed in the trench like something exhumed, the white plastic and the rusted iron and the failed collar between them, and they stood looking at it the way men will look at a thing they have uncovered, with a kind of respect that has nothing to do with the thing itself.

He should of never spliced plastic to that iron, Charlie said.

It held thirty years.

It held till it didnt.

That's all anything does.

Charlie looked at him. Then he laughed and shook his head and climbed up out of the trench. I'll get the saw, he said.

They cut the plastic back square and cut the iron back to where the wall rang sound under the hacksaw blade and the cutting took a long time and they took turns at it, one sawing while the other sat in the thin shade of the bank with his hat off. The heat came up out of the trench at them. Off in the orchard a mockingbird went through its catalog of other birds and quit and started again. When the iron came free Tom lifted out the cut section and hefted it and laid it up on the bank. Inside it was scaled near closed, the bore of it shrunk to half what it had been.

Look at that, Charlie said.

Yeah.

It's a wonder you had any pressure at all.

It's all like that. The whole iron run. From the well to the creek it's all like that.

Charlie was quiet a minute. He took the pipe and held it up and looked through it at the sky like a man sighting some instrument. Then he set it down.

Tom.

What.

You know what it would cost to relay that run.

I aint relaying it. I'm fixin this break.

You're fixin this break and next year you'll be fixin another one. Or this fall. And in two years there wont be enough water comin through that iron to keep a garden, let alone forty acres of trees.

Thirty-one, Tom said. There's nine acres of dead ones.

That's my point. You're makin my point for me.

I aint makin anybody's point. I'm tellin you the acreage.

They ate their dinner sitting on the tailgate in the shade of the liveoaks at the edge of the creek. Sandwiches Tom had made and a bag of peaches off the early Rangers, small and hard-fleshed from the dry spring but sweet. Charlie ate one and threw the pit out into the brush and wiped his hand on his jeans.

That's a good peach.

It's a fair peach. It ought to be twice that size.

It's a good peach, Tom.

The heat stood in the orchard. Down the long rows the air wavered above the ground and the trees stood motionless in it and the dead ones stood among the living with their bare limbs silver in the sun and not even the mockingbird singing now. Their father had planted the oldest rows the year Tom was born and there had been trees on that ground before that, his grandfather's trees, gone now, and the order of the rows ran back through the order of older rows the way a man's hand will hold the shape of his father's hand. Tom sat eating and looking out at it. He'd worked that orchard all his life save four years and he couldnt have said what it was he was looking at any more than a man can say what he sees in a fire.

Hollis called me again Thursday, Charlie said.

Tom didnt say anything.

You hear me?

I heard you.

He's gone to twenty-one hundred a acre. That's water rights and all. That's eight hundred and forty thousand dollars, Tom. Less the realtor.

There aint a realtor.

Less nothin then. Eight hundred and forty thousand dollars.

Tom folded the wax paper from his sandwich and put it in the bag. What's he want it for, he said. You never did say.

What difference does it make what he wants it for?

It makes a difference to me.

He wants to cut it up. Five and ten acre tracts. Sell em to people out of San Antonio that want a place in the country.

Ranchettes.

I dont know what they call em.

That's what they call em.

All right. That's what they call em then. Charlie got down off the tailgate and stood in the road. The trees aint his business, Tom. He aint buyin trees. He's buyin the well and the blacktop frontage and the view of the hills, and the day he closes he'll push every one of them trees into a pile and burn em and you and me both know it. And I'd as soon not watch it done either. But I been over the numbers and I been over em again and I cant make em say anything different. Mama's at eleven hundred a week up at that place. A week. The crop insurance is gone up again. The taxes is gone up because of what the land's worth now, which is a joke I'd laugh at if I could. The county appraises it like it was already ranchettes and we farm it like it was nineteen and sixty. You cleared what last year? Tell me the truth.

It was a bad year.

They's been six bad years. Tell me what you cleared.

Tom looked at him. I didnt clear anything, he said. You know I didnt.

You took money out of your own savings to make the spray bill.

Who told you that.

It dont matter who told me.

It was Bertha at the bank, wasnt it. She's got no business—

It dont matter who told me, Tom. Is it true or aint it?

Tom got down from the tailgate and walked a little ways out into the road and stood with his back to his brother. The hawk or another like it was up over the creek again. It is no small thing to know a piece of ground. A man can hold in his head every tree of forty acres, which ones bear heavy and which ones light, which took the cotton root rot first and in what order it would walk down the row taking the others, where the water stands after rain and where the deer cross and where his father stood when he showed him how to set a graft, the old man's thumb flat on the whipcut scion and his voice saying that the cambium had to touch, that it wasnt enough for the wood to touch, the wood was nothing, the life of the tree ran in a ring so thin you couldnt see it and if you didnt marry that ring to the other ring you had nothing but two pieces of dead wood bound up in tape and your hopes for them no different from hopes for the dead anywhere. A man can hold all that and there is no ledger for it and no column where it can be entered and no buyer obliged to count it in his price. He knew that. He'd always known it.

He turned around. Mama deeded it to both of us, he said. I cant sell without you and you cant sell without me.

I know how the deed reads.

Then there it is.

There it aint, Tom. Cause settin there bein stubborn dont pay the home in Brady and it dont pay the taxes and it dont put one drop of water through that pipe in the ground yonder.

I'll pay my half of Mama's. I always have.

Out of what? Out of savings? How long does that go on?

Long as it has to.

It cant go on long as it has to. That's what I'm tryin to tell you. There aint that much savings in the world.

A dustdevil came down the headland row spinning leaves and grit up into the bright air and crossed the road between them and fell apart in the brush along the creek and was gone. Charlie took off his hat and wiped his forehead on his sleeve and put the hat back on.

I aint your enemy, he said.

I never said you was.

You're standin there like I was.

I'm just standin here.

Charlie looked at the ground. Then he looked off down the rows. Daddy wouldnt of held on like this, he said. You think he would but he wouldnt. He sold them bottom forty in sixty-eight when the price was right and he never looked back at em. He wasnt sentimental about ground. He was sentimental about Mama and a certain bird dog and that was the end of the list.

He wouldnt of sold the orchard.

You dont know that.

I know it.

How do you know it.

Because he told me, Tom said. Settin in the truck at the gin in Fredericksburg, waitin on a norther to blow through. I was maybe fifteen. He said land was the only thing the Lord made that He didnt make a market in. He said the market in land was made by men and that men would tell you what your home place was worth the same as they'd tell you what your coat was worth, and that you could listen to em or not. He said the day you start hearin em is the day it's done sold, whatever the deed says.

Charlie was quiet. The cicadas filled up the silence.

He said that.

He said that.

Well, Charlie said. He didnt have a wife in Brady at eleven hundred a week, did he.

No, Tom said. He didnt.

They went back to the work in the worst heat of the day because that is when men go back to work who would rather not talk anymore. They sanded the iron back to bright metal and Tom set the dresser coupling and they drew the bolts down evenly, working opposite each other across the pipe, a half turn and a half turn, the way the old man had taught them, and then they cut the new section of plastic and primed the ends and the smell of the primer hung in the trench sharp as ether. Tom swabbed the cement on and they pushed the coupling home and held it.

Count it, Charlie said.

I'm countin.

You always cheat the count.

I never cheated a count in my life.

You cheated em when we was kids. Hide and go seek. You'd be off the porch fore I got to fifty.

That wasnt cheatin the count. That was countin fast.

Charlie laughed in spite of himself. They held the joint and the cement set and they let go of it carefully and looked at it.

What do you give it, Charlie said.

The glue?

The whole thing. The splice. The line. The orchard. The whole operation.

Tom sat back on his heels in the trench. Give it till evenin anyway, he said.

They backfilled by hand under and around the pipe, tamping the caliche in with the shovel handle so the line would bed even and not sag, and then they shoveled the trench full and mounded it and stood the rocks back on the bank where the water had moved them. It was past five when they finished. They loaded the tools and drove up the headland row to the well house and Tom opened the breaker and primed the pump and stood listening to it take hold, the old motor winding up and the gauge needle climbing and trembling and climbing.

What's she showin?

Thirty-one.

What'd she used to show?

Forty-five.

Charlie didnt say anything.

They drove back down to the creek and got out and stood over the filled trench and waited. There is a particular waiting that attends water in a pipe, an attention a man gives with his feet as much as his ears, and they stood that way for some time and then Tom walked out into the orchard to the first riser and opened the valve and the air came out of it coughing and spitting and then the water came, brown at first with the rust of thirty years and then clearing, and it ran out into the shallow furrow along the tree row and moved off down the row seeking its old courses, darkening the pale ground, and the smell of it on the dust came up to them, which is a smell that has no name but that every man raised on dry ground knows before he knows his letters.

She holds, Charlie said.

She holds.

They walked the line back to the creek and watched the trench but the ground stayed dry over the splice and they stood there a while anyway. Then they walked back up to the trucks. The sun was low over the hills to the west and the light had gone long and amber down the rows and the shadows of the trees reached row to row so that the living and the dead were joined in shadow whatever they were in the light. Doves were coming in to the water in the furrows, dropping down through the trees in twos and threes. Charlie leaned against his fender and lit a cigarette and they watched the doves.

Tom.

What.

I got to give Hollis some kind of a answer. He aint goin to wait the summer.

Then tell him no.

I cant just tell him no.

You can. You open your mouth and it comes right out. I'll say it slow so you can practice.

Charlie smoked. He looked at the end of the cigarette and then out at the trees. You aim to die out here, dont you, he said. Broke. Waterin dead trees. That's the plan.

They aint all dead.

That aint a answer.

It's the only one I got.

A coyote crossed the county road in the last light, trotting, its head sidelong to them, and dropped into the brush of the creek without hurrying and without looking back. Charlie watched where it had gone.

I'll tell you what, he said.

What.

Pull the dead ones this winter. Them nine acres. Sell Hollis the nine acres. It fronts the road, it's what he wants anyway. That carries Mama maybe two years and the taxes besides. And you put the well money, some of it, into relayin that iron run while there's still somethin to relay it for.

Tom stood looking down the rows.

It aint sellin the orchard, Tom. It's sellin nine acres of dead wood.

It's how it starts.

Everthing's how somethin starts. Supper's how dyin starts if you want to look at it thataway. You cant not act because actin leads somewheres. Everthing leads somewheres.

Tom didnt answer for a while. The water had reached the far end of the first row now and he could see the dark line of it lying along the ground in the dusk like something written there.

Daddy grafted half them dead trees hisself, he said.

I know it.

I was holdin the tape.

I know you was. I was there too, you know. It wasnt just you and him out here. I know everbody's got it told thataway, but it wasnt.

Tom looked at him. I never said it was, he said.

You never said it. Charlie dropped the cigarette and trod it out. Well, he said. He picked up his hat off the hood and put it on.

Tell Hollis the nine acres, Tom said. Tell him the nine and not a foot more, and tell him if I ever see a surveyor's stake one inch over that line I'll pull it and beat him with it.

Charlie smiled. I'll pass that along, he said. He opened the truck door and stood there. You want to come eat? Janie's got a roast on.

I better stay and watch the water.

The water dont need watchin. It's been runnin downhill a long time without you.

I better stay anyway.

All right.

Charlie got in and started the truck and then leaned out the window. That was a pretty good day's work for two old men, he said.

Speak for yourself.

I am speakin for myself. You're older'n me.

By eighteen months.

Older's older. He raised two fingers off the wheel and pulled out and Tom watched the taillights go down the county road and over the rise and the dark came up out of the creek and the first stars stood in the east. He walked back out into the orchard. The pump ran steady up at the well house, a small far drone like something the night itself was making, and the water moved in the furrows among the trees, and he walked down the row in the dark with the smell of the wet ground rising all about him and he put his hand out as he went and touched the trees one by one, the way you would touch the shoulders of men standing in a line, the living and the dead alike, because in the dark there was no telling them apart and no need to.
