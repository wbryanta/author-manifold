They rebuilt the Mirador in 1950, with steel joists and gypsum block and a sprinkler system that the Hales' own brochure described as the most advanced west of Chicago, and for twenty-one years I had managed not to see it. I want that understood at the outset. In twenty-one years I had been to Carmel and to Coronado and twice to Honolulu, I had stayed in hotels up and down the coast of California and signed the registers in my married name and let the boys carry my bags, and not once had I driven the seven miles off the highway to the bluff where the Mirador sits behind its windbreak of blue gum eucalyptus. I drove those seven miles on the fourth Saturday in June of 1970, because Cissy Hale was marrying a boy from Scottsdale named Gary Brevard in the rose garden there at half past five in the afternoon, and because my husband was at that time handling the Estero land partition for Cissy Hale's father, and because when the invitation came in April, engraved, the Garden of the Hotel Mirador, I read it at my own breakfast table and folded it back into its two envelopes and said nothing at all.

I was born Frances Mell in Niland, California, in 1930. My father followed the lettuce and then the cantaloupes and then did not follow anything, and in June of 1947 I went to work at the Mirador as a chambermaid, fourteen dollars a week and found, and slept with eight other girls in the staff annex under the mansard roof of the original building. On the fourteenth of March, 1949, at about four in the morning, the Mirador burned. Nine people died. Seven of them were girls I had eaten supper with that evening, ham and scalloped potatoes and canned pears, in the staff hall off the kitchen. I tell you this now so that you will understand that I am not a disinterested witness. I have never once claimed to be a disinterested witness.

I married, in 1954, a man named Carter Gates, who knew that I had worked, before business college, at a hotel that burned. Carter believed the hotel to have been in Bakersfield. I had never seen any reason to move it.

We arrived at five. There were boys in red vests parking the cars where the tennis courts used to be, and there was a girl at a skirted table handing out programs printed on heavy cream stock, and there was a new lobby, terrazzo, cool, with a stair that rose behind glass doors held open on magnetic catches. The old Mirador had an open staircase of varnished sugar pine that ran straight up the middle of the building like a flue, which is in fact what it became. The new doors release and close by themselves when the alarm sounds. I know about the magnetic catches because I asked the bell captain, a heavy man with a Marine Corps ring, and he told me at length and with evident pleasure, and Carter stood looking at his watch and wondering, I suppose, why his wife wanted to know about door hardware at a quarter past five on a wedding afternoon. "You're a funny one," Carter said, not unkindly, as we went out to the garden. Carter has said this to me at intervals for sixteen years. It is the closest he has ever come to a question.

They had planted the rose garden where the annex stood. I had worked this out from the road, before we ever turned in at the gates, by sighting on the water tower, which is the one thing on that bluff the fire did not take. The roses were Peace, mostly, with a border of something coral, and there were two hundred white folding chairs set up on the lawn, and a wind off the water that kept lifting the runner, and the mother of the bride, Nancy Hale, was fretting about the wind to anyone who would stand still for it. "It can't be helped, the wind," she kept saying. "June, you'd think you could count on June." From where Carter and I were seated I could count the fourth-floor windows of the new Garden Wing. There are twelve, and below each one a sprinkler standpipe runs down the wall, painted to match the stucco. The annex had five windows. I did not need to count those. The girl came down the aisle on her father's arm at five-forty and the wind dropped, as if bought off, and a soloist sang "O Perfect Love," and Cissy Hale, who was born two years after the fire, was married on the spot where Trinidad Olivares's bed had stood, third from the window, and nobody in two hundred chairs knew that but me, and I have no doubt that the line of the bride's veil was the prettier for it.

I will tell you about Trini Olivares now and then I am through telling. She was twenty-one in 1949, from Calexico, and she kept her tips in a Bull Durham sack pinned inside her slip because she did not trust the coffee can the rest of us used, and she wanted two things that she ever mentioned, a yellow dress and to see Mexico City. On the night of the thirteenth of March she left the annex window off the latch for me, because I asked her to. Remember that. She left the window off the latch because I asked her to.

The reception was in a yellow-and-white tent on the lower lawn, with a dance floor laid over the grass and a band from Santa Barbara and California champagne, the Hales being rich in the particular way that notices what champagne costs. I drank a glass of it and ate nothing and stood where I could see the water, and it was there, between the cake table and the bar, that I was found by a Mrs. Deane, of La Jolla, a cousin of the groom's mother, a small brown woman in green silk who had been to the Mirador many times and was eager that the Arizona people should understand its history.

"Of course you know about the fire," Mrs. Deane said.

"I've heard something about it," the groom's uncle said. He was a tall sunburned man named Walt Brevard, and he was looking over Mrs. Deane's head for the bar.

"Nineteen-fifty," Mrs. Deane said. "August of 1950. The whole hotel, gone, just like that, in an hour. Eleven people died. It was in Life magazine."

"Nine," I said.

Mrs. Deane looked at me. "I beg your pardon."

"Nine people. And it was March of 1949. Life never came." I heard my own voice and it sounded ordinary, a woman correcting a date, the way you might say the Petersons had bought the house in '61, not '62.

"I'm quite sure it was eleven," Mrs. Deane said. "There was a maid who carried a baby down five flights of stairs. She was given a medal by the governor."

"Three flights," I said. "The child was four years old, her name was Patsy Pruitt, and she bit. There was no medal. There was a luncheon at the Elks Hall in town, and the maid got a corsage and a fifty-dollar war bond."

There was a silence inside the noise of the band. Mrs. Deane was studying me with the expression of a woman who suspects she has been seated below the salt. "You sound," she said, "as if you'd been there."

"I've read the papers," I said. "I take an interest."

"Well, however many it was," Walt Brevard said, "it can't happen now. Boy Hale was telling me, this place is fireproof now. Completely fireproof."

Certain words set my teeth on edge. "Fireproof" is one of those words. "Miracle" is another, and we will get to "miracle," and "act of God" is three more. They are the words people use about a fire when the fire happened to someone else, and they remind me of the letter the Hale family sent in April of 1949 to each of its surviving employees, with a check for two hundred dollars, a letter that used the word "regret" once and the word "providence" twice and was signed by a secretary.

"They say," Mrs. Deane went on, lowering her voice into the register people save for delicious things, "that the reason so many of the help died was that the doors were locked. To keep the girls in at night, if you follow me. Of course that's just a story. Duncan Hale would never in this world—"

"The padlock was a Yale," I said. "Brass. The manager, Mr. Sutter, bought it at Hauser's Hardware in town the second week of January, because a girl had been seen on the outside fire stairs at two in the morning, and after that the fire door at the end of the annex was padlocked from ten at night until five-thirty, when the kitchen girls went down."

Nobody said anything. Out on the dance floor the band had gone into "True Love" and Cissy Hale Brevard was dancing with her father, her train looped over her wrist.

"What's all this about locked doors," Walt Brevard said. He had stopped looking for the bar.

"Ancient history, Walt," another man said, one of the Hale people, materializing the way they do. "Ancient history and barroom talk. There was an inquest, the whole thing was gone into."

"I'm sure it was," I said. In 1957 I wrote to the county and obtained the fire warden's report, which runs to eleven pages and finds that the fire originated in a sofa in the lounge, most probably from smoking materials, and traveled up the central staircase, and that the condition of the annex fire door "could not be established," the door having burned, and that the door "may have been obstructed." The word "padlock" does not appear in those eleven pages. I did not say this in the tent. I have learned what is and is not useful to say in tents.

"How could you possibly know a thing like that," Mrs. Deane said. "About a padlock. About a hardware store."

"Because I had occasion to be on those stairs," I said, and I excused myself, and I heard Mrs. Deane say behind me, to Walt Brevard, in the same delicious register, "Who is she," and I heard the Hale man say, "Carter Gates's wife, she's perfectly nice, Carter handles the Estero business," and that was that, the situation saved, because at a wedding the situation is always saved, that is what weddings are for.

I crossed the lobby with my empty glass in my hand. There is a door behind the new staircase that says STAFF ONLY and under it SOLO EMPLEADOS, and the trick with such doors is the same trick it always was, which is to walk through them as though you had a right to, and of course I had a right to, I had as much right to that door as anyone alive. The service stairs are concrete now, painted gray, lit with caged bulbs. I walked up four flights. I had on the wrong shoes for it, slingbacks, and I took them off at the second landing and carried them, and I want to say something here about shoes, but not yet.

The fourth-floor corridor of the Garden Wing is papered in a bamboo pattern and smells of nothing, which is the smell hotels buy now, and at the far end of it the red EXIT sign was lit over a steel fire door with a push bar, no key, no lock, no padlock, you lean on it and it opens, day or night, drunk or sober, awake or asleep, and I stood and looked at that push bar for what was probably a full minute. A girl was working the corridor with a turn-down cart, a girl of maybe nineteen in the gray uniform they use now, laying two foil-wrapped mints on each pillow, snapping off the bedspreads with the wrist motion you never lose, and she saw me standing there in my stocking feet with my shoes in my hand and asked was I lost, was I looking for the powder room.

"No," I said. "I'm not lost."

She waited, the way you wait on a guest who has been drinking, which is what she had decided I was, and I do not blame her, a woman alone in the staff corridor at eight in the evening holding her shoes. I asked her name. Her name was Imelda Reyes. I asked was she on until eleven and she said until eleven, yes, and I said the rooms looked very nice, and she said thank you, and there was nothing else to say that would not have been said entirely for my own benefit, so I said nothing else. I have thought since that I might have given her money, and I am glad I did not, because the money would also have been for my own benefit, and she would have known it before I did. She went on down the corridor with the cart, and I stood at the corridor window, the last one before the fire door, the window that in the old annex was the fifth window, Trini's window, third bed from the window, and I looked down at the tent glowing on the lawn like something radioactive and the dark line of the eucalyptus and beyond it the water, and I am going to tell you now what I remember about the fire, because people have asked me over the years—Carter has never asked, but people have—what I remember, and I usually say the smell.

What I remember is the windows.

I was twenty feet down the bluff path at ten of four in the morning, coming back from the Signal station road where a boy named Deke Forbes had parked his '41 Ford, and I was dressed and I had my shoes on, and I saw the light in the lounge windows and thought for one full second that someone had left the lamps burning. By the time I reached the lawn the staircase had it. I went in through the kitchen, which was still clear, and up the service stairs as far as three, because Mrs. Pruitt was on the lawn in a mink coat over her nightgown screaming that Patsy was in 308, and I got Patsy, who bit me on the collarbone and left a scar I have to this day, and I came down with her, three flights, not five, and handed her over, and then I turned around and looked up, and the annex girls were at the windows. I will not describe it past that. They were at the windows because the inside stair was fire and the fire door was padlocked, and the padlock was on the door because in January a girl had been seen coming up the outside stairs at two in the morning, and Mr. Sutter never knew which girl it was, and I am the only person now living who knows, and I have just told you everything I am ever going to tell you about why I take an interest.

Afterward there were nine days during which I was a heroine. MAID, 19, SAVES TOT IN HOTEL BLAZE. My mother cut the clipping out of the Press-Courier and kept it folded into her Bible at the Book of Daniel, and it was still there when she died, soft as cloth at the creases, and I have it now, in a drawer, with the war bond, which I never cashed. At the Elks luncheon they pinned the corsage on me, gardenias, and the county fire warden himself shook my hand, and everybody said it was a miracle I had been awake, a miracle I had had time to get my shoes on. That was the word they used at my end of the table, miracle, and at the Hales' end of the table I believe the words were act of God. Nobody in that hall, not the warden, not the Elks, not one reporter from one paper, ever asked the maid why she had her shoes on at four o'clock in the morning. I had an answer ready. I had it ready for years. The question has a statute of limitations, it turns out, like everything else in California, and the statute has run, and the answer is the one I gave you: Trini left the window off the latch because I asked her to, and if I had been in my bed where the padlock assumed I was, there would have been eight at the windows instead of seven, and that is the entire moral content of my survival, and I have never let anyone call it providence in my hearing since.

Before I went back down I unlatched the corridor window and raised it six inches. The night air came in off the water, eucalyptus and salt. I am aware that this unlatched no door for anyone, that the fire doors of the Garden Wing open at the touch of a hand, that the gesture saved no one and meant nothing and would have struck Imelda Reyes, had she seen it, as one more thing a drunken wedding guest might do. I did it anyway. I have done it, I will tell you now, in every hotel I have slept in for twenty-one years, in Carmel and in Coronado and twice in Honolulu, and Carter believes it is because I like air.

I put my shoes on at the bottom of the service stairs and came out through the STAFF ONLY door into the lobby, and old Mrs. Hale was there, Duncan Hale's widow, eighty-eight, parked in her wheelchair by the glass doors with a rug over her knees, waiting for someone to bring the car around. She put out her hand and caught my wrist, and her hand was like a bird's foot, and she looked up at me a long time.

"I know you," she said. "You were one of my girls."

"Yes," I said.

"You're the one who got the Pruitt child."

"Yes."

"God was watching that night," Mrs. Hale said. Her eyes were the pale boiled blue they go at the end, but they were steady on me, and I have wondered since exactly how much was in them. "God was watching over this house."

"Somebody was," I said, and I took my wrist back gently, because she was an old woman at her granddaughter's wedding, and the situation, as I have said, is always saved.

In the tent they were cutting the cake. Carter found me at the edge of the dance floor and put his hand at the small of my back. "Where in the world did you get to," he said. "You missed the toasts."

"Getting some air," I said.

"You're a funny one," Carter said, and we stood together and watched, and at nine o'clock Cissy Hale Brevard stood halfway up the new staircase, behind the glass doors held open on their magnetic catches, and threw her bouquet down into a froth of reaching girls, and a Brevard cousin from Tempe caught it, and everyone applauded, and the band played, and the doors did not close, because no alarm sounded, because nothing was burning, because the Mirador is fireproof now, everyone says so.
