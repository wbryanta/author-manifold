They worked until the light failed. The woman stayed at the edge of the grave through the afternoon, and when Anil glanced up from the pit she saw her there, motionless, her sari the colour of dried clay, so that she seemed at times a part of the earth that had been opened. At dusk the team covered the bones again with plastic and stones, and the woman watched this too, the way one watches a child being put to bed by strangers.

That night, in the room she shared with two other forensic specialists in the town of Nebaj, Anil wrote in her notebook by the light of a kerosene lamp. She did not write about the bones. She wrote about the woman's shoulder. The way grief had collected there, the way the body holds what the face refuses. She had learned this in her work—that the body remembers everything, that long after the voice is gone the bones keep talking. A fracture healed in childhood. The grooves in the teeth from a habit of holding pins or thread. The wearing of the right elbow from years of a single repeated motion. We are unfinished sentences, she wrote, and then crossed it out, embarrassed.

In the morning the woman was gone. The two men in the grave were not her husband and her brother. Anil never saw her again, but for years afterward, in laboratories in Oklahoma and in the cold basement morgues of other countries, she would find herself thinking of that posture, the kneeling, the elbows in the lap. As if there were a correct way to sit beside the dead. As if it could be taught.

Three years later, the letter came.

It arrived at her office in the form of a fax, the print already fading at the edges as if the message itself were reluctant. The Centre for Human Rights in Geneva was sending a forensic specialist to Sri Lanka at the invitation of the government—an invitation that everyone involved understood to be a gesture, a concession made to international pressure, a door opened just wide enough to be able to say later that it had been opened. The specialist would work for seven weeks with a local official. The specialist would investigate the reports of organized killings on the island. The specialist they had chosen was Anil Tissera, born in Colombo, citizen now of nowhere in particular, holder of a British passport, who had not set foot in her country for fifteen years.

She read the fax twice, standing by the machine, and then she went and stood at the window of the lab and looked out at the parking lot, the heat shimmering over the asphalt of the American Southwest, and thought: they have chosen me because I am a native. And then: they have chosen me because I am no longer a native. Both things were true, and she understood that the usefulness of her appointment lay precisely in the contradiction. She knew the language she had mostly forgotten. She knew the customs she had spent her adult life unlearning. She would arrive as an insider and an outsider at once, and each side would believe she belonged to the other.

On the flight from Heathrow she did not sleep. Somewhere over the Arabian Sea she took out the file they had given her and read again the careful, neutered language of the brief. Disappearances. Extrajudicial. Allegations. The words were like surgical instruments, designed to touch the wound without feeling it. She had spent years learning a similar vocabulary—blunt force trauma, perimortem, commingled remains—and she knew its purpose. You could not work if you wept. The language was a glove.

But beneath the language, on this flight, something else was moving in her. The plane began its descent in darkness and she saw, through the window, the lights of fishing boats scattered on the black water like a fallen constellation, and beyond them the long dark shape of the island, and she felt it before she could name it—not love, not homesickness, something older than either. The body remembering. She had swum in that black water as a girl. She had been famous for it, briefly, at sixteen, a swimmer, her photograph in the newspaper, the two-mile race at Mount Lavinia, the garland they put around her neck still wet from her wet skin. Her father driving her home afterward in silence, his pride too large for speech, his hand leaving the gearshift once to rest for a moment on her head.

Both her parents were dead now. They had died in a car accident on the Kandy road while she was in her second year at medical school in London, and she had not come back for the funeral. There had been reasons—exams, money, a marriage already failing—and she had repeated those reasons to herself for fifteen years until they wore smooth as river stones, and they still did not comfort her. There was no one left on the island who was hers. An ayah, perhaps, if the old woman still lived. A few cousins she would not recognize. She was returning to a country of strangers who would all claim to remember her.

The airport at one in the morning was bright and nearly empty. A man from the Ministry met her past customs, holding a sign with her name spelled wrong—TISSERIA—and she did not correct it. He was young and very polite and he asked her, in the car, whether she had been away long.

"Fifteen years."

"You will find it changed," he said, and then said nothing else for the length of the drive, and she understood that the sentence had been both a courtesy and a warning.

What she noticed first was not the change but the air. The window of the car was open a few inches and the air came in heavy and sweet, frangipani and diesel and the salt of the sea somewhere to the west, and underneath all of it the smell of rain on hot tarmac, and her whole childhood rose in her chest at once, undifferentiated, like water filling a glass. She closed her eyes. In the dark the checkpoints came every few miles—a barrier of oil drums and timber, a soldier's flashlight sweeping the back seat, resting on her face, on her hands, moving on. The young man from the Ministry handed papers through the window each time without being asked. No one spoke. The soldiers were teenagers. One of them, at the third checkpoint, was wearing rubber slippers below his fatigues, and she found she could not stop looking at his feet, the ordinary domestic feet of a boy, while the muzzle of his rifle traced its bored arc past the window.

This is the country now, she thought. Everyone awake at two in the morning. Everyone holding papers.

She was given quarters in a small guest house off Ward Place, and a laboratory—if it could be called that—in the disused wing of a hospital, two rooms with high ceilings and slow fans and a smell of formalin that had soaked into the very plaster. On the second morning she met the man who would be her counterpart, the official appointed by the government to work alongside her, to assist her, to accompany her—the brief had used all three verbs, and she had noted that none of them was trust.

His name was Lal Dissanayake. He was an archaeologist, in his late forties, employed by the Department of Archaeology, a man who had spent his professional life among the dead of the fifth and sixth centuries, among reliquaries and rock inscriptions and the brick foundations of drowned monasteries. He had a still, heavy face that gave away nothing, and hands—she noticed the hands first, as she always did—that were scarred and capable, a fieldworker's hands, the nails cut straight across. He wore his shirt untucked. He drank his tea without sugar, which in this country amounted almost to a political position.

"They tell me you were a swimmer," he said, when they had shaken hands.

"A long time ago."

"Here that is your identity. You could win the Nobel Prize and the Sunday Observer would write, 'former swimming star.'" He said it without smiling, and she could not tell if it was a joke, and this, she would learn, was the essential condition of speaking with Lal. Everything he said had a sealed compartment in it.

She did not trust him. She had decided not to trust him on the plane, before she knew his name, because he would be the government's man, the minder, the one whose reports would travel upward into ministries she would never see. He drove her to the sites in his ancient Land Rover and he answered her questions accurately and briefly and he watched her work with an attention that unsettled her, because it was not the attention of a policeman. It was the attention of a colleague. He knew bones. He had excavated them for twenty-five years. He simply knew a different century of them.

"In my work," he said once, as she lifted a fragment of pelvis from its bed of soil with a bamboo pick, "no one is waiting for the answer. The relatives are fourteen hundred years dead. There is a peace in that. You have chosen the noisier branch of the profession."

"I didn't choose it. It chose me," she said, and was immediately irritated with herself for the cliché, but he only nodded, as if she had said something exact.

In the third week they went south, to a sanctuary forest near a sixth-century monastic site, a government archaeological preserve where no one could enter without a permit, where Lal's own department had been conducting excavations of ancient burial caves. The Centre in Geneva had received reports—unverifiable, anonymous, the usual currency—of bodies being burned in that region, and the preserve appeared in two of the reports, and so Anil had asked to see it, expecting refusal, and there had been no refusal, only a delay of nine days while the permit moved through its offices, and she understood that the nine days might themselves be an answer.

The caves held skeletons, as the field reports had promised. Four of them, partially excavated, lying in the dry historical soil among shards of pottery and the small corroded ghosts of iron tools. Monks, probably, or lay devotees buried near holiness. The bones were the colour of the earth around them, stained through centuries, friable, eloquent in the way ancient bone is eloquent—speaking of diet, of arthritis, of long lives bent over labour or prayer.

Three of them were ancient.

She knew it within a minute of kneeling beside the fourth. She knew it before she could have defended the knowledge to a committee, the way you know a forgery in a museum—something wrong in the surface, in the weight of the light on it. The staining on the bone was not the deep, even amber of the others. It was superficial, ambitious, a coat of colour rather than a saturation. As if the bone had been dressed for the part. And there were other things: a hardness, a density under her fingers when she lifted the ulna, that fourteen centuries in soil should have leached away. The roots of the teeth still held their gloss. And there, on the left tibia, when she turned it to the light of her headlamp—a transverse fracture, perimortem, the bone broken green, broken in life or at the very edge of it, and around the ankle a faint circumferential abrasion of the kind that wire leaves.

She sat back on her heels. Outside the cave mouth she could hear birds, and Lal's trowel somewhere, and the ordinary afternoon going on. Inside her a door had opened onto a long corridor and she could see all the way down it: this skeleton was recent. This skeleton was perhaps five years old, perhaps three, and it had been placed here, in a government preserve, in a sealed historical site that only officials could enter, among the ancient dead, dressed in their colours, hidden in the one place no relative would ever think to kneel and wait.

Whoever buried this man here had a permit.

She did not say anything that afternoon. She labelled the skeletons one through four, in the neutral hand she used for everything, and she watched Lal as they packed the equipment, his heavy unreadable face, his scarred hands coiling the measuring tapes, and she thought: he is the government's man, and the government's men put this body here, and I am alone on an island where people disappear, and I have just found the thing I was sent to find, and the finding of it may be the most dangerous act of my life.

That night, at the rest house near the preserve, she could not sleep. The fan turned slowly overhead, stirring the mosquito net. Somewhere down the road a generator throbbed and went out, throbbed and went out. She lay in the dark and assembled what she had, the way she had been trained, evidence first, narrative later, and the evidence kept becoming narrative no matter how she held it. A man, mid-twenties to mid-thirties. A green fracture of the tibia. Wire at the ankle. Surface staining, applied—she would have to prove it was applied; there were tests, soil chemistry, the comparison of trace elements between the bone and its supposed grave—and if the soil in the bone's pores did not match the soil of the cave, then the bone had lived and died elsewhere and been brought.

Toward two in the morning she heard, through the thin wall, Lal's voice, low, speaking Sinhala, and the click of the rest house's only telephone being set back in its cradle. She lay rigid in the dark, translating. She had caught only fragments. The word for tomorrow. The word for nothing. The word—she was almost certain—for the fourth one.

In the morning he was on the verandah before her, drinking his bitter tea, looking out at the rain that had come overnight and turned the red road to a river of clay.

"You found something yesterday," he said. It was not a question.

She put her cup down very carefully, the way she had once set down a scalpel in an operating theatre when an instructor accused her of an error she had not made. "I found four skeletons. You were there."

"You found four skeletons and you photographed the fourth one eleven times and the others twice each." He still had not looked at her. The rain came off the verandah roof in a beaded curtain. "I am an archaeologist, Anil. I count things. It is the whole of my training."

"Then you tell me what I found."

He was silent for a long time. A crow landed on the railing, considered them both, and left. When Lal finally spoke, his voice had changed; it had gone down into some lower register she had not heard from him, quieter, and oddly younger.

"In 1971, during the first insurgency, I was a student at Peradeniya. My roommate was taken from the hostel at night by men in a jeep with no number plates. He was not political. He had once signed a petition about the price of meals in the canteen, that was the whole of his politics. We never found him. His mother came to Kandy every month for six years and sat in the office of the Assistant Superintendent of Police, and every month they gave her tea and told her the investigation was proceeding. She died in 1979. The investigation, I believe, is still proceeding." He turned and looked at her then, and his face was the same heavy unreadable face, but she saw now that what she had taken for blankness was a kind of containment, the face of a man who has been holding the same breath for twenty years. "You think I am here to watch you. You are correct. Everything you do is reported. What you must decide—and I cannot decide it for you, and I cannot prove anything to you, and you would be a fool to believe a proof if I offered one—is whether the watcher is watching for them or for himself."

"Whose telephone call was that, last night?"

"My brother's. He is a doctor in Colombo. In the accident ward." A pause. "He sees what comes in at night. He has seen it for ten years. I telephone him when I cannot sleep, which is often, and we talk about nothing, which is the only safe subject left in this country." He stood, and gathered his cup, and at the door he stopped without turning around. "The fourth skeleton. If it is what you think it is, then taking it out of that preserve will be like carrying a lamp through a house full of thieves. Everyone will see your face by its light. Think about who you tell, and in what order. The order is everything here."

They drove back to the caves through the rain. The road had washed out in one place and they waited an hour while a bullock cart was levered out of the mud ahead of them, and Anil sat in the Land Rover with the window down and watched the men working in the rain, their sarongs hitched up, laughing at the stuck cart, at the rain, at the foolishness of roads, and she thought about how a country could hold this, the laughter and the caves, in the same hand.

In the cave she worked all day and into the dusk, alone, while Lal kept the labourers at the far trenches. She took her soil samples. She took the cuttings of bone she would need, small as fingernail parings, from sites that would not compromise the skeleton's story. She mapped him—she had begun, privately, to call the fourth skeleton him, and then, because she had been trained by a teacher in Oklahoma who insisted that the unidentified dead be given working names, because a name made you careful, made the work an obligation rather than a puzzle, she gave him one. She named him Sailor, from the children's rhyme, tinker tailor soldier sailor, counted out on the buttons of a coat by girls in another hemisphere, in another life of hers. The fourth button. Sailor.

She lifted the skull last, in the failing light. It came free of the earth with the faint reluctant sound that she had heard in a dozen countries and that was the same in all of them. She held it in both hands. The arc of the cranium, the empty patience of the orbits. Somewhere this man had a mother, perhaps, still living, who lay awake in some village the way the woman in Guatemala had knelt in the grave, who was even now part of that vast silent population that waits. The half of the country that waits for the other half.

"Who were you?" she said, aloud, in the cave, in English, and then, surprising herself, in Sinhala, the phrase rising out of the buried part of her like a bubble through dark water, the language of her childhood, the language her ayah had scolded and comforted her in, and her own voice in that language was a stranger's, and it was hers.

Behind her, at the cave mouth, Lal stood with the lamp. He had come up quietly and she did not know how long he had been there. He looked at the skull in her hands and then at her face, and in the lamplight she saw him make a decision; she actually saw it, the way you see weather change.

"There is a man," he said. "In Colombo. He was my teacher. He is old now, and blind, and disgraced—they took everything from him over a question of evidence, a question very like the one you are holding in your hands. He lives in a forest monastery in the Grove of Ascetics, and he no longer speaks to officials, and he has forgotten more about reading the ground of this country than the entire Department will ever know." He lowered the lamp slightly, so that the light fell on Sailor's skull and their two shadows climbed together up the cave wall. "If you want to know how to carry the lamp through the house of thieves, he is the one who can teach you. But I warn you. He does not deal in certainty. He will not give you proof. He gave up on proof when proof was used against him. He deals in truth, which in this country is a different and much more dangerous substance."

Anil looked down at the skull, at the brown orbits filling with shadow.

"When can we go," she said.

It was not a question either, and Lal heard that, and for the first time since she had met him, he smiled—briefly, painfully, like a man flexing a wound to see if it still worked.

"Tonight," he said. "We drive at night now. It's safer. In this country, the dark has become the honest time."

They packed Sailor into the long padded crate between them, the way you would carry a sleeping child, and carried him out of the cave into the rain.
