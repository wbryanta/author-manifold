Anil knelt where the woman had knelt, and for a moment the warmth of the other body seemed to remain in the soil, in the pressed grass at the lip of the grave. She picked up her brushes and began again at the clavicle of the older man, working the dirt away in small strokes, and all afternoon she felt the woman's eyes on her hands, as if her hands were the only trustworthy things left in the country.

That was Guatemala. That was years ago.

Now, in the darkness of a plane crossing the Indian Ocean, Anil Tissera sat awake while the other passengers slept under their thin airline blankets. Below her, somewhere, was the water she had crossed at eighteen, going the other way, a scholarship girl with two suitcases and a swimmer's shoulders, certain she would never come back. Fifteen years. She had become a citizen of other places—of laboratories in Oklahoma, of mortuaries in Tucson, of the highlands where the dead were lifted out of the earth like roots. She carried a British passport and an American accent layered over the Sri Lankan one, and the Sinhala she had once spoken with servants and aunts had thinned to a child's vocabulary: words for food, words for weather, the phrase for *I don't understand*.

She had been sent by an international human rights organization in Geneva. The Sri Lankan government, under pressure, had agreed to a limited forensic investigation—one outside specialist, paired with one local archaeologist, seven weeks. Everyone involved understood the arrangement was theatre, a gesture made for foreign committees. Anil had read the file three times on the flight from Heathrow and knew almost nothing. Disappearances in all provinces. Bodies surfacing in rivers, in paddy fields, burning on roadsides at dawn so commuters would see them. No group claiming the dead, every group blaming another. The reports used a careful grammar, the passive voice of atrocity. *Were found. Were removed. Could not be identified.*

The plane began its descent in the last hour of night, and she saw the island appear below her the way it had appeared on the old schoolroom maps—a pendant, a tear, a pearl off the ear of the subcontinent, depending on which teacher was speaking. Then the lights of Negombo, the dark inland mass where there were no lights at all, and the sudden runway.

When the doors opened, the heat came into the cabin like a hand laid on her face. She had forgotten it, this air with weight, thick with kerosene and salt and something underneath that was just the smell of the country itself, vegetal and old. She walked down the metal stairs onto the tarmac at five in the morning and stood still for a moment while passengers moved around her.

*You were born here,* she told herself. It felt like information about someone else.

At the customs barrier a young official turned her passport over in his hands.

'Tissera. You are a Sri Lankan.'

'I was. I am. I've been away.'

'The famous swimmer,' he said, and smiled, and she was so startled she laughed. She had won a two-lengths race at Mount Lavinia when she was sixteen, her photograph in the *Observer* in a rubber cap, and apparently in this country that was still who she was. Not the forensic anthropologist. Not the woman who had testified in three languages about the angles of machete wounds. The swimmer.

'A long time ago,' she said.

'Nothing is a long time ago here,' the official said, stamping the page, and she would think later that it was the truest thing anyone said to her in her first week back.

The man from the Ministry was waiting beyond the barrier with her name on a card, the spelling slightly wrong. He drove her into Colombo as the light came up, past the lagoons and the fish stalls already laying out their silver catch, past a checkpoint where a soldier no older than seventeen looked into the car with a flashlight although it was nearly day, moving the beam across her face and her bags with a boredom that frightened her more than alertness would have. There were sandbags at the corners of certain streets. There were walls she remembered as white now grey with old rain, postered over with film advertisements and the faces of politicians, and over some of the faces someone had painted a single red line.

They had given her a small house off Ward Place, with a caretaker named Lalitha's nephew—he was introduced this way, as if his connection to a woman Anil had never met were his name—and a ceiling fan that turned with a hesitation in each rotation, a held breath, before continuing. She lay under it that first afternoon unable to sleep, listening to crows, and to a radio in another house playing a song she found she knew all the words to, words that had been waiting in her for fifteen years without her permission.

She met Sarath Diyasena two days later, in an office at the archaeology department that smelled of paper dust and cigarettes. He was in his late forties, heavyset, with a stillness about him that she could not at first read—either depth or concealment, she thought, and reserved judgment. He was the local half of the investigation, an archaeologist of some reputation, a man who had spent his career among the fifth-century monasteries of the dry zone, reading the island's history in brick courses and inscriptions and the pollen trapped in ancient mortar.

'They've given you me,' he said, 'because I know the ground. And possibly because I am considered safe.'

'Safe for whom?'

He looked at her for a moment, then turned to open a cupboard. 'That is the correct question. Hold on to it.'

He did not answer it.

He laid out maps for her, and the protocols, and the list of sites they were nominally permitted to examine—a list, she saw at once, composed of places where nothing would be found, old burial grounds, a beach where bodies had washed up two years before and been removed. Whatever was discoverable had been pre-selected to be useless. She had seen this in other countries. The investigation as inoculation: a small, controlled dose of truth administered so that the body politic could develop resistance to the real thing.

'And this,' Sarath said, almost as an afterthought, sliding a folder across the desk. 'A government reserve near Bandarawela. Caves. Sixth-century monastic burials, mostly. The site is restricted—you need a permit even to walk there, which means in practice only government personnel have had access for years. They've agreed to let us collect samples. Historical interest.'

'Why are you showing me this one as if it matters?'

He gathered the maps and did not look at her. 'Because the burials are sixth-century,' he said, 'and one of the skeletons recovered there has lead traces in the soil around it, and lead was not used in those interments, and because the bones, when I held them, were not old.' He said it flatly, the way she would learn he said everything that was dangerous. 'I should not have told you that here,' he added, glancing at the door. 'I have now told you anyway. So you know what kind of investigation this might become, if you want it to.'

She understood, sitting in that dusty office with the fan stirring the corners of the maps, that she was being offered a choice that was also a trap. A skeleton of recent date in a site accessible only to the government was an accusation written in calcium. To pursue it was to accuse. To accuse, in this country, in this year, was to put oneself and everyone near oneself within range.

'I want to see it,' she said.

They drove south and inland over two days, climbing out of the wet heat of the coast into the hill country, the air cooling and the light changing, tea estates stitched across the slopes in their dark green corduroy. Sarath drove without speaking for long stretches, and she found she did not mind; she watched the country go past and let it argue with her memory of it. At Kadugannawa they stopped and ate rice and curry off banana leaves at a roadside place, and he watched her eat with her fingers, watched the old skill come back into her right hand after one hesitant minute, and said only, 'So. Not entirely a foreigner,' and she could not tell if it was approval or warning.

In the caves at Bandarawela the air was cool and mineral and the flashlights made the walls swim. The historical burials lay in their niches, dust-coloured, the bones gone the texture of the rock around them, fifteen centuries of patience. And in the rearmost chamber, in a shallow scrape under a slab, lay the other one.

Anil knelt beside it and was quiet for a long time.

You could read a skeleton the way you read a letter from someone you had never met. The bones were a male, she thought, twenty-five to thirty-five years old at death, the long bones robust, the muscle attachments pronounced—a man who had worked with his arms and back. But it was the colour that spoke first. The historical bones in the outer chambers had the deep ivory-brown of true age. These were paler, the surface still slightly greasy under her gloved thumb. There was a partial fusion line still visible at the medial clavicle. There was soil staining that did not match the cave floor—this body had been buried elsewhere first, in earth, and moved here later, into a place where only certain people could go.

And there were the injuries. A healed fracture of the left forearm, years old, the kind a man gets raising his arm against a blow. Then the unhealed ones: hairline fracturing at the back of the skull, both hands damaged in a particular way she had seen before and hoped not to see again, and around the distal leg bones a faint encircling alteration of the surface, as if the legs had been bound and the body burned, incompletely, and then the burning given up on or interrupted.

'How long?' Sarath asked, behind her.

'Dead four to six years. Not fifteen centuries.' She sat back on her heels. 'Buried somewhere else, dug up, brought here. Someone went to trouble. You don't go to that trouble for a stranger, and you don't go to that trouble unless the first grave became unsafe.'

Sarath crouched and looked at the skeleton for a while. 'We should name him,' he said. 'For the records we'll keep the site code. But between us.' She had encountered this in every country—the teams always named them; you could not work for weeks over an unnamed person—and she had expected to be the one to suggest it.

'Sailor,' Sarath said. There were three other skeletons sampled from the outer chambers, already labeled in his notes with the rhyme she half-remembered from a childhood that had been partly English: Tinker, Tailor, Soldier. This was the fourth.

'Sailor,' she agreed, and the man on the cave floor stopped being evidence and became a task.

They removed him with a permit obtained for 'historical samples,' Sarath signing forms with his archaeologist's authority, both of them aware that the paper they signed was a kind of lie and that the lie was the only available container for the truth. They carried him out of the hills in a padded crate in the back of the jeep, and Anil rode the whole way with her hand resting on the crate's lid, a habit from Guatemala she had never broken—you did not let the dead travel unaccompanied.

In Colombo they were given laboratory space on a ship. This was Sarath's arrangement and it was, she had to admit, ingenious: the *Oronsay*, a decommissioned passenger liner rusting at anchor in the harbour, its first-class lounge stripped and fitted with trestle tables, the portholes giving a light that moved with the water. No ministry official would casually wander through. The generator ran their lamps and the small centrifuge; the bar of the old lounge, still bearing its brass rail, held trays of bones. At night the ship creaked and shifted, and Anil, working late, would feel the floor lift almost imperceptibly under her, the harbour swell passing beneath the dead like slow breathing.

She laid Sailor out in anatomical order on the long table and began the work she knew how to do, the work that was the same in every country and every regime: the patient interrogation of what remains. The skeleton would tell her how he died; it was already telling her. What she needed, what would turn a body into a witness, was who. A name was the one thing a skeleton could not say about itself. Everything else—age, labour, diet, injury, the rice-and-dried-fish chemistry of his bones, the particular wear on his ankle joints that she stared at for a full evening before she understood it—everything else accumulated on her clipboard in her small exact handwriting.

The ankle wear puzzled her until Sarath brought a friend to the ship, an old teacher of his named Palipana's student—everyone here was introduced through their teachers, their dead, their connections—a man who had grown up near gem pits. He looked at the casts she had made of the ankle joints and the curious symmetrical squatting facets, the stress markers in the lower spine, and said: 'This man worked crouched, in a low space, for years. And see the arms. He lifted above his head.' A miner, perhaps. Or a man who worked the pits—gem pits, plumbago mines, the graphite shafts down south. She thought of the folk song Sarath had recited to her one evening on the deck, half-singing it, embarrassed: *I went down the pits seventy-two fathoms deep, invisible as a fly, not seen from the pit head.* Only when I return to the surface is my life safe. Sailor had returned to the surface. His life had not been safe there either.

So they had a probability: a labourer, a pit worker, from a mining district, disappeared four to six years ago, his body buried, exhumed, and reburied in a government-controlled site. There were perhaps three regions he could have come from. There were lists—unofficial, kept by mothers' organizations and a few brave lawyers—of the disappeared from those regions, hundreds of names per district, each name trailing its small documentation: the date, the witnesses, the colour of the jeep that came at night, the things the men who came had said or not said.

It was at this point, Anil knew from other countries, that an investigation stopped being science and became danger. Bones were tolerated. Names were not. A skeleton with a name had a family, a funeral, a photograph in a newspaper, a court case, a minister forced to answer a question. A skeleton with a name was a stone dropped into the machinery.

'You understand,' Sarath said one night, the two of them sitting on the *Oronsay*'s deck with the lights of the harbour around them and the curfew silencing the city beyond, 'that I am not certain what I will do, if we find his name.'

She turned to look at him. 'What does that mean?'

'It means I live here. You will write your report and fly to Geneva and the report will be read aloud in a room with good lighting, and it will be true, and you will be safe. I will still be here. My brother will still be here. The people who buried Sailor twice will still be here.' He smoked for a while. 'I am not telling you I will betray the work. I am telling you that in this country the truth has to be handled like an explosive. You don't carry it openly through a crowd. You decide who can be near it, and when, or it kills the wrong people.'

'In my experience,' Anil said, 'the truth that's handled carefully gets handled until it disappears. I've watched it happen. Committees, redactions, the right moment that never arrives. The careful men were always, in the end, on the side of the burial.'

'And in my experience,' Sarath said, without raising his voice, 'the foreigners who insist on daylight go home afterwards. We bury the people the daylight burned.'

Neither of them apologized. It was the central argument of her time in the country and it was never settled, and years later she would understand that it could not be settled, that they had each been right in the way that people are right from inside their own danger.

She kept working. She took Sailor's skull to a man Sarath knew, an artist named for these pages Ananda, a gem-pit man himself once, who had lost his wife to a disappearance and lost himself afterward to drink, and who had, before all that, been a craftsman in the old tradition—an eye-painter, one of those who performed the final act on a holy statue, painting the eyes that brought it to life. Now he sat in a shed up-country with the skull on a stand and built a face onto it in clay, working from her tissue-depth markers, working drunk and then sober and then drunk, and what he produced at the end of those weeks was not, she saw immediately, a reconstruction. The technique was right but the face was wrong: it was too peaceful. It was a face without the last hour in it. He had given Sailor the expression of a man asleep beside his brother on a mat in the afternoon, and she stood in front of it and could not speak, because she understood that Ananda had not been able to make the dead man's face and so had made instead the face he needed the dead to have—had made, perhaps, his wife's peace, since no one had ever let him see her, alive or dead, again.

It was wrong as evidence. It was true as something else. They photographed it anyway and carried the photographs through the mining villages of three districts, Sarath asking the questions because her Sinhala marked her, and in the fourth village a woman looked at the photograph for a long time, the way the woman in Guatemala had looked into the grave, and said a name.

Ruwan Kumara. A toddy tapper who had hurt his ankle in a fall and gone to work in the local pit, who had been taken at night from the village by men whose faces were covered, after a billa—a hooded informant, a village ghost with eyeholes cut in his sack—had pointed him out as a sympathizer. There was a date. There were witnesses still living. There was, somewhere in a government record, because there always was, a notation.

Anil sat that evening with the name on her clipboard, under all the measurements, and felt the weight of the completed thing. This was what she had come for, what the organization in Geneva would call a representative case. One identified victim, chain of custody intact, recovered from a site only the state could enter. Representative. As if Sailor stood for the thousands, when the whole truth of him was that he stood for no one; he was Ruwan Kumara and nobody else, a man with a healed forearm and worn ankles whose wife had waited and whose name had taken four to six years and three sciences and one ruined artist to travel from a cave floor to a page.

'Now,' Sarath said quietly, reading it over her shoulder, 'comes the difficult part. Now we decide who we tell, and in what order, and what it costs.'

Beyond the portholes the harbour was dark, the city under curfew, the island going on with its triple war in the silence. Anil looked down at the skeleton on the table, complete now, named now, and thought of the families at the edge of the Guatemalan pits, spelling each other so that someone was always present, so that the evidence would not be lost again. She had believed then that her work ended where theirs began—that she delivered the dead to the living and moved on. She understood now, on this ship, in this country that was and was not hers, that there was no such border. The vigil was not something you attended. It was something you entered, and did not afterward entirely leave.

She drew the lamp closer and began, in her small exact handwriting, to write the report.
