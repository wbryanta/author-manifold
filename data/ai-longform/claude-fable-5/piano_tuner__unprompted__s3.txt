The fog was still on the fields when Emmett Voss carried his case out to the truck, and the case made the same sound it had made for fifty-one years, the soft knock of the tuning hammer against the wooden partition, the dry whisper of the felt strip coiled in its tin. He set it on the passenger seat where his wife used to ride and where, for a few years in the middle of his life, his son had ridden, knees up against the dash, working a pitch pipe like a harmonica until Emmett told him to quit before he went sharp in the head. The seat had a long crack in the vinyl now, mended twice with tape. Everything in the truck had been mended twice. That was the trade he was in. You did not replace a thing while it could still be brought back into tune.

He had four stops written on the back of an envelope, though he did not need the envelope. He could have driven the route asleep, and some mornings lately he believed he had. Pettibone, Schoeller, the Andresen girl, and then the church. He had arranged them that way on purpose, the way you arrange a program, the difficult piece last, when the hands are warm and the audience has forgiven you in advance.

The Pettibone place sat at the end of a gravel lane lined with black walnut trees that dropped their fruit on the roof of the truck every October like applause from somebody who didn't mean it. Lorna had the porch light on though the sun was already a hand's width over the soybeans. She came out in her cardigan before he had the door open, the way she always did, as if she'd been standing behind the curtain since six.

"I made the coffee strong," she said. "You're getting thin, Emmett. You're getting thin like an old fence."

"Fence keeps standing," he said.

The Kimball upright stood against the north wall of the parlor where it had stood since Eisenhower, walnut case gone honey-colored in the window light, and on its closed lid a city of photographs in brass and silver frames: Lorna and Walt at the lake, Walt in his Army uniform, the daughters in graduation gowns, grandchildren Emmett had watched grow up an inch a year in those frames without ever once meeting them. He moved the photographs the way he always did, in order, setting them on the sofa face-up so they could supervise, and Lorna watched him do it from the kitchen doorway with her hands folded under her apron.

"Lois says I ought to sell it," she said. "She says nobody's touched it since Walt."

"Walt never touched it either," Emmett said, lifting the lid. The smell came up at him, the smell that was the same in every piano and different in every house — felt and old glue and cold iron, and under it something of the family, cigarette smoke or bread or in this house the lavender Lorna had kept in little sachets in the bench since her mother taught her to. "Walt sat on the bench and read the paper."

"He liked it tuned, though."

"He did."

"He said a piano out of tune was like a clock stopped. Said it made the whole house feel like somebody'd died in it."

She heard herself, and Emmett let her hear it without turning around, gave her the kindness of his back while he laid the felt strip along the strings of the temperament octave, working it down between the trichords with his thumb so that only the middle strings would speak. He struck his fork on his knee and set its stem against the soundboard and the A came up through the wood like something rising in water, and he played the piano's A against it and listened to the two of them disagree.

It was not bad. Eight cents flat, maybe ten. A piano nobody plays goes flat slowly, evenly, all together, like a congregation aging in the same pews. It is the played pianos that go wild, the ones children hammer, the ones that sit by woodstoves. Untouched, a piano just settles, a half step a decade, dreaming downward.

He brought the A up and felt the pin turn in the block, that good firm creep, no jumping, the block still sound after seventy years because Lorna kept water in a jar inside the bottom panel all winter the way he'd taught her, and he set the pin with a little ease-back, the way you set a nail, and went on. Fourths and fifths, the fifths narrowed until they wavered slow as a porch swing, the thirds let run fast, seven beats, eight, the old arithmetic that nobody had ever asked him to explain and that he could not have written down to save his life but that lived in his hands like the multiplication table. Lorna brought the coffee and set it on the windowsill where it always went, far from the instrument, and sat in Walt's chair to listen, because to Lorna the tuning was the concert. He knew that. He had known it for years. There were a dozen widows on his route and the tuning was the concert for all of them, the only music those parlors got, a man in his eighties striking one note forty times.

When he finished he pulled the strip and played the unisons clean, then ran a few chords up the keyboard, C major in big handfuls, and the old Kimball rang like itself, a little tubby in the bass, sweet in the middle where the family had lived.

"There he is," Lorna said softly, as if the piano were somebody who'd been away.

He put the photographs back in their order. The Army picture last, top of the lid, left side, facing the window. She paid him with a check she had written before he arrived, and pressed a foil-wrapped loaf of zucchini bread on him at the door, and at the door she said, "Same time next year, then," and he heard the question under it, because word had gotten around the way it does, the feed store and the church bulletin and the daughters all telephoning each other.

"Lorna," he said.

"I know it," she said. "I know it, Emmett. I just wanted to hear what you'd say."

"I'd say keep the water jar filled."

"That's what I figured you'd say." She held the screen door against her hip. "Fifty years you've been coming up this lane."

"Fifty-one."

"And every time you leave, the house sounds right for about a week. Like it's holding its breath in the right key." She looked past him at the walnut trees. "You tell me what I'm supposed to do with a house that's gone flat."

He didn't have anything for that, and she knew he didn't, and she patted his arm to release him from it, and he went down the steps with the bread warm against his ribs like something alive.

The Schoeller place was eleven miles east, past the grain elevator and over the low bridge where the creek ran tea-colored under the willows. The Schoellers themselves were three years gone to Phoenix, the kids grown, the farm rented to a corporation that planted it lot-line to lot-line and kept no animals, but Dale Schoeller sent Emmett a check every spring with a note that said the same thing every spring: Couldn't get the piano on the truck. Keep her honest till we figure it out. Key's where it always was. They were never going to figure it out. Emmett knew it and likely Dale knew it, and the check was a kind of pension, or a kind of prayer, money sent back to keep one thing in the old house from dying.

The key was under the third flowerpot, which held the gray dirt of a geranium dead three winters now. Inside, the house had the cold even on a warm day that empty houses have, a cold that is really silence, and his boots were loud on the floors. The piano was in the front room under a bedsheet, a tall old Hamilton upright, and when he drew the sheet off, dust climbed up through the window light in slow galaxies.

A mouse had been in it. He could smell that before he opened it, and when he took the bottom panel off he found the nest in the corner by the pedal trapwork, milkweed fluff and chewed paper, abandoned, and one hammer in the tenor with its felt gnawed to the wood, and the D below middle C dead — string broken at the becket, the wire curled up in the piano's belly like a question mark. Forty years ago he'd have clucked his tongue and written an estimate. Now he sat down on the floor with his legs out in front of him like a boy, in the dust, in the light, and spliced the string, because he had the wire in the truck and the afternoon was his and nobody on earth was waiting for him.

The splice took him most of an hour, his fingers slower at the loop than they used to be, the eye needing the window light at just such an angle. While he worked he talked a little, the way he had gotten to doing in the empty houses. Not to anyone. To the piano, maybe. To the work.

"Daniel could hear a broken string from the yard," he said. "Come in from the yard and say, Dad, the D's gone, before he had his boots off."

The Hamilton said nothing, which was the right response and the one Emmett expected.

The boy had ears like that from the beginning. Four years old, and Emmett would strike the fork and Daniel would hum the A before the fork had stopped saying it, hum it dead center, and Emmett's wife Alma would look up from the sink with her face doing something complicated. By eight he rode along in the summers, and Emmett taught him the way you teach anything that matters, which is by letting the boy stand close and keep quiet. Listen for the beat, he'd say. Two strings can both be wrong and there's no beat. Two strings can be wrong together as smooth as you please. The beat only comes when one of them is trying to be right. And Daniel, eight years old, had looked up at him sideways and said, Then the beat's a good sign, isn't it, and Emmett had had to turn his face to the pin block because there was nothing wrong with what the boy said, nothing at all, except that it was wiser than anything Emmett had ever managed in fifty years, and it had come out of his own kitchen.

He brought the spliced string up to pitch slowly, in stages, the way you have to with new wire, letting it stretch and settle and stretch again, and then he tuned the whole piano, every note, the full two and a half hours of it, in a house where no one would ever hear it, for a man in Phoenix, for a mouse if the mouse came back, and when he finished he played a few bars of nothing in particular and the Hamilton stood there in the empty room sounding clean and ready, like a table set in a house where no one would ever again sit down to eat. He put the sheet back over it. He swept the nest out with the whisk broom he kept in the case for exactly this, locked the door, put the key under the dead geranium, and stood on the porch a while looking at the corn coming up in rows that went all the way to the windbreak without a fence or a path or a single thing a child could climb on.

The Andresen stop was the one he had added late, ten years back, and it was the only stop on the whole route where the piano got played. Karen Andresen had been a student of the music teacher in town before the school cut the program, and she'd bought old Mrs. Holt's spinet at the estate sale for forty dollars, and now her daughter Mae was nine and took lessons by video call from a woman in Minneapolis, which Emmett did not pretend to understand but had decided not to disapprove of, since the alternative was nothing.

Mae was waiting on the steps when he pulled in, and she had a list. She always had a list. She kept a piece of notebook paper folded in the bench all year and wrote down crimes as the piano committed them.

"The B flat above middle C buzzes," she read, walking backward into the house ahead of him, "but only when you play it soft, and the highest E sticks when it's humid, and there's a note in the bass that sounds like two notes."

"Which one."

She went to the spinet and played it, a G, and she was right, the unison had drifted and the note had a shimmer in it like a coin spinning on a counter.

"What do you call that," Emmett asked, setting his case down, "when it sounds like that?"

"A beat," Mae said, in the voice children use for things too obvious to dignify.

"And what's a beat?"

"It's when one of the strings is trying to be right."

He had told her that, of course, some visit or other, and she had kept it the way children keep things, without ceremony, in the same pocket as a lucky rock. He got out the fork and the hammer and the mutes, and she pulled a kitchen chair up beside him at the distance he had taught her, close enough to see, far enough that her breathing wouldn't be in his ear, and for an hour and some she watched his hands and asked her questions, why the fifths had to be a little wrong so the whole thing could be a little right, why he eased the pin back after pulling it up, why he hit the key so hard — to settle the string, he told her, you have to hit it harder than any music ever will, so it has nowhere left to slip to — and the questions were Daniel's questions, every one of them, asked in the same order, as if there were only one ladder up into this and every child found the same rungs.

Karen came in from the garden with dirt to her elbows and paid him in cash and walked him out, and on the gravel, out of the girl's hearing, she said, "Is it true you're done after today?"

"It's true."

"Who do I call?" She said it plainly, a practical woman, but he heard the size of it. The nearest tuner he could honestly recommend was ninety minutes away and charged for the drive.

"I'll send you a name," he said. "He's good. Young. He uses a machine, but he checks it with his ears, which is the right order."

Karen looked back at the house, where through the window they could both see Mae already on the bench, already playing, the spinet bright as new money. "She wants to do what you do," Karen said. "She told her teacher. The teacher said be a pianist, and Mae said no, the other thing. The fixing thing."

"It's a poor living," Emmett said, because it was the truthful thing to say, and then, because the truthful thing was not the whole truth, "Tell her to come find me in the fall. I've got tools that ought to keep working after I quit."

The light was going long and yellow when he came into town, and the church threw its shadow most of the way across the road. First Lutheran, white clapboard, the steeple in need of paint, the same notice board out front with the movable letters, and somebody had changed it for Sunday: BE STILL AND KNOW. He had a key to the side door on his ring, had had it for forty years, given to him by a pastor three pastors back, and the door stuck the way it always stuck and gave the way it always gave.

The sanctuary held its evening light the way a jar holds water, the windows on the west side going amber and rose, the pews in their long quiet rows. The piano stood down front to the left of the altar rail, a Yamaha grand, five foot something, bought by subscription in 1974, a hundred families giving what they could, and he had tuned it twice a year since the day it came off the truck. He had tuned it the week before the Christmas programs and the week before Easter and before weddings and, more times than he wanted to count, before funerals, including two he could not think about directly even now, but had to come at sideways, the way you look at something bright.

He set his case on the front pew and opened the lid to full stick and the church's own smell joined the piano's — candle wax and hymnals and the cold-stone smell that even wooden churches have.

Daniel had been eleven. Miss Pruitt, who taught half the children in the county in her front room, held her recitals here every June because she had more students than parlor, and the families filled the first eight pews with their dresses and their good shirts, and the children sat in the front row in a line with their feet not reaching the floor, dying in their separate ways. Daniel had gone fourth. The Bach prelude, the C major, the one that is only broken chords from beginning to end, the one people call easy who have never had to walk it across a stage in front of everyone they know. Emmett had tuned this piano two days prior and then come back the morning of and checked the unisons again, every one, because his boy's hands were going to be on it. He could still see him crossing from the pew, the white shirt Alma had ironed, the bulletin rolled into a tube in his fist until Miss Pruitt eased it away from him, the bench too far back so that he had to drag it in with that honking sound across the floor and everyone laughed, kindly, and Daniel went red to his collar.

And then the boy put his hands down and the church got the music.

He played it too fast at the start, nerves running it like water downhill, and then somewhere in the middle — Emmett could find the exact measure even now, could have put his finger on the page — he settled. He slowed into it. He started to listen to the building, you could see it happen, see him hear the way the room held each chord a half-second after he let it go, and he began leaving room for that, playing with the church instead of at it, and Emmett had sat in the fifth pew with Alma's hand digging into his and understood that his son was having a conversation with the air, and that nobody had taught him to, and that everything Emmett knew about strings and pins and beats was just the carpentry under what the boy was doing.

The applause had embarrassed Daniel almost to death. He'd bobbed his head like a chicken and fled to the front pew.

And that was all of it. That was the whole career. Not because of anything — that was the thing Emmett had never been able to make peace with, that there was no reason in it, no failure, no scolding teacher, no cruelty to point at. The boy just set it down. Twelve, thirteen, the lessons petered out, the piano at home went quiet, and when Emmett asked him, careful, oh so careful, asking it the way you reach toward a bird, Daniel had shrugged and said, I like hearing it more than making it, Dad. I like your part better. And he had gone on riding along in the summers, learning the pins and the wire and the felt, and he was good, his ear was better than Emmett's, everyone said so, Emmett said so, and there had been an understanding between them that had no paper to it but was as solid as a deed: the route would be his. Voss & Son, though they never had cards printed. They had joked about the cards. Daniel was twenty, and it was February, and the bridge over the creek — the same low bridge, the willows bare that time of year — had ice on it that didn't look like ice, and the county put up a guardrail the following summer, which Emmett drove past four times a year, every year, on his way to the Schoellers'.

He struck the fork against his knee and set it on the piano's plate and the A rose up pure and patient, four hundred forty times a second, as it had risen for him in parlors and churches and empty houses for half a century, the one thing in his life that had never drifted, never settled, never gone flat with grief or weather. He laid the strip. He began.

He took his time. There was no one to wait for him and the light was in no hurry either, sliding from the west windows up the wall as the sun went down, and he tuned the church piano the way you'd want the last thing you do to be done, every interval weighed twice, the unisons drawn so close they disappeared into single voices, the bass strings rolled and seated, the high treble — which he could no longer fully hear, he knew that, had known it for two or three years — tuned by feel and faith and the arithmetic in his hands, trusting the ladder he'd climbed ten thousand times to hold him on the rungs he could no longer see.

It was almost dark when he finished. He should have wanted the lights but he didn't. He sat down on the bench, dragged it in — it made the same honking sound across the floor, and he smiled at that, alone in the dark church, he actually smiled — and he put his hands on the keys and played the prelude. Badly. Slowly. His fingers were a tuner's fingers, strong and stiff and full of the wrong knowledge, and he dropped notes and re-found them, but the chords were only broken chords, the piece is only an unfolding of what a piano already wants to say, and the building did what it had done that June, held each handful of sound a half-second past his letting go, so that the church played along with him the way it had played along with the boy, and in the spaces the room left ringing, Emmett listened, because he liked hearing it more than making it; he had always liked the other part better, the fixing part, and somewhere in the middle of the piece he slowed into it and stopped counting and let the air have its say.

When it was over he sat with his hands in his lap until the last sound was entirely gone, which took longer than anyone would believe.

He packed the case. At the door he stopped, came back, and took the tuning fork out of his pocket and laid it on the music desk, where the next person to lift the lid would find it and wonder. Then he let himself out the side door, which stuck and gave, and stood a moment in the churchyard. The fields were losing the last of their color. Behind him the piano held its pitch in the dark, perfectly, for no one, for everyone, the way a kept promise holds whether or not anybody comes to collect it, and Emmett Voss got into his truck, set the empty space on the seat beside him in order, and drove home down a road he could have driven asleep, and maybe did.
