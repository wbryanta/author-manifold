The cannery whistle had blown at noon for sixty-one years, long enough that the gulls of Nessel Bay had bred its sound into their bodies, rising off the breakwater in a gray sheet a few seconds before it sounded, as if they could hear the steam gathering in the pipe. Esko Maki had set his watch by it as a boy and as a man and now as an old man, and on the first Monday of November, when the notice went up on the door of the Pacific Crown plant — typed, brief, the corporate seal like a thumbprint pressed into the page — he stood with the others in the parking lot and read it twice and then looked at the gulls, who knew nothing, who would go on rising at noon long after there was no whistle to rise for.

End of operations, March 31. Sixty-one years, and they gave it a date the way you'd give a date for a dentist's appointment.

His daughter Maren worked the line, had worked it since she was nineteen, and when she came home that evening to the house on Delling Street where the three of them lived — Esko in the back room that had been his and Aila's, Maren and the girl in the rooms upstairs — she set her hairnet on the counter and said nothing, and Esko, who had spent thirty-one years learning when not to speak on the bridges of other men's ships, said nothing either. It was the girl who spoke. Della was eleven that winter, long-boned and watchful, with her father's habit of standing in doorways as though deciding whether a room deserved her. She said, "Is it true about the plant?" and Maren said it was, and Della looked at her grandfather, and Esko understood the look, because he had been waiting for it for some time without admitting that he was waiting.

He took her out for the first time the following Saturday, in the sixteen-foot skiff he kept on a mooring inside the breakwater, an aluminum boat with a forty-horse outboard and a sun-bleached orange float cushion and nothing else of comfort about it. Maren stood on the dock with her arms crossed inside her coat. She had not said yes so much as she had stopped saying no, which Esko knew was as close to a blessing as he would get from a woman whose husband had gone down with six other men on the Aleutian Maid four winters past, in water so far north it might as well have been another planet, water that had given back two survival suits and a hatch cover and nothing else. Maren did not trust the sea and she was right not to. Esko did not trust it either. That was the first thing he meant to teach the girl, though he would never say it in those words.

"Life jacket stays on," Maren said. "I don't care if she's hot. I don't care if it chafes."

"It stays on," Esko said.

"And back by two."

"By two."

She looked at him a moment longer, and in her face he saw the whole argument they were not having — that the town was finished, that the water had taken enough from this family, that there was no future in any of it and so what exactly was he teaching the girl for. He had no answer that would survive being spoken aloud. He had only the feeling, lodged under his sternum like a swallowed bone, that a person ought to leave knowing the place she left. And one other thing, which he had told no one, which the doctor in Coos Bay had explained to him with a laminated diagram of an eye: that the centers of his vision were silting in, slowly, the way a channel silts when the dredge stops coming, and that within a few years he would see the world only at its edges. He could still read the water. He could not always, anymore, read a face across a room.

The morning was cold and bright, a north wind laying the bay down flat in the lee of the hill and corrugating it further out, and as he ran the skiff at half throttle past the moored trollers — a third of them for sale, their hulls greening at the waterline — he began the way his own grandfather had begun with him, in Finnish first and then in English, off a herring boat in another century.

"What do you see," he said.

Della looked at the water. "It's darker over there," she said, pointing toward the channel.

"Why."

"Deeper?"

"Deeper," he agreed. "Dark water is deep water, mostly. Not always. What else."

She studied it. He liked that she took her time. Her father had been quick, too quick, a boy who answered before the question finished, and Esko had loved him for it and worried for it and in the end the quickness had not mattered one way or the other, because no quickness saves you when a forty-foot sea boards a crabber iced to twice her weight. He pushed the thought down where he kept it.

"The water's all wrinkled there," Della said, "and smooth there. Like somebody ironed part of it."

"Wind," Esko said. "The smooth part is the lee of the hill. Wind can't reach it. You see a slick like that on open water, no hill around, you ask yourself why. Could be a tide line. Could be fresh water sitting on salt, where the creek comes in. Could be fish oil, if the bait's thick and something's been chopping it up from below. The water doesn't do anything for no reason. That's the whole lesson. Everything else is just learning the reasons."

She nodded slowly, filing it. He throttled back where the channel bent past the number-five buoy and let the skiff drift, and he showed her how the kelp lay over in the current like a dog's ear, all the fronds streaming the same direction, and how you could read the set of the tide from a hundred feet away by it. He showed her the seam where the ebb ran against the wind and the chop stood up short and steep and mean, and the slack water ten feet to either side of it, and he told her that a seam like that on a big scale, on a bar, on an inlet, was where boats died, because water fighting wind makes seas that break in deep water where no sea has any business breaking.

"You piloted ships through that," she said. It was not quite a question. She had heard the stories the way children hear stories, as furniture, there in the house her whole life without being looked at.

"Through the bar at the river mouth, yes. Thirty-one years."

"Were you scared?"

"Every time I wasn't scared, I made myself scared on purpose," he said. "Fear is just attention with the volume turned up. A pilot who isn't afraid of the bar has stopped paying attention, and the bar will notice before he does."

She thought about that while the gulls dropped and lifted around a troller cleaning its catch by the ice dock, and then she said, with the terrible directness of the young, "Was Dad paying attention?"

The water knocked along the hull. Far off, the noon whistle blew, and the gulls went up off the breakwater all together, a thousand wings making a sound like rain falling upward.

"Yes," Esko said. "He was paying attention. Sometimes the sea is bigger than attention. That's the part I can't teach you a way around, because there isn't one. I can only teach you to make the sea need to be very, very big before it can touch you."

She looked at him for a long moment, and something in her face settled, the way water settles when the wind drops, and he understood that he had passed a test he hadn't known he was taking. Children always know when they are being lied to about death. He had told her the truth and she had heard it land as truth, and after that the Saturdays belonged to them.

Through November and into December he taught her the bay the way you teach a language, vocabulary first. Cat's paws — the dark fans the wind drags across calm water, gusts announcing themselves three hundred yards out, so you're never surprised. The nervous water over the shoal off Henning Point, where the swell felt the bottom and stood a little taller and the surface jittered like a struck drumhead even on quiet days. The boil of a tide rip, water welling up smooth and round-shouldered from below, turning slowly, and the way debris collected along the rip's edge — sticks, foam, a dead murre once, once a child's sandal that neither of them mentioned again. He taught her that birds working low and frantic meant bait pushed to the surface by something underneath, and that one bird sitting alone far from shore knew something too, even if it was only that the fishing was finished. He taught her to look at the backs of waves rather than their faces. Anybody can see a wave coming at them, he said. The face is a threat. The back is information. The back tells you what the bottom is doing.

She wrote things down, which surprised him. She kept a school notebook with a tide table taped inside the cover, and she recorded the lessons in a small upright hand, with diagrams: arrows for current, hatching for chop, a careful drawing of kelp bent over in an ebb. Once he looked over her shoulder and saw she had written, the water doesn't do anything for no reason, and underlined it twice, and he had to walk to the stern of the skiff and pretend to check the fuel line.

In town the winter went the way last winters go. Vainio's hardware put a closing sign in the window in December, the old man saying he was retiring, though everyone knew he was only running ahead of the avalanche. The school board met about consolidating with the district up the highway, which meant a forty-minute bus ride for Della in the fall, which meant, Esko understood, that Maren was doing the arithmetic that every parent in town was doing, the arithmetic that always came out to the same answer, which was Portland, or Eugene, or her sister's place outside Olympia. At the plant they were running the last of the season's tuna and crab through the lines, and the company had stopped repairing anything; when the conveyor on line two seized, they cannibalized line three, and the workers moved through the cold wet rooms like people keeping a vigil, which is what they were.

Maren came home with her hands cracked from the brine and the cold and sat at the kitchen table with the want ads from three towns, and one night in January she said to Esko, not looking up, "Renata says the school in Tumwater is good. Says there's work."

"Probably there is," Esko said.

"You could come."

He was at the stove, and he kept his back to her so she could say it without watching it land. "I'm too old to learn new water," he said.

"It's a town. It's not water. There's a Fred Meyer."

"Everything's water," he said, which was not an argument, and she didn't treat it as one. She turned the page of the want ads. But she did not say anything more about Tumwater that night, and the Saturdays continued, and Esko understood that he had been given a season — one season, with a date on it like the date on the cannery door — and he began to teach faster.

He taught her the bar. Not by crossing it; he would not take a sixteen-foot skiff over the Nessel bar in winter, and he told her so, and told her why, and made the why itself a lesson. They drove instead in his truck to the overlook on the south jetty on a day when a long-period swell was running, and they stood in the wind with thermoses while below them the river met the sea and the sea refused it. The ebb poured out of the bay carrying the rain of three counties, and the swell came in from a storm a thousand miles offshore, and where they met, the water broke white across the whole channel, lines of breakers like teeth in a jaw, and the sound came up the jetty rocks as a single continuous concussion you felt in your chest.

"Closed," Della said. She had learned the word from him. The bar is closed.

"Closed," he agreed. "Now tell me when it opens."

She frowned. "When the swell drops?"

"When else? Watch."

She watched for a long time, and he watched her watch, which was easier now than watching the bar itself, the white water blurring in the failing center of his vision while her profile stayed sharp at the edge. After a while she said, slowly, "The waves come in bunches. Big ones, then a while, then big ones."

"Sets," he said. "Yes. And?"

"And the tide's going out now. You said ebb against swell makes it worse. So when the tide turns —"

"When the tide turns, the flood runs in the same direction as the swell, and the seas lie down, sometimes in twenty minutes, like a different ocean. Slack water before the flood, or the first of the flood — that's your gate. Every bar on this coast kills somebody every few years, and it's almost always somebody who knew the gate was there and decided he was too tired or too late or too good to wait for it. The bar doesn't open because you need it to. It opens when it opens. Your job is only to know when, and to want to live more than you want to be home."

She wrote it in the notebook with her gloves on, the letters big and ragged in the cold: want to live more than you want to be home. A wave broke against the jetty and the spray reached them, fine as fog, and she laughed, and the laugh went through him like warmth through a chilled hand, painful and necessary.

The day the fog caught them was the last Saturday in February. They had run south inside the bay to pull the two crab pots he still kept, more for the ritual than the meat, and the morning had been windless and mild, the water pewter-colored under a high gray ceiling, and he should have known. He told her afterward that he should have known: a windless mild day in February with the water warmer than the air is a fog day waiting for its cue, and the cue came while they were pulling the second pot, Della counting the keepers, her sleeves soaked. He looked up and the hill behind town was gone. Then the town was gone. Then the world came down to a circle of water fifty feet across, and the skiff sat in the middle of it like a model boat on a gray tabletop, and the air turned to milk.

"Okay," he said, keeping his voice in the register he had used on the bridges of ships when something failed. "Okay. Pot in the boat. Sit down. We're fine."

"I can't see anything."

"You can see everything you need," he said. "It's just arranged differently now. Tell me what you know."

She was scared; he could hear it; she was eleven. But she had the notebook in her head by then, and he listened to her climb down out of her fear rung by rung, the way he had climbed down pilot ladders in the dark onto the decks of ships, one hand for yourself, always.

"Tide's flooding," she said. "It turned at — at ten-forty. So the current's running into the bay. North. Towards home."

"Good. What else."

"The swell —" She put her hand flat against the gunwale and waited, feeling it the way he'd taught her, with the body and not the eyes, the long slow heave that no fog can hide. "It's coming from there." She pointed out toward the invisible mouth of the bay. "So if it's on our left side, we're going the right way."

"Good. What else."

She listened. The fog made its hush, and inside the hush the small sounds stood out the way stars stand out in the country dark: water dripping from the pot warps, a gull somewhere above the ceiling, and then, faint and irregular, the clank of the bell buoy at the channel edge, rocked by the same swell she had felt with her hand.

"The bell," she said. "Number five."

"And you'll smell the flats before we could ever run aground on them," he said. "Mud and salt grass. Low tide leaves the smell behind for hours. The water tells you everything. It just stops using your eyes. Now." He shifted on the thwart and made himself say it plainly, because the moment had arrived and the fog had only hurried it. "You're going to take us in."

"Me?"

"My eyes are no good in this," he said, and it was true, and it was also more true than she knew, and someday he would tell her the rest of it, or Maren would, or the silting would tell her itself. "Yours are. Your ears are. You have everything you need. I'll be right here with my hand on the kill switch, and if you do something that worries me, I'll say so before it matters. But you won't."

She took the tiller. The little outboard idled them forward into the milk at a walking pace, and she steered with her whole body rigid at first and then, gradually, less so, holding the swell on her left, correcting toward the clank of the bell until it passed close abeam — the buoy looming out of the fog all at once, red and rust-streaked and enormous-seeming, rocking and tolling like something at the bottom of a dream — and then she found the next thing and the next: the smell of the flats warning her off to starboard, the surface texture changing where the channel deepened, the wrinkle of current around a piling she heard before she saw. A troller's engine throbbed somewhere and she throttled back and waited, head cocked, until she had its bearing and its direction, and let it pass unseen. Forty minutes she steered, and Esko sat with his hand near the kill switch and never once touched it, and when the breakwater came out of the fog with its gulls hunched along the top like punctuation, she let out a breath that he heard over the engine, a long shaking exhale, and then she laughed, the same laugh as on the jetty, fear converted whole into joy, which is the oldest alchemy sailors know.

Maren was on the dock. Of course she was on the dock; the fog had rolled over the town an hour before, and she had stood there the way women in this town had stood on docks for a hundred years, and Esko braced for what he had earned. But Della was already telling it before the bow line was fast — the bell, the swell on her left, the smell of the flats, I brought us in, Mom, I did it, Grandpa didn't touch anything — and Esko watched his daughter's face do a complicated thing, fear and fury and something underneath them both that was older than either, and in the end what she said was, "You're soaked. Both of you. There's soup."

That night, late, Maren stood in the door of his room. "You knew the fog could come," she said.

"I should have. Yes."

"And you let her steer."

"I made her steer."

She was quiet. The furnace ticked. "She hasn't talked that much since Danny," Maren said finally. "Not about anything. Not in four years." And she went up the stairs, and that was the end of it, and the beginning of something else.

The plant ran its last fish through the line on the twenty-ninth of March, two days ahead of the date on the door, because the fish ran out before the time did, which everyone agreed was the right way for it to end, the place dying of its actual disease and not of a calendar. On the thirty-first they blew the whistle at noon for the last time, and the man in the boiler room — Aho, who had held the job for thirty years — let it run long, a full minute, then more, the sound climbing out over the bay and the breakwater and the gray hills, and the whole town stopped in it. Esko stood on the pier with Della beside him and felt the sound pass through him the way the jetty's concussion had passed through them in January, and the gulls rose off the breakwater in their gray sheet and circled and circled and did not know where to put themselves, and when the whistle finally died the silence afterward had a shape, like the hole in water after something heavy goes in.

In April the moving truck came for the family two doors down, and in May Maren put the question to him again, gently, at the kitchen table with the Tumwater school's enrollment papers between them, and he said what he had always meant to say, which was that they should go, and that he would stay, and that the house was paid for and the skiff was paid for and a man could live cheap where everything he wanted was free. She cried a little, dry-eyed the way she cried, and Della did not cry at all, which worried him until the last Saturday came and he understood why.

They went out one more time, the bay green-gold with spring, the light long. Off Henning Point she cut the engine without being told and let the skiff drift, and she took out the notebook, swollen now with a winter of salt damp, and she read the water aloud to him — the cat's paws fanning down from the northwest, three hundred yards, get ready; the kelp lying over on the last of the ebb; the slick where the creek's fresh water rode the salt; one cormorant low and hurried, meaning bait, meaning something under the bait — read it all the way you'd read to a man whose eyes were going, which she did not know she was doing, or perhaps she did, perhaps she had known for months, children always know. And Esko sat in the stern of his boat in the middle of the only water he had ever needed, listening to his granddaughter tell him everything the sea was saying, and thought that a place does not die while someone still speaks its language, and that he had done the one thing, the only thing, that an old man on a closing coast can do: he had put the water into a person who was leaving, where no company and no calendar could ever shut it down.

"Tide's turning," Della said, looking at the kelp. "We should go in."

"You take her," Esko said, and she did.
