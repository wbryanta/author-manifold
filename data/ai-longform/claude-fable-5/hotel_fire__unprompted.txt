The car park had been the kitchen garden once, and Marian sat in her daughter's borrowed car for a long minute trying to find the place where the rhubarb had grown, the bolted lettuces, the wall where Donal the porter used to smoke with his back to the wind. There was no wall now. There was tarmac and a row of bay trees in zinc planters and a sign that said GUEST PARKING ONLY in a typeface that hadn't existed in 1990. The hotel itself rose beyond it, white and broad-shouldered against the grey sky, and her first thought, the disloyal one, the one she had been steeling herself against for the whole drive down, was that it looked the same. It did not look the same. She knew that. The roofline was wrong, the windows of the third floor too tall and too few, the porch wider, the whole left wing a confection of glass that had never been there. But the shape of it against the sea, the way it sat on its rise like a liner run aground — that was the same, and her body knew it before her mind could intervene, and she had to put both hands on the wheel and breathe.

She had told Jo she'd come down the morning of the wedding, that there was no need for her to stay the two nights, and Jo had laughed at her down the phone and said, Mam, you're the mother of the bride, you're staying in the hotel, it's all booked, room twelve, sea view, don't fight me on this. And Marian had not fought her, because what would she have said. I worked there. You knew that. I worked there and one night in May the year before you were born it burned, and four people died in it, and I have not stood inside it since. She had never assembled those sentences in front of her daughter. They had lived in her for thirty-two years like furniture under dust sheets, and you did not whip the sheets off the week of a wedding.

She took her case from the boot — she had packed light, out of an old instinct she recognised and disliked, the instinct of someone who might have to leave quickly — and crossed the tarmac, and went up the steps, which were new, granite, with a ramp to one side, and through doors that opened themselves, and into the smell of lilies and new carpet and coffee, and not one molecule of it was the smell she remembered, the old smell of floor polish and gravy and salt damp, and she was grateful for that, almost weak with gratitude, standing on the doormat like a woman who'd forgotten what she came in for.

The girl at the desk was perhaps twenty, with a name badge that said KATARZYNA and a smile of genuine warmless warmth, the kind they trained into you. Checking in? Hartnett, Marian said. Room twelve. The girl's fingers went over the keyboard. Marian watched her hands. She herself had never stood behind that desk; chambermaids didn't, you came and went by the back corridor, you were a rumour to the guests, a turned-down bed, a folded point on a roll of toilet paper. The desk in those days had been mahogany, and Mr. Considine had stood behind it in his good suit with his reading glasses on a chain. The desk now was a slab of pale stone, lit from beneath. You're here for the Hartnett–Doyle wedding, Katarzyna said, delighted, as if she'd discovered it herself. The mother of the bride. Congratulations.

Thank you, Marian said. Is it — and she stopped, because she'd been about to ask is it the Atlantic Room, the wedding, and she didn't know if there was still an Atlantic Room, and didn't know what she'd do with either answer.

The Sandpiper Suite, the girl said, anticipating a different question. The ceremony's at two tomorrow, drinks on the terrace after if the weather holds. She slid a small folder across the stone, a key card inside it. Marian looked at the key card. She had carried keys here once, real keys, a master on a leather fob the size of a doorknob, heavy enough that you knew at every moment which pocket it was in. You signed it in and out of a book. The night of the fire she'd had it in her cardigan pocket because she'd come down at twenty past one to iron her dress for her day off — the laundry was the only place with a decent iron, and Josie was asleep above and she hadn't wanted to wake her — and that key, that habit, that dress that needed ironing, was the whole of the reason she was alive and standing here at fifty-three with a card that would open a door if you waved it.

Lifts are to your left, Katarzyna said.

I'll take the stairs, said Marian, and then, because the girl looked at her case, it's only the one floor, I like stairs, and that was true, she had never since trusted a building she didn't know the stairs of.

The staircase was new, of course, wide and carpeted in a grey-green weave, with a runner of brass stair rods that were ornamental, that held nothing down. The old staircase had turned the other way. She climbed against thirty years of muscle memory, her hand finding a banister six inches from where her hand expected it, and at the half-landing there was a tall window that looked over the terrace to the sea, and she stood at it longer than she meant to. The tide was out. The strand lay wet and shining all the way to the point, and a man was walking a dog along the waterline, and it was the same strand, exactly the same, indifferent, and something in her chest came loose a half-turn, like a screw.

Room twelve was on the first floor, sea side, and when she let herself in she stood in the doorway and read the room the way she had been taught to read rooms before she was twenty. The bed was well made — proper hospital corners, the counterpane centred, the pillows plumped and karate-chopped in the current fashion. The kettle tray was aligned, two cups, two glasses, the long-life milks fanned. The towels were folded in thirds and stacked by weight. Someone had hoovered into the corners and not just up the middle. Whoever does this room, Marian thought, is good, and is not appreciated, and she put her case on the rack and sat on the edge of the bed and looked at the wallpaper, which was pale blue with a faint pattern of grasses, and let herself, finally, deliberately, the way you'd lower yourself into cold water, think about Josie.

Josie Lonergan from Carrigfin, eighteen months older than her, who slept in the other bed in the attic room they shared, who could fold a fitted sheet in the air like a conjuror, who was saving for Boston, who had a laugh you could hear through two floors, who used to say of the worst guests, ah, they can't help it, they were dragged up, and of the best ones nothing at all, only pocketing the fifty pence under the pillow with a wink. Who on the night of the eleventh of May had gone up at half eleven dead on her feet because there'd been a wedding that day, the Hennessy wedding, a hundred and forty covers, and the function room thick with smoke and flowers and the band playing till one. Who was asleep in the attic with Bríd Mac and Annie Foley when something in the function room below — they never fully settled it, a cigarette in the floral swags, a fault in the wiring of the bandstand lights, the inquest hedged — began, around two, very quietly, to burn.

Marian had smelled it in the laundry before she heard anything. Smelled it and stood with the iron lifted, thinking the iron, the iron's after scorching something, and then heard the first alarm go, the old one, a bell like a school bell, and gone out into the back corridor into smoke already moving along the ceiling like a brown river upside down. What she did then she had told so many times in the weeks after — to the guards, to the inquest, to a reporter from the Examiner who called her a heroine in print, a word she had hated with her whole body — that the telling had worn a groove and the night itself had retreated behind the telling. She had gone up the front stairs because the back ones were full of smoke. She had the master in her pocket. She had gone along the first floor banging doors, unlocking the ones nobody answered, and an old man in number eight asleep with his hearing aids out — she had shaken him by the shoulder, a stranger, a commercial traveller from Limerick, and he had looked up at her with the unguarded face of a child, and she had put his coat on him like dressing a child. Forty-one guests on the register that night and thirty-seven of them came down the front stairs or the fire escape on the east gable, and Mr. and Mrs. Whelan came out a window onto the porch roof, and Major Sykes in number three would not be hurried and died of the smoke in his chair with his book in his lap. And the attic stairs, the staff stairs, the narrow uncarpeted flight that she had climbed every night of two years, were by twenty past two a chimney, and no one went up them, and no one came down.

She did not see Josie that night. That was the thing the reporters could never use and the thing that stayed. She did not see her at a window, did not hear her, had no last image to be tormented by, and so had spent thirty-two years being tormented by the absence of one — by Josie asleep, simply asleep, on her right side with her hands under her cheek the way she always slept, while Marian stood on the gravel in the noise and the hose-water wrapped in a stranger's coat with the master key still in her cardigan pocket, signed out in the book that burned.

She had left the town that August. Her mother thought it was the trauma and it was easier to let her think so, but in truth it was the kindness she couldn't stand — the way people stopped her on Main Street with their faces already arranged, the way the word brave attached itself to her like a burr. She'd gone to Dublin, done her hotel management cert at night, of all things, because it was what she knew, and met Brendan Hartnett at a cousin's twenty-first, and when their daughter was born in the autumn of 1993 she had said, before Brendan could open his mouth, Josephine. He knew the bones of it, Brendan; he had never once asked for the rest, which was one of the several reasons she had loved him for twenty-nine years and grieved him for three. And the child became Jo before she was out of nappies, and grew up knowing only that she was named for a friend of Mam's who died young, away down the country, before you were born — and had chosen, out of all the hotels on the western seaboard, with her own money and her own delight, this one.

Marian unpacked her dress and hung it in the wardrobe and went down to dinner with the Doyles and was, she believed, very good. She admired the menu and the centrepieces; she let Jo's father-in-law-to-be tell her about the road from Galway; she drank one glass of wine and laughed in the right places. Jo, across the table, shining, kept catching her eye, and once mouthed all right, Mam? and Marian nodded, all right, and was almost telling the truth. The function room — the Sandpiper Suite, they were shown it after dessert, the chairs already in rows, the florist's scaffolding bare and waiting — stood where the Atlantic Room had stood, more or less; the proportions were different, the ceiling higher, a wall of glass where the bandstand had been. She stood at the threshold while the others walked in exclaiming, and made herself note the exits. Two fire doors, push-bar, signage illuminated. Sprinkler heads at three-metre intervals, brass, discreet among the downlighters. Smoke detectors. A fire blanket cabinet by the service door. It was the most beautiful room she had ever seen.

In the morning, early, before the hairdresser and the steam of it all, she went out walking on the strand and came back along the east gable, and there, set into the new wall at shoulder height, where you would never see it unless you came this unfashionable way past the bins and the heat pumps, was a plaque. Grey limestone, small, the lettering filling with salt already. IN MEMORY OF THOSE WHO LOST THEIR LIVES IN THE FIRE AT THIS HOTEL, 12 MAY 1990. And the four names, in alphabetical order, which put Annie Foley first and Josie third, JOSEPHINE LONERGAN, between BRÍD MCNAMARA and MAJOR T. E. SYKES, the staff and the guest all together in the stone with no rank among them, which the hotel of 1990 would never have allowed, and which made Marian put her fingers to the letters and stand with her fingers there while a kitchen lad came out with a bucket and pretended, decently, not to see her.

You'd want to come round the front, missus, he said when she didn't move. It's all bins back here.

I know, she said. I know where I am.

The wedding was at two and it was, everyone agreed, perfect. The weather held; the light came off the sea and filled the glass wall and lay along the rows of guests like a benediction; Jo came up the aisle on her uncle Declan's arm in her grandmother's altered lace with her chin up and her eyes wet, and Marian stood in the front row in her green and watched her daughter — Josephine, the registrar said, do you, Josephine — make her promises in a room built on the footprint of the room where the Hennessys had danced until one o'clock on the last ordinary night, and she found that she could hold both things at once, the burning and the vows, the way the strand held both tides. She cried at the vows like everyone else. Nobody knew she was crying twice.

It was at the turn of the afternoon, between the meal and the band, when the guests had spilled onto the terrace and the staff were resetting the room, that she slipped away. She told herself she was going up for her wrap. On the first-floor corridor a housekeeping trolley stood outside room nine, and through the open door she could see a young woman wrestling a duvet into its cover with the duvet winning, hot-faced, behind schedule because the wedding had eaten the day's roster, Marian could read the whole afternoon in the state of the trolley. The girl looked up, mortified the way you were trained to be mortified, caught existing. Sorry — I can come back —

You're grand, Marian said, and then, because her feet had already taken her into the room, here. Inside out. Hands into the far corners, take hold of the duvet, and — she shook it, and the cover swallowed the duvet whole and fell into place, and the girl laughed despite herself.

You've done this before.

A long time ago, Marian said. In this hotel, as it happens. Before your time. She straightened the duvet on the bed, ran her palm flat across it, an old satisfying motion, the smoothing of a surface for a stranger. The girl was looking at her now with new attention, at the wedding outfit and the good shoes.

You're with the wedding below.

The bride's mine, Marian said. The girl's whole face changed, softened into something unguarded. Then, hesitating, in the tone of someone handing over contraband: There's a few still talk about it. The old ones. There's a man in the garden, Noel, he was here, you know, when — and she stopped, not knowing whether the missus knew, and Marian said, gently, I know. I was here too.

The girl looked at her then the way you look at weather coming in off the sea, with respect and an obscure wish to be indoors. Marian touched the duvet once more and went out past the trolley, and on the trolley, between the bin bags and the bottle of glass cleaner, was the room list, and on the room list the rooms were numbered the new way, and not one number on it was a number that meant anything anymore, and that was, on the whole, a mercy.

She found Noel before the band started, because she went looking. He was by the heat pumps with a pipe he wasn't supposed to have, a big slow man gone white, and he knew her across fifteen feet of gravel, before she said a word — Marian Connolly, he said, which had not been her name for twenty-nine years, and his eyes filled the way old men's eyes do, without drama, like a tide rising in a rock pool. He'd been seventeen, a kitchen porter, on the gravel that night same as her. They did not say much. He said the plaque was got in 2003 when the new crowd took over, that he'd kept at them about it. He said Josie's mother had come down from Carrigfin to see it set in, and stood where Marian had stood that morning, and that she'd died herself since, God rest her. He said, you did all anyone could, girl, and they both looked at the sea, because they both knew that sentence was a coat you put on a person, like the coat she'd put on the man from Limerick, and that it kept off some of the cold and not all of it.

Then she went in and danced. She danced with her new son-in-law, who was nervous of her and shouldn't have been; she danced with Declan; she danced in a circle of Jo's friends with her shoes off, and the band played and the glass wall went from gold to blue to black, and the lighthouse out on the point began its slow counting, and at some hour she stepped out alone through the fire door onto the terrace — the door opened at a touch, the bar smooth under her hand, and she stood a second blessing it — into the cold and the noise of the sea.

She heard the door again behind her. Jo, barefoot too, her dress glowing in the dark, came and stood against her side the way she had since she was small, fitting herself under her mother's arm though she was the taller now.

You're freezing, Mam.

I'm grand.

They stood. The lighthouse counted. Behind them the band, muffled by glass, started something slow.

I used to work here, Marian said, to the sea. A long time ago. Before any of it.

I know, Mam, Jo said.

You know I worked at the coast. You don't —

I found the cutting, Jo said. Her voice was very steady, the steadiness of something long rehearsed. In the biscuit tin, with the bank books. I was twelve. The girl in the photograph with the blanket round her, that was you. Marian Connolly, nineteen, who roused the guests. I read it about a hundred times and put it back, and I used to take it out when you were at work and read it again. She paused. There were two names underlined in biro. Josephine Lonergan was one.

The sea went on doing what it does. Marian found she was not able to speak, and found, under that, an enormous quietness, as if a wind that had blown for thirty-two years had dropped.

I didn't book it to ambush you, Jo said, quickly now, the rehearsal failing her, the words coming raw. I booked it because — because it's beautiful, and it's ours somehow, isn't it, even if you never said, it's where you're from in some way I can't — and I thought if I asked you'd say no, and I thought if I didn't ask — She stopped. I wanted her at my wedding, Mam. I wanted us both to bring her.

Marian put her other arm around her daughter and held her in the cold, the lace and the green crushed together, and over Jo's shoulder she could see, lit faintly by the spill from the windows, the line of the new roof against the sky, the third floor with its tall windows where the attic had been, where someone tonight was sleeping in a sea-view suite on the exact cubic metres of air where two iron beds had stood, and where a girl who could fold a fitted sheet like a conjuror had slept on her right side with her hands beneath her cheek.

She's here, Marian said. She was always going to be here.

They went in to the warmth and the music, and Marian danced one more dance, and went up at midnight by the stairs.

In the morning, early, before the breakfasts, before her lift, she did a thing she had decided on in the night. She stripped the bed in room twelve and folded the sheets at the foot of it, pillowcases on top, the way that saves the girl four minutes she'll never know she was given. She gathered her towels into one. She rinsed the cups and set them mouth-down on the tray. Then she took a twenty from her purse and slid it under the pillow on the bare mattress, the old way, the chambermaid's way, where only the one who makes the bed will ever find it, and she stood a moment in the blue early light with her hand flat on the pillow, smoothing it, smoothing it, and then she took her case and closed the door softly behind her, the way you do when somewhere, in some room of the sleeping house, there is a girl you don't want to wake.
