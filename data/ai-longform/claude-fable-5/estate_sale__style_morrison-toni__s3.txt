The chifforobe went first.

Two men in matching gray T-shirts angled it through the front door the way pallbearers manage a casket on narrow church steps, with murmurs and small adjustments and a tenderness that had nothing to do with love, and Della stood on the porch with the cash box under her arm and watched walnut catch the June light for the first time in sixty years. Outside light. The chifforobe had never been outside, not since 1954, when it came off Greenbaum's truck wrapped in moving quilts and her mother walked behind it up the porch steps with her hands lifted a little, not touching it, the way you walk behind a child learning to ride a bicycle. Now it stood in the yard by the peonies and a woman from Vermilion circled it twice, opened its long door, sniffed inside it, and said, "Does it come with the key?"

"It comes with the key," Della said.

Sixty-five dollars. Her mother had paid one hundred and ten for it, three dollars at a time, every Friday for most of a year, walking the money downtown herself because she didn't trust the mail and didn't trust Greenbaum's nephew, who wrote receipts in pencil. One hundred and ten dollars earned over washtubs and ironing boards in other women's basements, and Della took sixty-five for it from a woman who said she was going to paint it. Paint it gray, probably. They painted everything gray now. Della put the bills in the cash box and did not say anything about the long door, how it stuck in August and you had to lift up on the handle, or about the smell inside it, cedar and lavender sachet and underneath that something older, something that was just her mother, that would outlast the paint.

It was one o'clock. The sale had started at nine. By eleven the vultures were gone — the dealers, the early men with flashlights and loupes who came before the advertised time and knocked anyway, asking about gold, about coins, about guns, did her father leave any guns — and now came the slow ones, the Saturday people, the couples and the curious and the neighbors who had waited until they could come without looking eager. The yard was full of furniture and the furniture was full of weather it had never felt. The dining chairs sat in the grass like guests at a wedding nobody had planned. A box fan, unplugged, faced the street as though still trying. Della had carried the cash box since nine and her arm had stopped noticing it, the way an arm will, the way a person will, given enough hours, stop noticing almost anything.

Don't let nobody handle what you love with dirty hands. Odessa Pruitt said that, said it about books and about babies and about her name, and now strangers were handling all of it, every bit, with hands Della could not vouch for, and the strange thing, the thing Della kept turning over like a coin she couldn't read, was how little it hurt. Or how the hurt kept arriving late, after the item was already in somebody's trunk, a hurt like a phone ringing in a house you've just left.

A girl in cutoff jeans held up the green mixing bowl. The big one, the one with the chip on the rim shaped like a thumbnail. "How much for this?"

"Three dollars."

"Would you take two?"

In that bowl: seventy years of cornbread batter, the yellow of it, the wooden spoon knocking the sides in three-quarter time while the radio said snow, said war, said sale at Fisher's, said the Lord is my shepherd on Sunday mornings when Odessa was too proud to admit her knees couldn't take the kneeling anymore so she had church with the choir broadcast and a bowl of batter. In that bowl: potato salad for three funerals Della could name and probably a dozen she couldn't. In that bowl, once, a litter of kittens, one cold March, lined with a dish towel and set behind the stove, because Odessa Pruitt, who would wring a chicken's neck without changing expression, could not abide a cold kitten.

"Two's fine," Della said.

The girl carried it away against her hip, the way you'd carry the kind of thing it was, and Della made change for a man buying the porch glider and thought: she is going to put keys in it. Mail. It is going to sit by a door somewhere in this county full of keys and dead batteries and rubber bands, and it will never know cornbread again, and the bowl will not care, because bowls don't, and that was the lesson of the whole afternoon if the afternoon had a lesson, which Della doubted. Things didn't hold the love. People just stored it in them for safekeeping, the way you'd keep money in a mattress, and then the people died and other people came and bought the mattress, two dollars, would you take one-fifty, and drove off with it, never knowing they were rich.

Her brother Wesley had said sell it all. Said it from Detroit, on the phone, in February, with the ground still too frozen to take their mother, the funeral home keeping her like a held breath until the thaw. Sell it all, June — he was the only one who still called her June, the name before the name, the one Odessa used until Della turned twelve and got baptized and chose the fuller version for herself the way you'd choose a coat with room to grow into — sell it all and send me my half and don't make it a thing. As if a house could fail to be a thing. As if forty years of Wesley's absence had thinned the house out somehow, made it portable, made it math. He had come for the funeral and stood at the graveside in a good coat and left before the repast, and the women at Mount Calvary had things to say about that, said them with their eyebrows mostly, and Della had eaten two plates of food she couldn't taste and driven back to Columbus that same night because the house, that first week, was unbearable. The house still thought Odessa was coming back. The house had her glasses on the windowsill over the sink, folded, patient, facing the yard.

"Ma'am? Is the sewing machine electric or—"

"Treadle," Della said. "It works. Everything in this house works."

That came out harder than she meant it, and the man — youngish, bearded, a tote bag that said something about a bookstore — blinked and smiled and got down on his knees in front of the Singer like a man proposing. He pumped the treadle with his hand and the needle rose and fell, rose and fell, dry, threadless, stitching nothing to nothing, and the sound of it went through Della's body and came out the year 1961, came out a winter of choir robes, fourteen of them, wine-colored, because Mount Calvary's choir was marching in the state convocation and Odessa Pruitt had told Reverend Tillman she would not have her church looking like it shopped from a barrel. Six weeks of that sound after supper. Della doing homework at the kitchen table to the rhythm of it, the treadle like a heart under the floor, her mother's foot rocking, rocking, her mouth full of pins, and you did not speak to Odessa when her mouth was full of pins, you waited, the whole house waited, the way you'd wait out weather. The robes were so beautiful that Hartwell's white Methodist church asked, through an intermediary, what she would charge. She charged them plenty. She put the money in the chifforobe, in the long door, in the toe of a stocking, and that was the money that bought Della's first winter coat with a real fur collar, rabbit, gray, and Della had worn that coat like an annunciation.

"Forty for the Singer?" the bearded man said. The sticker said sixty.

"Sixty," Della said.

He paid it. They always paid it, the ones who knelt.

Heat came on by two. Real June heat, the wet Ohio kind, the kind that made the locusts start up early in the maples along Tappan Street, and Della moved her chair into the shade of the porch and watched a teenage boy go through the crate of records with two fingers, flick, flick, flick, fast as a bank teller, and stop dead at Sam Cooke. Watched him slide the record halfway out of the sleeve and tilt it to the light, checking for scratches, and she could have told him: no scratches. Odessa handled her records like communion. Saturday nights, after the kitchen was done and shining, she'd put on Sam Cooke and sit in the front room with her shoes off and her stocking feet up on the hassock, and that was the only hour of the week Della ever saw her mother completely still. Not resting between tasks. Still. Her eyes closed, her head back, one finger keeping time against the armrest, and the voice filling the front room like water finding its level, and if you came in she didn't shoo you but you understood you'd entered something, a held thing, and you sat down quiet on the floor and got held too.

"All the records a dollar each?"

"The Sam Cooke is five," Della said, surprising herself.

The boy looked at her. Something passed between them, quick, an acknowledgment, and he dug in his pocket and paid the five without a word, and Della almost called him back. Almost said, here, take it, just take it, just play it somewhere. But she kept the five. Odessa would have kept the five. Sentiment is fine, her mother used to say, but it don't compound interest.

People carried the house out the front door all afternoon. That was the door for it, the front door, which struck Della around two-thirty with a force that made her sit down. Sixty years that door had been for company, for the insurance man, for the pastor, for Easter. The family came and went through the kitchen door, always, off the driveway, past the trash cans and the clothesline — it was a law of the house so old it had stopped being a rule and become anatomy — and now the front door stood propped open with a brick from the flower bed border and the whole insides of the place were leaving by it, headboard and hassock, percolator and pie safe, leaving formally, leaving like company, like the house was finally hosting the party Odessa never threw, and everyone invited was a stranger, and everyone was taking home a favor.

Cissy would have laughed at that. Cissy laughed at everything that needed laughing at, which was how she got through, and then it wasn't enough to get her through, and she had been gone now — Lord — twenty-two years. Cissy would have stood on this porch with a cigarette she wasn't supposed to have and narrated the whole sale in her preacher voice. And the Lamb opened the second seal, and behold, a white man in cargo shorts, and he was given the gravy boat. Della laughed out loud, alone on the porch, one rough bark of a laugh, and a woman examining the doilies looked up at her and looked away fast, the way you do from grief in public, which is what they all assumed it was, and they weren't wrong, they just had the shape of it wrong. Grief wasn't the rock in her chest. Grief was the laughing with no one to laugh with. Grief was reaching for Cissy in her own mouth and finding her there, still funny, still seventeen forever, still ash in a brass urn that Della had already decided was not for sale, was in the trunk of the Buick right now in fact, wrapped in a beach towel, riding back to Columbus tonight along with the family Bible and the photographs and one cast-iron skillet, and that was all. That was going to be all. She had promised herself in February: you take what fits in the trunk, June, and you let the rest go to glory.

Willa Rance came at three. Della saw her coming from two houses down, taking the sidewalk slow with her cane, eighty-eight years old in a dress ironed sharp enough to draw blood, and Della came down off the porch to meet her because Odessa would have wanted that, would have required it. Willa Rance had lived on Tappan Street since before the war. She had been at Odessa's wedding, at her funeral, at every christening and catastrophe in between, and she stood now at the edge of the yard surveying the furniture in the grass with an expression Della knew from old photographs of flooded towns.

"They getting it all, then," Willa said.

"Most everything."

"Mm." Willa looked a long time at the dining chairs, the guests in the grass. "I sat in one of them chairs the night your daddy got hurt at the quarry. Sat in it till four in the morning. Your mama wouldn't go to bed and wouldn't cry and wouldn't let me leave, so I sat. We didn't say six words the whole night." She moved her cane an inch, planted it again. "You can't sell that part."

"No, ma'am."

"That part's still in there, though." Willa tapped her own breastbone, once. "Goes where you go now. That's the inconvenience of it." She would not take a chair for free, would not hear of it, but she let Della walk her up onto the porch and bring her a glass of water, and she sat in the shade for the better part of an hour like a posted guard, and Della noticed that prices held firmer while Willa Rance was watching. Nobody offered two for a three-dollar item under that gaze. When she rose to go she put her dry small hand on Della's wrist and said, "Your mama was hard because the world was hard, and she stood between you and the world. You understand what I'm saying. The hardness was the standing."

"Yes, ma'am."

"All right then." And she went back down Tappan Street the way she'd come, slow, ironed, ninety degrees in the shade, and Della watched her all the way to her own porch because that was what you did, you watched them safe home, it was the last neighborhood law still in force and Della and Willa Rance were just about the only two left who knew the statute.

The kitchen table went at four. A young couple bought it, the kind who finished each other's sentences about it — it's so — it's exactly — for the breakfast nook — and the husband ran his hand across the top and found the scorch mark and his face did a small landlord's frown. "There's a burn here."

"There is," Della said.

"Was it — I mean, did you want to adjust—"

"The price is the price."

She did not tell him. She had told no one all day, had kept the stories pent behind her teeth like Odessa's pins, but standing there making his change she let this one play behind her eyes one last time: September 1963, a Sunday, the radio on, the iron heating for the school clothes, and the news coming out of Birmingham like something tearing. Four girls. Sunday school. Odessa standing at the board with the iron in her hand and the iron coming down, slowly, not set on its heel but laid flat on the table itself, laid down like a body, and her mother standing there with her hand still on the handle, not moving, while the announcer's voice went on and on, and the smell of scorching maple rising up between them, and eleven-year-old Della afraid to speak, afraid the way you're afraid in church, and finally her mother lifted the iron and looked at the mark — a deep brown wing, a bird shape, burned all the way into the grain — and said, in a voice Della never heard before or after, We'll keep that. And they kept it. Sixty-one years it sat in that kitchen uncovered, never sanded, never hidden under a cloth, and when Della was grown she understood it was the only monument her mother ever permitted herself, the only mourning Odessa Pruitt ever wore where anybody could see it, and three generations of the family ate their suppers around that brown bird and knew, without it ever being preached, that you carry the dead to the table with you, that you eat with them, that you do not sand them away.

The couple carried the table out the front door. The wife held the legs end and walked backward, laughing, telling her husband left, your left, and the bird flew out into the June light, into the bed of a Tacoma, into a breakfast nook in some township where no one would know its name. Della stood in the doorway with the cash box and let it go. Goes where you go now, she thought. That's the inconvenience of it.

By five the yard had nearly cleared. The leavings looked the way leavings always look — ashamed. A box of drinking glasses nobody wanted. The exercise bicycle from Wesley's one Christmas of trying. Doilies. A man with a trailer offered her forty dollars for "everything left, the whole shebang," and Della took it just to be done with the word shebang, and he loaded it all in twenty minutes, even the brick from the flower bed, which made her laugh again, the bark again, alone again.

Then the house.

She went through it room by room in the long light. Her shoes were loud now; that was the first thing, the sound the house made without its furniture, a church sound, an inside-of-a-bell sound. In the front room the rug was gone and its ghost remained — a great pale rectangle on the floorboards, the wood inside it young and unfaded, the wood around it darkened by sixty years of light, so that the floor itself was a photograph, a negative, proof of what had stood where and for how long. Della stood at the edge of the rug's ghost like it was water. Over there the hassock. There the radio, then the television, then the bigger television. There the spot where Odessa's chair had pressed four small dents into the boards, four little graves the size of coins, and Della knelt down — her knees telling her about it — and put her fingers in them, one at a time, the way you'd touch the letters of a name cut in stone.

In the kitchen the glasses were gone from the windowsill. She had packed them this morning, her mother's glasses, folded, into the Bible box, but the sill held their shape anyway, a clean stripe in the dust, and through the window the clothesline still ran from the porch post to the maple, slack, gray, strung with nothing. Della went out the kitchen door — the family door, the true door — and crossed the grass and unknotted the line from the maple herself, worked the old swollen knot loose with her thumbnails the way her mother had taught her, no knife, never a knife, a knife is for people who can't be bothered, and she coiled the line around her elbow and her hand, elbow and hand, the old way, and the coil when she finished was the size of a wreath. She put it in the trunk with the urn and the Bible and the skillet. It did not count against her promise. It fit.

Inside again, in the emptied front room, the light had gone the color it goes at the end of June days, that deep butter color that forgives everything it touches, and it came through the curtainless windows in two long panels and lay down on the floor inside the rug's ghost, light inside the shape of the rug, like the house was furnishing itself one last time with the only thing it had left. Della stood in it. The cash box was in the car. The money was counted; Wesley's half was already an envelope. Tomorrow there would be the realtor's sign, the lockbox, the lawyer, the rest of it, the machinery, and she would drive back to Columbus tonight with the windows down and Sam Cooke not on the radio because she had sold him for five dollars to a boy with good hands, and she found she didn't regret it, the record was working, the record was out doing its work, flick, flick, stop, somewhere in this town tonight that voice would fill a room and hold whoever wandered into it.

She stood in the light until the light moved off her. The house ticked and settled around its own emptiness the way a body settles, and she listened to it, and she was not afraid of it, and when the panel of sun had slid all the way to the baseboard and thinned to a ribbon and gone, she said the name into the empty room, out loud, once, not as a question and not as a prayer but the way you say a word to keep it, the way her mother said we'll keep that:

"Odessa."

The room took it. The room had room.

Then she went out — through the kitchen door, past the trash cans, the law still the law — and pulled it locked behind her, and stood a minute in the blue beginning of evening with her hand still on the knob, the metal holding the day's heat like something alive, like a hand holding hers back, before she let it go.
