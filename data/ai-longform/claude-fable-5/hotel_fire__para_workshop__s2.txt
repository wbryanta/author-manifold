They had rebuilt it almost exactly, and Maren could not decide, standing on the pavement with her overnight bag, whether this was a kindness or a cruelty. The Esplanade had its windows back, its white stucco, its three storeys of wrought-iron balconies facing the sea like a woman holding her chin up. Only the proportions had shifted, the way a face shifts after illness. The new wing on the east side sat where the staff annex used to be, and the lettering above the portico was a different gold, brighter, the kind of gold that has never had to weather anything. A wedding car was parked in the turning circle, ribboned, and a boy in a grey waistcoat was carrying a box of glassware up the steps two at a time, the way she had once carried trays, and she stood there until the taxi behind her sounded its horn and she had to move.

At the desk a girl with a smooth ponytail asked if she had stayed with them before. Maren said no, which was true in the way that hotels measure truth, and the girl slid a key card across the marble and told her about breakfast hours and the spa and the wedding, which was the Kowalczyk-Reid party, and was that what she was here for, how lovely. The card was white plastic, weightless. For four years Maren had carried the passkeys of this building on a ring at her hip, eleven keys of varying age and temper, and the ring had worn a pale groove into the waistband of every skirt she owned, and even now, sixty-one years old, she sometimes pressed her hand to her right hip when she was nervous, feeling for iron that wasn't there. She did it then, in the lobby, under the new chandelier. The girl smiled at her. Second floor, she said. The lift is just behind you.

Her room was 214. She had never cleaned a room called 214, because in the old numbering the second floor began at twenty, but the bones of it were familiar, the bay of the window, the radiator's position under the sill, and she stood inside the door for a long moment as though waiting for permission to cross her own threshold. Then she did what she could not help doing. She ran a finger along the top of the picture frame. She checked the underside of the desk, the seam where the carpet met the skirting, the corners of the bathroom ceiling. Spotless. Someone here was good at her job, or frightened of someone, which in Maren's experience amounted to the same standard of cleanliness. The smoke detector on the ceiling was a small white god with a steady green eye, and she looked at it longer than she meant to. On the back of the door, behind the laminated tariff, was the fire evacuation map, a little floorplan with a red dot, you are here, and arrows leading along the corridor to the stairs. She read it twice, the way other people read a letter from someone dead. Then she hung up her dress for the wedding, blue, the good blue, and sat on the edge of the bed, which had been made by somebody with proper hospital corners, and let the building settle around her.

She had come to the Esplanade in the spring of 1984, twenty years old, with chapped hands and a reference from a guesthouse in Lyme. The housekeeper, Mrs. Vance, had looked at her fingernails before her face. The work was the work: thirty rooms a day in season, strip and make, the wet weight of towels, the smell of other people's nights aired out the window, the carpet sweeper that lost its temper on the third-floor landing. She learned the building the way you learn a language by living in it, not the grand staircase the guests used but the service stair behind the door that said PRIVATE, the linen store with its slatted shelves and its dumbwaiter shaft, the boot room where shoes left outside doors went down to be polished, the back corridor where the maids could move the length of the hotel without being seen, which was the point of them. Daria had the room next to hers in the annex, three years older, Polish father, laugh like a drain. They shared cigarettes on the fire escape and a hatred of room 38's bath, which had feet, and feet collect dust the way grief collects anniversaries. Daria taught her to fold a towel in thirds so the open edges faced away. Forty years on, in a hotel that no longer existed except in its own replica, Maren still could not fold a towel any other way.

She had been happy. That was the thing nobody let her keep afterwards, in the inquest, in the newspapers, in the careful voices of people who knew. They wanted the hotel to have been a tragedy from its foundations up, and it had not been. There had been Mr. Whitfield in 41, who came every October for a fortnight, a widower with a brown cardigan and a wireless he played low, who called her by her name and asked her, every evening when she turned down his bed, to leave the lamp on, my dear, I don't like to come back to the dark. There had been the Pellews that last week, honeymooners, twenty-three and twenty-five, who left their shoes outside the door of 38 every night, her white satin ones and his stiff new brogues, side by side and touching, and Maren had carried them down to the boot room and brought them back up shining and thought, without envy, exactly without envy, that she would like a life where someone put their shoes next to hers.

The fire started on the third of October, 1987, at around twenty past three in the morning, in the wiring behind the dumbwaiter shaft, where it smouldered against forty years of paint and the slatted shelves of the linen store before it found its voice. That was the inquest's account and Maren had no better one, because what she had was not an account. What she had was waking in the annex to a smell that her body understood before her mind did, an animal smell, wrong, and standing in the corridor in her nightdress while the air came towards her with a texture air should not have. She banged on Daria's door with the flat of her hand, both hands, and Daria opened it sleep-stupid and furious and then not furious, and Maren said the word and watched it land. Daria was eight weeks gone then. Nobody knew except Maren, who had held her hair back over the annex sink a week before and been sworn to secrecy. They went down the annex stairs together and across the kitchen yard in bare feet, the cobbles cold and wet, and it was only outside, turning, that they saw the third floor of the main building lit from inside like a paper lantern, and Maren heard herself say the keys, and went back in.

She did not tell this part often, and when she did she told it flat, because the alternatives were worse. The passkeys hung on their hook inside the housekeeper's office and she took them and went up the service stair into smoke that lay along the ceiling of the third-floor corridor like a living thing considering her. She went door to door on her knees in the end, banging, shouting, fitting keys with hands that would not be still. She got the Frasers out of 35, the German gentleman out of 36 who would not leave without his briefcase until she screamed at him in a voice she had never heard herself use. She got the corridor's far end open and people moving towards the stairs, coughing, a woman carrying a sleeping child whose face Maren would see for the rest of her life precisely because it was peaceful. And then the heat changed. It stopped being heat and became a pressure, a hand, and the corridor beyond 39 was orange at the seams of its doors, and Tom the night porter had her by the arm and was dragging her backwards, and she was screaming about 41, about Mr. Whitfield, she had the key, it was in her hand, the key to 41 was between her fingers and she could feel its teeth, and Tom, who was nineteen and crying, would not let go of her, and the ceiling at the corridor's end came down with a sound like the sea taking a wall.

Six people died at the Esplanade that night. Mr. Whitfield in 41. The Pellews in 38, who were found, the coroner said, close together, and Maren had spent years deciding what to do with that detail and had finally decided to keep it. A family of three in 40, the Ashtons, down from Coventry, whose surviving relatives she wrote to once, a letter she was not sure she should have sent and that was answered, two months later, with great kindness, which was somehow harder. The inquest found faulty wiring, found the alarm system inadequate to the building's age, found no fault with the staff, commended by name, and the coroner read it out, the conduct of Miss Maren Lillis, chambermaid, who re-entered the premises and whose actions saved an estimated eleven lives. Eleven. She had counted differently ever since. She had a number that was not eleven and she carried it on her hip where the keys used to be. The hotel was a black-windowed ruin for two years and then scaffolding and then, slowly, this. She had left town before the scaffolding. She had gone inland, as far inland as the county allowed, and run the laundry at a cottage hospital for thirty years, where the linen was white and counted and nobody slept above the second floor, and where, on October nights, she could stand in the drying room among the warm sheets and let the smell of clean cotton argue with her memory and sometimes win.

Daria had stayed. That was the difference between them, one of the differences. Daria had married a plasterer called Mick Reid who worked on the rebuild, had stood on the front holding her daughter's pram and watched the new windows go in, had said to Maren on the phone, in the voice she used for closing subjects, it's a building, Mar, buildings don't owe us anything and we don't owe them. The daughter was Hannah. Maren had held her at the christening with the careful terror of a woman handling proof. Goddaughter was the official word; what Maren privately understood was that Hannah had been in the corridor of the annex that night, the size of a plum, asleep inside her sleeping mother, and that when Maren's hand hit Daria's door it had been knocking for two. She had never said this aloud. Daria knew it anyway; it was in the way she handed the baby over, both of them not saying it, the not-saying becoming over the years its own kind of language. And when the invitation had come in January, thick card, silver ink, Hannah and Callum request the pleasure, and underneath, the Esplanade Hotel, Maren had sat at her kitchen table with it for a long time, and then telephoned, and Daria had answered on the second ring as if she'd been sitting by the phone, and said, before hello, you don't have to. And Maren had said, I know I don't, and heard Daria breathe out, and that had been the whole conversation, more or less, forty years compressed into a dialling code.

At half past one there was a knock at 214 and it was Daria herself, in lilac, her hair done, her face done, her eyes already ruined. She stood in the doorway and looked at Maren and then past her at the room, taking its inventory the way only another chambermaid would, and said, they've put you on the second floor, and Maren said, I asked for it, and Daria nodded once, as though confirming a thing about water finding its level. Then she said, come up, she wants you, the buttons are beyond me, my hands, and held up her hands, which had Mick's mother's rings on them and a tremor that was new since Mick died. So Maren went up. The lift was new and quick and she watched the numbers and got out on the fourth floor, which had not existed in 1987 except as attics and tanks, into a corridor with pale carpet and sconces, and the bridal suite was at the east end, roughly, as far as Maren could map the old bones beneath the new flesh, above the air where room 41 used to be. She noted this the way you note weather. Then the door opened on flowers and steam and a make-up girl packing brushes, and there was Hannah in her dress with the back of it open like a question, thirty-six years old, Daria's laugh, Mick's chin, and she said Auntie Mar in exactly the voice she'd used at six, and Maren crossed the room and began at the lowest button.

There were nineteen of them, satin-covered, each with its silk loop, and her hands knew this work, had known it before this work existed, the patience of small fastenings, the body warm under the fabric, breathe in, sorry, nearly there. Hannah watched her in the mirror. I wanted it here, she said. The hotel. Mum tried to talk me out of it and then she stopped trying, and I thought maybe that meant something. She paused. I hope it isn't awful. That I asked you. Maren reached the fourteenth button. Through the window the sea was doing its flat afternoon shine and below, on the lawn, chairs were being set in rows by boys in grey waistcoats. It isn't awful, she said. She found she meant it, or could mean it, which is the same thing said slower. Hannah's eyes in the mirror were her mother's eyes, October 1987, sleep-stupid and then not. Mum told me, Hannah said. When I got engaged. All of it. She'd never. I knew there'd been a fire, everyone knows there was a fire, but not. She stopped. Maren did the last button and settled the fabric across the shoulder blades and smoothed it with the flat of her hand, the gesture of forty years of beds. There, she said. You're done. And then, because the girl was still watching her in the mirror, she said, your mother walked out of this building twice. Once that night and once every day after, past it, with your pram. That's the part to keep. The veil was on a hanger by the wardrobe and Maren lifted it down and floated it over the dark head and arranged it, and the comb went in clean, and it was very simple, in the end, the white net falling, the light through it, the work of hands.

The ceremony was on the lawn at four. Maren sat in the third row on the bride's side between a cousin and a man who taught Callum geography, and the registrar said the words, and the gulls heckled, and when Hannah said her vows Daria made a sound two seats along that she turned into a cough, and Maren passed her a tissue without looking, the old back-corridor economy of women who have worked together. The hotel stood behind the whole thing, white and witnessing. There was a moment, during the signing, when Maren let her eyes go up the facade and count windows, third floor, east end, and found that the counting did what it always did, steadied her and cost her, both at once, the toll and the bridge being the same structure. Then everyone was standing and there was confetti, real petals, the boys in waistcoats sweeping them almost before they landed, and champagne on the terrace, and the long oblique light of early evening that the coast does better than anywhere, and Maren drank one glass slowly and spoke to the geography teacher about tides.

The reception was in the ballroom, which had been the dining room, and the speeches were good, Mick present in them the way the dead are at weddings, a chair's worth of silence everyone toasted. Maren ate what was put in front of her and watched the staff, because she could not help it, the choreography of plates, the girl who carried four glasses up one forearm, the boy who hadn't yet learned to listen to a table while looking at another. When the band started and the floor filled, she sat for a while with her hand on her hip and then got up, quietly, the way she had spent a career getting up, and went out through the lobby and along the corridor past the lifts to a door marked STAFF ONLY, and stood in front of it, and then pushed, because in her experience those doors were never locked during service, and they still weren't.

The service stair smelled of laundry. That was the first thing, the thing that nearly undid her where the facade and the ballroom and the buttons had not: clean cotton, hot from the press somewhere below, rising up the stairwell exactly as it had risen in 1985, in 1986, before any of it, the smell of the work itself, indifferent and continuous. She climbed to the third floor. The corridor behind the fire door was the back corridor still, narrower than the guest side, scuffed where the trolleys went, and halfway along it a linen room stood open with a cart outside, and a girl in a grey uniform was inside swearing softly at a shelf. She turned when Maren's shadow crossed the doorway, badge saying OLA, twenty-two at most, and said, sorry, are you lost, the function's downstairs, with the particular politeness staff keep for guests found where guests shouldn't be. I used to work here, Maren said. Chambermaid. A long time ago. Ola looked at her, at the good blue dress, recalibrating. Before the fire, she said, not quite a question; they tell us about the fire in induction, there's a thing in the garden, by the sundial. Then, because she was young and behind, she turned back to her shelf, where the bath towels had collapsed sideways, and Maren stepped in without asking and took the top one and folded it in thirds, open edges away, and set it down, and took the next. They worked for a minute in silence, two women and a wall of cotton, and Ola said, you fold like Magda, Magda's the head housekeeper, she's a nightmare about edges, and Maren said, she's right to be, and put the last towel up, square, and felt something in her chest sit down that had been standing since 1987. Wedding party's trashed the fourth floor already, Ola said companionably, and there's a man in 32 who irons his own pillowcases, I've seen the iron. Maren laughed, actually laughed, in the back corridor of the Esplanade, and the sound didn't bring the ceiling down. Thank you, she said, which confused the girl, and went out, and down, and through the side door into the garden.

The thing by the sundial was a low slab of slate, easy to miss, meant to be. Six names. The Pellews shared a line. Whitfield, Arthur James, had his October dates wrong by a day, and she stood there deciding whether to mind and decided that he wouldn't have. The music came across the lawn in pieces, bass mostly, and the lit windows of the ballroom threw long gold panels onto the grass. She heard Daria before she saw her, heels punishing the path, and then Daria was beside her with two glasses, holding one out, not looking at her, looking at the slate. They got his dates wrong, Daria said. I know, said Maren. I told them twice, Daria said. They're getting it recut. They stood. Inside, the band finished something and started something slower. I hear you in my sleep, Daria said, conversationally, to the sundial. Some nights still. Your hand on the door. Both hands, Maren said. It was both hands. Daria nodded, as though this were the correction that mattered, and perhaps it was. Then she took Maren's glass back off her, set both down in the grass like a woman clearing a table, and said, come and dance with me, my daughter's married and my feet are killing me and I'm not wasting the slow one, and Maren let herself be led across the gold panels and into the noise, and they danced, two old chambermaids in the dining room of the Esplanade, badly, gloriously, while the bride pointed at them and laughed, and the staff moved along the walls collecting glasses, unseen, the way they are trained to be, the way Maren saw every one of them.

It was past midnight when she got back to 214. She took off the blue dress and hung it and washed her face and turned down her own bed, thirds, edges away, and then she stood at the window for a while with the lights off, listening. A hotel at night has a sound, she had forgotten she knew it: the lift settling, water in pipes, a door far off closing on a laugh, ninety-odd rooms breathing. Somewhere above her the new married couple slept over the old air. She got into bed and reached for the switch and then did not touch it. She left the lamp on, my dear, the small steady gold of it, and lay back, and let the building hold them all till morning.
