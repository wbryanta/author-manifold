By two o’clock the house had given up most of itself. What remained stood out on the grass of Voss Street looking embarrassed—the chifforobe with its mirror catching clouds, the dining chairs lined up like children waiting to be claimed, the headboard leaned against the maple her brother used to climb. Strangers moved among it all, touching things. That was the part Vida had not prepared for, the touching. The price stickers she had managed, and the ad in the Shopper, and the change. But not the hands. A man in a Bengals cap ran his thumb along the table’s edge the way you test a blade. Two women turned her mother’s soup tureen upside down, hunting the mark. A girl no more than six sat herself down in Mercy Pride’s wing chair and bounced.

Mercy Pride was eleven weeks dead and her house was open to anybody with a dollar.

Vida sat on the porch behind a card table with a King Edward cigar box for the money, making change out of it, saying thank you, saying it’s marked, saying no, the glider isn’t for sale. She was fifty-four years old and she had driven down from Cleveland on Thursday with rubber gloves, contractor bags and a list, believing a list was the same as being ready. Friday she had emptied the medicine cabinet—Doan’s pills, witch hazel, a pink sponge roller with one white hair still wound through it—and that hair had cost her twenty minutes on the bathroom floor with her back against the tub. After that the list behaved. Tag it, stack it, sell it. The realtor wanted the house broom-clean by Monday. Broom-clean. As if Mercy Pride had ever left a house any other way. As if the woman hadn’t scrubbed this one on her knees every Saturday of her life until the linoleum gave up its pattern in self-defense.

The dining table went first, the way she knew it would. Real cherry, dark as a church pew, with legs turned like a woman’s calves. Two men carried it down the porch steps sideways and the table tilted in the afternoon light, showing its underside to the street—pale, unfinished, secret—and Vida had to look at her hands for a minute. Mercy bought that table from Sterns’ downtown, on layaway, a dollar fifty a week, starting in the spring of 1951 and paying out in the fall of 1958. Seven years. Saul was a baby when she started and a third-grader playing piano by ear when she finished, and in between she cooked at the foundry cafeteria five days a week and cleaned for the Hendersons out on Lake Road on Saturdays, and every Friday evening she walked to Sterns’ with the coupon book in her purse like it was a passport. The chairs took another four years. By the time the whole set sat in the dining room it had cost more than a decade, and Mercy guarded it accordingly. Felt pads. A blanket under the good cloth. Nobody ate on it. The Reverend ate on it twice, and the man from the insurance company never got past the kitchen in nineteen years of collecting. Vida and Saul did homework at the kitchen table, ate at the kitchen table, lived at the kitchen table, while the cherry table stood in the next room like a relative too fine to speak to them.

Once, the winter she was twelve, Vida had spread a towel on it and ironed a blouse there because the kitchen was full of Saul and his friends and their noise, and the iron’s heel left a scorch in the shape of a smile. Mercy did not whip her. Mercy did not say one word. She came home, saw it, set her purse down, and went to her room, and the quiet that came out from under that door for the next nine days was worse than any belt. Then a runner appeared, white with crocheted edges, laid exactly over the scar, and it stayed there for forty years. Vida had taken the runner off Friday morning and found the scorch waiting underneath, faded to gold, still smiling.

“Is this real cherry?” The woman asking wore linen the color of oatmeal and sunglasses pushed up in her hair, and she had driven in from one of the lake towns where the antique shops were—Vida had watched her Volvo nose up the street twice before parking. “Because I’ll give you forty. It’s got that burn.”

“It’s marked sixty.”

“With the burn, though.”

“The burn’s the history,” Vida said. “You’re getting it free.”

The woman laughed like they were friends now and counted three twenties out of a wallet thick as a paperback. While her husband and the man in the Bengals cap walked the table to the Volvo, she looked up at the house, at its green shingles and its porch posts Mercy had painted white every third summer without fail, and said, “These old houses. The bones are just wonderful. Are you handling the sale for the family?”

“I am the family,” Vida said.

“Oh,” the woman said, and then, recovering, “Well. It’s a lovely table,” and Vida said thank you and put the twenties in the cigar box and did not watch the table go into the dark of the wagon, because some things you let your eyes have and some things you don’t.

Booker Pride had been a rumor since 1963. Vida knew her father from a single photograph—a narrow man in a hat, leaning on somebody’s Hudson, laughing at something outside the frame—and from her mother’s silence, which was total and lifelong and somehow louder than cursing. So everything in that house Mercy had bought alone, piece by piece, year by year, out of cafeteria wages and the Hendersons’ Saturdays, and she kept it the way some women keep a grudge: spotless, covered, off-limits. The parlor furniture wore plastic until 1989. The good lamps had bulbs in them that were never turned on; there were everyday lamps for light. Growing up in that house was like growing up in a museum where the curator also did the cooking, and Vida had spent forty years telling the story that way—to Curtis, to her girlfriends, to Della when Della was old enough—funny at first, then not funny: my mother loved her furniture more than she loved me. It was a good story. It explained things. It explained why Vida’s own house in Cleveland had no parlor and no plastic and chairs you could put your feet on. It explained 1978, when she was eighteen and stood in this same doorway with a suitcase and Curtis Mayes idling at the curb in a Buick the color of grape pop, and Mercy looked at her once, up and down, and said, “Go on, then. But don’t call here crying,” and went back to her ironing. It explained the Christmases after, the two-hour visits, the phone calls you could have boiled an egg by. The story explained everything except why Vida kept telling it. A story you have to keep telling is a story that hasn’t finished with you yet.

A child struck the piano.

Just one note, high up, a tooth of sound, and Vida was off the porch and halfway across the grass before she knew she had stood. The piano was on the lawn near the lilacs, an upright Mercy had bought off the Lutherans in 1959 when they got a new one for their sanctuary, hauled here on Otis Tucker’s truck with six men and two near-disasters, and it was Saul’s. Saul played boogie-woogie on it when Mercy was at work and “Precious Lord” when she was home, and he could do a thing where his left hand walked downtown while his right hand stayed in church, and the whole street knew the difference between four o’clock, when school let out, and five-thirty, when Mercy got off, by what was coming through the Prides’ window screen. He was drafted in October of 1968. The telegram came in May. Mercy closed the lid over the keys the same week, and for forty-five years she dusted that piano every single day and never once opened it. The lid was open now—Vida had opened it herself this morning, telling herself it would sell better that way—and a little girl in pink barrettes stood on tiptoe with one finger still on the key, looking up at the woman bearing down on her with no idea what she had done.

Vida stopped. Took a breath. The girl’s father was already pulling her back, saying sorry, sorry, we were just—and Vida heard herself say, “Does she play?”

“She wants to learn. We been pricing them and—” He glanced at the sticker. Two hundred. He had on a Cedar Point T-shirt washed thin and the careful look of a man doing math.

“Can you move it?”

“My brother-in-law’s got a truck.”

“Then it’s hers for the moving,” Vida said. The man started to object, the way you do, and she said, “It needs playing more than I need two hundred dollars,” which was true, and also was not the reason, and the little girl, sensing something had swung her way, reached out and pressed the same note again, soft this time, and let it ring out over the grass and the chairs and the strangers, and nobody in the yard knew they were at a funeral except Vida, and she found she didn’t mind. It was a better funeral than the first one.

Lovey Mims came up the walk around three, leaning on her cane with one hand and carrying a pound cake wrapped in a dishtowel with the other, which violated several laws of physics but Lovey had been violating those since Truman. She was eighty-eight or ninety-one depending on which Bible you believed, and she had lived across the street since before Vida was born, and she sat herself down on the porch glider—the one thing not for sale—with the air of a woman taking her seat at a trial.

“They picking it clean,” she said, watching a man carry the soup tureen to his car.

“That’s the idea, Miss Lovey.”

“Mm.” Lovey unwrapped the cake, broke off an end, and handed it over like communion. “Mercy’d have a fit, all these feet on her floors.”

“Mercy’s having it,” Vida said. “I can feel it on the back of my neck.”

Lovey laughed, then didn’t. They sat awhile. A teenager bought the box of mason jars, four dollars. The wing chair went, and the whatnot shelf, and the figurines off the whatnot shelf one by one, the shepherdess and the cardinal and the praying hands, dispersing into the county like seed. The glider creaked under Lovey in the old rhythm, the exact rhythm, and Vida realized she had been hearing that creak across the street her whole childhood, summer evenings, two old women—except they weren’t old then—Mercy on this glider and Lovey on her own porch, calling across Voss Street to each other about tomatoes and the foundry and other people’s children.

“You know she sat up nights,” Lovey said.

“Ma’am?”

“Your mama. After Saul. After you went, too. I’d be up with my bladder, two, three in the morning, and look over here and there she’d be at the parlor window. Not crying. Not rocking. Just setting in the dark with her hand flat on that table. First I thought she was praying. Then I figured it out. She was counting.”

“Counting what?”

“What was still there.” Lovey took another piece of cake and chewed it down before she went on, because she was a woman who believed a story should cost you a little waiting. “You know how your mama come up here. Forty-seven, same train as me, near about. But you don’t know about Lowndes County. She told me one time, Christmas of fifty-three, we got into Albert’s blackberry wine and she told it once and never again, and I never carried it nowhere till right now, so listen. She was eleven. Her daddy got behind at the store—everybody was behind at the store, that was the store’s whole business—and one morning here come the man from the store with the sheriff and a truck, and they went in the house and they brought it out. All of it. The bedstead. Her mama’s pie safe her granddaddy built. The sewing machine her mama took in work on. The stove, child. They took the stove with the fire hardly out of it, and the laying hens out the yard, and her daddy stood there studying his shoes and her mama stood there humming, humming, like it was a washday, and eleven white men and one truck took everything four people had in this world before dinnertime, and it was legal.” Lovey set the rest of the cake down. “So don’t you tell me your mama loved furniture. Your mama didn’t love furniture. Your mama stood guard.”

Out on the grass a woman was opening and shutting the chifforobe’s doors, testing the hinges. The mirror swung its plate of sky across the yard, across the porch, across Vida’s face and away.

“She never said,” Vida said finally. “Not any of it. Not one time, Miss Lovey. Not Alabama, not—she never even said she was glad to see me. I’d come down at Christmas and she’d say, ‘You look thin,’ and feed me, and dust around me, and that was the visit.”

“Said.” Lovey made the word sound like something she’d found on her shoe. “You been waiting on said. Fifty years waiting on said.” The glider creaked. “Some women love out loud and keep a nasty house. Some love comes out crooked. Comes out as bleach and starch and don’t-sit-there. It ain’t less, girl. It’s just harder to spend.” She looked at Vida then, straight on, with her clouded-over eyes that didn’t miss anything that mattered. “And look at you now. Setting up here at a table taking money. Deciding. Sixty for this, free for that, no ma’am, not the glider. Your mama was eleven years old standing in a dirt yard watching it go and couldn’t do nothing but watch. You doing it with your own hands, on your own say-so. That’s the whole difference between a robbery and a blessing, and don’t you forget which one this is.”

The chifforobe sold at four to the woman who had tested its hinges, but the drawers had to be emptied, so Vida knelt on the parlor floor in front of it one last time. Top drawer: gloves, handkerchiefs, a sachet gone to dust. Second: the good tablecloth and the runner with the crocheted edges, and she set the runner aside without deciding why. Bottom drawer, under a lining of newspaper—the Chronicle, March of 1972, Nixon in China—her fingers found a hard edge, and she drew out a second cigar box, King Edward, twin to the one out on the card table holding the afternoon’s money, and she sat back on her heels and looked at it for a long minute before she opened it the way you look at a door you already hear something behind.

Receipts. Layaway coupon books, rubber-banded by year, 1951 through 1962, the paper gone soft as cloth from handling, paid stamped at the end of each one in red ink faded to rust. Saul’s funeral program, the crease worn translucent. And under those, letters. Vida’s letters. The dutiful two-page Christmas letters, the postcard from Niagara Falls, the announcement of Della’s birth with the little pink ribbon, the school picture of Della at seven, gap-toothed, that Vida had no memory of sending—every one of them opened, every one of them furred at the folds, the way paper gets when hands return to it in the dark, again and again, over years. At the very bottom, a photograph Vida had never seen: a girl of maybe twenty on a sagging porch somewhere hot, one hand on the rail, head thrown back, laughing with her whole mouth at something outside the frame, and it took Vida the length of three breaths to understand that the girl was her mother. Mercy before. Mercy in the country of before, where Vida had never been allowed to visit, laughing like a woman who didn’t yet know what could be carried out of a house.

She did not cry. She sat on the parlor floor with the box in her lap while strangers’ feet went up and down her mother’s porch steps, and she thought: she counted me. All those nights with her hand flat on the table, taking inventory of what was still hers, what hadn’t been taken yet, and I was in the count. I was in the box with the receipts. Paid, stamped in red. It was not the love Vida had wanted. It was not said, would never now be said. But it was love with a deadbolt on it, love put where the sheriff couldn’t get it, and Vida had spent fifty years standing outside the door listening for it and calling the silence empty when the whole time there had been something behind the door, breathing, keeping watch, wearing her letters soft.

By half past five the yard was grass again. The Tucker boys took the headboard and the kitchen table; a young couple from out toward Elyria took the bedroom set, shy about it, just married, and Vida knocked twenty off without being asked because Mercy would have looked hard at the girl’s thin coat. The piano went last, four men and a truck and the little girl in pink barrettes supervising from the sidewalk with her arms folded like a foreman, and when they got it up on the truck bed she played a fistful of notes that weren’t a song yet, and the sound came down off the truck and through the maple leaves, and Vida stood in the yard and let it land on her.

“Take the glider, Miss Lovey,” she said, when the street was quiet.

“Thought it wasn’t for sale.”

“It isn’t. It’s going across the street where it can see the house.”

Lovey ran her hand along the glider’s arm, the paint worn through to gray wood in the shape of other hands. “I been wanting this glider since Eisenhower,” she said, which was as close as she would come, and Vida said she’d carry it over directly, which was as close as she would.

Then the house. She walked it once, room to room, broom-clean, her footsteps loud now that there was nothing left to soak them up. The light came in low and long through the parlor window, the way it must have come in all those years onto plastic slipcovers and forbidden lamps, and it fell across the bare floor where the rug had been, and there it was: a great rectangle of darker wood, unfaded, the color the whole floor used to be, the color of 1951. The shape of what had stayed. Everything around it had been worn pale by light and feet and years, but under the rug, under the guarding, the wood had kept its first deep color, and Vida stood at the edge of that rectangle like a woman at the edge of water.

She knelt down and put her hand flat on it. The wood was warm where the sun crossed it. She stayed that way for a while, counting, though if anyone had asked her what, she could not have said—the table, the piano, the boy, the girl on the Alabama porch, the eleven white men and the truck, the letters worn soft, the note still hanging somewhere in the leaves outside. What was taken and what was given away and the whole difference between them, which her own hands now knew.

“I see you,” she said out loud, to the room, to the rectangle, to the window where a woman had sat up nights with her hand exactly like this. The house didn’t answer. It didn’t need to. Empty, it held everything.

She took the runner with the crocheted edges, and the cigar box with the receipts and the letters and the laughing girl, and left the other cigar box, the one with the money, sitting on her hip like a baby. She put the key under the mat for the realtor. On the porch she stood a minute in the long light, then picked up the glider, which was heavier than it looked and lighter than it should have been, and carried it across Voss Street her own self, on her own say-so, while Lovey Mims held the screen door, and somewhere down the block, faint, a piano said something that wasn’t a song yet but was going to be.
