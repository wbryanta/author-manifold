The road came up out of the water a mile before the town did, which surprised her, though it shouldn't have. Roads were built on grades, and grades survived. The asphalt was gone, scoured down to its bed of crushed rock, but the bed held its crown and its ditches the way a sentence holds its grammar after the words have worn away, and Maren Holt drove the county truck down it at ten miles an hour, below the old waterline, below the bathtub ring of bleached stone that circled the basin like a watermark on paper, down into the place where she had been born.

The ring was sixty feet above her by the time she stopped. That was what the drought had done. Sixty feet of lake gone into the sky over four summers, and at the bottom of all that absent water, Harwood, or what the water had agreed to give back of it.

She sat in the cab for a minute with the engine off. Through the windshield the basin was the color of an old photograph — grays and tans and the dull silver of cracked mud, mudflats split into hexagons big as dinner plates, and standing up out of the flats at intervals the dark geometry of foundations. She could see the grid from here. That was the thing nobody had told her, in all the years of telling: that the town would keep its grid. Main Street ran east toward the dead river channel exactly as the 1887 plat said it did, eighty feet of right-of-way, and Second Street crossed it at a true right angle, and the blocks lay inside the streets like panes in a window with all the glass knocked out.

She was sixty-seven years old. She had been seven the spring the gates closed. The arithmetic was the kind she did all day, every day, for the county — distances, closures, the difference between record and measured — and it still seemed wrong to her, the way her own life refused to close within tolerance.

Maren got out and dropped the tailgate and began setting up the base station on the highest stable ground she could find, a knob of sandstone the lake had swept clean. The routine steadied her, as it was meant to. Tripod legs pressed into rock. Tribrach leveled, bubble centered, the receiver threaded on and powered up, the small green light beginning its patient blink as it gathered satellites. She logged the point in her field book in the hand she'd been taught forty years ago, all capitals, no ambiguity: BASE 1, SANDSTONE OUTCROP 0.4 MI W OF HARWOOD TOWNSITE, and then the date, and then, because field books were legal documents and she had never once lied in one, she did not write anything else.

The county had sent her because she was the county surveyor and because the townsite, exposed, had become a problem of jurisdiction. Sightseers were coming down the old road on ATVs. Somebody had pried loose a chunk of the bank's vault door and tried to sell it in Selby. The historical society wanted documentation, the sheriff wanted a map of the hazards — open cisterns, well casings, the school's basement standing full of black water like a tank — and the Corps of Engineers wanted updated topography of the pool while they had the chance, sediment depths, the new shape of the old ground. Nobody at the courthouse had said anything about Maren having been born in Harwood. She wasn't sure anyone left at the courthouse knew. The people who would have known were mostly in the ground at Cedar Hill, in the section the relocation contractor had filled in the winter of 1963, and that was a piece of it too, the piece she had not written in the field book.

She shouldered the rover on its pole, hung the data collector at her hip, and walked into town.

The mud had dried enough to bear her in most places. Where it hadn't, she learned to read it, the faint difference in sheen, and went around. The flats were littered with the lake's leavings — mussel shells in drifts, white and violet, crunching underfoot like crockery; the skeletons of carp, rib cages delicate as combs; a trolling weight; a pair of sunglasses; a lawn chair, lake-furniture fallen out of forty summers of other people's recreation onto the streets of her town. The smell was low and mineral and faintly sweet with rot, the smell of a drained stock pond magnified by a square mile. Killdeer ran ahead of her in the empty street, crying their own names.

The first foundation she came to she knew without checking the plat, and she stood at its corner a moment before she let herself believe she knew it. Harker's Feed and Seed. Her father's store — his and her uncle Roy's. The foundation was poured concrete, eighteen inches wide, and along the east wall the anchor bolts still stood up in their row, each one wearing a collar of rust scale, threads gone to lace, and where the loading dock had been, two stub walls held a ramp of silt the lake had built grain by grain over sixty years. She had ridden that dock like a stage, like a ship. Her father used to set her on a sack of laying mash while he talked with farmers, and the talk in 1962 and 1963 had been one talk only, in the store, in the church, in her parents' kitchen with the radio off: the dam, the appraisers, the condemnation hearings in Selby where men in suits explained to a room of standing farmers that the river they had lived on was going to become a flood-control and recreation asset, and that fair market value would be paid.

The appraisers had come with paint. That was what she remembered with her body rather than her mind, the way she remembered the dock — men with brushes painting numbers on the houses, big white figures on clapboard, on brick, on the limestone of the church, so that for the last year of its life Harwood had walked around numbered like livestock. Their house had been 47. She remembered her mother standing in the yard looking at the 4 and the 7 as if they were a word in a language she refused to learn.

Maren set the rover's point on the foundation corner and took the shot. Northing, easting, elevation, all of it in the state plane system, tied to the continent, tied to satellites her father had not lived to see launched. FEED STORE, NE COR FNDN, she wrote, and then made herself add the parcel number from the old assessment rolls she'd pulled, because that was the work: to take what was here and bind it back into the record, so that the record and the ground agreed.

She worked east down Main Street through the morning. The town gave itself up in pieces, and she logged the pieces. The bank, its vault standing alone in the middle of its block like a tomb, the building burned off around it in '63 — they had burned most of the town, she had learned later, the structures nobody bought at auction and trucked away; the fire department from Selby used Harwood for practice for a month, and her family was already gone by then, twelve miles away in a rented house, but other families had driven back up the bluff in the evenings to watch, and her mother had called those people fools to their faces, fools to grieve in public for the benefit of the burn crews, and had then gone out to the rented porch every one of those evenings and watched the smoke stand up over the bluff line until dark.

The drugstore, recognizable by its terrazzo entry, the only floor in town fine enough to survive: a fan of pink and gray chips set in cement, scoured to a dull glow, with the brass strip of the threshold still bedded in it. She knelt and put her palm flat on the terrazzo. Cold. It had been cool in summer too, that floor; you came in barefoot out of August and the floor met your feet like water. Doc Pruett's wife had run the counter. Lime phosphates. A boy named Carl Reese had once eaten thirteen straws' worth of straw wrappers there on a dare and been sick in the street, and Maren stood up now in the middle of the flat gray basin and laughed out loud, one short syllable that startled the killdeer, because she had not thought of Carl Reese in fifty years, and the floor had handed him back to her like a receipt.

The school stood at the east end of Main where the plat said SCHOOL BLOCK, and the school was the worst of it and she had known it would be. Two stories of brick had been dynamited to rubble before the flood, but the wide front steps remained, seven concrete steps ascending to nothing, and the basement behind them lay open and full of water so dark it read as a solid. She shot the corners of the basement for the sheriff's hazard map, flagged it with pin flags, and did not look into the water longer than the work required. She had learned to read in that building. Mrs. Aldous, first grade, who in April of 1964 — the gates already closed downstream, the river already pooling in its lowest pastures, the school year amputated to let families move — had had each child print their name and the date on a sheet of tablet paper, and had told them the papers would go into a jar, and the jar into the cornerstone of whatever school they built in whatever place they all ended up, and there had never been such a school or such a place, the families of Harwood having scattered like a dropped handful of gravel, and Maren had wondered all her life what Mrs. Aldous did with the jar.

At noon Reyes came down from the rim with the second rover and a cooler, picking his way along the old road, and they sat on the school steps and ate.

“This is the strangest job site I've ever been on,” Reyes said. He was twenty-six and good with the equipment and decent enough to be quiet most of the time. “It's like surveying somebody's memory.”

“It's a townsite,” Maren said. “We've done townsites.”

“Ghost towns, sure. Up in the hills. But those died slow. Everybody got to leave one at a time.” He gestured with his sandwich at the gridded flats. “This one's like — it stopped. Like a watch.”

“It was condemned,” she said. “Eminent domain. They paid for it.”

“You sound like the Corps.”

“I sound like the record,” she said, and heard how it came out, and softened it. “The record's what we have. Take the north half of the grid this afternoon. Shoot every foundation corner you can find, cisterns, wells, anything open. Flag the wells double.”

He went off whistling, the pole on his shoulder like a pike, and she sat another minute on the steps of her school with her boots in the silt, and then she got up and walked, not down Main, but south on Third Street, toward Block 12, Lot 5, which she had not put on the afternoon's task list because it was hers.

The deed description had run through her head for sixty years the way other people carried psalms. Lot Five, Block Twelve, Original Town of Harwood, together with all improvements thereon. She could have staked it blindfolded from the plat, but she found she didn't need the plat. Her feet did it. Down Third past the foundations of houses whose families she could still name — Pruetts, Vogels, the Reese place where Carl of the straw wrappers had lived — left at what had been a hedge of lilacs and was now a low ridge of root crowns, black and fisted, still in their row. And there it was. The house itself had sold at auction; a man from Garnet had bought it for four hundred dollars and trucked it away in two halves, and for years afterward, her mother had refused to drive the road that passed it standing reassembled in a different field, wearing a different porch. What the lake gave back was the foundation, limestone block laid up by her grandfather in 1911, and the cistern, capped with a concrete lid that someone — her father, she was suddenly sure, on the last day, with the truck loaded — had slid into place and mortared shut so that no animal and no child would ever drown in it. And the front step. One slab of native stone, four feet wide, hollowed in its center by feet, her family's feet and the feet of everyone who had ever come to that door, a shallow dish worn in stone, and the lake in sixty years had not deepened it and had not filled it in.

Maren stood on the step. She set both boots in the worn place, where the boots fit, and stood facing the rectangle of air where the door had been, and for a moment the basin tipped and was full again, not of water but of evening: her mother's voice calling her in, the screen door's particular slap, the smell of fried side meat and the radio low, her father on this step in his sock feet smoking after supper, looking at a sky that he already knew was going to become the bottom of a lake.

She had brought the key. She had not admitted to herself that she was bringing it until she'd put it in her cruiser vest that morning along with the plumb bob and the lath pencils, but she had brought it, the key to the front door of a house that no longer stood anywhere near its door, a heavy old bit key her mother had kept in a teacup in three successive rented kitchens and then in the drawer of a nightstand at the care home in Selby, and had pressed into Maren's hand the last coherent week of her life, saying nothing, because by then her mother said almost nothing, but pressing it hard, like a woman setting a corner pin.

Maren took the key out and held it in the sun. Then she knelt at the end of the step where it met the foundation, worked a flat shard of limestone loose, set the key in the dry seam beneath it, and set the stone back. It was not in the record. It would not be in the field book. The lake would come back — the lake was already coming back; rain was on the upper basin this week, the Corps' forecast had the pool rising by Friday — and the key would go down with the step into the dark and lie at her family's threshold, which was, as near as she could work it out, where it had always belonged.

Then she shot the foundation corners, all four, and the cistern lid, and the step, and entered them in the collector under the parcel number, RESIDENCE FNDN, LOT 5 BLK 12, and her hand was steady, and that was the work too.

It was past three when she let herself go to the cemetery.

The cemetery had been on the terrace south of town, above the old floodplain but well below the pool — high enough that the founders had thought it safe from the river forever, which it had been; it had not been safe from the government. The relocation was in the record: two hundred fourteen interments removed in the winter of 1963–64 by a contractor out of Tulsa, reinterred at Cedar Hill in Selby, each grave certified. Maren had pulled the certificates from the county vault in the spring, telling herself it was due diligence for the project, and had read down the typed list until she found HOLT, THOMAS A., INFANT, D. 1953, and the certifying signature, the same signature on two hundred fourteen lines, executed at what she estimated to be forty graves a day in frozen ground by a crew paid by the unit.

Her brother had died four years before she was born. Eleven months old, a fever, three days. She had grown up in the long shadow of him without ever once seeing his face, because the only photograph had been lost in one of the moves after the flood, and that, her mother had said, was the dam's fault too, by then everything was, and Maren had grown up half believing it. What her mother had never stopped saying, in kitchens, at Christmas, finally in the care home where the nurses learned to nod, was that they hadn't moved him. That the contractor had dug where the big stones were and skimped on the small ones. That she had gone to Cedar Hill the day after the reinterment and stood at the new little marker and known, the way you know an empty house from the street, that there was nothing under it.

There had been no way to prove it, and no way to disprove it, and sixty feet of water over the question.

Maren walked the terrace with the rover on her shoulder and the relocation plat folded in her vest. The cemetery announced itself before she reached it: the ground changed, became a field of shallow rectangular slumps, row on row, each one a backfilled excavation that six decades of lakebed settlement had dished into legibility, so that the whole terrace was printed with the plan of itself, a negative of the cemetery, graves drawn in absence. A few stone bases remained where markers had been unbolted and taken to Selby; one marker itself lay toppled and left, a lamb on a small slab, the lamb's face dissolved to a suggestion, and she stood over it a long time before she checked the plat and confirmed it was not his.

His was Row 9, Lot 31, north section, the babies' rows, where the plots were half-size and close together. She counted rows. She paced the stationing off the plat, and then, not trusting her pacing, she ran the rover, staking the lot corner by coordinate off the cemetery's recorded origin, and the pole's bubble settled and the screen said she was standing on it.

Row 9 was different from the rows around it. She had not let herself expect anything, and so she had to look for a while before she would credit what she saw: the rows to the south were dished, slump after slump, regular as stitching. Row 9 was dished for six plots and then, from Lot 29 on, it was not. The ground ran smooth. Unbroken. The settlement that betrayed every excavation on the terrace had nothing to betray there, because nothing there had ever been dug up.

She stood on the smooth ground above Lot 31 with the rover pole in both hands like a staff and she did not cry, exactly; what happened was slower than that and went on longer. Below her, eleven months of a boy, four years older than the river's sentence and sixty years drowned, lay where his father and mother had laid him, in his own town, in his own ground, and every certificate in the county vault said otherwise, and her mother had known. Her mother had stood in a new cemetery in Selby in January of 1964 and known, and been told for the rest of her life, gently, by everyone including at last her own daughter, that grief plays tricks.

“All right,” Maren said aloud, to the terrace, to the rows. “All right.”

She set the pole on the center of the lot and held it plumb and took the shot, a long observation, three minutes of satellites, redundancy beyond any standard the job required, and in the collector she typed what she had never typed in thirty years of field work, a record made not for the county or the Corps or the sheriff but against forgetting itself: UNDISTURBED GRAVE, HOLT THOMAS A 1952–1953, ROW 9 LOT 31 HARWOOD CEM, NOT RELOCATED, and then the coordinates fixed themselves into the file, northing and easting to the hundredth of a foot, and her brother, who had no photograph and no face and for sixty years no certain place in the world, had a coordinate. He could be found now. Whatever the water did. As long as the datum stood, he could be found.

She flagged the row and wrote up the discrepancy in the field book in language the historical society and the state archaeologist would have to act on, dry language, the kind that moves agencies, evidence of incomplete relocation, recommend ground-penetrating radar survey of north section prior to repool, and her hand shook a little on repool and she let it.

The light was going long and amber when she climbed back to the truck. Reyes had finished the north grid and gone up; she could see his taillights on the rim road, and beyond him at the overlook the little crowd of parked cars that gathered every evening now, people from Selby and farther, looking down at the drowned town the way you look at an eclipse. The basin below her had gone the color of a dove. The grid of Harwood lay in it faint and complete, streets and blocks and the dark seams of foundations, like writing showing through the back of a page, and at the east edge, in the old river channel, the first water stood waiting, a long blade of it catching the last sun. The rain in the headwaters was already on its way down. By Friday the channel would be a creek, by June the school steps would be gone, by fall, if the years turned, all of it, the terrazzo and the lilac roots and the worn step with the key beneath it and the smooth unopened ground of Row 9, would lie under the lake again, and boats would pass over it trailing their bright wakes.

Before she broke down the base station, she drove the last monument of the day: a length of rebar with a stamped aluminum cap, set in a drill hole in the courthouse foundation at the corner of Main and Second, tied to everything she had shot, so that the whole town hung from it now in the file like a chart from a nail. On the courthouse step beside it, scoured to gold, she had found that morning the old brass benchmark disk, set by the Geological Survey in 1938, ELEVATION ABOVE SEA, still legible, still exactly where the record said, twenty-six years above water and sixty below it and not moved one hundredth of a foot. She had laid two fingers on it the way you'd touch a sleeping animal.

Now she stamped the date into the new cap, and below the date her license number, and stood up, and looked down Main Street one more time in the failing light, the whole length of it, from the feed store to the school, the way a child looks down a street she is allowed, this once, to walk alone.

Then Maren Holt collapsed the tripod, cased the receiver, latched the tailgate, and drove up out of the town of Harwood at ten miles an hour, climbing through the bathtub ring into the ordinary dusk, carrying the record with her, and the water came on slowly behind.
