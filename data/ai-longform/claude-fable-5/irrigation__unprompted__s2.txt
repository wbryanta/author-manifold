The leak announced itself the way bad news always did on that place, quietly and from below. Wade saw it first as a darkness in the row middle between the seventh and eighth trees of the old Suncrest block, a stain the size of a kitchen table where the dust had gone the color of coffee grounds, and by the time Garrett came down from the house with the second shovel the darkness had grown a sheen, a thin standing gloss that trembled when the wind moved over it, and a killdeer was running the edge of it crying as though the water had been put there to drown her eggs.

"How long you figure," Garrett said. He had on the clothes he'd brought from Denver, jeans that had never been kneeled in and a T-shirt advertising a brewery, and he stood at the rim of the wet with his shovel held crosswise like a man being handed something he hadn't asked for.

"Couple days at least. Maybe since I charged the line Thursday." Wade crouched and pressed his palm flat to the mud and felt the faint cold pulse of it, the line down there three feet under bleeding into the world. "It's the old steel main. Has to be. Dad buried it in sixty-one and it's been rusting from the inside ever since. Eats itself thin and then one June it lets go."

"You can hear it?"

"You can feel it." He took his brother's hand before Garrett could object and pushed it down into the muck, and Garrett's face changed, the irritation going out of it for a second, because there it was, a tremor like a wrist's pulse, the orchard's water running away into ground that didn't need it while four hundred trees stood in a heat that did.

It was the eleventh of June and already ninety by ten in the morning, the Bookcliffs to the north gone pale and shimmering, the Grand Mesa holding its last gray rags of snow up where the water all began. Below the orchard the valley spread out in its quilt of green and tan, vineyard and hayfield and the new subdivisions creeping up out of the river bottom like a tide that had decided not to go back out. Their father had been dead since February. He had died in the front bedroom with the window cracked so he could smell the smudge-pot oil from the Carlucci place across the road, frost season, everybody up at three in the morning burning money to save blossoms, and he had told Wade it was the best smell in the world and the most expensive, and then he had died, not that night but soon, and the orchard had come to the two of them in equal undivided halves, which their mother, had she lived to see it, would have called the only unfair thing the man ever did.

"We could just shut the line down," Garrett said. "Leave it off."

"Trees would notice."

"The trees aren't carrying any crop, Wade. Freeze got it. You told me yourself, ten percent, maybe less. You're irrigating leaves."

"You don't water a tree for this year. You water it for next year." Wade stood and slung the mud off his hand. "Fruit buds for next spring are setting right now, this month. Stress them now and you lose two crops off one freeze. That's how a place actually dies, if you're curious. Not all at once. In installments."

Garrett looked at him a moment and then looked away down the row, and Wade knew the word installments had been a mistake, an open door, because Garrett walked through it immediately. "Speaking of which. The operating note comes due in November. You got a plan for that, with a ten percent crop?"

"I'll talk to the bank."

"The bank's been talked to. Dad talked to them for fifteen years. There's a hundred and ninety thousand against this place, Wade, and that's before the equipment loans, and the only reason the number isn't worse is the land under it got valuable, which is exactly the thing you don't want to hear."

"You're right," Wade said. "I don't." He set the spade and stepped on it and the wet ground took the blade with a sound like a mouth. "Dig. We'll fight after."

For a while they dug. The mud came up heavy and black-brown, shot through with old peach roots like buried wiring, fine ones that snapped and fat ones the shovel rang off of, and the hole filled with seep water as fast as they could open it so that they were soon bailing with a cut-off bleach jug as much as digging, taking turns, one man down in it and one man hauling spoil back from the edge. The killdeer kept up her grieving. Heat sat on the back of Wade's neck like a hand. He had done this work, this exact work, with his father in this same block when he was fourteen, a different leak or maybe, who knew, the same one come back, the steel main running eleven hundred feet from the ditch headgate down the spine of the home forty, and his father standing in the hole stripped to his undershirt saying pipe in the ground is just a promise, it's not a fact, you got to keep going down there and renewing the promise. Garrett would have been nine that year. Garrett had been in the house, probably. Garrett had always had a gift for being in the house.

That wasn't fair and Wade knew it wasn't fair even as he thought it, the way you can know a tool is the wrong size and reach for it anyway. Garrett had picked fruit every August of his childhood, had driven tractor at twelve with a phone book under him, had gone down to Phoenix at nineteen with two hundred dollars and come back at no point at all, except funerals, except now. He was the brother who left. Somebody usually is. The leaving wasn't the sin Wade held against him. The sin Wade held against him was harder to name and was possibly not a sin: that Garrett could stand on this dirt and see through it, see the plat map and the cul-de-sacs and the number, see it the way the man from Mesa Land Partners saw it, who had come to the funeral lunch, the actual funeral lunch, ham buns in the Grange hall, and pressed his card on both of them with a thumb on the embossing like he was sealing wax.

"There," Garrett said.

The shovel had found it. They cleared along the top with their hands, the way you do, because a shovel blade is how you turn a leak into a geyser, and the old pipe emerged under their palms cold and slick, six-inch steel furred with rust and clay, and on its underside, where they couldn't yet see but could feel, the water came off it in a flat hissing fan that had hollowed itself a cavern in the subsoil, a hidden room the size of a bathtub, which explained the wet spreading so far. Wade lay on his belly at the rim and reached down and ran his fingers along the pipe's belly until he found it: not a pinhole. A split, a seam let go, four inches of opened lip with the water driving through it hard enough to sting.

"Walk up and shut the headgate," he said. "All the way. Then crack the drain valve at the stand so the line empties downhill, or we'll be working in a swimming pool."

"I know how the headgate works."

"I know you know."

Garrett went up the row with his long city stride, and Wade sat back on his heels in the hole with the water pushing past his boots and looked at the trees. The Suncrests were forty years old, past it really, half of them with bark canker scarring their crotches like old burns, and they should have been pushed out and replanted a decade ago, and would have been if a decade ago hadn't been when their mother got sick, and there it was, the whole history of the place if you knew how to read it, every block a sentence in a long run-on story about postponement. The new Cresthavens by the road, those were the good news, third leaf, beautiful trees, and they'd carry their first real crop next year if next year happened. He heard the headgate wheel squeal far off, eleven hundred feet of squeal carried down the hot air, and gradually the pulse in the pipe under his hand slowed and softened and faded the way a thing dies, and the cavern under the pipe gave up a long sigh of settling mud, and the orchard was, for the first time that morning, silent except for the killdeer and the far interstate and the leaves.

They broke at noon because there was no choice; the line had to drain before it could be cut. They sat in the shade of the equipment shed on two overturned buckets with the lunch Garrett had bought in town, gas-station sandwiches and a bag of ice they split between a cooler and the backs of their necks. Swallows went in and out of the shed eaves. Garrett ate half his sandwich and then held the rest like he'd forgotten it.

"I'm not the enemy," he said. "I want you to start there. Whatever else, start there."

"I never said enemy."

"You didn't have to. You've been saying it with your shoulders since I got out of the car." He turned the sandwich over, studying it. "Eleven thousand a acre is what they floated at the funeral, which was obscene, them floating anything at the funeral, I grant you. But I made some calls. Sounded them out a little, just talk. It's gone up. Land between the highway and the canal is going for fourteen, fifteen now, and we've got sixty-two acres, and you can do that arithmetic standing in a hole, I've watched you do harder."

"Nine hundred thousand," Wade said. "Give or take."

"Clear of the debt, four hundred apiece, give or take, and that's before anybody negotiates, and I would negotiate." Garrett looked at him. "Four hundred thousand dollars, Wade. You're fifty-one years old. You've got a knee that sounds like a dry hinge and no health insurance and you paid yourself, what, last year, what did you actually pay yourself?"

"That's between me and the place."

"It's between you and Carol, is what it was between, and she's in Junction now in an apartment, so I guess the place won that one too."

Wade set his sandwich down on the bucket lid very carefully, the way you set something down when the alternative is throwing it. The shed smelled of grease and dust and the swallow nests, mud over old mud. "You want to talk about Carol."

"No. I'm sorry. No, I don't." Garrett dragged a hand down his face. "I'm trying to say the thing nobody will say out loud, which is that this stopped working. Not because of you. You're the best operator in this family there ever was, better than Dad, Dad said so himself the last time I sat with him, you didn't know that, well, he did. He said Wade can grow fruit on a rock. And it still doesn't work. Freight, labor, the box price, the freezes coming later every year, the water getting fought over by three states and seven golf courses. You can do everything right and lose. That's not a farm anymore, that's a casino, and we're not even playing with our own money, we're playing with the bank's."

"And your answer is take the buyout and let them put eighty houses on it."

"My answer is take the buyout and let them put eighty houses on it, yes," Garrett said, and Wade had to give him that, the not flinching. "Because they're going to be there either way. Drive this road in twenty years and tell me different. The only question is whether the Vannoys are the ones with the money in the bank when it happens or the ones who fed the place every dime they had right up until the bank held the auction in the front yard. I watched that happen to the Kesslers. So did you. Norm Kessler standing there while strangers bid on his shop tools. I'm not going to be the man standing there, and I'm sure as hell not going to watch my brother be him."

The swallows came and went. Out past the shed door the heat stood over the rows in visible sheets and the trees held still in it, holding their leaves at the angle leaves take when they're rationing.

"You want my side," Wade said.

"I've heard your side."

"You've heard me be stubborn. That's not the same as my side." He picked the sandwich back up, didn't eat it, used it to point down the slope at the Cresthaven block. "Those young trees come into bearing next year. The Highline shares we own are worth more every year the river drops, and they're tied to ag use, but they're ours, and water is the last thing they make no more of. The fruit-stand trade alone went up forty percent the last three years because people will drive from Junction to buy a peach with the orchard standing behind it; they're buying the orchard, the peach is just the receipt. There's a man down valley grossing four thousand a weekend letting people walk his rows with a bucket. I'm not romancing you. I've got the numbers in a folder in the kitchen and they're skinny numbers but they're not nothing, and they all depend on one fact, which is that the trees are still here. Everything I could ever do depends on the trees being here. The day they're not, I'm just a man with a check, and a check runs out. Four hundred thousand at my age, you know what that is? That's a countdown clock with a little money in front of it."

"It's choices."

"It's an ending with the word choices painted on it." Wade rolled the sandwich back into its wrapper. "And there's the other thing, and you'll call it sentiment, so let me get out ahead of you and call it sentiment first. Dad's buried up at Orchard Mesa and Mom next to him and there's a dog under the lilac and a horse somewhere under the Cresthavens that you cried about for a month, you were six. I drink water that falls on the Mesa and comes down a ditch his hands helped clean. You want to know why I won't sell, it's because I can't figure out where I'd be standing afterward. Everywhere I've ever stood is inside that fence."

Garrett was quiet a while. The ice in the cooler shifted with a small glassy collapse.

"That's the best you've ever put it," he said finally.

"I've had the spring to work on it."

"It doesn't pay the November note."

"No," Wade said. "It doesn't."

They went back to the hole at one-thirty with the heat at its full weight. The line had drained and the cavern under the pipe had emptied to a black slick, and they widened the excavation until they could get a hacksaw frame around the steel, and then they took turns at the cutting, which was misery, the old pipe hard with age, the blade chattering, mud to their shins, and during this neither of them said anything about land because there was nothing in either of them to spare. Wade's shoulders went first and then Garrett took the saw and surprised him, found a rhythm and kept it, his brewery shirt soaked through and stuck to him, and Wade stood in the hole holding the pipe steady and watched his little brother work and thought how strange it was, how the body remembered a place even when the man had decided against it. Garrett sawed like their father, short of stroke, patient, letting the blade do it.

The cut section came free at last and Wade lifted it out and turned it to the light, and they both looked at the split, the metal around it gone thin as cardstock, lacy with rust, and then Wade saw the other thing and went still. Eight inches from the new break, clamped around the pipe's body, was a repair sleeve, an old two-bolt Dresser clamp with a rubber gasket, the bolts so corroded they'd half-melted into the bands, and the clamp itself painted, or it had been once, a dab of orange paint of the kind their father had used to mark everything he didn't want forgotten, tree stakes and gate latches and the handles of borrowed tools.

"He fixed it here," Wade said. "This same spot. This same stretch of pipe."

Garrett climbed half out of the hole to see. "When?"

"Paint like that, he stopped buying that orange in the eighties." Wade thumbed the flaking dab. "We're standing in a hole he stood in before either of us could've helped him."

"Or he stood in it alone."

"Probably he stood in it alone."

Garrett took the pipe section from him and held it, the clamp and the new split, the old wound and its bandage and the wound beside it, and Wade watched something move through his brother's face that he couldn't name and didn't try to. "He patches it," Garrett said slowly, "and it holds thirty-five years, and the pipe just splits somewhere else. Because the whole pipe's going. You see that, right? You see what I'm holding. He bought thirty-five years and the pipe is still the pipe. There's a point where you quit patching and put in new line, and there's a point past that where you quit putting in line at all."

"Or," Wade said, "thirty-five years is thirty-five years. He bought every summer between then and now with two bolts and a gasket. You'd hand those summers back?"

"That's not—" Garrett stopped. He set the pipe down on the spoil pile with a gentleness it didn't require. "No," he said. "I wouldn't hand them back."

The repair itself took less than an hour, which is the way of it; the digging is the work, the fix is the grace. Wade had a length of new six-inch and two compression couplings in the shed because he had everything in the shed, the shed was a museum of anticipated failure, and they cleaned the cut ends with a wire brush and rasped the burrs and seated the gaskets and drew the bolts down in turns, opposite corners, the way you torque a wheel, Wade calling it and Garrett wrenching, and then it was done and lay there looking trivial, a bright galvanized stitch in the long dark body of the buried line. They did not backfill. You leave a repair open overnight, their father's law, you let it prove itself before you bury it, because a leak hides faster than anything alive.

Then Wade sent Garrett up to open the headgate, a quarter turn first, then half, and he knelt at the hole with his hand on the new pipe and felt the water come back down the line, heard it before he felt it, a hollow swallowing rush descending toward him through eleven hundred feet of dark, and the pipe shuddered and went taut and cold under his palm and the couplings held, not a drop, not a bead, and out at the row ends the risers coughed and spat air and then ran true, and down all the furrows of the Suncrest block the water came walking, fingering out along the dry ground, darkening it, the smell rising off the dirt that there is no other smell like, and the killdeer flew up and away over the trees as if released from a contract.

Garrett came back down the row and stood beside him and they watched the water move. The light had gone long and orange. The Mesa held its snow up in the last of it like something offered.

"I have to go back Thursday," Garrett said. "Mia's got her thing Friday, the recital."

"I know."

"I'm not going to call the land people. Not this summer." He said it to the furrow, not to Wade. "We get through the season. We see what the young block does, we see the numbers in your folder, the real ones, you show me everything, the skinny and the ugly both. And in the fall we sit down like two grown men who own a thing together and we decide together, and Wade — if it's sell, then it's sell, and you don't make me the one who did it to you. I won't carry that. He left it to both of us. That was the meanest, kindest thing he ever did, and I think he knew it."

Wade was quiet. The water reached the seventh tree, the eighth, pooled and sank, went on. Somewhere up the line the new couplings held their silence, and under the lilac the old dog held his, and the whole place ran on toward dark, drinking.

"Fall," Wade said. "Both of us at the table."

"Both of us."

They gathered the tools in the last light, the saw and the brush and the shovels and the cut length of dead pipe with its orange-dabbed clamp, and Wade started to heave that piece toward the scrap pile and then didn't, set it instead in the bed of the truck up against the cab where it wouldn't roll, and Garrett saw him do it and said nothing, and they drove up the row with the windows down through the cooling air, through the smell of water on dust, past trees that did not know they were an argument, that knew only June, and the line, and the long patient business of setting next year's fruit in the dark of their buds, whether or not anyone would be there to pick it.
