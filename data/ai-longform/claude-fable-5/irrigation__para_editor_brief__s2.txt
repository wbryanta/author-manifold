The leak announced itself the way leaks do, which is to say modestly, almost apologetically: a patch of ground at the head of row fourteen gone dark and soft when everything around it was the color of crackers, and the pressure gauge at the pump house reading eleven where it should have read forty. Ray had seen it at six in the morning, walking the lines with his coffee, and he had stood over the wet ground for a long minute, not because he didn't know what it meant but because he wanted one more minute before it became a thing that had to be dealt with, a thing with a cost attached, in money and in the day.

By eight his brother was there with the shovels, because Ray had called him the night before about the pressure dropping and Mitchell had said he'd come out anyway, he had things to talk about, and Ray had known exactly what things, and had hung up the phone and stood in the kitchen listening to the refrigerator hum.

"Mainline," Mitchell said now, toeing the soft ground. He was wearing the boots he kept in his truck for visits to the orchard, boots that had never broken down across the instep the way work does it. "Or a lateral?"

"Main. The laterals are all downstream of the gauge. You don't lose thirty pounds to a lateral."

"How old is this run?"

"Dad put it in the year before you graduated. So."

"Thirty-one years."

"Thirty-two. You graduated in June. He laid pipe in March."

Mitchell looked down the row, where the Galas stood in their grass-choked strips, the fruit small and tight and green as marbles, June fruit, fruit that was still mostly promise. The trees on this block were old enough that their trunks had gone gray and scabbed, and a few of them, the ones Ray hadn't gotten to with the saw, carried dead wood up high like men holding their broken arms away from their bodies. Beyond the block the valley opened south, the river a suggestion of cottonwoods at the bottom of it, the far slope all cheatgrass and basalt, and above everything the kind of sky that didn't intend to make rain for ninety days.

"Well," Mitchell said. "Let's dig."

They dug. The wet ground gave easily for the first foot and then they were into the hardpan beneath the irrigated layer, the original soil of the place, which had never been persuaded of anything, and they took turns with the pick. The morning was already warm and would be hot by eleven, hot in the dry interior way, heat that didn't lean on you so much as it slowly evicted you. Ray worked with his shirt buttoned to the throat because that was how he'd always worked, and Mitchell stripped to his undershirt within twenty minutes, and Ray saw that his brother had gotten thick through the middle in the soft way of a man whose labor happens in chairs, and he said nothing about it, and was aware of saying nothing about it, which was its own kind of saying.

The pipe emerged at thirty inches, white PVC gone ivory with age, and the crack in it was not a crack but a split eight inches long, the pipe's wall peeled back like a lip. Water seeped from it steadily even with the pump off, the line draining itself into the hole they'd made.

"Frost heave, you think?" Mitchell said, squatting, hands on knees.

"Or it just got old. Things do that." Ray knelt in the mud and ran his thumb along the split. "Dad always said schedule forty was overkill. Bought it anyway. If he'd cheaped out we'd have been doing this fifteen years ago."

"There's a thought. Fifteen years ago I could've dug that hole in half the time."

"Fifteen years ago you wouldn't have been here."

He hadn't meant it to land the way it landed. Or he had, a little, in the part of himself he didn't audit. Mitchell stood up and looked at him over the hole, and the look was patient, which was worse than if it had been angry.

"I'm here now," Mitchell said.

"You are."

"And I drove out here to talk about Hartline's offer, and you know that, and you're going to make me dig for three hours first so you can pretend the conversation got squeezed out by honest work."

"The pipe broke, Mitch. I didn't break it for the occasion."

"No. But you're not sorry it broke."

Ray pulled the tape from his pocket and measured the split and the sound pipe on either side of it, and wrote the numbers on his palm with a carpenter's pencil because he had never in his life carried paper into a field. "I need a coupling and a stick of four-inch and the cement's probably skinned over in the can. I'm going to Harrelson's. You can come or you can keep opening this hole up — I want two feet clear on both ends so we can get the snap couplings on."

"I'll dig," Mitchell said. "It's what I'm dressed for."

The drive to Harrelson's Farm Supply was eleven minutes each way and Ray spent all twenty-two of them not thinking about the offer, which meant thinking about it. One point one million for the whole eighty: the house, the pump house, the shop, the water right, the trees that Hartline Orchards would push out within a year and replace with a high-density planting on trellis, Honeycrisp or whatever the variety of the decade was by then, ten-foot rows you farmed from a platform, the whole block netted against sun and hail like something quarantined. It was real money. Split two ways it was real money twice. Mitchell had done the arithmetic out loud on the phone in April — what Ray's half would earn in a plain index fund, what Mom's care cost a month at Riverview, how many months were left in her even by the generous estimates, how many were left in Ray, for that matter, at the rate he was going, farming eighty acres with one part-time man and a H-2A crew he could barely afford for six weeks at harvest.

The arithmetic was not wrong. Ray had checked it himself at the kitchen table in February, the bad month, when the packing house statement came and he saw what the Galas had actually brought after warehouse charges, and he had sat with the calculator and the woodstove ticking and arrived at the same numbers his brother had. The orchard had lost eleven thousand dollars last year. It would lose money this year, because the April frost had taken maybe forty percent of the bloom in the lower blocks and what was left wouldn't size well without more thinning labor than he could pay for. The numbers all agreed with each other and with Mitchell. The only thing in the county that disagreed was Ray, and he could not have said, under oath, exactly what his disagreement consisted of, except that it lived in his hands and not his head, and the head kept losing the argument and the hands kept not caring.

At Harrelson's, old Dave's son Kevin rang him up and asked how the crop looked and Ray said light, frosted, and Kevin nodded the way everyone nodded that year, the whole valley having been frosted together, and said you hear Pearsons sold, and Ray said he'd heard. "Hartline?" Ray asked, and Kevin said, "Hartline. They're buying everything between here and the bridge. Guy came in last week with a company card bought four hundred dollars of trellis wire like it was gum." Ray carried the pipe out on his shoulder and laid it in the truck bed and drove back, and the whole way the river kept appearing and disappearing between the hills, the river that was the entire reason any of it existed, every tree, every family, every quarrel.

Mitchell had the hole opened up generously and was sitting on the spoil pile in the shade of a Gala, drinking from the water jug, his undershirt soaked through. He'd found, in his digging, a toy: he held it up as Ray walked over. A Matchbox car, a little orange thing, the paint mostly gone to rust-freckled metal.

"This was mine," Mitchell said. "I'm almost sure. I had this exact one. The dune buggy."

"You buried half your toys out here. You used to dig roads in the rows till Dad caught you and tanned you for cutting roots."

"He never tanned me. He tanned you. By the time I came along he'd lost his conviction." Mitchell turned the little car over, spun a wheel that still spun. "Thirty inches down. That's not me burying it. That's it working its way down for forty years. Things sink."

"Things sink," Ray agreed, and dropped the new pipe by the hole, and went for the saw.

The repair itself was the kind of work that looked like nothing and demanded everything: the cut ends had to be square and dry, and the pipe was below the water table they'd made for themselves, so they bailed the hole with a coffee can, and Ray packed rags around the cut ends, and the sun came up over the shoulder of the hill and put the hole in full light, and they knelt on either side of it like men working on an animal. Ray cut out the split section and dry-fit the new piece with the couplings and marked everything with the pencil so the cement work could go fast, because the cement gave you seconds, not minutes.

"You want to talk about it while the primer dries," Mitchell said, "or you want me to wait till you've got cement on your hands and can't hit me."

"I've never hit you in my life."

"You hit me in 1989."

"You sold my bike."

"I sold your bike," Mitchell admitted. "Okay. So you've hit me once in your life and it was justified. Ray. The offer expires end of the month. Forsythe called me Tuesday — they're not bluffing about that, they've got a planting schedule, they want trees in the ground by next spring and they need the season to rip and rip again and fumigate. After June thirty it's not one-one, it's whatever they feel like, which is less, because every year you hang on the place is worth less. The trees are old. The system's failing — we're kneeling in the proof. The packout's been dropping five years straight."

"I know the numbers."

"I know you know the numbers. That's what kills me. You know them better than I do and you act like they're weather. Like they'll change if you just stand out here long enough."

Ray swabbed purple primer around the pipe end, the chemical smell sharp and almost sweet in the heat, a smell that was for him the smell of his father's hands, because his father had been the one who did all the cement work, kneeling exactly here or somewhere near here in 1991, telling Ray hold it still, hold it dead still, you've got four seconds to seat it and a quarter turn, and don't breathe the primer, it'll make you stupid, and your mother says we can't spare it.

"Mom asked about the place last week," Ray said. "Wednesday. I was there Wednesday."

Mitchell was quiet for a second. "What'd she ask."

"She asked if the Bartletts were done blooming. We took the Bartletts out in 2006, but I said yes, they bloomed heavy. She said good, tell your father." He capped the primer can. "Then she asked who I was."

"Ray."

"I'm not telling you that to win. I'm telling you because you asked."

"It's twelve-eight a month at Riverview," Mitchell said quietly. "Her savings carry her to about Christmas. After that it's us, and I can do some, Ray, I can, but Caitlin starts at UW in the fall and I am not going to pretend that isn't real either. The place could carry her. The sale could. That's not betrayal, that's her own land doing the last thing land does for a family."

"The last thing land does for a family," Ray repeated, not nastily, just turning it over the way Mitchell had turned the toy car. "You practice that one in the truck?"

"On the phone with my wife, actually. Three times a week. You think I like this? You think I drove out here to win something? I lie awake on it same as you. It's my name on the deed same as yours."

"It is," Ray said. "And your name hasn't pruned a tree since Clinton."

"There it is."

"There it is."

The primer had flashed off. Ray daubed cement on the pipe and inside the coupling, fast, full strokes, and said "now" and Mitchell, who had not forgotten everything, took the pipe and they seated it together, hard, a quarter turn, and held it, both of them holding it, four hands on one pipe in the bottom of a muddy hole, counting silently to thirty the way their father had taught them to count, one-Wenatchee, two-Wenatchee, while the cement set up and the argument waited for them at the lip of the hole like a dog that knew it wasn't allowed in.

They did the second joint the same way. Then there was nothing to do but wait — two hours minimum before pressure, and Ray gave it three on a mainline, always — and so it was eleven thirty and there was a wall of shade on the west side of the shop, and Ray got the cooler from the truck because he had, despite everything, made two lunches that morning, and that fact sat between them on the bench as plainly as if he'd said something out loud.

"You made me a sandwich," Mitchell said.

"I made two sandwiches. One of them is surplus."

"It's ham."

"It's what was in the drawer."

They ate. A kingbird worked the air at the end of the rows, climbing and stalling, taking something invisible each time. The pump was off and so the whole property had the silence it got maybe twenty days a year, a silence with depth to it, in which you could hear the grass tick as it dried and a truck downshift on the highway two miles off and, beneath everything, the river, not as a sound exactly but as a coolness the air carried up the draw.

"Tell me what it is," Mitchell said. "For real. Not the speech. I've heard the speech — Dad's trees, Grandpa proved up the water right, the family name. I'm asking you, you, what is it. Because from where I sit you've got a job that pays you negative eleven thousand dollars a year, no wife, no kids, a brother you mostly resent, and a million dollars sitting on the table that you keep looking at like it's a snake."

Ray ate his sandwich and looked down the rows. The question was fair, which was the trouble with Mitchell; he had always been fair right at the moments when Ray could have used him being a jackass.

"You remember the frost in '02," Ray finally said. "The bad one, the Easter one."

"I was in Spokane by then. I remember Dad on the phone."

"We ran wind machines and burned smudge pots, the old illegal ones, all night, three nights. Mom made coffee at two in the morning and brought it out in the truck with the headlights off so she could see the thermometers glow. And the third night it got to twenty-eight and held there and we just stood in the rows waiting to see whether we had a year or not. You can't do anything at twenty-eight. You've done it all already. You just stand with the trees." He folded the wax paper into a square, creased it, an old habit. "And I remember standing next to Dad at maybe four in the morning, and he wasn't praying, he wasn't talking, he was just standing there with his hand on a branch like you'd put your hand on a horse. And I thought: there is nobody else in the world standing here. This exact spot. If we weren't here it would just be cold air and nobody to mind. That's not a reason, Mitch. I know it's not a reason. You can't take it to the bank, you can't put it on a spreadsheet, Forsythe can't buy it because it isn't a thing. But it's what I've got. Somebody minding. Eighty acres in the world that get minded, and I'm the minder, and when I try to imagine signing the paper, what I see isn't the money. What I see is the night it gets to twenty-eight and nobody's standing there."

Mitchell didn't say anything for a while. The kingbird took something else out of the air.

"Hartline will run wind machines," he said gently. "Automated. Thermostats and a guy with an app forty miles away. The trees'll be minded. Better than we can mind them, that's the honest hell of it."

"That's not minding. That's monitoring."

"To the tree it's the same."

"It is not the same," Ray said, with more heat than he intended, "and you know it's not, or you wouldn't have driven your kids out here every October for the picking even after Dad died, even when it cost you a Saturday, even when Janet wanted to go to the coast instead. You didn't drive them out here for the apples, Mitch. You can buy apples. You drove them out here so they'd stand in the rows once a year and feel somebody minding something. You wanted them to have it even if it was only one day a year, even if it was only rented. So don't sit there and tell me it's the same to the tree."

Mitchell looked at his hands. They were blistering already, twin shines at the base of each thumb, and he pressed one experimentally and winced.

"No," he said. "It's not the same. You're right. And I still think we have to sell."

"I know you do."

"Both things are true, Ray. That's what I can't get you to sit with. Both things are true at once. It matters and it's over. That's most of life after fifty, things mattering and being over at the same time, and the people who can't hold both at once are the ones who go down with it. I watched Dad do it. He knew by '08, '09, he knew the warehouse consolidation was going to eat places this size, he told me on the phone — he told me, not you, because you'd have argued — and he kept on, and what it bought him was a heart attack at sixty-six in row twenty with a thinning bag still on his chest. You found him. You want me to watch you find a way to be found?"

The shade had moved. The bench was half in sun now and neither of them shifted down it.

"What if I bought you out," Ray said.

"With what?"

"Mortgage. My half as equity, borrow against —"

"They'd appraise it as a farm and it appraises as a failing one. You know what the bank told Pearsons. Nobody lends against trees coming out of the ground in five years anyway. You'd be borrowing against the same dirt Hartline wants, except they'd give you five percent of what Hartline would, and you'd spend the rest of your life servicing it. I ran it, Ray. In March. I ran it because I wanted it to work. You think I sat in my office trying to make the sale pencil? I sat there trying to make you pencil. You don't pencil. Nothing this size pencils anymore. That's not your failure. It's not Dad's. It's just the size of the world now versus the size of this."

Ray got up and walked out into the sun because he didn't want his face watched, and stood at the head of the row with his back to his brother, looking down the long ranked perspective of the trees, the way the rows gathered to a point the way all rows do, an orchard being among other things a machine for making a man feel both ordered and small. Forty percent of the bloom gone. Fruit like marbles. The pipe in the ground curing. His father's pencil marks were probably still on some of the rafters in the shop; his mother was asking after Bartletts that had been firewood for eighteen years; his brother sat in the shade with blisters rising on office hands, having run the numbers in March trying to save him.

"Three o'clock," Ray said, without turning around. "We'll pressure the line at three. If the joints hold, I want to run set four tonight, those trees are eleven days dry."

"Ray."

"I'm not saying no," Ray said, and the words cost him about what the pipe had cost, maybe more. "I'm saying the trees are eleven days dry, and whatever we sign or don't sign, nobody's signing anything by tonight, and the fruit doesn't know about Hartline. So we water. We finish the job. Then I'll look at the papers. Actually look. You can leave them on the kitchen table."

He heard Mitchell get up off the bench, heard the careful way he did it, a man minding his back, and then his brother was standing beside him at the head of the row, and they looked down it together the way they had stood and looked at things together their whole lives, side by side, never facing, because facing was for strangers and for fights.

"They're in the truck," Mitchell said. "The papers."

"Of course they are."

"I'll stay for the watering. If you'll have me."

"You'll be useless. You'll be asleep by nine."

"Probably," Mitchell said. "Put me where I can't break anything."

At three they buried the joint by hand, gently, like a graveside, and walked back to the pump house, and Ray put his palm flat on the old motor the way he always did, a touch with no function, and threw the switch. The pump took up its work with the deep settling hum that was the real voice of the place, the sound under every summer of their lives, and they walked the line together watching the gauge climb — twenty, thirty, thirty-eight, forty — and hold. At the head of row fourteen the ground over the new pipe stayed dry. Far down the block, one after another in their old stuttering sequence, the sprinklers came alive, each one throwing its circle of water up into the late light, where it hung for an instant, whole and silver, before it came down on the trees.
