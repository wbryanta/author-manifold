The kitchen table went first.

Two men in matching green T-shirts carried it out the front door sideways, the way you carry a casket that weighs less than grief says it should, and June stood behind her card table with the cigar box of change and watched its legs swing against the noon sky. Eight dollars. She had written the price herself the night before, kneeling on the linoleum with a roll of masking tape and a felt-tip pen, eight dollars for the table where Odessa Dunmore had pressed half the heads in Galilee, Ohio, every Saturday for thirty years; where she had counted out the hair money into piles—rent, church, light bill, shoes—licking her thumb between bills like each one had an argument in it she meant to win; where Percy Dunmore's plate had been set at supper for two years after he went up to Detroit for the Chrysler work, set and filled and scraped into the slop bucket, until one evening in 1969 Dess put the plate back in the cupboard without a word and that was the funeral he got. Eight dollars. The men in green loaded it into a pickup with a mattress already tied in the bed, and June made change for a ten and said thank you and meant nothing by it.

The house gave up its smell slow. Even with the doors propped and strangers tracking July through every room, it held on—bleach and Tube Rose snuff and the scorched-sweet ghost of Dixie Peach lifting off a hot comb, a smell June had spent forty years in Columbus not smelling, and now could not get out from under. The strangers didn't notice it. They came up the porch steps in twos and threes, wiped their feet out of habit or didn't, and moved through the rooms touching things. That was the part she had not prepared for. Not the selling. The touching. A woman in a sun visor ran her thumb around the rim of every piece of the rose-pattern china like she was checking teeth. A man held Dess's percolator upside down and shook it. They handled her mother's things with that estate-sale tenderness that is not tenderness at all, just appraisal, and June sat by the door making change and learning the difference.

ESTATE SALE, the sign said, stapled to the telephone pole at the corner of Delmore and Vine, and June had laughed a little, taping it up. Estate. A four-room house with a deep freeze on the back porch and a yard her mother had swept—swept, with a broom, like her own mother before her—until the dirt under the maple shone like a floor. The mill that had paid for the house closed in 1981. The men it let go sat on porches for a year, then two, then weren't on the porches anymore. Galilee got smaller the way a sweater does, not all over but in the places that took the strain, and Dess stayed through all of it, doing hair after the hair money quit being enough, working the line at the school cafeteria, putting up chow-chow in quart jars every August like winter was an enemy with a known address. The jars were on a table in the yard now. Fifty cents apiece. A man bought six and didn't ask what was in them.

Around one o'clock a white man in a seed-company cap came out of the bedroom and stood in front of her with his thumbs in his belt loops.

"That wardrobe in there," he said. "I'll give you forty."

"Chifforobe," June said.

"Whatever it is. Forty."

"Tape says seventy-five. It's walnut. My father built the base up under it himself when the leg split."

"It's got a split leg?"

"It had one. Nineteen fifty-eight. It's stood on that repair longer than you've been alive, I expect, and it'll stand on it longer than both of us."

He looked at her, and she watched him decide what kind of afternoon he wanted to have. There was a way certain men squinted at her, a way she had been squinted at across counters and desks and card tables her whole life, and she had a face she kept for it, smooth as a pressed collar, a face that said take all the time you need, the price isn't going anywhere and neither am I.

"Sixty," he said.

"Sixty," she said, "and you load it yourself."

"I'll come back at four with my boy and the truck."

"It'll be here."

He counted three twenties into her hand like he was dealing cards, and June wrote SOLD on a strip of tape and went and pressed it to the mirror of the chifforobe, over her own reflection, and stood there a second longer than she meant to. Inside that mirror was every Easter of her childhood. Dess behind her with the brush and the blue ribbon and the jar of pomade, yanking her head straight by a fistful of hair—be still—and June's eyes in the glass watering from the pull, and behind both of them, small in the corner of the silver, the bed and the window and the maple tree, the whole world she had run from arranged neat as a photograph and twice as permanent.

The bed went at two. A young couple, the girl carrying high and out front, seven months along at least, the boy with paint on his knuckles and a wallet he kept checking like it might have changed its mind. The girl sat down on the edge of the mattress and bounced, light, two times, with her hand spread on her belly, and looked up at June.

"Did anybody—" she started, and her color came up. "I mean. Was this—"

"She passed at County," June said. The lie came out smooth and sweet as cane syrup, and she let it. No need for that child to lie down at night with what June knew, which was that Odessa Dunmore had died in that bed on the second of March with the radio on the gospel station and her hands folded on the chenille spread like she'd been given notice and used it. The mailman saw the mail piling and called Ernestine Pope's grandniece, who had a key, and by the time June drove up from Columbus the radio was still going, still praising, a choir singing to an empty room about a land where we'll never grow old, and June had stood in the doorway with her coat still buttoned and understood that her mother had managed the thing she did best one final time, which was to leave June outside of it.

"Beds hold more borning than dying anyhow," the girl said, mostly to herself, patting the mattress, and June took their thirty-five dollars and helped the boy walk the headboard down the porch steps and did not cry, because crying was a Columbus thing she did in a car with the windows up, and this was Galilee, where her mother's eyes were still in the walls.

It was while they were strapping the box spring to the car roof that the child found the bird.

She was maybe eight, beads clicking in her braids, her mother three tables over holding a Pyrex bowl up to the light. The girl had drifted into the kitchen the way children drift, boneless and certain, and when June came back in she was standing at the window over the sink with the blue glass bird in both hands, holding it up so the sun came through it.

It wasn't priced. June had taken it off the sill that morning and then put it back, taken it off and put it back, and finally left it there because the sill looked wrong without it, looked like a sentence with a word gone, and she figured nobody would think a thing on a windowsill was for sale. She had figured wrong. The girl turned with it cupped in her palms, blue light spilling through her fingers, and said, "How much is this one?"

Wilbur. Everybody called him Bird before he was four years old, because before he was four years old he could whistle. Not whistle like children whistle—he could call. The cardinal, the jay, the mourning dove, so true the trees answered him, and Dess would stand at the screen door with her hands in a dish towel and say that child belongs to the air, half-bragging, half something else June only recognized later, when she had a son of her own and learned that pride in a child has fear folded all through it like marble cake. He was eight the August he begged to go to Tully's pit, and June was eleven, and Dess gave them a quarter for the icebox truck and said watch your brother like it was the easiest thing in the world to do, like the watching wasn't the same as the breathing, like eleven was old enough to hold somebody's whole life in the corner of your eye while a boy named Charles Ray told you that you had pretty hands. There was a transistor radio on the bank. There was a song. June never could remember afterward which song, and spent fifty years not listening to anything from that summer just in case, because however long that song was, that was how long she looked away, and when she looked back the green water was smooth as a tabletop and the air over it was empty of everything, even echo.

Dess did not whip her. That was the thing nobody understood, the thing June had tried once, drunk and thirty, to explain to a man she was married to at the time, and quit trying when she saw his face arranging itself into sympathy. A whipping ends. Dess came back from the pit with Bird's shoes in her hand—he had set them side by side on the bank, neat, he was a neat child—and she set them on the kitchen table next to the snap beans, and she looked at June for a long moment, and then she did not look at June, not in that particular way, not ever again. She bought the blue glass bird at the five-and-dime in the fall and set it on the windowsill over the sink, and from then on she did her crying into the dishwater, where it could pass for steam.

"That one's not for sale, sugar," June said, and her voice came out so strange that the child set the bird down quick, like it had warmed up in her hands. June picked the shepherdess off the whatnot shelf instead, a little Dresden-looking thing with a chipped crook, and folded the girl's fingers around it. "This one's free. Don't tell your mama I said so." The girl grinned with the gaps in her teeth and went, and June stood at the sink awhile, and then she put the blue bird in the pocket of her dress, where it lay against her hip the rest of the afternoon, cool and patient as a stone.

Ernestine Pope came at three, when the crowd had thinned to stragglers and flies. Ninety-one years old, upright as a Sunday, moving up the walk on a cane with a rubber foot, and June came around the card table to take her arm and got her hand slapped lightly for the trouble. "I been walking up these steps since Truman," Miss Pope said. "I expect I can finish out the term." She lowered herself into the porch rocker—NOT FOR SALE, the tape on it said, though June couldn't have explained to anybody why, since she had no more use for a rocker than for a plow—and sat looking at the yard, at the strangers picking over the jar table, at the bald spot in the grass where the men had set the kitchen table down to get a better grip.

"Dess swept this yard the morning she died," Miss Pope said. "Did you know that. March, cold as a witness, and there she was with the broom. I hollered over, told her she was a fool, and she told me—" The old woman's mouth worked at the memory like a seed in a tooth. "She told me a yard tells on a woman quicker than a daughter does."

"That sounds like her."

"Everything sounds like her if you stand still long enough." Miss Pope turned her head and looked at June with eyes gone pale blue at the rims, the way very old brown eyes do, like they are letting the sky in early. "She read me your letters. Every one. Out here on this porch with her glasses down her nose. 'My daughter down in Columbus runs that whole hospital, practically.' Showed me the newspaper when your picture was in it. Kept it."

June sat down slow on the top step. "She never said one word of that to me. Not one. I'd call and she'd tell me about the weather and Reverend Tillman's blood pressure and ask me was I eating, and that—" June opened her hands. "Thirty years of weather, Miss Pope."

"Mm." The rocker went back and forth twice. "Dess loved like she canned. Sealed it up tight and put it away in the dark. It kept. Lord knows it kept. It just wasn't no use to a living soul till the lid came off." She rocked. "I'm not excusing her, mind. Don't you sit there and let the funeral lie to you. Your mama was hateful on Tuesdays and proud all week long and she hurt you in ways she had names for and ways she didn't, and I loved her sixty-some years anyhow, the same as you did."

The flies droned. Down at the jar table a woman was holding chow-chow up to the sun like the visor woman had held the china, checking it for flaws, and June wanted suddenly and unreasonably to go down there and take it out of her hands.

"She never blamed you," Miss Pope said. "About Wilbur."

June's whole body went quiet.

"She blamed the water. Told me once, right in that kitchen, that Tully's pit had been waiting on a child since the day they cut it, that she had known that water was hungry her whole life, and she went on and put a quarter in two children's hands and let them walk to it." The rocker stopped. "What you carried all this time, girl, she was carrying the bigger half of. That's why she couldn't look at you. You had her own sin's face on you."

June looked at her hands. Pretty hands, a boy had called them once, the length of one song ago. "She could have said it," June said. "Any day for fifty years. She could have said it."

"She could have," Miss Pope agreed, and that was all, because she was ninety-one and had stopped wasting breath on smoothing what wouldn't smooth. After a while she took June's arm without asking this time and went down the steps and up the street toward her supper, and June watched her go and thought that the last person on earth who had known her mother on Tuesdays had just turned the corner of Vine Street, and the town was that much smaller, taken in again at the seams.

The man in the seed cap came back at four with his son, a big quiet boy who lifted his end of the chifforobe like it held nothing but air. Before they took it, June opened it to clear the drawers—mothball paper, a fan from Brackett's Funeral Home with a brown-skinned Jesus on it, shelf paper printed with violets—and the bottom drawer stuck the way it had stuck since 1958, and she gave it the lift-and-pull her hands remembered without being asked, and there, pushed to the back under the violet paper, was a King Edward cigar box tied around with string.

She carried it out to the porch steps and sat down with it while the truck pulled away with her mother's mirror riding backward, throwing the house's reflection at her all the way down Delmore Street.

Inside: a curl of hair in waxed paper, soft and black, pencil on the fold—June, 6 mos. A school picture of Bird from the fall before, gap-toothed, ears like handles, the collar of his shirt buttoned to the chin the way Dess buttoned them. Her own wedding announcement, scissored neat from the Columbus paper, the marriage outlived by the clipping by a good twenty years. The newspaper photo, 1998, June third from the left in a group of administrators nobody alive could name, and Dess had kept it like scripture. And under all of it, folded so many times it had gone soft as cloth, a piece of brown grocery bag.

Dear June, it said.

Dear June. Dear June. Dear June.

Eleven times, down the page, in pencil and blue ballpoint and one line of red, the hands of different years, some of them steady and some of them not, the last one wavering the way ninety winters waver. Eleven beginnings. And after every one of them, nothing. White space. The whole rest of the bag, empty as the air over green water.

June sat on the steps of the sold house while the light went long and yellow up the street, and she did not cry then either, but it was close, it was right up under the dishwater. All those years of weather. All those phone calls, her mother sitting in that kitchen with the bird on the sill, and somewhere in the chifforobe drawer this bag, this stack of doors opened and never walked through. Thin love ain't love at all, somebody had told her once, or she had read it, or dreamed it, but her mother's love had not been thin. It had been thick as preserves and sealed the same way, put up against a winter that came anyhow, and June had gone hungry in sight of a full cellar her whole life, and so had Dess, and there was nobody left now to be angry at, which she was learning was the loneliest thing about fifty-four.

The last stragglers drifted off. She gave the leftover oddments to the church van when it came—the fans, the doilies, the percolator nobody wanted, her mother's pound-cake pans, which she almost kept and then didn't, because some things you have to let go down the steps or you'll stay on the porch forever. She folded the card table. She walked the rooms once, the way you check on sleeping children. The house was nothing but rectangles now: bright squares on the wallpaper where the pictures had hung, four soft dents in the kitchen linoleum where the table had stood since before she was born, a clean stripe on the bedroom floor marking the bed's old shadow. An empty house is a held breath. She stood in the middle of it and let it hold.

Then she locked the front door of her mother's house for the last time—the closing was Tuesday; some young couple from Dayton, the agent said, wanted a town where things were quiet, and June had managed not to laugh—and she sat back down on the steps with the cigar box on her knees while the streetlights stuttered on down Delmore.

She unfolded the grocery bag one more time. Eleven beginnings, going down the page like steps going down to water.

"Dear Mama," she said, out loud, to the dark yard her mother had swept, and found that she had nothing to put after it either. So she answered them the only way left. She read each Dear June, every one, the steady ones and the wavering ones, and after each she said, soft, the way you talk in a sickroom, "Yes, ma'am. I'm here. I'm listening." Eleven times, on the steps of an empty house in Galilee, Ohio, until the saying and the silence came out even.

Then she folded the bag along its soft old creases and put it in the box with the curl of hair and the gap-toothed boy, and stood, and her hand found her pocket, and the glass bird was there, holding the last of the evening in itself the way it had held fifty years of kitchen light—blue, deep-water blue, the exact blue of something that has been waiting—and it would not let it go, and neither, she understood, climbing down the steps to her car, would she.
