The wedding was called for half past four and I arrived at the Mirasol a few minutes after three, not because I was eager to see the place again but because the road south of Carmel was empty and the car they had given me at the agency in Monterey was a Lincoln, and the Lincoln did seventy without my noticing. I mention the Lincoln because I want it understood that I arrived at the Mirasol the way the Mirasol's guests had always arrived, in a large car on an empty road, with an hour to spare and nothing in particular to do with the hour. The first time I came to the Mirasol I came on the Greyhound from Salinas with my mother, who ironed in the hotel laundry for twenty-two years, and we walked up the service drive past the garbage racks, and the trip took two hours and forty minutes. This time it took fifty. I have never decided what that difference is evidence of, although I have had thirty-two years to think about it.

A boy in a green vest took the Lincoln away from me and another boy in a green vest offered to carry the wrapped box that held a silver bowl from Shreve's, and I let him, and I stood for a moment on the gravel looking up at the hotel. The new Mirasol is pink where the old Mirasol was white. The new Mirasol has aluminum windows that slide where the old Mirasol had casements that cranked, brass cranks, and I knew the cranks because I polished them, eight rooms to a corridor, two corridors a shift. Everyone who writes about the Mirasol writes that it is unchanged, that it survives, that it is the last of something, and I have read these pieces in the airline magazines and the Sunday supplements and I have never written a letter to correct them. The windows are wrong. That is all I would have said. The windows are wrong, the tile in the loggia came from a salvage yard in San Jose in the spring of 1967, and the bougainvillea on the south wall, which the brochures call legendary, is younger than the bride.

I should tell you why I was at the Mirasol at all. The bride was Victoria Sands, called Tory, the only daughter of Dick and Eleanor Sands, and Dick Sands had been my husband's law partner in San Francisco for nineteen years. Howard has been dead three years now. The Sandses go on inviting me to things, out of loyalty to Howard or out of the particular dread that settles on people when they imagine a widow alone on a Saturday, and I go on accepting, because not accepting requires explanations and I have never been good at explanations. When the invitation came—engraved, a ceremony in the gardens of the Hotel Mirasol, Carmel Highlands—I held it for a while and then I wrote the date in my book. It is not a secret, what I am going to tell you. It has simply never come up. Things do not come up unless one brings them up, and I have made a long study of not bringing them up.

I worked at the Mirasol from June of 1964 until the night of November 29, 1966, and again for one summer after the reopening, June to September of 1968, and then I quit and moved to San Francisco and sold gloves at I. Magnin until I married Howard. In 1964 I was nineteen. I did turn-down, which began at six, and in the mornings I did the rooms, and I can tell you things about the Mirasol that the airline magazines do not know. I can tell you that the soaps were Camay, wrapped in paper, two to a bath, and that Mrs. Aja in the linen room counted the towels out and counted them back and could feel a frayed hem through the whole stack with one thumb. I can tell you that I could strip and make a bed in four minutes, miters and all, and that Mrs. Bayliss, who wintered every year in 412, called every maid Mary and left quarters under the pillow as if the quarters were a secret between her and God. I can tell you that the laundry, where my mother stood at the mangle, smelled of bleach and steam and scorch, and that the smell came up through the floor of the north service stairs, and that for some years afterward I could not walk past certain dry-cleaning establishments. That smell is gone from the new Mirasol. They have fixed that too.

On the night of November 29, 1966, a fire started in the flue of the Cypress Room fireplace, or in the wiring behind the Cypress Room fireplace—the county report allowed both, the way such reports do—and took the north wing in fifty minutes. Six people died. Mrs. Bayliss died in 412. A couple from Bakersfield named Greer died in 414, and a salesman whose name I once knew and have lost died in 406 with two sample cases of dental equipment, which detail was in all the papers because it was the kind of detail the papers like. The other two were maids. Their names were Luz Robles and Ofelia Cruz, and their names were not in all the papers, and when they were, they were spelled wrong.

I had an hour, as I have said, so I went into the lobby. The lobby of the new Mirasol is meant to be the lobby of the old Mirasol and is not, although the deficiency is nothing you could litigate: the beams are distressed, the floor is waxed Saltillo, the desk is in the right place. Off the lobby there is a corridor hung with framed photographs, and I had been told about the photographs—Eleanor Sands had told me about them on the telephone, the way one tells a person things one assumes will charm her—and I went and looked. Hotel Mirasol, 1925, men in white flannels on the lawn. Hotel Mirasol, 1939, a Packard at the door. Hotel Mirasol, 1953, Bing Crosby allegedly among the golfers. And then, between a menu from 1941 and a photograph of the loggia, Hotel Mirasol, 1965: thirty-one of us lined up in two rows on the south lawn with the sea behind, squinting, in uniform. I am in the second row, third from the left, twenty years old. The girl beside me, her arm through mine, is Luz Robles. No one in the photograph is identified. The caption says Staff.

"Frances. Frances Wells, you angel, you came early." Eleanor Sands had me by both hands. She was in pewter silk and she had the brilliant fixed look that mothers of brides have, the look of a woman conducting a fifty-piece orchestra through sheer will. "Have you ever seen anything like this place. Isn't it exactly heaven. Mother and Daddy honeymooned here in 1947, did I tell you, the same dining room, the same everything, nothing has changed, not one single thing—" She saw what I was looking at. "Aren't those darling. Look at the little maids. The same families worked here for generations, the man at the desk was telling me, it's that kind of place."

"Yes," I said.

"Howard would have adored this," Eleanor said, and squeezed my hands, and was gone, calling to someone about the placement of the cellist.

I looked at the photograph a moment longer. Luz is laughing in it, or about to laugh. She was always about to laugh. She could fold a fitted sheet in the air, snap, snap, a thing I never mastered, and she sang under her breath on the corridors, so far under her breath that you heard it only as a kind of warmth coming toward you past the doors. She was twenty-three in the photograph. She did not get older.

The ceremony was held in the gardens, which is what the program called them. The program, heavy cream stock, said The Rose Lawn, Hotel Mirasol. What the Rose Lawn is, is the footprint of the north wing. They never rebuilt the north wing. The insurance, the setback ordinances, I don't know the reasons; when the Mirasol reopened in 1968 the north wing was a graded rectangle of new sod behind a chain-link fence, and now it is a lawn with rose beds and a pergola, and the hotel rents it for weddings at a rate I saw posted, discreetly, in a leather folder at the desk. I sat in the fourth row of white folding chairs on the bride's side and did the survey I could not help doing. The arch where the minister stood, plumbago and white roses, stood more or less over the north service stairs. The first two rows of chairs were in the linen room. Mrs. Bayliss's room, 412, would have been forty feet above the third row, a little to the left, in the air. There is no way to say this to anyone at a wedding and I did not try. The cellist played, the fog stayed offshore, Tory came down the grass on Dick's arm looking the way brides look, which is to say like the entire argument for the way things are done, and I watched the air above the third row of chairs and thought about the cranks on the casement windows, which opened only halfway, by design, and about whether that design was ever reviewed by anyone after November of 1966, and then I made myself stop thinking about it, a skill I have.

At the cocktail hour I was given champagne on the loggia and was introduced, by Eleanor, to the bride's great-uncle, a retired judge named Arthur Dancy, on the theory, Eleanor said, that we would have everything in common. "Frances knows this coast," Eleanor said. "She and Howard used to come down all the time." Then she was gone again.

Judge Dancy was a large pink man with a cigar he was not permitted to light and the manner of a man accustomed to a courtroom in which he was the only licensed source of fact. He told me about the Mirasol. He told me the tile in the loggia was Andalusian, 1924, you couldn't get it anymore. He told me the hotel had never closed a day in its life, that during the war the Navy had wanted it and old man Castle—he was sure it was Castle—had told the Navy where to go. He told me Salinger had written something or other in one of the cottages, or possibly it was Steinbeck, in any case someone had written something.

"There was a fire here at one time," Judge Dancy said. "Before the war. Took the kitchens and one of the wings. They tell me you could see the glow from Point Lobos."

"November 1966," I said.

"Beg pardon."

"The fire was in November 1966. The night of the twenty-ninth. It took the north wing. Six people died."

He studied me with new interest, the interest of a man deciding whether he was being contradicted or assisted. "I think you'll find you've got the date wrong. I'd remember 1966. I was sitting on the bench in Monterey County in 1966."

"Two of them were maids," I said.

"In any case," Judge Dancy said, and moved his cigar to the other hand, "it was a different world then, this coast. You'd know that, if you knew it then. The service you got. The help lived in, in those days, whole families of them, devoted, absolutely devoted, they were like family themselves. That's all gone now. Nobody wants to do that work now."

The evening had turned warm for the Highlands and I was tired, and when I am tired certain words set my teeth on edge. "Help" is one of those words. "Devoted" is another. "Staff," under a photograph in which I have a name and Luz Robles has a name, is a third. When I am tired I remember the last bus to Salinas, which left the highway junction at six o'clock, before turn-down ended, which is the whole of the reason the help lived in, devotion having had, as far as I ever observed, nothing to do with it.

"They lived in because the last bus left at six," I said.

Judge Dancy looked at me for a moment. "You may be thinking of a different establishment," he said, and told me about Salinger again.

Dinner was in the dining room, which Eleanor was right about: it is the same dining room, in the sense that it occupies the same air. There were toasts. Dick Sands spoke about Tory's first pony. The groom—a polite boy named Walker Pruitt who is in business school and who will, you can see it on him already, be a good provider and a fair golfer and a mystery to his children—said the things grooms say. Then Walker's father stood up, a tall man with a glass of the good champagne, and toasted, among other things, "this magnificent hotel, seventy-four years of unbroken Mirasol tradition, the perfect place to begin an unbroken marriage," and everyone drank, and I drank too, doing the arithmetic. The hotel opened in 1924. Seventy-four years, less the nineteen months between November of 1966 and June of 1968, during which the unbroken tradition consisted of a chain-link fence, a graded rectangle of mud, and an arson investigation that closed without findings. Nineteen months is not much, set against seventy-four years. I am sure that is how the brochure writer saw it. The Greers of Bakersfield might see it differently, and Mrs. Bayliss, and the salesman with his dental equipment, but they are not in a position to be consulted, and Luz Robles is not, and Ofelia Cruz is not, and so the tradition stands unbroken, the way most traditions do, by a show of hands among the survivors.

I have not yet told you where I was on the night of November 29, 1966, and I notice that I have been arranging not to. I was on the south lawn. The south wing came through with smoke damage and broken glass and nothing worse, and I came through with it, and I stood on the south lawn in my uniform in the light of the burning north wing holding four towels over my left arm, because I had been in the middle of turn-down when the alarm went and the towels were in my hand and it did not occur to me to put them down. I stood there a long time. The fire department came up from the Highlands and the guests came out onto the grass in whatever they had, and a woman I had turned down a hundred beds for, a woman in a mink coat over her nightgown, walked up to me and took one of the towels off my arm without a word and without looking at me, and walked away holding it, and I have thought since that this was the truest transaction I ever witnessed at the Mirasol, the only one conducted entirely without pretense on either side. I also had my passkeys. In the confusion no one ever asked for them back, and I kept them, and they are in a drawer in San Francisco now, with Howard's watch and his reading glasses, two long brass keys on a ring stamped HM, and I could not tell you why I have kept them except that throwing away keys has always seemed to me an act requiring more certainty than I possess.

Here is the other fact. The fourth floor of the north wing was my floor that autumn. Mrs. Bayliss in 412 was mine; she had called me Mary for three winters. On the night of the twenty-ninth I was on the south corridors instead, because Luz Robles had traded with me. A corporal from Fort Ord named Donnelly had asked me to a dance at the Mission Ranch on the thirtieth, and I wanted the early night before it, and the south wing finished forty minutes sooner, and Luz traded, snap, snap, the way she did everything, and took the north fours, and that is where she was. I have done this arithmetic for thirty-two years and it does not resolve. It is not guilt, exactly, or it is not only guilt; guilt at least proposes that one could have known, and no one could have known, the trade was a trade either of us had made twenty times. It is more that the books do not balance and cannot be made to, and that I have stood all my life on the credit side of a ledger I never audited. I did not go to the dance. Corporal Donnelly, wherever he is, never knew why, because I never told him, and I never danced with anyone at the Mission Ranch again, which is the kind of private observance that does no one any good and that one keeps anyway, the way one keeps keys.

After the cake had been wheeled out and before it was cut, I left the dining room. No one notices a woman of my age leaving a wedding before the dancing; it is assumed to be a matter of fatigue, or of feet, and I let it be assumed. I walked back through the lobby and past the photographs without stopping, and then, instead of going out to the gravel and the boy in the green vest and the Lincoln, I turned down the side corridor toward the south wing, because I wanted to see whether the service door was where it used to be. It is not. The new service door is twenty feet farther along, and it has a porthole window in it now, some code or other, and as I stood there it swung open and a chambermaid came through it pushing a cart. The carts are fiberglass now, with a bag for the soiled linen hung on the end, and the soaps are little bottles, verbena, with the hotel's name on them. The girl was perhaps twenty. On the top shelf of the cart the towels were stacked in pairs, and I counted them without meaning to, the way you read a sign in a language you have tried to forget. Eight towels, four bath mats, the turn-down chocolates in a basket where the evening papers used to ride. Four rooms. The count was right.

I held the door for her. She said gracias without looking at me, and there was no reason on earth she should have looked at me: a woman in gray silk at the edge of a wedding, a silver bowl from Shreve's already on the gift table, a Lincoln waiting on the gravel. She went on down the corridor and I heard, or believed I heard, very far under her breath, something that might have been singing, and I stood in the doorway a moment with the smell of the linen coming off the cart, bleach and steam, the one thing in the entire rebuilt, repainted, rephotographed Hotel Mirasol that they had gotten exactly right, because it is not a thing you can get wrong, it is only a thing you can stop noticing. Turn-down at the Mirasol starts at six. It started at six in 1964 and it starts at six now, and it will start at six when Tory Sands Pruitt brings her own daughter here and tells her that nothing has changed. Then I let the door swing shut and walked out to the gravel, and the boy brought the Lincoln, and I drove north on the empty road with the windows down, and the Lincoln did eighty, and this time I noticed.
