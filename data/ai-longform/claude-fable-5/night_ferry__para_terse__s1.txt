The call came on a Tuesday, and by Thursday night I was standing in the vehicle line at North Sydney watching the ferry take cars into itself like a creature feeding, the loading ramp clanging under each set of tires, the marshallers in their reflective vests waving their orange wands in the dark. It was November and the wind off the harbour had teeth in it. I had not slept much since the call. When my turn came I drove up the ramp into the bright steel belly of the ship and a man in coveralls pointed me into a space between a transport truck and a pillar, and I sat for a moment with the engine off and my hands still on the wheel, listening to the boat hum all around me, before I took my overnight bag and climbed the stairs toward the passenger decks.

The constable's name was Pelley. He had a young voice and he was careful with it, the way you are careful with a tool you have not owned long. A man had been recovered from the water near Isle aux Morts, he said, three days before. No identification on him that had survived the sea, but inside the collar of his coat, a floater coat, the kind fishermen wear, someone had written a name in marker. C. MacKinnon. And a woman who kept a boarding house in Port aux Basques said a Colin MacKinnon had taken a room from her in the spring, on and off, when he wasn't out on the boats. The constable asked if I was related to a Colin MacKinnon, and I said he was my brother, and there was a pause on the line in which I could hear the man deciding how to say the next part, and I said it for him. You need someone to look at him. Yes, he said. If you're able. I'm sorry to ask it.

He had emailed a photograph that afternoon, with warnings attached, and I had opened it at my kitchen table in Sydney with the supper I wasn't going to eat going cold beside the laptop. The face in the photograph was swollen and grey and bearded and could have been my brother and could have been anyone, could have been our father, could have been one of those composite faces they make of men who never existed. The sea does its own work on a face. I knew that already, from a long time ago, and I closed the laptop and sat in the kitchen until the window went black and showed me only myself.

The passenger lounge smelled of diesel and fried food and wet wool. Truck drivers had claimed the good chairs by the windows, men who made this crossing the way other men ride elevators, already settled in with their boots off and their ball caps over their eyes. A television bolted near the ceiling played the marine forecast with the sound down: Cabot Strait, winds northwest thirty knots diminishing to twenty overnight, seas two to three metres. A woman across the aisle was knitting something long and grey, her needles going without her looking at them, her eyes on the black window. I had booked a cabin but I didn't go to it. I bought a tea I didn't want from the cafeteria and found a seat and felt the ship come off the dock, that first deep shudder and then the slow turning, the lights of North Sydney swinging past the windows and beginning to fall behind, and I thought, as I had been thinking for two days, that I was going to look at a dead man's face and tell a stranger whether it belonged to my brother, and that I had not seen my brother's living face in twenty-six years.

We grew up in Main-à-Dieu, which the tourists pronounce wrong and which means the hand of God, a string of houses along a cove on the eastern shoulder of Cape Breton, and our father fished lobster and cod out of a Cape Islander he had built half himself in our grandfather's shed. Colin was three years younger than me but he was the one with our father's hands, everyone said so, the one who could splice a line at ten and read weather off the water at twelve, and I was the one who got seasick in any kind of swell and was no good at hiding that I was counting the hours until we got in. Our father never held it against me, or never said so. He'd send me up to the house to help our mother and keep Colin aboard, and I'd watch them go out past the breakwater in the early light, two figures in yellow oilskins, the small one already moving around the deck like he was born on it, and I would feel relief and shame in the same breath, which is a mixture I have tasted all my life since, so maybe that's where I learned it.

The crossing takes about seven hours. Somewhere out in the middle of it there is a point where you can see no land at all, where the strait is just the strait, black water in every direction and the ship a lit thing crawling across it, and I have always thought there is something honest about that hour, the way it strips you of both shores. I went out on deck around midnight to find it. The wind had eased like the forecast promised but it was still cold enough to hurt, and the deck lights made a small yellow world of the rail and the lifeboat davits and the wet steel underfoot, and beyond that nothing, the dark going down and down. A man was out there smoking in the lee of a bulkhead, one of the truckers, and he nodded at me and said something about the weather being civil enough for November, and I agreed, and we stood there a while in the kind of silence men can keep together when they don't know each other, and then he flicked his cigarette over the side and went in, and I stayed.

I took my phone out and looked at the photograph again, under the deck light, with the wind trying to take the phone out of my hand. I don't know what I thought the cold and the dark would show me that my kitchen hadn't. The face was the same face, which is to say it was no one's face. The eyes were closed. There was a scar through the left eyebrow, and I stood at the rail for a long time interrogating my memory of my brother's eyebrows, of all things, a man's eyebrows, and I could not say. He'd been hit by a boom once, or was that the Targett boy. He'd gone through the ice on MacAulay's pond and split his chin, that I remembered, that was his chin, but the photograph showed a beard. Twenty-six years is long enough that a face can do anything. It can scar and weather and thicken and grow strange, and the sea on top of that. I put the phone away and gripped the rail and looked down at the water sliding past the hull, marbled white where the ship cut it, black beyond, and I thought about our father, because there was no way not to.

I was nineteen the summer he drowned and Colin was sixteen. There'd been a dance at the fire hall, and Colin had come in at three in the morning the way sixteen-year-olds do, and our father woke him at five to go haul gear before the weather that was coming, and Colin groaned something into his pillow, and our father, who never once in his life waited for anybody, said suit yourself and went alone. By noon the wind was up the way the radio said it would be, and by two o'clock Angus Ferguson came in saying he'd seen our father's boat off Scatarie going in a slow circle with nobody at the wheel. They got men out despite the weather and they found the boat with the engine still idling and a half-hauled trap line fouled on the hauler, and they did not find our father, not that day and not that week. Nine days later he came ashore on Scatarie Island, and because our mother could not do it and because Colin was sixteen and white as paper and had not spoken a full sentence since the boat came in empty, I went with my uncle Dan to Sydney to look at him. I was nineteen. I have spent a lot of my life trying not to remember what nine days of ocean does to a man, and the trying has worked the way trying like that always works, which is not at all, and so when Constable Pelley asked me on the phone whether I was able, I knew exactly what I was being asked, which is more than most people know, and I said yes anyway, because there was no one else. There was never anyone else. That is more or less the whole biography of me.

What happened after our father drowned took years to happen, the way the worst things do. Nobody blamed Colin. I want that understood, I want it on whatever record there is: nobody said it, not our mother, not the uncles, not the men on the wharf who took up a collection and finished hauling our father's gear for us. Nobody said it because nobody needed to. Colin walked around the village that fall like a boy carrying something too heavy to put down and too heavy to carry, and everyone looked at him with such tenderness that it must have been like being flayed. He quit school. He fished with the Fergusons for a while and then he didn't. He started drinking the way some men in our family drink, not loudly, just steadily, like bailing a boat that never stops taking on water. And I went to trade school in Sydney and became an electrician and married Ruth and had my daughter and did everything in the right order, and I came home on weekends and watched my brother sink by inches at our mother's kitchen table, and I said nothing for eleven years, and then one Christmas Eve I said everything.

He'd knocked the tree over. That was all. He came in late and full and he caught the tree with his shoulder and it went down in a smash of the glass birds our mother had hung on her mother's tree before her, and our mother was on her knees in the broken glass saying it's all right, it's all right, Colin, it doesn't matter, and something in me that had been waiting eleven years stood up. I don't remember all of what I said. I remember the end of it, because I built the whole thing to arrive there, I can hear in my memory the carpenter in me framing the sentence so it would bear maximum load: He'd be alive if you'd got out of bed. The kitchen went so quiet I could hear the glass settling. My mother on her knees with a glass bird in her hand looking up at me like I was the stranger. And Colin standing very still in the doorway with the snow not even melted on his shoulders, and he didn't say anything back, that was the terrible thing, he just nodded slightly, the way you nod when somebody finally says aloud a thing you've been telling yourself for years, the way you nod at a verdict you've already served the sentence for. He was gone before I got up in the morning. He left a note for our mother and nothing for me, which was a kind of note in itself.

After that there were postcards, for a while. Fort McMurray. Prince Rupert. A cannery in Alaska one summer, a card with a bear on it. They came to our mother and she'd report their contents to me over the phone in a neutral voice, like a woman reading the shipping news, and I'd say good, good, sounds like he's doing all right, and we'd let it go at that. When she was dying he came home, eight years sober by then he said, and he sat with her in the hospital in Sydney for the last three weeks of her life, and he and I managed to be in that room together for hours at a time, courteous as diplomats, talking about her medications and the weather and the Leafs, and the one time we were alone in the corridor and I opened my mouth to begin, he looked at me with a kind of exhausted gentleness and said, not now, Danny, and I took it to mean not yet, and I have wondered every day since whether it meant not ever. At the reception after the funeral he shook my hand. Both his hands around mine, a long moment, his eyes somewhere over my shoulder, and then he was a taillight going down the shore road, and the postcards stopped, and eleven years ago the phone rang at three in the morning and it was him, sounding sober, sounding far away, saying I'm in Newfoundland now. Working on the boats. I just wanted somebody to know where I was. And I stood in my dark kitchen in my bare feet and said that's good, Colin, that's real good, and there was a long silence with the sea-sound of long distance in it, and I had the thing right there in my mouth, the thing I'd owed him for twenty-six years by then, three words, maybe five, and what I said instead was, well, it's late. And he said, yeah. It's late. And that was the last of my brother's voice.

I must have slept some, in the chair, because the cafeteria was serving breakfast when I came back to myself and the windows had gone from black to a deep iron grey, and the knitting woman was gone, and the ship's motion had changed, that subtle gathering-in a vessel does when land is near. I went out on deck and there it was, the southwest corner of Newfoundland coming up out of the dark, the Long Range mountains flat-topped and dusted with the first snow, the land treeless and ancient-looking, rock that doesn't care, and below it the scatter of lights that was Port aux Basques, and somewhere east along that ragged shore, Isle aux Morts. I had looked it up, of course. Isle of the Dead, named for the shipwrecks, hundreds of years of them, a place where the dead came ashore so reliably they named the land after the habit. There is a famous story there of a fisherman and his daughter and a dog who swam out through the surf to wrecked ships and saved scores of the drowning, and I had read that at two in the morning at my kitchen table and thought: nobody swam out for whoever this is. He came in the old way.

The constable was waiting at the terminal, younger than his voice, holding a paper cup of coffee he handed to me before I'd said anything, which told me something about him. He drove me through the town, which was hunched and salt-bleached and beautiful in the hard early light, and he talked a little, carefully, about nothing, the weather, the run of the ferry, and then he said the doctor would be there and I could take whatever time I needed and that whatever I was able to tell them would be a help, even if it was nothing, even if it was no. People think no is a failure, he said. It isn't. It's information. And I understood he had given that small speech before, and I was grateful for it anyway.

The hospital was small and the room they took me to was smaller, and there is no use describing the particulars of such rooms; anyone who has stood in one knows, and anyone who hasn't is better off. There was a window high up with the grey morning in it. The doctor was a woman about my age who explained quietly what I would see and what the water had done and asked if I was ready, and I heard myself say, from very far away, that I had done this before, a long time ago, for my father, and she looked at me then, really looked, and said, then you know to breathe. And she folded the sheet back from the face.

I want to set down exactly what happened in me in that first second, because I have not stopped turning it over. What happened was that I hoped. Before recognition, before anything, in the small blind instant when the face was just shapes, some animal part of me hoped it was him, hoped it with a leap, and I have had to live with that since, have had to walk all the way around it to see what it was. It was not that I wanted my brother dead. It was that a dead brother can be carried at last, can be grieved, can be spoken to in the way the living speak to the dead, freely, the account closed and therefore finally open. A dead brother holds still long enough to be loved. That was the hope, and it lasted one second, and then my eyes did their work and the shapes became a face and the face was not Colin's.

It was not him. The brow was wrong, the set of the bones, the ears, of all things, the ears, which the sea had left alone and which were a stranger's ears. This was a man perhaps sixty, a worked-on man, a fisherman by his hands, which the doctor showed me when I asked, hands like our father's, like Colin's must be by now, but it was not Colin. I stood there longer than I needed to, to be sure, to be honest, turning my memory of my brother's face against this poor drowned stranger from every angle the years allowed, and then I said, no. It's not him. It's not my brother. And the doctor covered the man's face with a gentleness I will remember, and Constable Pelley wrote something in his notebook, and my legs informed me they would like very much to sit down.

In the corridor the constable brought me water and we sorted out what could be sorted. The coat with my brother's name in it: Colin had boarded at Mrs. Osmond's off and on for years, the constable said, and in the spring he'd gone out west for a stretch, or so he'd told her, and he'd left a box of gear in her basement, and gear walks, on the wharves, everybody knows that, a man needs a coat and a coat is there. The dead man would be run through the missing-persons files now, the fishing-vessel crews checked; somebody, somewhere, was missing him, and they would find that somebody, and that somebody would make some version of the crossing I had just made. Then the constable closed his notebook and looked at me and said, your brother wrote his name in his coat. I said, our mother made us do that, when we were boys. In everything. Mittens. Schoolbooks. She said the world takes what isn't named. And the constable smiled a little and said, well. He kept it up. Sixty-odd years old and still writing his name where his mother told him to. And something about that went through me like a wire, that small obedience persisting across a whole wrecked life, my brother at some kitchen table somewhere uncapping a marker, and I had to look at the window for a while.

I did not take the return boat that night. I walked from the hospital down into the town instead, down to the harbour where the long-liners lay against the wharf shifting in their lines, and I found Mrs. Osmond's boarding house by asking twice, a green house above the water with a dog in the window, and she was a small fierce kindly woman who made me tea I did want this time and told me what she knew, which was not much, but it was more than I'd had in eleven years. He'd talked about going back out to the Gulf in the spring, or out west again, or down to Lunenburg; he was a man who talked about going more than he went, she said, and mostly he sat in that chair you're in now and read his westerns and did the jigsaw puzzles with her grandchildren when they came, and he never touched a drop that she saw, and once, she said, just once, he showed her a picture he kept in his wallet, two boys on a wharf squinting into the sun, and said that's my brother, the tall one, he's an electrician down in Sydney, he done real good for himself. And she watched me hear that, and poured more tea, and had the decency to talk about the weather while I got my face in order.

His box of gear is still in her basement, which means he means to come back, or meant to, or it means nothing, gear gets forgotten, men drift; I know all the sentences. I left my number with her and with the constable and with two skippers on the wharf who thought they maybe knew him, a quiet fella, good hand, MacKinnon, yes. And in the morning I stood on the deck of the ferry going the other way, the land of the dead falling behind in the grey light, the strait opening out ahead of me, that hour coming when there would be no shore in any direction, and I took out my phone, and I found the eleven-year-old number that was probably nothing now, a dead line, a stranger's pocket, and I wrote: Colin. It's Danny. I was wrong, that Christmas. I've been wrong every day since. I'm here whenever you're ready, and I'll keep being here, and there's no hurry, because you're alive, and as long as you're alive there's no such thing as late. I read it over twice and I did not fix the parts that were clumsy, because we are a clumsy family with everything except boats, and I pressed send, and the message went out over the water to wherever my brother was, named inside his coat against a world that takes what isn't named, and I stood at the rail in the wind, in the middle of the strait, with no land anywhere, waiting.
