There is an hour on the night crossing when the lounges empty and the ship belongs to the few who cannot sleep. Garnet has found it before, on other boats, in other decades. The hour when the vending machines hum like chapels. When the truckers have gone down to their berths above the rumbling vehicle deck and the only voices are the recorded ones, looping in two languages about muster stations, about the location of life jackets, about children.

He stands at the rail on deck seven with the wind taking the heat off his face. October. The strait black below him and black above, the line between them visible only where the ship's lights fall and break apart. Somewhere out there, ninety fathoms down, the dead of the old *Caribou* lie in their drowned October, torpedoed in 1942 on this same run, a hundred and thirty-seven of them, nurses and stokers and a four-year-old whose name he once read on a plaque in the terminal and has never been able to forget. Leonard. The strait holds them the way a sentence holds a word you cannot say aloud.

His brother may be at the other end of this water, lying in a drawer in a building behind the hospital in Port aux Basques, and Garnet has been asked to come and look at his face.

*A man in his fifties*, the constable had said on the phone. Pelley, her name was. A voice with the bays in it. *Found at Cape Ray, sir, below the light. No identification on him. But there was a tattoo, and we had your brother's name flagged from an old file, and the description, well. We'd rather you saw him yourself than have me describe a man to you over the phone.*

What kind of tattoo, he had asked.

A pause in which he could hear her deciding something. *A bird, sir. On the left forearm. And under it a word we couldn't quite make out. It might be a name.*

He had been standing in his kitchen in Sydney Mines with the phone cord stretched to its limit because he had been reaching to turn down the radio, and for the rest of his life the news of his brother would be tangled with the weather forecast for the Magdalen Islands, northeast winds thirty knots, diminishing.

He did not tell the constable that he knew the bird. That he had been there when it went on, in a parlour above a laundromat in Halifax in 1981, Lowell nineteen and shirtless on the chair with his arm flat on the towel, grinning through it, and the needle drawing out of him a tern in flight, wings swept back like a thrown knife. And beneath it, later, in a different city, in their mother's handwriting copied from a recipe card the tattooist had pinned to the lamp — her name, *Eilidh*, the letters slanting the way she slanted them, because she had learned to write from a left-handed nun and carried that nun's hand through her whole life and into the skin of her younger son.

You couldn't make it out, Garnet thinks now, at the rail, because salt water eats ink the way it eats everything. Because a body in the strait in October is handled by the strait.

Or because it is not him.

He goes inside. The lounge is lit the colour of weak tea. A girl of maybe seventeen sleeps across three seats with her shoes off and her jacket over her face, and an old man sits upright by the window with his hands folded, awake, watching nothing, the way men of that generation could sit for hours inside their own weather. Garnet buys a coffee from the machine and it comes out scalding and tasting of the cup. He carries it to a table and sits and does not drink it. He has brought almost nothing. A change of shirt. His father's watch, which doesn't run. A photograph, in case they want a photograph, though the constable said they wouldn't; the photograph is for himself, he understands this, it is thirty years old, it is two boys on the government wharf at Little Bras d'Or, and one of them is laughing with his whole spine.

Lowell could swim before he could read. This was the family's one piece of mythology and they handled it carefully, brought it out at weddings and wakes. Four years old and gone off the end of the wharf after a dog, and when their father got down the ladder the boy was not drowning but swimming, badly, furiously, a small machine of elbows, towing himself toward the dog with his face in the water and lifting it only to breathe, as if someone had shown him, as if he'd been born with the strait already in him. Their mother said afterward she'd never feared water for him again, and she was right not to, and that is the thing Garnet cannot fit into the drawer in Port aux Basques. Lowell did not drown. Whatever else he did. Lowell in any water was the safest man in the county.

But a man in the water in October. A man going in off the rocks at Cape Ray, where the current runs like a decision already made. Cold takes the swimming out of you in minutes; it doesn't care what you were at four.

The ship rolls, long and slow, a beam sea. The coffee skins over. The girl under the jacket turns and her shoes slide a little on the floor and stop.

Their father fished swordfish out of Glace Bay in the years when there were still swordfish, and then he fished other things, and then the other things ended too, and he finished his working life on the gypsum boats, and the sea to him was a wage and he never once called it beautiful. He had hands like split firewood. He could put a hook through his thumb and finish the trawl before he looked at it. When Garnet thinks of him it is always from below, a boy's angle — the underside of the jaw, the cigarette, the gull-light moving over him. He died in March of 1989 in a hospital corridor, waiting for a bed, with his boots on, because he had told the nurse he wouldn't be staying.

After the funeral the two brothers stood in the kitchen of the house on Shore Road and divided their father with the cupboard doors open. There was nothing to divide. Tools, a watch, a rifle no one wanted, a tin of receipts going back to 1961. They were civil for two days. On the third day Lowell took their father's boat — the *Eilidh M.*, twenty-eight feet, Cape Islander, the only thing on earth the old man had ever kept painted — and sold her in Louisbourg to a man neither of them knew, and was gone west with the money before Garnet heard about it from the wharf.

He has had thirty-odd years to understand it and he has gotten partway. That for Lowell the boat was not the father; the boat was the harness. That selling her was the only door he could find with his grief standing in front of all the others. Garnet understands this now the way you understand a sentence in a language you studied long ago — slowly, with the dictionary, after the conversation has moved on.

At the time he drove to the airport in Sydney and caught Lowell at the gate, and what he said to him there, in front of strangers, with the snow coming down outside the glass like a wall being quietly built between them, he has never told anyone. He can say it to himself. He says it to himself now, with his lips not moving, in the tea-coloured lounge.

*It should have been you in that corridor. He'd have come to look at you.*

Lowell had not hit him. That was the worst of it. Lowell had looked at him with his bag on his shoulder, and nodded slightly, as though confirming a weight, and walked down the jetway, and the silence began.

Eleven years of it, and then the postcards. That was the strange mercy. They began arriving around the millennium with no warning and no return address, one or two a year, postmarked from the working edges of the country. Fort McMurray. Kitimat. Prince Rupert. One from Argentia with a picture of the old American base. Never more than a sentence. *Welding's good money if you don't mind being cold in two directions.* *Saw a pod of greys off the dock today, you'd have stayed all afternoon.* And once, only once, in 2004, on a card showing the Port aux Basques ferry terminal itself, six words: *Mum's writing is holding up fine.*

Meaning the tattoo. Meaning her name on his arm. Meaning: I carry her, you don't get to keep all of it.

Garnet has the postcards in a biscuit tin at home. He did not bring them. He thought about it, standing in the kitchen with the tin open and the weather report ending, and decided that if he brought them he would be carrying evidence, building a case, and he did not know which verdict he was building it for.

He gets up and walks. The ship at night is a town under curfew. The gift shop dark behind its grille, sweatshirts with puffins on them hanging like the apparel of a vanished people. The cafeteria chairs upturned on the tables, legs in the air, a field of dead beetles. In the corridor to the cabins a man in socks stands at a window with a baby against his shoulder, swaying with the ship, his hand covering the whole of the child's back, and Garnet passes them without a word and feels, absurdly, blessed.

He has a cabin but he doesn't go to it. He goes back out on deck, the lee side now, and finds the old man from the lounge there ahead of him, smoking, the cigarette's coal flaring and fading like a small lighthouse run by hand.

Crossing for work? the old man asks. Not really asking.

For family, Garnet says.

The old man nods at the water. She's civil tonight, he says. I crossed her in January of '74 and we were eighteen hours at it. Played cards with a priest and a man who'd lost his hand in a winch. He smokes. The priest cleaned us out, he says.

Garnet laughs, surprised by it, the sound coming out of him like something dislodged. The old man flicks the cigarette over the side, where the wind kills it instantly, and says goodnight, and is gone, and Garnet stays.

Here is what he knows about identifying the dead, which is nothing, which is everything anyone knows before they do it. That you will be taken down a corridor. That someone will speak quietly to you and use your first name. That there will be a smell you will pretend is something else. That the face will be wrong — not damaged necessarily, but wrong, vacated, the way a house is wrong the day the furniture goes, the rooms the same shape and the proportions altered anyway, because proportion was never about the walls.

He saw his mother dead, in 1996, in the good room at the funeral home in North Sydney, and Lowell did not come, or came too late — there was a story afterward that he had been seen at the cemetery the following week, alone, in the rain, a story Garnet chose to believe and then chose to doubt and now, tonight, chooses again to believe. Their mother in the casket had worn an expression of mild attention, as if listening to a kettle in another room. The undertaker had done her hair wrong, parted it on the wrong side, and Garnet had stood there unable to say so, and that small wrongness had hurt more than the death, had carried the death in through a side door his grief had not thought to guard.

If it is Lowell tomorrow, what will be parted on the wrong side. What small wrongness is waiting for him in that drawer to carry the whole thing in.

The wind shifts a point. Far off to the north there is a light, a ship or a rig or the first of Newfoundland, and he watches it until he is sure it isn't moving, and then it is land, the south coast, hours away yet, the long dark shoulder of it lying across the world.

Lowell at eleven, swimming the gut at Little Bras d'Or on a dare, the current there like a hand on you, and Garnet at fourteen rowing alongside in the punt, sick with the responsibility, shouting at him to take the oar, take the oar, and Lowell rolling onto his back mid-channel to grin at him, spouting water like a fountain in a rich man's garden, saying, You row, I'll swim, we'll see who's tired. Their mother on the far shore with her hands not wringing, just folded, trusting the water with her younger son the way other mothers trusted God.

Lowell at sixteen, lifting a man twice his size off Garnet outside a dance in Florence, not in rage but with a kind of terrible courtesy, setting him against a car like a delivered parcel, then turning to check Garnet's eyebrow with his thumb, frowning, gentle, saying, You have to watch the left, Garn, you drop your shoulder and they can see everything you're about to do.

You drop your shoulder and they can see everything you're about to do.

Lowell at nineteen under the needle, the tern going onto him forever. The artist had asked, why a tern, and Lowell had said, because they go the farthest. Which was true; Garnet looked it up years later in the library in Sydney, the Arctic tern, pole to pole and back, seventy thousand kilometres some years, a bird that lives almost entirely in transit, that sees more daylight than any creature on earth because it follows the summer around the planet, and he had sat in the library with the book open and understood that his brother had told him everything that day in the tattoo parlour and he had been too young to hear it.

Because they go the farthest.

He sleeps, finally, in the cabin, in his clothes, on top of the blankets, the way you sleep in a place you don't intend to admit you slept. The ship's engines come up through the bunk into his spine, a pulse not his own, and he dreams not of Lowell but of the boat, the *Eilidh M.*, her hull the green their father mixed himself from two cans because he was too cheap to buy the right colour, and in the dream Garnet is caulking her seams with cotton and the cotton keeps turning into their mother's handwriting, long slanted fibres of it, and he is pressing her words into the hull below the waterline where no one will ever read them, and the work feels neither sad nor strange, it feels like the only work there is.

He wakes to the change in the engines. Every ferry traveller knows it, the easing, the ship gathering itself for land. Through the porthole the sky has gone the colour of the inside of a mussel shell. He washes his face in the steel sink and the water comes out smelling faintly of the ship and he dries himself on the small hard towel and looks at himself in the mirror bolted to the bulkhead and sees, as he has seen for twenty years now and never once been ready for, his father's jaw, his mother's eyes, and somewhere underneath, in the bones' patient argument, his brother.

On deck the land is everywhere now, the Long Range Mountains standing up out of the dark with snow already on them, and Port aux Basques opening its narrow throat of a harbour, the houses set on the rock in their fierce colours, ochre and red and a blue that exists nowhere in nature, as if the town had decided generations ago to answer the grey of the strait with paint and had never once backed down. Gulls come off the breakwater to meet the ship. The wind smells of kelp and diesel and snow that hasn't fallen yet.

Constable Pelley is on the dock. He knows her before she moves, knows the particular stillness of someone waiting for a stranger they will have to be kind to. She is younger than her voice. She shakes his hand and says his name and asks about the crossing and he hears himself say civil, she was civil, the old man's word coming out of him like a borrowed coat, and she smiles slightly as if she knows the coat isn't his.

It's a short drive, she says. There's no rush, sir. We could get a coffee first if you'd want one.

No, he says. Thank you. I'd rather walk in with the sea legs still on me. Easier to blame the floor.

She looks at him then, properly, and something in her settles, some assessment completed, and she opens the car door for him not the way you do for the elderly but the way you do for the bereaved, which it occurs to him she does not yet know he is, which it occurs to him he does not yet know either.

The building is low and brown and could be anything — a credit union, a fisheries office. Inside it smells of floor cleaner over something else, the something else very faint, very patient. A man in scrubs meets them, soft-voiced, Walters or Waters, and there are forms, there is a corridor, there is a door, and all of it happens both quickly and outside of time, the way the worst hours do, so that afterward he will not be able to put the events in order, only the details: the hum of fluorescent light, a calendar on the wall still turned to September, the constable's hand hovering an inch behind his elbow, never touching.

The room is small. The drawer is not a drawer; that is the movies; it is a table, and a sheet, and the sheet is folded back already to the collarbones, they have done that before bringing him in, a kindness, so that the first thing is the face.

The strait has been at him. Garnet makes himself stand in it, the looking, the way you stand in surf, leaning into what wants to take your legs. The face is swollen and stilled and vacated and wrong, wrong in the way he knew it would be wrong, and for a long moment it is no one, it is the strait's own face, the face the water gives back when you ask it a question.

Then he sees the left eyebrow. The seam in it, white, a thread of scar above the eye, where a boy went over the handlebars on the Shore Road in 1969 and his older brother carried him a mile home on his back, both of them bleeding, both of them, for some reason neither ever explained to their mother, laughing.

The arm, sir? the soft-voiced man says, somewhere in another country. You mentioned the arm.

The sheet moves. The forearm is laid bare on the steel and the skin of it has been worked over by the water and the ink has gone to shadow, smoke under glass, but the shape is there, the swept-back wings, the thrown knife of the tern still flying after everything, going the farthest, and beneath it the letters, blurred, slanting left, a left-handed nun's hand handed down through a woman to her son's skin to this table, and Garnet does not need to read them, he has been able to read them in the dark his whole life.

That's my brother, he says. That's Lowell.

He hears the constable exhale. The man in scrubs draws the sheet up gently, but Garnet says, not yet, and puts his hand — they allow it, no one stops him — on his brother's forearm, on the bird, the cold of it entering him like the gut current at Little Bras d'Or, like water that has been waiting fifty years for him to come in after all, and he says, very quietly, so that the others in the room will tell each other afterward that they did not hear it, You drop your shoulder, Lowell. I could see everything you were about to do.

Outside, later, the snow has begun, the first of it, falling into the harbour and becoming the harbour. Constable Pelley stands with him by the car and tells him the practical things, the things that will have to happen now, and he listens and nods and retains none of it; there will be papers; she will write it all down for him; she is good at her work. Across the water the ferry that brought him is loading for the return, trucks going up the ramp into her side, and at noon she will swing out through the narrows and put her bow into the strait and carry her cargo of the living back over the dead of the *Caribou*, over Leonard who was four, over all the held names, and somewhere above her, if the season were different, terns would be passing, southbound, pole to pole, following the light around the curve of the earth, the way some brothers do, the way some hearts are built and cannot help it, going the farthest, sending back a sentence a year, and Garnet stands in the snow with his hand still cold from the cold of his brother and finds that what he feels, under the grief, threaded through it like white scar through an eyebrow, is something he has no decent right to and will keep anyway.

He could swim before he could read, he tells the constable.

She doesn't ask. She turns with him and looks at the water, the two of them standing there while the snow comes down on Port aux Basques, on the painted houses, on the long dark shoulder of the island, and the strait says what it always says, which is nothing, which is everything, and holds what it holds.
