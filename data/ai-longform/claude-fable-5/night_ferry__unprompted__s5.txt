The last cars went up the ramp a little before eleven, and Wes Parsons sat in his own with the engine ticking, watching a man in a reflective vest wave him forward into the lit mouth of the ferry as though into the side of a building that had decided, against all reason, to float. He had driven two days from Cambridge, Ontario, to get to North Sydney. He could have flown to Deer Lake and rented a car and been there by suppertime, and Donna had said so twice, gently, the way she said most things now, but he had wanted the distance to pass through him at the speed of road. He had not been able to explain this. He'd said something about hating airports. What he meant was that a man should not arrive at a thing like this quickly. There should be a toll paid in hours.

The car deck smelled of diesel and cold iron and the salt that gets into everything on a boat and never leaves. He took his overnight bag and the manila envelope from the passenger seat and locked the car, which the sign told him he needn't do, and climbed the steel stairs with the other passengers, truckers mostly, men with thermoses and the unbothered look of those who cross the Gulf the way other men take elevators. The stairwell rang under their boots. On deck five the carpet began, a pattern of blue waves that had been walked grey down the middle, and the fluorescent light made everyone look like they'd already had the bad news.

He found a reclining chair by a window black with night, set his bag on the seat beside it as a small territorial claim, and went looking for the bar before it closed. The ferry pushed off while he was standing at the counter; he felt it more than saw it, a shift in the floor, a low change in the engine's pitch, and then the lights of North Sydney sliding away in the window like something spilled. Seven hours across the Cabot Strait. He ordered a rye and took it to a table near the glass, where the only thing to see was his own reflection holding a drink, sixty-one years old, jowled, his father's forehead arrived on him at last like a debt collector.

The envelope sat on the table. In it was a photograph the constable had asked him to bring if he had one. Anything at all, she'd said, even an old one helps. The photograph was from 1978, taken on the wharf at Spoon Cove by their mother with the Instamatic she got with Co-op stamps: two boys, nineteen and seventeen, squinting, a cod held up between them by the gills like a flag of a country that no longer existed. Wes on the left in his Toronto Maple Leafs cap he wore to aggravate everyone. Lloyd on the right, laughing at something out of frame, his free hand hanging loose at his side, the little finger crooked at the second knuckle where it had healed wrong.

He had not opened the envelope since putting the photograph in it three days ago, but he didn't need to. He could see it with his eyes shut. He could see the finger.

"You're not a trucker," said a man at the next table. It wasn't a question. He was seventy maybe, with a fisherman's hands, the knuckles enlarged like he'd been stung all over by the same bee for fifty years, and a can of Black Horse in front of him, which meant he'd brought his own aboard or knew someone.

"No," Wes said. "Electrician. Retired."

"Going over or going home?"

It was a fair question on this run, where the two were not the same thing, and Wes was surprised by the second it took him to answer. "Home," he said, and the word came out of him like a tooth. "Originally. I've been away a long time."

"Where to?"

"Southwest coast. Spoon Cove, out past—"

"I knows Spoon Cove," the man said, with the mild offense of a man being told where the moon is. "I'm from Burgeo. Forty year on the water out of there. There's not a hole in that shore I haven't anchored in." He looked at Wes with more attention now, the way men of that coast read each other, taking the face apart for family. "Parsons, is it? You got the look of the Parsons crowd. There was Parsons in Spoon Cove."

"Wes Parsons," Wes said, and the man put out the swollen hand and Wes shook it.

"Art Hann." He drank from the can and set it down with care, as though it might be the last one ever made. "I knows a Lloyd Parsons. Knew him to see him this past good many years, anyway, around Port aux Basques. Plays the accordion something beautiful. He'd be what to you — cousin?"

The engine went on underneath everything, that deep marine heartbeat, and Wes looked at the black window and then back. "Brother," he said.

Something passed over Art Hann's face, a weather. He turned the can a quarter turn on the table. "Then I'm awful sorry, b'y," he said quietly. "I heard. Everyone down there heard. After the blow last week — they said a man was gone off the breakwater, or off his boat, nobody rightly knew which, and then Tuesday a longliner out of Isle aux Morts brought one in." He stopped, looked at his hands. "I didn't know it was confirmed."

"It isn't," Wes said. "That's why I'm on this boat."

Art nodded slowly, accepting the correction, and they sat for a moment in the kind of silence two strangers can only manage when there's a third party in it. Then, because he was a man for whom silence about the dead was a discourtesy to them, Art said, "He kept an old longliner up on the slip there, your brother. The Marion P. Working at her for years, replanking her, going to take her down the coast someday, he said. Did odd work, wiring, carpentry, bit of everything. Good company, Lloyd. Best kind. He'd play of a Friday night at the Legion and you couldn't get a seat." He looked at Wes carefully. "I never knew he had a brother."

"No," Wes said. "He wouldn't have mentioned it."

The bar closed at midnight. Art Hann shook his hand again before going off to wherever truckers and old fishermen sleep on a boat, and said, "Whatever it is was between ye, it's between one of ye now," which was either the cruelest or the kindest thing anyone had said to Wes in years, and he sat with it a long time before he understood it was meant as a release.

The Marion P. Their mother's name was Marion.

He took his rye glass back to the counter though no one was there to receive it, then went out through the heavy door onto the open deck aft, where the wind found him at once and went through his coat like it was reading his mail. The wake unrolled behind the ship, pale as a scar on the black water, going back the way he'd come, back toward the mainland, toward the life he'd built with such deliberation: the apprenticeship, the ticket, the company van, the house in Cambridge with the maple in front that he'd watched grow from a whip to a thing that dropped a yard of leaves every October. Forty-five years of grounded wire and proper code. A man could hide a whole life inside competence. He had.

The strait was running a low swell out of the southwest and the ferry took it on the quarter with a slow corkscrew motion that emptied the deck of everyone but him and a young fellow vaping by the rail, the blue light of his phone making him look drowned already. Wes stood at the stern with his hands on the cold rail and let the wind do what it wanted.

Their father had gone into this same water, or water continuous with it, in October of 1979, three weeks after Wes left for Toronto. Went out alone in the trap skiff after a day's drinking at Cyril Keating's, with weather coming that every man in the cove could read in the colour of the light, and they found the skiff the next morning off Otter Point, swamped, the painter still cleated, and found him three days after that at the landwash below Hartley's Garden, and Wes had not come home for it. He'd had reasons. He'd been three weeks into the job, no seniority, no money for the flight, and a foreman who'd told him plainly that the dead keep but jobs don't. The reasons were sound. He had spent forty-five years discovering that sound reasons rot like anything else.

Lloyd, seventeen, had identified the body. Lloyd had stood in the back room of Penney's Funeral Home in their one good blazer between them, the one they'd shared for graduations and weddings, and looked at what three days in the North Atlantic had made of their father, and said yes. Their mother couldn't and Wes wasn't there, so Lloyd did it, and never once in his life mentioned it, not in anger, not even drunk, and that omission had sat between them for decades like a bill that gathers interest precisely because it is never presented.

And now this. There was a symmetry to it so obvious that Wes mistrusted it, the way he mistrusted a circuit that tested perfect on the first try. The younger brother looks at the father; the older brother looks at the younger; and there it ends, because there's no one after. He had no children. Lloyd, as far as he knew, had none either. The Parsons of Spoon Cove, who'd fished that shore since before Confederation, since before the railway, since back when the merchant's ledger was the only Bible that got read on weekdays, came down to a man at a stern rail and a drawer in the cooler at the health centre in Port aux Basques, one of them to be identified by the other.

If it was him.

The constable on the phone — Pelley, her name was, she'd sounded about thirty — had been careful with her words in the way they must train them now. A male, recovered Tuesday by a fishing vessel, consistent in age and description with Lloyd Albert Parsons of Port aux Basques, who had not been seen since the night of the storm. No identification recovered. Some time in the water, she'd said, and Wes, who had grown up on that shore, knew what some time in the water meant and that she was sparing him, and was grateful and resentful at once. Mr. Parsons was listed as next of kin on an old hospital admission form, she said. From 2016. Did he know his brother had listed him?

He had not known. He had stood in his kitchen in Cambridge with the phone in his hand and the kettle screaming and Donna turning it off and watching him, and the fact of it had gone into him deeper than the news itself. 2016. Eleven years after their last words. Lloyd in a hospital bed for whatever reason — they wouldn't say, privacy — being asked who his people were, and saying, after everything, his brother's name. Spelling out, presumably, the address in Cambridge that he must have gotten from somewhere, kept somewhere, the way you keep a key to a house you've sworn never to enter.

The last words. He made himself look at them now, out here in the dark where there was nothing to soften them. Their mother's wake, March of 2004, the front room of the house in Spoon Cove so crowded with casseroles the kitchen table bowed, and Lloyd arriving on the second day already loaded, in off two years in Fort McMurray with money gone wherever his money went, and weeping over the casket with that big public grief that Wes, standing dry-eyed in the corner doing the arithmetic of the funeral costs, had found obscene. And later, in the kitchen, Lloyd's arm heavy around his neck, Lloyd's breath in his face, Lloyd saying we're all that's left now, b'y, we got to — and Wes had taken the arm off himself like taking off a wet coat and said, in a voice their mother could have heard if she'd been alive, You're just like him. You know that? You're him all over again.

He'd watched it land. He'd meant it to land. Lloyd's face had gone through surprise into something worse than anger, a kind of recognition, as if Wes had only confirmed a diagnosis Lloyd had long suspected, and he'd left the wake and not come to the funeral, and gone back West, and the next year, when Lloyd was unreachable and the lawyer needed an answer and the house was rotting on its sills, Wes, as executor, had sold it. To a couple from Oakville for a summer place. He'd told himself the will permitted it, which it did, and that he'd split the money to the cent and put Lloyd's half in trust, which he had, and that Lloyd had forfeited his say by being where no one could find him, which was a sound reason, and rot, all of it, rot — because the truth was he had sold it the way you cauterize something. Lloyd had come home that autumn to find an Oakville woman painting their mother's kitchen yellow, and had called Wes from the payphone outside Foote's store, the last payphone on that shore probably, and said, with a calm that was far worse than shouting: You'd sell the graves too, if they'd let you. And hung up. And that was the whole estate of it, nineteen years of it, except for one postcard, postmarked Port aux Basques, July 2011, a photograph of the very ferry Wes was standing on now, and on the back two words in Lloyd's terrible handwriting: Still here.

Wes had the postcard in his inside pocket. He'd carried it from Ontario the way you'd carry a passport.

The wind was getting into his bones, so he went back in through the heavy door and walked the length of the boat, past the rows of recliners where strangers slept with their mouths open under their coats, a child curled against a woman like a comma, a trucker snoring with the dignity of a bishop, and he sat in his chair by the black window and did not sleep. The ship rolled its slow figure through the swell. Somewhere below, his car waited in the dark hold with the photograph's envelope on the dash, and below that the water went down and down, two hundred fathoms, three hundred, the cold preserved dark where everything that this coast had ever lost was still, in some sense, in transit. He thought of the finger.

July, it would have been, 1968 or '69. Wes nine, Lloyd seven, aboard their father's boat at the wharf, told to stay out of the way and constitutionally unable to. They were playing some game involving the fish hold, prisoners or pirates, and Lloyd was down in it being whatever the game required and Wes was up top holding the hatch cover, the big wooden one with the iron strap, and Lloyd's hand had come up over the coaming at the moment Wes let the cover go — and to the end of his life Wes would not be able to swear whether he let it go because his arms gave out or because some swift mean nine-year-old reflex saw the hand and chose the half second. The sound the finger made. Lloyd's scream, and then, worse, Lloyd not screaming, going white and silent and cradling the hand, and their father coming up the wharf at a run. They never set it right; the doctor was in Burgeo and the weather turned and by the time anyone saw it the bone had made its own decision. The little finger of the left hand, crooked at the second knuckle, angled out like it was forever pointing at something just to the side of whatever Lloyd was looking at. Lloyd had made a comedy of it all his life. He'd hook it in his cheek and cross his eyes for the small cousins. He played the accordion with it — Wes had watched, at weddings long ago, the crooked finger riding the buttons, finding its notes by its own bent geometry, and felt every time a small specific pain like a splinter under a nail. Lloyd had never blamed him. Lloyd had told the story for laughs: and then me brother, the foolish article, drops the hatch on me. The absolution had somehow been worse than blame. You can argue with blame.

He must have slept some, because the window went from black to a deep iron grey without his noticing the change, and then the southwest coast of Newfoundland stood up out of the gloom all at once the way it does, no preamble, the long table of the highlands snow-streaked in their gullies even in June, bare rock the colour of an old anchor, and the land's bigness rearranged something in his chest as it always had and he resented it for still being able to. Other passengers were stirring, gathering at the windows with phones. The strait had gone smooth in under the land's lee. He could see the breakwater light at Port aux Basques, and the town behind it, low and scattered and patient on its rock, painted houses holding their colours up against all that grey like an argument the people there had been winning, barely, for two hundred years.

Constable Pelley met him at the terminal, younger even than her voice, parka over the uniform, and shook his hand and said she was sorry to be meeting him under these circumstances, and he followed her car through the early streets to the health centre. The town wasn't awake yet. Gulls stood on the wharf pilings facing the wind in their grave municipal way. Up on the slip near the harbour's elbow he saw, without being told, a longliner propped in its cradle, new planks pale against old ones along her port side like clean bandages, and he did not need to read the name on her bow and read it anyway, and drove on with his hands very steady on the wheel.

In a small room that smelled of nothing at all, Pelley explained what he already knew. That the water and the time in it had been unkind. That he should prepare himself for the face to be difficult, perhaps impossible. That an identification could rest on other things — marks, scars, old injuries — and that whatever he could or couldn't say today, there were other means, slower means, and no shame in needing them. She had his photograph from the envelope on her clipboard, the two boys and the cod, and she had looked at it longer, he thought, than procedure required.

"Whenever you're ready, Mr. Parsons."

There is no ready. He had understood that somewhere in the night, over the deep water. There is only the moment you stand up.

The room beyond was cold the way a church is cold. An attendant drew back the sheet to the collarbones, and Pelley was right; the face was an argument the sea had won; it told him nothing, or rather it told him everything about the sea and nothing about his brother, and for a long suspended moment Wes stood in the genuine open country of not knowing, where the man on the table was any man, every man, his father, himself.

"The left hand," he heard himself say. "Could I see the left hand."

The attendant moved the sheet.

The hand lay palm up, slack, broad as their father's, the skin gone the colour of wet sailcloth, and the little finger angled out from the second knuckle, away from the others, crooked, pointing as it had pointed for fifty-six years at something just to the side of everything, and Wes stood looking at the wound he had made on a July morning before either of them knew what the world would ask of them, the oldest sentence in their shared language, and he said, "That's Lloyd. That's my brother," and the room held still around the words.

They gave him a moment alone, which is a thing they do, and he had not known what he would use it for and found that he did know. He put his own hand over the cold crooked one, fitting his palm to it, and he did not say sorry, because sorry was a word for smaller debts, and he did not say anything about their mother or the house or the yellow kitchen. He said, "I saw the Marion P. coming in. She looks fit out, b'y. She looks near ready." And then, because there was no one left alive to hear it but the one it belonged to, he said his brother's name out loud, once, the way you say a name across water, and went out to sign the forms, and to ask Constable Pelley how a man went about bringing his brother home along the coast, to Spoon Cove, where there was ground in the ground, where the graves had never been sold, where two plots lay open beside their mother because their mother, who had known her sons better than they knew themselves, had paid for both.
