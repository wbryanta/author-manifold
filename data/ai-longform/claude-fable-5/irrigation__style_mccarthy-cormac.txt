They came up out of the dark of the house before the sun and stood on the porch drinking their coffee in the blue hour with the orchard below them ranked away into the gloom like an army of the contrite, the trees black and crooked and holding what fruit they held in secret yet, and neither of them spoke. A whippoorwill called from down along the creekbed where there was no creek anymore and had not been for two summers and the call came up to them thin and interrogatory and was not answered. Ellis poured the last of his coffee over the porch rail and watched it fall.

You ready, he said.

I been ready.

Then let's go.

The truck stood in the yard with the windshield furred in dust and Wade pulled open the door and the hinge cried out in the stillness like something killed. They rode out the two-track between the trees with the lights off, the world taking shape around them grudging and gray, and the dust rose behind them and hung in the air and did not settle. The trees went by in their rows. Peaches. Their grandfather's trees and their father's and now no one's, or theirs, which Ellis had begun to think was the same thing.

The break was in the lower forty where the line ran along the old fencerow toward the well. They'd seen it the evening before, the dark stain in the dust like blood from a wound long since clotted, the water gone subterranean again the way water will, seeking its own level, seeking the dark. Ellis stopped the truck and they got out and stood looking at the wet ground in the first light.

How long you reckon it's been leakin, Wade said.

I dont know. Week maybe. Maybe more.

That's a lot of water.

It is.

Water we paid to pump.

Ellis didnt answer. He got the shovels out of the truckbed and handed one across and they began to dig. The ground was wet on top and dry beneath, the moisture not yet gone deep, and they cut the sod away in squares and laid it aside as their father had taught them, as if the ground were a thing that could be opened and closed again like a book, and they dug down along the line of the pipe, and the sun came up over the eastern ridge and laid their shadows out long and thin across the orchard floor, two figures stooped and laboring like figures in some old frieze depicting toil itself, toil without object or end.

The pipe when they came to it was the old galvanized their grandfather had laid in 1958 and it was rotten with rust and split along a seam for the better part of three feet and the water had hollowed a cavity beneath it the size of a washtub.

Well, Wade said. There it is.

There it is.

Wade leaned on his shovel and looked at it. You know what this means.

It means we got to cut out that section and sleeve it.

It means the whole line's shot, is what it means. You fix this and it'll blow somewhere else in a month. You know that. Daddy knew it. He just never had the money to do anything about it and we dont either.

Ellis climbed down into the trench and ran his thumb along the split in the pipe. The metal flaked away under his hand like the scale of some ancient fish. He looked at the rust on his fingers.

Hand me the hacksaw, he said.

They worked through the morning. The sun cleared the trees and the heat came on slow and certain like a debt coming due and the cicadas started in the hedgerow, that long dry electric pulsing that is the very sound of heat, the sound heat would make if heat could speak. Ellis sawed at the pipe and the saw bound in the cut and he backed it out and started again. Wade squatted at the lip of the trench and watched him.

Hollis Vandermeer called the house again Tuesday, he said.

I know it.

You know what he's offerin.

I know what he's offerin.

Eleven hundred a acre, Ellis. For ground that wont clear two hundred a acre in a good year. And we aint had a good year since Daddy died.

We had a good year three years ago.

Three years ago. Wade spat into the dry grass. One year out of five. You want to tell me what kind of arithmetic that is.

It aint arithmetic at all. That's the point.

That's the point, Wade said. He shook his head and looked off down the rows where the heat had begun to stand and shimmer above the ground so that the far trees wavered as if seen through old glass or through water, the world gone unreliable at its edges. That's the point, he said again, quieter, as if testing the words for what they might weigh.

The saw broke through and the end of the pipe sagged into the cavity and the last of the standing water in the line ran out brown and then clear and then quit. Ellis stood in the trench with the cut section in his hands. It was heavy with rust and scale and he turned it over and looked down the bore of it, the inside furred and constricted like a diseased artery, and he thought that water had passed through this pipe for sixty-six years, water their grandfather had pumped and their father, water that had gone into the roots of these trees and become wood and leaf and fruit, become the peaches he'd eaten as a boy sitting on the tailgate of a truck that was junked before Wade was even born, and that none of that was an argument for anything and he knew it, and he stood holding the pipe anyway as if it were.

You want to drive into town or you want me to, Wade said.

I'll go. Measure that other end and see is it sound enough to take a coupling.

It aint.

Measure it anyway.

He drove into town with the windows down and the hot air beating through the cab and the road running out before him white and empty under the sun. The fields along the road were Vandermeer's now, most of them, sorghum and cotton in pivots, the great aluminum spans of the irrigation rigs wheeling slow over the crops like the armatures of enormous clocks keeping a time no man had set, and where the pivots didnt reach the corners of the fields lay fallow and bleached and given over to careless weed, and Ellis looked at the corners and not at the crops. In town he bought two couplings and a six-foot stick of PVC and a can of primer and a can of cement and the man at the counter was Dwight Calloway who had gone to school with their father.

Heard Vandermeer's been sniffin around your place, Calloway said.

He has.

Calloway counted out the change and laid it on the counter rather than in Ellis's hand, an old man's courtesy or an old man's distance, Ellis couldnt say which. Your daddy turned him down twice, Calloway said.

I know it.

Course your daddy could afford to. He counted out his days different than you boys can. He didnt have to make em pay.

Ellis took up the change and the sack. What's that supposed to mean.

It dont mean nothin. It means a man at the end of his rope and a man at the start of one aint holdin the same rope, that's all. You tell Wade I said hello.

Driving back he passed the Methodist cemetery where they were buried, all of them, grandfather and grandmother and father and mother, the stones standing in their rows on the rise of ground above the road much as the trees stood in theirs, ranked and patient and tended or untended according to the fortunes of the living, and he did not slow down and he did not look and he was aware of not looking the whole way past.

Wade had the trench cleaned out and squared and he'd cut back the broken end to sound metal, or what passed for sound, and he was sitting in the shade of the truck with his hat off when Ellis pulled up.

PVC, Wade said, looking in the sack. Daddy'd pitch a fit.

Daddy aint here.

No, Wade said. He aint.

They primed the fittings and cemented them and set the new pipe in the line and stood looking at it, the white plastic garish and clean against the rusted iron at either end like a splint on an old man's arm, like the prosthetic it was. The cement had to cure before they could charge the line and so they sat in the shade of the big tree at the end of the row, the one their grandfather had left when he cleared the field, a liveoak older than the orchard and older than the county and older maybe than the claims of men on this ground at all, and they ate the sandwiches Wade had made that morning and drank water out of the jug, the water blood-warm and tasting of the plastic, and the cicadas droned and quit and droned again, and high up a pair of buzzards rode the thermal off the ridge in slow concentric circuits, patient as creditors, and the brothers watched them.

Somethin dead up on the ridge, Wade said.

Somethin's always dead up on the ridge.

Wade lay back in the grass with his hat over his eyes. You remember when Daddy planted the lower forty, he said. You'd of been what. Nine.

Eight.

Eight. I wasnt but four, I dont hardly remember it. I remember the trees comin in on the flatbed. All them little whips with the roots balled up in burlap. I remember thinkin they didnt look like nothin. Like you couldnt get a peach off of one of them things in a hundred years.

You got one off in four.

I know it. Wade was quiet a minute. That aint my point though.

What's your point.

My point is he planted them trees borrowin money he didnt have on the strength of rain that didnt come. Nineteen years ago. And the well's pullin sand now, Ellis, dont tell me it aint, I seen the filter. The water under this ground is goin away and it aint comin back, not in our lives nor anybody else's, and a man can stand in the middle of his daddy's orchard and say I will not sell, I will not sell, and the water dont care. The water never heard of us.

Ellis chewed and looked out down the rows. The fruit was coming on, he could see it even from here, the trees in the lower forty heavy-set despite everything, the peaches green yet and hard as stones but numerous, and in three weeks or four they'd blush and soften and the smell of them would lie over the whole bottom in the evenings the way it had lain over it every June and July of his life, a smell he could not have described to a stranger and would not have tried, and he thought that there are things a man knows that are not knowledge and cannot be made into knowledge and are no use to him at all in an argument and are the whole of why he is having it.

Vandermeer'd doze every tree on the place inside a month, he said. You know that. He'd doze the trees and rip the stumps and laser-level the whole bottom and put cotton in it.

It's his money. Once it's his ground it's his ground.

That dont bother you.

Wade sat up. He took his hat off his face and held it and looked into it as though the answer might be printed in the sweatband. Yeah, he said. It bothers me. You think it dont bother me. I was raised in them trees same as you. But Ellis, bein bothered aint a livin. Renee's workin doubles at the hospital so my kids can have shoes and I'm out here losin money with a shovel. Half of eleven hundred a acre on six hundred and forty acres. You done that arithmetic?

I done it.

Then say it out loud.

I aint goin to say it out loud.

Three hundred and fifty-two thousand dollars, Wade said. Apiece. He put his hat back on. You could buy a lot of bein bothered with that. You could be bothered in comfort the rest of your natural life.

A redtailed hawk came down the row at the height of the trees, tilting, hunting, and passed over them without regard and went on, and its shadow ran along the ground beneath it rippling over the irrigation furrows like a thing made of cloth dragged at speed, and was gone. Ellis watched the place where it had gone.

You remember what he said, he said. At the end. In the hospital.

Dont, Wade said.

You remember it though.

He was full of morphine, Ellis. He didnt know what he was sayin. He thought I was Uncle Treece half the time. He thought it was 1974.

He knew what he was sayin when he said it.

He said hold the ground. Wade stood up and slapped the grass off the seat of his jeans. He said hold the ground, two words, hold the ground, like it was Vicksburg. He didnt say how. He didnt say with what. Dyin men get to talk like that. It's the one luxury they got, sayin grand things and leavin the bill on the table. And you been payin on that bill four years and you aim to pay on it till they put you up on that hill next to him, and you know what the ground'll do for you in return? Same as it done for him. Nothin. The ground dont hold us, Ellis. We hold it. That's the whole sorry trade and it always was.

Ellis got up and walked out into the sun and stood with his back to his brother looking down the row. The trees went away from him in their long perspective, the row narrowing to a point of green light at the far end the way all rows do, the way they're laid out to do, drawn toward a vanishing that is only geometry and yet stand in a row long enough, he thought, in enough kinds of light, and you will come to believe it is something other than geometry. He had stood in this row at every age he had ever been. He could not remember not knowing it.

Cement ought to be set, he said.

That aint an answer.

I know it aint.

They walked back to the trench. Ellis checked the joints and they held under his hand and he climbed out and they got in the truck and drove up to the wellhouse and Ellis threw the switch on the pump and the old motor groaned and caught and ran, and they stood in the dim tin-smelling heat of the wellhouse listening to it, the whine of the motor and beneath it the deeper sound, the sound of water rising in the column, water that had lain in the dark of the earth since before the trees, since before the grandfather, since before any name that anyone now living could put to this country, rising blind and cold through the casing toward the light and the use of men, and Wade put his hand on the discharge pipe and felt it go cold and then they drove back down to the lower forty to see about the repair.

It held. The line filled and knocked and steadied and held, and they walked the row and watched the emitters begin to drip and then to run, each tree taking its measure, the dark spots blooming in the dust at the base of each trunk like the first drops of a rain that would never come any other way, and Wade walked ahead of Ellis down the row checking the emitters and Ellis watched him do it, watched him stoop at a clogged one and clear it with the blade of his pocketknife without being told and without thinking, the way you do a thing your hands have done five hundred times, and Ellis thought: he will vote to sell and his hands will vote to stay and the hands dont get a vote. That's the trouble with this world. The hands dont get a vote.

By the time they'd walked the whole line the sun was low and the light had gone long and amber down the rows and the heat was lifting off the ground in a slow exhalation and the smell of the wet dust came up around them, that smell that is like no other smell, the smell of dry ground given water, which the grandmother used to say was what heaven would smell like if heaven knew its business. The buzzards had come down off the ridge and gone wherever buzzards go. A mourning dove called from the fencerow and another answered it from farther off and the two of them traded their soft hollow notes back and forth across the orchard in the dusk like watchmen calling the hour.

They put the tools in the truckbed and Wade lowered the tailgate and they sat on it, the metal still warm through their jeans, and neither of them moved to leave. Down the row the water ticked and dripped.

I told Renee I'd have an answer for Vandermeer by Friday, Wade said.

Ellis nodded slowly. You told her that.

I did.

It aint just your answer to give.

I know it. That's why I'm sittin here, aint it.

Ellis looked at his hands. The rust was still on them, ground into the creases, and he rubbed at it with his thumb and it didnt come away. Listen, he said. I aint goin to pretend the numbers come out different than they come out. They dont. You're right about the well and you're right about the line and you're probably right about the rest of it. He paused. But I'll tell you what I cant get past. You sell a thing, it's sold. There's no comin back to it. A man can be wrong about holdin on and fix it any year he wants. He cant never fix bein wrong about lettin go. That's a door that just locks behind you.

That's a door that locks behind you either way, Wade said. You think you can hold this place and decide later? Later's decidin right now, Ellis. Every season we lose, that's the decision gettin made for us a piece at a time. There aint no not decidin. There's just slow and fast.

The dove called again. The light was nearly gone out of the rows now, only the topmost leaves still burning, and below them the trunks stood in a blue shadow that was filling up the bottom like water filling a tank, and the first star stood in the east, or it was a planet, their father would have known and would have said, he could not pass a clear evening without naming what burned in it, and neither of his sons had ever learned the names because the father knew them and a thing your father knows is a thing you believe you will always be able to ask. Ellis looked at the star or the planet and did not know it.

Tell you what, he said.

What.

Buy me out.

Wade turned and looked at him.

You heard me. You want to sell, sell your half to me. I'll give you what Vandermeer's givin. It'll take me twenty years and the bank and probably kill me but I'll sign whatever your lawyer wants signed. You and Renee take your money and go on. Nobody'd fault you. I wont.

Wade was quiet a long time. Out in the dark the water ran. Somewhere down toward the dry creek a chuck-will's-widow had taken up where the whippoorwill left off that morning, as if the country kept that one voice going in shifts around the clock, the same question asked all night and all day with no answer expected or required.

You couldnt make them payments, Wade said finally. Not on what this place clears. You'd lose it all inside five years and have nothin. Not the ground nor the money neither. At least my way you walk off with somethin.

Probably so.

Then why in the hell would you offer it.

Because then it'd be the ground that beat me, Ellis said. Or the water, or the bank, or the weather. Somethin I could stand in front of. He looked at his brother in the dark. I just dont want it to be us, Wade. Out of everthing that could take this place I dont want it to be us that done it. That's all I know. I know it aint arithmetic.

Wade looked away down the rows. In the last light the trees stood each above its small dark circle of wet ground, drinking, and the smell of the watered dust came up to the two men on the tailgate, and Wade sat breathing it and not speaking, and Ellis did not press him because he knew the smell was making an argument no man could make and he was not above letting it.

I aint sayin yes, Wade said at last. To none of it. I'm sayin I'll think on it. I'm sayin maybe Friday dont have to be Friday.

All right.

I'm sayin maybe we get this crop in first. See what she makes. A man ought to know what he's sellin before he sells it.

All right, Ellis said. We'll get the crop in.

They sat a while longer. Then Wade slid off the tailgate and raised it and shot the latches home and they got in the truck and Ellis turned it in the row and they drove up out of the bottom with the headlights yellowing the dust before them, and behind them in the dark the water went on running out along the old line and the new, finding the roots of the trees in the dark as it had always done, asking nothing, promising nothing, neither theirs nor anyone's, and the orchard stood and drank in the darkness and the stars came out over it all unnamed.
