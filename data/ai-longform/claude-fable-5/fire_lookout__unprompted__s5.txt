The smoke came up out of Lodgepole Creek a little after ten in the morning, while Marion was washing her one good mug in the basin and thinking about nothing, which after three summers in the tower she had finally learned to do. She saw it first as a wrongness at the edge of her eye, the way you feel a person enter a room behind you. She set the mug down on the sill, still wet, and picked up the binoculars that lived on the hook by the east window, and she looked, and her stomach went out from under her before her mind had assembled a single word.

The column was thin and gray-white, leaning a few degrees with the upslope morning wind, rising from the bottom of the drainage about where the creek made its second bend. She did not need the Osborne to shoot the azimuth. The firefinder had been pulled out two seasons ago anyway, when the district decommissioned the tower and the lookout program with it, leaving only the brass ring of its mount bolted to the table like the fossil of an instrument, but she did not need it, because she had sighted along that line a thousand times in idleness, and she knew what lay at the bottom of it. Caro's cabin sat in that bend. Caro's cabin and the old corral and the spring box their grandfather had set in concrete in 1958, and nothing else for four miles in any direction.

She watched the smoke for a full minute, which is what the manual used to say to do, back when there had been a manual that applied to her. Watch it. Time it. New smoke can be dust, can be a water dog of mist burning off, can be somebody's slash pile with a permit number on file at the district office. But mist did not rise in a column at ten in the morning in the third week of a July that had not seen rain since the solstice, and nobody held a burn permit in this forest, not this year, not with the fuel moistures where they were, and dust did not hold its shape and feed itself and darken at the base the way this was darkening.

She went to the radio because her body went to the radio, the way a hand goes to a light switch in a house where the power has been out for days. The set was still mounted in its bracket below the south window, a gray Bendix-King base station that the district had never bothered to retrieve, and she keyed it and said, "Sawtooth Lookout, Dispatch," into the dead plastic, and heard nothing, not even the static that would have meant the thing was drawing power, because it was not drawing power, because the repeater on Greenhorn Peak had been pulled in April and the batteries in the tower's bank had been dead since June, and she had known all of this, had signed a paper in the district office acknowledging that she occupied the structure as a caretaker volunteer at her own risk and with no communications support, and had thought at the time that the phrase had a kind of honesty to it that she appreciated. No communications support. As good a description of her life as any.

Her cell phone was in the drawer under the bed, kept charged off the small solar panel for the one bar of service that sometimes ghosted in from the valley when the air was right. She climbed the inside of her own held breath getting to it. No bars. She held it out the window, held it over her head, walked the catwalk all the way around the tower's exterior holding it like an offering, like a censer, and the phone considered the wilderness in every direction and found nothing in it worth speaking to.

The smoke had doubled while she did this. It was building a head now, the top of the column curling over on itself, going from gray to a yellowed cream, and at its base she could see — or believed she could see, and three summers of looking had taught her that the difference mattered less than people thought — a faint shimmer, a wrongness in the air above the trees, that meant open flame.

The road was eight miles. The road was eight miles of switchbacks down the north side, and her truck was at the bottom of it at the trailhead because the last two miles to the tower had washed out in the spring and the district had declined to fix a road that led only to a structure they intended to let fall down. So: four miles of trail on foot to the truck, then the road, then the highway, then eleven more miles to the turnoff for Lodgepole Creek, then the creek road, which was bad, forty minutes if the culverts had held. Two hours, maybe two and a half, to get to a fire that was four miles away from her as the raven flew.

Or there was the other way. There was the way she and Caro had gone as girls, off the southeast shoulder of the lookout's knob, down through the whitebark and the boulderfield and into the head of the drainage itself, following the dry fork until it found water, following the water down to the bend. No trail. Steep enough in two places that their mother had forbidden it, which had been the whole reason to go. Four miles, maybe four and a half. She had not walked it in twenty-six years.

Marion stood on the catwalk with the phone in her hand and did the arithmetic that the body does before the mind consents to it, and then she went inside and dumped her day pack out on the bed and repacked it: the two liter bottles, filled; the first aid kit; the headlamp; a fire shelter, the old style, that had also never been retrieved and that she had kept under the bed the way other people keep a Bible; her knife; the phone, useless, but you carried it the way you carried the dead radio's call sign in your mouth, out of a faith in forms. She wrote on the back of a grocery receipt — Smoke Lodgepole Cr 2nd bend appx 10:10, gone down SE direct, M. Hale — and weighted it to the table with the wet mug, in case anyone ever came, which no one would, and then she was on the stairs, and the stairs were drumming under her boots in a rhythm her feet remembered from fire drills in a life when fire drills had been her job, and then she was on the ground, in the smell of warm granite and lupine, and the tower stood over her with its forty-one years of paint going to powder, and she left it without looking back, which she would think about later, that she had not looked back, that some part of her had already understood she was done with it.

The first mile was easy and terrible. Easy because it was all downhill through open whitebark, the trees spaced like a thought experiment about trees, the footing good. Terrible because the whole drainage lay below her like a diorama and she could not stop seeing the smoke, could not make her eyes do anything else, and the smoke was teaching her things she did not want to learn. It had gone darker. There was black in it now, threads of black braiding up through the gray, and black smoke meant heavy fuels, meant something man-made, meant — she made herself say it, out loud, to the trees, because the lookout's discipline was to name what you saw and not what you feared — meant possibly a structure. The cabin had a propane tank. The cabin had a wood roof that their father had always meant to replace and never had, and that Caro, in the fifteen years she had owned the place outright, had also never replaced, according to the one source of information Marion had, which was their cousin Dell, who emailed twice a year with the weightless cheer of a man determined to pretend his family was not what it was.

Caro might not be there. This was the thought Marion kept setting in front of herself like a chock under a wheel. Caro lived in town in the winters; the cabin was a summer thing; she might be in town, might be anywhere, might be standing in a grocery store right now holding a melon to her ear. But it was the third week of July. Caro had spent every third week of July of her entire life at that cabin, because their mother's birthday was the twenty-second, and after their mother died Caro had kept going, kept the vigil, kept the whole apparatus of remembrance running single-handed, and had let Marion know, in the last real letter that had ever passed between them, exactly what she thought of people who didn't.

You watch, Caro had written. That had been the phrase that ended it, the phrase Marion had carried for nine years like a stone in the shoe of her thinking. You watch from a distance. It's all you've ever done. You watched Mom die from three states away and you'll watch the rest of it the same way, and I want you to know that I see you doing it, even if you think nobody does.

And Marion had read that letter at a kitchen table in Spokane, and had not answered it, because every answer she drafted was either a surrender or a war, and then a year had gone by, and the not-answering had become a thing itself, a structure, load-bearing. And then, with the perversity that she had stopped trying to explain to anyone, she had applied for the lookout job on the mountain that stood over Caro's drainage, and gotten it, and spent three summers in a glass room four miles and four thousand vertical feet from her sister, watching. Making of the accusation a profession. She had told herself it was something else — solitude, recovery, the divorce, the layoffs, the word sabbatical deployed at her like a tarp over wreckage — but on the steep mornings, the honest ones, she knew that she had come to the tower to be near Caro in the only way that the architecture of their ruin allowed, which was at a distance, with optics.

Twice in three years she had seen Caro herself. Once a figure in the corral, small as a chromosome, doing something with a wheelbarrow. Once, last August, a figure standing in the creek at dusk, not moving, for a long time, and Marion had watched her sister stand in the water until the light failed, and had felt like a thief, and had kept watching.

The boulderfield, when she reached it, was worse than memory. Memory had been twelve years old with knees made of rumor. The rocks were granite the size of furniture, of vehicles, lichen-scabbed, and they shifted under her with deep sounds like doors closing in another part of a house. She went down through them with both hands working, her pack swinging, and partway through she became aware that the day had changed around her. The light had gone brassy. The smell arrived — not woodsmoke, not the campfire smell that human beings are bred to find comforting, but the other one, the chemical bitterness of things burning that were never meant to burn, paint and tar and insulation, and under it the green scream of live timber going up. The wind had swung. It was coming up the drainage at her now, carrying the fire's exhaust, and a fine gray snow began to settle on the boulders, on her arms: ash, weightless, some of the flakes still holding the ghost-shape of the thing they had been. A pine needle, perfect, that broke when she breathed on it. A square of something that might have been window screen.

She stopped on a boulder the size of a flatbed and made herself drink water and made herself think like a lookout instead of like a sister, because the lookout might keep her alive and the sister was going to get her killed. Fire runs uphill. Fire runs uphill faster than you can, faster than deer can, and she was proposing to walk downhill into the head of a drainage that a fire was burning in the bottom of, in July, in drought, with an upslope wind freshening. The drainage was a chimney and she was climbing down the inside of it. Every dead firefighter she had ever read about in the training materials — and she had read about all of them, the way you read about the saints of a church you are joining — had been above a fire in steep country when the wind changed.

But she could see the smoke from here, see under it almost, and what she could see was that the fire was small yet, an acre, maybe two, crawling in the creek-bottom fuels, throwing up its black core from one point — one point, the cabin, say it — and the slopes on either side were not running. Not yet. There was a window. Windows in fire behavior were real things, as real as windows in houses; they were also things that closed.

She kept going.

Below the boulders the forest closed in, lodgepole now, doghair-thick in patches where the old burns had regrown, and she lost her sight of everything, lost the smoke, lost the drainage, navigated by the fall of the land and the strengthening stink. The dry fork appeared under her feet as a vein of rounded stones and she followed it. Her body had gone into a state she remembered from labor, from the one labor, the child grown now and in Tucson and on Marion's side, technically, in a war the child had begged them both to stop fighting: a state in which pain filed its reports and the reports were noted and set aside. Her knee. Her lungs. A long bleeding scrape on her forearm from the boulders that she did not remember getting.

She thought about the last time she had walked down this fork, twelve years old, Caro fourteen, the two of them in canvas sneakers with a stolen pack of their father's cigarettes that neither of them could bring themselves to light, and Caro had said — Marion could hear it, the exact pitch of it, half scorn and half tenderness, the register Caro had spoken in her whole life — Caro had said, You'd follow me off a cliff, you know that? And Marion had said, You'd have to find one first, and they had laughed until they sat down in the dry stones and couldn't get up, and that was the whole memory, that was all it was, two girls laughing at nothing in the bed of a creek that wasn't there. You build a tower in your chest and you put one summer in it and you keep it staffed your whole life.

The water started as a sound. Then the fork was wet, then running, and the lodgepole gave way to spruce and the air went cool and riverine even with the smoke in it, and she was in the bottom now, on the floor of the chimney, two miles of creek between her and the bend. The smoke was overhead, a ceiling moving north, embered light coming through it so that the whole world had the color of an old photograph, and ash fell into the creek and rode the riffles down ahead of her like it too was going to see.

The first deer passed her at a half mile in. Then three more, then a hen grouse with her brood running like wind-up toys, all of them going upstream, all of them ignoring her completely, granting her honorary membership in the republic of the fleeing, and she understood that she was now the only living thing in the drainage traveling toward the fire, and she kept on.

She heard it before the second bend. People said roar and roar was wrong; it was more like weather, like a freight of wind that never crested, with a percussion inside it — pops, cracks, once a whump that she felt in her sternum and knew for the propane tank or what was left of it. The heat reached her in pulses through the trees. And then she came around the shoulder of rock that she and Caro had called the Elephant for its gray wrinkled flank, and the bend opened in front of her, and she saw it.

The cabin was gone. Not burning: gone, a black geometry collapsed into itself, one corner of the rock chimney standing out of it like a headstone, and around it an acre and a half of fire working outward through the brush and the jackstrawed deadfall, slow on the creek side, livelier on the slope, the whole scene wavering through heat like something rejected by the eye. The corral fence was a line of small individual flames, each post its own candle. The big spruce by the porch — the one their grandfather had refused to cut, the one in every photograph — was a forty-foot torch finishing itself, dropping burning limbs into the yard.

Marion stood in the creek. She had walked into the creek without deciding to, was standing shin-deep in it, and she heard a sound come out of her own throat that she did not recognize and could not stop, and she was filling her lungs to scream Caro's name into the fire — knowing it was useless, that no one inside that black geometry had been alive for an hour, that screaming was a thing you did for yourself —

— when she saw, downstream, in the wide gravel bar where the creek braided, below the fire and out of its reach, a figure sitting on a folding chair.

A folding chair. A green canvas folding chair, set up on the gravel bar like a seat at a regatta, and in it a woman in a quilted flannel shirt with a dog lying at her feet and a coffee thermos standing in the stones beside her, watching the fire the way you would watch a sunset, and Marion's legs took her down the creek at a stagger, through the braids, slipping, soaking herself to the waist, and the dog rose and barked once, and the woman turned in the chair, and it was Caro.

It was Caro at fifty-two, gray come in like frost, the same face, the same level eyes, and the eyes did something complicated when they took Marion in — went wide, went wet, went shuttered, all in the time it takes a match to flare and steady — and Caro said, in a voice scoured raw by smoke or by something, "Well, Christ."

Marion stopped ten feet away. Her lungs were heaving. Water ran off her. The fire spoke its weather behind them.

"You're alive," Marion said. It came out as an accusation. Everything between them always had.

"I'm alive." Caro looked at her — the scraped arm, the ash-gray hair, the wild ruin of her — and something moved across her face that was almost the old scorn and almost the old tenderness and could not decide. "You came down off that mountain. You came down the fork." It wasn't a question. "You came down the fork through this."

"I saw the smoke. The radio's dead. I saw the smoke come up from the bend and I —" Marion's voice quit. She gestured, uselessly, at the four miles behind her, the boulderfield, the chimney of the drainage, the entire nine years.

Caro looked at her for a long moment. Then she looked back at the cabin, at the standing corner of chimney with the heat bending the air above it.

"I called it in two hours ago," she said. "Drove out to the highway pullout where the signal is, called it in, drove back. They've got a crew coming up the creek road and they're talking about a helicopter for the slope if it makes a run. They told me to evacuate." She lifted the thermos slightly, set it down. "I'm evacuated. This is me evacuated. I'm not leaving till I've watched it."

Marion heard it then, faintly, under the fire's weather: an engine, low, far down the drainage, grinding up the creek road. Two hours ago. The fire had been called in before she had ever seen the smoke, before the binoculars, before the stairs, before the boulders. The whole desperate transit of her morning had been, in the world's accounting, unnecessary. She waited to feel foolish and found that she felt nothing of the kind.

"The propane," she said. "I heard it go. I thought —"

"I know what you thought." Caro's jaw worked. "I was in the creek getting water for the garden when it started. Chimney fire, I think, or the wiring, that old wiring, Dad's wiring — I don't know. I'll never know. It went up like it had been waiting forty years for permission." She was quiet. The dog leaned against her leg. "Everything's in there. Mom's everything. The photographs. The —" She stopped. Her hand made a small motion, encompassing it, declining to inventory it.

"I know," Marion said. "I know what was in there."

"Do you." But there was no charge left in it. Caro said it the way you fire a gun you know is empty, out of habit, to hear the click.

Then she reached down beside her chair, and Marion saw that there was a second chair there, folded flat on the gravel — the dog's shade, maybe, or just the way Caro had grabbed gear out of the shed with the heat on her back, two chairs because her hands had closed on two chairs. Caro picked it up and shook it open, the canvas snapping, and set it on the stones beside her own, facing the fire, and did not look at Marion while she did it, and did not say anything at all.

Marion came out of the creek. Her boots sloshed. She sat down in the chair, and her body, given permission, began at once to shake, and she let it. The dog came and put its chin on her wet knee. Down the drainage the engine ground closer, and overhead the smoke went on building its enormous, temporary tower into the blue, and the two of them sat on the gravel bar below the burning of everything their family had ever kept, side by side, watching, which was, Marion thought — as Caro unscrewed the thermos and filled the cup and handed it across without a word, without looking, the way you hand things to a person whose hands you have known longer than your own — which was, after all, the thing the two of them had always known how to do.
