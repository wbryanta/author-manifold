The hotel had kept its name. Moira had half expected them to change it, the way a family changes a christening plan when a child dies before the font, but there it was in brushed steel letters across the new portico: THE ATLANTIC HOTEL, and beneath it, smaller, *Kilmoran*, as though the town needed reminding of itself. The taxi let her out under the glass canopy and a boy in a grey waistcoat came for her case before she had her purse closed, and she let him take it, because that was what you did now, that was who she was now, a woman whose case was taken.

The facade was wrong in a way that only she, perhaps, in all the wedding party, would notice. The old building had been four storeys of Victorian brick with a mansard roof, and the windows had sat deep in their reveals like eyes under heavy brows. The new building was rendered white and the windows were flush and wide, great sheets of glass that gave back the September sea. They had built it broader and lower, as if the architects had been told, without anyone saying it aloud: nothing too high, nothing the ladders can't reach. She counted the floors. Three. The fourth, where the staff had slept, had not been rebuilt at all. They had simply left it out, the way you leave a name out of a story.

Inside, the lobby smelled of lilies and new carpet and the faint cucumber smell of expensive cleaning products, and a girl at the desk with a Polish lilt said, You're here for the Gilsenan wedding, of course you are, you must be the grandmother of the bride, and Moira said that she was, and the girl said, Isn't that lovely, and gave her a key card in a little cardboard sleeve. Room 212. Moira turned the card over in her fingers. In her day the keys had hung on a board behind the porter's desk, brass keys on brass rings, each with a fob of red leather worn black at the edges, and you could tell by the gaps in the board who was in and who was out, who was sleeping and who was down at the strand, the whole hotel legible at a glance like a page of music. Now everything was a blank white card. You could not tell anything from a card.

The lift was new too — there had been no lift, only the grand staircase for the guests and the back stairs for the rest of them — and as it rose she watched the floor numbers light and felt the small lurch in her stomach that she had felt for fifty years whenever a building closed around her. Second floor. The corridor was wide and bright, hung with framed photographs of the bay, and at every junction there was a green sign with a little running man on it, running for a door, running and running, and a map under perspex showing YOU ARE HERE in red, and the routes out drawn in confident green arrows. The new hotel was obsessed with leaving. Every twenty feet it told you how to leave. She stood for a moment in front of one of the maps and thought: we had nothing like this. We had a rope ladder in a wooden box at the end of the landing, and the box was painted shut.

Room 212 was nowhere she recognised. The geometry of the place had been redrawn entirely; corridors ran where rooms had been, and the room they gave her looked out over the side garden where the coal store used to stand, where Feeney the boots used to smoke his one cigarette of the morning in all weathers, cupping it against the wind off the water like a man holding a small bird. The bed was vast and white. She sat on the edge of it and did not unpack.

She had been nineteen the summer she worked here, and the work had lived in her hands ever since, the way swimming lives in the body. Even now, in her own house, she stripped a bed in four movements and made it in six, and her daughter-in-law had once watched her do it and said, Moira, you'd think you were being timed, and she had said, I was, once. Mrs. Crowe with her watch pinned upside down to her bosom like a nurse, standing in the doorway while you turned the mattress: a mattress turned weekly is a mattress that lasts, girls, and a hospital corner is a letter to the guest that says somebody in this house cares whether you live or die. Twenty-six rooms between two girls, and the two girls were herself and Josie Lynch from Erris, who could not make a bed to save her life but could make Moira laugh until she had to sit down on the floor of the linen room with her apron over her face.

She got up and went to the window. The tide was out and the strand lay shining all the way to the point, and a man was walking a dog along the waterline, and the dog kept running at the gulls and the gulls kept lifting and settling further on, unbothered, eternal. The same gulls, she thought. The very same gulls.

The ceremony was at four, in what they called the Seascape Suite, which stood where the old dining room had been — she worked this out by the windows, three tall windows facing the bay, and these at least the architects had got right, had perhaps even copied, because when she walked in on her son's arm the light fell across the rows of gold chairs exactly as it had fallen across the breakfast tables, and for a moment she could smell rashers and toast and hear the particular ring of hotel cutlery, heavier than house cutlery, on hotel china, thicker than house china, everything in a hotel built to survive being dropped.

Niamh was married at ten past four in a dress the colour of the inside of a shell, and Moira cried the correct amount, into a handkerchief that had been Colm's, and afterwards on the terrace there was prosecco and photographs and the wind took two hats and a reading of the seating plan, and a man she didn't know said to her, You must be very proud, and she said she was, and he said, And I hear you've a history with this place yourself, and she said, A summer, only, a long time ago, and turned to admire a child in a flower-girl dress so that the conversation would have somewhere softer to go.

Because the family had the story, of course, the way families have stories, worn smooth as sea glass and about as much use for cutting. Nana worked at the Atlantic the year of the fire. Nana raised the alarm. Nana got the people out. It had appeared in the speeches once before, at her seventieth, her son standing with a glass in his hand: and let none of us forget that this small woman here once carried a sleeping child down a burning staircase, and everyone had applauded, and she had smiled, and the applause had gone over her like weather, because the story as the family had it was true in all its facts and false in its whole shape, and she had never once corrected it, and the not-correcting had become, over fifty years, a labour in itself, a room she cleaned daily and no one ever saw.

At dinner she was placed between the groom's grandmother, a sweet deaf woman from Athlone, and Father Keane, who was retired now and ate with tremendous focus, and the speeches were long and kind, and Niamh's new husband called Niamh the best thing that had ever walked into his life, and Niamh's father told a story about Niamh aged six conducting a funeral for a jellyfish, and at some point between the dessert and the cutting of the cake, Moira rose, the way old women are permitted to rise, vaguely, in the direction of the ladies', and walked instead out of the Seascape Suite, down the bright corridor past the green running men, and stopped in front of a door marked STAFF ONLY.

She stood before it a moment. Then she pushed it open, because she had spent two months of her nineteenth year learning that a woman who walks through a door as though she has every right to is never stopped, and the door swung and she was in the back of the house.

It was the smell that did it. Not the front-of-house smell, the lilies and the cucumber, but the back smell, which no architect can redraw: laundry steam and bleach and the warm bready smell of sheets in quantity, hundreds of sheets folded and stacked, the smell of linen at scale. The corridor here was narrow and the light was fluorescent and there was a trolley parked against the wall with its bottles and cloths and its bag for the soiled linen gaping like a throat, and beyond it a door stood open on a linen room — racked to the ceiling, towels in white bricks, sheets in white slabs — and in the linen room, counting pillowcases with her lips moving, was a girl of about twenty in a grey housekeeping uniform.

The girl looked up and lost her count and Moira saw her decide, in the space of half a second, whether this old woman in wedding clothes was a problem.

I'm sorry, Moira said. I'm with the wedding. I used to work here. A long time ago. I was looking for — and she found she could not finish the sentence, because what she was looking for did not have a noun.

The girl's name badge said RŪTA. She had a tired clever face and a biro pushed through the knot of her hair.

In the old hotel? Rūta said. Before?

Before, Moira said.

Rūta set down the pillowcases and looked at her with frank interest, the way the young look at the very old when it occurs to them suddenly that the old were once real. They tell us about it in the training, she said. The fire. There is a plaque in the garden, you have seen it? With the names.

I haven't seen it, Moira said.

Three names, said Rūta. Two women and a man. And then, because she was young and the young say the thing: Did you know them?

The man was Feeney, Moira said. He cleaned the boots and carried the coal. He went back in for the cash box because he thought he'd be blamed. The cook was Mrs. Hanafin, she was sixty and slept like the dead, God forgive me, she slept like the dead. And the other one was Josie Lynch. She was nineteen. We shared a room on the top floor. There were two beds in it, hers under the window and mine by the door, and the window looked at the gable of the next roof, you couldn't see the sea from it at all, they kept the sea for the guests.

Rūta had gone very still, the stillness of a girl who knows she is being handed something.

It started below us, Moira said, hearing her own voice go on, level, as if reading a meter. In the flue of the kitchen range, they decided after, though nobody ever knew for certain. It was the twenty-ninth of August. A Friday. The hotel was full because of the regatta.

And you got out, Rūta said softly. They told us. A girl from the staff woke the second floor. She carried out a child.

I did do that, Moira said.

She had not planned to say the rest. She had carried the rest for fifty-one years through two children and four grandchildren and the long good marriage and the funeral two winters past where she had stood at the grave's edge dry-eyed because everything in her was needed just for standing, and she had supposed she would carry it the remaining distance, which could not now be far. But the linen room smelled of the linen room, and the girl had Josie's way of standing with her weight on one hip, and the door to the corridor was shut, and Moira was eighty-three years old and very tired of cleaning a room no one ever saw.

I wasn't in the building, she said. When it started. That's the part. We were locked in at eleven, the staff, Mrs. Crowe turned the key on the back stairs herself, but there was a window in the still-room with a broken catch and every girl who ever worked here knew it. There was a boy. Colm Gilsenan. He had a currach and a summer job at Mór's and a way of looking at you that took the legs from under you. I went out the still-room window at half eleven to meet him at the slip. Josie covered for me. She'd done it twice before. She lay in my bed by the door with the blanket up so if Mrs. Crowe looked in she'd see two sleeping girls. That was the arrangement. She lay in my bed, do you see. By the door.

Rūta did not say anything. Somewhere down the corridor a machine finished its cycle and sang three notes.

I was on the slip with Colm's jacket around me, Moira said, and I looked back up at the hotel because it was a beautiful thing at night, all its windows lit, like a liner, we used to say it looked like a liner, and I saw that the light in the dining-room windows was the wrong colour. Orange isn't the word for it. It moved. Lamplight doesn't move like that. And I knew before I knew. I was running before Colm understood I'd gone. I came in the front, the front door of the hotel, where I was never once allowed to walk in my life, and the porter was asleep and the hall was already filling with smoke along the ceiling, lying along the ceiling like a grey animal, and I rang the fire bell and I screamed, I had no idea I had such a scream in me, and then I went up the big staircase banging on doors, second floor, every door, and people came out in their nightclothes asking me was it a drill, asking me, as if there had ever been a drill in the history of that hotel, and a woman put a sleeping child into my arms because she had a baby in her other arm and I carried him down and out onto the gravel and his head never lifted from my shoulder, he slept through the end of the world, that child.

And then I went to the back stairs to go up. Because the staff floor had no bell. The bell system stopped at the third floor, the wiring had never been carried up, why would it, and the back stairs came up from beside the kitchen, do you see, the fire was in the back stairs before it was anywhere, it went up those stairs like it had an appointment. I got six steps. A man took hold of me — a guest, a big Englishman, he had me by both arms and he carried me out the way you'd carry a chair, and I was screaming her name into the smoke, and he kept saying, There's no one up there, love, it's stores up there, it's only stores.

The fluorescent light hummed. Rūta had one hand flat on the stacked pillowcases as if for balance.

They got Mrs. Hanafin's window open from the ladder, Moira said, but it was too late for her, the smoke had taken her in her sleep, which is what I pray, I have prayed it for fifty years, that it took Josie the same way, asleep, in my bed, before ever she knew. The coroner said it was likely. Likely. I have lived on that word the way you'd live on bread.

She found she was sitting on the linen-room stool, the very kind of stool, perhaps, low and round, and Rūta was crouched in front of her holding both her hands, and her hands were cold in the girl's warm ones.

I married him, Moira said. Colm. Three years after. We had fifty-one years and he never knew the whole of it either, he knew I slipped out, he never knew about the beds. The bride above — she stopped, and started again. The bride today is his granddaughter and mine. Niamh. She picked this place off the internet for the view and the dance floor and didn't I tell her grand, lovely, what a lovely choice. Because what would I say? My life came out of that night. My whole life. Every person at that wedding above exists because I climbed out the still-room window to kiss a boy at the slip. And Josie Lynch got the blanket pulled up to her chin and the door side of the room and nothing else at all, not a husband, not a child, not one more morning, and her people in Erris got a coffin they weren't let open and a hero in the newspaper who was me.

You were a hero, Rūta said. The child. The doors.

I was a girl who was lucky, Moira said, and rang a bell.

They stayed like that a while, the old woman and the young one, in the white-stacked room, and then Moira straightened her back, because there is a point past which a body trained by Mrs. Crowe does not slump, and she looked at the racks and said, in another voice entirely, the voice of a colleague: You keep a beautiful linen room. Whoever taught you, they taught you right. Cases in dozens, seams to the wall.

Rūta laughed, surprised into it, and wiped her face with her wrist. The supervisor counts on Fridays, she said.

They always count on Fridays, Moira said.

Rūta walked her back as far as the staff door and at the threshold she said, awkwardly, formally, like a girl saying a thing she has decided must be said: I will remember her name. Josie Lynch. When they tell us about the fire in the training, they say three staff, they don't say the names, only the plaque says. I will say her name to the new girls.

That, said Moira, would be a great kindness, and went through the door, and the back of the house closed behind her, and she was in the bright corridor again among the running green men.

In the garden, before going back in to the music, she found the plaque. It was set low in a granite stone by a bed of sea thrift, and the wind off the water had begun its long patient work on the bronze, and the three names were there, and the date, and the words IN MEMORY. She stood in her wedding shoes in the damp grass and read Josie's name with her lips moving, the way Rūta had counted pillowcases, and she said, out loud because there was no one but the gulls: It was a good life, Josie. I want you to know I didn't waste it. I made the beds right every day of it. And then, because Josie would have hated solemnity, would have flicked her with a tea towel for it: There's a free bar above, and you'd be disgusted at the carry-on.

The band had started when she came back into the Seascape Suite, and the lights were down, and the floor was full of the young, and Niamh came out of the crowd shining and flushed with her shoes already off and said, Nana, there you are, I was sending out search parties, and took her by both hands and drew her toward the floor, and Moira went. The song was nothing she knew but the floor under it was the same floor in the end, the same ground, the dining room, the breakfast tables, the heavy cutlery, the sea in the three tall windows gone dark blue now with the first lights of the town along the point, and above the dancers, at every exit, the green signs burned steadily, the little men running and running, and she thought: good. Let them burn all night. Let this building never stop telling its people the way out.

She danced two songs, which was one more than she had promised her hip, and then she sat where she could see the floor, and a waiter brought her tea without being asked, in a thick white cup that would survive being dropped, and she held it in both hands. Above the music she could hear, if she chose to, the building going about its night — the lift, the kitchen, somewhere far back a machine singing its three finished notes — the whole hidden labour of the place, girls she would never see carrying linen along narrow corridors, keeping count, seams to the wall, and one of them now carrying a name as well, light as a pillowcase, into all the rooms to come.
