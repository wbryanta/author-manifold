In the line of trucks at North Sydney he sat with the engine off and the window down, smelling diesel and kelp and the cold iron of the dock, and he watched the ferry take vehicles into itself the way a whale takes water, indifferently, in great swallows of noise. It was the last sailing of the day. Rain had followed him over the causeway and through the hills of Cape Breton and then quit at the coast, as if it too were unwilling to make the crossing. Above the terminal the sky was the colour of an old nickel.

The man with the orange wand waved him forward. He drove up the ramp into the lit belly of the ship and a boy in coveralls chocked his wheels and he climbed the steel stairs with his bag, past the diagrams of lifejackets, the arrows, the small drawings of human figures falling correctly into the sea.

His name was Walter Ross. He was sixty-one years old. In his coat pocket he carried a folded letter from the Royal Canadian Mounted Police detachment at Channel–Port aux Basques, and in his wallet a photograph he had not looked at in some years, and on his left hand a wedding ring he had not been able to remove since his wife's death because his knuckles had thickened with work and weather, so that her claim on him was now a fact of the body.

He had spoken with a Constable Mercer twice on the telephone. The constable had a young voice and an old courtesy, and he had laid out the facts the way you lay out tools before a job you don't want to do. A man taken from the water off Isle aux Morts by a fisherman checking pots. No wallet. No rings. In the breast pocket of the jacket, zipped inside a freezer bag, an envelope addressed to Walter Ross at a house in Margaree Harbour that Walter had not lived in for thirty years. The left hand missing two fingers. Aged anywhere from sixty to seventy, the sea being careless with such distinctions. We'd be grateful, the constable said, if you could come and have a look. He did not say at what. There was a long stillness on the line in which Walter could hear, behind the constable, a kettle beginning, or wind.

Calum, he had said, finally, to the kettle, to the wind. My brother's name is Calum.

If it's him, said Mercer.

If it's him.

The ferry left at dusk, hauling itself off the dock with a shudder he felt through his shoes, and Nova Scotia slid backward into its own weather. He stood at the stern rail with a few others — a trucker in a Leafs cap, a young couple wrapped in one coat, a woman holding her dog up to see the land go — and he watched the low hills dissolve, first into blue, then into rumour, then into nothing at all. The wake unspooled behind them, white and continuous, the only sentence the ship knew how to write.

Six hours of black water. The Cabot Strait at night is not a place, it is a duration. He had crossed it twice in his life, both times in daylight, both times in another century. Calum had crossed it more times than anyone could count, in trawlers and longliners and once, he claimed, in a dory with a drunk Frenchman, though Calum's claims were a currency that inflated as you held them.

Somewhere beneath the ship — he had read this years ago and it had stayed in him the way a hook stays in wood — lay the old Caribou, the ferry torpedoed on this same run in October of 1942, a hundred and thirty-seven gone in the dark, among them children in their nightclothes, among them the captain, Ben Taverner, who went down with two of his sons. A man and his sons in the one water. Walter stood at the rail until the cold found the gap at his collar, thinking of that other crossing going on forever below this one, the two ships passing each other every night, one of them lit and humming with televisions, the other patient in the dark with its dead.

His cabin was number 412, an inside room without a window, two berths though he had paid for one. He sat on the lower bunk under the kindly violence of the fluorescent light and took out the photograph.

Margaree Harbour, the summer of 1958. Two boys on the government wharf. Walter is twelve, narrow as an oar, his arms crossed in imitation of men he has seen. Calum is nine, and he is laughing, his head thrown back so far that the photograph holds mostly his throat, and he is holding up an eel.

The eel. He had forgotten the eel and now he could not stop knowing it. Calum had pulled it from under the wharf with a handline baited with a herring head, and it had come up out of the green water like a length of muscle that had decided against having a body, and every other boy on the wharf had stepped back and Calum had stepped forward. That was the whole of his brother, in one motion, at nine years old. The world produced a thing that was all writhing and slime and unkillable wanting, and Calum reached for it.

Their father took the photograph with the Brownie he kept wrapped in a shirt in the wheelhouse of the Edith R., a boat he had named for their mother before they were married, which their mother said was the only love letter Hugh Ross ever wrote, and the only one he needed to.

Their father drowned in October of 1963, off Cheticamp, hauling traps alone because Calum, who was meant to go with him that morning, had a fever, or said he had a fever, or had been out half the night at the Doucet girl's and could not be roused — the story had three versions and the family kept all of them, the way you keep three knives that each do one thing well. The boat came home. Their father did not. That was the strangeness no one in the house ever recovered from, the Edith R. found nosing against the ledges at Grand Étang with her engine still turning over, faithful and stupid as a dog, and Hugh Ross gone out of her as if he had simply remembered an appointment elsewhere.

Walter was seventeen. Calum was fourteen. Their mother went into the front room with the minister and when she came out she had become the woman she would be for the next twenty-four years, upright, dry-eyed, terrifyingly polite, a woman who played the organ at Knox Presbyterian with her spine straight as a wharf piling and never once, in Walter's hearing, said her husband's name aloud again.

No one blamed Calum. That was the thing. No one ever said it. The sentence lived in the house unspoken for years, behind the wallpaper, under the linoleum, in the clock on the kitchen shelf that their father had wound every Sunday and that Walter now wound, and the sentence did not need saying because Calum said it to himself daily, you could watch him do it, you could watch the boy at the supper table laying the charge and lighting it. He was gone at sixteen. Deckhand out of Louisbourg, then the Newfoundland boats, then the cable ships, then the rigs. He sent money sometimes, in envelopes without letters. He sent postcards from Goose Bay, from Aberdeen, from Reykjavik, and the postcards said things like still upright or this place has weather like a grudge or nothing at all, just C, the single initial like a hook left in the water to show he still fished these waters, still held some lease on them.

Walter stayed. Walter married Greta MacIsaac and learned surveying and spent forty years walking property lines through alder and bog with a transit on his shoulder, fixing the boundaries of things, driving iron bars into the earth at the corners of other people's certainty. It was honest work and he had loved it without ever saying so. You found the old monuments, the pins set by dead men, you proved the line, you marked it. There was a morning in 1981, in fog so thick he surveyed by sound, when he understood that he had chosen his trade as an argument against his brother, who recognized no line of any kind, and the understanding had embarrassed him, alone in the fog, and he had never told anyone, not even Greta, who would have laughed and then put her hand flat on his chest the way she did when he was being more serious than the occasion required.

Greta. Two years gone now. He turned the ring on his finger, in the cabin, under the light.

He could not sleep in a room without a window, that was the discovery of his sixty-second year, so he went up.

The bar on deck seven was nearly empty. A television above the liquor bottles showed a hockey game from out west with the sound off, the players swimming back and forth in their silence like fish in a bright tank. The trucker in the Leafs cap was there with a beer, and an old woman knitting something long and grey, her needles going with the regularity of an engine, and a crew member, a young Filipino man, mopping the same stretch of floor with great patience, as though the floor were a story he was being told slowly.

Walter bought a rum, which he did not usually drink, because it was what Calum drank, and he was surprised at himself, and drank it anyway. The ship rolled, long and easy, a big animal shifting its weight in sleep. Beyond the salt-bleared windows there was nothing. Not darkness, exactly. Absence with a horizon somewhere in it.

The last time he had touched his brother was the third of June, 1987, in the kitchen of the house in Margaree, three days after their mother's funeral, which Calum had missed. He came up the lane in a taxi from Sydney, forty miles of taxi, drunk in the particular way Calum got drunk, which was lucid and musical and cruel, and he came into the kitchen where Walter was boxing their mother's dishes, and he looked at the boxes, and he said, Well. The undertaker's son.

They were both past forty. They fought like boys. There was a fumbling and a crash of the dish boxes and then Walter hit him once, a short right hand he had not known he owned, and the ring — this ring, Greta's ring — opened Calum's eyebrow to the bone. The blood was very sudden and very red in that faded kitchen. Calum stood holding the lip of the sink, dripping into the white enamel, and then he began to laugh. That was the unforgivable thing, in the end. Not the missed funeral, not the years, not the morning in 1963 that neither of them had ever once spoken of directly. The laughing. He laughed with the blood running into his eye and he said, There he is. There's Dad's boy. I wondered where they were keeping you.

Then he washed his face at the tap, drank a glass of their dead mother's water, and walked back down the lane to the waiting taxi, and Walter stood in the kitchen with his split knuckle and the clock ticking and did not go to the door.

Thirty-six years. A postcard maybe every second year, then every fifth. One phone call, in the winter of 2003, two in the morning, Calum's voice arriving out of a roar of background noise that might have been a bar or might have been the North Atlantic, and Calum did not apologize and did not ask to be forgiven, he asked — Walter had stood in the dark hallway in his bare feet, Greta's voice sleep-soft behind him saying who is it, who is it — he asked whether the kitchen still smelled the same. That was all. Whether the kitchen still smelled the same, of woodsmoke and Sunlight soap and the inside of the clock. Walter said he had sold the house in 1990. There was a silence with the whole ocean in it. Then Calum said, Ah, well, in the voice of a man closing a book, and hung up, and that was the last of him, except for an envelope postmarked Bonavista in 2011 containing a newspaper clipping about a winch accident aboard a longliner, two crew injured, no names Walter recognized, nothing underlined, no note. He had puzzled over it for a week before he understood it was an announcement. Calum was telling him about the fingers. It was the closest thing to a letter his brother ever sent, an account of his own diminishment, mailed without comment, like a survey pin driven at a corner: this much of me is gone; the line of me now runs here.

The trucker left. The knitting woman left, trailing her grey wool. The young man finished the floor and stood his mop in the bucket with the gentleness of a man stabling a horse. Walter went out on deck.

Three in the morning in the middle of the Cabot Strait. The wind had organized itself into something serious. The deck lights made small sane circles and beyond them the dark went all the way down. He stood gripping the rail and let the cold do its work on him, and he thought about the children on the Caribou in their nightclothes, and about Ben Taverner and his sons, and about how the sea did not keep its dead in any order, fathers and sons and brothers all filed together under the one heading, and he said, out loud, to the wind, because no one says such things indoors: I don't know what I'll do if it's you. And then, more quietly, the truer thing: I don't know what I'll do if it isn't.

Because that was the shape of it. For thirty-six years his brother had been a possibility, a direction, a postmark. As long as Calum was somewhere — Aberdeen, Bonavista, a bar with the ocean in it — the kitchen door stood open in a way Walter had stopped admitting to himself. A man could go to Bonavista. A man could, any given Tuesday, get in the car. He never had. But the never-had was alive, it had a pulse, it was a different country from never-can.

He slept, in the end, two hours in the windowless room, and dreamed not of Calum but of the eel, the eel coming up out of the green water endless, more and more of it, the line going down forever, and his father's voice somewhere above saying, easy now, easy, you've got him, in a tone Walter had not heard for sixty years and woke from with his face wet, furious at himself, at the cheap tricks of sleep.

Port aux Basques came up out of the dawn like something the rock had decided to attempt against its better judgment. Bare hills the colour of rust and iron, houses in their brave paints — turquoise, ochre, a red like arterial blood — set down among the stone as if dealt from a deck. Fog lay offshore in a long bolt of grey wool. Gulls came out to meet the ship and rode the air off the superstructure without moving their wings, escorting them in like officials.

Constable Mercer was waiting at the terminal, younger even than his voice, with red hair cut close and a way of standing that suggested he had practised solemnity in a mirror and almost had it. He shook Walter's hand and said it was a dirty old crossing last night by the look of the boat, and Walter said he'd slept fine, and both of them let the small lie stand there between them, useful as a fence.

They drove up through the town to the hospital, the Dr. Charles L. LeGrow, a low building above the harbour, and Mercer talked, because silence in a car is a weight young men can't carry. He said the fisherman who found him was a Hatcher from Isle aux Morts, that the name of that place meant what Walter probably guessed it meant, Isle of the Dead, named for all the wrecks, and that the people out there had been pulling strangers from the water for two hundred years, it was practically the parish trade. He said the man had been in the water some days. He said this carefully, watching the road. He said, Mr. Ross, I'll be honest with you, the sea is hard on a face. We mostly hope for the hands, or marks. Scars and the like. You'll want to be ready for that.

I'm a surveyor, Walter said. Retired. We don't get the whole boundary. We get a pin here, a blaze on a spruce, an old fence line under the moss. You prove what you can prove.

Mercer looked at him sideways, and nodded, and seemed for the rest of the drive to be turning this over, a young man being handed a tool he hadn't known existed.

The room was in the basement and it was very clean and the light in it was the same fluorescent light as the cabin, as every room in which the body is made to answer questions. There was an attendant, a heavy quiet man named Organ — Walter saw it on the tag and thought of his mother at Knox Presbyterian, spine straight, hands on the keys, and almost laughed, and understood that the almost-laughing was Calum in him, arriving precisely now, after thirty-six years, to stand at his shoulder in a morgue and make jokes. The table. The sheet. The shape under the sheet smaller than a man, the dead always smaller, as if the soul had been a kind of inflation.

Whenever you're ready, Mercer said.

He was not ready. He nodded.

Organ folded the sheet down from the face the way you turn down a bed.

For a long moment there was nothing. That was the truth of it and he would keep it the rest of his life: for a long moment he looked at the face and it was no one, it was the sea's work, it was a stranger the water had begun and not finished, and a clean cold relief went through him, and a desolation under the relief like the second wave under the first. Then he stopped looking at the face the way the living look at faces — for the play of it, the weather of it — and began to look the way he looked at land. Structure. Monuments. The deep bones of the brow that were their father's, that he shaved under every morning of his own life. The set of the ears. The big slabbed shoulders of a man who'd hauled gear for fifty years.

Show me the left hand, he said, and was surprised at the steadiness of the voice, which seemed to be coming from someone employable, someone hired for this.

Organ brought the hand out from under the sheet. The two outer fingers gone, long-healed, the stumps smooth and seamed, an old amputation, decades old, the hand itself broad and ruined and capable, a hand that had been used the way you use a tool you don't expect to outlive.

And then — he did not ask for this, his own hand simply went out and did it — he reached and touched the cold brow above the left eye and tilted his head into the dead light, and it was there. A small white seam in the eyebrow, the size of a grain of rice, the size of the stone in a ring. Thirty-six years old, that mark. He had made it himself, in a kitchen that no longer existed, in a fury he had spent half his life pretending was finished, and the sea, which erases everything, which had taken the face and the fingerprints and very nearly the man, had not been able to take it. His own signature. The one line he had ever drawn on his brother, and it had held, it had proved out, a monument found under the moss.

Yes, he said. That's him. That's Calum Ross.

He heard Mercer let a breath go. There would be forms. There were always forms; the dead generate paper the way wounds generate heat. He signed where he was shown, in the small office upstairs with a window full of fog, and Mercer slid across the desk a manila envelope containing the effects, and on top of the effects, in its freezer bag, the letter. His name on it in Calum's hand — he knew the hand at once, off the backs of thirty years of postcards — Walter Ross, Margaree Harbour, the ink bled and feathered where the sea had got at it through the plastic, the address of a house sold in 1990, a letter mailed, if it had ever been meant for mailing, to a kitchen that existed now only in the two of them, and now only in one.

He did not open it there. He carried it out into the morning and down to the harbour and sat on a bollard among the gulls with the fog coming apart offshore in long ropes, and he opened it with his thumb, carefully, an old man's caution, and there was no letter in it. There was the photograph. The other print, the twin of the one in his wallet, the two of them made the same week in 1958 and carried since then in opposite directions around the whole of the Atlantic. The water had been at this one too. The faces were going, blooming into white, becoming weather. But the wharf was there, and the boys' bodies were there, the crossed arms, the thrown-back head, and on the reverse, in pencil gone almost to nothing, in their father's printing, not Calum's: W. & C. The eel.

Walter Ross sat on the bollard with the photograph in both hands while the ferry behind him sounded its horn for the morning crossing, the long note rolling out over the strait, over the lit ship and the dark one beneath it, over all the fathers and sons in their one water.

It is August. It is 1958. Their father is alive, standing in the sun with the Brownie at his eye, saying hold still, hold still now, and nobody holds still. The eel comes up out of the green water all muscle and refusal, more of it always, and every boy on the wharf steps back. His brother steps forward, laughing, and reaches.
