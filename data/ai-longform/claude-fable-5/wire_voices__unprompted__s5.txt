We sat four to the board on the night trick that year, in the toll office above the dime store on Commerce Street, and from seven until one in the morning the whole of Aldine, Kansas, passed through our hands on its way to somewhere else. The board was taller than a standing man, a black cliff drilled with jacks, and below each pair of jacks a small lamp that lit when a line wanted you. The cords hung in front of us in their weighted pairs, and when the traffic was heavy the board looked like something growing, vines of black rubber climbing toward all the cities we would never see. Wichita, Kansas City, Denver. Amarillo when the cattle were moving. In December, everywhere.

You learned the town by its calling. You learned that Dr. Pruett's wife called her mother in Salina every Tuesday and that the Lindquist boy called collect from Fort Riley and that his father always accepted before I finished saying the charges. You learned who called late and lowered their voices, and you did not say what you learned, because that was the first rule and the last rule, drilled into us by Mrs. Othel Banks, the chief operator, who had worked the board since before the war and who said an operator was like a window glass: light passed through her and she was not changed by it.

I believed that for a long time. I was twenty-seven that year and I had been a widow for two of them, and I think I liked the work because the headset made a kind of weather around my head, a steady rain of other people's lives, and inside that rain I did not have to think very much about my own. Glenn had been gone since the spring of 1966, killed in a place I had to find in the atlas at the library because nobody could tell me how to spell it. After the funeral my mother said I should come home, and instead I took the operator's examination and learned to say "Long distance" in a voice that belonged to the company and not to me.

The first time Samuel Keel called, it was a Sunday in January, the seventh, with snow ticking against the windows and the board quiet the way it goes quiet after church-visiting hours are over. His lamp came up at nine o'clock exactly. I plugged in and said, "Long distance," and a man's voice said, "Yes, ma'am. I'd like to place a call to Bakersfield, California. Person-to-person." A pause, the small sound of paper. "To Mrs. Ruth Caldwell." He gave the number slowly, the way men give numbers when they have written them down and do not trust their own handwriting, and I wrote the ticket and built the call westward, Aldine to Wichita to Denver, the route opening under my fingers like a road at night, and in Bakersfield a phone rang twice and a woman said, "Hello?"

"I have a person-to-person call for Mrs. Ruth Caldwell," I said. "Is she available?"

There was a silence on the Bakersfield end that lasted longer than such silences usually last. Then the woman said, "This is she," and I said, "Go ahead, please," and threw the key that was supposed to take me off the line.

I want to be honest about the key, because everything that came after depends on it. On a person-to-person call the operator stays on long enough to verify the party and start the timing, and then she leaves. That is the practice and that was my practice, nine hundred times out of a thousand. But there is a moment, two seconds, three, between verification and the key, when you are still in the call, and in that moment, on that first Sunday, I heard Samuel Keel say, "Ruthie," just the one word, and I heard the woman in Bakersfield breathe in like someone stepping into cold water, and she said, "Sam. My Lord. Sam." And then I threw the key, and the lamp burned steady, meaning the line was working, and I sat in the snow-light of the office and watched it burn for eleven minutes, which is what I wrote on the ticket, and which cost him, at the Sunday rate, more than most men in Aldine would spend on a telephone call in a season.

He called the next Sunday at nine. And the Sunday after. By February I had stopped being surprised when his lamp came up, and by March I had started, without ever deciding to, keeping the position at the south end of the board on Sunday nights, the one that caught his exchange, trading with Darlene Pohl when the schedule went against me. Darlene liked the north end because the heat register was under it. She never asked why I wanted the south, and I never told her, because I could not have told her in any way that did not sound like what it was.

What it was: I had become attached to two voices. There is no respectable way to say it. He had a low voice with gravel at the bottom of it, a voice that had been outdoors most of its life, and he said "ma'am" to me every time as though I were doing him a personal kindness instead of my job. Her voice was the kind they used to call whiskey-and-honey, a voice with laughter built into the walls of it even when she wasn't laughing, and she said "This is she" every single Sunday, formal as a hymn, even though by spring all three of us knew there was nobody else it could be.

I never listened to the calls. I want that understood, although I am the only one left to understand it, and I am not sure I believe myself entirely. I did not sit on the line. But the practice in our office was to check a long call at intervals, a few seconds, to confirm the connection was holding, the company's word for it was "observing," and I observed Samuel Keel's calls to Bakersfield more faithfully than I observed anything else in my life that year, including Lent. A few seconds, once or twice in a call. It is amazing what a person can build out of a few seconds a week.

I heard, in February, "—and I told him, I said, that fence was down before your cattle ever—" and her laughing, the laugh finally let out of the walls.

I heard, in March, "—thirty-one years, Ruthie, and I never once drove past a peach orchard without—" and threw the key faster than I meant to, my face hot, alone at the south end of the board.

I heard, the Sunday after they killed Dr. King in Memphis, the longest fragment I ever took, because the circuits were jammed all evening and I had to stay on to hold the connection through Denver. She was crying. Not for anyone she knew, I gathered, but the way a lot of people cried that week, at the size of the thing, and he was not telling her it would be all right. That was what I noticed. Glenn used to tell me everything would be all right, and he was wrong about all of it, and here was this gravel-bottomed voice from somewhere south of town saying instead, "I know it. I know it. It's a hard old world and it don't apologize." And her saying, "Then what do we do, Sam," and him saying, "We stay on the line, I guess," and I went out of the call like a burglar out a window, and sat with my hands in my lap until Darlene asked was I feeling well.

Thirty-one years. I did the arithmetic the way you pick a scab. Thirty-one years back from 1968 was 1937, and he had said it like a wound he was used to, and she was Mrs. Ruth Caldwell, which meant there was or had been a Mr. Caldwell, and the Keels were in the Aldine directory, Samuel T., a rural route box out toward Belmont township, and the directory listing said Keel, Samuel T. & Inez. And Inez. I read those two words at the counter of the office one slow Wednesday and put the directory back in its slot and told myself that a window glass is not changed by the light that passes through it.

I built their story anyway. You cannot help it; the fragments insist on becoming a thing. A girl in a peach orchard in 1937, Kansas or California or somewhere between, and the Depression moving people around like checkers, and a marriage that happened and a marriage that didn't, and then three decades of Christmas-card silence, maybe not even that. And then something, a funeral, a chance letter, a name in a newspaper, that put a Bakersfield number into an old man's careful handwriting. And every Sunday at nine, when the rates were down and Inez was, I supposed, at evening service at the Belmont Baptist church, which let out at ten-fifteen, he drove or walked to wherever his telephone sat and placed a person-to-person call, which costs more, but which costs nothing at all if the party doesn't answer, and which says, every time, before a word is spoken: it is you I am calling. Not your house. Not whoever is standing in your kitchen. You.

I knew it was a sin I was attending. I had been raised to know one when I heard one. But I had also sat at that board the night the Lindquist boy called from Oakland to say his orders had come through for overseas, and I had heard his father, who I knew to be a hard man, say, "I accept the charges," in a voice gone all to pieces, and I had decided sometime that year that the wires carried what they carried and that mercy was knowing the difference between the rules of the company and the rules of God, and that I was qualified to enforce only the first kind.

The summer came on. In June they shot the senator in the hotel kitchen in Los Angeles and the long-distance traffic ran heavy and strange for a week, the way it does when the country is talking to itself in the dark. Samuel and Ruth called through all of it, steady as the test signal. In July the call ran twenty-six minutes, his record, and I observed once and heard nothing but silence and almost broke in to check the connection before I understood that the silence was the call, that they were sitting two time zones apart with the receivers against their faces, listening to each other be alive, and that the company was charging him by the minute for it, and that he was paying.

In August she said, "You sound tired, Sam."

In September she said, "Did you see the doctor like you said," and he said, "I saw him," in a voice that closed like a gate, and she let it close, and they talked about her grandniece's wedding, and I went out of the call and sat looking at the board, all those small lamps, every one of them somebody, and I thought about the cough I had been hearing under his words since the wheat came in. Just a small cough. A clearing of the throat that had stopped clearing anything.

In October the calls got shorter. Twelve minutes, then nine, then seven, and his voice had moved up out of the gravel into someplace thinner, and on the last Sunday of October, the twenty-seventh, I verified the call and was about to throw the key when he said, "Ruthie, I want you to write down a number." And she said, "What number," and he said, "Just write it down," and gave her a number I recognized with my whole skin, because it was the number of the toll office, our office, the board I was sitting at, and he said, "If ever you can't get me, you call there and ask. They're good girls. They'll tell you what's what." And Ruth Caldwell said, "Sam, you're scaring me," and Samuel Keel said, "Hush now. It's a hard old world, is all. It don't apologize and neither do I," and I threw the key with my heart going like a sparrow in a stovepipe.

He knew, you see. He knew about me, or about whoever I was to him, the Sunday-night voice that said "Go ahead, please." He had heard me click on and click off for ten months, a few seconds at a time, and he had not reported me to Mrs. Othel Banks, and now he was leaving me to her like a man leaving a key with a neighbor.

The first Sunday of November was the third, two days before the election, and I traded with Darlene as usual and sat at the south end and watched the board, and nine o'clock came up the way it comes up, on the big regulator clock with the brass pendulum, and his lamp did not light. Nine-oh-five. Nine-fifteen. The Pruett line lit and I put Mrs. Pruett through to Salina with my hands while every other part of me stood out on a rural route in Belmont township in the dark. At nine-forty an inward call came down from Denver, and I took it, and the Denver operator said, in the flat singing voice we all used, "Aldine, I have a person-to-person call from Bakersfield, California, for Mr. Samuel Keel," and gave the number, his number, and I said, "Thank you, one moment," and rang the Keel line, and listened to it ring.

It rang the way a phone rings in an empty house. There is no real difference in the sound, the bell doesn't know, but I would have sworn then and I would swear now that you can hear it, the ring going out into rooms where nobody is moving toward it. I let it ring eight times, ten. The practice was six. On the fourteenth ring it was picked up, and a woman's voice, an old woman's voice, level and dry as a creek bed in August, said, "Keel residence."

"I have a person-to-person call for Mr. Samuel Keel," I said. "Is he available, please?"

The silence on the line was not like the silence Ruth Caldwell had made back in January. It had a different grain to it. Then the woman, Inez, it could only have been Inez, said, "Who's calling him?"

The rule was simple. On a person-to-person call, you give the originating city if asked. "The call is from Bakersfield, California," I said.

I heard her breathe. One breath. Inez Keel knew, I understood in that one breath, knew everything, had maybe known since January, the way wives know, the way the whole town probably half knew, and had carried it through ten months of Sunday evening services. And she said, in that dry level voice, not cruel, not kind, just final, "Mr. Keel passed away Thursday morning. You can tell Bakersfield that." And she hung up, gently, the click soft as a door latched in a sickroom.

The Denver operator was still on the line, waiting, and beyond Denver, Bakersfield, and beyond Bakersfield, on the end of all those miles of copper, Ruth. The proper procedure was to report to Denver: party unavailable. Denver would report to Bakersfield. Bakersfield would say to its subscriber, "I'm sorry, your party is unavailable, would you care to try the call later?" And Ruth Caldwell would hang up a telephone in California knowing nothing except a closed door, and she would try again Tuesday, and Thursday, and Sunday, ringing an empty parlor while Inez sat beside it not answering, and she might try for weeks before some other channel, a letter, a cousin, a clipping, told her what I already knew.

"Denver," I said, "release the connection at your end, please, and I'll handle the report direct." It was irregular but it was done sometimes, on trouble calls, and the Denver girl, bless her, didn't question it, and the circuit shuffled itself, and then it was only Bakersfield and me, and Bakersfield said, "Go ahead, Aldine," and clicked off, and then it was only me and the open line to a house in California, and I said, "Hello? Are you there, ma'am?"

"I'm here," Ruth Caldwell said. Then: "It's you, isn't it. The Sunday girl."

"Yes, ma'am," I said.

"He told me about you. He said, 'There's a girl on the wire Sundays, I can hear her come and go like a bird at a window.'" She gave a little laugh that had nothing of laughter in it. "He liked you. He liked anybody that paid attention."

"Ma'am," I said. The company voice was coming apart in my mouth like wet paper. "Mrs. Caldwell."

"Just say it," she said. "I knew when he gave me that number. I've known for three days, I think, the way you know weather. Just say it out loud so it's been said."

"He passed away Thursday morning," I said. "I'm so sorry. I'm so terribly sorry."

The line carried her silence to me across nine hundred miles, through Denver and the mountains and the long valleys, the same line that had carried thirty-one years' worth of Sundays, and finally she said, "Thursday morning. He always did like the mornings." And then, "Was it her answered the phone just now?"

"Yes, ma'am."

"Then she knows. Well. She always knew. We were the two that knew, her and me, and now we're the two that's left." Another silence. "What's your name, child? Your real name, not the company."

It was against every rule there was. "Arlene," I said.

"Arlene. I was Ruth Aldous before I was anything else. He used to call me out of an orchard by that name, Tulare County, the summer of '37, and I came every time. I want you to know something, Arlene, because there isn't one other soul on this earth I can say it to and have them understand what it cost." Her voice did not break. It bent, the way green wood bends. "Every Sunday at nine o'clock for ten months, I was nineteen years old. A telephone did that. You did that, you and your cords and your switches. Whatever they pay you, it isn't enough, and whatever you think you did wrong, listening at the window, you didn't. You were the witness. Everything that ever mattered to me happened with nobody watching, and you were the witness, and I thank God for you."

I sat at the south end of the board with the tears running down under the headset and into my collar, and the lamps lighting and Darlene taking my calls without a word, the way the girls covered for you when your face said cover for me, and I said, "Yes, ma'am."

"You'll want to close out your ticket," Ruth Caldwell said, steady again, the formality coming back over her voice like a sheet drawn up. "There's no charge on this one, isn't that right? Party didn't answer."

"That's right," I said. "Party didn't answer. There's no charge."

"There's always a charge," she said. "Good night, Arlene." And the line went dead from her end, the lamp going dark in front of me, one small light among the hundreds, out.

She never called again. I kept the south end of the board through the winter, out of habit or vigil, I couldn't have told you which, and Sundays at nine I watched the clock and the board the way you watch a road somebody used to come down. The election happened, and Christmas, and in January the men went around the moon and read Genesis out of the sky, and the long-distance traffic that night was the heaviest I ever worked, the whole country calling itself to say did you hear it, did you hear it, and I sat plugging cords until two in the morning, connecting voice to voice to voice, all of them lit up and reaching.

Mrs. Othel Banks was wrong about the glass, is what I finally decided. Light passes through a window, surely, but stand at one long enough, year after year, and the glass goes thin and rippled, it takes the shape of the weather it has stood against. I worked that board nine more years, until the dial equipment came through and they didn't need girls in Aldine anymore, and I carried out of that office no possessions but a headset I was allowed to keep and several hundred voices I was not, and of all of them the two I kept closest were an old man saying Ruthie like the first word of a prayer, and a woman in California saying This is she. Person-to-person. It is you I am calling. Not your house, not whoever is standing in your kitchen. You. Some Sunday nights, even now, at nine o'clock, in whatever quiet I happen to be sitting in, I feel the lamp light up under my breastbone, and I plug in, and I say, to the dark, to the dead, to the long-distance, "Go ahead, please." And then I do what I was trained never to do, what I did all along.

I stay on the line.
