The call had come at four in the afternoon, and by seven I was on the boat, watching Aberdeen slide backward into its own grey light. A constable with a young voice had read me the particulars off a screen, I could hear her scrolling: male, approximately sixty years of age, recovered from the water south of Lerwick on Tuesday morning. A wallet, much spoiled. A bank card in the name of C. Gillan. They needed someone, she said, who had known him in life.

Known him in life. As though there were another way of knowing a man. I wrote down the name of the hospital and the name of the sergeant who would meet the ferry, and then I stood in my kitchen for a long while with the kettle boiling itself dry, because I could not think what one packed for such a thing. In the end I packed a clean shirt and, for reasons I still cannot give an account of, a tie. As though identifying my brother's body were an occasion. As though Calum, who in fifty-nine years I never once saw in a tie except at our father's funeral and then at our mother's, would expect me to dress.

The boat was the Hjaltland, white and blue and smelling, once you were aboard, of chips and carpet and the faint diesel sweetness that lives in the bones of every ferry. I had booked a cabin. I had paid extra for an outside one, a porthole, as if the sea were something I would want to look at. The crossing to Lerwick takes twelve and a half hours. You leave in the evening, you sleep, or you don't, and you arrive in the early morning with the whole grey business of the day still ahead of you. People who do it often say the trick is to eat early, drink two pints exactly, and lie down before the boat clears the shelter of the land, because once she gets out into the open water of the strait she begins her long slow argument with the swell and there is no falling asleep after that, only enduring.

I ate early. I had the fish, which seemed in poor taste even as I ordered it, and a pint, and then a second pint, and then I sat in the forward lounge among oil workers and a school orchestra going north for a festival, their instrument cases stacked like small coffins along the wall, and I tried to remember the last words I had said to my brother.

It was eleven years ago, at our mother's funeral tea, in the function room of the Station Hotel in Stonehaven. He had come down on this same boat, or its sister, overnight, and arrived at the kirk twenty minutes late with the salt still on him, in a black jacket that didn't fit across the shoulders, and stood at the back. Afterwards, at the hotel, he came to me with two cups of tea and held one out, and I looked at it, and I said, We managed without you. We'll manage now.

That was not the last thing I said to him. The last thing I said to him was worse, and came an hour later, in the car park, and concerned our father, and money, and the four years I had spent driving our mother to Aberdeen Royal Infirmary and back while Calum was five hundred miles north gutting fish, or whatever it was he did, sending cheques I tore up because tearing them up felt like strength. The exact words don't matter, or rather they matter so much that I won't set them down. He listened to all of it with his head slightly bowed, the way he used to stand as a boy when our father went at him, and when I finished he nodded as if I had told him the weather, and said, Aye. All right, Ewan. And he got into his hired car and drove back to the ferry, and that was the end of us.

Every December after that a card came. Cheap cards, robins, snow on a kirk roof, the kind sold in packs of twenty in the post office. Inside, nothing but C. No message, no return address beyond the postmark, Lerwick, Lerwick, Lerwick. I never sent one back. The fourth or fifth year, under the C, in his terrible schoolboy hand, he had written a telephone number. A mobile number. I kept that card. It is in the drawer of my desk at home with the gas certificates and the dog's old vaccination book, and in eleven years I never once rang it, and on the boat that night, somewhere off Peterhead, I took out my phone and found I had, at some point I do not remember, entered the number into it under the single letter C, and I sat there with my thumb above it, a man of sixty-two, frightened of a telephone, frightened that it would ring out in an evidence bag in a police station in Lerwick, or worse, that it would ring in a kitchen somewhere and a living voice would answer.

I put the phone away. The boat had begun her argument with the swell.

There was an old man at the next table, drinking a half of dark beer with the patience of someone who has drunk ten thousand of them, and he caught my eye in the way that men of a certain age in that part of the world will, not intrusive, just open, leaving a door ajar in case you wanted through it. He had a Fair Isle gansey gone at one elbow and hands like something cured.

First time north? he said.

No, I said. But it's been a while.

Holiday?

No, I said, and meant to say business, the word was loaded and ready, and instead I heard myself say, My brother. Which was true in every direction at once.

He's up there, your brother? In Shetland?

Aye, I said. He's up there.

Good place for a brother, the old man said, and I have thought about that sentence many times since and still cannot decide what he meant by it, or whether he meant anything at all, or whether at a certain age every remark a man makes has gone soft and wide like an old chisel and covers more than it cuts. He told me about himself without being asked, the way lonely men do: forty years on boats, whitefish out of Lerwick and then the oil supply vessels, a wife in Gulberwick, a daughter in Perth he was just back from visiting. The daughter's husband had put in a kitchen you could land a helicopter on. He didn't see the sense of it, all that kitchen, but he allowed it wasn't his kitchen to see the sense of. He asked nothing more about my brother, which I was grateful for, and at half past nine he drained his glass, stood, steadied himself against the table as the boat dropped her shoulder into a trough, and said, You'll want to be lying down soon. She'll work tonight. And he went off down the corridor with the rolling, glued-down walk of a man the sea has never once managed to put on the floor.

I did not lie down soon. I went out on deck.

There is a small area aft where they let you stand, behind glass screens that take the worst of the wind, and from there you can watch the wake unspooling behind the boat, pale in the dark, a long seam stitched into the water and pulling itself out again as fast as it's sewn. The land was gone. There was a fish boat's light far off to the west, and above, when the cloud tore, stars in numbers you forget exist when you live in a town. The wind was from the northeast and cold enough to mean it, and I stood in it deliberately, the way you might hold your hand over a flame, because the cold was at least a simple thing, a thing with no history in it.

When Calum was nine and I was twelve, our father took us out to the breakwater at Stonehaven with two rods and one box of tackle between us, and somewhere in the middle of that afternoon I put a backcast into the side of my brother's left hand. The hook went into the web between thumb and forefinger, in past the barb, and he made one sound, a short high note like a gull, and then went white and silent and held his hand out in front of him as if it belonged to someone else. Our father cut the line, looked at it, and said, Right, and walked him back to the car and took the hook out himself with pliers on the kitchen table, no doctor, our mother holding Calum's arm still and Calum looking at the wall. And when our father asked how it had happened, before I could open my mouth, Calum said, I did it myself. Stupid. Wasn't watching my own line.

He was nine. I never asked him why he said it. I think I was afraid the answer would be simple, that he would shrug and say, You'd have got a hiding, which was true, our father's justice ran downhill and I was the elder and should have known better. The scar healed white and slightly raised, a crescent in the soft flesh, and for the rest of our boyhood I used to see it when he passed me a plate or took a ball out of the air, and feel something I had no word for then. I have a word for it now. You spend a life acquiring the vocabulary for things you did at twelve.

That was Calum entire, that lie. He took on weight that wasn't his. He took the blame for the byre window, for the dent in the Cortina, for the bottle of whisky that went down by a third the summer I was seventeen. He took on our father's contempt, which was a renewable resource, our father had contempt the way other men had opinions about football, and Calum stood under it with his head slightly bowed, and the year he turned twenty he went north to the fishing and never lived in our parents' house again. I stayed. I got the accountancy traineeship, the mortgage, the daughters, the proximity. I got to be the good son, which is a thing you can be while loving nobody very well, and he got to be the one who left, which is a thing you can be while loving everybody from a distance, and between the two of us we made a froze sea of it, and our mother died asking each of us, separately, on different visits, whether we had spoken, and each of us, separately, said, Aye, Mam, we're fine, and she knew it for a lie and let it stand, because she was tired, and because the lie was the last soft thing we could give her.

I went down to the cabin around midnight. The boat was working hard by then, long slow climbs and falls, the curtain swinging on its rail, the water in the toilet bowl tilting like a spirit level that couldn't find true. I lay in my clothes on the bottom bunk and the engine came up through the mattress, a deep continuous patience, and somewhere a door that hadn't been latched announced every swell. I did not sleep, or I slept the way you do on boats, in shallow bands, surfacing each time the pitch changed. At some point I dreamed, or remembered, the two of us in the single bed we shared until I was eight, top to tail, his cold feet by my face, his voice in the dark naming the boats in the harbour, the Girl Mina, the Argosy, the Sustain, a litany he had off by heart, and me telling him to wheesht and go to sleep, and him going quiet, and then starting again, low, as if the names themselves kept him afloat.

The tannoy woke me at six with weather and the breakfast service. Through the porthole, in the half dark, there was land: a low black coastline running with the boat, a lighthouse occulting somewhere astern, Sumburgh, I worked out later, and the water gone from open-sea grey to something more sheltered, more decided. I shaved with great care, as men do when care is the only thing left within their gift, and I put on the clean shirt, and I held the tie in my two hands for a long moment and then folded it back into the bag.

Sergeant Leask was waiting at the bottom of the passenger ramp with my name on a piece of paper, which was kind and unnecessary; we knew one another at once the way the bereaved and the official always do. She was perhaps forty-five, broad and calm, and she drove me through Lerwick in a marked car at the speed of a hearse, talking when I wanted talk and quiet when I didn't, a skill they cannot teach. The town was just opening. A man hosing fish boxes on a pier. Gulls auditing everything. She told me what she could. He had been working at the salmon farm out the far side of Bressay, had done for years; steady man, she said, kept himself to himself but well enough liked; lived in a room above the chandlery, paid his rent in cash on the first of the month. On Monday night the farm's small workboat was found loose of its mooring, drifting, engine off, and on Tuesday morning a woman walking her dog at the Sands of Sound found him. There would be an inquiry, a fiscal's report. The night had been calm, she said, and then said nothing further about the night, and I understood that the calmness of the night was the whole question, and that nobody would ever answer it, and that I would carry it like a stone in the shoe for whatever years I had left.

The hospital was small and clean and smelled of nothing, which must take a great deal of work. They put me in a side room first, with a box of tissues and a print of a beach on the wall, some white beach with turquoise water, nowhere within a thousand miles, and Sergeant Leask explained what would happen, that he was behind a window, that I could take all the time I needed, that I should only say what I was sure of. She had a folder with a form in it. There was a line on the form for relationship to the deceased, and I watched her pen hover and heard myself say, Brother, and the word came out of me whole and strange, like something swallowed long ago.

He was behind glass, on a table, covered to the chin in a purple cloth that somebody had chosen, in some office somewhere, out of a catalogue of cloths for exactly this purpose. The room beyond the glass was lit like a chapel.

I want to set down honestly what I saw, because there was a long minute when I did not know, and I had been warned there might be. The sea had had him a night and part of a day, and the sea is not gentle even when it is calm, and besides that there was the plainer fact that the last face I had studied belonged to a man of forty-eight, and this was the face of a man of fifty-nine, and the years do their own work without any help from the water. The hair was white at the temples and longer than he'd ever worn it. The face was heavier, the cheeks fallen, a deep crease either side of the mouth like our father's, exactly like our father's, which Calum would have hated, and there was a bruise or a graze along the left brow that I made myself stop looking at. It was him and it was not him. It was a stranger built from him. I stood at the glass and felt the certainty I had carried up the corridor dissolving, and a terrible door opened in me, a door with hope behind it, the worst hope there is, because if it was not him then he was alive somewhere, and if he was alive somewhere then nothing was finished and I could still ring the number, and I understood in that moment that I would rather it was not him even though some other woman's brother would then be lying on that table, and I was ashamed, and I have stayed ashamed.

Could I see his left hand, I said.

Sergeant Leask spoke into a phone on the wall, quietly, and on the other side of the glass a man in blue scrubs, whom I had not even registered standing there, came forward and drew the purple cloth back from the left arm, carefully, the way you'd uncover a sleeper you didn't want to wake, and lifted the hand a little, turning it.

It was an old man's hand now, the knuckles swollen, the skin gone loose and waxen and lined with work, the hand of forty years of nets and ropes and cold water. And in the web between the thumb and the first finger, white and slightly raised, a small crescent.

That's Calum, I said. That's my brother.

I am told I said it twice. I don't remember the second time. I remember putting my own hand flat against the glass, which you are probably not supposed to do, and the man in the scrubs not hurrying to cover the arm again, letting it lie there in the light a moment longer, which I will be grateful for until I die. I remember signing the form, and that my signature looked like a stranger's, and Sergeant Leask saying I had done well, as if it were a thing one could do well or badly, and perhaps it is.

Afterwards, in the side room, she brought me his effects in a clear bag with a tag. There was not much. Keys on a loop of orange twine. A lighter. A folding knife with the blade worn thin as a leaf from sharpening. Coins. The wallet, dried now, stiff as a shell, and inside it the bank card, a betting slip, a stamp, and a photograph gone almost entirely to cloud from the water, just two pale shapes against a darker shape that might have been a wall or a boat or a doorway, two figures, one taller, and no way on earth of saying who they were, and I sat and looked at that ruined photograph for a long time while Sergeant Leask went to fetch tea, and I did not turn it over, because the back was where people of our mother's generation wrote names and dates, and I could see ink had bled through, and I found I did not want to know, or rather I wanted to be allowed to know, which is different, and the sea had decided otherwise, and I let the sea's decision stand.

She asked if there was anything else I needed. I said I would like to walk, and she let me, with her card in my pocket and the arrangements all made for things I will not describe, the practical machinery that closes over a man.

I walked down through the lanes to Commercial Street and along to the small boat harbour. The chandlery was easy to find; I stood across from it and looked up at the two windows above the shopfront, one with a curtain half drawn, a geranium on the inside sill, doing well, recently watered, and I thought about the someone who would have to water it now, and I did not cross the street. At the harbour a man about my own age was mending creels in the stern of an open boat, paying me no attention at all, and I stood by the railing for the better part of an hour while the morning got on with itself around me, vans, gulls, a child being walked to school against her will, and I took out my phone and found the entry under C and I rang it. Somewhere in a building behind me, in a tagged bag in a property store, or in a drawer in that room above the chandlery, a telephone either rang or did not. Nothing answered. I listened to it ring out, the full count, and when the voicemail opened its small silence to me I said, It's Ewan. And then nothing else for a while, the harbour sounds going into the phone, the gulls, the creel man's pliers, and then I said, I got your cards, and I ended the call.

The boat south sailed at seven that evening. I had thought I might stay a day or two, but there was nothing for me to do that the sergeant and the undertaker could not do better, and I have learned that grief does not care where you stand. From the aft deck I watched Lerwick fold itself back into the land, the town lights coming up one by one as the dark came down, and then Bressay went by, and somewhere on the far side of it the cages of the salmon farm rode the water in the dusk, and then there was only the coast running south, low and black, and at last the Sumburgh light, swinging its long arm out over the strait, touching the boat and letting it go, touching and letting go.

I stayed on deck long after the other passengers went in. The boat picked up the open swell and settled into her work, and the wake went out white behind us, that long seam, stitching and unstitching, and I stood at the rail in the cold with my brother's lighter in my pocket, which Sergeant Leask had let me take, against some regulation or other, because the rest would follow in due course but a man should not go home from such a day with empty hands. The wind was from the northeast. The land sank. I named the boats in the harbour to myself, the ones I could remember, the Girl Mina, the Argosy, the Sustain, getting the order wrong, getting it wrong again, an old man at a railing saying boat names into the wind, and I kept at it, low, the way he used to, all the way out into the dark water, as if the names themselves could keep someone afloat.
