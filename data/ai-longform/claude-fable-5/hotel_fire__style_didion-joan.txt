The invitation arrived on a Tuesday in March, engraved, ecru on heavy ecru stock, the kind of invitation that requires two envelopes and a square of tissue, and I had read most of it before I understood what I was reading. Mr. and Mrs. William J. Brennan requested the honour of my presence at the marriage of their daughter Kelly Anne. The honour of my presence. Saturday, the fifteenth of June, at half past five o'clock in the evening. The Mirador, Las Brisas Point, California. I stood in my kitchen with the tissue in one hand and the invitation in the other and I noticed that the light over the sink was flickering, that it had been flickering for some days, and that I had been meaning to replace the bulb, and I thought about the bulb, and about whether I had bulbs in the drawer, and then I sat down at the kitchen table and replied, on the small enclosed card, that I would attend. I want to be exact about this. I did not hesitate. I did not weep or telephone anyone or pour a drink. I checked the box that said *accepts with pleasure* and I walked the card out to the mailbox in my bare feet and raised the little red flag, and only later, much later, did it occur to me to wonder what I had meant by pleasure.

The Mirador burned in the early morning of October 15, 1971. Nine people died. I can give you the names because the names have never left me, the way the names of people one actually knew can leave, slipping under the surface of the years: Guadalupe Ochoa, forty-four, of Mexicali, a chambermaid. Walter and Edith Frye of Visalia, married eleven days. A retired Navy commander named Holcomb, room 318, who had stayed at the Mirador every October since 1946 and asked for the same room every October and got it. A family from Fresno named Schiller, the parents and a boy of nine and a girl of seven, who had come down for the weekend because the children had never seen the ocean, or had seen it once, the newspapers differed on this point and I have never resolved it. The Santa Ana had been blowing for three days. Anyone who has lived in Southern California in October knows what that means, the air at four percent humidity, the eucalyptus dropping its oil, the static snap of car doors, the feeling that the whole coast has been primed like a wick. The fire began in the third-floor linen room of the north wing. The state fire marshal's report attributed it to the spontaneous heating of laundered kitchen rags, rags bearing residual cooking oils, which had been dried at high temperature and stacked while warm in a confined and unventilated space.

I stacked them.

I was twenty years old that October and I had worked at the Mirador since June, $1.80 an hour, a gray uniform with a white collar that we were responsible for laundering ourselves, a brass ring of keys that we signed out at three and signed in at eleven and that I can still feel, the particular weight of it, against my right hip. Lupe Ochoa trained me. She showed me how to make a hospital corner, lifting the mattress with her knee, folding the sheet at forty-five degrees, tucking with the flat of the hand, and she made me do it over until I could do it in eleven seconds, because she said the difference between a girl who lasted and a girl who didn't was eleven seconds a corner, four corners a bed, fourteen beds a shift. She showed me which doors to knock on twice and which doors to knock on three times and which guests left dollar bills under the ashtray and which left dimes, and she showed me how to fold a towel in thirds so that the monogram faced out, the rolling M of the Mirador, faced out and centered, every towel, every shelf, the linen room a wall of white with that one repeating letter, and I have thought since that I have never in my life been so good at anything as I was at folding those towels, and that this is either the saddest fact I possess or the only clean one.

Here is the part I told the inquest. On the night of October 14 I was working the four-to-midnight, and at approximately 10:40 I emptied the large Cissell dryer in the basement laundry, which contained, among other items, two loads of kitchen rags sent down by the chef, and I folded the rags and placed them in a wheeled canvas bin and took the service elevator to three and transferred them to the shelving in the third-floor linen room and closed the door. The marshal's investigator, a man named Dietz with a gray crewcut and reading glasses he kept folding and unfolding, asked me whether the rags were cool to the touch when I shelved them. I said they were cool. He asked me whether I was certain. I said I was certain. No negligence was found. The phrasing in the report is "no negligence on the part of any employee," and I have read the report — I obtained a copy in 1979, from the county, for a fee of four dollars, and I keep it in the same fireproof box as my divorce decree and the deed to my house — I have read the report enough times to recite it, and I want it on the record that the report cleared me, that the report cleared everyone, that the fire was an act of chemistry and wind and that acts of chemistry and wind have no defendants.

Here is the part I did not tell the inquest. There was a boy named Donnie Wechsler who drove a 1969 Camaro, gold, with a black vinyl top, and he was parked at the service entrance at eleven o'clock, and I knew he was parked there from 10:30 on because I could see the lot from the basement laundry window, and the rags were not quite done, and I ran the dryer the last fifteen minutes on high instead of letting them tumble cool, and I folded them fast, the way you fold when you are twenty and a boy is waiting, and I did not spread them on the table to breathe the way Lupe had shown me, *always let them breathe, mija, the kitchen ones especially*, and I stacked them deep on the shelf and I closed the door and I signed in my keys at 11:05 and I was in the gold Camaro at 11:08. Were they cool to the touch? I touched them with hands that had been touching hot laundry for seven hours. I said they were cool. I have spent thirty-one years deciding whether they were, and what I have decided is that there is no deciding, that the towels are folded now and forever in a room where the temperature can no longer be taken, and that "no negligence was found" is a sentence in the passive voice, and that I have built a life inside the passive voice the way other people build lives inside houses.

The fire was discovered at 2:51 a.m. by the night auditor, a man named Reyes, who smelled it before any alarm sounded because the Mirador in 1971 had no alarms to sound, no sprinklers, no illuminated exit signage, none of it required then for a structure of that age, a wood-frame Mission Revival structure built in 1928 with double-loaded corridors that drew like flues. I was asleep in the staff annex above the garages, which was attached to the north wing by a covered breezeway, and I woke to someone hammering on doors, and I got out with my shoes in my hand. I want to tell you that I remember the fire and what I remember is my shoes. Black flats, rubber soles, $6 at Leeds. I remember standing on the asphalt of the upper lot in my slip with the shoes in my hand and the wind coming hot off the hills and the north wing already a single orange thing, no longer a building with fire in it but fire in the shape of a building, and I remember thinking, with the flat lucidity that I have since learned to distrust in myself, that I should put my shoes on, and not putting them on. Lupe was on the third floor. She worked the overnight turndown that season because her husband had been laid off at the cannery and the overnight paid a fifteen-cent differential. They found her in the corridor outside 312 with her cart. I have been told, by people who meant it kindly, that smoke takes you before anything else does, that she would have simply gone to sleep, and I have accepted this the way you accept a hand on your shoulder at a funeral, which is to say I have stood still for it.

I left Las Brisas Point in November of 1971 and I did not go back, not for the inquest verdict, which was mailed to me, not when the hotel reopened in 1975, not in any of the years after, although I have lived for most of those years in Orange, forty minutes up the freeway, close enough that the Mirador appeared from time to time in the *Register*, a landmarks feature, a Mother's Day brunch advertisement, a small item when it changed hands in 1988 and again in 1996. I married a lawyer and was divorced from him, no children, and I went to work in title insurance, where I have remained. People ask what title insurance is and I tell them: I search the chain of title. I go back through the record, grant deeds, trust deeds, easements, liens, all the encumbrances a piece of ground accumulates, everything that has ever happened on or to a property that might cloud it, and when the chain is clean I issue a policy that says so. I certify clear title. I am, in other words, professionally engaged in establishing that the past does not encumber the present, and you may make of that what you like; I made nothing of it for twenty years and then one afternoon in a closing I heard myself say "free of all encumbrances of record" and had to leave the room.

I should say also, because it belongs to the record of how I have lived: I do not run a clothes dryer when I am not standing in the room with it. I sit on the aisle. In any restaurant, any theater, any church, I locate the exits before I locate my seat, and I count them, and in hotel rooms — I have stayed in hotels, I am not a curiosity — I read the diagram on the back of the door the way other people read the Gideon Bible, and I walk the corridor once, to the stairwell and back, before I will undress. These are not phobias. I have looked up the word and a phobia is irrational. These are the manners of a person who has seen what a building is.

Patsy Brennan was Patsy Kowalski in 1971 and she worked the counter at the Mirador coffee shop, the Cabrillo Room, eleven at night to seven in the morning, and she was on her break in the parking lot with a cigarette when Reyes started shouting, which is why she is alive, and which she will tell you within ten minutes of meeting you, lightly, as a funny story, *a Pall Mall saved my life, honey, tell that to the Surgeon General*. We were friends the way you are friends with the people you stood on asphalt with at three in the morning, which is a category of friendship that does not lapse no matter how little you have otherwise in common. She married Bill Brennan in 1973. Bill Brennan was a contractor, and Brennan Construction was one of three firms that rebuilt the Mirador, poured the new foundations of the north wing, and Patsy has always spoken of this as a romance — *Bill put it back, isn't that something* — and when she called me in February to say that Kelly had chosen the Mirador for the wedding, that Kelly had grown up on the story and wanted it, the place where Mom and Dad's whole life started, Patsy said, "I thought, why not, it's just a place now, it's a beautiful place," and then she said, "You'll come," and it was not a question, and I said yes, and that is the truthful account of why I checked the box that said pleasure. Because Patsy did not ask. Because for thirty-one years she has been the only person who never asked me anything.

I drove down on the fifteenth of June in the late afternoon with the marine layer still sitting on the coast highway, the hills gray-green and the ocean the color of nickel, and the Mirador appeared on its bluff exactly where it had always appeared, white walls, red tile, the campanile with its single bell, and I will tell you what I felt when I saw it, because I had wondered for three months what I would feel and had prepared for several possibilities. I felt nothing. I felt the way you feel passing a stranger who resembles someone dead: a hitch, an adjustment, nothing. A young man in a vest took my car. The lobby smelled of white flowers and chlorine and money, and the tile fountain was there, the original fountain, or a fountain quoting the original, Talavera tile and a soft recirculating sound, and along the corridor to the ballroom there were photographs in matched frames, the Mirador in 1928 with flags on the campanile, the Mirador in 1941 with sailors by the pool, a movie actress in dark glasses in 1957, the pool again, the lawn again, and then a photograph dated 1976, the reopening, men in wide lapels cutting a ribbon, and the caption beneath it said *lovingly restored*. Restored. Between 1957 and 1976 the wall offered nothing, and the wall was correct: there was nothing there to offer. A hotel is in the business of suggesting that nothing has ever happened in it, that every arriving guest is the first guest, every made bed an unprecedented bed, and I had been, at twenty, an employee of that suggestion, eleven seconds a corner, the monogram faced out, and standing in the corridor I understood that the photographs were only doing, with their gap, what I had been paid $1.80 an hour to do with a fitted sheet.

The ceremony was held on the Cabrillo Lawn at half past five. The Cabrillo Lawn is the long grass terrace on the north side of the property, with the bluff and the ocean beyond it, and it occupies, as nearly as I could establish by pacing it off against the line of the surviving south wing — and I did pace it off, in the hour before the ceremony, in my good shoes, while the florist's men were tying white ribbon to the last of the chairs — it occupies the footprint of the old north wing. The third floor of the old north wing, projected downward through thirty-one years of air, would intersect the lawn at approximately the height of a wedding arch. I sat in the fifth row on the bride's side. The fog had burned off the way it does in June by evening, all at once, as a kind of concession, and the light went long and yellow, and Kelly Brennan came down the grass aisle on Bill's arm in candlelight silk, and she was beautiful, they are all beautiful, that is the function of the dress, and the young man waiting for her did something with hospital software and had a kind face, and Patsy in the front row in champagne lace turned around once before the processional and found me and held up her hand, fingers spread, a gesture that meant nothing and everything, and I held mine up too.

During the vows I counted the sprinkler heads. I do not say this to be wry. There is a fire-suppression standard, NFPA 13, and the rebuilt Mirador met and exceeded it; I could see the heads at four-foot intervals under the eaves of the new arcade, brass, recessed, each one fused to open at 165 degrees, and there were smoke detectors in the arcade soffits and illuminated exit signs at the ballroom doors, and the ballroom doors were equipped with panic hardware, and I counted all of it during the vows the way another woman might have said a rosary, and when the officiant said *for as long as you both shall live* I was at twenty-six sprinkler heads and I thought, that is what the words mean now, the words mean twenty-six sprinkler heads, the words mean a building that has agreed in advance to be interrupted.

At the reception there were votive candles, perhaps two hundred of them, in small glass cylinders down the long tables, and there was an arrangement of white roses every three feet, and the napkins came out of a warming cabinet, because the caterer had warmed the bread and the napkins were folded around the bread, and a young woman in a black vest set one beside my plate and it was warm through the linen, warm and stacked, and I sat with my hands in my lap for a moment, and then I ate the bread. A young man called a banquet captain, twenty-five perhaps, badge that said BRANDON, refilled my wine and told the table, because someone asked, that the hotel dated to 1928 and was *one of the last of the great Mission Revival resorts, fully restored in the seventies*. I asked him, in a voice that I made pleasant, restored from what. He said, "Just, you know — time. Everything gets tired." He smiled when he said it. He had been born around 1977. The fire was not in his employee handbook and it was not on the corridor wall and it had no marker on the lawn, no plaque, nothing, nine names nowhere on the property, and I want to be careful here, because the easy sentence is that this is an outrage, and I am not certain it is an outrage. Lupe Ochoa did not work eleven years at the Mirador in order to become a plaque. The Schiller children came down to see the ocean and the ocean is still there, performing, every fifteen seconds, at the foot of the lawn. I no longer know what memory is for, is the difficulty. I have carried mine the way you carry a key ring after the locks have been changed.

The toasts ran long. Bill Brennan cried, which surprised no one; Patsy did not, which surprised no one either. At some point between the toasts and the cake I left the tent and walked the arcade of the south wing, the surviving wing, and at the end of it found what I knew I would find because hotels do not change their bones: a door marked STAFF ONLY, and beyond it, when a waiter came through with a tray and the door swung wide, the corridor, the rubber floor, the fluorescent light, and the smell. Clean laundry. Hot cotton and detergent, a smell that has ambushed me for thirty-one years in laundromats and department stores, and I stood in the doorway and a girl of about twenty in a hotel polo was at the far end pulling towels out of an industrial dryer and folding them fast, in thirds, stacking them deep on a wheeled cart, and the towels were steaming faintly in the corridor air. I went down the corridor. I had no right to be in the corridor and no one stopped me. I said, "Let them breathe." She looked up. I said, "Spread them out before you shelve them. Let them cool first. The kitchen ones especially." She looked at me, a woman of fifty-one in wedding clothes who had come through the wrong door, and she said, "Okay," in the flat agreeable tone the young use on the old, and I have no way of knowing whether she did it that night or any night after, and I have decided that this does not matter, that the sentence had waited thirty-one years to be said to someone and I had said it.

I went back out to the lawn. The band was playing something with horns and the bride was dancing with her father on a parquet floor laid over the grass, over the footprint, the third floor of air, and the votives were burning down in their glass cylinders, contained, certified, each small fire holding its permit. I took off my shoes. The grass was cold already with the night coming in off the water, and I stood at the edge of the light with my shoes in my hand, which is, as I have told you, the posture in which I survived, and Patsy came and stood next to me without saying anything, and we watched her daughter dance on the place, and after a while Patsy said, "It's just a place now," and I said, "Yes," and neither of us believed the other and both of us were grateful.

I stayed for the cake. I want that recorded too: I stayed for the cake, and I clapped when they cut it, and at a few minutes after midnight, as the last guests drifted toward the valet stand and the busboys began snuffing the votives between wet fingers, two by two down the long tables, the irrigation system came on across the dark north end of the lawn, automatic, timed, the heads rising out of the grass and turning, throwing their long arcs of water across the ground where the wing had stood, water falling and falling on that ground in the dark, thirty-one years late and absolutely on schedule, and I stood in my bare feet at the edge of it until my hem was soaked, and then I put my shoes on, and gave the boy my ticket, and drove north.
