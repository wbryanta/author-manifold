The car park was where the kitchen garden used to be. Nuala knew it the moment Deirdre swung the hired car between the white lines, because the mountain sat in the windscreen at exactly the angle it had sat when she used to come out the scullery door with a basin of peelings for the compost, and there was no other place on the grounds from which the mountain looked like that — hunched, patient, a grey animal asleep over the town. Everything else had changed. The hotel itself stood where it had always stood, but it was a different building wearing the old one's name, rendered and painted the colour of clotted cream, with a glass porch jutting out front like a conservatory, and a sign in brushed steel that said THE STRAND HOTEL EST. 1924, which was true the way a man's second wife might wear his first wife's ring.

"You're all right?" Deirdre said, not looking at her, pulling the handbrake.

"I'm grand."

"Because we can say you took a turn. Migraine. Anything you like. Katie would understand."

"Katie doesn't know to understand anything," Nuala said, "and she won't be finding out on her wedding day," and she got out of the car into the smell of the sea, which had not changed, salt and rot and something cold underneath, and stood for a moment in her good coat with her fascinator in her hand like a small dead bird, looking up at the windows of the third floor.

There had been no third floor, before. There had been a second floor and above it the attics, with their dormers like raised eyebrows, where the live-in staff slept two to a room under the slope of the roof, so close to the slates you could hear the gulls landing. Now there was a proper third storey with proper windows, sashes painted white, and behind one of them a girl in a black dress and white apron was drawing curtains back, letting in the light, and Nuala watched her do it with the particular efficient flick of the wrist that meant the girl had drawn ten thousand curtains and would draw ten thousand more, and felt something turn over in her chest, slow as a key in an old lock.

Thirty-five years. She had been nineteen. She did the sums sometimes in the night, the way you'd press a bruise.

Inside, the lobby was all pale wood and orchids and a fire of artificial coals burning in a glass box, and a young fella at reception with a county accent and an earring directed wedding guests towards the Atlantic Suite with a sweep of his arm. Nuala noted, without deciding to, the green running-man signs over two doors, the smoke detector above the reception desk, the second detector by the lift, the sprinkler heads sitting in the ceiling like steel daisies. She had read once that this was common among people like her, this counting, and that it never went away, and she had found the information neither comforting nor not. On the wall by the lift hung framed photographs of the hotel through the decades: a charabanc of excursionists in 1931, hats and parasols; American golfers in the fifties, grinning around cigarettes; a ballroom crowded for some dinner dance, 1974, the women's dresses long and shining. Then a photograph of the reopening, ribbon and scissors, a county councillor, 1994. Between 1974 and 1994 the wall offered nothing at all. Twenty years folded shut like a fan. She stood in front of the gap for a moment, then went on into the noise.

The Atlantic Suite was the old dining room, more or less — they had kept the proportions, or arrived back at them by accident, and the tall windows still gave onto the lawn and the dunes and the white scribble of the surf beyond — and it was full of people Nuala half knew, cousins and cousins' husbands and Katie's college friends in jewel colours, and Katie's young man's people up from Cork, all of them lit with that particular wedding brightness, perfume and steam-ironed shirts and laughter pitched a half-tone high. She found her seat for the ceremony, third row, family side. The chairs were gold-painted bamboo with white cushions. Where she was sitting, near enough exactly, had been table nine, which in her day was always given to the McInerney sisters from Limerick, two widows who came every July for a fortnight and tipped in coins counted out of a purse with a clasp like a beak, and who had both been dead now for decades, surely, of ordinary things, in their beds, with time to say what they wanted said.

Stop that, she told herself. And then the string quartet began something she didn't know, and Katie came up the aisle on her father's arm in a dress the colour of the inside of a shell, and Nuala stopped it.

She cried at the vows. Everyone did; it cost nothing and meant what it meant. Katie's voice went small and fierce on in sickness and in health, and her young man — Dara, an engineer, a long streak of decency with red ears — answered her like a man taking an oath in court, and outside the windows the tide went about its own business, and Nuala thought, as she had thought at every wedding and funeral and christening for thirty-five years, that Bridie Lyons would have loved this. Bridie loved a wedding above all things. On their afternoons off they used to walk into town and look at the dresses in Hartigan's window and Bridie would narrate her own wedding in instalments, the church, the flowers, the band, the going-away outfit, everything but the groom, who changed weekly and didn't matter; the wedding was the thing. She'd have been fifty-five now. She would have got stout, probably, like her mother, and dyed her hair an unconvincing auburn, and she'd be three rows back at some grandniece's wedding this very minute, crying into a paper tissue and rooting in her bag for another.

Bridie had got as far as the first-floor landing. That was where they found her. Nuala had not been told this so much as overheard it, in the weeks after, when she was staying at her aunt's in town with her hands bandaged and people in the kitchen talked in the low voices that travelled better than shouting. The first-floor landing, going up. Going back up. For her letters, was what some said — she had letters from a boy in Boston she kept under her mattress, tied with a hair ribbon — and others said no, she'd gone up to bang on Mrs. Devane's door, because the housekeeper was deaf as a post without her hearing aid in, and that was the kind of girl Bridie was, and the two stories were both true in the way that mattered, which was that Nuala had been holding her hand on the back stairs, in the dark and the heat with the smoke coming down the stairwell like dirty water filling a glass, and then she had not been holding it.

The applause brought her back. Katie and Dara were kissing, the whole room on its feet, and Nuala stood and clapped with her hands above her head like everyone else, and her face must have been doing the right things because Deirdre, beside her, only squeezed her elbow once and let it go.

The drinks reception was on the lawn, the day holding fine, prosecco and miniature things on slates, and Nuala did her duty, moving from group to group. She was good at weddings. She had a stock of questions and the patience to listen to the answers, and people came away from her feeling interesting, which was a kind of work she had learned, like everything else she knew, in service. You learned to be a mirror. You learned that nobody wants to know about you, the year you carried their breakfast trays, and that this is restful once you stop minding it. She talked to Dara's grandmother about hip replacements and to a bridesmaid about Lanzarote and to Katie's old boss about the price of houses, and the whole time some lower part of her was running its own inventory: the wind's off the sea, the staff door to the kitchen is propped open with a fire extinguisher, which is the same sin in 2024 as it was in 1989, and somebody should say something, and you will say nothing, because you are a guest.

It was at the meal that the fire came up. It was always going to. They were at table six — the round tables had numbers in little gilt frames, and she had laughed once, soundlessly, into her napkin at being assigned a table number in this room — and a man called Brendan, married to one of the Cork aunts, a big confident talker with a tan, leaned in over his beef and said, "Do ye know this place burned to the ground? The whole shebang. Eighty-nine or ninety. My brother-in-law was telling me. Three people killed in it, I believe. They'd to rebuild it from the foundations up."

"God," said the bridesmaid. "That's so sad. You'd never think it."

"Faulty wiring or a chip pan or some yoke. Different times. Sure there were no regulations then at all." Brendan shook his head with the comfortable sorrow of a man discussing a shipwreck three centuries old. "Three dead. Imagine."

"I heard that, all right," Nuala said, and asked the bridesmaid whether it was Lanzarote or Fuerteventura she'd said, and the table moved on, the way tables do, and under the cloth her hands lay in her lap, palms up, and she looked at the shine of the scar tissue across the right one, faded now to something you'd take for an ironing accident, and thought: a chip pan. The fire had started in the flue of the old solid-fuel range, which had needed sweeping since Easter and which Mr. Crowe, who owned the hotel and pitied himself for the expense of everything, had not had swept, and it had travelled inside the wall the way fire loves to do, secretly, upward, all evening while the guests ate their dinners and the staff scraped the plates, and it had come out into the roof space a little after four in the morning, into the dry hundred-year-old timbers above the rooms where the staff slept, and there had been no alarm up there, because staff quarters, in Mr. Crowe's economy, were not the hotel.

She had woken to a sound she still could not name. Not crackling. Bigger than that, and softer: a sort of breathing in the roof. And the smell, and Bridie's bare feet hitting the floorboards, and Bridie's voice, very calm, the calmest she ever heard her, saying get up, Nuala, get up now, and put on your shoes. Put on your shoes. She thought of that often — that Bridie, twenty years old, had thought of shoes. They went along the attic corridor knocking on doors and screaming, and the others spilled out, Annie and the two Mayo girls and Christy the night porter coming up from below shouting was anyone above, and then the lights went and the dark was total and hot and you went by your hands, and her hands knew the way; that was the thing nobody understood after, that her hands had saved her life, ten months of carrying trays and linen up and down those back stairs in all states of light and tiredness, her body knew every turn and tread of them, knew the third step from the half-landing was deeper than the rest, knew where the rail kinked. On the second floor she had hammered on the doors of guests, 24, 26, 27, and a man came out of 26 with a child asleep on his shoulder and a woman behind him with another, and the man was shouting which way, which way, and Nuala had taken the woman's wrist in one hand because the woman had frozen, simply stopped, the way a clock stops, and she had Bridie's hand in her other hand, and she had led them down into the smoke, and somewhere between the second floor and the first Bridie's hand had gone out of hers. Not pulled. Gone. Like a thing set down. And Nuala had brought the woman and the child to the bottom and out the scullery door into the cold wet grass of the kitchen garden — this car park, this exact rectangle of tarmac with its white lines — and turned around, and the doorway was orange, and Christy went in past her, and she went in after him as far as the smoke would let her, which was not far, and that was where she burned her hands, on the iron rail of the stairs that her hands knew, and then someone dragged her out by the collar of her nightdress and she sat in the cabbages with the woman from room 26, the two of them holding the child between them though the child had its own mother four feet away, and watched the roof where she slept fall into the room where she ate.

Three dead. Bridie Lyons. Christy Hannon, the night porter, who had gone back up twice and not come down the second time. And a Mr. Gleeson, a commercial traveller, sixty-one, in room 14, who it was thought had never woken at all, and that was held to be a mercy, and perhaps it was.

The beef was taken away and the pavlova came. The speeches were long and kind. Dara's best man told a story about a tent in Connemara that had the room roaring, and Katie's father — Nuala's brother-in-law, a quiet man — stood up and said that the first time Katie brought Dara home, Katie had done all the talking, and Dara had done all the washing-up, and he'd known then, and everyone laughed and cried a little, and Nuala laughed and cried a little, all of it real, all of it happening to her at one remove, like weather seen through good glass.

When the band was setting up, she slipped out.

She told Deirdre it was for air, and it half was. The corridor that led away from the function room towards the back of the hotel was carpeted in a pattern of fawn diamonds and lit with little brass sconces, and it ran where the old service corridor had run, but it had been civilised, opened up, hung with prints of currachs and bog cotton. She walked it slowly. Where the door to the stillroom had been there was a door that said HOUSEKEEPING, and it stood ajar, and out of it came the smell — and this stopped her dead in the fawn diamonds, one hand going to the wall — the smell of clean hotel linen, which is not the smell of clean linen anywhere else on earth, industrial laundry and starch and the faint scorch of the calender press, a smell she had not stood inside since she was nineteen, and for a moment she was nineteen, the whole costume of the intervening years gone off her like a coat handed across a counter: her wrists ached with carrying, her feet knew their cheap shoes, somewhere Mrs. Devane was calling that the twos and fours wanted doing before eleven.

"Are you lost?" A girl had come out of the linen room with a stack of towels to her chin. Early twenties, dark hair pinned, an accent from somewhere east — Lithuanian, maybe. Her name badge said GRETA. "The wedding is back this way."

"I know where the wedding is," Nuala said. "I was getting away from it for a minute."

Greta laughed, a quick true laugh she then tucked away as unprofessional. "It is loud," she conceded. "It will be louder. They have requested ABBA."

"They always do." Nuala nodded at the towels. "Turn-downs?"

"Eleven rooms." Greta shifted the stack. "The other girl went home sick, so." A shrug that contained the whole history of the trade: someone always went home sick, and the rooms did not care, and the rooms got done.

"The bridal suite as well?"

"Bridal suite first, before they come up. Petals." She said the word petals the way a soldier might say medals — with a soft contempt for the decorative arm of the service.

Nuala fell into step beside her without exactly intending to, and Greta let her, perhaps because guests did strange things at weddings and the wise course was to keep them in view. The bridal suite was on the second floor, at the end, sea-facing — and Nuala counted doors as they went and understood, with a sensation like a lift settling, that it stood where 26 had been, more or less, the room of the man with the sleeping child on his shoulder. The numbering was different now. Everything was different now. Greta carded the door open and went about her work, swift and exact, towels racked, lights set low, and she had the bed half-turned when Nuala, watching from the doorway, said, "You've the corner long."

Greta looked up.

"The turn-down. You've left the corner long; it'll slide off the bed when she sits on it." Nuala heard herself and was helpless to stop. "Fold it back to forty-five and tuck the near edge under six inches. It holds."

Greta looked at the bed, and then at the woman in the doorway in her wedding coat and her good shoes, with a small dead bird of feathers in her hand, and redid the corner the way she'd been told, and it held, and she stood back and considered it, and then considered Nuala. "You worked in a hotel," she said.

"I worked in this hotel," Nuala said, and it was the first time in thirty-five years she had said the sentence aloud, and the room did not fall down, and the sea outside the window kept up its long indifferent work, and Greta nodded slowly, the way you nod at the answer to a question you hadn't known you were carrying.

"Before the fire," Greta said. Not a question. So the staff knew. Of course the staff knew. The staff always know everything the framed photographs leave out; it travels down the years from hand to hand with the linen.

"During it," Nuala said.

Greta put the last pillow straight. Then she went to her trolley in the corridor and came back with two small bottles of water, the good kind from the minibar, and handed one to Nuala as though this were a recognised ceremony, and they stood by the window drinking water and looking at the dark coming up over the dunes, and Greta said there was a plaque, did she know, and Nuala said no, and Greta said it was below, by the staff entrance, the smokers knew it well, and that one of the old porters had it put there long ago and the management kept meaning to move it somewhere nicer.

It was nine o'clock and fully dark when Nuala found it, around the back where the bins were, a small square of bronze on the wall at shoulder height, gone green-brown at the edges, lit by the spill from the kitchen window. Below it on the step, a jam jar with the stubs of two candles in it, and she understood that the smokers did know it well, that girls like Greta stood here on their breaks year in and year out in the company of three names, and that this was, in its way, a better monument than any lobby could offer. She took off her glove and put her fingers to Bridie's name, the cold coming up out of the metal into her hand, her hand that had held and then not held, and she stood there long enough for her feet to ache in the good shoes, and she said nothing, because everything she had to say she had been saying for thirty-five years in the only language available, which was continuing.

Then she went back inside, through the corridor of fawn diamonds, towards the music. The band had got to ABBA, as foretold. Through the doors of the Atlantic Suite the light was gold and moving, and Katie was in the middle of the floor with her shoes off and her veil crooked, shining like the inside of a shell, and when she saw Nuala in the doorway she shouted Auntie Nuala over the heads of forty people and came and took her by both hands and pulled her in. And Nuala danced. She danced in the room that had been the dining room, on the spot that had been table nine, under sprinkler heads she had counted and a roof that had been swept, with the child of her sister's child laughing into her face, and through the tall windows she could see the lawn lights and beyond them nothing, the black breathing of the sea, and she thought: a building is only a building, it remembers nothing; the remembering is done elsewhere, in the hands. And her hands held Katie's, and held them, and did not let go until the song was finished and another one began.
