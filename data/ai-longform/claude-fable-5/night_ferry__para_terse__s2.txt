The call had come on a Tuesday, and by Thursday evening he was standing in the queue of foot passengers at the Aberdeen terminal, watching the freight lorries roll up the ramp into the ferry's lit mouth, and it seemed to him that the boat was eating them, one after another, patiently, the way the sea ate everything in the end. The constable on the phone had been careful with her words. A body had been recovered from the banks below Levenwick, she said, and there was reason to believe it might be his brother, who had been reported missing from his lodgings in Lerwick five weeks before, and would he be willing to travel north to assist with identification. She said *assist with identification* the way you'd say *help with the dishes*, something small and shared, and he had said yes before he understood what he was agreeing to, and then had stood in his kitchen in Stonehaven with the kettle roaring and not the faintest idea what to do with his hands.

He had not spoken to Alec in nine years. He had not known, until the constable told him, that Alec had been living in a shared house off the Hillhead with two Latvian men who worked the salmon cages, that he had been doing day labour on the pier and casual weeks on a whitefish boat when they'd have him, that the last anyone saw of him he was walking south out of the town on the dark road past the Knab with his hood up against the rain. Five weeks. The constable had not said *drinking* but the word stood behind everything she did say, the way their father had stood behind doors in the old house, a darkness you learned to read by its outline.

The ferry was called the *Hjaltland* and he had sailed on her before, or on her sister, years ago, in the other direction, eighteen years old with a duffel bag and the absolute certainty of a boy who believes that a place can be left. He found the reclining lounge, claimed a seat by the window with his jacket, and then could not sit. The boat shuddered as the bow thrusters worked her off the quay, and Aberdeen began to slide past the glass, the harbour lights, the grey granite of the town going violet in the dusk, the supply vessels at their berths lit up like small refineries. He went out on deck and stood at the rail while they came through the harbour mouth, past the breakwater where a man with a dog stood watching, and the dog barked twice at the ship, at all that moving steel, and then they were out into the open firth and the first long swell came under the hull like a thought arriving.

It was twelve hours to Lerwick. He had booked a cabin and regretted it already, the idea of lying down in a small white room with the engine humming through the mattress, alone with what was waiting at the other end. He went to the bar instead. The bar on the overnight boat is its own country, he had forgotten that. Two oilmen in branded fleeces playing cards with the joyless efficiency of men who have played ten thousand hands together. A young couple sharing earbuds, her head on his shoulder, his eyes closed. A table of lorry drivers, and the loudest of them, a heavy man with a beard going white in stripes, was telling a story about a crossing in January when the boat had hove to off Fair Isle for six hours and the lounge had been, he said, like the inside of a washing machine full of pensioners. The men laughed. He took his pint to a corner table and sat where he could see the black windows, which showed him nothing but the bar itself, reversed, and his own face hanging in it like something recovered from water.

That was the kind of thought he was going to have to manage, he understood. For two days now his mind had been doing this, turning every ordinary thing into rehearsal. The salmon fillet in the supermarket chiller. His neighbour's garden hose lying swollen and pale across the path. He drank the pint slowly and made himself think about Alec alive instead, which was harder, because the living Alec had been put away in a sealed place years ago and the seal had been part of how he himself had gone on living. Alec at fifteen, brown as a nut, standing on the thwart of their father's boat with a saithe flapping on the line and that grin on him, the grin that said the world was a thing that happened to other people. Alec was two years older and had been better at everything that mattered on the isle, the fishing, the football, the talking to lasses at the hall, and had been worse at everything that mattered after, and somewhere in their twenties the two columns had quietly traded places, and neither of them had ever forgiven the arithmetic.

The bearded lorry driver came past on his way from the bar and paused, the way certain men cannot help pausing at a man alone. Heading up for work, aye? he said. Family, he answered, and heard how the word came out of him, flat as a coin on a counter, and the driver, who had asked a thousand strangers a thousand idle questions and knew the sound of a wrong one, only nodded and said, Lang night, this run, you want to get your head down early, and moved on, kind in the way of men who know when to leave, and he was grateful to him, absurdly, almost to the edge of tears, which told him what kind of night it was going to be.

He went out on deck again around eleven. They were well offshore by then, the land a low smudge of light far to the west, Peterhead maybe, or nothing, and the wind had got up so that the deck doors needed both hands. The sea was running from the northeast in long ridges that the bow took with a deep, patient motion, lift and shoulder and fall, and the spray came back along the side decks in irregular bursts like handfuls of gravel thrown by someone unseen. A fulmar hung off the stern light for a while, stiff-winged, riding the ship's air without a single beat, and then banked away into the dark as if it had satisfied itself about something. He stood in the lee of the funnel housing with his hood up and let the cold do its work.

Their father had drowned in 1987, off the Mousa shore, in weather not much worse than this, hauling creels alone because his sons were thirteen and eleven and at the school, and because he was the kind of man who would not wait for anyone. They had found the boat the next day, going round in slow circles with the engine still running, which was the detail that everyone on the isle repeated for years, the engine still running, as if the horror lived there and not in the empty oilskin jacket fouled in the hauler. They never found him. He had grown up in a house where the sea had taken a man and given nothing back, not even a body to put in the kirkyard, and their mother had set a stone there anyway with their father's name on it and the words *Lost at Sea*, and underneath the stone there was only Shetland, only the ordinary ground. Maybe that was the start of it, he thought, the wedge driven in. Alec had gone straight to the boats at sixteen as if to spite the water, or to court it, and he himself had gone to the mainland and got a trade and a mortgage and a wife for eleven years and a job estimating quantities for a construction firm, a life built deliberately out of materials the sea could not reach, and look, he thought, look where the boat is taking you anyway.

He slept in the end, or something like it, in the cabin with his clothes on, on top of the covers, the engine coming up through the bunk in a steady churn, and the ship's long corkscrewing motion working at the edges of his sleep so that he dreamed in tilted rooms. In one of the dreams Alec was at the door of the cabin with his hood up and water running off him onto the carpet, not frightening, just standing there the way he used to stand in the door of the bedroom they'd shared, waiting to be noticed, and he said, in the dream, with mild reproach, You didna listen to it, and he woke with the words still in the room and the grey light of five in the morning coming round the curtain.

He knew what the dream meant. There was a voicemail on his phone, seven months old. He had seen Alec's name come up on the screen on a Sunday evening in March, an Aberdeenshire number he didn't recognise but the name attached from some ancient sync, ALEC, in capitals the way he'd entered it fifteen years before, and he had stood looking at it ringing in his hand and had not answered, and the phone had pinged a minute later with the little tape-spool icon, and he had not listened to it, not that night and not since. At first out of anger, the old reliable anger, the last conversation they'd ever had still hot in him after all the years, Alec at their mother's funeral lunch with six pints in him telling the table that Davie there had her in a home before the doctors had finished talking, Davie there couldna wait, and the things he had said back, in the car park of the Sumburgh Hotel, with the wind off the runway flattening the words against both their faces. And then later out of something worse than anger, a kind of superstition, because as long as the message sat unheard it was a door not yet closed, a hand not yet refused, and a man can live a long time on the maintenance of one unclosed door. The phone was in his jacket on the hook. He lay in the bunk and did not reach for it, and the ship ploughed north, and after a while he heard the note of the engine change.

Up on deck the morning was the colour of pewter and the land was there. Sumburgh Head first, away to port, the lighthouse pale on its cliff, and then the long low backs of the south mainland sliding by, Levenwick somewhere in that grey coast, he thought, somewhere along there is the beach, and he made himself look at it, the dark banks, the white water working at the foot of them, ordinary and terrible. Gulls came out to the ship. The couple from the bar were at the rail taking photographs of Mousa as they passed it, the broch standing on the island like a stone chimney left after a fire, and he could have told them things about that sound, that water, but he stood apart and watched Bressay rise ahead and the town open up behind its breakwater, the way it always had, grey roofs stacked up from the harbour, the kirk spire, the new white fish market, everything smaller than memory and more solid, and the boat came in past the Knab where his brother had last been seen walking, and tied up at Holmsgarth at half past seven in the morning.

The constable met him in the terminal. She was younger than her voice, fair-haired, with the unhurried courtesy that island police learn because they will meet everyone again. She drove him up to the Gilbert Bain in a marked car and talked, on the way, about the weather they'd had, three weeks of southeasterly gales, the Bressay ferry off for two days, as if he were a relation come for a wedding, and he understood that this too was a kindness, a ramp built of small words so that he would not have to step off any edges. At the hospital there was tea he didn't drink and a form and a quiet man called Mr Henderson from the procurator fiscal's office, and the constable explained, with her hands flat on the table, what he would see and what he would not. The remains had been in the water some weeks, she said. They would not be showing him the face. Identification in such cases rested on other things, and there would be DNA in any case, a swab from his cheek before he left, but the family viewing mattered, she said, to the family most of all, and he could take whatever time he needed, and he could stop at any time, and somewhere in the middle of her saying all this he found that he had stopped hearing the words and was listening only to the tone, the way you listen to weather on a radio in another room.

The room was small and cold and smelled of nothing, which was its own kind of smell. The body lay under a purple cover on a steel table and a technician stood at the head of it, and at a nod from the constable she folded the cover back from one side, carefully, the way you'd turn down a bed, exposing a forearm and a hand laid on a white cloth. He had braced for the wrong things. He had braced for horror and instead there was strangeness, the skin a colour that skin is not, the flesh swollen and slack on the bones, and yet it was a hand, unmistakably, a big-knuckled working man's hand with the nails broken short, and on the forearm above the wrist, blurred and greened by years and water but legible, a tattoo of a swallow with a banner under it, and in the banner a word, and he leaned in, and the word was MAM.

He had not known about the tattoo. That was the first thing, standing in that cold room, the understanding arriving like a slap that there had been years of Alec, whole years, a parlour somewhere, an evening, a decision, *Mam* in blue under a swallow, and he had known none of it, and the not-knowing was a country whose size he was only now being shown. Sailors got swallows for crossings, their father had told them that, one bird for every five thousand miles, and the other thing about swallows, the old thing, that if a sailor drowned the swallow carried his soul up out of the water. Alec would have known that. Alec forgot nothing the old man ever said.

It's the hand I'd know him by, he heard himself say, and the constable said quietly, Take your time, and he looked at the hand. The little finger. He was looking for the little finger and there it was, crooked, set at its angle, the last joint skewed outward the way it had been for thirty-odd years, since the night in the byre when they were fighting over something, the loan of a bike, a lass, nothing, the actual cause gone forever, and he had hauled the byre door shut in fury with Alec's hand on the jamb, and the crack of it, and the silence after the crack before Alec began to roar, and how their mother had driven them to the surgery in Levenwick with Alec's hand wrapped in a tea towel and Alec, white-faced, already turning it into a story, already grinning at him over the seat, you'll pey for that, Davie, you'll be peying for that the rest o your days. The finger had set crooked because Alec wouldn't keep the splint on, and it had become a fixture, a prop, Alec raising the pint with the little finger sticking out like a duchess, waggling it at him across the table, exhibit A, the rest o your days.

That's him, he said. That's my brother. And the room did whatever rooms do at such moments, which is nothing at all, the technician drew the cover gently back over the hand, the constable touched his elbow, Mr Henderson made a small note, and they took him out to the corridor where the light was ordinary and a porter went by pushing a trolley of lunches, and somebody, somewhere in the building, was laughing at something, and the world carried on with its perfect indifference, which he found he did not resent. They swabbed his cheek. He signed where he was shown. The constable said the words *formal confirmation* and *the fiscal* and *in touch within the fortnight*, and asked was there anyone in the town he wanted to see, and he said no, and then, surprising himself, asked if she could tell him exactly where on the beach, and she told him, and he walked out of the hospital into the wind.

The return boat didn't sail until seven that evening, so he had the day, and he spent it the only way it could be spent, walking. Down the Hillhead past the close where Alec had lodged, where a man in a Crocs and a dressing gown was smoking in a doorway and did not look at him. Along Commercial Street, the flagstones, the narrow lanes running up between the buildings like vents. Out along the south road past the Knab, the golf course, the cemetery in its walled slope above the sea, and he stood a while at the gate but did not go in to look for the stone with their parents' names and the lie about their father resting anywhere. The wind came over the wall full of salt. He thought about taking the bus down to Levenwick, to stand on the actual beach, and knew it for a bad idea, a scab he'd be picking the rest of his life, and didn't, and counted that, in a day of no victories, as one.

What he thought about, walking, was the hand. Not the colour of it, not the water's work, but the finger, and the byre door, and somewhere out by the Sletts with the spray coming over the road a doubt arrived in him, small at first, then not small. The byre door hinged on the left as you faced it. He had pulled it with his right hand, he could feel the pull still in his shoulder, and Alec had been inside coming out, so his hand on the jamb would have been, would have been which, and he stood on the sea road working it like a man doing sums on his fingers, turning his own body in the wind to play both parts. The hand on the steel table had been the right hand, he was nearly sure, the cover folded back from the body's right. Or had they stood him on the other side. He could not bring the room back. He had been in it ten minutes ago in the scale of his life and already it was silting over, the way everything did, the way Alec's face had, their mother's voice, the exact words said in the car park at Sumburgh, and what was left was a crooked finger and a blue swallow and the word MAM and his own voice saying that's my brother, and the swab would settle it anyway, he told himself, the science would settle it within the fortnight, and he stood on the road and understood that this was no comfort at all, because what he wanted settled the science could not reach. Whether it was Alec on that table. Whether it had been Alec for years, anywhere, the brother and not just the body, or whether the man he'd grown up with had gone into the water in 1987 with their father, and everything after had been two boys haunting each other up and down the length of a country.

The boat sailed at seven into a falling dusk and a sea gone strangely quiet, long and oily and grey, the gales blown out at last. He stood at the stern rail as Lerwick dropped away, the town's lights coming on in clusters, Bressay light beginning its slow turn, and the gulls followed the ship out past the Knab and then gave up on her one by one. When the land was a shadow and the wake ran back toward it like a road being unbuilt, he took the phone out of his jacket. The little spool icon, seven months patient. He pressed it and held the phone hard against his ear because of the wind.

A breath first. Pub noise behind, or telly, then it cut off, a door closed. Aye, Davie. It's me. Then nothing for so long he thought the message was done, just the hiss of a room far away in March, a man standing in it deciding. I was minding the day at the byre the day, Alec's voice said, and there was a sound that might have been a laugh. Dinna ken why. The finger's giein me bother in the cauld and I thought o you. Another pause, shorter. There's a thing I've been wanting to say to you, but no on this. No on a machine. I'll phone you again, eh. And then, almost lost in the moving of the phone away from his mouth, two words, Cheers, boy, and the click, and the woman's voice offering to save or delete, and the sea, and the engine, and his brother's soul, if the old ones were right, gone up out of the water on blue wings with their mother's name beneath it, or not, or walking some mainland road this minute with his hood up, the door still open the width of one unmade phone call. He pressed save. He pressed it again to be sure. The wake unrolled behind the ship, white and then grey and then nothing, all the way back to the islands, and he stood at the rail in the dark and listened to the message twice more, and then a third time, learning it, the way you learn a thing you will be keeping, for the rest, as the man said, of your days.
