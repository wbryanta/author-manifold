Every Sunday evening at seven o'clock, Emmett Doyle called Dubuque. He was as regular as the test tone. The lamp would light at my position on the long-distance board — I worked Sundays, four to midnight, and his line came up at seven so reliably that the other girls started calling him my gentleman — and I would plug in and say, "Long distance," and he would say, in a voice like gravel raked smooth, "Evening, miss. Person-to-person, please, Mrs. Alma Pruitt, Dubuque, Iowa," and then the number, which I knew by heart before the summer was out but always let him recite anyway, because there is a courtesy in letting a man finish what he has prepared to say.

This was 1968, in Arnett, Kansas, in the toll room above the Southwestern Bell business office on Commerce Street. There were six positions and on Sunday nights only two of us worked them, me and usually Patsy Greer, under a ceiling fan that pushed the heat around without removing any of it. The board was tall and black and full of small brass-lipped jacks, and the cords hung in pairs like the keys of some instrument played vertically. You learned to read it the way a farmer reads sky. A lamp meant a voice waiting. You took the back cord, you challenged the line, you wrote your ticket — the time, the points, the class of call — and you built the connection city by city, operator to operator, your voice traveling down the wire ahead of the customer's like a girl walking through a dark house turning on lights. For a person-to-person call you stayed on until the named party answered, because the charges didn't begin until they did. That was the rule, and the rule was also that the moment the called party said yes, this is she, you withdrew. You pulled your key back and you became wire again.

I was twenty-three. I want to say that I was good at withdrawing. I was, in the main. You hear everything in that job and you train yourself to hear nothing — the men calling their bookies in Kansas City, the woman who phoned a Wichita rectory every Friday and wept before anyone answered, the farm wives placing collect calls to sons who would not accept the charges. It all went through your hands and you let it go through, the way water goes through a culvert and the culvert keeps none of it. But the rule allowed you a few seconds. You had to verify the connection, confirm the party, make sure the circuit held. A few licit seconds, every operator knows them, the little vestibule of the call before you shut the door. And it was in those seconds, Sunday after Sunday, that I came to know Emmett Doyle and Alma Pruitt, or to believe I did, which in this life is frequently the same thing.

The first time, in the autumn of 1967, I noticed only his voice, because it was a good voice, low and deliberate, with that politeness older men had then, the kind that was not deference but a sort of formal kindness, hat-in-hand even over the telephone. I rang through to the Dubuque inward operator, and she rang the number, and a woman answered on the second ring as though she had been sitting with her hand on the receiver. I said, "I have a person-to-person call for Mrs. Alma Pruitt from Mr. Emmett Doyle in Arnett, Kansas," and she said, "This is she," in a voice that had a little husk in it, a screen-door creak, and then, before I had quite gotten off the line, she said, "Hello, old man," and he said, "Hello yourself," and there was in those four words such a worn, sweet groove, such evidence of repetition, that I sat there a half-second too long with my key open before I caught myself and withdrew.

After that I listened for them. I am not proud of it and I have never told anyone, not Patsy, not my mother, not my husband in all the years since. It was a small theft, a few seconds of hellos, and I told myself the company's own rules gave them to me. Hello, old man. Hello yourself. Some weeks it was Did you get my letter, and him saying It came Thursday, I've read it twice. Some weeks she started with the weather, the way Midwestern people do when the weather is standing in for everything else: We had frost. We had wind all week, I thought the elm would come down. Once, in March, she answered and said nothing at all for a moment, and he said, gently, "Alma," just her name, like a man setting a cup down very carefully, and I pulled my key back so fast the spring sang, because there are vestibules and there are rooms, and I knew the difference.

I made up stories about them, of course. Everyone on a switchboard becomes a novelist of two-minute means. He was Doyle and she was Pruitt, so they were not married, or were not married any longer, or had each been married to someone else, the someones now dead or gone. She was Mrs., he never said Missus when he asked for her, only the name, formal and entire, Mrs. Alma Pruitt, like an inscription. I decided they had been sweethearts before the first war and life had carried them four hundred miles apart and they had each done the long middle of it — the marriages, the children, the mortgages — with this one wire left between them, used once a week, on Sunday, when the rates were down and the house was quiet. Then I decided no, they were brother- and sister-in-law, joined by a dead spouse, talking the way you talk to the last person who remembers. Then once, in the spring, I heard her say, "Raymond would have been forty-four Tuesday," and him say, "I know it," and nothing else before I got off, and after that I stopped deciding anything. They had a Raymond between them, whoever he had been, and they had each other's Sundays, and that was as much as I was entitled to, and more.

You should understand what that year was, on a long-distance board. People think of 1968 and they think of the pictures, the smoke, the hotel kitchen, but I think of lamps. The night in April when Dr. King was shot, the board lit up like a field of fireflies and stayed lit until three in the morning, everyone in Arnett suddenly needing Memphis, Chicago, a daughter in St. Louis, anyone, the whole country reaching for itself through girls like me in towns like mine. In June it happened again. Grief travels by wire; I have felt it pass through my hands. And every few weeks my own mother sat by our phone on Maple Street waiting on word from my brother Danny, who was at Cu Chi with the 25th, and once a ham radio operator in Hawaii patched him through, and my mother, God love her, could not master saying "over," so she and Danny kept talking on top of each other across nine thousand miles, both of them laughing and neither hearing the other laugh, and the ham's patient voice in between saying, ma'am, you have to say over, over. I worked the board the next night and connected other people's sons to other people's mothers and was glad of the headset because nobody can see your face in a headset.

Against all that, Emmett Doyle at seven o'clock on Sunday was like the angelus. The world could be on fire and his lamp would light and his gravel voice would say Evening, miss, and for the length of one ticket the year stood still. He had a cough that winter that worried me — I heard it while the Dubuque circuit was building, the little smothered bark of a man covering the mouthpiece — and once Alma was sick in turn, in May, and answered so faintly that I asked her twice to confirm she was the party, and that call ran short; the supervision lamp came up after only six minutes, when their calls ordinarily ran twelve or fifteen, long enough that I would picture him watching the clock, weighing the overtime charges against her voice and the voice winning, minute by minute, as it always did. He asked for time and charges only once that I remember, in February, and when I quoted him the figure he was quiet a moment and then said, "Worth every nickel, miss," and I said, "Yes, sir," which was not in the operator's manual, and he laughed, a short, surprised sound, and hung up.

In October she told him the asters had come up wild along her back fence, volunteers from the year before, and he said he'd put that in his pocket and carry it around all week. I remember that because of what came after, the way you remember the last ordinary day before a storm by the smallness of its details. The asters, his pocket. I withdrew from the line. I wrote the ticket. The fan turned. Patsy at the next position was untangling a collect call to Amarillo. Outside, Commerce Street was getting dark earlier every week.

The next Sunday he didn't call. Seven o'clock came up on the chime clock above the board and went by, and I told myself the clock was fast, and then that he was at supper, and then that he had a cold, the cough, men get stubborn about a cough and won't talk on the phone where it can be heard. Seven-thirty. I handled a station-to-station to Topeka and a collect from a sailor in San Diego whose father accepted the charges before the boy's name was all the way out of my mouth. Eight. I found I was watching the board the way you watch a door. At eleven I told Patsy I had a headache, which was true by then, and at midnight I walked home under the bare elms telling myself a man is allowed to miss a Sunday, even a man like that, even a Sunday like theirs.

The Sunday after, at twelve minutes past seven, an inward call came up from Dubuque. The Iowa operator's voice, bright and procedural: person-to-person for Mr. Emmett Doyle, and the Arnett number, and I felt something go down through me like a dropped plumb line, because in more than a year Alma Pruitt had never once placed the call. It was his to place. That was the shape of them, whatever they were; he reached and she answered. I rang the Doyle number. I listened to it ring. You can tell a great deal from ringing, or you believe you can after enough years on a board — there are rings that are about to be answered, you'd swear the line itself tightens — and this was not one of them. It rang into a room. I could hear the room in it. Six rings, eight, ten. The Dubuque operator said, "No answer, shall I —" and then Alma's voice came over her, not waiting for the protocol, "Operator. Operator, will you try again at eight, please. He may be — try again at eight." And the Dubuque girl said certainly, and we took it down, and at eight I rang it again, twelve rings, fourteen, and the room at the other end held still and let it ring, and at last Alma said, very quietly, "Thank you. Cancel the call, please," and the circuit came down, and there was no charge, because the party had not been reached, and I sat there with the dead cord in my hand.

I was raised not to pry. But Arnett is a town of four thousand people and I had a name and the name had been in my hands every Sunday for a year. Monday I walked to the library on my way to shift and asked Mrs. Tillman for the week's Couriers, and I found it on the second-to-last page of Thursday's paper, between the grange notices and a sale on storm windows. Emmett J. Doyle, 67, of 311 Cherry Street, died at his home Tuesday, October 29. He had been a linotype operator at the Arnett Courier for thirty-nine years — and I thought, of course, of course, a man who had spent his working life setting other people's words in hot lead, line by line, no wonder he placed his calls like inscriptions. Retired 1962. Preceded in death by his wife, Norma, in 1959, and a son, Raymond, lost at sea in 1944. Survived by a sister in Pueblo, Colorado. Services had already been held. Burial in Fairview Cemetery. The whole of him fit in two column inches, the kind of measure he himself would have set a thousand times, and I stood at the library table and understood that whoever Alma Pruitt was, the paper had not known to count her among the survived-by, and that no one had told her, because the people who notify are the people in the obituary, and she was not in it.

I walked past the house on Cherry Street that week, once, on my afternoon off, not meaning anything by it, the way you visit a grave of someone you never met. A square white house with a deep porch and a glider on the porch and the storm windows not yet up, and in the side yard, along the fence, a stand of asters gone shaggy and purple in the cold, volunteers, I would imagine, from some year before. I did not stop walking. There was nothing there that belonged to me. But I will tell you that I went into my shift that Sunday knowing what I would do if the lamp came up from Dubuque, and knowing equally that it was against every regulation I had ever been taught, and not caring, which was new for me, that not caring; I have wondered since whether that was the actual end of my girlhood, more than my wedding or any of the rest of it — the night I decided in advance to break a rule for the sake of a stranger's voice.

It came up at seven-twenty. The Dubuque operator, the same bright procedure, person-to-person for Mr. Emmett Doyle. I said, "One moment, please," and I rang the number once, for form, for the recording angel of the company, and then I opened my key and said, "Operator to the calling party. Ma'am, are you there?"

"I'm here," Alma said.

"Ma'am." I had practiced it and the practice deserted me entire. "Ma'am, I'm the operator in Arnett. I — I'm not supposed to say anything to you beyond the handling of the call. But I've put your calls through, most Sundays, going back more than a year now, and I —" The Dubuque girl, bless her, had gone silent as snow; she could have cut me off and didn't. "Ma'am, Mr. Doyle passed away. The Tuesday before last. At his home. It was in the paper here. I'm sorry. I didn't want you to go on ringing that house."

The line did what long lines do, hummed faintly, carried the four hundred miles of itself. I counted the seconds the way you count after lightning. Then Alma said, "Thank you, operator," in a voice that had been set down very carefully, a cup on a saucer, and I thought she would release the line, and she didn't. She said, "You're the Sunday girl. The young one."

"Yes, ma'am."

"He remarked on you once. Said you had a kind voice for a telephone girl. Said you let him say the whole number even though you knew it." A sound that was nearly a laugh and was not one. "He noticed things like that. Thirty-nine years reading type backwards, he said you notice everything or you're no use at all." Another silence, and then, as if it were a piece of business that had to be settled before grief could be permitted into the room: "Was it quick? Did the paper say?"

"At his home," I said. "That's all it said. I'd like to think he was sitting down."

"He would have been," she said. "He sat down to do everything. He sat down to argue." And then her voice went somewhere else, not away from me but past me, down the wire toward Kansas. "Sixty years I knew that man. Sixty-one in March. I was at his wedding and he was at mine and we buried — we buried what we buried, the both of us, and these last years it was just the Sundays. People thought it was foolish, two old parties paying long-distance rates to talk about asters. My daughter thinks I don't know she thinks it." A pause. "What's your name, child? Not your number. Your name."

It was against the rules to give it. I had broken better rules already that night. "Doris," I said. "Doris Kessler."

"Doris," she said, the way you press a flower flat. "Doris, will you tell me there's no charge for this. He'd haunt me over it. A person-to-person where the person isn't reached."

"No charge, ma'am," I said, and my voice did something at the end of it that I could not help. "The party wasn't reached. There's never a charge for that."

"No," she said. "No, I suppose there never is." And she thanked me, and thanked the Dubuque operator too, by which I understood she had known all along that we were both there in the vestibule, had always known, all those Sundays, that their hellos passed through other women's hands — and she released the line, and the lamps went dark, and I wrote no ticket, because there had been no call, only three women on a circuit and a house in Arnett where a telephone sat in an empty room with its ringing finally done.

She called once more. A few weeks before Christmas, a Sunday, near seven, and the Dubuque inward girl said, half-apologizing, "I've got an odd one — party asking for the Sunday operator in Arnett, the one named Doris," and I opened my key and said, "This is Doris," and Alma said, "I won't keep you. I know you've your board." A silence with snow in it, four hundred miles of December. "Sundays got long, is all. I had the number written on the pad by the phone, his number, all these years in his own hand, and I find I can't make myself scratch it out, and I can't sit and look at it either, so I thought I'd put a different voice on the evening. That's all. That's the whole of my business, operator."

"You can call any Sunday you like, ma'am," I said, knowing the company would have my hide, knowing I'd say it again.

"No," she said, kindly, the way you decline a second helping in a house where they can't spare it. "No, a thing like this you do once. But I wanted to wish somebody a merry Christmas, and yours was the only number I had." We said our merry Christmases. She released the line. She never called again, and I never learned what became of her, and I have thought about her every December since, which is a kind of call too, I suppose, placed person-to-person and never answered and never charged.

Danny came home in the summer of '69, thinner, quieter, alive, and learned to say "over" to my mother across the kitchen table as a joke until she swatted him. I married Gene Kessler's cousin Walt in 1970 and kept my name on the payroll until 1974, when direct distance dialing finally reached the Arnett exchange and they tore the board out of the room above Commerce Street. I stood in the doorway while the men unbolted it. All those jacks, all those small dark mouths that had held the voices of the town, going onto a truck. Nowadays you dial the number yourself, and the call goes through nobody, through glass and air, and there is no girl in the middle with a key she might hold open one half-second too long. People call this progress and I expect it is. But I was the place where two voices met, once a week, for a year and some Sundays, and I am the last person living who heard them say hello to each other, and when I am gone the hellos go too. Hello, old man. Hello yourself. I withdrew from the line every time, the way I was trained to. I am withdrawing from it still. It takes longer than they tell you.
