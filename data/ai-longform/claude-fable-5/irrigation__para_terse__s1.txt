The leak announced itself the way bad news always did on the place, which was quietly, and in the early morning, and in a language you had to already be fluent in to understand. Wade saw it from the kitchen window while the coffee was still working its way down through the filter: a green streak in the dry grass along the edge of block three, a softness in the ground where there should not have been softness, not in June, not with the ditch allocation cut to sixty percent and every gallon spoken for twice over. He stood with his cup and looked at it for a long time, the way his father used to stand and look at things, and then he called his brother, who was asleep in the back bedroom that had been his bedroom forty years ago, and told him to put on boots.

Gary came out into the yard squinting, holding a piece of toast, wearing jeans that had never seen dirt and a pair of their father's irrigation boots that he had found in the mudroom. The boots were too big for him. They had always been too big for both of them, and the old man had been dead two years, and still neither one of them had thrown them out or grown into them.

"Mainline?" Gary said.

"Mainline."

"How bad?"

"Won't know till we dig."

They took the flatbed down the row with the shovels rattling in the back and a milk crate of fittings Wade had accumulated over thirty years of fixing this same system, couplers and primer and the blue glue, hacksaw, the four-foot length of schedule 40 he kept behind the seat the way other men kept jumper cables. The morning was already warm. The Cascades held a thin late snow on the high ridges, and below them the valley ran brown and gold down to the river, and the orchard sat in the middle of it like a green argument nobody had won yet. Eighteen acres of apples, Galas and Honeycrisp and a block of old Red Delicious that hadn't paid for its own picking in a decade, and a windbreak of Lombardy poplars their grandfather had planted, half of them dead now, standing along the north line like burnt matches.

The wet spot was worse up close. Water had been moving under the ground long enough to make a small marsh of the row middle, and the grass there was obscenely green, fat and glossy, and a killdeer ran away from them through it dragging one wing in its old theatrical lie. Wade cut a square of sod with the shovel and lifted it away and the hole filled immediately with cloudy water.

"Shut the pump off before we left?" Gary asked.

"Shut it off last night when the pressure dropped. This is what's still in the line plus what's in the ground."

"So it's been going a while."

"It's been going a while."

They dug. There is no conversation in the first twenty minutes of digging a hole in June; there is only the digging, and the sound of the shovels, and the sucking noise the mud makes when it decides to keep what you've taken from it and you have to take it again. Wade worked steadily, without hurry, dropping each load on the uphill side so it wouldn't run back in. Gary went at it the way he went at everything, hard and fast and then standing up to breathe with his hands on his hips, hard and fast and then standing up again. By the time they found the pipe, two feet down, both of them had sweated through their shirts and the sun had cleared the eastern hills and laid itself flat across the valley like a hand on a stove.

The pipe was the old eight-inch PVC their father had put in during the conversion, back when everybody was tearing out the hand lines and the wheel lines and going to micro-sprinklers, back when the future was a thing you borrowed money to get to faster. There was a crack running lengthwise along the top of it, maybe ten inches, the edges of it stained orange with thirty years of iron in the water, and at the center of the crack a hole the size of a thumb where the pressure had finally pushed through.

"There's your culprit," Gary said.

"Root, probably. Or it just got tired." Wade knelt in the mud and ran his finger along the crack. "Pipe gets tired same as anything."

"You're going to tell me this pipe is a metaphor, aren't you."

"I'm going to tell you to hand me the hacksaw."

They bailed the hole with a coffee can until the water stopped rising, and Wade measured and marked the pipe with a carpenter's pencil that wouldn't hold a line on the wet plastic, so he scored it with the saw instead, two cuts a couple feet apart to take out the whole cracked section, because patching a longitudinal crack was a fool's errand, his father's phrase, a fool's errand, and the orchard was already someone's errand and they had spent the last three days arguing about whose, and what kind of fool.

It had started Thursday night, at the kitchen table, with the folder Gary had brought up from Boise. Gary did commercial appraisal down there, had done well at it, drove a truck that had never hauled anything, and he had a client, or a friend of a client, an outfit out of Seattle that was assembling parcels along the river road, and the number in the folder was a number that did not have anything to do with apples. It had to do with view corridors and septic feasibility and what people would pay to look at the mountains from a deck. Gary had slid the folder across the table the way you'd slide a confession across to a man you were sure was guilty, gently, almost kindly, and Wade had read the first page and got up and done the dishes and not said anything at all, which in their family was the loudest available register.

Now Gary held the cut section of pipe up out of the hole and looked through it like a telescope. "You know what Mom's place runs a month," he said. "Aspen Court. You've seen the statements."

"I've seen them."

"Seventy-one hundred. Going to seventy-six in September, they sent a letter. And she's healthy, Wade. Her heart's fine. Her hips are fine. Dr. Patel said she could go ten years."

"Good."

"It is good. It's also nine hundred thousand dollars, give or take, that has to come from somewhere, and it isn't coming out of apples, because apples haven't put a dime in the bank since—when. Tell me the last year this place cleared real money."

Wade fitted the dry coupler over the end of the new piece of pipe, checking the slide of it, not gluing yet. "Sixteen," he said. "Sixteen was decent."

"Sixteen. Nine years ago. And since then?"

"Since then we've had two freezes, the hail year, the trade thing with China, and the warehouse paying sixty cents a pound for fruit that costs us seventy to grow. You know all this. You read the same papers I do."

"That's my whole point. I'm not telling you anything you don't know. The math doesn't math, that's all. It hasn't for years, and you keep standing out here doing arithmetic with a shovel."

"Hand me the primer."

Gary handed him the primer. The purple smell of it rose up out of the hole, chemical and sharp, the smell of every repair Wade had ever made, and under it the smell of the mud, which was the smell of the place itself, mineral and cold and old. He swabbed the inside of the coupler and the outside of the pipe, both ends, and then the glue, working quickly because the glue gave you about eight seconds of grace before it made up its mind, and he seated the coupler with a quarter turn the way his father had taught him, the way his father's father had probably taught his father something equivalent, the chain of small competencies running back up the valley into the dark, and held it while he counted thirty.

"You can't hold it together by hand," Gary said. "That's the thing. That's the whole thing I'm trying to say."

"I'm holding a pipe."

"You know what I mean."

Wade let go of the coupler and sat back on his heels. The killdeer had come back and was running its circles at the edge of the wet grass, crying its own name. Down the row the trees stood in their lines, the Galas heavy already with green fruit the size of walnuts, thinned in May by a crew Wade could barely afford, every apple on every tree representing a decision somebody had made, this one stays, this one goes, ten thousand small judgments per acre, and the trees themselves indifferent to all of it, wanting only water and light and to make more of themselves, which was the same thing everything wanted, and the only honest thing about the entire business.

"You remember laying this line?" Wade said.

"I remember some of it."

"You were what, fourteen. Dad had us in the trench all of July. Hottest summer anybody could remember, before the summers got like they are now. You passed out one afternoon down by block five and he poured a whole jug of ditch water over your head and told you to drink the rest of it and Mom like to have killed him because of the giardia."

"I didn't get giardia."

"You didn't get giardia. That was the summer you decided you were leaving. You told me so, right in the trench. You said, I'm not doing this when I grow up, I'm going to have a job where I shower before work instead of after."

Gary laughed, one short note, and looked away down the valley. "And you said—"

"I don't remember what I said."

"You said, somebody's got to grow your lunch, college boy." Gary picked up the cut section of old pipe and turned the crack to the light. "You were sixteen and you already talked like Dad. It used to scare me a little. It still does, honestly."

They glued the second coupler and set the new section in, and there is a moment in every pipe repair when the geometry refuses, when the new piece is exactly the length of the gap and therefore cannot enter it, and you have to flex the whole buried line, lever it up out of its bed with a shovel handle and spring the new piece in sideways, and the pipe groans and you are certain you are cracking it somewhere else, somewhere you won't find until August, and then it goes, it seats, it surrenders, and both men were lying in the mud by then, shoulder to shoulder in a hole their own height, breathing hard, and Gary started to laugh and then Wade did too, neither of them able to say at what.

They climbed out and sat on the dry uphill spoil with their boots hanging into the hole and drank the water Wade had brought in the dented Igloo that had been riding in one farm truck or another since the Carter administration. The glue needed an hour before pressure. The sun had real weight now. Swallows were working the air above the wet ground, stitching it, and somewhere up the hill a neighbor's wind machine ticked over once, somebody testing the motor, a sound like a single enormous bird.

"Walk me through it," Gary said. "If we keep it. Not the brochure version. The real version. Walk me through the next five years."

Wade was quiet for a while. He was not a man who spoke before the thing was built in his head. "Pull the Red Delicious this winter," he said finally. "Should've done it five years ago. That's three acres. Replant two of them to Cosmic Crisp or whatever the warehouse is pushing by then, eat the cost, no crop till year three, real crop year five. Lease the bottom ground to Verhoeven for his heifers, that's a little cash. Keep doing the cider thing with the Lindquist girl, that pencils better than people think. Refinance the operating loan if Helen at the bank will still look me in the eye. Pray it doesn't freeze. Pray it doesn't hail. Pray the river holds and the district doesn't cut us to forty percent, which they're talking about, I know they're talking about it."

"And Mom?"

"We sell the Henderson forty. The dryland piece. It doesn't grow anything but cheatgrass and it borders the Tilson development, they'd take it tomorrow. That's three, four hundred thousand. Buys her four years."

"And after four years?"

"After four years I'm five years older and we have this conversation again, except by then maybe the trees are bearing and the conversation is different."

"Or it's exactly the same conversation except you're sixty and you've put four more years and the Henderson money into the ground, and we sell anyway, at the bottom, exhausted, because that's how these things actually go. I see it all the time, Wade. That's my literal job, going out to places like this after, and putting a number on what's left. The family always hung on four more years. Always. There's always a Henderson forty. There's always a new variety that's going to fix it. And the brothers always end up not speaking, and I will tell you the truth, I care more about us speaking than I care about any tree on this place."

The swallows kept up their work. Wade picked a clod off the spoil pile and broke it in his hand, and it came apart along its own seams, dark and crumbed, full of fine white roots like nerve endings.

"Dad turned down four offers that I know of," he said. "Seventy-nine, eighty-six, ninety-eight, and the one in oh-six that would've set him up forever. Mom wanted to take the oh-six one. They didn't speak about it after a while, it just sat in the house with them like a third person. And then he died at sixty-eight in the east block with a pruning saw in his hand, and you can say that's a tragedy and you can say that's a man who got to die standing in the middle of his own life, and I have never been able to decide which, and I think about it every single day, and that is the most words I have said about it to anybody, so."

Gary didn't answer right away, which Wade noted, and was grateful for. A truck went by out on the river road, far off, pulling its long sound behind it.

"I was in Aspen Court on Tuesday," Gary said. "Before I drove up. She was having a good morning. She knew me for about ten minutes. And you know what she talked about, those ten minutes? Water. Whether you'd remembered to change the set on the upper block. Whether the screen on the pump intake was clean, because your father never cleans it, she said, present tense, he never cleans it and then he wonders. Sixty years of her life she wanted off this place, and now the place is the last thing in her head. The very last thing. So don't think I don't know what we're talking about. I know exactly what we're talking about. I'm just the one who has to say the number out loud."

"The number's a good number," Wade said. "I'm not pretending it isn't."

"It's a life-changing number."

"That's what I'm afraid of."

Gary turned and looked at him. Wade was looking down the rows, where the light came through the canopy in coins and the green fruit hung in its tens of thousands, and the line of poplars stood half dead against the brown hills, and he could see, the way you see a double exposure, the other thing laid over it: the surveyor's stakes with their pink flagging, the roads with names like Orchard View Lane going in where the rows had been, the model home with three apple trees left standing in the yard as a kind of taxidermy, and people on their decks in the evening looking at the mountains, decent people probably, people who would love it here, and not one of them would ever know that this exact square of ground had once been a hole two brothers lay down in together, in mud, laughing.

"I'm not signing anything this summer," Gary said at last. "The Seattle people aren't going anywhere. Land like this only gets more valuable, that's the cruel joke of it, it appreciates faster as farmland exactly because it's dying as farmland. So there's no gun to our heads. I want you to understand the option doesn't expire. But I also want you to understand the other math, the Mom math, because that one does expire, monthly, on the first."

"Sell the Henderson forty with me," Wade said. "This fall. That's real money, fast, and it doesn't take a single tree. Do that with me, and come back at Christmas, and we'll look at the books together, the real books, I'll show you everything, and if it still doesn't math, your word, then I'll listen. Actually listen. I'll sit at the table and I won't get up and do the dishes."

Gary smiled at that despite himself. "You washed every dish in the house. You washed dishes that were already clean."

"It needed doing."

"Nothing on this place ever needed doing as bad as you needed to be doing it." But there wasn't any blade in it, the way he said it, and after a moment he put out his hand, mud and all, and Wade took it, mud and all, and that was the contract, the only kind their family had ever really honored.

When the hour was up they drove back to the pumphouse and Wade cleaned the intake screen, because his mother was right, and primed the pump, and Gary stood at the panel and threw the switch when Wade nodded, and the pump came up through its scale of complaint into its steady working hum, and the gauge climbed, thirty, forty-five, fifty-two, and held. They drove back down the row faster than they needed to, like boys, and stood over the open hole and watched the new white section of pipe lying in the brown water of the trench, and it did nothing, which was everything, no seep, no bead, no whisper at the couplers, and down the block the micro-sprinklers came on one by one with small exhalations, each tree getting its measure, the whole system breathing again, and the smell that rose off the orchard then was the smell that no number in any folder would ever account for, water on hot dust, the oldest smell there is, the one that means you get to keep going for now.

They backfilled the hole by hand, tamping in layers, and replaced the square of sod over the scar like men closing a grave they expected to reopen. The sun was past noon. Gary's borrowed boots were ruined in the way that boots are improved by being ruined, and he looked down at them and said he might just keep them, if that was all right, and Wade said they were half his anyway, everything here was half his, that had always been the whole problem and the whole point.

They ate lunch on the tailgate in the shade of the poplars, bologna sandwiches Wade had made that morning before either of them knew what the day was, and a sleeve of saltines, and the last two beers from the shop fridge, warm-ish, and they argued about nothing for a while, the Mariners, the right way to sharpen a chain, whether the Lindquist girl's cider was actually good or just local, and it was the easiest hour they had spent together since the funeral. A red-tailed hawk turned above the dry hills. The wind came up the valley the way it did every afternoon, regular as a wage, and moved through the living poplars and made its big soft ocean sound and moved through the dead ones silently, and Gary watched the trees and said, almost to himself, that he could never figure out why their grandfather planted Lombardies when he had to know they only lived sixty years, and Wade said maybe sixty years looked like forever from where he was standing, and maybe that was the only way anybody ever planted anything.

In the evening, after Gary had showered and packed and they had gone in together to sit with their mother, who knew neither of them today but held both their hands and told them the water was on, she could hear it, listen, the water was on; after the truck that had never hauled anything had gone down the driveway and turned onto the river road with one tap of the horn; Wade walked out into the orchard alone in the long light. The sprinklers were still running. The wet ground gave under his boots, the good kind of giving. At the repair he stopped and stood on the fresh-laid sod, feeling the faint cool of the line under him, the water going by down there in the dark on its way to the trees, and he stood the way his father used to stand, looking at a thing for a long time, and he could not have told anyone, not Gary, not the bank, not himself, whether he was a man guarding his life or a man refusing to leave the scene of it, and the killdeer cried somewhere off in the dusk, its one word, its own name, over and over, the way you do when the thing you are is the only thing you know how to say.
