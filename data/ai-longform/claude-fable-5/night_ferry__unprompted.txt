The call had come the morning before, while I was standing at the kitchen window watching a magpie work at something in the gutter of the house opposite. A constable on the island, a woman's voice, careful in the way people are careful when they have practised what they will say. A man had been recovered from the water off the north end, she said. He had been in the sea some time. There was no identification on him, but he was of an age and a build, and he was wearing a gansey of the kind the men there wore, and Niall had not been seen since the back end of March. Niall Sutherland, she said, as though I might need reminding of my brother's name. They had gone to the house and found it shut up, the post behind the door. They had found my number through the records. Would I be willing to come north. She did not say to identify the body. She said to assist them. I said that I would, and after I put the phone down I stood at the window a long time, and the magpie finished whatever it was doing and flew off, and the gutter was just a gutter again, and I went upstairs and packed a bag with two shirts and a black tie I had not worn since my mother's funeral, twelve years before, which was the last time I had spoken to my brother.

I drove north all that day, through rain that came and went, and reached the port town in the early evening with two hours to spare before the boat. The terminal was a low building the colour of weather, and inside it smelled of wet anoraks and burnt coffee, and a handful of people sat among their luggage with the particular patience of islanders, who do not regard a journey as something to be hurried because the sea will take exactly as long as it takes. There were oil workers with kit bags, and an old couple with a Border terrier in a tartan harness, and a girl of maybe twenty asleep across three chairs with her boots still on. When they called the boarding I walked up the ramp with the rest, and the boat received us into its bright corridors, its carpet patterned to hide a thousand spilled drinks, and somewhere far below the engines were already turning over, a tremor you felt in the soles of your feet before you heard it, like the boat's own pulse.

We sailed at seven. I went out on the after deck to watch the mainland go, the way I had always done, even as a boy coming the other way, when leaving the island had felt like the beginning of my real life and the wake unspooling behind us had seemed to me a kind of writing, a long sentence saying I am gone, I am gone, I am gone. The town slid astern, its lights coming up as the dusk came down, and the headland with its lighthouse turned slowly past, and then there was only the open water, grey and heavy and unbothered, with a long swell running from the north-east. Fulmars hung off the stern on stiff wings, riding the boat's wind. The air had that clean cold smell that I had forgotten I knew, that I had not smelled in twelve years, and it went into me like a key into a lock, and a great deal of machinery I had thought rusted shut began, against my wishes, to move.

I ate in the cafeteria, fish and chips off a tray, because it seemed important to eat, the way it seems important to do ordinary things on the days when something is wrong, as though the ordinary things were ropes you could hold. Afterwards I took a whisky in the bar, where a television no one watched was showing snooker, and a man about my own age, broad in the shoulders and red in the face, looked at me twice from the end of the counter and then came along it. You'll be a Sutherland, he said. It was not a question. I said I was. Calum, he said, nodding to himself, as though confirming a sum. You won't mind me. Davy Henderson. Your Niall keeps a creel boat off the same pier as my brother. He looked into his pint then and I could see him deciding whether to say the next thing, and deciding to say it. There was a man taken from the water at the north end, he said. You'll have heard. They were saying it might be — he stopped, and shrugged, an apology made with the whole upper body. Folk say things, he said. Niall goes out west for weeks at a time, to the old place. He's done it for years. He'll be sitting out there mending creels and not knowing a thing about it, most likely. I said most likely, and we touched glasses without quite saying to what, and he went back to his end of the bar, and I sat with the snooker and the whisky and the knowledge that on the island the body and my name were already joined in people's mouths, the way they join such things, gently and terribly, like hands.

Later I went out on deck again. It was full dark by then, no moon, the boat's lights making a small moving room of brightness on the black water, and beyond that nothing, or rather everything, all of it unseen. I stood at the rail and thought about Niall's hands. This was not a thing I chose to think about; it arrived. When he was nineteen he lost the top joint of his left forefinger to a winch on the Mhairi Ann, our father's boat, and I remembered him coming up the path to the house with his hand wrapped in someone's shirt, white as a gull, and refusing to sit down in the kitchen because he did not want to bleed on Mam's chairs. He was four years older than me and he had taught me everything I knew that was worth knowing — how to gut a fish, how to read the water over the skerries, how to splice an eye into a rope, how to tell from the way the oystercatchers were behaving whether the weather would hold. He had a way of teaching that involved almost no words. He would do the thing slowly, once, watching you sideways, and then hand you the knife or the rope and stand there with his arms folded, and if you got it wrong he would take it back and do it slowly again, and the only praise he ever gave was to stop watching. The day he stopped watching me splice was one of the proudest of my life. I was perhaps eleven. I had not thought of it in decades, and now it came up out of the dark water entire, with the smell of tar and the cold of the rope still in it.

Our father was lost in November the year I was fifteen. The Mhairi Ann went out after haddock with three men aboard and a forecast that turned out to be a lie, and the storm came down off the north like a fist, and she never came home. They found wreckage on the west side for weeks afterwards — a hatch cover, a fish box with his registration burned into it, an oilskin jacket that my mother would not let anyone touch and that hung in the porch until she died. They never found the men. I have thought since that this is the cruelest arithmetic the sea does: it takes the man and leaves you the jacket. We stood at the kitchen window that night, the three of us, watching the dark where the sea was, and I understood that my mother was listening for an engine, and I understood that she would in some part of herself be listening for that engine for the rest of her life. Niall was nineteen, the finger not a year healed. Within a month he had taken a berth on another man's boat, and within five years he had a boat of his own, a smaller one, creels and long-lining, and he called her the Mhairi too, after our mother, and he never once in my hearing said our father's name again, and he never once stopped going out on the water that had taken him. I have wondered all my life whether that was courage or whether it was the opposite, a refusal so complete it looked like courage. I went the other way. I worked for my exams as if they were a tide I could catch, and I caught it, and at eighteen I stood on the after deck of the ferry and watched the island go down into the sea behind me and felt — God forgive me, I felt light.

The years did what years do. I was on the mainland, then further south, then married, then not. I came home for Christmases, then for alternate Christmases, then for occasions. When my mother's mind began to go it was Niall who noticed, Niall who took her to the doctor, Niall who, when she could no longer be left, moved back into the house he had grown up in and slept in his boyhood bed so that he could hear her if she rose in the night. Three years he did that, around the tides, around the creels, and I came up four times in those three years, and each time I told myself the lie that distance tells, which is that being far away is a circumstance and not a choice. The last time I saw her living she looked at me with great politeness and asked if I had come about the meter. Niall stood in the doorway with a dish towel over his shoulder and said nothing, and his saying nothing was the loudest thing in the room.

The quarrel, when it finally came, was at the funeral, or after it, in the kitchen, with the sandwiches still under their cloths and the neighbours gone home. It was about the house, on its surface. I said it should be sold; he said it should not; I said he could not afford to buy my half out and he said he would not insult either of us by trying. But the house was only the field we chose to fight in. What I actually said to him — and I can hear it still, I have heard it on a loop for twelve years, in airports and in the dark and in the dentist's chair — what I said was that he had buried himself alive in that house for a dead woman and a drowned man, and that staying was not loyalty, it was fear, and that he had never once in his life had the nerve to leave anything. And Niall, who never raised his voice, did not raise it then. He dried his hands very slowly on the dish towel, the shortened finger moving with the others, and he said, You were always leaving, Calum. Even when you were here, you were leaving. It's the only thing you've ever done well. Then he opened the door for me, courteous as an undertaker, and I walked through it, and that was the last time we spoke. Once, four years ago, I saw him in Aberdeen, on the fish market quay, fifty yards away, talking to another man, his hair gone grey under his cap. I stood there long enough for him to have turned and seen me. He did not turn. And I — I, who had crossed the road many times in my life — I did not cross the quay. I told myself there would be time. That is the other lie distance tells, and it is a worse one.

I took a cabin for the night, a narrow berth with the engines for a heartbeat, and I lay in it without sleeping while the boat worked through the swell, the long rise and the long fall, the shudder when she put her shoulder into a bigger sea. I rehearsed what the morning would ask of me. I had never seen a drowned man. I knew, in the way one knows things from newspapers, that the sea is not kind, that time in the water changes a face, that they would have done what they could. I thought: I will know him by the finger. Whatever the water has done, it cannot have done that, cannot have given back what the winch took thirty-five years ago. The thought was an anchor and I held to it, and somewhere in the small hours I went down into a half-sleep in which Niall stood at the end of the pier with his arms folded, watching me splice a rope that kept unravelling, and I woke with the wake of a dream's grief on me and the engines slower, and through the porthole there was a grey light, and in the grey light there was land.

The island came up out of the morning the way it always had, low and treeless and patient, the hills the colour of a tarnished spoon, the lighthouse on the south head still turning though the day was already light enough to make its beam a pale, redundant stroke. We came into the harbour past the breakwater I had fished off as a boy, and the town was there, the grey houses with their gables to the sea, a few more of them, a new shed at the pier, but the same town, the way a face after many years is a different face and the same face. The gulls came out to meet the boat, screaming their one comment on everything. I stood at the rail with my bag and felt the thing I had not let myself feel all night, which was fear, plain fear, cold in the gut, and under the fear something older and stranger, which was that the island did not care, had never cared, would receive me and my dread as it received the weather, without opinion.

The constable was waiting at the terminal, younger than her voice, in a fluorescent jacket too big for her, and she shook my hand and said her name was Tait and thanked me for coming as though I had done her a favour. She drove me up through the town in a police car that smelled of dog, past the war memorial, past the close where my grandmother had lived, talking carefully — the body had been found by a creel boat ten days ago, a mile off the north end; the man had been in the water perhaps two weeks, perhaps three; there had been nothing in the pockets but a folding knife and some coins; the post-mortem had found no injury that the sea could not explain. We have to warn you, she said, stopping the car outside the cottage hospital, that the appearance will be distressing, and that you may not be able to be certain, and that's all right, nobody will press you. She said it kindly and I understood she had said it before, perhaps many times, and I wondered what it cost her, living among five thousand people and meeting them on the worst mornings of their lives.

The room was small and clean and very cold, and it smelled of disinfectant with something underneath the disinfectant. A man in scrubs stood by a steel table, and on the table was a shape under a white sheet, and the shape was the size of a man and was not moving and would never move, and PC Tait stood at my shoulder and asked if I was ready, and I heard myself say yes, and the man in scrubs folded the sheet back from the face with a gentleness that I will remember as long as I live, as though the dead man might be cold, as though he might be woken.

The sea had done its work. I will not set down here what three weeks of water does to a face; it is enough to say that what lay on the table was both a man and the memory of a man, and that I looked at it and could not say. The hair was grey and thinning where Niall's had been grey and thick, but the water takes hair. The brow could have been his brow. The age was right, the breadth of the shoulders under the sheet was right, and for a long moment the room tilted and I felt the word yes forming in me like water rising in a well, because the mind wants to recognise, it leans toward recognition the way a plant leans toward light. Then I remembered my anchor. The hands, I said, and my voice came out wrong, and I said it again. Can I see his hands.

The man in scrubs drew the sheet down on the left side and lifted the arm with both of his, supporting it at wrist and elbow, and laid the hand where I could see it, palm down on the steel.

Four fingers and a thumb, entire. The forefinger whole to its tip, the nail still on it, a stranger's finger on a stranger's hand.

It's not him, I said. The constable said quietly, You're sure, and I said, My brother lost the top of that finger when he was nineteen. To a winch. It's not him. And I had to put my own hand flat on the cold steel edge of the table then, because relief, when it comes that suddenly, is not a feeling but an assault; it goes through you like a sea breaking over a deck, and it takes your legs, and it is indistinguishable, for the first moment, from grief. I stood there breathing while the man in scrubs covered the face again with the same gentleness, and I thought: somebody's brother. Somewhere a telephone will ring in a kitchen and a man will stand at a window and a voice will be careful with him. I found that I had begun, absurdly, to cry — not for Niall, who was not on the table, but for this man who was, and for whoever was at this moment going about an ordinary morning not yet knowing, and for myself, and for the twelve wasted years that had ended in a cold room with the wrong man's hand on the steel.

Outside, the morning had brightened. PC Tait stood with me in the car park while I got my breath, and she said they would put out word again for Niall, and check the west side, and I heard myself say, No. I'll go. There's a place out there, the old haaf station at the Bight, our grandfather's people had a hut — he goes out in the spring, he's done it thirty years, he'll have no phone and no notion of any of this. She offered to drive me and I said I would walk, and she looked at me a moment, the way the young look at the old when the old say something irrational and the young decide to permit it, and she said the road was nine miles, and I said I knew exactly how long the road was.

I left my bag at the hotel and walked out of the town the way I had walked as a boy, past the last houses, past the kirkyard where my mother and the memory of my father lay under one stone, and I did not stop there, not yet; I would stop on the way back, and I would not be alone when I did, or so I let myself hope, walking. The road went west over the moor between the lochans, and the wind came at me off the open Atlantic with that smell in it again, peat and salt and weather, and curlews went up bubbling off the heather, and the fear walked with me the whole way, because a whole forefinger on a dead man's hand proves only what it proves. The sea takes men quietly as well as loudly. Nine miles is a long time to hold two futures in your hands at once.

The road rose to the last crest in the early afternoon, and I stopped there, because below me was the Bight, the bay curved like a held breath, the old stone hut above the shingle where the haaf men had lain in summers a hundred years before any of us, and below the hut, swinging easy at her mooring in the grey-green water, a small white boat with her name in black on the bow, too far to read, and not needing to be read. And from the chimney of the hut, thin and constant and unbearable, climbing into the wind and bending away with it, there was smoke.

I stood on the crest for a moment with my heart going like an engine. Down by the hut a figure came out of the door, stooped, in a cap, carrying a creel in each hand, and set them down by the others ranked along the wall, and straightened with a hand at the small of his back, the way our father used to, the way I do now. He had not seen me. He bent to his work.

I started down the road toward him, and I did not stop.
