All afternoon strangers carried her mother out the front door a piece at a time, and the house let them. Faye had expected resistance—a doorframe too narrow for the chifforobe, a stair tread giving way under the davenport, some small spite from a house that had held Odessa Mills for sixty-one years and ought to know better than to give her up to people with cash in their fists and good weather on their side. But the house was obedient. It had always been obedient. It opened its doors the way Odessa had taught everything in her keeping to open: promptly, without comment, and at the convenience of whoever was asking.

So Faye sat behind the card table on the porch with a cigar box of change and a roll of masking tape and watched two men in matching gray T-shirts angle the davenport through the doorway with no more trouble than carrying out a casket, which, when she thought about it, is what they were doing—had been doing since noon, since the first car slowed at the curb of Beulah Street and a woman she had never seen in her life got out, walked into her mother's parlor like she had been baptized there, and bought the lamp with the milk-glass shade for four dollars.

Four dollars. Odessa had bought that lamp from Hartzell's in 1963 and dusted it every Saturday of her life including the Saturday before the Tuesday she died, and now it rode away in the back seat of a Pontiac next to a child's car seat, headed someplace Faye would never know about, to light up the faces of people who would never know whose hands had kept it bright. That was the trade. That was the whole afternoon. Things that knew everything, going home with people who knew nothing.

Renee had said sell it all, Mama. Said it from Atlanta in the voice she used for closing arguments, the voice Faye had paid for one tuition check at a time. You can't keep a house in Harmon, Ohio, from six hundred miles away, and you can't put a parlor in a one-bedroom in Cleveland. Sell it all and don't you dare cry over a pie safe. Faye's brother might have said different, might have wanted the table or the glove or the jar, but her brother had been dead since the summer of 1969, since Dutchman's Hole closed up over his head one July afternoon while four boys stood on the limestone lip hollering his name down at the water like the water might answer. So there was nobody to say different. There was only Faye, fifty-six years old, presiding over the dismantling of the only place on earth where she had ever been somebody's child.

The town came first, before the strangers. The town always came first. By one o'clock half of Calvary Baptist had passed through, fingering the doilies, lifting the lids of pots they had eaten out of at repasts and revivals, not buying, mostly, just looking, taking inventory of a dead woman's housekeeping the way they had taken inventory of her living. Mm. Dess kept her linens nice. Mm. Look here, she still had that punch bowl. They handled everything and purchased almost nothing, and Faye understood. Buying was beside the point. They had come to see whether grief was being conducted properly, whether the daughter who left at eighteen and came back for funerals knew how to fold a dead mother's slips, and whether there was anything in the drawers worth gossiping about. There wasn't. Odessa Mills had lived seventy years in this town without giving it one thing to chew on, and she was not about to start from the grave.

Earlene Pugh came at two, in her church shoes although it was Saturday, and stood on the porch surveying the card table, the masking-tape prices, the open door, the whole arrangement, with her mouth gathered up like a drawstring purse.

"So you doing it, then," Earlene said.

"Doing it."

"Selling your mama's bed to white folks from Marion."

"Selling Mama's bed to whoever's got thirty dollars, Miss Earlene."

"Mm." Earlene lowered herself into the porch chair that had a strip of tape on its arm saying $5 and made it clear by the way she sat that she was not part of the merchandise. "Dess would pitch a fit."

"Dess would price it higher," Faye said, and Earlene laughed before she could stop herself, a short bark she converted into a cough, because you were not supposed to laugh at a sale like this, in front of the open door of a dead friend's house, with strangers inside walking on her rugs. But it was true and they both knew it was true. Odessa Mills had never in her life let anything go for less than it was worth—not a hem, not a Sunday, not a grudge—and if she was watching this afternoon from wherever the church promised she was, her objection would not be the selling. It would be the four dollars.

"She was sharp to the end," Earlene said, softer. "I'll give her that. Sharp as a needle to the very end."

She was. Faye had sat by the hospital bed in Marion for eleven days and her mother's mind had not loosened once, not even at the last, when the nurses talked over her like she was furniture and Odessa, who could no longer lift her own hand, cut her eyes at them in a way that made them apologize without knowing what for. People said the dying went back, wandered, called out for their mothers, their childhoods, their sons. Faye had braced for it. Had sat there nights waiting for her mother to call Curtis's name, because Curtis was who her mother had been listening for since 1969, and surely now, with the door opening, she would call him. She never did. What Odessa said, the last evening she said anything, was: "You let your hem out yourself? It's crooked." And then, while Faye was still deciding whether to laugh or leave the room, her mother's hand had come creeping across the blanket—slow, deliberate, a five-day journey in five seconds—and taken hold of the hem of Faye's skirt and rubbed the fabric between finger and thumb, the way she had tested cloth at Hartzell's remnant table for fifty years. Held it. Kept holding it. Was still holding it when she went to sleep, so that Faye sat bent sideways in the chair for an hour, tethered to her mother by three inches of polyester blend, afraid to move, understanding finally that this was the sentence her mother had been constructing her whole life and there were no words in it. There were never going to be words in it. Her mother spoke starch. Her mother spoke hems. Sixty years of love delivered in alterations, every dress Faye ever owned taken in or let out at that Singer in the back room, perfect seams, perfect, because a crooked hem tells on you, baby, a crooked hem tells the world your mother don't care.

"You keeping anything?" Earlene asked.

"A box."

"One box."

"One box."

Earlene looked at her for a while, and whatever was in the look did not make it to her mouth. What she said instead was, "Her gray hat in there? The one with the little net to it?"

"It's in the front closet."

"What you asking for it?"

"For you?" Faye said. "Nothing. Go get it."

And Earlene Pugh, who had feuded with Odessa Mills over the pastor's anniversary committee for the better part of two decades, who had once not spoken to her across a fence for an entire winter over a remark about pound cake, went into the house and came out wearing the gray hat with the little net to it, settled down on her brow like Sunday, and her eyes were wet and furious about being wet, and she sat back down in the five-dollar chair and didn't say anything for a long time, and neither did Faye, and the strangers went in and out around them carrying lamps.

The dealer came at three. Faye knew him for a dealer before he was out of his truck—a long-bodied white man from Columbus with a clipboard and a way of touching things with two fingers, like a doctor with a bad diagnosis he was deciding how to deliver. He went through the house in eleven minutes. He came out and stood by the card table and told her, kindly, confidentially, the way you tell a child there is no Santa Claus, that most of it was junk, no offense, postwar veneer, no market for it, but he could do her a favor and take the pie safe and the sewing machine off her hands. Forty for the pair. The pie safe had some damage but he had a guy who liked primitives.

"Primitives," Faye said.

"Country pieces. Punched tin, that kind of thing. It's a niche market."

The pie safe had come up from Alabama in 1946 in the bed of a borrowed truck, with everything else her grandmother owned, which was the pie safe and a Bible and three children, one of them Odessa, fourteen years old, riding the whole way with her feet braced against it so it wouldn't shift. The punched-tin panels had stars in them. Faye had spent whole rainy afternoons of her childhood with her eye up against those tin stars, looking into the dark where the pies were. There was no word for what that pie safe was, but the word was not primitive, and the price was not twenty dollars.

"The pie safe is two hundred," Faye said. "The machine's not for sale."

"It was marked—"

"It was. It's not now." And she reached over and peeled the masking tape off the Singer's bench where it sat just inside the door, and balled the tape up small, and put it in her pocket, and looked at him.

He paid the two hundred. They always paid, men like that, once they understood you knew; it was only ignorance they were in the market for. She watched him carry the pie safe down the porch steps, stars and all, and load it like a coffin, and she let it go without a sound, because the safe was wood and tin and her grandmother was not in it; her grandmother was in the recipe for buttermilk pie that Faye carried in her own hand's memory; you could not sell that at a card table and no man from Columbus would ever touch it with two fingers. But the Singer stayed. The Singer was going to Cleveland if Faye had to carry it on her back up Route 71. That machine had put a roof on this house. After Faye's father died—quick, young, a man Faye remembered mostly as a laugh in another room—Odessa had sewn for half the county, white women's daughters' wedding dresses and choir robes and cheerleader uniforms for a high school that didn't let Faye cheer, sewn at that machine until two and three in the morning, and the sound of it was the sound of Faye's whole childhood: that urgent mechanical heartbeat down the hall, stopping, starting, stopping, her mother feeding cloth into it like feeding a child, like the machine was the one mouth in the house Odessa could fill all the way up.

It was in the Singer's bench, that morning, that Faye had found the envelope. She had opened the bench to inventory the attachments—the buttonholer, the zipper foot, all the little chrome feet in their cardboard boxes—and underneath the trays, flat on the bottom, wrapped in a piece of muslin, an envelope. Inside the envelope: every letter Faye had ever written home from Cleveland. Nineteen seventy-six to nineteen eighty-one, the years before phone calls got cheap, the years Faye told everybody, told Howard, told Renee, told herself, that her mother never answered her letters, never once, not even when Renee was born. Which was true. Odessa never answered them. She kept them. Kept them in date order with the stamps cut off—saved for some church drive, probably, waste not—and the folds of them gone soft and furred the way paper goes only one way, which is from handling, from being opened and read and refolded and opened again, fifty times, a hundred, by lamplight, by a woman alone in a house on Beulah Street with a dead son's room on one side of her and a gone daughter's room on the other, reading I am fine Mama, the job is fine, the baby is walking, reading it over and over and never answering, because answering would have been asking, and Odessa Mills did not ask. Want is a door you close yourself. She had said it all Faye's life. Faye had thought it was a lesson. Standing in the back room that morning with the envelope in her hands, she understood it had been a confession.

Earlene left at four, with the hat and without goodbye, which from Earlene was tenderness. The afternoon thinned out. A young couple came—a boy with paint on his boots and a girl far along, carrying the pregnancy high and walking with her hand pressed to the small of her own back—and they circled the kitchen table three times, whispering arithmetic. The table was marked twenty-five. It was the table Faye had eaten every meal of her childhood on, the table where Odessa pressed hair on Saturday nights, Faye between her knees and the hot comb ticking on the stove and the smell of singe and Royal Crown, the table with the iron-scorch in one corner the shape of a bird in flight, from the night in 1969, the night after they pulled Curtis out of Dutchman's Hole, when Odessa stood pressing a white shirt for no one and forgot her own hand was moving and stood there, iron down, looking at nothing, until the smoke brought her back. She never sanded it out. Forty-five years she wiped that table down every evening, around the bird, over the bird, and never sanded it out.

"It's got a burn," the boy apologized, embarrassed for the table, embarrassed to bargain over a dead woman's kitchen. "Would you take fifteen?"

The girl was running her palm over the scorch mark, idly, the way you stroke a thing you've already decided is yours. Her other hand stayed on her belly. There was a child in that child, and the child was going to eat at this table, spill at this table, hide under it, do homework on it, get its hair done beside it for all Faye knew, and someday somebody would ask about the burn and the girl would say it came that way, came with the table, and that would become the truth, and the bird would fly on without its story, which was maybe what wood was for. Carrying weight it didn't have to understand.

"Take it for ten," Faye said. "The chairs go with it."

It was the man with the grandson who broke her. Five o'clock, the light gone long and orange down Beulah Street, the house more echo than house, and an old man came up the walk holding the hand of a boy maybe six years old, the boy wearing a Cincinnati Reds cap pushed back off his forehead the way boys wear them when the cap matters more than the sun. The man's name was Gandy, he said; he'd known her mother to speak to, years back; he was sorry. He was looking for a bed for the boy, who'd come to live with him—he gave that fact a little pat as he set it down, came to live with me, and put nothing else around it, and Faye understood from the pat that there was a story there with a hole in the middle of it, a mother or a father subtracted, and that the man would stand in the sun all day before he'd say more in front of the child.

There was one bed left in the house. Faye had not put tape on it. It stood in the back bedroom, the small iron bed with the pipe frame painted white and repainted white, made up tight as a drum under a chenille spread, made up that way since July of 1969, the room dusted weekly for forty-five years, the baseball glove on the shelf, the jar of marbles on the sill catching light, the whole room held under glass by a mother who could keep anything in this world except the one thing. Faye had stood in the doorway of that room a hundred times in her life and never once all the way inside it. Even this morning, even with the envelope in her hand and the whole house hers to dispose of, she had reached in and taken the jar of marbles off the sill at arm's length, like reaching into water, and pulled back.

She walked Mr. Gandy down the hall. The boy went ahead of them both, the way boys do, and was in the room before she could feel anything about it, in the middle of the room, in the shrine, with his cap pushed back, and he put his hand flat on the iron footrail and gave the bed a shake to test it, possessive already, and said, "This one's mine?"

The bed rang a little under his hand. A small iron sound, a sound the room had not made in forty-five years, and Faye stood in the doorway and felt it go through her like the first crack in ice. Her mother had kept this room because keeping was the only language she had; she had kept the bed and kept the glove and kept the letters in the sewing bench, kept and kept and kept, sixty years of keeping, and not one thing she kept had ever come back to her. And here was a boy with a hole in the middle of his story, shaking the footrail, asking was it his.

"It's yours," Faye said. "The glove too. You play ball?"

He did. Of course he did. They all did, boys; her brother had been the best of them, everybody said so, said it for decades after, at funerals, on porches, that Curtis Mills could go get a fly ball like he had a rope to it, and the glove came down off the shelf and onto the boy's hand, far too big, swallowing him to the elbow, and he pounded his fist into the pocket the way they all do, the exact gesture, unchanged since Eden, and Mr. Gandy started to say something about price and Faye said five dollars and pretended not to see what was on his face, and the three of them carried the bed out in pieces—headboard, footboard, rails, slats—down the hall, through the parlor that was no longer a parlor, out the obedient door, into the orange light. The boy insisted on carrying a rail himself. It was too long for him and the end of it wandered and scraped, and nobody corrected him, and Faye stood on the porch and watched the bed go down Beulah Street in the back of an old man's truck with a boy's hand resting on it, and what she felt was not what she had braced for. It was lighter than grief and heavier than relief and she didn't have a word for it. Her family had never been the kind to have the words. They had been the kind to have the things, and now the things were going where things go, out into the world, into trucks and kitchens and back bedrooms, carrying their weight without understanding it, and somewhere tonight a boy would sleep in her brother's bed and dream a boy's dreams in it, and not one of them would be sad, and that, Faye thought, that was the resurrection the church kept promising. Not trumpets. Just use.

By six the house was empty except for the Singer and one box. She had taken the cigar box of money in off the card table—three hundred and some dollars, the price of a parlor, a kitchen, a life—and folded the card table itself into the trunk of her car, and now she walked the rooms one time in the long light, her footsteps loud as a stranger's. In the box: the jar of marbles. The thimble, worn through at the tip, an honest hole in it. The envelope of soft-folded letters. A photograph of her father laughing in another room. The buttermilk pie recipe in her grandmother's hand, which was also somehow her mother's hand, which was also, she had noticed lately on grocery lists, becoming her own.

In the kitchen closet the broom was still on its nail because nobody buys a used broom, and Faye took it down. Her mother's voice arrived before the first stroke, prompt as ever: Don't you sweep after sundown, girl. You sweep at sundown you sweep somebody out your life. All her childhood Faye had obeyed it without believing it, the way you honor a language you can't speak. Now she looked at the window, where the sun was sitting red on the roofline of the Pughs' house, going, not gone, and she began to sweep. She swept the kitchen and the hall and the parlor and the back bedroom, every room, toward the front door, the way Odessa had taught her, long even strokes, gathering up the last of it—dust, thread, a button, a cat's-eye marble that had escaped the jar forty-five years ago and waited under a radiator all this time to be found—gathering all of it to the threshold while the sun went down, and she stood in the doorway with the little gray pile at her feet and the empty house at her back, and she understood she was not sweeping anybody out. She was sweeping them loose. There was a difference, and her mother, who had held on to a hem rather than say goodbye, who had kept a room like a held breath for forty-five years, her mother of all women would know the difference, and would not pitch a fit, and would only say, if she said anything, that the corners wanted doing over.

Faye did the corners over. Then she swept the pile across the sill and out into the evening, where the wind off Beulah Street took it up and carried it, the dust and the thread and all of them, every one, out past the porch rail and into the wide dark coming, and she stood the broom by the door for whoever came next, and picked up the box, and pulled the obedient door shut behind her, and it closed the way her mother had taught everything to close. Promptly. Without comment. Holding everything.
