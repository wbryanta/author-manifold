The frost was still on the windshield when Emil Sorensen carried his tool case out to the truck, and he scraped it off with the edge of his hand the way he had done for sixty years, though he owned a scraper, because the cold against his palm was part of the beginning of a working day and he did not want to be spared it. The case went on the passenger seat, where it had always gone. Inside it, wrapped in flannel, lay the tuning hammer his father had bought him in Minneapolis in 1959, its rosewood handle worn pale where his thumb rode, and the felt temperament strip, and the rubber mutes like little black teardrops, and the fork that sang A when you struck it on your knee — four hundred forty vibrations a second, the one fixed star he had steered by his whole life while everything else drifted.

He had four stops written on the back of an envelope, though he didn't need the envelope. He had driven this route so long the truck could have driven it alone. East on County 11 past the Hendrickson place, north at the grain elevator that hadn't taken grain in twenty years, the gravel after that, the long swale where redwing blackbirds owned the cattails in summer and nothing owned them now, in late October, with the sloughs gone the color of old pewter. Four stops, and then the church. He had saved the church for last on purpose, and he had told himself it was geography, that Bethel Lutheran simply lay at the far end of the loop, and this was true the way the labels on his tuning pins were true, accurate and beside the point.

The first house belonged to Alma Reinertsen, who was ninety-one and had stopped playing when the arthritis took her left hand, which was eleven years ago now, though Emil had come every October since and she had paid him every October since, in cash, in an envelope with his name written on it in a hand that got smaller each year, as though she were rationing ink against a long winter. She had coffee waiting. She always had coffee waiting. They sat at the kitchen table first, because that was the order of things, and she told him about her granddaughter in Tucson and the hip the doctor wanted to replace and would not get to, not at her age, she said, why put new hinges on a door you're about to take off the frame. Emil laughed because she wanted him to, and looked out the window at the windbreak her husband had planted, the spruce grown black and enormous, three of them dead and standing dead, and he drank the coffee, which was weak, and which he had drunk weak in this kitchen for forty years.

The piano was a Story and Clark upright, 1948, in the parlor that smelled of cold and lemon oil. The parlor was not heated except when company came, and Emil supposed he was company, but the radiator ticked without conviction and his breath showed faintly while he worked. He opened the lid and the fallboard and removed the front panel and set it against the sofa, and the inside of the piano was clean as a kept grave — Alma dusted it, he knew, reaching in with a cloth tied to a yardstick because she could no longer kneel. The hammers showed almost no wear past the middle of the keyboard. Whatever music had lived in this instrument had lived in the middle octaves, hymns and Stephen Foster and the easy parts of Grieg, and the high treble was nearly virgin felt, struck so seldom in seventy years that the grooves were shallow as a fingerprint.

He set the temperament, working from A outward, the old equal temperament he had laid maybe ten thousand times, listening for the beats the way other men listened for their names. A third too wide here, beating fast and sour. He eased the pin a degree, less than a degree, the smallest motion a hand can make and still call it motion, and the beating slowed, and slowed, and settled into the gentle pulse that meant the interval was lying correctly — for that was the secret nobody outside the trade understood, that a piano in tune is a piano tuned to a system of careful small lies, every interval slightly false so that all keys could be played, no key perfect so that none would be unbearable. Pure intervals were for choirs and violins. A piano made its peace with imperfection or it made no music at all. He had thought about that often. He thought about it now, in Alma's cold parlor, working up the treble where no one would ever play, tuning notes that would not be struck again until he struck them next October, if there was a next October, and he knew, and she knew, and neither of them had said so over the coffee, that there would not be.

When he finished he played the way he always finished, not a piece, just a wandering — octaves, a few chords, a fragment of "Children of the Heavenly Father" because Alma liked it and would be listening from the kitchen. The piano sounded clean and patient. It sounded like a thing waiting. He put the panel back and closed the fallboard over the keys, and the click of it was soft and final, like a door pulled to in a house where someone is sleeping.

At the door Alma gave him the envelope and held his hand a moment longer than the handing-over required.

"This is the last time, then," she said. Not a question. The town paper had run a little item; his granddaughter had put something on the internet. Everyone on the route knew.

"Last round," he said.

"And nobody coming after."

"Nobody coming after. There's a fellow in Fergus Falls. He's good. I left his card."

"In Fergus Falls," she said, the way she might have said in Mongolia, and they both stood there a moment in the cold doorway with the truth between them, which was that the fellow in Fergus Falls would never drive ninety minutes to tune a piano nobody played for a woman who could not pay what the drive was worth, and that the Story and Clark would now begin its long slow going-flat, a few cents a year, the pins easing, the strings relaxing toward whatever pitch the wood and wire wanted for themselves, and that in ten years, in twenty, when the parlor belonged to someone else or to no one, the piano would still hold a shape of tune, a memory of tune, the ghost of this morning's work, the last human attention it would ever receive.

"Well," Alma said. "Thank you, Emil. For all of it."

"Thank you, Alma."

He did not look in the mirror as he drove out, because the rearview showed her standing on the step in her cardigan with her arms crossed against the cold, and he had learned a long time ago that there were things you carried better if you did not look straight at them.

The second stop was not a house anymore in any sense that mattered. The Voss farm had been empty four years, the family scattered, the land rented to a corporation that farmed it with machines the size of buildings and never set foot in the yard. But the youngest Voss daughter, Karen, lived in St. Cloud and paid Emil by check, mailed, to come once a year and tune her mother's piano, and she had written him a letter — an actual letter — when she heard he was retiring, asking him to come one last time, and enclosing the check, and a key.

The house had the smell empty houses get, dust and mouse and the sweetish smell of old wallpaper paste, and the light came through the windows the way it does in unlived rooms, lying on the floor in flat unwarmed panels. The piano was an Acrosonic spinet, a forgiving little instrument, and someone — Karen, he supposed, on a visit — had draped it with a bedsheet, and when he drew the sheet off the dust rose and hung in the window light and turned, slowly, the way dust does, as though the room were remembering how to breathe.

A spinet is an awkward tune; the action is dropped down behind the keys and everything is cramped and you work half by feel. His knees did not love the low bench and his shoulders did not love the angle, but his hands knew it, his hands had never forgotten anything, his hands were the youngest part of him. He found the piano nearly a half-step flat across the board — four years of an unheated house, the seasons swelling and shrinking the soundboard, the strings letting go of their pitch the way a hand lets go in sleep. He brought it up in two passes, rough and fine, and the strings groaned and pinged as they took the new tension, and twice he heard the dangerous creak of a pin in old wood and gentled it, coaxed it, settled it, talking to the instrument under his breath the way he had talked to instruments his whole life, easy now, easy, there you are.

While he worked he could hear, the way you hear things in empty houses, the sounds the room had held. Mrs. Voss had been a real player, taught for years, half the township's children had labored through their scales in this room, and the bench when he opened it was full of the evidence: Thompson's Modern Course, Book Two, with a child's name on the cover in pencil; Schirmer editions gone brown at the edges; a hymnal; a piece of sheet music called "Beautiful Ohio" that must have been her own, from her own girlhood, because the copyright said 1918. And under all of it, a recital program, mimeographed, the purple ink faded nearly to nothing: PIANO STUDENTS OF MRS. HAROLD VOSS, SPRING RECITAL, BETHEL LUTHERAN CHURCH, MAY 14.

No year on it. There didn't need to be a year on it. Emil sat on the low bench with the program in his hand and did not read the names, because he knew his son's name was on it, fourth or fifth from the top because the youngest played first, and he knew what was printed after the name — Sonatina in G, Beethoven, attributed — and he knew that if he read it he would see the church basement and the folding chairs and Greta in her blue dress beside him and Peter's cowlick that would not stay down no matter what was done to it, and he was not ready, not yet, not in the Voss parlor with three stops still ahead. He folded the program once, along its old fold, and put it in the breast pocket of his coat, and he did not ask himself whether that was stealing. Karen Voss would not miss it. There were things that belonged to whoever could not put them down.

He finished the spinet and played it, and the little piano did its honest best, bright and shallow and willing, and he covered it again with the sheet, which felt like a strange ceremony, tuning an instrument and then shrouding it, and he locked the house and put the key in the envelope to mail back, and stood a minute in the yard. The windbreak here was box elder, half down. A grain auger lay in the grass like the skeleton of some long animal. The corporation's corn stood right up to the fence line, harvested now, stubble to the horizon, and the wind came across it making the sound wind makes in stubble, a dry sound, a sound with no music in it at all.

The third stop was the Sorlie place, where there were still people — Marlys Sorlie, sixty-some, who kept the piano tuned for the grandchildren, who came at Christmas and did not play it, and the fourth was the old grange hall, where the township board paid for a tuning out of stubbornness and a budget line nobody had ever bothered to delete, and Emil did both of these the way a man says the middle verses of a hymn he knows by heart, fully and faithfully and with his mind elsewhere. At the Sorlie place there was lunch, ham sandwiches and pickles, and Marlys talked about her husband's heart and the price of propane, and at the grange hall there was nobody, just the key under the mat where the key had been under the mat since Eisenhower, and the old Hamilton upright on the little stage, scarred by decades of folding-chair collisions, its ivories yellow as October grass. He tuned it true. He tuned them all true. It had occurred to him, somewhere in the last few years, that he was no longer really tuning pianos. He was tuning rooms. He was setting in order something that the people who hired him could not name but knew was askew — putting one thing right in houses where so much had gone past righting, so that a person walking through the parlor could lift the fallboard and strike a key and hear, in the clean column of that one note, proof that care had been taken. That somebody had come, and knelt, and listened, and made it true. Whether anyone ever struck the key was almost beside the point. Almost.

The light was going long and amber when he came at last up the rise to Bethel Lutheran. The church stood where it had stood since 1894, white clapboard, steeple like a sharpened pencil, the cemetery behind it inside its iron fence, the parking lot gravel, the whole of it surrounded by harvested fields so that it looked, in the low light, like a ship at anchor on a brown sea. Services were once a month now, a circuit pastor, eleven or twelve in the pews on a good Sunday. But the council kept the building painted and the furnace ran, and Pastor Lindqvist — retired himself, filling in, everyone out here was filling in for someone — had left the door unlocked for him, as promised, and a note on the table in the narthex that said Emil — take all the time you need. Lock up or don't. God's not particular. — D.L.

The sanctuary held the day's last light in its high windows. The piano stood to the right of the chancel where it had always stood, a Yamaha console that had replaced the old Kimball in 1981 — he had moved the Kimball out himself, with three other men and a complaining dolly — and to the left stood the organ nobody could play anymore since Eunice Halvorson's stroke. He set his case on the front pew. The sound of the latches opening was very loud in the empty church.

He had not let himself think about it all day, and now he let himself think about it.

May 14. Peter had been nine. Not eleven — Emil had told himself eleven for years, and then found the date in Greta's things after she died, written on the back of the photograph, and it was nine, Peter had been nine, in a clip-on tie, and his feet in their hard church shoes had not quite reached the pedals, so Mrs. Voss had put a wooden box there, a crate stenciled SUNBEAM BREAD, for the children to rest their feet on. Fourth on the program. The Sonatina in G, the one every child played, the one Emil had heard through a hundred parlor walls on his rounds, moderato and then the little romanze, three minutes of music at the outside. Peter had walked up the aisle too fast, the way boys do, and sat, and looked at his hands, and started.

And the thing was — the thing Emil had spent fifty years trying to say to himself accurately, because he distrusted the way grief improved its stories — the thing was that it had not been remarkable. The boy had played it well, for nine, a stumble in the left hand in the romanze, recovered. What had been remarkable was only this: that midway through, Emil, sitting in the fifth pew with Greta's hand in his, had stopped hearing the wrong notes. He, who heard everything, who could not sit in a room with an out-of-tune piano any more than another man could sit in smoke, who heard the sour third in the radio jingle and the flat string in the school orchestra — he had stopped auditing. The music had come down the aisle of the church his grandfather had helped to build, played imperfectly by his son on an instrument he himself had tuned the day before, and for the length of it he had not weighed or measured anything at all. He had simply listened. He could count on his fingers the times in his life that had happened. Most of them were that one time.

There was no second recital. Not because of the thing people always assumed when he said first and only — there was no drama in it, no tragedy then, that came later and had nothing to do with pianos. Peter simply quit, the next fall, the way children quit, for baseball and then for engines, and Greta said let him, you can't pour music into a boy like fuel oil, and she was right, and Emil had agreed, and had meant it, and had driven his route for years past farms where some other man's child was practicing scales through the window screen, and had felt nothing he could decently complain of. Peter had grown up good with engines. Had gone where young men good with engines went, in 1968. The letters had come on thin blue paper, and then the letters had stopped coming, and then the other letter came, on different paper, and the funeral had been here, in this sanctuary, eleven steps from where the piano stood, and Mrs. Voss had played, because Greta asked her to, and Emil had sat in the fifth pew and heard, that day, every wrong note in the world.

He tuned the church piano in the failing light, and did not turn the lamps on until he had to. It was not badly out — he had tuned it in the spring, the building was kept at an even heat, the Yamaha was a stable, sensible instrument with no poetry in it and no vices either. He could have been done in forty minutes. He took two hours. He pulled the temperament twice, the second time for no reason a paying customer would have accepted, getting the fifths to beat at exactly the rate his father had taught him, just under one beat a second at the bottom of the temperament octave, a slowness you felt more than counted, like a pulse in the wrist of someone sleeping. He voiced three hammers that had gone glassy, pricking the felt with the needles, softening the strike, and he leveled a key that sat a hair low, a hair nobody but he would ever have seen, and he tightened the bench bolts, and he cleaned the keys with the cloth he kept for it, white keys first, then black, the way you were taught so the ebony stain wouldn't ghost across the ivory, though these were plastic and could not stain, and he did it anyway, because the order of things was the order of things.

Then there was nothing left to do, and he knew there was nothing left to do, and he sat down on the bench.

The church was dark beyond the one lamp. The windows had gone from amber to gray to nothing. He could hear the furnace far below and the wind working at the northwest corner of the building the way it had worked at it for a hundred and thirty years without result. He took the program out of his breast pocket and unfolded it and laid it on the music desk where the hymnal usually sat, and in the lamplight he read the names at last, the purple ink almost gone, and there it was, fourth from the top, Peter Sorensen — Sonatina in G (Beethoven), and his eyes did what they did, and he let them, because there was no one to see, and because Greta had told him once, near her own end, that the trouble with him was he tuned his grief like everything else, easing the pin a degree at a time, never letting it sound full out, and that someday he would have to just strike the string and let it ring.

He set his hands on the keys. He had never been a player, not really; he was a listener, a man who served music the way a sexton serves a church, keeping it swept and warm for others. But every tuner can play a little, and every father who has sat through a child's practicing has the piece in his fingers somewhere, worn into him like a path through grass. He found the G with his right thumb. He began.

He played it slowly, and badly in places, his left hand stumbling where Peter's had stumbled, in the romanze, the same measure, which seemed to him a thing the piece itself remembered and required. The notes went up into the dark of the sanctuary one at a time and came back down changed, the way sound does in a high wooden room, rounded and forgiven. The piano was perfectly in tune. Every interval was telling its small necessary lie, and the lies were all in agreement, and the agreement was beautiful, and somewhere in the second page he stopped hearing the wrong notes. He stopped auditing. For three minutes, in the empty church at the end of the route, the old man at the piano was not weighing or measuring anything at all.

When it was finished he sat with his foot on the pedal and let the last chord ring, and ring, the strings giving it up slowly, the way strings do, the sound thinning to a thread, to a suspicion, to the memory of a sound, and he kept the pedal down long after any ordinary ear would have called the church silent, because his was not an ordinary ear, and he could still hear it, the faint gold ghost of G major hanging in the dark above the pews, and he did not lift his foot until even he could not honestly say whether what he heard was the piano or himself.

Then he closed the fallboard, and packed the flannel around the hammer his father had bought him, and latched the case, and turned off the lamp. At the door he stood a moment with his hand on the switch panel, looking back into the dark where the piano stood in tune, and would stand in tune for a season, holding the work whether anyone came to find it or not.

He locked the door. Pastor Lindqvist had said God wasn't particular, but Emil was, and always had been, and it was much too late to stop.
