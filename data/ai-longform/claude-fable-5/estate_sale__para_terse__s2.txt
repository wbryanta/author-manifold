The estate sale company had priced her mother's dining table at one hundred and twenty-five dollars, and by one o'clock a man in a Bengals cap was offering ninety, and Diane heard herself say that would be fine before she had fully understood what she was agreeing to. He had a son with him, a teenager with earbuds in, and together they tipped the table on its side in the dining room where Diane had eaten approximately eleven thousand meals, and walked it out the front door into the white March light of Brandt's Mill, Ohio, and that was that. The table had been in the house since 1961. Her mother had paid for it on layaway from Stechschulte's Furniture in Lima, eight dollars a month, and had told that story so many times it had worn a groove, the way she'd driven up Route 65 every fourth Saturday with the payment in an envelope because she didn't trust the mail with money.

Diane stood at the front window and watched the man and his son fit the table into the bed of a pickup, legs up, like a dead animal. The son threw a moving blanket over it without being asked, which she appreciated, then resented herself for appreciating. It was a table. It was wood and varnish and one burn mark from a casserole dish in 1974 that her mother had covered with a trivet for fifty years rather than refinish.

The woman running the sale was named Sandy Kowalczyk, and she had a fanny pack of small bills and a way of saying "everything's negotiable, hon" that Diane had found grating on the phone and now found weirdly soothing in person. Sandy's company was called Fresh Start Estate Services, which Diane thought was a name designed by someone who had never stood in a dead mother's living room while a stranger held up her dead mother's gravy boat and asked if it was Pfaltzgraff. The flyer Sandy had stapled to telephone poles around town said EVERYTHING MUST GO, which was true the way a weather report is true, plainly and without mercy.

It was the second day of the sale and the good stuff had mostly gone yesterday, Sandy told her, the Singer in the cabinet, the cedar chest, the bedroom suite. Diane had not come yesterday. She had driven down from Cleveland Heights this morning, two and a half hours of flat brown fields under a sky like dishwater, telling herself she didn't need to be present for any of it, that Sandy had keys and a contract and a thirty-five percent commission, and then she had taken the exit anyway, and then she had taken Cable Road, and then she was sitting in the driveway behind a strange Buick at eleven forty in the morning, and a woman she didn't know was coming out of her mother's front door carrying her mother's ironing board.

The house was a story and a half, white aluminum siding, built in 1948 by men who had just come back from the war and wanted everything square and modest and done. Her father had bought it in 1959 with his Lima Locomotive Works wages, before the works closed, before he moved over to the Ford plant, before any of the things that became the architecture of Diane's childhood. He had died in 1998, in the recliner that Sandy had sold yesterday for forty dollars, and her mother had lived in the house alone for twenty-six more years, refusing Florida, refusing assisted living in Lima, refusing finally even the woman from county services who came to check on her, until in January she had refused, in her sleep, at ninety-one, to wake up. Her neighbor found her because the kitchen light had been on all night and that wasn't like Lorraine.

Diane moved through the rooms with her coat still on. It was cold in the house because the front door stood open for the traffic of strangers, and the furnace, which her mother had kept at sixty-five her whole widowhood out of a thrift so deep it was almost a religion, couldn't keep up. People drifted in twos and threes, mostly women, mostly her own age or older, picking things up and turning them over to look at the bottoms, the universal gesture of the estate sale, as if the truth of an object were always underneath it. In the kitchen a woman was going through the drawer of utensils, the potato masher and the egg beater and the church-key opener, all of it heaped now in a cardboard flat marked $1 EACH OR 6 FOR $5, and Diane watched her select the masher, the one with the green-painted wooden handle worn pale at the grip, and thought, my mother's hand was the thing that wore that paint away, and the woman would never know it, and there was no way to tell her that wouldn't sound deranged.

"You're the daughter," the woman said, not looking up. Small towns. Diane said she was. "Lorraine was in my circle at St. John's. She made the best pierogi at the Lenten dinners, every year, nobody could touch her." The woman held up the masher. "This is probably the very one."

"Probably," Diane said, and the woman pressed two dollars into her hand instead of Sandy's, folding Diane's fingers over the bills like she was giving her something instead of taking it, and Diane stood there holding the money while the woman left with the masher in her purse.

Her mother was not Polish. Her mother was German on both sides, Brandt's Mill stock going back to the canal days, but she had married into a parish full of Kowalczyks and Nowaks and had learned pierogi the way she learned everything, by deciding she would and then grimly outperforming everyone who'd grown up doing it. Diane could see her at the kitchen counter, that counter, the yellow laminate with the gold boomerang pattern, pinching dough with her thumbs, a cigarette going in the ashtray on the windowsill until 1983 when she quit cold the day Diane's father had his first heart scare, and never spoke of it again, the way she never spoke of anything she'd defeated.

In the living room two men were negotiating with Sandy over the piano. It was a Wurlitzer spinet, walnut, and Diane had taken lessons on it from Mrs. Dieckmann for six years, every Tuesday at four, hating most of it, and had quit when she made varsity volleyball, and her mother had said fine, quit, but the piano stays tuned, and it had stayed tuned, forty-five more years, tuned every spring by a blind man named Carl and then by Carl's son, for no one. Nobody had played it since maybe 2001, Diane at Christmas, badly, "O Holy Night" with her father eleven months dead and her mother standing behind her not singing. Sandy was asking three hundred. The men were offering one fifty and saying things about how nobody wants pianos anymore, you can't give them away, they were doing her a favor, they'd have to rent a dolly. They were not wrong. Diane had read the same articles everyone had read, landfills full of pianos, the instrument of the American parlor century dying in a single generation. She caught Sandy's eye and gave a small nod, and Sandy said two hundred and the men said one seventy-five and Sandy said done, and the men high-fived, actually high-fived, in her mother's living room, and Diane went out the back door and stood on the stoop in the cold.

The backyard was the same. That was the thing about the backyard. The maple her father had planted the year she was born was sixty feet tall now and somebody else's problem. The clothesline poles still stood, T-shaped, galvanized, the lines themselves long gone, and she could see her mother out there in any weather above freezing, wooden pins in a canvas apron, because the dryer cost money and the sun was free, sheets cracking in the wind off the fields. The neighbor's dog, a new dog, a dog Diane didn't know, came to the chain-link and looked at her, and she looked at it, and neither of them had anything for the other.

Her brother had not come. Gary lived in Phoenix and had said, when she called about the sale, that he trusted her judgment, that he didn't need anything from the house, that the past was the past, which was the kind of thing Gary had been saying since approximately 1979, when the past was barely the past at all. He had taken his settlement with their mother out in distance, two thousand miles of it, and she had taken hers out in proximity, the dutiful daughter in Cleveland, close enough for guilt to operate efficiently, and it was hard to say anymore which of them had paid more. He'd flown in for the funeral, stood at the graveside at Gethsemane Cemetery in a topcoat too light for January, and flown out the next morning. At the lunch after, in the church basement, he had eaten two pieces of sheet cake and told their cousin Donna that Mom had been a complicated woman, which in the family dialect was the closest thing to an accusation anyone was permitted to make.

Complicated. Diane turned the word over while she watched the dog. Her mother had been about as complicated as a hammer. She had been hard and useful and built for one purpose, which was to keep a household running through whatever came, layoffs, recessions, a husband's heart, a son's silence, and she had done it, and the cost of it had been distributed among everyone in her vicinity in ways that were still being calculated. She had never once in Diane's hearing said the words I love you, and had also never once missed a single thing Diane did, not a recital, not a game, not a graduation, sitting in the bleachers with her purse on her knees like a woman waiting for a bus, and which of those facts was the true one was the kind of question that could eat a whole life if you let it.

Inside, somebody wanted to know about the Hummels. Diane came back in to find Sandy holding the little boy with the umbrella, three more figurines lined up on the mantel like defendants. The asking woman had reading glasses pushed up into her hair and the firm pleasantness of a serious collector. Diane's mother had received the Hummels one per Christmas from her own sister, Aunt Trude, between 1968 and 1985, and had displayed them with a fidelity that Diane had understood, eventually, to have nothing to do with liking them. You displayed what your sister gave you. That was the whole of the law. Trude had been dead since 2004 and the Hummels had gone on standing on the mantel another twenty years, a monument to an argument neither sister would ever have admitted to winning. The collector bought all four for sixty dollars and wrapped them in newspaper she had brought herself, sheet by sheet, with a tenderness that Diane found she could not watch.

She went upstairs. The sale was downstairs and in the garage; the upstairs had been mostly emptied by Sandy's crew into the staging that was the downstairs, and the two small bedrooms under the slope of the roof stood bare except for the things nobody would buy. Her old room. The wallpaper had been changed twice since it was hers but the window was the window, looking down on the driveway and across Cable Road at the Brinkmans' house, which was now somebody else's house, everything in this town was now somebody else's house. She had lain on a bed under this slope of ceiling and listened to WLS out of Chicago on a transistor radio, the signal skipping in off the night sky, and had understood with total clarity at fifteen that her life would happen elsewhere, and she had been right, and standing here at fifty-six in an empty room she could not have said what exactly the rightness had gotten her. A marriage, ended amicably, which was its own small indictment. Two children grown and decent and far away, one in Seattle, one in Brooklyn, both of whom had said gentle useless things on the phone about how hard this must be, Mom, and both of whom had asked, carefully, whether there was anything of Grandma's they should keep, in the tone of people asking whether they needed to check a bag.

In the closet of her old room, on the shelf, Sandy's crew had missed a shoebox. Diane took it down expecting Christmas ornaments or canceled checks, her mother kept canceled checks the way other people kept photographs, and inside instead were photographs. Not the albums; the albums were downstairs, priced, unbelievably, at five dollars apiece, FAMILY PHOTOS INCLUDED, which Diane had told Sandy to pull from the sale and Sandy had said hon, you'd be surprised, people buy them for the frames. These were loose, curled, black and white with deckled edges, and they were of her mother before any of it. Lorraine at maybe seventeen, 1950, '51, sitting on the hood of a car with her ankles crossed, laughing at whoever held the camera with her head thrown back. Lorraine in a swimsuit at what must have been Lake St. Marys, hands on her hips, mugging. Lorraine and two other girls in front of the Ohio Theatre in Lima, arms linked, all of them mid-stride, caught walking toward the photographer like they owned the sidewalk and the town and the decade. Diane sat down on the bare floor of her childhood bedroom and went through them twice. There were perhaps thirty. In not one of them was her mother the woman Diane had known. The woman Diane had known did not throw her head back. The woman Diane had known smiled for cameras the way you signed a form. Somewhere between the girl on the hood of the car and the woman with the purse on her knees, something had been traded away, and her mother had never said what, or for what, and now the transaction was sealed permanently, and all Diane had was the before picture, a fistful of before pictures, of a laughing stranger she would have liked, she thought, very much to have met.

She heard the front door downstairs, voices, the scrape of something heavy. She put the photos back in the box and the box under her arm and went down.

They were taking the davenport. That was her mother's word, davenport, nobody said it anymore, the gray-green couch with the nubbled upholstery that had replaced the previous couch in 1989 and was therefore, in the geological time of that household, practically new. A young couple, late twenties maybe, the girl pregnant enough that she carried nothing and directed everything, the boy and his friend walking the couch out the door in the shuffling crouch of amateur movers. "It's so clean," the girl said to Diane, apologetic and delighted. "You can't find them this clean. Everything's so cheap and it's all so clean." Her mother would have loved that, would have lived on it for a week. Cleanest house at her own estate sale. The girl had also bought, Diane saw, the kitchen clock, the one with the cord, electric, that had hummed above the stove for sixty years, and Diane almost told her, it runs four minutes fast, my mother knew and never fixed it, she said she'd rather be early her whole life than late once, and didn't, because the clock would run fast in a new kitchen now and the girl would figure it out herself, or wouldn't, and either way the joke now belonged to no one.

By three the traffic thinned. Sandy and her helper, a silent enormous nephew named Brett, began consolidating, carrying the unsold things into fewer and fewer rooms, and the house started to do the thing Diane had been bracing for since morning, which was to become rooms. Just rooms. The dining room without the table was a smallish square room with worn carpet and a chandelier from 1985. The living room without the piano and the davenport and the recliner was a box with a picture window. She had read that this happened and had not believed it would happen to her, the way you don't believe weather reports, and here it was: the house was leaving the house. What remained was a structure in a soft market in a county that had been losing population since before she left it. The realtor had been honest. Don't expect much, he'd said. It's a solid little house. There's just not the demand.

The neighbor came over around three thirty, the one who had found her mother, a widow herself named Carol Yoder, and stood in the kitchen with Diane and did not pretend to shop. "I keep looking over and seeing the cars," Carol said, "and I had to come tell you before it was all gone. Your mom, the last few years, she'd watch for my kitchen light, and I'd watch for hers. We never talked about it. But if mine wasn't on by seven she'd call, and if hers wasn't on, well." Carol looked at the window over the sink, at the windowsill where the ashtray had sat and then, for forty years, a violet in a plastic pot. "That's how I knew. The light." She took Diane's hand briefly, dry firm grip, her mother's whole generation had that grip, and said, "She talked about you all the time. Diane this, Diane that. Diane's getting an award from the hospital. Diane's kids. She kept the article on the refrigerator till it yellowed." Diane had not known there was an article on the refrigerator. The refrigerator had been hauled away two weeks ago by the appliance man, and whatever was on it had gone wherever those things go, and she stood in the kitchen and felt the specific vertigo of learning you were loved in a dialect you were never taught to hear, and that the last fluent speaker had died in January.

She bought back three things from her own mother's sale. She paid Sandy out of pocket and Sandy tried to wave it off and Diane insisted, because the accounting mattered, because her mother would have insisted. The potato masher she could not buy back; it was gone, it was in a purse in a Buick somewhere on Route 81. She took instead: the trivet, cast iron, shaped like a rooster, that had covered the burn on the table for fifty years and now had no burn to cover; one canning jar from the dozens in the cellar, a blue Ball jar with a zinc lid, because the cellar smell of those shelves, vinegar and dust and cold concrete, was the closest thing her body had to a definition of the word mother; and the photographs, the shoebox, which had no price because Sandy had never seen it, and which Diane paid five dollars for anyway and made Sandy write a receipt. Family photos. $5. Sandy wrote it without comment, and Diane folded the receipt into her wallet like evidence.

At four thirty Sandy started the half-price hour and the last stragglers picked the bones, lamps, the card table, the box fan, a sack of zippers and rickrack from the sewing room, somebody's whole afternoon of small intentions walking out the door at fifty cents an item. At five Sandy counted out, snapping bills, licking her thumb, and gave Diane a figure that would not quite cover the funeral flowers, and said what she presumably said to all of them, "It went good, hon. It all went to people who'll use it. That's what they'd want." Diane signed where Sandy pointed. Brett carried out the last unsold odds to the truck for donation, and then the truck pulled out, and then the strange cars were gone from the street, and Diane stood alone in the house for the first time since the morning her mother was buried.

She walked the rooms once with her coat on and the shoebox under her arm. The light was going, the long gray Ohio dusk that starts at four in March and takes its time. In the empty dining room the carpet showed the table's ghost, four pale circles where the feet had stood since Eisenhower, the pile crushed beyond any vacuuming, and she stood looking at the marks for a while. Eight dollars a month. The envelope, the Saturday drives. The story worn into its groove. She thought about turning up the furnace and didn't; the gas cost money and the house was sold to no one yet and her mother's thermostat ethics turned out to be load-bearing, turned out to be in her own hands now like the wear in a green-painted handle.

In the kitchen the last light came through the window over the sink, falling on the yellow counter where the dough had been pinched, where the cigarette had burned, where the violet had sat. The room hummed faintly, and she realized after a moment that it didn't, that the hum she heard was the memory of the clock, four minutes fast, sold and gone, that her ear was supplying the sound the way an amputee supplies a limb. She'd rather be early her whole life than late once. Diane stood at the sink until the light failed, and then she did the only thing left in the house to do, the thing two old women had done for each other across a side yard for years without ever speaking of it. She reached over and turned on the kitchen light, and let it burn, and locked the front door behind her, and stood in the driveway a moment in the cold, looking back at the one lit window, before she got in the car and drove north.
