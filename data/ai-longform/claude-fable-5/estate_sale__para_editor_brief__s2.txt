By one o'clock the dining room table was gone, and Peg stood in the space where it had been, looking at the four pale rectangles its feet had pressed into the carpet over fifty-one years. A man named Kowalski and his son-in-law had carried it out in two pieces, the leaves separately, wrapped in moving blankets the estate company provided, and Peg had held the storm door for them the way her mother used to hold it for the Schwan's man, hip against the frame, saying watch the step, watch the step, there's a lip there. The table had cost a hundred and forty dollars. Her father had bought it in 1968 from a furniture store in Lima that no longer existed, in a town that had since lost its glass plant and most of its downtown, and he had refinished it twice in the garage with a chemical stripper that made the whole yard smell like a hospital, and now it was riding away strapped in the bed of a Silverado to Kowalski's daughter's house in Findlay, where, the son-in-law said, they'd been looking for a real wood table, everything anymore was veneer.

"Everything anymore," Peg agreed, and went back inside.

Brenda from Two Sisters Estate Services had set up her cash box on a card table in the front hall, with a roll of orange stickers and a calculator and a thermos of coffee she'd brought from home. Brenda was sixty-something, broad in the shoulders, with reading glasses on a beaded chain, and she ran sales like this every weekend from Lima to Bucyrus, and she had told Peg on the phone, kindly but plainly, that the family member should not be present if the family member could help it. People dickered, Brenda said. People said things about the merchandise. It was easier on everybody if the family stayed home and got a check in the mail.

Peg had come anyway. She had driven up from Columbus that morning in the dark, two hours of fog lifting off the bean fields, and she could not have said exactly why, except that the alternative was sitting in her townhouse imagining it, which seemed worse, and also that there was no one else. Her brother Dale lived in Tucson and had flown in for the funeral in March and flown out four days later, having claimed their father's hand tools, the deer rifle, and a framed photograph of the 1971 Latham Mills football team on which he appeared, second row, helmet under his arm. Everything else, he had said, generously, magnanimously, standing in the kitchen with his suitcase already by the door, everything else was hers to deal with.

So she was dealing with it. It was the second Saturday of October, warm for October, the maples on Sycamore Street going orange at the crowns, and strangers were moving through her mother's house touching things.

That was the part no one had prepared her for, the touching. She had braced for the selling, the prices, the sight of the furniture going out the door. She had not braced for the woman in the purple windbreaker who stood at the china cabinet — the cabinet itself stickered at eighty-five dollars — opening and closing its glass door, opening and closing, testing the little brass latch, the latch Peg's mother had asked her to oil with sewing machine oil maybe four years ago, because Dorothy by then did not trust herself on the step stool and the oil was on the high shelf in the pantry. The woman in the windbreaker opened and closed the door and frowned, as though the latch had been put on this earth to disappoint her, and then drifted on toward the kitchen without buying anything.

"You get used to it," Brenda said from the card table, not looking up from her receipt pad. "Or you don't, and then you should probably go sit in your car for a while. No shame in the car."

"I'm fine," Peg said.

"Everybody's fine," Brenda said. "I've had grown men go sit in the car over a tackle box."

The house was a story and a half, white aluminum siding, built in 1954, bought by her parents in 1962 for eleven thousand five hundred dollars, a figure her father had repeated all his life with a kind of disbelieving pride, as though he'd gotten away with something. Three bedrooms, one bath, a kitchen that looked out on the backyard and the alley and the Hartzlers' garage. Her mother had lived in it sixty-one years, the last nine of them alone, the last two of them with a woman named Cheryl coming three mornings a week to help with the bath and the pills, and she had died in it, in March, in the back bedroom, in the bed that was now stickered at forty dollars, frame and headboard, mattress not included because nobody bought a mattress at an estate sale, the mattress had gone to the curb on Tuesday and been taken by someone before the trash truck came, which Peg tried not to think about.

Now there were perhaps a dozen people in the house and more arriving in twos and threes, parking along Sycamore where the curb was crumbling. Some of them she half recognized, faces from church or from the IGA or from her childhood gone soft and gray. A man stood in the living room running his thumb along the arm of her father's recliner, which had not been her father's recliner exactly — that one had worn out in the nineties — but its successor's successor, a blue corduroy La-Z-Boy stickered at twenty-five dollars in which her mother had spent most of her last winter watching game shows with the volume up loud enough to hear from the driveway. The man pulled the lever and the footrest banged up and he nodded the way men nod at machinery.

"This work all right?" he asked the room generally.

"It works fine," Peg said.

"They get broke, the mechanism."

"It works fine."

He bought it for twenty. Brenda made change from the cash box and wrote a receipt that nobody needed and the man and his wife wrestled the chair out through the front door, and where it had sat there was another shape pressed in the carpet, and a dust line on the wall where the headrest had touched, a faint oval of darker paint, like a shadow that had decided to stay.

Peg went into the kitchen and drank a glass of water from the tap. The glasses were stickered, four for a dollar, jelly-jar glasses with faded carousel horses on them, and she drank from one anyway and washed it and set it in the drainer, and then realized the drainer was stickered too, fifty cents, and that her washing it had been absurd, and she laughed, alone in the kitchen, one short laugh that didn't sound entirely like a laugh.

The kitchen window over the sink looked at the same backyard it had always looked at. The clothesline poles were still there, T-shaped, set in concrete by her father a lifetime ago, the lines themselves long gone. The garden plot along the alley fence had gone to grass years back, but you could still see where it had been, a rectangle of slightly different green, the way you can see old roads from airplanes. Her mother had stood at this window with her wrists in dishwater for sixty years, watching that yard. Watching Peg and Dale on the swing set, watching the laundry, watching the weather come in from the west the way it does in Ohio, the whole sky operating like a single slow machine. Peg stood where her mother had stood and felt, briefly and without warning, not grief exactly but vertigo, the sense of standing in a footprint much larger than her own foot.

"Excuse me." A young woman in the doorway, mid-twenties, ponytail, baby on her hip. "Is the sewing machine still here? The Facebook post had a sewing machine."

"In the back bedroom," Peg said. "The little one. It's a Singer."

"Does it run?"

"It ran in February." Her mother hemming a pair of Cheryl's work pants as a thank-you, bent to the machine in the lamplight, glasses down her nose, the motor's old burring hum that Peg could have identified blindfolded out of a thousand machines. "The foot pedal cord is wrapped up in the bench seat. There's thread in there too. And the attachments — the buttonholer and all that — they're in a green tin."

The young woman looked at her with sudden comprehension. "Is this your house?"

"My mother's."

"I'm sorry," the girl said, and meant it, and the baby grabbed at her ponytail, and Peg said it was fine, that she hoped the machine got used, that it was a good machine, that her mother had made her wedding dress on it, which was true, the first wedding, 1974, and the girl said oh my gosh, and bought the machine for thirty-five dollars, bench and tin and all, and her husband carried it out to a Corolla and Peg stood on the porch and watched it ride away down Sycamore Street and was surprised to find that this one, of all of them, was the one that wet her eyes. Not the table. The machine. There was no logic to it. Grief did not consult her about the order of things.

Her phone buzzed. Dale. How's it going. Don't let them steal you blind those estate people take 40%.

35%, she typed. It's going fine.

Hummels? he wrote.

Sold this morning, she wrote. Woman from Ada bought all 11. She did not tell him the price, which was less than he was imagining, because everyone's imagination had been set in 1987 when those figurines meant something, and Brenda had been gentle about it, the market for Hummels, she'd said, is people who already have Hummels, and they're trying to sell theirs too.

Should have done eBay, Dale wrote, and Peg put the phone in her pocket.

Through the early afternoon the house emptied in increments. The cedar chest went, after a long negotiation Brenda conducted at the card table with a dealer from Kenton who pointed out a water ring on the lid as though it were a moral failing, and Brenda came down ten dollars and not a penny more, and the dealer paid it, because they both had known he would, the whole exchange a kind of square dance with steps fixed generations ago. The bedroom set went to a couple furnishing their first house out on County Road 9 — the bed, the dresser, the vanity with the round mirror her mother had sat at every morning of Peg's childhood doing her hair, the smell of Aqua Net, the little cut-glass dish of bobby pins. Peg helped the wife carry the mirror to their truck herself, walking down the porch steps with her own face floating in the glass, then the porch ceiling, then the bare blue sky.

"We'll take good care of it," the wife said, which people kept saying, as though the furniture were livestock, as though it had feelings, and the strange thing was that Peg was grateful every time, grateful past all sense.

The piano did not sell. Nobody bought pianos anymore, Brenda had warned her; you couldn't give them away; she had a guy in Marion who hauled them for fifty dollars and took them, Peg did not ask where. The piano stood against the living room wall with its sticker — FREE TO GOOD HOME — and people walked past it all afternoon as if it were a radiator. It was a Wurlitzer spinet, walnut, bought in 1970 so that Peg could take lessons from Mrs. Eikenberry on Walnut Street, four years of lessons that had produced in Peg a permanent ability to play "Für Elise" badly and the first half of "The Entertainer" and nothing else. Her mother had never played at all. But she had dusted it weekly for fifty years, and kept sheet music in the bench, and on the top of it the school portraits had stood in their frames, Peg and Dale aging left to right across the walnut in two-year increments, gap-toothed to feathered hair to caps and gowns, and those photographs were now in a box in Peg's trunk, and the piano's top was bare except for the dust shadows of the frames, eight small standing ghosts.

A boy of about ten came in around two-thirty with his grandmother and pressed middle C, once, and looked around guiltily.

"You can play it," Peg said.

"I don't know how."

"Neither do I," she said, "and I had lessons," and the boy grinned and played a careful, terrible scramble of notes, both hands, and for a minute the house had music in it, and then the grandmother bought a Pyrex bowl and a snow shovel and they left.

In the back bedroom, in the closet, the clothes had already gone to Goodwill in August — that had been its own afternoon, its own weather system — but the shelf above the rod had held boxes, and the boxes were in the den now, stickered as lots: SEWING NOTIONS $5, CHRISTMAS $10, PHOTOS/PAPERS NOT FOR SALE. Peg had been through the papers twice and still kept finding things. That morning, taping the box shut, she had found a church cookbook from 1979, Latham Mills United Methodist Women, mimeographed and comb-bound, and inside it, in her mother's hand, marginal notes on other women's recipes — good but too sweet beside Arlene Hartzler's apple crisp, use butter NOT oleo beside someone's banana bread, and beside a casserole submitted by a Mrs. Donald Frey, simply, no, underlined. Peg had laughed out loud, alone in the den at seven in the morning, laughed and then cried briefly and efficiently, standing up, the way you cry at her age when there is a sale starting in two hours, and put the cookbook in her purse.

Now it was three o'clock and the light was changing, October light, low and gold and particular, coming through the west windows the way it had come every fall of her life, lighting up the dust the strangers' feet had raised. The crowd had thinned. Brenda had begun marking things down — orange stickers slashed with a Sharpie, half price, make an offer. A man bought the lawnmower out of the garage for fifteen dollars after pulling the cord eleven times, and Peg stood in the driveway while he loaded it and looked at the garage's interior, the pegboard with the painted outlines where her father's tools had hung, hammer-shape, wrench-shapes in descending sizes, the tools themselves in Tucson now, the outlines staying. Like the chalk lines at a crime scene, she thought, and then was ashamed of the thought, and then thought it was exactly the kind of thing her mother would have said, dry and a little dark, over the dishes, and felt better.

Mrs. Knecht's daughter came through around three-thirty. Peg knew her face before she could find the name — Susan, Suzanne — they had ridden bikes together until the Knechts moved to Fort Wayne in 1976. Susan was heavyset now and walked with a cane and was in town for her own errand of this kind, her aunt's place over on Garfield, and she had seen the sale signs and turned in off pure muscle memory.

"I used to be scared of your basement," Susan said.

"Everybody was scared of that basement."

"Your mom kept the canning down there. She'd send us down for green beans and I'd make you come with me."

"There's still canning down there," Peg said. "Brenda wouldn't sell it. Liability."

"Is the jar lid thing still on the wall? The — your dad nailed all the lids to a board and screwed the jars into them, for nails and screws."

"It's still there," Peg said. "Nobody wanted it."

"Nobody knows what anything is anymore," Susan said, without bitterness, just reporting, and she bought a corn-stick pan and a deviled-egg plate and hugged Peg on the porch, a real hug, and said your mom was a good lady, she gave me a Popsicle every single time, and Peg stood on the porch a while after she'd gone.

Her daughter texted from Chicago at four: how is it, do you need anything, and Peg looked at the question for a long time. The honest answers were unsendable. She typed almost done. House looks bigger empty, and her daughter sent back a heart, and that was right, that was all there was room for in those little boxes, and it was enough, nearly.

At four-thirty Brenda called the last markdowns, and a flurry of late bargain people moved through, the ones who timed it this way on purpose, and the end tables went, and the kitchen chairs, and the box of Christmas, somebody's whole December for ten dollars, the ornaments Peg and Dale had made of flour dough in 1969 still mixed in with the glass balls, and Peg let them go, she had decided weeks ago to let them go, you could not keep the flour-dough ornaments and also keep your footing, you had to choose, and she had chosen, and she stood at the window and watched the box ride off in a minivan and held her own elbows and let it.

What didn't sell: the piano. The china cabinet, in the end, the latch lady's frown apparently prophetic. A box of National Geographics. The portrait of a sailing ship that had hung over the couch since before memory, which everyone had always silently agreed was ugly and which now had no defenders. Brenda's donation guy would come Monday with a box truck and take all of it to a charity in Lima, and the piano man would come for the piano, and then the cleaners, and then the realtor's photographer, and by November the house would be on the internet at ninety-four thousand dollars, freshly painted inside, smelling of nothing, looking like no one.

Brenda counted the cash box at the card table while the last car pulled away. She wet her thumb and counted twice and wrote the figure on a receipt and turned it so Peg could see. It was less than the dining room table had been, in 1968 money, for everything, for the whole interior of sixty-one years. It was also exactly what Brenda had estimated, almost to the dollar, because Brenda knew her business, and Peg signed the paper and Brenda folded up the card table and the two of them stood in the front hall.

"You did good," Brenda said. "Staying. Most can't."

"I sat in the car once," Peg said. "Around noon."

"The sewing machine," Brenda said. It wasn't a question. "It's always the sewing machine or the workbench. Furniture's just furniture. The machines, that's their hands." She slung her bag over her shoulder. "Lock up good. Check's in the mail Wednesday."

Then the house was empty, and Peg was alone in it for what she understood would be close to the last time, and the sun was going down behind the Hartzlers' garage the way it did in October, fast and orange, throwing the window shapes long across the bare floors. Her footsteps had an echo now. She walked the rooms once, the way you check a hotel room before checkout. In the back bedroom the carpet held the dents of the bed's casters. In the bathroom, the medicine cabinet hung open and empty, and the shelf paper inside was a pattern of small yellow roses, and she had never once in her life looked at that shelf paper and now could not stop. In the hall, the doorjamb to the kitchen carried the pencil marks, PEGGY 1965 at a height below her hip, DALE climbing past her in '71 and never letting her forget it, the marks going up the white paint like a tide gauge, and these could not be sold or boxed, they would be painted over by a man with a roller in two weeks, and she stood with her finger on PEGGY 4 yrs for a while, and then took her phone out and photographed the jamb, badly, twice, knowing the photographs would not hold what the wood held, taking them anyway.

In the kitchen, the junk drawer had been emptied into a FREE box that no one had wanted, and she went through it standing at the counter in the last light: rubber bands gone brittle, a church key, birthday candles, the stub of a carpenter's pencil, two keys to nothing, a magnet from Stechschulte's Garage with a phone number whose exchange hadn't been used since the eighties, and one wooden spoon, the wooden spoon, its bowl worn flat on one side from sixty years of being right-handed against the bottoms of pots. Her mother had stirred everything with it. It had been waved at children. It had cooled on the saucepan's edge, balanced just so, ten thousand evenings. It was worth nothing, which is why it was still here, which is why everything that mattered was still here, in this box, free, unwanted, hers.

She took the spoon and the carpenter's pencil and the magnet, and after a moment the church key too, and put them in her purse with the Methodist cookbook, and washed the carousel glass again for no reason and dried it this time and put it in her purse as well, wrapped in a paper towel.

Then she turned off the kitchen light. She went through the house turning off lights, a thing her mother had done behind her for eighteen years, who do you think pays the electric, and pulled the front door shut and locked the deadbolt and the knob, and stood on the porch in the blue dusk. Down Sycamore the streetlights were coming on one at a time, not all at once, each on its own clock. Somewhere a leaf blower shut off and the quiet came up out of the lawns. The car was at the curb, packed: photographs, papers, one lamp she had not been able to do without, and now a purse containing a spoon.

Peg sat down on the porch step instead, just for a minute, the concrete cold through her jeans the way it had been cold through fifty Octobers, and watched the streetlight at the corner flicker and catch and hold. Behind her the house stood empty with its dark windows, lighter by sixty-one years, waiting for whoever was next. She would cry in the car, probably, somewhere around Kenton, at some bean field, and that would be all right. For now she sat where she had always sat, hip against the iron rail, and let the dark come down Sycamore Street, and when the chill finally got into her shoulders she stood, the way her mother used to stand, hand on the rail, a small grunt of effort she heard herself make and recognized, and went down the steps and did not look back at the house, and then, at the car door, looked back at the house.
