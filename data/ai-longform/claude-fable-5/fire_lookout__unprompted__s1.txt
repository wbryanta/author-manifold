The storm had come through after midnight without rain, all voltage and no mercy, and Helen had stood at the windows counting strikes the way she had counted them for twenty-six summers, saying the seconds under her breath. Eleven hits she could place with confidence. Four more behind Gospel Ridge where she could only see the glow come up against the clouds like something surfacing in dark water. She had written them in the log out of habit, though no one would read the log, though the log was hers now in the way that everything in the tower was hers now, which is to say it belonged to a program that had ended and a building that was scheduled to come down in October, panel by numbered panel, to be reassembled at a heritage site near the highway where people could climb the stairs and take photographs and never once be afraid of weather.

She slept badly and got up before the sun and made coffee on the propane ring and carried it out to the catwalk to do what she still did, decommissioned or not, which was to look. The whole eastern country was clean. Beaverhead, the Sixmile drainage, the long burn scar from '07 gone silver-green with regrowth. She moved her eyes the way old Pruett had taught her in her first season, in quadrants, slow, letting the country come to her instead of hunting it, because a hunting eye skips and a patient eye snags. Water dogs lifted off the creek bottoms, those innocent morning mists that fooled every rookie, and she named them and dismissed them one by one. By eight the dew was off the rails. By nine the air had that dry crackle to it, the smell like hot stone, and the little anemometer she'd nailed to the corner post twenty years ago, against regulations, was ticking around in a fitful southwest breeze.

She saw it at 9:40. She knew it was 9:40 because her hand wrote it down before her mind had fully agreed to what it was: a thin gray feather standing up out of the timber maybe nine miles off, due west and a little south, rising straight for a hundred feet and then shearing east where the wind found it. Not a water dog. Water dogs lay down in the drainages like sleeping animals. This stood up. This had a root.

She went inside to the fire finder. They had taken the radio in June, two men in a green Ford who had been apologetic about it, unbolting the Midland base from its shelf, coiling the antenna lead, carrying down the batteries one at a time like pallbearers, but they had left the Osborne because it was riveted to history more than to the floor, a brass and steel instrument from 1934 that nobody in the Supervisor's Office wanted the liability of moving. She lifted the dust cover off it now and laid it aside and bent to the sight, walked the ring around, settled the crosshair on the base of the smoke where it stood up out of a fold in the land, and read the azimuth twice because her hands wanted it to be wrong. Two hundred sixty-one degrees, thirty minutes. She straightened up and looked at the map under its yellowed acetate, the map she could have drawn freehand in the dark, and ran the bearing out from the little black circle that was her, and watched the pencil line cross Sixmile, cross the divide, and drop into the Tamarack Creek drainage and pass within half an inch of the small square her own hand had inked there years ago, before everything, with the letters D.M. beside it. Dee's place. Their father's place first, then Dee's, the way everything that had been theirs together had sorted itself into hers and hers.

Helen stood very still with the pencil in her hand.

It could be a half mile from the cabin. It could be two. The fold of the land at that distance swallowed precision; a smoke could stand up out of Tamarack and be anywhere along a mile of creek bottom, and the bottom was thick with lodgepole that hadn't burned in eighty years, ladder fuels all the way up, and the storm had walked right through there at one in the morning throwing fire at the ground.

She picked up the handheld from the shelf where it lived, the cheap programmable she'd bought herself when the Forest pulled the real radio, and keyed it and said her old call sign into it because there was nothing else to say, "Gospel Butte, Gospel Butte," and got the silence she had known she would get. The repeater on Carter Peak had been struck two weeks ago and the Forest had not fixed it because the Forest was watching this country now with cameras and a contract plane out of Salmon, and the cameras would see this smoke, probably already had, an algorithm somewhere was probably drawing a box around it on a screen in an office, and a plane would come over this afternoon or tomorrow morning and a crew would be ordered if it was worth ordering, and all of that would happen at the speed of budgets, and none of it knew or cared that there was a sixty-one-year-old woman in that drainage who had not answered Helen's letters in six years and who might at this moment be standing on her porch watching fire come down through the trees at her.

Or who might be in town. Dee went to town Thursdays. Helen knew this the way you know things about people you no longer speak to, which is to say from their one mutual cousin, in fragments, at Christmas. It was not Thursday.

The road was eight miles. Call it road; it was road for the first two miles below the tower, the old administrative track, washed to cobble and kneecapped by the slide in '19, and then it was trail, switchbacking off the shoulder of the butte through whitebark and down into the lodgepole, and it came out at the Tamarack trailhead where her truck sat under a tarp gone stiff with sun. From the trailhead the county road ran down the creek, and the spur to Dee's place cut off it at the old sheep bridge, three miles below. Eleven miles, then, to the cabin, eight of them on her own legs, downhill the whole way, which people who had never walked eight downhill miles thought was a gift.

She gave herself four minutes to pack and used six. Water, the three-liter and the spare bottle. The first-aid kit. A bandanna. The leather gloves she split wood in. Her jacket, because she had been cold on a fire line once in August and never forgot it. She wrote in the log, because the log was the last official thing left to her and she would keep its dignity if no one else would: 0940 smoke az 261°30' est 9 mi Tamarack drainage. Radio inop. Departing on foot 0952. Then beneath it, in the same hand, because there was no one to see: Dee's place is in that drainage. She propped the door open the regulation crack so the heat wouldn't blow the windows, and she did not lock anything, and she went down the four flights of stairs with her hand sliding on the rail the way it had ten thousand times, and at the bottom she stood for one second on the actual ground, which always felt strange after weeks aloft, soft and uncommitted, and then she walked.

The first two miles she could still see it whenever the road bent west: the column thickening, going from feather to plume, white-gray at the top and dirtier at the base, which meant it had gotten into heavier fuel. It was not yet what the old timers called organized. It was a fire still deciding what it wanted to be. She walked fast on the cobbled road, faster than was smart, feeling her knees take the grade, and she did arithmetic to keep from doing other things with her mind. Three and a half miles an hour if she pushed. Two and a half hours to the truck. Twenty minutes down the county road if the truck started, which it would, she ran it every Sunday. Call it three hours to the sheep bridge. A fire in lodgepole with an afternoon wind behind it could do terrible things in three hours. A fire in green creek-bottom timber on a morning like this might also just sit there and smolder and let the night humidity put it half to sleep. Fires were like people. You could know everything about the conditions and still not know what they would do.

When the road quit and the trail took over, the timber closed and she lost the smoke, and that was worse. As long as she could see it she could read it. Now there was only the smell, faint at first, then not faint, the sweet rotten incense of duff burning, riding up the slope on the wind that was supposed to be carrying the fire away from her, east and downstream, away from the cabin if the cabin was where she thought and the fire was where she thought, and she said this to herself in Pruett's voice, his flat instructional kindness: wind's your friend this morning, don't borrow trouble, and she answered him the way she always had, silently, insubordinately: the wind turns at two o'clock in that drainage. It turns up-canyon at two o'clock every cloudless day of summer. She had watched it do so from the tower for twenty-six years, watched the smokes of a dozen Tamarack fires lay east all morning and then stand up confused at midday and then bend west, upstream, up toward the sheep bridge and the spur and the small square inked on the acetate.

She had been on the tower when their father died. That was the whole accusation, when you boiled six years down to a sentence, and the worst of it was its accuracy. July of that bad smoke year, four fires on the district, the relief lookout quit in June and never replaced, and Dee calling the Forest office, who relayed it up on the radio in the dispassionate language of relays: your sister advises your father's condition has worsened, advises you should come if able. If able. Helen had stood holding the mic and looked out at the Sixmile smoke, which was forty acres then and crowning in the afternoons, and at the new start on Beaverhead that nobody but her had eyes on, and she had keyed up and said she would come down at the end of her shift Friday. He died on a Wednesday. She had told herself for six years that the words she chose were if I can be spared, that it was the country she was thinking of, the crews who worked under that smoke and needed someone above it, and for six years a quieter voice had kept the other ledger: that the hospital room frightened her in a way the lightning never had, that her father had stopped knowing her in March and she could not bear to be unknown by him again, that the tower was the one place on earth where her watching was worth something, and she had chosen it the way a drinker chooses, calling it duty.

At the funeral Dee had not raised her voice. That was the thing people misunderstood about Dee, hearing the story later; they imagined shouting. Dee had stood with her in the gravel lot of the funeral home in her good navy dress and said, You can see the whole county from up there, can't you. And Helen said yes. And Dee said, Everything except what's right in front of you, and got in her car. The letters Helen wrote afterward, two the first year, one each year since, had gone unanswered, and Helen had kept the cabin in her morning glass anyway, every day at first light, swinging the binoculars to that fold in the land and finding the gray roof and the woodsmoke standing off the stovepipe in cold weather, and she had never told anyone, because what was it. It was not an apology. It was just watching, which was the only fluent language she had, and the one her sister had no use for.

The trail dropped through the whitebark, through the krummholz bent like arrested wind, and into the lodgepole where the air went still and hot and resinous. Her knees were singing by the fourth mile. She ate huckleberries off the bushes at the switchback corners without stopping, staining her fingers, a habit from years of this trail, and she thought about the summer she and Dee had picked four quarts on this same slope when they were nine and twelve, their mother still alive then, the cabin still smelling of their mother's coffee and the rosehip tea she made and the rowboat their father patched every June with marine paint the color of a robin's egg, and how Dee had eaten her whole quart on the walk down and lied about it with a purple mouth, and their father laughing so hard at the lie that there was no possibility of punishment, and Helen, who had carried hers down intact, feeling the first cold edge of the thing she would feel all her life: that there was a way of being loved for your appetites and a way of being relied on for your restraint, and that she had been sorted early, and had cooperated with the sorting.

She heard the plane at one o'clock, or near it; she had stopped looking at her watch by then because the news was always the same news, you are not there yet. A high drone from the south, the contract air attack ship making its lazy recon circle, and she stood in a gap in the canopy and watched it ride the thermals over the divide and thought: good, it's on the board, somebody's writing numbers about it, and felt how little comfort there was in being seen by a system. The smell was strong now. Once, on a long straight stretch of trail, a single flake of ash came down through the trees in front of her, gray and weightless, rocking on the air like a leaf, and she watched it all the way to the ground.

The truck started on the second try. She left the tarp where it fell. The county road ran down along Tamarack Creek through the green bottom, and through the windshield she could finally see it again, and it had become a fire while she walked. The column stood off the north slope of the drainage maybe two miles ahead, leaning, dirty at its base, with that pulsing quality at the core that meant it was running in the understory and finding the occasional tree to take whole; every minute or so a black bloom rolled up through the gray where one candled. And the lean of it. She put her head out the window to be sure. The wind had turned. It was two o'clock in the Tamarack drainage and the wind had turned up-canyon, the way it turned every cloudless day, and the fire was backing and flanking westward now along the slope, upstream, and the sheep bridge spur was a mile inside the direction it was going.

She drove the spur faster than the spur allowed. The smoke came down to meet the road in tatters, sunlight going orange and particulate, and the creek flickered alongside through the trees, absurdly bright, hauling its cold indifferent water past everything. Then the timber opened into the old meadow, and there was the cabin, gray-roofed, intact, the garden fence, the woodshed, the rowboat overturned on sawhorses with its robin's-egg bottom to the sky, and there, between the meadow's edge and the fire's nearest finger, which was a creeping, smoking, ankle-high front working through the duff under the big trees a hundred yards off, throwing up flame only where it found a jackpot of dead branches, there was her sister.

Dee was in her garden clothes with a bandanna over her face and their father's long-handled shovel in her hands, and she was cutting line. Or she was doing what a person who had watched it done, decades ago, from a porch, believed cutting line to be, which was not far wrong: scraping the duff back to mineral soil in a ragged scratch maybe sixty feet long between the fire and the woodshed, throwing dirt on what crossed it. She had a garden hose, fed off the gravity tank on the slope, lying charged and trickling in the grass. She had been at it for a while. Helen could read that in the line itself, in how the early part of the scratch was wide and neat and the recent part was shallow and desperate, the work of arms that were sixty-one years old and had been swinging a shovel since morning.

Helen took the spare shovel from its nail on the woodshed wall, where it had hung since their father drove the nail, and pulled her gloves on walking. Dee saw her when she was thirty feet out. She stopped, straightened, and the two of them looked at each other through the smoke over the bandanna, over six years, and Helen waited for any of the sentences she had imagined on the way down, and Dee lifted her free hand and pointed at the south end of the scratch line where a finger of creeping fire had hooked around it and was making quietly for the dry grass of the meadow, and said, through the cloth, "It's getting around me on that end."

So Helen went to the south end and cut line.

They worked for three hours and spoke perhaps forty words, all of them operational. The fire was not, in the end, a monster; the monster ran east of them along the north slope and crossed the creek far downstream and would be eleven hundred acres by morning and have a name and a number; what came at the cabin was its trailing western flank, low and patient and survivable if you met it, and they met it. Helen widened the scratch to a real line, eighteen inches to soil, and tied it into the creek on the south end the way you were taught, anchor it to something that won't burn, and Dee worked the hose along behind her, knocking down what jumped. The wind faltered at six as it always faltered. The light went long and copper through the smoke. At some point Helen looked up from the line and saw that the snag that had started it all, a big lightning-shattered fir two hundred yards up the slope, split white down its whole length, had burned through at the base and fallen, and was lying in its own black ground, smoking like something exhausted.

When it was holdable, when there was nothing along the whole line but smolder and the slow gray breathing of the burned ground, they sat down on the porch step because their legs decided it, more or less simultaneously, the way the legs of sisters will. Dee pulled the bandanna down. Her face was striped with soot and sweat and her hands, Helen saw, were shaking with the fine fast tremor of spent muscle, and Helen looked at her own hands and found them doing the same. The rowboat had survived. The garden had survived, mostly; a corner of the fence was charred where an ember had found the post. Up the drainage the main fire put its column into the evening sky, leaning away from them now, somebody else's emergency, and a helicopter went over high with a bucket and did not slow.

"I saw it from the tower," Helen said. "This morning. Quarter to ten."

Dee was quiet for a moment. "Radio's out, I heard. They took it all out of there."

"In June."

"So you walked."

"I walked."

Dee looked out at the black ground, at the line, at the shovel lying where Helen had dropped it, their father's and the spare, side by side in the scorched grass. The creek kept up its noise. A nighthawk went over, booming, hunting the insects the smoke had stirred.

"You can see this place from up there?" Dee said. "From your tower. You can actually see it."

"The roof. The stovepipe. Smoke off the chimney when you've got the stove going." Helen turned the water bottle in her hands. "I glass it every morning. First light. Before anything else."

Dee didn't answer that. Helen hadn't expected her to; she knew what she had just confessed, six years of it, and she knew Dee knew, and they sat with it between them on the step like a third person neither of them was ready to introduce. Far off the fire muttered. Closer, in the black, a single stump glowed and dimmed and glowed, breathing.

"They're taking the tower down in October," Helen said. "Hauling it to the highway for the tourists. This is my last summer up there."

Dee bent forward, elbows on her knees, and looked at the burned ground for a long time, and when she spoke her voice had the gravel of smoke in it and something underneath the gravel that Helen had not heard in six years and had begun to believe she would never hear again.

"Then I guess you'll need somewhere to watch from," Dee said, and got up, knees cracking, and went inside to put the coffee on, leaving the door open behind her, and Helen sat on the step in the failing light with her father's shovel at her feet and watched the smoke stand off the drainage, the way she had watched all her life, except that now there was a lit window at her back, and the sound of water filling a pot, and she found she could keep her eyes on the fire and still know exactly what was right behind her.
