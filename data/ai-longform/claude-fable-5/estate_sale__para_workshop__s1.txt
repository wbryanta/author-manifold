The man who bought the kitchen table carried it out the front door upside down, its legs in the air like an animal that had given up, and Lisa stood at the bottom of the stairs with a roll of orange stickers in her hand and let him pass. He had a son with him, maybe seventeen, in a Bengals shirt, and the boy held the screen door open with his hip, and between the two of them they got it down the porch steps and across the grass to a pickup with a dog in the cab. Forty dollars. Her mother had bought that table in 1971 from the furniture store on Main Street that was now a vape shop, and Lisa had eaten ten thousand breakfasts at it, had done her homework on it, had sat at it the night her father told them about the layoffs at the plant, and had sat at it again in March with the hospice nurse, signing forms while her mother slept down the hall, and now it was going to live in somebody's garage in Coshocton County and hold paint cans, probably, or a deer rifle in November, and that was fine. She had told herself that all of it was fine before the sale opened at nine that morning, and she was going to keep telling herself that until five o'clock, when Sandy from Heartland Estate Services would count the cash box at the bottom of the stairs, take her thirty percent, and drive back to Zanesville.

It was the first Saturday of June and already hot in the way Ohio gets hot, wet and personal, and the house had no air conditioning except the window unit in her mother's bedroom, which had sold by ten fifteen. The front and back doors stood open and a box fan that had not sold turned slowly in the dining room, pushing the smell of the house around — old paper, lemon Pledge, and underneath it something Lisa could only call her mother, a smell she could not have described to anyone but would have known blindfolded in a strange city — pushing it around and out the doors, where it was being replaced, hour by hour, by the smells of strangers. Perfume. Cigarette smoke clinging to a man's shirt. Somebody's fast-food bag left on the porch rail.

"You doing all right, hon?" Sandy asked her, not for the first time. Sandy was sixty-something, broad and kind, with reading glasses on a beaded chain and a fanny pack she wore in front like a marsupial. She had done, by her own count, over four hundred of these sales. She had told Lisa on the phone in April, the family usually does better if they're not here. I've seen it. People will say things in front of you they wouldn't say at a funeral, and they don't even know they're doing it. But Lisa had driven the two hours from Columbus anyway, alone, because her brother Gary was in Phoenix and had managed the whole death and its aftermath by phone, in a voice that got warmer the further he was from anything he might be asked to do, and because the alternative was sitting in her own kitchen all day picturing it, which would be worse. She was almost sure it would be worse.

"I'm fine," Lisa said. "I'm going to do the upstairs stickers."

"You already did the upstairs stickers."

"Then I'm going to check on them."

Sandy looked at her over the reading glasses, the look of a woman who had stood in four hundred living rooms watching four hundred daughters and sons hover like ghosts at their own hauntings, and said, "There's pop in the cooler on the back porch. Drink something. It's a steam bath in here."

The upstairs was quieter. Most of what was up there had been priced low and picked over early — the bedroom set from the spare room where Gary used to sleep, the bookcase, the lamps. Her own old room had been her mother's sewing room for thirty years and was nearly empty now except for the closet, where the sale didn't reach, and where there was nothing left anyway but wire hangers and a winter coat of her mother's that Lisa had not been able to put in the Goodwill pile and had not been able to take home either, so it hung there in the dim by itself, navy wool, waiting for her to decide. She did not go in. She stood in the doorway of her mother's room instead and looked at the bed, stripped, the mattress with its orange sticker — $25, OBO — and the rectangle of brighter wallpaper where the mirror had hung over the dresser until eleven o'clock, when a young woman with a baby on her hip had bought the dresser and a man who might or might not have been with her had carried it out. The wallpaper was little blue flowers, forget-me-nots or something pretending to be, and her mother had hung it herself in the eighties, up on a ladder at fifty with pins in her mouth, and now there was this one rectangle of it that had never seen daylight, blue as the day it went up, and it looked to Lisa like a door painted on a wall in a cartoon, the kind of door only one character can ever get through.

From the window she could see the front yard, the card tables of smalls — that was Sandy's word, smalls, for the Pyrex and the ashtrays and the Christmas decorations and the milk glass — and the people moving among them with the slow, grazing patience of livestock. She recognized some of them. That was the thing about doing this in Hartwell instead of hiring it all out and staying away: she recognized people. Dale Yoder from the church was down there right now turning a bird feeder over in his hands, and earlier Mrs. Pruitt, who had taught Lisa fourth grade and had to be ninety, had been wheeled up the driveway by her granddaughter and had bought a tablecloth and held Lisa's hand with both of hers and said, Your mother ironed the church linens for forty years and never once asked for a thank-you, and Lisa had said, I know it, because she did know it, and Mrs. Pruitt had said, Well, and that had been the whole conversation, and it had nearly taken Lisa's legs out from under her in a way that none of the lawyer's calls or the death certificate copies — you needed so many copies, that was a thing nobody told you — had managed to do.

She went back down. On the stairs she passed a couple in their late twenties coming up, the girl saying, "— could strip it and do, like, a whitewash," and Lisa said excuse me and they said sorry and flattened against the wall the way you do, and she understood from the girl's phone, held up and filming, that they were going to buy the dining room hutch, the big oak hutch with the glass doors where the good china lived, and that the girl was going to paint it. White, probably, or that gray-green color, sage, that was on everything now. Lisa stood at the bottom of the stairs and listened to them open and close the glass doors above the dining room — no, that was wrong, the hutch was in the dining room, they were just walking around up there, the hutch was right here, ten feet from her, and a different woman entirely was standing in front of it.

"Is the china separate or does it come with?" the woman asked Sandy.

"China's separate. It's Noritake. Service for twelve, no chips. Sixty for the set."

"Sixty." The woman said it the way you'd repeat a strange word in a foreign language. She was Lisa's age, more or less, with a canvas tote that said LAKE LIFE. "Would you take thirty?"

Sandy glanced at Lisa, the agreed-upon glance, decisions over twenty dollars get checked with the family, and Lisa looked at the china through the wavy old glass. Her mother had gotten it piece by piece, she knew the story, everyone in the family knew the story, a place setting at a time from the grocery store promotion in the sixties, a teacup one month, a saucer the next, S&H green stamps and patience, four years to assemble service for twelve, and it had come out at Thanksgiving and Easter and the day Lisa got married and the day, eleven years later, that she came home and sat at the kitchen table — the table now riding to Coshocton County upside down — and told her mother the marriage was over, and her mother had said nothing useful but had gotten down the good china for just the two of them on a Tuesday, which Lisa understood now, at fifty-six, to have been the most articulate thing her mother ever did.

"Sixty," Lisa heard herself say. "It took her four years to put that set together. Sixty's already a crime."

The woman with the tote bag looked at her, recalibrating — ah, family — and there it was, the flicker of caution, of pity, of doing math about whether the pity was worth thirty dollars. "It's a beautiful pattern," the woman said, gentler now. "I'll take it. Sixty's fine."

"You want it wrapped?" Sandy said. "We got newspaper."

And so for twenty minutes Lisa wrapped her mother's china in the Coshocton Tribune at the dining room table that was not the dining room table anymore, just a surface waiting for its own buyer, and the woman with the tote bag helped, and they did not talk much, but the woman wrapped carefully, Lisa noticed, slowly, tucking the corners, and at the end she said, "I lost my mom in 2019. I bought half her stuff back from my own sister-in-law." She laughed a little. "It's at my lake place. I never use it. I just — I have it." And Lisa nodded and put the last teacup in the box and thought, that is the choice, then. You can have it and never use it, or you can let a stranger have it and never know. There was no third thing. She had spent the whole spring looking for the third thing.

The afternoon got hotter. Sandy made her drink a grape pop from the cooler, sitting on the back porch steps for ten minutes while the sale ran itself, and Lisa looked at the backyard, which was not for sale and was therefore the only part of the property that still entirely belonged to the dead. The peonies along the fence were blooming themselves stupid, big blowsy heads bent to the grass, ants all over the buds the way her mother always said had to happen — they can't open without the ants, Lisa Ann, that's just a fact — which was not a fact, Lisa had looked it up decades ago, the peonies open fine without ants, but she had never once told her mother, and now she was the only person on earth holding both halves of that, the wrong fact and the truth, and the wrong fact felt like the more valuable of the two. The clothesline was still up. The garden plot had gone to grass years ago, after the second hip, but you could still see the shape of it, a rectangle of slightly different green, the way you could see where the dresser had stood by the wallpaper. The whole property was rectangles of brighter color where something used to be.

Her phone buzzed. Gary: How's it going. Selling well? She typed Fine. Busy. and then, after a moment, They just bought the china, and watched the dots appear and disappear and appear, and finally: Good. One less thing. She put the phone face down on the step. Her daughter Em had texted that morning from Chicago — thinking of you today mom. don't let them lowball you on grandma's sewing machine, that thing is a tank — and that was the difference between her brother and her daughter, and possibly between men and women, or possibly just between Gary and everyone else.

The sewing machine. She went back in to check on it and found a girl crouched in front of it, college age, with a nose ring and a measuring tape, and the girl looked up with the guilty quickness of someone caught praying and said, "Is this a 401? It is, isn't it. It's a Singer 401."

"I genuinely don't know," Lisa said. "It was my mother's."

"It's a 401," the girl said. "These are — okay, I don't want to say this in front of the price tag, but these are incredible machines. They'll sew through leather. They'll sew through anything. They don't make them like —" She stopped herself. "Sorry. Is it weird if I'm excited? I feel like it's weird."

"What would you make with it?"

"I do upholstery," the girl said. "Like, I'm trying to. I redo old chairs. I've been using my roommate's machine and it's plastic garbage." She put her hand flat on the machine's beige enamel the way you'd touch a horse. "Did she sew a lot? Your mom?"

"Every curtain in this house," Lisa said. "My Halloween costumes. My prom dress, 1986, lavender, with sleeves you would not believe. She hemmed pants for half the town for twenty years. She had a coffee can full of buttons that I am not selling, by the way, I'm keeping the buttons, I don't know why, don't ask me why."

"You keep the buttons," the girl said, with sudden and total seriousness, as if Lisa had stated a law of physics. "Obviously you keep the buttons." And Lisa took the orange sticker that said $45 and wrote $30 on it, and when the girl protested she said it's going to a working home, that's worth fifteen dollars to me, and the girl carried the machine out to a Corolla with a coat hanger antenna, hugging it to her chest like a heavy beige baby, and that one didn't hurt. Lisa stood on the porch and watched the Corolla pull away and waited for it to hurt and it didn't. That was the thing nobody told you, along with the death certificates: you couldn't predict which ones would hurt. The mattress, nothing. The china, a deep ache she could manage. The kitchen table had gone through her like a knitting needle, and the sewing machine, which by rights should have been the worst, lifted off her like a bird.

By two thirty the house was thinning out, the sale and the crowd both. The hutch went — the young couple did buy it, and the girl filmed its passage down the porch steps for whatever audience she had, and said to the camera, you guys, wait till you see the glow-up on this piece, and Lisa stood inside the screen door where she couldn't be seen and thought, glow-up, and thought of her mother saying you don't paint oak, you'd paint oak over my dead body, and then thought, well, and had to sit down on the stairs for a second because she was laughing, or it was shaped like laughing, in the way that those things are sometimes shaped like each other.

Dale Yoder bought the bird feeder and also, after circling it three times, her father's recliner, which had outlived her father by twenty-six years because her mother would not part with it and had kept it in the den under an afghan like a shrine you could watch Jeopardy! from. Dale gave her forty dollars and his condolences in the same handshake and said, "Your dad and I bowled Tuesdays at Star Lanes, you know, before they tore it down," and Lisa said she remembered, and she did — the smell of the alley, lane wax and cigarettes, sitting in the molded plastic seats doing homework while the men's league rolled, her father's ball a green Brunswick with gold flecks, and where was that ball? It had not been in the house. It had not been in the basement or the garage. Somewhere between 1999 and now her mother had quietly let it go, had managed her own private estate sale across two decades, one object at a time, so slowly nobody saw it happening, and Lisa understood with a small cold drop in her chest that the house she had been grieving since March, the house full of everything, had itself been a remainder, the visible tenth, and that her mother had stood in rooms like this herself, alone, deciding, and had never once mentioned it.

The piano did not sell. Sandy said pianos never sold anymore, you couldn't give them away, the charities wouldn't even take them, and so the spinet sat in the living room with its sticker marked down from $100 to $50 to FREE — MUST MOVE YOURSELF, and people opened the lid and plinked at it and walked away. Around three a little girl, six maybe, came in with her grandfather and climbed onto the bench without asking anyone and played the only thing she knew, the first eight notes of "Heart and Soul," over and over, and her grandfather said honey, don't, and Lisa said, "She's fine. Let her." Her mother had played hymns on that piano on Sunday afternoons, badly and without shame, "In the Garden" and "Softly and Tenderly," her foot heavy on the pedal so the whole thing blurred into a wash of sound that came up through the floorboards into Lisa's room where she lay reading, and if Lisa had a single sound that meant childhood it was that, hymns through a floor, and now it was "Heart and Soul," eight notes, looped by a stranger's grandchild, and that was the last music the house would get, and it was fine. She said it to herself again. It was fine.

At four o'clock Sandy started the wind-down, the everything-half-off hour, and the last grazers moved through, and Lisa walked the rooms one more time. In the kitchen, the doorway to the dining room still had the pencil marks going up the trim — LISA 1975, LISA 1977, GARY 1977 a foot lower, GARY 1981 suddenly two inches above hers, the year he never let her forget — climbing the white-painted wood like a tide chart, and a man buying the box fan saw her looking and said, "Houses used to have those. My folks' place had those." He paused. "New owners'll paint over it first week, I expect. They always do."

"I expect they will," Lisa said.

He left with the fan, and the house went quiet behind him, and Lisa stood there and then went out to her car and got the screwdriver from the emergency kit Em had given her for Christmas, and came back in, and at four forty in the afternoon, in front of God and Sandy and nobody else, she put the flat head under the edge of the door trim and worked it loose, six feet of pine with eighty years of paint on it, popping the finish nails one by one, the wood crying out a little at each one, and Sandy came in from the hall and watched and said nothing, just held the bottom end as it came free so it wouldn't whack the floor. There was a stripe of raw, unpainted wall behind it, another rectangle, another door in the cartoon. Lisa stood holding the trim board like a vaulter's pole and felt slightly insane and entirely calm.

"I had a man take a stair banister once," Sandy said. "Whole banister. Said he learned to walk holding it." She took her glasses off and cleaned them on her shirt. "You'd be surprised what people take. It's never the valuable thing. I've watched people walk past sterling silver to get a spoon rest."

"It's in the car already," Lisa said. "The spoon rest. Shaped like a tomato. I took it Thursday."

"There you go," Sandy said.

They counted out at five. Two thousand one hundred and forty dollars, minus Sandy's cut, for the physical residue of two lives — three, counting her father's, four counting her own first eighteen years — and Sandy zipped the fanny pack and hugged her, smelling of sunscreen and peppermint, and said, "It's a good house. It held a lot," which was either the most generic thing a person could say or the truest, Lisa couldn't tell, and Sandy's van pulled out, and the street did what small-town streets do at five on a Saturday in June, which was nothing, gloriously nothing, a sprinkler going somewhere, a dog complaining about it.

Lisa went back inside. The rooms had their voices now, the way empty rooms do — her footsteps came back at her off the bare floors, and the light came in low and gold through the curtainless windows and lay down in long panels across the carpet, and in the carpet were the shapes: four pale dents where the hutch had stood since before she was born, the broad shadow of the piano's absence still ahead of it, since the piano sat there yet, free, unmoved, the last resident. She went upstairs and took the navy coat off its wire hanger and put it on, in the June heat, in the empty sewing room, and put her hands in the pockets, and in the left one there was a wadded Kleenex and a peppermint and a grocery receipt from January — milk, bananas, bird seed, $11.83 — and she stood in the coat and read the receipt twice and put it back in the pocket, exactly where it had been, because it belonged to the coat now, it was the coat's, and she would keep them together.

She carried the coat and the trim board out to the car and laid the board across the folded back seat, the pencil names riding faceup, and stood in the driveway a moment. Through the front window she could see the piano in the gold light, lid open, waiting, and on Monday she would call the church, or the school, or she would not, and it would go to the landfill with the mattress and the box of curtain rods, and either way it had played this afternoon, eight notes, a child who didn't ask permission, which was the only kind of grace her mother had ever trusted anyway, the kind that just walked in.

She locked the front door, and then, out of forty years of habit her hand performed before she could think, she went around and checked that the back door was unlocked, the way her mother always left it — in case somebody needs in — and she stood with her hand on the knob for a long moment, the metal warm from the day, the peonies bent over in the side yard, the ants working the buds open whether they were needed or not, and then she locked that one too, and walked to the car, and drove out past the vape shop and the church and the lot where the bowling alley used to be, doing twenty-five the whole length of Main Street, the speed limit of her childhood, the trim board shifting softly behind her at every turn like something settling down to sleep.
