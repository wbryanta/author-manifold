He sat upright in the dark for a long time with his hands gripping his knees and the breath going in and out of him. The dream had not left him altogether. It hung in the room like smoke that would not clear.

You was a-hollerin somethin awful, she said.

I'm all right.

What was it?

It wasn't nothin. Go on back to sleep.

She turned with effort in the bed, the cornshuck tick rasping under her, and he could hear her breathing there beside him, shallow and quick the way it had got to be these last weeks with the child crowding her lungs. He lay back down but he did not sleep. He lay listening to the house tick and settle and to the wind worrying at the shakes and somewhere far off down in the glade a dog or what might have been a dog crying with a thin and wretched insistence until even that ceased and there was nothing but the dark itself and the sound of her breathing and his own heart going in his chest like something penned.

He rose before light and went out. The morning was gray and birdless, a fog standing in the bottoms so that the trees went up out of it limbless and dead-looking like things growing in a flood. He stood on the porch boards in the cold and made water off the edge of them and stood awhile longer looking down toward the road, which was no road but a wagon trace grown with chickweed and plantain, and beyond it the dark mass of the woods that went unbroken to the river. No smoke rose anywhere in all that country. They had been on this place four months and in that time no one had come along the trace save a man hunting a strayed cow and a pair of children he'd watched from the doorway, watching back, until they went on. He had picked it for that. He got the axe from under the porch and went around back and split wood until his shoulders ached and the light was full and the fog had begun to burn off in tatters, and when he came in with the wood she was up and at the stove.

You don't need to be up, he said.

I cain't lay there no more. My back's give out with layin.

He stacked the wood in the box and stood watching her. She moved with one hand pressed flat to the small of her back, her belly enormous beneath the shapeless gray dress, and her face when she turned to him had a hollowed look, the bones come up in it, her eyes large and dark and fevered-looking.

It won't be long now, she said. I can tell it.

He didn't say anything.

I said it won't be long.

I heard ye.

She set the pan on the stove and laid strips of fat meat in it and stood watching them blister and curl. Culla, she said.

What.

They's a woman over crost the creek somewheres. That man huntin his cow said it. Said his wife knowed her. A granny woman. Said she'd come for a dollar or whatever a body had to give. Said she'd come for a settin of eggs oncet.

No.

She didn't turn around. She stood with the fork in her hand and the meat hissing.

I cain't do it by myself, she said. I don't know nothin about it.

Women has done it by theirselves since the world commenced, he said. Ain't nobody comin here. You hear me? Nobody.

She turned then and looked at him and he looked away from her, out the one window with its sacking curtain at the gray and risen day.

It ain't her'd be doin the judgin, she said quietly. It'd just be a woman to help.

Ain't nobody comin, he said again, and he took up the bucket and went out to the well.

The days went on. He worked at small things, mending the fence of the empty lot, caulking the splayed boards of the house with clay, things that needed no doing or that did not need him to do them. He set rabbit snares along the field edge and twice brought in rabbits and once a possum that she would not cook. The weather turned and turned again, a spell of rain and then a hard bright cold with the leaves going over all at once into yellow and rust, and in the nights now he could hear the geese passing high up in the dark, going south, their cries drifting down thin and far like cries from another world. She grew bigger and slower and kept more and more to the bed, and there got to be a thing between them in the house that neither of them spoke, that sat with them at the table and lay with them in the dark, and he took to staying out late at the woodlot or down along the creek and coming in only at dusk.

It was on one of those evenings that the tinker came. He heard him before he saw him, a jangling and clanking down the trace like a peddler's hell, and then the cart hove into view at the bend, a high ramshackle thing hung all over with pans and dippers and bits of harness and shoes strung by their laces, and the tinker between the shafts of it like a draft animal himself, leaned into the strap across his chest, a small man, old, in a black coat gone green at the seams, hauling his rattling world up the grade toward the house. Culla stood in the yard and watched him come.

The tinker stopped at the edge of the yard and straightened from his strap and pushed back his hat. Hidy, he said.

Hidy.

Right brisk evenin.

Culla didn't say anything. The tinker looked at the house and at the lot and at the woodpile and back at Culla, his eyes quick and small and amber-colored like a bird's.

I got needles, the tinker said. Pins, thread, buttons. Got pans, got a awl and leather if ye got harness wants mendin. Got coffee. Real coffee. He smiled, showing a graveyard of teeth. Got some other things too, for them as wants em, he said, and he tilted his head a little, watching.

We don't need nothin.

Everbody needs somethin, the tinker said. He had begun to unhook one side of the cart where a wooden flap hung on leather hinges. Just let me show ye. Don't cost nothin to look.

I said we don't need nothin. We ain't got no money noway.

I trade, the tinker said. Eggs, hides, ginseng if ye dig it. He had the flap half down. Lord, I've traded for things ye wouldn't believe. Traded for a fiddle one time didn't have but two strangs.

Put it up, Culla said. We got nothin to trade and nothin needs buyin.

The tinker stopped and looked at him. Behind Culla the door of the house had opened and Rinthy stood in it with her hand on the jamb, her shape unmistakable against the dark of the room, and the tinker looked past Culla at her for a long moment and then back at Culla, and something passed over his face, some small adjustment, like water going over a stone.

Well, the tinker said. He hooked the flap back up, taking his time about it. I see how it is, he said.

You don't see nothin.

The tinker settled the strap across his chest again and took up the shafts. I come by here ever spring and ever fall, he said. Have for twenty year. They's been all manner of folks on this place and I've knowed ever one of em. He looked at Culla once more with his quick amber eyes. Likely I'll see ye again, he said.

Likely you won't, said Culla.

The tinker turned his cart in the trace, the pans swinging and clattering, and went back the way he'd come, down the grade and around the bend, and the jangling of him hung in the evening air a long time after he was gone from sight, fading and rising on the wind and fading again, until at last there was nothing, and Culla turned and the door of the house was empty and the dark was coming down out of the woods.

You never had to run him off thataway, she said at supper. He wasn't doin nothin but peddlin his stuff.

Eat your supper.

A body'd think it was a crime for anybody to so much as set eyes on this place.

Eat your supper, he said. I ain't goin to say it again.

In the night three days later she woke him with her hand clamped on his arm. It's commenced, she said. He could feel her trembling. Light the lamp, she said. I'm afeared in the dark.

It went on through that night and through the day that followed and into the second night. He kept the fire going and water hot in the kettle and he did the things she told him to do and the things he could think of himself to do, which were few, and the rest of the time he sat in the chair by the stove or stood in the open door in the cold while she labored and cried out in the bed, and he had never heard such sounds out of a human person, sounds that did not seem to belong to her or to any living thing he knew, and at some point in the second night while the wind got up outside and rain began to lash the boards he found that he was praying, his lips moving over old words he hadn't said since boyhood, and he stopped himself and stood with his fists shut at his sides. Toward the smallest hours the rain slacked and her cries changed and she called him to her and he knelt at the foot of the bed in the yellow lamplight with the shadows of them flung huge and shuddering up the walls, and he received into his two hands, slick and bloodied and appalling, the child.

It was a boy. It hung in his hands wet and dark-haired and outraged-looking and then it convulsed and squalled, a high tearing wail that seemed too big for it, and he wrapped it in the flannel she had cut and hemmed in secret weeks ago and that he had pretended not to see. She lay with her hair pasted to her face in strings, gray, emptied, her eyes burning at him.

Give him here, she said. Let me see him.

He's puny, Culla said. He didn't move.

Give him here.

He laid it on the bed beside her and she got her arm around it and turned her face to it and he stood looking down at the two of them and then he turned away and saw to the things that needed seeing to and after a while when he looked again the child had quieted and she had quieted and the two of them lay in a kind of stupor in the trodden bed, and the lamp guttered and ran a black thread of smoke up the chimney glass, and outside the rain had stopped and the wind moved in the trees with a great hollow rushing like the sound of the river got loose and roaming in the dark.

She slept. He sat by the stove and watched her sleep. He sat there a long time. The fire fell and shifted in the grate and he did not mend it. Whatever he held council with in those hours sat in no chair and cast no shadow and when at last he rose his face had nothing in it at all. He took the child up out of the crook of her arm with a thief's slowness, watching her face, and she stirred and muttered and did not wake, and he wrapped the flannel close and took the lamp in his other hand and blew it out and went out the door into the last of the night.

There was a moon somewhere behind the broken rack of clouds and the woods stood in a shifting half-dark, the trees dripping, the path down toward the glade slick and black with rain. He went down it with the child against his chest. It weighed nothing. It made small sounds, mewling, working its face against him, and he did not look at it. He crossed the trace and went into the woods and the dark came up around him and he went on by feel and by the gray tatters of sky in the high branches, down through dripping laurel and over stones furred with moss, the land descending, the air growing rank and cold with the smell of standing water, till he came out in the lower glade where the dead trees stood up out of the marsh grass pale as bone and the fog lay along the ground in pools. He stopped there. The child stirred against him and let out a thin cry and he stood holding it in that gray waste with the fog moving at his knees, and what he did next he did quickly, not letting himself think it, kneeling and laying the wrapped shape down in a bed of moss between the roots of a tree, tucking the flannel about it as if cold were the only enemy abroad in those woods, and then rising and turning and going back the way he had come, fast, and then faster, crashing through the laurel with his arms before his face, and behind him in the fog the child began to cry, and he could hear it for a long way, that sound coming on thin and amazed through the dark trees, and when he could not hear it anymore he stopped and stood gasping with his back against a tree, and the woods all about him stood silent and attentive like things called to witness.

When he came in she was awake. She was sitting up in the bed with her hand moving over the covers beside her and her eyes were enormous in her wasted face.

Where's he at, she said. Culla. Where's he at.

He died, Culla said. He stood just inside the door with the gray light behind him. He was too puny, he said. I told ye he was puny. He just quit breathin. Wasn't nothin nobody could of done.

She looked at him. Her hand had stopped moving on the covers. She looked at him for so long and with such stillness that he had to turn and shut the door for something to do with himself.

You're a liar, she said. Her voice was very small.

He died, Rinthy. I'm sorry of it but he died. I took him out and buried him fore ye woke. I never wanted ye to have to see it.

Where.

What?

Where'd ye bury him at.

Out yander. Up back of the lot.

She didn't cry. He had braced himself for her to cry and she did not, and that was worse. She lay back down with her face to the wall and her shoulders drawn up small and she lay that way through the morning and through the day, and when he brought her broth she would not take it, and when he spoke to her she did not answer, and the house got a silence in it that the fire could not warm out, and that evening he took the spade up behind the lot in the failing light and dug a small grave and filled it again and mounded it and set a flat creek stone at the head of it, and he stood over his work in the dusk like the author of some old and dismal rite, and the first stars came out above the ridge, and down in the glade the fog was rising again among the dead trees, white and slow, like something that had been promised.

The tinker had camped that night in a sink off the river road two miles below, and he heard it first in his sleep and took it for a bobcat, for they will cry like that, like a woman or a child, and he lay in his blankets under the cart listening. It came again, thin and rhythmic and failing, and he sat up. He was an old man and he had heard most of what the night had to say in his time and this was not a bobcat. He pulled on his boots and took up the lantern and lit it and went up out of the sink and stood in the road turning his head. Then he went into the woods. He went a long way, the lantern swinging its yellow bell of light over the wet leaves, the cries leading him on and stopping and starting again weaker, until he came down through the laurel into the fog of the glade and stood among the pale dead trees, and there in the lantern light between the roots, in its sodden flannel, gray-faced and barely crying now, its small fists quivering, he found it.

God amighty, the tinker said.

He knelt and took it up. It was cold as river clay and it turned its face to him with a blind rooting motion and he held it inside his coat against his shirt and knelt there a moment in the fog looking up the dark of the ridge to where he knew the house to be, although no light showed, and his face in the lantern light was a thing of hollows and his eyes were at work behind it. Then he rose and went back through the woods to the road and down to his camp, and he built up his fire and warmed sugarwater in a pan and fed it to the child off the tip of his little finger, drop by drop through what remained of the night, talking to it all the while in a low cracked singsong, old endearments, old nonsense, and in the dawn he packed his cart and put the child in a willow basket among the pans and bore west along the river with his strange freight, the cart jangling, going small and antic down the gray country like a figure out of an allegory no one had remained to read.

She did not get up for nine days. He cooked and fetched and kept the fire and she lay turned to the wall, and milk came in her and soaked the bedclothes and after a while it went away, and her eyes when she did look at him had something in them now that had not been there before, something patient and unappeasable, and he could not hold them. On the tenth day he came in from the snares at noon and the bed was empty. He found her up behind the lot, kneeling at the mounded grave in her nightdress with the spade beside her and her bare hands black to the wrist with the dug earth, and the hole was open and the raw clay sides of it stood empty, and she knelt there looking into it and she did not look up at him at all.

They wasn't nothin in it, she said. Her voice was wondering, almost mild. You buried a hole, Culla. They never was nothin in it.

Hogs has been at it, he said. Or dogs. Rinthy. Listen at me.

She rose. She stood facing him across the violated grave with the clay on her hands and her hair wild and the nightdress flagging about her in the wind off the ridge, and she looked like something hard used by God and not done with yet.

He ain't dead, she said. I'd of knowed it if he was dead. A mother knows. You give him to somebody or you laid him out somewheres, one. That tinker. She said it as if reading it from somewhere just past his shoulder. That tinker come by here three days afore, she said. You give him to that tinker or that tinker's got him some way, and you needn't to say nothin because your face has done said it.

Rinthy.

I'm a-goin to find him, she said. She came past him down the slope toward the house, walking carefully, like one walking a footlog, and he turned and watched her go.

You cain't, he called after her. You ain't able. You cain't even hardly stand up. You don't even know which way he went.

She didn't stop and she didn't turn. I'll ast, she said. Folks'll tell a thing to a woman huntin her chap that they'd not tell to airy soul else. I'll ast at ever door in this world if I have to.

She left in the morning, in the dress she'd been churched in years ago and a shawl and a poke with a quarter loaf and what oddments she owned, going down the trace in the early light, small and upright and alone, and he stood on the porch and watched until the bend took her and she did not look back at all. He stood there a long time after. The sun came up over the ridge and laid the shadow of the house out long and skewed across the bare yard and a jaybird came and sat in the dead tree by the lot and watched him and said nothing. Toward noon he went in and gathered what little there was to gather and rolled it in a blanket and tied it and shut the door of the house behind him, there being no lock to lock, and he went down the trace the way she had gone, west, toward the river, under a sky the color of wet slate, and the country took them both as it takes all things, without comment, and the trace stood empty behind them in the wind.

That same evening and forty miles on, the three came up out of the river bottom to a farmhouse standing in its locusts in the dusk, and a dog began to bark and then quit barking, and the bearded one knocked at the door with a gentleness surprising in him, and when the lamp came toward it through the house, swimming yellow in the curtained glass, the three of them stood waiting on the porch boards with their faces void and orderly as men come on lawful business, and whatever passed within those walls that night the lamp burned in the window until very late, and in the morning the chimney stood smokeless against the sky, and the dog lay in the yard, and no one came to tend the lowing cattle, and along the river road going west there were three sets of tracks in the rain-soft clay, single file, unhurried, and the buzzards had already begun their high and patient wheeling, writing their slow script on the sky above the locusts for any to read who might pass, though for a long time no one passed at all.
