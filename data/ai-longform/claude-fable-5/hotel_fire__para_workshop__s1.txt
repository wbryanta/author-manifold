The smell was wrong, and Agnes was grateful for it. She had braced herself, on the drive up from the station, for beeswax and coal smoke and the faint kipper-grease of breakfasts past, the smell that had lived in her uniform and under her fingernails for two years of her young life. Instead the lobby of the Belmore smelled of fig and cedarwood, pumped discreetly from somewhere, and of lilies in a vase the size of a font, and of money. The reception desk had moved. It used to stand against the east wall, dark oak with a brass bell that Mr. Carruthers polished himself every morning because he trusted no one else with brass. Now it floated in the centre of the room, a slab of pale stone lit from beneath, and behind it a boy in a grey waistcoat smiled at her with his whole face.

"Checking in? Mrs. Holt? Lovely. You're with the wedding party." His fingers moved over a screen. "Have you stayed with us before?"

"A long time ago," she said.

"You'll find it's all changed," he said happily, as if this were the best news he could offer, and slid a key card across the stone. A card. She had carried a chatelaine of brass keys once, heavy as a Christmas stocking against her hip, each one stamped with its room number, and the master key kept on a separate ring that Mrs. Pargeter handed over each morning the way a priest handles the host.

Room 214. Second floor. She had asked, when Ren made the booking on her behalf, for the ground floor, and had been put on the second, and she said nothing about it now, because she had been trained at nineteen never to make a fuss in a hotel, and the training had outlasted everything it was meant to serve.

The lift was new. Everything was new. The fire had taken the staircase and most of the first and second floors and the whole of the attic, and the Belmore had stood with its windows boarded for two years while the insurers and the family argued, and then it had been rebuilt behind its original façade — that was the phrase the website used, behind its original façade, as though the front of a building were its face and everything else negotiable. Riding up, Agnes counted the sprinkler heads in the lift-lobby ceiling. Four. In the corridor, one every three metres, small chrome blossoms, and smoke detectors with their green pinprick lights, and the fire doors held open on magnetic catches that would release at the first alarm and swing shut to compartmentalise the building, because that was the word now, compartmentalise, she had read about it in the years after, she had read about all of it, fire loads and flashover and the thirty-year-old wiring in the linen store, until her husband Stephen had said gently, love, I don't know what you're looking for in there, and she had stopped reading where he could see her.

Her room was beautiful. Of course it was. Grey wool throw, headboard upholstered in something nubbled and expensive, a bathroom with underfloor heating and two kinds of robe. She stood in the doorway and read it the way she had once read forty rooms a day: the bed made by someone right-handed and rushed, the top sheet's fold a half-inch shy of true, one cushion karate-chopped and one not, the water glasses set down without coasters. A supervisor somewhere was strict but not strict enough. She caught herself doing it and made herself stop, and went to the window instead and pushed it open as far as its restrictor would allow — ten centimetres, regulation, nobody falls and nobody jumps — and breathed in the lake.

That, at least, had not changed. The lake lay flat and pewter-coloured under a sky deciding what to do, and the fells went up behind it in their old folds, bracken rusting on the lower slopes, and a hire boat dawdled near the far shore exactly where hire boats had dawdled in 1990. She had stood at an attic window with Renata on their half-hour break, the two of them sharing a cigarette they were not allowed, blowing the smoke out over the slates, and Renata had pointed at the water and said, when I have my guesthouse, every room will see the lake. Even the bathroom. Even the cupboard for brooms.

Renata Wiśniewska from Łódź, who was twenty-three and kept her savings in a biscuit tin with a robin on the lid and her mother's letters under her mattress, who taught Agnes to say dobranoc and dziękuję and a swearword she swore was a blessing. Who could strip and make a double bed in four minutes flat and made it look like dancing. Agnes had not said her name aloud in years. She found she could not say it now either, even alone, even to a window. She closed the window most of the way and went down to find her daughter.

Ren was in the garden marquee directing the placement of two hundred chairs, in jeans and a man's jumper, her hair already pinned for tomorrow under a silk scarf, and when she saw Agnes she came across the grass at a run like she was eight again. "You're here, you made it, was the train hideous? Isn't it gorgeous? Mum. Isn't it gorgeous?" She spread her arms at the lawn, the cedar, the marquee with its swags of greenery, the hotel standing above its terraces with the late sun on its old stone face. "When Daniel and I came to look at venues I just knew. And then I thought — Mum worked here when she was a girl. It's perfect. It's like a full circle. Don't you think it's like a full circle?"

"It's like a circle," Agnes said, and kissed her, and held her a moment longer than the kiss required.

What Ren knew was this: that her mother had worked at a grand hotel on the lake at nineteen, before nursing college, before Stephen, before everything; that there had been a fire one night, ages ago, before Ren was born; that her mother had climbed out of a window, can you imagine, Granny Agnes shimmying down a drainpipe — Ren had told the story at dinner parties, Agnes had heard her tell it, had heard it grow a drainpipe it never had. It was Agnes's own fault. She had made the fire into an anecdote decades ago, sanded and sealed it, a story with a window in it and nothing else, because an anecdote was a thing you could hand to people and they would hand it back. The truth was not like that. The truth, handed over, stayed in the other person's hands and changed how they looked at you, and Agnes had not wanted her daughter to look at her any way but the way she did now, flushed and bossy and lit up, dragging her by the wrist to inspect the table plan.

She got through the rehearsal dinner. She sat between Daniel's aunt and a cousin from Vancouver and answered questions about the fells and agreed that yes, the hotel was stunning, and ate two bites of everything, and excused herself before the toasts because the room had grown warm and full of candles, dozens of small live flames in glass holders down the long table, and she had needed, quite suddenly, to count the exits. There were three. She stood in the corridor with her back against the wall, under a sprinkler head, breathing. A waiter asked if she was all right, madam, and she said yes, thank you, just the heat, and he believed her, because she had spent thirty-three years making herself a person who was believed when she said she was all right.

She slept badly, which she had expected, and woke at five, which had once been her waking hour for two years and apparently still lived in her somewhere like a pilot light. She lay in the dark of room 214 and let it come, because it was going to come whether she let it or not, and she had learned that it was kinder to herself to open the door than to hold it shut.

The fire had started in the linen store on the ground floor a little after two in the morning, an October night, the lake fog right up against the windows. The inquest said wiring; the wiring had been complained about; the complaints existed on paper, which mattered to lawyers and to nobody else. Agnes and Renata slept in the attic, in a room with two iron beds and a skylight, and what woke Agnes was not the smell but Renata's hand hard on her shoulder and Renata's voice saying her name in a tone Agnes had never heard her use. The corridor light had a haze around it. That was all, at first — a haze, as if her eyes hadn't woken with the rest of her — and then the smell arrived, not woodsmoke, nothing like a bonfire, but a thick chemical bitterness, mattresses and carpet and varnish, the building's own body burning, and from the stairwell a sound like wind where there was no wind.

They went down. They banged on doors as they went, the night porter was already on the first floor doing the same, voices everywhere, guests in the corridor in nightclothes holding the wrong things — a man with his shoes in his hands, a woman with a framed photograph, a child carried out asleep and sleeping through all of it, who would be a person of forty now somewhere, with no memory of the night at all. On the first floor Agnes hammered on the door of room 9, where Mr. Astley stayed every October for the fishing, an old man who took his hearing aids out at night and set them on the nightstand in a saucer; she had seen them there a hundred mornings, two little commas in a saucer. She hammered and shouted and the smoke in the corridor was getting down inside her, and the porter was bellowing from the stairhead, and she hammered once more and moved on to room 10, and room 10 opened, and room 9 did not, and that was the whole of it, that was the decision, except it had never felt like a decision, it had felt like a corridor moving her along it.

They were nearly out — they were at the second landing going down, the air better there, the front doors visible below, when Renata stopped. Agnes felt it through their joined hands, the stop, like a dog hitting the end of a lead. The tin, Renata said. The letters. And Agnes said no, and Renata said thirty seconds — trzydzieści sekund — she said it in both languages, she was already turning, and Agnes had her by the wrist, she could feel to this day the exact knob of the wristbone under her fingers, and a man came down past them carrying a woman who couldn't walk, and the staircase jostled, and the wrist was gone. Agnes stood at the bottom of the stairs on the gravel in the fog with the heat on her face counting. She counted to thirty. She counted to thirty many times. The staircase fell at around four o'clock, they told her later; she was wrapped in a car blanket in the boathouse by then, and they kept her away from the front of the building, and she let them, and that was the second decision that had never felt like one.

The window in the anecdote was real, in its way. The kitchen passage had filled and a pot-boy had put a chair through the scullery window and a dozen of them had gone out over the sill, and there was a photograph in the local paper that week of the broken window with the caption STAFF ESCAPE, and Agnes was in the photograph, a thin girl in a coat someone had lent her, looking at the ground. She had told Ren about the window. She had never told her that thirty feet from where that photograph was taken, the biscuit tin with the robin on the lid was found intact in the wreck of the attic, the lid heat-warped but holding, and that the hotel's solicitor had asked Agnes, as the deceased's known associate, whether she could supply an address in Poland.

Two died: Renata, and Mr. Astley, whose hearing aids were in a saucer. The inquest commended the staff, Agnes by name, for the prompt and orderly evacuation. She was nineteen. She went home to her mother's house and did not work in a hotel again, and in the spring she applied to nursing college, where the nights were long and the corridors were full of doors that she was required, paid, obligated to keep opening until somebody answered, and she had stayed forty hours a week inside that obligation for thirty years and called it a career, and perhaps it was.

At seven she got up and showered and stood at the window in one of the two robes watching mist lift off the lake in slow ropes, and at nine Ren came to her room in her wedding dress with the back unbuttoned, holding it up at the front like an armful of washing, because she wanted her mother to do the buttons and not the hairdresser, and Agnes did the buttons, twenty-two of them, silk loops, her hands absolutely steady, while her daughter watched her in the mirror.

"You're quiet," Ren said.

"I'm concentrating. These buttons were designed by a sadist."

"Full circle, though," Ren said softly, to the mirror. "You at nineteen, lugging sheets up those stairs. And now look." And Agnes met her eyes in the glass and smiled, and the smile was real, that was the strange thing, it was entirely real and it was also a door held shut, and she finished the last button and kissed the back of her daughter's neck above the silk.

The ceremony was in the garden under the cedar, which had survived. Agnes had not known that until she saw it, and the sight of it went through her like cold water: the old cedar of Lebanon at the lawn's edge, which had stood close enough that night that its eastern side was scorched — she remembered it in the weeks of photographs, the burnt flank, the needles gone — and which had simply gone on living, had closed over the damage, so that you had to know, you had to stand on the east side and look up into the dark of it to see where the bark grew strangely, a long healed seam. She stood on the east side. Then she took her seat in the front row, and her daughter came down the grass aisle on nobody's arm, by her own choice, with her chin up, the way she had walked into every room since she could walk, and Agnes watched her and let the day be the day.

It was a good wedding. The vows made people laugh and then made them cry in the correct order. Daniel's father gave a toast about circles and roots that glanced off Agnes harmlessly. There was a band, and lanterns strung through the marquee — she checked, first thing, walked past close: LED, battery, cool to the touch — and the staff worked the room in the old black-and-white, and her body knew their choreography before her mind did, the servers' weight always on the balls of the feet, trays carried at the shoulder through gaps that didn't exist until they did. She danced once with Daniel, who was nervous of her and shouldn't have been, and once with the cousin from Vancouver, and then at half past nine she stepped out for air and kept walking, through the side door, into the corridors of the hotel.

On the second floor she met a housekeeping cart.

It stood outside 218 with its door wedged, the cart loaded for turn-down: chocolates in a box, water bottles ranked like ammunition, a caddy of cloths folded in thirds. The girl came out backwards, pulling the hoover lead after her, and startled when she saw Agnes — a guest, in wedding clothes, standing in her corridor at night.

"Sorry, madam. I can come back—"

"No, no. I'm the one wandering." Agnes looked at the cart. The cloths in thirds, the chocolates, the little torch for under the beds. "Turn-down for the whole wedding block? On your own?"

The girl blinked. "Yes. Eleven rooms." She was perhaps twenty-two, dark-haired, with the particular tiredness of the double shift, and a name badge that said IOANA.

"I used to do your job," Agnes said. "Here, actually. This hotel. A long time ago."

"Here?" Ioana looked at her dress, her shoes, recalibrating. "Before the renovation."

"Before the renovation," Agnes agreed, and then, because the corridor was quiet and the girl had a steady, unhurried face, she said, "There was a fire."

"A fire?" Ioana frowned, genuinely searching. "I didn't know there was a fire. Nobody said." She glanced down the corridor at the sprinklers, the green-lit exit sign, as if seeing them for the first time. "When?"

"Nineteen ninety. Before you were born."

"Long before," Ioana said, with the cheerful brutality of the young, and then, more carefully: "Was it bad?"

"Two people died. A guest. And a chambermaid." Agnes heard her own voice say it, level, the corridor very still around the words. "She was my friend. We shared a room in the attic."

Ioana stood with the hoover lead looped in her hand. She did not say I'm sorry for your loss, or anything she had been trained to say. She said, "What was her name?"

And there it was, asked plainly by a tired girl in a corridor, the question nobody had asked Agnes in thirty-three years, because to ask it you first had to know there was a name to ask for.

"Renata," Agnes said. "Renata Wiśniewska. She was from Łódź. She was saving for a guesthouse." Her voice held. "She was better at your job than anyone I ever saw. She could make it look like dancing."

Ioana nodded slowly, as if filing it somewhere permanent. "Renata," she said, trying the weight of it, and the name was alive in the corridor for a moment, spoken in a young accented voice outside a door, the way it used to be spoken forty times a day. "The attic is suites now," Ioana offered, after a moment. "Lake View Suites. Six hundred a night. We live out, there's an annexe by the car park." She made a small face that said everything necessary about the annexe. Then she reached into the caddy on her cart, took a facecloth, and — Agnes never knew why, whether it was shyness or tribute or just the need to be doing something with her hands — began to fold it, and Agnes's hands moved before she had agreed to anything, took a cloth herself, and folded it into a fan in five moves, the old Belmore fan that Mrs. Pargeter had insisted upon, a fold no chain hotel taught anymore, and set it on the cart. Ioana stared at it. "We don't do that one," she said.

"You should," said Agnes. "It was ours."

She found Ren at midnight on the terrace, shoes off, dress hem dark with dew, eating cake off a napkin while the band played the slow last songs inside. Agnes sat down beside her on the cold stone balustrade and for a while neither of them said anything, and the lake said what lakes say.

"I want to tell you about your name," Agnes said.

Ren turned her head.

"You've asked me before and I said I just liked it. That wasn't true. You were named for someone. A girl I worked with here. Renata." And then, because the door was open now and the only way through a door is through it, she told her: the attic room, the biscuit tin with the robin, the guesthouse with a lake view from every cupboard, the cigarette on the breaks, dobranoc, the night, the corridor, room 9 and room 10, the landing, the wrist, the counting at the bottom of the stairs. She told it without the drainpipe. She told it in an even voice, looking at the water, and her daughter sat beside her in her wedding dress and did not interrupt once, which was its own kind of miracle, and when Agnes finished, Ren was crying without any noise, her cake forgotten on its napkin.

"Why didn't you ever—" Ren stopped, started again. "Why now?"

"Because she should have been at your wedding," Agnes said. "And because someone in the world besides me should know her name. It can't only be mine to carry. I'm getting old. Names need more than one person."

Ren put her head on her mother's shoulder, careful of the pins in her hair even now, even crying, her mother's daughter to the bone. "Renata," she said, to the lake. "I'm Renata."

"You're Renata," Agnes said.

She did not sleep much, and at six she gave up and dressed and went down through the silent hotel and out by the side door into the grey before sunrise. Behind the new spa, where the kitchen yard used to be, one wall of the old building still stood — retained, the website would have said, a feature — local stone, eight feet high, with espaliered pears trained across it now and a gravel path along its foot. She walked it slowly until she found what she was looking for, what nobody else would ever look for: a shadow in the stone, a darkening that was not lichen and not weather, low down near the old scullery corner, where the heat had come through and the stone had taken it and kept it. She crouched and put her hand flat against it. It was cold, of course. It was just stone. She stayed anyway, her hand on the wall, while the sky behind the fells went from grey to a thin gold, and she said, out loud, alone, the word she had been taught in an attic by a girl who made work look like dancing: "Dziękuję." Thank you. And then, because it was morning and not night, and because she had got the order of things wrong for thirty-three years, saying goodnight in her head all this time when what she had owed was a greeting: "Dzień dobry, Renata."

In her room she packed, and then, before the porter came for her bag, she made the bed. She didn't have to; she knew better than anyone alive that she didn't have to. She made it the old way, hospital corners, the counterpane drawn to a fingertip's tolerance, the pillows plumped and chopped, the way Mrs. Pargeter had taught her in 1989 — a room should look as if no one has ever been unhappy in it, girls — and she stood back and looked at the lie of it, the smooth and perfect lie of it, and then she sat down on the edge of the bed, deliberately, and let her weight print the cover, and stood up and left the crease where it was. On the pillow she put twenty pounds and a note on hotel paper: For Ioana. You keep it beautifully. The fold is yours now. Then she opened the window its permitted ten centimetres, so the room would smell of the lake when the girl came in, and picked up her bag, and went down to where her daughter was waiting in the bright ruined morning of the day after a wedding, and did not look back at the façade, which was original, and was only a face.
