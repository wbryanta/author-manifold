He woke rigid and sweating in the dark with her hand on his arm and the cry still in his throat like something lodged there. He could hear her breathing beside him and beyond that the night going about its business in the trees, an owl somewhere down by the creek calling and ceasing and calling again with a patience that seemed older than any grief. He sat with his hands flat on his thighs and waited for his heart to quit its hammering.

What was it you was dreamin? she said.

Nothin.

You was hollerin like somethin had aholt of ye.

It wasnt nothin, he said. Go on back to sleep.

She lay back in the shuck tick with a slow rustling and he could feel the heat of her along his side and the great taut curve of her belly when she turned, that swelling which had grown all summer like some blind tuber feeding in the dark of her, and he edged away from it as he always did until he lay at the very rim of the bed with the cold of the cabin air on his back. He lay there with his eyes open. Through the chinking he could see one star, very small and pale and adrift in the immense and clockless night, and he watched it until it had passed from the gap in the logs and gone wherever stars go and still he did not sleep.

When the first gray light came up out of the east he rose and pulled on his brogans and took his coat from the peg and went out. The door dragged in its frame and he lifted it by the latch and eased it to behind him so as not to wake her. The world stood in a cold smoke. Mist lay along the creek bottom in pale ropes and shoals and the trees came up out of it black and dripping and a rooster was crying somewhere far off in that gray vacancy though they kept no rooster nor any fowl at all nor was there a neighbor within three miles to keep one. He stood on the rotting porchboards and listened to it call and call again and then quit and he wondered had he dreamt that too.

He split kindling at the block with the old polled axe, the sound of it flat and dead in the fog, going up no distance at all before the morning swallowed it. He carried the wood in and built up the fire and set the kettle and when he turned she was sitting up in the bed watching him with her thin face and her hair wild about it, a child's face still for all the woman's burden she carried before her, and her eyes followed him about the room the way a cat's will, with no judgment in them and no mercy either, just watching.

You want some mush? he said.

I caint hold nothin down of a mornin no more.

You ought to try and eat.

I never said I wouldnt try.

He cooked the mush in the blackened pot and ladled it into two bowls and they ate at the plank table without speaking, the fire ticking and settling behind them. Outside the fog had begun to burn through and a thin yellow light lay on the floor in a long quadrangle from the one window. She ate slowly, her arm curled around the bowl like a child guarding it, and when she had done she set the spoon down and looked at him.

Culla.

What.

It caint be long now. I can feel it movin all hours. It liked to of never let me sleep last night.

He looked at his bowl.

I'm scared, she said. I dont know nothin about it. I need somebody with me that knows. They's a granny woman lives up past the forks, that Widow Naylor. Effie Tyler told me about her the time we was at the store. She said she'd come to anybody, didnt matter how far.

You dont need nobody.

How would you know what I need?

I said you dont need nobody. He rose with his bowl and set it in the pan. People comes around here it'll be nothin but talk. Talk and then more talk. You want em talkin?

She didnt answer. He stood with his back to her looking out the small warped pane at the clearing, the stumps standing in the burned-over ground like gravemarkers, the road that was no road but a wagon track going off through the trees toward a world that had forgotten them or never knew. When he turned again she had lain back down with her face to the wall and her shoulder rose and fell with her breathing and he could not tell was she crying or not and he did not ask.

I'm goin to run the snares, he said. They might be a rabbit.

She said nothing. He took down the worn cottonsack from its nail and went out.

The woods stood open and bare-floored under the pines, an old stillness on everything, the light coming down in shafts through the high dark canopy and the ground giving softly underfoot with its centuries of rot. He went along a path that only he knew, past the first snare hung limp and empty in its run, past the second sprung and holding nothing, and at the third a rabbit hung strangled and cold with its eyes filmed blue and a black thread of blood at one nostril and he knelt and freed it and held the small stiff body in his hands a moment, feeling no triumph nor any pity either, and put it in the sack. Beyond the snares the ground fell away toward the creek and he went down through dogwood and laurel to where the water ran coiling over greenblack stones and he squatted there on his heels and watched it go. A waterthrush bobbed and curtsied on a rock midstream. Leaves came down the current turning slowly like things bound on errands of small importance and passed on out of sight. He thought about the dream but it had begun to come apart in the daylight the way they do, leaving only the dread of it, a stain on the day like the smell of something burnt. He thought: A man caint be held to account for what he dreams. And then he thought of her in the cabin with her belly and what was in it and his face heated and he rose suddenly and spat into the creek and went back up through the laurel as if he could walk out ahead of his own thoughts.

Coming up the wagon track toward the cabin he heard the bell before he saw anything, a thin tonkling like a cowbell but lighter, and then around the bend in the track came a hunched figure all hung about with wares, pans and pots and dippers swinging and clattering and flashing dull silver where the sun got in among them, a tinker bent under his rig like some pilgrim doing penance with his sins strapped to his back in tinware. He had a small gray jenny on a lead behind him similarly hung and a dog of no clear breed trotting at the off side. He raised one hand when he saw Culla standing in the track.

Howdy, he called.

Culla didnt answer. The tinker came on and stopped a few feet from him and eased his shoulders in the straps and looked him over with small bright eyes out of a face like a dried apple.

Right warm for October, the tinker said.

I reckon.

You live up this way?

What do ye want?

The tinker grinned, showing a graveyard of brown teeth. Why, I'm in trade, he said. Pots, pans, needles and pins. Combs. I got combs would make a bald man weep. He shrugged one shoulder so that his goods chimed all about him like a steeple full of cracked bells. I carry notions too. Thread, buttons. Sweet soap. They's not a woman drawin breath that dont need somethin I got.

We dont need nothin.

You got a woman up in here?

We dont need nothin, Culla said again, and his voice came out harder than he had purposed it, and the tinker's eyes went small and shrewd and traveled past him up the track to where the cabin roof showed gray among the trees, and a thin string of smoke above it, and came back to him again.

Well, the tinker said. No harm in astin. He shifted his load and clucked at the jenny. I come through here twiced a year, he said. Spring and fall, like the birds. You all think of somethin you need, I'll be back through, Lord willin.

Culla stood out of the track and the tinker went by him with his slow chiming and his sour smell of woodsmoke and old grease and the dog skirted wide around Culla's legs with its hackles up and its eyes sidling, and he stood and watched until the whole apparition had clattered out of sight beyond the trees and the bell died away and there was just the woods again and the silence and high up a buzzard turning on the blue like a scrap of char gone up from a fire. He stood a long time after there was nothing left to watch. Then he went on to the cabin.

Who was that? she said when he came in. I heard a bell.

Wasnt nobody. A tinker.

Did he have thread? I'm near out of thread, Culla, and the baby'll need—

He's done gone, Culla said. He set the sack on the table. They's a rabbit. I'll dress it directly.

She looked at him from the bed and there was something in her face he didnt want to see and he took the rabbit out to the porch and skinned it there, hooking it by the gambrels to a nail and peeling the hide down off it like a glove off a hand, and the naked blue body of it swung small and obscene in the slant light and he was a long time over the work, longer than the work asked.

In that same dusk far downriver the three came to a crossing where a flatboat ferry hung on its cable in the swirling current and a shack stood on the bank with a lamp already burning in it though the sky still held light. The bearded one halloed the shack and after a while a man came out buttoning his coat and looked at them and at the river and back at them again.

Be a half dollar for the three of ye, he said.

They said nothing. They came down the clay bank and onto the boat and stood at the rail looking at the water and the ferryman looked at them once more and then bent to his windlass as though he had decided something it were better not to put words to. The river was running heavy with rain from some weather upcountry that had never reached here and it bore on its face whole congregations of drift, fenceboards and bottles and the bloated shape of something that might have been a shoat or might not, all wheeling past in the gray water with a kind of stately hurry. Midway across the youngest of the three leaned and spat into the river and watched the spittle whirl away. No one spoke. On the far bank the ferryman made fast and stood by the rail and the bearded one stopped in front of him and looked at him with eyes that gave back the lamplight from the shack across the water like the eyes of a dog by a night fire and the ferryman did not ask again for his half dollar nor for anything else, and they went up the bank and into the dark of the trees, and a long while later, telling it, the ferryman would say only that he'd ferried them and that he'd not do it again for any money, and when they asked him why not he would look off down the river and not answer.

The pains came on her two nights later. He woke to her voice saying his name, just his name, over and over with a flat patience worse than screaming, and he rose and lit the lamp and her face in the small yellow light was wet and white as tallow.

It's come, she said. Culla, it's a-comin.

He stood holding the lamp. The shadows of him reeled hugely on the wall.

You got to go for the granny woman, she said. You got to go now. It's a long ways and it's night.

I aint goin nowheres.

Culla.

I aint, he said. You'll be all right. Women has babies all the time. Aint nothin to it. They done it forever fore there was ary granny woman.

She cried out then, a long animal sound that didnt seem to come from her at all but from somewhere under the floor or out in the dark, and her hands gripped into the tick and he stood frozen with the lamp until she came back from wherever the pain had taken her and lay panting.

You go, she said. Her voice had gone small and old. If you wont do nothin else in this world for me you go and get somebody.

And tell em what? He set the lamp on the table. His own voice shook. Tell em what, Rinthy? Who do I say the daddy of it is when they ast? You want me to say? You want the whole county knowin?

She turned her face away from him to the wall and would not look at him again and he stood there in the middle of the floor like a man arraigned and then her body bowed up with the next pain and there was no more talk of going anywhere.

It went on all night. He kept the fire up and boiled water because he had heard that water was boiled at such times though he could not have said for what, and he sat by the bed and let her crush his hand in hers, that small hand grown strong as a man's with the pain, and the lamp burned down and he filled it and the night outside stood at the windows immense and indifferent and once far away he heard the owl again, asking its one question of the dark over and over. Toward the last she was beyond knowing him or anything else, gone away to that country women go to alone, and he talked to her without knowing what he said, a low steady murmur such as a man might use to gentle a frightened animal, and when the child came at last in a hot rush of blood and water into his two waiting hands he knelt there holding it and could not move.

It was a chap. Small, bluish, slick as a fish, with the cord pulsing at its belly and its face crumpled shut like an old man's. He held it and it lay inert in his hands and he thought it dead and something in him leapt up at the thought with a gladness that sickened him even as he felt it, and then the small chest heaved and the thing opened its mouth and cried, a thin wavering cry no louder than a kitten's, and the crying went into him like a blade.

He did what had to be done. He tied the cord with string and cut it with his knife and wrapped the child in a piece of clean meal sacking and laid it on the bed at her side because there was nowhere else to lay it. She had swooned or slept, her face emptied out, gray as ash in the guttering light. The child cried its small cry and worked its blind mouth and he stood over the two of them with his bloody hands held out from his sides like a man who has come from some terrible labor of slaughter and he looked at the child a long time. Looking for whose face was in it. Hers. His. Both being one face in the deep wrong at the root of it.

Toward daybreak she woke and saw the child beside her and gathered it weakly against her and her face when she looked up at him was lit with something he had no name for and could not bear to see.

It's little, she said. Aint it little.

It come early, he said. They dont always live, comin early thataway.

It's livin, she said. Listen at it.

He went out and stood on the porch. The east was going gray and then a cold rose color and the trees took shape out of the dark one by one as if they were being called into the world that minute and had not been there before. He was tired in a way he had not known a man could be tired. He thought of the tinker on the track and the buzzard turning and the dream of the dark sun, and it seemed to him that all of these were one thing and that the thing had a shape he could almost see, the way you can almost see a figure in a thicket at dusk, and that it was coming toward him with a patience nothing could turn.

She was three days fevered and weak and the child cried and slept and nursed and she would not let it from her arms even sleeping. He cooked and fetched water and watched. On the third night the fever broke and she slept deep, deeper than she had since the birth, the child in the crook of her arm, and he sat by the dying fire and watched the two of them for a long time. The lamp was out. Coals breathed in the hearth. Somewhere off in the dark of the world a dog was barking, miles away, with long silences between, and he listened to it and to her slow breathing and at some hour with no name to it he rose and crossed the floor and stood over the bed.

He told himself nothing. There were no words in him at all, only his hands moving, lifting the child away from her arm so gently that she only sighed and turned, and the child itself made no sound, warm and slack with sleep in its sacking, and he wrapped his coat about it and took up his shoes in his other hand and let himself out into the night and sat on the porch step and put his shoes on one-handed with the child against his chest and then he went down into the dark of the trees.

There was a moon somewhere above the overcast and the woods stood in a deep blue gloaming through which he went like the last man on earth, the child a small heat against him, the only heat anywhere. He went a long way. Past the snares, past the creek, splashing through at the shallow ford with the cold water going into his shoes, up the far slope through the laurel hells where he had to go bent and crabwise with branches clawing at him as if the woods themselves would have stayed him from his errand, and out at last into a glade he knew, a small bald in the deep timber floored with moss and old leaves where in the spring there were violets but where now there was nothing, just the gray light sifting down and the great trunks standing about the edges like the columns of a roofless and ruinous church.

He knelt and laid the child down in the moss.

It stirred and worked its face but did not wake. He squatted there on his heels looking at it. He could just see it in the dark, a smudge of paleness, hardly anything at all. He thought how small a thing it was to have such a crime hung in it, no crime of its own, and his mind shied from that the way a horse will shy from something dead in the road, and he stood up.

It will die of the cold, a voice in him said, and not feel nothing. It will just go to sleep deeper.

Or somethin will come.

He stood a moment longer. Then he turned and went back the way he had come, fast at first and then faster, blundering through the laurel with his arms before his face, and behind him in the glade he heard or thought he heard the child begin to cry, that small wavering cry going up alone into the dark of the trees where no ear was to hear it, or no ear that he would have wished on it, and he did not stop and he did not look back. He crossed the creek and the cold of it meant nothing to him now and he came up through the woods toward the cabin in the first gray of dawn with his coat empty and his arms empty and his face like a thing carved.

She was still sleeping when he came in. He built up the fire with great care, quietly, and set the kettle, and sat down at the table to wait for her to wake, rehearsing nothing, his mind a held breath. Outside the light came on slow and ashen. In the woods to the south a crow called once and another answered it from a long way off and after that nothing, and far downriver, miles yet but coming, the three moved out of a cedar brake into the gray morning and stood looking out over the country before them, the fields and the folded hills and the thin smokes of scattered habitations rising straight up in the windless air, and the bearded one looked at it all for a long time with his flat agate eyes and then they went on.
