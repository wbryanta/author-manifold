The trucks went down into the hold first, chained at the axle, and then the cars, and then the foot passengers were let up the steel stairway with their bags and their sleeping children, and Calder Vaughan went up among them carrying nothing but a canvas satchel that held a clean shirt, a razor, and a manila envelope from the office of the Chief Medical Examiner that he had not yet been able to open all the way.

The ferry left North Sydney at ten in the evening. From the rail he watched the town's lights gather themselves into a single low line, then a smear, then nothing. The strait took everything in. Behind him the funnel breathed its diesel breath over the deck and a man in a yellow slicker walked the perimeter with a flashlight, checking the lashings on the lifeboats the way a farmer checks gates at night, without expectation, out of an old courtesy to disaster.

It was October. The water of the Cabot Strait that month is the temperature of a cellar floor. A body in it does not last in the shape you knew. He had learned this on the telephone four days earlier from a woman named Mrs. Organ, who worked for the coroner in Port aux Basques and who had a voice like someone reading a recipe she trusted. One belt, leather, brass buckle, she had said. One jackknife, bone-handled, blade worn to a sliver. One ring of keys, four keys, one of them a church key. Boots, gone. Wallet, none. In the breast pocket of the oilskin, a tobacco tin, and in the tin a letter, the ink mostly lifted by the sea, but the envelope addressed — and here she had paused, and he had heard paper turning — addressed to a Mr. Calder Vaughan, of Bridgewater, Nova Scotia. That's yourself, is it.

That's myself, he said.

The boat they found adrift off the Burgeo islands was registered to an E. Vaughan. The body came ashore nineteen days later at a place called Fox Roost, which he had since looked at on a map for a long time, as if the name itself were a piece of evidence, as if his brother had chosen it.

He had not spoken to Emmett in thirteen years. He carried that number the way other men carry shrapnel — most days unfelt, then a turn of weather and there it was, lodged against the bone.

Inside, the lounge was lit like a hospital cafeteria. Men played cards at a bolted table, slapping them down without speech, the game itself a kind of language they'd been born to. A girl of perhaps six slept across two chairs under a man's parka, one red shoe on, one fallen. An old woman knitted something grey and endless, her needles ticking against the engine's hum, and beside her a transistor radio gave out fiddle music so quietly it might have been coming from inside her wool. Calder bought tea in a paper cup and sat by the black window where there was nothing to see but his own face floating on the strait, and the strait moving through his face.

There are men you know by their hands. His brother was one of these. Emmett's hands at sixteen were already their father's hands — broad as plates, the knuckles like a row of walnuts, a white seam in the web of the left thumb where a cod hook had gone through when he was twelve. Calder had been ten that day. He remembered the hook's eye standing up out of the flesh like a tiny silver periscope, and Emmett looking at it with interest, not crying, saying push it through, Cal, you can't back a barb out, and Calder, weeping enough for both of them, pushing it through. Afterwards their mother soaked the hand in salt water and Javex and Emmett ate his supper one-handed and proud, and that night in the bed they shared he let Calder touch the wound once, lightly, the way you would touch the breast of a dead bird, to know what it was.

You will want to know what happened between them. Everyone wants the rupture, the scene in the kitchen, the door. Calder himself, on certain nights, wanted it — wanted there to have been one clean act he could hold up to the light and turn. But what happened between them was mostly an absence of happenings, a slow withdrawal of weather. There was their father going down with the *Lena B* off Scaterie in 1961, all four men aboard, no bodies, so that the funeral was four photographs on the altar rail and the priest blessing air. There was Emmett at sixteen quitting school to go on the boats because someone had to, and Calder at fourteen staying in school because Emmett had decreed it, signing his own name to that life so his brother would not have to. There was their mother dying eleven years later in the back bedroom of the house in Ingonish, and Calder, by then a surveyor in Bridgewater with a wife and a mortgage, selling the house that winter without asking, because Emmett was somewhere on the Grand Banks and unaskable, because the taxes, because the roof — because of a hundred reasonable things that, laid end to end, made one unreasonable thing.

And there was Lena. He would allow himself her name and no more of her, even now, even on a night boat in the middle of a strait. Lena, who had been Emmett's girl first, in the summer of the hook scar's tenth anniversary, and Calder's wife after. The two facts sat in him like stones of different weights and he had never found the scale that could compare them.

Emmett came back once, in the spring after the house was sold. He stood in the emptied parlour where strangers' furniture had not yet arrived, picked up the brass compass that had been their father's — Calder had kept it aside for him, had written his name on a paper tag and tied it to the gimbal — and a photograph of their mother at nineteen standing in front of a church in a dress the colour, she always said, of buttermilk, though the photograph was black and white and you had to take her word. Emmett put both in his coat. He looked at the walls. He said, You painted it. Calder said the buyers had wanted it freshened. Emmett nodded as if this confirmed a long diagnosis, and walked out to his truck, and that was the conversation, all thirteen years of it, four words and a nod.

Twice in the years after, letters had come — one from St. Anthony, one from a place called Isle aux Morts, which Calder did not at the time think to translate. He had not opened them. He could no longer say why, except that not-opening had become a discipline, like fasting, and he was a man who finished what he started. They were in a drawer in Bridgewater under the good tablecloth. He thought of them now with a sensation like the deck tilting, though the deck was steady; the strait that night was almost kind.

He went out once around midnight to walk the deck. The fog had come down, or they had gone up into it; the ship's horn sounded every two minutes, a long animal note that the fog took and held and gave back smaller. Under the lifeboat davits a young man stood smoking, deckhand by his boots, and they nodded to each other and watched the nothing together for a while.

Going over for work? the deckhand said.

Going over for my brother, Calder said, and found that the sentence could end there, that it was, in one sense, simply true.

She's a good crossing for it, the deckhand said, meaning the calm, and flicked his cigarette into the dark, where its small light fell a long way before the water put it out.

In the cabin he lay on the lower bunk in his clothes with the envelope from the Medical Examiner on his chest and the engine coming up through the steel into his shoulder blades, a tremor so constant it became a kind of stillness. He had got the envelope half open in Bridgewater, far enough to see the words *advanced immersion changes* and *visual identification may be of limited* — and had stopped there, at the cliff edge of *limited*, and folded the flap back down. Now in the dark he opened it the rest of the way by feel, like a blind man, and did not turn on the light, and so read nothing, and held the pages on his chest like a poultice, and slept.

He dreamed, as he had known he would, of the quarry.

The quarry above Ingonish had filled with rainwater sometime before either of them was born, green water of unknowable depth, and the drowned machinery was still down there — a steam donkey, men said, and lengths of chain, and a horse, though every flooded quarry in the world has its horse. In July the top six feet of the water was warm as tea and below that lay a cold that grabbed your ankles like a hand. Emmett taught him to swim there, and then taught him the other thing, the thing Emmett was famous for among the boys of three coves: the holding of breath. You don't fight it, Emmett said, treading water, his wet hair black as a seal. You go down past the fighting. There's a door at the bottom of needing to breathe and you go through it and on the other side it's quiet. Then he would sink, deliberately, his face upturned and open-eyed through the green, and Calder would count — one steamboat, two steamboat — sometimes to a hundred and twenty, once, the famous once, to a hundred and forty-four, while the surface healed over and the gulls went about their arguments and the world contained, for that long, no brother at all. And then the water would break and there he'd be, gasping and grinning, reborn, saying what did I make, what did I make, and Calder would tell him the number, and the number was love, there being no other word for it available to boys.

In the dream he was counting and the count would not end.

He woke at five to the changed note of the engines and the loudspeaker saying Port aux Basques in the flattened voice of all loudspeakers. Up on deck the fog was breaking into hills — bare grey hills with houses pinned to them in white and ochre and a green like old paint, and the harbour opening its arm. Gulls came out to meet the ship as if they had been sent. He stood at the rail with the satchel between his feet and watched Newfoundland assemble itself out of the mist, rock by rock, and thought: he looked at this. Whatever else was true, his brother's eyes had taken in this same arrangement of stone and water, on some morning, perhaps on many mornings, and so the harbour was already a relic, already something handled by the dead or the possibly dead, and Calder found he was holding the rail too hard, and let go.

The constable met him at the bottom of the ramp, a tall boy with red wrists coming out of his cuffs, who introduced himself as Pelley and took the satchel out of Calder's hand before he could object, the way you'd help a man much older, and Calder understood from this how he must look. They drove through the town in a yellow patrol car. The streets went up and down like swells. Outside a shop a woman was washing her window with newspaper, and a dog trotted diagonally across the road on urgent personal business, and none of it knew anything about anything, and Calder loved it for that, briefly and completely, the whole indifferent morning town.

Pelley talked because silence was beyond his courage. He said the boat had been found by a longliner out of Ramea, riding light and easy, sea anchor out, which was a queer thing — a man doesn't stream a sea anchor and then fall overboard easy; or rather he does, men do, men have, the sea anchor holds her head to the weather and a man goes to clear a fouled line and the one wave that's been looking for him his whole life finds him. He said Mr. Vaughan — the other Mr. Vaughan — had been three years on the coast, out of Isle aux Morts mostly, kept to himself but not strange with it, fixed engines for half the harbour, never took money from widows. He said this last as if reciting a beatitude. He said the body had come in at Fox Roost on a Tuesday, found by two boys who would be telling it for sixty years.

The funeral home was a white house with a wheelchair ramp and a maple in the yard that had gone entirely red, every leaf, as if it had made a decision. Inside it smelled of carnations and something under the carnations. The coroner was there, a Dr. Bishop, small and grey and gentle, and Mrs. Organ from the telephone, who turned out to have a face exactly like her voice, and who gave Calder tea he did not want and which he drank to the bottom.

Dr. Bishop spoke to him in a side room first. He used the words from the envelope. He said the sea is hard on the face, Mr. Vaughan, I won't pretend otherwise; the face is mostly what we are to each other and it is the first thing taken; you may find there is little there to know him by. He said we go by other things. Old fractures. Teeth — though your brother, if it is your brother, seems never to have sat in a dentist's chair in his life, which I'm told was common enough where you were boys. Marks. Scars. He said, is there anything on the body you would know.

His left hand, Calder said. There's a scar in the web of the thumb. A hook went through it when he was twelve. And he heard his own voice deliver this as evidence, as coordinates, this thing that had lived in him for forty years as a touch in the dark, the breast of a dead bird.

Bishop looked at him for a moment with the kindness of a man about to subtract something. The right hand is gone, he said. The left — the sea takes the skin of the hands, Mr. Vaughan. Like a glove. I'm sorry. There may be deeper marking. We will look together.

The room was at the back and it was cold and it was lit too well. There is no way to write the body that does not betray either the body or the reader, and so: it lay under a sheet on a steel table, and the sheet made a landscape, and the landscape was the wrong size. That was the first thing, before the sheet was folded back from the head, before anything — the length of it. Emmett stood six foot one in his socks. He had stood six foot one in Calder's mind every day for thirteen years, a measurement renewed nightly like a flag. This under the sheet was a smaller country.

They shrink, you will tell me. The spine settles, the sea does its arithmetic, memory makes our brothers tall. Calder told himself all of it, standing there, while Bishop folded the sheet down with a tenderness that must be learned in some school no one speaks of.

The face was, as promised, not a face. He made himself look at it the way he had learned to look at survey maps of country he'd walked — translating, allowing for the projection. The hair was right, black going to iron, and there was a great deal of it; Emmett would be fifty-three and their father's people kept their hair into the grave and famously past it. The brows were right, or the bone under where the brows had been. The ears — and here something in him lurched toward certainty — the left ear was cauliflowered at the top rim, and Emmett's was, from boxing in the navy reserve one winter in Sydney; but then half the men of that coast and that generation had ears like that; the ear proved only that this had been a man, in that place, in that century.

The left hand. Bishop lifted it for him, holding it from beneath the wrist as you'd hold something you were teaching a child to hold. The skin of the palm was gone and the hand was not a hand he could have picked from a basket of hands and called brother. But in the web of the thumb, in the deeper tissue, there was a track — a pale cord of old scarring running through, the doctor said, full thickness, an old penetrating wound, decades old, healed without medical closure. Such as a hook, Bishop said. Such as a hook pushed through, Calder said, because you can't back a barb out, and his voice did something on the last word and Mrs. Organ, who was standing by the door with her hands folded, looked at the floor to give him privacy.

And yet. He stood there with the cold coming up off the steel and the certainty would not finish arriving. The scar was there and the height was wrong. The ear was right and was nothing. The hair, the brows, the boat with its careful sea anchor, the bone-handled knife — their father's knife, he'd known it from Mrs. Organ's first description, blade worn to a sliver because their father sharpened it on the doorstep every Sunday of his life — the letter in the tin with Calder's name on it, carried in the breast pocket, carried over the heart, carried how long? Written when? Mailed never. All of it pointed one way and none of it was a face, a voice, a brother surfacing out of green water saying what did I make.

He understood then the thing he had perhaps crossed the strait to understand: that he had lost the means of knowing his brother long before his brother was lost. That thirteen years of not-opening had been a quarry slowly filling, and that what lay on the table was the drowned machinery at the bottom of it, true and unverifiable, and the fault for the unverifiable part was not the sea's.

Mr. Vaughan, Bishop said quietly. There's no hurry. Sit with him if you like. Some do.

So Calder sat. They left him a chair and left the room, and he sat by the steel table in his coat in the cold light, and after a while he did what he had not done in any of the four thousand days. He talked.

He told the body about Lena, who had been dead herself six years now, a thing Emmett might never have learned, out there fixing widows' engines on a coast of rock. He told it about the letters under the tablecloth, and that he would open them now, in order, on the ferry going back, by the black window, and answer them both even so, even now, because a letter is owed an answer whatever the postal arrangements of the dead. He told it the number — a hundred and forty-four — in case it had forgotten what it once made. He said the house in Ingonish had burned in 1968, owned by strangers, so the paint hadn't mattered, none of it had mattered, the roof, the taxes, the freshening; he had sold their childhood to people who lost it in an hour, and he was sorry, and the word was small and he said it anyway, the way you put one stone on a cairn knowing the cairn needs a thousand.

Then he stood, and put his hand on the ruined left hand, on the web of the thumb, lightly, the way you would touch the breast of a dead bird, to know what it was.

In the front room they had the form ready on a clipboard, and a pen, and the line that said *Identified by* and the line that said *Relationship to deceased*. Bishop watched him with the question held politely out of his face. Calder thought of all the honest answers — *probably*, *I believe*, *the scar is consistent* — the whole grey vocabulary of men who measure for a living. Then he thought: there is a door at the bottom of needing to be sure, and you go through it, and on the other side it's quiet.

It's him, he said. It's my brother. Emmett Vaughan. And he wrote the name in his surveyor's hand, each letter plumb, making it true the way names make things true, which is the only way most things are ever made true at all, and he signed beneath it, and asked could he have the knife.

They gave him the knife and the keys and the tin with the letter in it, the words washed to ghosts, his own name on the envelope faint as a watermark. The evening ferry took him out past the breakwater at dusk, and he stood at the stern rail with the bone handle warm in his fist, watching the island lower itself back into the fog, hill by hill, and somewhere in the middle of the strait, over the deepest of that cellar-cold water, he began, very quietly, in the diesel wind, with no one near enough to hear him, to count.
