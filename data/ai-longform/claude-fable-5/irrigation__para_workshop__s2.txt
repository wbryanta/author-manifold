The wet spot showed itself a little after seven, a dark tongue of mud in the middle row of the Suncrest block where no water had any business being, and Dean stood over it the way a man stands over a thing he has been expecting and dreading both. The ground gave under his boot like a sponge. Forty feet on, the line ran fine — he could hear the microsprinklers hissing their small steady hiss all down the lower rows — but here the pressure had found a way out underground and was bleeding into the dirt, and had been, by the look of it, most of the night.

"That's not good," Toby said behind him. He'd come up the row with two cups of coffee, still in the clean shirt he'd flown in wearing, and he handed one over and looked at the mud as if it were a problem on paper, something that could be solved by frowning at it.

"It's the mainline," Dean said. "Four-inch. Right about where Dad spliced it, if I had to guess. Eighty-eight, eighty-nine. I was in high school."

"You can tell that from here?"

"I can tell that from twenty years of it almost going."

He drank half the coffee standing there, because it would be the last still moment of the day and he knew it, and then he walked the quarter mile back to the pump shed by the ditch and threw the breaker. The hum that lived under everything on the place went quiet. In the silence the orchard reasserted itself: a mockingbird running through its repertoire on the power line, the dry-paper rattle of leaves, somewhere up the slope a tractor that belonged to Vogel, who had two hundred acres and a line of credit and sons. Dean got the shovels and the digging bar out of the shed, and the five-gallon bucket with the saw and the primer and the can of cement in it, the can he'd been meaning to replace because PVC cement went off after a year open and this one was older than that. He put it all in the bed of their father's truck, which was his truck now, or theirs, or nobody's — the estate hadn't decided yet, the way the estate hadn't decided anything — and drove it down the row to where Toby stood waiting with his sleeves rolled.

They dug. The first foot was easy, mud you could move with your hands, and below that the ground turned to the gray hardpan the whole bench was cursed with, and they took turns with the bar, lifting it and dropping it, the shock running up through wrist and elbow and shoulder, then shoveling out what the bar had broken. By eight thirty Toby had blisters starting at the base of both thumbs and was too proud to say so, and Dean could see him not saying so, and let him not say it, because there was a part of Dean — he wasn't proud of it but there it was — that wanted his brother's hands to hurt. Just for a day. Just so the conversation they were circling could happen between two men with the same dirt under their nails.

The conversation was on the kitchen table back at the house, four pages from a company called Pacific Crest Orchard Partners out of Fresno, offering one point one five million dollars for the forty acres, the water rights, the equipment of value, which God knew wouldn't take long to inventory, and the house. The offer expired Friday. It was Tuesday.

"You sleep all right?" Dean asked, because he could feel Toby winding up to talk about it and he wasn't ready yet.

"I slept in my old room with a box of Mom's quilting on the other bed. What do you think?"

"I think you slept better than I do."

The hole was three feet across now and going down. Water kept seeping in from the saturated ground around the leak, so they were digging soup, and Dean sent Toby to the truck for the coffee can he kept under the seat, and they took turns bailing, and the water came back, and they bailed again, and somewhere in there the sun cleared the eastern trees and the day put its hand on the back of their necks and left it there.

They found the pipe at three and a half feet. Dean got down in the hole and worked along it with his fingers, the way their father had taught him, eyes closed, reading it like Braille — and there it was. The old splice. A length of newer pipe, newer in 1988, joined to the original line with a coupling on one end and on the other end with what their father had used when he didn't have a coupling, which was a sleeve of inner-tube rubber and three hose clamps, rusted now to a kind of lace. The rubber had finally given out. When Dean pressed it, water pulsed up through the split like something alive.

"Look at this," he said. "Look at this and tell me about the integrity of this operation."

Toby crouched at the edge of the hole. "He fixed it with a tire."

"He fixed everything with a tire. The man had a religious objection to buying the right part." Dean said it the way you say things about the dead when the dead have been dead five months, with the anger still in front of the grief, holding the door for it. "I told him a hundred times this was going to go. He'd say, lasted this long."

"It did last this long."

"That's not a defense of the method. That's luck wearing the costume of a method." Dean hauled himself out of the hole and stood dripping mud to the knee. "That's this whole place, Toby. Everything underneath everything is rubber and hose clamps. You want to talk about the offer, that's my opening statement."

"I didn't say anything about the offer."

"You've been saying it since the airport. You say it with your face."

Toby looked off down the row, where the trees stood in their lines, the fruit on them green-gold and the size of golf balls, the June drop scattered in the dry grass underneath like spent shells. He had their mother's face, was the trouble, the long jaw and the eyes that went sad without permission, and he'd had it at the funeral too, standing at the graveside in a coat that fit him, while Dean stood there in their father's good jacket because he hadn't owned one of his own since his wedding, and that had been a while.

"All right," Toby said. "Since we're saying things. I think it's a fire-sale price for forty acres with water in this county, I think they'll pull every one of these trees inside of a year and put in high-density almonds or pistachios or whatever the spreadsheet says, and I think Dad would rather have burned it."

"Dad's not paying the operating loan."

"And I think you decided to sell it before he was cold, and everything since then has been you building the case."

Dean stood very still. The mockingbird had moved trees and started over. "Get the saw out of the bucket," he said. "We've got to cut out three feet of this and I want it glued before the heat of the day, because the cement flashes too fast over ninety and the joint won't hold."

They bailed the hole again, and Dean got down in it with the handsaw and cut the line on the good pipe either side of the splice, square cuts, taking his time, while groundwater welled up around his boots and Toby knelt above him bailing with the coffee can in a steady mechanical rhythm, fifty cans, sixty, keeping the water just below the cut so the pipe ends stayed dry enough to take primer. It was close, ugly work, and they did it without talking, and there was a comfort in that which neither of them would have named. The cut section came free at last and Dean handed it up — four feet of pipe with their father's repair in the middle of it, the rubber gone brittle as old harness, the clamps bleeding rust — and Toby took it and held it across his knees and didn't set it down for a long moment.

"You want to bronze it?" Dean said.

"I want to know how a man patches a mainline with a tire and keeps a place going forty years, and we can't."

"He had Mom. He had peach prices in 1979. He had a packing shed in town that hadn't closed yet, and a co-op, and a body that didn't quit on him till he was seventy-eight." Dean pulled himself out of the hole. "And he had me. Don't leave that off the inventory."

They needed three feet of four-inch and two couplings. There was pipe behind the shed — there was everything behind the shed — but when Dean went through the fittings bins he had two three-inch couplings, a drawer of galvanized elbows, and no four-inch anything, and the supply house in town was forty minutes round trip and the hole was filling with water all the while. It was Toby who found it. He'd been going through the shed shelves the way you go through a dead man's shelves, half looking for the part and half just looking, and he came out into the light holding a coffee can — Folgers, the red faded to pink — packed with fittings, every one of them cleaned and wiped with oil, and in among them two four-inch couplings, new in their dust.

"He had them," Toby said. "He had them the whole time."

"He was saving them," Dean said, and heard how it sounded, and laughed, a short ugly laugh with no floor under it. "For something important."

They cut the new section up by the truck where it was dry, dry-fit the whole assembly, marked everything with a carpenter's pencil. Then came the bailing again, in earnest now, both of them, can and shovel-blade and a cut-down jug, getting the water down and keeping it down, and Dean swabbed the pipe ends with the purple primer that stained his fingers and made the air over the hole shimmer with solvent, and then the cement, working fast, push and quarter-turn and hold, the way their father's hands had done it while two boys watched from the edge of an earlier hole in an earlier June, and the joint set up, and the second joint, and then there was nothing to do but wait. An hour, the can said, before pressure. Two would be better, with the line as old as it was.

It was eleven thirty and ninety-four degrees. They walked up out of the row to the big tree by the equipment shed, the seedling peach their father had found growing out of the burn pile in 1973 and had grafted from ever since, a tree that made fruit too soft to ship and too good to sell, and they sat in its shade with their backs against the trunk, and Toby brought sandwiches from the house and two beers wrapped in a wet dish towel, and for a while they ate and said nothing and watched the heat stand in the rows.

"I ran the numbers on the plane," Toby said finally. "If we kept it. If I put in what I could."

"What could you put in?"

"Some. Not nothing." He turned the beer in his hands. "Things are — Sarah and I are working some stuff out. I don't know how it lands. So I can't promise a number. But I could come down more. Take the harvest weeks off. I've got leave I never use."

"Harvest weeks," Dean said. "Plural. You'd come for the harvest weeks."

"I'm trying to —"

"There's no harvest weeks, Toby. There's three hundred and sixty-five days, and the frost calls come at two in the morning, and you stand out there with the wind machine and a thermometer praying for one degree. One degree. And I did that this April and lost the lower block anyway, and then I came in and made Dad's breakfast and changed Dad's sheets, because by April he didn't always make it to the bathroom, and you want to know something? He'd cry about it. Not about dying. About the sheets. About me seeing." Dean was looking straight out into the orchard. "And you'd call Sunday nights, and he'd sit up straight in the chair with the phone and tell you the bloom looked strong. Bloom looked strong, Toby. I'm standing in the kitchen listening to him lie to you so you'd keep thinking of the place the way you wanted to think of it, and then he'd hand me the phone and you'd say, sounds like things are pretty good down there."

Toby didn't say anything for a while. A scrub jay dropped into the seedling tree above them, found nothing ripe, swore at them once, and left.

"I sent money," he said quietly. "Every month for six years. You think I don't know things weren't good?"

"I know you sent it. I deposited it. It's in the operating loan along with everything else, which is to say it's gone, which is the point I keep trying to make and you keep hearing as some kind of moral failing. The place loses money. Not because Dad was careless, not because I'm not him, not because the trees did anything wrong. Forty acres of soft fruit can't carry itself anymore, not at our scale, not with our water, not with what the packers pay. Vogel survives because he's two hundred acres and his kids work for free. Pacific Crest survives because it's not a farm, it's an instrument. We're a museum, Toby. We're a museum with a mortgage."

"Then why does it gut you," Toby said. "If it's just arithmetic, why do you look like that when you say it."

And there it was, the thing Dean had been keeping off to one side of himself since February, and his brother had walked over and put a hand on it, the way he'd always been able to do, the younger one, the one who left, the one who got to love the place because he didn't have to carry it.

"Because I'm fifty-four years old," Dean said, "and the only thing I know how to do is keep something alive that's dying. And I'm tired. And I can't tell anymore if I'm tired the way you get tired and rest, or tired the way Dad got tired. And every time you fight me on this, what I hear is that I'm supposed to keep going till there's nothing left to sell and nothing left of me either, so that you can keep a picture in your head. You've got a picture, Toby. I've got a body. They're not the same thing."

Toby set the beer down in the grass very carefully, as if it might go off. "That's not fair."

"No," Dean agreed. "It isn't."

"I left because there wasn't room. You know that. There was you and there was Dad and there was one farm, and the two of you fit it like — I was the spare. I was the spare part in the coffee can." He laughed, surprising both of them. "Saved for something important."

"He never thought of you like that."

"I know what he thought. I'm telling you what it was like to be nineteen in that kitchen." Toby pulled his knees up, city slacks ruined now, mud to the shin, blisters open on both hands, and he looked, for the first time since the airport, like somebody Dean knew. "And then it turns out you spend thirty years building a life out of not-this, and the whole time, the floor under the life is that this is here. That I could be the one who left because you were the one who stayed. You sell it, I'm not losing an asset, Dean. I'm losing the thing I left. It stops meaning anything that I went, if there's nothing it was instead of."

The heat ticked in the shed's metal roof. Down the rows the leaves hung without moving, and under them the dropped green fruit lay in the grass, and farther down, where the line had been off all morning, Dean could almost feel the trees noticing, the way he'd come to believe they noticed, the soil drying back from the feeder roots, the whole block holding its breath.

"It meant something that you went," Dean said at last. "It meant Mom had one thing she didn't have to worry about. You don't know what that bought her. Or me."

Toby looked at him. "You never said that."

"There's a lot of things. We're not a family that says, we're a family that splices." He got up, knees announcing themselves, and looked at his watch. "It's been an hour ten. Give it another twenty and we'll pressure it up slow."

They spent the twenty minutes backfilling around the repair by hand, leaving the joints themselves exposed so they could watch them under pressure, tamping the mud and hardpan back in layers, and then Dean walked up to the pump shed alone. He stood a minute with his hand on the breaker. Through the doorway he could see the ditch running by, full and brown and indifferent, the same water that had made the place possible and was now, by the acre-foot, worth more than anything the place could grow with it, which was the whole story of the valley if you wanted it in one sentence. He threw the breaker. The pump caught, groaned, found its note. The hum came back up under everything.

By the time he walked back down the row, Toby was kneeling at the hole like a man at a manger. The line had filled — they could both hear it, the hollow rush going to silence as the air worked out through the lateral valves — and the pressure came up, and the two new joints sat there in the wet ground doing the one thing a good joint does, which is nothing. Not a bead of water. Not a whisper. Down the rows the microsprinklers caught, sputtered, spat air, and then one after another steadied into their small silver circles, hundreds of them, the whole block coming on like a town at evening, and the smell rose up off the dry ground the way it does only the first minute, dust and water making that smell there's a word for that nobody uses, and the two of them stood in the middle of it not talking.

"Hold, you son of a gun," Dean said to the pipe, and it held.

They watched it the way you watch a thing you don't quite trust yet, ten minutes, fifteen, and then Dean walked the row with the shovel over his shoulder, checking heads, clearing a clogged emitter with the tip of his knife, straightening a riser the gophers had tilted, and Toby walked it with him, and the water ticked against the leaves, and the trees stood in their lines drinking, and the light went long and yellow the way June light does when the day finally relents, around six, when the shadows of the trees lie down across the rows and touch each other.

At the end of the row Toby stopped. "I'll sign it," he said. "Friday. I'll sign."

Dean turned around.

"You're right," Toby said. "About all of it. The arithmetic, the body, what I've been asking you to be so I could be what I am. You're right and I've known you were right since February, and I've been making you fight me for it because as long as you were fighting me, somebody was still fighting for it, and it didn't have to be me." He looked down the wet row, at the silver circles going off into the trees. "I don't want a museum either. I want you to have a life. I'll sign."

And Dean stood there with the shovel on his shoulder in the orchard his grandfather had planted and his father had spliced together out of rubber and luck and his own two hands had kept alive past all sense, and he found that the thing he had wanted for five months, now that it was handed to him, sat in his chest like a stone. Down in the Suncrest block the repair was holding. The water was finding the roots in the dark under the ground, where everything that mattered on the place had always happened out of sight. By fall the trees would be somebody's line item. They'd be pulled by spring, likely, and the wood pushed into piles and burned, and something efficient planted in laser-straight rows, and the seedling tree by the shed would go with the rest, and there was no arithmetic anywhere that said otherwise.

"The offer's good till Friday," Dean heard himself say. "Water's good tonight. Suncrests'll be ripe in five weeks. Dad's tree maybe four." He shifted the shovel. "Man could think about whether anything has to be signed before a harvest he's already paid for."

Toby was quiet a moment. "Five weeks."

"I'm not saying different than I've been saying. I'm saying five weeks."

"And then?"

"Then we'll see what we see."

They walked back up toward the house in the long light, past the open hole they'd fill tomorrow, past the cut-out section of pipe lying in the grass with their father's repair still clamped uselessly to the middle of it, and Toby picked it up as they went by and put it in the bed of the truck, not saying why, and Dean not asking. Behind them the sprinklers ran on into the dusk, all down the rows, that steady patient sound, water going where it was sent, and the two of them went up to the house to wash their hands and find something for supper, and the orchard stood in the falling dark and drank.
