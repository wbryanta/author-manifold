They left the house in the dark and drove out through the gate and down along the headland road with the truck lights yawing over the rows and the dust standing in the lights behind them like something let loose from the ground itself. The orchard trees went filing past in their long ranks, gray and shapeless in that hour, and where the dead ones stood among the living they stood like the burnt survivors of some old pestilence, leafless and black and holding up their limbs in attitudes of supplication or of warning. Wesley drove with one wrist hung over the wheel and Travis rode with his elbow out the window and neither of them spoke. In the east the sky had begun to pale along the rim of the mountains and a mockingbird was already at its catalogue of cries somewhere out in the dark and would not quit.

They parked at the end of the seventh row where the standpipe rose out of the ground rusted and canted and they got down and stood looking out at the trees in the gray light. The ground down the row was dry and cracked and floured with dust and the leaves on the trees hung slack and dull even at that hour, before the sun had come to make its claims upon them. Travis walked out a little ways and squatted and took up a handful of the dirt and let it run through his fingers and stood again and wiped his hand on the leg of his jeans.

How long's it been down?

Nine days. Ten.

Travis looked down the row. He didnt say anything.

I know it, Wesley said.

You could lose this whole block.

I said I know it.

They got the shovels and the bar out of the truckbed and Wesley took up the wooden box of his father's pipe tools, the handles of the wrenches worn pale and smooth as bone, and they set out down the row. The break was easy enough to find. A hundred yards along there was a low place in the ground gone green with a sudden uncalled grass, bright as something painted there, and the dirt about it lay dark and sodden in a country where nothing else was wet at all. The water had been finding its way up out of the ground for some time and it had made of that small place a separate season. Doves had been coming to it. Their tracks were stitched everywhere in the mud like script.

Wesley stood looking down at it. The line was his grandfather's line, laid in the year before the war, and the old man had hauled the pipe up from Valentine on a flatbed truck with no brakes to speak of and he had buried it three foot down the whole length of the orchard with a mule and a slipscraper and the labor of his own back, and all of this Wesley knew the way you know things that have been told to you so many times that they come to seem like things remembered, and he stood above the wet ground with the shovel in his hand like a man called upon to open a grave the occupant of which he'd known in life.

You want the east side or the west? Travis said.

Dont matter.

Then I'll take the east.

They dug. The sun came up over the Sierra Vieja and laid their shadows out long and thin down the row and the light came warm across their backs and the doves flushed up out of the trees and crossed overhead with their wings whistling and dropped down into the next block over. The dirt came up dark and heavy where the water had been at it and drier as they squared away from the leak, and they worked without talking, the shovels gritting in the ground in a slow alternation like the workings of some rude engine, and after a while Travis stopped and took off his hat and wiped his head with his forearm and put the hat back on.

I forgot what this was like, he said.

It aint changed.

No, I dont reckon it has.

He stood the shovel in the spoil and looked back up the row toward the truck. You remember when the old man had us cleanin this line out with rods? That summer it kept gettin roots in it?

I remember it.

Hundred and four degrees. Him standin over us tellin us we was slow.

We was slow.

We was twelve year old.

Wesley bent and threw a shovelful up onto the bank. He never asked nothin of nobody he didnt ask of hisself.

That's a fine sayin. I've heard it before.

It's true all the same.

There's lots of things true, Travis said. He took up the shovel again. That dont always make em worth a man's life.

They came onto the pipe a foot down on Wesley's side, the old steel scaled and pitted and bleeding rust into the wet earth, and they followed it along and cleared it and found the split, a seam opened up along the underside the length of a man's hand, and the water still standing in the trench from where it had wept itself out over the days. Wesley knelt and ran his thumb along the split and looked at his thumb.

It's give out, Travis said.

It's one seam.

It's one seam this week.

We got pipe at the barn.

You got forty foot of pipe at the barn and you got a mile and a half of this in the ground and ever foot of it the same age as that piece right there.

Wesley stood up out of the trench. We goin to fix it or we goin to hold services over it?

Travis looked at him a long moment. Then he shook his head and reached and got the hacksaw out of the box.

They cut the pipe out on either side of the split, taking turns at the saw, the blade singing thinly in the morning quiet and the orange dust of the rust sifting down into the water below, and when the section came free Wesley lifted it out and set it up on the bank and they both stood looking at it. The inside of the pipe was furred over with scale and tubercles of rust so that the bore of it was near closed in places, a dark and ruined gullet, and Travis nudged it with his boot.

You been pushin water through that.

What there was to push.

That well aint comin back neither. You know that. It's eight foot lower than it was when Daddy was alive and it was twenty foot lower then than when he was a boy. You can patch pipe from here till doomsday. You cant patch what aint under the ground no more.

It rains, the water comes back.

When's it rained?

It'll rain.

You sound like him.

Wesley looked at him. He didnt sound no way at all about it, he said. He just done what the day in front of him called for and got up the next mornin and done it again.

And died in a tractor seat at sixty-one year old owin the bank.

He didnt owe the bank nothin when he died.

No. Because he sold the Alamito pasture to pay em. Eight hundred acres his own daddy had proved up and he sold it and never said one word about it at the supper table, not one, and you want to tell me what that done to him because I watched it. You was in school. I watched it.

Wesley picked up the cut section of pipe and carried it over and laid it in the spoil and came back. We need the forty foot, he said. This trench wants openin another eight feet anyways. That seam'll run.

All right, Travis said.

They walked back up the row to the truck and drove to the barn and loaded the new pipe and the couplings and the dope and the big wrenches, and while Wesley was hunting the second pipe wrench in the dark of the toolroom Travis stood in the bay door looking out at the lots, the empty corrals, the loadingchute silvered and split with sixty years of weather, the windmill above the stocktank turning slow and dry in a wind that wasnt hardly a wind at all. Swallows came and went under the eaves above him. When Wesley came out with the wrench Travis was still standing there.

What, said Wesley.

Nothin.

Say it.

I aint said nothin.

You're fixin to.

Travis turned and looked at him. You know what Lattimore's man offered. I dont have to say it.

No you dont.

Eleven hundred a acre, Wes. For dirt that wont bring three fifty at a courthouse sale. They dont want the trees. They want the water rights and they'll doze ever tree on the place inside of a month and they know the water's failin same as we do and they're offerin it anyway because it suits somethin in their books somewheres and there wont come another offer like it. Not in your life nor mine.

It aint yours to sell.

Half of it is.

Wesley stood with the wrench in his hand. The swallows dipped and swerved in the bay door light between them. Then you can sell your half, he said, and he walked past him to the truck.

They drove back out and neither of them spoke. The sun was well up now and the heat was coming into the day and the mountains to the west had gone flat and blue and standing above them to the south a hawk wheeled on the first thermals, tilting and tilting, scribing out its slow helix against a sky that was without color almost, as if the heat had bled it. They opened the trench out where the seam ran soft and they cut the pipe square and dressed the ends with a file and Wesley doped the threads of the coupling and they set the new joint and took it up with the wrenches, one on either side of the trench, leaning against each other's pull, their boots cutting into the soft dirt of the bank, and the pipe creaked and the dope oozed gray at the collar and they took it up another quarter turn and stood off blowing and wiping their faces.

By then it was noon or near it. They sat in the shade of a big tree in the eighth row, one of the old Comice pears Wesley's grandfather had put in for reasons no one now knew, since there'd never been a market for them, and they ate the lunch Wesley had packed in the dark of the kitchen that morning. Cold biscuits and ham and a jar of tea gone warm. Travis leaned back against the trunk and looked up into the canopy where the small hard green fruit hung among the leaves.

These never made nothin, did they.

Made enough for the house. Mama canned em.

I remember the jars in the cellar. Rows of em.

She's still got some down there. There's jars down there older than your boy.

Travis smiled. Then he stopped smiling. He sat turning the jarlid in his fingers like a coin.

I went and seen her Tuesday, he said.

I know it. She told me.

Did she know you?

Most of the day she does. Mornins mostly.

She called me Carl, Travis said. Sat right there and called me by her dead brother's name and told me I ought to have wrote her more from overseas. Carl never come back from overseas. I didnt know what to say to her. I just set there.

You done right. You cant correct em. The nurse said.

I know what the nurse said. Travis pitched the jarlid into the grass and looked out down the rows. The light came through the leaves and moved on the ground at their feet in small coins and the cicadas had started up in the heat, that long dry seething that seems less a sound than a property of the air itself, of the heat, the very voice of it. Her room out there costs thirty-one hundred dollars a month, he said. You want to tell me where that comes from when the fruit checks quit? You think I dont know what you cleared last year? I seen the gin statement, Wes. I seen it layin on the sideboard at Christmas and I read it and I wisht I hadnt.

Wesley didnt say anything.

I aint sayin it to be hard, said Travis.

I know what you're sayin it for.

Do you.

You're sayin it cause you think if you say it enough times it'll quit bein a thing you're doin to me and start bein a thing the numbers done. But numbers dont do nothin. Men do it and put the numbers out front of em like a shield.

That's a fine speech.

I aint done. You want to know what I think. I think a piece of ground is a kind of promise. I think when that old man laid this pipe he wasnt layin it for hisself, he never eat a peach off these trees till he was near fifty, he laid it for people that wasnt born yet and some that aint born now, and when you sell it off you aint just sellin dirt. You're tellin all of em, the dead ones and the ones that aint come yet both, that the promise dont hold. That it never held. That it was just dirt the whole time and them lives got spent on dirt.

Travis sat very still. Far off, down toward the river, a truck passed on the highway and the sound of it came to them small and was gone. He spent his life on it, Travis said. That much is so. And his daddy before him. But I'll tell you what else is so. The dead dont hold deeds. You can call it a promise if you want. I call it eleven hundred dollars a acre and Mama took care of and your boy not standin in a trench at fifty with his back gone like that old man and like you're goin to be, and if the dead got anything to say about that they can come say it to me theirselves. I'll listen. I'll be right here.

It aint about the dead.

You just now said it was.

It's about what a man's for.

A man aint for any one thing. That's the trouble with you. You think there's a right shape to a life and it looks like his.

There's worse shapes.

There is, said Travis. I've seen some of em. I seen one of em settin on a porch in Pecos that sold out in seventy-eight and drunk it and I seen another one that didnt sell and the bank took it anyways and he didnt even get the money, he just got the leavin. You think holdin on is a virtue. Sometimes it's just the slow way of losin.

Wesley screwed the lid onto the empty jar and put it in the sack and rose and stood looking down the long aisle of the trees to where the heat stood shimmering off the ground at the end of the row so that the farthest trees seemed to float untethered above the earth, unmoored and wavering, the whole orchard at its farthest reach become a thing half dreamt.

We got pipe to lay, he said.

They laid the forty feet through the heat of the afternoon. They doped and made up the joints and they checked the fall with a string and a line level that had been their father's and the small green bubble in its vial of oil was perhaps the only thing in all that country that hour that was not subject to the heat, and they shoveled the dirt back over the pipe and tamped it along the trench with the flat of the shovels. Once Travis hollered and stepped back and a rattlesnake came pouring out of the spoilbank where it had lain up in the cool of the turned earth and crossed the open row in its slow floodings and went in under the drip line of the trees. They stood watching it go. It moved through the bars of light and shadow without haste, as if it held title there, as if it were the residue of some older order upon which the orchard had been but lately and lightly imposed, and Travis raised the shovel and then lowered it again and let the snake go on.

Daddy'd of killed it, he said.

It eats gophers. Gophers eat roots.

Travis looked at him. You'd take the snake's part against your own brother, wouldnt you.

The snake aint tryin to sell my orchard.

Travis laughed in spite of himself, a short dry laugh, and shook his head and bent back to the work.

By evening they were done. The sun was low over the mountains and the light had gone long and red down the rows and the heat was lifting out of the ground the way it does, you could feel it rising past your face like something leaving. They walked back up to the standpipe and Wesley went on to the well house and Travis stood in the row and waited, and after a while he heard the motor start, faint and laboring, and he stood watching the trench line, the long ribbon of raw dirt running away down the row, and presently there came a knocking in the pipe under the ground and a sound of air chuffing at the standpipe and then the water came.

It came up brown at first and then clearing, threading down the dry furrows in the last light, and the dirt drank it with a faint hissing all down the row, the cracked ground sealing and darkening, and the smell of it rose into the evening, that smell of rain on dust that is like no other smell and that men in dry countries will tell you is the finest smell there is and may be right. Travis stood and watched it come. The water found the low places and stood in them glittering with the red of the sky and went on, and a killdeer appeared out of nowhere and ran piping along the wet margin as though it had been waiting somewhere all those nine days for this and perhaps it had.

Wesley came back down the row and stood beside him and they watched the water move down through the trees.

She holds, Wesley said.

She holds.

They stood there. The sun went down behind the Sierra Vieja and the mountains stood black against the burning and then the burning dimmed and in the east the moon was rising full out of the dusk, pale and rinsed and enormous over the orchard, so that for that little time the two lights hung opposed across the world with the trees and the water and the two men held between them.

I aint goin to sell out from under you, Travis said. He said it watching the water, not looking at him. I want you to know that. I'll not say I think you're right because I dont. I think this place'll break you and I think you know it and I think you've made some kind of a peace with it that I cant make and maybe that's the difference in us and maybe it always was. But I'll not be the one. You'll not look down a row someday and think it was your brother done it.

Wesley didnt answer for a while. The water came on past them down the furrows, whispering in the dirt.

I know what it costs you to say that, he said.

No you dont. But that's all right.

What about Mama.

We'll figure Mama. I been talkin to a fella about the Alamito money, what's left of it. There's ways. We'll figure it.

All right.

The frogs had started up down at the stocktank, you could hear them now in the stillness, and the first nighthawks were out over the trees, falling and booming in the dark air, hunting whatever rose off the watered ground. Travis put on his hat.

I told Janie I'd be back by dark.

You're done missed that.

I reckon I have. He looked back down the row a last time. Out there the water lay in its long lanes catching the moon, lane after lane of light going away under the trees as far as a man could see, like something poured out of the moon itself and set running in the earth. You'll be out here at daylight movin them sets, he said.

I will.

By yourself.

By myself.

Travis nodded slowly. Well, he said. If you was to find a extra shovel around the place. In case some fool showed up wantin to dig.

I might could find one.

I aint promisin nothin.

I know it.

They walked up to the truck and got in and Wesley turned it around in the headland and they drove back up along the rows the way they'd come, the lights swinging through the trees, and behind them in the dark the water went on spreading through the orchard with no need of them or of any man, finding its way among the roots of the living trees and the dead ones alike, asking nothing, settling, going down.
