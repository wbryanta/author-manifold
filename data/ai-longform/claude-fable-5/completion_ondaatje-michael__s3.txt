Anil knelt where the woman had been and felt the warmth still in the soil, the small depression her weight had made. For the rest of the afternoon she worked in silence, lifting the dirt away from a clavicle with a brush whose bristles were softer than a child's hair. She had learned this much in her training, that you approached the dead the way you approached the sleeping. You did not wake them roughly.

That was Guatemala. That was four years before she came back to the island where she was born.

She arrived in Colombo in the early dark of a March morning, the plane dropping through cloud so that the lights of the coast appeared suddenly, scattered and dim, like coals raked apart. She had been away fifteen years. From the window she could see the black shoulder of the sea and then the runway lights, and as the wheels touched she felt nothing she could name. Not return. Not arrival. A neutral hum in her chest, the way a tuning fork sounds before it is brought to the wood.

In the terminal the air was the first familiar thing—the dense wet heat that pressed against her even at four a.m., carrying kerosene and frangipani and something underneath, vegetal and old. A man in a pale suit held a card with her name printed in marker. ANIL TISSERA. Her name, which she had taken for herself at sixteen, bought from her brother with a hundred saved rupees and a packet of cigarettes, and which she had carried since like a passport more genuine than the one in her hand.

'You are the woman from Geneva,' he said.

'From Geneva. Yes. Though I've never lived there.' The organization's headquarters were in Geneva. She herself had lived in London, in Oklahoma, in Arizona. She was thirty-three years old and she carried her profession in two aluminum cases that the customs officers opened and looked into and closed again quickly, as if the calipers and the bone chisels and the small folded body bags were obscene.

'There is a car,' the man said. 'You will rest today. Tomorrow you meet Mr. Sarath Diyasena. He is the archaeologist who is paired with you.'

'Paired.' She tested the word.

'For the investigation. The government wishes a local representative to work alongside.'

Of course it did. A human rights inquiry conducted at the sufferance of the very state that was being inquired into. She had read the file on the flight from Amsterdam: seven weeks, one forensic anthropologist, one government-approved archaeologist, a mandate worded so carefully it was almost a poem. To examine and report upon. To determine where possible. Where possible. She had underlined that in pencil and then erased it.

The car took her along the airport road in the dark. Coconut palms moved overhead, and at intervals there were checkpoints lit by single bulbs, soldiers younger than her students had been, rifles held the way boys hold cricket bats, loosely, with a terrible casualness. At each barrier the driver slowed and said a word and they were waved on. She watched the soldiers' faces as they passed. None of them looked at her. They looked at the car, at the plates, at the dark beyond the car. They were reading a different language.

She had grown up here. Until she was eighteen this had been the whole of the world—the lane in Colombo Seven, the swimming meets, her father coming home late from surgeries with the smell of the hospital on him, her mother's radio playing at dusk. Then the scholarship, England, the gradual shedding. She had unlearned the habit of this place the way you unlearn a sport, muscle by muscle, until what remained was only a kind of phantom coordination, an ache when it rained.

She slept that day in a guesthouse near the sea and woke at noon not knowing what country she was in. This had happened to her in Guatemala too, and in the Arizona desert. It was, she sometimes thought, the one permanent condition of her life. You woke and for three seconds you were nowhere, you were only yourself, a body in a bed, and then the world assembled itself around you piece by piece—the fan, the light through the curtain, the particular birds—and you remembered what you were for.

Sarath Diyasena was older than she expected, perhaps forty-nine, a heavy quiet man with reading glasses pushed up into grey-flecked hair. They met in an office on the third floor of a building that had been an insurance company and was now nothing in particular, government somehow, the corridors lined with metal cabinets nobody had opened in years. He shook her hand and looked at her for slightly too long.

'You were the swimmer,' he said.

'What?'

'Anil Tissera. You won the two-mile race, the open-water swim at Mount Lavinia. There was a photograph in the papers. Nineteen seventy—'

'Six.' She felt herself flush, absurdly. 'I was sixteen. That's a long memory.'

'It's a small country. We keep everything.' He said it without irony, or with an irony so deep she couldn't sound it. 'You should know that. Whatever else you've forgotten. Nothing here is ever really lost. It's only buried.'

'Buried things are my profession.'

'Yes,' he said. 'Mine also.'

They drank tea out of glasses while a clerk brought in files tied with red string, the knots stiff with age. The mandate, the letters of authorization, the lists. The lists were what she had come for and what she could hardly bear to read: names of the disappeared, compiled by mothers' organizations and parish priests and one retired schoolteacher in Matara who had recorded in a child's exercise book, in handwriting that grew smaller as the pages filled, the names of one hundred and seventy-four people taken from his district between 1988 and 1990. Beside each name, the date, the time if known, the witnesses if any, and a column the schoolteacher had headed, in English, Returned. The column was empty all the way down.

'Where do we begin?' she asked.

Sarath set his glass down. 'There is a site,' he said. 'In the south, near a place called Bandarawela. A cave shelter. Sixth century—monks were interred there, it's been a protected archaeological reserve since the twenties. Government land. No one enters without a permit from my department.' He paused, and she understood the pause was the whole point, that everything before it had been approach. 'Last month one of my students was doing a survey of the lower chamber. He found skeletons. Three of them are old. Sixth century, as they should be.'

'And?'

'There is a fourth.'

The drive south took most of a day. They went in a jeep Sarath drove himself, refusing a driver, and she understood that too without being told: a driver was a witness, and a witness was a report, and a report went somewhere. The road ran along the coast through Kalutara and Beruwala, the sea on their right flashing between coconut trees, and then turned inland and began to climb. Paddy gave way to rubber, rubber to tea. The air cooled. At a bend above a valley he stopped the jeep and they ate lunch out of a tiffin carrier his housekeeper had packed—rice, dried fish, a sambol so hot it made her eyes water, and she was glad of it, glad of one thing her body still remembered how to do.

'Tell me about him,' she said. 'The fourth one.'

'You'll see him tomorrow.'

'Tell me anyway. You've already looked. You wouldn't have brought me otherwise.'

Sarath ate slowly, looking at the valley. 'The three monks were buried. Properly, in a prepared niche, with care. The fourth was not buried at all. He was placed. Pushed into a recess at the back of the chamber and covered with rubble. And he is not sixth century.' He wiped his fingers on a square of newspaper. 'There is fusing in the joints consistent with a man between twenty-eight and forty. There is soil in the bone that does not belong to that cave. He was buried somewhere else first, and moved. And whoever moved him chose a government reserve. A place no villager can enter. A place where no one would ever dig except an archaeologist—and archaeologists, when they find bones in a sixth-century site, assume they are looking at the sixth century.'

'It's a good hiding place,' Anil said.

'It is a perfect hiding place,' said Sarath. 'That is what frightens me. It required knowledge. It required a permit, or the power not to need one.'

Neither of them said the next thing. It sat between them in the jeep all the rest of the afternoon, through the tea country and the eucalyptus and the first chill of altitude: that the only people who could pass freely into a protected reserve, in this country, in these years, came from the government itself.

The rest house at Bandarawela had eight rooms and they were the only guests. That night she lay under a blanket—a blanket, after Colombo's heat—and listened to rain begin on the tin gutters, and thought of Cullis, whom she had loved badly in another hemisphere, and of her father, dead now four years, killed not by the war but by an ordinary car on an ordinary road while driving to a delivery at two in the morning, the way he had driven all his life. She had not come back for the funeral. She had been in a tent in the Guatemalan highlands with a radio that worked one hour a day, and by the time the message reached her he was already ash. Her mother had died two years before him. So she had returned to an island that held no one who was hers, and perhaps, she thought, that was the only way she could have returned at all. Free. Or unaccompanied, which a tired mind can mistake for freedom.

The cave was a forty-minute walk from the road, up through a plantation and then along a ridge of bare rock. Sarath's student, a thin serious boy named Chitra's brother—she never did fix his actual name in her mind that first day, only that he was somebody's brother, everyone here was somebody's brother—had set hurricane lamps inside, and their light made the chamber breathe, shadows leaning and straightening on the stone. The three ancient skeletons lay in their niche like a quiet argument for the possibility of peace: composed, attended, sixteen centuries of stillness in their long bones.

The fourth lay where the rubble had been lifted from him, on black plastic sheeting, and the moment Anil crouched beside him she stopped being a returning daughter, a swimmer, a woman who had slept badly. The work took her the way water takes you when you dive well, totally and without seam.

He had been a strong man. The muscle attachments on the arm bones were pronounced—a laborer, or someone who did one repeated motion for years. There was a healed fracture in the left forearm, old, from childhood maybe, a fall from a tree. There was betel staining on the teeth. And there was the other thing, which she found in the first hour and did not speak about until she had checked it three times in three different lights: a fine transverse fracture across the hyoid, and notches—she would not let herself say cut marks, not yet, not aloud—on two of the cervical vertebrae.

'He didn't die in this cave,' she said at last.

'No.'

'He didn't die in his bed, either.'

Sarath was quiet. The lamps hissed. Outside, far down the valley, a single bird repeated one note over and over, patient as a clock.

'I want to call him something,' she said. 'I can't keep saying "the fourth skeleton." If we're going to find out who he was, he needs a name in the meantime.'

'Choose one.'

She looked at the long composed body, the femurs ivory in the lamplight, the skull turned slightly aside as though listening for someone coming. The three monks beside him had been named centuries ago, were in the chronicles. He had nothing.

'Sailor,' she said, not knowing why—perhaps the old child's rhyme, tinker, tailor, soldier, sailor, the way it sorted unknown men into fates. 'We'll call him Sailor.'

They worked on Sailor for eleven days in the cave, and then, when Sarath had arranged the papers that allowed it—papers were his genius, she came to see; he moved through the bureaucracy the way she moved through bone, knowing where the seams were—they transferred him in a crate marked ARTIFACTS, 6TH C., to a disused wing of a hospital outside Ratnapura, three rooms with high colonial ceilings and a smell of carbolic that the years had pressed into the very plaster. There was a steel table. There was, miraculously, electricity most evenings. There was a tamarind tree outside the window in which, at dusk, hundreds of mynahs argued themselves to sleep.

It became, for a time, her whole world, that wing. She would wake before light in the rest house and be at the table by six, and the work was the old work, the work she could have done blind: the patient interview of the body. Because that was what it was. You asked, and the bones answered, in the only language left to them. Age—the pubic symphysis said between thirty and thirty-five. Height—the femur said five foot eight. The wear on the right shoulder and the asymmetry of the arm bones said a man who had swung something heavy through the same arc thousands upon thousands of times, and it was Sarath, watching her demonstrate the motion, who said quietly: 'A miner. Or a man in a gem pit. They work the sluice and the basket the way you're showing. There are gem fields all through this district. Ratnapura—it means city of gems.'

'Then he might be from here. From near here.'

'He might have been taken from near here,' Sarath said, 'and hidden a hundred and twenty miles away. That is how it was done. You did not leave a man where his village could find him. A body found is a fact. A body missing is only a rumor, and a rumor can be denied forever.'

She wrote it all down in her precise blue notebooks, and at night she lay awake assembling and disassembling him, this man she knew now more intimately than she had known some lovers—knew the childhood fall from the tree, the betel habit, the years of the swinging basket, the bad tooth that must have ached in the cold weather—and did not know his name, his face, his mother, the day he was taken, the men who took him, the hour and place and reason of his dying, except that the hyoid and the vertebrae kept saying their one terrible sentence over and over in the dark.

There was a man in Colombo, Sarath said, who could give Sailor back his face.

His name was Ananda Udugama, and he had been an artificer, one of the hereditary craftsmen who painted the eyes onto the great Buddha statues—the Netra Mangala, the eye ceremony, the moment a statue stops being stone and becomes sacred, and the painter does not look directly at the face but works backward over his shoulder with a mirror, because no man can meet that gaze straight on while making it. Ananda had not worked in three years. His wife, Sirissa, a schoolteacher, had walked out of her village one morning toward her school and never arrived, and her name was now one line in one of the exercise books, in the column without entries, and Ananda had gone down into arrack the way other men go down into mines.

He came to Ratnapura in a brown sarong and a clean pressed shirt that had been mended at both cuffs, and he stood for a long time at the steel table looking at the skull, and Anil, watching his face, saw something she had seen on the woman crouched in the Guatemalan grave, and looked away to give him room.

He worked for nine days. He would not let them watch the middle stages. He took plasticine and wooden pegs cut to the tissue depths Anil calculated for him, and he sat with the skull in the corner room with the door closed, and sometimes through the door she heard him talking, low, in Sinhala, a current of words like a man keeping a sick friend company through a fever. On the evenings when he had drunk too much he did not work at all; he sat under the tamarind tree until the mynahs settled, and once she sat down near him, not too near, and they stayed there in a silence that was, she understood later, the longest conversation she had in all her months on the island.

On the tenth morning he opened the door and let them in.

The head stood on a wooden block by the window, and the light came onto it sideways, and Anil's first feeling was anger, because it was wrong, it had to be wrong—the face was at peace. It was the face of a man of thirty asleep after work, the lids heavy, the mouth at rest, almost, not quite, smiling. No man whose hyoid said what Sailor's hyoid said had worn that face at the end. She began to say so and stopped, because Sarath touched her arm, and because she saw then what Ananda had done, what he had needed to do. He could not give Sirissa a grave or a face or even a fact. So he had given this unknown man, this stranger's husband, this woman's brother, the death he wished for everyone he loved: a quiet one, a sleep. It was not forensic reconstruction. It was a prayer made out of plasticine. It was also, she slowly admitted, looking at the breadth of the cheekbones, the particular jaw, recognizably a face—a face that someone in a village in the gem country might stop in front of and put a hand over her mouth.

'We can't use it as evidence,' she said quietly.

'No,' Sarath said. 'But we can use it as a question.'

They photographed the head from eight angles, and Sarath had prints made in a shop in Ratnapura whose owner asked nothing, and they began to carry the photographs to the villages around the gem pits, to temple festivals and Sunday markets, the way the families in Guatemala had traveled from excavation to excavation, except that now Anil was on the other side of the search, the one arriving with the possibility in an envelope. She watched dozens of faces look at Sailor's face. She learned to read the half-second before speech. Most shook their heads slowly. Some looked too long. One old woman held the photograph at arm's length and then against her chest and then at arm's length again, and finally said no, no, it was not her son, her son's ears were different, and gave it back, and stood watching them all the way down the road as if they were taking her son away a second time.

In the seventh village, a place of forty houses strung along a red road above a worked-out gem field, a young woman with a baby on her hip looked at the photograph and did not say anything at all. She turned and walked into a house, and they waited in the sun, and after a time an older man came out, a man with a healed stoop and gray stubble, and he looked at the photograph for less than a moment.

'Ruwan,' he said. 'This is Ruwan Kumara. He worked the pit at Eratne. They came for him in the night, the year of the troubles. There was a billa with them—a man with a sack on his head, with eyes cut in the sack, who pointed. The billa pointed at Ruwan and they took him in a lorry and we have never—' He stopped. He gave the photograph back carefully, holding it by the edges, the way you hold something that can still be hurt. 'Who will you tell?' he asked. 'Who is it that you will tell?'

And Anil, standing in the red road with the sun straight overhead and the envelope in her hands, heard the question travel through her and keep going, down through the years and the borders, through Guatemala and the schoolteacher's exercise book and the woman kneeling in the grave, and she understood that this was the only question, that it had been the question all along. Not who he was. They knew now who he was. Not how he died. The bones had said. But who, in a country where the takers and the judges and the keepers of the files might be the same men in different shirts, who would she stand in front of, and say his name, and be heard?

'Everyone,' she said. The word came out of her before caution could reach it. 'We will tell everyone.'

Sarath looked at her then, a long look she could not fully read—warning in it, and fear, and underneath the fear something steadier that might have been the beginning of agreement—and he said nothing on the walk back down the red road, nothing in the jeep for an hour, and when he finally spoke it was almost to himself, his eyes on the white sunstruck distance where the road met the sky.

'Then we must be careful,' he said, 'to live long enough to do it.'
