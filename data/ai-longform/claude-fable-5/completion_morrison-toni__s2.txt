He was not, on the whole, an anxious man, but he kept Regina to a walk where the trail narrowed and listened past the suck of her hooves for whatever the trees might be hiding. Once, a doe and her fawn broke cover ahead of him and he laughed aloud at his own startle, the sound of it strange in all that green silence. The Lenape were mostly gone from here, dead or driven, and what remained of their path was being swallowed year by year—blackberry, fox grape, the patient lean of saplings—so that a man riding it felt less like a traveler than a trespasser in a graveyard whose stones had been carted off.

By midday the heat had a body to it. He stripped off his coat and folded it over the saddlebow and let Regina drink at a creek the color of weak tea. Squatting beside her, scooping water over his own neck, he considered, not for the first time, the strangeness of his being in this place at all. Jacob Vaark, ratter's son, orphan of a Bristol poorhouse where the matron broke ice in the wash basins with her elbow and called it charity; Jacob Vaark, who had eaten bread softened in beer for the first six years of his remembering and counted himself lucky for the beer. That such a boy should now own one hundred and twenty acres of a northern colony, patented and recorded, with meadow and hardwood and a stream that never failed even in August—it argued either for the wild generosity of God or for His complete indifference, and Jacob had never settled which.

The land had come to him from an uncle he had never met, a man whose name he shared and whose face he could not imagine, dead of the bloody flux in a settlement Jacob had needed a mapmaker to find for him. There were conditions attached to such patents—improvement, habitation, a wife—and he had met them all with a kind of stunned diligence, as a man pays debts in a dream. The wife he had ordered, more or less, the way one orders hinges or window glass: letters exchanged, a passage paid, and one cold morning on a dock he had stood searching the faces coming down a gangplank for a woman answering to the description her father had sent, which was honest in its particulars and silent on everything that mattered. Rebekka. Sturdy, the father had written, and not quarrelsome. The father had not written that she laughed easily, or that she would stand in the doorway of their first rough house watching snow come down over the meadow with an expression of such astonished gratitude that Jacob had to turn away from it, or that she would bury three infants and one five-year-old daughter in that same meadow and go on standing in the doorway anyway, her gratitude gone quiet but not, he believed, entirely gone.

He did not like to think of the graves. He thought instead, remounting, of the business that had brought him so far south, and his mood soured accordingly. Lending money had never been his intention. He was a trader—furs, lumber, whatever the ships wanted—and trade he understood: a thing for a thing, value passing hand to hand in daylight. But a man who trades acquires paper, and paper breeds, and somewhere in the breeding Jacob had found himself holding the note of one Senhor Mathias d'Acosta, a tobacco planter on the Chesapeake side, papist, Portuguese by way of the Madeiras, who had borrowed against a crop and a ship's cargo with the airy confidence of a man for whom misfortune was something that happened to laborers. The ship had been held a month in quarantine; a third of the cargo had died in the holds and been thrown to the sharks; the crop had come in poor and the price poorer. The interest alone was now considerable, the principal untouched, and d'Acosta's letters had passed from courtly delay to courtly silence. So Jacob had come himself, three weeks of sloop and saddle, to look the man in the eye, which was, he had learned, the only ledger some men could read.

The trail gave onto a cart road in the late afternoon, and the cart road, after a mile of worm fence and weedy tobacco, gave onto an avenue of planted trees, too young yet to meet overhead but pruned and spaced with an ambition that told Jacob everything before he saw the house. And then he saw the house. It rose at the end of the avenue like a rebuke to the country around it: brick, two full stories and dormers besides, with a fanlight over the door that must have crossed the ocean packed in straw and prayer. Gates of wrought iron stood open, and worked into the iron, gilded, was a word Jacob took at first for the family name and then made out to be Portuguese. He asked the boy who came running for the horse what it meant. The boy, who could not have been more than ten, said without looking up, "It means Beloved-of-God, sir," and led Regina away.

Beloved of God. Jacob stood a moment in the drive, looking from the gilded gate to the fields beyond it, where figures moved slowly between the rows in the deadening heat, and felt the particular distaste that ostentation always raised in him, sharpened now by the knowledge that some portion of this brick and gilt had been purchased with his own unreturned silver.

D'Acosta received him as though Jacob had crossed three colonies expressly to be delighted. He was a soft, scented man of perhaps forty, with mournful eyes and beautiful hands, dressed in a heat-defying quantity of velvet, and his wife, when she appeared, was his echo in silk—small, powdered, hung with so much ornament that she chimed faintly when she moved, like distant harness. They insisted he stay the night; the tide of their hospitality was not to be swum against. He was given a room with a bed wider than some cabins he had slept in, and water scented with something, and at supper he was fed for two hours off plates he suspected were worth more than the meal's whole cost in provisions—though the meal itself was heavy, over-spiced, swimming in sauces that seemed designed to disguise rather than present the food. Jacob, who liked his meat to taste of the animal it had been, ate sparingly and praised everything and watched his host the way he would watch weather.

The talk was of Lisbon, of the insolence of the Virginia burgesses, of the laziness and ingratitude of laborers, of a fever lately gone through the quarters—here Senhora d'Acosta crossed herself and her husband's mournful eyes grew more mournful still, though Jacob understood quickly enough that the mourning was for the investment. Twenty-three, d'Acosta said, holding up his beautiful hands as though the number rested in them. Twenty-three in a single season, do you conceive of it, senhor, and the priest two days' ride away so that they went into the ground without so much as— He did not finish, being interrupted by the arrival of a syllabub.

It was not until the wife had withdrawn and the two men sat over Madeira—of which d'Acosta was extravagantly proud, his family having some ancient connection to its making—that Jacob laid the note on the table between them and said, plainly, that he had come for his money.

What followed was a performance Jacob admired even as it disgusted him. D'Acosta grieved. D'Acosta expostulated. D'Acosta invoked the sea, the quarantine officers of Barbados, the perfidy of the English market, the Almighty's inscrutable will, and the certain triumph of next year's crop, which would be—he kissed his fingers—a marvel. He proposed extensions, partnerships, shares in the marvel. Jacob declined them all with the patience of a man who has been poor and therefore knows exactly what money is, which a man born to it never does. The candles burned down an inch. At last d'Acosta, with the air of one driven to a desperate and distasteful expedient, suggested that since coin was, at this regrettable moment, not to be had, perhaps the senhor would accept payment in kind. He had, he said, property. Excellent property. He waved a hand toward the dark windows, toward the quarters Jacob could not see.

Jacob understood him and felt his stomach close like a fist. "I am a trader in goods," he said. "I don't deal in flesh."

D'Acosta smiled the smile of a man hearing a quaint provincial scruple. Flesh, senhor? It was no different, surely, than dealing in the labor flesh performed, which every man in every colony did who did anything at all. Did not the senhor's furs come off the backs of natives paid in rum and shoddy? Did not his lumber fall to indentured axes? D'Acosta said none of this aloud; he said it with his eyebrows, with the tilt of his glass, and Jacob, to his own irritation, heard it anyway, because some version of it lived already in his own head and woke at night sometimes and walked around.

"Tomorrow," d'Acosta said, rising, "before you go. Only look. Looking costs nothing."

In the wide bed Jacob slept badly. The scented water, the weight of the linen, the silence of a house where every sound of labor had been banished to outbuildings—all of it oppressed him. He dreamed, as he had not in years, of the poorhouse: the long room, the row of cots, the boy in the next cot who coughed all one winter and then did not, and whose cot was filled within the week by another boy as though boys were a crop. He woke before dawn with the dream still on him and lay listening to the first birds and to, very far off, a bell—not a church bell; a plantation bell, flat and peremptory, calling the rows to the rows.

He had meant to refuse breakfast and be gone. Instead, because refusal would have been a rudeness and rudeness a weakness, he found himself after breakfast walking with d'Acosta along the front of the quarters while the people who had not yet gone to the fields were stood out for him in a line, in the climbing heat, to be looked at.

He had seen slaves all his life. He had seen them on docks and in markets and in the kitchens of men he did business with, and he had managed, in the way of men everywhere, to see them and not see them in the same glance. He had never before walked down a line of them as their possible purchaser, and the difference, he discovered, was total. The difference was in their eyes—or rather in the discipline of their eyes, which looked at nothing, at the air a foot above his hat, while their owners' eyes did all the looking; and it was in his own eyes too, which against every instruction from his conscience began, within a dozen steps, to appraise. Sound teeth. A milky eye. A back whose scars d'Acosta hurried him past with a murmur about a former overseer, since dismissed. Jacob felt the appraisal happening in himself the way a man feels fever coming, helplessly, knowing the name of the thing taking him over and unable on that account to stop it.

"None," he said when they reached the end of the line. His voice came out harder than he intended. "I'll take the note's worth in tobacco at next harvest, secured against the land itself, and I'll have it written so before I leave. That is the whole of my offer."

D'Acosta began a lament about the indignity of liens upon ancestral— but Jacob had stopped listening, because at the corner of the cookhouse a woman was standing watching him. She was not in the line. She had a child by the hand, a girl of perhaps six or seven in a shift gone gray with washing, and another, younger, riding her hip, and she was looking at Jacob directly, in violation of all the discipline of eyes, with an expression he could not read and would spend years failing to forget—not pleading, exactly; closer to the look of someone solving a problem of terrible arithmetic at great speed. The girl child had her mother's same wide-set eyes and was wearing, absurdly, a pair of women's shoes many sizes too big, in which she had planted herself with the gravity of a magistrate.

D'Acosta, following his glance, brightened in a way Jacob instantly mistrusted. The cook, he explained. A seasoned woman, valuable, not to be parted with—but there, you see, senhor, was the difficulty of his position made flesh, for the woman bred well and the increase ate. Perhaps the senhor's wife could use—

"No," Jacob said.

But the woman had begun to move. She came across the swept dirt of the yard at a pace pitched precisely between haste and the appearance of haste, and before d'Acosta could do more than frown she had stopped before them, eyes down now, proper now, and said in a low rapid voice, to the ground, to Jacob's boots, "Please, senhor. Take the girl."

The yard went very quiet. Even the younger child on her hip stopped its fidgeting, as though it understood that something was being decided.

"Take my daughter," the woman said again. She did not look up. Her voice did not break or rise; it was the voice of someone who had rehearsed, who had perhaps rehearsed all night, who had begun rehearsing the day the fever went through the quarters or the day before that or the day the girl was born. "She is quick. She does not sicken. She can sew already, and carry, and she minds. Please. You."

That you. Not take her, anyone; not take her, away. You. As though the woman had stood at the cookhouse corner reading him the way he read weather, the way he read debtors, and had concluded something about him in the space of a morning that he himself could not have sworn to. Jacob looked down at the girl in the enormous shoes. She was looking up at him—she had not learned yet, or had this moment forgotten, the discipline of eyes—and her face held no fear and no understanding either, only attention, the pure unbearable attention of a child waiting to learn what the adults have arranged.

He thought, against his will and with a pain that astonished him, of the meadow at home and the small stones in it. He thought of Rebekka at the doorway. He thought: a child in the house. And in the same breath he thought: this is how it is done, this is exactly and precisely how it is done, a scruple dissolved in a mercy, a purchase wearing the clothes of a rescue, and twenty years hence I will tell myself I did a kindness and the telling will even be true, and the truth of it will change nothing about what the iron gate says and what the gilt cost.

D'Acosta was already talking, smooth as the Madeira, about valuation, about deducting such-and-such from the principal, about papers his clerk could draw before noon. The mother stood motionless, her eyes on Jacob's boots, her whole body a held breath.

"The girl and her mother both," Jacob said. "I'll not split them."

It was the mother who answered, before d'Acosta could, and what she said Jacob did not understand then and understood ever after only in pieces, turning it over at night the way a man turns a stone he cannot throw away. "No," she said. "Only her. Only the girl." And she raised her eyes at last, one moment only, and in them Jacob saw the arithmetic finished: a woman who had weighed every roof and every man within her knowledge and every roof and every man beyond it that rumor could reach, and had concluded that the best of all possible fates she could procure for her daughter was to be handed, small and alone, to a stranger with a northern voice who flinched at the word flesh—and that she herself, remaining, was the price of the bargain's smallness, the coin that kept the sum low enough for a flinching man to pay.

D'Acosta named a figure. Jacob, sick at himself, halved it, and they settled in between, and the clerk drew the paper, and by noon it was done: the lien on the land for the balance, and the girl, whose name, the paper said, was Florens, set against the note at a value that would shame Jacob every time he remembered it, which was often.

There were no farewells that he witnessed. He did not know whether this was the mother's mercy or d'Acosta's efficiency or some etiquette of grief he was not privy to. When Regina was brought around, the girl was simply there at the mounting block, a small bundle in her arms—a comb, he would learn later, a strip of cloth, nothing else—still in the enormous shoes. The hostler boy made to take them from her, the shoes being worth something, and the girl made a sound, one sound, low, that stopped him. Jacob said, "Leave her the shoes." He reached down and swung her up before him onto Regina's withers, light as a creel of fish, and felt her go rigid and then, after a mile, not rigid, and after five miles felt her head tip back against his coat front in sleep, the way his own daughter's head had used to do, and rode the rest of that day in a confusion of tenderness and shame so thoroughly mixed that he could no more separate them than separate the gold from the fog he had walked through coming ashore.

She did not speak until evening, when they had made a cold camp off the trail and he had given her bread and the last of his cheese. She ate the way poorhouse children ate, fast and guarded, and when she finished she looked at the dark trees and then at him and asked, in a voice rusty with the day's silence, whether they were going on water. He said yes, two days more, then a sloop, then north. She considered this. Then she asked—and he understood that this was the question the whole day had been carrying—whether her mother knew the way there. Whether, was her meaning, the way could be followed.

Jacob Vaark, who had told profitable lies to harder audiences, found he could not arrange this one in time. The pause itself answered her. She did not cry. She turned her face to the trees again and put her hand inside her bundle, around the comb, and after a while she slept, and he sat up longer than he needed to, feeding the fire he had decided after all to light, telling himself the smoke was worth the risk for the warmth, knowing the warmth was not for him.

Far north, on the hundred and twenty acres, Rebekka would take the child in without a question asked aloud, having her own arithmetic and her own graves; and Lina, who had survived the death of her whole village and been seasoned by Presbyterians and sold for blankets, would look at the girl in the too-big shoes for a long moment and then begin, without a word, to braid her hair, claiming her the only way claiming was left to her. All of that was still to come. For now there was the fire, and the sleeping girl, and the trees full of dark, and Jacob composing in his head, as he did every night of every journey, the house he meant one day to build—not the house he had, which was sufficient and honest, but the other one, the one that had been growing in his mind for years and had today, though he did not yet admit it, acquired a fanlight and an avenue of trees and a gate of wrought iron with no word on it at all, because he was not a man for mottoes; the grandness alone would say what it said. He fell asleep before he caught himself at it.

In the morning the girl was awake before him, sitting with her bundle in her lap, watching the trail behind them where it bent and vanished, and when he stirred she stopped watching it and never, in his sight, watched it again.
