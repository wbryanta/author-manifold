The taxi let her out at the bottom of the drive because the top of it was choked with wedding cars, and so Nora Devane walked the last hundred yards to the Atlantic Hotel the way she had walked them as a girl of nineteen, uphill, into the wind, with the sea making its long grey argument behind her. The gravel was new. The gravel was the first thing she noticed, pale and even and raked, where there had once been potholed tarmac that held the rain for days, and she thought, they would never have let us track that across the back hall, Mrs. Frawley would have had us out with a broom before breakfast.

The façade they had kept, or rebuilt to look kept. Three storeys of grey limestone, the bay windows on the first floor, the word ATLANTIC in raised letters above the porch, repainted gold. If you had only ever seen the postcards you would say nothing had happened here. But the roofline was wrong. She saw it at once, the way you see a familiar face that has had something done to it. The old hotel had carried a row of dormers along the top, six little windows like raised eyebrows, and behind those windows the staff had slept, two and three to a room, under slanted ceilings you learned not to sit up quickly beneath. The new roof was clean and blind. They had not rebuilt the attics. There was no reason to. Nobody housed their chambermaids in the rafters anymore; the girls who turned the beds now drove in from Ennistymon and went home at four.

She stood a moment at the foot of the steps with her overnight bag in one hand and her good coat over her arm, and she let the building look at her. That was how it felt, anyway. Thirty-eight years. She had driven past it twice in all that time, once at her mother's funeral when the cortège took the coast road, once by accident in fog, and both times she had kept her eyes on the white line. Now here she was with an invitation in her handbag, cream card, embossed: *Aoife Lynch and Conor Beirne request the pleasure*. The pleasure. Aoife had rung her in February to tell her where the wedding would be, and there had been a silence on the line that Aoife had let run its full length, which was like her, and then Aoife had said, I want her in the room, Nora. It's the only address I have for her. And what could you say to that. You could say yes. You said yes.

The doors were glass now and opened themselves. Inside, the lobby had been done in the manner of all rebuilt seaside hotels, driftwood greys, lamps like buoys, a gas fire behind glass where the great open hearth had been, and the air smelled of lilies and warm sugar and underneath that, faintly, of fresh paint, because they were always painting, hotels, they never stopped. There was no smell of smoke. She had known there would not be and still some animal part of her had braced for it, walking in, the way your tongue goes to a tooth that was pulled years ago.

"Checking in? Are you with the wedding?" The girl at the desk was perhaps twenty-two, her hair in the kind of plait that took two people or twenty minutes, and her name badge said SIOBHÁN and under it TRAINEE.

"I am. Devane. Nora Devane."

The girl's fingers went over the keyboard. Behind her, where the wall of pigeonholes had been, the wooden ones with the brass numbers and the heavy keys hanging on their fobs shaped like lighthouses, there was now a single large photograph of the beach at low tide, very beautiful, completely empty. Nora used to love the sound of the keys, the way Mr. Considine would take them down and put them up all evening, a soft irregular music behind everything, and the weight of a pass-key in your apron pocket so you always knew which side you'd hung it.

"Room two-eleven," Siobhán said, and slid across a card in a paper sleeve. "Second floor, the lift's just there. Breakfast's from seven. And the wedding party's gathering in the Moher Suite from half two."

"Is that the old ballroom?"

The girl's face did the small polite thing faces do when you mention an old anything. "I think it might be, yes. It's the big room at the back, looks over the garden. Were you here before, so?"

"A long time ago," Nora said, and took the key, which weighed nothing.

The lift was where the telephone box had been. She rode it alone, watching herself in the bronze mirror, a woman of fifty-eight in a navy dress, hair done yesterday in Ennis, and the woman in the mirror looked composed, looked like any wedding guest, and Nora thought, you'd never know, looking at her. The corridor on the second floor was carpeted in a print of grey waves and lit so evenly there were no shadows anywhere, and the numbers on the doors were sans-serif and brushed steel. Two-eleven. In the old hotel, room 211 had been Mrs. Hennessy's, the widow from Limerick who came every June for a month and tipped in coins she kept in a sweet tin, and who liked her sheets turned back at a precise angle and her window open exactly four inches regardless of weather. Nora had stripped that bed perhaps two hundred times. She let herself in.

It was a nice room. That was the truth of it, and she stood in the middle of it and admitted it. A good big bed, white waffle cover, four pillows where there used to be one and a bolster like a rolled carpet. Sea view, the window double-glazed and sealed, with a little laminated card explaining how to work the air. A kettle, a hairdryer, a flat television, a Bible in the drawer same as ever, the one constant. She put her bag on the rack and then, because she could not help herself, she ran her finger along the top of the headboard, and along the picture rail, and crouched to look under the bed, and everything was clean, everything was immaculate, and she felt a ridiculous flush of something that was almost pride and almost grief, as if the standard had been kept up in her absence, as if anyone now living knew there had been a standard, knew about Mrs. Frawley holding a white glove to the light, saying, a hotel is a promise, girls, every room is a promise that the world can be put right by morning.

Teresa Lynch had been the one to show her the hospital corners, her first week, June of 1979. Nora was nineteen and Teresa was twenty-three and already had the baby, Aoife, who lived with Teresa's mother above the chemist in town, and Teresa cycled in for six and cycled home after the dinner service three nights a week and slept the other nights in the attic, in the second bed in the room they shared, the room with the dormer window third from the west end. Tuck it like you mean it, Teresa said, flipping the sheet, snapping it flat, her hands quick as cards. A loose corner comes back on you by Thursday. Everything Teresa said sounded like a proverb because of the way she said it, dry, sideways, with the cigarette unlit in the corner of her mouth because Mrs. Frawley would have skinned her. She used to sing on the back stairs where the carpet ended and the sound went up the stairwell like up a chimney. She used to keep a photograph of Aoife tucked into the frame of the speckled mirror, a fat solemn baby in a knitted hat, and she'd touch it going out the door the way other people touched the holy water font.

Nora unpacked her dress for the afternoon and hung it on the bathroom door for the steam to take the creases, and then she sat on the end of the bed she had not made, and she took out of her handbag, from the inside zipped pocket where it had travelled, the pass-key.

It was iron, old-fashioned even then, the shank worn bright where forty years of apron pockets had polished it before it ever came to her, and the brass fob still read ATLANTIC HOTEL — STAFF in letters half rubbed away. She should have handed it in. There had been no one to hand it in to. She had walked out of the Regional Hospital eleven days after the fire with her hands bandaged and her eyebrows gone and someone's borrowed coat on her back, and the key had been in the brown envelope of her effects along with two pounds forty and a hairclip, because it had been in her pocket when they brought her in, because it was always in her pocket, because a maid without her pass was no use to anyone. The hotel by then was a black crown on the hill with the rain falling through it. There was no door left that the key would open. She had kept it in a drawer in three different houses across thirty-eight years, under tea towels, under tights, and every time she moved house she held it for a minute and then put it away again, and she had never once been able to say to herself what it was she was keeping it for.

The fire began on the fourteenth of November, 1981, a Saturday, in the early hours, somewhere in the range of the kitchen flue, they said afterwards, though the inquest hedged it about with maybes. The hotel was quiet, off-season, eleven guests on the books and the staff up in the attics, and Nora woke not to noise but to smell, that was the thing nobody believed, the smoke came before any sound at all, a smell like a wet day gone wrong, and she lay in the dark for what might have been ten seconds or two minutes thinking someone had left the range damped badly, and then Teresa's voice came across the dark room, flat and wide awake: Nora. Get up now and don't put your shoes on, you'll move faster.

The corridor was already grey and breathing. They went along it doubled over, knocking on doors with the flats of their hands, and Bridie came out and the two Galway girls came out, and at the west end the little window over the gable was stiff with paint and Teresa put her elbow through it as neatly as breaking an egg, and the cold came in like a blessing. The kitchen porter's ladder lived on the flat roof over the sculleries, it had lived there for years against all regulation, and that was what saved them, the ladder and the parapet and the November cold. Nora was on the roof with her bare feet on wet lead, holding Bridie's arm, when she turned around and Teresa was not behind her. Teresa was in the window, half out, and then she said, Mrs. Hennessy's below on the second, she'd sleep through the Last Trumpet — and that was wrong, it was off-season, Mrs. Hennessy came in June, but there were two women from Dublin in 211 and 213 that weekend, sisters, and Teresa had turned their beds down herself at nine o'clock, and she had her pass-key, and she went back in. Nora said her name once. She has thought since, every year of her life she has thought, that she could have said it louder, or said it differently, or gone in after her, and every year of her life she has also known, with the cold clarity of the lead under her feet, that the corridor behind Teresa closed like a throat within the minute, and that the only thing going after her would have changed is the number on the plaque.

One of the Dublin sisters survived. She was found on the second-floor landing with a wet towel over her face that she had not put there herself. Teresa was found on the stairs between the second floor and the first, six steps from a window. Six people died that night, three staff, three guests, and the town stood in the rain at three funerals in one week, and Teresa's mother held the fat solemn baby on her hip at the graveside, and Nora stood at the back with her bandaged hands inside her borrowed sleeves, and when Teresa's mother found her afterwards she did not say anything that anyone might expect. She said, you'll not be a stranger to that child, and it was not a question, and Nora had not been. Christmases and birthdays and the long awkward teenage years, the driving lessons, the college move, the phone calls. Godmother was the word on the certificate but it was never the right word. There was no right word. She was the woman who had been in the room when Teresa's voice last said an ordinary thing.

At half two Nora went down in the lift in her green dress and her good coat over her arm and the pass-key, for no reason she could have defended, in her handbag. The Moher Suite was the old ballroom, she saw that the moment she came through the doors, not because anything of it remained but because of the proportions, the room remembered its own shape even if nothing else did, the long south wall of windows giving onto the garden, the high ceiling, though the plaster roses were gone and the parquet was gone and in their place was something pale and seamless that the chairs stood on in white rows. She had polished that parquet on her knees. She had served at two weddings in this room as a loan to the dining staff when they were short, had carried trays of sherry between dancing couples in 1980, and the band had played "The Tennessee Waltz" and Mr. Considine had danced one dance with his wife, stiff as a deal table, and everyone had cheered.

The room filled. She knew almost nobody, which was its own mercy; she took a seat on the bride's side, halfway back, beside a young couple who told her they had come from Sydney for it, twenty-six hours, and wasn't the hotel gorgeous, you'd never get this light at home. The quartet began. Conor stood at the top of the room and breathed like a man on a high board. And then the doors opened and Aoife came in on her uncle's arm, and the whole room rose, and Nora rose with it and her heart went sideways in her chest, because Aoife at thirty-nine, dark-haired, dry-eyed, walking neither fast nor slow, was so entirely her mother coming down the back stairs that for one full second the years folded shut like a fan.

She got through the ceremony the way you get through weather. The vows were short and plainly written and the better for it. When the registrar said the names, Aoife Teresa Lynch, the middle name went through the room and a few heads on the bride's side bowed slightly, the older heads, the town heads, and Nora looked at the long windows and the garden beyond them where the wind was combing the new olearia hedges, and she let the tears come down without touching them, because touching them only draws attention.

At the reception there was a plaque. She found it without looking for it, which is to say her feet found it, in the corridor between the ballroom and the bar, exactly where the door to the back stairs used to be. The wall there was plastered flat now, no door, the stairs gone with the attics, but someone, sometime, in the rebuilding, had set a small bronze rectangle into the wall at shoulder height, and it said IN MEMORY OF THOSE WHO DIED HERE, 14 NOVEMBER 1981, and then the six names in two columns, and Teresa's was the second name on the left, TERESA MARY LYNCH, AGED 25. Nora stood in front of it while behind her the bar filled and a tray of prosecco went by at the level of her elbow, and she put two fingers flat against the cold of the bronze, against the wall where the door had been, the door she had gone through ten times a day for two and a half years, and she said, inside herself, in the flat dry voice that was the only voice for it: well. You'd want to see her now.

"You knew them."

It was an old man in a porter's waistcoat, though he was long past portering anything, eighty-five if he was a day, standing with a glass of red lemonade in his spotted hand, and it took her four full seconds and then it came up out of the depth like a fish: "Christy. Christy Mullane."

"They keep me on for the look of me," Christy said. "Forty hours a year, opening doors and lying about the weather." He peered at her, and she watched him do the same work she had done, the dredging, and then his face went soft and shocked all at once. "Nora Devane. Holy God. Nora Devane that was on the roof."

"The same."

"You never came back."

"I never did."

He looked at the plaque, and then at the wall around the plaque, the way you look at the space where a person used to stand. "I told them where to put it," he said. "The architects had it for the lobby, a nice spot by the fire, says I, no, the back stairs, that's where it goes, and they looked at me like I'd two heads, sure there's no back stairs in the plans, says they. That's the very point of it, says I." He took a slow drink of his lemonade. "She used to sing on them stairs. You'd hear her on the second floor and she'd be up on the third."

"'The Spinning Wheel,'" Nora said. "And she only knew the one verse."

"Round and round it'd go," Christy said, "all the way to the attics," and the two of them stood there laughing quietly at the wall, which is a thing you can apparently do, thirty-eight years on, laugh at the wall, with the prosecco going by and the band tuning up beyond the doors.

She was at table six, and the dinner was good, and the speeches were long in the way that means the family is happy, and when Conor's brother sat down at last Aoife stood up, which was not on the schedule, Nora could see the hotel coordinator's face do a small controlled thing at the side of the room. Aoife did not have notes. She thanked everyone, and then she said: "Some of you know why we chose this place and some of you are too polite to ask. My mother worked in this hotel. She made the beds and she carried the trays and she died here when I was one year old, going back up a staircase that isn't even there anymore, for two women she'd never been introduced to. One of them lived to be ninety because of it." She paused, and the room was so quiet Nora could hear the sea through the new sealed windows, or thought she could, which amounted to the same thing. "I didn't get to have her at my wedding. So I brought my wedding to her. That's all. You can all start crying now and pretend it's the wine." And the room broke into the kind of laughter that is the near side of weeping, and Aoife sat down, and across four tables her eyes found Nora's and held them for one beat, two, and Nora raised her glass an inch off the cloth, and Aoife nodded, once, like her mother signing off on a finished corridor of rooms.

Later, when the band was loud and the floor was full, Aoife came and took both her hands. "You came up the drive on foot," she said. "Christy told me. You're an awful woman, you could have been collected."

"I wanted the walk."

"Did you." Aoife looked at her, the dry sideways look, the unlit-cigarette look, second-generation. "And how is it? The place. Truly."

Nora considered. Around them the new room held its light and its noise the way a good room should, and somewhere under the seamless floor was the parquet's ghost, and behind the flat wall the stairs' ghost, and none of it could hurt anyone anymore, and all of it was still there, the way everything is still there. "The beds are well made," she said. "She'd pass it. She'd find something, mind. A loose corner somewhere. But she'd pass it."

Aoife laughed and was claimed by the dancing, and Nora sat a while longer, and then she did the thing she had perhaps come to do, although she had not let herself know it until she was doing it. She went out to the corridor, quiet now, the bar noise muffled, and she stood at the plaque, and she took the pass-key out of her handbag. There was a radiator below the bronze, an old-style column radiator, chosen no doubt by the same architects for the look of it, and behind it, between the iron and the wall, was a gap of two inches of warm dark. She held the key a last moment, the worn shank, the rubbed brass, ATLANTIC HOTEL — STAFF, and then she reached down and set it on the bracket behind the radiator, flat against the wall where the door had been, on the staff side of everything, where no guest would ever find it and no painter look. Handing it in. Thirty-eight years late, but the night porter's desk keeps long hours.

She went up at midnight. In room 211 the curtains were heavy and the bed was turned down, the corner of the duvet folded back at an angle Mrs. Hennessy would have measured and approved, and a small chocolate sat on the pillow like a coin left for the boatman. Nora stood at the window a while with the lights off, watching the white edge of the tide work and rework the dark, putting the beach right, undoing it, putting it right again, all night, every night. Then she got into the bed that someone else had made, a girl from Ennistymon maybe, with quick hands and a bus to catch, and she pulled the sheet to her chin, and the corner held, and she slept.
