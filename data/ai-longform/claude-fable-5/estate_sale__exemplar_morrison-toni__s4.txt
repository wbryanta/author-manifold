By one o’clock the house had given up its bedrooms. June sat at a card table on the porch with the cashbox and the roll of orange stickers and watched two men she had never seen before angle her mother’s chifforobe through the front door, tipping it the way pallbearers tip a casket on church steps, gentle and businesslike at once, and she made change for them—a five and three ones—without letting her face do anything at all.

Forty dollars. She had priced it herself on Thursday, walking through the rooms with Patti from the estate company, Patti with her fanny pack and her reading glasses on a beaded chain, saying lowball it, hon, you want it gone, you don’t want to be hauling it to the dump Sunday night, and June had nodded and written 40 on a sticker and pressed it to the door of the chifforobe, over the keyhole, the keyhole she had put her eye to in 1958 to watch her mother cry, the one time, the only time, standing in her slip with a telegram in her hand. Forty dollars for that. The men loaded it into a pickup with Pennsylvania plates and tied it down with yellow rope and June watched the truck go down Bellam Street past the Hood house and the Tatum house and the house where the Skinners used to be before the plant closed and everybody scattered like the contents of a dropped purse, and she thought: there goes the keyhole. There goes the dark inside where I hid from a whipping in 1961, breathing cedar and my dead father’s one good suit, while Odessa Mayfield walked the rooms of this house calling my name in a voice that promised murder and sounded, she understood only now, fifty years too late and from the distance of a card table, exactly like fear.

The town was called Gideon and it was dying in the patient, unhurried way of Ohio towns, not all at once but tooth by tooth, like an old mouth. The mill whistle that had divided every day of her childhood into before and after had not blown since 1981. Half of Bellam Street was plywood windows and Queen Anne’s lace standing in what used to be yards. But the people who came to an estate sale came from everywhere, drawn the way certain birds are drawn to fresh-turned dirt, and all morning they had moved through her mother’s rooms with their heads tilted, touching things, turning plates over to read the bottoms, opening drawers as though drawers held nothing private, as though a drawer were not a held breath. By one o’clock they had the bedrooms. By two they would want the kitchen.

“You doing all right out here?” Patti, in the doorway, fanning herself with a paper plate. September had come in hot, a wet, locust-loud heat, and the cicadas were sawing away in the maples like they were being paid by the hour.

“I’m fine.”

“You don’t have to sit out here, you know. I can run the box. Most family members, they go get a coffee. Come back at five when it’s over.”

“I’m fine,” June said again, and Patti, who had done two hundred of these and knew the difference between fine and fine, went back inside to the woman who was asking could the price come down on the sewing machine.

It could. Everything could come down. That was the arithmetic of the day, the arithmetic she had agreed to on the phone with her brother Lucas in Oakland, who was not coming, who had not come to the funeral in March either, who had said in the dry voice he had been using on her since 1968, Sell it all, Junie. Every stick. Don’t make it holy. It’s a house, not a reliquary. And she had said all right, all right, because what he meant underneath was don’t you make me feel it from two thousand miles away, and she had spent her whole life agreeing not to make people feel things. She was fifty-six years old. She had buried a husband, raised a son, retired from twenty-nine years at Cook County with a plaque and a sheet cake, and she could sit at a card table in front of her mother’s house all afternoon making change for strangers and not feel one thing. She had decided this on the drive down from Chicago, somewhere around Gary, with the lake on her left flashing between the mills like something signaling her. Decided it the way you decide weather. It would not rain today. She would not feel anything today. Lowball it, hon.

A young white couple came out with the vanity, the one with the clouded mirror, the mercury gone leprous in the corners so that for the last twenty years Odessa had powdered her face in a glass that ate her edges, that gave her back to herself dissolving. The girl carried the mirror separate, hugging it to her chest, her own face swimming in it upside down, and she stopped at the table and said, “This is so beautiful. Was it your mom’s?” and June said yes, and the girl said, “I’m going to refinish it. I do that, I have a whole Instagram,” and June said that’s nice, and took the twenty, and did not say: my mother sat in front of that glass every Sunday of her life pinning on a hat, and the hats got smaller as the church got smaller, and the last hat was no hat at all, just her gray head bare in a folding chair at the storefront on Pershing because First Calvary couldn’t keep its roof, and she went anyway, every Sunday, dissolving in the corners and going anyway. She did not say it. The girl would refinish it. The glass that held forty years of Odessa Mayfield’s face would be replaced with new glass from a hardware store, clean glass, glass with nothing in it, and that was what people wanted, June supposed. Glass with nothing in it.

Inside, the rooms were getting loud. Not with the strangers—they murmured, mostly, estate-sale people had funeral manners—but with absence. An emptied room shouts. She had not known that. Each thing carried out the door left behind it a shape of louder air: the bright rectangle on the wallpaper where the picture of the Last Supper had hung, screaming its cleanness; the four dents in the parlor rug where the piano had stood since before June was born, the piano nobody played after 1965, after Lucas quit, after their father died and Odessa shut the lid over the keys the way you pull a sheet over a face, and it had stood there forty years holding its breath, holding its one held chord, and a man from Sandusky had bought it for thirty-five dollars this morning and now the dents in the rug were the only proof. Loud, those dents. Louder than the piano had ever been allowed to be.

A little after two, the man in the fishing vest found the quilts.

He came out onto the porch with all three of them stacked over his arm like he was carrying a sleeping child, and June was standing before she knew she had stood.

“These got no stickers on them,” he said. “What do you want for the lot? They’re rough—this one’s got staining, see, and the binding’s shot on this star one—but I’ll give you sixty for all three. I deal a little. Primitives. African American textiles, that’s a category now, you’d be surprised.”

She would not be surprised. She looked at the quilts on his arm. The top one was the star one, eight-pointed stars in feed-sack cotton, pieced in Greenville, Alabama, by her grandmother Birdie, a woman June had met twice in her life and whose hands she still knew, because the hands were in the quilt, in the stitches that went small and even for ten inches and then wandered, then righted themselves, then wandered—a woman quilting by kerosene after picking all day, falling asleep and waking with the needle still in her fingers. The stains the man was discounting were Birdie’s house and Birdie’s babies and a flood in 1936. June had slept under that quilt every winter of her childhood, in the back room that got no heat, and Odessa would come in late from the laundry smelling of bleach and other people’s clothes and put her cold hand under it to find June’s feet, to check were they warm, and that was all. No kiss. The hand on the feet, and gone.

“Those aren’t for sale,” June said.

“They were in with the linens.”

“They were in the wrong place, then.” She held out her arms.

“I’ll go eighty.”

“Mister.”

He looked at her, and she let him see her face, the face she had been keeping off all day, and whatever was in it moved him backward half a step, the way a thing in a doorway will move a man backward, and he laid the quilts across her arms with both hands, suddenly careful, suddenly a man at an altar, and said no harm meant, ma’am, and went back in to pick over the Pyrex.

She stood on the porch holding them. Eight pounds, maybe, the three together. Heavier than that. She carried them to her car and laid them on the back seat and locked the doors in a town where nobody locked doors, and stood a minute in the driveway with her hand on the hot roof of the car, breathing. Everything could go, she had told Lucas, told Patti, told herself the whole length of Indiana. Everything must go—wasn’t that what the signs said, the going-out-of-business signs, the whole dying-mill-town liturgy of her girlhood: everything must go. But standing in the driveway she understood that the selling was not the thing. Anybody could sell. The thing was what you claimed. Out of a houseful, out of a lifetime, out of a woman, what you stood up and put your arms around and said: this is not on the table. This was never on the table. More important than what she let go of was what she claimed, and she had just found out what it was, eight pounds of it, locked in a Buick.

“Junie Mayfield. I know that’s you, ’cause don’t nobody else stand pigeon-toed like that no more.”

Ernestine Hood was coming up the walk on a cane, eighty-eight years old, in a lavender housedress and tennis shoes, last of the originals, the only porch light left on Bellam Street besides the one June would turn off for good tonight. June went down the steps and they held each other and Ernestine smelled the way she had smelled in 1959, talcum and snuff and something fried, and June’s eyes went hot and she blinked them dry before she pulled back.

“You selling the glider?” Ernestine nodded at the porch glider, green metal, paint gone to lace at the arms.

“Five dollars.”

“I’ll give you nothing for it and you’ll carry it down to my porch yourself, how about that.”

“That sounds about right.”

They sat on it, the two of them, and it complained under them in the voice it had been complaining in since Eisenhower, and they watched a woman carry the kitchen chairs out two at a time, and Ernestine said, “Lord, there go the chairs,” the way you’d say there go the geese, something seasonal, something that was always going to happen.

“She sat me down in one of them chairs in 1971,” Ernestine said, “when Hood passed, and fed me, and didn’t say six words. Best six words anybody didn’t say to me that whole year.”

“That sounds like her.”

“What does?”

“Not saying.”

Ernestine turned her head slow, the way old women turn, the whole body consulted. “You still carrying that.”

“I’m not carrying anything. I’m selling a house.”

“Mm.” The glider creaked. Across the street a boy went by on a bicycle too small for him, knees out like wings. “You know what she did every evening of her life, your mama? After you went north?”

“Watched her programs.”

“Read me your letters. Out here. This very seat. Had her glasses way down her nose and she’d read me every word, twice if I let her, and she had this voice she put on for it, churchy, like it was scripture. When you made supervisor she read me that one three different evenings, like I hadn’t heard it, and I said, Dess, you told me, and she said, ‘I know I told you. I’m telling you again.’” Ernestine laughed, and then didn’t. “And every night, every night God sent, she turned that porch light on before she went up. Thirty years, Junie. I used to see it from my kitchen. One time the bulb went and she called my grandson over here at nine at night to change it, wouldn’t wait for morning. I asked her once what she was burning it for, electricity proud as it is, and she looked at me like I’d asked her why she breathed and said, ‘Case June come in late.’”

The cicadas filled up the space where June was supposed to say something. Eight hundred miles. Thirty years. Case June come in late.

“She never said that to me,” June said finally. “Not anything like that. You know what she said to me, the morning I left? I’m standing there with my suitcase, eighteen years old, scared to death, bus ticket in my glove—and she’s ironing. Ironing, Ernestine. And she doesn’t even look up, she says, ‘Go on, then.’ Two words. I waited on that porch for—I don’t know what I waited for. Something. And she gave me ‘Go on, then,’ and the iron going the whole time, and I cried from here to Toledo.”

Ernestine was quiet awhile. Then she said, “You ever iron when you couldn’t do nothing else? When the thing in front of you was so big the only thing your hands could hold was the little thing?” She pushed the glider with her foot. “Your mama loved the way some folks pray in a language don’t nobody speak no more. It’s real prayer. It just don’t come out in words you can use.” She gathered her cane. “Thin love would’ve waved goodbye nice and pretty. Thin love is sweet that way. Hers wasn’t thin.” She stood, joint by joint, waving off June’s hand. “Bring me my glider before dark. And the skillet—don’t you let no stranger have Dess’s iron skillet, I will haunt you my own self.”

The skillet was already gone—June ran inside with her heart knocking and found it still on the stove, sticker-less, black and patient, seasoned by sixty years of Sunday chicken, and she carried it out to the Buick and laid it on the quilts like a stone on a grave, to hold everything down.

It was in the same trip back through the kitchen that she found the hot comb. Somebody had swept it into a box marked KITCHEN MISC—$3 WHOLE BOX, in with egg beaters and a potato ricer and a church-fan from the Bynum Funeral Home, and it lay there among the miscellaneous like it was miscellaneous, the long black handle, the teeth still carrying, she would have sworn it, she did not check, a ghost-film of Dixie Peach. Saturday nights. The kitchen table—sold an hour ago, the table with the long amber scorch in the oilcloth pattern of the wood itself, and she had watched a man load it without a word—the kitchen table, and the blue ring of the gas flame, and the comb heating, and June on a stack of telephone books with a towel around her shoulders, and the radio low because the neighbors, and her mother’s left hand gripping her skull like a melon at the market. Hold still. The sizzle when the comb met the grease, the smell that meant Saturday all over the neighborhood, every kitchen on Bellam Street sending up the same smoke like a tribe signaling itself, and Hold still or lose an ear, girl, and once she had not held still and the comb had kissed the top of her ear and she had screamed and her mother had pressed her thumb on the burn—her thumb, that the hot handles of forty years had cured past feeling—pressed it there and held it, not saying sorry, never saying sorry, saying nothing, just the thumb, the pressure, the smell, the radio, until the hurt gave up and went off somewhere else. That was the language. That was the whole vocabulary right there. A thumb on a burn. A hand under a quilt finding your feet. A porch light. A woman ironing because the thing in front of her was too big to hold.

June put the hot comb in her purse like a thief.

By four the house was down to its dregs and echoes. The young couple expecting a baby—she could see it, the girl carrying high and proud, hand at the small of her back—stood in the doorway of the big bedroom looking at the bed, the dark four-poster, and the boy was counting bills in his palm and not having enough, and June heard herself say twenty, just take it for twenty, and helped them wrap the headboard in moving blankets herself. Her mother had slept in that bed alone for forty-six years, on her own side, the other side flat and made, like company expected. June watched the headboard go down the porch steps in the arms of a boy who would lie in it tonight with his pregnant wife, and somewhere in that bed forty years of one woman’s solitude would dissolve into two people and then three, and that was all right. That was better than all right. Some things you sold and some things you claimed and some things you set loose, like doves at a wedding, and watched go.

It was Patti who found the shoebox, on the closet shelf in the big bedroom, under the hatboxes. “This looks personal, hon,” she said, and handed it over and went away, because two hundred sales had taught her when to go away.

June sat down on the floor of her mother’s emptied bedroom, in the loud bare light of it, with the box in her lap. Thom McAn, said the lid. Inside: a baby tooth folded in waxed paper gone yellow and stiff. A plait of hair, hers, ribboned, from the great shearing of 1956. Every Mother’s Day card she had ever sent, the drugstore ones, the ones she had picked in four minutes in a Walgreens on Pulaski, signed Love, June and nothing else, all of them, forty-some of them, kept in rubber bands. Her letters—and she saw before she touched them that they had been opened and read until the folds wore soft as cloth, read until some of them were coming apart at the creases and had been mended, Lord, mended, with Scotch tape gone amber, her own dull dutiful letters about weather and work and the boy, scripture after all. And at the bottom, under everything, a Greyhound ticket. Gideon to Chicago. One way, then she looked again: round trip. Dated August 9, 1987. Unused. Both halves still attached, still waiting, the cardboard gone soft as the letters from handling, from being taken out, June understood, and held, and put back, taken out and held and put back, for twenty years and more, by a woman who bought a ticket to her daughter and never got on the bus. Why. There was nobody left to ask why. The laundry. The pride. The fear of cities, of arriving unannounced, of announcing and being a trouble, of the look on a daughter’s face that might be one degree short of glad. Or none of that. Or she got dressed that August morning, hat and gloves in the heat, and sat in the front room with her suitcase, and the bus came through Gideon at 11:40, and at 11:25 her hands wanted something little to hold, and there was ironing. June would never know. The not-knowing sat in her lap in a shoebox and it weighed more than the quilts, more than the skillet, more than the piano, more than the house. She had spent thirty years keeping accounts on a woman who could not say it, and the whole time the woman had been saying it, in tooth and plait and tape and ticket, in a dead language, fluent, every day, into the dark.

She did not cry, or she did; afterward she could not have said; the bedroom was very loud and bare and the light through the curtainless window had gone the deep gold of after-five, and at some point Patti put her head in and said that’s everything, hon, you did good, sign here, and the last car doors slammed out front and Bellam Street settled into its evening quiet, the quiet of a street with one old woman and one porch light left.

June carried the glider down to Ernestine’s, the two of them laughing at her for staggering. She put the shoebox on the front seat of the Buick where a passenger would sit. Then she went back up the walk of the empty house, through the rooms with their shouting rectangles and dents, past where the table was, where the piano was, where the chifforobe kept its keyhole and its dark, up the stairs and down again, hearing her own feet too plainly, and on her way out the door, with her hand on the switch by the frame, the switch her mother’s hand had found in the dark ten thousand times, she stopped. The house was sold. The power would be off by Friday. There was no one in the whole spread of Ohio coming in late to this door tonight, and she was the last of them, and her car was packed, and it was time.

She turned the porch light on anyway, and left it burning, and drove north.
