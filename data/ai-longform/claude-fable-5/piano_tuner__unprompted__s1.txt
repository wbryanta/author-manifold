The frost had come early that year, and when Theodore Lindqvist carried his tool case out to the truck in the gray of six-thirty, the grass cracked under his boots like something brittle giving way. He set the case on the passenger seat where it had ridden for fifty-one years—the same case, oxblood leather gone the color of old saddles, the brass latches worn to a soft gold by his thumbs—and he stood a moment with his hand on the door, looking back at the house. Inga had been gone eleven years now, but he still half expected to see her at the kitchen window, lifting her coffee cup in that small salute she gave him every working morning of their marriage. The window held only the reflection of the sky, which was the white of unbleached muslin, promising snow by evening or the day after.

Four stops. He had written them on the back of an envelope, though he needed no list. The Hollanders'. The Vandeberg place. Mrs. Aldous Pruitt, who was ninety-four and would offer him coffee he would have to drink. And the church. He had arranged the order the way a man arranges the order of anything he does not want to finish: the longest road first, the shortest last, so that the end, when it came, would come quickly and there would be no time left in the day to feel it.

The truck started on the second try, which he took as a good omen, and he drove out past the grain elevator with its faded co-op sign and onto the county road, where the fields lay shorn and stubbled to the horizon. He had driven this road so many times that his hands made the turns before his mind suggested them. When he was younger he had counted the telephone poles to pass the miles. Later he had listened to the radio. Now he drove in silence, because the silence had become a kind of company, and because at eighty-one a man's own thoughts were loud enough.

The Hollander farm sat two miles off the blacktop at the end of a gravel lane lined with dying elms that nobody had cut down because nobody was left who cared whether they fell. Carl Hollander had died in March. His daughter, Susan, lived in Minneapolis and came back one weekend a month to keep the pipes from freezing and the mice from claiming everything outright. She had called Theodore in September. You'll think I'm foolish, she said. Nobody's played it since Mother. But I can't sell the place with it sounding the way it sounds. It's like the house is sick. He had not thought her foolish. He had heard the same sentiment, in different words, from half the county over the years. People could live with a piano nobody touched, but they could not live with one gone sour. An untuned piano in a house was like an unanswered letter on a desk. It accused.

Susan had left the key under the boot scraper, as her father always had. The kitchen smelled of cold linoleum and the faint sweetness of apples gone soft somewhere. He went through to the front room, where the piano stood against the north wall the way it had stood since before he ever came to this house—a Kimball upright, walnut, built in the twenties, with a mirror panel above the fallboard in which he had watched himself age one visit at a time. The bench held a stack of National Geographics and a sewing basket, and he moved these to the sofa with the care of a man handling another family's relics, which he was.

He opened the lid and the smell came up at him, the smell that was the same in every piano and different in every piano: felt and dust and old glue, and under it the particular smell of this house, woodsmoke and Marie Hollander's lavender, though Marie had been dead since before the millennium turned. He played a slow chromatic scale up from the bass. The piano answered him like an old dog rising—willing, arthritic, glad in spite of itself. It was nearly a quarter tone flat across the middle and worse in the treble, and three of the hammers in the fifth octave were so moth-chewed they struck the strings with a sound like knuckles on a door.

He set the fork against his knee and struck it and held it to his ear. A440, the one fixed star in his sky. The fork had been his father's, brought from Sweden in a wooden case lined with green baize, and it had not drifted in a hundred years, which was more than could be said of anything else he knew. Then he laid the felt temperament strip along the middle strings, fitted the tuning lever onto the first pin, and went to work.

There was a state a man entered when tuning, and Theodore had spent more of his life inside that state than outside it. It was not unlike prayer, he had decided long ago, though he had never said so to Pastor Brink or to the pastors before him, because it would have sounded either pious or blasphemous and he did not know which. You struck the interval and you listened, not to the notes but to the space between them, to the beating where the sound waves crossed and quarreled, and you turned the pin by degrees too small to see, and the quarrel slowed, and slowed, and resolved into something that was not agreement exactly—a piano tuned to pure intervals would be unplayable, every key its own tyrant—but a negotiated peace, each note giving up a little of its perfection so that all the keys could live together. Equal temperament, the books called it. His father had called it, in his hard-vowled English, the small honest lie.

He worked through the temperament octave, then up, then down into the bass where the long copper-wound strings spoke in their whale voices, and the morning passed through the front room windows in a slow rectangle of light that crossed the floor and climbed the piano and warmed his hands where they worked. Twice he stopped to ease a sticking key, and once to vacuum, with the little brush he carried, the husks of dead ladybugs from the keybed. When he finished he removed the strip and played, because he always played to test his work—played the same thing he had played for fifty years, the opening of the Schumann, the little one, "Of Foreign Lands and Peoples," which was simple enough that his hands could still manage it and beautiful enough that it told him the truth about a piano. The Kimball sang it back to him gamely, the moth-eaten hammers thudding their three dull notes in the treble like a singer with missing teeth. He could have replaced the hammers. He had the felt in the truck. But Susan was selling the house, and the piano would go to an auction barn or a landfill, and there are kindnesses that become foolishness if you look at them too long. He closed the lid and the fallboard, returned the magazines and the sewing basket to the bench, locked the door, and put the key back under the scraper. In the truck he sat a moment before turning the engine over. Forty-six years he had come to this house. Carl Hollander had once given him a quarter of a butchered hog in lieu of payment, the winter of the farm crisis, and Inga had rendered the lard and they had eaten off that hog until Easter. There ought to be somewhere a man could set such facts down, he thought. Some ledger. The elms moved their dying branches against the white sky, and he drove out the lane and did not look in the mirror.

The Vandeberg place was nine miles east, and he ate his sandwich driving, the wax paper unfolding on the seat where Inga used to sit. Liverwurst and mustard, the same sandwich for half a century, because a man who spends his days adjusting things by hairsbreadths wants nothing adjusted in his lunch. The radio tower lights blinked red over the fields even in daytime. He passed the school, consolidated now, the old brick building in Mertensville standing empty with plywood in its windows—he had tuned the piano in that gymnasium every August for thirty years, before the consolidation, a Baldwin that took abuse like a mule and held its pitch through basketball seasons and graduations and the stamping of a thousand children's feet. Gone now. He had asked once what became of it and the man at the district office didn't know what he was talking about.

Lily Vandeberg met him at the door in her coat, on her way out to the dentist in Willmar, and she apologized for not staying, and he told her, as he had told her mother before her, that a tuner works better alone anyway, the way a fish swims better unwatched. She laughed at that, the way her mother had, and her breath hung in the cold of the porch. Henry's in the back room, she said, as if the piano were a person, and in this house it nearly was: Henry Vandeberg had bought the Story & Clark for his wife in 1958 and named it after himself as a joke, and the joke had outlived him, and outlived Doris, and now their granddaughter kept Henry tuned out of a loyalty she probably could not have explained to anyone who asked. Nobody in the house played. Lily had told him so without embarrassment, years ago. The grandkids bang on it at Christmas, she said. Mostly it just stands there. But you can't have it standing there wrong.

You can't have it standing there wrong. He had thought often that this could be carved over the door of his whole trade, what was left of it. The pianos of his route had stopped being instruments somewhere in the last twenty years and become something else—monuments, he supposed, or hostages, or the household gods the Romans kept, the little figures by the hearth that did nothing and meant everything. He tuned monuments now. He was a polisher of memorial stones, except that his stones could be brought back, for an hour at least, to the exact pitch at which somebody's mother had once played "Whispering Hope" on a Sunday evening with the supper dishes still in the sink.

Henry took two hours. The back room was cold and his fingers complained, and he stopped midway to run them under warm water at the kitchen tap, looking out at the Vandebergs' windbreak, the spruce trees Doris had planted as seedlings, taller now than the barn. The piano had a cracked bridge in the bass that he had been nursing along for a decade, and he could hear that the crack had walked another inch since spring, the lowest octave gone wolfish and false, and there was nothing for it. He tuned around the wound the way you learn to walk around a bad knee. When he played the Schumann through, the bass growled under the melody like weather on the horizon, but the middle of the piano, where the living was done, where the hymns and the carols and the chopsticks lived, came up clean and true, and he sat back and let the last chord hang in the cold room until it was gone entirely, until even the imagination of it was gone, and then he packed his tools.

He left the bill on the music desk as always, knowing Lily would mail a check with a note in her round schoolteacher hand, and knowing too that this was the last bill, and that he had not told her so, had not told any of them, because he could not have stood the fuss. He had decided in the summer, after the second time his hands went numb on the lever, after Dr. Patel said the word neuropathy in the tone of a man saying the word winter. A tuner whose hands cannot feel the pin is a danger to other people's pianos. He would write letters in the spring, maybe. There was a young woman in St. Cloud, factory-trained, good with the electronic gadgets; he would send his route to her the way his father had sent his route to him, except his father had handed him the route at a kitchen table with aquavit in two glasses, and Theodore would do it with a stamp, because that was the shape the world had taken.

Mrs. Aldous Pruitt's house smelled of coffee already when she opened the door, which meant resistance was useless and he didn't attempt any. She was a small bent woman with a face like a winter apple and a memory that had begun to slip its moorings in the gentlest possible way—she lost Tuesdays and the names of vice presidents but kept everything that mattered, kept whole decades in perfect order, kept the words to every hymn in the Concordia. Her piano was a Wurlitzer spinet, a wedding gift from Aldous in 1953, and it was a mediocre instrument and had always been a mediocre instrument, and Theodore had tuned it ninety times or more with the devotion other men gave to Steinways, because Mrs. Pruitt sat in the room every time, in the wingback chair, with her hands folded, listening to the tuning itself as if it were the concert. Most people fled the sound of tuning. The endless repeated notes, the sour seconds and bare fifths, drove them to other rooms. Edith Pruitt listened the way some people watched birds.

I always think, she said, when he was deep in the fourth octave, that it sounds like somebody trying to remember something. The same note over and over. Like a word on the tip of the tongue.

And then it comes, he said.

And then it comes, she agreed, and they were quiet again, and the note came, and he moved on.

Over coffee she told him, as she always told him, about her sister in Tacoma, and about Aldous and the hailstorm of '62, and then—he had not expected it, though perhaps he should have, the past being the country where she now mostly lived—she set down her cup and said: Your boy. Your Stephen. I heard him play, you know. At the church. We all went. Aldous said afterward, that boy didn't learn that here. Meaning it the kind way. Meaning he had something from somewhere else. Somewhere up. She pointed her crooked finger at the ceiling, at the sky above it.

That was 1969, Theodore said. His voice came out level, a tuned string.

Was it. She looked into her coffee. He played the one—she moved her hand in the air, fingers rippling—the water one.

Debussy, he said.

That's it. Doesn't seem like a thing that could come out of a piano, water. But it did. She looked up at him, and her old eyes were perfectly clear, the fog all blown off them for a moment. We were sorry, Theodore. The whole township was sorry. I don't know if anybody ever said it plain. People here don't say things plain.

They said it, he said. In their way. People brought food for a month.

Hot dishes, she said, nodding gravely, as if the word were itself a kind of liturgy, and in this county it nearly was. She walked him to the door and held his hand in both of hers a moment longer than goodbye required, and he understood that she knew, somehow, in the strange weather of her mind, that he was not coming back, and that this was her plain saying, and he held her hands too and said nothing, and drove away with the coffee burning in him like a swallowed coal.

The church stood at the crossroads it had stood at since 1891, white clapboard, the steeple needing paint, the cemetery behind it where his parents lay, and Inga, and the boy. Immanuel Lutheran. The afternoon had thickened toward dusk already, the way November afternoons did, the light going granular and blue. Pastor Brink had left the side door unlocked as promised; the pastor served three congregations now, riding a circuit like a tuner himself, and was twenty miles away at a confirmation class. Theodore let himself into the smell that every country church shares, candle wax and hymnal paper and the cold held in plaster walls, and his footsteps were loud on the wooden floor and he was glad of the solitude the way the dying are glad of a window.

The piano stood to the left of the chancel, where it had always stood. A Yamaha console now—the congregation had bought it in the eighties—but before it, on the same spot of floor, had stood the old Ivers & Pond grand, dark as a coffin and resonant as a barrel, donated by a wheat baron's widow before the First World War, and it was the Ivers & Pond that Stephen had played on the third Sunday of May, 1969, eleven years old, in a clip-on tie, his shoes polished by his mother that morning until they held the window light.

Theodore set down his case and did not open it yet. He sat in the front pew instead, where he had sat that day, and let the church fill up around him with everything it held.

The boy had not wanted a recital. The recital had been Theodore's idea, or rather his pride's idea—he had taught Stephen himself, evenings, on the Chickering in their parlor, and the boy had outrun him by age nine, outrun the local teachers by ten, and Theodore had driven him to Minneapolis once a month for lessons with a Hungarian woman who charged what a tuning paid and said, the third visit, in front of the boy, which Theodore had never forgiven her though everything she said was true: You understand what you have here. You understand this is not a hobby. And Theodore had wanted the township to hear it. He had wanted, if he was honest, and he had had fifty-four years to become honest, the farmers whose pianos he tuned to know that the tuner's son could make those same eighty-eight keys do what their pianos, perfectly tuned, standing perfectly silent in their parlors, were for.

Stephen had played the Bach Invention in F, and a Chopin waltz, and then the Debussy—"Reflets dans l'eau," far too hard for an eleven-year-old, the Hungarian woman had said absolutely not, and the boy had learned it secretly anyway, at home, while Theodore tuned other people's pianos, and announced it from the bench that Sunday as casually as a man announcing a hymn number. And the water had come out of the old Ivers & Pond. That was the only way Theodore had ever been able to say it to himself. The water came out of the piano. Light on water, the underside of light on water, the way a dock looks from beneath when you are a boy swimming in a Minnesota lake with your eyes open. The farmers had sat in these pews in their church clothes with their chapped hands folded and the water had moved over all of them, and when it ended there had been a silence before the applause, two full seconds, three, and Theodore knew, because he was a listener by trade, that the silence was the truer ovation. You could tune your whole life and never make a room sound like that. The silence after.

In August of that same year the boy went swimming in that same lake, off that same dock, with the Carlson brothers, on an afternoon so hot the tar bubbled on the county road, and the Carlson boys came running up the gravel to the resort office, and the resort man called, and Theodore was forty miles away with his head inside the Mertensville school's Baldwin, tuning, hearing nothing but beats, the slow quarrel of intervals resolving, while the thing that could never resolve was already finished and cooling under the moving water.

He had not stopped tuning. People expected him to and he had not. He had taken two weeks, and then Inga had handed him the oxblood case one morning without a word, and he had driven to the Hollanders', because the Hollanders' was scheduled, and Carl Hollander had met him in the yard and looked at him a long moment and said only, She's in the front room, gone flat as ever, and that sentence had been the kindest thing anyone said to him that year, because it was a sentence from the world where things could still be set right by degrees, where a man could put his hands on a problem and turn it true.

He stood, finally, his knees announcing themselves, and opened the case. The Yamaha was a good piano, sensible, even-tempered in every sense; Pastor Brink had asked for the tuning before Advent, and Theodore would give the congregation an honest Advent tuning, his last anywhere, and that was a fitting way for the books to close. He struck the fork and held it singing to his ear—his father's A, Stephen's A, the one pitch that had passed unaltered through all the hands of his life—and laid the strip, and began.

The church grew dark around the piano as he worked. He did not turn on the lights. His hands knew the geography blind, and the windows held the last blue of the day long after the floor had gone black, and the notes went out one at a time into the empty nave, the same note over and over, like somebody trying to remember something, and then the next, and then the next. He worked slowly. He had nowhere to be. The numbness came into his fingers in the upper treble and he waited it out, flexing his hands in the dark, and went on, and the little church held each tone the way it had held a century of hymns and the one afternoon of water, and gave each one back.

When the last unison came true he sat for a while with his hands in his lap. Then he removed the strip, folded it, and laid it in the case, and he played—not the Schumann this time. His hands found the opening of the Debussy, the first slow chords, the only measures of it he had ever taught himself, in secret, the way his son had learned the whole, and he played them three times over, badly, an old tuner's stiff hands on a console piano in the dark, and the water did not come out of it, the water was not his to summon, it had never been his; but the chords were true, every interval honestly negotiated, the small honest lie told as well as it could be told. He let the last one ring. He listened to it leave the strings, leave the air, leave even the imagination of the air.

Then he closed the fallboard, latched the case, and carried it out into the first fine snow, which was falling without wind, straight down, onto the church and the crossroads and the stones behind the church, evenly, all over the township, on every house where a tuned piano stood silent in a dark front room, holding its pitch.
