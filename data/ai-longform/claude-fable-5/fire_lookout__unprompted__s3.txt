The smoke was there at four in the afternoon, when the light had gone brassy and the day's heat stood in the tower like a guest who would not leave. Maren had been washing her one plate and her one cup in the basin, and when she looked up through the south windows—out of habit, out of forty years of habit that was not even all hers but her father's before her—she saw it: a gray feather standing up out of the timber across the valley, leaning slightly east, fattening at the base even as she watched.

She set the cup down without drying it and went to the fire finder.

The Osborne sat in the center of the room on its wooden pedestal, the brass ring polished by decades of forearms, the map beneath the glass yellowed and water-stained and still, after everything, accurate. The Forest Service had taken the radio in April when they decommissioned the station, and the logbooks, and the weather kit, but nobody had wanted to carry the fire finder down eight miles of trail, so it stayed, the way an altar stays in a church nobody attends. She put her eye to the rear sight and swung the ring until the crosshair of horsehair bisected the column at its base, and read the azimuth off the brass: one hundred ninety-seven degrees, and a few minutes more she didn't bother with, because she already knew. She had known before she touched the instrument. She had known, in the animal part of her, standing at the basin with the cup in her hand.

One ninety-seven was Slate Creek. One ninety-seven was the drainage where the creek came down off Bandbox Ridge through old larch and lodgepole regrown after the '34 burn, where the trail from the tower met the dead-end road, where, two hundred yards upstream of the road's end, on a half-acre of meadow their father had bought in 1961 for the price of a used truck, her sister Lou kept the cabin.

Maren stood very still with her eye against the sight, as if stillness could revise the bearing. The smoke rose. It was not white, which would have meant grass or moisture; it was gray shading to a dirty yellow-brown at the base, which meant timber, duff, the deep dry punk of a forest that had not seen rain in five weeks. She estimated distance the way her father had taught her, by the known landmarks—the smoke stood this side of Bandbox, beyond the bare knuckle of rock they had always called the Thumb—and called it six miles by air. By her own legs it was eight miles of switchbacks to the road and then two more up the road to the cabin, except that the smoke was rising from somewhere in those last two miles, or near enough that the difference was a matter of wind.

She went to the corner where the radio used to live. The mount was still screwed to the wall, four holes and a bracket and a coil of disconnected antenna lead, and her hand went to it anyway, the way the tongue goes to the socket of a pulled tooth. She had a handheld of her own, bought used in May out of stubbornness, but the repeater on Greenough Mountain had been down since the lightning on Tuesday—she had heard the dispatcher say so, faintly, through static, before even that faint voice was gone—and the handheld now received nothing and reached nothing. She keyed it anyway and said her old call sign into the hiss, the sign that had been retired along with the station, and gave the azimuth and the legal description of Lou's parcel, township and range, because she knew it the way she knew her own birthday, and then she said, "Anyone copy," twice, and listened to the static answer, and clipped the radio to her belt anyway, because hope was a thing you carried whether or not it weighed anything.

The phone was a flip phone older than most marriages. There was a place on the catwalk, the northeast corner, where on certain evenings in certain weather a single bar of signal would appear like a rare bird, and she went out and stood there now with the phone held at the precise angle of past successes, and the screen said searching, and went on saying it, and she understood that the same lightning that had killed the repeater had likely taken something else too, some relay on some other ridge, and that she was, for all practical purposes, a woman alone on a mountain in the nineteenth century.

Tuesday night. She had sat up for the storm the way she always did, in the chair with the glass insulators under its legs, which her father had insisted on and which she half believed in, and she had watched the dry lightning walk the ridges with nothing underneath it, no rain at all, just the strikes coming down like the strokes of a pen signing something. She had counted eleven and logged them in her own notebook, the unofficial one, the one nobody would ever collect, with bearings and times, because what else was she for. Strike number seven, 9:42 p.m., one hundred ninety-six degrees, a hard close crack and a ball of light low on the slope above Slate Creek. She had watched that spot through the binoculars for two days. Nothing. Sleeper, she had written on Wednesday, with a question mark. Holdover. Some strikes went down into a snag or a root and smoldered there in the punky wood for days, a coal the size of a fist sleeping under the duff, waiting for the right hot afternoon, the right wind, to wake. This was the right hot afternoon. The wind had come up out of the northwest an hour ago, ten or twelve miles an hour, and northwest wind in that drainage funneled down the creek. Down the creek meant toward the cabin. Toward the road. Toward Lou.

She had not spoken to her sister in three years, unless you counted the time eleven months ago when she had driven past the cabin on her way up to open the tower for the season and Lou had been standing in the meadow with a chainsaw in her hands, the saw silent, and they had looked at each other through the windshield for the length of time it takes a truck to pass a half-acre at fifteen miles an hour, and neither of them had raised a hand.

Maren went back inside and stood in the middle of the room, and the room, which had held her whole adult life in fourteen feet by fourteen feet, seemed suddenly very small and very far from everything. The bed with its gray blanket squared. The shelf of books gone soft-edged with damp winters. Their father's barometer, which she tapped every morning the way he had, two knuckles, gently, as if waking it. This was her last summer; the lease the Forest Service had granted her—caretaker, they called it, which meant nothing, which meant they hadn't wanted the liability of the tower standing empty—ran out in October, and after that the tower would be offered to the recreation-rental program or dismantled, depending on a budget she would never see. She had thought, when she signed the papers in March, that she was buying herself one more season of the only vantage she had ever trusted. She had not thought about what a vantage was for, if you could see everything and tell no one.

You watch, Lou had said to her at the funeral, in the church basement with the folding tables and the ham sandwiches, her voice low and even because Lou never shouted, Lou had never once in her life needed to. That's what you do. You went up that mountain and you watched, and I was down here for nine years wiping her chin, and now you want to stand at the front of the church and tell everybody what she was like.

It had not been the whole of it. The whole of it was older and had more rooms. But that was the sentence Maren had carried up the trail every June since, the way you carry a stone in your boot for miles because stopping to take it out would mean admitting it was there.

She packed in eight minutes. She had done the calculation while her hands worked, and the calculation said go, and said go now, before the wind strengthened with the evening, before the smoke column, which was already leaning harder, bent down into the drainage and made the trail a question. Three liters of water. The first-aid roll. Headlamp, batteries, the wool shirt because the night would come whatever happened. A bandanna she soaked at the basin and tied loose around her neck. She wrote a note in block letters and weighted it with the brass cup of the fire finder on the map table—SMOKE AT 197°, SLATE CREEK, GONE DOWN TRAIL 4:40 PM, M. CARLISLE—because someone might come, a hiker, a pilot might see the smoke and someone might come, and a note in a tower was the oldest radio there was. Then she stood for one second in the doorway, looking back at the room the way you look at a face, and went down the stairs, fifty-two steps, her boots ringing on the steel, the whole tower humming faintly under her like a struck tuning fork.

The trail dropped off the summit through whitebark and beargrass, the beargrass blooming this year, hundreds of cream-colored torches standing in the open slopes, and she went down through them fast, faster than was wise on the loose scree of the first mile, her knees taking it, her knees being told they would complain later, that there was a schedule and complaint was not on it. The smoke was out of sight now; the shoulder of the mountain hid it; and that was somehow worse, descending toward a thing she could no longer measure. In the tower she had been the eye of the country. On the trail she was a body in the timber like any deer.

The deer, in fact, were moving. She passed two does and a yearling coming uphill through the huckleberry with a directness deer did not usually have, not browsing, just walking, and they barely turned their heads at her, and she understood that she and they were reading the same page from different sides.

Their father had been the lookout before her, twenty-six summers, and before the lookout he had been a smokechaser in the years when they still sent one man with a pulaski and a canvas water bag down off the peaks to dig line around lightning strikes alone. He had raised his daughters up here in the summers, both of them, and the cabin on Slate Creek had been the other pole of the world: the tower was where you watched, and the cabin was where you lived, and the eight miles of trail between them was the length of the family. Lou had always loved the cabin and Maren had always loved the tower and they had teased each other about it the way children tease about left-handedness, as if it were a trait and not a prophecy. Lou learned the pump and the splitting maul and the garden that the frost killed every year and she planted every year anyway. Maren learned the fire finder and the cloud types and the great patience of the horizon. You'd live on the moon if it had a railing, Lou had said to her once, at fifteen, without malice, both of them lying on the catwalk counting meteors, and Maren had laughed because at fifteen it was funny.

The trail crossed the first creek, a thread of it, half what it should be in July, and she knelt and wet the bandanna again and drank and went on. Two miles down now. The timber closed over her, lodgepole in dense dog-hair stands, the kind of fuel that fire ran through like rumor, and she made herself walk and not run, because a turned ankle here was a death in the newspaper, FORMER LOOKOUT FOUND, and she had read enough of those over the years, men and women who had made one fast decision in steep country. The light through the canopy had changed. It had gone the color of weak tea, and then, as she descended, the color of strong tea, and she could smell it now, that smell that was not like a campfire because a campfire was domesticated and this was the wild original of it, pitch and duff and something mineral underneath, the smell of the ground itself burning.

The estrangement had not begun at the funeral. That was only where it had been said aloud. It had begun, if it began anywhere, in the nine years of their mother's long unraveling, when Lou had moved back to the valley and Maren had not. There had been reasons; there are always reasons; Maren could still recite them, the job in Spokane, the marriage in Spokane that was itself coming apart with a slowness that consumed everything, the visits she did make, six a year, then four, the checks she sent, which Lou cashed without comment, which was its own kind of comment. And the summers. She had kept the summers in the tower through all of it—it was paid work, she told herself, told Lou, told the silence on the phone—three months a year on a mountain with no road while her sister drove their mother to Missoula and back, to Missoula and back, learning the names of medications the way Maren knew the names of clouds. And the terrible thing, the thing Maren had never said to anyone and had barely said to herself, standing at the rail at sunset with the whole burning west in front of her, was that the summers had been a relief. That she had climbed up out of her mother's dying the way you climb above a smoke layer into clean air, and that the watching she did up there, the vigilance she was so proud of, the noticing of every gray feather on every horizon, had been a way of not watching the one thing.

Lou knew it. That was what the sentence at the funeral had meant. Lou had always been the one who knew things about Maren before Maren did, being younger and therefore, in the way of younger sisters, positioned all her life as the audience of Maren, the student of her, until the student knew the subject cold.

Four miles down, at the overlook where the trail came out on a rib of granite, she could see the fire itself for the first time, not just its smoke. It was across the drainage, a quarter mile of slope going at once, not crowning yet but candling—single trees igniting whole, whoosh, a tree's entire life converted in four seconds into a pillar of flame the size of the tree, then the next one—and the sound reached her across the gulf of air, a low continuous breathing with snaps inside it, like an enormous animal eating something with small bones. The smoke went up beyond all proportion now, a true column, building its own weather at the top, the cauliflower head of it catching the late sun and glowing a horrible beautiful rose. The fire was upstream of the cabin and across the creek from it. The wind was pushing it down-drainage and the creek was low. She did the arithmetic she did not want to do and the arithmetic said maybe. It said the fire would reach the water in an hour or two and the water might not be enough to hold it, not with this wind, not with spot fires lofting embers a quarter mile ahead, and the cabin sat in its meadow on the far side like a coin on a table in a room where someone has started throwing matches.

She thought, for the first time clearly, framed in words: Lou might not be there. Lou had a house in town; the cabin was weekends, was summer; it was entirely possible the meadow stood empty, the truck gone, and Maren was descending into a fire to rescue an unoccupied building. And under that thought was the other one, its shadow: Lou might be there. It was Friday. Lou came up Fridays. Lou had come up Fridays for thirty years, with the same fidelity Maren brought to the tower, the two of them keeping their separate sabbaths six miles apart and never speaking, and Maren realized that she had been keeping track of her sister's Fridays all this time the way she kept track of weather, peripherally, automatically, a column in the unofficial notebook of her attention, and that this was either love or its fossil, and that there was no way to know which until it was tested.

The last two miles of trail dropped through the old burn from 1934, where the larch their father's generation had watched go up had been replaced by the lodgepole that was now ready, in the way of that country, to go up again. Everything cycled. He used to say that, at the fire finder, turning the ring: It all burns, sooner or later; the job's not to stop it; the job is to see it and say where it is. As if seeing and saying were the whole of a person's duty. She had built a life on that proposition and the life had held right up until the moment, in a church basement, when her sister had pointed out the difference between a witness and a deserter, which is sometimes only the difference between the top of a mountain and the bottom.

Ash began to fall when she was a mile from the road. It came down through the still air under the canopy—the wind was all above her now, all in the upper story—and it fell with no sound at all, flakes of gray and some of them still holding the shapes of the things they had been, a needle, a flake of bark, perfect and weightless, a fir cone's scale, falling on her shoulders and her hair like the negative of snow. She was running now where the trail allowed it. Her knees had filed their complaint and been overruled. The smoke at ground level was a haze that thickened and thinned; she breathed through the wet bandanna; her eyes ran. Twice she heard, off across the creek, the deep cough of a tree going over, its root-wad burned out from under it.

The trail met the road at the old wooden sign, SLATE CREEK TRAIL NO. 141, BONNER POINT LOOKOUT 8, and the figure eight had been gouged by some long-ago knife into a crooked heart, kids, decades of kids, maybe even the two of them. She turned up the road. The road was a smoke tunnel, visibility a hundred feet, the sun gone entirely, replaced by an orange dusk at six-thirty on a July evening, and she ran up the middle of it past the markers she knew blind—the culvert, the leaning snag, the boulder shaped like a sleeping dog—and then the meadow opened on her left, and the truck was there.

The truck was there, and Lou was on the roof of the cabin.

She was on the roof with a garden hose, in jeans and a soaked flannel shirt, her gray hair plastered down, wetting the shakes, and she had already done the rest: Maren saw it all in one sweep, the propane tank's connections shut and the tank itself wrestled away from the wall, the firewood thrown clear of the porch in a great scattered apron, the shutters down, a lawn sprinkler, absurd and perfect, ticking back and forth on the woodshed roof, the little gas pump down at the creek hammering away, drafting from the pool their father had rocked in sixty years ago for exactly this and for no other reason. Lou was sixty-one years old and she was standing on a roof in falling ash conducting a one-woman defense of half an acre, and she had not seen Maren yet, and Maren stood at the edge of the meadow for a moment in the orange light and watched her sister the way she had watched everything in her life, completely, from a distance, and then she crossed the distance.

Lou heard her boots on the gravel and turned, and the hose kept running, splattering on the shakes, and for a moment her face did several things at once, too fast to read, the way a horizon does when weather moves through it. Then it settled. It settled into something Maren had no name for but recognized anyway, the way you recognize the smell of a house.

"The repeater's down," Maren called up, because facts were the only door she knew how to open. "Radio's dead. I saw it from the tower and came."

Lou looked at her. The fire breathed across the creek. Somewhere up-drainage a snag went off like a struck match, lighting the smoke from inside.

"You came down," Lou said. Not a question. An entry in a record.

"I came down."

Lou turned back to the roof and worked the hose along the ridgeline, and Maren stood in the falling ash with her heart going like the little pump at the creek, and then Lou shifted her feet on the wet shakes and said, without looking down, in the voice she might have used for anything, for pass the salt, for thirty years ago, "Hold the ladder."

Maren put both hands on the rails and set her boot against the bottom rung and held it, and felt, rung by rung through the aluminum, the weight of her sister coming down into her hands.
