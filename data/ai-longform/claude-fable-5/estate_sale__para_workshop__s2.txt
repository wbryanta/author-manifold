By one o'clock the dining room set was gone, and Carol stood in the space where it had been, looking at the four pale rectangles the table legs had pressed into the carpet over fifty years. The carpet around the rectangles had faded to the color of weak tea. Inside them it was still gold, the gold her mother had picked out in 1971, arguing with the man at Rinehart's Floor Covering until he threw in the padding for free. Carol remembered the argument because she'd been there, nine years old, mortified, studying the sample books while her mother's voice went up and up like a kettle.

A man in a Carhartt jacket was asking her something. He'd asked it twice.

"I'm sorry," she said. "What?"

"The chairs in the basement. The folding ones. Sticker says two dollars but there's six of them, so I was thinking ten for the lot."

"Sure," Carol said. "Ten's fine."

He counted the bills into her hand like he was dealing cards, and she put them in the green zippered bank pouch Sandy had given her that morning. Sandy ran the estate sale company out of Bucyrus and had a system, laminated signs, neon dot stickers, a folding table by the front door with a cash box and a credit card reader stuck to her phone. Sandy had told Carol she didn't have to be there at all. Most families aren't, Sandy said. Most families find it easier not to be. Carol had heard the kindness in it and ignored it, the way she ignored most kindness lately, the way you'd step around something wet on the floor.

The house smelled like other people now. That was the thing nobody warned you about. Forty strangers moving through five rooms since nine a.m., and the smell of her mother's house — Jergens lotion, coffee, the faint mineral breath of the basement — had been walked out the door on their coats. What was left smelled like cold air and cardboard.

She went back to her station, which was the kitchen, because Sandy had the living room and the foot traffic was lighter back here. The kitchen was where the small stuff was: the Pyrex, the cookie cutters, the drawer of rubber bands and twist ties her mother had saved for forty years, which Sandy had emptied into a Ziploc bag and stickered at fifty cents, and which, astonishingly, someone had bought before ten o'clock. A woman had paid two quarters for her mother's rubber bands and carried them out smiling. Carol had wanted to follow her into the yard and ask what she was going to do with them. She had wanted, briefly and insanely, to buy them back.

A young couple came in and started going through the cabinets. They were maybe thirty, the girl in a stocking cap with a pom-pom, the boy holding a cardboard box already half full of things from the living room — a brass lamp, a stack of doilies, the wooden recipe box. Carol's eyes went to the recipe box and stayed there.

"This is such a great house," the girl said to her. "Did you know the family?"

"It's my mother's house," Carol said. "Was."

The girl's face did the thing faces did. "Oh — I'm so sorry. I'm sorry, that was — "

"It's fine," Carol said, and meant it, mostly. "She was eighty-nine. She had a good run." That was the phrase she'd settled on at the funeral in November and it still worked, it was still true, it still closed the conversation like a door on a soft hinge.

"Everything's just so well taken care of," the girl said, which was its own kind of condolence, and a true one. Eleanor Voss had taken care of things. She'd ironed pillowcases. She'd kept the original boxes for appliances so they could be returned if they failed, and none of them ever failed, because she'd also kept the manuals and followed them. In the garage there was a coffee can of nails sorted by size, labeled in her mother's handwriting, the upright Palmer-method cursive of a woman educated by nuns in the 1940s, and that morning a retired machinist had bought the whole can for a dollar and told Carol, his voice gone rough, that nobody sorted nails anymore.

The recipe box was stickered at three dollars. Carol had stickered it herself, two nights ago, sitting at the kitchen table with a glass of wine and Sandy's roll of neon dots, and at the time it had felt like proof of something — that she could do this, that she was not going to be one of those people paralyzed by a dead woman's handwriting on index cards. She had her own copies of the recipes that mattered. The molasses cookies. The chicken and noodles. She'd photographed them years ago, after the first small stroke, when she'd flown in from Charlotte and spent a week being efficient about her mother's mortality in ways her mother found insulting.

"You bake?" she asked the girl.

"I'm trying to learn. Everything's online now but there's something about cards, you know?"

"There's a recipe in there for icebox pickles," Carol said. "It says one lump alum. We never figured out how big a lump was. She made them every August and they were never the same twice."

The girl laughed, and the boy shifted the box on his hip, and Carol heard herself keep going, telling them about the pickles, the crock in the basement, the cheesecloth, her mother slapping hands away from the crock all of July, and she understood she was doing a thing she had watched other people do at other sales — she was sending the object off with its story, like pinning a note to a child's coat. The couple listened with the generous patience of the young. Then they paid for their box of things, eleven dollars total, and the recipe box went out the door in the crook of the girl's arm, and Carol stood at the kitchen window and watched them load it into a Subaru and felt nothing, and then, about ninety seconds later, felt all of it at once, briefly, like a wave breaking over a piling, and then it drained away and she was fine.

Her brother had not come. Doug lived in Phoenix and had a hip replacement scheduled and had said, on the phone, Honestly, Carol, you're better at this stuff, which was true and which she resented anyway, the way she'd resented it at twelve, at thirty, at the funeral, where she had been better at that stuff too. Doug wanted the gun cabinet shipped and his share of whatever the sale brought, minus expenses, and he'd asked, almost as an afterthought, whether Mom still had Dad's bowling trophy, the one from the league championship in '78, and Carol had spent an hour in the attic finding it for him. That was Doug's grief: one trophy, freighted to Arizona. She didn't judge it. She'd read enough in the past three months to know that grief was the most poorly distributed substance on earth, that it pooled in strange low places and skipped the obvious ones entirely.

Sandy stuck her head into the kitchen. "Hon, there's a gal out here asking about the piano. Wants to know if you'd take seventy-five."

The piano was stickered at two hundred, which Sandy had told her was already a giveaway price, because nobody bought pianos anymore, pianos were furniture-shaped grief, every estate sale in Ohio had one and they all went to the landfill eventually, their cast-iron harps sold for scrap. Carol had taken lessons on that piano from Mrs. Diebold on Sandusky Street, a dollar fifty a lesson, and had hated it, and quit at thirteen over her mother's objections, and her mother had kept the piano tuned for fifty more years anyway, paying a man from Mansfield to come twice annually, as though Carol might walk back in at any moment and sit down and resume "Für Elise" at the measure where she'd abandoned it in 1975.

"Who's asking?" Carol said.

"Young gal. Got a couple kids with her."

Carol went out. The living room was emptier than it had been an hour ago — the davenport gone, the console television gone, sold ironically, she assumed, to a man with a beard who said it would be perfect for his bar. The woman standing by the piano was younger than Sandy had made her sound, late twenties maybe, with two kids, a boy about seven and a girl a little older, and she had the particular tiredness Carol recognized from her years teaching middle school in Charlotte, the tiredness of a woman doing arithmetic in her head constantly, gas and groceries and what was left.

"It's a Wurlitzer," Carol said. "It holds a tune."

"My daughter's been taking lessons at the church," the woman said. "She practices on a paper keyboard. Her teacher printed one out." She said it flatly, no bid for sympathy in it, just a fact, and the girl, who was running her finger along the fallboard without opening it, looked up at her mother with an expression that asked permission for hope.

"You got a truck?" Carol asked.

"My brother does. He could come Saturday."

"Take it for nothing," Carol said. The words came out ahead of any decision she was aware of making. Sandy, at her table, raised her eyebrows but said nothing; Sandy worked on commission but had apparently seen this before too. "There's a bench that goes with it. The music's inside the bench. Some of it's older than I am."

The woman started to argue, the way you had to, and Carol shook her head, and the woman stopped, and her eyes went bright and she looked away at the wall where the davenport used to be, and the girl opened the fallboard and pressed one key, middle C, very softly, as if the piano might be sleeping. The note hung in the stripped room. It was in tune. Of course it was in tune. Her mother had paid the man from Mansfield in October, five weeks before she died, and Carol had found the receipt in the desk, $110, piano tuning, paid in full, and at the time it had seemed like more of her mother's pointless thrifty diligence and now it seemed like something else entirely, like a message her mother hadn't known she was leaving, like the woman had kept the instrument ready for a child she would never meet.

Carol wrote the address of the brother's Saturday pickup on the back of one of Sandy's flyers and went back to the kitchen and stood at the sink and ran the water for no reason and turned it off.

Out the window the backyard was the dun color of February, the garden plot a rectangle of dead stalks her mother had been too frail to put in last spring, though she had talked about it on the phone as a settled plan, the tomatoes, the half-runners, as if frailty were a scheduling conflict that would resolve. The clothesline poles stood like rusted goalposts. Carol had hung wash on that line as a girl, complaining the whole time, and the smell of line-dried sheets was something she'd spent forty adult years and a great deal of money on detergents failing to reproduce, and you couldn't sticker a smell, you couldn't sell the way August light came through wet pillowcases, and so all of that would simply stop existing when the house sold in spring, and the new people would probably take the poles down with a reciprocating saw in an afternoon.

The afternoon thickened. Two o'clock, two thirty. A dealer came through, a man Sandy clearly knew and didn't like, who flipped things over to look at their bottoms and offered forty percent of sticker on everything he touched and got it, because that was how three o'clock worked at an estate sale. He bought the Hummels, all eleven of them, the little beige children with their geese and umbrellas that her mother had collected one at a time, birthdays and anniversaries, that had stood on the shelf above the television her whole life, dusted weekly, untouchable. Carol as a child had believed they were worth a fortune. Her mother had let her believe it. The dealer paid sixty dollars for all eleven and wrapped them in newspaper with quick indifferent hands, and Carol watched her mother's treasures disappear into print and understood that they had never been worth anything except the worth her mother's caring had put on them, which was the only kind of worth in the house, in any house, and which could not be transferred at any price.

"You doing okay?" Sandy asked her, around three thirty, in the lull. Sandy had brought a thermos and gave Carol coffee in a styrofoam cup. They stood in the living room, which was nearly empty now — the wall-to-wall gold carpet with its archipelago of dark unfaded shapes where everything had stood. There was the long rectangle of the davenport. The squares of the end tables. The big square of the television. You could read the room like a floor plan of forty years.

"I keep thinking I should feel worse than I do," Carol said. "Or better. One or the other."

"It's not a feeling kind of day," Sandy said. "It's a doing kind of day. The feeling comes later, usually in the car, usually at a red light. That's what everybody tells me."

"You're good at this."

"I've done two hundred of these. You want to know the secret?" Sandy blew on her coffee. "Every house is the same house. Doesn't matter if it's a doctor or a farmer. There's always a drawer of rubber bands. There's always a piano nobody plays. There's always one kid who comes and one kid who doesn't." She said it without cruelty, almost tenderly, the way a nurse talks about bodies. "And there's always one thing the family thinks they don't want until somebody else picks it up. You watch for it. You'll know it when you see it."

Carol had already taken the things she wanted, or thought she had. They were in her rental car: a shoebox of photographs, her mother's wedding ring, the cast-iron skillet, a quilt her grandmother had made, the Bible with the family names in the front going back to 1894, names entered in five different hands, ending with her father's death in her mother's writing in 2009, and below it a blank line where someone — Carol, it would have to be Carol — would write Eleanor Marie Voss, November 14, and after that, who. Who would write Carol's name, and in what book. Her son lived in Seattle and was kind and distant as weather, and when she died he would handle her estate with three phone calls and a service that did everything online, and this was not a tragedy, she told herself, this was just the direction water flowed.

At four a man came in alone. He was Carol's age, maybe a little older, heavyset, in a Buckeyes cap, and he moved through the picked-over rooms slowly, not like a shopper, and finally he came to the kitchen doorway and stopped.

"You're Carol," he said. "Carol Voss."

She looked at him a moment. The town did this to you; it kept people in storage and released them, terribly changed, when you least expected it.

"Gary Schoenfeld," he said. "From Whetstone Street. We rode the same bus."

"Gary," she said, and the boy surfaced inside the man — Gary Schoenfeld who'd thrown a snowball with a rock in it and cried harder than the kid he hit, who'd worked at the Sohio station all through high school, whose mother and her mother had been in Altar Guild together for decades. "Gary. My God."

"I saw the sale in the paper and I figured. I was sorry to hear about Eleanor. She brought my mother supper every week for a year when Dad was dying. Did you know that? Every Thursday. Chicken and noodles, mostly. Never missed."

Carol had not known that. She tried to fit it next to what she did know, her mother's hardness, the slapped hands and the kettle voice, the woman who had told Carol at her own divorce, by way of comfort, Well, you picked him. The chicken and noodles every Thursday for a year did not contradict any of it. That was the unbearable thing about the dead, she was learning: they stopped being arguable. They became all of their facts at once, and you had to hold the whole contradictory weight of them with no chance of ever getting an explanation, an apology, a single further word.

"She never mentioned it," Carol said.

"She wouldn't. Your mother wasn't one to mention." Gary looked around the gutted kitchen. "I'm not really here to buy anything. I just wanted to see the house once more, I guess. We used to do homework at that table." He nodded at the empty space where the kitchen table had stood until noon, when a young farmer had carried it out, the table with the crescent burn mark where Carol had set a hot lid down in 1976 and waited, sick, for three days for her mother to notice, and her mother had noticed within the hour and said only, Well, now it's ours, which Carol had spent decades repeating to herself without fully understanding, and which she had repeated to the young farmer, who'd looked at her politely, taking custody of a mark that would never mean anything to anyone again.

"There's coffee," Carol said. "It's Sandy's, but she shares."

Gary stayed twenty minutes. They stood in the kitchen with styrofoam cups and traded the names of the dead and the moved-away, and he told her the Sohio station was a vape shop now, and the high school had consolidated with Crestline, and Rinehart's was an antique mall, which meant, Carol thought, that someday her mother's gold carpet might come back into that very building and be sold as a remnant of a vanished style. When Gary left he shook her hand with both of his and said, "Eleanor was a good woman," and Carol said, "She was a hard woman," and Gary said, without missing a beat, "Around here that was the same thing," and put his cap back on and went out into the gray afternoon.

By five the light was failing and the trickle of buyers had stopped. Sandy went through the rooms making a list of what hadn't sold — a box of National Geographics, a humidifier, the artificial Christmas tree, odds and ends — and said a charity truck would come Tuesday for the rest, and the cleanout crew Thursday, and after that the realtor, and Carol could be on a plane Friday if she wanted, the whole apparatus of disappearance was scheduled and paid for. Sandy counted out the cash box on the folding table, licking her thumb, stacking the bills, and the day's take came to two thousand one hundred and forty dollars, less commission, for the entire material residue of Eleanor Voss's eighty-nine years, and both women had the grace not to say anything about the number.

After Sandy's van pulled out, Carol walked the house alone in the dusk. She didn't turn the lights on. The rooms were the size they'd been when she was small; emptiness had restored their original acreage. In her old bedroom the radiator ticked. In her parents' room the closet stood open, bare hangers, and on the high shelf, missed by everyone, sat one thing: her mother's good hat, the navy one with the small net veil, worn to church for fifty years and to two funerals Carol could specifically remember, in its original hatbox from a department store in Marion that had closed before the moon landing. Sandy had missed it or left it; the closet shelf was deep and dark.

Carol took the box down and lifted the lid. The hat sat in its tissue like something nesting. It was worth nothing. It was the most worthless object in the house — out of style in 1965, never coming back, fitted to one dead woman's head. There was a single white hair caught in the veil's netting.

She stood in the dark bedroom holding the box. You'll know it when you see it, Sandy had said, and here it was, the thing she didn't want, the thing she could not put on the charity truck, the thing nobody on earth would pay a quarter for, and her knees gave a little and she sat down on the bare floor where the bed had been, in the rectangle of unworn carpet, and she put the hatbox in her lap and she cried, finally, not at a red light but here, hard and briefly and without elegance, the way her mother would have done it, alone, in the dark, where it wouldn't trouble anyone, and when it was finished she wiped her face with the heel of her hand and laughed once at herself, a short sound in the empty house, because she could hear exactly what Eleanor would have said: For heaven's sake, Carol, it's a hat.

She carried it out under her arm. On the porch she stopped to lock the door, working the same loose deadbolt her father had always meant to fix, and across the street the streetlight came on, the same streetlight, buzzing the same pink-orange it had buzzed when she was a girl being called in for supper. The cold smelled like coming snow. Down the block a dog barked twice and quit. She set the hatbox on the passenger seat and belted it in, which was ridiculous, and she knew it was ridiculous, and she did it anyway, and pulled out of the driveway of the empty house slowly, the way you drive away from a hospital, and at the corner of Whetstone she stopped longer than the stop sign required, looking back once in the mirror at the dark windows, and then she went on, because the light was green now, or would be soon, somewhere, and there was a plane on Friday, and somewhere in Ohio tonight a girl was practicing scales on a real piano, pressing the keys softly at first, then less softly, filling a strange house with the sound.
