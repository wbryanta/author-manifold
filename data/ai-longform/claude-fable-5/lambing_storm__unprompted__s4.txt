The snow had begun as rain at three in the afternoon, and by the time Maren finished her coffee at the kitchen window it had turned, the drops slowing and thickening until they fell in wet clots against the glass, and she knew. April twenty-third. The forecast had said flurries, a possibility of accumulation in the higher elevations, and the farm sat at twenty-two hundred feet, which the radio men in Spokane had never once in her life considered a higher elevation, and she had thirty-one ewes still to lamb.

She rinsed her cup and set it in the rack and went to the bottom of the stairs.

"Eli."

A pause, the creak of his bed. "Yeah."

"Get your coveralls. It's turning."

He didn't argue. He had not argued about anything since February, which she would have given a great deal to change, because his arguments had been one of the ordinary noises of the house, like the furnace kicking on, like Daniel's boots dropped one and then the other by the back door, and the house had gone so quiet that she sometimes stood in rooms listening for what was missing.

They walked out together into the falling snow, and already it was sticking to the gravel of the yard, already the light had gone strange, that bruised yellow-gray that meant the storm had weight behind it. The lambing shed stood a hundred yards from the house, a long low building Daniel's father had raised in the sixties and Daniel had reroofed the summer Eli was born, and the ewes were calling inside it, the deep complaint of pregnant animals who felt the pressure dropping in their own bodies. Sheep lambed on storms. Everyone said it and nobody could tell you why, but it was true, the falling barometer pulled the lambs out of them like the moon pulled tides, and Maren had checked the pens at noon and found two ewes pawing the straw and another standing off in the corner with that inward look, and now there would be more.

"How many you figure tonight?" Eli asked. He was fifteen and had his father's way of asking questions flatly, so you couldn't tell if he wanted the answer or was only filling the air.

"Six. Eight." The snow creaked under her boots, which was a sound it should not make in April. "Maybe more if it drops fast."

Inside, the shed was warm with the bodies of animals, that thick lanolin smell, ammonia and dry straw and the green smell of the hay in the feeders. Forty ewes in the loose pen at the far end, the lambing jugs along the near wall, four of them occupied already by ewes that had lambed in the past two days, their lambs tucked against them or wobbling at the milk. Maren walked the loose pen slow, the way Daniel had taught her when she was twenty-three and new to all of it, a town girl who had married into sheep. You don't look for trouble, he'd said. You look at all of them and trouble shows itself. The ewe in the corner had her tail up now and was turning circles, lying down, getting up. Another, a big Columbia cross with a torn ear tag, stood pressed against the rail breathing through her mouth.

"The corner one's close," Eli said. He'd come up beside her without her hearing him. He moved quietly now, another new thing. "Water bag's showing."

"Get a jug ready. Fresh straw. And bring the kit down from the shelf."

He went. She watched him go, the set of his shoulders in the brown coveralls that had been Daniel's and were still too big for him, though less too big than they'd been at Christmas, and she made herself stop watching and turn back to the ewes.

The corner ewe lambed easy, a single, big and yellow with birth fluid, and the ewe was up and licking it before Maren had even gotten the gate of the jug open. They moved the pair together, Eli carrying the lamb low so the ewe could smell it the whole way, walking backward, murmuring to her, come on, come on then, and the ewe followed her lamb into the jug the way they did when it went right, and Maren clipped the gate and felt the small mean satisfaction of one down.

By six the snow was coming sideways past the shed's one west window and the light was failing, and the Columbia cross was in labor and not progressing. Maren scrubbed her arm to the elbow in the bucket of warm water and disinfectant Eli had hauled from the house, and she went in, and found what she had been afraid of, a head and one leg, the other leg folded back at the shoulder.

"What is it," Eli said. Not a question, exactly.

"Leg back." She closed her eyes to see with her fingers. The ewe groaned and her contraction clamped down on Maren's wrist hard enough to bruise, and she waited it out, and when it passed she pushed the lamb's head back into the dark wet heat of the ewe, against everything in her own body that said you do not push a thing back that is trying to be born, and she followed the shoulder down, found the knee, cupped the small sharp hoof in her palm so it wouldn't tear the ewe, and brought the leg forward. It took three contractions. Her shoulder burned. The shed had gone dim around the work, the way it did, the whole world narrowed to the few inches she could not see and could only feel.

"Okay," she said. "Okay. Pull with her now."

The lamb came in a rush of fluid, limp, the cord trailing, and it did not move. Eli had it before she said anything, had it by the hind legs, swinging it gently, then down in the straw rubbing its ribs with a feed sack, hard, the way you had to, and Maren cleared its mouth with two fingers and tickled its nostril with a piece of straw and it sneezed and shuddered and drew its first breath in a thin wet rattle.

"There," Eli said. Just that. He kept rubbing. The lamb bawled, weak and furious, and the ewe answered it, turning her big head around, and something in Maren's chest unclenched.

"There's another one in there," she said. "She's not done."

The second twin came on its own, ten minutes later, smaller, sliding out into the straw like an afterthought, and they jugged the three of them together and Eli dipped the navels in iodine, holding each lamb against his chest with one arm, and Maren stripped the ewe's teats to break the wax plugs and saw the milk come, and they stood for a moment in the lantern-colored light of the shed watching the lambs root and butt and find the udder.

That was when the lights went out.

The hum she hadn't known she was hearing stopped, the heat lamps over the jugs went dark, and the shed dropped into a blackness so total that for a moment Maren could see nothing at all, only hear the storm, which without the electric hum seemed suddenly very loud, the wind working at the roof, the snow hissing against the west wall.

"Mom."

"I'm here. Don't move till your eyes adjust."

"The heat lamps."

"I know."

She knew. Four jugs of new lambs, now five, six counting the twins, and the temperature in the shed would hold for a while on the bodies of the ewes but the night was coming down hard and wet lambs chilled fast, and a chilled lamb stopped sucking, and a lamb that stopped sucking died, sometimes in two hours, sometimes in one. She stood in the dark and ran the inventory in her head. Lanterns in the tack room, two of them, kerosene, if the kerosene was good. Flashlights. The generator.

The generator. Daniel had bought it the year of the ice storm, and Daniel had maintained it, run it the first Saturday of every month, changed its oil in the fall, and Daniel had been dead since the eighth of February, and Maren had not so much as looked at the thing.

"Get the lanterns," she said. "Matches are in the coffee can on the shelf, left side. I'm going to try the generator."

"I'll do the generator."

"Eli."

"Dad showed me." His voice came steady out of the dark, and then, quieter, "Last fall. When he winterized it. He showed me the whole thing."

She stood with her hand on the rail of the jug and the lamb-sounds small in the blackness, and she thought of all the things Daniel had shown the boy that she did not know about, the whole quiet curriculum of those fifteen years, knife sharpening and engine timing and how to read the sky, and how she would never now learn the full inventory of it, would only discover it piece by piece like this, in the dark, when something failed.

"Take the big flashlight," she said. "It's in the truck."

He went out into the storm and she found the lanterns by feel, and the matches, and the kerosene was good. She hung one lantern from the nail over the jugs and carried the other down the line, checking. The twins were both at the teat. The corner ewe's single was asleep against its mother's flank. In the loose pen, two more ewes had gone down in the straw, and one of them was straining.

The wind hit the shed in a long shuddering gust and somewhere outside a door banged, the machine shed maybe, and she heard, under the wind, the cough and stutter of the generator turning over, and turning over, and not catching. She made herself stay with the laboring ewe. The water bag came, and broke, and the feet showed, two of them, points down, the nose between them like a diver, textbook, thank God, and she let the ewe work and listened to her son in the storm fighting his father's machine.

It caught on what must have been the tenth pull. She heard it roar and steady, and the heat lamps over the jugs flickered and came up orange, half power, the generator wired to the shed's one essential circuit just as Daniel had planned it, because Daniel planned for everything, Daniel had planned for every disaster except the one that came for him on a clear cold morning with no warning at all, his heart simply stopping between the house and the barn, the coffee cup still in his hand, unspilled, sitting upright in the snow beside him where he fell. She had thought about that cup more than she could account for. The way it sat there. The way some things stay upright.

Eli came back in plastered white down his front, snow in his eyebrows, breathing hard.

"Choke was set wrong," he said. "Somebody'd left it half open."

Somebody. Daniel, in November, the last time he'd run it. Her son had just touched the last setting his father's hand had made, and corrected it, and she could see in his face that he knew it, that the knowledge had passed through him out there in the dark and the snow, and that he had started the machine anyway because the lambs needed the heat. He didn't say anything else. He stamped the snow off his boots and looked down the shed.

"That one's lambing," he said.

"Textbook so far. Watch her. I'm going to bring the bottle ewes some grain and then check the rest."

The night settled into its rhythm then, the rhythm she knew, that her body knew even this year when everything else had come unmoored. The storm outside and the small economy of births inside. The ewe Eli watched delivered twins without help, and he jugged them himself, and Maren watched him do it and did not assist and did not advise, watched him walk backward murmuring with the lambs held low, and she thought he looks like, and stopped the thought, because it didn't help, and because it wasn't even quite true. He looked like himself. He did the work like Daniel, the same patience, the same economy, but the face that bent over the lambs in the lantern light was the face she had known since it was a day old, her own boy, and the grief that ambushed her then was not for Daniel at all but for Eli, for what the winter had taken out of his face, the way he had stopped arguing, the way he moved so quietly now through the rooms of his life.

At ten she went to the house and made sandwiches and a thermos of coffee and another of cocoa, and stood for one minute in the dark kitchen with her hands flat on the counter, just breathing, and then went back out. The snow was eight inches deep in the yard and still coming, drifting against the east side of every building, and the path she'd broken an hour ago was already filling. Inside the shed the generator droned and the lambs were a chorus of small voices and Eli sat on an overturned bucket beside the jugs with a lamb in his lap.

"This one's not sucking," he said. "The little twin. She got knocked off the teat and I don't think she ever got a full feed. She's getting that hunch."

Maren knew the hunch, the way a chilling lamb stood with its back arched and its head down, drawing in around the cold spot growing at its center. She knelt and put her finger in the lamb's mouth. Cool. Not cold yet. Cool.

"Tube her," she said. "You milk the ewe, I'll hold."

"I've never done the tube."

"Then it's time."

She showed him the way she'd been shown, two decades ago, by a man whose hands she could still see doing it. You measure the tube against the lamb, mouth to last rib, and mark it with your thumb. You wet it. You lay the lamb upright on your knees, head level, never back, and you slide the tube along the roof of the mouth and let the lamb swallow it, and you go slow, and you check, you put the end of the tube to your ear and listen, because if you hear breathing you're in the lung and you start over, and if you hear nothing, the soft nothing of the stomach, you fit the syringe and let the milk run down by gravity, never pushing, an ounce, two ounces, the warm colostrum he'd stripped from the ewe with his cold hands, and you watch the lamb's sides fill out and you feel it, the moment the animal stops being a thing that is leaving and becomes a thing that is staying.

Eli did it right the first time. His hands shook slightly and he did it right, and when he drew the tube out the lamb smacked its mouth and complained, and he laughed, a short startled sound, the first laugh she had heard from him in she did not know how long, and he caught her looking and his face did something complicated and he turned back to the lamb.

"She'll want it again in two hours if she's not sucking," Maren said. "We'll check."

"I'll check."

"We'll both be up. There's three more in the loose pen that won't make it till morning."

He nodded. He set the lamb back in the jug against its mother and watched it a moment, and then he said, not looking at her, "Dad always said lambing was the bill coming due. For getting to live out here."

"He said that to me too."

"He said you pay it gladly or you pay it bitter but you pay it either way." Eli picked a strand of straw off his knee. "I keep waiting to feel like it's worth it. The way he made it sound."

Maren sat down on the hay bales across from him. Outside the wind had found a new note, lower, the snow gone fine and hard. Somewhere down the line a ewe rumbled to her lamb, that low chuckling sound they made for nothing else, only for their own.

"You don't have to feel it tonight," she said. "Tonight you just have to do it."

"Is that what you do?"

He looked at her when he said it, and it was not a boy's question. It was the question of someone who had been lying awake in the room above hers all winter listening to the house the same way she had been listening to it.

"Yes," she said. "Most days that's what I do. I do the work and I let the feeling come or not come." She turned the thermos cup in her hands. "It's coming back, though. Slower than I'd like. Tonight, when that first one stood up and nursed — there was a second there. It was small but it was real."

Eli nodded slowly, the way he did when he was filing something away, and then the ewe with the torn ear tag began banging her jug rail, and a ewe in the loose pen went down pawing, and the night took them back.

The hard one came at two in the morning. A yearling ewe, her first lamb, and the lamb was breech, and big, and the yearling was small and exhausted by the time Maren understood what she was dealing with. She went in and found the tail where the head should be, and the hind legs tucked forward under the lamb's body, and she worked by feel with her cheek against the ewe's heaving flank, the lantern haloed in the steam of their breath, and she could not get the second leg. Her hand was cramping. The ewe had stopped pushing and lay flat out in the straw with her eyes half closed, and Maren felt the situation tipping, felt the night reaching for this one, and she said, "Eli. Your hands are smaller."

He scrubbed in without a word. She talked him through it, her own hand resting on the ewe's flank, her voice low and even though her heart was loud. Tail first means you find the hocks. Follow the leg down. Cup the foot. He knelt behind the ewe with his arm gone into her and his eyes shut, his face gone away to that inner place where the work was, and she watched her son's face while he felt for a life in the dark, and the wind hammered the shed, and the generator droned, and after a long time he said, "Got it. Got the foot," and brought the first leg, and then, faster, the second, and then they pulled together with the ewe's last contractions, downward and out in the long curve of it, and the lamb came backwards into the world in a slick of fluid and lay still.

It was a long lamb, deep-bodied, and it lay still, and Eli swung it and rubbed it and Maren cleared the airway and rubbed it, both of them working over it in the straw, the feed sack rasping, the ewe lifting her weary head to watch, and the lamb did not breathe, and did not breathe, and then it did. One breath. A spasm of a thing. Then another. Then the shuddering ratchet of life catching in it, and it bawled, and Eli sat back on his heels in the bloody straw and put his forearm over his eyes, and his shoulders went, once, twice, and Maren let him be. She toweled the lamb and set it at the yearling's head and the young ewe began, hesitantly, and then with gathering conviction, to lick her firstborn, talking to it in that low chuckle, learning it, claiming it, and by the time Eli took his arm down from his face it was only wet around the eyes and neither of them said anything about it.

Toward five the storm began to ease. The wind dropped first, and then the snow thinned, and through the west window the sky showed a seam of paling gray. Eleven lambs since afternoon, all alive, ten mothered up and the small twin tubed twice more and finally, at four-thirty, found nursing on her own, butting the udder with her tail spinning, and Maren stood watching her with the lantern in her hand and felt the tiredness arrive all at once, the way it did when the need for it passed.

They walked back to the house through snow to their knees, into a world gone absolutely silent and absolutely white, the fences buried to the second wire, the yard light still dead but the eastern sky kindling behind the hills so that the whole unbroken sweep of snow was turning the faintest rose. Their breath hung in the air. Behind them in the shed the ewes called and the lambs answered, all those new voices, and the sound carried in the stillness the way sound only carried after a storm.

At the back door Eli stopped and looked out over the fields. The snow had drifted against the machine shed in a long sculpted wave, and the apple tree Daniel had planted the year they married stood blossoming under its load of white, every flowering branch sleeved in snow, and it should have looked like ruin and instead it looked like nothing Maren had ever seen, the pink blossom showing through the white where the wind had scoured it, the whole tree lit by the rising light, blooming and buried at once.

"Bill came due," Eli said.

"It did."

He stood a moment longer. "We paid it, though."

"We did," she said, and the boy almost smiled, and they went in to sleep for two hours before the morning check, leaving their boots by the back door, one pair and then the other, the small ordinary thunder of them filling the quiet house.
