The line for the ferry began moving a little after ten, and Wade Decker eased his truck forward between the transports with his window down, though the night had gone cold, because he wanted the smell of the water. Diesel and creosote and underneath it the old iron smell of the North Atlantic, which he had not smelled in eleven years and which his body received like a language it had pretended to forget. The loading lights of the terminal at North Sydney threw everything into a hard orange clarity — the chained ramps, the men in vests waving their wands, the white flank of the vessel rising over the wharf like the side of a building — and beyond all of it the strait lay black and total, no horizon, only the place where the lights stopped.

He had driven two days from Ontario. He could have flown to Deer Lake and rented a car and been there in an afternoon, and Constable Bungay had said as much on the phone, gently, the way you suggest things to the bereaved, but he had said no, he'd drive, and he hadn't been able to explain it to her or to himself except that some part of him needed the distance to be felt. Needed to pay it out behind him mile by mile like rope. You could not arrive at a thing like this quickly. You had to come at it the long way, the old way, over the water in the dark.

A man in a vest leaned to his window and looked at his ticket and his licence and waved him on, and Wade drove up the ramp into the white steel cavern of the vehicle deck, the truck's tires booming on the plates. He parked where they pointed him, nose to the bumper of a camper from Quebec, and shut the engine off and sat a moment with his hands on the wheel. Around him the transports came on one after another, the whole ship shuddering faintly with their weight, and the air filled with exhaust until the deckhands began chaining things down and the drivers climbed out and made for the stairs with their little overnight bags, unhurried, men who had done this a hundred times. Wade took his jacket and the envelope of papers off the passenger seat and followed them up.

He had not booked a cabin. There were reclining chairs in a darkened lounge on the seventh deck, rows of them facing forward toward windows that showed nothing but the lounge reflected on the night, and he put his jacket on one to claim it and then could not sit. The ship was still loading. He could feel the trucks coming aboard far below like a slow pulse. He went along the corridor past the closed gift shop and the bank of vending machines to the cafeteria, which stayed open for the night crossing, and stood in the line with three truckers and a young couple and a woman holding a sleeping child against her shoulder, and he bought tea and a roll he did not want, and carried them to a table by the window.

The call had come on a Tuesday. He had been on a job in Cambridge, hanging drywall in a new build, white dust on his forearms, his phone going in his pocket twice before he answered it, and a woman's voice had said his name as a question — Wade Decker? — with the accent of home laid over the flat procedural tone of police everywhere, and he had known, in the half-second before she said anything else, that it was Russ. Though it was not, exactly. That was the thing. That was why he was on this boat.

A fishing vessel, she said, the *Effie M.*, registered to Russell Decker of Rose Blanche–Harbour le Cou, had been found adrift two miles off Cape Ray on the morning of October the ninth, no one aboard, the gear hauler still engaged, the engine run dry. Six days later a body had come ashore on the rocks below Isle aux Morts. She said the name of that place without pausing on it, the way locals do, the French worn smooth as beach glass, but Wade heard it the way his father used to translate it for tourists with a grim satisfaction: Isle of the Dead, sure. Named for all the wrecks. The body, Constable Bungay said, was consistent in age and build with his brother. But the sea had had it for six days, and she would be doing him no kindness to pretend that made nothing difficult. And there was a further matter, which she gave him plainly, and he was grateful for the plainness: the same gale that week had taken a deckhand off a dragger out of Burgeo, a man of thirty-nine, also not recovered, also without people nearby. The body carried no identification. Dental records had been requested but his brother's dentist had retired and the files were somewhere in transit between Port aux Basques and Corner Brook. So it would help, she said. If he could come. If he could look.

Were there any marks she should know of, she had asked. Scars, tattoos, anything distinguishing. And Wade had stood in the half-finished kitchen of a stranger's house in Ontario with the phone at his ear and his mouth open, and after a moment he had heard himself say, No. Nothing I can think of.

He drank his tea. The ship's horn sounded somewhere above him, enormous and mournful, twice, and the lights of North Sydney began very slowly to slide backward past the window. A trembling came up through the deck and into the table and into the tea, rings spreading on its surface from the centre outward, and Wade watched the rings instead of the town going away.

Nothing I can think of. It had not even been a lie when he said it. It had been a kind of blankness, a white wall coming down, the same wall that came down whenever, in eleven years, he had picked up the phone with his brother's number already keyed in and then set it down again. But the wall never held for long, and it did not hold now, and what was behind it was a July morning in 1979, Wade fourteen and Russ not quite thirteen, the two of them out on the *Effie M.* with their father hauling lobster gear in a lop off Harbour le Cou, and their father stepping into the wheelhouse for not more than a minute, and Wade at the hauler showing off, running it the way he'd seen the men run it, and Russ reaching to clear a snarl of rope from the block at the wrong moment, or Wade engaging it at the wrong moment, and which of those it had been was a question Wade had spent forty-odd years adjudicating in the small hours and had never once settled. The sound Russ made. The rope going red. Their father coming out of the wheelhouse and not shouting, which was worse than shouting, wrapping the hand in his own shirt and steaming for the harbour with his jaw set and Russ white as a gull between his knees. The top joint of the left ring finger, gone above the second knuckle, gone into the water, the doctor in Port aux Basques tidying what was left into a smooth blunt thing that healed pale and shiny and that Russ, by Christmas, was using to amaze smaller boys.

He never blamed me, Wade thought. Not once, not even when they fought as men over money and the house and everything else there was to fight over. In the worst of it, the day of their mother's funeral, with the two of them squared up in her kitchen among the casserole dishes the neighbours had brought, Russ had thrown plenty at him — that he'd left like the place was a fire he was escaping, that he'd come home only to price things — but never the finger. He had carried that one wound of Wade's making for forty years without ever once setting it down between them, and Wade understood, sitting in the humming cafeteria of the night ferry, that this was precisely why he hadn't been able to say it to the constable. Anything distinguishing. Yes. There is a mark on him that I put there. You will know my brother by what I did to him.

The cafeteria emptied out by degrees. The truckers went off to their cabins. The woman with the child found a corner of bench seating and built a small nest of coats and lay down with the child inside her arm, and the lights were dimmed to a brownish half-dark, and the ship took up the long open swell of the strait and began to move in earnest, a slow pitch and lean, the structure of it creaking somewhere deep like a house settling. Wade got up and went out through the heavy door onto the open deck astern.

The wind took him at once, colder than he'd dressed for, and he went to the rail and held it. There was nothing to see and everything. The ship's own light fell a little way onto the water rushing past below, showing the black of it veined with white, and beyond that the dark was so complete that the wake glowed by contrast, a long pale road unspooling behind them toward Nova Scotia until it bent away and was gone. No moon. Stars in the gaps between clouds, and once, far off to the west, the loom of a lighthouse turning, a brightening and a dying in the murk, too far to count its seconds. Somewhere out ahead, a hundred-some miles of this. His brother's water now, he thought, and then made himself think it properly, without flinching: his brother was perhaps in it. If the man lying in Port aux Basques was the Burgeo deckhand, then Russ was still out here, in the strait or along the coast among the sunkers, in October water, and might come ashore in a month or never, and Wade would go home to Ontario with his grief postponed like a connecting flight, and that — he gripped the rail — that possibility was its own species of horror, because he had discovered on the drive east, somewhere around Edmundston, that there was a part of him, small and unkillable, that wanted the body not to be Russ. Wanted the door left open. Wanted the eleven years of silence to remain a thing that could still, theoretically, on some future Christmas, with one phone call, be ended. As long as the body was unidentified his brother was alive the way money in a wallet is unspent.

He stayed at the rail until his hands ached and then a while longer. He thought about the house. That was what the fight had been about, in the end, when their mother died — the house above the harbour in Rose Blanche with its kitchen window full of the breakwater, the house their father had built two rooms at a time as the children came. Wade had wanted it sold. He'd had reasons; he could still recite them; they had even been true. He had a mortgage in Ontario and a marriage in its last year and the fish were gone — had been gone since '92, since the moratorium emptied the harbour of everything but memory — and the house was worth little and would be worth less, and what was Russ going to do, live alone in a dying outport tending their parents' graves? And Russ had stood in the kitchen with his arms folded and said he'd buy Wade's half out, then. And he had. Borrowed against the boat and his licence and paid Wade twenty-two thousand dollars, which Wade had taken, and the debt had bent the rest of Russ's life into the shape of work, lobster in the spring and whatever else the quotas allowed, alone on the *Effie M.* because crew shares were a luxury, and every one of the few accounts of him that had reached Wade over the years — through their cousin Pearl, through the aunt in Stephenville before she died — had carried the same picture: Russ getting on, Russ keeping the place up, Russ out alone in weather other men watched from the wharf.

He went out alone in October to haul his gear ahead of a forecast, Constable Bungay had said. That was the supposition. The hauler running, the engine run dry. A trap coming up heavy, maybe, a sea over the quarter at the wrong moment, a slip on a wet deck — it took so little, alone. No man overboard alarm on a forty-year-old longliner. The boat circling or wandering on its own until the fuel was gone, then drifting on the tide toward Cape Ray like a horse going home with an empty saddle.

Wade went back inside near two in the morning, his face stiff with cold, and found his chair in the dark lounge. Across the aisle an old man was awake, sitting up straight with a cap on his knee, white hair flattened, looking at nothing, and when Wade settled himself the old man turned and looked at him with the frank unhurried appraisal of the outports and said, low, so as not to wake the sleepers: You'd be a Decker. Wade said he was. The old man nodded as though confirming something to himself. Knowed your father, he said. Fished out of La Poile, me. Your father was a hand. And the boy — Russell. He kept that boat some good. I seen her at the wharf in Port aux Basques in the spring, painted up like new money. The old man said it with a plain admiring finality and then faced forward again, his business concluded, and Wade sat very still with his eyes burning in the dark. He kept that boat some good. The verb tense a small mercy or a small cruelty, he couldn't tell which — the old man not knowing, the news evidently not yet gone along the coast, Russ for one more night merely a man with a well-kept boat. Wade did not correct him. He was, he realized, fiercely unwilling to be the one to move his brother from one tense to the other. Not yet. Not until he had to.

He slept, somehow, in pieces. The ship rolled and the chairs creaked all together like rigging and twice he woke not knowing where he was and the second time the windows ahead had gone from black to a deep iron grey. He got up and washed his face in the men's room, looked at himself under the fluorescent light — his father's heavy jaw coming up in him more every year, the grey at the temples that Russ would have too, would have had — and then he took his jacket and went forward and up to the observation deck.

Land. It came out of the half-dark off the port bow the way that coast always came: not gently, no gradual lowness, but the Long Range mountains standing straight up out of the sea with their flat stone heads in cloud, the Table Mountains above Port aux Basques streaked with the first snow, everything below them barren and bald and glistening, a coast like the rim of something broken. The sea in the strait had laid down toward dawn and the water inshore was the colour of pewter and the gulls had found the ship and hung off her quarter, tilting, crying their one cold question. A few other passengers came up to watch, holding paper cups of coffee, and nobody said much. The ship found the channel buoys and made her slow turn, and the town opened up around the harbour the way it always had — the white and green and ochre houses scattered over the bare rock as though dropped there, the church, the fuel tanks, the long government wharf — and Wade stood with his hands on the rail and let it come at him, all of it, eleven years and then forty more behind those, until the horn sounded overhead and the announcement called drivers to the vehicle deck.

Constable Bungay was younger than her voice, parked by the terminal in an unmarked SUV, and she shook his hand and said she was sorry to meet him this way, and he followed her through the waking town to the health centre on its hill. She talked to him plainly in the corridor, which he was grateful for: that the gentleman was not presentable in the face, she'd not pretend otherwise; that there was no shame in needing time, or in not being certain; that uncertain was an answer too, and the dental records would come. There was a watch, she said, found in the wheelhouse cubby of the *Effie M.*, an old Bulova with a stretch band, and Wade said before she finished describing it: that was our father's, and his voice came out wrong, and she gave him a moment by pretending to consult her folder.

The room was small and clean and cold and smelled of nothing, which was its own kind of smell. The body lay on a table under a white sheet and a man in scrubs stood by it with his hands folded, and Constable Bungay asked Wade if he was ready and he said yes, not knowing if it was true, and the man folded the sheet down from the face with a practised tenderness, the way you'd turn down a bed.

She had been right and the sea had been thorough. Wade looked, because he had come all this way to look, and what he felt first was not horror but a terrible shyness, as though he had walked in on a stranger sleeping; the face was a face the water had been making decisions about for six days, and it answered none of his questions, neither was nor wasn't, a man of about sixty with grey in his stubble, that was all, that was as far as the face would take him. He heard himself breathing. The constable said, quietly, take your time, Mr. Decker, and he stood in the cold room over the man who may or may not have been his brother and understood that he was at the exact centre of the strait he had crossed in the night, equidistant from both shores, and that he could stand there forever if he let himself.

The hand, he said. His voice was a stranger's too. Could I see — the left hand.

The attendant glanced at the constable and she nodded, and he moved the sheet from the left side, carefully, and lifted the arm a little so the hand lay open on the white cloth, palm up, then turned it over.

The water had not been kind to the hands either. But the bones of a hand do not change, and the shape of it was the shape Wade had been carrying behind the white wall for forty-three years: a broad working hand, the knuckles swollen with rope and weather, and the ring finger ending in a smooth blunt roundness above the second knuckle, healed pale, healed shiny, healed a lifetime ago on a July morning in a lop off Harbour le Cou while their father stood at the wheel with his jaw set and Wade huddled in the stern with the guilt entering him like cold water, where it had stayed.

It's him, Wade said. That's Russell. That's my brother.

He did not cry then. He signed what she gave him to sign, in the corridor, on a clipboard, his name coming out steady, and she handed him a clear plastic bag with the watch in it, and he held it up to the window light and saw it was still going, the second hand sweeping around its yellowed face, wound by the motion of a wrist that had stopped, or wound by Russ himself on the morning of the ninth before he went out, a man putting on his father's watch to haul gear ahead of weather. Wade put it in his breast pocket. Outside, the morning had come up clear and hard, the sun off the harbour so bright it hurt, and from the steps of the health centre he could see the ferry at her berth being loaded for the return crossing, trucks filing into her side, and beyond the breakwater the strait lying blue and innocent to the horizon, the whole hundred miles of it, as though it had never held anything, as though it had never kept anything for six days and then given it back changed. There would be calls to make. Pearl. The funeral home. The minister in Rose Blanche, if there still was one. There was a house above a harbour twenty-five miles east along the coast road with a kitchen window full of breakwater, and a boat at a wharf painted up like new money, and all of it was his now, every plank of it, the whole inheritance he had once sold for twenty-two thousand dollars, returned to him with interest he could never pay. Wade stood on the steps in the cold sun with his father's watch ticking against his chest, and he let the gulls do the crying for a while, and then he went down to his truck and turned it east, toward home, the long way, the only way, with the water on his right hand all the way there.
