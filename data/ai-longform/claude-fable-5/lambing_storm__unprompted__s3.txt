The snow had started as rain at noon, and by the time Marian carried the last of the hay down the alley of the lambing shed it was coming sideways, fine and dry as flour, hissing against the metal roof in long pulses that made the ewes lift their heads. April twenty-sixth. The forsythia by the porch had bloomed two weeks ago and now stood somewhere out there in the white, holding its yellow under an inch of ice, and she thought of it the way she thought of many things lately—briefly, and then deliberately not.

Eli was at the far end of the shed bedding the jugs with straw, working fast because the cold had teeth in it already and would have more by dark. He was fifteen and had grown four inches since October, since the funeral, as if his body had decided that someone in the house needed to take up the space Dale had left and had appointed itself without consulting anyone. He still moved like a boy, though. He shook the straw out in big careless armfuls and half of it landed on his boots.

"Spread it to the corners," she said. "They'll lamb in the corners. They always do."

"I know where they lamb, Mom."

She let it go. He knew. He'd been in this shed every spring of his life, had been born himself in late March, two weeks early, while Dale paced the hospital hallway in Miles City with lanolin still in the creases of his knuckles. The nurses had teased him about it. He'd told the story every year on Eli's birthday, and this year nobody had told it, and the birthday had passed in a kind of careful silence, with a store-bought cake and the television on.

Marian counted the drop pen with her eyes the way she did forty times a day in lambing season. Eleven ewes left to lamb, by her book, and three of them bagged up tight and sunken in front of the hips, which meant tonight, which meant the storm would midwife them whether anyone liked it or not. Sheep lambed on falling barometers. Dale used to say a ewe would hold a lamb in for a week of fair weather just to drop it in the worst hour of the worst night God could arrange, and he said it every year, fondly, the way you complain about a relative you love.

The wind came up another notch around five. She heard it find the gap under the west door and start its low whistle, and she sent Eli to the house for the lantern, the headlamps, the thermos, and supper, whatever supper was. He came back twenty minutes later, snow to his knees, carrying a grocery sack and the news that the radio said power was already out in Rosebud County and crews wouldn't move until the wind died.

"They said gusts to fifty overnight," he said. He set the sack on the lid of the grain bin and pulled his hat off and his hair stood up with static. "They said up to two feet."

"They always say two feet."

"Sometimes there's two feet."

"Sometimes there is," she agreed.

They ate standing up, sandwiches she didn't taste, and the shed filled slowly with the particular noise of a storm working on a building—the tick and ping of the roof, the doors shouldering against their latches, the long exhale of the wind rounding the corner. Underneath it the ewes made their small sounds, the rustle and grunt, the murmur a ewe makes to a lamb that hasn't been born yet, that low nickering deep in the throat that they make to nothing, to the future, hours before there is anything to talk to. Marian had heard it ten thousand times and it still moved something in her, and tonight the something it moved was raw, so she chewed her sandwich and watched the drop pen.

The first ewe went down at six-forty, a four-year-old with a blue ear tag, number 212, a good mother with a history of twins. She pawed a nest into the straw in the corner, exactly as predicted, lay down, got up, lay down, and Marian watched her without going near, because the first rule was that you didn't fix what wasn't broken, and a ewe like 212 would do this better alone. Eli came and stood at her shoulder and watched too. He had his father's stillness when he watched livestock, the patience that had skipped Marian entirely—she watched like a worrier, cataloguing what could go wrong, and Dale had watched like a man at a ball game, easy, interested, ready to be pleased.

The water bag came, and then a nose and two white feet in the right arrangement, toes down, like a diver, and the ewe pushed three times and the lamb sluiced out into the straw, steaming, and the ewe was up and around and licking before Marian had taken a full breath.

"One," said Eli.

"She'll have another."

She did, eleven minutes later, smaller, also breathing, and Marian and Eli went in then with iodine for the navels and moved the family into a jug, Marian carrying the lambs low and backward so the ewe could keep her nose on them, walking that slow shuffle, Eli swinging the panel gate shut behind them. The ewe stamped at him and he grinned. The lambs found the teats with the blunt drunken insistence of all new lambs, tails going like flags, and for a minute the shed felt almost ordinary, almost like any of the twenty springs before this one.

The power went at eight-fifteen.

There was no flicker, no warning. The two bulbs over the alley simply ceased, and the heat lamp over the warming box died with them, and the dark came in like water filling a tank. One of the ewes startled and the rest caught it and there was a surge of bodies against the panels, and then Eli's headlamp snapped on, a cold white cone swinging across the pens, and Marian heard her own voice come out flat and calm, the voice she'd been manufacturing since October.

"Easy. Light the lantern. There's matches in the medicine cabinet."

The lantern was Dale's father's, a kerosene barn lantern older than anyone living, and when Eli got the wick going and hung it from the nail on the center post, the shed turned amber and close, the corners going soft and the shadows of the panel fences laddering up the walls. It was, Marian thought, probably what the shed had looked like in 1950. The thermometer by the door read twenty-six degrees and falling.

"The warming box," Eli said.

"I know."

The warming box was a plywood crate with a heat lamp, and a chilled lamb went into it the way a chilled lamb had gone into one for fifty years, and now it was just a crate. Without power the well pump was dead too, which meant the water in the buckets was all the water there would be, and the buckets would start freezing soon. She did the arithmetic she had been doing alone for six months, the arithmetic that used to be a conversation: hot water—the kitchen stove was propane, so they could heat water on the range, fill jugs, ferry them out. Chilled lambs could go in a sink of warm water if it came to that, or against skin inside a coat, the oldest technology there was. The generator. There was a generator, a portable one in the shop, and Dale had run it twice a year to keep it honest, and Marian could not remember, standing in the lantern light, whether anyone had run it since last spring.

"Go see if the generator starts," she said. "It's under the bench, the red one. Gas is in the can by the welder. Choke it, pull it, if it doesn't fire in ten pulls leave it and come back. Don't fight it in the cold."

"I know how to start a generator."

"Then go start it."

He went out into the wind, and the door cracked open and a tongue of snow licked six feet up the alley before he got it shut, and she stood in the lantern light listening to the storm and feeling the precise size and shape of being alone, which was something she had become a connoisseur of. There were varieties. There was alone in bed at two in the morning, and alone at the kitchen table with the checkbook and the bank's letter about the operating loan, and alone in the truck when a song came on, and now there was this one: alone in a lambing shed in a blizzard with eleven ewes, no, ten ewes left to lamb, while her son fought a cold engine a hundred feet away in the dark, and the particular cruelty of this one was that it was the alone she had specifically been promised she would never have to feel. Dale had said it the regular way people say it, not solemnly, just in passing, you'll never do a lambing alone, kid, and then in October his heart had quit in the cab of the swather in a field of second-cutting alfalfa, and the neighbor had seen the swather sitting still too long, and that had been the whole of it. Fifty-one years old. The cardiologist they never got to see had been booked out till December.

Through the wall, faintly, under the wind, she heard the generator turn over, catch, die. Turn over, catch, die. Then a longer silence, and she had begun composing what she would say to make it all right—it's fine, it's old gas, we'll do it the old way—when it caught and held, ragged at first and then smoothing, and the bulbs over the alley glowed up to half-strength, browned and trembling but alive, and the heat lamp over the warming box came on like a small red sun.

Eli came back in plastered white on his left side, breathing hard, trying not to look pleased and failing.

"Plug's fouled, I think," he said. "It'll run. I don't know for how long. Dad had a spare plug somewhere."

"Drawer under the bench, in the coffee can."

"That's where I'd have looked."

"I know it is."

He looked at her then, in the half-strength light, and something passed between them that was not quite spoken, an acknowledgment that they were each carrying a map of Dale around, the coffee can, the spare plug, the choke, the corners where ewes lamb, and that the maps mostly overlapped but not entirely, and that between the two of them they might, possibly, have the whole territory.

Two more ewes lambed before midnight without trouble, singles, big and vigorous, and the jugs filled, and the routine had them: iodine, strip the teats, check the suck, fresh water, the small bureaucracy of birth. Marian wrote in the lambing book with cold fingers, tag numbers, sexes, the shorthand she'd used for two decades. The book had Dale's handwriting in it up through last May and then hers, and the line where the handwriting changed was a thing she did not look at, the way you don't press a bruise.

At twelve-forty, the old ewe went down.

She was a six-year-old, 87, big-bodied, a ewe Dale had liked, and she had been restless since ten, and now she was stretched flat in the drop pen pushing hard and producing nothing. Marian watched her through two contractions, then three, and saw the water bag long gone and no feet, and felt the cold drop of certainty in her stomach.

"Catch her," she said. "Gently."

Eli moved into the pen with his arms wide, and the ewe lurched up and tried him twice and he turned her into the corner with his body the way his father used to, no grabbing, just geometry, and then he had her, knee over the neck, hands quiet on her shoulder, talking to her in a low voice, nonsense, the way you talk to a frightened animal, it's all right, it's all right, you're fine, dummy, you're fine. Marian stripped off her coat and rolled her right sleeve to the shoulder and the cold bit her bare arm like a dog. She washed in the bucket—the water already skinning over with ice, and the soap wouldn't lather—and slicked her hand and arm with lubricant from the bottle that lived in the medicine cabinet, and knelt at the back of the ewe in the trampled straw.

"Hold her now."

She went in slow, fingers in a cone, against the contraction and then with it, and read the lamb in the dark of the ewe the way you read anything by feel, and what she found was wrong in the familiar way: a head, big, coming alone, both forelegs back, the lamb's nose already pushing into the birth canal with its shoulders jammed at the pelvic brim like a man trying to dive through a window with his arms at his sides.

"Head back or legs back?" Eli asked. His voice was steady. He'd seen this. He'd watched his father do this, holding the ewe just like he was holding her now.

"Legs back. Both."

"Can you get them?"

"Have to push him back first."

It was the part that felt like a violence even though it wasn't, pressing the lamb's wet head back into the ewe against everything the ewe's body was trying to do, the contraction clamping her arm to the elbow, hard enough that she had to stop and breathe through it, forehead almost against the ewe's flank, the ewe groaning, Eli murmuring his nonsense, the lantern hissing, the wind hammering the west wall in long runs like surf. Between contractions she gained an inch, two, got the head back over the brim, traced down the slick curve of the lamb's neck to the shoulder, the knee, cupped the small sharp foot in her palm so it wouldn't tear the ewe, and brought it up and forward. One. Then back in for the other, deeper, her cheek against wet wool, her arm dead past the elbow, and the second leg came harder, and the ewe pushed and Marian's hand was crushed flat and she waited it out and then the leg came, and she lined it all up—nose, two feet, the diver—and said, "Pull with her, not against her," mostly to herself, and on the next contraction she drew the lamb out in one long slide and it landed in the straw, yellow with meconium, limp.

Not breathing.

She cleared the nose with two fingers, slung the lamb up by the back legs and swung it, once, twice, fluid stringing from its mouth, laid it down and rubbed its ribs hard with a twist of straw, rough, rougher, the way you'd rub a stain out of a carpet, and Eli had let the ewe go and was on his knees on the other side of the lamb, and she heard him say, "Come on," very quietly, "come on, come on," and she took the straw away and pinched the nostrils clear and tickled inside one nostril with a clean stem of straw, and the lamb sneezed.

It sneezed, and shuddered, and its ribcage hitched and caught a rhythm, ragged, then real, and it lifted its heavy wet head an inch off the straw and complained about everything, the cold, the light, the indignity, in a small cracked voice, and the ewe answered from three feet away with that low nicker, and Eli sat back on his heels and laughed once, a strange sharp sound, almost a cough, and wiped his face with the back of his wrist and left a streak of birth and straw across his cheek and didn't know it.

"There's another one in there," Marian said, because there was, she'd felt it, and because if she stopped moving she was going to feel the size of the last five minutes and she could not afford to. She washed and went back in, and the second lamb was small and right-side-up and came like a letter through a slot, breathing on its own before its hips were clear. Twins. Both ewe lambs. She wrote it in the book later in handwriting she barely recognized.

They got the family jugged and the big lamb, the hard one, was slow to stand—chilled from the long labor, its mouth cool when Marian put her finger in, which was the test, Dale's test, a warm mouth waits, a cold mouth doesn't—so it went under the heat lamp in the warming box while Eli held the ewe and Marian milked colostrum into a bottle, the thick yellow first milk coming in slow ropes, her hands so cold the milking warmed them. She tubed the lamb because it wouldn't suck, sliding the soft catheter down the left side of the throat, counting the length against its body the way you did, watching it swallow, four ounces of warmth straight into its stomach, and within twenty minutes it was up on its knuckles in the box, then up on its feet, hollering, and she carried it back to its mother and watched it slam into the udder like it had been doing this for years.

It was after two. The generator was still running. The snow had drifted halfway up the east door, and when Eli forced it open to check the gas, the world outside was not a world, just a moving whiteness with no top or bottom, the yard light invisible though it was forty yards away. He topped off the tank with his back to the wind and came in iced like a cake and stood over the heat lamp with his hands out, steaming.

"You should sleep some," Marian said. "Couch in the office. I'll wake you if anything starts."

"You sleep. I'll watch."

"Eli."

"Mom." He didn't say anything else. He just looked at her, and he was fifteen, and there were circles under his eyes and the streak still on his cheek, and his look said what they did not say in this family, which was: I am not going to the house, I am not leaving you out here, this is mine too. And she understood that to send him in now would be to take something from him that he was in the middle of building, something he was building specifically because of the hole in their lives, building it with straw and cold and a fouled spark plug, and that it would matter for the rest of his life whether she let him finish it.

"All right," she said. "We'll trade in shifts. You're first. Wake me at four."

She lay down on the straw bales along the south wall with a horse blanket over her and did not sleep, exactly. She drifted in the lantern light with her eyes half closed and listened to her son moving up and down the alley, checking jugs, breaking the ice on buckets with the heel of his hand, talking quietly to the ewes, and at some point she heard him in the far pen saying something in a low voice that was not nonsense, that had the rhythm of an actual telling, and she could not hear the words and did not try to, because she knew roughly what they would be, and who they were for, and that they were not for her. The wind filed away at the building. The lambs in the jugs made their small sounds. Somewhere in the dark above the rafters the storm went on manufacturing itself, indifferent, enormous, and under it the shed held its pocket of amber light and animal heat like a cupped hand holding a match.

Two more ewes lambed before dawn, one at four and one at five-thirty, both easy, and Eli handled the four o'clock himself before he woke her, had it jugged and iodined and was stripping the teats when she sat up, and he reported it to her in clipped proud sentences, single ram lamb, big, sucking, and she checked his work and found nothing to fix and told him so, and he shrugged like it was nothing, and carried the shrug carefully out of the room like a full cup.

The wind began to die with the light. By seven the snow was falling straight down, fat and slow now, almost gentle, the storm spending its last change, and by eight there were seams of blue tearing open in the west. Marian shut off the generator and the silence afterward was enormous, and into it, one by one, came the sounds it had been covering: lambs, ewes, the drip of melt already starting at the south eave, a meadowlark, impossibly, from the fence line, singing into two feet of snow as if it had filed a complaint and this was it.

They walked up to the house together through drifts that came over Eli's knees, the sky breaking open above them, the snow so bright it hurt. The power was still out. She lit the stove with a match and made eggs and they ate them at the kitchen table in their coats, too tired to talk, and the lambing book lay between them where she'd dropped it, open to the night's page, six lambs, no losses, the handwriting going ragged toward the bottom and then, in the last two entries, not hers. He'd written the four o'clock lamb in himself. Ram, single, lg, suck good, the same shorthand, the third hand in the book now, and she looked at it for a while and then closed the cover and put her hand flat on it, and Eli watched her do it and looked out the window at the white and yellow morning—because there it was, the forsythia, bowed under its ice and blooming anyway, blooming straight through it—and neither of them said anything, and nothing needed saying, and after a while she got up and poured them both more coffee, his half milk the way he still liked it, though she suspected that by next spring he would be drinking it black.
