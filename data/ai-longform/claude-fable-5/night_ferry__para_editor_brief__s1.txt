The boat left Aberdeen at seven, and by half past the city had thinned to a smear of sodium light along the water, the granite gone grey-orange and then gone altogether. Tom Sinclair stood at the stern rail with his bag still over his shoulder, as though he had not yet decided to be aboard. The wake unspooled behind them, pale and ropy, and the gulls that had followed the ship out of the harbour gave it up one by one and slid away sideways into the dark.

He had not been on a boat of any size in thirty years. He had told himself, in the queue of cars and lorries at the terminal, that this was nothing, twelve hours, a cabin with a bunk and a kettle, no different from a night in a cheap hotel that happened to be moving. But the smell had got into him the moment he walked up the ramp — diesel and rope and salt and the faint sweetness of paint — and it was not the smell of a hotel. It was the smell of Gamrie harbour on a Saturday morning, his father's boat in against the wall, the boxes coming up, and Davey already down on the deck at eleven years old, in his element, in everybody's way.

The policewoman who had phoned him on Tuesday had a Shetland voice, careful and lilting, and she had said the word *believe* several times. We believe him to be your brother. We believe he had been living in Lerwick for some time. A man had been found at the tideline below Gulberwick, she said, on the Saturday morning, by a woman walking her dog. He had been in the water perhaps a week. There was no wallet on him, no phone, but a man answering his description had been lodging in a house on King Harald Street under the name David Sinclair, and had not been seen since the previous Friday, and among the things in his room there was an address book, and in the address book, under T, there was Tom's name and a phone number two numbers out of date, which had taken them a few days to run down. She was sorry to be asking, she said. But they needed a formal identification, and the landlady — she stopped herself, and said only that it was better coming from family. He understood later what she had not said: that the sea and a week had done their work, and the landlady had looked and could not swear to it.

Was there anything, she had asked, any marks he could tell them about. He had stood in his kitchen in Cults with the kettle roaring behind him and tried to assemble his brother out of forty years' distance. He's three years older than me, he said. He'd be sixty-one now. Dark-haired, though I suppose — aye. About my height. There was a tattoo, a swallow, on the left forearm, he got it in Peterhead when he was seventeen, my father nearly put him through a wall for it. And there's a scar, he said, and then heard himself stop. A scar through his left eyebrow. The left, he was sure. From when he was a boy.

How did he come by it, she asked, just for the record, and Tom had said he didn't mind, it was that long ago, some accident on the shore.

The bar on B deck was half full when he went down. Lorry drivers mostly, men who knew the boat the way other men know a local, sitting with pints they barely touched and watching football with the sound off. A young couple with rucksacks were folded into each other in a corner booth. A man in an oilskin jacket the colour of egg yolk sat alone at the bar with a dram and a chaser, and when Tom ordered a whisky he turned and looked at him with frank northern appraisal and said, "Working, or going home?"

"Neither," Tom said. "Bit of business."

"Lerwick?"

"Aye."

"This time of year." The man shook his head slowly, as if Lerwick in November were a wound a man might choose to inflict on himself for obscure reasons. He himself was going up to a salmon farm in Yell, he said, three weeks on, one off, and the cages had to be checked whatever the weather, and the weather was promised dirty from Wednesday. He talked the way men talk on boats and in waiting rooms, without need of much answer, and Tom found it restful, the litany of cages and feed barges and a foreman from Stornoway who was a good enough lad but tight as a duck's arse with the overtime. Twice the man paused as if to give Tom room to declare himself — what business, what line, whose man are you — and twice Tom looked into his whisky and the man, with the tact of his kind, moved on.

Davey would have talked to him, Tom thought. Davey would have known the salmon farm, or known a man who'd worked it, or known the foreman's brother. Davey at seventeen, on the bus to Peterhead with a kitbag, turning at the door to say something to their mother that Tom had not heard from the top of the stair, and their father in the front room with his Bible open on his knee and the door shut, not coming out. In Gamrie in those days the Brethren held the high ground and the harbour both, and their father held the house, and Davey could not be held. That was the whole of it, Tom had decided at various points in his life, in various moods. Davey could not be held, and Tom could, and each of them had paid for his nature in the currency of the other's.

He took a second whisky up onto the open deck aft of the funnel, where there was a smoking shelter with a bolted-down bench and a hooded light, and stood out of the wind's main force with his collar up. The ship had a long slow roll on her now, out past the shelter of the land, and the sea was nothing, an absence, a blackness that disclosed itself only at the rail directly below, where the hull pushed the water away white. Far off on the port side a light occulted — Rattray Head, he thought, or further on than that already, he had lost his feel for the coast. There had been a time he could have named every light from Kinnaird to Duncansby the way other boys named footballers, because Davey could, and whatever Davey carried Tom had carried a copy of, smaller and less sure.

The scar. He had told the policewoman it was an accident on the shore, and so it had gone down in the record, and so it had gone down in the family too, fifty years deep, because Davey had said so first. They had been on the back of the east breakwater on a Sunday afternoon, which was itself a sin, the Sabbath being for the meeting and the house and nothing else, and they were throwing stones at a fish box that had drifted into the lee of the wall. Tom was eight. He could still feel the particular stone, flat and heavier on one side, a skimmer really, wrong for throwing overarm, and he had thrown it overarm anyway just as Davey stooped into the line of it to pick up a stone of his own. The sound it made. Davey went down on one knee with his hand to his face and the blood came through his fingers and dripped on the concrete, fat dark drops, and Tom stood with his arms at his sides and began, shamefully, to cry — for Davey, he told himself after, but really for himself, for what was coming, for their father's face and the belt behind the scullery door. And Davey got up. He got up with his eye already closing and blood to his wrist and he said, "I fell. You were never here. Go home the shore road and dinna run." And he had walked up to the house alone and stood in the kitchen and said he had fallen on the breakwater, on the Sabbath, and taken what came of that, which was plenty, and he had never once, in fifty years, in any anger, in any drink, said otherwise. The white seam through his left eyebrow. Tom's mark on him. The only thing of Tom's he had taken north.

A girl came out onto the deck with a cigarette she couldn't light, and Tom cupped his hands round her lighter for her without being asked, and she nodded her thanks and stood at the other end of the shelter, and they watched the dark together for the length of her smoke, companionable as strangers at a graveside, and then she went in.

He had booked a cabin, an inside one with no porthole, and he lay on the lower bunk in his shirt with the lights off and the boat working around him — long shudders as she came down off a sea, the gargle of water in pipes, somewhere a door that clapped and clapped until someone wedged it. Sleep stood off from him like the gulls had. He thought about the funeral, because there was no night for thirty years he had set out to think about Davey and not fetched up at the funeral. Their mother had died in the March, quick, a bleed in the brain, kinder than what she'd feared, and Tom had done everything, the undertaker, the flowers their father's meeting would have frowned at, the notices, and he had got a number for Davey off a man in Fraserburgh who'd had it off a man in Scrabster, and left messages at a harbour office in Kirkwall. And Davey had come. He had come into the kirk at Macduff twenty minutes late in a black suit borrowed or hired, too wide on him, and stood at the back, and at the graveside he had stood apart with his hands crossed in front of him like a man at a match, and at the hotel after he had taken a drink, and another, and by four o'clock he was telling a table of their mother's cousins a story about their father, an unkind story, a true story, the one about the wireless on a Sunday, and laughing in a way that asked the table to laugh, and the table not knowing where to look. And Tom had taken him by the elbow into the corridor by the toilets, the smell of carpet and air freshener, and said the thing. He had had thirty years to misremember it and had not managed to. You couldn't even bury her sober, he had said. Faither was right about you. Run away then. It's the only thing you ever finished.

Davey had looked at him. That was all. He had looked at him the way he'd looked up from the breakwater with his hand to his eye — surprised by the blow but not, somehow, by where it came from — and he had set his glass down on the windowsill very carefully, the way drunk men do when they decide to stop being drunk, and walked out through the fire door into the car park, and that was the last time Tom Sinclair had seen his brother in the flesh.

There had been the phone call, eight or nine years after. A pub somewhere, by the noise of it. Davey's voice was thicker but it was him, it was unmistakably him, and he hadn't said sorry and nor had Tom; he had talked for a while about a boat he'd been on out of Kirkwall, and a winter in Hammerfest of all places, and then with elaborate carelessness he had asked if Tom could see his way to a couple of hundred quid, just till a settlement came through, a wages thing, an agent who owed him. Tom had sent it, two hundred pounds, by transfer to an account whose number Davey had read out unsteadily, getting it wrong once. He never knew if it arrived. He had told himself for years that the money was the last thing between them and that the silence after it was Davey's choice, freely made, and on the bunk in the dark with the strait going by beneath him he allowed himself at last to find this account of things contemptible.

He must have slept, because the tannoy woke him — a recorded voice, mild as a minister, saying that breakfast was now being served on deck five, and that they would shortly be arriving in Lerwick, and that the local time was six fifty-five. He shaved at the steel basin, badly, in a swell that had eased to a long lazy heave, and went up.

The land, when he saw it, was low and black against a sky just beginning to come grey, and it resolved as the light grew into hills without a single tree, brown and bald and rained-on, with the lit windows of crofts scattered like dropped embers. The Bressay light was still turning as they came up the sound, its beam gone milky in the dawn. Lerwick gathered itself out of the gloom: oil tanks, a power station chimney, masts, the old waterfront houses standing with their feet in the sea the way he'd seen in photographs, grey gable after grey gable, and gulls again, the same gulls for all he knew, picking the boat up at the harbour mouth as if they'd waited all night.

She was at the bottom of the ramp with a paper held over her head against the drizzle, a young woman in uniform with red hair coming loose from under her hat, and she knew him without asking — he wondered what she had been told to look for, and supposed the answer was: a man looking like this. "Mr Sinclair. I'm Constable Moar. We spoke on the phone." Her hand was cold and firm. "Thanks for coming up. I ken it's no small thing." She had a car parked on the quay, and as she drove she talked a little, the same careful lilt, about the crossing and the weather coming on Wednesday — the whole town, it seemed, was watching Wednesday — and then, as the road bent up past a school, she said in a different register, "I'll tell you how it goes, so there's no surprises. We go to the Gilbert Bain, that's the hospital. There's a room. He'll be on a trolley, covered, and when you're ready they'll show you his face, and you take whatever time you need. You don't have to touch him, but you can. And then you just tell us what you can tell us. If you're no sure, you say you're no sure. That's no failure. There's other ways and means these days." She glanced at him. "And I should say it plain, because folk are sometimes no prepared. He was in the sea a while. He'll no look like he looked."

"He'd not look like he looked anyway," Tom said. "It's been thirty years."

She took that without comment, and he was grateful to her for it.

The hospital was small and low and smelled of every hospital. They went down a corridor past a porter's trolley of folded laundry, through a door that needed a code, into a room with a window of frosted glass and four chairs against the wall as if for a family, and in the middle of the room the trolley, and on the trolley the shape under the sheet, longer than he expected, because you forget, looking at a covered man, that men are tall. A technician in scrubs stood at the head, a heavyset man with reading glasses pushed up into his hair, and he said good morning very quietly, and Constable Moar said, "Whenever you're ready, Mr Sinclair. There's no clock on it," and Tom stood for a moment hearing the building hum, and then said, "Aye. Go on."

The technician folded the sheet back from the face with two hands, evenly, like a man turning down a bed.

He had told himself on the boat that he would know at once, one way or the other, that blood would speak. It did not. The face was a face the sea had had: swollen along the jaw and discoloured there, the skin abraded across one cheekbone where the tide had worked him over stones, the lips parted slightly, the hair — grey, nearly white, and Davey had been dark, but thirty years, thirty years — pushed back wet-looking from a high forehead. It was an old man's face. It was nobody's face and everybody's; it was the face of every man over sixty he had stood beside at a bar in Aberdeen; and then, for one vertiginous second, it was their father's face, exact, the brow and the deep eye sockets and the long upper lip, their father risen and travelled north and drowned, and Tom heard himself make a small sound, and Constable Moar shifted her weight beside him but said nothing.

He made himself stop looking at the whole and look at the parts. He looked at the left eyebrow.

It was there. A thin white seam, finer than he remembered, running aslant through the outer half of the brow where no hair grew, an old, old mark, healed before half the people in this building were born. The sea had taken the colour out of everything else but it could not take the colour out of that, because it had never had any; it had been white on the breakwater fifty years ago under the blood, white at the kirk door every Sunday after, white across the grave in Macduff. Tom put out his hand, and then asked, belatedly, with a look, and the constable nodded, and he laid two fingers against his brother's eyebrow, very lightly, on the scar, the way you might touch wet paint to see was it dry. The cold of him came up through Tom's fingers and went all the way in.

"That's Davey," he said. "That's my brother."

"You're sure," she said, gently, because she had to.

"I did that," Tom said, and his voice came out level, which surprised him. "The scar. I did that when I was eight years old. I threw a stone on the shore and I caught him, and he told them he fell, so I'd not get the belt. He told everybody he fell. He told it that long he likely believed it." He took his fingers away. "I'm sure."

There was paper after, of course, there is always paper; a small office with a radiator ticking, forms in duplicate, his signature going strange to him the third time he wrote it. Constable Moar brought tea in a machine cup and then, setting a clear plastic bag on the desk, said, "These were in his room. They'll be released to you properly once the Fiscal's done, but you're allowed to look." A wristwatch with a cracked face, stopped, on a cheap expanding strap. A tobacco tin, the paint worn through to the steel at the corners. An address book, red, swollen with damp at some point in its life and dried wavy. And a photograph, unbagged separately, in a stiff card sleeve from a chemist's that no longer existed: two boys on a harbour wall, the small one squinting, the bigger one with his arm out along the small one's shoulders not quite touching, the way boys do, and behind them the boats and the bright forgotten water, and on the back, in their mother's round hand, *David and Thomas, Gamrie, July*. No year. She had not thought a year would be needed.

"He had that propped on the windowsill," the constable said. "His landlady says. Where he'd see it from the bed."

The boat south did not leave until seven that evening. Constable Moar offered to run him out anywhere he liked, and he said no, thank you, he'd walk, and she gave him her card and the undertaker's number and shook his hand again at the hospital door, and then he was alone in Lerwick at half past ten in the morning with nine hours and the rest of his life in front of him. The drizzle had stopped. The light over the harbour was the colour of pewter going bright, and the wind off the water was clean enough to hurt.

He walked down through the narrow flagstoned street where the shops were opening, and out along the front, past the small boats lying against their fenders, until the town gave out and there was just the road and the sound and the low brown island across the water with the lighthouse on its far cliff. He stood at the rail above the tide for a long time. Below him the water moved among the weeded stones with a sound he had known before he had known anything, the lifted-and-set-down sound, patient, unfinished. Somewhere along this coast, on a Friday night, for reasons that would now never be anybody's business, Davey had gone into that sound and the sound had kept him a week and then laid him on the sand at Gulberwick for a woman and a dog to find, sixty miles of open sea from the breakwater where he had knelt in his own blood at eleven years old and thought, first of anything, before the pain even, of getting his brother home clear.

Tom took the photograph out of his inside pocket — she had let him keep it, the constable, the Fiscal could chase her for it — and held it in both hands against the wind, and looked at the two of them, and then out at the water, and then at the two of them again. Tomorrow there would be the undertaker, and the question of where, and he knew the answer to where already, knew it as plainly as he knew his own name: not Gamrie, not their father's ground. Here. He had run this far for a reason; let him lie where he'd run to. But that was tomorrow. For now Tom put the photograph away, buttoned his coat to the throat, and began to walk north along the shore road toward King Harald Street, to find the house, and the window, and the landlady, and to ask her — slowly, in no hurry at all, with the whole grey day given over to it — what his brother had been like.
