The leak had announced itself the way leaks always did on the old place, which was to say modestly, almost apologetically: a green seam in the dry middle of the orchard, a stripe of cheatgrass and mallow gone lush where nothing had been watered since April. Wade saw it from the truck at seven in the morning and knew before he got out what it meant, and how the day would go, and roughly how much it would cost him, and he sat for a minute with the engine ticking and his coffee going cold in the cupholder before he let himself open the door.

His brother was already walking the line with a shovel over his shoulder like a man in a mural. Nathan had come up from Sacramento two days ago for what he kept calling a visit, though he'd brought work gloves and his own boots, and he had been out of the house this morning before Wade finished his eggs. He'd found the wet spot too. He was standing in it now, sneakers — he'd left the boots on the porch — going dark at the toes, looking down at the saturated ground with an expression of tenderness that irritated Wade more than he could have explained.

"Mainline," Nathan said, when Wade came up.

"I know what it is."

"Right here, looks like. Maybe a little south."

"It's never where it looks like." Wade took the shovel from him without asking and pushed it into the soft ground, and it went in too easily, the way a knife goes into rotten fruit. Eighteen inches down the soil was the color of coffee grounds and smelled of cold iron. He widened the hole, working in a circle, and Nathan went back to the truck for the other shovel and the digging bar, and for a while neither of them said anything, which was the longest peace they'd had since Thursday.

The mainline was their father's, laid in 1971, the year Nathan was born, which their father had liked to mention as though the two facts had something to do with each other. Six-inch PVC, schedule 40, run down the center avenue of the orchard from the pump at the canal turnout, with risers every other row feeding the lateral lines. It had been patched so many times that an aerial photograph of the repairs, if such a thing could be taken, would have looked like a medical chart of an old man's arteries. Wade had personally cut into this pipe perhaps forty times in his life. He knew its sound when it was full, a faint subterranean hum you could feel through your boot soles if you stood still, and he knew the particular silence of it broken.

They found the break a little after eight, four feet south of where Nathan had guessed, which Wade did not point out, because Nathan would only have said four feet, Wade, come on. The pipe had cracked along the top in a lightning-bolt line maybe ten inches long, and the water — they'd run the pump only six hours, two nights ago, the first irrigation of the season for the late blocks — had carved out a cavity under the line big as a washtub, so the pipe hung unsupported across it like a bridge over a small private canyon.

"Gopher," Nathan said.

"Gopher started it. Then the ground gave and the pipe sagged and snapped. Same as the one by the shed in — " Wade stopped, because he'd been going to say in '09, when Dad was still climbing down in the trenches himself, and he didn't want to bring their father into the hole with them this early in the day. "Same as before," he said.

The peaches around them were Fay Elbertas, sixty trees to the row, planted the same year as the pipe and therefore, by orchard arithmetic, fifteen years past the age at which a sensible man would have pushed them out and replanted. They had not been sensible men, any of them. The trees were big and dark and gnarled, scaffold limbs thick as a person, the kind of trees you couldn't buy anymore, couldn't even really photograph, because the new plantings were all pedestrian-height things on dwarfing stock, ladder-free, designed for crews and machines. These trees had been pruned by hand by three generations of one family and they looked like it: each one slightly eccentric, slightly defiant, holding its fruit high. The fruit was sizing now, hard green-gold drops the size of walnuts, June fruit, the orchard's last argument on its own behalf.

"You'll need a coupling," Nathan said, squatting at the edge of the trench. "Six-inch repair coupling, slip fix maybe, glue, primer. We got any of that in the shop?"

"We," Wade said.

"Fine. Do you have any of that in the shop."

"I have a slip fix that's been sitting on the shelf since Obama. The gaskets in those things dry out. I'll have to go in to Pearson's."

"I'll go."

"You don't know what to ask for."

"I just asked for it. Out loud. You heard me ask for it."

Wade climbed out of the trench, knees announcing themselves, and stood looking down at the broken pipe and the cavity under it, the wet walls already drying pale at the edges where the morning was getting into them. It was going to be a hot one. The forecast said a hundred and one by four o'clock, and the air had that early stillness to it, the held breath the valley took before a hot day, doves muttering somewhere in the row middles and the highway a half mile off making its long single sound.

"We'll both go," he said. "I'm not standing here watching a hole."

In the truck Nathan put his sneakers on the dash, which he had been doing since he was nine years old, and which their father had never once succeeded in stopping, and looked out the window at the rows going by, the strobe of them, light and shadow, light and shadow.

"How many acres is the leak block again?" he said.

"Twelve."

"And you've got, what, forty bearing?"

"Thirty-eight. The Elbertas, the O'Henrys, the six acres of Flames out front. Plus the ten we pulled in '21 that's just sitting in star thistle."

"And the offer was on all of it. The whole eighty."

Wade drove for a while. The road into town ran between an almond orchard on one side, young trees in laser-straight rows with drip line stapled down each berm like a hem, and a field of nothing on the other, disced ground, fallowed, the dust off it sifting up behind a quail. He had told Nathan about the offer on Thursday night, two beers in, at the kitchen table, with the letter from Carraway Land & Orchard Co. lying between them where Lorene had set it, and he had been regretting it more or less continuously ever since.

"Forty-one hundred an acre is below market," Nathan said. "Even for a starting number. Even if you were going to do it, which — "

"It's not below market for a place with a sixty-year-old mainline and trees that ought to be firewood. Carraway doesn't want the trees, Nate. They want the dirt and the water account. They'll push every tree on the place inside of a month and have almonds in by spring, and they'll put in their own pipe, and the only thing on that eighty acres they're actually paying for is the district water and the well."

"Then it's worth more, not less. The water alone — "

"You've been reading articles."

"I've been reading articles, yes. That's allowed. People read articles."

Pearson's Irrigation sat at the edge of town between a tire shop and a church that had been a feed store, and inside it smelled the way it had smelled since the brothers were children, PVC solvent and rubber and dust, a smell Wade found he could not separate from the feeling of something on the place being broken. Dewey Pearson was dead eleven years now; his daughter Marisol ran the counter, and she had Wade's account up on the screen before he reached it.

"Mainline again?" she said.

"Mainline again."

"Six-inch?"

"Slip fix if you've got it. And a quart of the heavy-body and a primer."

"You'll want the gray glue for six-inch, the regular's not rated." She was already moving down the aisle. "You should be flattered, you know. Half my customers anymore, it's all drip fittings and filters. You're the last man in this county still busting open big pipe like it's 1985." Then she saw Nathan and her face did the thing local faces did, the recalibration. "Nate Lindqvist. Lord. I heard you were in government now."

"Building department. It's barely government."

"He inspects other people's foundations," Wade said. "While his own sinks."

It came out meaner than he'd meant, and there was a beat in the dusty light of the aisle where all three of them heard it. Marisol laughed it off and rang them up, and gave them, the way she always did, the price she would have given their father, and on the way out she said, the way everyone in town now said, "Sorry again about Tom. He was a good one," and the brothers said thank you in unison, the words long since worn smooth, and got back in the truck.

They didn't talk on the way back. The heat was up now, the asphalt going liquid at the far end of the road's perspective, and Nathan had his arm out the window, hand riding the air the way he used to as a kid, and Wade thought, as he had been thinking for four months, about the morning in February he'd found their father in the shop, sitting on the stool by the bench vise with a carburetor in pieces in front of him, perfectly orderly, every screw in a coffee can lid, and the old man simply not in himself anymore, gone out of his body so quietly that the radio was still playing. Eighty-six years old and he'd died in the middle of fixing something, which everyone at the funeral agreed was right, was fitting, was just like Tom, and which Wade had not been able to stop turning over since, because it seemed to him less like a fitting end than like a sentence handed down. This is what you get. You get to die mid-repair. The repairs do not end. The repairs win.

Back at the trench he laid out the fittings on a feed sack and they dug. There was no way around the digging; you had to expose three feet of clean pipe either side of the break, undercut beneath it so you could work the saw and the coupling all the way around, square up the walls so the trench didn't keep shedding dirt onto the joint while the glue was wet. It was slow and it was hot and it was, Wade had to admit somewhere underneath his sweat, easier with two. Nathan was fifty-three and soft through the middle from his city job, but the work was still in his body somewhere, the old economy of it; he didn't fight the shovel, he let it fall, he carried dirt low. By eleven they had the pipe lying clean and exposed in a neat rectangular trench like something at an archaeological dig, which is what Nathan said it looked like, and Wade said, "It is one."

"You're in a mood."

"I'm in a trench."

"You've been in a mood since Thursday. Since before Thursday. You've been in a mood since February, Wade, and I understand that, but I'm trying to have one conversation with you about the rest of our lives and you keep handing me one-liners."

Wade set the hacksaw against the pipe, marked his cut with a pencil from behind his ear, and didn't answer. The saw made its small busy sound in the big quiet. Water still standing in the line dribbled out of the cut, surprisingly cold, smelling of the canal, of algae and snowmelt that had started the year in the Sierra and come down two hundred miles of concrete to die in this hole. He made the second cut and lifted out the cracked section and held it up, and the two of them looked at the lightning-bolt fracture, and at the inside of the pipe, which was furred with fifty years of mineral scale, the bore narrowed like an artery, like the chart again, and Nathan reached out and took it and turned it in his hands.

"Dad glued this," he said.

"Dad glued every inch of it. Him and Uncle Ray, over one summer. He told me they did the whole half mile in nine days and Ray got so high off the primer fumes he sang Marty Robbins all the way down the last hundred feet."

Nathan laughed, and then his laugh did the thing laughs were doing that year, ran out of road and turned into something else, and he set the piece of pipe on the trench edge gently, like it might bruise.

"This is what I'm talking about," he said. "This. Right here. You can't put a number on this."

"Carraway put a number on it. Three hundred twenty-eight thousand dollars. That's the number on it."

"On the dirt. Not on this."

"Nate." Wade sat back on his heels. The sun was straight overhead now, the shade pulled in tight under the trees like an animal hiding from the heat, and he could feel his shirt stuck to him in a map of his own back. "Let me tell you about this. This is the third mainline break since Dad died. This is the second pump rebuild in four years, eleven grand. This is a packing shed that won't take Fay Elbertas anymore because nobody east of here has heard of a peach you can't ship green. Last August I left forty tons on the trees because the picking would've cost more than the check. I stood out there in the dark — you can ask Lorene — I stood out in the rows at two in the morning listening to fruit hit the ground. You ever heard that? A whole orchard dropping? It sounds like rain. It sounds exactly like rain, which is the one thing it never does anymore, and I stood there and I thought, my father is eighty-six years old and asleep in that house dreaming this place is saving us, and it is eating us, it has been eating us for twenty years, and I am the only one awake who knows it."

Nathan was quiet for a moment. A jay went over, dragging its harsh sound behind it.

"You never said any of that on the phone," he said.

"What was the phone going to do."

"I would have come up."

"You came up. You came up Thanksgiving and you walked the rows with him and the two of you stood out there pointing at things, and he came in glowing, Nate, glowing, telling Lorene that you said the Elbertas had one more good decade in them. One more good decade. You inspect buildings. You red-tag things for a living. You knew better."

"He was eighty-six."

"That's right. So you told him a story. And now he's gone and you want me to live in it."

Nathan stood up out of the trench, which took him two tries, and walked off a little way down the row, and stood with his back to his brother, hands on his hips, head down, in the exact posture their father had assumed maybe ten thousand times in that orchard, the posture of a man consulting the ground, and Wade looked at his brother's back and felt the anger go out of him the way water went out of a cut line, in a rush and then a dribble and then nothing, leaving him empty and cool.

They primered and glued the coupling on the north end in silence, because that part of the job allowed no conversation anyway — heavy-body cement set in seconds in that heat, you swabbed both surfaces and drove the coupling home in one motion and gave it the quarter turn and held it against the push-back, counting, and there was a kind of mercy in that, in a task that took both their hands at once and all their attention. Nathan held the pipe in alignment while Wade seated the fitting. Their forearms were touching. The glue smell rose up out of the trench, sweet and savage, the smell of every repair the place had ever demanded of them.

"Hold," Wade said.

"I'm holding."

"Thirty more seconds."

"You know what Dad used to say. He used to say a glue joint sets in the time it takes a Lindqvist to forgive somebody, which is to say keep holding."

"He never said that."

"He said it to me."

"He made things up for you. You were the one who left. He had to keep you interested."

The slip-fix coupling went on the south side, the kind with the expanding sleeve you could telescope out to bridge the gap, and when it was glued and extended and seated they sat on the trench lip with their feet hanging into it like boys on a dock and ate the lunch Lorene had sent out, which she had packed, Wade noticed, with the precision of a woman who knew her husband and her brother-in-law would otherwise have worked through and grown vicious by three. Cold chicken, white bread, a thermos of sweet tea, peaches — last summer's, canned, in a jar with a screw lid and a fork rubber-banded to the side. Nathan opened the jar and the smell came up, August in a jar, sun and sugar, and he ate one whole half with the fork and closed his eyes.

"That's a Fay," he said.

"That's a Fay."

"You can't buy that. You understand that, right? Not at any store in Sacramento, not at the fancy markets, nowhere. That doesn't exist anymore except in this jar and on those trees."

"I know what it is, Nate. I grew it."

"Then how do you sit here and — " Nathan stopped himself, visibly, took a breath, started over in a lower gear. "I'm not trying to fight you. I'm trying to figure out how you carry both things. Because you do. I watched you this morning. You touched that broken pipe like it was Dad's hand."

Wade screwed the lid back on the thermos with great care.

"You carry both things because there's no truck big enough to set one of them down in," he said finally. "That's how. You want to know what I want? Honestly? I want somebody to tell me I'm allowed. That's all. Dad never once in his life told me I was allowed to stop. Right up to February he was talking about replanting the back ten. And now he can't ever tell me, and you come up here with your sneakers on the dash telling me I can't either, and so I'm fifty-eight years old asking permission from a peach orchard. From trees, Nathan. I lie awake asking trees."

Nathan looked at the jar in his hands.

"I can't give you permission," he said. "Half of it's mine. That's not the same as permission."

"No. It isn't."

"But I'll tell you what I can't do. I can't drive up here next June and see almonds. I can't do it. I'll get to the corner by the canal and I'll see drip tape and whips on a wire and I will not be able to find the place where my life happened. It'll be gone like it was never — and I know that's romance, I know you pay the bills and I show up twice a year and eat the peaches. I know what I am in this. But Wade. There has to be something between glowing and gone. Lease it. Pull the Elbertas ourselves and put the twelve acres in something that pays. Sell the back forty and keep the front. Sell all of it but the house and ten acres and the old trees and let me — I've got my pension coming in two years. Let me come back and lose money on ten acres of peaches like a fool. Like our people. There has to be something between."

The heat had crested. The orchard stood in it without moving, every leaf at rest, the fruit hanging in its thousands, sizing by some increment too slow to see, taking the water the broken line had failed to bring it out of whatever the deep ground still held. Far off the highway went on with its sound. Wade looked down the avenue their father and Ray had trenched in nine days in 1971, half a mile of it, and he found that the picture in his head, the one that had been sitting there since Thursday like a stone — bare disced dirt, the trees in burn piles — had shifted slightly, had let in, at one edge, a small stubborn green corner. Ten acres. The house. The old trees. A fool brother in actual boots.

"The glue's had two hours," is what he said. "Let's pressure it up before we get sentimental and forget to check our own work."

They left the trench open — you always left it open overnight, their father's law, let the joint prove itself before you bury it — and drove to the pump stand at the canal, and Wade primed the pump and threw the switch, and the motor wound up and the old hum came down the half mile of pipe, and the two of them walked back along the avenue in the long light, six o'clock light now, the heat finally tipping over, swallows out, the shadows of the trees laid clear across the row middles like rungs of a ladder going all the way to the end of the place. At the trench they stood one on each side and watched the repair. The pipe filled. They could hear it filling, the air spitting out of the riser two rows up, and then the line went quiet and taut and full, and the joints held, both of them, dry as bone, the new white couplings absurdly bright against the old scaled pipe, like grafts, like the clean wood you set into an old tree to make it bear new.

"Holding," Nathan said.

"Give it the night."

"It's holding, Wade."

"I said give it the night."

But he was smiling, a little, down into the trench, and the water moved under their feet through their father's pipe into their father's trees, and neither brother moved to go in, though supper would be on, though nothing was settled, though the letter from Carraway still lay on the kitchen table under the salt shaker where Lorene had weighted it against the evening breeze. They stood in the cooling rows while the risers down the block came up one by one, each with its small rush and gargle, and the dry ground began everywhere around them its old work of taking the water in, which made a sound, if you stood still enough, like something drinking, like something deciding to live another year, and they stood still enough.
