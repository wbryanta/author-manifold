The snow had started as rain, which was worse, because it soaked the ground first and then froze it, and by the time the flakes came down fat and sideways the whole world had a shell on it like something fired in a kiln. Marian stood in the doorway of the lambing shed at four in the afternoon with the light already failing and counted the ewes in the close pen one more time, though she knew the number. Eleven heavy. Two of them pawing at the straw and lying down and getting up again in that restless circling way that meant tonight, and one of those was the old Cheviot cross with the bad hip who had needed help every year of her life, and whom Tom had always called, with a tenderness he never extended to people, his difficult woman.

The wind came around the corner of the shed and pushed at her like a hand.

"Mum." Davey was behind her, fifteen years old and grown four inches since October, since the funeral, as if his body had decided to do its mourning in bone. He had the heat lamps in his arms, the cords coiled over his shoulder. "Where do you want these."

"Far pens. The jugs along the north wall." She stepped aside to let him through. He smelled of diesel and wet wool, and under that, faintly, of the cigarettes he thought she didn't know about, and she said nothing about it, the way she had said nothing for six months, because there was a ledger somewhere in which she owed him more than she could pay and the least she could do was not collect on small debts.

The shed was long and low, built by Tom's grandfather, rebuilt in pieces by every man since, so that the south wall was stone and the north wall was block and the roof was three different ages of corrugated steel that drummed under the snow in three different voices. The ewes' breath rose in the cold air and hung there. The whole place smelled of lanolin and ammonia and the sweet dust of hay, and it was the smell, more than anything, more than the bed or the chair or the coat still hanging in the back hall, that she could not be in without him. Twenty-six lambings in this shed. She had done every one of them beside him and she knew the work as well as he had, better in some ways — her hands were smaller, she was the one who went in when a lamb came wrong — but knowing the work was not the same as carrying it, and she was learning the difference this April, night by night, the way you learn cold water by standing in it.

"Forecast says it goes till morning," Davey said from the north wall. He was kneeling, wiring the heat lamp to the pen rail with a length of baler twine, doing it the way Tom had done it, the same wrap and tuck, and she had to look away.

"Then we'll be here till morning."

"I've got school."

"Not tomorrow you don't. Nobody's driving anywhere tomorrow."

He didn't answer, but she saw the set of his shoulders ease a little, and she understood, with the small bitter clarity that grief had given her about everything, that he was relieved, and that the relief shamed him. He had stopped wanting to be at school. His friends didn't know how to talk to him and so they had stopped talking to him, and he came home every day at four and put on his coveralls without being asked and went out to the stock, and some part of her knew she should be worried about that, should be pushing him back toward the world, and some other part of her, the tired animal part, was simply grateful for his hands.

The Cheviot cross went down at half past five and stayed down, straining. Marian climbed into the pen and knelt behind her in the straw. The ewe's flank heaved. A water bag had come and broken and there was one yellow forefoot showing and nothing else, no second foot, no nose, and she felt the old cold drop in her stomach that twenty-six years had never cured her of.

"Davey. Bucket and the gel. And bring the torch, the light's no good back here."

He came at a run with the bucket of warm water sloshing, and he held the torch steady while she stripped off her coat and pushed her sleeve up past the elbow and soaped her arm. The ewe groaned. Snow hissed against the steel roof above them like sand thrown by the handful.

"Hold her head. Talk to her."

"Saying what?"

"Anything. Doesn't matter. She just wants the voice."

He took the ewe's head in both hands and bent over her and began to talk, low, and Marian couldn't hear the words under the wind, but she heard the tone, and the tone was Tom's, the exact pitch of it, gentle and unhurried, *now then, now then, you're all right*, and her eyes stung and she blinked it back because she had her hand inside a sheep and there was a lamb in there with its head turned back along its body and crying about anything was a luxury for people who weren't needed.

She found the curve of the neck, followed it, got two fingers into the angle of the jaw. The ewe strained against her and she waited it out, hand still, riding the contraction like a swimmer riding a wave, then drew the head around slow, slow, cupping the muzzle in her palm so the teeth wouldn't tear, and felt it come into line, felt the second foot where it had been folded back at the knee, hooked it forward, and then it was all there, the diver's posture, nose on knees, and she pulled with the next strain and the lamb came out in a slick rush of fluid and membrane into the straw, yellow with staining, limp.

"Is it—" Davey started.

She had it up by the back legs already, swinging it, then down, clearing the mouth with two fingers, a stalk of straw up the nostril, and the lamb sneezed and shuddered and drew a breath that rattled and then another that didn't, and began, weakly, to complain about the world it had arrived in.

"There," she said. "There." She laid it at the ewe's head and the old ewe turned and began to lick, that hard rhythmic nudging lick, chuckling deep in her throat, the sound a ewe makes only at this and never any other time in her life, and Davey sat back on his heels in the straw and laughed, one short surprised bark of a laugh, the first she'd heard out of him since November.

"There's another in there," Marian said. "Give her a minute."

The second came easily, the way seconds often did, as if the first had argued the case for both of them. Twins, both ewe lambs, both up and wobbling within the half hour, and Marian penned the three of them in a jug with the heat lamp glowing red above and stood watching the lambs butt at the udder, and felt, for one clean moment, nothing but the work. It was the only place grief couldn't follow her all the way in — the last few inches of the work, where there was no room for anything but the thing itself.

The power went at seven.

It went the way it always went, no warning, the heat lamps dying all together and the one bulb over the door dying with them, and the shed dropping into a darkness that was total for three seconds and then resolved, as her eyes adjusted, into the gray rectangle of the doorway full of flying snow. A ewe bleated. Down the line of pens the lambs, suddenly cold, began to cry.

"Davey?"

"I'm here." His torch came on, the beam swinging.

"Generator's in the machine shed. You know how—" She stopped. He knew how. Tom had shown him last spring, the two of them out there an entire Saturday, the boy thirteen then and sulking about it, wanting to be anywhere else, and Tom patient as ground water: *you'll want to know this someday and someday doesn't send a letter first.*

"I know how," Davey said.

"Take the big torch. And your hat, don't argue."

He was gone twenty minutes. She moved down the pens in the dark with the small torch in her teeth, checking the lambs born that afternoon, tucking them against their mothers' flanks, and the wind worked at the north wall like something patient, finding every gap in the block, and she thought about the boy out there crossing the yard in a whiteout and made herself stop thinking about it. The yard was forty meters. He knew the yard blind. He had grown up in it. But she had learned this year that the worst thing did not ask permission, that it could come on a clear dry afternoon in October on a road Tom had driven ten thousand times, and since then her mind ran ahead of her everywhere like a dog off the lead, finding disasters, bringing them back, laying them at her feet.

Then she heard the generator catch, cough, catch again and hold, its drone threading under the wind, and the heat lamps bloomed red down the north wall one after another, and the bulb over the door came on, and a minute later Davey came through the doorway plastered white down his whole front side, beating his hat against his thigh, and she turned away so he wouldn't see her face.

"Drinks like a horse," he said. "There's maybe six hours of fuel in it. There's the can in the shed but after that we're into what's in the truck."

"Six hours gets us to one in the morning."

"I'll go out and top it at midnight, then."

"We'll see."

"Mum. I'll go out and top it at midnight."

She looked at him. The torch light came up from below and made him older, made hollows under his cheekbones, and she could see the man he was going to be, was being made into right now, this winter, this night, faster than she would have chosen. "All right," she said.

They ate standing up at nine, leaning against the gate of an empty pen — flask tea, cheese sandwiches she'd made that morning when the forecast turned, a sleeve of biscuits Davey produced from somewhere in his coveralls. Two more ewes had lambed in the meantime, both clean, no help wanted, and the shed had settled into the deep hum of a night going mostly right: the generator, the wind, the irregular percussion of snow sliding off the steel in sheets, the lambs' thin voices and the ewes answering, each pair finding each other by sound in the red half-dark.

"Dad used to say the storm brings them," Davey said. "The pressure dropping. Said you could lamb half the flock in one bad night."

"He did say that."

"Is it true?"

"I don't know. He believed it. That was usually enough to make things true around here." She heard how it came out and softened it. "It might be true. Old shepherds say it. The vet says it's nonsense. Eleven heavy ewes and a blizzard, we'll have our own data by morning."

Davey almost smiled. Then he was quiet for a while, picking the crust off his sandwich the way he had done since he was four, and she waited, because she had learned that his silences had shapes, and this one had the shape of something coming.

"I keep doing things the way he did them," he said finally. He didn't look at her. He looked at the heat lamps. "Like the twine on the lamps. I don't even decide to. My hands just do it. And then I notice and it's like—" He stopped.

"Like he's standing behind you."

"Like I'm wearing his hands." He said it fast, embarrassed by it, and ducked his head, and she felt the words go into her like cold air into a lung.

"I talk to him," she said. It was the first time she had told anyone. "Out here, mostly. Not in the house. In the house it feels mad. Out here it just feels like working."

"What do you say?"

"Tonight? Tonight I told him his difficult woman needed turning again and his daughter did it without him." She had meant it lightly and it didn't land lightly; it landed in the middle of the shed and sat there, and Davey's jaw worked, and the wind leaned hard on the north wall, and one of the heavy ewes down the line got up, turned twice, and lay down with a grunt like a sack dropped.

"I should have gone with him that day," Davey said. "He asked me. To the sale. I said I had stuff on. I didn't have stuff on, I just didn't want to sit there all afternoon."

"Davey."

"If I'd been in the truck maybe he drives different. Slower. Maybe we leave ten minutes later because I'm faffing about finding my boots. Ten minutes and the deer's not on the road, or it is and we're not."

"Or you're in the truck," she said, and her voice came out harder than she intended, and she let it, because some things should be said hard. "You're in the passenger seat where it came over. That's the other version, Davey. There's a hundred versions and you don't get to pick which one you torture yourself with and pretend it's the truth. I've run them all. Every night from October to about February I ran them all. You know where it gets you? It gets you to four in the morning with your heart going like a hare and the same world in the morning that was there the night before."

He was crying, she realized. Standing straight up with his sandwich in his hand, not hiding it, tears just coming down his face in the red light, and he was fifteen and four inches taller than her and she reached up and put her hand on the back of his neck the way she had when he was small, and he let her, and the two of them stood like that in the smell of lanolin and ammonia and sweet hay while outside the storm did what storms do.

The Suffolk ewe went into trouble at half eleven.

She was a first-timer, a gimmer, big-bodied and carrying big, and Marian had been watching her sidelong for an hour — the lying down, the getting up, the staring at her own flank like it had insulted her — and when the water bag came and an hour passed and nothing followed it, Marian was already soaping her arm.

"Torch," she said, and Davey was there.

What she found inside stopped her breath for a second. Heads. Two of them, or a head and something else, a jumble — feet, too many feet, and no way at first to know which feet belonged to which lamb, everything pressed together in the dark like a drawer of spoons. Twins coming at once, both trying the door together, and the gimmer too tight and too tired and too young to do anything but strain against the jam and make it worse.

"What is it?" Davey said.

"Two at once. Tangled." She closed her eyes. It helped, sometimes, to take the eyes out of it, to be only the hand. Tom had taught her that her first year, kneeling behind her in the straw with his hand light on her shoulder: *stop looking, Mar, you can't see anything anyway, the hand knows more than you do, let it read.* She let it read. A head — follow the neck down — shoulder — leg — that leg, yes, and that one, both the same lamb, that one's whole, that one's first. The other head she pushed, gently, back into the dark, back into the room behind the door, and the gimmer screamed and Davey flinched and held her anyway, talking, talking, his father's pitch, *now then, now then.*

"You're pushing one back?"

"One at a time. There's only room for one at a time." Her arm was inside to the elbow and the contractions came down on it like a gate swung shut, again, again, and she waited each one out and worked in the spaces between. Sweat ran into her eyes. The generator droned. Somewhere down the line another ewe was lambing unattended, she could hear it, the grunt and gush and then, thank God, the chuckle, mothering it up on her own, good girl, good girl, one less.

The first lamb came hard, big-headed, the gimmer crying out, and then it was out and breathing and Davey had it without being told, swinging it, clearing it, laying it by her head, and Marian was already back inside for the second, which had turned in the meantime, of course it had, and presented her a tail.

"Breech," she said. "Get the gel. And get ready to be quick."

Backwards lambs drowned. That was the whole of it. The cord pinched or broke while the head was still inside, and the lamb breathed where there was nothing to breathe, and you had from the moment the hips came until the moment the head cleared, and that was all you had. She found the hind legs folded down, worked them up one at a time, joint by joint, cupping each sharp little hoof in her palm as it came past the tight ring of the cervix, and then she had both hind feet in her hand and she looked at Davey.

"When I pull, it comes all at once or not at all. You have it the second it's out. Don't wait for me to tell you. Swing it, clear it, and if it doesn't breathe you rub it like you're trying to start a fire. Hard. You won't hurt it. Yes?"

"Yes."

She waited for the contraction, joined it, and pulled — steady, down toward the hocks, the gimmer bawling — and the lamb came in one long terrible smoothness, hips, ribs, shoulders, head, and was in Davey's hands before it touched the straw. It was dark-tongued and slack. He swung it, cleared the mouth, laid it across his knees and rubbed, both hands, hard, the way you'd scrub frost off glass, and nothing, and he kept on, and Marian knelt with her arm dripping and did not let herself reach in and take it from him, and kept not letting herself, and then the lamb's ribs hitched. Hitched again. It coughed wetly and shook its ears with a sound like a wet rag snapped and began to breathe, raggedly, then evenly, and Davey looked up at her over the top of it with an expression she would keep for the rest of her life: astonished, lit, fierce, his father all through it like a watermark.

"There's your data," she said, when she could trust her voice. "Storm brings them."

By three the snow had stopped and the wind had swung north and dropped, and the cold came down out of a clearing sky like something lowered on a chain. Davey had been out twice to the generator. Nine of the eleven had lambed. The shed was full of the sound of it, the suckling and rustling and the low contented chuckling, fourteen new lambs under the red lamps, and Marian walked the line one more time with the torch off, just listening, the way Tom had walked it, because the ear caught trouble before the eye did.

She found Davey asleep sitting up against the gate of the Suffolk's jug, his chin on his chest, one hand still resting on the rail above the breech lamb as if he'd been keeping it there on purpose, keeping a finger on the pulse of the thing he'd brought back. She stood looking at him a long time. Then she took the horse blanket off the rail and put it over him, and he stirred and didn't wake, and she sat down in the straw beside him with her back against the same gate, shoulder to shoulder, the way she and Tom had sat through how many of these nights, passing the flask, not talking, and she felt the cold of the block wall through her coat and the warmth of the boy along her arm.

Out the doorway the yard was blue-white under stars, every fence post wearing six inches of snow like a hat, the whole farm transfigured and silent, and the light over the door laid a yellow path partway out into it and gave up. Two ewes still to go. They'd go at dawn, probably, or tomorrow night, in their own time, the way everything here came — lambs and storms and seasons and grief — in its own time, asking nobody.

"We're all right," she said, quietly, to the shed, to the dark, to whoever was listening. "Difficult woman had twins. The boy's got your hands. We're all right."

A ewe answered her from down the line, a low interrogative blat, and a lamb answered the ewe, and Davey's breathing went on slow and even beside her, and Marian closed her eyes, just for a minute, just until the next one came.
