The taxi could have taken her right up to the doors, but Junie asked to be let out at the bottom of the drive, where the gateposts still stood with their stone pineapples, weathered to the colour of old teeth, and she paid the driver and stood for a moment with her overnight bag in one hand and her good shoes pinching, looking up at the hotel through the avenue of limes.

Thirty-one years. The trees had thickened. The drive had been resurfaced, smooth tarmac where there had been gravel that she used to hear under the wheels of the guests' cars from the third-floor windows while she worked, a sound like the sea drawn backwards. The facade was the same and not the same. The same long white front with its rows of windows, the same balustrade along the roofline, but the west wing — she made herself look at it — the west wing was new, of course, rebuilt in the early nineties, and even now, after all this time, the brick under the render had settled differently, so that if you knew, if you had reason to know, you could see the faint seam where the old building ended and the new began, like a graft on an apple tree, like skin that has healed over and never quite matches.

THE MARINE HOTEL, the letters said, gold on the pediment. The font was different. Heavier. She remembered the old letters, thinner, more confident, the way she used to look up at them on her half-day coming back from the town with her wages folded into her cardigan pocket, nineteen years old and her feet aching, thinking that the word HOTEL was the most glamorous word in the English language and that she lived inside it.

She walked up the drive. A young man in a grey waistcoat came down the steps and reached for her bag, and she let him take it, which felt strange, and she nearly, nearly walked past the main doors and round to the side, to the staff entrance by the bins where the kitchen porters used to smoke, before she caught herself. The staff entrance was gone anyway. There was a spa there now, she'd seen it on the website, frosted glass and a picture of a woman with hot stones on her spine.

The lobby smelled of lilies and floor polish and something pumped through the air system, fig or amber, the smell that all hotels had now, and underneath it, very faintly, so faintly she could not be sure she wasn't inventing it, the old smell: laundered cotton and silver polish and the ghost of a thousand cooked breakfasts gone up through the building like weather.

"Checking in?" said the girl at the desk. "Or are you here for the wedding?"

"Both," Junie said. "Calloway. June Calloway."

The girl's nails clicked on the keyboard. Behind her, where the pigeonholes had been, the wooden pigeonholes with the brass room numbers and the heavy keys on their fobs shaped like anchors, there was now a smooth panel of pale wood and a screen showing the beach in slow motion, waves folding over and over without sound. Junie had stood on the other side of that desk exactly once in the old days, the morning Mr Petherick called her in to tell her she was being moved up from the annexe rooms to the main house, third floor, which was a promotion of a kind, and she had stood there with her hands behind her back looking at all those keys and thinking: I will hold every one of those before I'm done.

"You're in three-twelve," the girl said, sliding a card across. "Third floor, turn left out of the lift. The wedding party's mostly on three and four. Ceremony's at two in the Garden Room, and then the reception's in the ballroom. Is there anything else I can help you with?"

"No," Junie said. "I know my way."

The girl smiled, not hearing it, and Junie took the card — a card, weightless, you could post it through a letterbox — and went to the lift, which was new, and did not take it, and climbed the stairs instead, because her legs remembered the stairs, because for four years her legs had done those stairs forty times a day with armfuls of linen, and some things you do not unlearn.

Three-twelve. In her time the third-floor rooms had run from 301 to 318, odd numbers on the sea side, and 312 had been a garden-side double with a squeaking wardrobe door that no amount of soap on the hinge would silence, and Mrs Forsyth, the housekeeper, used to say, leave it, June, it lets us know when they're hiding the towels in their suitcases. The room she let herself into now had no relation to that room. The walls had moved. The whole corridor had been re-cut in the rebuilding, the rooms made bigger, fewer of them, so that the geometry she carried in her body — eleven paces from the service cupboard to 309, the swing of the connecting door, the loose board outside 315 that you learned to step over so as not to wake the late sleepers — none of it corresponded to anything anymore. She stood in the doorway of three-twelve like a woman who has come home to find the furniture rearranged by strangers, and then she went in and put her bag on the rack and sat on the end of the bed.

The bed was beautifully made. She could not help it; she checked. Hospital corners, the counterpane taut enough to bounce a coin, the pillows plumped and karate-chopped in the modern style, which Mrs Forsyth would have called vulgar. Somebody had done this room properly. Somebody — she looked at her watch, half eleven — somebody had done this room about two hours ago, a girl probably, a girl with a trolley and a list and sixteen more rooms to do before the wedding guests arrived, and Junie sat on the work of that girl's hands and felt the strangest thing, a kind of vertigo, as if she were sitting on her own younger shoulders.

She had been good at it. That was the thing nobody ever asked, and so she never said: she had been good at the work. Quick and quiet and exact. She could strip and make a double in four minutes. She knew how to fold a towel so it sat like a bird on the rail, how to read a room in one glance from the door — honeymooners, commercial traveller, a marriage going bad — by the way the pillows had been used, by what was on the nightstand, by whether the two halves of the bed had reached for each other in the night or turned away. Chambermaids know everything, Sandra used to say. We're the priests of this place. We hear all the confessions and we can't tell a soul.

Sandra Mitchell. Twenty-three years old, from Plymouth, with a laugh you could hear two floors down and a fiancé in the merchant navy whose photograph she kept tucked into the frame of the mirror in her attic room, the room at the end of the staff corridor, room fourteen, three doors past where Junie stopped knocking.

There it was. She had wondered, on the train down, how long she would last before she let herself think it straight through, and the answer was: until she sat on a bed in this building. Three doors. The corridor full of smoke at two in the morning, smoke with a body to it, a brown muscular thing that came along the ceiling first and then dropped, and Junie in her nightdress banging on doors with the flat of her hand — Maureen's door, then Dot's, then the twins from the kitchen — and the heat coming up through the floor through the soles of her bare feet like an iron through a sheet, and she had got as far as door eleven, Pauline's door, and Pauline had come out coughing with her dressing gown over her face, and then the lights died, and the smoke shut over them like water, and someone — Pauline always swore it was her, Junie was never sure — someone had grabbed her wrist and pulled, and they had gone down the back stairs on their hands and knees, counting the turns, because Junie's hands knew the back stairs blind, her hands had known them the way they knew the corners of a sheet, and that knowledge had carried four of them down through the dark and out into the yard by the bins where the air hit them like cold water and the windows of the west wing stood up against the night printed with orange.

Three more doors. Eight, ten paces. She had done the arithmetic for thirty-one years, on buses, in church, in the bath, at her own kitchen table at three in the morning: eight paces, four seconds, one fist on one door. The inquest said Sandra had likely never woken, the smoke had got to her sleeping, she would not have suffered, and the coroner's voice when he said it was kind, and Junie had sat at the back of the room in her interview suit and understood that "she would not have suffered" was a sentence built for the living to live in, a little room with the walls moved, and that she would live in it because there was nowhere else, and she had, mostly, for thirty-one years, she had.

She unpacked her dress and hung it in the bathroom and ran the shower hot to steam the creases out, an old trick, and at quarter to two she went down to the Garden Room in her lilac dress and her good shoes, and she was Auntie June.

That was who she was to all of them, to her niece Carol weeping happily in the front row, to the bride, Carol's Hannah, twenty-six and lit up like a lamp, to the groom's people from Sheffield, to the children skidding on the parquet: Auntie June, Great-Auntie June really, who had never married, who'd worked away down south when she was a girl before she came home and did her years at the surgery, reception and then practice manager, steady Auntie June who sent proper birthday cards with money in. Not one of them knew. Why would they? When the invitation had come in February — that thick cream card, and the name of the hotel printed in copperplate, the name leaping off the card at her like a hand from under a bed — she had sat at her kitchen table for a long time, and then she had written back in her best hand: Wouldn't miss it for the world.

Hannah had found the hotel on a website. Best wedding venues on the coast. She didn't know. Carol didn't know; Carol had been six in 1987 and the fire had been three days of news a long way away, and Junie afterwards had said only that the hotel had closed and she'd come home, which was true, which was a hem of the truth turned under and pressed flat.

The Garden Room had been the dining room. They had taken out the wall to the old conservatory and glassed the whole thing so the light came in off the lawns, and where Junie had once carried early-morning trays past the dumbwaiter there was now an aisle scattered with rose petals, and Hannah came down it on her father's arm to a string quartet, and Junie cried, because everyone cries at weddings and because the light through the glass was the colour of that long-ago morning after, when she had stood on the lawn in a fireman's coat over her nightdress and watched the sun come up behind the smoking roof, and because the girl walking down the aisle had Carol's chin and Carol's mother's eyes — Junie's sister's eyes — and was alive, was so completely, carelessly alive, in this building, and didn't know what a thing that was, and shouldn't have to know, and that was right. That was what the rebuilding was for. That was what all of it was for.

At the reception a man from the groom's side, red-faced and friendly, leaned over the table during the speeches' lull and said, "Apparently this place burned down once, did you know that? The taxi driver was telling us. Years back. People died, he said."

"Did it," Junie said, and turned the stem of her glass.

"You'd never know now," the man said, looking around at the chandeliers, the swagged silk, the cake on its silver stand. "You'd never know."

"No," Junie said. "You'd never know."

Between the meal and the evening do there was the lull there always is, the band setting up, the older guests drifting to sofas, the children fed cake and gone feral on the lawn, and Junie slipped out of the ballroom and went up the stairs to the third floor, not to her room, just to walk the corridor, just to put her feet on it. Halfway along, outside three-oh-eight, a housekeeping trolley stood against the wall, stacked with the white architecture of the trade — bath sheets, hand towels, the little soaps, the bags of tea — and the door of the linen store beyond it was propped open with a wedge, and the smell came out of it and took her by the throat.

Clean cotton, warm from the press. Starch and steam and a ghost of lavender. The smell had not changed because it could not change, because it was the smell of the work itself, and Junie stood in the doorway of the linen store with her hand on the frame and breathed it in, and for a moment she was not fifty in lilac, she was nineteen in her green uniform with her cap pinned crooked, and Sandra was beside her with an armful of pillowslips saying, smell that, Junie, that's better than any perfume, that's the smell of money other people have, and laughing her two-floor laugh.

"Can I help you?"

A girl had come out of three-oh-eight with a bundle of stripped sheets against her chest. Twenty, maybe. Dark hair scraped back, name badge: BETHAN. Tired around the eyes, the particular tiredness of a girl who has turned a whole floor of rooms on a wedding day, and wary, the particular wariness of staff finding a guest where guests don't go.

"No," Junie said. "I'm sorry. I was just — " and then, because the smell had undone something in her, she said, "I used to do your job. Here. A long time ago. Before it was rebuilt."

The girl's face changed. "Here? In this hotel?"

"Third floor," Junie said. "This floor. Though it was a different shape then."

"Was it — " the girl began, and stopped, and Junie watched her decide not to ask, the way you learn to decide not to ask, and instead the girl shifted the sheets in her arms and said, "It's a good floor. You get the sea side."

"You get the sea side," Junie agreed.

The girl moved past her into the store and dropped the sheets into the cage, and on her way out she took a folded clean sheet from the shelf and it slipped, half opened, and without thinking — entirely without thinking, her hands ahead of her mind by thirty years — Junie caught the far corners, and the two of them snapped it taut between them, folded it in halves, in quarters, walked it together, the girl's hands meeting hers at the fold, the old two-person dance that no one teaches you in words, and the girl laughed, surprised, and said, "You haven't lost it."

"No," Junie said. "It turns out you don't."

She wanted to say something else. She wanted to say: when you knock on doors, knock hard. She wanted to say: learn the back stairs with your eyes shut. She wanted to say a name to someone, just to say it aloud in this building, but the girl had sixteen rooms and a wedding crowd coming up drunk later, so Junie said only, "They work you too hard on days like this. Make sure you sit down with a cup of tea," and the girl said, "Chance'd be a fine thing," and grinned, and went back to her trolley, and Junie went down the back stairs — they had kept the back stairs, fire doors every landing now, green running-man signs glowing, sprinkler heads in every ceiling like little brass thistles, she had counted them all weekend without meaning to — and out through a side door into the garden.

She found it where she had thought it might be, where the staff garden used to be, behind the yew hedge: a bench, teak gone silver, and on its back a small brass plate, polished — somebody polished it, somebody had it on a list — IN MEMORY OF THOSE WHO LOST THEIR LIVES HERE, 21 NOVEMBER 1987, and beneath that six names, and the fourth name was SANDRA J. MITCHELL.

Junie sat down on the bench with her glass of wedding champagne going flat in her hand. The music started up in the ballroom behind her, muffled through the glass and the hedge, the first dance, something slow that she half knew. The light was going down over the sea the way it does in September, gold then grey then the lighthouse starting its turn far out, and the windows of the hotel came on one by one above the lawn, the whole long front of it lit up like an ocean liner, the west wing too, the new old west wing, every window yellow and whole.

"Well, Sand," she said quietly. "They've made a job of it."

She told her about the wedding. She told her about the dress and the cake and the man from Sheffield and the girl Bethan who folded a sheet like a Plymouth girl, and about the fiancé in the merchant navy, whom Junie had met once, years after, in a café in Exeter, married by then, kind, sad, and they had not known what to say to each other and had said it anyway. She told her about the smell of the linen store. She did not tell her about the three doors, because she had told her about the three doors a thousand times in a thousand kitchens at three in the morning, and Sandra — the Sandra she carried, the only Sandra there was now — had said the same thing back every time, in her two-floor voice: you knocked on eleven doors, Junie Calloway. Count those.

When the champagne was finished she went back inside and danced. She danced with the groom's red-faced uncle and with two of the feral children, one standing on each of her aching feet, and at half past ten Hannah found her by the bar, flushed and barefoot, her shoes hooked on her fingers, and hugged her hard and said, "Auntie June, isn't it the most beautiful place? Didn't we choose well?" and Junie held her great-niece's warm living shoulders and said, "You chose perfectly, my love. It's a beautiful hotel. It always was."

In the morning she woke early, before her alarm, before the gulls had properly started, the way she had woken at nineteen, the body remembering the shift. She packed her bag and put on her travelling clothes, and then, because she could not help it and did not try to, she stripped the bed. She pulled the sheets and folded them into a tidy bundle at the foot of the mattress the way the laundry liked them, pillowslips inside, and she gathered the towels into one and set them by the bathroom door, and she stood the little soaps and the tea things in a row so the trolley girl could see at a glance. Four minutes. It still took four minutes.

On the desk she left a ten-pound note, and after a moment she took the hotel pen and the hotel paper and wrote, in her best hand, the hand she'd learned for the registration cards: For Bethan, or whoever has this floor. From one who knows. And then she carried her own bag down the back stairs, past the brass thistles and the green glowing men, and out through the lobby where the screen still poured its silent waves, and walked away down the drive under the limes, and at the gateposts she stopped and turned, the way you do, the way she had on her last morning all those years ago in a borrowed coat.

The hotel stood in the early light with its windows blank and calm, the seam in the west wing only visible if you knew, and on the third floor a casement swung open and a white shape billowed out and was hauled in again — a girl airing a room, shaking a sheet into the morning — and Junie watched until the window closed, and then she went on down to the town to catch her train.
