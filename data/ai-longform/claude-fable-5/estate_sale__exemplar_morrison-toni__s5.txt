The dining room went first. A house empties the way a body does, extremities first, and by one o'clock the strangers had carried out the sideboard, the china cabinet, the eight chairs with the needlepoint seats Geneva had worked one winter when Ruth was nine and forbidden to sit on them. Forty dollars a chair. The woman who bought them had a van with a magnetic sign on the door and she counted the money into Ruth's hand the way you'd pay off a debt to somebody you didn't like, slow, watching her own fingers, and Ruth stood on the porch of her mother's house and watched the chairs go down the steps upside down, their legs in the air like something slaughtered.

She had set up a card table in the front hall with a cigar box for change and a roll of masking tape and a black marker, and she had a system, or she'd had one at ten o'clock that morning when the system still seemed like a thing that could protect her. Green dots for furniture, prices on the tape. Kitchen things on the kitchen table. Linens folded in the bedroom, coats on the bed. The bed itself she had not put a price on, and nobody had asked, as if even strangers understood that the bed a woman died in was not for sale, although Ruth suspected it was simpler than that, that nobody wanted a dead woman's bed in October in Ohio, in a town where everybody knew exactly which woman had died in it.

This was the house on Decatur Street, which nobody on this side of town called Decatur Street. They called it Piano Street, had called it that for fifty years, because of Geneva. Because for fifty years, on Saturdays, the children of the town had come up these same porch steps with their quarters knotted in handkerchiefs and their fingernails inspected at the door, and the sound of scales had come out of the front parlor from eight in the morning until supper, up and down, up and down, like somebody climbing the same staircase all day long and never getting anywhere. Three generations of this town had passed under Geneva Pryor's hands. They had felt the ruler, light as a sparrow landing, on the back of their wrists when their wrists collapsed. They had heard her say curve, curve, you are holding an apple, in that voice she had, that voice like ironed cotton. Half the choir directors in the county had started in that parlor. There was a man playing in a hotel in Pittsburgh right now who sent a card every Christmas, until last Christmas, when there was no longer anybody to send it to.

That was one Geneva. That was the Geneva the town had owned, the one they had buried five weeks ago with two hundred people standing up in Mount Calvary because the pews ran out, the one Reverend Suggs called a institution, a cornerstone, a mother to the musical life of this community. Mother to the musical life. Ruth had sat in the front pew in her good gray coat and turned that phrase over like a coin she suspected was counterfeit.

Because there was the other Geneva. The one who lived in the house when the students went home. The one who could walk into a room Ruth had just cleaned and stand in it, saying nothing, until the room confessed. The one who never raised her voice or her hand because she had something better, she had withdrawal, she could take herself back out of a room while still standing in it, could close like a bank, and a child shut out like that will spend years pounding on the glass, and then one day she will stop pounding, and that day is the worst day, although nobody marks it on any calendar. That Geneva. This Geneva had taught the whole town's children patience and praise, an apple in every curved hand, and had given her own daughter the metronome. Tick. Tick. You are rushing, Ruth Ann. The music knows what time it is even if you do not.

"How much for the table?"

A young couple in the kitchen. The husband had a beard and the wife had a way of touching things with one finger, like she was taking their temperature. The kitchen table. Enamel top, chipped at one corner, the chip shaped like Florida, and underneath the table the cross brace where Ruth used to hook her feet on Saturday nights while Geneva pulled the hot comb through her hair, the stove going, the comb clicking against the burner, the smell of Dixie Peach and singed hair, hold still, the one hour of the week her mother's hands were on her and stayed on her, and even then it was correction, even then it was a kind of tuning. Hold still. You want to look like something on Sunday or don't you.

"Sixty," Ruth said.

"Would you take forty?" the wife said. "It's pretty beat up. I mean it's perfect, honestly, you can't fake this kind of distress, people pay for this look, but the chip—"

"Sixty," Ruth said.

The wife looked at the husband. The husband got out his wallet, which is what husbands are for at estate sales, and while he counted the twenties the wife ran her one finger along the chip shaped like Florida and said, "I'm sorry for your loss. Was she your grandmother?"

"My mother."

"Oh." The finger lifted. "She must have been—I mean, this house. Everything's so cared for."

"Yes," Ruth said. "Everything was."

They carried the table out between them, tipped on its side, and the cross brace where her feet had hooked went past her at eye level, and Ruth looked at it the way you look at a face on a passing bus, somebody you went to school with, somebody whose name you'd have to be reminded of, and then the door was empty again and the kitchen had a bald place on the linoleum where the table had stood since Eisenhower.

She was fifty-six years old. She had buried a husband in Chicago and raised a daughter to grown and watched that daughter raise one of her own, and she had come back to this town four times in thirty-eight years, twice for funerals, and she had told herself on the drive down, somewhere around Toledo, that this was an errand. A transaction. You empty the house, you sweep it, you hand the keys to the lawyer, you drive back north before the lake-effect snow starts and you let the dead bury the dead, the way Geneva herself would have done it, dry-eyed, efficient, everything labeled. Ruth had believed she could do the whole thing without sitting down once in any of the rooms. She was wrong.

Etta Mae Frye came at two o'clock, which Ruth had known she would, because Etta Mae had called twice that week to say she wasn't sure she could make it.

"Lord, look at this," Etta Mae said in the front hall, and she wasn't looking at anything, she was looking at all of it, at the bald places and the masking-tape prices and the strangers moving through the rooms with their hands on things. "Look at this. Geneva would die."

"She did," Ruth said.

"You know what I mean." Etta Mae set her pocketbook on the card table like she was planting a flag. She was eighty-one and built close to the ground and she had been in this house ten thousand times, choir business, circle meeting, the two of them putting up tomatoes in August with the radio on. "Strangers walking through her house with their shoes on. You couldn't have a private sale? Called the church first?"

"The church got the dishes Tuesday. Two boxes."

"The everyday dishes. I'm talking about—" Etta Mae lowered her voice, which for Etta Mae meant it carried only into the next room. "Where's the cake stand? The cut-glass one. She told me, must have been ninety-five, ninety-six, she stood right in that kitchen and told me, Etta, when I go, you see to it nobody fool gets my cake stand."

"It's in a box in the pantry with your name on it."

Etta Mae's mouth opened and shut. Whatever she had loaded up to say, she had to put it down unfired, and it cost her, Ruth could see it cost her. "Well," she said. "All right then." She patted the pocketbook. "She had her ways but she didn't forget people. That's one thing. She did not forget."

"No," Ruth said. "She didn't."

A man went by with the floor lamp from the parlor, the one with the fringed shade, and Etta Mae watched him the whole way out the door like a deacon watching somebody leave service early. Then she turned around and looked at Ruth, really looked, the way old women in that town looked at you, an inventory, a weighing, and she said, "You favor her now. You know that? You didn't use to. Now you do. Around the mouth."

"People keep saying that."

"Because it's true. Truth don't wear out from saying." Etta Mae sat herself down in the one chair that hadn't sold, a kitchen chair Ruth had set by the card table, and she settled into it like the afternoon was hers now, which it was. "She read me your letters, you know."

Ruth was making change for a woman buying pillowcases and her hands kept doing it, two ones and a five, but something in her chest went still, the way a house goes still. "What letters. I didn't write letters."

"Christmas cards. The ones with the picture. Your girl in her graduation robe. The baby. Geneva would bring them to circle meeting and pass them around and tell us what was happening up there like she had the news direct." Etta Mae laughed, a short laugh, almost a cough. "Marie made supervisor. Marie bought a house in—where is it—"

"Evanston."

"Evanston. Like she'd been to Evanston. Like she'd been invited." Etta Mae said the last part with her eyes somewhere else, out the open front door, where the leaves were coming down in the yard in that slow sideways October way, no wind, just letting go. "She never told you she did that."

"She never told me anything. We didn't—" Ruth closed the cigar box. "I sent the cards to be polite. So she'd know we were alive. She never answered one."

"No," Etta Mae said. "She wouldn't have." And she said it the way you'd confirm a fact about weather, about the lake, about something old and known and past all argument, and Ruth wanted to grab her by her churchwoman shoulders and shake her and say, then why, if everybody knew, if the whole circle meeting knew, why did nobody ever pick up a telephone and tell me my mother carried my child's picture in her pocketbook, why did I get to be sixty miles from sixty years old before anybody mentioned it, but you don't shake Etta Mae Frye, and anyway Ruth knew the answer. The answer was that it was Geneva's to tell. The whole town had spent fifty years keeping Geneva's parlor confidences, her ruler-light secrets, and they weren't about to stop just because she was dead. The dead in that town kept their privileges. The dead in that town were just members who'd moved away.

By three the bedroom was gone. The coats went to a woman who tried each one on in front of the vanity mirror before the vanity itself sold, turning to see herself sideways, the gray wool, the black with the velvet collar, and Ruth stood in the doorway and watched her mother's shoulders appear on a stranger's body, one coat after another, like a Resurrection performed badly, in the wrong order, with nobody rising. The woman took three coats for twenty dollars and Ruth let her. There is a price past which it isn't selling anymore, it's release, and Ruth had crossed it sometime after lunch and the crossing felt like nothing, that was the strange part. She had braced all week for grief and what came instead was lightness, an emptiness that traveled, room to room, keeping pace with the emptying, and she did not know yet whether the lightness was freedom or just the numbness that walks ahead of pain like a man with a lantern walking ahead of a wagon.

A dealer wanted the doorknobs. He went through the house with a screwdriver in his shirt pocket asking original? original? and Ruth said the doorknobs stay with the house, and he said the house is going to get bought by somebody who'll gut it anyway, you know that, right, these old places on these old streets, and Ruth said the doorknobs stay with the house in a voice she had never heard come out of her own mouth before, ironed cotton, and the man left without buying anything and she stood in the hall with her wrist curved like she was holding an apple.

The piano she had priced high on purpose. Four hundred, on masking tape, stuck to the upright's closed lid, and all day people had lifted the lid and plinked at it and let it back down. It needed tuning. It needed somebody. It stood against the parlor wall where it had always stood, in the rectangle of afternoon light that had crossed it every clear afternoon since before Ruth was born, and she had not touched it. She had dusted around it. She had sold the sheet music out of the cabinet beside it, hymnals and Czerny and the John Thompson books with three generations of children's names penciled inside the covers, fifty cents apiece, and she had not touched the piano, because she knew, the way you know a stove is hot without putting your hand on it, that the piano was where the whole day was waiting for her.

It was the bench that did it. A woman wanted the bench separate, for a hallway she said, you put a cushion on it, and Ruth lifted the hinged seat to empty it out, and there under the brittle stack of recital programs, under "Pupils of Mrs. Geneva Pryor Present An Evening of Music, June 1961, June 1964, June 1970," under all those mimeographed evenings, was a manila envelope soft as cloth from handling, and in the envelope were the Christmas cards. Every one. Thirty-some years of them, and the newspaper clipping of Ruth's wedding from the Chicago Defender that Ruth had never sent, that somebody must have sent for her, and Marie's birth announcement, and Marie's graduation program with one name underlined in pencil, light, the way you'd touch a wrist, and a photograph Ruth had no memory of mailing, herself at maybe forty, squinting on a porch in Evanston with the baby on her hip, and on the back of it, in Geneva's hand, the hand that had written curve your fingers on a thousand assignment books, two words. My girl.

Which one of them she meant, Ruth or Marie, there was no knowing, and Ruth stood in the parlor with the photograph and understood that there would never be any knowing, that her mother had managed even this, had died and left behind a love with no address on it, a love folded shut in a piano bench, the way some people leave money in a mattress, hidden so well it was the same as never having it. All those years Ruth had told it to herself one way: her mother's love was thin, was rationed, was scales and correction and the metronome's permission. Tick. Tick. She had built a life on the far side of that arithmetic, had loved her own daughter loud and sloppy and out in the open on purpose, had said the words at every leave-taking like a charm against inheritance. And here was the envelope. Soft as cloth. Handled and handled and handled, you could see it, in the dark, under the music, where nobody could catch her at it. Thick love, after all. Thick as a fist. So thick it could not pour.

"You all right?" the bench woman said.

"The bench isn't for sale," Ruth said. "I made a mistake with the tape."

The piano went at four-thirty. A man came in with a little girl, eight maybe nine, hair in two braids with red ball barrettes, and the man stood in front of the upright with his cap in his hand the way men used to stand and said his daughter was taking lessons at the school but they didn't have an instrument at home, she'd been practicing on a paper keyboard the teacher printed out, you put your fingers on the paper and imagine the sound, and he'd seen the sign out front, and he knew four hundred was fair, more than fair, but would she consider two-fifty, he could get his cousin's truck around in twenty minutes.

The little girl had her hands behind her back. She was looking at the piano the way you look at a horse, wanting to touch it, knowing it was alive.

"Go ahead," Ruth said to her. "Lift the lid. It won't bite. It'll think about it, but it won't."

The girl played something, two-handed, hesitant, a school-recital piece with a wrong note in the left hand that she flinched at and went back and fixed, went back and fixed it without being told, and Ruth heard the parlor fill up the way it used to fill, the sound going into the plaster and the floorboards where fifty years of the same sound had already gone, the house taking the music in like the house had been thirsty for five weeks, and she heard, under the school piece, all of it, the scales, the Czerny, the Saturday mornings, the hymn her mother played alone on Sunday nights after supper when she thought everyone was asleep, Precious Lord, take my hand, the only time Geneva's wrists ever collapsed, the only ten minutes a week that woman ever let the apple drop.

"Two hundred," Ruth said. "And the bench goes with it. A piano needs its bench."

She had meant to keep the bench. She heard herself give it away and understood that she'd been wrong about that too, that the bench without the piano was just a box that used to hold a secret, and the secret was out now, was in the envelope in her pocketbook, was going home to Evanston where Marie would cry over it the easy open way Marie cried, and Ruth would watch her and envy her, the way you envy people their easy weather.

It took four of them to get it down the porch steps, the father and the cousin and two men off the street who put down what they were carrying to help, because that's still how it goes in that town, and they walked it down the steps slow, end first, calling to each other, easy, easy, you got it, and the piano went down the steps the way the casket had gone down the church steps five weeks before, the same tilt, the same gravity, the same gathering of men's hands under a weight too important to drop. The little girl walked alongside with her hand flat against its flank. Ruth stood on the porch with her arms folded and the cold coming up the street and did not cry, and then the truck pulled away and she did, briefly, without ceremony, the way you stop at a stop sign.

By six it was over. The leaf woman, the coat woman, the bearded husband, all of them gone, the cigar box fat with creased bills, the yard sign down. Etta Mae had left at five with her cake stand, pausing at the door to say, "Come to circle meeting in the morning before you drive back. They'll want to see you," which in that town was both an invitation and a summons, and Ruth had said she'd see, which they both knew meant yes.

She walked the rooms once. The house was hollow now and it had a hollow house's voice, her footsteps coming back at her off the bare plaster, every room saying the same thing, which was nothing, which was everything, and in the parlor the rectangle of last light lay on the floor where the piano had stood, lying across the unfaded wood in the shape of the absence, and Ruth stood in it. There was no chair to sit in so she sat on the floor, fifty-six years old, in her mother's parlor, in her good slacks, and she put her hands flat on her knees.

Then her hands began to move. She didn't ask them to. They found the fingering on the bones of her knees, scales first, C major, both hands, the thumb passing under the way the thumb learns once and keeps forever, and then the hymn, the Sunday-night hymn, her fingers walking it out on her own body in the empty room with the light going, and somewhere under fifty years her wrists remembered to curve, lightly, lightly, as if even now they were holding an apple, as if even now somebody might be listening from the kitchen, keeping time.
