He knew it, and was not surprised when, rounding a stand of pine, he saw a young raccoon caught in a break of tree limbs, struggling, its hind leg wedged where two branches crossed. Jacob dismounted and worked the leg free, taking a clawing for his trouble, and watched the animal limp off into the brush without a backward look. Gratitude, he thought, was not in the gift of beasts, nor much in the gift of men, and he wiped the blood from the back of his hand on his breeches before remounting. Regina shivered her flanks against flies and they went on.

The trail widened, narrowed, disappeared altogether in places where freshets had carried the ground away, and Jacob let the mare pick her own way while his mind ran ahead of them both to the business waiting at the end of the ride. A Portuguese tobacco planter, Senhor D'Ortega, owed him money—a debt now two seasons old, deferred once with elaborate apology and once with no apology at all—and the letter summoning Jacob to the man's estate to settle accounts had a perfume of trouble about it even before he broke the seal. Men who could pay did not write such letters. Men who could pay sent the money. He had lent against a shipload of tobacco; the ship, he had since learned, sat a third of its cargo lighter than promised at the bottom of a Barbados harbor, or so the story went. Stories from that latitude shifted like weather.

Still, he had come. Partly for the debt, partly because the journey itself answered something in him. Jacob Vaark had been an orphan once, a ratty piece of London left at the door of a poorhouse, then a ward, then a runner of errands for men whose boots he was not fit to clean, by their lights, and the wide raw country he now rode through never stopped astonishing him with its indifference to all of that. Here a man's parentage drowned in the noise of water and birds. Here the question was not whose son are you but what can you do, and Jacob could do a great deal. He could read, cipher, judge timber and pelts, smell rot in a deal before the papers were drawn. An uncle he had never met had died and left him a hundred and twenty acres in a northern climate of hard winters and harder Dutchmen, and the patroonship had made him, at last, a man with land under his name. Land. He said the word to himself sometimes the way other men said the names of women.

By midday the heat had thickened and the air buzzed. He stopped at a tavern raised up on the bank of a slow creek, an establishment of two rooms and a sagging porch where a half-dozen men sat over cider and argued the price of hides. Jacob took bread and a bowl of fish stew and listened more than he spoke, which was his habit. The talk was of tobacco, always tobacco, the leaf that ate the ground beneath it and demanded fresh ground, more ground, ground without end, and of the hands needed to work it. One man, a Welshman by his vowels, complained that his indentured pair had run and that the notices he posted brought him nothing but lies and expense. Another laughed and said the remedy was known to everyone: buy Africans, who could run nowhere that their skin did not advertise them. There was general agreement on this point, delivered in the flat tones of men discussing the merits of oxen, and Jacob finished his stew and paid and left, carrying with him a vague distaste he could not entirely account for. He told himself it was the flesh trade itself he disliked—the auctions he had glimpsed in the harbor towns, the smell of the ships—and that a man could grow rich in this world without dirtying himself in it. Rum, now. Furs, timber, lending at honest interest. There were clean ways.

The afternoon ride took him across the neck into Maryland, which pleased him, since whatever else might be said of a papist colony, its laws of trade were looser and a private man could do private business there without the Crown's fingers in his purse. The country changed as he rode: the woods fell back, and fields opened on either side, and in the fields he began to see them, the bent figures among the green rows, and behind them, on horseback, the figures who were not bent. By evening he came in sight of D'Ortega's place, and he reined Regina in at the head of the drive to look at it.

The house stood at the end of an avenue of young sycamores, and it was the grandest dwelling Jacob had ever seen outside of prints—brick, two storeys and attics, with a fanlight over the door that caught the late sun and burned like something sacred. A house built on smoke, he thought. On leaf and smoke and the bowed backs in the fields he had passed. He felt two things at once and was honest enough to know it: contempt for the man who had built a palace on borrowed money he would not repay, and a thin, unwelcome envy, fine as a splinter, that worked into him as he rode up the drive. His own house in the north was sound and warm and his wife Rebekka kept it well, but it was a farmer's house. It did not announce. This one announced.

A boy ran to take the mare. Jacob noted the boy's bare feet, the welt on his forearm, the way he did not raise his eyes, and then D'Ortega himself was on the steps, arms wide, calling out a welcome in an accent that turned every word into a small ceremony. He was a soft man, was Jacob's first judgment, soft in the hands and the mouth, with a wig too heavy for the climate and a coat too fine for the hour. His wife appeared behind him, narrow and powdered, and behind her some quantity of children, six or eight, Jacob could not fix the number, all dressed like dolls and all staring.

Supper was an ordeal of plenty. Jacob, who had eaten with relish at rough tables from Virginia to the Hudson, found he could barely swallow at this one. The courses came and came—pigeon, a ham glistening under cloves, oysters carried inland at God knew what cost, custards, Madeira—and through all of it D'Ortega talked. He talked of Lisbon and of Angola, where his family held interests; he talked of the iniquity of English shipping laws and the perfidy of Dutch captains; he talked of his children's accomplishments and his wife's lineage and the quality of his leaf, which he swore was the finest on the bay, sweet-scented, none of your common Orinoco. What he did not talk of was the debt. Each time Jacob steered toward it, D'Ortega lifted a hand as though brushing away an insect and signaled for another dish.

It was the serving that unsettled Jacob most. The food came on silver, and the silver came in black hands, and the hands moved around the table all evening, silent, pouring, removing, while D'Ortega and his wife behaved as though the dishes traveled by themselves. Once, a young man pouring wine let a drop fall on the cloth, no more than a drop, and Jacob watched Madame D'Ortega's face go smooth and cold in a way that promised the evening's true accounting would be done elsewhere, later, out of guests' hearing. Jacob looked at his plate. He thought of Rebekka serving their own table herself, laughing, sucking a burned finger, and he wanted suddenly and powerfully to be home.

In the morning the reckoning could be put off no longer, and D'Ortega led him on a tour instead of to a strongbox—through the curing barns hung with browning leaf, past the smithy, the dairy, the rows of low cabins set well downwind of the great house—talking all the while of next season, of certain arrangements with certain factors, of a ship even now being fitted out. Jacob let him finish. Then he said, plainly, that he had not ridden eight days for tobacco that did not yet exist, and that the sum was the sum, and the date was long past.

D'Ortega's face performed sorrow. There was, he confessed, a difficulty of coin. Coin was scarce in the colony—this much was true, Jacob knew; the whole bay did its business in leaf and paper—and his losses at sea had been cruel, the insurance disputed, the lawyers feeding on the dispute like crows. But he was not a man to leave a debt of honor unsatisfied. He proposed, therefore, payment in kind. And he turned, and Jacob followed his turning, and saw that they had stopped, by no accident at all, at the head of the lane between the cabins, and that the man's open hand was offered not toward his barns or his stock but toward the human beings assembled there.

They had been gathered for viewing. Jacob understood it in a breath—the tour, the route, the timing of it—and his anger at being managed was so quick and pure that for a moment he could not speak. Two dozen of them, perhaps, stood in the beaten dirt: men with the gray dust of the fields on their legs, women with infants shawled against them, a scatter of children pressed back against skirts. They did not look at him, most of them, and the not-looking was itself a labor he could see them performing. D'Ortega walked the line talking of this one's strength and that one's skills, the cooper, the smith's striker, a woman who was, he said, a cook of rare ability, trained in his own kitchen, and worth more than her weight in any commodity Jacob could name.

Flesh was not his commodity, Jacob said. He heard his own voice come out flat. He wanted his money or its worth in goods he could carry or consign: leaf, bills of exchange, silver plate if it came to that. He had a farm, not a plantation; he worked it with a hired man and the help of two women already in his household and had no use for more, and no stomach—he did not say this part aloud—for what the lane was showing him. The smell of the cabins reached him on the turning air. One of the children coughed and was hushed.

D'Ortega pressed. Jacob refused. They walked back toward the house arguing in the courteous, lethal manner of men who each believe the other a thief, and the figures of the lane dissolved behind them, returned by an overseer's word to whatever the day demanded. At the kitchen garden D'Ortega paused to make some point about the cost of the brick, fetched specially, fired badly, half of it wasted, and it was there, in the pause, that Jacob saw the woman standing in the doorway of the cookhouse with two children, a boy still unsteady on his legs and a girl of perhaps seven or eight in a pair of broken shoes too big for her, a woman's cast-off shoes, absurd on her thin ankles, and something about the shoes—their ruin, their wrongness, the child's evident pride in them—caught at him in a place he kept boarded over. He had had a daughter. Patrician, named after no one, who had died at five of fever, and before her two sons who had not seen a second spring, and his wife's grief and his own had settled into the foundations of his northern house like damp. He looked away from the girl. Looking away was also a labor, and he performed it.

In the house, over yet more Madeira that he did not want, the bargaining resumed and at last began to move. Jacob would take a portion in leaf at a valuation he himself would set; a portion in a note secured against the next crop and witnessed by a third party of Jacob's choosing in the town; and for the remainder, D'Ortega, spreading his soft hands, swore there was nothing—nothing!—unless the Senhor would reconsider the matter of a servant, in which case he might have his pick, any man or woman on the place, and the difference settled entire.

Jacob considered the cook. He surprised himself by doing it. The thought arrived dressed in reason: Rebekka was worn, the farm's work was past what their household could carry, Lina could not do everything, and a skilled pair of hands was a skilled pair of hands however they came. He even said the words, half to test them in the air: the cook, then. And watched D'Ortega flinch, and recover, and begin a speech about the impossibility, the particular impossibility, of that particular woman, who was needed, who was essential, who was—Jacob saw the man's wife in the doorway, and saw the look that passed, and understood with a small interior recoil what was essential about her, or believed he did, and the splinter of envy he had carried since the drive turned entirely to disgust. He stood. He said the leaf and the note would do, and the rest could go to the lawyers, and he would take pleasure in the taking, for a Maryland court would weigh a Protestant lender against a papist gentleman in ways the gentleman might not enjoy.

It was then that the cook came out of the kitchen wing into the passage where the men stood, as though she had been listening at the wall, as perhaps she had, and Jacob never afterward forgot it, though he told no one, not even Rebekka, the whole of it. The woman did not look at D'Ortega. She looked at Jacob Vaark, a stranger, a creditor, a man she had served at table the night before without once raising her eyes, and she pushed the small girl forward, the girl in the ruined shoes, and said, Please, Senhor. Not me. Take her. Take my daughter.

The passage was quiet. Somewhere in the house a child of the D'Ortegas was practicing scales on a virginal, the same six notes over and over, and under them Jacob heard the woman's breathing. He understood that he was being asked to do a thing and could not work out what the thing was. To take a child from her mother—he had been a child taken from no one, left rather, deposited like a parcel, and he had hated all his life the ones who left him, and here was a mother thrusting her own away with both hands, begging the parting as though it were rescue. The girl herself stood very still in the big shoes and looked at the floor, and her stillness was the stillness of a creature that has learned what stillness can buy.

D'Ortega began to speak, something smooth, some recovery of his footing, the child was worth little, was small, ate like a bird, the Senhor would be doing a charity at a fool's price, and Jacob heard in the words the man's relief, that the cook would stay, that whatever arrangement the household ran on would run on undisturbed, and his disgust doubled and turned in his chest like an animal. He almost refused for that reason alone, to deny the man his relief. But the woman had not moved. Her eyes stayed on Jacob's face with an attention so absolute it was a kind of grip, and her hands stayed on the girl's shoulders, and the hands were not pushing her away, he saw now; they were holding her up, presenting her, the way one holds a thing of value to the light.

Afterward, riding north with the note in his satchel and the bills of lading for the leaf and a paper conveying to him one female child, name of Florens, he assembled reasons the way a man stacks stones. The child would be company for the women. She would be small trouble and in time real help. Rebekka, who had buried her own girl, might find in her—he did not finish that stone; it would not sit level. He told himself the priest who lurked about the D'Ortega place, the lean one who taught the kitchen children their letters against all sense and most law, would have had her sooner or later believing in saints' bones, and that a sober household was a mercy in itself. He told himself the mother knew her own business. He told himself, lastly and least comfortably, the truth, or a corner of it: that he had wanted to leave that house having taken something out of it, and the silver was entailed and the cook refused him, and the child was what the place would yield.

The girl rode pillion behind a carter as far as the landing, then sat in the bow of the sloop with her knees drawn up, watching the water. She was sick twice and apologized twice, in a small voice, in words that came out half Portuguese. She did not weep. Jacob found her silence harder than weeping would have been and busied himself with the captain's charts. Once, in the night anchorage off the Virginia capes, he woke to a sound and found her awake too, sitting up in her corner of the deck under the blanket the mate had thrown her, and her lips were moving, and he supposed it was the priest's prayers and let her be. It was not prayers. But he never asked and she never told him, and what she was saying, over and over, into the dark above the black water, was what her mother had said to her at the last, the words bent close to her ear in the passage while the men signed papers in the next room—words the girl already could not fully remember, and would spend years trying to, turning them over the way you turn a stone looking for the writing on its underside.

The mother stood in the yard as long as the cart was in sight, and then longer, holding the little boy's hand. What she knew, she could not have said to the girl in the time there was, and could not have made her believe in any time at all: what the master's eyes did when they fell on a girl growing tall, and the mistress's eyes after; what the lane behind the cookhouse was at night; what it had been on the ship, before the ship, in the barracoon, in the canoe on the wide brown river—the things that had been done and the things that were coming, as certain as weather. She had seen the tall man from the north look at the child's foolish shoes and look away, and she had read the looking away. A man who turns from a thing because it pains him is not safe, no. But he is different from a man who does not turn. She had one gift to give and one moment to give it in, and she had given it, and whether it was mercy or only the smaller cruelty she would ask herself in the dark for the rest of her life, with no one to answer.

North, the weather broke early that year. Lina smelled the change before the geese confirmed it and began drying beans against a hard winter, and Rebekka stood in the yard one October morning when the hired man called from the river path, and saw her husband riding up from the landing with a child seated in front of him on the saddle, a thin dark girl in shoes far too big, clutching the pommel with both hands, and Rebekka's heart did several things at once, not all of them kind, before she wiped her palms on her apron and went down to the gate to see what the Lord and Jacob Vaark, who between them never consulted her, had sent her now.
