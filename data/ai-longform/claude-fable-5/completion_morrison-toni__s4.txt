Still, he preferred this trail to the open road, where the danger had a different face: not the arrow or the musket of a stranger but the document, the question, the men in twos and threes who stopped a traveler to examine his business as though business were a thing that could be held up to the light like a coin and bitten for its trueness. Regina's ears swiveled at sounds he could not hear, and he trusted her more than he trusted himself in this country. He had been here twice before, both times in spring, when the dogwood made the woods look hung with laundry, and both times he had left with the feeling of having escaped something he could not name—a slow drowning, perhaps, in the sweetness of the air itself, which lay on the skin like syrup and made men languid and cruel at once.

He had come now on a sour errand, to collect a debt from a man he disliked, and the dislike was itself a kind of comfort, a thing to chew on as he rode. The man was a Portuguese, or claimed Portuguese blood, with papers from the Maryland proprietors and a plantation set on a bluff above a brown river, and he owed Jacob the better part of thirty pounds for goods advanced two years prior—iron, cloth, rum—against a tobacco crop that had rotted in casks somewhere between his wharf and Bristol because the ship carrying it had been impounded, or so the letters said, for irregularities of registry. Jacob did not believe in the ship, or in the rot, or in the letters, which had grown more flowery as they grew less promising, each one wrapping the absence of money in longer ribbons of regard. Esteemed friend. Most worthy and Christian gentleman. He had laughed aloud at the last one, reading it by candle in his own small house far north, and Rebekka had looked up from her sewing to ask what amused him. Nothing, he had told her. A rich man begging in lace.

The trail rose through pine and then dropped him into a bottomland where the air thickened and the insects came at him in clouds. He pulled his hat low and let Regina pick her way. Twice the path forded streams the color of steeped tea, and at the second crossing he stopped to let her drink and to eat the bread and hard cheese the sloop's master had pressed on him at parting. Sitting his saddle in the stream, chewing, he watched a heron stand in the shallows downstream with the patience of a deacon, and he thought, not for the first time, that the land itself was without sin. Whatever was vile here had been carried in, the way rats came in the holds of ships. The land took everything and judged nothing—took the bones of the slaughtered tribes, took the hanged rebels, took the babies dead of fever before they could be named, and every spring put out the same extravagant green over all of it, like a hostess too well-bred to mention what had happened at last night's table.

He was an orphan, or had been; the condition, he believed, never wore off. It was the one thing he knew about himself before he knew his own face. A poorhouse in England, then a kind of apprenticeship that was a kind of servitude, then the sea, then this raw sprawling country where a man with no name worth defending could make one. The inheritance had come like weather—an uncle he had never met, dying without other issue, leaving him one hundred and twenty acres of patroon land in a climate the lawyers' letter described, accurately, as harsh. He had gone to see it expecting nothing and had stood on a rise above his own frozen river and felt something close around his heart that he decided to call ownership because he had no better word. It was not greed. He was sure it was not greed. Greed was what he was riding toward now, the bluff-top house of a man who bought human beings the way other men bought hogs, and worked them to death, and bought more, and called the arithmetic Providence. Jacob lent money and goods at interest, yes, and the interest was honest and the goods were sound, and if some of his debtors trafficked in flesh that was their sin to carry, not his. He told himself this in the rhythm of Regina's walk, and the telling required repeating, which he noticed, and did not examine.

By midday the trail joined a road, rutted and wide, and the country changed. Worm fences staggered along on both sides, and beyond them tobacco stood in ranks, and among the ranks moved figures bent at the waist, so many and so evenly spaced that from a distance they looked sown there, a second crop coming up dark between the rows. None looked at him. A white man on a horse passed in the other direction, touched his hat, and slowed as if to talk; Jacob touched his own and did not slow. Farther on, a child—he could not tell if it was boy or girl—stood at the edge of the road with a gourd of water, holding it up to him without a word, eyes on his stirrup. He shook his head gently and rode past, and then for half a mile could think of nothing but the gourd, the small cracked hands around it, and whether refusing had been kindness or the opposite, and who had set the child there, and what would happen if the gourd came back full.

The ferry crossing cost him fourpence and a quarter hour of the ferryman's opinions, delivered around a pipe stem, concerning the price of seed, the treachery of the proprietors, the insolence—his word—of freedmen, and the certainty, attested by signs, of a hard winter. Jacob gave him grunts where grunts would serve. On the far bank a stone marker leaned in the weeds, and past it the road climbed, and at the top of the climb he saw the house.

It was the kind of house built to be seen from a river: brick hauled at God knew what cost, two chimneys, windows of true glass flashing in the afternoon sun, a lane of young trees planted in pairs leading to the door with the optimism of a man who expected his grandchildren to walk under their shade. Jacob hated it on sight and noted, with the small cold honesty that was his most useful faculty, that the hatred had envy folded into it like an egg into batter. He sat Regina at the top of the rise for a long moment, looking. His own house was timber and clapboard, snug, sufficient. Sufficient was a word he had always carried like a charm against the world's appetite. The word felt lighter than usual in his hand as he looked down the lane of paired trees.

A boy ran out to take the horse before he had fully dismounted, and a second appeared from nowhere to carry his satchel, and he surrendered both with the discomfort of a man being undressed. The master of the house came out onto the steps himself—a soft-bodied man of perhaps forty, dressed for a city that was three thousand miles away, in a wig in that heat—and spread his arms as though the thirty pounds were a misunderstanding between brothers.

"Senhor Vaark. You honor my house."

"Mister will do," said Jacob. "And the honor will be brief. I'm wanted home."

But he was not allowed to be brief. He was given water scented with something, and then wine he did not want, and a tour of the gardens conducted by the wife, a pale exhausted woman with rings on every finger and eyes that had stopped expecting anything, who showed him roses struggling in the wrong soil and spoke of Lisbon in the tone other women used for the dead. He was shown the chapel, private, Romish, with a painted saint whose lifted eyes seemed to Jacob to be avoiding the view from the window, which gave onto the quarters. He was shown the stables, which were better built than the quarters, and remarked on the fact in his mind and kept his mouth shut. Supper could not be escaped. It came in courses, served by silent women in clean caps, and the food was rich past reason—wildfowl drowned in sauces, fish, a pudding, sweets—and his host talked through all of it, of bloodlines and Madeira and the iniquity of shipping agents, and the wife pushed food around her plate and the candles multiplied themselves in the window glass, and somewhere below the floor or beyond the wall, faint as memory, someone was singing something slow.

When at last the cloth was drawn and the wife had withdrawn and the two men sat alone with brandy Jacob did not touch, the matter was opened, and it went as he had known it would go. There was no money. There was, his host explained, palms turning upward in graceful helplessness, the misfortune of the ship, and the further misfortune of the season, and the necessity of certain purchases that could not in conscience be deferred—and here the man actually gestured at the room, at the candlesticks and the saint and the glass, as though these were necessities, as though conscience had chosen them. The next crop, however. The next crop was a certainty. If Mister Vaark would extend.

"I have extended," Jacob said. "Twice. Paper does not feed my household, sir, nor does the next crop, which is the same crop you sold me two years ago and which has been ripening ever since without ever coming in. I'll have payment or I'll have the law."

The law, the man observed gently, sat in this colony at his own table once a quarter and shot birds over his own fields in autumn. He said it without menace, as one mentions the weather, and that was the worst of it. Then, smoothing the moment as if smoothing a sheet, he proposed the other thing. Goods, then, if not coin. He had goods. He had, in fact—and here he rose, brandy in hand, suddenly hospitable again—goods of the very best kind, and Mister Vaark must come and choose for himself, in the morning, in good light. Tobacco in the warehouse, some plate, and—he said it walking toward the window, his back turned, his voice as smooth as the glass he looked through—hands. He had more hands than the place could profitably employ. A man of Mister Vaark's holdings, far north, land-rich and labor-poor, could take his pick and call the debt square, and both of them would sleep the sleep of the just.

"I don't trade in flesh," Jacob said.

"No man does," said his host, turning, smiling, "until the price is right. Forgive me—a jest. You trade in rum, I think. Ask the rum where it has been."

Jacob slept badly in a bed too soft, under a ceiling too high, listening to a house that ticked and settled around him like something digesting. In the gray of morning he walked out alone before his host was stirring and stood at the head of the lane and considered simply taking his horse and his loss and going. Thirty pounds. He could carry it. It would hurt, and Rebekka would never know the size of the hurt because he would not tell her, and in three years it would be a story he told against himself. He had nearly decided it when the smell of bread reached him from the kitchen house, set apart from the great house in the southern fashion, and his stomach, which had been clenched all night against rich food, turned him toward it without consulting him.

The cook gave him bread and did not look at him, and he sat on a stool by the door like a boy and ate it, and it was the first honest thing he had been given since he stepped off the sloop. The kitchen was full of steam and the smell of fat and the quick economical movements of women who had been up for hours. A small girl in a dress made of sacking stood at the table shelling beans, and when she felt him looking she went still all over, the way a rabbit goes still, and he looked away, ashamed without knowing of what.

The choosing, when it could no longer be put off, was conducted in the yard between the kitchen and the quarters, and his host managed it with the same fluency he brought to everything, naming names, naming skills, naming prices as though reciting psalms. A sawyer. A man good with horses. A woman who could be relied on for ten more years of children, said with a connoisseur's nod. They stood in the sun, the named ones, and Jacob made himself look at them because not looking seemed worse, a squeamishness that cost him nothing and them less, and what he saw in most of the faces was nothing at all—a flat, achieved absence, a door closed and the house behind it emptied long ago. Only the cook looked at him. He noticed it and could not stop noticing it. She stood a little apart with a nursing boy on her hip, the small bean-shelling girl pressed against her skirts, and her eyes were on him with a directness that should have gotten her punished and that she did not lower, and he understood that something was being decided in her that had nothing to do with the prices being recited in the warm air.

"Take her," his host was saying of the breeding woman, "and the matter is closed between us, with my friendship into the bargain."

"No," Jacob said. The word came out of him flat as a plank. "No woman. I keep a small place. My wife—" He stopped. He would not bring Rebekka into this yard even as a word. "Give me the tobacco at honest weight and I'll bear the rest as a loss and a lesson."

His host began on the tobacco, the impossibility of honest weight when the inspectors were thieves, and Jacob stopped listening, because the cook had stepped forward. She did it quietly, with her eyes down now, and she spoke to the master in a Portuguese too rapid for Jacob to follow even if he had known any, and the master answered sharply, and she spoke again, lower, and then—Jacob saw it happen, saw it and did not at first believe it—she pushed the little girl forward by the shoulder. The girl stumbled a step and stood. She had thin legs and a high round forehead and her mother's eyes, and she was looking back at her mother, who was not looking at her. The mother was looking at Jacob, holding the boy's small hand against her chest, and her eyes were doing something he had no word for—not pleading, which he had seen and could withstand. It was more like instruction. She was telling him something with all her might across the yard and the language and the gulf, the way one tells something urgent to a person on a departing ship when the wind takes every word.

"The cook," said his host dryly, "wishes me to inform you that the child is healthy, eats little, and is quick to learn. She wishes me to say"—he paused, with the faint amusement of a man translating an animal—"that you have a kind face. Mothers, senhor. They imagine things."

"I don't want a child," Jacob said, and heard how it sounded, and could not unsay it. The girl stood in the dirt between the two groups of people, in nobody's group, and she did not cry, which was somehow the unbearable part. A child should cry. Her stillness was a skill, and the existence of the skill, the hours and lessons that had gone into making it, rose up in Jacob's chest like water in a tilted boat.

He thought of Rebekka. He thought of the babies they had buried, three in five years, the last one named and the first two not, and of how Rebekka had stood at the small graves with her face closed like a fist, and of Lina, the native woman he'd taken on, hungry for something to mother. He thought—and this thought he despised even as it did its work—that the girl would grow into her hands, that small places needed small workers, that it was rescue, that the alternative for her was this yard and this master and the breeding woman's fate ten years on. Every justification presented itself in good order, like courses at last night's supper, rich and unwholesome, and beneath all of them, mute and real, was the mother's face, still telling him the thing he could not hear.

"Her, then," he said. "Her and the difference in tobacco, weighed in my sight, and we are quit of one another."

The papers were drawn in the cool of the front room by his host's own hand, and Jacob signed his name under a sentence that contained the words one negro girl child as though they were words like any others, like barrel or hundredweight, and the ink dried and the thing was done in less time than the supper had taken. He did not see the parting, if there was one. When he came out the girl was standing by Regina with a bundle the size of a loaf, wearing, he noticed, a pair of shoes far too big for her, a woman's shoes, broken at the heel, and she stood in them as in two small boats. He looked for the cook and found her in the kitchen doorway with the boy on her hip, and he touched his hat to her, not knowing what he meant by it, and her face did not change at all, and that, too, he carried away with him.

He put the girl up behind his saddle, where she held to his coat with a grip light as a bird's, as though she had been taught that even her holding on must not be a burden, and he rode back down the lane of paired trees without looking at the house. At the ferry the ferryman looked at the child and opened his mouth and Jacob looked at the ferryman and the mouth closed. On the far bank the road ran along the river for a time, gold now in the late light, and he found he was talking, telling her things—the horse's name, the names of trees, the distance they would travel, the sloop, the sea, the cold of the north which she could not imagine and which he described badly—not because she answered, for she did not, but because the silence behind him was the mother's silence handed down, and he could not ride inside it.

Toward evening she spoke once. He had stopped to rest the horse where a spring came out of a hillside, and he handed her bread, and she took it with both hands and said, in English, carefully, the way one sets down something breakable: "Do we go to where the mothers are?" He did not understand the question and never would, and he said what men say. He said it would be all right. He said there was a kind woman where they were going. He said she would have shoes that fit.

That night, rolled in his coat under an oak with the girl asleep an arm's length away, breathing in small even sips, Jacob Vaark lay looking up through black leaves at the indifferent stars and did his accounts. The debt was settled. The loss was bearable. He had not traded in flesh; he had accepted a child in lieu of money owed, which the law and the language and every man he knew agreed was a different thing, and he assembled the difference piece by piece in the dark and could not get it to stand. What stood instead, unbidden, was the house on the bluff—the brick, the glass, the lane of trees—and a thought came to him with the warmth and shame of brandy: that he could build such a house. Bigger. Finer. A house with a gate of iron, with grand windows, on his own rise above his own river, built not on the backs of others—he was already telling himself the story—but by his own enterprise, his own sweat, honest money honestly got. Sufficient had been his word. He turned it over now and found it worn thin, like a coin handled too long, the king's face rubbed away.

Beside him the girl murmured in her sleep, in Portuguese or in no language at all, and went quiet. The fire fell in on itself. Far off a dog, or something not a dog, called twice and was answered. Jacob slept at last and dreamed, as he would dream for years without ever recalling it by morning, of fog the color of gold, and of walking through it toward something he could not see, holding tightly to a small hand that he believed, in the dream, was leading him out.
