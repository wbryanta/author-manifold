He sat shivering in the dark with the quilt about his shoulders and his heart going in him like something trapped. What was it? she said. What did ye dream?

Nothing. Go on back to sleep.

You was a-hollerin.

I said it wasn't nothing. Sleep.

She turned heavily in the shucks beside him, one hand riding the mound of her belly, and after a while her breathing went slow and even again. He sat on. Through the chinking of the logs a wind came thin and cold and bore upon it the smell of the river and of dead leaves and of something else, something old, like stone wet in a cave. He listened. Out in the dark a thing called once with a sound like none he could name, neither owl nor fox nor any creature of his ken, and did not call again. He lay back at last with his eyes open in the black and he could hear his own blood passing in his ears like a multitude going by on a road, going somewhere, going away. He did not sleep again that night, or if he slept he did not know it, and he came up out of the gray hour before dawn as a man comes up out of water, gasping a little, with the dream still on him like a caul.

He rose and built up the fire and set the kettle on and stood at the one window watching the light assemble itself out of nothing in the east beyond the trees, a light gray and grudging and without warmth, the trees coming each from the dark in its turn like figures summoned to account. The river was running smoke in the bottoms. When he turned she was sitting up in the bed watching him.

You want some meal? he said.

I best eat I reckon.

He stirred meal into the water and salted it and they ate from the same blackened pot, she on the bed with her bare feet hanging and he on the stool by the hearth, and there was no sound but the click of the spoons and the fire working and far off a crow announcing the day to no one. She watched him while she ate.

I been thinkin, she said.

He didn't look up.

I been thinkin you ort to go and see could you get a woman. To be here. It cain't be long now and I don't know nothin about it, what to do.

There ain't no woman to get.

They's that Mrs Salter on the pike. Or the granny woman they tell about lives up on the . . .

I said there ain't none. You don't know what you're talkin about.

She looked at the floor. I'm afeared, she said. That's all. I never said it before but I'll say it now. I'm afeared.

Women has done it since the start of the world, he said. They done it in fields and in boats and in caves I reckon and nobody to hand them so much as a rag. You'll be all right.

It ain't right, she said, very low, what we . . .

He stood up so quick the stool went over. Hush, he said. You hush about that. There ain't nothin to talk about and there ain't goin to be nobody comin here, do you hear me? Nobody.

She didn't answer. She set her spoon in the pot and turned her face to the wall and he stood a minute with his fists opening and closing at his sides and then he took up the axe from behind the door and went out.

The morning was cold and the grass furred white with frost and his breath hung about him as he went down through the dead garden to the woodlot. He worked all morning splitting rounds of red oak, the axe rising and falling with a measured violence, the wood going apart with a dry shriek, and he did not stop until the sun stood high and small and pale above the ridge like a coin seen through water. Then he sat on the chopping block and looked out over the country. The land lay gray and folded away to the south, ridge over ridge into a blue smoke of distance, and nowhere in all of it a roof or a fence or a thread of chimneysmoke save his own, and that thin and failing. A buzzard wheeled in the high vault, tilting, righting itself, riding some river of air invisible to him, and he watched it until it slid beyond the ridge and was gone. He thought of nothing. He had got good at that or believed he had. After a while he heard it.

It came up out of the south along the old wagon road that nobody used, a thin jangling and clatter, faint and fitful with the wind, and he stood and waited and directly there hove into sight around the bend of the road a tinker laboring along behind a cart, the cart hung all over with pans and buckets and bits of tin that swung and banged and threw the sun in little splinters, and the tinker himself bent into the traces like a beast, a small man and dark, in a hat that age had brought to no color at all. He came on. When he saw the man standing in the lot he raised one hand and called out something lost in his own clamor and came on still and fetched up at last at the edge of the yard, breathing, grinning out of a face like a dried apple.

Howdy, he said.

Howdy.

Right brisk this mornin.

It is.

The tinker let the traces down and straightened himself by degrees, a hand in the small of his back. He looked at the house and at the woodlot and at the man and his grin never altered. I got needles, he said. Pins, thread, buttons. Combs. I got coffee, real coffee, not that chicory they sell ye in town. Got pots and pans as ye can see and I sharpen anything wants a edge. Got remedies. He patted a wooden box lashed atop the cart. Remedies for whatever ails ye, and some, he said, dropping one eyelid slow as a lizard, for what ails her.

There ain't no her.

The tinker's eye went past him to the line strung between two posts by the chimney corner and to what hung there drying, and the man knew without turning what it was the tinker saw and felt the heat come up his neck.

That your washin yander? the tinker said.

I do my own.

I see ye do, said the tinker, and he was grinning still but the grin had changed in some way, had become a thing the face was doing on its own. Well, he said. A man alone needs needles same as the next. Needs coffee.

I don't need nothin.

Everbody needs somethin, the tinker said. That's been my experience of the world and I've went up and down in it a right smart. He squatted by his cart and undid a leather budget and began to lay out his goods on a cloth in the road, naming them as he laid them, awls and snuffboxes and clasp knives and cards of buttons and twists of tobacco and at the last, with a look about him up the empty road and down it, certain small books which he riffled with his thumb and offered up with that lizard's wink again. The man looked at none of it.

I told ye I don't need nothin. I got no money.

The tinker squatted there with his goods spread in the dirt like a man at some mute auction of his own life. He looked up. I'd trade, he said. I'd trade for meal or shucks or a settin of eggs. I'd trade for near anything. I ain't et since yesterday mornin.

We got nothin to trade.

We, the tinker said.

I, the man said. I got nothin.

The tinker began to take up his goods again, slowly, one thing and then another thing, folding the cloth over them, and he did not grin now. When he had lashed the budget shut he stood and took up the traces and settled them and looked at the man a last time.

I come through this country twict a year, he said, and I knowed your daddy when he was livin, and I'll tell ye somethin for nothin since nothin's the trade ye favor. A thing hid don't stay hid. I've went up and down in the world like I said and I never yet seen one that did. The ground itself won't keep a secret. It'll grow ye out a witness, grass or weed or somethin with eyes. You remember I told ye.

Get on, the man said. His voice was not loud but something had come into it and the tinker looked at him and nodded as if some price had been settled between them after all, and he leaned into the traces and the cart lurched and took up its jangling and he went away up the road to the north with his clamor failing slowly in the cold air, a small dark shape and then a sound only and then nothing, and the man stood in the yard a long time after he was gone.

When he came in at evening she was at the fire stirring a pot and she did not turn.

Was they somebody come today? she said. I heared a rattlin.

Wasn't nobody. Tinker passed on the road is all.

He never stopped?

He never had nothin we needed.

She turned then and looked at him and her eyes went over his face the way a hand goes over cloth feeling for the flaw, and he bore it and gave back nothing, and at last she turned to the pot again. They ate in silence and the dark came down and they lay in the bed in the dark, not touching, two people breathing in a black room at the end of the world, and far down in the bottoms the river told over and over its one gray syllable, and that night he did not dream at all, or none that followed him up into the day.

The three came west along the river through that same cold, going as before in their single file with the bearded one ahead, and on the second day they came upon a ferry landing where a flat hung on its rope in the slack water and a cabin stood above it on the bank with smoke standing straight up out of the chimney into the windless morning. They stood looking at it for a while the way cattle will stand looking at a gate. Then they went up. What passed there no one saw. A man had lived at that landing alone with a dog and a Bible and a tin box and when next a traveler came down to the crossing some days on, hallooing the far bank, no answer came, and the smoke was long cold, and the dog lay in the yard with its grin gone sideways, and of the ferryman there was no sign at all then or ever, and the flat swung gently on its rope, and the rope creaked, and the river went on. By then they were miles to the west. They had among them now a tin box with the hasp pried back and a long coat that had not been the bearded one's and they made their fire that night in a sinkhole out of the wind and sat about it eating salt meat that was not theirs either, their faces hung above the coals like things summoned there, and the one who never spoke at all sat turning a small gold piece over and over in his fingers, watching the firelight run on it, until the bearded one held out his hand, and the coin passed, and the dark drew in about them like a drawstring and they slept.

The cold broke and a warm wind came up out of the south and it rained for two days, a gray seething on the roof, the yard gone to standing water, and she moved about the room with her hand at her back, heavier now it seemed by the hour, and would stand long whiles at the window watching the rain come down the glass.

It's soon, she said one evening. I can tell it. It's drapped.

He looked up from the harness he was mending and looked down again. All right, he said.

That's all ye aim to say. All right.

What would ye have me say?

I don't know, she said. Somethin. Anything. She had begun to cry, not loud, her face still to the window. I just don't want to die in this place with nobody knowin I was ever even alive in it.

You ain't goin to die.

You don't know that. You don't know nothin about it, you said it your own self, women has done it in caves. Well I ain't a cave woman. I'm just a girl and I'm afeared and you set there mendin harness for a mule we ain't even got no more.

He sat with the leather in his hands. The fire ticked. After a while he said: I'll boil the water and have rags ready. I helped daddy with the stock enough. It ain't no different.

It is different, she said. But she said it quietly, and to the window, and not to him.

It came at night, as such things will, in the black hours when the fire had fallen to a red mutter and the first he knew was her hand on him again the way it had been the night of the dream, only gripping now, and her voice gone small and strange. It's started, she said. He got up and lit the lamp and built the fire and set water on and she labored. She labored through what remained of the night and through the dawn and into the day, the rain begun again outside, and her cries when they came rang in the little room and went out through the chinking into the dripping woods where nothing heeded them, and he did what he could, which was little, holding her hands when she sought them and wiping her face and saying it's all right, it's all right, in a voice he did not know for his own. In the afternoon it was born. He took it up in his hands slick and bawling, a wrinkled homunculus the color of a skinned rabbit, and he cut the cord with his beltknife as he had seen done with stock and tied it with thread off the spool and wrapped the child in a piece of an old shirt because there was nothing else, and all the while it cried with a thin and rhythmic insistence like something the rain had brought.

Let me see it, she said. Her voice was a husk. Let me just see it.

It's a chap, he said. He did not give it to her. He stood holding it by the fire with his back half to her.

Give it here.

You need to rest.

Give it to me.

He gave it to her because there was no way in that moment not to, and she took it and folded it against her and looked into its furious small face, and what came over her own face then he could not watch, and he went and stood at the window. The rain had stopped. Out over the bottoms a low sun had found a slot beneath the clouds and the wet woods stood drenched in a light of unearthly clarity, every branch distinct, every twig, the river burning like a vein of poured metal, and high up one buzzard, or maybe the same one, rode its wire of air and tilted and was gone.

It needs to suck, she said behind him. She was crooning to it. It needs to and I don't know . . .

It'll figure it out, he said. They do.

In the night she slept at last, the child in the crook of her arm, both of them breathing, and he sat by the fire and did not sleep. He sat there a long time. The fire fell and he did not mend it. He could hear them breathing. Somewhere in the timbers of the house a beetle was at its slow work and he listened to that, and to the river, and to a thought that had been in him so long now it had quit being a thought and become only a place where he lived. He rose at last and took the child up from beside her so gently that neither of them stirred and he wrapped his coat about it and took the lantern unlit from its nail and went out.

There was a moon coming and going in the clouds and he went by that, down through the garden and along the field edge and into the woods, and the woods at night were a country he had never been to though he had walked them all his life, the trees gone strange and tall, the dark between them absolute, and he went on. The child made small sounds against his chest. Hush, he told it. Hush now. He crossed the branch on the stones and went up the far slope through the laurel and came out in a glade he knew, a small bald in the woods, moonlit now, the dead grass silver, and at the center of it he stopped. He stood there a long time. The moon swam in the clouds. He knelt and laid the bundle down in the grass and the child worked one arm free of the shirt and its hand opened and closed on the air, on nothing, on the cold, and he looked at that small hand and then he stood and turned and walked away fast, and then he ran, crashing through the laurel, down the slope, across the branch, his breath sobbing in his chest, and behind him in the glade the thin crying rose and went out over the dark woods where nothing heeded it, or where he prayed that nothing did, and a thing he could not have named went out of the world with every step he took, and would not come back into it.

She was awake when he came in. She was sitting up in the bed with her arms empty and her eyes huge in the lampless dark.

Where is it, she said. Where's it at.

He stood in the door with the night behind him. It died, he said. The words came out of him like things long rehearsed and even so they barely came. It just died. They do sometimes. It wasn't nothin nobody could of done.

She didn't say anything. She didn't cry. That was the worst of it, that she didn't cry, but only sat looking at him across the dark of the little room with her arms folded empty over her belly, and outside the clouds shut on the moon, and the river ran on in the bottoms saying what it said, and far off in the night to the west, by a fire in a sinkhole, three men slept on the cold ground with their mouths gaped to the starless sky, turning, all of them, all that country, slowly toward morning.
