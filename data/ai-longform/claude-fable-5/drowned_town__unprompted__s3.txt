The lake gave the town back in October, in the third year of the drought, and the county sent Lena Mercer to map it because she was the surveyor of record and because, although nobody in the commissioners' office said so out loud, she was the only one left on the payroll who had been born there.

She came down the old boat ramp at first light with the truck in low gear, past the courtesy dock that now stood twelve feet above anything resembling water, its ladder descending into air. The drawdown had been going on all summer, a foot a week, then two, the lake retreating from its rim like a tide that had forgotten how to come back. Where she parked, on a shelf of cracked gray clay printed all over with the tracks of herons and four-wheelers, the shoreline of her childhood maps would have put her under sixty feet of water. Now the water was a dull pewter sheet half a mile off, shrunken into the old river channel, and between her and it lay the town of Carrow, or what the lake had decided to keep of it.

She had seen the photographs people were posting — drone shots, mostly, the grid of streets visible from above like a healed scar, the foundations pale rectangles in the mud. The commissioners wanted documentation. There was talk of the state historical office, talk of looters with metal detectors, talk of liability if some kid broke his leg in a cistern. Get it on record before the rain comes back, they said, as if the rain were a relative whose return date was known. She had agreed to it the way she agreed to most assignments in her sixty-eighth year, with a nod and a request for a field assistant, and had then gone home and stood in her kitchen for a long time with her hand flat on the counter, not thinking anything she could have named.

Theo arrived twenty minutes after her, which was about right for Theo. He was twenty-four, the county's GIS technician, raised in a subdivision that had been a dairy farm, and to him the drowned town was the most interesting thing that had ever happened in Bell County, which was possibly true. He got out of his car already talking, about a man on the internet who claimed there was a safe in the bank vault that had never been opened, and Lena let the talk run off her like weather while she set up the rover, threading the receiver onto the pole, watching the screen hunt for satellites above a town that had been platted by men dragging a sixty-six-foot chain through river-bottom hayfields in 1911.

She had brought the original plat. Not a scan — the linen itself, signed out of the courthouse vault with the clerk watching her hands as though she might eat it. *Town of Carrow, Sorrel River, surveyed and platted by E. R. Mott, County Surveyor, June 1911.* Bearings in degrees and minutes, distances in chains and links, the streets named for trees that mostly hadn't grown there: Linden, Walnut, Sycamore, and then, in a failure of imagination or an excess of hope, Main. Mott's penmanship was beautiful in the way of men who had been beaten into it as boys. Lena had retraced his work a hundred times on paper, reconciling old deeds along the lake's edge, but she had never stood on it. The town had gone under in March of 1964, when she was seven years old, and the law had kept on treating those sixty blocks as real property, conveyed and inherited and taxed at the bottom of Lake Pierce, all the years since.

"So where do we start?" Theo said.

"Where Mott started," she said. "Northeast corner of the original town. There should be a stone."

There was not a stone, of course; there was a depression in the clay where a stone might once have been, and a beer can from some decade with a pull tab she hadn't seen since the seventies, preserved in the anaerobic dark like everything else down here. But forty feet on, half-buried at the lip of what had been the Sorrel River bank, she found the brass disk of a geodetic benchmark set in a concrete post, stamped 1937, its face green-black and its lettering still legible under her thumb: the elevation of a world that had been deliberately put away. She set the rover point on it and watched the numbers settle, and the difference between what the disk said and what the satellites said was inside a tenth of a foot. Something in her chest unclenched slightly. The town still agreed with itself. Whatever else the water had done, it had not moved the ground.

They worked north along Main Street through the morning. The street itself was unmistakable once you learned to read it: a band of harder material, the old chip-seal surface scoured down to its gravel bones, flanked by concrete curbs that surfaced and dove through the silt like the backs of long pale animals. The mud between the curbs had dried and curled into plates, and the plates crunched under their boots with a sound like stepping on saltines. Everywhere the lake had left its signature — a lace of dried algae on every vertical face, drifts of mussel shells banked against the foundations like gray snow, a smell that was not quite rot and not quite mineral, the smell of the lake's bed exposed, which Lena thought she would carry in her clothes for days and did not entirely mind.

The foundations announced themselves in order, and she found that she knew the order. That was the thing she had not prepared for. She had told herself she'd been seven; she had told herself that what she carried was not memory but the residue of her mother's stories and the one shoebox of photographs and her own decades of staring at Mott's plat until the names of the blocks were as familiar as her own deed. But the foundations came up out of the mud in sequence — the long footprint of Detweiler's store with its iron pipe still standing where the awning posts had been, then the smaller square of the barbershop, then the alley, then the bank — and her body knew them before her mind did. Her feet slowed at the alley mouth of their own accord, because the alley was where the older boys had pitched pennies and a seven-year-old girl was supposed to walk fast there and not look. She stood on the buried threshold of Detweiler's and her hand rose, unbidden, to the height where the door handle had been, a brass lever worn gold in the middle, and met nothing, and she lowered it and coded the point into the collector — FND COR, foundation corner — and moved on, and didn't say anything to Theo, who was photographing the bank.

The bank's vault stood alone, the building shorn away around it, a concrete cube with its steel door hanging open on frozen hinges. Whoever had hoped for a forgotten safe had been sixty years too late; the vault had been emptied in 1963 along with everything else worth carrying, and the lake had filled it instead with three feet of silt as fine and dark as cocoa. Theo crouched at the door taking pictures while Lena shot the corners. The relocation, they had called it. The word still had a smooth official sound in her head, like a coin handled by too many people. The Authority had sent appraisers in the summer of '62, men in short-sleeved white shirts who walked the streets in pairs with clipboards, and Lena's clearest memory of that whole year was her mother watching two of them measure the front porch with a tape and saying nothing, just standing inside the screen door with a dish towel over her shoulder, perfectly still, while the men called numbers to each other in the voices of men reading numbers.

They had paid, of course. They had paid what the clipboards said the houses were worth, which was a number arrived at by people who did not have to live inside it, and the families of Carrow had taken the checks and moved up the valley to Hannon and Pierce City and the new brick ranchers along Route 9, and the men of the town — this was the part that Lena's father never spoke of and her mother spoke of only once, late, near the end — the men of the town had been offered work on the clearing crews. Good wages. Better than the mill. And so the fathers of Carrow had spent the autumn of 1963 cutting down their own shade trees and burning their own barns and pulling down their own houses with chains and a county dozer, because anything that might float or foul a propeller had to come down before the water came up, and they had been paid by the hour to do it. Her father had run a chainsaw that fall. He came home every evening smelling of two-stroke smoke and ash and he washed at the outside spigot before he would come in, even in November, even when the water from the spigot was needle-cold, and at the time she had thought it was the dirt he was washing off.

"Ms. Mercer." Theo's voice, careful. He had learned in one morning to be careful. "Is this the school?"

It was the school. Two long parallel foundations and the broad concrete apron where the buses had turned around, and at the edge of the apron, improbably, the lower half of the flagpole, a galvanized stub rising four feet out of the mud with its cleat still bolted on. Lena had attended first grade and most of second in that building. She remembered the cloakroom smell of wet wool, and a teacher named Miss Ainsley who had wept openly at her desk one Friday afternoon in their last spring, and how the children had sat in a silence so complete that Lena could hear the radiator ticking, all of them understanding without being told that the weeping had nothing to do with them and everything to do with all of them. She shot the corners of the school and the base of the flagpole, and she had Theo pull a tape along the apron so they could check it against the plat, and it checked, and there was a strange comfort in that, in the apron being exactly as long as it had always been, neither grown nor shrunk by anything that had happened on it or over it.

They ate lunch on the tailgate, facing the water. The lake out in the channel was the color of an old nickel, and on its far side the drawdown had exposed the approach of the highway bridge that had carried the road out of the valley, the road every family had taken in the end, in trucks loaded the way trucks are loaded by people who do not believe they are really leaving. Theo asked her, around a sandwich, what it had been like — the leaving — and Lena considered the question with more patience than she would have given it from an adult, because Theo asked it the way you'd ask about a shipwreck or a war, an event with edges.

"It was slow," she said. "That's what nobody tells you. You think of a flood as fast. It took the lake two years to fill. The water came up the valley about as fast as a garden grows. We were gone long before it got to our street, so I never saw the house go under. Nobody did. It went under on some particular night, I suppose, with nobody watching. The water doesn't make an occasion of it."

"Did you ever come back? Boat over it, or—"

"My father used to fish the lake," she said, which was true, and was also the entire answer, because what she remembered was being eleven and twelve in the bow of the aluminum johnboat while her father sat in the stern not fishing so much as drifting, the rod untended in his hands, his eyes on the water in a way that she understood even then was a kind of looking down through it, and how he never once said the word Carrow on the lake, not once, though on shore he would say it freely, in stories, where it was safe. On the water the town's name stayed in his mouth like a stone. He had taken work at the dam in 1966, in the powerhouse, and worked there nineteen years, and Lena's mother said he never saw any irony in it and Lena believed that was a thing her mother needed to be true. *I helped put the lid on,* he said once, exactly once, at the kitchen table in Pierce City, late, with whiskey in front of him, and Lena, fourteen, doing homework, had not looked up, and he never said it again.

In the afternoon they worked the residential blocks, and the work changed under them. The commercial foundations had been almost archaeological, impersonal, but the house lots were small and the lake had been gentler with them, and the things that surfaced were domestic. A porch step with the boot-worn dip still in it. A cast-iron bathtub squatting alone on its claw feet in the middle of a lot, the house long burned and dozed from around it, the tub left because it would not float — full now of silt, with a cottonwood seedling growing out of it, two years old by its leaves, a tree that had germinated in a bathtub at the bottom of a lake during a drought, betting everything on the rain staying away. Theo photographed it from six angles. Lena shot its location, coded it, and did not let herself think about it for longer than the shot took, because by then they had crossed Walnut Street and were one block from Sycamore, and she was rationing.

Her mother had kept a doorknob. Lena hadn't known until she cleaned out the house in Pierce City after the funeral and found it in the bottom drawer of the bureau, wrapped in a dish towel — a white porcelain doorknob with a hairline crack, and with it a skeleton key and a small flat tin that had once held throat lozenges and now held buttons and one marble, a green-and-white swirl, the kind they had called a cat's eye. Her mother had never mentioned any of it. Her mother had been, in all the years after, a person of relentless forwardness, who repainted the new house every few years and joined the Methodist women in Pierce City and said, when Carrow came up, that there was no use crying over spilled milk, which was a sentence Lena had needed forty years to hear correctly — to hear the violence of it, a town called spilled milk by a woman who had been carried out of it on a tide of other people's planning. The doorknob in the towel in the drawer was the only rebuttal her mother ever filed. Lena had it still. It was in her own bureau now, in the same towel, because some things you do not transplant out of their wrappings.

Sycamore Street ran east off Main, four blocks, and the Mercer house had stood on the third block, north side, second lot from the corner, and Lena walked it with the rover pole in her hand like a pilgrim's staff and her heart doing something she observed from a small professional distance, the way she'd observe a level bubble drifting off center. The first block: the Hartzells' foundation, the Pruitts', a stone retaining wall still holding its terrace after sixty years of weight and dark. The second block: nothing on the south side but mud, the lots scoured to grade, and on the north a brick chimney base like a tooth. Then the corner, and the iron pipe that she knew without checking would be the lot corner Mott's successors had set when the block was subdivided in 1928, because she had the deed in her files, she had had the deed in her files for thirty years, *Lot 9, Block 14, Town of Carrow,* still vested in the names of Raymond and Dorothy Mercer, both deceased, taxes abated by an act of the legislature in 1965, the parcel carried on the county books all this time like a name kept in a family Bible.

The foundation was low, fieldstone, and it had spread a little, and the front steps were gone, but the threshold stone of the front door was there, a single piece of sandstone four feet long, swept clean by some current of the failing lake as if in preparation. Lena stood at the bottom of where the steps had been. The lot was forty feet wide and the house had been twenty-six, and the space on the west side was where the garden had been, and the space on the east was where the sycamore had stood, the actual sycamore, one of the few trees in town that had honored its street name, and its stump was there, cut a foot above the old grade by her father's saw or a neighbor's, the cut face silted and black but the flare of the roots still gripping the earth in their old attitude of permanence. She had had a swing in that tree. The board of the swing had been a piece of barn siding and the ropes had been hay rope and her father had said, pushing her, *don't pump so high, you'll go over the top,* which was a thing fathers said and not a thing that could happen, and she had pumped higher.

"Theo," she said. "Take the truck back to the school apron and shoot the bus turnaround again. I got a bad fix earlier."

The fix had been fine. He went anyway, either believing her or extending her the kindness of seeming to, and his footsteps crackled away across the mud plates and there was no sound after that but a heron somewhere down the channel and the wind moving over a town with nothing left for wind to move.

She stepped up onto the threshold stone. It held her, as it had held everyone, as it had held the appraisers and the moving men and her mother going out the door the last time with the doorknob, Lena now understood, already unscrewed and wrapped in her purse. Inside the foundation the silt lay smooth and unbroken, a floor more level than the house's floors had ever been, and Lena stood at her own front door and looked into the rectangle of her first rooms and let it come. Not images, mostly. Heat from a wood stove on the right side of her face. Her mother's voice two rooms away, pitch but not words. The specific darkness of the stair landing and the way the third step spoke under any weight. A smell of bread and kerosene and wet wool that no place since had ever assembled in quite that proportion, and that her body had apparently kept, intact, in some drawer of its own, wrapped in some towel of its own, waiting sixty years for the water to go down.

She planted the rover pole on the threshold stone, leveled the bubble, and let the receiver average its position for a long time, far longer than the work required, three hundred epochs, the coordinates refining themselves out past any meaningful precision while she stood holding the pole like a standard-bearer. In the data collector the point wanted a code. The county's schema had codes for foundation corners and curb lines and benchmarks. She keyed past all of them and typed into the description field, with two thumbs, slowly: *MERCER HOUSE — FRONT DOOR — L. MERCER B. 1956 IN NE ROOM.* It would go into the county's record. Some technician in some decade to come would find it and wonder, or not wonder. It was the most unprofessional thing she had done in forty years of practice, and she stored the point and felt the way she imagined her mother had felt with the screwdriver in her hand at the front door, the moving truck idling: not better. Witnessed.

There was one more thing, and she had saved it the way you save the hardest field for last light. The cemetery had been on the rise at the east edge of town, and the rise meant it was the first ground to come back and would be the last to go under again. The Authority had moved the graves in '63 — most of them — to the new cemetery in Pierce City, and Lena had been to those relocated stones, ranked too straight in their new ground like transplants that never quite took. But her father had refused them one. Davy. Born 1959, died 1961, fever, twenty-six months old, a brother Lena remembered only as a warmth and a sound, a baby laughing somewhere below her in the house. When the exhumation men came with their forms her father had met them at the gate of the cemetery and said no, and said it in such a way that the men had written something on the form and gone away, and Davy Mercer had stayed. *Let the water keep him,* her father said, the only sentence anyone ever got out of him on the subject. *He's not going to be dug up by strangers and planted in Pierce City like a shrub.* Her mother had not argued. Her mother had, Lena thought, agreed in the deepest part of herself, the part that kept doorknobs.

The rise stood dry now, fully out, fringed with dead mussels, and the iron fence around the old plot had rusted to lace but still stood, and inside it perhaps a dozen stones remained of the few families who had refused — tilted, scoured, their faces softened by sixty years of water moving slow and patient as a thumb. She found it in the Mercer corner, where she had known it would be: a small stone, half her forearm's length, and on its crown the carved lamb that the monument man in Hannon had cut for all the infants of three counties, worn now nearly back into the stone, a suggestion of a lamb, a memory the rock was having of a lamb. The name was gone. The dates were gone. Lena knelt in the gray clay and put her bare hand flat on top of the stone, on the lamb, the way you'd rest a hand on the head of a child, and the stone was warm. That undid her more than anything in the whole long day — that the stone had taken the October sun all afternoon, up here in the light and the air after sixty years, and was warm, like something that had been waiting to be warm.

She did not shoot the point. The county had no business with this one. She knelt there while the light went long and copper across the mudflats and the streets of Carrow lay all around her in their drowned and patient grid, and out west, over the dam she could not see, the sky was building gray at its base, the first serious gray in three years, and the radio that morning had said rain by the weekend, real rain, the drought's back breaking at last. The water would come back. It would come up the valley about as fast as a garden grows, and find the curbs and the bathtub and the threshold and the lamb, and put the lid back on, with nobody watching, on some particular night. She stayed until she heard the truck returning, Theo's headlights jolting across the flats, and then she stood, and pressed her palm once more to the warm crown of the stone, and walked back down through the town of her birth, taking the streets the long way, by name.
