The invitation was engraved, on heavy cream stock, and it requested the pleasure of my company at the marriage of Stephanie Anne Doyle to Scott Edward Mercer on Saturday the fifteenth of June at half past four in the afternoon, the ceremony and reception to follow on the ocean lawn of the Casa del Rey, on the coast highway south of Santa Barbara. The favor of a reply was requested by the first of May. I held the invitation for some time before I understood that what I was looking at was not the name of the hotel but the fact of the hotel, the fact that the hotel still stood, or stood again, and that people were married on its lawn, and that I had been asked to come and watch.

I had not been to the Casa del Rey since the early morning of October 2, 1970, when I stood on the seawall in a nylon slip and a man's raincoat and watched the south wing burn. I want to be exact about this. I did not "flee" the hotel, as the Santa Barbara paper had it, nor was I "rescued," as my mother told her sister in Modesto, nor did I, as Patsy Doyle has said at every one of the perhaps eleven dinners we have had together in the twenty-six years since, "get out by the grace of God." I was not in the hotel at all. I was on the seawall because there was a Santa Ana blowing that night and the heat in the staff annex was unbreathable and I was nineteen years old and could not sleep, and I had walked down to the water at a few minutes after two in the morning for no reason that I could name then or can name now. I have had twenty-six years to improve upon the question of why I was on the seawall and not in my bed, and the question does not improve.

Nine people died in the fire at the Casa del Rey. Seven of the nine slept in the staff annex. I tell you that not because it proves anything but because it is the kind of fact that, once you know it, arranges all the other facts around itself, the way iron filings arrange themselves around a magnet. The hotel had 114 rooms, and on the night of October 1 it had 96 registered guests, and of those 96 guests two died, an elderly couple from Pasadena named Hartshorn who had taken Room 211 every October since before the war and who were found, the coroner's report said, in their bed, together, "in attitudes of repose." The other seven were a cook, a dishwasher, a gardener, a night porter, and three chambermaids. One of the three chambermaids was Carol Ann Pruitt, who was eighteen years old, who came from Porterville, who set her hair on frozen-orange-juice cans and kept a hope chest under her bed with a dotted-swiss tablecloth in it and clippings from Modern Bride, and who slept four feet from where I would have been sleeping had there not been a Santa Ana that night, had I not been nineteen, had the question improved.

The annex had one interior stairway. There was a second door, at the end of the second-floor corridor, a door that gave onto an exterior wooden staircase, and that door was kept padlocked, because the year before some towels and a case of Lux soap had gone missing and Mr. Brandt, the manager, had decided that the help was walking the inventory down the back stairs at night. I want to be exact about this too. The door was padlocked. The fire marshal's report mentioned the padlock in a single sentence, on page four, and the coroner's jury returned a finding of accidental death, the accident being a cigarette left smoldering in a banquette in the Cabrillo Lounge, and no charge was ever brought against anyone, and Mr. Brandt moved to Scottsdale, where I believe he managed a golf resort, and where I believe he has since died. I have thought about the padlock more or less continuously for twenty-six years, in the sense that one thinks continuously about the weather, which is to say not at all and then entirely, without warning, in the middle of a sentence, in line at a bank.

I replied to the invitation by the first of May. I accept with pleasure, I wrote, because that is the formula, and because Patsy Doyle had been Patsy Whitfield in 1970 and had waited tables in the Casa del Rey dining room and had been the only person in my life since who knew the hotel as a place where we had counted sheets and carried trays, and because Stephanie was her only daughter, and because there are debts that have no instrument and no schedule of payment but are debts all the same. Also — and I will say this only once, because it does not bear saying twice — I wanted to see it. I had driven past the Casa del Rey perhaps six times in twenty-six years, always at highway speed, always with my eyes arranged in a particular way, and I had a feeling, which I did not examine, that the hotel and I had unfinished business, that one of us owed the other a visit.

In 1970 I made a dollar sixty-five an hour at the Casa del Rey and I cleaned fifteen rooms a day, six days a week, and I believed, in the way that one believes things at nineteen, which is to say bodily, without argument, that the job was temporary and the life it belonged to was temporary and that the actual life, the one with my name on it, was being held for me somewhere up the coast, the way the desk held mail for guests who had not yet arrived. I knew the hotel the way you know a body. I knew that the cart took twenty-two sheets and that the sheets were counted out by Mrs. Echevarria, the head housekeeper, who trusted no one, with reason, and that the second-floor corridor of the main building was eleven degrees cooler than the third because of the magnolia outside, and I knew which rooms had ocean views and which had what the desk called garden views, meaning the parking lot and a hedge of oleander, and I knew that the guests who took the garden views left no tips and stripped their own beds out of some instinct of apology, and that the guests who took the ocean suites left dollar bills under ashtrays and the bathrooms of catastrophes. I knew the smell of the place, chlorine and eucalyptus and cut grass and, under it, always, salt, and for many years after the fire I could not be near any two of those smells together. The third I could manage. Two was the threshold. I never understood why two.

Carol Ann Pruitt believed things too. She believed that if you slept with a piece of wedding cake under your pillow you would dream of the man you were going to marry, and she had done it twice, once at her cousin's wedding in Tulare and once at a wedding she had crashed at the hotel itself, slipping a piece of cake into a napkin off the buffet, and both times, she told me, she had dreamed of the same man, a man she had never met, a tall man standing in a doorway with the light behind him so she couldn't see his face. She told me this in our room in the annex with her hair on the orange-juice cans and her face shining with Pond's, and I remember that I laughed, and I remember that she did not, and I remember that she said, with the particular dignity of an eighteen-year-old from Porterville defending the only future she had been issued, that it didn't matter if I believed it, that the cake worked whether you believed it or not.

The wedding was called for half past four. I left Sacramento at seven in the morning, which gave me five hours for a four-hour drive and an hour I did not account for to myself, and I drove down 99 and over the Grapevine and along the coast with the radio off, and at a quarter past one I passed the sign. CASA DEL REY RESORT AND SPA, the sign said, in a typeface that had been designed, you could see, to suggest the 1920s without committing to them, and below that, on a changeable letterboard of the kind that the original hotel would never have permitted, WELCOME MERCER DOYLE WEDDING. I drove past it. I drove four miles up the highway to a motel called the Wayfarer, where I had reserved a room, and I checked in, and the girl at the desk asked if I was there for the wedding at the Casa del Rey, everybody this weekend was, and I said yes, and she said it was supposed to be beautiful down there, the weddings, they did them right on the water, and I said I had heard that.

Patsy had offered to get me a room at the hotel itself. The wedding had a block of rooms, a rate. I told her it was a question of money. It was not a question of money.

I want to describe the hotel as it is now, because the description is the story, or as much of the story as I am able to tell in order. The Casa del Rey burned in October of 1970 and was rebuilt by 1973 and was sold in 1979 and again in 1988, and it is operated now by a hospitality group out of Dallas, and the brochure in my motel room — the Wayfarer kept a rack of them — called it "an oceanfront legend, lovingly restored in the grand tradition of the California coast." Lovingly restored is not, strictly speaking, accurate. The main building is new from the foundation. The tower is new. The arcade is new, though the arches reproduce the old arches closely enough that for one moment, getting out of the car in the June overcast, I felt the afternoon tilt. What is original is the seawall, and the row of Canary Island palms along the drive, which survived because palms do, because a palm will stand there burning like a torch all night and put out green in the spring, and a single magnolia, and the floor of the old lobby, the Spanish tile, which they pulled out of the rubble and relaid in the new lobby, and which a brass plaque beside the concierge desk describes as dating from the hotel's "storied past." The plaque does not say what the story was. I stood on the tile for a while. People went around me.

Where the staff annex stood there is now a wing of junior suites and a spa. I knew this before I came; I had called the hotel in May, on a pretext I no longer remember, and asked the girl who answered what was on the south end of the property, and she had named the spa and its treatments. Standing on the lawn at four o'clock with a glass of lemonade that a boy in a white jacket had given me, I worked out the geometry. The fitness center occupied, as nearly as I could determine, the airspace of the second-floor corridor. The padlocked door would have opened into what is now a room where, according to the brochure, one could take a class called Sunrise Stretch at seven each morning. There is no marker. I had not expected a marker. Hotels do not put up markers, for the same reason the desk does not give out room numbers of the recently deceased, and I understood the reasons, and I stood on the lawn understanding the reasons and counting, without having decided to count, the exits. Four sets of French doors from the ballroom. Two exterior staircases on the new wing, steel, code-built, unlocked — I had walked over earlier and put my hand on the latch of the lower gate, and it had swung open, and I had stood there with the gate open in my hand until a gardener looked at me, and then I had closed it. Sprinkler heads in every soffit. I counted eleven sprinkler heads from where I stood on the lawn. The lemonade had mint in it.

The fog burned off at four-twenty, which is the kind of thing that happens at weddings on that coast and gets called a blessing. The string quartet played Pachelbel, because string quartets at weddings play Pachelbel, and Stephanie came down the grass aisle on Dan Doyle's arm in a dress with a ten-foot train that two small girls had been deputized to manage and could not, and the train dragged and gathered cut grass the whole length of the lawn, and no one minded, and Patsy cried, and the minister said the words, and behind the minister the Pacific lay flat and pewter-colored and entirely indifferent, the way it has lain behind every human arrangement ever made on that lawn, and at seven minutes to five Stephanie Anne Doyle was married. I checked my watch when the minister said it. I could not tell you why. It is a habit I have, fixing the time of things. The fire was reported at 2:47 in the morning. The Hartshorns' clock, the coroner's report noted, had stopped at 3:15. These are the kinds of facts I keep, the way Carol Ann kept clippings.

At the reception I sat at table nine with a deaf great-aunt from Fresno and two couples from the groom's side, orthodontists and the wives of orthodontists, and one of the wives asked me how I knew the family, and I said that Patsy and I had worked together when we were girls, and she asked where, and I said here, actually, at this hotel, and she said how wonderful, it must be so changed, and I said yes, it was changed. She waited a moment, in case there was more, and there was not, and she turned to the great-aunt and began to speak loudly about the centerpieces. I want to record that I was grateful to her. There is a courtesy in people that operates below their knowledge, a way they have of stepping around a hole in the floor they cannot see, and the wife of the orthodontist stepped around it, and the band played "Moon River," and the boy in the white jacket brought champagne, and the lights came up in the arcade as the sky went down, and the new hotel was, I will say it, beautiful. That was the thing I had not prepared for. I had prepared for grief and I had prepared for anger and I had prepared, most carefully, for nothing at all, for the possibility that I would stand on the lawn and feel the way one feels at the rebuilt anything, which is to say nothing, a mild architectural interest. I had not prepared for the hotel to be beautiful, for the palms to be lit from below, for the tile to shine, for the whole apparatus of pleasure to be running again as smoothly as if it had never stopped, as if the apparatus itself had never noticed. Of course it had never noticed. The apparatus does not notice. That is what the apparatus is for.

Patsy found me at the edge of the dance floor during the band's break. She had Dan's tuxedo jacket over her shoulders and her shoes in her hand and she stood next to me without saying anything for a while, the two of us watching Stephanie laugh at something in the middle of a circle of bridesmaids, and then she said, not looking at me, "I asked her if she wanted to get married somewhere else. When they came to look at it. I told her there were other places."

"It's the nicest place on this part of the coast," I said. "It always was."

"She doesn't know," Patsy said. "I mean she knows. She knows there was a fire, she knows people — she knows the history. She doesn't know I was here. She doesn't know you were." She turned the shoes over in her hand. "I couldn't see what it would give her."

"Nothing," I said. "It would give her nothing." And that was true, and we both knew it was true, and we stood there a while longer inside the truth of it, two women in their middle forties at the edge of a dance floor, and what I thought about, while the band came back and tuned and Patsy was pulled away by a cousin, was the morning after the fire, when the two of us had stood on the highway shoulder with the other survivors and the Red Cross blankets, and a deputy had gone down the line writing names in a notebook, and when he got to me he asked, "Guest or employee?" — those words, in that order — and I had not understood for a moment what he was asking, and Patsy had answered for me. Employee, she said. Both of us. He wrote it down and moved on. I think about the notebook sometimes. Two columns. I never saw it, but I know how it was ruled.

They cut the cake at nine. It was a tall cake, five tiers, white on white, sugar magnolias, and Stephanie and Scott did the business with the linked arms and the smashed frosting, and everyone applauded, and the staff — and I watched the staff all evening, I will admit that, I watched the girls in the black skirts clearing glasses and I knew their feet were finished and I knew which of them was new by how she stacked, I cannot help knowing it, it is in the hands — the staff wheeled the cake away and brought it back in slices on china plates with a fork laid across each one at four o'clock, which is correct, which is how Mrs. Echevarria taught it, the fork at four o'clock, and a girl set a plate in front of me, and I looked at it for a long time.

I did not eat it. I sat with the plate in front of me through the toasts — Dan's toast, in which he cried, and the best man's toast, in which there was a story about a houseboat — and through the first hour of dancing, and when the table broke up and the great-aunt was taken home, I folded the slice of cake into a cocktail napkin, the way you would fold anything you did not intend to explain, and I put it in my purse, and I found Patsy and kissed her, and I told Stephanie, who did not know who I was and was radiant anyway, radiant in the indiscriminate way of brides, that I wished her every happiness, and I walked out through the lobby across the old tile, and a valet brought my car, and I tipped him five dollars, and I drove four miles up the highway with the windows down.

There was a wind coming up off the land. Not a Santa Ana — June is wrong for it, the wind in June comes off the water by every rule there is — but it came off the land that night, dry and warm and smelling of the hills, and I drove through it and did not turn the radio on. At the Wayfarer the office was dark and the ice machine hummed in its alcove and somewhere a television was on with the sound low. My room was Room 14. I note the number because I note numbers. I hung the silk dress in the alcove that was meant for a closet, and I washed my face, and I took the napkin out of my purse and unfolded it on the bedspread. The cake had crushed somewhat in the purse. The sugar magnolia had broken at the stem. I sat on the edge of the bed and I thought about Carol Ann Pruitt explaining to me, with her hair in the juice cans and her face shining, that the cake worked whether you believed it or not, and I thought that she was eighteen years old when she said it and that she has been eighteen years old now for twenty-six years and will be eighteen years old forever, in Porterville, in a plot I have visited twice and could drive to without a map, and that the tall man in the doorway with the light behind him never did come for her, or came in the one form nobody's dream is supposed to mean, and I turned off the lamp and put the cake, napkin and all, under my pillow, because there was no one to see me do it and no one to laugh, and because it works whether you believe it or not.

I lay there for a long time listening to the ice machine and the wind in the motel oleanders and the trucks going north on the highway, the long haul up the coast, and at some point I slept. In the morning the napkin had come apart and there was sugar in the bed, and I shook out the sheets myself before I left, the way the guests with the garden views used to do, out of some instinct of apology, and I drove home. I had dreamed, as far as I can tell you, of nothing at all. No doorway, no man, no light behind him. Or this: I dreamed of nothing, and woke at 2:47 with the wind moving the oleanders, and lay in the dark of Room 14 counting the exits, of which there was one, and the smoke detector's small light burned red and steady on the ceiling above me, and I watched it the way you watch a thing you have decided to trust, and after a while it was morning, and that is all, and I am telling you the time because the time is the part I am sure of.
