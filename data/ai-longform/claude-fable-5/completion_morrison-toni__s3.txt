He knew he might ride for hours with no company but geese flying over inland waterways, and suddenly, from a copse or a stand of pine, come upon a vagabond, a fugitive, a party of armed men with reasons of their own for being where the road was empty. So he kept Regina at a trot, watched the spaces between trees, and let his mind do what it always did on long rides: tally, sort, remember, plan. The trail was soft from a week of rain and the air so thick with pollen and the smell of brine that breathing was a kind of eating. Mosquitoes rose in clouds from standing water; he pulled his hat lower and gave himself over to the rhythm of the mare, who seemed to know the path better than he did and lifted her feet daintily over roots as though she disapproved of them.

The fog he had walked through still clung to him in patches of feeling. Gold, he kept thinking. Like moving through metal made soft. It was nothing like the fogs of his boyhood, which were gray and wet and smelled of coal smoke and the river, fogs you wore like a sodden coat. He had been born into one of those, more or less; his mother, a girl of no consequence, dying as he arrived; his father, a Dutchman gone before that, leaving him nothing but a surname that sounded like a cough and the long bones that now made him too tall for most beds. He had been a ward of the parish, then a child of the poorhouse, where he learned his letters from a curate with a withered arm and learned everything else from boys whose ambition was a full belly and whose cunning, had it been capital, would have purchased counties. He did not hate that life. He had decided long ago not to hate it, the way a man decides not to limp though the leg pains him, because hatred of one's beginnings was a tax paid forever on money never received.

What he hated, what he allowed himself to hate, was waste. Cruelty was waste of a particular, showy kind, and he had seen enough of it dispensed by men of quality to know that quality was a costume. The orphanage master who flogged boys for the pleasure of the sound; the magistrate who sentenced a girl of twelve to branding for a stolen loaf and went home to a goose dinner; the gentlemen here, in this raw and dripping country, who had written into law their right to do anything at all to certain persons and called the law order. Jacob Vaark had come up from nothing and he meant to rise, but he intended to rise the way water rises, by patience and by finding the level honestly. He lent money. He traded goods—fur, lumber, iron pots, rum when he had to, though rum sat uneasy with him. He did not deal in flesh. It was a point of some pride with him, perhaps the only pride he permitted to show, that there was nothing in his books a Christian could blush at, although he knew Christians, plenty of them, who blushed at nothing.

The inheritance had been the great surprise of his life and he still turned it over in his mind like a coin found in the lining of a coat. An uncle—a man he had never met, never heard named without contempt—had died possessed of one hundred and twenty acres of patroon land in a northern colony, half meadow, half wood, dotted with the kind of stones that broke plows and built fine walls. The letter finding him had taken two years, passed hand to hand through clerks and shipmasters, and when at last it reached him he was twenty-two, working a ledger in a countinghouse and sleeping under the stairs. He read it three times by candle stub and did not sleep at all. Land. To a boy who had owned nothing, not even the name they called him by, land was not property. It was a body. It was a self that could not be flogged or transported or sentenced. He took ship within the year.

Now he was past thirty, and the land had a house on it, modest but tight against weather, and a wife in the house, and graves behind it. He let the graves come into his mind and then made them leave, the way he always did, by attending hard to the present: the trail bending, a doe and her fawn flowing across it like brown water, the mare's ears swiveling. Rebekka had crossed the ocean to marry him sight unseen, sent by a father glad to have one less mouth and a daughter glad—she told him later, laughing, in the dark—to escape a country where the choices for a girl of her station were servant, prostitute, or wife, and wife to whom was the only question her father considered. She had come down the gangway green from the voyage, squinting in the light, and looked up at him with frank assessment, like a farmer at a fair, and something in his chest, some latch he had not known was fastened, came open. They suited. That was the word she used and it was the right one, plain as bread. They suited.

The children did not stay. That was how he put it to himself, because the other ways of putting it could not be said even silently. Three sons, none living past the cutting of teeth, each small grave dug by his own hands because he would not let another man do it. And the girl, Patrician, who lasted five years and was the light of the place, killed by the kick of a horse he had bought cheap and should not have. He had shot the horse, which solved nothing, and watched Rebekka go somewhere behind her own eyes for a season, and come partway back. The farm went on. Lina, the native woman he had purchased—no, hired; he made himself say hired though money had changed hands with a Presbyterian—Lina ran the dailiness of it with a competence that shamed them both, and the place needed more hands, and the hands kept coming and going, and this was the shape of his life: a good wife, good land, no heir, and a long reach of years ahead that wanted filling with something. Lately the something had begun to take a shape he did not entirely like, a shape with gables and a double chimney, but he did not look at that thought directly. He was here on business.

The business was a debt, and the debt belonged to a tobacco man in Maryland, a Catholic, a Portuguese by extraction, who had taken delivery of goods and credit from Jacob's syndicate and then watched a third of his crop rot in a wet year and a shipload of his purchased Africans die of some flux before they could be landed, and who now, by the letters of his factor, proposed to discuss the matter like gentlemen. Jacob had small faith in the discussion. Men who proposed to discuss debts like gentlemen meant to pay them like gentlemen, which was to say never, or in promises, or in some third thing the creditor did not want. But the sum was too large to write off and too awkward to sue for in a Papist proprietorship where the courts dined at the debtor's table. So he had come himself, by sloop and saddle, to look the man in the eye, which in Jacob's experience was worth more than any instrument with a seal on it.

Toward noon the trail joined a cart road and the cart road ran down to a river too wide to ford, where a ferry worked on a rope—a raft of squared logs and a ferryman the color of the river itself, gray-brown and patient. While they waited for a wagon to be brought aboard, Jacob fell into talk with the wagon's owner, a leathery smallholder hauling barrel staves, who spat over the side and offered opinions free of charge. Tobacco was down. Tobacco was always down, except when you had none, and then it was up. The big men along the rivers were eating the little men one farm at a time, lending against the next crop and taking the land when the crop failed, and the law was a dog that bit whoever the big men pointed at. The smallholder said all this without heat, the way a man describes weather. He asked Jacob his trade and Jacob said merchandise and lending, and the man looked at him a moment and said, no offense meant, that lenders and big men were two names for one animal, and Jacob said, no offense taken, that he had been poor longer than the man had been alive, which was not quite true but true enough in the spirit. The ferryman laughed at that, a sound like the rope creaking, and said nothing else the whole crossing.

On the far bank the country changed. The woods fell back and the fields began, long green rooms of them walled in rail fence, and in the fields, bent figures, dozens, then scores, hoeing between the rows in a heat that made the air shake. Jacob had seen Africans before, in ports, on decks, in the kitchens of taverns, but he had not before ridden for an hour through a country that they tilled entirely, mile upon mile, while the only white faces he saw were two boys on horseback at the margins, idle, with whips coiled at their saddles like sleeping snakes. The figures in the rows did not look up at his passing. That was the thing he found he could not stop noticing. A horseman on a road draws eyes the way a thrown stone draws ripples; it is nature. These rows had been taught out of nature. He rode on with the back of his neck prickling, and told himself it was the heat.

The plantation he sought announced itself a mile before its gates with a smell: cured leaf, sweet and brown and heavy, underlaid with something less pleasant, privies and mud and too many bodies. Then the gates themselves, brick pillars topped with stone balls, absurd in that wilderness, like cufflinks on a scarecrow, and a name worked in iron above them in letters Jacob could not at first make out and then could: a Portuguese word, something boastful about joy or glory. The drive beyond was lined with young sycamores set at intervals so regular they offended him, he could not say why—perhaps because each tree must have been carried and planted and watered by hands that would have been better used elsewhere, and the trees existed for no purpose but to lengthen the approach and instruct the visitor in his smallness.

The house, when it appeared, was the largest he had seen in the colonies. Brick below, timber above, glazed windows in ranks, a roof of slate that must have come over as ballast and cost the worth of a small farm to raise. Before it, a sweep of raked gravel; behind it, visible past the corner, the gray weathered backs of outbuildings and quarters going down toward the river in diminishing order of dignity, like a procession arranged by rank. A boy ran out to take Regina, an African boy of perhaps ten, barefoot, in a clean shirt clearly issued for the purpose, and Jacob dismounted and stood a moment flexing the ride out of his legs and looking at the door, which was painted a blue so deep it was nearly black and fitted with a brass knocker shaped like a fist.

The man of the house came out himself, which told Jacob something, since great men did not come out themselves unless they wanted something or feared something, and a debtor wanted and feared both. He was younger than Jacob expected, soft through the middle, dressed for an audience with a governor: a coat with worked buttonholes, a wig in this heat, shoes with heels that crunched the gravel in small mincing bites. His greeting was elaborate and his English good, furred over with accent. He pressed refreshment, lamented the roads, praised the mare, asked after the voyage, all of it skimming and quick, and his eyes the whole while did sums. Jacob knew the eyes. He had eyes like them himself and was not flattered to recognize them on a man he had come to dislike before arriving.

Within, the house was cool and dim and crowded with intention. Turkey carpets on tables, a clock with a painted face, silver candlesticks ranked on a sideboard like grenadiers, a portrait of the host got up as a Roman, one plump hand on a globe. Everywhere Jacob's eye fell it found money spent to be seen spent, and beneath the beeswax and lavender he caught, faintly, the same smell as the fields, the smell of the system that paid for it all, seeped into the plaster. A wife appeared, small, pretty in a finished way, draped in silk the weather punished her for wearing; she spoke charming nothings and studied Jacob with the particular intensity of a woman calculating whether a guest was a danger to her household, and Jacob bowed over her hand and thought: she knows the accounts better than he does.

Dinner was an offense of plenty. Two kinds of fowl, a ham, a fish brought up from the river that morning, jellies, a pudding, Madeira poured by silent men in livery whose feet were bare beneath the fine coats. Jacob ate moderately and drank less and listened. His host talked of the proprietor's court, of his cousins in Lisbon, of the iniquity of shipmasters, of the flux that had carried off—he said it exactly thus—a third part of my cargo before ever it was landed, sir, an act of God against which no man may be held, and at this he looked at Jacob with eyes rounded into innocence, and Jacob looked back and said mildly that God's acts were between a man and God, but the bills were between a man and Jacob Vaark, and bore signatures.

The discussion that followed lasted into the candlelight and went as Jacob had known it would. There was no money. There was tobacco in the sheds, but pledged. There was next year's crop, and the year after's, words spent like coin by a man who had no coin. There was, his host suggested, leaning forward with the air of one conferring a favor, property of another kind. He had, he said, more hands than he could presently employ. Seasoned, healthy, some with skills. He would be desolate to part with any, but to satisfy a debt of honor between gentlemen—and here the Madeira made a small ceremony of being poured again—he would open his quarters to Senhor Vaark's selection. Take your pick, the round eyes said, and call us square.

Jacob set his glass down. He said that he did not trade in slaves. His host received this with the brief polite blankness of a man told that his guest did not trade in air, and then smiled and said that he understood entirely, that the northern colonies were differently circumstanced, but that surely a man of business understood also that value was value in whatever vessel it traveled, and that a strong hand sold readily in any port between Boston and Barbados, so that to accept was not to keep, and to keep was not to approve, and in any case—the smile widening—what alternative did Senhor Vaark propose? The sheds were pledged. The crop was God's. The courts, he did not say, are mine, but the candle flames said it for him, steady in that windless room.

Jacob sat in his anger and examined it, as was his habit, to see how much of it was principle and how much was being beaten. Some of each, he concluded. He had ridden a week to collect a debt and was being offered payment in a coin he had sworn never to handle, by a man who knew he had sworn it, or guessed, and enjoyed the knowing. To refuse outright was to ride home empty. To accept was to become, in his own ledger, the thing he had told the smallholder at the ferry he was not. He thought of his graves, for no reason he could name. He thought of the fog, the hot gold of it, how a man inside it could see his own boots and nothing more, and how he had kept walking anyway because standing still in fog was the one sure way to be lost. He said at last, in a level voice, that he would look. Looking committed him to nothing. His host rose at once, delighted, calling for lanterns, as though Jacob had proposed a walk among roses.

The quarters were down the slope, two rows of cabins facing each other across a lane of beaten earth, and the lanterns made the lane into a tunnel of moving light through which the inhabitants were summoned and arranged. Jacob had seen men sold; he had never walked a line of them with a lantern lifted to each face by a third party, the faces still and shuttered, the eyes aimed past his shoulder at the dark, and he found that his own face had gone still and shuttered too, that some treacherous instinct of commerce in him was assessing shoulders and teeth and the soundness of legs even as his stomach turned. His host kept up a patter, this one a smith, this one bred to horses, this one strong as any two, and the woman there a fine cook, and Jacob heard it as one hears rain on a roof, and walked, and hated the walking, and did not stop.

Near the end of the line, by the last cabin, a woman stood with two children. She was perhaps thirty, straight-backed, with a cloth tied over her hair, and she held a small boy on her hip, a nursing child still by the look of him, fat-cheeked, asleep against her shoulder. Beside her, half hidden in her skirts, stood a girl of seven or eight, thin, with great waiting eyes, wearing a woman's cast-off shoes so far too large that she had the look of a wading bird. Jacob would have passed them as he had passed the others, with a nod that committed nothing, but the woman did a thing none of the others had done. She looked at him. Not past him, not through him—at him, a long deliberate look that took in his boots and his hands and his face and lingered on his face, reading it as he had felt no one read it since Rebekka on the gangway, and what she found there seemed to decide her, because she shifted the sleeping boy higher on her hip, and with her free hand moved the girl forward, out of her skirts, into the lantern light.

His host, mistaking everything, began to speak of the cook again, but Jacob did not hear him. The woman said something. Her voice was low and the words were for him alone and half of them in no tongue he knew, but the half he knew was plain. Take the girl, she was saying. Her, the words said, and the hand on the girl's thin shoulder said it, and the eyes said it, fixed on his with an urgency that had nothing in it of selling and everything in it of throwing a child from a burning house into the arms of a stranger because the stranger's arms were not the fire. Take my daughter. The little boy slept on her hip, and her hand held him close, and her eyes gave the girl away, and Jacob stood in the lane between the lanterns and understood that he was being asked to perform a mercy by committing the very act he had come down the slope despising, and that the woman had seen in his face—God knew what she had seen in his face. Reluctance, perhaps. Perhaps that was all it took in this place to look like deliverance: a man who hesitated.

The girl looked up at him. She did not cling to her mother. She stood where she had been put, in her terrible shoes, and looked up at him with an attention so complete it was like being touched, and Jacob Vaark, who did not trade in flesh, who had a wife gone quiet and four graves behind his house and a debt in his pocket worth more than this child's price ten times over, heard himself say, before the thinking part of him had finished its sums, that he would consider the girl. His host began at once to protest her age, her smallness, to offer the cook instead, the cook being valuable and the man being a fool even in his cunning, and the woman's eyes never left Jacob's face, and somewhere up the slope, in the great house, the clock with the painted face was striking an hour no one in the lane had any use for.
