The ferry left Port Hardy in the last hour of light, and for a while the town stayed visible behind them—the fuel dock, the breakwater, a few houses with their windows already lit against the early dark of October—and then the rain came down the strait like a curtain drawn by hand and there was nothing but water.

Eino Maki stood at the stern rail until his coat soaked through. He was sixty-one years old. He had not seen his brother in twenty-three years.

In his breast pocket was the letter from the coroner's office in Prince Rupert, folded into quarters, and folded inside it a photograph of two boys on a float in Rough Bay, 1959, one of them holding a dogfish up by the tail. He could no longer say with certainty which boy was which. They had been that alike, for a few years. Then they had not been alike at all.

The deceased is a male of approximately sixty years of age, recovered from the harbour on the morning of October 9. The deceased was known locally by the name Maki. We would ask that you attend at your earliest convenience.

Known locally by the name. As if a name were a coat a man put on in one town and left hanging in another.

Inside, the passenger lounge smelled of wet wool and diesel and the cafeteria's gravy. A tree planter slept across three seats with her boots still laced, her hands folded under her cheek the way children are drawn sleeping in old books. Two truckers played cribbage without speaking, moving the pegs with great delicacy, their hands huge and scarred and gentle on the small board. An old Heiltsuk woman sat upright by the window with a paper cup of tea, going home to Bella Bella, watching the dark go by as if it were country she recognized field by field.

Sixteen hours up the Inside Passage. He had a cabin booked, a berth, a metal sink that folded out of the wall. He would not sleep. He knew this the way you know weather coming, in the joints, in the chest.

Veikko. Even the name had gone strange in his mouth from disuse. As a boy he had shouted it a thousand times a day across the water, down the boardwalk, up into the cedar where his brother climbed too high, always too high, his white-blond head among the branches like something nesting there. Veikko, come down. Veikko, wait. Their mother calling it from the porch in her low voice that carried farther than shouting, the two syllables crossing the garden, the woodpile, the drying nets, finding the boy wherever he was hidden.

In Sointula in those years everyone's name was like that—Toivo, Helmi, Aino, Urho—names carried over an ocean by people who had come to build heaven on an island and had built instead a fishing village, which was perhaps the same thing in the end, the dream gone modest, gone practical, settled into net lofts and a co-op store and a graveyard among the salal where the headstones argued quietly in two languages.

Their father, Toivo Maki, had a gillnetter called the Helmi K, named for their mother, and the brothers grew up in the smell of bluestone and cedar corks, in the green underwater light of the net loft where the mended web hung down like the insides of some great instrument. Their father could put his hand into a heap of net and know by touch where the tear was. He spoke perhaps two hundred words a day and most of them concerned weather or fish. He was not unkind. He was a wall a boy could lean against, and they leaned, both of them, and did not know what they had until the wall was gone.

Eino went down to the cafeteria and ate because the body asks to be fed even on such a night, especially on such a night. Halibut and chips, a smear of tartar in a paper cup. Through the window the lights of a tug went by southbound, towing something low and enormous he could not see, only the strain of it, the tug's stern dug down into its own wake.

You always ate fast, he thought. As if someone might take it.

He had begun, somewhere off Pine Island, to address his brother directly. He noticed this the way you notice you have been humming. There seemed no reason to stop.

You ate fast and you swam farther out than anyone and you laughed in church, that high broken laugh, and Pastor Salo looked at our mother and our mother looked at her hands. You were the one strangers remembered. I was the one who put the tools back.

The first thing Veikko stole was nothing—plums from the Anderson trees, everyone stole those. The second was a skiff, taken at midnight and returned by morning with a thwart cracked, and the third was money from the co-op till where he worked the summer he turned seventeen, and after that there was no counting, the thefts becoming a weather around him, small and constant, until he left the island at nineteen on a packer headed north and the house went quiet in a way that was partly grief and partly, God forgive them all, relief.

He came back, of course. Men like Veikko always came back, between seasons, between women, between versions of themselves. He would step off the ferry with a duffel bag and a new scar and gifts that were too expensive and slightly wrong—a silk scarf for their mother who wore only wool, a German knife for their father who had used the same puukko for forty years—and for two weeks the house would be loud and bright and exhausting, and then one morning the bed would be empty and something else would be missing, the binoculars, the chainsaw, forty dollars from the coffee tin, and their mother would say nothing, only set three places at the table instead of four, her face like the bay in November.

Eino went out on deck. The rain had stopped or rather the ship had moved out from under it. The strait here was open to the west, to Queen Charlotte Sound, and the swell came in long and slow from the dark, lifting the ferry and setting it down, lifting and setting down, the rhythm of a thing breathing in its sleep. He could see no land. He could see the green and red of the running lights and, far off, a single white speck that might have been a troller anchored in a nook of the invisible coast, a man asleep in there with his stove going, his coffee pot wedged behind the rail.

This was the water that had taken their father.

September 1972. Cape Caution, which is not a name anyone laughs at who has fished there. The Helmi K running south loaded with dog salmon, too loaded, and the southeaster coming up faster than the forecast, and Veikko aboard as crew because Eino had stayed in town that trip—he was getting married in three weeks, there was a house to fix, he had asked his father's permission to miss the trip and his father had nodded once and that was all.

The boat went over off the cape sometime before dawn. What happened in the wheelhouse in those last hours no one would ever fully know, because the only man who came out of the water was Veikko, found eleven hours later by a seiner, lying across a hatch cover with his arms through the lifting straps, so cold he could not speak for a day. Their father was never found. The sea kept him entirely, the way it keeps perhaps one drowned man in three, taking him into its administration, its archives, beyond petition.

What Veikko said, when he could speak: the lashings on the deckload let go. The fish shifted. She went over in two minutes, maybe three. Father was below, off watch, asleep.

What the island said, in kitchens, on floats, never to either brother's face: Veikko was on watch. Veikko had been drinking in Namu the night before they left; men had seen him. Veikko ran her too close in under the cape to save an hour. Veikko, Veikko. The name their mother had carried from Finland in her body like a seed, turned over and over in other people's mouths until it was polished into accusation.

Eino had never asked him. That was the thing he carried, heavier every decade. He had stood beside his brother at the memorial service—no coffin, only a photograph of the Helmi K on an easel and the church full of rubber-booted men—and he had not asked, and his silence had been a question anyway, and Veikko had heard it. Of this Eino was certain. His brother had stood there in a borrowed suit with his hands still bandaged from the hatch-cover straps, and somewhere in that hour he had heard the question his brother was not asking, and a door had swung shut between them so quietly that it took both of them years to hear the latch.

The ship's intercom announced, in a tired voice, that the cafeteria was closing. The Heiltsuk woman had fallen asleep sitting perfectly upright, her tea finished, her hands in her lap. The tree planter had turned over. One of the truckers had won at cribbage; the board was put away; they sat now looking at nothing, side by side, like men in a pew.

In his cabin Eino lay on the berth with his boots on and the lamp lit and took the postcards out of his bag.

There were nineteen of them, spanning twenty-three years. He kept them in a cigar box that had been their father's, that still smelled faintly, impossibly, of tobacco half a century gone. The cards were unsigned. They carried no messages. Only his own name and address in his brother's handwriting—that headlong hand, the letters leaning forward like men walking into wind—and the postmarks, and the pictures.

Prince Rupert, the harbour at dusk. Stewart. Ketchikan. A grizzly on a card from Hyder, Alaska. Ocean Falls in the rain, the great ruined mill. Masset. Prince Rupert again, and again, the town recurring through the years like a chord a hand keeps returning to. The last one had come four years ago: a humpback whale breaching, the kind of card sold in a wire rack by a till, and on the back nothing, nothing, only the name Eino Maki and the address of the house in Sointula where Eino still lived, where their mother had died, where the plum trees their father planted had grown old and hollow and still each August produced fruit.

He had answered none of them, because there was nothing to answer and no return address ever, and he understood after a while that they were not letters. They were soundings. A man on a dark coast, dropping a line over the side every year or so to confirm the bottom was still there. I am alive. You are alive. The water between us is this deep.

The last time they had spoken was at their mother's funeral, March of 1991, sleet coming sideways off Rough Bay. Veikko had arrived on the morning ferry already drunk, or drunk again, in a good black coat with salt stains at the hem, and at the graveside he had wept loudly, theatrically, his grief embarrassing everyone precisely because it was real, because it was the only grief there raw enough to be indecent. And afterwards in the parking lot of the Finnish hall Eino had said something. He had gone over it ten thousand times and still could not retrieve the exact words, only their shape, which was the shape of every word he had swallowed since 1972, all of it coming up at once. Something about the boat. Something about watch-keeping. Something, finally and unforgivably, about Namu, about the drinking, the men who had seen.

Veikko had not hit him. That was the worst of it. He had stood in the sleet and listened all the way to the end, and then he had nodded—once, like their father—and gotten into a borrowed car and driven onto the afternoon ferry, and that was the last of him in the flesh. After that, only the postcards. Only the soundings. The water between us is this deep.

Around midnight the ship came into Bella Bella, easing through Lama Passage with the searchlight stroking the shoreline, the trees leaping up white where the beam touched them and falling back into black. Eino went out to watch the landing. The village was a scatter of lights on the rain, a dog barking somewhere, three pickup trucks waiting at the head of the dock with their wipers going. The Heiltsuk woman went down the ramp carrying her bag, and a young man came forward out of the truck lights to take it from her, and the way he bent his head to her, the way she touched his arm—Eino had to look away, out at the dark channel, until the lines were cast off and the village swung astern and became a constellation, then a glow, then nothing.

He thought: somewhere up ahead in that dark town there is a steel drawer, and in the drawer a man, and the man is wearing my family's name like a coat, and tomorrow they will slide him out into the light and ask me.

He thought: there are things I would know him by that no photograph holds. The two missing fingers on the left hand—the winch on the Karina B, Rivers Inlet, 1967; Eino had been aboard, had wrapped the hand in a flour sack, had held his brother's wrist all the way to the hospital at Bella Bella while Veikko, white as a halibut's belly, told jokes. The crooked eyetooth. The way the hair grew in two directions at the crown, so that no comb ever fully governed it; their mother licking her thumb, pressing it down, the hair springing up again behind her hand like grass behind a deer.

And the scar through the left eyebrow.

He had not let himself think of the scar in years and now, in the cabin, in the dark, with the engines coming up through the berth into his spine, it arrived whole. Summer 1957. He was nine, Veikko eleven. The fight had been about nothing—a knife, whose turn with the good knife—and Eino had thrown the rock not at his brother but at the piling beside his brother, except that his arm at nine did not know the difference, and the world had narrowed to the sight of Veikko sitting down slowly on the float with blood sheeting over his eye, sheeting down, an unbelievable amount of blood, and Veikko laughing. Laughing. Blood in his teeth and the strait flat calm behind him. You'll hang for this, Eino, he said. They'll hang you in Vancouver, and laughed, and would not stop laughing even when their mother held his head under the pump.

He told their parents he had fallen on the winch. He told everyone he had fallen on the winch. He carried Eino's crime through his whole boyhood under his eyebrow and never once, not in anger, not drunk, not in the parking lot of the Finnish hall with sleet coming sideways, never once did he set it down or trade on it.

You took my mark and kept it, Eino thought. And I would not even carry your question for you. The one nobody asked aloud. I let you carry that one too.

He slept after all, somewhere north of Klemtu, the way grief sleeps, shallowly, surfacing every hour to check the angle of the engines, and woke at six to a grey light in the porthole and Grenville Channel sliding past close enough to touch—the mountains coming straight up out of the water on both sides, streaked with falls, the low cloud lying in shelves across the timber. A deckhand was hosing the foredeck. The tree planter stood at the rail with her sleeping bag around her shoulders, looking up at the country with an expression he recognized from church.

He drank coffee. He shaved at the folding sink with great care, as if the dead were owed grooming, as if his brother might be brought out and find him unkempt and laugh that high broken laugh. He put on the clean shirt he had carried for this. He stood at the rail as the channel opened and Prince Rupert came up out of the rain ahead—the grain elevator, the container cranes standing over the harbour like wading birds, the town stacked on its hill, every roof streaming.

They docked at eleven. He was the last passenger off.

The coroner's office had sent a woman to meet him, younger than he expected, in a green raincoat, holding a sheet of paper with MAKI printed on it, which struck him as unnecessary in a crowd of nine people and also, somehow, as kind. Her name was Grace. She drove him up through the wet streets and talked, carefully, about the rain, about the ferry, about anything that was not the drawer, and he was grateful in the way you are grateful to someone who walks beside you on ice without taking your arm.

He found there were procedures, in the hospital basement. Forms first. A small room with two chairs and a box of tissues placed on the table at an angle suggesting much thought had gone into its placement. Grace explained that the man had been in the water some days. That he should prepare himself. That sometimes the face—she chose her words like someone crossing a creek on stones—sometimes the face is not the best way. That there were other means, dental records, and if at any point he wished to stop—

There were two missing fingers, Eino said. Left hand. Here, and here.

Grace looked at her folder and then did not look at it, and he understood that she had known this already and had been deciding whether to offer it, and he understood from her face what the folder said.

They went down a corridor that smelled of nothing, which is its own smell. The room was cold in a way that had intention behind it. A technician stood by the wall. The drawer was already out, the shape under the sheet longer than he expected—he had been remembering a boy, he realized; for twenty-three years he had been corresponding with a boy on a hatch cover, a boy in a cedar tree—and Grace asked if he was ready and he heard himself say yes in their father's voice.

The technician folded the sheet down to the collarbones.

The water had done its work and time had done its work before the water ever got its chance. This was an old man. The white-blond hair gone to yellowed white, thin over the crown—and there, even now, even here, the crown's stubborn double growth, the hair lying in two directions like grass a deer has lain in. The face was and was not a face he knew. It was their father's face, was the terrible thing; the sea had given their father back after fifty years, wearing his son. The mouth slightly open. The crooked eyetooth.

The left hand, Eino said.

The technician brought the sheet back from the left side and the hand lay there on the steel, and the two fingers were gone, the old amputations healed smooth and silvered, the hand he had held wrapped in a flour sack all the way across Fitz Hugh Sound while its owner told jokes through his teeth.

Mr. Maki, Grace said. Take whatever time you need.

But he was looking now at the eyebrow. He leaned in. The skin was altered, slackened, the colour of things long underwater, and still it was there—the pale seam running up through the left brow, the hair interrupted along its length, the little crime he had thrown across a float on a calm afternoon in 1957 and that his brother had carried away from him and kept, kept through Namu and Rivers Inlet and the cape and the funerals and the hotels of this rain-soaked town, kept like the postcards, kept like a sounding line. I did that, he wanted to say to the room, to the technician, to Grace in her green coat. That one is mine. He put it on for me when we were children and he never once took it off.

Is this your brother? Grace asked, in the gentle official voice, because the form on her clipboard required the words.

And Eino Maki stood in the cold room with his hat in his hands and understood that he had been wrong on the ferry, all night he had been wrong: a name is not a coat. A name is the one thing the water cannot keep. The sea had taken their father whole, boat and bones and pocketknife, and had still not gotten his name; it lived in a church register and on a stone among the salal and in Eino's own mouth this minute. He looked at the scar, his scar, their scar, and he reached out before anyone could decide whether to stop him and laid two fingers, lightly, on the cold ruined eyebrow, the way their mother used to lick her thumb and press down the springing hair.

Yes, he said. This is Veikko. Veikko Maki. My brother.

He signed where they showed him, the pen moving in his father's slow deliberate hand, and then he asked Grace, who hesitated only a moment, whether there was a place nearby that sold postcards, because there was an address in Sointula, a house with old plum trees, and after twenty-three years it was his turn, finally his turn, to send word with nothing written on it—only a name, and a picture of the harbour, and the depth of the water between them sounded at last.
