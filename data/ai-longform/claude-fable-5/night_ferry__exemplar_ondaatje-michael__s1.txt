The call reached him at the steel plant in the middle of the afternoon shift, a foreman shouting his name down the length of the rolling mill, and Calum lifted his visor and walked through the orange light of the furnaces to the small glassed-in office where the telephone receiver lay on its side on the desk like something that had been put down carefully, the way you put down a thing you do not want to touch again. A voice from Newfoundland. A constable named Pittman speaking from Port aux Basques, asking for the family of Eamon Teale, was he speaking to the family of Eamon Teale, and Calum stood there in his leather apron with the smell of scorched flux on him and said yes, twice, the second time because the line was poor and the first yes had been lost somewhere under the Cabot Strait.

A body had come up in the gear of a longliner off Rose Blanche. A man in his forties. No wallet. There was a name stitched inside the collar of the oilskin, an old habit of crews who shared a fo'c'sle, and the name was Teale, and the only Teale that anyone could find on either side of the gulf was Calum, in Sydney, who had not spoken to his brother in eleven years.

He took the night ferry. There was no other way to go and he would not have wanted another way. A plane would have been an insult to the distance. He drove down to North Sydney in the early dark and left the truck in the gravel lot and walked up the gangway among the deckhands and the long-haul drivers, carrying nothing but a canvas bag with a clean shirt in it and a King Edward cigar tin that held eleven postcards, none of them signed.

All that evening the ship loaded beneath him. He stood at the stern rail and watched the trucks reverse one by one into the lit cavern of the car deck, the marshals with their flashlights conducting them like men guiding animals into a hold, and he thought of his father, who had hated ferries, who said a man should never trust a vessel he could not see all of at once. His father had drowned within sight of land. Fourteen years ago now. There had been a service and a wake and a stone in the cemetery at Ingonish with nothing under it, and three years after that there had been the last spoken words between the brothers, and then nothing, then postcards, then nothing.

The ship pulled out at eleven. He felt the vibration of it come up through the soles of his boots and travel into his teeth and stay there, a low note he could not stop hearing, and the lights of North Sydney rotated slowly behind the stern and assembled themselves into a single yellow smear and then a thread and then they were gone and there was only the dark of the strait, which is a different dark from the dark of land, having no fixed point in it, nothing for the eye to hold.

When they were boys their father had taken them out beyond the harbour in the *Marguerite D.* and cut the engine and let the boat drift in exactly this kind of darkness, and he had made them name the sounds. Water against the hull. The block knocking on the mast. A gull somewhere, complaining. Their father believed a man who could name the sounds of the sea at night would never panic on it. Eamon had been better at this, as he was better at everything that had to do with water. Eamon could swim by the time he was four. Eamon would go off the government wharf in May, when the water was still full of winter, and come up grinning with his lips blue, and the old men mending nets along the wharf would look at him the way you look at a seal, with affection and a kind of distrust, as if he belonged to the other element and was only visiting theirs.

Calum was the one who belonged to fire. He had gone into the plant at seventeen and learned to weld, learned the blue-white arc that you must never look at directly, that will burn the surface of your eyes if you love it too openly, so that all your life you regard the most beautiful thing in your trade through a small rectangle of green glass. Sometimes, lying in bed, he would get the flash anyway, from some careless glance sideways at another man's work, and he would wake at three in the morning with the feeling of sand under his eyelids and lie there weeping involuntarily in the dark, and his wife, when he had had a wife, would hold a cold cloth over his face and tell him about the things she could see that he, temporarily, could not. The moths at the window. The shape of the spruce against the streetlight. He had loved her for that, the inventory of the visible.

She was gone now too, Eleanor, not dead, only gone, to her sister's in Halifax and then further, and that was another estrangement, but an ordinary one, the kind that has paperwork.

The cafeteria on the boat deck stayed open until one. He sat there with tea going cold in front of him and the cigar tin on the table, and across from him, without asking, a woman sat down. She was perhaps fifty, with a man's denim jacket over a flowered dress, and she set down her own tea and a paper plate with a date square on it and looked at the tin.

– My father kept his fishhooks in one of those, she said.

– Postcards.

– May I?

He pushed the tin across the table, surprising himself. She opened it with one thumbnail and lifted the postcards out and went through them slowly, fronts and backs, lighthouses mostly, Cape Anguille, Lobster Cove Head, the squat red one at Channel Head, one of a caribou standing in fog. The backs were blank except for his address in a handwriting that leaned hard to the right, as if written on a moving vehicle, and the postmarks: Corner Brook, Stephenville, St. Anthony, Burgeo. The last one was six years old.

– No words at all, she said.

– No.

– Then they're not postcards. They're knocking. Somebody knocking on a door to find out if you're still home.

She said she was going across for a wedding, her niece marrying a Gallant from the Codroy Valley, and she talked for a while about the Gallants, and he found that he was arguing with her, gently, about whether a wedding in April was bad luck, neither of them believing their own position, both of them keeping the talk alive only because it was easier than the alternative, which was the strait. Their conversation had the rhythm of an old habit though they had known each other twenty minutes. When she stood to go she put her hand flat on the lid of the tin, once, the way you touch a coffin, and she did not ask him why he was crossing, and he understood that she knew it was for nothing good, and that her not asking was the largest courtesy anyone had offered him in some time.

He did not take a cabin. He sat in the forward lounge in a vinyl chair under fluorescent light with men sleeping all around him in attitudes of collapse, boots off, mouths open, a transistor radio somewhere playing fiddle music at the bottom of its volume, and he leaned his head against the cold glass of the window and felt the ship take the swells of the open gulf, a long climb and a long fall, a long climb and a long fall, and somewhere in the middle of that motion he stopped being able to keep the wake out.

The wake had been at the house in Ingonish, three years after their father drowned. It was their aunt's wake, Rena, their father's sister, the last of that generation, and Eamon had come back for it from wherever he had been, the Lakes, somebody said, the ore boats, and he stood in the kitchen in a suit that did not fit him with a glass in his hand and he was twenty-nine years old and beautiful in the ruined way that some men get, and at some point in the evening, late, when the house had thinned out, the two of them were alone by the stove and Eamon said something about the *Marguerite D.*, something small, something about the engine, about how he'd told the old man the head gasket wouldn't last the season.

Eamon had been at the wheel the day she went down. A squall off Money Point in October, the engine failing exactly when an engine must not fail, and the seas putting her on the rocks, and Eamon coming ashore alive at Bay St. Lawrence with his hands torn open from the rocks and their father not coming ashore at all, not for nine days, and then quietly, at night, on the sand at Aspy Bay, as if he had wanted no witnesses.

Nobody had ever said it was Eamon's fault. The fishery officers had not said it, the other crews had not said it, the priest had certainly not said it. The weather was the weather. The engine was twenty years old. But it had sat in Calum for three years, unsaid, the way water sits in a sealed compartment, and that night in the kitchen, with the smell of Rena's lilies coming in from the front room, when Eamon mentioned the head gasket, Calum heard himself say it. He said, You were at the wheel. He said, He gave you the wheel because he trusted you. And then he said the other thing, the thing with no floor under it, and Eamon set his glass down on the stove very carefully, the way you put down a thing you do not want to touch again, and walked out of the kitchen and out of the house in his funeral suit and down the dark road toward the highway, and that was the last time. Eleven years. Eleven postcards, none of them signed, none of them words, only the knocking.

I have tried for eleven years to remember the colour of that kitchen. This is a strange thing to want, but grief attaches itself to inventory. I can remember the lilies and the bottle of rum with the paper bag still twisted around its neck and the oilcloth on the table, which was patterned with small blue anchors, and I can remember my brother's hands, which were our father's hands, broad in the palm and short in the finger, with the top joint of the left ring finger missing, gone since he was sixteen, taken by a winch drum on the *Marguerite D.* while I stood three feet away and did nothing for a full second, a second I have lived inside many times, and then took off my shirt and wrapped his hand in it and held it against my chest all the way in, both of us not crying with a tremendous effort, our father driving the boat with his jaw set, and Eamon saying to me, low, so the old man wouldn't hear, It doesn't hurt, Cal, it's the strangest thing, it doesn't hurt at all. The hurt arriving later, on land, the way it does.

But the colour of the kitchen is gone. I think it was green. Our mother had painted it before she died, when Eamon was a baby, and nobody had painted it since, and I want it to have been green, the pale green of the glass in a welder's mask, but I cannot swear to it, and there is no one left to ask.

I slept for perhaps an hour in the vinyl chair and woke with the sense of someone studying me, but it was only the window, full of grey. The gulf at first light has no horizon; the water and the sky are the same colour and the same texture and the eye gives up trying to divide them. There was drift ice off the port side, broken floes spaced out like a sentence with most of its words missing, and gulls were keeping pace with the ship without effort, hanging in the air beside the lifeboats, and I went out on deck into the cold and stood there while Newfoundland assembled itself slowly out of the greyness ahead, the low headlands first, then the snow lying in the gullies of the hills like ash in the seams of a hand.

The woman in the denim jacket was at the rail further forward. She raised her tea to me. Neither of us spoke. The ship came in past Channel Head and its squat lighthouse, the same lighthouse as on the third postcard, and I took the cigar tin out of my bag and held it and did not open it.

Pittman was waiting at the bottom of the ramp, out of uniform except for the jacket, a man younger than his telephone voice, with the careful manner of someone who has done this errand before and learned that there is no good way to do it and that the kindness is in the smallness of your talk. He drove me up through the town in a Land Rover that smelled of dog. Wooden houses painted ochre and white and a hard blue, stacked on the bare rock as though the rock had grown them. Clotheslines already out in the cold morning, shirts standing horizontal in the wind, an entire family of empty shapes facing the gulf.

– He'd been working out of Rose Blanche, far as we can tell, Pittman said. – Two seasons, maybe three. Kept to himself. Skipper says he was the best hand he ever shipped. Says he never once saw him sleep.

– That would be him, I said.

– You'll want to prepare yourself. The water—

– I know what the water does.

He glanced at me and let it go. The hospital was a low building above the harbour, and the room was in the basement, as these rooms are, below the waterline of the ordinary world, and we went down a green corridor — and I noticed it was green, the exact green, and something in my chest moved sideways — and a man in a cardigan met us and there were two or three sentences of procedure that passed over me like weather, and then there was the table, and the sheet, and the shape under the sheet, which was the size of my brother.

I had decided on the boat that I would look at the face out of respect and identify him by the hands. I had it arranged in my mind, the order of it, the way you sequence a weld so the metal doesn't warp from the heat. The face first, briefly, because a man's face deserves to be looked at by his family whatever has happened to it, and then the left hand, the ring finger, the missing joint, my shirt wrapped around it forty years ago and the blood coming through the cloth in a shape like an island.

The man in the cardigan folded the sheet down.

The sea had been at him, and the gear, and the nine days or twelve days or whatever the number was, and what was on the table had a beard gone entirely white, and I stood there and understood that I could not say. That the face would not tell me. I had carried his face across eleven years, correcting it for age the way you hold a star chart up against the moving sky, and it had been useless cargo. This face belonged to the water now and the water does not return what you lend it, only the weight of it.

– The hand, I said, and my voice came out of me strangely. – The left hand.

The man in the cardigan moved the sheet.

The top joint of the ring finger was gone. The old amputation, healed over decades ago, the skin of the stump smooth and pale, a thing finished long before the sea got involved. And I took the hand. I don't know what is permitted in such rooms, nobody had told me what is permitted, and Pittman made a small motion and then did not complete it, and I held my brother's hand, which was our father's hand, broad in the palm and short in the finger, cold in a way that no living thing is ever cold, not even Eamon coming up out of the May water off the government wharf with his lips blue, and I said it doesn't hurt, I said it out loud in that room, it doesn't hurt at all, it's the strangest thing, and Pittman looked at the floor and the man in the cardigan looked at the wall, granting me the privacy of their averted faces.

– That's my brother, I said. – That's Eamon Teale.

There were effects. They came in a manila envelope and a plastic bag: a folding knife, a few bills gone soft as cloth, a brass compass I did not recognize, and a wristwatch I did. Our father's watch, an Elgin, the crystal scratched into fog, the hands stopped at some hour that meant nothing, that the sea had chosen at random. It had not been on our father when he came ashore at Aspy Bay. I had wondered, in an ugly year, whether Eamon had taken it. He had taken it. He had worn it for fourteen years on the wrist of the hand I had wrapped in my shirt, on boats, in weather, across the whole unsigned silence, our father's stopped time strapped against his pulse.

I signed what they gave me to sign. There were arrangements to discuss, and we discussed them, Pittman and I, in the parking lot, in the wind, and they took no time at all, the arrangements, because there was no one to consult. He would come back across the strait with me on Thursday's boat. The dead ride below, on the car deck, in the cold cavern among the trucks, and the living ride above in the vinyl chairs, and the same swells lift both of them the same amount, which seemed to me, standing in that parking lot, like the only fair thing the gulf had ever done.

I walked down to the harbour afterward because there was nowhere else I was willing to be. The fishing boats were in, it being the wrong season or the wrong weather, and men were working on gear along the wharf in the way men do, without hurry, their hands knowing the work so completely that their faces were free to watch me come. One of them would have shipped with him. Maybe several. I did not ask. I sat on a bollard with the cigar tin on my knee and opened it and went through the postcards one more time, fronts and backs, the lighthouses, the caribou in fog, the handwriting leaning hard to the right as if written on a moving vehicle, because it had been, all of it, his whole life after us written on a moving vehicle, and the woman on the ferry had been correct, they were not postcards, they were knocking, and I had been home the whole time, I had been home every single time, sitting in the lit kitchen, listening, not opening the door.

Our mother had sung. This is nearly all I have of her, that she sang, in the green kitchen, something in Gaelic that neither of us ever learned the words to, so that as boys we sang the shapes of the words instead, the vowels of it, out on the water with the engine off while our father made us name the sounds. Water against the hull. The block knocking on the mast. Two boys singing the shell of a song whose meat was lost. I sat on the bollard in Port aux Basques with my brother lying below the waterline of the town and I sang it the old way, badly, the vowels only, and the wind took it off the end of the wharf and out over the harbour, where the gulls hung without effort, and carried it in the direction of the strait, which on Thursday we would cross together, the two of us, one last ferry, one going home above and one below, and the same grey water holding us both up the whole way across.
