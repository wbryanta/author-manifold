The kitchen window was just paling when he heard the truck come up the lane and he stood and poured the last of the coffee down the sink and rinsed the cup and set it on the sideboard and took his hat off the peg by the door. Outside the air was cool yet and smelled of dust and the dry weeds along the fence and the truck sat idling in the yard with its one good headlamp burning weakly against the gray and his brother sitting behind the wheel with his arm out the window.

You eat? Wes said.

I eat.

You bring the hacksaw?

It's in the shed. Pull around.

They loaded the truck in the half dark, the long-handled shovels and the mattock and the pipe wrenches and the saw and a coil of wire and a wooden box of fittings their father had collected over forty years and never thrown away, couplings and unions and lengths of nipple pipe gone scaly with rust, and they rode out down the lane between the trees with the dust standing in the lamplight behind them. The orchard at that hour was a dark architecture against the paler dark of the sky and the dead trees among the living were no different from the living, all of them shaped alike out of the gloom, and a man who knew no better might have thought the place whole.

The water had come up in the lane two days ago. A dark stain in the dust at first that Harlan had taken for oil dripped from some passing rig and then a softness underfoot and then by evening a slick of standing water seeping up out of the ground a hundred yards from any ditch, and he had walked down and stood looking at it and then walked back and shut off the pump at the well and the next morning the stain was still there, glistening like something the earth had decided to give back.

They got out and stood looking at it in the first light. The mud had skinned over at the edges and cracked in small plates and in the center it still shone. Wes pushed at it with the toe of his boot.

That's the old main, he said.

I know it.

Daddy laid that line in what, fifty-eight?

Fifty-six. I helped him.

You wasnt big enough to help him in fifty-six.

I carried fittings. That's helpin.

Wes looked off down the rows where the light was beginning to find the trees. Half of em are too far gone to care whether they get watered or not, he said.

The other half aint.

Well, Wes said. He spat and looked at the mud. Let's get to diggin, then.

They dug. The ground gave easy at first where the water had loosened it and then they were into the hardpan and the shovels rang on it and they took turns with the mattock, swinging it up and over and down in a slow rhythm, and the sun cleared the ridge to the east and laid their shadows out long and thin across the lane like the shadows of figures much taller than themselves, attenuated and strange, laborers out of some older world bent to a task the world had set them. By seven oclock they were three feet down and the hole was filling with seepwater faster than they could bail it with the cutoff jug and Wes stood in the hole with the water over his boots and felt along the pipe with his fingers.

Here it is, he said.

How bad.

Split. Six, eight inches. He stood and wiped his hand on his jeans. Rusted through from the bottom like they do. You cant see it but you can feel her.

Can we sleeve it?

Wes shook his head. It's gone all around. You sleeve this and she'll let go a foot further on inside a month. We need to cut out a joint of it and put new back.

We dont have a joint of new.

Wes climbed up out of the hole and stood looking down into it. The water lay over the pipe smooth and brown and untroubled. No, he said. We dont.

They drove into town with the windows down and the heat already coming up off the blacktop in long wavering sheets and the country to either side burned pale and the cattle standing motionless in what shade they could find as if they'd been placed there and forgotten. Wes drove with two fingers on the wheel. He didnt say anything for a long time and then he said it.

Caudill called me again Tuesday.

Harlan watched the country go by.

You hear me?

I heard you.

He's at two-forty now. That's forty more than March.

It'll be forty more than that by fall, then. If I just keep not answerin the phone.

It dont work that way and you know it. There's a number where he quits callin.

Then he quits callin.

Wes drove. A hawk rose off a fencepost ahead of them and beat slowly out over the burned grass and they passed beneath it and Harlan turned and watched it settle on the next post down as if it were only waiting for them to be gone.

Two hundred and forty thousand dollars, Wes said. You know what that ground appraised at when we probated? You was standin right there.

I was standin right there.

Well.

A thing aint worth what a fool will pay for it. It's worth what it is.

That dont even mean anything.

It means somethin to me.

They got the pipe at the supply house, a twenty-one foot joint of six-inch galvanized, and a dresser coupling and a can of joint compound, and the man at the counter wrote it up and looked at the ticket and looked at them.

This goin out to your daddy's place?

It's our place now, Wes said.

I know it. The man tore the ticket and handed it across. I just always think of it as his. He set them first trees with my daddy helpin him. Nineteen and forty-nine. He looked at them both a moment as if he would say something more and then he didnt say it. You all take care, he said.

They rode back out with the pipe lashed to the rack and singing faintly in the wind and neither of them spoke. The town fell away behind them and the country opened out and to the south where the Sanchez place had been there were streets laid in now, curbed and guttered and empty, pale concrete ribbons looping through the creosote with little wire flags fluttering at the corners of lots where no house stood nor any tree, and a sign at the highway with a painted picture of a family that existed nowhere on this earth.

Look at that, Wes said.

I see it.

That's comin this way whether you want it or not. That's just water runnin downhill.

It can run around me.

It dont run around nobody. It never has.

They turned in at the lane and the trees took them in and the light fell broken through the leaves and moved over the hood of the truck and over their faces, and even failing, even half dead, the orchard had a coolness to it that the open country did not, a smell of leaves and standing dust and faint rot that Harlan had known so long he could not have said whether it was pleasant or not but only that it was the smell of this place and no other place had it.

The work went slow. They sawed the old pipe by turns, crouched in the muck of the hole with the seepwater rising about the blade and the saw binding in the cut and the rust coming away red on their hands, and the old pipe when it parted showed paper thin at the bottom of its bore, eaten away from within all these years while it carried the water that ate it, and Wes held the cut end up out of the water and looked at it and shook his head.

It's a wonder she run this long.

Daddy laid pipe to last.

Nothin lasts. You're lookin right at it.

It lasted fifty years. That's close enough to last for me.

By noon they had the old joint out and lying in the weeds at the edge of the lane like something exhumed, and they walked up under the big cottonwood at the head of the rows and sat in the shade and ate what Harlan had brought, cold biscuits and ham and a jar of his wife's pickles that was no longer his wife's because she'd been gone two years now but he still called them that, and they drank water out of the cooler jug and leaned back against the trunk of the tree and looked out down the rows.

The orchard ran south from where they sat, forty acres of it, the rows drawn off straight as a surveyor could draw them and the trees in their ranks marching away into the heat shimmer, and you could read the history of the place in those rows like a record kept in no other script. The old Jonathans nearest the house that the father had set in forty-nine, gnarled and black-barked and half of them stumps now or standing dead with their dead limbs upraised. Then the Romes from the fifties, and then the block of Red Delicious he'd put in the year their mother died because a man had told him the market wanted them, and the market had wanted them for about ten years and then it hadnt. Off at the far south end stood twelve rows of dead trees entire, killed in the freeze of eleven years ago and never pulled, gray and barkless and polished by the wind, and on a winter evening with the light behind them they looked like the standing remnant of some burned procession halted forever in its march, and even now in full June leaflessness among the leafed they had a gravity to them, an authority, as if the dead trees and not the living were the true text of the place and the living only a gloss upon it.

Wes finished eating and folded the wax paper and put it in the sack. He took out his cigarettes and lit one and sat smoking.

You picked what last fall, he said. It wasnt a question and Harlan didnt answer it like one.

You know what I picked.

Eleven hundred boxes. Off of forty acres. Daddy used to take eleven hundred boxes off of six.

It was a bad year.

They're all bad years now, Harlan. That's what I'm tryin to get you to look at. The water table's droppin two foot a year. The well's pullin sand. You're irrigatin through a main that's rustin out underneath you faster than you can dig it up. And the trees that aint dead are forty and fifty year old and you know what a fifty year old apple tree is worth.

I know what it's worth.

It's worth firewood.

I know what it's worth.

Wes smoked. Down the rows a cicada started up and ran its long dry ratchet out to nothing and quit.

I aint try in to take somethin from you, Wes said. Half of it's mine to sell or not sell, same as yours. I could force a partition tomorrow if I wanted to be a son of a bitch about it.

But you dont.

No. I dont. He looked at the end of his cigarette. I got Danny startin college in the fall. I got a note on the house. Sherry's worked nights goin on three years. And my half of this place earns me about four hundred dollars and a backache per annum and I drive out here ever time somethin breaks because I cant stand to think of you tryin to do it alone. Now you tell me what I'm supposed to want.

Harlan looked out down the rows. I aint got an answer for you, he said.

I know you aint.

If I had one I'd of give it to you a long time ago.

They sat. The heat stood in the rows and the leaves hung without moving and somewhere off toward the river a dove called and waited and called again.

You remember irrigatin at night? Harlan said.

Wes smiled in spite of himself. I remember you fallin in the ditch.

Daddy'd take the lantern and walk the rows and we'd follow the water down. You could hear it comin before you could see it. Comin down the row in the dark. He'd say, listen at her. Here she comes.

I remember it.

I used to think there wasnt nothin in the world could sound like that. Water comin to trees in the dark.

Wes dropped the cigarette and set his heel on it. That was forty years ago, he said. That man is dead and that world is dead and sittin out here gettin sunstroke aint goin to raise either one of em.

I never said it would.

Then what are we talkin about.

I dont know, Harlan said. I think we're talkin about whether a thing quits bein worth doin just because it's dyin.

Wes looked at him a long time. That's a hell of a question, he said.

I know it.

You got an answer to it?

No. But I notice you come out here at five oclock in the mornin to help me dig.

Wes stood and picked up his gloves and slapped them against his leg. That dont prove what you think it proves, he said. Come on. That pipe aint goin to lay itself.

The afternoon was the worst of it. They wrestled the new joint down into the hole and it would not seat and they hauled it out and dressed the cut end of the old pipe with a file, taking turns, the file shrieking thinly in the heat and the filings coming away bright as silver against all that rust, and they tried it again and drew the coupling up bolt by bolt by turns with the one wrench that fit, and the sweat ran on them and dripped from their hatbrims and the seepwater in the hole had gone warm as bathwater and stank of iron. A garter snake came down the wet edge of the lane in the midafternoon and stopped and raised its head and regarded them where they labored half buried in the earth and then went on, pouring itself into the weeds without haste, as though it had seen men in holes before and knew how such things ended.

Hold it, Wes said. Hold her right there.

I'm holdin it.

He took up on the last bolt and then took up on it again and laid the wrench up on the grass and felt all around the coupling with his fingers and sat back on his heels in the water.

That's got it or it dont, he said.

They climbed out and stood in the lane, mudded to the waist, and Harlan walked up toward the house to throw the pump. Wes stood at the hole. The trees ran away from him on either hand and the light had begun to go long and yellow in the rows and he stood looking at the orchard as a man might look at a thing he was trying to commit to memory or trying to be quit of, and it may be that no one could have told the difference, not even him. Up at the house the pump motor caught and ran and he heard it gather and load up as the line filled, and he watched the new pipe where it lay in the seepwater and the water trembled over it and then stilled, and he crouched and watched it a long time and the coupling held and held and no thread of silt rose anywhere along it.

When Harlan came back down the lane Wes was filling the hole.

She's dry, Wes said.

I figured she would be.

You figured no such a thing.

They filled the hole and tamped it by turns with the flat of the shovel and stood the old ruined joint of pipe up against a tree because neither of them could say why a man would haul it off and then they walked down the rows to where the ditches took the water, and Harlan pulled the boards on the head gate and the water came boiling up brown out of the riser and spread and found the first ditch and went down it.

They followed it. There was no reason to and they followed it anyway, walking slow in the cooling light, watching the water run ahead of them down the row, the dry ditch drinking it at first so that it advanced and stalled and advanced again, gathering trash and bits of leaf and pale dust that rode upon it, and the smell came up out of the ground that water makes on long dry earth, which is like no other smell, and which a man who has farmed dry country will know on his deathbed when he has forgotten the names of his children. The water ran down the row and the light ran red along it and the trees stood over it, and the dead trees took their portion of the water the same as the living and gave nothing back, and the water did not distinguish among them, nor does water anywhere distinguish, having neither memory nor preference but only the one law, and it went where the ground carried it and shone where the light found it and the two men walked behind it like mourners or like heirs.

It'll take all night to get across, Harlan said.

I know it. You goin to set up with it?

I'll change the sets at midnight. It aint nothin.

It aint nothin, Wes said. He shook his head.

I see it.
