The ferry left at nine, and by half past eight the cars were already deep in the hold, stacked nose to tail under the orange sodium lights, and Colin Tulloch stood at the rail of the open deck with his bag at his feet, watching the town let go of itself in pieces. First the cranes of the harbour, then the church spire, then the grey terraces climbing the hill, each thing surrendering its outline to the dusk until there was only a smear of lights and the green and red beads of the channel markers sliding past, close enough that he could hear the water sucking at their iron bases. It was October. The air had that mineral cold in it, the cold that came off the water and not the land, and he had forgotten it, the way you forget a voice until it speaks again.

The call had come two days before, on a Tuesday, while he was at his desk going over a drainage schedule for a school extension that would never be built on time. A woman's voice, careful, trained in carefulness. Sergeant Manson, she said, of the island constabulary, and was he Colin Tulloch, and did he have a brother, Alec, born 1966. He had said yes to all of it, and then there had been a small silence on the line, a silence with a shape to it, and he had understood before she said anything else, the way you understand weather coming.

A body had been recovered from the water on the east side of the island, she said. A man had been reported missing six weeks earlier from an address at Skelvoe, a man known locally as Alec Tulloch, and the body was consistent with — she used that word, consistent — but the sea had had it for some time, and there were no dental records, his brother having apparently never in his adult life sat in a dentist's chair, and the DNA comparison would take weeks through the lab in the south, and in the meantime the procurator fiscal would be grateful, if Mr Tulloch were willing, if he could come north. To assist. There were identifying features that family sometimes knew. She did not promise him anything. She was good at not promising, he thought afterwards. It was probably most of the job, up there.

He had not seen Alec in eleven years. He had not spoken to him in eight. He had told Ruth this on the phone from the terminal, as though she didn't know it, as though the whole stiff cold architecture of it weren't a thing they had lived inside their entire marriage, the brother in the north like a sealed room in the house. She had offered to come and he had said no, and she had not argued, and he had been grateful and then, walking up the gangway, obscurely wounded that she hadn't argued harder, which he recognised as childish and carried with him anyway, the way you carry a stone in your shoe up a long stair because stopping to deal with it has become more trouble than the pain.

The cabin was an inside one, no porthole, two bunks though he was alone, a strip of fluorescent light over a steel sink and a folded towel the size of a tea cloth. He sat on the lower bunk and felt the engines come up through the frame of the ship, a deep churning that settled after a while into something almost like a pulse, and a recorded voice told him in two languages where the muster stations were, and he thought: Alec would know all this without being told. Alec had crossed this strait a hundred times, two hundred. Alec had crossed it the first time at seventeen with a kitbag and their grandfather's oilskin coat, going north to the island the family had come from two generations back, going against the whole tide of the century, which ran south, which ran towards Aberdeen and Glasgow and London, towards desks and drainage schedules. Their father had stood in the kitchen and called him a fool, and Alec had said, not unkindly, that there were worse things, and had gone.

Colin had been fourteen. He remembered the house afterwards, the way it rang. Not with absence exactly. With relief, almost, which was worse. Their father had been a man who needed someone to be wrong near him at all times, and with Alec gone the duty had passed to Colin, and Colin had solved it the way he solved everything, by becoming so quietly, comprehensively correct that there was no surface left to strike. Good marks, good apprenticeship, good firm, good pension. He had built a life the way you build a sea wall, course on course, and it had held, and standing now in the thrum of the cabin he could not entirely remember what it had been holding against.

He went up to the bar because the cabin was unbearable. The lounge was half full: a clutch of oil workers in branded fleeces playing cards with terrible concentration, a young couple sharing earphones, an old man asleep upright with his hands folded on his stomach like a man laid out, which Colin looked away from. He bought a whisky he didn't much want and took it to a window seat, though the window by now gave back nothing but his own reflection hung over black water, a heavyset man of fifty-four with his father's jaw and his mother's worried forehead, and behind the reflection, occasionally, the lights of a fishing boat, far off, rising and falling.

Ruth rang while there was still signal. He told her about the cabin, the towel, the card players, narrating the small things because the large thing could not be narrated. She let him do it for a while and then she said, gently, the way you put a hand on an animal that might startle: What will you do if it isn't him?

He looked at his reflection. The boat had begun to work now, out past the headland, a long slow lift and lean, and the whisky slid its amber ellipse around the glass.

I don't know, he said. Come home, I suppose.

And if it is?

Come home, he said. There was a silence with the sea's static in it. Then she said she loved him, and the signal went, taking her voice with it mid-word, and he sat for a long time with the dead phone in his hand while the island of his reflection rode up and down on the dark.

The truth, which he had not said to Ruth, which he had barely said to himself, was that he did not know which answer he was crossing towards in hope. That was the rot at the centre of it. A decent man would know. A decent man would be praying, in whatever way unbelieving men pray, on a ferry, with whisky: let it not be him, let it be some other poor soul, let Alec be alive in a bothy somewhere with a dog and a stove and his unanswerable contempt intact. And part of Colin did pray that, the old reflexive loyalty of the younger brother, the part of him that was still nine years old following Alec along the breakwater in the rain. But there was another part, and he had felt it stir on the phone with Sergeant Manson, a part that had thought, before any grief arrived, with a kind of exhausted clarity: then it's over. The sealed room opened at last, and found to be empty, and the house finally just a house.

He finished the whisky and didn't buy another.

The quarrel — though quarrel was a weak word for a thing that had ended a family, the way skirmish is a weak word for some battles — had been about the croft. Eighteen acres of sloping ground above a voe on the island's west side, a house of two rooms and a but-end, their grandfather's, then nominally their father's though he never went north, then, when their father died, the brothers' together. Alec had been living in it by then for years, keeping a few sheep, doing winter work on the boats, mending the roof with whatever the sea brought him. He never asked for it to be put in his name. That was Alec all over: he believed possession was a matter of presence, that the ground knew whose boots were on it, and that paper was the mainland's disease.

Then their mother began to go. It started small, the way it does — keys, names, the gas left hissing — and then it was not small, and it was Colin, four streets away, who found her in her nightdress at the bus stop at two in the morning asking for a town that had not existed since her childhood. It was Colin who sat in the assessments, Colin who learned the vocabulary, sundowning, respite, capacity, Colin who held her hand through the long horror of her lucid intervals, when she surfaced and knew exactly what was happening to her and looked at him like a woman looking up through ice. The home that could take her properly cost more in a year than Colin earned. He wrote to Alec. Alec wrote back a single page saying bring her north, I'll see to her, which was so far beyond impossible — a woman who needed nurses, hoists, locked doors, on an island croft with a man who was away three weeks of four on the boats — that Colin had read it as he was perhaps not meant to read it, as a gesture, a flag planted on the moral high ground, costing nothing.

So he had done what the power of attorney let him do. The croft was sold to an incomer from the south who wanted it for holidays, and the money went into the care home's maw, where it lasted four years, which was one year longer than their mother did.

Alec came to the funeral. He stood at the back of the crematorium in a black suit that had been someone else's, and at the purvey afterwards, in a hotel function room with sandwiches curling under clingfilm, he had crossed the carpet to Colin with his glass in his hand and said, quietly, in front of Ruth and two of their mother's neighbours: You sold the ground out from under me to pay strangers to watch her die.

And Colin, who had four years of nights in him, four years of being the one in the room, had said the thing. He could still feel the shape of it in his mouth, all these years on, like a tooth the tongue keeps finding. He had said: It was never your ground. It was collateral. You squatted on it for twenty years and called it heritage. You never made anything of it, Alec. You never made anything of anything.

There had not been a punch, quite. There had been Alec's hand on his lapel, and the glass going over, and the neighbours' faces, and then Alec had let go of him with a small downward push, the way you discard something, and walked out, and that was the last time Colin had stood in a room with his brother. There had been three phone calls in the eight years since, all from Alec, all unanswered, the last one eight months ago, a missed call with no message, an entry in a log. Colin had looked at it for two days and told himself he would ring at the weekend, and the weekend had come with its lawn and its supermarket and its sleep, and gone.

He slept badly, in the way the ship seemed to want, shallowly, surfacing every hour into the dark of the cabin not knowing for a moment which way he was lying or which decade it was. Around three he gave it up, dressed, and went out through the heavy door onto the after deck.

The cold took him like water. The deck was empty, wet, lit by one caged bulb, and beyond the rail the strait went about its ancient business in the dark, invisible except where the ship's wake unzipped it into a long pale seam that closed again astern. There was no moon. There was, when he tipped his head back and let his eyes adjust, an enormous sediment of stars, more than he had seen in thirty years, and beneath them the sense — not sight, something older than sight — of land off to the west, a denser blackness, headlands without names or with names he had never learned. Alec would have the names. Alec at twelve had stood with him on the breakwater at home and pointed at lights on the water and named them, that one's a liner, that one's a seiner, you can tell by how she rides, and Colin had believed every word, and most of it, he understood later, Alec had been inventing on the spot, with total conviction, which was its own kind of truth.

He thought about the body. He had been not thinking about it with great discipline for two days, and at the rail at three in the morning the discipline went. Six weeks missing, Sergeant Manson had said, some weeks in the water. He knew enough — every coastal boy knows enough — about what the sea does. The sea is not cruel, their grandfather used to say, on the one visit north Colin had made as a child; the sea is busy. It would not be a face. They would not be asking him to look at a face. They would be asking him to look at the things the busy sea had not got to, or had no use for: hands, marks, mendings. And he stood in the cold and made an inventory of his brother, and was ashamed at how short it was. The swallow tattooed on the left forearm at nineteen, blue-black, badly done in a back room in some port, their father white with rage. The top joint of the left forefinger, lost to a winch when Alec was twenty-five — the letter had come weeks after the fact, jaunty, more of me for the gulls. And the scar through the right eyebrow, a centimetre of seam where no hair grew, which Alec had carried since he was eleven, since a summer evening on the shore when Colin, nine years old and losing at something, had thrown a flake of slate harder than he meant to, and the blood had come shocking and immediate, sheeting down Alec's cheek, and Alec had put his hand to it and looked at the redness on his fingers and then at his little brother's appalled face and said, I fell. Mind that. I fell on the rocks. And had said it at home, holding the story over Colin like an oilskin held over a smaller boy in rain, and had never once, in all the wars that came after, in all the years and the funeral and everything, used it. He had owned a thing he could have struck Colin with at any time, and had carried it instead, in his eyebrow, into the north.

Colin stood at the rail until the cold got past his coat and into the meat of him, and the seam of the wake unspooled, and somewhere below the waterline of his mind a thought moved that he could not get to come up where he could see it.

Dawn happened the way it does at sea in autumn, grudgingly, a greyness leaking up out of the east until the water became visible to itself, pewter-coloured, heaving in long smooth swells with the wind combing white off their tops. And there ahead, at first no more than a low interruption of the horizon, then gathering detail with every mile — cliffs, a lighthouse still pulsing wanly against the daylight, green-brown slopes scattered with white houses like dropped sugar — was the island. Other passengers came out to the rail with cardboard cups of coffee. The oil men. The couple, sharing one earphone again. Gulls picked up the ship and ran with it. Colin watched the land come on and felt nothing he had a word for: not dread, not grief, but a kind of pressure change, the feeling in the ears when the road climbs.

Sergeant Manson met him at the terminal. She was younger than her phone voice, fair, solid, with the island in her vowels, and she shook his hand and said he'd be tired, and didn't make him talk in the car. The town slid past, harbour-front, grey stone, a chandlery, a charity shop with a child's bicycle in the window, and then they were at the hospital, a low modern building with rain beginning to bead its glass, and in a small room with a box of tissues placed on the table with terrible forethought she went through it all again, slowly, giving him time. The man known as Alec Tulloch had lodged for eleven years with a Mrs Pottinger at Skelvoe, had kept a small boat, had been last seen taking it out on an evening in late August with the weather coming up. The boat had been found against the rocks of a skerry. The body had been found five weeks later by a man walking his dog, in a geo on the east side, where the tides bring things. She said: I have to be honest with you, Mr Tulloch. A visual identification, in the ordinary sense, isn't possible. What we'd ask is whether you recognise particular features. And whatever happens, we'll have the DNA in time. There's no wrong answer today. Nothing rests on you alone.

Which was a kind thing to say, and untrue, and they both knew it.

A swab first, the inside of his cheek, an absurdly gentle procedure. Then a corridor, and a door, and a small clean room with a viewing window and a curtain on the far side of it, and an attendant in blue, and Sergeant Manson at his shoulder saying, take all the time you need, and the curtain going back.

The shape under the sheet was a man's. They had arranged it so that what could be shown was shown and nothing else: the left forearm, laid out on the white; the left hand; and above the folded edge of the sheet a portion of the upper face, eyes closed, the rest discreetly veiled. Colin made himself look the way he made himself do everything, in order, course on course.

The forearm. The skin was wrong, the colour of things the sea returns, and the flesh had thickened and slipped, but in it, blurred, drowned a second time under the surface of the damage, was blue-black ink. He could not have sworn to a swallow. He could have sworn to wings.

The hand. The fingers were not whole — the busy sea — and he heard himself ask, in a voice that came out level, whether the forefinger, the top of it, whether that was old or — and the attendant said quietly that the pathologist's note recorded the loss of the distal joint of the left forefinger as healed, historic, decades old. An old mending. The gulls' share, taken long ago.

And then the face, what there was of it. He had been braced for the wrongness and the wrongness was there, and he stood in it and did not look away, and looked at the right eyebrow, and the room got very quiet, the way rooms do when you put your whole self through your eyes.

There was a seam. Or the ruin of one, or the suggestion of one, a centimetre of pale interruption where the brow hair failed — or where the water had taken it — he could not tell, he could not honestly, before God, tell whether he was seeing the scar or making it, throwing it there himself across forty-five years, the flake of slate leaving his hand a second time. He stood for a long minute with the two possibilities lying in front of him on the table under the one sheet.

I fell. Mind that.

What he understood, in that minute, was that no one was going to do this for him. Not the lab in the south, not in his heart, not where it counted; the lab would issue a fact, in six weeks, to a file. What was being asked of him here, now, was not a fact. It was a claiming. A man had lived eleven years in a rented room above a voe, had kept a boat, had rung a number in the south three times and been left to ring out, and now lay on a steel table with wings on his arm under a borrowed sheet, and the question in the room was not really is this Alec Tulloch. The question was: does this man have a brother.

Yes, Colin said. His voice did what voices do. He cleared it and said it again, properly, for the record, for the sergeant's notebook, for the procurator fiscal, for the busy sea. Yes. That's Alec. That's my brother.

There were forms. There is no grief so old or so strange that there are not forms, and he was glad of them, the boxes and the block capitals, and when he came to the line that asked for the name of the deceased he wrote ALEC JOHN TULLOCH in his careful surveyor's hand, and realised, watching the letters appear, that he had not written his brother's name in twenty years, perhaps thirty, that the last time might have been a Christmas envelope addressed at their mother's kitchen table, and that the name looked at once entirely strange and like something he had been writing all his life under everything else, the way water moves under a frozen loch.

The return ferry sailed at seven. Sergeant Manson offered him the day, said Mrs Pottinger would want to see him, there were things of Alec's, and he said tomorrow, said he would stay the night after all, there was a hotel by the harbour, and was surprised to hear himself say it. He walked down to the water in the thinning rain and stood where the small boats lay against their fenders, lifting and settling, lifting and settling, and the gulls went over with their cold hilarious cries, and across the strait, invisible, four hundred miles of south held his whole correct life ready for him to resume it. He stood a long time. Somewhere behind the town a church bell counted the hour and lost interest. Then he took out his phone, and found Ruth's name, and below it, where it had sat for eight years because he had never had the courage either to return it or to delete it, the other name, the missed call, the log entry, the flag on the high ground; and he rang Ruth, and while it rang he watched the water, which was not cruel, which was only busy, and when she answered he said, It's him. I found him. I told them he's ours.
