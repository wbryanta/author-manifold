By nine o'clock the hole was hip-deep and Nathan stood in it, mud to the knees of his good jeans, handing up shovel-loads of gray adobe clay that came out of the ground in slabs like cut liver. The leak had been seeping all spring, probably longer. Curt had noticed the wet spot in April, a green stain in the dead grass along the lane where nothing green had a right to be, and had told himself it was snowmelt, then runoff, then a low place collecting dew, until the spot grew cattails and the pressure at the far risers dropped so badly that the last twelve rows of Suncrests got nothing at all. Now it was the second week of June and ninety-one degrees by midmorning, and the two of them were down in the ground together for the first time in twenty years, looking for the crack in a pipe their father had buried when Reagan was president.

"There," Curt said. He was on his knees at the lip of the hole, pointing with the trowel. "See it weeping?"

Nathan wiped his face with the back of his wrist and looked. The mainline was six-inch PVC, gone the dull ivory of old bone, and along its top ran a hairline fracture maybe fourteen inches long, fine as a pencil stroke. Water beaded out of it even now, with the headgate closed since dawn, the pipe still bleeding what it held.

"Frost got under it," Curt said. "Or the clay heaved it. This ground moves like the ocean if you let it dry out and wet up enough times."

"It's been thirty-eight years. Pipe doesn't owe us anything."

"It owes me one more season," Curt said, and that was the first shot fired, though neither of them acknowledged it. Curt stood and slapped the clay off his knees and went to the truck for the chain saw file and the hacksaw, and Nathan kept digging, undermining the pipe so they could get the saw all the way around it, and the sun climbed and the orchard around them stood in its long silver-green rows, the leaves already curling a little at the edges, twelve hundred peach trees waiting on two middle-aged men and a hole in the ground.

The letter was in the truck. Nathan had seen it that morning on the bench seat, folded in thirds, the cream-colored letterhead of Triple Creek Land Company showing at the crease. He had put it there himself three days ago, which was the whole reason he'd flown out from Denver, and Curt had not signed it, had not mentioned it, had instead met him at the airport and said the mainline's cracked, I could use a hand, as if Nathan had crossed two states to dig a ditch.

"You read it yet?" Nathan said, when Curt came back with the saws.

"Read what."

"Curt."

"I read it." Curt knelt and started the hacksaw against the pipe, drawing it slow to set the kerf. "One point six million. Forty-one lots. They're going to call it Orchard View Estates, which I guess is honest, since the view of the orchard is all that'll be left of it." The saw bit and squealed. Water dribbled from the cut and ran down into the mud. "Offer's good through the first of July. I can read fine."

"It's a good number. Hendricks got less per acre two years ago and his ground was better than ours."

"His ground was not better than ours."

"His water was better."

"Nothing's wrong with our water that the ditch company couldn't fix if they'd dredge above the flume like they've been promising since Dad was on the board." Curt finished the first cut and moved down the pipe, measuring with his eyes, and started the second. He was forty-nine, three years older than Nathan, and he still had their father's forearms, ropy and sun-cured, though his back had started to go and he stood up out of a crouch now in stages, like a folding chair opening. "Hold that end."

Nathan held it. The cut section came free, two feet of pipe with the fracture running along its top like a seam in marble, and Curt turned it in his hands the way you'd examine a dead animal for what killed it.

"There's your problem," Curt said.

"That's not our problem. That's a symptom of our problem. Our problem is that this place lost money four of the last five years and the year it didn't lose money it made eleven thousand dollars, and Mom's care runs eighty-five hundred a month, and you've been making up the difference out of an account that I happen to know is down to about the price of that pipe."

Curt set the cut section on the grass at the edge of the hole, gently, as if it might still be of use. "You been looking at the accounts."

"I have power of attorney same as you. Somebody had to look."

"I look at them every night, Nathan. I don't need a man who adjusts hail claims for a living to fly out here and tell me the math." He hauled himself up out of his crouch, stage by stage. "We need a slip coupling and primer and a quart of glue. I've got the coupling at the shop. You want to ride along or you want to stand in the hole and practice your speech?"

They rode along. The lane ran a half mile through the orchard, the truck rocking in the ruts, dust hanging behind them in the dead air, and on either side the trees went by in their rows, Suncrest and Cresthaven and a block of Elbertas so old that their father had been a boy when they went in, trees black-barked and arthritic, half of them dead in the crown, that any sensible grower would have pushed out decades ago. Curt sprayed them, thinned them, propped their split scaffolds with two-by-fours. They made maybe forty boxes of small, freckled, outrageously sweet peaches a year, and Curt sold them off a card table at the farmers market to people who drove from two towns over and called them the real ones, and the whole operation, Nathan had once calculated, lost about three hundred dollars per tree per year if you valued Curt's labor at anything above zero. Curt valued his labor at zero. That was the kind of math the place ran on.

The shop smelled the way it had smelled in 1985, the way Nathan suspected it would smell at the bottom of his own grave: spilled diesel, juniper dust, the cold-iron breath of the anvil, and underneath everything the sweetness of rotted fruit, decades of culls thrown to the chickens that no longer existed against the foundation that did. Curt went down the wall of bins their father had built from apple-crate lumber, each one labeled in grease pencil in the old man's square handwriting, and came back with the coupling, a length of new pipe, the purple primer, the glue.

"He bought six of these couplings in 1991," Curt said. "Told me a man who buys fittings one at a time deserves the trip to town. This is the last one."

"That's a sign."

"It's a fitting." But Curt almost smiled, and for a quarter mile back down the lane it was nearly all right between them, the way it could still sometimes be, the truck rocking, the dust rising, two boys from this dirt riding to a job their father might have sent them on, and then Curt said, "I'm not signing it," and the quarter mile was over.

"Then tell me what we do instead. Honestly. Walk me through it."

"We bring in this crop. It's a good set, best in six years, the freeze missed us in April when it got everybody up-valley. Prices'll be up because of that same freeze. We could clear sixty, seventy thousand."

"Which covers eight months of Sage Meadows. Then it's June again and we're having this exact conversation in this exact truck, except Mom's account is empty and the offer's gone."

"Or it isn't gone. Or there's another offer. Land doesn't get cheaper."

"Curt. The packing shed in town closed. The co-op closed in 2011. You haul fruit ninety minutes each way to a packer who docks you for size because his lines are set up for Texas fruit. Every year there's one fewer of everything you need to do this and one more of everything that wants you gone. You know this better than I do. You're not arguing with me, you're arguing with the valley."

Curt drove. His jaw worked once, twice, chewing on something he didn't say. They came back to the hole and the wet grass around it, and a magpie that had been investigating the cut section of pipe lifted off and rowed away over the trees, and Curt shut the truck off and sat with his hands on the wheel.

"You know what she said to me Tuesday," he said. "Mom. I go Tuesdays and Fridays. She was at the window, they have her room facing the parking lot, which I've asked them twice to change, and she turns around and looks at me and says, 'Walt, did you get the water on the lower piece? It's drying out down there.' Forty years gone and she's worried about the lower piece." He looked at Nathan. "I told her yes. I told her the water was on and the trees looked fine. First thing she's said to me in a month that made sense, and it was about this dirt, and I lied to her about it."

"She doesn't know, Curt."

"I know that. I'm telling you what I know about her. The wiring's mostly burned but what's left, what's down at the bottom where the fire can't get at it, is this place. So don't tell me what the land's worth. I've got the appraisal. I'm telling you what it's for."

He got out of the truck and carried the fittings to the hole, and Nathan sat a moment longer in the heat coming off the dash and then followed him, because there was no answer to that, or none he could afford to say, which was that what it was for was paying for her to be fed and turned and kept clean by professionals in a building with a sprinkler system, that sentiment didn't bill monthly and Sage Meadows did.

The repair itself was simple and exacting, the way their father's jobs always were. They squared the cut ends with the file, beveled them, dry-fit the new section with the slip coupling collapsed, and Curt marked the depth with a carpenter's pencil he kept behind his ear in apparent defiance of the decades. The work narrowed them down. There is a kind of conversation that two men can only have over a third thing, with their eyes on the third thing, and the pipe was the third thing, and so while Nathan held the new section steady and Curt swabbed the purple primer around the old pipe's end — the smell of it sharp and chemical and instantly, vertiginously the smell of every summer of their childhood — Curt said, "You remember when he put this line in?"

"I remember the trench. I remember it went on about nine years."

"Eighty-six. The summer after the bank called the operating note and he had to sell the lower forty to the Kowalskis to cover it." Curt laid the primer down and picked up the glue, stirred it with the dauber. "He buried this pipe with money he didn't have on land he'd just been forced to cut in half, because he said the ditch lost a third of the water to seepage before it ever reached a tree and he was done growing cottonwoods for the neighbors. Mom thought he'd lost his mind. He told her, 'Ellen, you don't quit watering just because you've been bled.'" He glued the coupling, glued the pipe, and looked at Nathan. "Ready. Push it home and hold it. Count of thirty."

They pushed it home. The glue grabbed, the joint seated with a small wet sigh, and the two of them crouched in the hole holding a piece of plastic pipe perfectly still and counted silently in the dark of the earth's opened seam while the sun beat on their backs, and Nathan thought, not for the first time, that there was no argument he could make that would survive contact with this — with the smell of the primer and the count of thirty and the dead man's pencil behind his brother's ear. You couldn't argue with a liturgy. You could only refuse to kneel, and he was kneeling.

"Thirty," Curt said. "Other end now."

They did the other end. Then there was nothing to do but let it cure, two hours at least with the line this size before they dared put pressure to it, and Curt, who had thought of this, produced from the truck a cooler with bologna sandwiches and a bag of last year's frozen peaches half-thawed into slush, and they sat in the shade of a Cresthaven at the edge of the lane and ate, and the orchard ticked and breathed around them. The fruit was the size of walnuts, hard and green and fuzzed, impossible quantities of it, the limbs already beginning to bow. June drop had come and gone and the trees had kept more than they shed. Curt was right about the set. It was the best Nathan had seen since before their father's first stroke.

"Tell me something," Curt said. He was peeling a sandwich apart, inspecting it, a habit from boyhood. "When you picture it sold. The forty-one lots, the cul-de-sacs. You picture yourself driving past it ever?"

"I don't drive past anything out here. I live in Denver."

"That's not an answer."

Nathan looked down the row. The light came through the canopy in coins and the irrigation furrows, dry now for weeks at this end, were cracked into hexagons like the floor of a dead lake. "No," he said. "I don't picture driving past it. I picture Mom's bill getting paid on the third of the month without you lying awake. I picture you fifty-five years old with two good hips and a job that comes with a paycheck instead of a loan officer. I picture you maybe meeting somebody, since we're picturing things, because Deb's been gone six years and you live with a dog and twelve hundred trees and the dog died in February."

"Leave the dog out of it."

"I picture you free, Curt. That's what I picture. I know you hear it as me killing something. I'm telling you from out here it looks like a man chained to a thing that's drowning, telling everybody the chain is a family heirloom."

Curt was quiet for a long time. A hawk turned over the east block, and somewhere up the valley a baler was running, the steady cardiac thump of it carrying for miles in the still air.

"You know why you got to leave?" Curt said finally. His voice wasn't angry, which was worse. "You ever actually think about the mechanism of it? You got to go to Fort Collins and then to Denver and have your career and your townhouse and your seat on a plane twice a month because I stayed. Somebody had to be the one who stayed, and it was decided — nobody decided it, but it was decided — by about 1992, and it was me, and that meant it wasn't you. Dad never asked you to come back. Not when he had the stroke, not when the hail took the whole crop in '09, not at the end. You know why? Because he had me. I was the reason you got to be free, little brother. So when you sit under this tree and offer me freedom like it's something you've got in your pocket, you'll forgive me if I notice where it came from."

Nathan sat with that. The slush of peaches had gone warm in the bag, syrup-sweet, smelling like August.

"You're right," he said.

Curt looked at him, suspicious of the concession.

"You're right, and I've known it for thirty years, and I let it be that way because it suited me, and I'm sorry, and none of it changes one line of the arithmetic. That's the part I can't get past, Curt. You can be right about all of it — about Dad, about me, about what Mom's holding onto at the bottom of the fire — and the number at the bottom of the page is still the number. Being owed something doesn't pay for anything. I'd know. I write checks to people who are owed things for a living, and the check is never the thing they were owed, and they cash it anyway, because the alternative to cashing it is nothing."

Curt folded the sandwich bag into a small square, the way their mother used to, to wash and reuse. He put it in his shirt pocket. "Glue's got another hour," he said, and got up in his stages and walked off down the row, and Nathan let him go.

He found things to do. He filled the hole partway, packing the clay around the repaired section by hand, leaving the joints exposed so they could watch them under pressure. He coiled the loose wire by the riser. He walked the lower piece, the twelve rows that had gone dry, and the trees there told the story in their leaves, dull and inward-curled, the little fruit on them already soft-shouldered, and he stood among them in the heat and understood, in his body if not in his ledger, what their mother had meant at the window. It's drying out down there. The dread of it was older than money. It was the oldest dread there was, maybe — water failing to arrive at the roots of something you were responsible for — and his brother had been living inside that dread alone for six years, and Nathan had been receiving photographs of blossoms on his phone every April and texting back nice.

When the two hours were up and then some, Curt came back down the lane with the long steel key for the headgate over his shoulder like a rifle, and the two of them walked up to the ditch together without discussing it. The ditch was running well, for June; the Mesa had held its snow late this year. Curt fitted the key, looked at Nathan once, and cranked the gate.

The water went into the pipe with a sound like a held breath released, a hollow swallowing rush that traveled down the buried line faster than a man could walk, and they walked behind it anyway, following it back down the lane to the hole, and stood over the repair while the line filled and the pressure came up. The new joints sat there, purple-stained, dry. A minute. Two. Curt crouched and ran his thumb along the coupling, and his thumb came away dusty.

"Holding," he said.

They opened the riser valves down the lower piece one by one, and the gated pipe along the rows began to spit and then to flow, and the water moved out into the furrows that had been dry since May, finding the cracks, darkening, the dead-lake floor going the color of fresh-turned earth row by row. It made a sound when it reached the dry ground, a faint hissing crackle, the soil drinking faster than the water could spread.

"Dad used to say that sounds like rain," Curt said. "Water hitting dry ground. He'd stand right about here with his thumbs in his belt like he'd invented it."

They watched it run. The sun was leaning west now and the light had gone long and copper down the rows, and the water glittered in the furrows as it crept, and the trees stood over it, and Nathan thought that he had adjusted claims on hailed-out wheat and burned subdivisions and one entire flooded town, had assigned dollar values to more loss than most men ever see, and had no number for this, for water arriving where it was needed while two brothers watched, and that the absence of a number was precisely Curt's whole argument, and that Curt was right, and that they were going to sell anyway.

Curt knew it too. Nathan could see it in the way he stood, thumbs in his belt, unconsciously, the old man's posture settling over him like weather. He'd known it since March, probably. Maybe since the dog.

"Here's what I'll sign," Curt said. He didn't look over. "I'll call their man Monday and tell him we accept contingent on a September closing. After harvest. They'll take it; dirt-work season's done by then anyway, they won't break ground till spring regardless. I bring in this crop. Every peach. I sell the good fruit to the packer and the Elbertas off the card table like always, and the money goes in Mom's account, and the day the last bin leaves this place I'll sign whatever you put in front of me." Now he looked over. "But the trees get water till then. Every drop they've got coming. I'm not going to stand in a dying orchard. I'll stand in a finished one, but not a dying one. That's the difference, and if you don't understand the difference, take my word that there is one."

"I understand the difference," Nathan said, and found that he did, or was beginning to.

"And the Elberta block. I want it in the contract that I can take scion wood off those trees in the winter, before the dozers. Grafting wood. There's a guy in Hotchkiss runs a nursery, he'll work with me. Those trees came from Grandpa Holt's homestead. The variety's nothing special but the strain is ours. I'm not letting it end in a burn pile."

"I'll have the lawyer write it in."

"All right then." Curt let out a breath he might have been holding since April, since March, since 1992. "All right."

They stayed until the water reached the far ends of the rows, which took until nearly dusk, the two of them walking the lower piece with shovels, opening a furrow here, checking a gate there, not talking much, and the swallows came out over the orchard and worked the air above the wet ground, and the smell rose up around them, mud and leaf and the green almost-scent of the hard little fruit, the smell of the place succeeding at the only thing it had ever been asked to do. At the bottom of the last row Curt stopped and crouched, in stages, and put his hand flat on the dark soaked ground the way you'd put a hand on an animal's flank, and Nathan stood behind him and said nothing, and the water kept coming down the row toward them, slow and bright in the last light, sounding, exactly as their father had promised, like rain.
