By two o'clock the house had given up most of its weight. June stood in the doorway between the front room and the hall with a cigar box of small bills in her hands, watching two men in matching gray t-shirts angle her mother's davenport through a door it had entered forty-one years earlier, when men with different faces and the same careful grunting had angled it in. The plastic was off it now. Her mother had kept that sofa under plastic so long the brocade beneath had stayed the green of new June leaves, a green nobody in the family had been allowed to sit on except at Christmas and after funerals, and now a woman from Elyria had bought it for thirty-five dollars and the men were taking it out into the ordinary light of a Saturday afternoon where anybody at all could put their body on it.

Thirty-five dollars. Odessa Pruitt had paid two hundred and ten for it in 1962, on time, a dollar fifty a week carried in a separate envelope so it would never get confused with rent money or insurance money or the money for Brother's shoes, and when the last payment was made she had sat the whole family down on the kitchen chairs facing it, as if it were television, and said, Now. That's ours. Nobody can come take that.

Nobody had come to take it. June had let it go.

The sign at the curb said ESTATE SALE in her own block letters, and underneath, smaller, EVERYTHING MUST GO, which was a lie the way most printed things were a lie, because some things were not going anywhere—the cedar chest was in the back of June's car already, wrapped in a moving blanket like a body, and the cast-iron skillet was on the passenger seat beside it, black and patient, and nobody had been allowed upstairs into the back bedroom where the hospital bed had been until Tuesday, when the rental company came for it, and where the smell of camphor and Jergens lotion and the particular under-smell of her mother's dying still hung in the curtains. June had shut that door and put a chair in front of it. People understood a chair in front of a door. They turned around on the landing and came back down and bought hatboxes instead.

There were hatboxes. Lord, there were hatboxes. Eleven of them, stacked in the hall like a wedding cake, and inside them the hats of a woman who had not missed a Sunday at Mount Olive Baptist in fifty-six years except for the Sunday after Hen died and the four Sundays at the end when her legs forgot what they were for. Felt hats and straw hats and one navy hat with a half-veil that Odessa called her funeral hat, as though she had only one occasion of grief in her and one hat to meet it in. A white woman with a canvas tote bag that said LIFE IS BETTER AT THE LAKE was going through them now, trying on the funeral hat in the hall mirror, turning her chin left and right.

"These are just darling," the woman said. "Were they your mother's?"

"Yes."

"She had wonderful taste."

"She had a wonderful head," June said, and the woman laughed the way people laugh when they don't know whether something is a joke, a little air pushed out of the nose, and put the hat back and bought a salt cellar instead. Fifty cents. June made change from the cigar box, which had been her father's, which had held washers and fuses and a 1943 steel penny that Brother used to ask to see the way other children asked for stories, and which was not for sale either, though it had a price tag on it because Lucille had gotten carried away with the price tags Friday night, sticking them on everything, both of them half a glass of sweet wine into the work, and June had laughed until she had to sit down on the stairs and then had cried so hard and so suddenly that Lucille just stood there in the wreckage of priced things and said, All right now. All right. Let it come.

It had come. Then it had gone back to wherever it stayed, and Saturday arrived clear and warm, one of those October days Ohio hands out like apology, sugar maples burning slow up and down the street, and the people came.

They came the way they had always come into this town, which was on rivers of work and rumors of work, except the work was gone now—the plant had been closed since '83, its windows the milky blue-gray of cataract—and what came down Mulberry Street these days were people hunting other people's lives at a discount. They parked their trucks half on the curb. They walked into the house Henderson and Odessa Pruitt had bought in 1959 from a Hungarian widow named Mrs. Szabo, the only person on the street who would sell to them, the rest of the block standing behind their screens watching the moving truck like it was weather coming. Bought it whole, in money orders, because the bank downtown had a way of losing the applications of certain people, a forgetfulness so reliable you could set a clock by it. Her father used to say the house cost twice what it cost. He never explained the arithmetic. He didn't have to.

Now they walked through it touching things. That was the part June had not prepared for, although Lucille had tried to tell her. Not the selling. The touching. A man in a Bengals cap ran his thumb along the dovetail joints of her father's toolbox the way you'd check a horse's teeth. A young couple, sweet-faced, in matching glasses, turned the kitchen chairs upside down hunting for a maker's mark, hoping somebody famous had made them so they could carry them to Columbus and sell them again, and June wanted to say, somebody famous did, his name was Henderson Pruitt and he was famous on this street for thirty years for being the man who could fix anything except the things that broke his children, but she said three dollars apiece, four for ten, and the couple took all four and seemed to feel they had gotten away with something, and they had, and it was fine, and it was not fine, and both of those were true the way both of those had been true all afternoon.

"You holding up?" Lucille asked. Lucille Boggs, seventy-one, in her good slacks, had taken over the kitchen table where the linens were laid out, doilies and tatted-edge pillowcases and the tablecloth with the cherries that had only come out for Thanksgiving, and was selling them with the air of a woman doing the buyers a favor they had not earned. Lucille had buried two husbands and a sister and was on the usher board and knew that grief was mostly logistics until suddenly it wasn't, and her job today was to be there for the suddenly.

"I'm holding," June said.

"You eat?"

"I will."

"That's not what I asked you."

A child came down the hall holding one of the porcelain figurines from the whatnot shelf—the shepherdess, the one with the chipped crook, chipped in 1971 when June was dusting too fast because the Jackson 5 were going to be on television—holding it out in two hands like something rescued from water, and behind the child came the mother saying how much, and June heard herself say a quarter, and the child's whole face came open like a window. Twenty-five cents. The shepherdess had stood on that shelf for half a century, presiding, untouchable, the law of her mother's front room, and now she was going to ride to some other town in a child's lap and stand on some other shelf or get her head broken off by Tuesday, and either way she was a quarter, and June put the quarter in the cigar box and thought: this is what an afternoon can do. It can reprice the world.

Her mother would have hated all of it. That was the thing June kept arriving at and walking away from and arriving at again, the way you keep touching a sore tooth with your tongue. Odessa had been a keeper. Not a hoarder—everything in its place, everything wiped down—but a keeper, a woman who understood that for people who had come up from nothing, things were not things. Things were proof. The good dishes proved Sunday. The chifforobe proved the wages of 1957. The piano in the front room, which nobody played after Brother left, proved that her children had options whether they took them or not, and Odessa had paid to have it tuned every other year until the end, paid a man to come and make perfect an instrument no hands would touch, and when June, exasperated, home on a visit, had asked her why, her mother had looked at her over her glasses with the long patience she saved for her daughter's smart questions and said, "Because it's mine to keep right."

The piano had gone at noon. A church over in Oberlin bought it, two deacons and a teenager with a dolly, and that one June could live with, that one had a shape she could hold—the piano going off to be played at last, hymns finally coming out of it after fifty years of furniture silence, her mother's investment maturing in some other sanctuary. She had watched them ease it down the porch steps, all that black lacquered weight, and when one of the deacons said, "We'll take good care of it, sister," she had nodded and not been able to say anything at all, and the man had understood that too, and tipped his hat, an actual hat, the kind of hat that would have known her mother's hats.

But the dining table. The dining table was where the afternoon turned.

A man had been circling it for the better part of an hour. June had clocked him the way her mother had taught her to clock men in stores—not staring, just keeping him in the side of her knowing—a heavyset man in a golf shirt who picked things up and set them down with a little exhale through his nose, a sound that priced everything lower than its tag. The table was oak, claw-footed, with two leaves stored in the hall closet, and it had been her grandmother's, which meant it had come up from Alabama in 1937 in the back of a truck with everything else Mama Ceil owned, which was not much, which was that table and a cedar chest and four children and a Bible with the births written in it and a grief she did not discuss. The table had crossed the Ohio River. June had written $200 on the tag and felt like a thief writing it, a thief against her own people, because what was the number for a table that had crossed the river? There wasn't one. Two hundred was just the sound she made instead of howling.

"I'll give you sixty," the man said.

"The tag says two hundred."

"It's got water rings. There's a crack starting in that leaf." He pointed with his chin, hands in his pockets. "Sixty's fair. End of the day you'll be begging somebody to haul it."

And there it was. June had been polite for five hours. She had been polite to the woman who asked if the family had owned the house or just rented, polite to the man who wondered out loud what the neighborhood used to be, with a little weight on used to, polite to the couple who called her mother's bedroom suite "primitive" with delight in their voices, as though primitive were a flavor. Politeness was a skill set; she had inherited it along with the table; her mother could be polite at temperatures that would kill livestock. But there was a place underneath the politeness, and the man in the golf shirt had found it with his chin and his sixty dollars.

"It's not for sale," June said.

"Tag's on it."

"Tags come off." She peeled it off, slow, and put it in her apron pocket. "It's not for sale."

The man looked at her for a moment, deciding something about her, some old decision men like him kept on file, and then he shrugged with his whole soft body and said something under his breath on his way out that she chose, as an act of mercy toward her own afternoon, not to hear. Lucille had drifted over with a stack of doilies like a hand of cards.

"You keeping it?"

"I can't keep it. I don't have a dining room. I have a condominium in Cleveland with a kitchen you can touch both walls of."

"Mm," said Lucille, which in Lucille meant the conversation was not over, only resting.

It was almost four when the answer walked in. June would tell it later, to her own daughter, on the phone, the way her mother had told things—not as coincidence but as arrangement, because Odessa did not believe in coincidence, Odessa believed the world was a made thing and the maker paid attention—a young woman came in, late twenties maybe, two kids orbiting her, a boy and a girl, and tired the particular way June recognized, tired in the wrists, tired from carrying. She moved through the picked-over rooms touching nothing, pricing everything with her eyes, doing arithmetic June could read from across the room because she had watched her mother do it in stores for eighteen years: the math of almost. And the young woman stopped at the table. Put her hand flat on it. Stood there.

"That's a good table," June said, coming over.

"It's beautiful." The woman took her hand back like she'd been caught. "We just moved into a place over on Tenth. We've been eating on a card table." She laughed a little, embarrassed at having said it, and her daughter leaned against her hip, and her son was looking at the claw feet, crouched down, his finger tracing a talon.

"It came up from Alabama," June said. "Nineteen thirty-seven. My grandmother brought it. It seats twelve with the leaves in."

"I don't think I could—what are you asking?"

June heard the number leave her before she had finished deciding it. "It needs to go to a house where people eat together. That's what it costs. You got a way to haul it?"

The woman looked at her. Looked at her in that long, level, are-we-doing-this way, the way women look at each other over the heads of children when something serious is changing hands, and her eyes filled and she set her mouth against it and won, mostly, and said, "My brother has a truck."

"Then call your brother."

Lucille, at the kitchen table, was busying herself with the doilies and not smiling, which was how June knew she was smiling. And later, when the brother came, and the three of them carried the table out into the gold-slanted four-thirty light, the leaves wrapped in towels, June stood on the porch and watched it cross the yard her father had seeded and reseeded against the shade of the maple, and she thought: it's still moving. The table had been moving since 1937. It had only paused here, sixty-some years on Mulberry Street, a long pause but a pause, and now it was moving again, north by ten blocks, into a house with a card table and children, and her grandmother—who June remembered only as a smell of snuff and starch and a lap like a shelf—her grandmother had not carried that table out of Alabama so it could die in a condominium in Cleveland or be sixty-dollared by a man with his hands in his pockets. She had carried it so people could eat off it. Some debts you paid forward because the people you owed were gone.

After that, the afternoon let down like a fever breaking. The crowd thinned. The light went long and amber, and the empty rooms began to do what empty rooms do, which is talk. Footsteps had a new sound in the front room without the rug—the rug had gone at one, rolled and tied, on the shoulder of a man who carried it like a body to a pickup—and June walked through the house in that new sound, hearing it, the house saying its bones out loud for the first time since 1959. Here was the rectangle of unfaded wallpaper where the wedding photograph had hung, Hen serious in his one suit, Odessa nineteen and daring the camera. Here was the burn scar on the kitchen counter, hidden under the breadbox for forty years, from the night Brother heated the comb for June's hair because their mother was working a double for the Mecklins across town, and he did her edges so badly and so tenderly that she wore a scarf for a week and never told. Brother. The house was full of him in the way only an empty house can be full of someone—he had been gone since '69, gone to the army because the recruiter came to the school like he had come to all the schools on that side of town, harvesting, and gone to Vietnam, and then home but not home, and then to Detroit, and then to wherever Detroit had taken him, and Odessa had kept his room exactly until 1980 and then quietly, without announcement, stopped keeping it, which was its own kind of funeral. There had been nothing of his left to sell today. That had been done long ago, in increments, by grief.

Lucille found her standing in the kitchen with the cigar box.

"That's it then," Lucille said. "I sent the last ones off. Woman tried to buy the curtains out the upstairs window. I told her this isn't that kind of sale." She set the cash from the kitchen drawer on the counter, folded in a rubber band. "You'll want to count it."

"I don't want to count it."

"I know it. Count it anyway. Money likes to be counted, sweetheart, it's the only respect it understands." Lucille put her hand on June's back, between the shoulder blades, flat and warm, the church hand, the hand that had steadied two generations of women at gravesides, and they stood like that for a while in the kitchen where Odessa Pruitt had cooked maybe twenty thousand meals, the stove sold, the table sold, the chairs sold, the room down to its yellow walls and the light coming through the window over where the sink still was, because sinks stay.

"She'd have hated this," June said. "All those people. Touching."

"No," Lucille said, after a minute. "She'd have hated it for about an hour. Then she'd have repriced everything and run it better than you." And the laugh came up out of June from somewhere below the day, helpless, her mother entire in that one sentence, Odessa with her cigar box and her sharp eye, Odessa who could fold a dollar so tight it whimpered, and Lucille laughed too, and the empty kitchen took their laughing and gave it back bigger, the way empty kitchens do.

When Lucille had gone, June made the last walk alone. Up the stairs, her hand on the banister her hand knew better than it knew her own face. She moved the chair from the back bedroom door and stood in the doorway and did not turn on the light. The curtains held the last of the sun. In this room her mother had gotten small, month by month, a great keeper of things becoming a thing kept, and in this room on a Tuesday in August she had opened her eyes near the end and looked at June with terrible clarity and said, "Don't let them nickel and dime you," and June had thought she meant the hospital, the insurance, the funeral home, all the industries of ending—and maybe she had—but standing in the doorway now June understood it wider, understood it as the whole instruction, the summary of a life: they will try to give you sixty dollars for what crossed the river. Don't you take it.

"I didn't," she said to the room.

Downstairs, she took the key off her ring. The buyers of the house, a young couple, were getting it Monday through the lawyer, but tonight, one more night, the key did what it had always done. She thought about her mother's habit, the spare under the third porch board, kept there for fifty years for children and neighbors and any of God's stray people who might need in, a habit June and Brother had hollered about for decades—Mama, anybody could just walk in—and her mother saying, "Anybody never has. Family always did." June lifted the third board, which still lifted, and laid the spare key in its old dark slot, for nobody, for everybody, for the house itself, and let the board back down.

The sign was still in the yard. EVERYTHING MUST GO. She pulled it up and laid it flat in the grass, face down, because everything had not gone and never would; some of it was in the car, and some of it was ten blocks north getting set with plates by now, supper steam rising over claw feet, children's elbows learning that oak; and some of it could not be carried by anyone, would stay in the walls and under the porch boards and in the long October light on Mulberry Street whether the new people knew it or not, the way a tuned piano stays tuned a while in an empty room, holding its pitch in the dark, ready, in case anybody ever sat down to play.

June got in the car. The skillet rode beside her like a quiet passenger, like a black moon, sixty years of seasoning in it, her mother's hand in it, her grandmother's hand in that, and she put her own hand on it once at the stop sign, just to feel the cold iron that had held all that fire, and then she drove north out of town with the maples burning down behind her, counting nothing.
