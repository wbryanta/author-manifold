The taxi set me down at the bottom of the drive because I asked it to, because I wanted the approach on foot, the way I used to take it at six in the morning with my coat collar up against the sea wind, and the driver said are you sure, love, it's a fair walk in those shoes, and I said I knew exactly how far it was. The Clarence stood where it had always stood, white against a grey October sky, and from the gates it looked unchanged, which was the first lie it told me. The proportions were right. Somebody had been careful with the proportions. But the windows were too clean in a way I couldn't have explained to anyone who hadn't washed them, and the third floor — I counted up without meaning to, two, three — the third floor had windows where the linen store used to be, blind brick in my day, and behind one of them a girl in a white dressing gown was holding a glass of something and laughing at someone I couldn't see.

I had not been back in thirty-two years. I had driven past, twice, in the early days, when the front of it was still scaffolded and shrouded and you could see the black licking up from the window frames like the building had been crying mascara. After that I arranged my life so the coast road didn't go that way.

My niece was getting married in the ballroom at four o'clock.

Hannah had asked me at Christmas, in her mother's kitchen, with the particular gentleness the young use when they think they are handling something fragile and historic, whether I would mind. We've looked everywhere, Auntie Edie, she said, and it's just the loveliest place on the whole coast, but if it's too strange for you we'll find somewhere else, honestly. And my sister had stood by the sink not turning around, and I had heard myself say, don't be daft, that was a lifetime ago, I was only a chambermaid there for five minutes, which was true in the way that the front of the hotel was true. I worked at the Clarence for two years, four months and eleven days, and on the last of those days nine people died in it, and one of them was Julie Aspden, who was eighteen, and who was in our room under the eaves because I was not.

The drive was raked gravel now instead of tarmac. There was a man in a long green coat opening doors, which there had not been since before my time even, and he said welcome to the Clarence, madam, are you here for the wedding, and I said I was, and gave my name, and walked into the lobby.

They had moved the desk. That was the first thing. The desk used to run along the left wall, dark mahogany with a brass rail, and the new one floated in the middle of the floor like an island, pale oak, two young people behind it with tablets instead of the great leather ledger Mr. Carmody used to write in with his terrible fountain pen. The floor was new stone made to look like old stone. The smell was wrong — paint, lilies, money, that scent they pipe through expensive places now, fig or vetiver or whatever it is, designed to smell like nothing that ever happened. The old Clarence smelled of toast and floor polish and the sea, and underneath everything, faintly, always, of cigarettes, because in 1992 the whole world smoked and so did the hotel.

The staircase was where the staircase had been. They had rebuilt it to the original plans — I knew this because I had read everything, over the years, every planning notice, every heritage-trust newsletter, in the way you can't stop touching a tooth that aches — and it rose out of the lobby in its double curve exactly as it always had, except that the original had been mahogany seasoned for ninety years and this was something engineered and fire-retardant and lacquered to look like memory. It burned in eleven minutes, the old one. The inquest was very precise about that. Eleven minutes from the first alarm to the stairwell becoming, in the coroner's phrase, untenable, which is a word I have thought about for thirty-two years. Untenable. As though the staircase were an argument the building could no longer defend.

I checked in. The girl with the tablet found my name and said, you're in the Crow's Nest wing, fourth floor, and I must have made a face, because she said, it's our nicest wing, sea views, and I said I was sure it was. There was no fourth floor in my day. The fourth floor was the attic, and the attic was where the live-in staff slept, eight of us girls in four rooms under the slates, and it had one staircase, at the east end, behind a door that was kept locked at night so we couldn't sneak down to the bar, and that staircase was the first thing the fire ate.

The lift was new and silent and had a brushed-steel plate certifying its safe working load and its last inspection. I read every word of it. I read fire notices the way other people read horoscopes. In the corridor on four I counted the exits — there were three, illuminated, generous — and noted the sprinkler heads sitting in the ceiling like small chrome thistles, one every few yards, and the smoke detectors with their patient green eyes, and I thought, as I always think, where were you, and then I let myself into room 412.

It was a beautiful room. I want to be fair about that. It was a beautiful room with a king bed and a view of the sea going pewter under the cloud, and a freestanding bath, and a folder of thick cream card telling me about the spa. I stood in the middle of it in my coat for a long time. I had never slept in this hotel as a person. I had slept in it for two years as staff, which is a different thing, a thing done above the guests' heads, out of sight, six to a bathroom, and I had cleaned perhaps ten thousand rooms like this one — not like this one, plainer, shabbier, candlewick bedspreads and trouser presses — and I had never once, not for one night, been on the receiving end of the bed.

I turned back the corner of the duvet to look at the sheet, because I couldn't help it. Whoever had made the bed had done it well. The corners were proper hospital corners, forty-five degrees, tucked tight. Mrs. Frame, who ran housekeeping then with a ring of keys at her waist like a medieval chatelaine, would have stood over you until you got it right, and Julie got it right before I did, and taught me, both of us giggling, on a stripped bed in room 207 one wet Tuesday — like an envelope, Edie, you're posting the mattress, look — and the memory came up through my hands so fast and so whole that I had to sit down on the bed I had just disturbed, in my coat, with the sea going on and on outside the window, doing what it does, which is not caring.

Julie Aspden was from Bolton and wanted to be a holiday rep in Spain, which was the most glamorous future either of us could imagine. She had a laugh that got us both in trouble and a boyfriend called Gary she was forever finishing with, and on the night of the seventeenth of October she said she'd cover for me if Mrs. Frame did her rounds, because I was going down the fire escape to meet Michael Doyle, the porter, to smoke his cigarettes and let him put his cold hands inside my coat. If Frame knocks I'll say you're in the bath, she said. She was sitting up in her bed with a magazine and her hair in a towel. That's the last of her. That's the whole of the last of her: the towel, the magazine, ta-ra then, don't do anything I wouldn't.

The fire started behind the third-floor linen store, in the wiring, they said, old cloth-covered cable that had been smouldering in the wall for nobody knows how long, and it found the laundry chute and the lift shaft and went up the building's throat. I was on the fire escape on the west side, half past midnight, Michael's jacket round my shoulders, when the windows of the third floor turned the wrong colour, one after another, like someone walking along a corridor switching on lamps, and I remember saying, quite calmly, that's not right, before either of us understood, before the first glass went and the night cracked open.

I went back in. I want that said, though there's no one to say it to. I went in through the kitchen door and up the back stairs banging on doors on two and three with Michael behind me bellowing, and we got people out, the inquest counted them, the inquest was kind to me, everyone has always been kind to me, and when we got to the east end the door to the attic stairs was open and the stairwell behind it was a chimney, it was a thing you could not put a hand into, and a fireman took me round the waist while I screamed names up into it, and that is the part of the story that I have never told at a kitchen table, and never will, because there is no shape you can give it that doesn't sound like asking to be forgiven.

Nine died. Five of them were ours — staff, I mean, girls and one kitchen boy, in the attic, in their beds or near them. Julie was found by the window. The window in our room didn't open more than six inches; there was a screw in the frame, against the draughts. I have thought about that screw more than I have thought about almost anything in my life.

I hung my dress on the bathroom door and ran the tap and stood at the window of room 412, which opened fully — I checked, I opened it all the way and put my head out into the cold — and below me the wedding cars were beginning to arrive, ribbons rippling, and the gravel filled up with hats.

The ceremony was in the ballroom at four, as advertised. I took an aisle seat near the back, near the doors — I always sit near the doors, my family thinks it's modesty — and the room was honey-coloured and high and strung with greenery and fairy lights, and a string quartet played something gentle, and I located the exits, four of them, and the extinguishers, two visible, and then I made myself stop and be a person at a wedding. Hannah came down the rebuilt staircase on her father's arm — they'd propped the ballroom doors so we could all watch the descent, that's why people get married at the Clarence, for that staircase — and she looked the way brides look, like a struck match in the best sense, all light, and I cried like everybody else and possibly for an extra reason or two, and when she passed my row she found my eye for half a second and I gave her a wink, and her mouth wobbled, and on she went.

They were married in nine minutes. I timed it without meaning to. Eleven minutes is longer than people think.

At the reception I was put at a table of aunts, which is where I live now, in the republic of aunts, and we ate the dinner and clapped the speeches, and Tom's best man told a story about a stag weekend that I'm certain Hannah will be relitigating into her forties, and the toasts went round, and a woman to my left, an aunt on the groom's side, said, isn't it a gorgeous building, you'd never know, would you, and I said know what, and she lowered her voice the way people do and said, there was a terrible fire here once, donkey's years ago, people died, I read it on the website, they've a little memorial garden — and then my sister, two seats down, said, Edie used to work here, before, in a bright voice like setting down a tray, and the aunt said, oh, isn't that funny, and I agreed that it was funny, and the band started up.

I found the memorial garden between the cheese course and the first dance. It was off the west terrace, through a glass door, a square of low box hedging and a bench and a young rowan tree, and on the wall a brass plate, not large, lamplit, IN MEMORY OF THOSE WHO LOST THEIR LIVES AT THE CLARENCE HOTEL, 18 OCTOBER 1992, and the nine names in two columns. They had her as Julia. Julia Aspden. Nobody on this earth ever called her Julia; her own mother called her Julie at the funeral, in the eulogy, in front of God. I stood with my glass in the cold and looked at that one wrong letter, that little Sunday-best A the brass engraver or some solicitor's record had put on her, and I took the hem of my good silk scarf and I polished the plate, all of it, both columns, the way you'd do a bathroom tap, until my breath stopped catching, and then I went back inside, because the music had changed and they'd be looking for the aunts to dance.

I danced. I want that on the record too. I danced with my brother-in-law and with a nine-year-old page boy who took it very seriously, and I drank two glasses of champagne, and it was, by any measure a fair witness could apply, a lovely wedding.

It was getting on for ten when I slipped out. There's a door beside every hotel ballroom that guests are trained not to see, a flat door painted the colour of the wall, and the Clarence's new one was within a yard of where the old one had been, as though service is a river that re-cuts the same channel no matter how you rebuild the banks. I pushed it and it gave, and I stepped through into the other hotel — the true one, the one behind the one — strip lighting and lino and a trolley of dirty glasses, the band's music suddenly muffled like weather, and the smell at last was honest: detergent, steam, hot electrics, food. My shoulders came down an inch. I could have wept at a corridor.

I walked along it with my champagne glass in my hand like a woman who'd merely lost her way, past the swing door to the kitchens, past a noticeboard with a rota on it — the names on the rota were Magda and Ionela and Princess and Dario, where ours had been Julie and Sandra and Pauline and me, the same work flowing through different countries now, the same six a.m. — and at the corner by the service lift I came upon a linen trolley, parked, loaded, and a girl in a grey uniform pulling pillowcases off it, late turn-downs, and she startled when she saw me and said, sorry, madam, this area is staff only, are you lost?

I should have said yes. Instead I watched her hands. She was wrestling a pillow into its case the slow way, the way you do in your first month, stuffing it down by the corners, and before I had decided anything I had set my glass on the trolley shelf and said, you'll be all night doing it like that, look, and I took a pillow and a case off the stack, and folded the pillow in half along itself, dropped it in, let it open like a wing inside the cotton, shook once, done. Five seconds. She stared at me, this girl, twenty perhaps, tired around the eyes the way I knew from the inside, and she looked at my dress and my grey hair and my wedding shoes, and she said, you worked housekeeping.

Here, I said. A long time ago. Before you were born. Before your mother was born, possibly.

She smiled then, properly, and held out a pillow, and so it was that at ten at night in the back corridor of the Clarence Hotel a wedding guest in raw silk and a chambermaid from Cluj cased nine pillows between them in under a minute, and laughed, and I showed her the trick with the duvet too, the inside-out grab, and her radio crackled and she had to go, and as she steered the trolley away she looked back and said, it's a nice hotel to work, they're not bad here, there's a sprinkler in even the cupboards now, you know — as if she knew, as if it was written on me — and the lift took her, and I stood in the corridor until the music from the ballroom found me again through the wall.

At midnight they did the sparklers. Everyone out onto the drive in coats over their finery, two long lines, a tunnel of small fires for the bride and groom to run through, and a young cousin came down the line lighting us one from another, flame passed hand to hand, and I felt the old animal in my chest sit up when mine spat into life. I held it at arm's length. I held it and I made myself look at it, the white core, the sparks falling and dying on the gravel where I'd walked ten thousand mornings, and Hannah came running through the tunnel of light with her dress gathered up in both fists and her husband's hand in the small of her back, shrieking, radiant, and as she passed me — I will swear to this — she turned her head and said, thank you for coming back, Auntie Edie, just that, and was gone into the flashbulbs, and I stood holding my little fire until it burned all the way down to my fingers, and I did not drop it. I set it, dead, in the bucket of sand they'd put out, because somebody sensible had put out a bucket of sand, and I could have kissed whoever it was.

In room 412 the lamps were low and the curtains drawn and the bed had been turned down — Magda, or Ionela, or my girl from the corridor — the corner of the duvet folded back in a clean diagonal, a chocolate on the pillow, the radio playing something quiet, all the small mercies of the trade performed for me by hands I'd never see. I stood at the foot of the bed for a while. Then I did what I suppose I had come back to do, though I hadn't known it until my hands started: I stripped the turn-down and remade it. Pulled the sheet drum-tight, squared the corners at forty-five degrees, posted the mattress like an envelope, plumped the pillows with one blow each the way Mrs. Frame liked, folded the diagonal back sharper than any inspection could fault. A bed you could bounce a coin on, Julie used to say, that's the standard, and we'd bounce a ten-pence and catch it, and once we couldn't stop laughing and Mrs. Frame's keys came chiming down the corridor and we hid in the bathroom like criminals, two girls with our whole lives in front of us, in the attic hotel, in the year of our Lord 1992.

I opened the window six inches and then, because I could, all the way. The sea was loud and the night came in cold and clean over the sill. Down on the drive the last sparklers were going out one by one. I counted my exits — the door, the window, the green-lit stairs at the end of the corridor — the way I say my prayers, which is to say faithlessly, devoutly, every night of my life. Then I turned off the lamp and got into the bed I had made, in the house that was wearing the old house's face, and I said goodnight to her, out loud, by her right name, and the sprinkler head glinted above me in the dark like a small chrome flower, keeping its one promise, and I slept.
