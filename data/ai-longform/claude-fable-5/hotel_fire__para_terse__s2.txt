The hotel had grown back wrong, the way skin does. I knew that before I was out of the taxi, before the new portico with its heated flagstones, before the doorman in a greatcoat the old Windermere never issued. The proportions were almost right. The tower stood where the tower had stood, the long lake-facing wing reached toward the boathouse as it always had, and the name was carved over the entrance in the same fat Edwardian letters. But the windows were larger now, and there were more of them, and the whole building had the smooth, poreless look of a thing made all at once instead of accumulated. Scar tissue is like that. It does the job of skin. It keeps the weather out. It just doesn't crease where the body wants to bend, and it never grows hair again, and if you have spent thirty years nursing burned people, as I have, you can spot it across a room.

The doorman wished me a good afternoon and reached for my bag, and I let him take it, which took an act of will I doubt he registered. Forty-two years ago I would have been the one reaching. Not for luggage—chambermaids didn't touch luggage, that was the bellmen's kingdom and they defended it—but I knew the reflex, the slight forward tip of the body that says your burden is my work. I was nineteen the last time I walked under that name. I had a navy uniform with white piping that we had to launder ourselves, and a passkey on a lanyard, and chilblains, and a best friend named Renata Sousa who taught me to fold a towel in thirds with the rounded edge facing out because, she said, a raw edge looks like an apology.

The lobby smelled of lilies and lit candles and money, and under those, faintly, of new carpet adhesive, which the management probably believed was no smell at all. The grand staircase had been rebuilt wider and more sweeping than the original, with a runner the color of cabernet, and a young woman in a headset was directing two men carrying an arch of white roses up it. The Aldous-Whitfield wedding, the easel by the elevators announced. Welcome.

"Checking in? Welcome to the Windermere," said the clerk, a boy with a careful beard, and then, reading his screen, "Oh—welcome back, Ms. Mott. It says here you've stayed with us before."

"Not exactly stayed," I said, and let it lie there. Claire must have noted something on the reservation. He upgraded me, beaming, to a lake-view room on the fourth floor, and I heard myself say thank you, the fourth floor would be fine, while some older animal in me sat up and turned its head.

Four was my floor. December of 1976, I had fourteen rooms on four, the east end, and Renata had the west end of five, and on the night of the Pelletier wedding we were both working late turndown because the banquet ran long and the guests stayed down in the ballroom drinking sidecars and the housekeeper, Mrs. Vance, said we could not turn a bed with a guest in the room and could not leave until every bed was turned, which was the kind of arithmetic the Windermere ran on. I remember the music coming up the stairwell, a band doing Cole Porter at three-quarter speed. I remember the cold on the window glass when I drew the curtains in 412, and Mrs. Aldous in the armchair with her shoes off and her feet on the radiator, telling me she had no intention of going back down to watch people younger than her gallbladder do the hustle. She was sixty then, which seemed to me a fabulous antiquity. I am older now than she was that night.

I rode the new elevator up with the bellman, who told me the hotel had won an award for its restoration, and I said wasn't that something. My room was 408. The corridor was wider than the old one, the doors farther apart, everything pale and hushed, and the room itself was lovely, I will say that, with the lake lying pewter-colored under a low December sky and the far hills already going blue. The bed was king-sized and turned down although it was barely three o'clock. The towels in the bath were folded in thirds, rounded edge out. I stood there longer than I should have, looking at that fold, and then I tipped the bellman too much and sat on the edge of the bed and let the building settle around me.

It started in the kitchens. That was the inquest's finding, anyway—a flue fire that found its way into the walls, into the chases and voids of a hotel that had been wired and re-wired and plumbed and re-plumbed for seventy years, a building that was by then mostly cavity, mostly air and old fir studs furred with seven decades of dust. The kitchen staff had been running flambé carts all night for the Pelletier banquet, cherries jubilee at every third table, and there were people in town who said until the day they died that it was the flambé, but the inquest said flue, and the hotel's insurers liked flue, and flue it has been ever since. What I know is that it was in the walls a long time before it was anywhere a person could see it. I know because I smelled it. I was coming out of 415 with my cart and I smelled hot dust, the smell an iron makes left face-down, and I stood in the corridor turning my head like a dog, and Carl from engineering came up the service stairs and said somebody had complained about the radiators and he was checking, and we agreed it was probably the radiators, because a hotel is a machine for agreeing that everything is probably fine.

The alarm, when it finally rang, rang downstairs first, and people in the ballroom laughed. I have read the accounts; I didn't see it; I was on four. They thought it was part of the evening, or a drill, or a prank, and the band kept playing for a minute or two, and then the maître d' got up on the bandstand, and by then smoke was coming through the heating registers into the ballroom like something being poured, and the laughing stopped all at once, the way a tap stops.

On four I heard the bells and I felt the building change. I can't say it better than that. A building you work in every day has a sound, a pressure, a breath, and the Windermere's breath went wrong. The lights stayed on. That surprises people; they imagine darkness, but the lights burned almost to the end, very calm and yellow, while the smoke came down the corridor ceiling in a layer you could have rested a tray on. I banged doors. Most of four was downstairs at the banquet, thank God, the rooms empty, the beds turned, my evening's work all done and about to burn. Mrs. Aldous answered her door in a dressing gown with her reading glasses pushed up in her hair, irritated, then not irritated. She wanted to take her jewelry case and I let her, and she wanted to take her good coat and I let her, and she wanted to find her shoes and I said, "Mrs. Aldous, you'll walk down in your stockings or I'll carry you," which was a wild thing for a chambermaid to say to a guest, and she looked at me for one second and then she put her hand in mine.

I did not take her to the grand staircase, which was already a chimney, though we didn't know that yet; I took her to the service stairs because they were my stairs, because I went up and down them thirty times a day, because I knew there were sixty-seven steps between four and the ground and I knew them in the dark, and it was dark in there, the one place the lights had failed, dark and surprisingly cold, the smoke not bad yet at our end. I had wet two hand towels in 412's sink—the rounded fold gone forever to ruin, you think of insane things—and we held them over our faces and I counted the steps out loud, sixty-seven of them, my hand on the rail and her hand on my apron strings, gripping so hard that the next morning I found the waistband had cut a welt across me. At the bottom there was a door, and the door was not locked—I want that said, the guest stairs were not locked—and we came out into the service yard into falling snow, and the snow was the most beautiful thing I have ever seen, before or since. It was coming down in big slow theatrical flakes and behind us the kitchen wing was making a sound like a freight yard, and out on the front lawn, when we came around the corner, the wedding was standing in the snow. All of it. A hundred and fifty people in evening clothes on white snow under that orange light, and the bride in the middle of them in her gown, not crying, just standing with her train soaking through, watching the Windermere burn, and her new husband holding her elbow as if she were the thing that might catch.

Eleven people died. Seven were guests, asleep on the upper floors of the east tower where the smoke went first and fastest. Four were staff. One of the four was Renata. She had cleared her end of five and she had gone up, not down, because Mr. Eccles lived on six. He was a permanent resident, a deaf old gentleman who had been at the Windermere since before either of us was born, who left his hearing aids in a saucer on the nightstand every evening, and Renata knew that the way she knew everything about everyone on her floors, and she went up to get him. They found them together in the corridor outside the linen room on six, very close to the door of the staff stairs—the staff stairs, the back ones over the annex—and that door, the inquest heard, was locked, because the staff stairs let onto the annex roof and there had been pilfering that autumn and the locking of that door at night was, the hotel's counsel said, regrettable. Regrettable. I was in the hearing room when he said it, three weeks out of the hospital, my voice still ruined from smoke, and I have hated that word for forty-two years with a steadiness that has outlasted almost everything else I felt that winter.

Mrs. Aldous wrote to me in January. Then in February, and then more or less every month for the rest of her life. She paid for my nursing school—she did it through a lawyer, in a way I couldn't refuse without making a production—and when I tried, once, years later, to thank her properly, she put down her fork and said, "Doreen, I bought my own life from you at a discount and we will not discuss the terms again." She came to my graduation and sat with my mother. She sent a savings bond when my daughter was born. When she died, six years ago, at ninety-six, her son called me before the obituary ran so I wouldn't read it cold, and at the funeral her granddaughter Claire, whom I had met at maybe a dozen Christmases, hugged me so long that people began to look. So when the heavy envelope came in October—Claire Aldous and Marcus Whitfield request the honour of your presence—and I saw the venue, I sat at my kitchen table for a long time, and then I checked the box that said joyfully accepts, because Vivian Aldous would have expected nothing less, and because I had spent forty-two years not going back, and that is long enough to know the not-going-back has become its own kind of room you live in.

The rehearsal dinner was that first night, in a private dining room off the new ballroom. I wore the gray silk I bought for my retirement party and I drank one glass of wine slowly and I was doing fine, genuinely fine, until Claire stood up with her glass. She is a lovely young woman, a pediatric resident, with Vivian's habit of tilting her chin when she means business, and she talked about her grandmother, and about choosing the Windermere, and I felt it coming the way you feel a wave gather under a boat. "Some of you know," she said, "that Grandma Vivian almost died in this hotel. In the fire, in 1976. And the reason she didn't—the reason any of us are here, honestly, the reason my dad exists—is sitting at table three." And the room turned, all those young faces, and she said, "Doreen carried my grandmother down four flights of stairs through the smoke," and they applauded, they stood, some of them, and I sat there with my ruined glass of wine and smiled and lifted a hand, because what was I going to do, stand up at a rehearsal dinner and issue corrections? I didn't carry her. She walked down on her own two stockinged feet, gripping my apron, complaining at the bottom that I had rushed her. The carrying got into the story somewhere over the years, the way stories grow muscle they never had, and Vivian herself used to wave it off and then, late in life, stopped waving, let it stand, maybe even came to remember it that way. The story had been folded so many times, rounded edge out. And there was no Renata in it at all. There never is. A story wants a survivor and a savior and a building; it doesn't want a woman going up while everyone else goes down, because that story doesn't end at table three with applause.

I went looking for the plaque the next morning, early, before the wedding-day machinery started up. I knew there would be one; there is always one. It was in a corridor between the gift shop and the business center, brass, tasteful, about the size of a cafeteria tray. In Memoriam, it said, and the date, and then seven names, the guests, in alphabetical order, Aldous mercifully not among them, and underneath the names a line: and four members of the hotel staff who perished in service. In service. I stood in front of it while a man in a fleece vest wheeled a luggage cart past me, and I read it three times to be sure, and then I went to the front desk and asked the bearded boy, pleasantly, whether anyone knew the names of the four staff members. He was kind about it. He looked, he asked a manager, the manager looked, and they came back apologetic: the plaque had been installed by the previous ownership group during the rebuild, the records from that era hadn't transferred, he could put me in touch with the county historical society. I said the historical society wouldn't be necessary. I knew the names. I had carried them a long way. Renata Sousa. Carl Jablonski. Edward Tine. Ana Pereira. I said them to him across the marble desk, and he wrote them down on a notepad with real care, like a man taking a room-service order he intended to get right, and probably the page went into a drawer and then into a bin, but I had said them in that building, out loud, to a person whose job was to listen, and I had been waiting to do that since the Carter administration.

The wedding was at four, in the ballroom, with the lake going to iron behind the tall windows. The candles down the aisle were electric—fire code, the planner told someone near me, with a little laugh, no open flame anywhere in the building except the kitchen, and I thought, good, and I thought, too late, both at once, the way I have learned a person can. I counted the exits without deciding to. Four in the ballroom, lit, unobstructed. There were sprinkler heads in the ceiling every ten feet, little chrome blossoms, and I looked at them through most of the processional. Claire came down the aisle on her father's arm in a gown with a long train, and the train dragged over the cabernet carpet, and for one bad moment it was snow under it, and then it was carpet again, and she was just a bride, radiant, particular, alive, and I cried like everybody else, for mostly the same reasons as everybody else.

At the reception I danced once, with Claire's father, who told me his mother had talked about me on her last day in the hospice, and I said I hoped she'd said something scandalous, and he laughed and said she'd said, "Tell Doreen the count was sixty-seven," and I had to stop dancing for a second, right there on the floor, because Vivian had remembered the steps. All those years. I'd counted them aloud to keep us both from screaming and she had carried the number sixty-seven through forty years and two husbands' worth of life and handed it back to me through her son on a dance floor. He held my elbow until I could move again, the way the groom had held the bride in the snow, as if I were the thing that might catch.

I left the party around eleven and didn't go to my room. I walked the fourth-floor corridor east, past 408, past 412—there is a 412 again, brass numbers, someone's shoes outside it for no reason anyone polishes shoes anymore—to the end, where a gray door said STAFF ONLY and had a keycard reader where the old knob used to be. I stood with my hand flat against it. Behind it, the stairs would be concrete now, sprinklered, lit by battery packs, sixty-seven steps or some other number entirely. The door opened while my hand was on it and a young woman with a linen cart nearly walked into me, and we both apologized, and she had her name on a badge, Marisol, and a face still soft with the surprise of night shift, twenty maybe, twenty-one. I should have let her pass. Instead I heard myself say, "I used to work here. This floor. A long time ago," and she smiled the polite hostage smile the young give the old, and I said, "Learn the stairs. All of them. In the dark, I mean—count them sometime, so you know them without your eyes," and her smile changed into something that was actually looking at me, and she said, "Because of the fire," not a question, and I said yes, and she said her trainer had mentioned it, one line in orientation, a fire in the seventies. I told her there had been a chambermaid named Renata Sousa who worked the floor above this one, who folded towels in thirds with the rounded edge out, which they still do here, whether anybody knows why or not, and who went up the stairs when everyone else went down, and Marisol stood in the doorway with her cart of clean linen and listened to all of it, every word, at one in the morning, on the clock, and when I finished she said, "Renata Sousa," repeating it carefully, the way you repeat a thing you intend to keep, and that, I think, is the only monument I have ever managed to build, and it is the right size.

In the morning there was a send-off on the front lawn, the lake flat and white-lidded with the first skin of ice, everyone in coats over their party clothes, and the planner's assistants went down the line handing out sparklers. I almost said no. My hand said no before I did, a small refusal in the tendons. Then I took one, because Vivian would have, and a groomsman came along with a lighter cupped against the wind, and my sparkler caught and stood hissing in my glove, throwing its little furious white seeds onto the snow, and Claire and Marcus ran laughing down the corridor of fire we made for them, her train carried over someone's arm, and the cold ate the sparks the instant they were born, and behind us the Windermere stood in the gray morning with all its new windows lit, wearing its old name, holding its breath the way buildings do. I held mine until the sparkler burned down to the wire, and then I held the wire, hot through the glove and then warm and then nothing, and I said her name once, just under the cheering, and gave the dead stick to a boy who was collecting them in a sand bucket, and went inside to pack, sixty-seven steps from anywhere at all.
