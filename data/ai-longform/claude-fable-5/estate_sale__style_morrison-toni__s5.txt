They took the piano first.

Four men, none of them born when Odessa Pruitt bought it, walked it down the porch steps on a quilted mover's blanket, and Dell stood at the card table by the front door with the cigar box of change and watched it go the way you watch a casket go, with your hands folded and your face arranged. Eighty dollars. She had written it on a round green sticker and pressed the sticker to the lid two days ago, in the morning, when pricing things still felt like arithmetic. An upright Kimball, walnut gone the color of strong tea, three keys in the middle octave that stuck in wet weather and a bench whose hinge had been broken since Eisenhower. Her mother had bought it in 1953 from the white Methodists over on Reid Avenue, who were getting a new one, and her father had borrowed Lonnie Greer's truck to haul it, and the whole of Delmar Street came out on their porches to watch a piano arrive at a colored family's house like it was the Queen of Sheba coming up the walk. Now it went the other direction, down the same steps, tilted at an angle that made Dell's stomach tilt with it, and the man who'd bought it counted four twenties into her palm without looking at her and said it was for his daughter, who was musical.

Was she, Dell thought. Well.

It was the second Saturday of October and the light came through the oaks on Delmar Street in coins, and the leaves were turning the wasteful red they turned every year whether anybody stayed to see it or not. Dell Pruitt Sayles, fifty-six years old, had driven down from Columbus on Thursday with a roll of green stickers and a roll of orange stickers and a ledger book and a folding card table, and she had gone through her mother's house room by room putting a price on everything in it, and she had done it dry-eyed, because she had decided in March, at the funeral, that she had cried what she was going to cry. The orange stickers meant firm. The green stickers meant she would listen. Her mother had been dead seven months and the house had sat shut up through a whole spring and a whole summer, breathing its own air, holding its smell — Argo starch and fried onions and Garrett snuff and the lavender water Odessa patted on her throat for sixty years — holding it the way a closed hand holds warmth, and when Dell unlocked the door on Thursday the smell had come out to meet her on the porch like somebody glad of company, and that was the one time she had to sit down on the glider for a minute before she could go in.

The glider was sold now too. A man named Tucker bought it, and she had to look at him twice and then a third time before the boy swam up out of the man, Nathaniel Tucker, who used to walk her home from Dunbar Elementary carrying her books in a stack on his head to make her laugh, who went into the Army and came back quiet and went into the post office and stayed there thirty years. He gave her fifteen dollars for the glider and stood on the porch with his hat in his hand.

"Half this town courted on porch gliders," he said. "You couldn't take a girl no place else. Her mama sitting just inside the screen door with her ears turned up like a dog's." He laughed, and the laugh had gravel in it. "Your mama could hear a hand move in the dark from forty feet."

"She could hear it before it moved," Dell said, and Nathaniel Tucker laughed the gravel laugh again and said he was sorry, sorry about Miss Odessa, the whole street was sorry, what was left of the street, and he carried the glider to his truck by himself, slowly, refusing the help of the young men who were there to scavenge, and Dell understood that he wanted to carry it himself, that the weight of it was part of what he was buying.

Strangers came all afternoon. That was the thing nobody told you about an estate sale, that it was mostly strangers, white people from out in the township with trucks and trailers, dealers from as far as Sandusky with flat eyes that priced a thing before they touched it, young couples who said vintage and said piece — that's a great piece — and touched everything with the pads of their fingers like the house was a museum where the guards had stepped out. They went through Odessa Pruitt's rooms with their heads swiveling. They opened her drawers. Dell had cleared the underthings out herself on Thursday night — Don't you ever let strangers handle your underthings, her mother used to say, talking about hospitals, talking about nursing homes, talking about the end she did not in fact escape, since the end came in a bed at St. Joseph's with a tube in her arm and a nurse's aide named Brittany changing her like a baby — Dell had cleared the drawers and bagged the slips and the brassieres and the rolled stockings and put the bags in her trunk, because some things would go to the church clothing drive and some things would go nowhere at all, would go with Dell to Columbus and into a closet and stay there, and she was not going to examine why.

But everything else had a sticker. The davenport with its fitted plastic that had crackled under every Sunday visitor since 1964, plastic her mother kept on it the way other women kept their figures, so the green of the upholstery underneath was still the green of the day it left Mahler's Furniture, a green nobody had sat on directly in forty years. Sold, twenty-five dollars, to a woman who said she was going to take the plastic right off, and Dell wanted to tell her — don't, you'll let the time out — but she made change instead. The chrome kitchen set. The Philco that hadn't worked since the Bicentennial but that Odessa kept because Pruitt bought it and Pruitt's hands had carried it into the house, and a thing a dead man's hands had carried did not leave by anybody else's. Sold anyway, five dollars, to a boy with a beard who said he collected radios. The mahogany whatnot shelf with the souvenir plates — Niagara Falls, the Wisconsin Dells, places her parents never went, plates that came from other people's trips, brought back by neighbors as proof that somebody from Delmar Street had stood at the lip of falling water. Sold, the shelf and the plates separately, which seemed wrong to Dell, like selling the bird and the cage to different houses, but she had decided on Thursday she was not going to be sentimental, she had told herself that out loud in the empty kitchen — Dell, you are not going to be sentimental — and the kitchen had taken her voice and given nothing back.

It was the chifforobe that broke the agreement she had made with herself.

A young couple from out toward Vermilion bought it, midafternoon, the wife circling it twice with her chin in her hand. It stood in the back bedroom where it had always stood, tall and dark and double-doored, with the long mirror gone cloudy at the bottom corner like a pond icing over, and the deep drawer at the base that did not open for children. Dell had hidden inside it the summer she was seven, in among her mother's church dresses, the navy and the gray and the one wine-colored crepe, breathing lavender and mothball in the dark while her brother Wilbur stomped through the house pretending he couldn't find her, calling her name in a voice cracking from boy to man — Dell. Dell. I know you in here somewhere, girl — and she had stayed so still and so long that the game ended and she didn't know it, and she came out into a kitchen where the lights were on and supper was started and nobody had missed her, and the strange grief of that, the grief of hiding well, had never entirely left her body. Wilbur dead at nineteen in a river in a country she'd had to find in his atlas. The chifforobe still standing.

"We're going to strip it," the wife told Dell pleasantly, counting out forty dollars. "Get all this dark finish off. Lighten it up. They did everything so dark back then, didn't they."

"They did," said Dell.

"And then we'll distress it. Make it look old."

"It is old," said Dell.

"Older," the wife said, and laughed, and her husband laughed, and Dell put the forty dollars in the cigar box and said let me just clear the drawer.

The deep drawer at the base. She had been through it once already, Thursday night — she thought she had been through it — but kneeling in front of it now with the young couple waiting in the doorway like a procession waiting on a coffin, she found that her Thursday hands had only skimmed it, had taken the top layer, the obvious layer, the folded tablecloths and the fan from Greer & Sons Funeral Home with Jesus knocking at a door, and had not gone down to the bottom, where there was newspaper lining yellowed to the color of weak coffee, and under the newspaper a flat Lucky Strike tin with the green almost rubbed away, and the tin would not come open until she remembered — her thumbs remembered before she did — that you pressed the corner first.

Inside: a lock of hair, almost weightless, tied with white thread, and a strip of paper pinned to it that said in her mother's narrow uphill pencil Wilbur Pruitt 1948. A second lock, finer, browner, Dell Pruitt 1961. A two-dollar bill. A milk tooth. And folded into a square smaller than a square should fold, a letter Dell had written from Cleveland in October of 1979 — she knew it was hers without unfolding it, knew the cheap blue paper she'd bought at the Rexall on her lunch hour — a letter she did not remember writing and could not, kneeling there, bear to read, because the paper had been folded and unfolded so many times that the creases were going to dust, the way only a thing that has been read a hundred times goes to dust, and Dell had written her mother maybe six letters in her whole life and called her every Sunday at four o'clock for thirty years and had believed, had genuinely believed, that the calls were the better gift.

"Ma'am? Is everything okay with the drawer?"

"The drawer's fine," Dell said. "The drawer's empty," and she stood up with the tin in both hands like something that might spill, and she walked through her mother's house and out the front door past the card table and put the tin on the front seat of her car and shut the car door on it, and stood in the driveway a moment with her hand flat on the warm roof of the car, in the coin-light coming down through the oaks, and a man she had never seen before walked by her carrying her mother's sewing machine.

The Singer. Treadle base, iron lace, sold, twenty dollars, orange sticker, firm. Her mother's right foot rocking on that treadle through every winter evening of Dell's girlhood, the needle's small machine-gun stutter, hems and Easter dresses and choir robes for half of Mount Calvary, and turned collars, always turned collars, because a collar wears out on top first and a woman who knew what she was doing got a shirt's second life out of it with an hour's work, and Odessa Pruitt always knew what she was doing. The man carrying it now had it tipped against his chest with the iron base swinging, and Dell wanted to tell him to mind the base, the base was the soul of it, but she went back to her card table instead and made change for a box of mason jars.

Ernestine Polk came at three, which Dell had known she would, because Ernestine Polk had been coming at three o'clock for fifty years, to this house, to every house, arriving in the dead middle of the afternoon when the coffee was fresh and the gossip had ripened. She was eighty-some now and came up the walk on a cane, in a coat too heavy for the day, and she did not look at the furniture going by her on the path; she looked at Dell.

"I'm not buying nothing," she announced. "I come to sit."

So Dell brought a kitchen chair out — sold, two dollars, but not collected yet — and Ernestine Polk sat down beside the card table like a second cashier, and together they watched a stranger carry the whatnot shelf to a van.

"Your mother fed this whole street in fifty-eight," Ernestine said, conversational, watching the shelf go. "The strike winter. You'd've been too little. The men out four months and the company waiting on us to get hungry, and your mama had that big yellow bowl going every single day. Biscuits. Beans and biscuits, beans and biscuits, till spring. Wouldn't take a dime. Wouldn't take a thank-you, hardly. You'd come to the back door and she'd hand it out like you was doing her the favor, taking it off her hands." Ernestine's eyes followed a young man hauling out the bed slats. "Folks remember a hard word forty years," she said. "But they remember biscuits longer. Half the people at her funeral, you didn't know who they were, did you. That's who they were. Biscuit people."

"She never told me that," Dell said.

"She never told you lots of things. That's what mothers are. A house with one room locked." Ernestine put both hands on the head of her cane. "You sell the bowl yet?"

Dell had not sold the bowl. The bowl was in the kitchen, the big yellow ware mixing bowl with the chip on the rim like a missing tooth, and it had a green sticker on it that said $3, and Dell rose from the card table without saying anything and went into the kitchen, past the strangers, and peeled the sticker off the bowl with her thumbnail and carried the bowl out and put it on the front seat of her car next to the Lucky Strike tin. When she came back, Ernestine Polk had sold a box of doilies to a woman from Avon for four dollars and was holding the money out flat on her palm, satisfied, like a child who has done a chore.

The afternoon went down slow. The dealers came back through at four for the second pass, the vulture pass, offering half for what hadn't moved, and Dell let some of it go at half because the alternative was the dumpster, and there are things you can put in a dumpster and things you can't, and the line between them does not run where you'd think. The chrome canisters, FLOUR SUGAR COFFEE TEA, half price, gone. The cedar chest, gone, and its smell going with it, all those Christmases of folded wool. Her father's tools from the back porch — Pruitt twenty-six years at the stove works until the stove works closed and then nine years of whatever a proud man could find, dead at sixty-one with his lungs full of the foundry — his tools went to a retired schoolteacher who said he was learning woodworking, and who held the old block plane in his two hands and looked at the sheen the wood had taken from decades of one man's grip, and said, quietly, somebody used these right, and Dell charged him less than the sticker because he was the only person all day who had said the true thing.

The bed she would not sell. People asked — it was a good bed, a high oak headboard with carved wheat sheaves, and a man from Sandusky offered her ninety dollars and then a hundred and ten and got a look on his face when she kept saying no, a look that said he had finally found the bottom of her, the place where the sale stopped — but the bed was the bed her mother and father had bought used in 1946 and slept in for forty years and her mother had slept in alone for thirty more, on her own side, never spreading out, keeping Pruitt's side smooth as a promise, and Dell had turned her mother in that bed last winter, the bad winter, the winter of falls, had turned her mother's body which had gone light as the lock of hair in the tin, and her mother half asleep had said Pruitt, quit, in a girl's voice, a courting voice, a voice from before Dell was anything at all, and Dell was not going to take a hundred and ten dollars for that. The bed was going to Columbus in a U-Haul whether Dell had a room for it or not. Some things you keep not because you want them but because you are the only place left where they can be true.

By five the house had gone hollow enough to echo. Footsteps sounded different in it. The light came in low and level through windows with no curtains — the curtains had sold, three dollars a pair, to a woman who held them up to the light and checked the hems, and Dell had watched her check the hems and thought, those hems will hold, lady, you don't know whose hems those are — and the level light lay across floorboards that hadn't been bare since Truman, and showed the ghosts on the wallpaper: pale rectangles where the pictures had hung, a pale oval where the mirror had been, the whole room printed with the shapes of what had stood against time for it. Dell walked through the rooms with the cigar box under her arm. Three hundred and some dollars in it, mostly singles, a few checks she'd probably have trouble with. Seventy years of a house, three hundred and some dollars. The arithmetic was an insult and the arithmetic was beside the point and both of those things were true at once, the way her mother could love her and lock rooms against her at once, the way Dell could grieve this house and have spent thirty-eight years making sure she lived two hours from it.

In the kitchen doorway she stopped. The door jamb. She had walked past it forty times that day, taking money, and only now, in the empty house, did the jamb step forward and present itself: the pencil marks. WILBUR 1953. WILBUR 1955, '56, '58. The marks climbing the wood like a vine, and then a long gap, and then lower down, starting over, DELL 1965, DELL 1967, in her mother's uphill hand, the dates and the heights, two children measured against the same wood, one line stopping forever at a height a boy reached in the spring of 1967 and Dell's line going on past it, going above it, which she remembered now — she was eleven, and her mother marked the jamb and stepped back and looked at the new mark sitting higher than Wilbur's last mark, and her mother's face did something Dell had no name for at eleven and had a name for now, and her mother said, flat, you're done growing in this kitchen, and never measured her again.

You could not sell a door jamb. You could not pry it loose and put it in a car. Whoever bought this house — and somebody would, the realtor said, the street was coming back, the realtor said, young couples were buying on Delmar again, painting the porches gray-blue, lightening everything up — whoever bought it would paint the jamb the first week, two coats, and Wilbur Pruitt's whole recorded climb toward a river in Asia would go under the roller without anybody knowing it was there to go.

Dell took out her phone and photographed the jamb, every mark, close up, and knew while she did it that a photograph of a measurement is not a measurement, that the picture would live in the phone the way ashes live in an urn, and she did it anyway, and then she stood in the kitchen while the light went from gold to rose to ash, and she let herself do the thing she had not done since March, which was nothing. She just stood in it. The house ticked and settled around her, talking to itself the way old houses do at dusk, and the smell was still there under everything, fainter now with the doors standing open all day, lavender and starch and snuff, a smell that had no furniture left to live in and would be gone entirely by the first hard frost, and Dell stood in her mother's empty kitchen and said, out loud, to the room, "All right." Not a question. Not an apology. The word you say to a long day. The word her mother said at the end of every task, snapping the last sheet onto the line, sliding the last pan into the oven, closing the lid of the Singer: all right. Meaning finished. Meaning bear it. Meaning both.

She locked the back door and the front door and left the key under the mat for the realtor the way she'd been told, which struck her as a foolish way to guard an empty house until she remembered there was nothing in it now to take. On the front seat of the car the yellow bowl rode beside her, with the Lucky Strike tin sitting down inside it like an egg in a nest, and at the corner of Delmar and Twelfth the streetlight came on above her, and in the mirror the house stood back in the blue dark with its windows bare and its porch swept and its glider gone, looking the way a face looks when the story is over and the teller has closed her mouth: complete, and emptied, and belonging now to whoever came along next needing one.

Beans and biscuits, Dell thought, turning toward the highway. Beans and biscuits till spring. And nobody told me. And I never asked. The bowl shifted against the seat belt when she took the turn, and she put her right hand out flat on the cool curve of it, the way you'd steady a child, and kept it there a long time, well past the edge of town, well past needing to.
