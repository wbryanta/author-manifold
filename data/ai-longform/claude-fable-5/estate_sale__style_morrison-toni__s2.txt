They came for the piano first. Two men in matching gray shirts with a dolly and woven straps, and they tipped it off its casters the way you'd tip a coffin, and Neva stood on the porch with her cigar box of change and watched her mother's piano go down the steps sideways, sounding its low strings against a man's shoulder, a soft furred groan, like something talking in its sleep. Seventy-five dollars. The woman who bought it had wanted it for sixty and had said so twice, standing in the front room with her purse held against her chest as though the house might reach for it, and Neva had looked at her until the woman found the other fifteen folded in a side pocket she'd known about all along. Now the woman waited by her station wagon while the men breathed and figured the angles, and Neva thought of how it took her mother two years of other people's ironing to buy that piano. Two years of Niagara starch and pressing cloths and other women's tablecloths, two years of white shirts so stiff they could have walked to church by themselves, and at the end of it a Story & Clark upright, walnut, delivered in October of 1957 by men who would carry it as far as the bottom porch step and not one inch farther, so that it sat out there in the cold like a guest nobody would claim until Deacon Otis Greer and three men from Mount Calvary came after their shift and walked it through the door on their backs and her mother fed them ham and yeast rolls and played "Pass Me Not, O Gentle Savior" while they ate, the first song the house ever heard from her hands.

Her mother played hymns. Only hymns, or so the town believed, so the children believed, so Reverend Tate believed when he asked her to take over for Sister Pruitt's failing eyes, and she did, eleven years at that church piano, never a wrong note, never a flourish either, because flourish was a kind of bragging and Odessa Greene did not brag, she endured, which she considered a higher art. But Neva knew, because she had been eight years old once and supposed to be asleep, that on certain nights, late, when the house had given up its heat and the street had given up its noise, her mother played something else. Something slow and rocking that was not in the Baptist hymnal, that had a left hand in it like a man's slow walk, and her mother would play it with the soft pedal down, almost a secret from herself, and once Neva had crept to the doorway and seen her mother's face in the lamp light and gone straight back to bed, because the face was not the face that fixed her plate or checked her hems. It was a face from before. From whatever her mother had been before she was anybody's mother, before Ohio, before the foundry took Hosea Greene and the church took the rest. Neva never asked. You did not ask Odessa about before. Before was a country she had emigrated from and burned the boats.

The sign had been in the yard since Thursday. ESTATE SALE SAT 9-4, EVERYTHING MUST GO, in her son's block printing, because Marcus had driven up from Atlanta for the funeral in March and not since, and his contribution to the dismantling of his grandmother's life was a sign and a phone call every Sunday in which he said, Mama, just sell it all, don't make a museum out of it, like he was warning her off a ledge. The dealers came at seven, before she'd unlocked anything, two of them, a married pair from Columbus with careful shoes, and they walked the porch and cupped their hands at the windows and the man said, through the glass, We're interested in primitives, anything primitive, and Neva, on the other side of the glass with her coffee, considered the word for a while. Primitive. Her mother's pie safe with the punched-tin doors that Hosea had made the year they married. Her mother's churn that had come up from Georgia on a train in 1943 wrapped in a quilt and held on a lap for fourteen hours because it had been her mother's mother's. Primitive. She opened the door at nine o'clock exactly and not a breath before, and she charged the Columbus man double what she charged anybody else, and he paid it, both of them understanding the arithmetic perfectly and neither saying so.

By noon the house had been walked through by half the county, or it felt that way. People she knew and people she didn't and people she half knew, which was worse — women who'd been girls when she was a girl, gone thick and gray like her, picking up her mother's egg beater, her mother's percolator, turning them over to look for prices on freezer tape. Strangers opened the drawers of the chifforobe in the front bedroom and put their hands in. That was the thing she had not been ready for, the hands. The hands of strangers going into drawers her own hands had been slapped away from for the first eighteen years of her life. The chifforobe held the good things. The good things were not used; that was what made them good. The tablecloth with the cutwork that came out twice — for Reverend Tate's anniversary dinner and for the repast after Wendell's funeral — and went back in folded around tissue paper like a relic. The gloves. The good gloves, white kid, that her mother wore to Cleveland exactly once, in 1964, to hear a man sing at a church so big it had a balcony, and came home and said only, It was nice, and put the gloves away for thirty-nine more years. A woman in a red windbreaker held those gloves up to the light now and said to her friend, These are real kid, feel, and the friend felt, and Neva took two dollars for them and did not watch them go out the door, because some things you let go of by not looking.

Ernestine Pope came around one o'clock, not to buy. She came up the walk slow on her bad hip with a pound cake in foil, because Ernestine Pope had been bringing pound cake to this house for fifty years and saw no reason a sale should interrupt it, and she sat on the porch glider, which was tagged at fifteen dollars and would sell out from under her before three, and she watched the people come and go like she was reviewing troops. Lord, look at them, she said. Picking her clean. Dessa would have a fit. And Neva said, Mama would have charged admission, and Ernestine laughed her wheezing laugh and then didn't laugh. Your mama, she said, and stopped, and started again the way old women do, taking a run at it. Your mama was the stubbornest woman God ever wasted patience on. I knew her sixty years and she never once told me a thing she didn't decide to tell me a week in advance. But I'll say this. When Pope passed and I couldn't get out the bed for grieving, she came every morning at six and made the coffee and didn't say one word to me. Not one. Six weeks of mornings. You know what that is, to sit with somebody six weeks and not pester them with talk? That's love with its workclothes on. That's what your mama had. She didn't have the Sunday kind. Ernestine ate her own pound cake and Neva took fifteen dollars for the glider from a young couple who carried it off still warm, and Ernestine stood in the yard after that like a woman at a graveside, then went home.

The quilts she had laid out on Wendell's bed, six of them, and the Columbus woman came back through at two for those, fingering the indigo one, the wedding-ring one, lifting the corner to check the stitching with a little jeweler's loupe she took from her pocket, actually a loupe, screwed into her eye like the quilt was a diamond she suspected of being glass. These are wonderful, the woman said, in the voice people used for things made by hands they imagined as simple. I'll give you forty for all six. Neva looked at the quilts. Her mother's stitches, eight to the inch, even as type, done winters by the kitchen stove with her feet on a brick, and in the indigo one were pieces Neva could read like print: the gray flannel was Hosea's work shirt, the one they gave back to her folded; the sprigged yellow was Neva's own Easter dress, 1956, that she'd torn on the Crawford fence and cried over; the deep blue was the dress her mother wore to Wendell's funeral and never wore again, cut up that same winter, put under the needle, made to lie quiet in a pattern. Forty dollars. Neva said, They're a hundred apiece, and watched the woman's face do its arithmetic of insult and desire. The woman took two, the wedding ring and a nine-patch, and counted out the bills like each one had to be talked into leaving, and when she reached for the indigo Neva said, That one's not for sale, in her mother's exact voice, flat as an iron, and lifted it off the bed and folded it over her own arm and stood holding it, and the woman said, Well, and left, and Neva stood in her dead brother's room with the quilt against her chest, breathing.

Wendell's room. Her mother had kept it for him for thirty-four years. Not a shrine — Odessa didn't believe in shrines, shrines were Catholic and excessive — just kept. Dusted. The bed made tight enough to bounce a dime, because he'd learned that at Fort Benning and shown them, laughing, bouncing a dime off his own bed while their mother stood in the doorway with her arms crossed pretending not to be pleased. The footlocker came back in March of 1969 and his body came back in April and his room stayed his. His ball glove was in the closet, eleven years old when he oiled it last, fifty-one years old now, and a man bought it this afternoon for three dollars for his grandson, and Neva let it go because Wendell would have. Wendell gave everything away; that was his whole nature; he'd give you his sandwich and tell you he wasn't hungry and his stomach would growl in church and he'd grin. Their mother never spoke of him after the funeral. That was her way, and the town read it as coldness, and the town was a fool. Neva had been home from Columbus one weekend in the eighties and gotten up in the night for water and found her mother sitting in Wendell's room in the dark, in the chair, not crying, not praying, just sitting, her hands open in her lap like she'd set something down. Neva had gone back to bed without water. The flag, folded into its triangle, had been in the bottom drawer of the chifforobe all these years under the tablecloth, and Neva had taken it out this morning before the sale opened and put it in her car, on the front seat, like a passenger.

The kitchen table she'd priced at twenty and nobody wanted it because of the burn. A long brown scald in the enamel top, shaped like a fish or a state, and Neva could have told any of them what it was — it was July 12, 1955, it was the foreman from the foundry standing on the porch with his hat off, it was her mother hearing about Hosea with a flatiron in her hand and setting the iron down on the table without a stand, without a thought, and standing there while the man talked, and the iron sat and burned its shape in, and the smell of scorched enamel filled the kitchen and nobody moved to lift it, and that smell was the smell of her father's death forever after, so that Neva at fifty-five still could not pass a dry cleaner's vent without her chest pulling tight. Her mother was thirty-one that day, with two children and a mortgage on a house in a town where the bank had needed talking into giving Hosea the loan at all, and she did not remarry, and she did not break, and she did not explain. She took in laundry and then she did days work and then she did alterations, and her hands at the end were a record of all of it, the knuckles swollen smooth and shining like river stones, and in the hospital in February when those hands couldn't do anything anymore they still moved on the blanket, hemming. Even sleeping, hemming. Neva had sat by the bed and watched her mother's hands finish work that wasn't there and understood she was watching the truest prayer the woman had ever prayed.

When the men came for the bed — the iron bed, the bed Neva was born in, the bed her mother died wanting to get back to and didn't — Neva went out the back. She stood in the yard by the clothesline poles, which were not for sale because nobody buys clothesline poles, two iron T's that Hosea had set in concrete in 1949 and that would outlast everyone who had ever hung anything on them. The lines were long gone but Neva could see them. Could see sheets. She had been small in a country of sheets, white walls of them lifting and bellying, snapping, the cold wet weight of them against her face when she ran the rows, and the smell — sun and bluing and the faint scorched sweetness that meant her mother — and her mother moving down the line with clothespins in her mouth like a woman holding her own words prisoner, and somewhere a radio from the Crawfords' window, and the whole bright laundered world swaying. That was happiness. Nobody had told her at the time, and that was the cruelty of it; happiness should announce itself, ring a bell, so you'd know to stop and put your hand flat against it. Behind her, through the screen door, she heard the bed frame knock the hallway wall, heard a man say easy, easy, and she stood looking at the clothesline poles until the truck doors slammed out front, and then a while longer.

It was after that, going back through the front bedroom to straighten what the hands had scattered, that she found the photograph. The chifforobe was sold but not yet collected, its drawers hanging open like questions, and the bottom drawer had a lining of newspaper gone brown — the Lorain Journal, June of 1951, she would read later — and the lining had shifted, and under it, flat against the wood, face down, was a photograph the size of a playing card. Scalloped edges. She turned it over. A girl was laughing in front of a porch that was not this porch, a Georgia porch, a girl maybe seventeen in a flowered dress with her head thrown back, actually back, mouth wide, one hand on the shoulder of a tall boy in suspenders who was looking not at the camera but at her, at the laughing girl, with his whole face. On the back in pencil, in a round young hand Neva had never seen her mother use: Me & Tipp, Macon, 1939. Neva sat down on the floor of the bedroom with people moving through her mother's house buying her mother's spoons, and held the picture, and looked at the girl. She had never in fifty-five years seen her mother laugh like that. Had never heard of any Tipp. The girl in the picture did not know yet about Ohio, about foundries, about folded flags or flatirons; she was just standing in some Macon yard in 1939 being delighted, being looked at by a boy as if she were the answer to a question he'd been asking all his life, and she had hidden herself in the bottom of a drawer under the good tablecloth for sixty-some years, and kept herself, the way she kept everything good, by not using it. Neva put the photograph in her shirt pocket, against her chest, and went back out to make change.

The afternoon wore down. The sun came around to the front of the house the way it did, and lit the empty front room a color Neva had no name for, butter going brown, and what was left was the leavings: the dented pots, the jelly glasses, the hats. The hats had not sold. Eleven of them in their boxes, church hats, navy and cream and one wine-colored one with a net veil that her mother had worn the day they made her head of the Deaconess Board, and the white women had looked at them like artifacts and the young women had looked at them like jokes, and at half past three Neva carried the boxes out to the porch and waited, and sure enough here came the church women on their late pass, Velma Greer and the Tate sisters and old Miss Charlene Biggs who was ninety-four and had sung in the choir with Odessa since Roosevelt, and Neva said, Y'all take these. They argued, because you have to, and she insisted, because you have to, and then each woman chose a hat with great seriousness, trying them at the porch window glass, and Miss Charlene took the wine-colored one and set it on her white head and turned to be admired, and for a minute the porch was full of Odessa's hats on living heads, nodding, and Neva had to go inside and run water in the kitchen sink for no reason. A little girl came through last with her father, maybe six, and stood worshipping the bisque shepherdess on the mantel, the one chipped figurine that had survived two children and five decades, and Neva watched her wanting it with her hands behind her back, the way children are taught to want, and gave it to her, and the girl carried it out the door in both palms like an egg, like fire, like something that might still hatch.

At four she counted the money at the burned table. Six hundred and eleven dollars and fifty cents, in a bank envelope and the cigar box. She sat with it. Fifty-four years in this house, the piano and the pie safe and the bed she was born in, the gloves and the glider and Hosea's chair, all of it converted now, made portable, six hundred eleven and fifty, and the number was not an insult, exactly, because her mother would have approved of every dime of it — Odessa had no sentiment about objects, only discipline — but it was a number you could put in your purse, and a life should not fit in a purse. Through the window the yard had gone gold and the sign tilted in it. Marcus would call Sunday and ask did everything go and she would say yes, everything went, and that would be true the way a tombstone is true.

She walked the house once, empty. Her shoes were loud in it. The rooms had given up their furniture and kept their shapes, pale squares on the walls where pictures had hung, the floor unfaded in a piano-sized patch by the front window like a shadow that had decided to stay. The house smelled like itself still, under everything: camphor and coffee and Fels-Naptha and time. In the kitchen the table stood alone, unsold, hers now by default, and she put her hand flat on the burn the way she had wanted to put her hand on the sheets, on the swaying bright morning, and held it there. In her shirt pocket the girl from Macon laughed against her heart. She had the button tin in the car, and the scissors, the good shears nobody was ever allowed to cut paper with, and the indigo quilt, and the flag, and the girl, and that was the estate; that was what was left of Odessa Greene after the strangers' hands; and standing in the kitchen Neva understood that it was, after all, the right inventory, that her mother had spent fifty years sorting herself into exactly these few keepable things and the rest was just furniture, was just what a woman stands among while she does the real work, which is invisible, which is hemming in her sleep.

She locked the front door at last and stood on the porch in the long light. Down Dunbar Street the houses sat the way they always had, fewer of them lived in now, the foundry quiet these thirty years, the maples enormous. Somebody was grilling somewhere. A boy went by on a bicycle too big for him, standing on the pedals, working. Neva bent and lifted the corner of the mat and put the key under it, the way her mother had done every day of her life in this house, though there was no one coming, though the buyers would get their own keys from the lawyer, though it was foolish — because the key under the mat was never about the key. It was about somebody. It said somebody might come, and if they came, let them in. She settled the mat over it and went down the steps her father had poured, past the sign her son had lettered, and did not look back at the house, because some things you let go of by not looking, and she drove out of town with the windows down, the flag on the seat beside her, and the girl in her pocket laughing, laughing, all the way to the county line.
