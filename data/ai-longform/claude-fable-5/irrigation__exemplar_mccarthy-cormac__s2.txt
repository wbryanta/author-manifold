They came down through the orchard in the first gray light with their shovels over their shoulders like soldiers of some meager and defeated army and the old dog came behind them through the wet grass with her head down. The trees stood in their long rows in the dark and dripping stillness and the trees were old and many of them were dead and they passed dead trees that stood barkless and silver among the living like things placed there as a warning, no leaf upon them nor had been these several years. To the east beyond the river breaks the sky had begun to pale and a dove was calling somewhere down along the ditch in the cottonwoods and there was no other sound at all save their boots in the grass and the dog's breathing.

They found the leak where Dale said it would be, midway down the fourth row, where the ground had gone soft and black and the water stood seeping up through the orchard grass in a slow welt as if the earth itself had been wounded and were bleeding there in the dark. Curtis stood looking down at it. He pushed his hat back and looked down the row at the trees and at the mountains to the west still holding the night in their folds.

How long you reckon it's been runnin.

A while. The grass is growed up in it.

Curtis spat and prodded at the wet earth with the blade of his shovel. It's the line, he said. It aint just a joint weepin.

I know it.

You knowed it yesterday.

I figured it. I wanted you to see it for yourself.

So I'd know what we was up against.

So you'd know.

They began to dig. The light came up slowly out of the east the way it does in that country, gray and then rose and then a long reef of gold fired along the rim of the tablelands, and the orchard took shape around them row by row out of the dark as if it were being made anew each morning out of nothing and given to them again to do with what they would. The earth came up heavy on the shovel blades, black and sour with the leaked water, and they slung it to one side and bent and dug again. The dog lay in the grass with her muzzle on her paws and watched them.

By the time the sun cleared the breaks they had the pipe uncovered for six feet and the hole was knee deep and filling with water faster than they could throw it out. The pipe was concrete pipe that their grandfather had laid in the ground in nineteen and forty-six, gray and pocked like old bone, and where the root of a cottonwood had got under it and lifted it the pipe had cracked the whole circumference and the crack ran lengthwise besides for the better part of a foot.

Curtis stood in the hole with the water rising over his bootlaces and looked at it.

That's a root done that.

It is.

That tree aint even ours. That's Allison's tree.

The root dont know that.

Curtis bent and ran his thumb along the crack underwater the way a man might examine the wound of a horse. He shook his head. We need to shut the water off out of the ditch fore we can do anything with this.

It's off. I shut the headgate last night.

Then where's all this water comin from.

It's what's standin in the line. It's a quarter mile of pipe between here and the gate. It's got to weep out somewheres and this is where it's at.

Curtis straightened up and looked at his brother. Well, he said. Then I reckon we bail.

I reckon we do.

They bailed with a coffee can and with a cutoff bleach jug that Dale had brought down in the toolsack and the sun rose up the sky and the morning grew warm and then hot and the cicadas started up in the trees down along the ditch, first one and then a thousand, that dry electric shimmer of sound that in that country is the very voice of the heat itself. They took off their shirts and hung them on the limbs of an apple tree and the limbs were so old and so bowed that the shirts hung nearly to the ground. The water in the hole sank and welled and sank again and finally lay in a thin gruel of mud at the bottom and they stood looking at the broken pipe lying in its bed like something exhumed.

Daddy's got a joint of that pipe in the barn, Dale said. Up in the loft.

How do you know what Daddy's got in the barn.

Cause I looked. Last week. Fore I ever called you.

Curtis climbed up out of the hole and sat on the grass at the edge of it with his forearms on his knees and his hands hanging. He was the older by six years and the gray had come into the hair at his temples and there were white scars on his hands and his hands looked older than the rest of him. He looked down the row of trees. A kingbird sat the top wire of the fence at the end of the row and flew and came back again.

You know what Stearns offered me, he said.

I know what he offered.

Two hundred and forty thousand dollars. For ground that aint cleared expenses in six years.

It cleared expenses in oh nine.

Oh nine. Curtis said it the way a man might name some distant and fabulous country he'd heard tell of but never seen. He picked a grass stem and put it in his teeth. You know what he wants it for. He dont want apples. He wants the water. He'll let these trees stand here and die and pump the water over the hill to them houses he's buildin and in ten years there wont be nothin here but dead wood and tumbleweeds and there wont nobody care, neither. Includin you, cause you'll be somewheres else with your half of the money.

Dale climbed out of the hole and walked over to the tree and got his shirt and wiped his face with it and put it over his shoulder. He stood looking west to where Sierra Blanca floated pale and blue over the haze of the valley with the last of the spring snow lying in the high cirques like something the mountain could not bring itself to let go of.

I aint goin somewheres else, he said.

That's the hell of it, said Curtis. I know you aint.

They walked up to the barn through the heat of the midmorning. The barn was their grandfather's barn and it leaned slightly into the prevailing wind as if it were listening for something out of the southwest and the boards of it had gone that deep ash gray that wood goes in that country and the heads of the nails stood out from the boards where the wood had shrunk back from them over the years like the world drawing back from its own articulations. Inside it was dim and hot and smelled of dust and old hay and ancient distillations of horses long dead. Their father's tools hung on the wall on nails their father had driven, the wrenches in their rows by size, the shadow of each tool drawn around it on the boards in paint so that a missing tool would announce itself by its silhouette, and there were three or four such empty shapes on the wall like the outlines the police draw around the dead, and no one now alive knew what had become of those tools or when.

Dale climbed the ladder into the loft and shifted bales and dragged the joint of pipe to the edge and lowered it down to his brother and Curtis took the weight of it and set it in the floor dust. It was four feet of the same gray concrete pipe and it had lain in that loft for fifty years against just such a day as this one. Curtis stood looking down at it.

The old man kept everthing, he said.

He did.

You know why?

Cause he never had nothin and come up never havin nothin.

That's part of it. Curtis nudged the pipe with his boot toe. He kept everthing cause he intended for this place to go on. A man dont save a joint of pipe for fifty years for himself. He saves it for whoever's standin here when it's needed. He was lookin at me and you fore we was ever born. That's a kind of faith, I reckon. Like buryin money in a ground you caint come back to.

Then how can you sell it.

Curtis didnt answer. He bent and got his end of the pipe and Dale got the other and they carried it out the big door into the white blast of the day. They got the sack of mortar mix from the tackroom and a mason's trowel and a hammer and a cold chisel and they loaded all of it into the wheelbarrow with the pipe balanced crossways and Dale pushed the wheelbarrow down the lane between the orchard rows with the iron wheel of it printing a thin and wandering line in the dust behind him like the track of some lone and narrow creature bound out across that country on business of its own.

They broke out the cracked section with the hammer and chisel, kneeling in the hole in the heat, taking turns, the chips of old concrete flying and the dull iron clink of the chisel keeping a slow time like a bell rung for a parish of two. The cottonwood root lay athwart the pipe bed thick as a man's arm and white where the chisel had scarred it and Dale cut it out with the axe in eight or ten strokes and threw the cut length of it up out of the hole and the old dog rose and went and smelled of it and lay down again.

Tree'll miss that, Curtis said.

Tree's got more.

They everone got more. That's the trouble with this country. Everthing in it is fixin to outlast you and it knows it.

Noon stood over the orchard. The shade of the trees drew in under them small and round like dark pools going dry. They fitted the new joint into the run and Dale mixed mortar in the wheelbarrow with water dipped from the ditch and Curtis packed and banded the collars at either end with the trowel, working the mortar in with his fingers, smoothing it, turning his head sideways to sight along the line. His hands were sure with it. Their father had been a fair mason and a fair welder and a fair veterinarian and a fair mechanic, the way a man must be who farms poor ground far from town, and what the sons knew of these trades they had from him, standing at his elbow in the cold or in the heat, handing up tools, learning by watching as it was learned in the world for ten thousand years before there was a word for learning at all.

They washed the trowel in the ditch and sat in the shade of the biggest of the trees and ate the lunch Dale had brought, cold fried chicken and lightbread and a thermos of tea, and they passed the thermos lid back and forth between them and the cicadas ran their long seams of sound up and down the heat and quit and started again.

I went and seen Mama Tuesday, Curtis said.

How was she.

She knowed me. Took a while. She kept callin me Royce.

Royce.

Her brother. Dead since before you was born.

Dale ate and looked out down the rows. The heat stood and shimmered between the trees and a hawk turned high up in the white sky, riding the updraft off the breaks, and its small shadow trolled down the orchard rows and was gone.

That home costs four thousand dollars a month, Curtis said. You know what I get for cattle. You know what you get for apples. I aint askin you. I'm tellin you what I lay awake with.

I know it.

You dont know it. You know it like a man knows a thing he's heard. I know it like a man knows a dog's settin on his chest.

Dale screwed the lid back on the thermos and set it in the grass. We could lease the water, he said. Keep the ground.

Stearns dont lease. He told me. He buys or he goes elsewheres.

Then let him go elsewheres.

There aint no elsewheres. We're the elsewheres. He's done bought Allison's and he's done bought the Pruitt place. We're settin here like a tooth in a jawbone where all the other teeth is pulled.

Dale didnt say anything. The old dog had come and laid herself down against his leg and he put his hand on her head and the dog sighed.

Let me ask you somethin, Curtis said.

Ask it.

When you walk this orchard. Of a evenin. What is it you see?

I see the orchard.

No you dont. You see Daddy out here on that ladder thinnin fruit. You see Granddaddy's name on the headgate where he wrote it in the wet concrete. You see your own self a kid huntin doves down that ditch. That's what you see. Curtis turned and looked at him and his eyes were pale in his burnt face. The thing you're wantin to keep aint a thing money can take nor keep neither one. You could sell this ground tomorrow and walk it in your head till the day you die and not wear it out. And you could keep it and watch it die tree by tree till what's standin here is just the bones of it and that's a thing you caint unsee, and them bones'll eat the rememberin too, till you caint even call back what it looked like when it was a live thing and worth the lovin. I seen it happen to better places than this one. The land dont owe you nothin, little brother. It dont even know you're here.

Dale sat with his hand on the dog. Somewhere up the valley a truck passed on the highway and the sound of it came and went.

Then who'll know? he said.

What?

If it dont know we're here and the world dont know and everbody that remembers is dead or in Roswell callin strangers by dead men's names. Who'll know this place was ever loved? Somethin that aint witnessed, it's like it never happened at all. Somebody's got to stand for a thing. Stand in front of it. Even if it dont know. Specially if it dont know.

Curtis looked at him for a long time. Then he looked away down the rows.

Daddy used to say a near identical thing, he said.

I know it. Where do you think I got it.

In the slack of the afternoon they walked the line out, all the quarter mile of it, watching the ground for other wet places and finding none, and they walked the orchard too while the mortar set, and Curtis stopped at trees and broke twigs and looked at the cambium and counted on the trees what fruit had set after the April freeze and it was little enough. They stood at the end of the north rows where the wire fence ran and beyond the fence on what had been Allison's ground the surveyors' stakes stood in their pale rows with their plastic flaggings limp in the heat, lath stakes such as a man might use to grow a garden up, save that what was staked to grow there was streets.

I remember when Allison's daddy planted them trees yonder, Curtis said. There aint a tree standin in it now. Took em out with a D7 in a day and a half and burned em in piles. You could see the smoke from the porch. He stood with his hands in his back pockets looking at the stakes. I dont say it's right, he said. I never said it was right. I said it's comin. Right's got nothin to do with what comes. The world dont keep them accounts. It just rolls. You can stand in front of it if you want. I knowed men that did. I can take you to where they're buried.

You aim to sign with him, then.

I aint signed nothin.

That aint what I asked you.

Curtis took off his hat and wiped the sweatband with his thumb and put it back on. I aim to do whatever me and you agree to do, he said. Half of this is yours. I cant sell your half and I wouldnt. But I'll tell you what I told Stearns. I told him I had a brother, and he asked me was you reasonable, and I told him no, my brother is twenty-eight years old and he has got a old dog and a older tractor and not one reasonable bone in him, and he has set in a apple tree at six years old and told me he'd live here all his life, and what worries me aint that he was wrong. It's that he might of been right.

Dale smiled in spite of himself and looked at the ground.

What did Stearns say to that.

He said the offer stood till September.

Then we got till September.

We got till September.

In the last of the day they went up to the headgate where their grandfather's name was written in the concrete with the date below it, the letters drawn awkward and slanting with a stick by a man more used to a plow than a pen, and Dale set the wheel and turned the water in slow, a quarter turn and wait, another quarter, the way their father had taught them, so the air could find its way out of the line and not hammer it, and they walked down through the orchard alongside the buried pipe as though escorting something, listening to the water moving in the ground beneath their feet with a sound like distant talk. At the patched joint they stood and watched and the mortar held and the ground stayed dry and Curtis nodded.

That'll do, he said.

It will if it dont freeze fore it cures.

It aint goin to freeze in June.

It froze in April.

It did do that, didnt it.

They opened the risers and the water came welling up out of the gated pipe and moved out into the rows, slow and silver in the dying light, feeling its way down the furrows among the grass and over the dry ground with a faint hissing like something whispering its way back into a country it had been long banished from, and the smell of it on the dust rose up around them, that smell that is older in the nostrils of men than any other thing they know. The sun was down behind Sierra Blanca and the western sky stood in tiers of fire and ash and against it the dead trees and the living stood all one in their black silhouettes so you could not tell from looking which was which, as if in that light at least there was no judgment rendered, or none yet. Swallows were working low over the wet rows after the insects the water raised and the old dog walked out into a furrow and stood in the running water and drank.

Curtis loaded the tools in the wheelbarrow. He stood a minute looking down the rows at the water going.

You want to come up to the house and eat? Dale said. I got beans on from yesterday.

I better get back. I got heifers to look at.

You could look at em in the mornin.

I could. Curtis lifted the handles of the barrow and set it down again. I'll tell you what I'll do, he said. I'll come Saturday and we'll prune out that dead wood in the south rows. It oughtnt to stand there. Dead or no dead, sale or no sale. A thing ought to be kept right while it's yours to keep.

All right.

You got a extra saw?

Dale looked at him. Just in case some fool showed up wantin to prune, he said. Yeah. I got one.

Curtis almost smiled. He pushed the wheelbarrow off up the lane in the dusk and the iron wheel went tick tick tick over the stones and Dale stood at the head of the rows and watched the water. It moved away from him down the long darkening aisles of trees, finding the low ground, finding the roots, going where water goes, which is wherever it has been before. The first star stood over the breaks to the east. He could hear his brother's truck start up by the barn and he heard it pull out onto the county road and take the grade and fade and there was just the water then, and the crickets starting, and the dark coming down out of the trees where it had waited out the day. He whistled up the dog and she came out of the furrow shaking the wet from herself and they went up the row toward the house together, the man and the dog, and behind them in the dark the water ran on among the trees, asking nothing, knowing nothing, neither whose it was nor what it served, as it had run there for a hundred years and would run for as long as there was any to send it, and not one minute more.
