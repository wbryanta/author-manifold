They came down through the orchard in the first gray light with the shovels over their shoulders and the dog trotting before them through the wet grass and neither of them spoke. The trees stood in their rows like a congregation of the patient dead and the fruit hung small and green among the leaves and in the low places mist lay along the ground the way smoke will lie in a windless morning. The older brother walked in front. He was forty-one years old and he walked with a slight hitch from a horse that had fallen with him eleven years prior and he carried in his free hand a coil of black poly pipe and a paper sack with the fittings in it that rattled faintly as he went.

You can see where she's been runnin, he said.

The younger brother looked where he was looking. Down the row the ground was darker and the grass greener in a long seam and at the end of it a wallow of mud had formed and a killdeer rose off it crying and flew low and dragging one wing in the old feint and then thought better of the whole performance and rose and was gone.

How long you reckon, the younger one said.

Week maybe. Maybe more. Pressure's been off since before that.

A week of water in the ground and not in the trees.

It's June, the older one said. Water in the ground in June aint the worst thing there is.

It aint the best.

No. It aint the best.

They walked on. The light was coming up out of the east behind the mountains and the mountains stood very black against it and then less black and the first sun came through the rows and laid long bars of shadow across the orchard floor and the mist began to come apart and stand in rags and then it was gone altogether as if it had never been. The dog quartered out ahead of them with her nose down. The orchard had been their father's and his father's before him and it ran to forty acres of apples on the bench above the river, old trees, Romes and Winesaps and Jonathans, varieties that the buyers no longer asked for by name, and a third of the trees were dead or dying of the fireblight and stood leafless among the living with their black branches curled like things burned.

They found the break where the line crossed under the headgate road. The water had hollowed the road from beneath and a piece of the roadbed had slumped into the hollow and lay there cracked like a fallen slab of masonry and beneath it the pipe showed, split along its length, the split fluted open and gray with mineral.

Well, the older brother said. He set down the coil and the sack and stood looking at it.

That's the same joint Daddy fixed, the younger one said.

It aint the same joint. It's two foot from it. He fixed the coupling. This here's the pipe itself.

Same difference.

It aint though.

The older brother stepped down into the wash and squatted and put his hand on the pipe and ran his thumb along the split as if he were gauging the age of a wound. Then he stood and climbed out and took up his shovel.

We'll have to open her up four foot either side, he said. Get under it.

They began to dig. The ground near the break was saturated and came up in slick gray spadefuls that hit the spoil pile with a sound like meat slapped on a board and farther out the ground was dry and hard and rang under the shovels. They worked without talking. The sun cleared the rows and the day's heat came into the orchard the way water comes into a lowland, first in the open places and then everywhere, and the doves called from the dead trees and quit calling and the cicadas took it up. By eight oclock the brothers had their shirts off and their backs shone and the trench stood open the length of a grave and half again and the broken pipe lay exposed along the bottom of it like something exhumed.

The younger brother stood in the trench and leaned on his shovel. He was thirty-four and lighter built than his brother and he had been three years in Albuquerque and two in Phoenix and he had come back in March when their father died and he had not yet said how long he was staying. He looked up out of the trench at the rows running away in their long perspectives, the live trees and the dead.

Marsh called the house again Thursday, he said.

The older brother didnt answer. He was kneeling at the edge of the trench cutting the poly pipe with a hacksaw and the saw made its small busy industrial sound in all that quiet.

You hear me?

I heard you.

He's up to eleven now. Eleven five if we close before September.

The older brother finished the cut and held the cut end up and looked down the bore of it and blew through it and set it aside. Eleven five, he said.

Yes.

For the whole of it.

The whole of it. House and all.

The older brother took the sack of fittings and shook out a coupling into his palm and turned it in his fingers. You know what he wants it for.

I know what he wants it for.

Forty houses. Fifty. Whatever the county'll let him pour.

People got to live somewhere, the younger one said.

They dont got to live here.

They got to live somewhere, Tom.

The older brother put the coupling back in the sack and folded the top of the sack down and set it in the shade of the spoil pile as if it were a thing that could suffer from the sun. Hand me up that saw blade if it fell down in there, he said.

It didnt fall down in here.

All right.

They cut out the split section and the water that had stood in the line came welling up out of the cut ends dark with sediment and pooled in the bottom of the trench around their boots and slowly drained away into the ground that had drunk so much already. The older brother dressed the cut ends with his knife, beveling them, blowing the curled shavings away, and he set the coupling on the upstream end and the younger one braced the pipe against his boot while the older one drove the new section home with the heel of his hand, three blows, four, the pipe lurching its fraction of an inch with each blow, and then the same at the downstream end, and then they stood looking at the joinery in the bottom of the trench.

That'll do or it wont, the older one said.

It'll do.

We'll let her set before we charge it.

They climbed out and sat in the shade of a Winesap that was half dead, the south scaffold black and leafless and the north side green and bearing, and they drank from the water jug and passed it between them and the dog came and lay panting in the grass with her tongue out the side of her mouth. Down through the rows they could see the river bottom and the cottonwoods along the river standing in their deep green and beyond the river the highway and on the far side of the highway the new roofs of the development that had gone in two years ago, rank on rank of them, pale and identical, shimmering already in the heat like something not entirely real.

Eleven five, the older brother said. He said it the way a man will say a word in a foreign language, testing the shape of it.

It's real money, Tom.

I know it's real money.

It's more than this place'll clear in twenty years. In thirty. You know that better than I do. You're the one keeps the books.

I keep the books.

Then you know.

The older brother capped the jug and set it in the grass. He took his hat off and looked inside it and put it back on. Out in the sun the heat stood over the orchard floor in visible waves and a hawk turned in the high blue going north and the cicadas droned on and on like the sound of the heat itself.

You remember when he put this line in, the older one said. The pipe.

I was little.

You were eight. Nineteen and ninety-eight. He bought it on a note from the bank in town when the bank in town was still the bank in town and he laid two thousand foot of it with a rented trencher and me behind him gluing joints till my hands like to come off. He said it'd see him out. He was right about that much.

He was right about a lot of things and wrong about as many.

He was.

He was wrong about Granny Smiths. He was wrong about the cider house. He was wrong about Mama stayin.

The older brother looked at him.

I'm just sayin he wasnt a prophet, the younger one said. Bein dead dont make a man right.

It dont make him wrong neither.

No. It dont.

They sat. The dog rolled onto her side in the grass and sighed.

You want to know what I think, the younger one said.

I expect I'm goin to know it either way.

I think you're keepin a graveyard. I think a third of these trees are dead and another third are dyin and the packers wont take Romes anymore at any price and the well's makin sand and you're out here at your age diggin up pipe your daddy laid so you can put water to trees that the blight's goin to take inside of five years anyhow. That's what I think. I think you're tendin graves, Tom, and callin it farmin.

The older brother was quiet a long time. A breeze came up out of the river bottom and moved through the orchard and turned the leaves pale side out and passed on and the trees were still again.

Might be, he said.

That's all you got to say.

What do you want me to say.

I want you to say somethin. I want you to argue it or I want you to agree to it but I'm damned if I'll sit here while you nod at me like I'm tellin you the weather.

The older brother picked a stem of grass and put it in his teeth and took it out again. You done the figures, he said. I dont doubt your figures. A man can figure anything. You could figure what Daddy was worth by the pound I expect, if you knew the goin rate.

That aint fair.

No, the older one said. It aint. He looked off down the rows. But here's what your figures dont have in em. There's water been comin to this ground a hundred and twelve years. The first of it come down a ditch our great-granddaddy dug with a mule and a slip and you can still walk the line of that ditch up under the bench if you know what you're lookin at. You sell to Marsh and he'll cap the well and tear out the trees with an excavator in about a day and a half, I've seen em do it, they push em into windrows and burn em, and then that's done. Not done for us. Done. There wont be no buyin it back and there wont be no plantin it again because they'll have poured slab over the whole of it, and whatever it was that come down to us, whatever you want to call that thing, it stops with you and me on the day we sign. That's in the figures too. It just dont have a number wrote next to it.

The younger brother shook his head slowly. You talk like the land knows.

Maybe it does.

It dont know, Tom. It's dirt. It dont know we're here and it wont know when we're gone. The water dont care whose ditch it runs in.

Then why'd you come back.

The younger one looked at him.

You heard me. Why'd you come back. He was dead before you got here. You couldnt of come for him.

I come to settle the estate.

You could of settled it from Phoenix. People do.

The younger brother didnt answer. He sat looking out at the heat and after a while he reached and got the jug and drank and put the cap back on and set it down.

We ought to charge that line, he said.

Let it set another few minutes.

You said.

I know what I said.

They let it set. The sun stood near noon and the shade had shrunk in close around the trunks of the trees and a tractor was working somewhere far off, the sound of it coming and going on the breeze. The older brother got up after a while and walked down the row to where the dead trees began and stood among them with his hands in his pockets looking up into the black branches as a man might stand in a ruined church, and the younger one watched him do it and looked away.

They walked up to the wellhouse together. It was a low structure of cinderblock with a tin roof gone brown and inside in the dimness the pump motor sat on its concrete pad among shelves of oil cans and foot valves and lengths of wire and a calendar from a parts house in town dated four years gone, a photograph of mountains that were not these mountains. The older brother threw the switch and the motor came up moaning and found its pitch and held it, and they stood listening to it the way men listen to an old engine, hearing in it things they could not have named, and then they walked back down to the trench with the sound of the pump behind them and the line filling ahead of them, and they could hear the water coming down the pipe before they reached it, a hollow rushing in the earth like something alive let out of a cell.

The joint held. Water beaded at the coupling and stood in droplets and the droplets did not grow, and the older brother knelt and laid two fingers against the pipe and felt the cold come into it and the faint trembling of the water going by inside, going down to the live trees, and he stayed that way a minute, kneeling in the trench with his fingers on the pipe like a man taking a pulse.

She'll do, he said.

They backfilled. It went faster than the digging, the way it always does, the earth falling in on itself with a sound like rain, and they tramped it down in layers and crowned it over the line and threw the slab of broken roadbed up out of the way and by two oclock they were done and stood sweating and looking at the scar of raw earth as men will look at finished work, and then they shouldered the shovels and walked up to the house and ate at the kitchen table in the smell of the old house, woodsmoke and dust and forty years of fried meals soaked into the plaster, and they did not talk while they ate. Their father's hat still hung on the peg by the door. Neither of them had moved it and neither of them had spoken of moving it and it hung there yet, sweat-stained and shaped to a head that was in the ground.

In the afternoon they changed the sets. The water was running well now with the line whole and they walked the rows opening valves and closing them, moving the water across the orchard in its old order, and the dark stain of it spread down the rows behind them and the smell of wet earth rose into the heat and the doves came in to walk the wet ground. The younger brother worked one side and the older the other and they met at the ends of the rows and turned and went back and it was old work and their bodies knew it and for a long time they let the work stand in the place of talk.

It was near six when the younger one said it. They were standing at the bottom of the orchard where the land fell away to the river and the light had gone long and yellow and the shadows of the trees lay across the rows like the rungs of an enormous ladder.

I got to give Marsh an answer, he said. He wont hold it open forever.

Then tell him no.

It aint just mine to tell him no. Nor yours to tell him neither. It's halved between us. That's how Daddy left it and you know why he left it that way.

Why.

Because he knew you'd never sell and he knew I'd never stay and he thought if he chained us to it together we'd have to work it out. That was his last piece of farmin, Tom. Us. He planted us in this thing like trees and walked off and died.

The older brother looked at the river. The cottonwoods had gone gold-green in the low sun and swallows were working the air above the water in their hundreds, turning all together, scattering, turning again.

I cant buy you out, he said. You know I cant. There aint a bank in three counties'd write that note on this ground.

I aint asked you to buy me out.

What are you askin then.

The younger brother took his hat off and wiped his forehead with the back of his wrist and put the hat on again. I dont know, he said. That's the truth of it. I had it all said in my head drivin out here in March. Sell it, split it, shake hands, go on back. And then I get here and it's, hell, I dont know what it is. I fixed pipe today I helped lay when I was eight years old. I knew where the break'd be before we walked down there. I could of told you within twenty foot. He shook his head. A man oughtnt to know a piece of ground that well and not want it. Or want it that bad and not be able to say so. One or the other. Not both.

The older brother said nothing. Far down the valley a truck crossed the river bridge and the sound of it came to them small and late.

Eleven five, the younger one said. And I stand down there where the line was busted and I think, that water's been comin to this ground a hundred and twelve years. You see what you done to me. I had it all said.

I didnt do nothin to you.

You said it out loud. That's doin somethin. There's things that keep, long as nobody says em.

They stood. The pump droned up at the wellhouse and the water moved unseen in the earth beneath them and out along the rows the new sets were darkening the ground in their slow tides.

Tell you what, the older brother said. Give it the season.

The season.

Through harvest. Marsh aint goin nowhere, whatever he says. Men like that dont quit wantin a thing because you made em wait, it just sweetens it for em. Give it through harvest and we'll pick what these trees'll give and we'll haul it to whoever'll take it and come November we'll sit down at that table and one of us'll say sell or one of us'll say stay and whatever gets said we'll abide it. Both of us. No more of this.

And if it's sell.

Then it's sell.

You'd sign.

I'd sign. The older brother looked at him. I'd hate it like death and I'd sign.

The younger one was quiet. The sun was down on the rim of the western hills now and the light was leaving the orchard floor and pooling in the upper branches and then it left those too and the swallows over the river had given way to the first bats, small and frantic and silent.

And if it's stay, the younger one said.

Then you stay. Or you deed me your half against shares and go on back, I dont care which. But you decide it lookin at it. Not from Phoenix. Not off of Marsh's figures. You decide it standin on it with apples in your hand.

That aint fair either, you know. Makin a man decide a thing standin on it.

I know it, the older brother said. I'm countin on it.

The younger one laughed then, short and quiet, the first laugh of the day, and shook his head and looked at the ground. You're a son of a bitch, he said. You're worse than he was.

I had longer to study him than you did.

They walked back up through the dark coming on. The dog fell in behind them. At the head of the orchard the older brother stopped at the valve there and knelt and put his hand to the riser and felt the water going by within it, cold out of the deep rock, older than the trees, older than the ditch the mule had cut, indifferent and abundant and bound for the roots of things, and he closed the valve a quarter turn the way his father had taught him, that the line not hammer in the night, and stood, and the two of them went on up to the lit kitchen window with the shovels on their shoulders, and behind them in the dark the water ran on down the rows in its appointed order, finding the low places, going where it had always gone, and the orchard drank and said nothing, the live trees and the dead alike, all of it drinking, all of it silent, all of it waiting on November like everything else in that country, which has waited on worse and outlasted it, and will outlast this too, whatever is decided, whoever signs.
