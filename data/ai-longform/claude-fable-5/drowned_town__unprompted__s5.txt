The lake gave the town back the way it had taken it: slowly, and without apology. All through the spring the water had retreated down the long slope of the old valley, leaving rings on the hillsides like the growth lines in a sawn trunk, and by June the newspapers were sending photographers, and by July the county had sent Ruth.

She parked the truck at the end of what had been the Carrow Bridge road, where the asphalt simply stopped at a guardrail and the old grade continued down into the basin, cracked and silver with dried silt. Sixty-one years of water had not erased the road. It had only buried it, and now the burial was being undone, foot by foot, as the reservoir shrank toward its dead pool. She sat in the cab a while with the engine off and the windows down. The morning smelled of mud and something older under the mud, anaerobic and sweetish, like the inside of a jar that had been sealed too long. Below her, perhaps a mile off and two hundred feet down, the streets of Wheelock lay open to the sky for the first time since she was seven years old.

Oscar arrived twenty minutes behind her, the way he always did, with a thermos of gas-station coffee and the drone cases stacked in the bed of his truck like luggage for a trip nobody wanted to take. He was twenty-six and had grown up in the new town, the one the government built on the ridge in 1962 and named, with the bureaucratic tenderness of that era, New Wheelock, as if the old one were merely a draft.

"Lake Apperson," he said, looking down at what was left of it, a pewter-colored remnant pooled against the dam at the far end of the valley. "My granddad always said they should've named it Lake Apology."

"Your granddad was from down there?"

"His people were. He was born after." He glanced at her sideways, careful. Everyone in the county office had been careful with her since the assignment came through, since someone had remembered, or been told, that Ruth Hale had been born in the town she was now being paid to map. "You sure you don't want me to take the foundations grid? You could run the control points up top, stay out of the muck."

"I'll take the foundations," Ruth said. "Fly your pictures. Stay out of my shots."

The county wanted documentation. That was the word in the work order — documentation — as though the town were a crime scene or a patient. There were liability questions, the engineer had explained: exposed wells, open cellars, a hundred hazards uncovered by the drought, and people coming down anyway, climbing the fences, walking the silt with metal detectors and phone cameras. Map the street grid, locate the open shafts, tie everything to modern coordinates before the rains came back, if they came back, and the lake reclaimed its property. Three weeks, maybe four. After that, the engineer said, it goes under again and we'll have it on record forever. He had said forever the way young men say it, easily, like a unit of measure.

They carried the equipment down the old road grade. The silt had dried in great curling plates that broke under their boots with a sound like crockery, and beneath the plates the mud was still damp, still gray, holding the cold of the lake bottom though the air above it was ninety degrees by ten o'clock. Mussel shells lay everywhere in drifts, bleached to the color of old teeth. Halfway down they passed a fence post, then a line of them, the wire long gone, the wood gone black and dense as iron — pickled, Oscar said, preserved by the same darkness that had ruined everything else. Cottonwood stumps stood along what had been the creek, sawn flat by crews in 1961 so the timber wouldn't foul boat propellers, and the stumps too had survived, ranked along the buried channel like the pilings of a pier that had never been built.

The town announced itself first as geometry. That was what struck her, standing at the foot of the grade where the road leveled out: not ruin, but order. The streets were still streets. The silt lay thinner over the old brick paving than over the yards, so the grid showed through the gray like a pattern under a worn rug — Main Street running east toward the drowned creek, the numbered streets crossing it at intervals her own feet still knew, though she had not walked them since the spring of 1961 and had walked them then with a child's strides. Curbstones broke the surface in long dotted lines. A fire hydrant stood at the corner of Main and Third, scaled with rust and dried algae, absurd and upright, like a man who had refused to leave.

"It's all still here," Oscar said quietly. He had stopped beside her. The drone case hung from his hand, forgotten.

"The water doesn't take things," Ruth said. "It just keeps them where they are."

She set the first control point at the corner of the courthouse foundation because she knew, before she knelt and brushed away the silt with her gloved hand, what she would find there. The courthouse had been the proudest building in Wheelock, two stories of quarried limestone with a clock that had been removed, she remembered, by men on a flatbed truck while the whole school watched from across the street — and on the southeast corner of its base, set by a federal crew in 1937, there had been a benchmark. A brass disk the size of a coffee saucer, domed, stamped with an elevation above sea level and a warning against disturbance, $250 fine. She had stood on it as a girl because her father told her it was the most certain thing in the county. Everything else is somebody's opinion, he'd said. That right there is a fact.

She found it under three inches of mud. She wiped it with her thumb and the brass came up green-black and then, where she rubbed harder, gold. U.S. COAST AND GEODETIC SURVEY. ELEV 1147.32 FEET. The fine print circling the rim like a coin's. She sat back on her heels and looked at it for a long moment, this small monument that had spent six decades at the bottom of a lake insisting on its number, and then she set the rover pole on it and let the receiver gather satellites, machines four hundred miles overhead conversing with a disk set by men who were all dead now, and when the position resolved she found the old elevation agreed with the new datum to within a tenth of a foot. The fact had held. She entered it in the field book in her small upright hand and wrote beside it, against every protocol of professional notation: Recovered in good condition.

They worked the grid for nine days before she let herself go to the house.

It was not avoidance, she told herself, or not only that. It was method. You ran your control first, established your framework, and worked from the whole to the part — that was the discipline her first party chief had beaten into her in 1974, the first woman on his crew and he never let her forget it, from the whole to the part, Hale, or your errors compound. So she shot the courthouse and the bank, whose vault stood intact and doorless in the middle of its vanished building like a small mausoleum. She shot the school, where the wide front steps emerged from the silt still bearing the iron pipe rails children had slid down against all instruction, and she stood on the third step where her class had been photographed in the fall of 1960, twenty-two children squinting, the photograph itself now in a box in her closet in town, and she located the corners of the building to the hundredth of a foot. She shot the Methodist church, its floor a clean rectangle, its bell long since hauled to New Wheelock where it hung in a brick tower and rang, Oscar told her, every Sunday, with what she imagined must be a confused and homesick sound. She mapped forty-one open cellars, eleven wells, one cistern large enough to swallow a car, and the courthouse vault stairs descending into black water that had never drained, would never drain, the last of old Wheelock that would stay drowned even now.

People came down despite the fences. She watched them from a distance through the total station's scope: families mostly, moving slowly along Main Street with the particular hesitant gait of people in a cemetery, stopping, pointing, crouching. An old man stood for an hour one afternoon at the corner of Fourth and Locust, alone, his hat in his hands, and Ruth did not go ask him what was wrong with him standing inside a posted hazard area, because she knew what was at the corner of Fourth and Locust. The Avery house had stood there. She had taken piano lessons in its front room, badly, from Mrs. Avery, who smelled of lavender and disappointment, and the old man at the corner had the long Avery jaw, and there are trespasses the law was never meant for.

The metal-detector men were another matter. She ran two of them off the school grounds on the eleventh day, a pair of strangers with mesh hats and a five-gallon bucket already a third full — doorknobs, hinges, a brass faucet, a child's roller skate, all of it furred with corrosion, all of it pried out of the mud that had held it faithfully for sixty years.

"It's a reservoir bottom," the taller one said. "Public land. Army Corps."

"It's a closed hazard area, and it's somebody's town," Ruth said, and heard in her own voice a tone she didn't entirely recognize. "Dump the bucket."

"You can't make us dump the bucket."

"No," she agreed. "But I can write down your plate, and the sheriff's a friend of mine, and the federal antiquities statute carries more weight than you'd think for a place that's only been historic since June." This was mostly invention. They dumped the bucket. Afterward she stood over the small mound of hardware in the silt and could not have said what she wanted done with it, only that it should not leave the valley in a stranger's truck. She picked up the roller skate. The leather strap had survived; the wheels turned, stiffly, when she pushed them with her thumb. She set it on the school steps where the next family of pilgrims would find it, and walked back to her instrument, and did not work well for the rest of the afternoon.

On the twelfth day she sent Oscar up top to process imagery, told him she'd radio if she needed the second rod, and walked alone up Walnut Street to the fourth lot from the corner, north side, where she had been born.

She had told herself she was prepared, having been preparing for twelve days, having been preparing, perhaps, for sixty-one years. She was not prepared. The maple was what undid her — the stump of it, sawn flat like all the rest, four feet across at the cut, standing in the front yard exactly where her memory had kept it. Her father had hung a rope swing from a limb that would have been, she calculated automatically, eleven or twelve feet above the present grade, and she had spent whole summers in its arc, and she had assumed without ever examining the assumption that the tree was simply gone, dissolved, the way she had assumed the whole town was gone — because that was the word everyone had always used. The town is gone, honey. Wheelock's gone. But the town had never gone anywhere. She was the one who had gone. The town had stayed home, every brick of it, in the dark, and the maple's roots were still gripping the same dirt they had gripped when she was learning to pump her legs and her mother was calling from the porch not too high, Ruthie, and the lake's only contribution had been silence, pressure, sixty winters of cold standing water like a held breath.

The house had been small. She knew that now, kneeling at the foundation; she had not known it then. Twenty-six feet by thirty, the tape said, a foursquare cottage her grandfather had built in 1919, and the stone foundation stood open to the sun like a diagram of itself: front room, kitchen, the back bedroom where she had been born during an ice storm in February of 1954 because the roads were glazed and Dr. Pruett could not come and Mrs. Ellender from next door could, with her hands scrubbed to the elbow and forty years of farm births behind her. Ruth had heard the story so many times it had the shape of scripture. Her mother liked to end it the same way: And the lights went out at midnight and you came at one, so you've never been afraid of the dark a day in your life, and Ruth would say, dutifully, into her sixties, on the phone with her mother in the nursing home in Garfield, No, Mama. Never a day.

She stepped over the foundation wall into the front room.

The floor was silt, of course, level gray silt with a crust that broke under her boots, but beneath her boots, she knew, beneath eight or ten inches of lake bottom, the joists were probably still there, black and pickled like the fence posts, and beneath the joists the cellar her father had dug by hand, and in the cellar — she made herself stand still and breathe — in the cellar, against the north wall, the shelves of her mother's canning. They had left it. That was one of the things you learned about the leaving, one of the small obscene details: the government men appraised the house but not its weight, not what could and couldn't be carried, and her mother had stood in the kitchen on the last morning doing arithmetic with the truck bed, and the canning lost. A hundred and some jars, tomatoes and green beans and blackberry preserves, the whole stored summer of 1960, abandoned on the shelves because there was no room and no time, and her mother had not cried over the house or the church or the maple but had cried over the jars, briefly, furiously, with her back turned, and then dried her face and never mentioned them again in forty years. The stove they had also left. It sat now in the open rectangle of the kitchen, the one object in the whole excavated town still standing in its working position: a cast-iron range, six hundred pounds if it was an ounce, orange-black with rust, its oven door hanging open like a dropped jaw, and on its top, fused to the iron by corrosion, a kettle.

Ruth sat down on the foundation wall and let it happen, whatever it was — not weeping exactly, something dryer and slower than weeping, a kind of recalibration. She was sixty-eight years old. She had surveyed this county for forty-one years; she had laid out subdivisions and settled fence disputes and staked the bypass that killed the downtown of the town that had killed this one; she had buried her father in 1988 and her mother in 2009, both of them up in the relocated cemetery on the ridge where the government had moved the graves in '61, eight hundred disinterments performed in one summer by contractors, so that even the dead of Wheelock had been made to evacuate. And in all that time she had been able to point at the lake and feel almost nothing, because a lake is an answer that admits no further questions. You cannot grieve a sheet of water. But a kitchen — a kitchen with the stove still in it —

The last day. She had not let herself think about the last day in years, and now it arrived whole. April 1961, the water already up over the low fields east of the creek, rising not like a flood, with violence, but like the contents of a filling tub, patient and total. The school had closed in March. On the last day Miss Kendrick had let the children take things — the readers, the globes, the rolled maps that hung above the blackboard on spring rollers — because it will all go to the warehouse otherwise, children, and warehouses are where things go to be forgotten. Ruth had taken the county map. She could not have said why; she was seven; the other girls took the storybooks. But she had wanted the map, the big linen-backed one with the townships ruled in red and Wheelock printed in letters that seemed to her enormous, the size of the town's importance, and she had carried it up the hill to the new house rolled on her shoulder like a soldier's rifle, and it had hung on her bedroom wall through her whole growing up, Wheelock at its center, until the year the state quietly issued new maps with the blue amoeba of Lake Apperson where the red-ruled sections had been. She owned both maps now. She had brought them, in fact, in a tube behind the truck seat, and had not yet admitted to herself why.

She knew why. She stood up, wiped her face on her sleeve, and went and got the rover.

What she did that afternoon appeared in no work order. She set the pole point on the silt at the center of the back bedroom — having measured from the foundation corners, having checked her measurements twice, from the whole to the part — and she let the receiver gather its satellites, and she fixed the position of the room where she had been born to the hundredth of a foot in the coordinate system of the modern world. Then she did the maple stump, and the front threshold, where there had once been a doorframe with pencil lines climbing it, Ruthie 2, Ruthie 3, Ruthie 4½, the wood long gone to the warehouse of the lake. Then she walked the block and did the Ellender foundation next door, for the hands that had caught her. In the field book she recorded each point with its number and description in the dry idiom of her profession — FND COR HSE, STUMP 48" DIA, THRESHOLD STONE — and then, because a field book is a legal document and outlives its keeper, because someone in some records room would hold these pages in fifty years, she added one line beneath the coordinates of the back bedroom, in the careful lettering her first party chief had drilled into her: This point witnessed by the undersigned, who first occupied it Feb. 9, 1954.

The rain came on the nineteenth day, ahead of every forecast. She and Oscar were closing the last traverse on the east side when the sky over the dam went the color of slate and the wind arrived smelling of wet dust, that smell that ends droughts, and Oscar whooped — he couldn't help it, he was twenty-six and his lawn was dead — and then caught himself and looked at her, stricken.

"It's all right," Ruth said. "It's what water does."

They got the instruments under tarps and stood in the lee of the courthouse foundation while the first drops cratered the silt around them, each one a small dark coin, and then the coins ran together and the gray crust everywhere began to slick and shine and the streets of Wheelock gleamed under the rain as they must have gleamed on a thousand ordinary evenings when the town was alive and supper was cooking on six hundred pounds of cast iron up on Walnut Street. The remnant lake at the foot of the valley pocked and hissed. It would take months, she knew, the engineer's models said two wet winters at least, but the direction of things had turned that afternoon, and everyone standing in the basin could feel it. The water was coming back for its town.

Before they climbed out she walked once more to the benchmark at the courthouse corner. The rain had washed it clean; the brass shone dully in the storm light, ELEV 1147.32, the fact her father had promised her. She crouched and put two fingers on it, the way you would touch a headstone, the way the old Avery man had held his hat. The point did not need her. That was the consolation of the work, the only one it offered and the only one she had ever required: the coordinates were taken now, the town was fixed in the frame of the turning earth, and the water could come back forever and would alter nothing but the light. She had closed the traverse. The error of sixty-one years had come out smaller than anyone could have hoped.

She stood, and her knees told her about it, and she walked up the old road grade through the rain with the field book buttoned inside her jacket against her chest, and behind her, in the bootprints she left in the silt of Main Street, the water was already beginning to stand.
