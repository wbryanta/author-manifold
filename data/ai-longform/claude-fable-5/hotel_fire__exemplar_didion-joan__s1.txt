The Miramar burned on the morning of October 14, 1953, and was rebuilt within two years, poured concrete and steel sash, advertised in the Sunday travel sections as completely fireproof, but I did not see it again until the fifth of June, 1976, when I drove down from Pasadena with my husband to watch Cecily Bryden married on the south lawn. The invitation had come engraved on paper so heavy it would not fold flat in the drawer where I kept it. Mr. and Mrs. Roydon Bryden, Jr. requested the pleasure of our company at the marriage of their daughter Cecily Anne to Mr. Beauregard Whitfield of New Orleans, the ceremony to take place at half past five in the afternoon at the Hotel Miramar, reception to follow. Garrett had handled the refinancing of the Miramar in 1971 and again in 1974, and considered Chip Bryden, if not a friend, then someone whose daughter's wedding one attended. I had never told Garrett that I worked at the Miramar the year I was nineteen. The occasion to tell him had not arisen, or I had not allowed it to arise, which I see now is a distinction without much in it.

Certain facts. The fire was reported at 4:19 on the morning of October 14, 1953, by the driver of a milk truck making his run down the old coast road. The Santa Ana had been blowing four days. Six people died, and I can name them, have been able to name them at any hour of any night for twenty-three years: Luz Robles, twenty, a chambermaid; Gene Schultes, sixty-one, the night clerk; Commander Hugh Catto, United States Navy, retired; a salesman of irrigation pipe named Vernon Kessler; and Mr. and Mrs. Arthur Pruitt of Salt Lake City, who had been married three days. The fire marshal's report attributed the fire to careless smoking in the service wing, person or persons unknown. The service wing was the wooden annex over the laundry where the girls slept two to a room, and where the door to the back stairs was kept locked at night because a girl had once taken towels. I shared a room in that annex with Luz Robles. The morning of the fire I was not in it.

I had come to the Miramar in June of that year from Hanford, where my mother cooked for the Elks Club and raised her girls to finish what they started and not call attention to themselves. When I am frightened I do not call attention to myself. I count. I counted the morning of the fire, standing on the south lawn in my slip while the annex went up like a struck match, and I have counted since in better hotels than the Miramar ever was: the doors between my bed and the outside at the Mark Hopkins, at the Beverly Wilshire, at a pensione above Rapallo where there was only one door and I lay awake until dawn. That morning at the Miramar there had been two doors between Luz Robles and the outside, and one of them was locked.

We took the coast road down, Garrett driving, the oleander on the median dusty and past bloom. The fog had not come in. You could see the hotel from a mile up the road, white against the eucalyptus windbreak, bigger than I remembered and at the same time not big at all, three stories of poured concrete where the old shingled pile had stood, a building with nothing about it that could burn or, burning, matter. Completely fireproof. A boy in a green jacket took the car. In the lobby there was a printed history of the hotel on a stand by the desk, and while Garrett asked about the bar I read it. The Miramar has welcomed guests to this curve of coast since 1931, the history said. Completely renovated in 1955. Between those two sentences six people had died, and the history got from one to the other without recourse to either fact.

"You're frowning," Garrett said.

"I never frown."

"You're frowning at the brochure."

"I was reading about the renovation," I said. "It seems to have been very thorough."

The ceremony was held on the south lawn at half past five, the light by then gone gold and the wind off the water just strong enough to move the women's skirts. There were a hundred and forty white chairs in fourteen rows of ten, an aisle of white runner, an arch dressed in orchids that the program said had been flown from Hilo that morning. I counted the chairs during the prelude. I located, without appearing to look for them, the four ways off that lawn: the terrace doors, the path to the beach stairs, the gap in the hedge by the tennis courts, the service gate. The service gate was new, or rather the wall it opened through was new, a long white wall planted with roses where the annex had stood. They had not rebuilt over the footprint. They had put roses on it, Queen Elizabeth roses, pink and scentless and blooming very well, and a hundred and forty people sat with their backs to that wall and watched Cecily Bryden come down the aisle on her father's arm.

She was a pretty girl, the bride, with that blondness that has been refreshed in every generation, and the Whitfields had come out from New Orleans in numbers and sat through the ceremony with the look Southerners get in California, the look of people determined to find no fault aloud. The bridesmaids wore apricot. A judge performed the ceremony, a friend of Chip Bryden's from the club, and he kept it short, and afterward the band on the terrace started in on "Satin Doll" before the bride and groom had cleared the runner, which seemed to me to say something about the Brydens, although I could not have said exactly what. At Sarah Whitfield's end of the front row a very old man sat in a wheelchair with a blanket over his knees in June, and I knew before anyone said so that this was old Roy Bryden, who had owned the Miramar in 1953, who had stood on this same lawn in his overcoat at five in the morning watching his hotel burn and saying to no one in particular, over and over, that he had told Gene a hundred times about the wiring.

"That's the grandfather," Garrett said at the reception. "Built the place."

"His father built it," I said. "He rebuilt it."

Garrett looked at me. "I didn't know you knew the family history."

"It was in the brochure," I said, which was true as far as it went.

The reception was in the ballroom and on the terrace, and it was a large party, the kind of party where the champagne keeps coming and the toasts run long and the mothers of both families have stopped speaking to the caterer. Didi Bryden found us by the terrace doors. Didi Bryden was the bride's mother, a thin woman in gray silk who had the air of having saved this particular situation many times before, and she took my hand and held it and told me how much it meant to Chip that Garrett had come, that Garrett of all people knew what this place meant to the family, that the Miramar was really less a hotel than a member of the family, if I knew what she meant.

I said that I knew what she meant.

"Of course the original building was Victorian," Didi Bryden said. "All shingle and gingerbread. A firetrap, really. Thank God Roy rebuilt before our time, you couldn't get the permits today. You can't imagine the wiring in those old places."

"I can imagine it," I said.

"Everything's on its own circuit now," Didi Bryden said. "Sprinklers in every room. You could light a bonfire in the lobby and it would put itself out." She laughed, and pressed my hand again, and moved off toward the Whitfields, and I stood holding a glass of champagne I had not drunk from and looked at the sprinkler heads set flush in the ballroom ceiling, one every twelve feet, and counted them, fourteen down the long side, and Garrett asked if I wanted to dance and I said in a while.

I will tell what I have not told. On the night of October 13, 1953, the wind had been blowing since the Friday before, the kind of wind that drops the humidity until your hair crackles on the pillow and the eucalyptus bark peels and lifts in the dark, and nobody at the Miramar had slept well in four days. Commander Catto had complained twice at the desk about the wind in his casement. The Pruitts, who were on their honeymoon, had quarreled in the dining room loudly enough that the waiters mentioned it. Luz Robles had a cough she could not shake and I lay in our room listening to her cough and to the wind working at the annex, which was a wooden building and spoke in a wind, and at half past three I got up and took my uniform and went down the inside stairs to the linen room to press my collar and cuffs for the morning, because we went on at six and I could not sleep anyway. The linen room was warm and white and quiet, shelves of folded sheets to the ceiling, and the iron heating on its plate, and I sat on the stool with the door shut against the wind and I smoked one cigarette. The girls were not allowed to smoke anywhere on the property. I smoked one cigarette, and I will say what I have always been able to say, which is that I put it out under the tap and saw it black and wet in my hand before I dropped it in the bin with the cuttings and the lint. I pressed my collar. I unplugged the iron, and I will say what I have always been able to say, which is that I remember the plug in my hand. Then I went out through the service door to stand in the wind because the linen room had made me warm, and I was standing in the dark by the garbage cans counting the lights still on in the main building, three of them, when I smelled it.

What I smelled was not smoke at first. It was heat, if heat has a smell, the smell of dust on a furnace coil, and then it was smoke, and by the time I got around the corner of the annex there was light in the linen room window, light of a kind that has no business in any window, and the bin was on the wall the stairs shared. I have gone over the next several minutes more times than I have gone over anything else in my life and they do not improve with handling. I screamed at the annex. Windows opened. Girls came down the outside stairs at the north end and across the gravel in their nightgowns, and I stood counting them as they came, eight, nine, ten, and the count stopped at eleven and there should have been twelve, and the back stairs at the south end, the inside stairs, the stairs that came down past the linen room, were locked at the bottom because a girl had once taken towels, and the wind took the annex roof the way a wave takes sand. The main building caught from the annex. It was shingle and gingerbread, as Didi Bryden said, and it went in forty minutes. Commander Catto, the firemen decided afterward, never woke; he had been taking something to sleep through the wind. The Pruitts were found on the third-floor landing, together, which I have always hoped meant they had stopped quarreling. Gene Schultes went up the stairs after them and did not come down. Vernon Kessler jumped from the second floor and broke his neck on the flagstone walk, the only flagstone in forty feet of lawn. And Luz Robles, who coughed, who could not shake the cough, who was twenty, was in our room at the locked end, eleven feet from a door that opened from the outside only with Gene Schultes's key.

A fireman found me at the edge of the lawn, still counting. He could not make me stop counting, he told the marshal, and the marshal wrote that down, and three days later a man from the insurance company sat with me in the coffee shop of a motel up the coast road where the survivors had been put two to a room and asked me where I had been when the fire started, and I was nineteen years old, and my mother had taught me not to call attention to myself, and I said I had been asleep and woke to the screaming. He wrote that down too. He gave me an envelope with eighty-five dollars in it and a bus ticket to Hanford and showed me where to sign, and I signed where he showed me, and the marshal's report, when it was published in the paper that winter, said careless smoking in the service wing, person or persons unknown.

I have had twenty-three years to consider whether I am the person or persons unknown. I saw the cigarette black and wet in my hand. I remember the plug in my hand. I also know that there was an iron, and a bin of lint, and a wind, and a wall of folded linen, and that I was the last living soul in that room, and that memory is a hotel that has been completely renovated. I am no closer now than I was in the coffee shop of the Sea Breeze Motel.

They cut the cake at eight. At a quarter past eight a waiter touched my elbow on the terrace and said that Mr. Bryden, the elder Mr. Bryden, would like a word, and I followed him to the corner of the ballroom where the wheelchair had been placed out of the draft, and the old man looked up at me out of a face that had gone the color and texture of the paper the invitation was engraved on, and he said, "You're one of Mae Kell's girls."

I had not heard my mother's name spoken aloud in eleven years. "I'm Frances," I said. "Frances Kell. Frances March now."

"You were here when she burned."

"I was here."

"I knew you the minute you came down the aisle behind the others," Roy Bryden said. "You walked across that lawn like you were counting the chairs." Garrett had come up behind me; I could feel him there, not speaking. "You were the one on the grass," the old man said. "The fireman couldn't make you quit counting. Counting what, I always wanted to know."

"The girls," I said. "I was counting the girls."

"You got to eleven."

"I got to eleven."

He looked at the band, and at the dancing, and at his granddaughter being turned under the lights in her white dress, and he said, "I paid for the Robles girl's funeral. The family was in Guadalupe. I paid for the Mass cards, the flowers, all of it." He said it the way a man recites the provisions of a will, and I understood that he had been saying it to himself for twenty-three years, the way I had been saying the six names, and that it had done him about as much good. "The marshal said one of the girls was smoking," he said. "I never believed it. I told him so. Those girls worked twelve hours a day, I told him. Those girls didn't have time to smoke." He was watching my face when he said it and I have wondered since whether he was watching my face the way Warren Bogart watched faces, to see what would move in them, or whether he was an old man at his granddaughter's wedding saying the only absolution he had to the only person left who could use it. I did not ask. I have never had patience with games, but this did not seem to be one, or if it was a game it was one we had both been playing alone for so long that playing it together looked like mercy.

"It was the wiring," Roy Bryden said. "I told Gene a hundred times about that wiring."

"I know you did," I said.

"They tell me you can't burn the new one." He laughed, the dry forced laugh of a man hitting upon a phrase. "Completely fireproof. You could light a bonfire in the lobby."

"So I'm told," I said.

In the car, much later, Garrett would say, "You never mentioned you worked at the Miramar," and I would say that it never came up, and he would drive a while and say, "It might have come up in 1971. I refinanced the place in 1971, Frances, I had the file on my desk for six months," and I would say that I supposed it might have, and that would be, for Garrett, the end of it, because Garrett is a lawyer and a kind man and believes that what is signed and dated is settled. But that was later. At half past nine I left him with Chip Bryden and the bond talk and walked out of the ballroom and down the back corridor past the kitchen, where no one stops a woman in a good dress because no one can imagine what she would want there, and I found the linen room. It was where it had been, more or less; they had rebuilt the service wing in concrete block on the other axis, but a linen room wants to be near the laundry and the laundry wants to be near the water heaters and so the linen room was within twenty feet of where I had sat on the stool at half past three in the morning in 1953. The door was open. A girl was in there folding, a girl perhaps nineteen, in the green uniform they wear now, and she looked up at me without alarm and said could she help me, and I said I had lost my way looking for the powder room, and she told me how to go. Shelves of folded sheets to the ceiling. A sprinkler head in the middle of the ceiling, and a smoke detector by the door, and a red extinguisher in a glass-front case, and a sign in English and Spanish that said no fumar. The girl went back to folding. She had the radio on very low. I stood a moment in the doorway counting the sheets in the nearest stack, and then I went back the way I had come.

On the way I stopped at the rose wall. The terrace lights did not reach that far and the roses were just shapes in the dark, and the wind had come up a little, off the water now, the ordinary onshore evening wind that means fog by midnight, nothing like the other wind, the wrong-way wind, the one that dries you out and stands your hair on end and waits. I put my hand flat against the wall. The concrete still held the heat of the afternoon, the way concrete does, and I stood there with my hand on the warm wall over the place where the annex had been, and I said the six names, Luz Robles, Gene Schultes, Hugh Catto, Vernon Kessler, Arthur Pruitt, Margaret Pruitt, not aloud, and not as a prayer exactly, because I was not raised to prayer, but in the way you count the doors between your bed and the outside, because it is the thing you do and the doing of it is the only thing that has ever been asked of you that you could be sure of performing.

At eleven the bride and groom left for the airport. The Brydens had arranged a send-off, and waiters moved among the guests on the front steps handing out sparklers from silver trays, and lighting them, so that Cecily Bryden Whitfield ran to the car through a double line of her friends and family all holding fire, a hundred small fires spitting and dying along the drive of the completely fireproof Hotel Miramar, and everyone laughing, and the Whitfields of New Orleans finding no fault aloud. A waiter put a sparkler in my hand. I held it unlit until Garrett, laughing, took the lighter from the waiter and lit it for me, and the thing caught and hissed and threw its white sparks against the dark.

"Hold it away from your dress," Garrett said.

I held it away from my dress.
