It was the only courtesy the families could offer, this stepping back, and the team accepted it the way one accepts a gift from someone who has nothing else to give. Anil knelt by the two skeletons and continued lifting the soil away with a bamboo pick, an inch at a time, while behind her the woman stood at the lip of the grave with her hands at her sides, watching the slow undressing of the earth.

Years later, in another country, Anil would think of that woman whenever she crouched over bones. The posture of her. The way grief had settled in the body like sediment, into the shoulders first, then everywhere.

She left Guatemala in the rainy season, the airfields slick and steaming, and for a while she worked in Oklahoma, where the dead arrived in drawers and the air-conditioning hummed like a hive and nothing was personal. She liked the impersonality at first. The numbered cases, the fluorescent honesty of the labs. The dead in America came with files. They came with dental charts and insurance records and weeping relatives who were kept in another room, behind glass, given coffee in paper cups. Death there was administered.

Then the letter came from Geneva, and a week after that the call, and a month after that she was on a plane descending through cloud toward the island where she had been born and which she had not seen for fifteen years.

She landed at Katunayake airport before dawn. The flight had crossed the Indian Ocean in darkness and she had slept badly, waking again and again to the small map on the seat-back screen, the white aircraft inching over blue, and beneath that blue, she knew, the long drowned slope of the continental shelf, the cables, the wrecks, the unrecorded dead of every century. When the doors of the plane opened the air came in and found her, warm and wet and smelling of kerosene and frangipani and something older underneath, something she could not name but which her body named for her. Her skin remembered before she did.

She was thirty-three years old. She had left at eighteen.

In the arrivals hall a man held a card with her name on it. ANIL TISSERA. She stood for a moment before identifying herself, looking at the name the way one looks at a photograph of oneself taken by a stranger. The name had been her brother's, the second, unused name of her brother, and she had wanted it the way some children want a knife or a horizon, and at thirteen she had bought it from him with a hundred saved rupees, a pen set he had admired, a tin of fifty Gold Leaf cigarettes, and a sexual favor he had demanded in the last stages of the bargaining and which she had refused, holding out, until he settled for the cigarettes. Her parents had been scandalized for a season and then had given in, the way they gave in to weather. From then on she answered to nothing else. The two names she had been christened with she let fall behind her like ballast.

'You are the swimmer,' the man with the card said, as he took her bags.

'No,' she said. 'Not anymore.'

But it was the first thing everyone here would say to her, she discovered, in the weeks that followed. Not the forensic scientist, not the woman from the international human rights organization in Geneva, not even the one who had been away too long. The swimmer. At sixteen she had won the two-mile swim at Mount Lavinia, the race from the hotel rocks out around the buoy and back through the swells, beating boys two years older, and there had been a photograph in the Observer, her thin arm raised, her hair flat against her skull like a seal's. The country had a long memory for its small champions and no memory at all, it sometimes seemed, for its dead.

The drive into Colombo took an hour. She kept the window down. Cinnamon Gardens went by in the half-dark, the high walls, the flame trees, then the canals with their green stink and the kabaragoyas sliding off the banks, and men already up, bathing at standpipes, soaping their black hair white. At a checkpoint a soldier no older than seventeen leaned into the car with a flashlight, looked at her face, looked at her passport, looked at her face again. There was a moment when nobody in the car breathed. Then he stepped back and waved them through with the barrel of his rifle, casually, the way a man waves flies from food.

'You'll get used to it,' the driver said.

'I don't want to get used to it,' she said, and saw his eyes find hers in the mirror and then go away.

She had been sent as part of a human rights investigation, one forensic anthropologist, paired by agreement with one local archaeologist appointed by the government. That was the shape of it on paper. Geneva had negotiated for two years to get even this: a seven-week window, limited access, all reports to be filed jointly. She understood what the arrangement meant. It meant a watcher. It meant that every bone she touched would be touched also by the state, and every conclusion she came to would have a hand resting on its shoulder.

His name was Sarath Diyasena. He was forty-nine, an archaeologist, and when she met him two days later in an office near the Kynsey Road morgue he stood up slowly, as if his bones had been excavated rather than grown, and shook her hand and said, 'So. They sent us a Colombo girl.'

'I haven't been a Colombo girl for fifteen years.'

'It doesn't come off,' he said. 'I've seen people try.'

He was darker than she was, heavyset, with grey coming into his hair in two wings above the ears, and a stillness about him that she would spend weeks trying to read. In another country she would have called it calm. Here she did not yet know what to call it. He poured her tea without asking and stirred condensed milk into it, also without asking, and slid it across the desk, and that small assumption—that she would take her tea the way everyone here took it, sweet and pale, the way she had taken it as a child—irritated her and moved her at the same time, and she drank it.

'You've read the brief,' he said.

'I've read what Geneva gave me. Disappearances. Mass graves rumored in three provinces. Bodies recovered from rivers and burned beyond—'

'Yes,' he said, before she could finish, and she understood it was not impatience but something else, that he did not want the words said aloud in that room, or perhaps in any room. He looked past her at the door for a moment. Then he said, 'I have something to show you. Not here. Tomorrow.'

What he had to show her was a ship.

The Oronsay had been a passenger liner once, in the time of her grandparents, carrying tea and rubber and civil servants and lovesick adolescents between Colombo and Tilbury. Now it lay permanently moored in an unused quay of the harbor, gutted, deconsecrated, its first-class lounge a storeroom and its hold a long cool dark cave smelling of rust and bilge and formalin. The government had given it to nobody in particular, which meant Sarath had quietly taken it. It would be their lab. No electricity except a generator they ran in the mornings, no telephone, the gangway watchable from a single point.

'No one comes here,' Sarath said, walking her down a corridor where their footsteps rang on steel. 'That is the most important quality a laboratory can have in this country. Not equipment. Solitude.'

'Solitude,' she said, 'or secrecy?'

'In Sri Lanka, at this time, they are the same word.'

She wanted to argue with that. She had come back armored in the vocabulary of her training—evidence, chain of custody, transparency, the truth-claims of the measurable world. In Oklahoma, in Arizona, in the Guatemalan highlands, she had learned to believe that a bone could be made to speak and that what it said could be entered into a record, and that the record was a kind of justice, or at least the beginning of one. One village can speak for many villages, a lawyer had told her once. One victim can speak for many victims. She had written the sentence in the back of a notebook and carried it across three continents.

But she said nothing, that first day, walking through the iron belly of the ship. She was watching Sarath's back and trying to decide what he was. The government's man, sent to mind her. Or an honest scientist surviving inside a dishonest time the only way one could, by silence, by indirection, by keeping his real thoughts in an inner room. Or both. In the months to come she would decide each of these things in turn, and un-decide them, and there were nights much later when she lay awake in the dark of the walawwa in the south, listening to rain come across the paddy fields like applause, still not knowing.

On the fourth day he took her to the caves.

They were government-protected archaeological reserves near Bandarawela, sanctuaries of a sixth-century forest monastery, and only officially sanctioned researchers could enter them. Sarath had the papers. In the second cave, in a crevice sealed for centuries by a fall of rock, there were skeletons—he had located three on an earlier survey and believed there might be more. Old monks, he said. Interred or simply left, fifteen hundred years ago, in the dry mineral dark where nothing decayed at the surface's pace.

They worked through the morning by lamplight, and it was good work, ancient work, work without grief in it. The dead of the sixth century ask nothing of you. She lifted a calvarium the color of tobacco and turned it in her hands and felt only the clean curiosity she had trained for, the pleasure of the puzzle. Sarath labeled the skeletons in his notebook in his small architect's hand: Tinker, Tailor, Soldier. An English counting rhyme he must have learned at school under the old colonial syllabus. They worked mostly without speaking. She found she liked working beside him. He moved soil the way she had been taught to, with patience, as if the earth itself deserved courtesy.

It was in the afternoon that she found the fourth one.

It lay deeper than the others, half wrapped in the rotted remains of plastic sheeting, and the moment her brush crossed the iliac crest she stopped, because she knew. Her body knew before her mind assembled the reasons. The bone was wrong for that cave. It had weight where the others were husks. There were trace ligaments still mineralizing at the joints. There was, when she lowered her face close to it, the faint sour breath of recent earth.

'Sarath.'

He came and crouched beside her and looked for a long time and said nothing.

'This one is not sixth-century,' she said. 'This one is not sixty years old. Look at the staining. There's been burning, partial, and then burial somewhere else, in soil with high acidity—a paddy field, maybe—and then reburial here. Here, in a site only government people can enter.'

'You can't know all that yet,' he said quietly.

'I will know all of it,' she said. 'That's what I do.'

He sat back on his heels. Outside the cave mouth, far below, a hawk turned over the valley on a thermal, and the afternoon went on being the afternoon, immense and indifferent. When he spoke again his voice was so low she had to lean toward him.

'If you are right,' he said, 'do you understand what you are holding?'

'Evidence.'

'A landmine,' he said. 'You are holding a landmine. And you are asking me to help you carry it through a country at war, to a courtroom that may not exist, past people who have killed for much less than a skeleton.' He rubbed his thumb slowly across the dust on his palm. 'I'm not saying no. I am saying you must know the weight of it before you lift it. In your other countries the truth is the end of the matter. Here the truth is sometimes just a flame against a sleeping lake of petrol.'

'Then why did you bring me here?' she said. 'You knew this site. You chose this site.'

He didn't answer. He took the rhyme out again instead. 'Tinker, tailor, soldier,' he said, touching the air above each of the three old monks in turn. Then his hand moved over the fourth skeleton, the new one, the wrong one, and paused.

'Sailor,' he said.

So the skeleton had a name before it had anything else, before sex or age or stature or cause of death, and the name attached itself to the bones the way her own bought name had once attached itself to her, and afterward neither of them ever called it anything else. They carried Sailor out of the cave wrapped in a woven mat, like a man being taken home sick from the fields, and laid him in the back of the jeep, and drove down out of the hills in the failing light through three checkpoints, and at each one Sarath spoke softly in Sinhala and showed his ministry pass and the soldiers waved them on, and at each one Anil sat looking straight ahead with her heart going hard, an accomplice now, she understood, a smuggler of the dead.

That night on the Oronsay she could not sleep. She had taken one of the old passenger cabins, a first-class single with a porthole painted shut, and she lay on the narrow bunk listening to the harbor—water against the hull, a generator somewhere, once the long mournful note of a freighter standing out to sea—and she thought about the woman in Guatemala, sitting in the grave on her haunches as if the dead were only sleeping. She thought: I have spent my whole adult life learning to read the dead, and I have never once sat with them. Not the way that woman sat. She got up before light and went down through the cold steel corridors to the hold and switched on the lamp over the table where Sailor lay, and stood there with her arms folded, not measuring anything, not recording anything, just standing, until the light through the high rust-holes turned from grey to gold.

Then she went to work.

The bones told her things in the order bones always do. Male. Twenty-five to thirty-three years old. Five foot eight, give or take. The pelvic girdle had taken his sex from him easily; his age came harder, from the ribs, from the pubic symphysis, from the slow doors of the cranial sutures, some closed, some closing. There were healed fractures in two ribs from years before his death, childhood perhaps, a fall, a beating, who could say. There was old hairline damage in the left ankle and remodeling in both heels of a kind she had seen in the literature but never before under her own hands—the signature of a man who had worked squatting and braced, day after day, in some repeated labor. The arms had been broken, both of them, not long before death. And in the back of the skull, the thing itself: a small entrance, a larger exit, the radiating fractures running away from it like the dry beds of rivers.

'They shot him after,' she told Sarath, when he came down at noon with rice and dhal in a tin carrier from a stall on the quay. 'After the arms. After the burning, maybe—there's char on bone that was already broken, look, the heat went into the fracture. They broke him, they burned him, then they shot him, then they buried him, then they dug him up, and then they buried him again where only the government could go. Each step is a sentence. The skeleton is the confession.'

Sarath set the food down and looked at Sailor for a long time.

'Whose confession,' he said. It was not a question.

'You know whose.'

'I know that there are three sides killing in this country,' he said, 'and that all three of them bury their work, and that you have been here eleven days.' He unstacked the tins, the rice, the curry, two spoons, with the same care he gave to potsherds. 'Eat first,' he said. 'You don't eat. I've noticed it. You think feeding the body is a betrayal of the work. Eat, and then I will tell you how we are going to find out who he was.'

It was the first time he had said we.

What he proposed was not in any of her manuals. To identify the dead you worked from the body outward—records, files, antemortem X-rays, the apparatus of the documented world. But Sailor had come from the undocumented world, from some village where no one had ever taken an X-ray of anything, and so, Sarath said, they would have to work from the world inward. The heel bones, the squatting facets, the singular wear of a singular labor: somewhere there was an occupation that made a body exactly like this one, and where there was such an occupation there was a place, and where there was a place there was a village, and in that village there would be a woman standing at the edge of a field who had been waiting for years for someone to come up the road and tell her what she already knew.

'There's a man,' Sarath said. 'He was my teacher. Palipana. The greatest epigraphist this country ever produced. He could read a stone the way you read a femur. They drove him out of the universities years ago—a scandal, a long story, partly true, mostly not—and now he lives in the forest near Anuradhapura, in the ruins of a leaf monastery, with a girl who looks after him. He's nearly blind. He sees better than anyone I know.'

'And he'll help us?'

'He won't call it help. He'll call it conversation. We'll take the skull.'

She looked at him. Outside, through the rust-holes, the harbor light moved on the steel like water.

'You understand,' she said carefully, 'that if we do this, we do it all the way. To the report. To Geneva. To names, if there are names. I need to know that you understand that, because I can't tell, Sarath. Eleven days, and I can't tell which side of the door you're standing on.'

He was quiet for so long that the generator filled the silence and became a kind of speech.

'When my wife died,' he said at last—and it was the first she had heard of a wife—'I went up-country and did fieldwork for two years. Pots. Beads. The harmless dead. I told myself it was scholarship. It was hiding. Every archaeologist in this country is hiding in the sixth century, did you know that? It's crowded back there.' He smiled, briefly, without anything in it. 'You're asking whether I serve the government. I serve the dead. It happens that for a long time it has been safer not to say so aloud.'

He picked up his spoon and gestured at her untouched plate.

'Eat,' he said. 'Tomorrow we drive north. It's a long road, and after Vavuniya it belongs to no one.'

That night she dreamed of water. She was swimming the two-mile race again, sixteen years old, out beyond the breakers off Mount Lavinia where the sea turned from green to a darker thing, and below her in the dream the water was full of the sleeping and she swam above them stroke after stroke, and they did not reach for her, they only watched her pass, the way the families had watched at the lip of the grave in Guatemala, patient, keeping vigil, certain that sooner or later someone would come down to them through all that blue and gold and say their names.

She woke at five with the harbor still dark and the ship ticking around her as the steel cooled, and she lay there and said it aloud, once, to the cabin, to the country, to whoever it was that listened in such hours.

'Sailor,' she said. 'I'm going to find out who you were.'

In the hold below her, on the table under the switched-off lamp, the bones lay in their patient order, sixty-one fathoms down in the dark of the ship, waiting, like the miner in the song, to be raised again into a life that could be called safe. Blessed be the scaffolding. Blessed be the wheel. Blessed be the chain.

At six, Sarath's horn sounded on the quay.
