The water had been going down all summer, an inch and a half a day at the worst of it, and by the last week of August the river had crawled back into the channel it had kept before anyone thought to stop it. Delia Kessler stood at the foot of the old boat ramp with her field book in one hand and the rover pole in the other and looked out at what the lake had been hiding for sixty years. The ramp itself ended in air. Below it the lakebed spread away in cracked gray plates, and below that, half a mile out where the morning light was still finding its angles, lay the streets of Verbena, Texas, drowned in 1964 and surfacing now like something the water had finally decided it could not digest.

She had driven out from the county seat before dawn, the way she did when a job required her to be alone with it before anyone else arrived to make it ordinary. The gate at the river authority's lot had a new chain and a new sign, NO TRESPASSING, LAKEBED CLOSED, but she had the key and the letter and the title of County Surveyor, Caddell County, which she had held for nineteen years, and which entitled her, among other things, to walk on what other people were forbidden to see. The commissioners wanted a record. Liability, they said. Foundations exposed, wells uncapped, a hazard to the curious and the foolish, and besides, the original plats were old and the lake had moved things, and if the drought ever broke they would lose it all again under a hundred and ten feet of water, so go and map it, Delia, while it's there to be mapped. None of them knew she had been born in it. Or they knew and had decided, with the tact of men who handle road contracts, not to mention it.

The drawdown had left rings on the hills like the rings inside a tree, each one a year the water had stood at that line, written in mineral, in bleached stone and dead mussels. She counted them going down as she walked. Below the lowest ring the ground turned to silt, fine as flour where it was dry, and it took her boots with a sound like stepping on old paper. Mussel shells everywhere, thousands of them, opened and emptied and chalking white. The skeleton of a carp the size of a dog. Killdeer running ahead of her in their stitching, frantic way, crying their own name. The smell was the thing nobody warned you about — the lake bottom had been sixty years without oxygen and it gave that up slowly, a black smell, sulfur and rot and something older than either, and she breathed it for ten minutes and then stopped noticing it, which she supposed was its own kind of mercy.

The first thing of the town she came to was the highway bridge, or the stubs of it, two concrete piers standing up out of the flats with their steel long since salvaged or rusted away. State Highway 71 had crossed the Paloma River there, and her father had driven it every day of his working life, to the gin in Carness and back, and she stood between the piers and took her first shot of the morning, letting the rover settle, watching the screen until the solution fixed. Northing, easting, elevation. She wrote the numbers in the field book in pencil, in the small upright hand she had learned from a man named Otto Brandt, who had been county surveyor before her and had learned it from someone before him, all the way back, she liked to think, to J. T. Ambrose, who had platted Verbena in 1909 and whose field notes she had photocopied from the deed records and carried now in a plastic sleeve in her vest. Beginning at a stone mound at the NW corner of the Calder Survey, Ambrose had written, thence south along the river 42 chains to a live oak 14 in. dia. marked X, a hundred and fifteen years ago, when the town was eleven years old and had a future.

She had learned to measure the world from the men who came to take it. That was the truth she had never said aloud to anyone, not to Otto, not to her husband while she'd had one, not to the commissioners who confirmed her appointment every four years with a unanimity that meant they had stopped seeing her. In the spring of 1961 the men from the river authority had come to Verbena in white shirts with their transits and their level rods, and they had set up in the middle of Main Street as if the street belonged to them, which, in every way that would come to matter, it already did. Delia had been four years old. She had stood at the edge of Hutto's store porch and watched a man bend to the eyepiece of an instrument that looked like a brass and black-enamel insect on three wooden legs, and she had understood nothing of condemnation or eminent domain or acre-feet, but she had understood that the man was looking at her town through a glass eye and writing it down, and that the writing was a kind of power her father did not have. Her father had stood on the same porch with the other men and said the words they all said — they can't, they won't, the land's been Kesslers' since before the war — and the man at the instrument had not even looked up. Three years later the water came. She became a surveyor, she sometimes thought, the way other people married into the family of someone who had wronged them: to be in the room when it happened again. Or to make sure it was at least written down honestly. She had never fully decided which.

Main Street, when she found it, was a difference in texture. The asphalt was gone — lifted by the water decades ago, or eaten — but the base gravel held a line through the silt, paler and firmer than the ground around it, and the foundations confirmed it, coming up on either side in low broken walls the color of teeth. She walked it slowly with the plat unfolded over her arm, matching the world to the drawing. Lot 1, Block 4, R. Hutto — the store. The concrete steps were still there, four of them, going up to nothing, and on the second step a scatter of glass gone faintly purple in whatever sun had reached it through a hundred feet of water, manganese glass, older than the flooding by decades, the broken bottles of men who had loafed on that porch before her father was born. Lot 3, the barber shop, nothing left but the square hole of a cellar with water still standing in it, black and absolutely still, a piece of the lake that had refused to leave. Lot 5, the bank, and there was the vault, which she had been told about and still was not ready for — the building gone entirely, every brick scavenged or scattered, and the vault standing alone on the lakebed like a monument to itself, its steel door open, its concrete walls streaked with rust the color of dried blood. Inside, silt to her ankles and one rotted ledger swollen to the size of a loaf of bread. She shot a corner of it and wrote bank vault, standing, door open, and could not think of anything to add, and that was the morning's first bad moment, the field book's language failing her, because she remembered the vault door being shut and Mr. Pyle in his sleeve garters being important about it, and there was no column in a survey record for that.

She worked the town the way she worked everything, in a closed traverse, so that the errors would have nowhere to hide. Down Main to Depot Street, where the railbed ran straight as a sentence and the depot itself was a rectangle of foundation stones with the iron throat of a stove still seated in its corner. Out Depot to Church Street, where the Methodists' slab held a perfect ghost of its floor plan, the bolt patterns of the pews still visible in rows, and where someone — recently, since the drawdown — had stood a cross made of two pieces of driftwood lashed with fishing line. She did not record the cross. Then up Church to the school, and the school stopped her for a while.

It had been brick, two stories, the grandest thing in town, and the brick had gone wherever brick goes, but the front steps survived, wide enough for the whole student body to be photographed on, which it had been, every spring, and one of those photographs hung in the Caddell County museum with her own face in it, second row, 1963, a small girl squinting, hair in the braids her mother fought into her every morning. Delia climbed the steps. From the top of them you could once see the whole town. From the top of them now she could see the whole town again, and that was the strangeness she had been circling all morning without naming: the drought had not revealed ruins so much as it had revealed the plat itself. With the houses gone and the trees gone — the trees had been cut before the flooding, every one of them, by crews who left the stumps at knee height, and the stumps were all still there, black and hard as iron, a forest of amputations — with everything vertical removed, the town had been reduced to its survey. Lines and corners. Lots and blocks. The drawing Ambrose had made in 1909 was lying at her feet at one-to-one scale, rendered in concrete and silt, and she was walking on it taking its measure all over again, the last surveyor checking the first one's work. The thought should have pleased her. It sat in her chest like a stone.

A little after noon she saw the man. He was a quarter mile off near where the gin had stood, moving slowly, bent, and her first thought was relic hunter, one of the ones the sheriff had been chasing off all month — they came at night mostly, with metal detectors and five-gallon buckets, and pried up door hinges and pump fittings and sold them on the internet as Atlantis of Texas. She walked over to run him off and saw before she got there that he was too old for running off. He was eighty if he was anything, in a straw hat and a pearl-snap shirt buttoned to the throat in the heat, and he was not digging. He was standing at the edge of a concrete apron with two rusted pipe stubs rising out of it, and he had taken his hat off.

"This was Ruff's Texaco," he said, when she was close enough, without turning around. "My daddy's. Them two pipes is where the pumps set." He turned and looked at her without surprise, as if people materialized on the lakebed all the time, which, she supposed, this summer, they did. "You with the river authority?"

"County," she said. "Surveyor. I'm mapping it."

"Mapping it." He looked at the rover pole, at the bright orange vest, at her face, and she watched him do the arithmetic that everyone over seventy in this county eventually did. "You ain't Win Kessler's girl."

"I am."

"Lord God." He put his hat back on, as if the situation now required it. "Delia. You was knee-high. Y'all was out on the Calder road." He pointed with his chin, accurately, toward Calder Street, toward the part of the flats she had been leaving for last without admitting she was leaving it for last. "Toby Ruff," he said. "I was in school with your uncle Frank. I been coming out here every morning since it come up out of the water. I don't know what for. My wife says I'm going to fall in a well and that'll be the end of me, and I told her there's worse ends." He looked down at the apron of his father's filling station, at the silt drifted over it like dirty snow. "You know what I keep thinking? I pumped gas right here the last summer. Sixty-three. Cars lined up down Main, everybody hauling their stuff out, and we pumped gas for every one of them, and then in October the man come and condemned the tanks and that was that. We pumped the gas for our own funeral." He laughed, a dry sound. "What's the county want it mapped for? It's mapped. It was always mapped. That was never the problem."

"Record," she said. "Before the water comes back."

"It's coming back, is it."

"They're saying rain in the headwaters by the weekend. A real system. If it does what they think, this is all under again by spring."

Toby Ruff nodded slowly, looking out across the flats, and she saw that the news did not grieve him, or not simply. "Maybe that's right," he said. "It ain't decent, somehow. Out in the air like this. Like opening a casket." He touched his hat brim to her, an old courtesy. "You give my best to —" and then he stopped, because there was no one left to give his best to, her father thirty years dead and her mother eleven, and they both stood in the wreckage of the sentence for a moment. "Well," he said. "You watch them wells, Delia Kessler." And he walked away north toward the shoreline, slow and upright, putting his feet down carefully on the streets of the town, and she watched him until he was small.

She ate standing up, crackers and a tin of sardines and warm water from the jug, and then she could not put it off any longer and walked out Calder Street to the third lot from the corner.

The house had not burned and had not been moved. Some had been jacked onto trucks and hauled to Carness or Lampasas and set on new foundations, where they stood to this day looking faintly wrong, like transplanted teeth, and some the owners had burned themselves rather than leave for the water, and there had been a fight about that in the Kessler kitchen one night in the spring of 1964, her father's voice low and terrible, her mother's flat with a finality Delia had never heard before: You will not burn my house, Win. You will leave it stand and let the water have it whole. And he had. They had carried out what could be carried — Delia remembered the iris bulbs most of all, her mother on her knees in the front bed digging them with a kitchen fork, wrapping them in damp newspaper like things that could feel — and they had shut the front door, and her father had, absurdly, locked it, and they had driven away. He never came back, never once in thirty years drove the lake road, took the long way through Carness to avoid even seeing the water. Her mother came back exactly once, in 1981, rented a boat from the marina and had a man take her out and idle over the spot, which he found by lining up the water tower with the bluff, and she sat looking down into the green murk for a quarter of an hour and said nothing at all, then or ever, about what she had or hadn't seen.

The foundation was a low rectangle of poured concrete, and the front steps were there, two of them, and the threshold stone her grandfather had set, a single piece of limestone four feet long, hauled from the river bluff in 1912, worn in the middle by feet. The door her father had locked was gone, of course, dissolved, and the lock with it, somewhere in the silt was a brass mechanism still faithfully holding shut a door that no longer existed. She stood on the threshold stone. East room, west room, kitchen ell. The floor plan was entirely legible, the joist piers in their rows. There was the square footprint of the wood stove. There, off the kitchen, the cistern, and its concrete cap was intact, and she stood looking down at the cap for a long time before she let herself kneel.

In the summer of 1959, when she was two, her father had repoured the cistern cap, and her mother had carried her out and her father had taken her hand — she did not remember this; it had been told to her so many times it had grown the colors and weather of memory — and pressed it flat into the wet concrete, and beside it her mother's hand, and beside that his own, and scratched the date beneath with a nail. The silt lay over the cap an inch deep. She swept it with the flat of her hand, gently, the way you'd brush hair from a sleeping child's face, and the gray bloomed up around her wrists and settled, and there they were. Three handprints in a row, the smallest no bigger than a sycamore leaf. Sixty years of water had softened the edges, rounded them the way the river rounds stone, but they were unmistakable, the spread fingers, the deep heel of her father's palm, and the scratched numerals beneath: 7-59. The water had lain on this, she thought. A hundred feet of it, dark and cold and patient, pressing down on the shape of her hand for sixty years, and the shape had pressed back.

She took off her glove and fit her hand over the small print. It did not fit, of course. Her hand had gone on without her, had grown and worked and aged and earned its license and held instruments and pencils and her mother's dying hand in the hospital in Carness, and the print had stayed two years old at the bottom of a lake. Her fingers overlapped its fingers like a mother's over a child's. The concrete was cool under her palm, the only cool thing on the whole burning flat, holding the temperature of the deep water the way stone holds the cold of a church.

She was a long time getting up.

What she did then she did deliberately, in the full knowledge that it was not strictly required by the scope of work. She set the rover point in the center of the small handprint and let it observe for three full minutes, far longer than necessary, until the solution was as tight as the equipment could make it, millimeters, and she wrote the coordinates in the field book. In the description column, where the conventions of her profession required her to state what physical object marked the position, and whether the surveyor had set it or found it already existing, she wrote: Fnd. monument — handprint of a child in concrete cistern cap, approx. 3 in., good condition. Found, not set. That was the entire point, she thought, and the only thing the field book's language had been equal to all day. She had set nothing here. It had been here all along, holding its position under the water with an accuracy no instrument of hers could improve, and her whole life's work, it turned out, had been the slow approach to this one observation: to come back with the glass eye and the steel pole, to be the one looking through the instrument this time, and to find the monument standing, and record it, and certify it true.

The light was going long and yellow when she closed the traverse back at the bridge piers, and the closure was good, eleven hundredths over the whole circuit of the town, well inside tolerance, the errors small and honest and distributed. She packed the rover and stood for a minute looking back across the flats. The killdeer had gone quiet. Far off, the bank vault threw a shadow forty feet long. And to the northwest, over the headwaters, over the country where the Paloma rose, the sky had built itself into something she hadn't seen in two years: a wall of cloud the color of a bruise, flat-bottomed, flickering inside with light, still too far away for thunder but coming, the weathermen right for once, the drought breaking on schedule like a debt called in. By spring the water would lie on the handprints again, on the threshold stone and the school steps and the open vault, and the lake would go back to keeping what it kept. But it would be in the record now. Northing, easting, elevation, on the county datum, in the bound book, in her own upright hand, in the line of hands going back to Ambrose and his live oak marked X — and whatever the water did after this, it would be doing it to a place that had been found, and witnessed, and written down by one of its own.

She walked out the way she had come, up through the rings of the years, and the first wind off the storm reached her at the truck, smelling of rain on ground a hundred miles away, and she stood in it a while with the door open before she drove home.
