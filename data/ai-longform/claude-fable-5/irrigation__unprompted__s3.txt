The water told them first, the way it always did. Frank saw it from the pumphouse a little after seven, the gauge needle slumped down near twenty when it should have stood at forty-five, and by the time Wade came up the lane in his rented sedan, dust rising behind him like something the car was trying to outrun, Frank had already walked the mainline half a mile and found the place where the ground had gone soft. A dark stain spreading across the row middle between two Suncrest peaches, the soil glistening, a thin sheet of water easing up out of the earth as quietly as sweat.

"There it is," Frank said, when Wade had picked his way over. Wade was wearing the boots he kept at the house for visits, boots that had cost more than Frank's and looked it, the leather still holding a shine in the creases. He stood at the edge of the wet ground with his hands on his hips and looked at it the way you'd look at a problem belonging to somebody else.

"How deep's the line through here?"

"Thirty inches, give or take. Dad laid it in eighty-one. We were both on the shovels for that, if you remember."

"I remember," Wade said. "I was twelve. You promised me five dollars to take my half of the trench and never paid it."

"Statute of limitations," Frank said, and went to get the shovels.

It was the second week of June and the fruit was sizing, hard green peaches the size of golf balls hanging in clusters they should have thinned harder a month ago, and the heat was already coming on at eight in the morning, a dry pressing heat that smelled of dust and leaf and the faint sweetness the orchard gave off in summer that Frank had stopped being able to smell years ago except when he'd been away, which was almost never. He killed the pump on his way past, and the silence that followed had a weight to it. Without the pump running, the place sounded like what it was. Birds. A dog barking down at the Kalbachs'. Nothing else moving on thirty-eight acres.

They dug. The first foot came up easy, the wet soil parting like cake, and then they hit the harder clay underneath and the work slowed to the old rhythm, bite and lift and throw, bite and lift and throw, the two of them facing each other across the lengthening hole the way they'd faced each other across a thousand jobs in this dirt. Wade had kept himself in shape, Frank would give him that. He was forty-nine and still flat through the middle, the gym kind of fit, and he moved the shovel well enough, though Frank could see him favoring his hands, which had gone soft. By the second foot Wade had blisters starting and didn't mention them, and Frank didn't either, because mentioning them would have been a kind of point scored and they were both saving their points for later. They both knew later was coming. Wade hadn't flown in from Denver in June, taken three days off, to help dig a hole.

"There she is," Frank said. The shovel had rung against the pipe, a dull plastic knock, and he got down on his knees and worked with his hands, scooping the mud out, until the line lay exposed in the bottom of the trench, the white PVC gone the color of old bone, and there at the joint of his guess was the wound: a crack running eight, ten inches along the top of the pipe, and beneath it, wrapped around the line like a fist, a root as thick as Frank's forearm.

"Cottonwood," Wade said.

"Dad's cottonwood." The tree stood eighty feet off at the head of the lane, planted the year their mother came to the place as a bride, which made it sixty-one years old, and its roots had been finding water all that time, patient as anything alive, and one of them had found the line and laid itself alongside and slowly, over years, over decades maybe, squeezed until the pipe gave. "Hundred and fifty feet from the trunk," Frank said. "They'll go three hundred for water. Further."

"You'd think he'd have known better than to run the line this close."

"He knew. He didn't have anywhere else to run it. The lane's the lane." Frank sat back on his heels in the mud. "Everything on this place is a compromise somebody made fifty years ago. You live inside other people's compromises. That's what this is."

"That's a hell of a way to talk about it."

"It's a hell of a way to live," Frank said, and stood up, his knees complaining, and went to measure the break.

They would need a four-foot section of pipe and two couplings, and Frank had pipe in the shed but only one coupling, and so there was the question of the drive to town, eleven miles to the irrigation supply in Palisade, and Wade said he'd go, and Frank said no, they'd both stay, he had a coupling that would work if he cleaned it up, an old one off a junction they'd abandoned when they pulled the Bartletts out of the east block. Wade looked at him a moment.

"You're going to fix a sixty-thousand-dollar problem with parts out of a coffee can."

"It's a forty-dollar problem."

"You know what I mean."

"I know what you mean," Frank said. "You've been meaning it since you got off the plane. You might as well say it while we wait on the trench to drain."

But Wade didn't say it then. He sat down on the spoil pile with his ruined hands open on his knees and looked down the row, and Frank followed his look, and they sat there together regarding the trees. The Suncrests were forty years old and showing it, gummosis on half the trunks, dead wood in the tops they hadn't had the labor to prune out, and beyond them the younger block of Cresthavens that Frank had planted nine years ago with money he was still paying back, and beyond that the empty ground where the frost-killed block had been pushed out and burned, two acres of bare dirt with the disc harrow sitting in the middle of it like something abandoned in a retreat. The light was getting white and hard. Down in the trench the water seeped and pooled.

"Larkspur House called me Tuesday," Wade said finally. "Not you. Me. Because I'm the one whose checks clear on the first."

"My checks clear."

"Your checks clear on the eleventh. The twelfth. Mom's care is fifty-eight hundred a month, Frank, and it's going up in September, they told me, eight percent, and that's if she doesn't move to the memory floor, which she will, you know she will, you saw her at Christmas. The memory floor is seventy-two."

"I know what the memory floor costs."

"Then do the arithmetic with me. Just once, out loud, the two of us. The place grossed what last year?"

"You know what it grossed. You do my taxes."

"It grossed ninety-one thousand dollars, and it cost a hundred and six to run, and that's with you paying yourself nothing, drawing nothing, living like a hired man in your own father's house. And the year before that the frost took everything and the crop insurance covered the spray bill and not one thing more. You're not farming, Frank. You're conducting a funeral that's lasted eleven years."

Frank got down into the trench. The water had drained off enough to work, and he took the hacksaw and set it against the pipe a foot back from the end of the crack and began to cut, and the saw made its small patient sound, and he let it answer for him for a while. The root he would cut too, cut it out in a section so it couldn't find the new pipe for a few years, though it would find it eventually, roots always did, you couldn't keep water secret from a tree. The smell down in the trench was the smell of the orchard's insides, cold mineral mud and broken root, and it was a smell he knew the way he knew his own hands, and he thought, not for the first time, that there was no way to say any of this to Wade without sounding like a calendar in a feed store. The land is a trust. The land is who we are. All of it true and all of it worn smooth as a creek stone and worth nothing in an argument.

"Hold the end," he said instead, and Wade got down into the trench across from him, into the mud, good boots and all, and held the pipe steady while Frank finished the cut.

They cut the other end, and lifted out the broken section with the root still clenched around it, and Wade turned it in his hands. "Look at that," he said, quieter. The root had grown to the shape of the pipe, flattened itself along the curve, and where it crossed the crack a fine white root hair had actually entered the pipe, threaded itself in through the split and feathered out inside in a pale fringe, drinking from the line directly. "It was inside. It was drinking out of the main."

"They'll do that."

"It's almost—" Wade stopped. He set the section down on the grass at the edge of the trench, carefully, the way you'd set down something that was still alive, and maybe it was. "Hayden Creek Partners called me again," he said. "Last week. They've gone to one point three."

"For the water."

"For the place. The ground, the trees, the house if you want them to take it, though you could carve out the house and two acres, I asked. But yes. It's the water. It's the ditch shares. You know what it is."

"They'll pull every tree on it inside a year. They're not farming peaches. They're parking water for the Front Range and they'll dryland the ground and lease it for grazing and in twenty years it'll be cheatgrass and a pivot point on somebody's spreadsheet in Connecticut."

"In twenty years," Wade said, "you'll be seventy-four."

Frank climbed out of the trench and went to the shed for the pipe and the couplings and the primer and the cement, and the walk took four minutes each way and he was grateful for all eight of them. The shed was their father's shed still, would always be, the workbench black with sixty years of oil, the coffee cans of fittings and bolts and the wooden bins Earl had built with little brass label frames, and the labels still in his hand, that hard upright hand like fence posts, ¾ COUPLER, GATE PARTS, MISC. Frank found the four-inch coupling in MISC where he'd known it would be, and stood a moment in the dim heat with it in his hand. There was a way in which Wade was right about the funeral. Frank knew it the way you know a thing at three in the morning and refuse to know it at noon. Their father had died in the east block on a ladder in September with a picking bag a third full of Bartletts on his chest, and they'd found him in the grass with the pears spilled around him, and Frank had finished the harvest that fall because somebody had to, and then he had pruned that winter because the trees needed pruning, and there had never afterward been a day when the next thing didn't need doing, and eleven years had gone by like one long held breath. He had been forty-three. He had been, briefly, married. He carried the fittings back up the lane.

Wade had cleaned and dried the cut ends of the pipe with his shirttail and was sitting in the shade of the nearer Suncrest with his back against the trunk, and Frank saw that he had been crying, or near it, and that he had arranged himself so it wouldn't show and it showed anyway, the way it always had, since he was a kid, the redness at the rims of his eyes that he could never govern. Neither of them said anything about it. Frank cut the new section to length and they dry-fit everything, and it went together true, and then there was nothing to do until they glued it but wait for noon to come and go, because their father had taught them never to glue PVC in the heat of the day if they could help it, the cement flashed off too fast, and though it probably made no difference at all, neither of them was going to be the one to break the rule.

So they ate. Frank had made sandwiches at six that morning the way their mother used to, ham and butter and white bread, no embellishment, and he'd put them in the same green Coleman cooler that had gone to the field for fifty years, the hinge mended with wire, and Wade laughed when he saw it, a real laugh, the first of the day.

"God. The cooler."

"It still works."

"Everything here still works," Wade said. "That's the trouble. Nothing here is ever broken enough to throw away. It just keeps almost working, forever, and it eats you." But he took his sandwich, and they ate in the shade with their backs to the tree, shoulder to shoulder because the shade was narrow at noon, and the heat lay on the orchard like a hand, and the cicadas had started, that long electric seam of sound that ran through every summer of their lives, and somewhere under it the silence of the unrunning pump.

"You remember the flood year," Wade said. "Eighty-three or four. When the ditch broke up at the Hartmans' and came down the lane."

"Eighty-four."

"And Dad got us up in the dark and we were out here with shovels building a berm in our pajama bottoms, and Mom brought coffee out to us at like four in the morning, in her robe, and you were—you must have been fifteen. And I remember the water coming down the lane in the dark, you could hear it before you could see it, and I was scared to death, and Dad wasn't scared at all, he was almost happy, and I couldn't understand that. I used to think about it after I moved away. He was happy because the water was here and the problem was here and his boys were here and there wasn't anywhere else anything was happening. The whole world was forty acres. I thought it was a kind of greatness." Wade folded the wax paper from his sandwich into a small square, creasing it with his thumbnail, an old habit, exact. "And then I thought it was a kind of blindness. And now I don't know what I think, except that he's dead, and it didn't save him, and it isn't saving you."

"I don't need saving."

"You're a hundred and ten thousand in debt to the production credit association, your trees are dying faster than you can replace them, you can't hire a crew because you can't pay a crew, and you spent last winter alone in that house. Renata called me, you know. In February. You didn't know that. She called to ask if you were all right, because you wouldn't tell her, and I had to say I didn't know, because you wouldn't tell me either."

Frank looked down the row. From where they sat the rows ran west and you could see the way the land tilted toward the river, the gift of the whole place, the reason his grandfather had bought it in 1948, fall of cold air off the mesa rolling down through the trees on frost nights and on out to the river, sliding away, most years, before it could kill. Most years. He could see the heat standing over the river bottom now, a silver shaking in the air, and the bluffs beyond gone pale as paper.

"What I know," Frank said slowly, "is that if I sign that paper, it's not a sale. It's an extinction. Hayden Creek doesn't buy farms, they buy the death of farms. The water goes in a pipe over the divide and waters lawns in Aurora, and this"—he moved his hand, taking in the rows, the heavy green, the hard small fruit by the thousands overhead—"this never exists again. Not here, not anywhere. You can't get it back. There's no number where you can get it back. And I know I'm the one who's broke and you're the one with the spreadsheet, and I know how I sound. I sound like Dad. But somebody has to be the one who says it out loud: a thing that took a hundred years to make and can never be made again is worth more than one point three million dollars, even if no one on earth will pay more, even if I'm the only one left who thinks so."

"And Mom?" Wade said. It came out quiet, almost gentle, which made it worse. "Is it worth more than her? Because that's the actual question, Frank. Not lawns in Aurora. The memory floor at Larkspur is the question. Her last years are the question. She gave this place her whole life, and now the place can give her something back, or it can stand here being sacred while she runs out of money in a shared room. Dad's dead. The land doesn't know it's the land. She knows when she's cold. She knows when nobody comes."

The cicadas filled up the space where an answer should have gone. Frank found he was looking at his own hands, the split nails, the cracked thumb he'd been meaning to tape, and he thought of his mother at Christmas, how she had looked out the window of the common room at the parking lot and said, in a voice of perfect contentment, that the Cresthavens were sizing nicely this year, and how he hadn't corrected her, and how Wade had gone out to the rental car and sat in it for ten minutes. He thought: there is no arrangement of this in which I am not betraying someone. That was the thing nobody told you about land, about family, about staying. They told you about hard work. Nobody told you that the day would come when every road out of the orchard ran through somebody you loved.

"Glue's probably fine by now," he said, and got up.

They worked without talking. Frank primed the pipe ends and the couplings, the purple primer staining his fingers, the sharp solvent smell cutting through the green smell of the orchard, and then the cement, and they made the joints fast, a quarter turn and hold, his hands and Wade's hands holding the line steady in the bottom of the trench, both of them braced and motionless in the mud for the long count their father had taught them, longer than the can said, because the can didn't know about your water and your pressure and your particular luck. The new white section shone between the bone-colored old runs like a graft. Like a splice in a film, Frank thought, where the picture jumps and the people are suddenly older.

They let it cure the full two hours, longer than they needed, and spent the time backfilling around the line by hand, tamping the soil in under the pipe so it would lie supported, doing it right, doing it the slow way, and the afternoon went long and gold around them, and at some point without either of them deciding to, they stopped arguing, not because anything was settled but because the work didn't have room for it. Near five Wade carried the cut-out section with its strangler root up to the shed and set it on the workbench, and when Frank asked him why, he said he didn't know, he just didn't want it on the burn pile, and Frank let that be.

Then Frank walked down to the pumphouse and threw the switch, and the pump took up its old note, and he walked back up the lane slow, listening, the way you'd listen to a heart. The line knocked once, twice, air working through, and held. At the trench Wade stood watching the joint, and it stayed dry, and the gauge, when Frank checked it, stood at forty-six, a pound better than it had run in years, and all down the rows the micro-sprinklers came up one after another, far off and then nearer, each one a small hiss opening into a circle of falling water, hundreds of them, the whole orchard beginning to whisper at once, and the dry ground darkening under the trees in spreading rings, and the smell rising, that smell of rain on dust that has a name Frank could never remember, the oldest smell there is.

Wade stood in the lane with his ruined hands at his sides, his blisters open, mud to his knees, and watched the water reach the trees.

"I'm not signing anything in June," Frank said. "Let me get this crop off. Let me get one decent crop off, and we'll sit down in October, you and me, with the real numbers, and Mom's numbers, all of it on one table. And I'll listen. I'm telling you I'll listen. But not in June. Not with the fruit on the trees."

Wade was quiet for a long moment. The light was coming level through the rows now, catching the water as it fell so that every tree stood in its own small weather, its own brief shining rain, and the cottonwood at the head of the lane turned its leaves over silver in the first of the evening breeze, the tree that had broken the line drinking now, openly, lawfully, with everything else.

"October," Wade said. "And Frank. I'll come back for the harvest. Two weeks. I've got the time saved up. I'm not saying it changes anything."

"I know it doesn't," Frank said, and the two of them stood there in the lane while the water went where it was needed, neither of them moving to go in, the way you'll stand at the edge of a thing you've mended, not because it needs you any longer but because for one more minute you can't quite believe it holds.
