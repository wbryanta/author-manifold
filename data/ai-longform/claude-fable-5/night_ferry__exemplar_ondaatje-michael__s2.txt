All night the ferry moved north against the tide. Niels Holt stood at the rail on the upper deck with his coat buttoned to the throat, watching the lights of fish camps slide past on the far shore, each one a small yellow wound in the darkness. The strait was calm. Below him the wake unfolded and unfolded, pale green over black, and he thought how water at night gives up nothing, how you could drop a man into it and the surface would close over him like a sentence finished.

The call had come two days earlier, to the shop in Victoria where he worked among the pianos. A constable named Beaudry, phoning from a town four hundred miles up the coast, his voice careful, rehearsed, the voice of a man who had made such calls before and knew the silences they opened. A body had come up in a prawn fisherman's line off the Milne Banks. A man's body, nine days in the water, perhaps longer. There was a float house registered to an Anton Holt, and in the float house a seaman's card, and on the card the name of a brother in Victoria, written years ago in Anton's narrow hand, as if even then he had been arranging for this, leaving a thread that someone could follow back.

– We need someone who knew him, Beaudry had said. To be certain.

Niels had not spoken to his brother in eleven years. He had been kneeling inside an old Heintzman upright when the shop owner brought him the phone, and all through the conversation he kept one hand flat against the soundboard, the way you might rest a hand on the flank of a sleeping animal. Afterwards he sat on the piano bench for a long time. Then he finished the tuning, because the instrument was open and a piano left open overnight drifts, loses itself, and he could not bear the thought of leaving anything unresolved in that room.

Now he took the tuning fork from his coat pocket and struck it against his knee and held it up into the wind. A 440. The note bloomed and was taken instantly, swallowed by the engines and the dark water, and he thought, that is what we sound like. That is the size of a life against all this.

Anton had taught him to listen. This was the thing nobody knew about his brother, who in the family stories was always the wild one, the one who fought in the parking lots of dance halls up and down the Island, who lost a deckhand's wage in a single night of cards at Alert Bay. Anton at sixteen holding a long screwdriver against the block of their father's diesel, the handle pressed to the bone behind his ear. Listen, Niels. Like this. You can hear the injector that's failing. You can hear which cylinder is lonely. And Niels, eleven years old, taking the screwdriver and pressing it to his own skull and hearing for the first time the interior of a machine, its knocking heart, its grievances. He became a tuner of pianos because of that afternoon in the boat shed, though he had never told anyone this, least of all Anton. Some debts you keep hidden even from the creditor.

Their father, Mathias, had built the shed himself on the tideflats at Cowichan Bay, and built the boat inside it, a forty-two-foot troller he named the Hekla after a mountain in a country he had never seen. The smell of that shed — oakum and creosote, linseed, the copper stink of bottom paint — was the smell of his entire childhood, and herring scales on every surface like spilled sequins, and the two boys moving around their silent father, learning the grammar of his hands because he gave them little else. When Niels was nine a hook went through his left palm, a big stainless salmon hook, in past the barb, and he screamed, and it was Anton who held his wrist down against the gunwale, Anton who was thirteen, and said, you can't pull it back, the barb won't let you, you have to push it through. Their father came with the cutters and pushed the hook on through the meat of the hand until the barb broke the skin on the far side, and snipped it, and drew the shank back out clean, and all the while Anton kept talking into Niels's ear, a low stream of nonsense, the names of boats in the harbour, the names of stars, so that the pain had something to braid itself into. The scar was still there, a pale comma in the palm, and on the ferry deck Niels opened his hand and looked at it under the deck lights as if it were a ticket he would be asked to produce.

He went inside around two in the morning because the cold had finally got past the coat. The cafeteria was nearly empty, lit like an aquarium, a few loggers asleep upright in the booths with their boots on the seats opposite. A woman sat alone at a table by the window with a pot of tea and a paperback she was not reading. She was perhaps sixty, grey hair cropped close, a face that had been weathered into kindness or had started out that way. When he sat two tables off she looked over at him without embarrassment, the way people look at each other at that hour, when the usual fences are down.

– You were out on deck a long time, she said. I could see you from here.

– I don't sleep well on boats.

– Nobody does. They just pretend.

Her name was Marian. She was a midwife, going up to Kelse Harbour where her daughter was expecting, though she said she would not deliver the child herself. Bad luck, she said, to catch your own blood. She had worked thirty years out of the small coast hospitals, flown into camps in float planes, and once delivered a child on a seine boat in a March gale off Cape Caution, the mother braced in the fo'c'sle bunk, the skipper holding the boat's head into the sea for six hours while below him his granddaughter arrived in the world. She told this without flourish, breaking a biscuit into her tea. Niels listened the way he listened to pianos, for what was underneath.

– And you, she said. What takes you north?

– My brother.

– A visit, then.

– A funeral, he said. Maybe. I won't know until I get there.

She did not ask him to explain. She poured tea into the spare cup that had come with her pot and slid it across the gap between the tables, and they sat with the black windows beside them, the strait invisible, present only as motion, a long slow breathing under the floor.

– Hands, she said after a while. That's how I'd know anyone. I've held a thousand newborns and no two of them had the same hands. People think faces. Faces lie all the time. Hands can't.

He turned his own over on the tabletop, palm up, and she glanced down at the scar and back up at him, and said nothing, and he was grateful for that, for her whole way of being in the world, which was to attend without seizing.

I was twenty-three the night of our mother's funeral, and it had rained all day, the kind of rain that makes the coast feel like the inside of a bell. We buried her above Cowichan Bay and came back to the house and the relatives ate and left, and around midnight I found Anton down at the shed, drunk, loading gear aboard the Hekla by the light of a trouble lamp. Our father had been dead three years by then and the boat had sat on its lines doing nothing, and I had been paying the moorage out of my apprentice's wage, and there was Anton with his duffel and a case of rye, casting off the springlines of a boat he had not once come south to look at. I stood on the dock in the rain and I said the thing I said. I have turned it over for eleven years the way you turn a stone in your pocket until your thumb has worn a hollow in it. I said, he died ashamed of you, and the rain came down between us, and Anton stood in the lamplight with a coil of line in his hand and looked at me for a long moment, and I saw that I had got past the barb, that there was no pulling it back. He didn't answer. That was the punishment, that he didn't answer. He went into the wheelhouse and started the engine, and I stood there listening to the old diesel, and even then, even in my fury, some part of me was listening the way he had taught me, hearing the loneliness in the third cylinder, and I thought, he'll fix that off Nanaimo, he'll hear it too. The Hekla went out past the breakwater with no running lights and I never saw my brother again. He sent no address. Twice in eleven years a card at Christmas with no return on the envelope, postmarked from towns I had to find on a chart. I told people he had gone north, the way you'd say a man had gone into weather, and they nodded, because on this coast that is a complete sentence.

The ferry came into Kelse Harbour a little after eight in the morning, in a low silver light, the town arranged along the shore the way coast towns are, as if the buildings had been left there by a high tide. Marian touched his sleeve as they queued for the ramp.

– Whichever way it goes, she said, eat something today. People forget.

Beaudry was waiting at the foot of the dock beside a white truck, a heavy man, younger than his telephone voice, with the open, slightly wincing face of someone doing a job his body objected to. He shook Niels's hand and took his bag despite the protest and put it in the truck bed, and they drove up through the town past the netlofts and the closed cannery and a hotel with a cormorant painted on its blind side, and Beaudry talked, gently, steadily, the talk a kind of handrail he was holding out.

– Your brother kept to himself out there. Float house in Stannard Inlet, an hour by water. He ran a few prawn traps, did engine work for the camps. People liked him, the ones that knew him. He fixed the school generator one winter for nothing.

– That sounds like him, Niels said, and was surprised to find it was true, that he could still vouch for the shape of a man across eleven years, the way you can recognize a piece of music from three notes through a wall.

– I have to tell you what to expect, Beaudry said, slowing the truck. Nine days in salt water. It isn't a face anymore, not really. We don't ask you to look at the face. There are other ways. Hands, marks, old breaks. Anything you can give us.

The hospital was a low green building above the harbour, and they went in through the back, down a corridor that smelled of disinfectant laid over something older, and into a small cold room with a steel table and a window of pebbled glass that let in light but no world. A young attendant stood by the wall with his hands folded in front of him like a boy at church. The body lay under a blue sheet. Niels was aware of his own breathing, of the tuning fork in his pocket against his thigh, of the absurd persistence of objects.

– Take whatever time, Beaudry said.

They folded the sheet back from the left arm first. The hand lay palm up on the steel, and the sea had been at it, the skin loose and pale as something proofing, but it was a hand still, a working man's hand, the nails broken in the old familiar way, grease driven so deep into the creases that nine days of ocean had not lifted it. Engine work for the camps. Niels leaned close. He was looking for the comma — and then stopped, because the comma was his own, his own palm, he had confused the bodies, brother and brother, the way they had been confused all their lives, Holt boys, which one are you. Anton's hands had their own history. The top joint of the left index finger gone, taken by a gurdy when he was nineteen, Anton coming up from the dock with the hand wrapped in his shirt, white-faced, joking. Niels looked. The finger was whole.

Something in him pitched, a sickening lift, hope or its opposite, he could not have said which.

– The other hand, he said.

They came around the table. The right hand. And there it was — not the finger, the finger was whole on this side too, but he had remembered it wrong, he saw now he had remembered it wrong all these years, or the body was a stranger's, and for a moment the room tilted between those two possibilities, his memory and this man, and he understood that he had crossed a strait in the night to stand at the exact place where they could not both be true.

– There were things found with him, Beaudry said quietly, watching his face. In an oilskin pouch, inside the jacket. Sometimes that's easier.

The attendant brought a small steel tray. A folding knife with a haft of yellowed bone, and on the bolster, worn nearly away, the initials M.H. Niels picked it up without asking. His thumb found the nail nick and opened it, and the blade came out stiff with salt, and he saw his father's hand opening this knife a thousand times, in the shed, on the boat, at the kitchen table cutting twine, and he saw that Anton had carried it for eleven years, this object, north into his exile, the father he was supposed to have shamed riding all that time against his heart.

And a photograph, water-fattened, the emulsion lifting in places like skin. Two boys on a dock. The print had gone soft and pale, the faces dissolving into the silver, and it could have been any two boys on any dock on this coast, one taller, one squinting, the masts behind them. He knew the dock. He knew the day. He could not have proven any of it to a court.

– The collarbone, Niels said. His voice came out strange. He broke his collarbone when he was fourteen. Fell off the shed roof. It healed with a lump. You could feel it through his shirt. He used to make me feel it. He called it his barnacle.

Beaudry nodded at the attendant, and the sheet was drawn down from the shoulder, the face kept covered, a mercy arranged with a towel, and Niels did what he had not imagined himself doing, he put his hand on his brother's shoulder, on the cold of it, which was not like any cold he had touched before, not ice, not stone, a cold with no other side to it, and he ran his thumb along the line of the clavicle, and felt, midway, the old healed knot, the barnacle, the ridge of bone that had set crooked thirty-five years ago while their mother held a bag of frozen peas against it and Anton grinned through his tears and said it didn't hurt, it never hurt, nothing ever hurt him.

It might be there in half the men on this coast, he thought. Men fall. Bones knit how they knit. He stood with his hand on the shoulder a long time. The light through the pebbled glass moved very slightly, a cloud somewhere out in the morning, and he thought of Marian saying hands can't lie, and he thought, but memory can, memory does nothing else, it is a tide working at every shore it touches. The finger he had wrong. The knife was the knife. The photograph was two boys.

– Is it your brother? Beaudry asked, in the formal voice now, the one that went into reports.

What he understood, in that cold room, was that the question was not entirely about the body. The question was whether Anton Holt would be claimed or would go into the ground as a finding, a file, an unattended death, his float house auctioned, the knife into an envelope in an evidence room. Eleven years ago Niels had stood on a dock in the rain and unclaimed him with one sentence. Here was the sentence offered back, reversed, the barb cut, asking to be drawn through.

– Yes, he said. That's Anton. That's my brother.

He signed where they showed him. He asked for the knife and the photograph and they gave them to him in a paper bag, with apologies for the bag, and he declined to see the float house, and then changed his mind on the hospital steps and asked Beaudry if a boat could take him out, and Beaudry said the fisheries officer was going up the inlet at noon and would carry him.

Stannard Inlet was an hour north through water the colour of pewter, steep dark timber coming straight down into it on both sides, waterfalls hanging in the green like pulled threads. The float house rode on its raft of cedar logs in a small bight, weathered grey, a stovepipe, a sawhorse, prawn traps stacked in lazy towers. Tied alongside, low and patient on her lines, was the Hekla. He had not let himself expect her. Her paint was gone to chalk and her name was barely a ghost on the bow, but she sat the water the way his father had built her to sit, and Niels stood on the float and put his hand flat on her planking the way he laid a hand on a soundboard, and waited, as if something might come through.

Inside the house was the order of a solitary man. Tools racked by size. A bunk made up tight. On the table a coil of leader, a coffee cup rinsed and turned upside down, and a child's exercise book in which Anton had kept his accounts in pencil, traps and pounds and prices, and on the last written page, below the figures, undated, three words and an unfinished fourth, Tell Niels I — and then nothing. The pencil lay in the gutter of the book. A man writes three words and the kettle boils, or a boat comes into the bight, or the light changes on the water and he goes out to look at it, intending to come back. Niels stood holding the book. Tell Niels I. He read it as a sentence completed by its own breaking off, the way a suspended chord can be more final than the tonic, and he closed the book and put it inside his coat with the knife and the photograph.

On the float, in the last of the afternoon, he took out the tuning fork and struck it against the Hekla's cap rail and set the stem of it against the old wood, and bent his head, and listened. The note went down into the hull, into the timbers his father had steamed and bent in the shed at Cowichan Bay, and came back changed, darkened, carried, the boat answering with its whole interior the way a body answers, and he stayed bent like that until the sound had gone entirely out of the metal and into the world.

He thought of the summer he was eight, before any of it, when Anton taught him to swim off the float at the bay, the water so cold it took the language out of you, and how he had panicked, gone vertical, clawing, and his brother's arms had come around his chest from behind, and Anton's voice was at his ear, unhurried, amused, the voice that later talked him through the hook, through everything. I have you, he said. Lie back. Look at the sky. I have you. And Niels had lain back against him in the freezing water and the sky was up there, enormous, moving, and he understood for the first time that you could be held and adrift at once, that these were not opposites, that they might in fact be the same thing.

The fisheries boat sounded its horn at the mouth of the bight. He cast a long look at the Hekla, which he would come back for, he knew this now, he would come north again in the spring and take her south himself, alone, listening to her engine through a screwdriver the whole way down the coast. Then he stepped aboard the other boat and they swung out into the inlet, and behind them the float house turned slowly on its lines, and the water closed over their wake, giving up nothing, holding everything, the way it had always done.
