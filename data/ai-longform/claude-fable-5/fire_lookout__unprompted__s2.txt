The smoke was the color of a healed scar when she first saw it, a pale seam against the timber, and Kit stood at the east windows of the tower for a full minute without moving, the coffee going cold in her hand, telling herself it was dust from the quarry road or a trick of morning light in the cottonwoods along Slate Creek. The lookout had taught her this much over eleven summers: that the eye wants smoke, that the eye will make smoke out of pollen and fog and the exhalations of warm rock after rain, and that the discipline of the job — even after the job had ceased to exist, even after the Forest Service letter with its language about structural deficiency and program consolidation — the discipline was to wait, and to look again, and to let the thing declare itself.

It declared itself. The seam thickened at its base and began to lean, a soft gray column with a darker heart, rising out of the drainage three air miles east and a thousand feet below, and Kit set the coffee down on the fire finder's map table without looking and put the binoculars to her eyes and felt the morning go strange around her, because she knew that drainage. She knew it the way you know the inside of your own mouth. Slate Creek came down out of the cirque under Bonner Peak and ran six miles through lodgepole and old burn before it hit the river road, and two-thirds of the way down, on a bench above the creek where their father had filed on five acres in 1971, sat the cabin where her sister Frankie had lived alone for the last three years, since their mother died, since the funeral, since the last words the two of them had ever spoken to each other, which had been Frankie's, in the gravel lot of the Lutheran church, and which had been: Then don't.

The smoke was coming from the bench. Or near it. From this height and angle she could not see the cabin itself — a shoulder of timbered ridge hid it, had always hidden it, all these summers she had sat in her glass room and looked east in the evenings at the fold in the land where her sister was and was not visible — but she could see the smoke standing up out of exactly that fold, and she did what eleven summers had trained her to do even though no one was paying her to do it anymore. She bent to the Osborne fire finder, the old brass instrument the district had never bothered to remove when they pulled her radio call sign and her pay, and she swung the sighting ring and laid the crosshair on the base of the column and read the azimuth off the graduated circle. One hundred two degrees, thirty minutes. She pulled the topographic map from under the glass and walked the bearing out from the tower's surveyed point with the edge of an envelope because she could not find the ruler, and the line crossed Slate Creek at the bench. At the bench. Not above it, not below it.

She straightened up and stood very still in the center of the fourteen-by-fourteen room, in the cube of light she had lived in longer than she had lived anywhere as an adult, and she listened to her own heart and to the flag line ticking against the steel of the tower in the morning wind, which was out of the west at maybe eight miles an hour, which meant the fire, if it ran, would run up the drainage and away from the river road, up through three-year drought timber toward the cirque, and that anything on the bench would have only the creek between itself and the head of it.

The radio had been dead for nineteen days.

It had died the way old things die, without drama, the receive light dimming over a week of evenings until one night the district's repeater traffic — other people's weather, other people's lightning maps, the comfortable distant gossip of a fire season she was no longer part of — simply did not come, and she had taken the unit apart on the table by lantern light and found the corrosion on the battery terminals and cleaned it and put it back together and gotten nothing, and she had felt, that night, a thing she was ashamed of now, standing here with smoke in her windows. She had felt relieved. The silence had seemed like a gift, the last summer sealed at last into pure privacy, no voices, no obligation to listen, nothing between her and the weather and the long slow inventory of light she had come up here intending to make of her final season. She had not driven the nineteen miles to town for a replacement. She had told herself she would. She had not.

There was no cell signal on the mountain. There never had been; you could sometimes get a bar on the catwalk in a north wind if you held the phone above your head like an offering, and she went out onto the catwalk now and held the phone above her head and turned in a circle and got nothing, the little icon with its slash, and the smoke to the east had doubled while she was inside, the column now leaning definitely upslope, and at its base she could see — or the eye wanted her to see — a flicker of orange in the green, like a struck match held behind a screen door.

The road down was eight miles. Not a road, properly: the first two miles a foot trail switchbacking off the summit cone through whitebark and scree, then the abandoned lookout supply road, two-track gone to alder and washout, that came out finally on the river road at the bottom, where there was a Forest Service gate and beyond it, four more miles of pavement to the junction where Slate Creek Road went up. Her truck was parked at the gate, at the bottom of the eight miles, because the supply road had been impassable to vehicles since the flood year, which was the same reason her groceries came up on her back, which was one of the reasons the district had decommissioned the tower, and she stood on the catwalk doing the arithmetic of it. Two and a half hours down if she pushed. Then the truck, then the drive around the toe of the ridge to the mouth of Slate Creek, twenty minutes, then the creek road, if the creek road was open, if the fire had not crossed it. Three hours, call it, between her and the bench. Three hours between her and whatever was already happening there in real time, in flame time, which is a different time, which does not wait.

Or she could stay, which was the trained thing, the lookout's thing — the lookout does not leave the lookout, the lookout reports — except there was no one to report to and no instrument to report with, and the whole apparatus of her usefulness, she understood, standing there with her hands on the catwalk rail, had quietly been dismantled around her over the years while she sat still at the center of it, until what remained was a woman alone in a glass box watching her sister's drainage burn.

She went inside and packed in four minutes. Water, the first aid kit, the folding shovel from under the bed, gloves, the bandana, the headlamp because you always took the headlamp, two of the protein bars she'd been rationing. At the door she stopped and looked back once at the room — the narrow bed, the propane stove, the shelf of swollen paperbacks, the fire finder gleaming in its circle of map — and she had imagined this moment all summer, the last leaving, end of September, the click of the padlock, some ceremony of farewell she'd half-written in her head, and instead it was this: 8:40 on an August morning, the door banging behind her, her boots loud on the steel stairs, forty feet of switchbacking grate-metal down through the cold morning air, and the smoke standing in the east like a raised hand.

She thought about Frankie on the scree. You could not think about footing on the scree, the body did the footing, and so the mind went where it went, and it went to Frankie at nineteen, brown as a hazelnut, standing hip-deep in Slate Creek with a fly rod, laughing at something, the laugh carrying up to the cabin porch where Kit sat with a book she wasn't reading. It went to Frankie at forty-six in the church parking lot with their mother three days in the ground, Frankie's face closed like a fist, saying You watched it from up there. You watched the whole thing from up there with your binoculars, and Kit saying that wasn't fair, that she had come down, she had come down every two weeks all that last summer, and Frankie saying, Every two weeks. She asked for you every day. And Kit, because she was tired, because grief had made her cruel or honest or both, saying She asked for a lot of people. She asked for Dad. She didn't know who was in the room, Frankie — and Frankie's face when she said it, and then the thing Kit had said next, which she had spent three years not repeating to herself and which she did not repeat to herself now, on the scree, with the smoke filling more of the eastern sky every time she lifted her eyes — something about Frankie needing their mother's dying, needing it the way some people need a war, and Frankie had looked at her for a long moment and said, Then don't. Don't come down. Stay up there where everything's three miles away. And Kit had stayed up there. Three more summers she had stayed up there, and in the winters she had stayed in town and not written, and the one letter Frankie had sent — a single page, two Decembers ago, Kit had recognized the handwriting through the envelope and put it in the drawer under the maps and not opened it, told herself she would open it when she was ready, and it was in the drawer still, eight miles above her now and rising, unopened in a tower she might never climb again.

The trail dropped off the summit cone into the whitebark and the air changed, took on the resin smell of the living forest, and below the whitebark the lodgepole began, and she could no longer see the smoke, which was worse. In the tower the fire had been information, azimuth and color and rate of spread, a thing held at the focal distance the job was built on. Down in the trees it became what fire is to an animal: a smell she kept thinking she caught and couldn't be sure of, a sound she kept stopping to listen for under the sound of her own blood. She came onto the old supply road at the first washout and turned down it and ran where the grade let her run, walked where the alder closed in, and her knees, which were fifty-nine years old and were the other reason this was the last summer, registered every downhill yard like a small itemized bill.

She thought, going down, about why she had taken the lookout in the first place, the real why, under the whys she gave at dinner parties in the years when she still went to dinner parties — the romance of it, the Kerouac of it, the divorce. The real why was that she was good at distance. She had always been good at distance. As a child she'd been the one on the porch roof while Frankie was the one in the creek; as a woman she had built a working life out of the porch roof, fourteen feet square with windows on all sides, where attention could be total because it was attention to things that could not need her. A storm cell could not need her. A smoke could not need her; you called it in and other people went toward it. For eleven summers she had performed the act of care at three miles' distance and been thanked for it, paid for it, and when their mother got sick it had turned out that she did not know how to do it any closer in. That was the sentence. She had assembled it slowly, over three silent years, the way you'd assemble a stove from a diagram, and now she had it whole and it did her no good at all, eight miles of downhill, no good except that it kept her legs moving: she had not known how to do it any closer in, and her sister had known how and had done it, every day for nineteen months, the bedpans and the rages and the three-a.m. terrors and the slow soft-spoken erasure of the person who had been their mother, Frankie had done all of it alone in that cabin while Kit watched the weather, and the punishment Frankie had assigned her was the only one with any teeth in it. Stay up there. Be what you are.

At the four-mile mark, where the road crossed a spring seep and the mud held tracks — elk, a bear, days old — she got her one open view to the east through a burned snag patch, and what she saw stopped her with her hands on her knees. The column had collapsed. Where there had been a single climbing tower of smoke there was now a low gray smear flattened against the drainage, blueing at the edges, and she stood there reading it with her heart slamming and could not decide what it meant, because it could mean the fire had laid down — wind shift, the morning humidity hanging on in the creek bottom — or it could mean it had already eaten what it was going to eat and was settling into the wreckage. Smoke goes ambiguous exactly when you need it to speak. Eleven summers had taught her that too. She drank a third of her water and went on, faster, the shovel handle knocking her shoulder blade in rhythm, and somewhere in the next mile she became aware that she was bargaining, silently, in the old way, with whatever ran the weather: not the cabin, fine, the cabin, but not — and could not finish it even silently, and the not-finishing was its own discovery, because she had spent three years telling herself that what she felt toward Frankie was a settled thing, scar tissue, a healed seam. Pale, like the smoke had been at first light. And here was her body, fifty-nine years old and falling down a mountain, telling her what it had been instead, which was a live coal under duff, smoking along underground all this time, waiting for wind.

The truck started on the second try. The river road was empty, which meant nothing — it was always empty — and she drove with her hands too tight on the wheel, watching the smear of smoke fatten and thin over the ridgeline like something breathing. No engines passed her. No plane worked overhead. Of course not: there was no detection on this fire, she was the detection, the entire apparatus of detection for this drainage on this morning had been one unpaid woman in a condemned tower with a dead radio, and the column would have to get over the ridge and big enough to be seen from the highway forty miles south before anyone with a pump and a crew knew it existed. She passed the Hutchins place at the river junction and laid on the horn, three long blasts, the rural code for trouble, and saw no one, and didn't stop, and swung up Slate Creek Road in a spray of gravel.

Smoke came down the road to meet her at the second crossing — not a wall, a presence, a thin acrid drift through the timber, sunlight in it turning the whole understory the color of weak tea — and she rolled the window up and then back down because she needed to smell it, needed the information in it, and the information was woodsmoke and something else, something chemical and wrong, paint or fabric or foam, the smell of made things burning. She took the last mile too fast, fishtailing in the corners, and came around the rock outcrop below the bench with her foot already off the gas, ready for anything, ready for a crown fire standing in the dooryard, ready for the cabin a black ribcage, and what she came around the corner into was this:

The cabin standing whole in its clearing. The grass of the bench blackened in a ragged hundred-foot circle that had run from a burn pile to the foot of the porch steps and stopped, stopped in a charred scallop two yards from the wood, and her sister, in their father's old canvas coat with the sleeves rolled, beating at a smoldering line along the fence with a wet feed sack, gray-haired now, Frankie, gray as their mother, working alone in the smoke with the creek glittering stupidly behind her and a garden hose lying spent in the burned grass like a killed snake.

Kit was out of the truck before it had fully stopped, shovel in hand, and she crossed the black ground — it crunched, it breathed heat up through her boot soles — and fell in on the fence line ten feet from her sister and started throwing dirt, and for a long time, ten minutes, fifteen, neither of them said anything at all, because the fire did not allow it; it was down but not out, creeping in the fence-line grass and smoking in the roots of a juniper and putting up sly little flags at the edge of the timber that each had to be walked down and beaten and buried, and the two of them worked the perimeter in opposite directions and met and passed and worked it again, wordless, coughing, in a choreography neither had to invent because they had done it before, thirty-five years before, the summer their father burned the lower pasture and lost it into the fence row, the two of them teenagers flailing with wet sacks while he swore, and Kit's body remembered her sister's body, where it would be, which way it would turn, the same way her feet had remembered the scree.

When the edge was cold — when they had walked it twice and laid their bare palms on the black ground in a dozen places the way their father had taught them, feeling for heat like feeling for fever on a child's forehead — Frankie straightened up at last and looked at her. Soot to the hairline. Three years older. The burn pile behind her had collapsed into a low mound of glowing structure that Kit could read now that there was time to read it: bed slats. The black coiled springs of a mattress. The arched scorched skeleton of the cane chair that had stood by their mother's window, and shoals of paper ash, and a melted slag of something that had been the green plastic of the oxygen tubing, hundreds of feet of it, that had run through that cabin like a vine the last year of their mother's life.

"You burned her room," Kit said. Her voice came out wrecked from the smoke. "In August. In a drought. You burned her room in August."

"I waited three years," Frankie said. "I couldn't get a fourth winter looking at it." She dropped the feed sack and pushed her wrist across her forehead, leaving a clean stripe. "It got into the grass. I had it ringed and the wind came up the creek." She said it flatly, no apology in it, an incident report, and then she looked away east up the drainage and back, and something in her face came apart very slightly at the seams, and she said, "I figured if it got away from me, somebody would see it." She paused. "You'd see it."

Kit stood in the burned grass with the shovel in her two hands and let that arrive. Down at the creek a kingfisher rattled. The smoke off the pile rose thin and almost white now, almost prayer-colored, going up out of the drainage in the dying wind, and she understood that it had been read in only one place in the world this morning, that there had been exactly one set of eyes positioned to read it, and that her sister, who had not written, who could not call, who would have stood in this dooryard last night with a match and a three-year silence, had known that.

"The radio's dead," Kit said. "I couldn't call anybody. I just came."

"I know," Frankie said. "Nobody else is here." She looked at the truck, at the shovel, at her sister's ruined knees and soot-grimed face, the whole eight downhill miles of her, and her mouth did something that was not a smile but was descended from one. "Took you about three hours."

"Two fifty," Kit said.

They sat down after a while on the porch steps, above the scorched scallop where the fire had stopped, because there was nowhere else to sit and no way yet to go inside, and Frankie brought out water and they drank it watching the pile burn down to a fact, and Kit thought about the letter in the drawer under the maps, eight miles up and unopened, and knew she would climb back up for it, tomorrow or the day after, one more time before they took the tower down, and that whatever was in it had just been said anyway, said in the only language her sister had left to reach her with, smoke standing up out of a fold in the land, saying what smoke has always said to the one watching: here. Look. Come down.
