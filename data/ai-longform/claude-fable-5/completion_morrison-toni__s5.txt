He had handled a musket since boyhood, but he kept it sheathed and relied instead on the horse's ears, which swiveled and flattened at sounds he could not yet hear. The trail was old and patient, worn smooth by feet that had crossed it for a thousand years before any Englishman dreamed of tobacco, and it forgave nothing. Roots humped up through the dirt like the knuckles of buried men. Twice Regina shied—once at a snake the color of wet leaves sliding off a stone, once at nothing he could name—and twice he gentled her with his palm flat on her neck, murmuring the nonsense syllables that horses everywhere seemed to find persuasive. The gold fog had burned away behind him but its strangeness clung. He had the sensation, riding under oaks hung with moss like the beards of drowned prophets, that he had stepped out of one world at the shoreline and into another that had not yet decided what it was.

Near midday he passed three native men standing at the edge of a clearing, a gutted doe slung on a pole between two of them. They watched him with a stillness that was neither welcome nor warning, simply the stillness of men who had learned that movement invited consequence. He raised one hand from the reins, slow, and held it there. The eldest of the three tipped his chin a fraction—an acknowledgment, perhaps, or a measurement—and Jacob rode on, feeling their eyes on his back for longer than was comfortable. He did not blame them. In this colony a white man on a good horse was either trouble arriving or trouble departing, and the difference mattered little to those who had to bury the results. He thought of Lina, far north, who had survived the death of her whole village and carried it behind her face every day like a stone she had agreed to swallow. The English had a gift, he sometimes thought, for making orphans, and then a second gift for forgetting they had made them.

He knew something of orphanage himself. That was the wound that ached in damp weather. His mother, a girl of no consequence, had died bearing him; his father, a Dutchman who had given him nothing but a surname difficult for the English to pronounce, vanished into the maw of the city before Jacob could walk. There had been the poorhouse, then the printer who took boys for the labor and beat them for the food they ate, then years of running the docks—fetching, carrying, learning to read upside down the bills of lading that passed under his nose, learning that paper, properly understood, was worth more than muscle. He had expected nothing of the world and the world, for thirty years, had obliged him. Then came the letter, delivered by a lawyer with ink-stained cuffs and a manner of speaking that suggested he was performing a charity by breathing the same air: an uncle Jacob had never met, dead in the new world, and one hundred and twenty acres of a patroonship dissolved into English hands, lying fallow in a climate the lawyer described, with a shudder, as Dutch. To inherit land—he, a ratty orphan whose first memory was the smell of other children's urine—was an alteration so violent that for a week he told no one, certain the paper would dissolve if he spoke of it. It had not dissolved. It had become Vaark's place, his hills, his pond, his hundred tribulations of stump and stone, and he had become a landowner, which in this world was a species of resurrection.

The wife had come later, also by paper. He had advertised, frankly, the way a man advertises for a brace of oxen, and Rebekka had crossed an ocean to a husband she knew only as a name and a promise of no church beatings, sound teeth, and a roof. He remembered standing on the dock at her arrival, terrified she would be sour or mad or pious in the punishing way, and watching instead a sturdy young woman come down the plank carrying her own box, refusing the hand of the sailor who offered, scanning the crowd until her eyes found his and stayed. They had liked each other at once, which neither had expected, and then loved each other, which neither had dared expect, and the only cruelty their marriage had suffered came not from each other but from God, who took the infants one after another—three boys who never saw a second spring—and then took Patrician, five years old, thrown from a kicked horse, the one child who had lived long enough to have a laugh he could remember. He could remember it still. That was the trouble with riding alone; the mind unbridled went wherever it liked, and it liked the graves.

So he thought instead of money, which was the purpose of this miserable journey. The man he rode to see, a Portuguese tobacco planter named Senhor D'Ortega, owed him a sum that had begun as a courteous loan and curdled into an insult. D'Ortega had lost a third of a slave cargo to fever and shipboard despair—the despair of the cargo itself, which had a way of leaping into the sea when the hatches opened—and the Crown's men had fined him besides for unloading the sick where he ought not. To cover the catastrophe he had borrowed, with flourishes and seals and invocations of his lineage, from several lenders, Jacob among them. Jacob had agreed against his own judgment, partly for the interest, partly because refusing a gentleman to his face was a skill he had never properly acquired, his orphan's hunger for respectability betraying him at the precise moments it should have protected him. Two harvests had come and gone. The letters from Maryland grew more ornate as they grew less useful, each one a little cathedral of excuse. Finally Jacob had written that he would come himself, and D'Ortega, with what Jacob imagined was a perfumed sigh, had replied that he would be received with all honor. Honor. Jacob spat lightly to the side of the trail. He would have preferred coin.

He crossed the wide water in the afternoon on a ferry poled by a leathery man and his son, the boy no more than ten, working the sweep with his whole small body and watching Jacob's boots as if memorizing what prosperity looked like from the ankle down. The heat lay on the river like a lid. Mosquitoes came out of the reeds in congregations. The ferryman talked the whole crossing—of the price of shoats, of a hanging in the spring, of a neighbor's indentured girl who had run and been caught and had her term doubled, which the ferryman pronounced fair with the satisfaction of a man who had never owed anyone a year of his life. Jacob paid him, overpaid him slightly to stop the talking, and rode up the far bank into Maryland, where the Papists ruled and a man could buy anything, absolutely anything, if he had the means and did not inquire too closely into its origins.

Jublio announced itself a mile before he reached it. The fields did that—tobacco in long shimmering ranks, and among the ranks the bent figures, dark, spaced like notes on a staff, making the music that paid for everything. Then the gate: two brick pillars, absurd in that wilderness, topped with carved pineapples imported, no doubt, at ruinous cost, and a crushed-shell drive that ran white and straight as a sermon up to the house. The house itself stopped him. He had to rein in Regina and simply look. It rose three stories in honey-colored brick, windows tall as men, a double stair sweeping up to doors wide enough to admit a carriage, and the whole of it set down in the green like something dreamed by a city and abandoned here. He despised it instantly and completely, and underneath the despising, in a chamber of himself he did not often visit, something else stirred—a low, patient covetousness, the orphan's old arithmetic. Why him and not me. He throttled the thought, as he always did, and rode up the white drive feeling the shells crunch under hoof like small bones.

D'Ortega came down the stairs himself, arms wide, rings catching the light, a man upholstered rather than dressed, smelling of clove water at twenty paces. His grief over his finances, he wanted Jacob to understand at once, was matched only by his joy at this visit; Jacob's patience was Christlike; the season had been cruel; the King's officers were thieves with seals; and would Senhor Vaark do him the honor of dining and sleeping under his roof, since the accounts—here a pained smile—were a matter requiring leisure and wine. Jacob, who had ridden since dawn and would have signed away a year of his life for a basin of water, said that he would be pleased. A black boy in livery too heavy for the climate took Regina's bridle. Jacob watched the boy lead her off and noticed that the livery's previous occupant had been larger, and wondered, not wanting to, what had become of him.

The dinner was a campaign. Course followed course as if the kitchen meant to bury the debt under sauces—a fish smothered past recognition, fowl, a haunch of something, puddings, Madeira poured by a silent woman whose eyes never rose above the level of the glasses. Mistress D'Ortega presided in silk the color of a bruise, handsome and exhausted, producing conversation the way the fields produced tobacco, in cultivated rows. The sons, two padded boys with their father's small mouth, ate gracelessly and were praised for it. Talk turned, as Jacob had dreaded, to religion, D'Ortega lamenting the persecutions endured by the faithful with a wave of his ringed hand, and Jacob answered in the vague way he had perfected, being himself a man whose church was solvency and whose sacraments were performed at a desk. He found, watching his host's mouth work through the food and the self-pity together, that his distaste had ripened into something almost restful. There was no question of liking the man. The only question was the money, and the money, it became clear over the puddings, did not exist.

It came out the next morning, in the office, between professions of honor: the harvest was committed, the creditors in Lisbon were savage, the fines, the fever, the sea itself conspired—but Senhor Vaark must not think him without resource. There was property. Here D'Ortega rose, suddenly brisk, and led the way out the back of the house, and Jacob understood with a falling sensation what species of property was meant, and followed anyway, because two harvests of patience had earned him at least the satisfaction of refusing to the man's face.

They walked the quarters in the early heat. D'Ortega conducted the tour like a man showing horses—this one strong, this one bred twice already, this one skilled at the curing barns, teeth, hands, the soundness of legs—and the men and women stood for it with a remoteness Jacob could not read and did not want to. He kept his hat brim low and his voice flat. Flesh was not his commerce, he said. He was a farmer and a trader; he dealt in fur, lumber, wampum, credit; he had a small place and small needs. He said it twice. D'Ortega heard it the way the sea hears objections. The figures were rehearsed at him—what a prime hand fetched at auction, what generosity it was to offer at such a discount, how the debt might be cleared in a single stroke and their friendship preserved. Friendship. Jacob looked down the row of cabins, at the smoke of cookfires going up thin and straight in the windless morning, and felt the trap of the thing: there was no money, there would be no money, and a suit in a Maryland court, before Maryland judges, against a Catholic gentleman of standing, would cost him the debt over again and the years besides. The choice was flesh or nothing. D'Ortega had known it before Jacob's boot touched the white shells of the drive. That was the use of the dinner, the Madeira, the tour. He had been managed, and the knowledge made the back of his neck burn.

He was forming the words of a final refusal—nothing, then; he would take the loss and the lesson—when they passed the cookhouse, and a woman stepped into the doorway with a kettle in her hands. She was perhaps thirty, perhaps less; the work made guessing cruel. Beside her, half hidden in her skirts, stood a girl of seven or eight years in a pair of cast-off shoes far too large, a thin child with enormous eyes, and on the woman's hip a baby boy worked his fist against his mouth. Jacob would not have stopped—he was practiced at not stopping—but the woman looked at him. Not at D'Ortega, whose glance she met the way one meets weather, but at him, Jacob, directly, a thing he understood was forbidden and therefore deliberate, and her eyes did something he had no word for. They chose. They moved from his face to the girl and back to his face, and they were saying something with all the force of speech and none of its safety, and what they were saying, he comprehended slowly, the comprehension arriving like cold water, was take her. Take this one. The woman's hand rested on the girl's head, light as a leaf. Her other arm held the boy close, closer. Take the girl, the eyes said, and Jacob, who had buried three sons and a daughter, who had seen what choosing cost his own wife when there was nothing left to choose between, stood in the Maryland sun feeling the bottom of the morning give way.

D'Ortega, scenting movement, began at once to talk—the woman was his cook, valuable, not to be considered, but the child, the child was nothing yet, unformed, eats little, and Senhor Vaark might have her for the debt entire if he wished to close the matter cleanly. Jacob heard himself bargaining, which astonished him; heard himself observe that a child was a cost, not a payment, that she would be years eating before she was worth her keep; heard D'Ortega counter; heard the sum settled. Through all of it the woman did not move. The girl looked at her mother once, twice, not understanding, beginning to understand. Jacob told himself, in the rapid voice a man uses when outpacing his own conscience, that this was no purchase but a rescue; that his wife, hollowed by graves, might find some use for a child about the place, some softening; that whatever Jublio was, his farm was not that, would never be that; that the girl would eat at a table and learn letters if Rebekka had the patience for it; that he was, in the only sense available to him on this ground in this colony in this year, doing good. He told himself this and partly believed it, and the part that did not believe it he folded small and put away with the other things he did not look at.

The papers were drawn before noon, D'Ortega's clerk scratching the girl into the ledger of cleared accounts as if she were a hogshead of leaf. Her name, the clerk said, was Florens. Jacob could not take her north slung over a saddle, so it was arranged that she would travel with a priest, a thin nervous Father who came and went between the river parishes and was bound north within the month, and who, D'Ortega let it be known with rolled eyes, had taught the child her letters in secret against all sense. Jacob paid the Father's passage money himself and gave him a paper with the route written out, and the priest looked at him with a face full of questions he was too prudent or too defeated to ask. As Jacob mounted, the girl stood in the yard in her terrible shoes, and the mother stood behind her, and Jacob made the error of looking back. The woman's face had closed like a door. Whatever she had spent in those few seconds of looking, she had no more of it, or could not afford to show what remained. The girl raised one hand, not quite a wave, a hand simply lifted into the air as if testing it, and Jacob touched his hat because he did not know what else a man did, and rode down the white drive with the shells cracking under him and the great honeyed house at his back like an accusation he had agreed to share.

He did not go straight to the boats. He rode instead to a tavern at the landing and sat in its brown shade with ale he barely tasted, doing sums. The debt was cleared; that was the cold fact of it, and a man who could not live with cold facts had no business lending money. But the sums kept sliding off the page of his mind and the house kept rising in their place—the tall windows, the double stair, the sheer thereness of it, built out of leaf and sweat that was not D'Ortega's, occupied by a fool, and yet standing, gracious and permanent, while Jacob's own good house in the north sat low and brown as a beaver lodge. Why him and not me hummed again, and this time Jacob did not throttle it. He turned it over. He examined it in the tavern light. A man need not handle the cargo to profit by the trade; that was the whole genius of the age. There was sugar in Barbados, and rum out of sugar, and money out of rum, and the distance between his hand and the cane fields was an ocean wide—wide enough, surely, for a conscience to live comfortably on the near shore. Other men, godly men, men with pews and reputations, drew income from that quarter and slept soundly. The labor that made the sugar was no concern of the investor, any more than the worm was the concern of the man who wore silk. He drank his ale slowly, building the house in his mind—not D'Ortega's house, finer, honester, a house with a gate worthy of it, iron, perhaps, worked by a proper smith—and by the time the ale was gone the thing was settled in him, though he would have denied, if asked, that anything had been settled at all.

Riding to the water in the long light of evening, he passed the fields again, the bent figures still among the rows though the sun was failing, and he made himself look at them, as a kind of payment. Then he made himself stop looking, as a kind of mercy to himself. The fog was coming in off the bay again, gray now, the gold all spent, and the ferryman's boy poled him across into the dusk talking of nothing, and Jacob Vaark, landowner, lender, husband, four times bereaved, owner now—the word snagged in him like a hook and he worked it loose—guardian, rather, of one small girl in shoes too big for her, watched the Maryland shore dissolve behind him and thought of his wife's face when he told her, and of what he would say, and of what he would leave out.
