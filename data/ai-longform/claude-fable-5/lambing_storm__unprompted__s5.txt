The snow had started as rain, which was worse, because it meant everything in the yard was glazed before the temperature dropped, and by the time Maren crossed from the house to the lambing shed at four in the afternoon she had to walk in the long flat-footed shuffle Eli used to laugh at, calling it her penguin walk, calling it from the shed door with a lamb tucked in each arm like loaves of bread. The wind came off the ridge in gusts that bent the yard light on its pole and made the snow travel sideways, so that it never seemed to land, only to pass through, endlessly, from one darkness to another.

Inside the shed the air was warm with the bodies of forty ewes and smelled of lanolin and iodine and wet straw, and her son Thomas was kneeling in the third jug pen with his sleeve rolled past the elbow and his cheek pressed flat against the flank of a ewe who was straining and getting nowhere.

"How long has she been at it," Maren said.

"Since I came out. Maybe forty minutes before that. There's a nose but no feet."

"Head back or legs back?"

"Legs, I think. I can feel the shoulder."

She hung her coat on the nail by the door, the nail that had held Eli's coat all winter until she made herself take it down in March, and she washed her hands and forearms in the bucket of warm water Thomas had already carried out from the house, because he thought of those things now, he had learned to think of them, and she tried not to feel the small grief that lived inside even her pride in him. A boy of fifteen should not have to be this good at this.

"Let me," she said, and he moved aside without argument, which also was new.

The ewe was a four-year-old Border Leicester cross with a Roman nose and an opinion about everything, and she swung her head around to look at Maren with one yellow eye as Maren slid her hand in alongside the lamb's skull. The nose was right there, slick and warm, the little tongue already moving, which was good, the lamb was alive, but Thomas was right, both forelegs were folded back at the shoulder like a diver's, and there was no room, there was never any room, you worked in a space the size of a coat sleeve and you worked by feel and by memory and by the voice of a dead man saying *push the head back first, Mar, you can't bring the leg up past the chin, there's no geometry for it.*

"Hold her," she said, and Thomas leaned his weight across the ewe's shoulders.

She cupped the lamb's muzzle in her palm and pushed it back into the dark, back against the contraction, which felt like pushing against the sea, and the ewe bawled and Thomas murmured something low to her, nonsense, the way you'd talk to a child in fever. Maren found the knee, then the cannon bone, then the small hard cup of the hoof, and she folded her fingers over it so the hoof wouldn't tear the wall of the ewe, and she drew it forward in one long patient pull, and then the second leg, and then the nose again, riding now on its two front legs the way it was supposed to, the way a lamb dives into the world.

"Okay," she said. "Next push."

The ewe pushed and Maren pulled, downward, toward the hocks, and the lamb came in a rush of fluid and membrane and lay steaming in the straw, and Thomas was already there with a handful of straw, stripping the caul from its face, clearing the nostrils, swinging nothing — the lamb shook its head and sneezed on its own, wet ears flapping, and the ewe turned around and began the low rumbling chuckle that ewes make only at this one moment in their lives, a sound they make at nothing else on earth.

"Ewe lamb," Thomas said, checking. "Big one."

"That's why."

"You want to strip her teats or should I."

"You do it. I'll see who else is thinking about it."

She walked the length of the shed in the yellow light of the bulbs strung along the ridge beam, and she looked at each ewe the way Eli had taught her twenty years ago when she was a town girl who had married into all of this, looked at the hollows in front of the hip bones, at the way a ewe stood apart from the others, at the ones pawing the straw into nests, at the ones staring at the wall with the inward look. Two were close. One, the old black-faced ewe they called Magpie, was lying in the far corner with a water bag already showing, and Maren stood and watched her for a minute and decided she was doing fine on her own, and that was when the lights went out.

The dark came down like a dropped blanket. For a moment there was nothing, only the wind working at the tin roof and the rustle and shift of forty animals, and then Thomas's voice, very level, from the jug pens.

"Mom."

"I know. Stay where you are till your eyes adjust."

"The lamp's hanging by the door. The big one. I filled it yesterday."

"You filled it."

"Dad always said April. He said you fill the lamps in April whether you think you'll need them or not."

She stood in the dark of the shed she had stood in ten thousand times and could not for a moment have said where the door was, and then the faint gray rectangle of the window resolved itself, snow-light, the strange luminance a blizzard gives off even at night, and she walked toward it with her hand out and found the lamp on its hook and the matches in the tin beside it, where they had been since before Thomas was born. The match flared. The lamp caught and steadied, and the shed came back in a smaller, older form of itself, amber instead of white, the shadows of the ewes thrown huge against the board walls, and Thomas knelt in the pen with the new lamb under the ewe, holding it to the teat, his shadow on the wall like a man's.

"Power lines on the ridge road," she said. "It'll be a while. Could be morning."

"Heat lamps are out, then."

"Heat lamps are out."

He didn't say anything, but she watched him do the arithmetic, the same arithmetic she was doing. Lambs came wet into the world, and a wet lamb in a cold shed lost heat faster than its new body could make it, and the heat lamps over the jug pens were how you tilted the odds, and now the odds were untilted, and outside the thermometer was falling through the twenties with the wind taking it lower.

"We'll dry them harder," she said. "Towels. And we'll bring the weak ones to the house if we have to."

"The stove's still going. I banked it before I came out."

"Then we're fine."

They were not fine, exactly, but it was a thing you said, and he nodded because he understood the function of it. He had become fluent, this winter, in the things people said and the function of them. *He's in a better place. He'd be so proud of you both. If there's anything you need.* Thomas had stood beside her in the church hall in his outgrown suit jacket receiving casseroles and sentences with the gravity of a diplomat, and on the drive home he had said, looking out the window at the snow on the fields, *Nobody knows what to say, so they all say the same thing,* and she had said, *That's right,* and they had not talked about it again, but something had been settled between them in that exchange, some agreement about how they would proceed. They would not pretend. They would also not collapse. There was a farm, and the farm did not know that Eli was dead, the farm went on making its demands with the magnificent indifference of all living systems, and there were days when she hated it for that and days when it was the only thing that got her out of bed, and most days it was both at once.

Magpie delivered her lamb without help, a strong ram lamb that was up and butting at the udder inside twenty minutes, and Maren penned them together and dipped the navel in iodine and stripped the teats and stood for a moment with her hand flat on the old ewe's back, feeling the warmth come up through the fleece. Five lambings, this ewe, and never a moment's trouble. Eli used to say she could do it in her sleep and probably did.

The wind rose. It found every gap in the board-and-batten walls and pushed thin knives of cold through them, and the lamp flame leaned and recovered, leaned and recovered. Thomas brought the radio from the tack room, the old battery one, and they listened to a man in a town forty miles away read closures and warnings in a voice like warm gravel — *travel is not advised, repeat, travel is not advised* — and then a song from before either of them was born, and Thomas left it playing low, and the music threaded itself in under the wind and the rustle of the straw and made the shed feel like a lit boat on a dark sea.

"Sixteen," he said. He was looking at the chalkboard by the door where they kept the tally. "Sixteen down, twenty-four to go."

"They won't all come tonight."

"They might. Storm drops the pressure. Dad said —" He stopped.

"Say it."

"He said ewes lamb on a falling barometer. He said it like it was science."

"He read it somewhere and then he believed it the way he believed everything, which was completely." She found that she was smiling, and that it didn't hurt, or that it hurt in the way a deep bruise hurts when you press it on purpose, to check. "The year you were born there was a storm like this. Not April — March. He was out here alone all night because I was in the hospital having you, and his sister stayed with me, and he called the nursing station at six in the morning to tell me we'd had eleven lambs and a son, in that order."

"In that order."

"He thought it was the funniest thing he'd ever said. He told it for fifteen years."

"He told it at my birthday. Every birthday."

"Yes, he did."

The boy bent his head over the lamb he was drying, a small one, the second of twins from a yearling ewe, and he worked the towel along its spine in hard strokes, and Maren did not look too closely at his face, and he did not look too closely at hers, and the radio played and the storm leaned on the walls of the shed like a shoulder against a door.

They came after that the way Eli had always said they would, on the falling glass, as if the lambs themselves could feel the weight of the sky letting go. A set of twins, straightforward. Then a yearling who panicked and ran from her own lamb, circling the pen with her eyes white-rimmed, until Maren cornered her and held her and Thomas put the lamb to her nose and they stood there, the three of them and the lamb, for ten minutes, until the smell of it found whatever ancient switch it was looking for and the ewe's whole body changed, softened, and she began to chuckle and lick and the thing was done, the oldest transaction in the world completed one more time. Then a big single, backward, hind feet first, and that one had to come fast or not at all, and Maren pulled it in one long burning haul with Thomas bracing the ewe, and the lamb hung limp and she swung it by the hind legs in a low arc to clear the fluid from its lungs, once, twice, and laid it down and rubbed its chest hard with the heel of her hand and was about to say the sentence she did not want to say when it coughed, and shuddered, and drew the first ragged breath, and Thomas made a sound that was not quite a laugh and not quite anything else.

"Tough," he said.

"They want to live. People think they're stupid animals. They want to live as much as anything does."

By midnight the cold in the shed had teeth. The water in the buckets grew skins of ice and Thomas broke them with his fist, and they took turns going to the house to feed the stove and warm their hands and carry back jugs of hot water, and the path between house and shed filled in behind them each time, so that each crossing was new, each crossing was the first crossing anyone had ever made, the yard light dead on its pole and the flashlight beam full of streaming snow and the house a dim shape that existed only on faith until you were ten feet from it. On one crossing Maren stopped halfway, in the middle of the white roar, and let it take her for a moment — the dark, the cold, the snow flowing past her like the visible body of time itself — and she said his name once, out loud, into the wind, where no one could hear it, not even her, and then she went on to the house and filled the jugs.

The small twin from the yearling went down around two. Thomas found it flat in the corner of the jug, its mouth cold when he put his finger in, which was the test, the mouth told you everything, and he had it inside his coat against his bare chest before he even called to her, the way he'd been taught, skin to skin, and they carried it to the house through the storm and put it in the cardboard box by the stove that had held every weak lamb of the last twenty springs, the box itself nearly an heirloom, its flaps soft as cloth from handling. Maren mixed the colostrum they kept frozen, thawed it slow in warm water — *never the microwave, Mar, you'll kill everything good in it* — and Thomas fed the lamb with the stomach tube because it was too weak to suck, his hands steady, measuring the tube against the lamb's body from mouth to last rib, checking, checking again.

"Dad showed me in February," he said, not looking up. "Before he —. He showed me on a dead one first. He said you never learn it on one that matters."

"He showed me the same way. Nineteen years ago."

"Was it weird? Coming here. Not knowing anything."

"Everything was weird. I'd never touched a sheep. I thought lanolin was something that came in a bottle." The lamb's stomach was filling, a small visible rounding. "Your father used to say I learned fast for a town girl, and that was the highest compliment he had, and it took me five years to understand that."

"That's enough," Thomas said, meaning the milk, and drew the tube. The lamb lay in the warmth of the stove with its eyes half-closed, and its breathing was already different, deeper, the body taking up the fuel and turning it into heat, into intention, into the will to be a sheep. "I'll stay with it. You go back out. Swap in an hour."

"Two hours. Sleep if you can."

"I can't sleep."

"Then don't, but lie down anyway. That's an order from management."

He lay on the couch with his boots on, which she did not mention, with the lamb box at arm's reach, and she went back out into the storm, and the storm took her in like a familiar.

Toward four the wind began to lose conviction. She could hear it happening, the gusts coming further apart, the tin roof settling, and in the spaces between gusts there was a new silence so total it had a texture, the silence of deep snow, the world wrapped and muffled and put away. Twenty-two lambs on the chalkboard. The lamp was burning low and she refilled it from the can in the tack room, working by feel, and when she lit it again she stood in the middle of the shed and looked at what the night had made: the jug pens full, ewes folded down with lambs tucked along their bodies, the small heads coming up to look at her with that brand-new look, the look of creatures to whom everything is the first thing. The smell of milk and iodine and straw. The breath of the flock rising visible in the lamp light, a single slow communal cloud.

He should be here. That was the thought, whole and simple, standing in the middle of her like a post driven into ground. Not the soft-edged grief of the church hall, not the ambush grief of finding his handwriting on the feed calendar, but this: he should be standing right there, by the third pen, with straw in his hair and that look he got at four in the morning in lambing season, exhausted and lit up at the same time, like a man at the end of a long card game he was winning. The unfairness of it was so large and so plain that there was nothing to do with it. You could not pull it like a leg. You could not swing it clear and rub its chest. It simply was, the way the storm was, and you worked inside it.

The door opened on a wedge of blue dark and Thomas came in stamping, snow to his knees, the lamb from the house riding inside his coat with its head out the collar like a strange second face.

"It's sucking," he said. "Took the bottle at three thirty and screamed at me at four for more. I figured it could come back to its mother before she forgets it."

"Good. Put it in and watch."

They put the lamb back in the jug with the yearling and its twin, and the yearling smelled it all over, twice, with great suspicion, and then chuckled and let it nurse, and Thomas crouched at the rail with his arms folded on the top board and his chin on his arms and watched it the way other boys watched screens, with total absorption, and Maren looked at the back of his neck, still a child's neck, and the width of his shoulders, no longer a child's shoulders, and she thought about the things that get handed down a line of people, not the farm, not the flock, but the smaller stranger inheritance: fill the lamps in April. Never the microwave. You can't bring the leg up past the chin. The mouth tells you everything. A man could die in February and keep arriving all spring, sentence by sentence, in the hands of his son.

"Snow's quitting," Thomas said.

"Almost."

"How many still to go?"

"Eighteen. But nothing's close. We might get a few hours."

He stood and stretched until his spine cracked, and looked down the long row of pens, and she saw him do the thing Eli always did, the slow proprietary survey, the counting that wasn't counting, just the eye touching every animal once, and she did not know if he had learned it by watching or if it was simply in him, and she decided it did not matter, that it came to the same thing.

"Twenty-three," he said, and pointed.

In the corner pen the last ewe of the night had lambed without a sound, sometime in the past hour, alone, asking nothing of anyone, and the lamb was already dry and folded against her and the ewe gazed out at them with the flat golden patience of her kind, as if to say it had all been managed, as if to say it would all be managed, with them or without them, snow or no snow, year upon year upon year.

Thomas wrote the number on the chalkboard. The chalk was a stub. He cupped it in his fingers the way you hold a thing that's nearly used up, and made the figures large and clear, and set the stub back on the rail.

Outside, the sky in the east had begun to go the color of skim milk, and the snow had stopped entirely, and the world when they opened the shed door was so white and so still that they both stood in the doorway without speaking, the warmth of forty sheep at their backs and the cold of the new day on their faces, the drifts curved against the fences like something poured, the yard unmarked, the power line down across the lane in a long black calligraphy on all that white, and far off, faint, the sound of the county plow working its way up the valley toward them, toward the farm, toward whatever the day would ask next.

"Breakfast," Maren said. "Then we dig out the lane."

"Then we sleep."

"Then one of us sleeps."

Thomas laughed, a short real laugh, white in the cold air, and pulled the shed door closed behind them, gently, so as not to startle the new ones, and they walked up to the house through snow to their knees, breaking the path together, the boy going first part of the way and the mother going first part of the way, trading off without discussing it, the way you do when the work is long and shared and there is no one keeping track.
