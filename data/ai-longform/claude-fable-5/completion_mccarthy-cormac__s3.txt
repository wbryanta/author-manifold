He sat upright in the bed for a long time with his heart laboring and the dark of the cabin pooled about him like something tidal and slowly receding and he could hear her breathing next to him, shallow and waiting.

What was you dreamin? she said.

Nothin. Go on to sleep.

You was a-hollerin.

I weren't hollerin.

You was. You like to woke the dead.

He lay back. Through the chinking of the logs he could see a thin seam of paler dark and he watched it as if something might cross there. Go to sleep, he said.

She shifted her weight and the shucks in the tick rasped beneath her and she lay with her hands crossed over the swollen mound of her belly the way she had taken to lying these last weeks, like something votive, an attitude of offering. He could feel the heat coming off of her. After a while she said: It's been a-kickin.

He didn't answer.

Culla.

What.

I said it's been a-kickin.

I heard ye.

She was quiet then. Far off in the woods a barred owl called and ceased and called again, that strangled interrogation, and then there was nothing but the night going on and the small breathings in the room and the creak of the cabin timbers contracting in the cool. He lay with his eyes open until the seam of light in the wall had gone gray and then a sourceless pewter color and then he rose and pulled on his brogans and went out.

The morning came up out of a fog that lay along the creek bottom like spilled milk and the trees stood out of it blackly and dripping. He stood on the stone doorslab and made water into the weeds and looked out at what there was to look at. A shed of poles and rived boards canted into the hillside. A garden patch fenced with brush where the beans had come to nothing and the corn stood in sparse and yellowed ranks like the survivors of some pestilence. Beyond that the woods, going up dark and absolute toward the gap, and no road to this place but a wagon track long disused and healing over with sumac and sawbriar, and that suited him, no road and no neighbors and none to come visiting and none to see.

He took up the axe from where it leaned against the doorjamb and went to the woodlot and worked there through the early morning, splitting the rounds of a dead oak he'd felled in the spring, the white wedges of wood leaping away from the blade and the sound of it going off through the fog and coming back changed and hollow from the far side of the hollow like the work of some other man laboring in another world. When he came in she had cooked mush in the blackened pot and they ate it without speaking, she sitting careful and upright on the stool with her belly between her and the table so that she had to reach.

We could use some meal, she said. They ain't but a hand of it left.

I know it.

And lard. I been savin the lard but they ain't much.

I know what they is and ain't, he said. I live here too.

She looked at him and looked away. Her face had grown thin while the rest of her grew great, the skin drawn tight over the bones of it and her eyes huge and dark in their sockets so that she had taken on the look of something consumed from within, fed upon. I just said it, she said. I never meant nothin.

He rose and took up his bowl and set it in the pail of gray water by the door. I'll get meal when they's a way to get it, he said. We ain't got nothin to trade and I ain't a-leavin you here by yourself. Not now.

I'd be all right.

You don't know what you'd be.

He went out. The fog had burned off and the sun stood over the gap and the day came on hot and windless, the locusts shrilling in the dead trees down by the creek with a sound like something being sawed endlessly in two. He hoed in the dying garden without belief, chopping at the baked ground, and the dust rose about his ankles and settled on the bean leaves until they were the color of the ground itself, and once he stopped and stood leaning on the hoe and listening, because he had heard or thought he heard the ring of metal somewhere down the old track, a small chiming like harness bells or tinware.

It was tinware. The tinker came up the track in the midafternoon hauling his cart behind him in its constellation of dangling pans and cups and skimmers and small implements of obscure purpose, all of it swinging and clashing with his steps so that he advertised himself a half mile through the woods like some pied and shabby angelus. He was a small man, gnarled and black with sun, and he came on steadily with the cartpoles in his fists and his eyes already taking the measure of the place, the shed, the garden, the gray cabin with its mud chimney, and finding it all poor enough but stopping anyway because stopping was his trade.

Howdy, he said.

Holme stood in the garden with the hoe. He didn't say anything.

The tinker lowered the poles of the cart and straightened himself by sections, a hand to the small of his back. Hot, ain't it, he said.

It's seasonable.

Seasonable. The tinker grinned, his teeth like a row of burnt stumps. I reckon it is at that. He looked about. You the man of the place?

They ain't no other.

Well. The tinker patted the rim of his cart and the hung tinware swayed and chimed softly. I got pans, pots, buckets. Needles, thread. I got coffee, real coffee, not that chicory nor burnt corn neither. I do tradin of all kinds. Hides, ginseng, beeswax, feathers. He squinted up at Holme. Anything a man might have surplus of.

We ain't got no surplus.

Everbody's got somethin.

Not us.

The tinker looked toward the cabin. In the dark of the doorway there might have been a movement, a paleness withdrawing, or it might have been nothing. He looked back at Holme and something passed over his face, a slyness, an old knowledge. I carry other wares too, he said, lowering his voice though there was none to hear in twenty miles save who he lowered it from. For them as has need of em. I got remedies. Female remedies. I got—

You can get on, Holme said.

The tinker held up one hand, placating. I never meant nothin by it. A man in my line hears things, sees things. I just—

I said get on.

The tinker studied him a moment with his small black eyes and what was in them was neither fear nor anger but a kind of patient accounting, as if he were setting something by against a later date. Then he stooped and took up the cartpoles and turned the cart in the track, the tinware swinging and crying out, and went back the way he had come. Holme stood watching until the chiming of him had died down the hollow and the woods had taken back their silence, and then he went on watching the empty track for a while longer, and then he went in.

You seen him, he said.

She was sitting on the stool by the dead hearth with her hands in her lap. I never, she said.

You was at the door.

I just looked. He couldn't of seen me.

It don't matter could he or couldn't he. I don't want nobody up here. I done told you.

I never ast him to come, she said. I cain't help who comes.

He stood looking down at her. She didn't look up. Her hands lay in her lap palm upward like things she had been given to hold for someone else and her hair hung lank about her face. After a while he said, less harshly: It won't be long now. Then it'll be done with.

She raised her face to him then and her eyes were wet but she was not crying. What'll be done with? she said. What is it you aim to do?

I don't aim to do nothin.

Culla.

I'm goin to see to the stock, he said, though there was no stock to see to save three hens and the dominicker rooster, and he went out.

The days went on. The heat held and the creek shrank in its bed until it ran thin as a knifeblade over the slick black rocks and the leaves of the tulip poplars yellowed and fell out of season and lay in the standing pools like coins. He hunted with the old musket some evenings and twice brought home squirrels and once a groundhog that she stewed with the last of the cornmeal dumplings, and they ate, and they didn't speak of the thing that swelled between them in that cabin like a third party to their silence, and the moon waxed and stood full over the gap and waned again, and in the dark of it her time came.

She woke him in the deep middle of the night, not with words but with the rigid arching of her body in the bed beside him and the breath sucked between her teeth. He rose and lit the lamp with hands not quite steady and in the small yellow bloom of it her face looked up at him from the tick, sheened with sweat already, her eyes enormous.

It's a-comin, she said.

You don't know that. It might be false. Granny Aldritch said sometimes they's false—

It ain't false, she said. And then her body bowed again and she turned her face into the shuck tick and made a sound he had never heard a person make.

He stood holding the lamp. Outside the crickets dinned and dinned and beyond them lay the woods and beyond the woods the gap and beyond the gap, six miles or ten, the nearest soul that walked upright, and she had asked him in the weeks past to fetch a midwife, to fetch the old negress that lived down the river who they said had caught half the children in that end of the county, to fetch anybody, and he had said no, and he said it now again before she even asked.

Cain't nobody come, he said. You know that. They ain't time noway.

You could of went last week. I ast ye and ast ye.

And have it told everwhere? Have em come up here and see? He set the lamp on the table and the shadows reeled and steadied. We don't need nobody, he said. Women has done it by theirselves since the world commenced. You'll be all right.

She didn't answer. The pain had her again and she rode it with her teeth shut and her fists wound in the ticking and when it passed she lay panting and looked at the wall.

It went on through the night. He kept the fire up and set water to heat in the kettle because he had heard that water was wanted at such times though he could not have said for what, and he tore an old shift of hers into squares of rag, and between her pains the silence in the cabin grew so immense that he could hear the lampflame burning, a small dry sibilance like something whispering, and he wished it would hush. Toward the gray edge of morning her pains came hard upon one another with no rest between and she began to call out, not words, or not words he knew, and he knelt at the foot of the bed in the lampyellow dark with his sleeves rolled and his hands shaking, and the world narrowed to that bed and what was being transacted there in blood and water like some dread sacrament come down from times older than the word for it.

It was full light when the child came. It came into his hands blue and slick and silent and for a long moment he knelt holding it and it did not move and something in him that he did not name rose up and said: there. Said: it is finished and none need ever know. And then the child jerked in his hands and gargled and cried, a thin small wail like a cat's, and the thing in him sank back down to wherever it kept.

What is it? she said. Her voice was a ruin. Culla. What is it?

He looked at it. It's a chap, he said. A boy.

Give him here.

He did not give it. He laid it on the rag he'd spread and wiped it after a clumsy fashion and tied the cord with thread and sawed through it with his knife, and the child wailed its small thin wail and worked its blind face, and she on the bed raised herself on her elbows with her hair pasted to her face in dark scrawls.

Give him here, she said. Let me see him.

You need to rest. You're bleedin yet.

I want to see him.

He's poorly, Holme said. He come early and he's poorly. Lay back down.

He ain't poorly. I can hear him. He sounds stout. Culla—

Lay down, I said.

She lay back because she had no strength to do otherwise, and her eyes followed him about the room, and the child mewled in its rags by the hearth, and the morning came up white and hot beyond the door, and all that day she drowsed and woke and asked for the child and drowsed again, and he gave her water and a broth of the squirrel bones and told her again it was poorly and ought not be handled, and by dark she was fevered lightly and slept.

He sat by the hearth through the early night with the child in the crook of his arm, not looking at it. It had hushed its crying and lay breathing in small rapid breaths and once it worked one arm free of the rags and its hand opened and closed on the air, on nothing, like a thing rehearsing a grip it would never in this world be given anything to take hold of. He looked at the hand and looked away. The fire burned down. The crickets went on and on.

When the moon rose he wrapped the child closer in the rags and rose with it and stood listening to her breathing in the bed and then he went out, pulling the door to behind him softly, and crossed the yard and went down past the garden and took the path along the creek into the woods.

The woods at night were a place he knew and they had never held terror for him but they held it now. He went quickly with the child against his chest and the moonlight came down through the rents in the canopy and lay on the path in pale scales like the light at the bottom of a river, and the things that moved at night in the woods moved away from his coming or hushed and let him pass, and he could feel the small heat of the child through the rags and through his shirt, and he did not think. He had taught himself in these last hours not to think and he went like a man on an errand the nature of which has been withheld even from himself, the body proceeding on its own dark instructions.

He left the path where the creek bent south and went up through a stand of hemlock where the moonlight failed altogether and the dark was total and grained and he went with one hand out before him, and beyond the hemlocks he came out into a glade he knew, a small bald of moss and stone in the bend of the creek where the moon fell unbroken and the grass stood silver and still. He stopped there. The child stirred against him and made a small sound and was quiet.

He stood a long time in the middle of that glade. The moon hung over the rim of the mountain and the creek talked its idiot syllables in the dark beyond and the world lay all about him in absolute indifference, and at last he knelt and laid the child down in the moss at the foot of a stone and unfolded the rags partly from its face the way one might to give a sleeper air. It did not wake. Its face in the moonlight was gray and ancient and serene, like the face of something that had already made the journey he was sending it on and come back and could have told him what was there had it the words.

He rose. He said nothing because there was nothing he would allow himself to say and no one to say it to, and he turned and went back the way he had come, fast, and then faster, crashing through the hemlock dark with his arms before his face, and behind him in the glade there was no sound at all and that was worse than any sound, and he ran. He fell once at the creek crossing and rose wet to the knee and ran on and did not stop until he came out of the woods below the garden and saw the cabin standing gray and silent under the moon with no light in it, and he stood there bent with his hands on his thighs, heaving, and the sweat cold on him.

When his breath came back he went up to the shed and sat in the black inside of it on an upturned keg for a long time. Then he took the spade from the corner and went around behind the shed and dug in the soft ground there, a small hole, no bigger than would hold a shoebox, and he filled it in again and mounded it over and stood the spade against the wall and looked at what he had made in the moonlight. Then he went in.

She woke at the closing of the door. He could hear the shucks shift and her breath change and her voice came out of the dark thin as a thread.

Culla?

It's me.

Where's he at? Where's the chap?

He stood in the dark by the door. The lamp was out and he was glad of it, that she could not see his face nor him hers. He died, he said.

The silence that came then was longer than any of the night's silences and he stood in it and waited.

He never, she said.

He did. I told ye he was poorly. He just quit breathin. They wasn't nothin to be done.

I'd of heard him. I'd of heard him cry.

He never cried. He just quit. They do thataway when they come early. It wasn't nobody's fault.

Where's he at? I want to see him.

I done buried him.

The shucks rasped. She was trying to rise and he crossed the room in the dark and put his hands on her shoulders and pressed her back and she fought him with what little she had, which was nothing, and then quit fighting and lay still under his hands, and he could feel her trembling, a fine deep shudder like a bird held.

You never even let me see him, she said. Not one time. Not even dead you never.

It was better not. You'd of just grieved worse.

Where'd you bury him at?

Out back of the shed. I'll show ye when you're able. I'll make him a marker if you want.

He ain't even got a name, she said. You cain't bury somebody ain't even got a name. The trembling had gone over into weeping now, a low steady keening with no peak and no end to it, and he took his hands away from her and stood up.

Lots of folks is buried with no name, he said. Half this world, I'd say. It don't make no difference to them.

She didn't answer him. She wept. He went to the hearth and sat on the stool there in the dead dark and listened to her and after a while he stopped listening, or stopped hearing, and he sat with his hands hanging between his knees until the chinks in the eastern wall went gray, and somewhere in those hours it came to him out of nowhere and unbidden, the small chiming of tinware going away down a wooded track, and he sat very still and thought about the tinker, how long he might have lingered in that country, and what such a man might find, going as he went by the old paths and the creek roads, going everywhere, seeing everything, and Holme told himself that it was miles and days gone, that no one passed that glade, that by morning the thing would be done that the woods do with what is left in them, and he believed it the way a man believes what he must to rise in the morning, and the light came on and the rooster called from the shed, and in the bed she had cried herself to sleep at last with her face to the wall, and her arms, even sleeping, were curved before her in the shape of a holding, cradling the empty air.
