He was up before daylight and he stood on the porch in the dark drinking his coffee and listening to the pump running down at the well-house and to the silence where the water should have been. The orchard lay below the house in long rows dark against the paler dark of the valley floor and beyond it the mountains stood black and without dimension against a sky already going gray along the rim. He drank the coffee and set the cup on the porchrail and walked down through the yard and out along the headgate ditch with the dry weeds hissing at his boots. The ditch was dry. He squatted and put his hand flat in the bed of it. The silt was cool and cracked into small panes like something fired in a kiln and there was no memory of water in it at all.

He was still standing there when the truck came up the county road with its one good headlight swinging through the trees and pulled in at the gate and shut off. The door creaked and slammed. He could hear his brother's boots in the gravel and then the boots stopped.

You aint even started the coffee, have you, Charlie said.

There's coffee on the stove.

I mean fresh.

It was fresh an hour ago.

Charlie came on down through the gray light carrying a thermos under one arm. He was a heavier man than his brother and he wore a cap with a feedstore emblem on it gone shapeless with age and sweat. He stood looking at the dry ditch.

Pump's runnin, he said.

I know it.

Then where's the water at?

That's what I'd like to know.

They walked down the lane between the trees. The light was coming up out of the east and the trees stood out of the mist of their own shadows row on row, the old Jonathans their grandfather had set out and the younger plantings their father had made in the years when there was money for it, and among them and through them the dead ones standing gray and barkless with their limbs upheld like things in supplication, and they did not speak of the dead ones because there was nothing to say of them that had not been said. Doves were calling from the cedar brakes along the arroyo and somewhere up the valley a dog barked twice and quit.

They found it in the lower lane where the buried main crossed under the road to the west forty. The ground there was black and wet in a long seep and the lane itself had slumped and the water had come up through the caliche and run off down the ruts and pooled and soaked away and the mud was already crusting at its edges in the early heat.

Well, said Charlie. There she is.

There she is.

Charlie pushed his cap back and looked at the wet ground and looked back up the lane toward the well-house a quarter mile distant.

How long you reckon it's been blowed out?

Pressure was down when I cut the pump on yesterday evenin. I thought it was the foot valve. It pumped all night into this.

All night.

Yes.

Charlie shook his head. He spat into the mud. Pumpin water at four dollars a foot of lift and pourin it in a hole in the road, he said. If that aint this place all over I dont know what is.

Go ahead, said Wes.

I aint said nothin.

You was fixin to.

I was fixin to say we need a shovel.

They went back up to the barn and got the shovels and the mattock and a hacksaw and the two repair clamps that had lain in a coffee can on the shelf since their father bought them at the co-op in a year neither of them could name, and they put it all in the bed of Charlie's truck along with a comealong and a logchain and a five gallon bucket and they drove back down the lane and Wes walked over to the well-house and pulled the breaker on the pump and came back. By then the sun was over the shoulder of the mountain and the mist had burned off the rows and the heat was standing up out of the ground like something let out of a cellar.

They dug. The mud came up heavy on the shovels and they slung it to either side and the hole filled slowly with seepwater the color of creamed coffee and Wes bailed with the bucket while Charlie dug and then they traded. By eight oclock they had the pipe uncovered, six inch steel their father had trenched in with a borrowed Case backhoe in nineteen and sixty-one, and they stood in the hole up to their bootlaces in water and looked at it. The pipe was scaled and pitted and along the bottom of it where it had lain in the wet ground for sixty years it was rusted out in a seam you could lay your hand in.

Charlie bent and ran his thumb along the blown seam and straightened up and wiped his thumb on his jeans.

That aint a leak, he said. That's just where it finally quit pretendin.

It'll clamp.

It might. Wes, the whole line's like this. You know it is. We clamped it twice last summer. It's rotten from the well plumb to the road.

Then we'll clamp it a third time.

Charlie looked at him. Then he looked off down the rows of trees. A hawk turned in the white sky above the arroyo and they could hear the first of the cicadas starting in the cottonwoods like something being wound up that would not be wound down again till dark.

Hand me the saw, Charlie said.

They cut the pipe on either side of the rotted seam, taking turns on the hacksaw, the blade chattering and singing in the old steel, and the cutting took the better part of an hour and they ruined two blades at it. When the section came free Charlie heaved it up out of the hole and it lay in the lane shedding rusty water and they stood looking at it the way men will stand and look at a thing they have removed from the earth, as if it might bear some inscription or testament, and it bore none. It was only pipe. Whatever it had to say it had said into the ground for sixty years and the ground kept its own counsel.

While Wes was rasping the burrs off the cut ends Charlie stepped up out of the hole and went to the truck for the thermos and that was when he saw the snake. It had come down into the cool of the trench at the far end where the water stood and it lay half coiled with its head raised, a bullsnake four foot or better, old and heavybodied and patient.

Wes, said Charlie.

I see him.

Charlie picked up the sharpshooter spade.

Leave him be, said Wes.

He's in the hole.

It's his hole too. He was down there fore we ever was. Wes reached and got the mattock and put the flat of the haft under the snake's middle and lifted it and the snake poured itself off the haft into the weeds along the lane and went away through the dry grass without hurry, like a thing that had business elsewhere and had only been delayed in it.

Daddy'd of chopped him, Charlie said.

Daddy chopped a lot of things.

That's the God's truth.

They sat in the shade of the truck and drank coffee out of the thermos cap, passing it between them, and Charlie took his cigarettes out of his shirtpocket and lit one and sat smoking with his forearms across his knees.

Man called me again Tuesday, he said.

Wes didnt say anything.

You hear me?

I heard you.

He's gone to twelve hundred a acre. That's for all of it, house and water rights and the whole shootin match. That's better than four hundred thousand dollars, Wes. Split two ways.

I can cipher.

I know you can cipher. I'm wonderin if you can hear.

Wes turned the thermos cap in his fingers and poured the dregs out into the dust and watched the dust ball it up.

What's he want it for, he said.

What do you care what he wants it for?

I care.

Charlie smoked. Houses, he said. What does anybody want anything for anymore. He'll doze the trees and lay it off in five acre ranchettes and folks from El Paso'll come up here and build them a Santa Fe style house and keep two llamas and a horse they dont ride.

And the water?

The water goes with it. Water's half of what he's payin for. More than half.

Wes screwed the cap back on the thermos and set it in the dirt between his boots.

Daddy's buried up yonder, he said. And Mama. And his daddy. You aim to sell them too?

That aint fair and you know it.

I dont know what's fair. I'm askin.

The graves get deeded out. A easement. They do it all the time. You could visit whenever you wanted.

Visit, said Wes.

Yes, visit. God amighty, Wes. People visit graves. That's what graves are for. They aint for farmin around. He pitched the cigarette into the lane and stood and picked up his shovel. We goin to fix this pipe or set here and study about the dead?

We're goin to fix the pipe.

Then let's fix it.

They set the new section, a length of pipe Wes had hauled from town in the spring against this very day, and they fitted the repair clamps over the joints and tightened the bolts in turn, each man on his knees in the mud working the wrenches, their hats off and the sweat running on them, and they did not talk while they worked because the work was old between them and needed no language. They had done this or things like this all the days of their boyhood, the one holding while the other turned, and their hands knew each other's hands the way the hands of old dancing partners are said to do, and whatever was crossways between them lay up out of the trench in the heat and waited and the work went on beneath it.

At noon Wes walked up to the well-house and threw the breaker and they stood over the open trench and watched the clamped joints while the line filled and groaned and ticked. A thin spray stood up off the near clamp like a wren's plume and Charlie went down into the hole and put a quarter turn on two of the bolts and the plume shrank and quit. Water moved in the pipe under their feet with a sound like a thing alive and the line held.

Well, said Charlie.

Well.

She held.

She held.

Let it run while we eat. If she's goin to let go she'll let go agin a full head.

They went up to the house and washed at the spigot by the porch and Wes brought out what there was, light bread and bologna and a jar of his neighbor's chowchow and the last of a pot of beans, and they ate on the porch in the shade with their hats on the floor beside them. Below them the orchard stood shimmering in the heat and beyond it the valley ran south in benches of pale grass and farther yet the desert, white and trembling, like something at the bottom of a sea drained off in an age before men, which it was.

You remember haulin them tubes, Charlie said. Settin siphon tubes with Daddy fore daylight.

I remember.

He'd stand there and watch you set ever one. Wouldnt say nothin. Just stand there. And if you got it primed wrong he'd take it away from you and prime it hisself and hand it back and still not say nothin.

That was his way of teachin.

That was his way of everthing.

Wes folded a piece of the bread and wiped his plate with it.

He wasnt a hard man, he said.

I never said he was hard. I said he was quiet. There's a difference, but I be dog if I always knew what it was when I was nine year old standin in a ditch in the dark.

He thought the place would talk for him. That if he showed you the place that was the same as tellin you.

Charlie looked at him. Maybe it was, he said. Maybe that's the trouble. Maybe it told you and it didnt tell me, or it told us two different things.

What did it tell you?

Charlie was a long time answering. He sat looking down the rows. It told me, he said, that a man can pour his whole life into eighty acres of ground and the ground dont pour nothin back. It takes the sweat and it takes the money and it takes the woman's good years and then a freeze comes in April or the water table drops another ten foot and it just looks at you. It dont owe you and it knows it dont owe you. Daddy died standin in that equipment shed with a grease gun in his hand and he was four years behind at the bank when he done it. You call that a covenant. I call it a debt that compounds.

Nobody made him stay.

Nobody made him. Charlie laughed, but not as if anything were funny. No. Nobody made him. He looked at his brother. Nobody's makin you either, he said. That's what I come up here to say. Nobody is makin you. You're fifty-two years old and you're alone in this house and Carol's gone and June's in Lubbock and she aint comin back, Wes, she aint never comin back to pick apples, and you're out here clampin your daddy's pipe with your daddy's wrenches and tellin yourself it means somethin.

It does mean somethin.

What. Tell me what. I'd purely love to know.

Wes set his plate on the floor of the porch. Out in the yard a lizard ran along the top of the gatepost and stopped and did its small pushups and ran on. He watched it go.

You want me to say it pays, he said. It dont pay. You want me to say June'll take it on. She wont. You want me to say I dont get lonesome out here. I get lonesome enough to talk to that snake we dug up this mornin. But I'll tell you what I know. Them trees out there was planted by a man been dead forty years and they're bearin yet. There's water been comin down that ditch since before there was a state to record the right in. I turn a valve and somethin happens in the world that aint about me. The water goes down the row and the ground takes it and the trees take it up and along about September there's apples, and not one part of that needs my opinion. A man can be a part of a thing like that or he can sell it. But he cant do both, and he cant buy it back after. There wont be no buyin it back. They'll doze them trees in a day and a half. They'll cap that ditch and pipe the water to a subdivision and in twenty years there wont be a soul alive that knows there was ever a orchard in this valley at all. It'll be like it never was. And I'm just contrary enough to think that whether a thing ever was or not ought to depend on somethin besides money.

Charlie sat quiet for a while. Then he said: That's a fine speech. I notice it dont have my name in it anywheres.

Your name's on the deed.

That's right. It is. Half of all of it. The debt half too.

I know what you're owed.

Do you.

Yes.

Charlie picked up his hat and turned it in his hands and looked into it like a man consulting some text printed in the sweatband.

I aint tryin to take nothin from you, Wes. I'm tryin to get out from under somethin fore it falls on the both of us. There's a difference there too.

I know it.

Do you.

Yes, said Wes. I do.

They went back down in the worst of the heat and shoveled the spoil back over the pipe and tamped it and laid the cut-out section of old pipe in the truckbed where it rang dully on the steel, and they walked the lower rows together opening the gate valves at the head of each row, and the water came. It came talking in the pipe and then welling up silent at the risers and moving out down the furrows in fingers and tongues, finding its grade the way water has found its grade since before there was anything to watch it do so, darkening the pale dust, and the dry ground made a small seething sound as it drank that you could hear if you stood still, and both of them stood still and heard it.

There was light yet when they finished but it was the long copper light of evening and the shadows of the trees lay clean across the wet furrows and quail were calling from the arroyo. They stood at the truck and Charlie smoked and they watched the water glint far down the rows where the sun caught it.

That clamp wont last, Charlie said.

I know it.

Couple of years. Maybe three.

Maybe three.

Then it's the whole line. Eight, ten thousand dollars of pipe. Plus the trenchin.

I know what it is.

Charlie smoked. The smoke stood off his cigarette dead still in the evening air and then bent and went away.

I aint goin to call him back yet, he said. The man. I aint sayin no. I'm sayin not yet.

All right.

You understand me. I aint sayin no.

I understand you.

Come September if them old Jonathans dont make, or if that well drops another foot, we're goin to have this conversation again, and it's goin to come out different.

Come September, said Wes.

Charlie dropped the cigarette and trod it out. He looked at his brother and he looked out over the orchard where the dead trees stood among the living, gray and patient, like the orchard's own memory of itself, and the living trees dark and full and the water running silver down the rows in the last light.

It is pretty, he said. I'll give it that. It was always pretty. That was half the trouble.

He got in the truck and cranked it and backed around in the lane and Wes raised one hand and Charlie raised one finger off the wheel the way their father had done, the way men in that country had saluted one another out of trucks for a hundred years, and pulled out onto the county road, and Wes stood listening to the truck run down through the gears at the cattleguard and out along the valley until the sound of it was lost among the sounds of the evening.

He walked the rows once more in the dusk. At the foot of the lowest row he knelt and put his hand in the water where it came on, cool out of the deep rock, older than the trees, older than the names in the deedbooks at the county seat, older than any name at all, and he held his hand in it while the dark came down out of the mountains and the first bats dropped and swerved above the rows, hunting whatever rose from the wet ground. The moon was coming up over the eastern rim, a day past full, and it laid a thin light on the water so that the furrows ran like seams of ore down the dark of the orchard, and a man seeing it who knew nothing of pumps or clamps or debt might have thought the earth itself was venting some bright element it had held against just such a night. He rose and wiped his hand on his thigh and stood a while longer. Far off a truck on the highway, then nothing. The water moved past his boots in the dark with a small incessant whisper and what it whispered it had whispered to his father and would whisper when there was no one of his name to kneel and hear it, and he knew that, and knew that knowing it changed nothing, and he turned at last and walked up through the moonlit rows to the dark house to wait for September like everything else that lived in that valley, the trees and the snake and the quail in the arroyo, all of them alike in the one same patience, waiting on water and weather and on the turning of a world that did not consult them, and never had, and asked of them only that they be there when it came round.
