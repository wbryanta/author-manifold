The snow had begun as rain at noon, the way late storms always did, pretending to be something gentler before showing its true face. By four it was sleet ticking against the shed's metal roof like thrown gravel, and by six, when Margaret Tully came out with the lantern and the lambing bag, the world had gone white and sideways, the yard light a smeared halo, the fence posts wearing six inches on their northern shoulders. April twenty-seventh. The forsythia by the porch had bloomed three days ago. Now its yellow was buried under snow the texture of wet cement.

Inside the shed the air was warmer by twenty degrees and dense with the smell of lanolin and ammonia and molasses-sweet grain, and the ewes turned their long faces toward her in the lantern light, fifty-some pregnant animals murmuring low in their chests. Daniel was already there. She heard him before she saw him, the soft particular voice he used with the sheep, lower than his speaking voice, almost a hum. He was sixteen and had grown four inches since October, since the funeral, and his wrists hung out of his father's barn coat, which he had taken to wearing without ever asking her and which she had never said a word about.

"Forty-one's started," he said. "Pawing and circling since I came out. And the big Border Leicester cross, the one with the blue mark, she's off by herself in the corner not eating."

"How long on forty-one?"

"Hour, maybe. Water bag about twenty minutes ago."

Margaret hung the lantern on the nail by the first jug and looked down the row of pens, the plywood dividers Tom had built nine years ago, his pencil marks still visible on some of them where he'd measured twice the way he measured everything twice. Ten jugs, four occupied already with ewes and their day-old lambs, the heat lamps glowing red over the corners where the lambs lay folded like dropped laundry. The forecast had said six to ten inches. The radio at supper had revised it: eighteen to twenty-four, winds gusting fifty, power outages likely across three counties. And nineteen ewes still due in the next ten days, and sheep being what they were, the barometric drop would pull half of them into labor tonight. Tom used to say storms shook the lambs loose like apples.

She did not let herself finish the thought of Tom, the way she had learned not to finish those thoughts, the way you learned not to put weight on a bad knee.

"Get the warming box plugged in before we lose power," she said. "And fill both kettles and put them on the stove in the house. If the lines go we'll want hot water already made."

"You think the lines'll go?"

"I think they'll go by nine."

He went, and she watched him cross the yard in the throw of the security light, leaning into the wind, the snow erasing him by degrees until the house door swallowed what was left. Then she let herself into the big pen and walked slowly through the flock to ewe forty-one, who stood in the far corner with her neck stretched out and her upper lip curled back, straining.

The first lamb came easy. It came the way lambs were supposed to come and so rarely did, two small hooves and a nose laid neatly on top of them like a diver, and the ewe did the work herself while Margaret knelt on the straw and did nothing but watch, and the lamb slid out into the world in its glistening caul at six fifty-five by her watch, steaming in the cold air, shaking its wet head so its ears slapped. The ewe turned and began the loud guttural chuckling that ewes made only at this one moment in their lives, a sound like nothing else, and licked the lamb's face clear. Margaret stripped the membrane back from its nostrils anyway, out of habit, and checked under its tail. A ewe lamb. Good. The second twin came eleven minutes later, hind feet first, which was fine, which was nothing, and she pulled it gently down in an arc when the ewe strained and swung it once by its back legs to drain its airways and laid it at its mother's head.

By the time Daniel came back with the kettles seen to, she had the family moved into a jug, the lamb's navels dipped in iodine, the ewe's udder stripped to make sure the wax plugs were out of her teats and the colostrum flowing thick and yellow as cream.

"Two on the ground," she said. "Both ewes. Both nursing."

"That's good money," Daniel said, which is what his father would have said, in his father's exact cadence, and Margaret busied herself with the iodine cup so he could not see her face.

The power went out at eight forty.

It went the way it always went, no flicker of warning, the heat lamps dying all at once and the yard light vanishing from the cracks around the door, and the shed dropped into a darkness so complete that for a moment there was nothing in the world but the sound of fifty sheep breathing and the wind working at the roof seams like something trying to get in. Then the lantern, which ran on kerosene because Tom had never trusted anything that needed an outlet, asserted its small yellow circle, and Margaret heard Daniel exhale.

"Generator," she said.

"I know."

"You remember how—"

"Dad showed me," he said, too quickly, a door shutting. "I've got it."

She did not say be careful and she did not say take the good flashlight, though she wanted to say both, and he went out into it. The generator was in the machine shed forty feet away and she stood in the lambing shed door and watched his flashlight beam swing through the horizontal snow until it disappeared behind the corner, and she stood there longer than she needed to, the cold coming through her coat, counting in her head the way she had not been able to stop doing since the morning in November when Tom had gone out to fix the loader and not come back in, when an hour had passed and she had told herself an hour was nothing and it had been everything, the artery in his chest already finished with its work by the time she crossed the yard. Aneurysm, the coroner said. Nothing anyone could have done, the coroner said, as if that were a comfort, as if doing were not the entire architecture of their lives.

The generator caught on the third pull. She heard it even through the storm, the ragged cough and then the steady drone, and the heat lamps bloomed red again down the row of jugs, and she went back inside and was checking the Border Leicester cross when Daniel returned, snow packed into the folds of his collar, his ears scarlet.

"Tank's about three-quarters," he said. "Maybe ten hours if we run everything. Six if we're stupid about it."

"Then we won't be stupid. Heat lamps and the warming box only. We'll work by lantern."

"There's the spare diesel in the—"

"That's for the tractor if we have to plow out for the milk truck road. Lamps and the box. Come look at this ewe."

The Border Leicester cross was a big animal, a hundred eighty pounds, and she was down in the corner pen on her side with her head thrown back, and Margaret had been watching her for ten minutes and had seen her strain four times and produce nothing, not a water bag, not a foot, nothing, and the ewe's sides heaved with a labor that was going nowhere.

"Something's wrong with the presentation," Margaret said. "Hold her head."

She scrubbed her hand and arm at the bucket, the water already cooling, worked the lubricant up past her wrist, and knelt behind the ewe in the straw. Daniel straddled the ewe's neck and held her, murmuring his low not-quite-words, and Margaret slid her hand inside and closed her eyes the way Tom had taught her to do, because the eyes wanted to help and could not, and the hand had to learn to see on its own.

What her hand found was a tangle. A head, turned back along a body. Legs—she counted slowly, tracing each one up to its shoulder or its hip the way you had to, because in a tangle of twins the legs lied to you, offered themselves in pairs that belonged to different lambs—three legs, four, and the joints told her two were front and two were hind and they did not belong together. Twins coming at once, jammed in the pelvis like two people trying through a doorway, and the first one's head bent back besides.

"Twins," she said. "Tangled. I have to sort them."

"What do I do?"

"Exactly what you're doing. If she thrashes, lean on her shoulder, not her neck."

The wind hit the shed broadside and the whole structure groaned and ticked. Margaret pushed the nearest lamb back between contractions, into the room of the uterus where there was space to work, and the ewe groaned and strained against her arm, and the contraction clamped down on her wrist hard enough that she felt her own pulse beating against the bone. She waited it out. You could not fight a contraction; you could only wait, and work in the slack between them, and so the work went in stolen intervals, thirty seconds at a time, her cheek pressed against the ewe's damp flank, her arm gone in nearly to the elbow. She found the bent-back head and cupped the lamb's muzzle in her palm, her thumb and middle finger in the eye sockets, the only grip that worked, and drew it around in a slow arc until the nose pointed true. Then the legs. The right legs, his legs, this lamb's legs, traced from shoulder to fetlock twice to be sure, and she cupped each small sharp hoof in her palm as she brought it forward so it would not tear the ewe, and somewhere in the middle of it she realized she was talking, narrating in a voice barely above breath, and that Daniel was listening with a stillness that was its own kind of attention.

"Head back is the worst common one," she heard herself say. "Breech is worse but rarer. You always push back before you reposition. You never pull against a contraction. You never pull a head without at least one leg or you'll hang it up at the shoulders."

"Dad said you were better at this than him," Daniel said. "Smaller hands."

"Your father said a lot of things."

"He said it like it bothered him," Daniel said, and there was almost a smile in it, the first she'd heard in days, and then the lamb was aligned, nose and two forefeet like a diver, and on the next contraction she drew it down and out and into the lantern light, limp and yellow-stained and unmoving.

"Towel," she said, and it was already in her hand; he was already there. She cleared the airway, swung the lamb, laid it down and rubbed its ribcage hard with the towel, harsh circles, the way you'd never touch a thing so small unless you knew that gentleness was the enemy now. Nothing. She took the piece of straw from her coat pocket—she had carried a piece of straw in that pocket since February out of pure superstition—and tickled the inside of the lamb's nostril, and the lamb sneezed, and shuddered, and drew a breath that lifted its whole ribcage, and bawled.

The second twin came in four minutes, and the third—a third, undeclared, unsuspected, the reason for all that crowding—came seven minutes after that, small as a cat, and the ewe lay flat and exhausted with three lambs steaming in the straw beside her, and Daniel laughed out loud, a bark of disbelief, and Margaret sat back on her heels with her arm slimed to the shoulder and felt, for the first time in five months, something that was adjacent to joy.

It did not last, because nothing was allowed to last on a night like this, but she noted it. She had gotten into the habit of noting such things, the way a person keeps receipts.

By midnight there were four more on the ground and two more ewes in labor and the storm had stopped pretending it would ease. The thermometer by the door read nineteen degrees. The generator droned on in the machine shed, and twice Daniel went out through the drifts to check its fuel, and twice he came back caked white, and the second time she made him stand under a heat lamp himself until his hands worked again, and he stood there in the red glow among the lambs, his eyes closed, swaying slightly with exhaustion, and she thought: he is sixteen. She thought: he should be asleep, he should be failing a math test tomorrow, he should be texting some girl. And underneath those thoughts the other one, the one she could not afford: I cannot do this without him, and it is wrong that I need him this way, and I need him.

At half past midnight the small triplet started to go down. She found it flat in the corner of the jug, away from the heat lamp, its mouth cold when she put her finger in—and a cold mouth was the whole verdict, a cold mouth meant the lamb had stopped being able to make its own heat and was sliding toward a place it would not come back from. She carried it to the warming box, the plywood chamber with the hairdryer Tom had rigged through a hole in its side, and while it warmed she milked colostrum from the ewe into a jar, the ewe too tired to object, and showed Daniel the stomach tube.

"You have to know this," she said. "Sit down. Hold him like this, sitting up, like he's a person."

"What if it goes in the lungs?"

"That's why you measure first." She laid the tube along the lamb's body from mouth to last rib and marked the length with her thumb. "And you watch him swallow it. He'll gulp at it. If it's in the airway he'll cough and fight and you'll know. If he goes quiet and keeps swallowing, you're in the stomach. And you check—" she put the end of the tube to her ear before attaching the syringe— "you listen. Stomach sounds like a stomach. Crackle and gurgle. Lungs sound like wind."

"Like tonight," Daniel said.

"Like tonight."

He threaded the tube himself, his big new hands suddenly precise, his face bent close to the lamb's in the lantern light, and she watched him listen at the tube's end with an expression of such fierce concentration that her chest hurt, and he said, "Stomach," and she checked and he was right, and they ran two ounces of his mother's colostrum into the lamb slow and steady, and within the hour the lamb was up on its absurd legs in the warming box, butting at the walls, looking for an udder, furious at the world, alive.

"You know what Dad would say," Daniel said, watching it.

"What would he say."

"That a lamb that mad is a lamb that lives."

"He stole that from his father," Margaret said. "Your great-grandfather said it first, and probably he stole it too." She closed the lid of the box partway. "Nothing on this farm is original. Including the way we're both still standing here when one of us could be sleeping in shifts. Go in for two hours. I'll wake you."

"You go in. I've got this."

"Daniel."

"Mom." He said it flatly, not arguing, just laying the word down between them like a tool on a bench. "I'm not going in. There's two in labor and you can't hold a ewe and pull at the same time and the last time you slept was—when? Tuesday?" He picked up the iodine cup and refilled it from the bottle without being asked. "We do it together or it doesn't get done. That's just the math."

That's just the math. That was Tom's phrase too, Tom who priced hay and culled ewes and refused vacations all with that same gentle ruthless arithmetic, and Margaret stood looking at her son and understood that she could fight the grief or fight the storm but not both, and not him too. "All right," she said. "Together. But you eat something. There's the sandwiches from supper in the bag."

They ate standing up, leaning against the grain bin, and outside the wind found a new pitch, something almost vocal, and the snow hissed against the north wall in waves. The flock dozed and shifted. A new lamb in jug three was learning to bleat and doing it constantly, experimentally, like a child with a whistle. Daniel ate three sandwiches and drank the thermos coffee she'd have once forbidden him and said, with his eyes on the middle distance, "I keep waiting for him to come through the door."

Margaret stopped chewing.

"Every time the wind bangs it," Daniel said. "I look up. Every single time. It's like my neck does it without me." He folded the waxed paper into smaller and smaller squares. "Does that stop?"

She considered lying to him. He was sixteen and exhausted and it was one in the morning in a blizzard and a lie would have been a kindness, and he would have known it was a lie, because he was his father's son and could count.

"No," she said. "It changes. It gets further apart. But I don't think it stops. I looked up when you came in with the kettles." She drank her coffee. "I don't mind it anymore, most days. It's the only time I get to expect him."

Daniel nodded slowly, and put the folded paper in his pocket, and did not say anything else, and that was the whole conversation, and it was also, she understood, the longest either of them had spoken about it since the funeral, and the shed held it the way the shed held everything, warmth and ammonia and breath, without comment.

The hard one came at three. An old ewe, a six-year-old who'd never given trouble in her life, and the lamb presented enormous and single, a great blunt head and one leg, the other shoulder locked back, and this time the lubricant was nearly gone and the water in the bucket was cold and Margaret's hands had been wet and chilled for nine hours and would not entirely obey her. She worked at the locked shoulder until her fingers cramped, lost it, found it, lost it again when the ewe went down thrashing, and Daniel held the animal and Margaret heard her own breathing go ragged and heard, in her head, with terrible clarity, the silence where Tom's voice should have been, the calm running commentary he kept up over her shoulder on the bad ones, easy now Meg, you've got room, take the time the ewe gives you, and the silence rose up in her throat like water and for a moment, kneeling in the bloody straw at three in the morning in the lantern light, she could not move at all.

"Take the time the ewe gives you," Daniel said quietly.

She looked up at him. He was watching her steadily over the ewe's shoulder, his father's coat sleeves shoved past his elbows, his face very young and very still.

"He used to say that," Daniel said. "I figured maybe you needed somebody to say it."

She got the leg. It came forward in her cupped palm on the next slack interval as if it had been waiting for permission, and the lamb came huge and slow and alive, a single ram lamb of thirteen pounds at least, and the old ewe turned to it chuckling, and Margaret sat back against the pen boards and let her arms hang and laughed once, shakily, at nothing, at everything, and Daniel grinned at her, white teeth in a filthy face, and outside the wind went on and meant nothing by it.

Toward five the storm began to lose conviction. The gusts came further apart, and in the gaps between them was a new quality of silence, thick and feathered, the sound of snow falling straight down. The generator droned. Eleven lambs had come since dark and ten were living and one—a twin from the last ewe of the night, born too small, born already half gone—had not, and Daniel had carried it out and set it on the snow behind the shed without being told how or where, which meant he had watched his father do it, which meant he carried more of these nights inside him than she had known. The dead were the rent. Tom had said that too. You did not farm without paying it, and you did not let it bankrupt you either.

When the last ewe was settled in the last jug with her single surviving lamb nursing hard, knees buckled, tail spinning like a wind-up toy, Margaret went to the shed door and pushed it open against the drift and stood looking out. The snow had gone fine and slow, almost ornamental. The east was lightening behind the cloud, a gray that would be a paler gray and then, eventually, day. The yard was transformed, every fence wearing its white twin, the buried forsythia a soft mound, the world made strange and clean and indifferent. Behind her the shed muttered and rustled, full of new voices.

Daniel came and stood beside her in the doorway, and they looked at it together, and she could smell the cold coming off the snow and the warmth coming off him, lanolin and iodine and sweat.

"Chores in two hours anyway," he said. "Hardly worth sleeping."

"Worth it," she said. "Two hours is two hours. I'll take the first watch."

"You took the last three."

"I'm the mother. It's not a democracy."

He laughed, the real laugh, the one that had gone missing in November and come back tonight on its own legs like everything else born in that shed, and he went toward the house through the unbroken snow, leaving a single line of deep tracks, and Margaret stood in the doorway a while longer, watching the light come up gray and slow over the buried fields, counting nothing, expecting no one, and behind her, in the warm dark, a lamb that had not existed yesterday called out, and its mother answered, and that was the sound she stood and listened to until the snow stopped altogether and the morning, such as it was, arrived.
