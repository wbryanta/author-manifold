The dining room table went first, which Diane had not expected. She'd thought it would be the tools in the garage, the way men had been circling them since noon, picking up her father's planes and squinting down the soles like they were sighting rifles. But it was the table—two young men in matching gray T-shirts angling it through the front door while a woman who could not have been thirty held the storm door open with her hip and called instructions nobody followed. The table had a leaf in the basement that Diane had forgotten to bring up, and she almost said something, almost ran down the stairs to get it, and then she thought: let them find their own ways to make it bigger. Let them discover the trick of the latch underneath, the one her father used to do blind, reaching under the apron with one hand while he talked about Nixon or the price of corn, the table coming apart with a groan and sliding open on its wooden runners like something being born.

The woman from the estate company, Sandy, had told her she didn't have to be there. A lot of families don't come, Sandy said. Honestly, hon, most don't. It's easier on everybody. Sandy had a lanyard and a fanny pack heavy with bills and a system of colored dots—green for furniture, orange for kitchen, yellow for the things she called smalls, which turned out to mean everything that could be held in one hand, which turned out to mean most of a life. Diane had driven up from Pittsburgh anyway, two and a half hours on I-70 and then the state route past the grain elevator and the Methodist church and the Marathon station that used to be a Sohio, and she had parked across the street from the house she grew up in because her own driveway, her mother's driveway, was full of strangers' cars.

It was the last Saturday in September and the maples on Linden Street had started to turn at their crowns, that first rusting at the top like a match held to paper. The afternoon was warm but the light had already gone autumnal, slanted and apologetic, the kind of light her mother used to call good drying weather, back when there was a clothesline strung between the garage and the walnut tree and Diane's job was to hand up pins from the canvas bag. The clothesline was gone. The walnut tree was gone too, taken down in 2009 after the ice storm split it, and her mother had mourned it for a year, longer than she mourned some people, and had kept a bowl of its last walnuts on the kitchen windowsill until they went chalky and Diane's brother threw them out on a visit, which her mother never quite forgave.

Diane stood in the front hall with a styrofoam cup of coffee Sandy had brought her from the gas station and watched a man in a Bengals jacket carry out the brass floor lamp from the den. He had it over his shoulder like a fence post. Twelve dollars, the tag said. Her mother had bought that lamp at Lazarus in Columbus in 1971, a special trip, and had been so pleased with it she'd left the price tag on the cord for a month so visitors might happen to see it and know it was new and from a good store. Forty-some years of evenings under that lamp—her father with the Zanesville paper, her mother with her crossword and later, after her eyes went, with the large-print library books that came in the mail in plastic sleeves. Twelve dollars. Diane found that she could not decide whether this was an outrage or exactly right, whether the lamp was being robbed or released, and she had been deciding and undeciding this about everything in the house all day.

A woman about her own age, maybe a little younger, stopped beside her with an armload of dish towels and a Pyrex bowl, the yellow one from the nesting set. "Did you know the lady who lived here?" the woman asked, friendly, just making talk.

"She was my mother," Diane said.

The woman's face did the thing all their faces did, a flinch and then a softening, and she said she was so sorry, she hadn't realized, and was it all right, was it okay that she—and she lifted the bowl slightly, as if offering to put it back, to put everything back, to walk it all backward into the cupboards.

"It's fine," Diane said. "She'd be glad it's getting used. She hated waste." Which was true, and which Diane had said maybe nine times since noon, and which worked on people like a blessing; they nodded and relaxed and carried their armloads out to their cars feeling not like scavengers but like stewards. Her mother had hated waste. Lorraine Hartzler had washed and reused aluminum foil, had saved the rubber bands from the newspaper on the kitchen doorknob until the doorknob looked like it was wearing a sea anemone, had kept a drawer of bread bags and a drawer of bows steamed carefully off of birthday packages going back to the Ford administration. Some of those bows were on a card table in the dining room right now, in a shoebox marked SMALLS .25 EACH OR BOX $3, and so far nobody had wanted them, and Diane was trying not to care, and was failing in a small persistent way, like a stone in a shoe.

Her brother had not come. Gary lived outside Phoenix and had a bad back and a timeshare situation that week that he had explained at length on the phone, and Diane had said it's fine, Gary, honestly, there's nothing to do but stand around, and he had said send me anything you think I'd want, and she had spent an evening in August walking through the house with her phone, texting him photos. Dad's tackle box? Dad's bowling trophy from the Elks? The painting of the covered bridge from over the davenport? He'd wanted almost nothing. The flag from their father's funeral, which was already at his house. The tackle box, in the end, which she had shipped at a cost greater than its value, the lures rattling inside like teeth. She did not blame him exactly. He had done his grieving at the funeral in March and considered the matter, if not closed, then at least filed. Gary had always been able to file things. Diane had inherited their mother's archive instead, the part of the brain that keeps everything, the bows and the bread bags, and so it was Diane who stood in the front hall while a teenage girl bought her grandmother's hand mirror for fifty cents and asked Sandy if it was real silver.

"Plate," Sandy said, without looking up from making change. Sandy knew everything. Sandy could tell Haviland from Homer Laughlin across a room. Sandy had run, by her own count, over four hundred sales in Muskingum and Licking and Coshocton counties, and she moved through the house with a tenderness Diane had not expected, straightening doilies, repositioning a teacup so its handle faced out. The first day they met, walking the rooms in August, Sandy had stopped in front of the china hutch and said, "Your mom took care of her things," and Diane had felt absurdly proud, like she'd been told her mother was beautiful.

Around two o'clock the piano people came. They were a couple in their forties with a daughter of about ten, and they had called ahead about the spinet, and the daughter sat down on the bench—the bench with the needlepoint cover her mother had stitched during the long winter Diane's father was at the VA in Chillicothe—and played something halting and modern that Diane didn't recognize, and then, finding her footing, the first lines of "Für Elise," and the sound of it in the half-empty living room was so exactly the sound of 1974, of Diane herself at ten with Mrs. Pruett's pencil marks in the margins of the lesson book, that Diane had to go stand in the kitchen. She had quit piano at twelve. Her mother had let her quit but had kept the piano tuned for forty years anyway, paying a man named Dale to come twice a year and lean into its strings, and when Diane once asked her why, her mother had said, "In case somebody wants to play," in a tone that closed the subject. Nobody had wanted to play. And now somebody did, this girl with a purple scrunchie on her wrist, and her parents were counting out bills into Sandy's hand, and Dale, wherever he was, would never come again, and Diane stood at the kitchen sink and looked out the window at the place where the clothesline had been and let herself cry for ninety seconds, timed almost formally, the way you'd let a kettle boil, and then turned off the faucet of it and blew her nose on a paper towel from the roll that was itself for sale, the wooden holder anyway, orange dot, two dollars.

The kitchen was the hardest room and she had mostly stayed out of it. It still smelled like her mother's kitchen, which is to say like fifty years of coffee and onions and Joy dish soap settled into the plaster, and people kept opening the drawers, which was the point, that was what they were there to do, but every drawer scraping open was a sound from her childhood being operated by the wrong hands. The junk drawer with its pencil stubs and its church keys. The drawer with the good tablecloths. A man bought the entire contents of the utensil drawer for five dollars, swept it into a grocery bag like he was clearing a poker table, and in that bag, Diane knew, was the wooden spoon, the one with the handle burned to a taper on one side from being left against the lip of a pot sometime around 1968, the spoon her mother had stirred everything with, vegetable soup and fudge and the paste for paper-mâché the year Diane was a globe for the school pageant—and Diane almost said wait. The word got as far as her teeth. Then the man was out the door and down the steps, and the spoon was gone to wherever spoons go, and she thought, you cannot keep a spoon and call it a mother. She had told herself this kind of thing all day. Sometimes it worked.

She had kept some things, of course. There was a box in her trunk across the street: the recipe tin with her mother's handwriting going from a young woman's loops to an old woman's wires across three hundred index cards; the photo albums; her father's watch; her mother's wedding ring and the cameo from her mother's mother; the afghan from the back of the davenport, crocheted in colors that had no name outside of 1973. One box. She had been severe with herself, because she had read the articles and heard the warnings, women her age with storage units full of their parents' houses, paying rent every month on rooms of sealed grief. One box, she'd said, and one box it was, and the discipline of it had felt almost athletic in August, and felt today, with the house draining out the front door, like a wound she'had given herself on purpose.

Arlene Kessler from next door came over around three, picking her way across the leaf-strewn grass with her cane, and stood with Diane on the porch. Arlene was eighty-six and had lived beside the Hartzlers since 1963, and she surveyed the strangers carrying out the card table and the box fan and the framed print of the covered bridge, and she said, "Well, I don't know how you stand it," which was somehow the most comforting thing anyone had said all day, because it did not pretend the afternoon was anything other than what it was.

"You stand it because you're standing it," Diane said. "There isn't a trick."

"Your mother sat right there," Arlene said, pointing her cane at the empty rectangle of unfaded paint where the glider had been until eleven-fifteen this morning, forty dollars, a young couple with a truck. "Every evening, June through September. We'd holler back and forth. She kept track of everybody's comings and goings on this street like it was her job." Arlene's eyes were wet but her voice was steady; she was of the generation that did not let the two things touch. "She knew before I did when my Roger's truck didn't come home. Came over with a coffee cake before the police even called." Diane had not known that. She was learning, this afternoon, how much of her mother had been distributed around this town, held in trust by neighbors and church women and the man at the pharmacy, and how the estate sale was only the visible liquidation, the dots and tags, while the other estate—the coffee cakes, the casseroles, the kept track of—could not be sold or carried out, and would just slowly stop being mentioned, and then stop being remembered, and that this was not tragic, exactly. It was just how it went. It was the going itself.

A man bought her father's recliner for fifteen dollars. He sat in it first, right there in the den, rocked it once, pulled the lever so the footrest banged up, and Diane watched him do it from the doorway and thought of her father in that chair the last year of his life, 2004, smaller every month, the chair seeming to grow around him like a catcher's mitt, and how after he died her mother had not gotten rid of it, had not even moved it, had vacuumed around it for fourteen years and set the folded newspaper on its arm some mornings out of a habit she would not discuss. The man heaved himself up, satisfied. "These old La-Z-Boys," he said to Diane, "they don't make the mechanism like this anymore," and she said no, they surely don't, and helped him walk it through the door on its side, taking the weight of the part of it where her father's head had been.

By four the rooms had begun to echo. That was the thing nobody warned you about, the acoustics of it, how a house gave up its furniture and started to sound like a house being shown to buyers, all hard surfaces and footsteps, the past stripped out of the air itself. The living room held only the davenport now—too worn to sell, Sandy had warned her, they'd likely have to pay the haulers to take it—and the marks on the carpet where everything had stood, dark green rectangles in the sun-faded green, the room's whole history written in shadows of nap like the floor plan of a ruin. In her parents' bedroom the bed was gone and the dresser was gone, and the wallpaper behind where the headboard had been was bright, the roses on it still vivid, protected sixty years from the light, and Diane stood looking at that panel of unfaded roses for a long time, the wall remembering what the room had forgotten.

A little girl, four maybe, wandered into the hall with a snow globe from the smalls table, the one with the cardinal in it, Ohio's bird, that had sat on her mother's bedside table since some forgotten Christmas, and the girl's mother came hurrying after her saying put that down, honey, we're not—and Diane said, "She can have it." The mother protested, fishing in her purse, a quarter, the tag said a quarter, and Diane said, "Please. My mother would love that," and she meant it; she could hear Lorraine's voice exactly, give it to the child, what am I going to do with it, and the little girl shook the globe and the snow whirled around the cardinal, and Diane felt for a moment that the quarter-sized transactions of the afternoon had finally produced one correct thing, one object arriving precisely where it was supposed to go.

Gary called at four-thirty. She took it on the back step, looking at the garage with its doors open and its walls bare now, the pegboard with the painted outlines of tools that were no longer there, her father's careful silhouettes, hammer, hammer, hacksaw, like the chalk lines at a crime scene. "How's it going," Gary said, and she said it was going, almost everything was selling, Sandy thought they'd clear four, maybe five thousand after her cut, and Gary said that was better than he'd figured, and there was a silence, and then he said, "Is it bad?" in a voice she hadn't heard from him in years, his actual voice, the one from the bedroom across the hall, and she looked at the pegboard and said, "It's the right thing and it's bad. Both. The whole time." He was quiet, and then he said he should have come, and she said, "It wouldn't have been better with you here. It would just have been bad for two of us," which was generous and probably not true, and he knew it, and accepted it, because that was the trade they had been making their whole adult lives, and he said put a flower on Mom for me when you go past the cemetery, and she said she would, and they hung up, and she sat on the step a minute longer in the cooling light. Somebody had bought the bird bath. There was a circle of yellowed grass where it had stood, and she thought of her mother at the kitchen window doing dishes, narrating the birds like a sportscaster. There's that grackle again. He runs everybody off. He's a real stinker.

The last hour was markdowns, Sandy moving through the rooms with her marker, everything half off, and the late people came, the dealers and the deal-hunters, and the house gave up its dregs: the bows went, the whole box, three dollars, to a woman who said she wrapped presents for the nursing home, and Diane could have kissed her; the bread bags went to no one and Sandy bagged them for the trash without comment; the cedar chest went, twenty dollars, smelling when it was opened of cedar and mothballs and something underneath that was just her mother, just Lorraine, the smell of her closet, of being a child hidden among her dresses, and Diane stood at the window and watched two men slide the chest into a minivan and drive it away down Linden Street, taking the smell with it, the last of it, out past the Methodist church and gone.

At six Sandy counted out the cash box on the kitchen counter, dealing the bills into stacks with a bank teller's snap, and the total was four thousand, two hundred, and sixty dollars, and Sandy took her thirty percent and folded the rest into an envelope and pressed it into Diane's hands with both of hers, which was probably a thing she did at all four hundred sales and which worked anyway. "She had a lovely home," Sandy said. "You could tell she was happy in it." Then the gray-shirted young men carried the davenport out to the trailer for the haulers, the very last thing, and Diane signed a paper on the hood of Sandy's car, and then the cars were gone and the street was just the street, leaf-littered and amber, a dog barking somewhere over on Maple, the streetlight in front of Arlene's flickering on the way it had flickered on for fifty years.

Diane walked through the house once with the lights off. The rooms had given up their echo now and gone simply quiet, the way a field is quiet. In the kitchen the window over the sink held the last of the light, and on the sill, overlooked by everyone, missed by Sandy's dots, sat her mother's measuring cup, the tin one with the painted red handle worn to silver where her hand had gone around it ten thousand times, flour and sugar and rice, the cup that had measured out every Sunday of Diane's childhood. It was worth nothing. It would not have sold for the quarter. She stood holding it in the dim kitchen, and she understood that her box in the trunk had been waiting all day for one more thing, that the discipline had only ever been a way of saving room, and she put the cup in her coat pocket where it sat against her hip with a small tin weight, like a hand.

She locked the back door, then the front, and stood on the porch where the glider had been. Across the street her car waited under the maple, its windshield already scattered with the first let-go leaves. The house behind her was empty and the envelope in her purse was full and neither of those facts was the afternoon's true accounting, which had no figure, which had gone out the door in armloads to be set on other tables, played by other hands, shaken by a child to make the snow fly up around a cardinal in some house she would never see. Her mother had hated waste. Diane stood a moment longer in the blue hour, the porch boards familiar under her feet for the last time, and then she went down the steps and crossed the grass and did not look back at the windows, because she knew exactly what was in them now, which was nothing, which was the sky.
