The fork had belonged to his father, and before that to a man in Decorah whose name had worn off the case the way names wear off everything eventually, and when Werner Albrecht struck it against his knee that morning and held it to his ear, the A came up out of the steel the same as it always had, four hundred forty vibrations a second, indifferent to the date, indifferent to him. He stood in the gravel beside the truck and let the note die against the cold. Then he put the fork in the breast pocket of his coat, where it rode over his heart like a pen, and got in and drove.

October had come down hard on the county that year. The bean fields were off and the corn was going, and the combines moved through the distance trailing their slow ribbons of dust, so that the whole horizon seemed to be smoldering. Werner drove with the window cracked because he liked the smell of it, the dry sweetness of harvest, and because the heater in the truck had two settings, off and Florida, and he was not a man for Florida.

He had four stops written on the back of an envelope on the seat beside him, though he did not need the envelope. He had driven this route for fifty-one years, first with his father in the Studebaker and then alone, and the route had shrunk around him the way a sweater shrinks, the same shape but smaller, until what had once been thirty households between the river and the county line was now four, and after today would be none. He had told them all in August. He had said it plainly, the way you say a thing you have practiced: I'm hanging it up after the fall round. His hands had decided it for him, truthfully — the left one especially, which had begun to tremble in the mornings until coffee settled it, and a tuner who cannot trust his hands is a man telling lies for a living. But he had not said that part. He had said eighty-one years old and let them draw their own arithmetic.

The first stop was the Voss place, eleven miles out on the blacktop and then two on gravel, and Lorraine Voss had the storm door open before he had the truck in park, as if she had been standing behind it since dawn, which, knowing Lorraine, was not impossible.

"I made the coffee cake," she said. "So don't tell me you can't stay, because I made it."

"I can stay," Werner said.

The piano was where it had been since 1958, an upright Hamilton in walnut against the north wall of the parlor, under a gallery of photographs that had been added to and added to until the wall looked like a quilt. Lorraine's husband Dale, dead nine years. The four children in their school portraits, ascending the wall in chronological strata, the haircuts dating each layer like rings in a tree. The grandchildren, scattered now to Rochester and Denver and one of them, Lorraine had told him, all the way to Scotland, doing something with computers that she described with a wave of her hand, as if it were weather.

Nobody had played the Hamilton since the youngest, Connie, left for nursing school. That was — Werner did the figure without wanting to — thirty-four years ago. He had tuned it twice a year all the same, spring and fall, and Lorraine had paid him in cash and coffee cake all the same, and neither of them had ever once said the obvious thing, which was that they were maintaining an instrument the way some people maintain a grave.

He lifted the lid and removed the front board and set it against the wall. The smell came up at him, the smell of every old upright on his route: felt and dust and dried glue, wood that had spent sixty years swelling in July and shrinking in January, a smell like the inside of a violin or the back of a closet, the smell of patience. He pulled the bench around, laid out his roll of tools on the felt cloth he spread across the bass strings — the hammer, the mutes, the strip of red temperament felt his father had taught him to weave through the trichords with two fingers, fast as a woman braiding hair.

"How far's she drifted?" Lorraine asked from the doorway.

He struck the A above middle C, then the fork, and held them in his head against each other. "Quarter tone, about. She always goes flat in the bass over a summer. The humidity."

"Dale used to say this house breathes."

"It does. They all do."

He worked, and she went away to her kitchen, and the house filled with the sound that had been the sound of his life: single notes struck and struck and struck again, the slow grind of the pin turning in the block, the beat between two strings slowing like a heart calming — wah-wah-wah, wah... wah... — until it disappeared into stillness and the unison locked and rang clean. People thought tuning was a matter of the ear, and it was, but it was just as much a matter of the wrist, of setting the pin so it stayed, of leaving each string at rest and not merely in tune. His father used to say: anybody can get a string to the right pitch. The craft is getting it to stay there after you've gone. You tune for the silence after you, not the sound while you're here.

The treble on the Hamilton was bright and a little glassy, the hammers worn to hard grooves, and two of the dampers in the tenor were lazy, and the D-flat below middle C had a buzz he traced to a loose screw in the fallboard hinge, which he tightened, and none of it mattered, because no one was going to play it, and all of it mattered, because that was the agreement. When he finished he ran a slow chromatic scale up the whole keyboard, bottom A to top C, eighty-eight notes, the way he always ended, listening to the temperament hold hands with itself all the way up.

Lorraine had come back to the doorway somewhere in the middle of it. When the last C faded she said, "Play something, Werner."

"You know I don't play."

"Fifty years of this and you don't play. I never believed that."

"A man can know how a clock works," he said, putting the front board back, "and still not have anywhere to be."

She laughed, and they had the coffee cake at the kitchen table, and she paid him forty dollars from a flowered envelope in a drawer that he suspected had been filled and labeled in August, and at the door she took his hand in both of hers, which were small and warm and spotted as his own, and said, "Connie's been after me to sell it. The piano. I keep telling her, who'd tune the house then? A house needs a true note in it somewhere." Her eyes were wet, briefly, and then they weren't, because she was of his generation and they did not linger in such places. "You drive careful."

"Always have."

"That's a lie, I've seen you on the blacktop."

The second stop was the Pederson farm, except there were no Pedersons in it anymore; there was a young couple named Brask who had bought the acreage and the buildings two years back, and the piano — a Story & Clark console, mahogany, decent — had conveyed with the house like the water softener. The wife, Amber, met him in the yard with a baby on her hip and apologized for the dogs, who were not apologizing for themselves.

"I almost called you to cancel," she said, leading him in through a kitchen full of unpacked plans — paint chips fanned on the counter, a wall half stripped. "We don't play. I keep saying we'll get rid of it. But then I heard you were retiring, and I don't know. It seemed like the thing to do, having it done right once. Does that make sense?"

"It makes sense," Werner said.

The Story & Clark stood in what the Pedersons had called the front room, and the Brasks evidently called nothing yet, because it held only the piano, a treadmill, and boxes. Sigrid Pederson had played hymns on it every Sunday evening of her life with the metronomic faith of a woman who believed God preferred them slightly too slow. Her arthritis had taken the left hand first and then the right, and for the last years she had played only the melodies, single-finger, and had Werner tune the whole instrument anyway, all two hundred twenty strings, for the sake of the forty she could still reach.

The console had drifted badly — the new owners ran the furnace dry in winter, no humidifier, he could see it in the soundboard, a hairline check starting near the bass bridge — and he spent the better part of two hours on it, the baby observing him gravely from a bouncing chair in the doorway, the dogs eventually losing interest. Amber brought him water he hadn't asked for and stood listening for a while.

"It's kind of hypnotic," she said. "The same note over and over. How do you not go crazy?"

"You stop hearing the note," Werner said, easing the hammer a hair counterclockwise. "You hear what's between them. Two strings the same pitch, almost. The almost makes a wobble. The wobble's what you tune. When it quits wobbling, you're done."

"Huh," she said, and then, with the directness of the young, "Doesn't it bother you? Tuning them when nobody plays?"

He had been asked this before, in various clothes, by various decades of people, and he gave her the answer he had settled on around 1985. "A piano's the only piece of furniture that can be wrong," he said. "Everything else in a house just sits there. A piano sits there out of tune. Seems to me if you're going to keep one, you owe it the truth."

She laughed, and the baby laughed because she did, and when he finished and ran his chromatic scale she said, unprompted, "Maybe I'll learn. I always sort of wanted to," and he said that the piano would be ready whenever she was, which was a true thing and also the kind of true thing that mostly never came to pass, and he charged her thirty because she was young and the house was eating their money, and she tried to give him fifty and they settled where such things settle.

He ate his lunch in the truck on the shoulder of County 9, a sandwich Margaret would have made better, though Margaret had been gone six years and the sandwiches were his own fault now. Through the windshield the land went on doing what it did. A hawk rode the air above the ditch grass without moving its wings, just tilting, correcting, tilting, and Werner watched it the way he had watched things all his life, with a kind of professional interest in anything that held itself steady against drift.

The third stop he almost skipped. The old Maas schoolhouse — district number 4, closed and consolidated away in 1967 — had been bought decades back by the township and used for elections and 4-H and then, gradually, for nothing, and the piano in it was a beast, a five-foot-ten upright grand from before the first war, ebony gone gray, a Schiller out of Oregon, Illinois, with a tone in the bass like a church bell heard underwater. The township paid him fifteen dollars a year out of some line item nobody had ever bothered to delete, and he had a key on his ring, and twice a year he let himself in and tuned a piano for an audience of folding chairs.

The door swung in on the smell of chalk that had outlived the chalkboards. The light came through the tall west windows in long dusty planks and laid itself across the floor, and the Schiller stood at the front where the teacher's desk had been, and Werner stood in the doorway a moment because he was an old man now and had earned the right to stand in doorways.

His son had gone to school in this room. Three years of it, before consolidation, walking the mile and a quarter with the Halverson kids, and it was here — Werner had heard the story from the teacher, Miss Brandt, because Thomas never told stories on himself — that the boy had been found at the Schiller during recess, eight years old, picking out by ear the hymn from the previous Sunday, both hands, the harmony approximate but the approximation intelligent, and Miss Brandt had stood at the back of the room with her coffee going cold and let recess run eleven minutes long.

Werner had not taught him. That was the part people never believed, the way Lorraine never believed he couldn't play. The boy had been in the truck and in the parlors and under the lids since before he could walk; he had teethed, practically, on the sound of unisons closing; and one day the language that Werner could only spell, letter by letter, the boy stood up and spoke. It was no stranger, Werner had decided, than a carpenter's son growing up to live in the houses.

He tuned the Schiller and it fought him, the way it always fought him, the old pins gone smooth in their holes so that each one had to be set with a jeweler's caution, eased flat and drawn up and seated, and the tremble in his left hand came back in the middle of the tenor section and he laid the hammer down and stood at the window until it passed, looking out at the schoolyard where the pump still stood, handle rusted upright like a salute. Then he went back and finished. The bass on the Schiller, when it was right, was the best bass in the county, better than anything in the churches, better than the spinet monstrosities they sold now, and when he had it right he stood with one finger on the lowest E and held the key down and listened to the note go on and on into the empty room, the strings of the whole instrument humming sympathy above it, every wire faintly answering the one, and that, he thought, that under his finger, was the whole of what he had to say about the world and his time in it, and there was nobody in the folding chairs to hear, and he let the key up.

The church was last because the church had always been last. St. John's Lutheran, brick, 1911, on its low rise three miles from town with its cemetery folded around it on two sides like an arm. He had ordered his route around it for fifty years the way you save the best bread for the soup. The congregation was down to maybe forty on a good Sunday, sharing a pastor with two other towns, and the organ had needs beyond anyone's budget, so the piano carried the services now — a six-foot Yamaha grand, newer than anything else in the building including most of the congregation, bought in 1994 with memorial money.

Bought, in part — this was the fact he carried in the same pocket as the fork, the fact that rode over his heart — with money given in memory of Thomas Albrecht, 1949–1972. There had been other names on the gift list, the church did things properly, but Margaret had written the check at this very era's beginning, and the brass plate inside the lid said In memory of our members departed, and Werner knew which member he meant when he read it, and he supposed everyone was entitled to read a plate in their own direction.

The sanctuary was unlocked, as it had been unlocked since 1911. He let himself in through the side door by the sacristy and stood while his eyes adjusted. The afternoon had gone long and gold, and the west windows — Christ the Shepherd, Christ at Gethsemane, glass the color of jarred preserves — laid their stains across the pews, and the air was the air of all empty churches, cool, waxen, holding its breath.

The recital had been in May. 1961, so Thomas was eleven. Miss Halvorsen, the teacher in town he had been sent to once it was clear the matter exceeded the family, had arranged it: seven students, the church because it had the best instrument then available, which had been the old Kimball grand, gone now forty years. Folding tables of bars and coffee in the basement after. Margaret had made him wear the gray suit from his confirmation though it was already short in the sleeve, and the boy had walked up the center aisle in it with his wrists showing and his ears red, and had sat down at the Kimball, and Werner had gripped his own knees in the fourth pew, because he knew precisely, to the cent, how that piano stood — he had tuned it three days prior and then, unable to help himself, come back the morning of and checked it again — and he had thought, whatever happens now, the instrument will tell the truth under him. It was the only thing he had been able to give. He could not teach the boy a single phrase. He could give him a true keyboard, the way another father gives a level field.

Thomas had played the Schumann. "Träumerei," out of the Kinderszenen, slow, that melody that climbs and reaches and falls back, climbs and reaches further and falls back, like someone trying to remember something good. He had rushed nothing. Eleven years old and he had rushed nothing, had let the long notes have their whole length, and in the fermata near the end — Werner could still feel it, the way you feel a stair that isn't there — the boy had held the chord and held it, the sound decaying out into the brick and the glass and the held breath of a hundred farm people, held it past comfort, past sense, until the silence and the sound were the same thing, and then he had let it resolve, so softly, and Werner had sat in the fourth pew with water running down his face in front of the entire congregation of St. John's and had not cared, the only time in his life he had not cared.

There had been no second recital. Not because of any failing — Miss Halvorsen had wanted him for everything, had talked of Concordia, of further teachers in the Cities — but the boy had a privacy in him like a stone in a field, and he played at home, evenings, for the kitchen and the dog, and he played hymns for church when asked, sitting in with no fuss and standing down with less, and he said once, fifteen years old, drying dishes while Margaret washed, that playing for people felt like reading his mail out loud. So they let him be. You let them be; that was the whole of raising the boy, learning the difference between a closed door and a locked one. Then the war wanted him, and got him, and the army discovered he could type, which probably saved him for an extra year, and then it didn't save him, and the telegram era was over by then so it was two men in a car, in March, with the yard half thawed, and Margaret saw the car from the kitchen window and Werner heard the bowl she was holding meet the floor and that sound — that, of everything — was the sound he had never gotten out of his head, not in fifty years of true unisons, the one wrong note that would not stop beating.

He laid his roll of tools on the felt across the Yamaha's strings.

The piano hardly needed him. It never did; it was a modern instrument in a stable room, played twice a week by Arlene Stoltz, who used too much pedal and would until the Lord called her home, and it drifted in a year less than the old uprights drifted in a season. He tuned it anyway, every string, taking his time, working through the temperament octave like a man saying a rosary, fourths and fifths, checking thirds, the beats counting themselves out under his ear — and his left hand, he noticed, was perfectly steady now, in the cool of the church, at the end of the day, steady as it had been at thirty, as if it had only been waiting to see whether he was serious.

The light moved along the pews while he worked. It went from the fourth pew to the fifth to the sixth, gold to amber to the dull rose of the lowest glass, and by the time he reached the top octave, those last small notes like dropped coins, the sanctuary was nearly dusk and he was tuning by lamplight from the little brass lamp on the music desk. He ran his chromatic scale, eighty-eight notes, bottom to top, and the instrument was true, and he was finished, and he understood that he was finished meant more than the piano now, and he sat on the bench without intending to and did not get up.

A man can know how a clock works and still not have anywhere to be. He had said it that morning and it had been true for fifty years and it was not, he found, entirely true tonight. He looked at his hands on his knees. He had never learned to play; that was a fact; but a man who has heard a piece of music several thousand times in his head, slowed down, taken apart, every interval of it measured against his own breastbone — such a man knows where the notes are, the way you know where the light switch is in your own dark house.

He put his right hand on the keys. He found the F, and the one melody note above it, and then the next, the line climbing, reaching. It was clumsy. It was terribly slow, and the left hand could offer only the barest bones of the harmony, single bass notes where there should have been hands full of it, and twice he stopped and had to find his place against the architecture in his head. But the piano told the truth under him, every note exactly where it claimed to be, and in the dark church the line climbed and reached and fell back, climbed and reached further, and when he came to the place where the boy had held the chord, Werner held it. He held it past comfort, past sense, the sound dying out into the brick and the glass, the lamp making its small circle, the cemetery dark beyond the windows with its rows facing east, and he held it until the silence and the sound were the same thing.

Then he let it resolve, so softly. He closed the fallboard, and rolled up his tools, and tapped the fork once against his knee out of habit, A, four hundred forty, true as ever, and put it back in his pocket over his heart, and turned off the lamp, and went out the side door into the smell of harvest, leaving the church the way his father had taught him to leave everything — tuned for the silence after him.
