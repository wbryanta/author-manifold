Christ, the light though, he said. I keep coming back to the light. You could be standing on the deck of that transport with a hundred other guys, guys who had seen everything there was to see, guys who had been at Eniwetok, at Bikini, guys who had stood in the smoke at Nagasaki two weeks after, and every one of them would go quiet when the sky started to go pink. Dead quiet. You could hear the water against the hull. You could hear somebody's wristwatch. And then the count, and then the light, and I swear to you the light was not of this world, the light was light from before the world, and afterwards nobody would say anything at all for a while, and then somebody would say something stupid, somebody always said something stupid, and we would all laugh too hard and go below and have breakfast. Powdered eggs. I remember the powdered eggs. Funny what you remember.

You remember the gardenias, he said. I remember the eggs.

He said this to her across a table on the lanai of a hotel bar near the Honolulu airport in the spring of 1975, while the big transports kept coming in over the reef runway with their lights on in the rain, one after another, all that March and April, out of Saigon. He said it to her twenty-three years after the morning at Schofield he was talking about, twenty-one years after the last of the shots he was talking about, and when he had finished talking he turned his glass on the wet table, making rings, and looked not at her but at the runway lights.

Water under the bridge, he said. Spilt milk. Tell me about yourself, Inez. Tell me about life in the great world.

I want you to notice the way he says it, the great world, as if the great world were a small thing he had once held in his hand and put down somewhere and not bothered to pick up again. I want you to notice that he does not ask about her husband, although her husband had at that time been on the cover of one or another national magazine at least nine times, and that she does not mention her husband, although mentioning her husband was by then what Inez Victor did, mentioning her husband was by then the major part of what Inez Victor could be said to do. I want you to notice these things because this is a hard story to tell.

Call this a deposition. Call it notes toward a novel I have not been able to write in any of the usual ways. I had wanted to write a novel of a certain kind, a novel with a hundred years in it, a novel that began with ships. I had wanted to begin in 1842, with the first Christian to land at Honolulu, a ship's chandler out of Hartford with a Bible and a bill of lading, and to move through the sugar years and the annexation years and the war years, the dynastic chronicle, the verandas and the silver and the dengue fever, the Chinese cook and the koa-wood floors and the four o'clock rain, and to arrive only in the last chapters at Inez. I made notes for that novel. I have the notes still. "Heat as moral climate," one note reads. "The colors: pink, green, the white of the reef. Nobody here has ever seen snow." Another reads, simply, "the light at dawn."

That novel would have had a setting that worked on the reader the way the place works on the people in it, which is to say like an anesthetic, and it would have had a plot that moved with the deceptive languor of an outgoing tide, and it would have been a lie. The colonial novel. The novel of manners at the edge of the Pacific. The trouble was that the manners had stopped meaning anything by 1952 at the latest, the trouble was that the verandas were being torn down for high-rises with names like the Ilikai, the trouble was that history, during the years I am talking about, was happening somewhere over the horizon to the west, on the atolls, in the air, and the Christians of Honolulu were giving dinner parties under the monkeypod trees and not noticing. Or noticing only the light. That spring everybody in Honolulu said the sunsets were unusual. The sunsets were unusual because of the testing, but it was not a thing one said.

So I gave up the hundred years. Consider what remains. Consider what I actually have.

I have Jack Lovett, dead now, a man whose occupation, on those documents that required one, he variously gave as army officer, businessman, aircraft broker, consultant. I have a photograph of Jack Lovett standing on a metal landing strip somewhere that is identified on the back, in pencil, in a hand not his, only as "J., '53." I have the manifest of a charter flight from Saigon to Hong Kong on the twenty-ninth of March, 1975, on which the name LOVETT, J. appears between the names of a Chinese banker and a man listed as a dealer in agricultural equipment, and I can tell you that no agricultural equipment was on that flight. I have what Jack Lovett said to me once in the bar of the Cathay Pacific lounge at Kai Tak, which was this: "Anybody who tells you what I do for a living is lying, including me."

I have Inez. I have Inez Victor, born Inez Christian in the Territory of Hawaii on the first day of January, 1935, Inez Victor whom I knew slightly in New York in 1955, when she was working as a junior editor at Vogue and I was writing merchandising copy down the hall, Inez Christian as she still was then, twenty years old, raised under the monkeypod trees, given to wearing a gardenia in her hair at parties where everyone else wore black, and given also to a way of looking at the men who talked to her at those parties, an attentive, level, faintly absent way, as if she were listening less to what they said than to a frequency just behind it. Men construed this look variously. Harry Victor construed it as depth.

I have Harry Victor. Everyone has Harry Victor. Harry Victor of the Alliance for Democratic Institutions, Harry Victor of the Senate Foreign Relations Committee, Harry Victor who in March of 1975, the same March the transports were coming in over the reef runway with their lights on, was exploring, as the phrase went, a candidacy for the presidency of the United States, and who was exploring it that particular week in Honolulu, which is the only reason Inez Victor happened to be sitting on the lanai of a hotel bar near the airport when Jack Lovett came through. Harry Victor had a way of standing with his head slightly bowed when he was introduced at fund-raising dinners, a way of letting the applause wash over the bowed head, that certain women found devotional and that Inez had stopped seeing at all somewhere around 1962. "Marvelous," Inez would say when interviewers asked her what it was like, the public life, the candidacies, the cameras. "A marvelous opportunity to see the country." She said it in 1968 and she said it in 1972 and she was still saying it in 1975. I once watched her say it twice in a single afternoon, in Eugene, Oregon, to two different reporters, with the identical small pause before "marvelous," and when the second reporter left she looked at me for a moment and said, "You want to know what it's actually like? You lose track. That's what it's actually like. You lose track of what you said and where you said it, and then you lose track of which of it was true."

I am resisting narrative here. Notice that.

The heart of the narrative, the part a more orderly novelist would have led with, is this: in the spring of 1952 Jack Lovett, then a major attached in some unspecified way to CINCPAC, met Inez Christian, then seventeen years old, at a dance at Schofield Barracks to which she had driven her younger sister Janet in their grandmother's Packard, and for approximately four months, between January and April of that year, between his trips down to the shot islands he never named and she never asked about, Jack Lovett and Inez Christian conducted what would now be called an affair and what was then called, by the two people who knew about it, both of them servants in the Christian house on Manoa Road, nothing at all. He was thirty-six. He was married, although Inez did not learn this until March, and when she learned it she considered it for a day or two and found, to her own mild surprise, that it changed nothing, which was perhaps the first indication that Inez Christian's expectations of the world had a different shape than the world expected. In April his orders changed. He brought her a carved box he said was from Manila. He told her he would write, and did not, or wrote and did not mail it, the story varied later, and in any case it came to the same thing. In June she sailed for the mainland and Sarah Lawrence. In 1955 she married Harry Victor in the chapel at Punahou with two hundred guests and ten bridesmaids and a kahili bearer at the door, and her grandmother said, audibly, during the vows, that the Victors were people nobody had ever heard of.

Between 1952 and 1975 Inez Victor saw Jack Lovett exactly four times. I want to be precise about this because a good deal of imprecision entered the record later. She saw him in 1960, in the lobby of the Hotel Indonesia in Djakarta, where Harry was leading a congressional study mission and Jack Lovett was doing whatever Jack Lovett was doing in Djakarta in 1960; they had a drink while Harry was at a briefing, and Jack Lovett told her two funny stories about the riots then occurring six blocks away and put her in a car when the lights went out, and the next morning he had checked out. She saw him in 1964 at Janet's wedding in Honolulu, where he appeared, uninvited but somehow not unexpected, at the reception, and danced with her once, and said, while they were dancing, "I see you on the news now," and she said, "I'm not on the news, Harry's on the news," and he said, "I stand corrected," and that was 1964. She saw him in 1969, for an hour at the Hong Kong airport, an hour that neither of them ever discussed and about which I know only that it occurred, because it appears in the surveillance digest that was later read into the record of a closed hearing of a subcommittee whose business had nothing to do with either of them. And she saw him in March of 1975, on the lanai of the hotel bar near the Honolulu airport, where he sat down across from her without being asked, looked at her for a while, and began, with no preamble at all, to talk about the light at dawn during the Pacific tests.

Twenty-three years and that was where he started. Not how are you, not it's been a long time. The pink sky. The painter who never got it. The air smelling of gardenias, never mind there were no gardenias. As if the sentence had been suspended in 1952 and he was finishing it.

Inez told me about that night twice. The two accounts differed in small ways, and the differences are themselves part of the record. In the first account, the one she gave me in 1975, when the events were fresh and she had reasons for flatness, she said only that she had run into Jack Lovett, that they had talked about the old days, that he had told her some stories about the testing she supposed were classified once and probably weren't anymore, and that around midnight Billy Dillon, who handled such things for Harry, had come looking for her because the wire services had picked up what her father had done that evening on the other side of the island, and after that, she said, the evening was no longer about Jack Lovett. In the second account, the one she gave me two years later, on another island altogether, she included what she had left out. She included the dancing.

There was a small combo at the hotel bar, three Filipino musicians doing standards, and at some point between Jack Lovett's account of the fizzled Livermore device and the arrival of Billy Dillon they had played "I'll Be Seeing You," and Jack Lovett had stood, not asking, simply standing and holding out his hand, and Inez Victor, wife of the senior senator from New York, exploratory candidate for the presidency of the United States, had stood up and danced with him in the rain-smelling dark while the transports came in over the reef, and what he said while they were dancing was this: You haven't changed, Inez. And what she said was: I have, though, Jack. That's the trouble. That's exactly the trouble. And then Billy Dillon was standing at the edge of the dance floor in his seersucker jacket snapping his fingers, not in time to the music, and saying, Inez, we've got a situation.

A situation. I will get to the situation. The situation is the reason there is a story at all, the situation is what got into the papers and stayed there through the spring, but I find that I keep deferring it, and the deferral is not entirely a writer's trick. Inez deferred it too. Inez deferred it for twenty minutes that night, a fact Billy Dillon never understood and never forgave. "Her father shoots two people and she wants to finish the dance," Billy Dillon said to me once, years later, in the Brasserie on East Fifty-third Street, still aggrieved. "You explain it. I gave up." I did not try to explain it to Billy Dillon, because the explanation was not one Billy Dillon was equipped to use. The explanation was that her father shooting two people was, in the strict sense, nothing new, was only the family weather finally breaking, whereas the dance was twenty-three years old and had never once been finished.

Of the shootings themselves I will give you, for now, only what the first wire story gave: that at approximately 7:40 P.M. on the evening of March 25, 1975, Paul Christian, 63, a member of one of the islands' oldest kamaaina families, had walked into a beach cottage at Lanikai and shot and killed his daughter, Janet Christian Ziegler, 37, and a guest, and had then driven himself to the Honolulu police station downtown, where he presented the gun, a .357 Magnum, to the desk sergeant with both hands, like a gift, and said—this was in the second-day stories—"I did what was necessary in regard to my family's honor." What honor required, what family meant, what regard Paul Christian had ever actually paid to either: these were questions the wire stories did not take up, and the Christians had spent a hundred and thirty years constructing a wall of monkeypod trees and trust officers precisely so that such questions would not be taken up, and within eighteen hours the wall was gone and the networks were doing stand-ups on Manoa Road, and the reason the networks were doing stand-ups was not Paul Christian and was not Janet. The reason was Inez. The reason was Harry. SENATOR'S FATHER-IN-LAW, the chyrons said. The candidacy, Billy Dillon said on the phone to New York that first night, has got itself a Hawaiian problem.

I keep the clips in a folder, and what strikes me now, reading them again, is how little anyone managed to learn, how perfectly the flat sunny diction of the wire services matched the flat sunny surface the family had always presented, as if the islands themselves conspired in the genre. Tragedy strikes prominent family. Prominent family requests privacy in this difficult time. Nothing in the clips about the years Paul Christian had spent in hotel rooms in Tunis and Cuernavaca living off remittances and grievance. Nothing about the letters he had been sending Janet all that winter, letters about the trustees, about plots, about people who had pushed the Christians aside, letters Janet read aloud at lunch at the Outrigger for laughs. Nothing about what Janet's guest did for a living, or why a man in his line of work might have been at Janet's beach cottage at 7:40 in the evening, although Jack Lovett, when he heard the guest's name, went silent in a way Inez noticed and did not understand until considerably later, and that silence is also part of the story, the part that took me longest to get.

Aerial views are what I do. I should admit that. When I try to think about the events of that spring I see them first from a great altitude, the way you would see them from one of the transports: the curve of the reef runway, the rain squalls standing offshore like pillars, the small bright wound of the city on the dark island, and somewhere down in the grid two women's lives intersecting with the lives of several governments. Only with effort do I descend to the particular, to the lanai and the wet table and the rings the glass made, and I have come to believe the difficulty is not mine alone. The difficulty is in the material. The era resists the close-up. You try to focus on a marriage and you get the Pacific Command. You try to focus on a dance and you get the evacuation of Saigon. Jack Lovett would have said, did in effect say, that this was the only sane way to see it, that the personal life was a cover story people told themselves, that down on those atolls he had learned the one lesson worth learning, which was that the brightest light anybody would ever see had nothing to do with anybody. And yet there he was at the edge of the dance floor for twenty-three years. There he was, every few years, in another lobby, another airport, on the perimeter of Inez Victor's cover story, checking the exits.

"You know what I remember about you," he said to her that night on the lanai, before the music, before Billy Dillon. "I remember you never asked. Everybody asked. The wives asked, the reporters asked, the goddamn congressmen's daughters asked, what's it like, what do you do down there, is it true what they say. You never asked one question."

"I didn't have to," Inez said. "You just told me. The light, the eggs. You've been answering for twenty-three years."

He looked at her for a while then. The transports kept coming in. On the television set over the bar, with the sound off, a man was standing on the roof of a building in a city eight thousand miles away, and people were climbing a ladder toward him, and the man was reaching down.

"Fair enough," Jack Lovett said. "Fair enough. Dance with me, Inez."

That is the scene I keep. A novelist is supposed to choose, and I choose that one: the wet table, the silent television, the woman in the white dress standing up. Everything else in this story—the bodies at Lanikai, the candidacy, the hearings, the man on the manifest between the banker and the dealer in agricultural equipment, the place Inez Victor lives now and the work she does there and what she said to me the last time I saw her, which was that colors and moisture and the smell of certain flowers had been for her the markers of the only life she would have called real—everything else radiates out from that scene, the way the light radiated out from the shots, so bright, he always said, that you could see the bones in your hands. He always said that. You could close your eyes, put your hands over your face, and see the bones in your hands. It was not something you could describe to anyone who hadn't seen it, he said, and then he would describe it, every time, and Inez would listen every time, and that, as near as I can make out, after all the depositions and all the clips and all the aerial views, is what there was between them. He kept describing it. She kept listening. In the great world this is not generally accounted a love story, but I no longer know what else to call it.
