The leak showed itself the way leaks always did, which was late, and indirectly, and in the wrong place. Walt noticed it first as an absence: the pressure gauge at the pump house reading thirty-one when it should have read forty-four, the micro-sprinklers in the Suncrest block hissing instead of spinning, throwing their water in sulky little arcs that wet the trunks and nothing else. Then, walking the mainline road with his coffee going cold in his hand, he found the presence of it — a patch of ground at the foot of row eleven gone dark and soft as cake, with a sheen of standing water in the wheel rut and a killdeer running theatrics around it as if she'd laid eggs in the only wet dirt in the county.

He stood over it a long moment. June already, the fruit on the trees hard and green and the size of walnuts, the thinning done, the whole orchard leaning into the part of the year when water was the only conversation that mattered. He could feel the leak under his boots, the line down there bleeding into the subsoil, water he had paid for and prayed over going to feed cheatgrass and a killdeer's drama. He poured out the coffee, walked back to the house, and woke his brother.

Denny came out onto the porch in jeans and a T-shirt from a 10K in Aurora, his feet bare on the gray boards, blinking at the light the way people did when they'd been away from it. He had driven over from the Front Range on Thursday with a duffel bag and a manila envelope, and the envelope had been sitting on the kitchen table for three days now, squared to the edge of the table the way their father used to square his bills, and neither of them had touched it since the first night.

"Mainline's broke," Walt said. "Down by row eleven in the Suncrests."

"How broke."

"Broke enough I shut the pump off. You want to dig, or you want to stand around remembering how?"

Denny looked at him a beat, deciding, Walt figured, whether that was a joke or the opening shot, and then decided, the way he'd been deciding all weekend, that it could be a joke if he let it. "Let me find my boots," he said.

The trencher had a cracked hydraulic line of its own and had been sitting under a tarp behind the shop since April, one more thing on the list of things that cost money to fix and made money for nobody, so they dug by hand. Walt brought the long-handled shovels and the square spade and a five-gallon bucket of their father's fittings, coffee cans of couplings and slip-fixes and saddle clamps all swimming together in there, thirty years of saved parts, because Earl Mercer had never in his life thrown away a piece of pipe longer than his forearm. The morning was cool yet, the air coming down off the Bookcliffs with a little of the night still in it, and the doves were going in the Lombardy poplars along the ditch, that low sorrowing note that Walt had heard every morning of his life and did not hear anymore unless he made himself.

They started wide of the wet spot, because the break was never where the water surfaced. Water traveled along the outside of the pipe, followed the path of the old trench, came up where the ground let it, and a man who dug straight down at the puddle earned himself an extra hour of digging and a faceful of mud. Walt set them six feet uphill and they cut the sod and went down through the dry crust into the damp, and then into the saturated layer where each shovelful came up shining and heavy as liver.

"I forgot how much I hate this," Denny said, but he said it cheerfully, his shirt already stuck to his back. He'd kept himself in shape, Walt would give him that. Fifty-one years old and he ran his 10Ks and his half marathons on the bike paths of the suburbs and he could still move dirt. He had soft hands now, though. Walt had noticed it when they shook on Thursday, the smoothness of his brother's palm, like shaking hands with a realtor.

"You used to be good at it," Walt said. "Dad always said you dug a cleaner trench than me."

"Dad said that to whichever one of us wasn't listening."

That was true enough that Walt laughed, and for a while there was just the work, the suck and slap of the mud, the shovels ringing when they kissed a rock, the killdeer still carrying on twenty feet off with her broken-wing routine, dragging herself piteously away from a nest that didn't exist. By eight thirty they had the pipe exposed for a six-foot run, four-inch PVC laid by their father and a crew of cousins in 1988, the year Denny graduated, and the water was rising in the trench faster than the break would show itself, so Walt walked back and opened the drain valve at the low end of the line and they sat on the spoil pile and ate the peanut butter crackers Denny had brought in his jacket pocket and waited for the line to bleed down.

"You hear the gauge this morning?" Walt said. "Thirteen pounds we were losing. That's been going on a while, I'd guess. Week, maybe two. I should've caught it."

"You can't catch everything."

"You can if there's two of you," Walt said, and heard it come out harder than he meant, or exactly as hard as he meant, he honestly couldn't have said. Denny ate his cracker and looked out at the orchard, the rows running away north toward the river in their long green perspective, and didn't take it up.

The break, when they found it, was where the breaks always were: at a joint. A bell coupling, cracked along its length, hairline at the top, opening to a quarter inch at the bottom where the pipe had been bedded badly, where there'd been a stone, probably, under the bell since 1988, and thirty-six winters of frost heave and thirty-six summers of pressure had done their patient work. The crack was furred with fine white roots. Peach roots will find water through a wall if you give them time.

"There's your culprit," Denny said, kneeling in the mud, running his thumb along the crack the way you'd check a blade. "Dad's crew bedded it on a rock."

"Dad's crew was us."

"That's what I said."

They needed a foot and a half of new pipe and two repair couplings, the telescoping kind, and Walt knew without looking that the bucket of coffee cans held everything but exactly that, so they drove into town in the flatbed with the windows down because the AC had quit in 2019. Town was eleven minutes away and had been eleven minutes away their whole lives, and on the way they passed the Kessler place, which was forty houses now, beige and gray with little fenced yards, and the Antonelli place, which was a self-storage facility with a lighted sign, and the old Vance orchard, where the trees had simply been pushed out and burned and the ground laser-leveled and left bare, growing its crop of survey stakes with pink ribbons that ticked in the wind like something counting.

Denny looked at all of it and Walt watched him look at it, and neither of them said anything until they were in the irrigation aisle at the farm store, Denny holding a four-inch coupling in his hand, turning it over.

"Sixty-one dollars," Denny said. "For this."

"Glue's gone up too. Everything that touches water's gone up."

"That's kind of my point, Walt."

"I know what your point is. Your point's been sitting on the kitchen table since Thursday."

Denny set the coupling in the cart with exaggerated care, the way you set something down when what you want to do is throw it. "I drove out here to spend a few days with my brother," he said. "I figured the envelope could introduce itself."

"It did. It's been introducing itself every time I sit down to eat."

At the register the girl rang them up and asked, the way they all asked now, "You guys still farming out there?" — not unkindly, more in the tone you'd use asking after someone's elderly relative — and Walt said yep, still at it, and she said that's so great, and Denny paid before Walt could get his wallet out, which Walt let him do, and then resented for the whole ride home.

By noon the heat had come up in earnest. The Bookcliffs had gone pale and shimmery, the air over the gravel road wobbled, the orchard held its leaves still in that midday paralysis, and the trench, when they got back down into it, was a slick brown oven. They cut out the cracked coupling with the cordless saw, and the smell of cut PVC mixed with the smell of the mud and the smell of the primer, that purple chemical sweetness that Walt's hands had carried so many summers it was practically a season of its own. The line had to be dry to glue, bone dry, and the cut ends kept weeping — there was a half mile of pipe uphill of them still holding water in its low spots — so they stuffed a wad of white bread up the line the way their father had taught them, an old plumber's trick, the bread damming the seep long enough for the glue to grab, dissolving harmlessly later and flushing out the first sprinkler downstream.

"I told a guy at work about the bread thing once," Denny said, kneeling across the trench from him, holding the new section steady. "He thought I was making it up."

"City people think everything's made up."

"I'm city people now, you know."

"I know it," Walt said. "I've known it twenty-eight years."

They primed both ends, and the couplings, working fast because the glue gave you seconds, not minutes, and Denny held and Walt swabbed and they shoved the joints home with a quarter turn the way you were supposed to, and held them, kneeling face to face over the open line, holding the pipe like men holding a litter, like men holding something between them that would spring apart if either let go, thirty seconds, sixty, while the solvent welded the plastic into one thing. Their father had made them count it out loud when they were boys. Neither of them counted out loud now but Walt could hear the counting anyway, in the old man's voice, patient and contemptuous of impatience, one Mississippi, two.

"Now it sets," Walt said. "Two hours, this heat, before we put pressure to it. Three to be safe."

"So we wait."

"So we wait."

They washed at the standpipe and carried lunch out to the shade of the big Suncrest at the head of row one, the tree their father had always called the mother tree though it wasn't, it was just the oldest left standing, planted the spring Denny was born, which was the kind of coincidence their mother had loved and curated. They ate ham sandwiches and drank water that tasted faintly of hose, and the shade moved slow over them, and the green peaches hung overhead in their thousands, hard and secret, each one a promise the orchard might or might not keep. Last August a hailstorm had come down the valley in eleven minutes and turned the whole crop to cull. The August before that, the price of number ones had dropped so far that Walt had let two blocks hang unpicked, the smell of rotting fruit so sweet and thick by September that he'd had to keep the truck windows up driving past his own trees.

"How much is Mom's place now," Denny said. He said it quietly, into the middle of the quiet, which was its own kind of strategy.

"Sixty-four hundred a month. Goes up in January. They sent a letter."

"And the account."

"You've seen the account. You're on the account."

"I want to hear you say it."

"Eleven months," Walt said. "Maybe thirteen if the crop comes in. Then we're selling something whether we want to or not, and the difference is whether we sell it standing up or sell it on our knees."

Denny was quiet for a while. A tractor was working somewhere over on the Lujan place, the sound rising and falling. The killdeer had finally given it up and gone.

"Hollis is offering one point six," Denny said. "That's real money, Walt. That's Mom taken care of for the rest of her life, however long that is, God love her. That's something for Casey and June's school. That's you not lying awake doing math at three in the morning, which I know you do, because I hear you get up, because I've been sleeping in my old room for three nights listening to this house, and this house sounds exactly like it sounded when Dad was the one getting up at three to do the math."

"Dad never sold."

"Dad sold the lower twenty in '92."

"To pay for —" Walt stopped.

"Say it."

"It doesn't need saying."

"To pay for my college," Denny said. "Yeah. I know what the lower twenty bought. I've known it every day of my life, Walt, you think I don't? You think I sit in my office and don't know what I'm sitting on? I'm sitting on twenty acres of Elbertas, that's what my whole life is built on, and Dad never once — not once, not at my graduation, not at my wedding, not on his deathbed — said a word about it. You're the only one who ever brings it up, and you bring it up without bringing it up, which is worse."

The fairness of that landed somewhere under Walt's ribs and sat there. He looked down the row instead of at his brother. Heat shimmer stood between the trunks. A hundred and forty yards off, the new joint was curing in its trench, invisible, becoming one thing.

"I'm not blaming you for college," Walt said finally.

"Then what are you blaming me for? Because it's something. It's been something since Thursday. It's been something since the funeral, if I'm honest."

"For getting to choose," Walt said. The words came out before he'd inspected them, which was not his habit, and he heard their truth at the same moment Denny did. "You got to choose. You looked at this place at eighteen years old and you weighed it and you went, and that was right, probably, that was probably the smart thing, and I — " He turned his water bottle in his hands. "Nobody ever asked me. It was just understood. Walt'll stay. Walt's the steady one. And I did stay, and I'm fifty-six, and Carol's gone, partly over this place, you want the truth, and the trees are old and the water's short and the only people who want this ground want it dead, and now you drive over from Denver with an envelope and you're asking me to choose. Now. After it's already chosen me."

Denny didn't say anything for a long time. When Walt finally looked over, his brother was sitting with his forearms on his knees, looking at the dirt between his boots, and his eyes were wet, which Walt had not seen since the funeral, and before that not since they were boys.

"I used to think you stayed because you loved it more," Denny said.

"I do love it."

"That's not what I said."

A little wind came through and the leaves moved and went still. Far off, the tractor stopped, and the silence afterward was enormous, the way it always was, the whole valley a held breath.

"What happens to the water," Walt said, "if Hollis gets it. You know what happens. They take the shares off the ditch, they pipe it or they sell it to the district, and that's a priority date of 1903 — Dad's grandfather's name is on that decree, Den. A hundred and twenty years of beneficial use, and it ends with us, and you can't ever get it back. Houses don't give it back. You can tear a house down. You can't un-sell water. Everything else on this place I could maybe stand to lose. The trees are mortal anyway. But the water — " He shook his head. He didn't have the end of the sentence. He'd never had the end of that sentence; it was a sentence their father had never finished either, and his father before him, all of them trailing off at the same place, at the edge of the thing too large to say.

"Mom asked me about the water," Denny said. "Saturday, when I went up to see her. She didn't know my name. She introduced me to the aide as 'one of the Mercer boys,' like I was a neighbor kid. And then she grabbed my wrist — she's still strong, did you know that? She grabbed my wrist and she said, 'Is the water on? Tell Earl the water's on Tuesday, he'll lose his turn.' " Denny laughed, a wet, broken sound. "Forty years of taking her turn on that ditch. It's the last thing in her, Walt. Underneath everything else they took, that's the bedrock. Is the water on."

"Is that an argument for selling or against?"

"I don't know," Denny said. "I genuinely don't know. I've been trying to figure that out since Saturday."

They sat with that. The shade had moved off them and neither one shifted to follow it. Walt's shoulders had begun the deep ache that the shovel always left, the ache he'd had every June of his life, that he'd miss, he understood suddenly, the way you'd miss a tooth.

"Here's what I know," Denny said at last, slowly, like a man feeling for the next plank of a bridge. "I know I can't make you sell, and I wouldn't, it's half yours and it's all yours in every way that counts. And I know I can't farm it with you — don't, let me finish — I can't, Walt, I have a life, I have Renee and the kids and a mortgage and I'm fifty-one, and if I quit all that and came back it would be a costume, you'd see through it in a week and so would the trees. But I can't keep doing this either. Driving over twice a year, leaving envelopes on the table, watching you get ground down and pretending we're not both waiting for the freeze or the hail or the bank to make the decision so neither of us has to. That's cowardice. We're being cowards, both of us, and Dad would've said so."

"Dad would've said get off your asses, the glue's set."

Denny snorted, wiped his face with the back of his wrist, leaving a streak of mud along his cheekbone like warpaint. "He would've, too."

They gave the joint its third hour anyway, doing small chores in the heat — Denny straightening props in the Elbertas, Walt flushing the filter station — working apart from each other but within sight, the way they used to as boys when one of them was in trouble and the other was being careful not to be. Then, at five, with the light just beginning to lean and go gold, they walked together to the pump house and Walt primed the pump and looked at his brother and his brother nodded, and he hit the switch.

The pump caught and the line filled with a sound like a long swallow, the air hammering out of the risers down the row in spits and gasps, and Walt stood with his hand on the gauge watching the needle climb — twenty, thirty, hesitating, and his stomach hesitating with it, and then forty, forty-three, forty-four — and hold. Holding. Down the quarter mile of the Suncrest block, the micro-sprinklers came alive one after another in sequence, each one coughing and then spinning up into its full silver circle, the whole row blooming with water in the low sun, and somewhere down at the second riser a fist of white bread shot out into the grass and a magpie dropped on it out of nowhere like it had been notified in advance.

They walked the trench line and stood over their joint. The ground above it stayed dry. Dry as a sermon. The new fitting down there in the dark, holding, the water going where water was meant to go, into the roots of trees their father had planted the spring Denny was born.

"Look at that," Denny said softly.

The sun was coming low through the rows now, lighting the spray, and every sprinkler down the block wore its own small rainbow, dozens of them, hanging and dissolving and re-forming, and the smell came up off the wetted ground — that smell, dust and water, the oldest smell Walt knew, older in him than language — and the leaves seemed to lift, although that was likely his imagination, trees didn't work that fast, but he let himself believe it anyway, that the whole orchard had felt the pressure come back and squared its shoulders.

Denny stepped off the trench line and put his hand into the throw of the nearest sprinkler, the way he'd done as a kid, palm flat, letting the circle of water slap across it, once, again, again.

"I'll call Hollis Monday," he said, not looking at Walt. "Tell them we need more time. Not no. Time. And I'll come back over in August for harvest, real harvest, two weeks, I've got the vacation banked. And we'll sit down with the actual numbers and Mom's actual numbers and we'll decide it like brothers instead of leaving envelopes around like ransom notes. Can you live with that?"

Walt watched the water cross his brother's hand, cross it, cross it.

"I can live with that," he said.

They stood there a while longer, neither moving to go in, the light going amber and then rose along the Bookcliffs, the sprinklers ticking out their circles, the water reaching the far trees last the way it always did, the way it always would for at least one more season, and the doves started up again in the poplars, and somewhere out in the wet grass the killdeer began, very quietly, to lie about her nest again, and the two of them stood in the failing light of the failing orchard and listened to the sound of the water, which was on.
