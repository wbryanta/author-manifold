The estate sale company had told her to be there by noon, though the sale itself had started at nine, and so by the time Diane pulled into the driveway of the house on Linden Street the front lawn was already scattered with what she could only think of as evidence. A card table near the porch held her mother's Pyrex bowls, nested in their fading colors, primrose and gooseberry and one she'd never known the name of, a kind of milky turquoise. A man in a Bengals cap was holding the turquoise one up to the light as if candling an egg. Two box fans sat in the grass with masking-tape prices on their grilles. A child's tricycle she didn't recognize at all — had that been in the garage? whose had it been? — leaned against the mailbox post with a tag that said FIVE DOLLARS in someone else's handwriting.

She sat in the car for a minute with the engine off, the August heat building immediately against the windows, and watched a woman come out the front door carrying the lamp from the den. The lamp had a base shaped like a ginger jar, cream-colored with a faded scene of bridges and willows, and a shade gone soft yellow with forty years of light passing through it. The woman carried it the way you'd carry a sleeping toddler, the base against her hip, one hand steadying the shade, and she went down the porch steps Diane had learned to count as a child — seven, with the fourth one that gave a little — and across the grass to a minivan, and that was that. The lamp from the den left Linden Street forever, and the world did not pause to note it, and Diane got out of the car.

Sherry from Heartland Estate Services met her in the front hall with a clipboard and a fanny pack heavy with cash. Sherry was younger than Diane had expected when they'd spoken on the phone, maybe forty, with a price gun holstered at her side like a deputy. "It's been steady," Sherry said. "Real steady for a Thursday. The dining set's got two parties circling, so don't be surprised if you hear me playing them off each other." She said it cheerfully, like a coach. "Tools went first thing, like I told you they would. Men were lined up at eight-thirty for the tools."

"My father's tools," Diane said, and heard how it sounded — not an accusation, just a fact pulled up from somewhere — and Sherry's face did a practiced thing, sympathy without slowing down.

"They went to people who'll use them," Sherry said. "That's the way I like to think of it. Now. You don't have to be here at all, hon, that's what you pay us for. But if you're staying, the kitchen's a good place to stand. Folks won't bother you back there."

Diane did not go stand in the kitchen. She stood in the front hall, in the path of traffic, in the spot where the telephone table had been since 1971 and where it still was, tagged at twelve dollars, the little drawer hanging open and empty, the phone book shelf bare. The wallpaper behind it was a different color than the rest of the wall, a rectangle of the original gold preserved like a photograph of a photograph. She had taken phone calls at that table through her entire adolescence, the cord stretched to its limit around the corner into the coat closet for privacy, her mother's voice from the kitchen saying, Diane, other people in this house may need to use that phone, though no one ever had, not once that she could remember, not urgently. Her mother had just needed to say something. Her mother had been a person who narrated the household's order aloud as a way of maintaining it, the way some people hum.

Arlene had died in March, in the back bedroom, with hospice and a hospital bed and the venetian blinds her son-in-law had once repaired badly so they hung at a permanent slight diagonal. Diane had been there for the last eleven days of it and then had gone back to Columbus, two hours and a whole life away, and had returned now in August because the house had finally sold, to a young couple who were getting it for what Diane considered an insult of a price and who wanted it broom-clean by the first of September. Her brother Doug, in Tempe, had voted by phone to sell everything. Don't ship me anything, he'd said. Donna and I are downsizing ourselves, we don't have the room, just — whatever it makes, it makes. He had said it kindly. He had meant it kindly. He had not offered to come.

A man wanted to know about the freezer in the basement. Did it run. Diane said it ran, it had always run, that freezer had run since the Nixon administration, and the man laughed and said they didn't make them like that anymore and went down the basement stairs sideways like a crab. Below her she could hear voices in the basement, strangers' voices in the basement, and above her, footsteps in the bedrooms, and she understood that the house had been turned inside out, that its privacy was abolished, that for one afternoon it belonged to everyone, and after that to someone else, and never again to her.

In the living room, a young couple stood over the dining table, which had been moved out from the dining room to be seen better in the light. The table was maple, heavy, with two leaves and a finish her mother had protected so fiercely that Diane had eaten on placemats atop a tablecloth atop a table pad for her entire childhood and had not seen the actual wood until she was perhaps thirty, at some Thanksgiving when the pad had been replaced, and had been startled by how beautiful it was, the grain like weather, like a map of currents. There was a burn mark at one end, a pale crescent, where Diane at fourteen had set down a curling iron — she could still feel the silence that had followed, her mother's hand at her own throat, the way the punishment had never quite been named but had instead suffused several weeks like a gas. The young woman was running her finger over the crescent now.

"You can sand that out," her husband or boyfriend said. "That's nothing. That's character."

"It's solid maple," Sherry said, materializing. "You don't find this anymore. This table's done sixty years and it's got sixty more in it."

"What happened here?" the young woman asked, meaning the burn, asking the room generally, and Diane heard herself say, "Curling iron. 1976."

The couple turned. The young woman's face opened. "Oh — is this — was this your —"

"My mother's house," Diane said. "I did that. The burn. I was fourteen. I'd give you the full story but my mother told it better."

The young woman laughed, a real laugh, and put her hand flat on the table, and Diane saw her decide, saw the table become hers in that moment, before any money changed hands — saw that the burn mark had sold it, the story had sold it, that the girl wanted a table with a history even if the history would come detached, would dissolve, would become within a year that funny thing the lady said at the estate sale and within five years nothing at all, a crescent of pale wood with no cause. The couple paid Sherry a hundred and forty dollars. They would come back at four with a truck and a brother-in-law.

Diane went into the kitchen after all.

The kitchen had been picked at but not gutted. The canister set was gone — flour, sugar, coffee, tea, in descending sizes, harvest gold — and the good knives, and the stand mixer, but the drawers still held their sediment: rubber bands looped over the knob of the junk drawer, twist ties in a margarine tub, a church key, a melon baller, the egg slicer with its little harp of wires. Tags everywhere. The whole drawer of utensils was priced at eight dollars, the contents undifferentiated, the melon baller and the egg slicer and the good wooden spoon worn to a slant on one side from decades of her mother's stirring, all of it eight dollars together, and Diane stood looking at that wooden spoon for longer than she could account for. Then she took it out of the drawer and put it in her purse. It was, she supposed, technically shoplifting, or technically nothing, since it was technically hers, since all of it was technically hers; that was the unsolvable arithmetic of the day. Everything in the house belonged to her, and she wanted almost none of it, and what she did want she could not have, because what she wanted was not the spoon.

Through the window over the sink she could see the backyard, the clothesline poles still standing though the line itself had been gone for years, the two T-shapes facing each other across the grass like a doorframe with no house around it. Her mother had hung wash out until she was eighty-one. Sheets in winter, frozen stiff, brought in like sheets of lumber and thawed over the backs of chairs. There is no reason to pay the electric company to do what the wind does free. The new couple would take the poles down, Diane thought. They'd put in a trampoline, or one of those pre-fab sheds, or nothing.

"Excuse me — do you work here?" A woman in the doorway, holding the deviled-egg plate. "Is this milk glass? It doesn't have a price."

"It's milk glass," Diane said. "Two dollars," inventing it, and the woman looked pleased and went off to pay Sherry, and Diane thought, that plate has carried deviled eggs to every funeral lunch at St. Paul's for fifty years, including, in March, in other hands, to my mother's. The plate had come back from the funeral lunch washed, with a strip of masking tape on the bottom reading ARLENE V. in her mother's own hand, because her mother had labeled it decades ago for exactly such journeys, so that it would always come home. It was leaving home now with the label still on. Diane could have peeled the tape off and didn't. Let the name travel. Let someone in some other kitchen, some year, turn the plate over and wonder.

The afternoon developed a rhythm. Surges and lulls. At one-thirty a surge: retirees mostly, moving through the rooms with the unhurried thoroughness of people who attended sales the way others attended church, who knew each other, who greeted each other over the card tables — Didn't I see you at the Kessler sale Saturday — and who handled objects with a tenderness that was, Diane decided, partly appraisal and partly something better than appraisal. An old man stood a long time in front of her father's barometer, which still hung by the front door because Sherry had priced it high. He tapped the glass the way you were supposed to, the way her father had tapped it every morning of his life, and watched the needle, and nodded at whatever it told him, and moved on without buying it.

Mrs. Pohl from across the street came at two, not to buy. She was ninety-one and came across Linden Street on the arm of her granddaughter, in real shoes, dressed as if for an appointment, and she took Diane's hands in both of hers in the front hall and said, "I keep looking over here expecting to see her on that porch."

"I know," Diane said.

"Forty-six years," Mrs. Pohl said. "She was on that porch the day we moved in. She brought over a tuna casserole and told Ed where to put the garbage cans so the raccoons wouldn't get them." She looked past Diane into the living room, where strangers were tipping the sofa to check its underside. "Well," she said, and her face did something complicated, grief making room for the practical Midwestern settlement with grief, "well, that's how it goes. You can't keep a house like a museum. She wouldn't have wanted that, your mother. She hated clutter. She'd have run this sale better than that girl with the fanny pack, I'll say that."

"She'd have priced everything higher and refused to bargain," Diane said.

"She'd have made them wipe their feet," Mrs. Pohl said, and the two of them laughed in the front hall, briefly, helplessly, and the granddaughter looked relieved, and a man edged past them carrying the telephone table out the door.

There was a bad moment around three. Diane had gone upstairs — she had been avoiding upstairs — to check whether anything remained in her old bedroom, which had been a sewing room for thirty-five years and held nothing of hers, and on her way back down the hall she passed the open door of her mother's room and saw a woman trying on her mother's winter coat. The good coat, the dark green wool with the fox-trimmed collar, bought at Lazarus in downtown Columbus on a trip Diane half-remembered, an extravagance her mother had defended for the rest of her life on cost-per-wear grounds. The woman had it on and was turning in front of the closet mirror, and the coat fit her, and she said to her friend, "Feel this wool," and the friend felt it, and the woman flipped the collar up against her jaw exactly the way Arlene used to, the identical gesture, the fur against the cheek, and for a moment — the mirror, the green wool, the angle of the head — Diane's whole body misfired, and she had to put her hand on the doorframe.

The woman saw her in the mirror. "Oh — am I in the way? Are we allowed up here?"

"You're fine," Diane said. "It's a good coat."

"It's a beautiful coat. They want fifteen for it. Do you know if they'd take ten?"

"Pay the fifteen," Diane said, and her voice came out with so much of her mother in it, the flat unanswerable authority, that the woman blinked and said okay, okay, fifteen, and Diane went downstairs and out the back door and stood by the clothesline poles in the heat until her breathing settled. From outside she could hear the sale continuing without her, the screen door's spring stretching and snapping shut, stretching and snapping, the sound of her entire childhood's summers, every entrance and exit of her life from age zero to eighteen announced by that exact sound, and she thought: I should take the screen door. The thought was insane and she examined it from a distance. The spring, then. She could take the spring. She did not take the spring. She stood in the yard and let the sound happen over and over, banking it, and went back inside.

The piano did not sell. Sherry had warned her it wouldn't. Nobody can give a piano away anymore, hon, it's a crying shame, it's the cell phones — Sherry's theory of civilization had several pillars and the cell phones were load-bearing. The piano was a Wurlitzer spinet on which Diane had taken four years of lessons from Mrs. Castle on Erie Street and at which her mother, who had never had a lesson in her life, sometimes sat on winter evenings and picked out hymns with two hands that did not fully cooperate with each other, "In the Garden" and "What a Friend We Have in Jesus," singing under her breath, not performing, barely audible from the kitchen, doing something private in the middle of the living room. People lifted the lid all afternoon and plinked the same five or six notes — everyone played the same notes, Diane noticed, some universal estate-sale riff — and put the lid back down and read the tag, FREE TO GOOD HOME MUST MOVE YOURSELF, and walked on. At four-fifteen a woman with a teenage son stood at it longer than the others, and the son sat down and played, actually played, a few bars of something classical and then, grinning, a few bars of something from a video game, and the mother said they had no truck and no money to hire movers, and Sherry said, free is free but moving's on you, and they went away to make calls, and Diane found that she was rooting for them with an intensity that embarrassed her, standing in the kitchen doorway gripping her own wrist.

The hidden thing she found by accident, at the slack end of the afternoon. The buyers of the dining set had come back with their truck and their brother-in-law, and in moving the table out they'd had to take the drawers out of the buffet that was going with it, and in the bottom drawer, taped to the underside of the drawer above, where it had ridden invisibly for who knew how many years, was an envelope. The brother-in-law found it and handed it to Diane with the slightly ceremonial unease people have around other people's sealed envelopes. "This'd be yours," he said.

She thought it would be money. Her mother's generation hid money the way squirrels hid acorns, in coat linings and coffee cans and the family Bible; Diane had already gone through the house in April for exactly this reason and had found ninety dollars in a sock and two hundred in an empty oatmeal canister marked, with magnificent indifference to security, RAINY DAY. But the envelope was light. Inside was a prescription pill bottle, the label scraped off, and inside the bottle, rattling like seeds, were teeth. Small teeth. Baby teeth. And folded around the bottle, a half-sheet of paper in her mother's handwriting, the upright Palmer-method hand that had labeled the deviled-egg plate and signed forty years of report cards: Diane's — and a column of dates, 1962, 1963, 1963, 1964, each tooth logged like a transaction, the last entry 1968, and then beneath the column, in different ink, added later, who knew when, maybe decades later: Could not throw these away. Foolish. A.V.

Diane stood in the dining room — which was no longer a dining room, which was a square of carpet with four deep round dents where the table's feet had pressed for sixty years — holding her own teeth in a pill bottle, and the brother-in-law said, "Everything okay?" and she said, "Yes," and he and the young couple carried the buffet out the front door, down the seven steps, the fourth one giving a little under their weight, and Diane read the half-sheet of paper twice more. Could not throw these away. Foolish. Her mother, who threw everything away. Her mother, who had hated clutter, who had narrated order, who had given away Diane's entire childhood — the books, the dolls, the artwork — in ruthless purges throughout the seventies, who had been so unsentimental that Diane had constructed an entire adulthood, two hours south, in partial protest of that unsentimentality, filling her own house with keepsakes as a form of argument. Foolish. A.V. The note was addressed to no one. It was addressed to whoever found it, which was to say it was a confession sealed in a drawer, made to the future, made to her. She put the bottle and the note in her purse, next to the wooden spoon.

By five-thirty it was nearly over. Sherry's policy for the last hour was half off everything, and the final wave came through, the dealers and the deal-hunters, and what they did not take Sherry's crew began boxing for the charity truck that would come Saturday. The rooms emptied with shocking speed at the end, the way a bathtub seems to drain faster at the last. The barometer sold, finally, half off, to the old man who had tapped it; he came back for it at quarter to six, and Diane watched him carry it to his car holding it level in front of him with both hands, like something that could spill. The teenage boy and his mother came back too, triumphant, with an uncle and a borrowed landscaping trailer, and four strangers and Sherry and Diane herself got the Wurlitzer down the porch steps on a dolly, everyone shouting contradictory instructions, the fourth step giving its little groan under five hundred pounds of piano and history, and when it was strapped to the trailer the boy sat down on the trailer's edge in the driveway and played a few bars right there, the piano facing the street, the sound rolling out over Linden Street in the long gold light, and Mrs. Pohl, who it turned out had been watching from her porch all afternoon, applauded across the distance with her small hands.

Then they were gone, all of them. Sherry counted out the cash on the kitchen counter, minus commission, and had Diane sign the clipboard, and said it had been a real good sale, a real good turnout, you'd be surprised how many aren't, and squeezed Diane's forearm with genuine if efficient warmth, and her crew loaded the card tables, and the trucks pulled away.

Diane walked through the house once. Her footsteps were loud now; the house had a new acoustic, the sound of itself empty, which was a sound it had perhaps never made before in her hearing — there had always been furniture, always carpet and curtains and upholstery drinking up the echoes. The living room held the dents in the carpet, constellations of them, here the sofa, here the piano, here her father's chair. The rectangle of gold wallpaper glowed behind where the telephone table had been. In the den, on the floor, a single playing card, the queen of clubs, escaped from some sold deck. Upstairs the closet in her mother's room stood open and bare except for the wire hangers and the smell, which was still, faintly, unmistakably, her mother's smell, and would be until the new people painted.

She went out onto the porch and pulled the front door shut behind her and heard the screen door's spring stretch and snap one more time. The evening was coming on the way Ohio evenings did in August, the heat releasing its grip grudgingly, the light going amber and horizontal down the street, lighting up the Pohls' windows, the maples, the lawns. Somewhere a few blocks over a mower was running. Diane sat down on the top step with her purse in her lap and took out the pill bottle and shook it gently, and the teeth made their small dry sound, dice, seeds, hail on a window, and she sat there for a while, not crying exactly, holding the rattle of herself at six years old, saved against all stated policy by a woman who had called it foolish and done it anyway, and the fourth step did not creak because no one was on it, and behind her the house stood empty and ahead of her the street went on with its evening, and when the streetlights came on she stood up, tested the lock, and counted the steps on her way down.
