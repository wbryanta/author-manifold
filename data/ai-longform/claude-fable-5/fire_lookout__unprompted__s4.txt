The smoke rose at six minutes past two, and Iris saw it before it was anything, when it was still just a wrongness in the light over Bell Creek drainage, a slight thickening, the way milk goes into tea. She had been watching that country for eleven summers and she knew its weathers and its dust devils and the pale exhalations of pollen the lodgepole gave off in June, and she knew this was none of those. She stood up out of the canvas chair so fast it went over backward behind her, and she did not pick it up.

The tower was no longer a tower in any official sense. The Forest Service had struck it from the detection network three years ago, when the cameras went in on Granite Butte, and what remained was a fourteen-by-fourteen glass room on sixty feet of cross-braced steel that Iris occupied under a caretaker agreement renewed each spring with less enthusiasm by a district ranger she had never met in person. This was the last summer. In September a contractor would come and decide whether the structure could be dismantled for salvage or would have to be dropped where it stood. She had signed something acknowledging this. She had told herself all season that she was at peace with it, the way you tell yourself things in a glass room with nobody to contradict you.

The Osborne fire finder still worked because she had kept it working, oiling the ring, keeping the sight hairs taut, though there was no one to call her readings to. She bent over it now out of eleven years of habit and swung the brass ring until the smoke sat behind the vertical hair, and she read the azimuth twice to be sure: two hundred eleven degrees and thirty minutes. She laid the tape on the map under the glass and ran her finger out along that bearing, over Sheep Ridge, down the long fall of timber on its south side, into the bottom of Bell Creek, and her finger stopped where she had known it would stop, where it had been heading since before she touched the instrument, at the little penciled square she herself had drawn nine years ago and never erased. Della's cabin. Or near enough to it that the difference didn't matter.

She straightened up and made herself breathe. Distance, four miles line of sight, maybe four and a half. The smoke was building now, no longer a thickening but a column, gray-white at the top and dirtier at the base, leaning a little northeast on the afternoon wind. Grass and brush, she thought, reading it the way she'd been taught a long time ago by a man named Carl Odegaard, who'd held this tower for thirty years before her and who used to say smoke tells you everything if you have the patience to be told. White smoke, fast and feathery: light fuels. If it darkened it was getting into the timber. If it went black it had found something man-made, tar paper, propane, the creosoted logs of an old cabin.

It was the eleventh of August and there had been no rain since the third week of June. The grass on the south slopes had cured out yellow by the Fourth of July. The fire danger sign down at the highway junction, which she had not seen in two months but did not need to see, would be pegged at extreme.

She picked up the radio because her hand did it without consulting her. The radio was a kingwood-colored Bendix that had been old when she inherited it, and it had died on the second of August, not dramatically, just a fading, the dispatch traffic from the working frequencies growing thinner and more distant over a week until one morning there was only a faint frying sound, and then not even that. She had taken the back off and found nothing she knew how to fix. She'd reported it the next time the relief packer came up with her groceries and propane, and the packer, a heavyset kid named Dustin who treated her with the elaborate courtesy people extend to the very old or the slightly mad, had said he'd pass it along, and that had been nine days ago. She keyed the mic anyway and said, into the dead plastic, "Granite, this is Sentinel Point, I have a smoke," and heard her own voice go nowhere, absorbed by the glass and the maps and the silence, and she set the radio down with a gentleness that surprised her, as if it were the radio's feelings she had to consider.

No phone. There had never been a phone. There was a cell signal, theoretically, on a rock outcrop two miles north along the ridge, where she had twice in eleven years gotten a single bar by holding the phone above her head like an offering, but two miles north was two miles farther from Bell Creek, and the smoke was not going to wait while she stood on a rock conducting an experiment. The road — the road was eight miles of switchbacked two-track down the east face to the trailhead, and her truck was at the trailhead because the last three miles were closed to vehicles, had been since the washout in May, and from the trailhead it was eleven more miles of gravel to the highway and the nearest occupied building. Eight miles down, on foot, took her two and a half hours on a good day, and she had not had a good day, knee-wise, since sometime in July.

But the trail — the old trail, the one Carl had shown her, the one nobody maintained anymore — dropped off the west side of the ridge straight down into Bell Creek. It was steep enough in two places that you went down it with your hands, and it was blowdown and beargrass for the last mile, but it was, by the map and by her memory, three and a half miles from the tower to the creek bottom, and the creek bottom was a half mile of game trail from Della's cabin.

She stood at the glass for thirty seconds, which is a long time when you are counting it against a column of smoke, and she understood with a clarity that felt almost chemical that the choice in front of her was not really about routes. Eight miles down the road was the responsible thing, the thing that ended with engines and a helicopter and trained people, hours from now, and it was also the thing that did not require her to arrive at her sister's cabin and find whatever there was to find. Three and a half miles down the west face was the thing that ended with Iris herself, alone, fifty-three years old with a bad knee and a shovel, standing in front of a fire. She had not spoken to Della in nine years. She had drawn the penciled square on the map nine years ago, the first summer after their mother died, and she had spent some part of every clear day since then not looking at that drainage on purpose, which is a way of looking at something all the time.

She went down the sixty feet of stairs faster than she had gone down them all summer.

In the storage box at the base of the tower she kept the tools the Forest Service had never bothered to collect: a pulaski with a handle she'd replaced herself, a round-point shovel, a five-gallon backpack pump she filled from the cistern, hating every pound of it even as she filled it, because forty-some pounds of water on her back down that grade was a punishment, and also because she knew what five gallons amounted to against a fire that could be seen from four miles away, which was nothing, which was a gesture. She took it anyway. She took the pulaski and left the shovel. She took two quarts of drinking water, a headlamp, the first-aid kit, a wool shirt because the nights came down cold even in August, and she wrote on the chalkboard by the door, because Carl had taught her you always leave word: 8/11, 1420, SMOKE BELL CK NEAR DELLA'S, GONE DOWN W. TRAIL. She looked at the words for a second. They were the first time she had written her sister's name in years, and her hand had produced it without hesitation, the D round and certain, and something about that undid her a little, that the name lived that close to the surface.

The west trail began as a memory of a trail and got worse. For the first mile it followed the ridge spine through whitebark pine, half of them dead and silver, beetle-killed, standing like the masts of a drowned fleet, and she could see the smoke the whole way, off her right shoulder, fattening. It had a shape now, a definite stalk and a head that was starting to flatten where it hit the wind shear aloft, and the base of it had gone a brown that she did not like, the color smoke takes when it is eating brush in quantity. She tried to calculate acreage and made herself stop. Carl's voice in her head: don't tell the smoke what it is, let it tell you.

Then the trail dropped off the spine and she lost the smoke and there was only the descent, which required everything. The grade was vicious. Her knee announced itself on the third switchback and by the tenth it was a hot wire from hip to ankle, and she fell into the rhythm she'd learned for it, leading with the good leg, planting the pulaski handle like a cane, the pump sloshing against her spine with a sound like a slow heartbeat. Blowdown across the trail every hundred feet, gray trunks she went over or under or around, and the beargrass slick as wet hair underfoot. She thought, the way you think on a long descent, in fragments that the body's labor breaks apart and reassembles.

She thought about the last conversation, which had not been a conversation. The kitchen of their mother's house in town, four days after the funeral, the two of them dividing things, and how civil it had been right up until it wasn't. Della had wanted the cabin and Iris had not contested it, had not wanted it, and that should have been the end, but Iris had said — and she could still hear herself saying it, could hear the exact pitch of her own voice, reasonable, surgical — that it made sense for Della to have the cabin since Della had been there, after all, for everything, since Della had been the one who decided everything, the medications, the hospice, the do-not-resuscitate, all of it decided by the time Iris ever heard about any of it. And Della had set down the box she was holding, their mother's canning jars, set it down so carefully that the jars didn't even ring, and said: You were in a tower, Iris. You were sixty feet up a ladder in another county for the last three summers of her life. You watched for smoke. You want to know what I watched?

And Iris had said the thing. She had never repeated it to anyone, never said it aloud since, but it lived in her intact, every word, the way shrapnel lives in flesh. She had said that some people climb towers and some people stand at deathbeds and arrange to be indispensable, and that she wasn't sure which of them had been hiding. She had meant it to wound and it had wounded, and the worst of it, the part that nine years of looking away from Bell Creek had not eroded, was that she had watched it land on her sister's face and felt, for one full second before the shame arrived, satisfied.

Della had not thrown her out. That would have been easier. Della had picked the box of jars back up and carried it to her truck and made two more trips for the rest of her things, past Iris standing in the kitchen, and at the door the last time she had paused and said, not even angrily, with a kind of terrible administrative finality: All right. We know where we are now. And they had known where they were for nine years. There had been one card, the second Christmas, in Della's handwriting, signed only with her name, no message, and Iris had spent six weeks composing replies in her head and sent none of them, and after that, nothing. The penciled square on the map. The drainage she didn't look at. Two women in one county, twelve miles apart as the raven flies, orbiting their mother's death like two stones around a dark star.

The trail bottomed into the creek and she stopped and drank, kneeling, the pump dragging at her shoulders, and when she stood she could smell it. Not see it — the canopy was thick along the water — but smell it, woodsmoke and something underneath the woodsmoke, a chemical bitterness, fabric maybe, or foam, or paint, and her heart did something arrhythmic and animal. She went down the creek at a pace her knee billed her for with every step, through alder that grabbed and dogwood that whipped, and the smell got stronger, and then she could hear it, a sound like wind in aspens except there was no wind down here in the bottom, and then the timber opened into the long meadow she had not seen in nine years, and she saw.

The cabin stood. That was the first thing, the thing her eyes went to before anything else and held onto: the cabin stood, the cabin was not burning, its green metal roof dull under the smoke but whole. The fire was in the meadow east of it, between the cabin and the timbered toe of the slope, an acre, maybe an acre and a half of burned and burning grass, and at the heart of the black, the origin, obvious as a signature, was a burn pile. A big one, the size of a small car, still flaming hard at its core, and around it the grass fire had run with the upslope afternoon wind and gotten into a patch of juniper at the meadow's edge, and one juniper was torching as Iris came out of the trees, going up all at once with that sound they make, a sound like tearing canvas, throwing embers uphill toward the timber.

And in front of the burn, between the fire and the cabin, working a shovel along the flank with a fury that read at a hundred yards as either expertise or panic, was Della.

She was gray. That was what nine years had done that the smoke now exaggerated: her sister's hair, which had been dark like their father's, had gone iron gray, and she had it tied back, and she was in jeans and an untucked flannel shirt and she was beating and scraping at the fire's edge, throwing dirt, moving along the line, and losing. Anyone could see she was losing. The flank she worked held, but the head of it, upslope by the junipers, was nobody's and was traveling.

Iris crossed the meadow at a hobbling run and did not call out until she was close, because she had learned not to startle people near fire, and then she said, "Della. The head. Forget the flank, we have to hit the head before it's in the trees."

Della turned. There was a moment — Iris would keep that moment, would take it back up the mountain with her and keep it for whatever years she had — when Della's face did everything at once: the blank shock of an apparition, because what stood in front of her was her estranged sister materialized out of the timber with a pump on her back like some hallucination of the guilty; and then recognition; and then something that was not relief and was not anger but was made of both, compressed; and then, because Della had been their father's daughter too and knew fire, the face simply set, and she said, "It crowned in that juniper. There's two more it'll take."

"I know. Pump's full. You flank, I'll knock it down ahead, we pinch it."

"Iris—"

"After," Iris said. "Everything after."

They went at it. The pump's wand threw a stream pathetic against the torching juniper but real against the grass fire's leading edge, and Iris worked it the way Carl had taught her thirty years ago when she was a seasonal with everything still ahead of her: short bursts at the base of the flame, never the flame itself, and behind her Della came with the shovel, scraping to mineral soil, throwing dirt on what the water knocked down, and the two of them moved along the head of the fire in a coordination that required no speech, that fell into place between them with a horrible easiness, as if their bodies had spent nine years rehearsing. The second juniper caught and went up and they let it, fell back, came in again behind it. The smoke shifted and took them and they coughed and worked blind and the wind, in the only mercy of the afternoon, began about five o'clock to lie down the way the upslope wind does when the day's heat breaks, and the fire's head, robbed of it, slowed, and they pinched it against an old game trail worn to bare dirt, and held it there, and held it.

By seven the open flame was down everywhere but the burn pile itself, which would smolder for days, and the meadow was a black acre and a half steaming in the long light, and the two of them walked the perimeter twice, Iris spraying the last of the water on smokes along the line, Della turning over hot spots with the shovel, and neither of them had yet said anything that was not operational. Iris's knee had gone past pain into a kind of static. Her hands were black to the wrist. There was a burn hole in the wool shirt she didn't remember getting.

It was Della who stopped first. She stood the shovel in the dirt and leaned on it and looked at the burn pile, and Iris came and stood beside her, six feet away, the distance two strangers keep, and looked at it too, and saw, now that there was time to see, what it was. What it had been. In the collapsed and glowing heart of it there were bedsprings. There was the scrolled iron foot of a bed frame she knew, that had stood in the back bedroom of the cabin her whole childhood, their mother's bed, the bed their mother had been moved out of, at the end, to town. There were the charred ribs of a dresser. There was, half-consumed at the pile's edge where Della's flank attack had starved the fire, a wooden box, and Iris recognized it with her whole body before her mind got there: the letter box, cedar, their grandmother's, the box of every letter their mother had kept.

"You were burning her things," Iris said.

"I was going to do it in October," Della said. Her voice was wrecked with smoke and sounded almost like their mother's. "First snow. I had it planned for first snow, like a sensible person. And then this morning I was carrying coffee past that room and I just — couldn't have it in the house one more day. Nine years I couldn't touch it and this morning I couldn't have it." She wiped her face with the back of her wrist and left a black streak. "I put one match to it. In August. Forty years in this country and I put one match to a burn pile in August." She laughed, one syllable, with nothing in it. "You must think—"

"I don't think anything," Iris said.

"You came down the west trail." It was not a question. Della was still looking at the pile. "Nobody's come down that trail in years. The radio's out, isn't it. You'd have called it in if the radio worked. You came down that trail with a pump on your back because you saw smoke in my drainage."

"Yes."

"How fast?"

"Two hours. About."

Della was quiet. The smoke off the meadow rose straight now in the evening calm, thin and white, the smoke of a thing finished. Somewhere up the burned slope a single snag popped. "I'd have lost the cabin," Della said. "Maybe not. Maybe the wind would have done what it did anyway. But I was losing it when you came out of those trees, and I knew I was losing it, and I had already started deciding what I'd grab." She turned then, and looked at her sister fully, the first full look, and Iris made herself stand in it. "You watched for smoke," Della said, and the words were the old words, the kitchen words, the wound, and Iris braced — and then heard how Della had said them. Not as accusation. As something turned over in the hand, examined in new light. "All these years. You watched for smoke."

"It's what I know how to do," Iris said. "It's the only thing I —" and then her voice did something it had not done in years and she stopped and breathed and said it plainly, because the meadow was black and the light was going gold and she was too tired to carry it any farther: "I drew your cabin on my map. The first summer. I've looked away from it every day for nine years. That's two looks a day at least, Della. Both ways. That's thousands."

The shovel handle creaked under Della's weight. Down the meadow the creek made its noise, the noise it had made under every word either of them had ever said in this place. "There's water in the house," Della said finally. "And I'm not letting you back up that trail tonight on that knee, so don't construct the argument." She pulled the shovel out of the dirt. "We'll have to walk this line again at midnight. And at first light. It'll throw smokes for a week."

"I know," Iris said. "I'll stay till it's cold."

They started toward the cabin through the unburned grass, the two of them, not touching, their long shadows running out ahead eastward across the black ground, and behind them the pile breathed and ticked and settled, sending up its thin column into the perfectly still air, where anyone watching from a high place could have seen it for what it now was: a smoke going out.
