The frost was still on the windshield when Emil Sorensen carried his tool case out to the truck, and he scraped it clear with the edge of his hand the way he had done for sixty years, though his hand was no longer good for much scraping and the cold went into the knuckles and stayed there. The case went on the passenger seat where Ruth used to sit. It was an old leather doctor's bag, the handles wrapped in electrical tape, and inside it were the tuning lever with the rosewood grip worn pale, the felt temperament strip rolled like a bandage, the rubber mutes, the Papps mute for the high treble, a few spare strings coiled in waxed paper, and the tuning fork, A440, that his own father had given him in 1948 with the words that a fork never lies and never flatters, which is more than can be said of most things a man will listen to in his life.

He had four stops written on the back of an envelope, though he did not need the envelope. He had driven this route in every weather there was, in years when the gravel roads were drifted shut and he chained up all four tires, in years when the heat made the corn tick and creak like something straining at its moorings. The route had once taken a full week. There had been a time, in the fifties and into the sixties, when every second farmhouse in the county had a piano in the parlor, and the pianos got played, and a man who could tune by ear and was honest about what a soundboard could and couldn't do had more work than daylight. Then the televisions came, and then the kids went to the cities, and then the kids' kids didn't come back at all, and one by one the pianos fell silent and went out of tune in the patient way that wood and wire go out of tune, which is to say without complaint, drifting flat all together so that nobody noticed, the way a family can drift apart and still sit down to supper.

Now the route was one day. Four stops. After today there would be no stops, because he had told the doctor he would quit driving by Thanksgiving, and he was a man who kept his word even when the word had been pried out of him.

The sun came up red and small over the Lindqvist section as he drove east, and the stubble fields lay silvered, and a line of geese went over very high, talking among themselves about somewhere else. He turned in at the Magnusson place a little after eight.

Hulda Magnusson had the coffee on, as he had known she would, and a plate of pepparkakor that were store-bought now, though neither of them said so. She was eighty-six and her hands shook worse than his, and she led him into the front room where the piano stood against the north wall where it had stood since before either of them was born. It was a Hamilton upright, oak case gone almost black, with a tapestry runner across the top and on the runner the photographs: her husband Alvar in his army uniform, the daughters at their confirmations, the grandchildren in school pictures that stopped getting replaced around 1995, so that those children were frozen at nine and eleven forever, gap-toothed, in sweaters nobody made anymore.

"It's gone sour again," Hulda said, which is what she said every year, though no hand had touched the keys since her sister died, and that was — he did the arithmetic without wanting to — eleven years now. The sister, Inga, had played hymns on Sunday evenings with the pedal down too far, everything swimming in its own echo, and Alvar would sit with his eyes closed pretending to be moved when he was in fact asleep.

"We'll see what she says," Emil said, and opened the lid and pulled the bench out and set his bag on the floor.

He struck A above middle C and held the fork to his ear and the two tones lay against each other and wavered, the slow pulse of a string gone flat, maybe six cents, not bad for a year of standing in a room heated by a woodstove that dried the air to nothing every winter. He fitted the lever onto the pin and felt for the place where the pin would move, that small certain give, and listened for the beats to slow, slow, and stop. There was a moment in every unison when the wavering ceased and the tone went still and pure and seemed to come not from the string but from somewhere behind the string, and after sixty years that moment still gave him a quiet animal satisfaction, like a door closing flush in its frame.

Hulda sat in the wing chair with her coffee and watched him work, which was also what she did every year, and they did not talk much, because the work required listening and she respected that, having been married forty-nine years to a man who spoke perhaps a thousand words a season. While he laid the temperament in the middle octave, fourths and fifths, each interval beating just so, just slightly imperfect in the way that lets all keys be equally and bearably imperfect, she told him anyway, in pieces, about the daughter in Tucson and the hip and the price the elevator was paying for soybeans, and he said mm at the right places, and the room filled slowly with order, note by note, the way a field fills with rows.

When he finished he played a few chords — he was no pianist, he had never been a pianist, his gift was in his ears and not his hands — and the old Hamilton sounded as good as a sixty-year-old upright with a hairline crack in the soundboard was going to sound, which was good enough, which was honest.

"Inga would be pleased," Hulda said.

"It'll hold till spring," Emil said, and then, because it had to be said, "Hulda, this is my last round. I'm hanging it up."

She looked at him a long moment over her cup. "Then who'll tune it?"

"There's a young fellow out of Sioux Falls. Does it all with a machine, a little computer. He's all right. I'll leave you his card."

"A machine," Hulda said, in the tone she would have used for a casserole made from a box. She did not say what they both knew, which was that nobody would ever call the young fellow, that the piano would now begin its long last slide, a few cents a year, flat and flatter, the felts mothing away, until one of the grandchildren, settling the estate from a phone in another state, told the auctioneer it wasn't worth the cost of moving and it went to a man with a trailer who broke it up for the cast iron.

She made him take cookies in a sandwich bag. At the door she put her shaking hand on his sleeve and said, "You were good to keep coming all these years, Emil. We none of us play anymore. You knew that."

"A piano in tune is a piano in tune," he said, "whether anybody's listening or not," and he believed it the way he believed in things he could no longer have explained to anybody, and he drove on.

The second stop was the old Pratt place, and there was nobody there at all. The Pratts had gone to Rochester in 2009 when Lyle's kidneys gave out, and the house had been rented twice and then not rented, and now it stood with its windows blanked by sun and its porch sagging like a lip, and still every November Emil came, because Carol Pratt sent him a check every October from Rochester with a note in her schoolteacher's hand: For the piano, if you're still willing. The key was under the geranium pot, though the geranium was eleven years dead, a gray fist of stems.

Inside, the house had the smell that empty houses get, dust and cold plaster and a sweetness underneath like dried apples, the smell of time itself, if time had a smell, and he supposed it did and this was it. The furniture was mostly gone but the piano was there, because pianos are always there; a piano is the last thing to leave any house, being too heavy for sentiment and too sentimental for sale. It was a Kimball console, mahogany, 1962 or thereabouts, bought new the year their Debbie started lessons, and on the music desk there was still a hymnal and a yellow Schaum book, Book Two, the one with the red cover gone pink, open to a piece called "The Juggler" that Debbie Pratt had presumably never gotten past, because the page was soft and gray at the corner from being turned to and the next page was crisp as the day it was printed.

He tuned it in the cold with his coat on, his breath showing. The pins were getting loose in the block — the dry years had shrunk the wood — and a few of them wouldn't hold a fine setting, would slip back a hair as soon as he eased the lever off, and he had to set them with a little jerk, sharp of true, and let them settle down onto the pitch. There was a trick to it, leaving a thing very slightly wrong so that it would come right on its own. You couldn't force a pin any more than you could force a season or a son. You could only set it where it would settle.

He thought about Daniel then, for the first time that day, though it was not true that there was a first time each day, because Daniel was not something he thought about so much as something he thought inside of, a weather, and what happened in the Pratts' cold parlor was only that the weather turned and he noticed it. Daniel at six, standing at his elbow in some farmhouse just like this one, forty miles and sixty years from here, while Emil tuned, and the boy saying, "There. It stopped wobbling," before Emil's own ear had caught it. Daniel had the ear. He had it the way some kids have a throwing arm, casually, unearned, an embarrassment of it. He could name a pitch from the squeal of the barn door. He could tell which cow's bell was which in the dark. Ruth said he got it from Emil, and Emil said no, his own ear was a workman's ear, trained, calloused; the boy's was something else, something that had skipped down out of nowhere like a hawk into a hayfield.

He finished the Kimball and played one chord, C major, and it stood in the empty house astonishingly loud, with nothing to soak it up, no curtains, no people, and it rang on and on in the bare rooms and he stood listening to it die away into the corners. Then he closed the lid over the keys, and squared the Schaum book on the desk, and let himself out, and put the key back under the dead geranium, and on the porch he stood a minute looking out at the fields, and then he wrote in his receipt book, Pratt — tuned and in good order — no charge this year, and tore the page out and slipped it under the door, where nobody would ever read it, which did not seem to him a reason not to write it.

By noon he was at Carl Odegaard's, and Carl was the one stop where the radio of the day got turned off in him and something else came on, because Carl was ninety-four and blind and entirely alone except for the girl from the county who came Tuesdays and Fridays, and Carl loved the tuning. Not the result. The tuning itself.

"Is that Sorensen?" Carl called from his chair when Emil knocked and came in, as he had been told to do. "Or is it the angel of death? Either way, wipe your feet."

"It's both," Emil said. "We carpool now. Saves gas."

The Odegaard piano was a Wurlitzer spinet, a mean little instrument, all a spinet's sins: short strings, cramped action, a bass like somebody humming through a comb. Greta Odegaard had played it every evening of her married life, schottisches and waltzes and hymns, while Carl sawed along on the fiddle, and the neighbors had come Saturdays in winter and rolled the rug back, and that was a county and a century that was gone now as completely as Nineveh. Greta had been dead since '03. The fiddle hung on the wall by the stove. Carl could not see it there, but he knew where it was, and sometimes, he had told Emil once, he reached up and touched the strings, just to hear them mutter.

"Go slow today," Carl said, when Emil had set up. "Don't rush her."

So Emil went slow. He always went slow at Carl's. He struck the fork and let it sing, and Carl said, "A. There she is. The one fixed star." And then for two hours Emil worked up and down the little spinet, fourths and fifths, octaves, unisons, the long patient grammar of it, and Carl sat with his white head tipped back and his eyes closed — though it made no difference, the closing, it was a habit of attention left over from when he could see — and listened to the beats the way another man might watch clouds. Now and then he spoke. "That one's fighting you." "There — clean as well water." "That string's false, isn't it, got a wow in it that won't tune out." And he was right; it was a false string, corroded somewhere along its length, beating against itself, and nothing to be done but get it as close as God and the wire allowed.

"You know what I like about it," Carl said, while Emil packed up. "It's the only music where you can hear the man thinking. A fellow plays you a song, he's telling you something he already knows. A fellow tuning, he's finding out. I can hear you finding out. Greta used to say, when Sorensen comes it's better than the Grand Ole Opry, and I'd say to her, that's because Sorensen don't sing."

Emil told him then too, about quitting, because he owed Carl that, and Carl was quiet for a moment, his hands flat on the arms of his chair, big hands gone soft, the hands of a man who had milked by hand for fifty years.

"Well," Carl said. "Then I've heard it for the last time. A man ought to be told when it's the last time. Mostly we aren't, you know. Mostly the last time goes by dressed up as an ordinary Tuesday." He turned his face toward Emil, and his eyes, which had not seen anything in nine years, were aimed very precisely. "Play her something before you go. I know you say you can't play. Play anyway. Nobody here but the blind and the dead, and we're not fussy."

So Emil sat down at the spinet he had just tuned and laid his stiff hands on the keys and played the only thing his hands still knew without being told, which was the first eight bars of Schumann, the "Träumerei," very slowly, with two wrong notes that he heard like stones dropped in a still pond, and then he stopped, because the rest of it was beyond him and always had been.

"Now that," said Carl, "was terrible. Thank you."

The light was going long and yellow when he came at last to the church.

Bethel Lutheran stood where it had stood since 1891, white clapboard, square steeple, a windbreak of spruce planted by men whose names were on the stones behind it. The congregation was down to perhaps twenty souls and a circuit pastor who came alternate Sundays, but the church was kept painted and the furnace kept working, because there are things a county will let go and things it will not, and Emil had a key, had had a key since 1958 when the council voted him sexton of the piano, an office that existed nowhere in the synod's books and that he had held longer than any pastor had held the pulpit.

Inside, the air was cold and still and smelled of candle wax and old varnish and the particular dusty sweetness of hymnals. The afternoon sun came through the west windows, which were not stained glass, only pebbled amber, and lay across the pews in long warm bars. The piano stood to the left of the chancel, a Chickering baby grand, donated in 1948 by the Halvorsen family in memory of their boys, and it was, and had always been, the best instrument in forty miles, with a tone in the middle register like a voice you trusted.

He set his bag on the front pew and opened the lid and propped it, and for a while he just stood with his hand flat on the curve of the case, the way you'd stand with your hand on the shoulder of an old horse.

Daniel had played here. December the sixteenth, 1962. Eleven years old, in a tie borrowed from the Pratt boy because his own had gravy on it discovered too late, his hair wetted down by Ruth in the truck with her own spit and palm, his face white as the clapboard. Miss Engebretson's students, all nine of them, a recital squeezed in before the Christmas program, the church full because in that county anything was full, weddings, funerals, recitals, threshing demonstrations; the people came because coming was what there was. Emil had tuned this same Chickering two days before, had gone over it twice, had voiced the hammers in the middle two octaves with a needle until the tone came round and even, and he had told no one that he did it, not even Ruth, certainly not the boy.

Daniel had played the Bach invention, the one in C, the two hands chasing each other like swallows, and he had played it too fast, the way the gifted always play too fast, hungry, and there was one stumble in the left hand near the end that nobody noticed but the two of them, and the boy's eyes had flicked up — Emil was in the fourth pew, by the post — and Emil had given him nothing, no wince, no nod, only steadiness, and the boy had gathered the stumble up into the music like a man gathering a dropped rein, and finished. And then the Schumann. The "Träumerei." And in the Schumann he had not been fast or hungry or eleven. He had sat very still and let the melody rise and lean and settle, beat by beat, and somewhere in the middle of it Emil had understood, with a clarity that frightened him, that his son had something he himself would never have, that the boy could go past the place where Emil lived, that all the tuning, all the fourths and fifths, all the patient grammar, had been in the family one generation as preparation, the way a man clears rock from a field he will never himself harvest. Ruth had taken his hand in the pew. He had not been able to look at her.

In April the river took him. The Vermillion, swollen and gray with the melt, and the ice along the banks rotten in that lying spring way, and the Pratt boy ran two miles to the Lindqvist place with his coat gone and his lips blue, and that was the whole of it. It had no music in it and never would. Sixty years and it had not acquired any meaning, any shape, any lesson; it was simply the false string in the middle of him, beating against itself, and nothing to be done but tune around it as close as God and the wire allowed.

He struck the fork against his knee and set its stem on the Chickering's case, and the A bloomed up out of the wood, and he began.

He worked slowly, the way Carl liked, because today he was tuning for Carl too, and for Hulda, and for Inga's wet-pedaled hymns and Greta's schottisches and Debbie Pratt's juggler who never learned to juggle, and the church filled with the old patient sound of intervals being argued into agreement. The light moved along the pews as he worked. His hands ached and he rested them when they needed it. The Chickering held its pitch beautifully; the pin block was sound; whoever tuned it next — the young fellow with his machine, or nobody — would find it had been left in good order, and that was a kind of letter a man could write, the only kind Emil had ever been any good at.

It was nearly dark when he finished the last unison in the treble, those small bright strings up where the tone is hardly tone anymore, just shine. He put the lever away, and rolled the felt strip, and closed the bag. Then he sat down on the bench, and after a while he played middle C, once, and listened to it go out into the church, into the amber windows and the hymnals and the cold air, and not come back.

He did not try the Schumann. He had played it for Carl and two wrong notes was enough wrongness for one day, and anyhow it had never been his to play. Instead he took the tuning fork from his pocket — the fork that never lied and never flattered, that his father had put in his hand in 1948 — and he struck it one more time and stood it on the music desk, stem down, where it hummed its one fixed star of a note, fainter and fainter, and he left it standing there. The next hands to open that lid would find it and wonder, and that was all right. A thing in tune is a thing in tune, whether anybody is listening or not.

He buttoned his coat and went down the aisle in the dark between the pews, his steps loud in the empty church, and at the door he stopped, the way you do, and looked back. He could not see the piano now, only the paler shape of its raised lid against the chancel, like a wing half lifted.

"Goodnight, then," he said, to the instrument, or the building, or the boy, and he went out and pulled the door to behind him, and heard the latch go home, flush in its frame, and stood a moment on the steps in the first few flakes of the year's snow before he walked to the truck and started for home.
