The invitation was engraved and was correct in every particular, and on the flap of the inner envelope there was embossed a small picture of the Mirador, the tower, the flag on the tower, the long arcade above the seawall, and beneath the picture the words THE MIRADOR · SOLEDAD POINT · 1911, as if the hotel had stood on Soledad Point without interruption since 1911. The Mirador burned to its foundation lines on the night of February 21, 1948. Eleven people died, nine of them girls who slept in the staff annex behind the laundry, and I know the number because I was a chambermaid at the Mirador in February of 1948, and because in the years since I have had occasion, more than once, to repeat it.

The Yardleys rebuilt within fourteen months. Everyone said they rebuilt it exactly as it had been, every tile fired from the original molds, every arch true to the photographs, and everyone said you could not tell the difference. I could tell the difference. The arches of the new arcade were perhaps a hand's width broader than the old arches. The tower stood a few feet farther from the sea, although no one would believe me about the tower. The bougainvillea was new bougainvillea, planted thick to look old. The grand staircase turned left where the old staircase had turned right, a change I have never seen mentioned in any of the anniversary brochures, and there was no annex behind the laundry because there was no laundry where the laundry had been. Where the annex had been there was a rose garden.

I was at the Mirador on the fourteenth of June, 1972, because Victoria Yardley was marrying Whitman Hauser of Pasadena, and because my husband had, before he died in March, sat for eleven years on two boards with the bride's father. I was the widow of Porter Mills. The Yardleys had no reason to know that the widow of Porter Mills had once counted their monogrammed linen and turned down their beds and carried their towels up the south service stairs, and I had no reason to tell them. I sent the reply card back the day it came. I am good at occasions. I am good at all occasions where the point is to appear to be having one feeling while having another.

They were married at four o'clock on the seaward lawn, the fog standing offshore in the obliging way it sometimes does in June, holding its distance until the photographs were taken. Tory Yardley wore her grandmother's lace and carried stephanotis, and during the recessional a strap of her bustle gave way, and I had the pins out of my bag before her mother could turn around. A chambermaid carries pins. A widow, it turns out, also carries pins. "Aren't you clever," Babe Yardley said, watching me work. "I used to be handy," I said, and Babe Yardley laughed as if I had said something charming, which in a sense I had.

"They'll do the pictures in the rose garden," Babe Yardley said. "The roses photograph beautifully. Daddy Yardley put that garden in himself, you know, the year they rebuilt. Wouldn't let the landscape people touch it."

"Is that right," I said.

"Three generations married out of this hotel," Babe Yardley said. "I had my own reception in that ballroom in 1949. Daddy Yardley wouldn't hear of anywhere else. He said the Mirador was the family's heart."

"It's a remarkable place," I said.

The roses were Peace and Charlotte Armstrong and they were doing very well. Roses are said to like ash. I watched the photographer arrange the bridesmaids among them, six girls in apricot organza standing more or less where the second-floor corridor of the annex had run, and then I went to find a drink.

In February of 1948 I had forty-one rooms on the second and third floors of the south wing, mornings, and turn-down at six. I was nineteen. I made eleven dollars a week plus room and found, the room being half of a nine-by-eleven on the upper floor of the annex, the other half belonging to June Echeverría, who was twenty-one, who came from a sheep family outside Bakersfield, and who intended to be a stewardess for TWA as soon as she had saved the fare to Los Angeles and the price of the charm course the airline was understood to favor. June Echeverría taught me to do a hospital corner in one motion and to iron a collar without scorching it and to smoke without coughing. She kept a postcard of the Pyrenees wedged in the frame of our mirror, sent by a grandfather she had never met, and she would tap it on her way out the door, for luck, the way other girls touched holy water. Mrs. Anaya, the housekeeper, counted the sheets against us every Friday and locked the annex doors at ten-thirty every night. The locking of the doors was for our protection. That was the phrase. For our protection.

On the night of February 21 the hotel gave the Camellia Ball, as it did every February, and the Hal Pruett orchestra came down from San Francisco, and the guests came from Hillsborough and Burlingame and Pasadena and a few, the ones the other guests watched sideways, from Hollywood. The girls were allowed to look on from the mezzanine rail until ten. At ten-thirty Mrs. Anaya locked the annex. The fire was called in at ten past two. I was not in the annex when the annex burned. People who learned, in the years afterward, that I had been at the Mirador that night sometimes asked me where I was instead, and I gave various answers, and none of the answers was true, and I am not going to give the true one now. I was nineteen. I watched the annex burn from the croquet lawn wearing a dinner jacket that did not belong to me, and if you have followed me this far you will understand why I am not going to say whose it was.

The main building had standpipes and a sprinkler system and survived until almost four, long enough for every guest to walk out the front doors carrying jewel cases and, in one instance I remember distinctly, a chafing dish. The annex was a frame building with a single interior stair, and under each window, in compliance with the county code, there was a coiled rope, and the ropes had been painted over so many times that they came off their cleats in stiff useless hoops, like something shed by a snake. I did not see June Echeverría at any window. I have been assured that this means the smoke took her first, that smoke is merciful. People assure you of things like that and then watch your face, wanting something back, and I have never known what it is they want.

The county held an inquiry. The fire marshal favored the laundry flue. The Yardleys' lawyers favored a cigarette, and a cigarette became the preferred version, since the girls were known to smoke in their rooms and were in no position to say otherwise. The families got fifteen hundred dollars apiece and signed releases, and the survivors got their back wages and signed releases, and I signed mine in a temporary office at the golf club, where Mr. Frank Yardley sat behind a borrowed desk with the ocean behind him and pushed an envelope across the blotter with two fingers. There was one hundred and forty dollars in the envelope. "You're a good girl," Mr. Yardley said. He said that the Mirador would rise again, that I would always have a place there. I took the envelope and the 4:10 Greyhound to San Francisco and I did not come back for twenty-four years and four months, and when I came back it was by engraved invitation.

The reception was in the new ballroom. The new ballroom has four exits, two at the seaward end and one behind the bandstand and one through the service pantry, and I counted them during the toasts, which is a habit I have, and located my own chair in relation to each, which is also a habit I have. The napkins had been pressed that morning in the hotel laundry and smelled faintly of the mangle. When I smell a mangle I think of June Echeverría. When I think of June Echeverría I count the exits. There were four. I have already said that.

I was seated with a Geary cousin of the groom's mother, a big pink man named Ned Geary who had been at Bohemian Grove with everyone he mentioned, and with his wife, Bunny, and with a young couple from Newport Beach who danced every dance and are not part of this. Over the squab Ned Geary discovered that I had not heard him tell about the fire.

"Forty-eight, forty-nine, in there somewhere," Ned Geary said. "The whole shebang went up like celluloid. A Camel and a Christmas tree, that's all it takes, one Camel and one dry Christmas tree."

"It was February," I said.

"February, was it." Ned Geary was not a man who paused. "Point is, she went up like celluloid, and they got every last soul out, which when you consider the era, no codes to speak of, was a miracle of God. I believe they lost a night watchman. One night watchman."

"Eleven," I said.

"Beg pardon?"

"Eleven people died."

Ned Geary looked at me with the particular geniality men of his kind reserve for women who have contradicted them. "I'd have heard that," he said.

"Staff, though, weren't they," Bunny Geary said. "If anyone. Those little Mexican girls they used to keep."

"Basque," I said. "Some of them were Basque."

There was a silence of the sort that a band exists to cover, and the band did not cover it, because the band was between numbers. "You sound like you've got it cold," Ned Geary said. He was fingering the stem of his glass and watching me the way men watch a hand of cards they have decided not to bet.

"I happened to be there."

"At the fire?"

"At the hotel," I said, and then the band came back with "More," and the young couple from Newport Beach got up, and Bunny Geary asked me where I had found my dress, and I told her, and that held us safely through the cake.

A waiter began to clear during the orchestra's break. I had squared the dirty napkins and stacked the four plates nearest me, rims true, silver laid across the top plate at the angle Mrs. Anaya required, before I noticed what my hands were doing. Bunny Geary noticed too, and looked away, the way one looks away from a tic, and I put my hands in my lap and asked her whether she found Maui spoiled. She did. Everyone we knew did. It is one of the entirely reliable subjects.

Old Mr. Yardley had been placed on the terrace, out of the band's range, in a wheelchair with a mohair rug over his knees in spite of June, and after the cake Babe Yardley brought me to him, steering me by the elbow with the light insistence of a hostess balancing her debts. "Daddy Yardley," she said, raising her voice. "This is Marian Mills. Porter's widow. You remember Porter Mills."

He took my hand. His hand was cold and dry and weighed nothing, but the eyes were the eyes that had watched me across a borrowed desk at the golf club, and they moved over my face slowly, with the unembarrassed thoroughness of the very old.

"I never forget a face," Mr. Yardley said.

I waited. Babe Yardley was already half turned toward the next obligation. The nurse adjusted the rug. Out on the lawn the bridesmaids were having their pictures taken again, against the sunset this time, the roses behind them going gray in the failing light.

"You know what's in those walls," Mr. Yardley said. He lifted one finger toward the ballroom. "Steel. Steel and block. Two-hour doors, every door in her rated for two hours. She could burn all night and never show it in the morning."

"And the girls?" I said.

He did not ask which girls. That is the thing I keep turning over, that he did not ask which girls. "The girls sleep in town now," he said. "Seaside, Del Rey Oaks. We bus them in." His eyes had gone toward the ocean and come back. "You're a good girl," he said, in exactly the voice in which he had said it across the blotter in 1948, and whether he said it because he knew me or because it was a thing he had said to women for seventy years I could not tell, and I have decided that I do not need to know, and I am lying when I say that, and both of those statements are true. The nurse turned the chair toward the lights. "He tires," she said, to no one.

I did not dance. Porter had been dead three months and not dancing was expected of me, and for once what was expected of me and what I wanted coincided exactly. I drank one more glass of champagne standing at the seaward doors, watching the fog finally come in over the point, sliding up the lawn the way it used to slide up the lawn, indifferent to the improvements, and then I picked up one of the wedding favors from the silver tray by the door. The favors were matchbooks, white, with TORY AND WHIT and the date stamped in silver. Someone had decided that, and ordered them, and no one in three generations of Yardleys had seen anything in it at all, and I want to be clear that I do not think they should have. It has been twenty-four years. It is their hotel. It was always their hotel; that was the entire point of everything I have been telling you.

I went out through the terrace and along the gravel past the rose garden, where the photographer's lights had been struck and the roses were only shapes, and around the end of the new wing to the service drive, which was where I had known it would be, because there are only so many places to put a service drive. There was a loading dock and a smell of steam and rinse water from the vents, and on the edge of the dock a girl in a maid's uniform sat with her shoes off and an unlit cigarette in her hand, going through her pockets for a match. She was perhaps nineteen. They are always perhaps nineteen.

"Here," I said, and lit it for her with the first match from Tory and Whit's wedding, and she steadied my hand with hers the way you do, not thinking about it, and then she saw the dress and the pearls and got half up.

"You lost, ma'am? Guests aren't really supposed—"

"I'm not lost," I said. "Sit down. Where do they have you living?"

"I drive in from Seaside. Me and two other girls share the gas."

"Good," I said. She looked at me, waiting for the rest of it, and there was no way to give her the rest of it that would have been anything but a stone handed to a stranger, so I did not give it to her. I asked her name instead. It was Debra. I put the matchbook in her hand and told her the tips ran better at the south end because the south rooms got the sun in the morning and people are kinder in the sun, which was true in 1948 and is, I would imagine, true now.
