The boat left Aberdeen at seven in the evening, and by half past the city was already going behind them, its lights strung out low along the water like something spilled. Martin stood at the stern rail with his coat buttoned to the throat and watched the granite turn grey, then blue, then nothing, and when there was nothing left to watch he kept watching anyway, because going inside meant sitting down, and sitting down meant being alone with the thing he was crossing the water to do.

The call had come two days before, a Sunday, while he was washing the lunch dishes. A constable with an island accent, careful, reading from notes. A man had been recovered from the water off the west side, she said, as if the sea were a cupboard a man might be taken out of. He had been in the water some time. There was no identification on the body, but a man answering his description had been reported missing from a lodging house in the town three weeks earlier, and the name on the missing report was Alexander Geddes, and the lodging-house keeper had said there was a brother somewhere south, and it had taken them this long to find him. Could he come north. There were tests they could run, she said, dental records, DNA, but these things took weeks, and a visual identification by next of kin, where possible, was — she had paused there, hunting the word — quicker.

Quicker. He had stood with a plate dripping in his hand and said yes, of course, and asked when the boats went, and written the sailing time on the back of an envelope in handwriting he did not entirely recognise as his own.

Eleven years. He did the sum again at the rail, though he had done it a dozen times since Sunday, as if it might come out differently. Eleven years since their father's funeral, since the last words either of them had spoken to the other, which had been his words, his alone, and which he could still recite the way you can recite a thing you have spent a decade failing to forget. Alec had come north for the funeral two days late — had missed the funeral itself, in fact, had arrived when the sandwiches were curling on their trays and the cousins were leaving, smelling of the train and of drink, and had stood in the kitchen of the house Martin had spent three years nursing their father in, and asked, not unkindly, not even greedily, just stupidly, what was happening with the house. And Martin, who had not slept properly in three years, who had cleaned their father and fed him and listened to him ask for Alec every day until he stopped asking, had set down the teapot he was holding and said: He stopped asking for you after the first year. You should be glad of that. It made things easier for everybody.

He could have called it back the moment it left him. He had watched it land. Alec had nodded slowly, the way he did, the way he had nodded at teachers and foremen and policemen all his life, a nod that conceded everything and surrendered nothing, and he had picked up his bag from the hall and gone out the door, and that had been the whole of it. Eleven years. One postcard, four years ago, no return address: a lighthouse on the front, white against a sky the printer had made too blue, and on the back in Alec's terrible left-leaning hand, Still afloat. A.

The wind got up as they cleared the firth and he went inside.

The boat was the usual boat: a bar with carpet patterned so that nothing showed on it, a shop selling jumpers and fudge and paperbacks, a lounge of reclining seats where freight drivers already lay under their jackets with the practised stillness of men who could sleep through weather. He had taken a cabin, a single, inside, no porthole, and he looked at the key card in his hand and could not face it yet, and so he bought a whisky at the bar and carried it to a table by the black window and sat down with his back to the television.

He tried to make himself think about practical things. The ferry got in at half seven in the morning. A police officer would meet him at the terminal — Sergeant Inkster, the constable had said, she'll be in plain clothes, she'll know you. The mortuary was at the hospital, ten minutes up the hill. The return boat sailed at seven that evening, so he would have a day in the town, whatever happened, a day in a place he had never been and his brother had apparently lived. He had packed a change of shirt without knowing why. You did not need a clean shirt to look at a body. Perhaps you did. Perhaps that was exactly what a clean shirt was for.

The whisky went down without touching him. At the next table a young couple were playing cards with the laminated safety instructions for a table-mat, laughing at something, and beyond them an old man in a good tweed coat sat very upright with his hands folded, going home or going away, you couldn't tell. Martin found he was looking at the men, all the men, the way he had been doing for two days now without meaning to — measuring them against a brother who in his mind was still thirty-four, still lean and sun-darkened from deck work, still wearing that green oilskin jacket he'd had since school. Alec would be forty-five now. Forty-five. Martin could not build the face. He could get the eyes, which were their mother's, and the nod, and the hands, but the face between kept sliding, and that, he understood, was part of what frightened him about the morning. Not that he would look at the body and know it was Alec. That he would look and not know anything at all.

There was one thing. He held onto it the way you hold a rail in weather. When Martin was ten and Alec was eight, on a Sunday outside the church at home, Martin had swung the car door shut without looking, the old Cortina with the heavy doors, and Alec's left hand had been on the pillar. The top joint of the little finger. He could still hear the sound the door made, a sound that was wrong in a way he couldn't have named, and then the silence before Alec screamed, which was the longest silence of his childhood. The joint couldn't be saved. Their father had never once blamed him out loud, which was its own kind of sentence, and Alec — Alec had turned it into a party piece within the year, holding the shortened finger up at school, telling boys a conger took it, a winch took it, a Rottweiler. He had never told anyone the truth that Martin knew of. It was the kindest thing anyone had ever done for him, and his brother had done it at nine years old, and Martin had repaid it, all told, the way he had repaid everything.

The left little finger. He had told the constable on the phone, and she had gone quiet and said she would note it, and something in the quality of her quiet had told him it was not going to be as simple as that, and he had not asked why.

He gave up on the bar at eleven and went down to the cabin. It was the size of a confessional. He lay on the bunk in his clothes with the lights off and the boat working under him, the long climb and fall of it once they were out into open water, the shudder when she came off a sea, and somewhere behind the bulkhead a steady mechanical ticking that was probably nothing and sounded like a clock. He thought he wouldn't sleep and was right. Around two he stopped pretending and put his shoes on and went up through the sleeping boat, past the lounge of breathing men, and pushed out through the heavy door onto the deck.

The wind took the door from his hand and slammed it behind him. It was blowing maybe a six, the night absolutely black except where the boat's own lights fell on the water rushing past below, white-veined, hammered-looking. No land anywhere. He got into the lee of the funnel housing and stood there with his eyes running from the cold, and thought: he was in this. Somewhere west of here, in water like this water, for three weeks. The thought arrived not as horror but as a kind of terrible arithmetic — the temperature, the days, what the sea did, what lived in it — and he made himself stand in the wind and do the sums because it seemed obscene to be warm and not do them. The constable's careful voice: he had been in the water some time. Some time. He had looked it up, of course, on Sunday night, in the worst tradition of the sleepless, and then closed the laptop and sat in the dark of his kitchen until it got light.

A crewman came around the housing with a torch, checking lashings, and stopped when he saw him. All right? the man shouted over the wind, and Martin called back that he was fine, just getting air, and the man looked at him a second longer than he needed to — they must watch for men alone at rails at night, he realised, it must be part of the job on a boat like this — and then nodded and went on, and Martin went back inside, obscurely ashamed, and lay on the bunk again, and somewhere in the small hours the sea eased, and he slept without knowing it and dreamed of nothing he kept.

The tannoy woke him at seven. Through the door, in the passage, the sounds of a boat arriving: bags, children, the bing-bong of announcements about car decks. He shaved badly in the little steel mirror, put on the clean shirt, and went up. Through the salt-greased windows of the lounge the islands were going by close on the port side, treeless, the colour of old rope, the hills coming down into the sea without negotiation, and then the town opened up around the harbour, grey roofs and gable ends shouldering each other down to the water, and it was raining, a fine sideways rain that didn't fall so much as travel.

Sergeant Inkster was a broad fair woman of about fifty in a green waterproof, and she did know him — picked him out of the foot passengers without hesitating, which made him wonder what the description had been, or whether grief had a walk. She shook his hand and said his name and thanked him for coming, as if there had been a version of this where he didn't, and led him to an unmarked car parked against the terminal wall with two gulls standing on it like an honour guard.

She drove him up through the waking town and talked, gently and deliberately, about everything that was about to happen, and he understood that the talking was a procedure too, that it was the bubble wrap around the morning. The mortuary was at the hospital. The viewing room was a quiet room, she said, carpeted, there would be glass, no — she corrected herself — no glass here, it was a small place, he would be in the room itself if he wished, or he could view photographs first if he preferred, some people preferred. And she had to tell him, she said, slowing for the hospital turn, she had to prepare him, that the appearance of someone who had been in the sea for a period of weeks was very greatly changed. Very greatly. The face especially, she said, and the hands. The sea was not — she searched, the wipers going — the sea was not respectful of the things we know people by.

The hands, he said. It was the first thing he had said since the harbour.

Aye, she said, and looked at him once, kindly and levelly, and he saw she knew exactly what he was asking. The wee finger, she said. I read the note. I'll be honest with you, Mr Geddes, because you've come a long way and I think you're a man who'd rather have it straight. There's loss to the extremities. It happens, in the water, with the time involved. The pathologist can't say with certainty whether the finger you describe is an old injury or — recent. The tissue won't tell us. So I can't give you that to lean on. I'm sorry.

The car park was almost empty. She switched the engine off and the wipers stopped mid-stroke and the rain closed softly over the windscreen, and they sat for a moment in the drumming quiet.

There's the DNA, she said. We can do it all that way if you want. You can get back on that boat tonight without ever going in there, and no one in this world would think less of you.

I'd think less of me, he said.

Inside, the hospital smelled like every hospital, and then they went through a door at the end of a corridor and it smelled like nothing at all, a scrubbed cold null smell that he understood at once was worse. A small anteroom: four chairs, a box of tissues, a print of a beach on the wall, of all things, a beach. A man in scrubs came and spoke to him, the pathologist or his deputy, young, with a beard, very gentle, and went through it all again — the changes, the swelling, the colour, the word immersion doing a great deal of quiet labour — and said that the body would be covered, and they would show him only what he asked to see, and he should take whatever time he needed, and there was no wrong answer in that room. People say I don't know, the young man said. That's allowed. I don't know is allowed.

There were belongings first. He hadn't expected that. A clear bag on the table in the anteroom, signed and dated: the contents of the room at the lodging house, the sergeant explained, the man had paid weekly, the landlord had kept the room a fortnight and then called them. A folding knife with a fish-scaled handle. A wage slip from the fish factory, made out to A. Geddes, three weeks old. A paperback western, swollen with damp, the bookmark a betting slip. A phone charger and no phone. And a photograph, sleeved in plastic, soft at the corners from handling: two boys on a harbour wall, squinting, the older one's arm not quite around the younger one's shoulders, hovering behind him in that way boys have, claiming without touching. He knew the wall. He knew the jersey he was wearing in it. He had not known the photograph still existed anywhere in the world.

That's us, he said, and his voice came out wrong, and the sergeant looked at the beach print and gave him a minute, and the minute was procedure too, and he was grateful for all of it, the whole kind machinery.

Then they took him in.

The room was small and cold and very clean, and the body lay on a steel table under a sheet of surprising whiteness, and a second sheet, folded, lay across where the face would be, so that what he was first asked to look at, by his own request, was the left hand, which the young man uncovered with a care that was almost ceremonial, turning back the white cloth the way you'd turn back a page.

It was not a hand he knew. He made himself stay looking at it. It was swollen and the colour was a colour hands are not, and the skin — he stopped himself naming what had happened to the skin. The little finger ended early. So did the ring finger, partly. So, on the other side, did things he would not look at twice. The sea was not respectful. The one mark he had carried north like a coin in his pocket, the one fact his own ten-year-old hand had put into the world fifty years ago, lay there before him made worthless, dissolved back into the general damage, and he understood that he had been brought all this way to be given nothing, and that he was going to have to do this with nothing, the way, he supposed, you did everything that mattered.

There was a tattoo on the forearm. He hadn't been told about it and it went through him like cold water: Alec had no tattoos, Alec at thirty-four had no tattoos — and then the eleven years arrived all at once, the whole uninhabited country of them. It was a swallow, old-fashioned, navy-blue gone green, the kind deckhands got, and under it a word, a name, the letters blurred by the water and the years to a soft shadow: Inga, he thought it said, or Inge. He stood looking at the name of a woman he had never heard of on the arm of a man who might be his brother, and what undid him was not doubt but the opposite — a sudden conviction with nothing under it, like a stair tread in the dark: that there had been a woman called Inga, in Lerwick or Bergen or Aalesund, and Alec had loved her enough to be inked, and Martin knew nothing about it, nothing, not her face or her fate or whether she had ever heard his own name spoken — Aye, there's a brother south — and that this, this was what eleven years actually were, when you held them in your hand. Not an absence. A life. A whole life, run in parallel, with weather and wages and westerns and a woman's name, and he had let all of it happen out of his sight because of one sentence over a teapot.

The face, he said.

Mr Geddes —

The face. Please.

They folded the second sheet back, and he looked.

Afterwards he could not have said how long he stood there. The young man had been honest: it was very greatly changed, it was a face the sea had been at, swollen and strange, the features risen and softened like something proving, and a stranger could have stood where Martin stood and seen only the changes, only the wreck. He waited for certainty and it did not come. He waited for the eyes to be their mother's and they were closed and could be no one's. The face kept sliding, the way it had slid in the bar of the ferry, between a thing he knew and a thing he had never seen, and he understood at last that there was no test left, no finger, no tattoo, no flash of the heart that would arrive and decide it for him; that I don't know was allowed, and true, and would put his brother in a drawer for six more weeks while machines worked through his blood; and that the other thing, the thing rising in him, was not knowledge either, but it was older than knowledge and it had come up the stairs of him slowly all night on the boat.

He looked at the brow. There, fine as a thread, slanting through the swollen skin above the left eye, was a pale line that could have been a scar and could have been the sea's work, and he heard the sound of a sledge going over on the kirk brae forty-nine winters ago, Alec laughing before the blood even showed, and he thought: it is not allowed, not for me, I don't know is not allowed for me. Somebody had to claim him. Somebody had to stand in a cold room and say the name and make it stick, the way Alec at nine had stood in the playground and said a conger took it and made that stick, lying for him, covering him, doing for his brother the one simple loyal thing — and nobody else in the world was going to do it, because nobody else in the world was left.

Yes, he said. His voice held. That's him. That's Alec. That's my brother.

The room exhaled around him; he felt the relief of the others like a change in pressure. There were forms in the quiet carpeted anteroom, and he signed them under the print of the beach, and where it asked for the name of the deceased he wrote Alexander James Geddes in a steady hand, the full name, the confirmation name, and watched the ink go dry and understood that he had just done the thing words do, that he was making a fact the way he had once unmade one.

The sergeant drove him back down to the town and asked if there was anywhere he wanted to go, and he had her let him out at the lodging house, a tall harled building above the small-boat harbour with a card in the window saying vacancies, and he stood across the narrow street from it in the travelling rain and did not go in. A light burned in an upstairs room. Below, in the harbour, the moored boats nodded and pulled at their lines, all facing into the weather the way moored boats do, patient, pointed home. He had eight hours until the ferry south. He bought tea he didn't drink in a café where the woman called him my dear, and he walked the length of the harbour front twice, on stones his brother's boots had certainly worn a little smoother, and when the boat sailed at seven he was at the stern rail again, watching the grey town fold itself back into the grey hills, and he stayed there long after the land was gone, with the photograph of the two boys in his breast pocket, given over to him with the rest, the older one's arm hovering forever just above the younger one's shoulders, almost, almost touching down.
