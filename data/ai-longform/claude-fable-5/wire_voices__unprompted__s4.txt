The board at Holt was forty positions long, and in the winter of 1968 I sat at number eleven, which faced the window onto Commerce Street, though by the time the evening rates came on at eight o'clock there was nothing in the window but my own reflection and the lights of the board doubled in the glass, little constellations rising and going out. I had been an operator for nineteen years by then. I could write a ticket without looking at my hand, quote the rate to Denver or Des Moines or Mobile while plugging up a second call, and tell from the first syllable a person spoke whether the call would be easy or whether it would cost us both something. You learn voices the way a farmer learns weather. You learn them better, maybe, because a voice is all you are given. No face, no hands, no way of standing in a doorway. Just the breath and the wire.

He came up on my position the first Sunday after New Year's, a few minutes past nine. The lamp lit, I keyed in, said, "Long distance," and a man's voice said, "Good evening. I'd like to place a call, person-to-person, to Mrs. Lenore Pruitt, in Galesburg, Illinois." He gave the number slowly, the way older people do, as if each digit were a coin he was counting out of his palm. His voice was low and a little roughened at the edges, like a record that has been played many times but kept carefully in its sleeve. I asked his name for the ticket and he said, "Kemp. August Kemp," and there was something in the way he said his own name — almost apologetic, almost amused — that made me write it more neatly than I write most names.

The rules were plain about listening. You stayed on the line only long enough to make certain the connection held and the right party had been reached, and then you came down and the conversation was theirs, sealed away from you, no more your business than a letter you carried but did not open. I was a good operator. I want that understood. But there is a moment, three or four seconds, when you must remain on the line — when the distant operator says she has Mrs. Pruitt, and you say go ahead, please, and you wait to hear that they have found each other before you withdraw. Those seconds are lawful. And in those seconds, that first Sunday, I heard a woman say, "August?" — just his name, with a lift at the end of it like a door coming open — and I heard him say, "It's me. It's only me," and she laughed, a low laugh, the kind a person laughs when something has been set right, and I came down off the line with my face warm as if I'd been caught at a keyhole, though I had done nothing wrong at all.

He called again the next Sunday at nine, and the Sunday after that. Person-to-person every time, which costs more, but means you pay nothing unless your party is there — and she was always there. By February I had stopped trading my Sunday evenings to Donna Schaffer, who hated them, and Donna thought I had lost my mind, sitting the dead shift on purpose, the board mostly dark, the radiators ticking, Mrs. Avis the chief operator doing her crossword at the supervisor's desk. But at nine o'clock the lamp at my position would light, and it was him, and I would say "Long distance" as though I did not know, and he would say "Good evening" as though he did not know either, and we would conduct the whole ceremony fresh, his number and her number and his name and her name, like a liturgy neither of us would have dreamed of abbreviating.

What I knew of them I knew in seconds, gathered the way a bird gathers thread — a strand at a time, never the whole spool. I knew that she called him August and never Gus. I knew that he had a dog, because once when the connection went through I heard him say, before he even greeted her, "Hold on, the dog wants out," and her laughing again, and the sound of a screen door somewhere in Kansas while a woman in Illinois waited on the line, paying station rates for the privilege of hearing a screen door. I knew that they had both buried somebody, because one Sunday in March, in my lawful seconds, she said, "Walter's birthday was Tuesday. I went out to the cemetery and the ground was still froze," and he said, "I know it," in a voice that knew it from the inside, and I came down off the line and sat looking at the board with my hands in my lap.

I made up the rest. I am not ashamed to say it now, though I would have been then. I decided they had grown up together, in one town or the other, and that life had married them off in different directions the way it does, briskly, without consulting anyone, and that the people they had married had been good people, mostly, and had died, and that somewhere in the settling of an estate or the forwarding of a Christmas card the two of them had found each other again — two old voices on opposite sides of the Mississippi, with nothing between them now but four hundred miles and the toll rates, which after eight on Sunday were as low as the company ever let them get. I gave them a porch apiece. I gave him a garden because once, in April, he said to her, "The bulbs you sent came up, every one of them," and there was such plain astonished pride in it, a man past sixty marveling at tulips, that I had to press my fingers to my mouth.

I was thirty-nine years old that year. I lived with my mother on Cottonwood Street, in the house I was born in, and on my days off I did the marketing and took her to Dr. Pohl's and changed the library books. There had been a man once. Raymond Otis, who went to Korea in 1951 and did not come back, and whom I have now known dead far longer than I ever knew him living, so that what I miss when I miss him is mostly a girl's idea of a man, a uniform and a laugh and a plan we had about California. I tell you this not for pity but for accuracy, because I have asked myself many times since why two strangers' nine o'clock call became the fixed point my whole week turned on, and the honest answer is that I had stood for nineteen years at the place where other people's voices crossed, holding the cords, and August Kemp and Lenore Pruitt were the first two voices that seemed to me to prove something. That it was not too late, I suppose. That a wire could carry it, whatever it was, undiminished, week after week, and that somewhere on the far side of everything that goes wrong there might still be a person who said your name like a door opening.

That was the spring the whole country came through the board like a fever. The night in April when Dr. King was shot in Memphis, every lamp on every position lit at once and stayed lit until past two in the morning, sons calling mothers, mothers calling sons, everybody needing to hear that everybody else was still where they had left them. In June it was the same again, for the other one, the senator, and all that summer there were the Army calls, the ones we dreaded — a boy at Fort Riley calling home before he shipped, trying to sound easy about it, or worse, the calls after, the ones where you put the connection through and then heard, in your lawful seconds, a sound come out of a mother that you carried home with you on the bus. The world was loud that year. The board roared with it. And every Sunday at nine, through all of it, the lamp at position eleven lit, and it was a man in Holt calling a woman in Galesburg to tell her the tulips were done blooming but the iris had started, and I swear to you it steadied me more than church.

Once — only once — he spoke to me beyond the ceremony. It was a Sunday in July, hot, the windows propped with rulers, and the circuits to Illinois were tied up with storm trouble somewhere past Kansas City, and I had to tell him there would be a delay, that I would call him back when I had his party. He said that would be fine. And then, before I could come down, he said, "It's the same girl every week, isn't it. The same operator." I said it generally was, on Sundays. "I thought so," he said. "You have a way of saying Galesburg. Like you'd been there." I had never been farther east than Kansas City in my life. I said I hoped he wouldn't have long to wait, and he said, "I've waited longer for worse," and laughed a little at himself, and we hung up, and forty minutes later I got his circuit and rang him back and put him through, and when Lenore Pruitt came on the line and said "August?" I confess that for the first time I stayed up a second longer than my lawful seconds, just one, because he said, "There she is," — not it's me that time, but there she is, as though she were the one who had been found.

The last Sunday in September he sounded tired. I noticed it the way you notice a stair that gives slightly under your foot — nothing you could report, nothing you could write on a ticket. His breath came a little behind his words, as if the words were walking and the breath was trying to keep up. He placed the call same as always, and it went through same as always, and in my seconds I heard her say, "You sound worn out," and him say, "It's only the weather turning," and I came down, and that was the twenty-ninth of September, and I have had a long time now to remember that I came down.

The first Sunday in October the lamp did not light at nine. I sat watching it the way you watch a kettle, telling myself the things you tell yourself — a cold, company, the dog run off, the Series on the radio. At nine-forty a call came up from a filling station out on Route 9, a trucker calling Amarillo, and I put it through with my eyes still on position eleven's dead lamp, and at eleven o'clock I unplugged my headset and went home, and on the bus I composed reasons the way other women compose grocery lists.

The second Sunday in October, at six minutes past nine, a lamp lit — but it was an inward call, the Galesburg operator coming through on the toll circuit, and I knew before she said a word, the way you know. "Holt? I have a person-to-person call for Mr. August Kemp," and she read off his number, his own number, which I had written on tickets forty times in my own hand, "from Mrs. Lenore Pruitt. Will you try that line, please?" I said I would try it. I plugged up and rang, and I sat there in the long-distance office in Holt, Kansas, listening to a telephone ring in a house I had never seen, in a kitchen or a hallway I had furnished entirely out of my own head, and nobody came. The ring has a sound when nobody is coming. I don't care what anyone says about a ring being a ring. There is a hollowness to it, a way it falls back on itself, and I heard it that night, and I let it go ten rings, which was the standard, and then twelve, which was not, and the Galesburg operator said, "No answer?" and behind her voice, far down the line, I heard Lenore Pruitt say, "Let it ring, please. He's slow to it sometimes. Let it ring." So I let it ring. I let it ring while the Galesburg operator and I held our cords and said nothing to each other, two strangers in two states keeping a vigil neither of us was paid for, and somewhere in Holt — eight blocks from where I sat, ten, I didn't even know — a telephone rang and rang into rooms that did not answer. After a while the Galesburg operator said, gently, the way we are trained to say it, "I'm sorry, your party does not answer. Shall I try again later?" and I heard Lenore Pruitt say, "Yes. At ten. Try at ten," and we came down.

At ten we tried at ten. At ten-forty we tried again, off the books, because the Galesburg operator rang me inward and said only, "Holt, will you try that number once more," and I understood that four hundred miles away a woman was sitting by her telephone with her hand on it, and that the operator there had decided the same thing I had decided, which was that the rules were written by people who had never listened to anything. No answer. The third time, before the Galesburg operator could give the closing words, Lenore Pruitt spoke up on the line herself, which parties are not supposed to do to the distant operator, and said, "Miss. Miss, in Holt. Are you the Sunday girl?" The Galesburg operator started to say something about procedure and then didn't. I said, "Yes, ma'am. I generally work Sundays." There was a silence on the line, the special silence of long distance, that faint sea-sound the open circuit makes, miles of it, all that night country between us. Then she said, "He didn't call last week." I said, "No, ma'am." Not he may have called another position, not perhaps the ticket was misrouted, none of the things I could have said. Just no, ma'am, because she had not asked me as an operator. She said, "You'd know. You'd have put him through." "Yes, ma'am," I said. "I'd have put him through." Another silence. Then she said, "Thank you, dear," in a voice that had already begun to put something down that was too heavy to carry, "you've a kind way of speaking, he always said so," and she was gone before I could answer, and the Galesburg operator and I stayed up on the dead circuit a second longer than either of us needed to, the way you stand a moment at a graveside, and then we came down.

I could have looked. That is the thing I have turned over most in the years since. I had his name and his number; the city directory sat on the shelf behind Mrs. Avis's desk; the Holt Daily Record printed its notices on page six same as always. An operator is forbidden to make personal use of information that comes to her over the wire, and I had recited that rule to new girls a dozen times, but that is not why I waited. I waited because as long as I did not look, the lamp could still light some Sunday at nine. I know how that sounds. I kept the appointment three more weeks, sitting position eleven through three more dark Sundays, and Galesburg did not come inward again either, and that told me that Lenore Pruitt had looked, or had been told, and knew what I was keeping myself from knowing, and that her vigil was over while mine went on out of pure cowardice dressed up as hope.

It was Donna who ended it, without ever knowing she did. A Tuesday afternoon at the end of October, working the directory rack beside me, she said, "Did you see they're auctioning off the Kemp place out on Schiller Street, the whole contents, my mother wants to go look at the dishes," and went on talking about dishes while the board in front of me swam and steadied. I asked her, when I could, what had become of Mr. Kemp. "Oh, his heart," she said, plugging up a call to Wichita. "Found him in the garden, I heard. Right at the start of the month." Right at the start of the month. The first Sunday, the one when the lamp did not light. He had not failed to call. He had been past calling, and I had sat there inventing colds and ballgames for him while the frost came down on his iris beds.

On my day off I walked out Schiller Street, which I had no earthly reason to do and did anyway, in my good coat, like a woman going to pay a call. It was the kind of house I should have known it would be — white frame, deep porch, a yard that someone had worked over for years and that was already, in three weeks, beginning to forget it. The auction notice was tacked to the porch post. Through the front window I could see the furniture pushed together and numbered, and on the porch itself, on a table with a tag on it, sat the telephone, black, heavy, ordinary, the same instrument they put in every house in this town, and I stood on the sidewalk and looked at it for a long time. That was the thing his voice had come up out of, every Sunday at nine, traveling eight blocks to my board and four hundred miles past it. Somebody would buy it for a dollar, or the company would reclaim it, and it would sit in some other hallway and carry some other voice, because the instruments don't know anything; we are the ones who know. There were bulbs coming loose in the turned-over border by the steps, dug up by the auction men or the frost, I couldn't say. I am not going to tell you I took one. I am only going to tell you what is blooming this spring in the bed by my mother's porch, and that I have no receipt for it.

I thought for a while about writing to her. Mrs. Lenore Pruitt, Galesburg, Illinois — it would have found her; it's not so big a town. I got as far as Dear Mrs. Pruitt more than once. But everything I knew, I knew from my lawful seconds and my unlawful ones, from standing at the crossing place of other people's voices, and it seemed to me finally that a letter from me would only prove to her that the wire had ears, and she deserved to remember those Sunday calls as a closed room with two people in it. Besides, what would I have said? That the tulips came up, every one? She knew that. He had told her himself, with me listening, in April, when the world was on fire everywhere else and on his street it was only spring.

I worked the board at Holt eleven more years, until the company cut over to direct distance dialing for good and they didn't need forty girls anymore, or twenty, or ten. People dial each other now without ever hearing a third voice, and I suppose that is progress, and I suppose it is also a country full of telephones ringing into empty houses with no one anywhere on the line to say, gently, that the party does not answer, no one to be asked are you the Sunday girl, no one to keep the vigil while it rings. But that last stretch of years, every call I handled, I gave it the second. That's all it was — a second, lawful, before I came down off the line — long enough to hear whether the voices had found each other, whether the door was coming open at the other end. There she is. It's only me. Forty positions of lamps lighting and going dark, all those threads of talk crossing the night, and me at number eleven with my hand on the cords, listening just long enough to be sure, which is the whole of the job if you do it right, and is not nothing. It is not nothing to be the one who connects them. Somebody has to hold the wire while the door opens. For nineteen years and then eleven more, in Holt, Kansas, that was me.
