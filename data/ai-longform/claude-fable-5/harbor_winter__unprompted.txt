The tide turned at 6:14 that morning, and Einar Holt was on the seawall to watch it happen, the way another man might stand at a grave at the hour of a death he had not been able to prevent. He had been retired eleven years, but his body still woke at the times the water demanded, slack and flood and ebb tugging at his sleep like the moon tugging at the bay, and he had given up resenting it. Below him the harbor lay gray and unhurried, the fishing fleet — what remained of it, nine boats where there had once been sixty — nodding at the floats, and out past the breakwater the channel showed its first faint seam of movement, a wrinkle where the dark water met the darker, and he watched it the way he had watched it for fifty years, which is to say with his whole attention and no expectation of thanks.

His granddaughter came down the hill at seven, late, her boots unlaced, eating toast out of her coat pocket. She was eleven that winter, the winter the cannery closed, though they did not know yet that it would be the last one; the announcement would not come until December, pinned to the door of the union hall like a notice of eviction, which it was. Her name was Maren, after his wife, who had been dead nine years and would not have approved of the unlaced boots. Her mother, Einar's daughter, was in Anchorage working the winter at a hotel, sending money, calling Sundays. So Maren lived in the house on Kestrel Street with the old man, and went to the school where her class was eleven children and shrinking, and in the early mornings, because she had asked once and he had not known how to refuse, he was teaching her to read the water.

"You're late," he said.

"School isn't till eight."

"The tide isn't waiting for school." But he moved over on the wall to make room for her, and she climbed up beside him, and for a while they said nothing, which was the first lesson and the one she had taken to fastest. Her mother had never been able to sit still next to water. Maren could sit still next to anything.

"Tell me what you see," he said.

She looked. He watched her look, which was its own pleasure, the kind he did not name even to himself. Her eyes went out past the breakwater first, the way he had taught her — far field before near, because trouble comes from far and arrives at near — and then walked back in along the channel, and she frowned, and he waited.

"The water's going out," she said. "But there's a — " She stopped. She did not have the words yet, and he had told her not to reach for words she didn't own. Describe it like you'd describe it to a blind man, he'd said. She tried again. "Out past the green can. The water's moving out, but the surface looks like it's standing still. Like a tablecloth pulled tight."

"That's the wind against the tide," he said. "Ebb running west, wind coming west to meet it. Wind flattens the chop going its own way and stands it up going against. So the water looks calm, but it's the kind of calm that's lying to you. An hour from now, when the ebb hardens, that flat patch will be short steep sea, four feet, close together, the kind that comes over the bow of a small boat before the bow has finished with the last one. You take a skiff out through that, thinking it looks easy because it looks flat, and the channel will educate you."

"Has it educated people?"

"It has drowned people," he said. "Education is for the ones watching from shore."

She nodded. She had a notebook, a school composition book with a marbled cover, and she wrote in it sometimes, but not now; he had noticed she only wrote down the things that didn't matter, the names and numbers, and let the important things go into her some other way, and he thought that was right. The names of the buoys would be on a chart forever. The look of a lying calm had to live in the body or nowhere.

At eight she slid off the wall and went to school, and Einar walked the long way home, past the cannery, because he always did. The plant took up the whole south end of the waterfront, corrugated steel gone the color of an old nickel, steam rising from the roof vents into the cold, and even from the road you could smell it — brine and fish and hot metal and the sweetish industrial smell of the canning line, a smell that the town had stopped smelling generations ago the way a man stops hearing his own heartbeat. The morning shift was on. He could hear the line running, the rattle and hiss of it, and trucks idling at the loading dock, and somebody's radio. Forty years ago there had been three canneries and the whistle from this one had governed the town more surely than the church bell, and men had set their watches by it, and children had known by its different cries whether their fathers would be home for supper. Now there was this one plant running one line, buying from nine boats, and the company that owned it was owned by another company that was owned, he had read, by people in a city he could not have found on a map, and everyone in town spoke of the cannery the way you speak of an old relative whose dying has gone on so long it has become a way of living.

The announcement came on the eleventh of December. Einar heard it the way he heard everything, at the harbormaster's office where the old men drank coffee that the harbormaster, who was fifty and to them a boy, made too weak on purpose, he suspected, to limit their visits. Closing at the end of the season. End of March, when the winter fishery wrapped up. Severance for those with fifteen years, which in that town was nearly everyone on the line, and a meeting at the union hall Thursday for the rest of it, the language of transition assistance and retraining, words that came out of a different ocean entirely.

He walked home through a town that had heard the news ahead of him, the way towns do, the news arriving everywhere at once like weather. Lights on in kitchens at midday. Ruth Pavao standing on her porch in her line apron, smoking, looking at nothing. Two men outside the hardware store not talking. At the school, he supposed, the children were being told or not told, and either way they knew; children read their town the way he read water, by what stood up wrong on the surface.

Maren came home at four and found him at the kitchen table with the chart spread out, which is where he went when he didn't know where to go. She stood in the doorway with her backpack still on.

"Mrs. Andrade cried in the middle of math," she said. "Her husband works there. And Davi said they're moving to his uncle's in Tacoma probably."

"Probably a lot of people will."

"Are we?"

"No," he said, too fast, and then made himself slow down, because the girl deserved better than an old man's reflex. "I don't know what your mother will want. The hotel work is steady. She might want you up there with her."

Maren came and looked at the chart, the bay rendered in its bloodless official blue and white, soundings in fathoms, the channel buoys printed as tidy diamonds, none of it looking anything like the living thing outside the window. She put her finger on the cannery dock, where the chart showed a pier symbol and the word RUINS in italic beside an older structure that had burned before her mother was born.

"Will they put that word on the cannery now?"

"Eventually," he said. "Charts are honest. They just take their time."

That winter the lessons changed, though neither of them said so. Before, he had been teaching her the water the way you teach a child anything — because she asked, because it filled the dark mornings, because a thing known is better than a thing not known. Now there was something else in it, a kind of urgency he was ashamed of and obeyed anyway. He was eighty-one. The town was going to lose the thing that had made it a town. And the knowledge in him — fifty years of it, every bar and back-eddy and the way the current quartered off Devils Elbow at half-ebb with a westerly behind it, the things that were written nowhere because they could not be written, that had passed hand to hand from pilots before him going back to men who had sounded this bay with lead and tallow — all of it was going to go into the ground with him unless it went into someone, and there was no one left for it to go into. The pilot association had let the local station lapse years ago; the big ships came so rarely now that a man came down from the city when one did, with electronics that told him things Einar had carried in his spine. So it would be her or no one. He knew it was a useless gift, knowledge of a water that commerce had finished with. He gave it to her anyway. A man hands down his father's watch even when nobody needs to wind a watch anymore.

He taught her the back-eddy behind Gull Rock, where the ebb curled around and ran upstream along the shore, so that a boat hugging the rocks could make way against a six-knot channel current — and how the seam between the eddy and the main stream showed itself as a line of foam and debris and confused small water, and how crossing that seam in a small boat you had to meet it with speed and an angle or it would take your bow and spin you. He taught her to read kelp: how the fronds lay down and pointed the way the current ran, streaming like flags, and how kelp standing in a tangle meant slack or an eddy. He taught her the colors — that shoal water going green over sand and brown over the flats, that a deep channel kept its slate, that a tide rip showed darker because the surface stood rougher and gave the sky back differently. He taught her that smooth water in a windy bay meant either shelter or oil, and that a slick line running straight where no line should be straight meant something underneath — a bar, a wreck, a change — because nature, he said, doesn't draw straight lines on the surface unless something underneath is holding the pen.

He taught her the birds. Gulls working a patch meant bait pushed up, which meant something pushing it. A cormorant flying low and hard with purpose was going to fish; cormorants standing on the day-marks with their wings hung out meant the fishing was done and the tide had gone slack. He taught her that fog has a grain to it like wood, thick and thin in bands, and that sound in fog is a liar — that a horn can seem to come from astern when it's broad on your bow, because fog bends sound the way water bends light, and so in fog you trust time and compass and the count of your own engine, and your ears last of all.

"Then what are your ears for?"

"Surf," he said. "Surf doesn't lie. You hear surf, you're already too close, but at least you know which way too close is."

She wrote SURF DOESN'T LIE in the composition book, and underneath it, he saw later, in smaller letters: everything else does, a little.

In January her mother called and said the hotel had offered her full-time year-round, and there was an apartment, and a school three blocks away, and Maren listened on the kitchen phone with her face doing the thing it did, which was nothing, which was a stillness Einar recognized because he had spent a lifetime producing it himself in wheelhouses while everything underneath him was telling him to shout. She said, Okay, Mom. She said, I know. She said, Can I finish the school year here at least, and there was a long crackling pause that Einar, drying the same plate over and over at the sink, could not interpret, and then Maren said thank you in a voice that had relief in it for anyone who knew how to listen, and hung up, and said, "June. I'm here till June," and went up to her room, and Einar stood in the kitchen doing arithmetic he didn't want to do. June. The cannery had March. He had — well. He had what he had. You ran the channel you were given.

The hard weather came at the end of January, a week of southeast gales stacking water into the bay so the tides ran a foot and a half over the tables, and it was during the second gale that the Della Rose went up on the spit. She was one of the nine, a forty-two-foot dragger run by Manny Brava's grandson, who was thirty and not a fool but had grown up with electronics and had learned the bay as a picture on a screen, and the screen had not told him what the storm tide had done to the spit, how the sand had moved in one night the way Einar had watched it move all his life, the seaward face cutting back forty yards and the dredge spoil grounds reshaping themselves like a sleeper rolling over. Young Brava came in at dusk in driving rain, reading his plotter, which showed him in good water, and put her hard aground on sand that had not been there in October.

The town went down to the water the way it always did. Coast Guard was forty minutes out from the station up the coast, and the tide was dropping, and a dropping tide with a sea running would pound the Della Rose's planks open against her own hardness before any cutter arrived. Einar stood on the seawall with half the town and Maren beside him in her yellow slicker, and he watched two of the other boats jockeying outside the broken water, wanting to get a line to Brava and not knowing how to come at him, and he found that he was talking — not deciding to talk, just talking, the way he had talked into radios for fifty years, low and flat and certain — and the harbormaster was beside him with a handheld, holding it up to Einar's mouth like a man holding a cup for an invalid, and Einar talked the Karen B in along the back-eddy behind the spit's hooked end, the eddy that didn't show on any chart, that ran contrary and gave a quarter mile of workable water in the lee of the breaking bar, and he told her skipper where the seam was and what it would do to his stern when he crossed it and to meet it at speed with his quarter, and the Karen B crossed the seam and lay in the eddy as quiet as a duck while sixty yards away the bar broke white and murderous, and she floated a line down to the Della Rose on the current — Einar told them which current, because there were three running in that quarter mile and only one of them went where they needed — and they got the towline made fast, and on the last of the tide before she dried out entirely they pulled the Della Rose off the sand stern-first into the deep water, and the town on the seawall made a sound that Einar had not heard the town make in years.

Afterward, walking home in the rain, Maren said nothing for three blocks, and then she said: "The eddy behind the spit. You never taught me that one."

"It's new," he said. "Newer. The spit changed in 'ninety-eight and the eddy came with it. I was going to get to it in the spring, when we could go around it in the skiff. Some things you can't teach from the wall."

"What if you — " She stopped.

"What if I die first," he said, because one of the things he had decided about this child was that he would not make her carry both the question and the asking of it. "Then it goes untaught, and somebody learns it the hard way, or the spit changes again and it stops mattering. Knowledge of water is written on water, Maren. That's the deal. You don't get to keep it. You only get to use it and hand it on, and even handed on, it's dissolving. The bay I knew at your age is gone. The one I'm teaching you is going. You'll stand somewhere at my age and know a water that doesn't exist anymore, and some child will think you're talking about a dream." He stopped, because he had said more than he meant to, the rain running off the bill of his cap. "It's still worth knowing," he said. "That's the part I can't prove. You'll have to take it on faith until you're old enough to know it for yourself."

"I already know it," she said, and the flat certainty in her voice was so much her grandmother's that he had to look out at the harbor for a while before he could trust himself to go on walking.

The cannery ran its last pack the third week of March. There was no ceremony, or rather the company provided none and the town made its own: the boats came in heavy from the last opener and the whole town drifted down to the waterfront to watch them unload, and Ruth Pavao and the other line women worked the final shift in their aprons with their hair covered the way their mothers had, and at the end of it somebody — nobody ever said who, and Einar suspected the harbormaster — got into the works and blew the whistle. The whistle had not been blown in years; the shifts had long since been called by ordinary means. But at 4:30 on the last afternoon it opened up over the town, the great hoarse steam-throated cry of it, and it blew long, far longer than any shift call, and dogs howled, and gulls went up off the cannery roof in a white sheet, and all along the streets people came out onto porches and stood, and Einar, on the seawall with Maren, felt the sound go through him and take with it more than he had agreed to give. The whistle blew until whoever was blowing it had said what they had to say, and then it stopped, and the silence afterward was a new silence, the first hour of a silence that was going to last, and the gulls came back down onto the roof, and that was the end of the canning of fish in that town, which had gone on for one hundred and nine years.

In April, with the plant dark and the For Sale signs coming up along Kestrel Street like a crop, Einar took the skiff out of winter storage and they went around the bay together, two and three mornings a week, finishing the curriculum. He showed her the eddy behind the spit from inside it, let her feel the seam take the bow and taught her hands what her eyes already knew. He ran her across the bar at every stage of tide that was safe and described the stages that weren't. He let her steer. He sat in the stern with his hands in his lap — hands that had brought ships the length of two city blocks through a channel you could spit across at low water — and watched an eleven-year-old read the bay ahead of her bow, calling it as she went, lying calm to port, kelp streaming north-northwest, slick line ahead, something under it, coming back on the throttle, and he checked her and corrected her less and less, until one morning in May, in a building fog, she brought them home around Gull Rock on the back-eddy by feel and sound and the count of the engine, never once asking him, and he understood that the handing-on, the part of it that could be done, was done. The rest she would have to dissolve and rewrite herself, on whatever water she ended up beside.

She left on the last Saturday in June. Her mother drove down for her, and there was a supper, and in the morning the car idled in front of the house on Kestrel Street while bags went in, and Maren came back up the walk one more time, and Einar thought she meant to hug him again and braced his old bones for it, but instead she put the composition book in his hands.

"You keep it," she said. "I don't need it. It's in me, like you said. But you should have it, so you know what got across."

He did not open it until the car had gone up the hill and over, and then he sat on the front step in the early sun and read it through, the names and numbers, the buoys and the bearings, and on the last written page, in her careful small hand, a list with a heading: THINGS THE WATER SAYS THAT GRANDPA TAUGHT ME TO HEAR, and the list ran down the page and onto the inside back cover, and the last item on it was not anything he remembered saying, though he supposed he must have lived it in front of her, which was the older way of teaching: A thing can be ending and still be worth reading all the way to the end.

He sat with the book on his knees for a long time. Then he laced his boots — properly, both of them — and walked down the hill to the seawall, because the tide was due to turn at 9:51, and somebody ought to be there to see it.
