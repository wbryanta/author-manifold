Anil arrived in Colombo at the beginning of March, on a night flight from Europe that came in low over the dark island so that for the last twenty minutes there was nothing below the plane but blackness and then, suddenly, the scattered lights of the coast, like embers shaken from a fire. She had been away fifteen years. She pressed her forehead against the cold oval of the window and tried to recognize something—a shape of bay, a headland—and could not. The country she remembered had been a daylight country. She had never once, in all her childhood, seen it from the air.

In the terminal the heat found her before anything else did. It came through the gangway corridor like a hand laid flat against her chest, familiar and accusing. She had forgotten the way the body surrendered here, the way the shirt clung within minutes, and she stood in the immigration line among returning aunties with their boxed appliances and felt herself being slowly reabsorbed.

The man at the desk turned the pages of her passport with a wetted thumb.

'You were born here.'

'Yes.'

'But this is not a Sri Lankan passport.'

'No. I work for an organization in Geneva. The papers are all there.'

He read the papers for a long time, longer than was needed, and she understood that the reading was the message. Then he stamped the passport without looking at her again and said, 'Welcome home,' in a voice from which she could draw nothing at all.

She was thirty-three years old. She had left at eighteen with two suitcases and a scholarship and a determination, almost chemical in its purity, never to return. She had studied in London and then in the American Southwest, had become a forensic anthropologist, had knelt in pits in Guatemala and the Congo and on a hillside in Croatia where the soil was so full of shell casings it glittered. She read bones the way other people read handwriting. She could tell from a femur how a man had walked, from a hyoid whether he had been strangled, from the bloom of microfracture in a wrist whether he had raised his hands against what was coming. The dead spoke a language of fixed grammar and no lies, and she had come to prefer it, in some hours, to the language of the living.

The Geneva organization had negotiated for nearly a year to send someone. The government, pressed by reports it called fabrications, had finally agreed to a single forensic specialist, who would be paired—the word in the documents was paired, like socks, like dancers—with a local archaeologist of the government's choosing, for a period of seven weeks, to investigate what the documents called, with the great delicacy of documents, allegations of organized disappearance. When the file came across her desk and she saw the name of the country, she sat very still for a few minutes. Then she signed her name where the signature was wanted.

Her colleagues thought it a homecoming. She did not correct them. But sitting in the taxi now, moving through the warm three a.m. dark toward Colombo, past shuttered shops and dogs asleep in the middle of the road, past a checkpoint where a boy of perhaps nineteen leaned a flashlight and the muzzle of a rifle through the window with the same casual motion, she knew it was not a homecoming. You cannot return to a country in the middle of its nightmare and call it home. The place that had been home existed now only in her, the way the dead exist in the bones they leave—a structure, an evidence, around which the living tissue has gone.

The name Anil was not the name she had been given. She had been given two florid ancestral names she never used, names that belonged to grandmothers, to photographs, to the sound of her mother calling across a garden. Anil had been her brother's unused second name, a thing lying around the house like an outgrown shirt, and at twelve she had wanted it with a wanting she could not explain, sleek and slim and two-syllabled, a name like a knife you could carry. She had bargained for it over a whole monsoon season—her brother extracting cigarettes, money, finally a concession involving her bicycle—and when it was hers she put it on and never took it off. Everything else she had shed along the way, country, religion, a marriage of eleven months, the languages of her childhood gone to rust in her mouth. The name she kept. It was the one thing she had chosen so early and so completely that it seemed less a possession than a bone.

Mr. Ruwan Senanayake was waiting for her two days later in an office of the Archaeological Department, a room of teak cupboards and ceiling fans and maps foxed at their corners. He was perhaps forty-five, in a grey suit despite the heat, and he rose when she came in but did not quite smile.

'Miss Tissera. They told me you were Sri Lankan.'

'I was born here.'

'Yes. That is not always the same thing.' He said it without malice, the way one notes the weather, and gestured her to a chair. On the desk between them was a single folder, very thin. 'You have been sent to find crimes. I have been assigned to assist you. I want you to understand from the beginning that I am an archaeologist. My specialty is the sixth century. I am not a policeman, and I am not'—he paused, choosing—'a politician.'

'Neither am I.'

'No. You are something more dangerous. You are someone who has been away.' He opened the folder, turned it, slid it toward her. 'You think the truth is a simple thing here. That it is only hidden, and that hiding is a kind of door, and you have the instruments to open doors. I am telling you—and I will tell you only once, because it is not safe for me to keep telling you—that here the truth is not a door. It is a flame in a high wind. If you carry it openly through a village, the village burns. Do you understand?'

'I understand that's what people say,' Anil said, 'in every country where I've done this work. It's what they said in Guatemala. The families who waited at the graves didn't say it.'

He looked at her for a while, and something moved behind his face that she could not yet read—it would take her weeks to learn to read him, and even then she would never be certain—and then he stood and took his umbrella from the stand, though there was no cloud in the sky.

'Come,' he said. 'I will show you where you will work.'

The laboratory they had been given was an abandoned ward in the grounds of a hospital near the harbour, a long room with high windows and the ghosts of forty beds in the worn places on the floor. Two trestle tables. A storeroom with a lock to which she and Senanayake held the only keys—a detail he had insisted on, she learned later, against considerable resistance. From the windows you could see the masts of fishing boats and, beyond them, the grey hulls of the navy. At night a generator ran somewhere in the hospital compound, and its sound entered her work the way the sound of cicadas had entered her childhood, so completely that she only heard it when it stopped.

In the first weeks they travelled. The organization's mandate allowed them access to certain sites; the government's interpretation of the mandate narrowed the access; Senanayake's quiet negotiations at provincial offices widened it again by degrees she did not witness and he did not explain. They drove south along the coast road in a department jeep, the driver a silent man named Piyadasa who kept a small Buddha on the dashboard and a hand always near the horn. At every checkpoint Senanayake leaned across with the documents and spoke in a low, swift, untranslatable courtesy, and the rifles went back up to shoulders, and they passed.

She saw the country in fragments, the way one reads a damaged text. A burnt bus on its side in a paddy field, rice growing up through the window frames. A wall of photographs outside a small church, hundreds of photographs, young men mostly, each with a date and beneath some of the dates a second date and beneath most of them nothing. A man selling lottery tickets at a junction where, Piyadasa said quietly, not to her but to the road, six bodies had been left on tires one morning two years before, and the tires set alight, and the schoolchildren made to walk past on their way to school, because the bodies were the lesson.

She wrote everything down. She had been trained to write everything down. But she began to understand, in those weeks, that her notebooks were filling with a kind of weather report—accounts of the storm's damage that said nothing about the storm. The dead she was being shown were the acknowledged dead, the ones with photographs and second dates. What the families whispered to her, in doorways, at the edges of fields, when Senanayake had walked ahead and she had lingered, was about the others. The taken. The ones who went into a white van or a jeep without plates and entered a category for which the language of her profession had no clean word. Not dead, because death is a finding and there was no body to make the finding on. Not alive. Disappeared—a word that made the vanishing sound like a property of the person, a thing they had done, rather than a thing done to them.

'You collect stories,' Senanayake said to her one evening. They were eating at a resthouse in the southern hills, the only guests, a single bulb above the table and moths writing their frantic script around it. 'Stories are not evidence.'

'They tell me where to dig.'

'They tell you where someone wants you to dig. Half of what you are hearing is true. The difficulty is that it is not always the believable half.' He poured the tea himself, an oddly domestic gesture in a man so formal. 'When I was a student we excavated a monastery site near Ritigala. Eighth century. We found, in the relic chamber, a child's milk tooth wrapped in a gold leaf. Everyone had a theory. A royal child. A saint's relic. A grieving mason who hid his own child's tooth in the holiest place he would ever build. We argued for a year. Do you know what we finally wrote in the report?'

'What?'

'"Human deciduous tooth, provenance and significance unknown." ' He drank his tea. 'It was the only true sentence available to us. I have spent my life learning to be satisfied with such sentences. You have spent yours learning not to be. That is the wall between us, Miss Tissera. It is not politics.'

'Anil,' she said. 'My name is Anil.'

He inclined his head, and from then on he used it, though it always sounded, in his mouth, slightly in quotation marks, as if he knew it was a name she had bargained for and was honouring the bargain rather than the name.

It was in the fifth week that they went to the caves.

The site was in a government archaeological reserve in the dry-zone hills, a sanctuary closed to the public, where sixth-century monks had hollowed meditation cells into the rock and where, in a deeper cave, an old monastic burial ground had been disturbed by treasure hunters the previous year. The department wanted the scattered remains documented and reinterred; it was, Senanayake said, useful work that would also demonstrate to certain officials that the foreign specialist could be occupied harmlessly. He said this with a dryness she had learned to recognize as his form of honesty.

The cave was cool after the furnace of the climb, and smelled of bat guano and stone dust and, faintly, of the incense that pilgrims had burned there for fourteen centuries. Their lamps made the walls move. The looters had dug a pit and abandoned it, and human bones lay where they had been flung, ribs and long bones and the brown domes of skulls, all of them tea-coloured with age, mineralized, light as paper. Anil knelt and began the slow grammar of her work: gridding, labelling, lifting each bone onto trays, and the familiar calm came over her, the calm she distrusted and needed, in which the dead were a problem of order and she was the order's instrument.

There were the remains of at least four individuals. Three were ancient. She knew it through her fingers before any test could confirm it—the weight, the colour, the dry click of fossil-feeling bone.

The fourth was not.

She had the right humerus in her hand when she knew. It was heavier. It had, at its core, when she turned it to the lamp, the faint greasiness of bone that has not finished giving up its fats—bone that had been a man within recent years, not centuries. She sat back on her heels in the moving lamplight and made herself breathe.

'Mr. Senanayake.'

He came and crouched beside her. She handed him the humerus and then one of the ancient femurs, and watched him weigh them, one in each hand, like a man at a market, and watched his face as he understood. The cave was very quiet. Somewhere above them the bats shifted in their thousands with a sound like distant rain.

'This is a restricted reserve,' she said quietly. 'No public access. Patrolled. Whoever put a body here could come and go past the guards.'

'You are making an inference.'

'I'm making an inventory. The inference makes itself.' She took the bone back from him, gently, because her hands wanted to be gentle with it now, and laid it on the tray. Partial skeleton, she would write that night. Male, by the pelvis. Stature approximately five foot eight. Age at death twenty-eight to thirty-five. Bones partially burned—but burned after death and after stripping, the burn marks in the wrong places for a body burned whole, a clumsy attempted erasure. And then transported here, into the deep stratum of the old dead, where one more skull among skulls would never be questioned. Someone had understood that the best place to hide a murdered man was among the legitimately dead. Someone had understood it well enough to choose a sixth-century grave.

They carried the trays down the hill themselves, the two of them and Piyadasa, telling the guard at the gate only that the department required the remains for conservation. In the jeep nobody spoke for a long time. Then Senanayake said, looking straight ahead at the road:

'You understand what you are now carrying.'

'Yes.'

'I don't think you do. Those caves have been under government protection for six years. The list of people with access is short, and everyone on it is on it because someone powerful put them there. If this skeleton becomes a public accusation before it is an unanswerable proof, it will be taken from you. There will be an inquiry that finds nothing, a report that says provenance and significance unknown, and you will be on a plane, and I—' He stopped. He smoothed the document case on his knees. 'And the man on that tray will have been disappeared a second time. They can do it, you know. They are very good at it. They can disappear the dead.'

'Then we make him unanswerable,' Anil said. 'We give him back his name before anyone knows we're looking for it.'

In the laboratory that night she laid him out in anatomical order on the second trestle table, under the good lamp, and stood looking at him for a long time. It is a thing the textbooks do not describe, that moment, the assembled stranger. In Oklahoma, in the long fluorescent nights of her training, the lab had a tradition of naming the unprovenanced skeletons—frivolous names, protective names, because you cannot work for weeks over a man and keep calling him a number. She looked at this one. The clavicles were strongly marked, the shoulder girdle developed far beyond the legs, the muscle attachments of the arms and back ridged like the work of years. A particular flattening, a particular robustness she had seen before only in one population of bones.

'You were a swimmer,' she said aloud, in the empty ward, to the lamp, to the harbour dark outside the windows. 'Or a diver. Years of it. Since you were a boy.'

So he became Swimmer. She wrote the name in pencil on the corner of the trestle table, small, where an inspecting official would not see it, and Senanayake, finding it there two days later, looked at it for a moment and said nothing, and from then on, between themselves, in the privacy of the long ward, the dead man had his interim name, the way she had once had hers, a name bargained out of circumstance, a name to be going on with until the true one could be recovered.

She had been a swimmer herself. It came back to her now at strange hours, working over him—the two-lap pool at the club in Colombo, the six a.m. light on the water, her father timing her with the watch he otherwise used for his patients' pulses. At sixteen she had won a national schools title, and there had been a photograph in the newspaper, a thin furious girl half out of the water, and for a season strangers had known her name, the old name, the discarded one. It was the closest she had come to mattering in the country of her birth. She had assumed, leaving, that the swimming was one more thing she was leaving, but it had stayed in her the way it had stayed in him, written into the bone, and on the worst nights of that seventh week, when the work was a wall and the generator droned and the city beyond the windows kept its curfew silence, she would talk to him about it, quietly, professionally, one swimmer to another, while her calipers moved over the geography of his vanished strength.

He had been bound. The wrist bones told her. He had been struck twice on the head, and the second blow had killed him, and the angle of it said he had been kneeling. He had healed, years before his death, from a fracture of the left forearm—a parry fracture, the arm thrown up against a blow, so that even his old mendings testified, even his survivals were depositions. And in the pitted surface of one heel bone she found the trace that would, in the end, be the thread: the mark of an old occupational injury she had seen in the files of one industry only, in the medical records of one kind of village—the divers and miners of the gem pits and the graphite shafts, men who went down into the earth for a living, seventy-two fathoms deep, invisible as flies, whose lives were safe only when they returned to the surface.

Somewhere there was a village that had lost such a man. Somewhere there was a woman who came out at dawn to a doorway and looked down a road. Anil had seen her already, without knowing her face—had seen her years before, on her haunches in a Guatemalan grave, elbows in her lap, keeping the vigil that the living keep over the half-revealed. The grief of love in that shoulder. It was the same vigil everywhere. It was the same shoulder.

'Seven weeks,' Senanayake said, on the night she showed him the heel bone and what it meant. 'You have nine days left of them.'

'Then we'd better drive north,' she said. 'To the mining country. Tomorrow.'

He stood at the high window with his hands behind his back, looking out at the masts and the navy grey and the dark, and she understood that he was counting something—risks, friendships, the years left in a careful life—and that the counting was almost done.

'There is a man,' he said at last, 'my old teacher. He left the department years ago, after a scandal that was not entirely a scandal. He lives now in the forest near the old monasteries, half blind. He knows the mining villages; he excavated there as a young man; the people still trust him, because he is no longer anyone.' He turned from the window. 'If we go to him, I cannot protect you afterwards. I am not sure I can protect you now. But he will be able to read what we cannot.'

'What can he read that we can't?'

'The living,' Senanayake said. 'You and I, Anil—we have both spent too long among the dead.'

She covered Swimmer with the white sheet, the way one covers the sleeping, and locked the storeroom, and turned off the lamp. Outside, the generator stuttered and resumed. Somewhere in the harbour a patrol boat moved without lights. In nine days she would be on a plane or she would not, the report would exist or it would be made never to have existed, and the war would go on continuing, as it does, in one form and then another. But the bone in the storeroom had a name now, even if it was a borrowed one, and a village waiting somewhere to be found, and she had learned one thing in the pits of three continents that she held to now in the dark ward as she pocketed the key: the dead are patient. They have given up everything except the truth.
