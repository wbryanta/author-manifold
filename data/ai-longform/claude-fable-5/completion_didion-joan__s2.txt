Down there in the Pacific you knew what you were looking at. Down there the event had a size to it, a scale, the scale was the whole horizon, and at the instant of the shot you would see the bones in your hands through closed eyes, through the goggles, through your closed eyes and the goggles both, and then the heat would roll across the water and hit your face like the door of an oven swinging open, and you would stand there on the deck of the transport with your back to it the way they told you and feel the light on the back of your neck like a sunrise that had come up wrong, come up in the wrong place, come up too fast, and when they said you could turn around you turned around and there it was. Climbing. Boiling up through its own shadow. Pulling the water up after it. You want to know what nobody tells you, nobody ever got this either, no photograph ever got it: the thing was beautiful. The thing was the most beautiful thing you ever saw and you stood there with the salt drying on your face thinking you should be ashamed and you were not ashamed. There would be no talking on the transport going back in. Grown men, technical people, hard cases, nobody saying word one. Then by breakfast everybody talking again, the weather people, the diagnostics, the yield, has anybody seen the yield, but in the dark coming back in, nothing. Zip. None. Some things you don't talk about. Some things there's no point. You had to be there.

He said to her.

Jack Lovett said to Inez Victor, who was born Inez Christian, in the bar off the lobby of a hotel on Kalakaua Avenue in Honolulu in the spring of 1975, twenty-three years after the morning he was describing and one month, less a few days, before the morning the last helicopter lifted off the roof of the embassy in Saigon.

"I brought you a shell," Inez said. "From Manila. You brought me a shell. A frangipani shell on a string."

"Off Johnston. I just told you. I bought it off a reconnaissance pilot who'd come in from Clark." Jack Lovett signaled the bartender by lifting two fingers, not looking at him. "You wore it that night at the Outrigger. You wore it with a white dress."

"I never wore it," Inez said. "I have it still, but I never wore it."

"You wore it once," Jack Lovett said. "You wore it once and I was there."

Inez Victor looked at the rain falling past the lit torches on the lanai and did not say what she could have said, which was that the night he was thinking of, the night at the Outrigger, the white dress, was a night in 1952, and that she had been seventeen years old, and that what she had worn on the string around her neck that night was not a shell at all but a small jade ring that had been her mother's, but never mind. Never mind, she had learned to say, was the one wholly indispensable phrase in any dealing with Jack Lovett. The facts in Jack Lovett's stories floated free of their moorings. The dates shifted, the islands changed names, Manila became Johnston became Kwajalein, and yet the stories were never exactly lies, that was the thing she could not have explained to anyone, certainly not to her husband. The stories were the truth told by a man for whom the truth had been for thirty years a professional liability. "All right," she said. "I wore it once."

"You see," Jack Lovett said.

Let me put my own cards on the table.

I am not a detached narrator here, not the disembodied voice novels of an earlier period used to posit, the voice that knew the weather in the provinces and the contents of every heart. I have a name, a history, a residual position in this story, and a stack of notes I have been carrying from desk to desk for some years now, notes I made at the time and notes I made later, and one of the things I have had to accept in the writing of this book is that the notes do not behave. The notes do not march. The notes do not want to be a novel. Consider that a warning, or consider it the only honest way into the events I have to relate, which were themselves events without narrative shape, events that occurred not in sequence but in a kind of phosphorescent slow drift, the way debris circulates in a harbor.

I first knew Inez Christian in the autumn of 1959, in New York, when we were both new at a fashion magazine on Lexington Avenue, two girls from the wrong coast doing eight lines of caption copy a day for an editor who wore a hat at her desk. Inez was the one the photographers wanted. Inez was the one who could stand in a borrowed Mainbocher in an unheated studio on West Fifty-fourth Street and look as if she had just remembered something amusing and infinitely remote, and I will tell you now what I came to understand only much later, which is that the look was not a trick. Inez was in fact always remembering something amusing and infinitely remote. Inez was remembering Honolulu. Inez was remembering, although it would be twenty years before I knew this, a married Army officer almost twice her age who used to drive her out the Kamehameha Highway past the cane fields to Schofield Barracks with gardenias wilting in her hair.

I had meant, when I first sat down to it, to write quite a different book.

I had meant to write a study of the Christian family, a leisurely chronicle in the colonial manner, four generations of missionary blood thickening into merchant blood in the kingdom and then the republic and then the territory and then the state of Hawaii: the original Christian off the brig Albatross in 1837 with his consumptive wife and his crates of hymnals; the son who let the hymnals mildew and planted cane; the grandson who incorporated; the great-grandsons who diversified, as the family's own brochures put it, into land development, into inter-island shipping, into hotels, into, by the time it mattered to this story, the steel and dredging contracts that would follow the American flag west and south across the Pacific for the better part of the century. I had the title pages of that book. I had its opening images, and since I am unlikely now to use them anywhere else, here they are: colors, the colors of bougainvillea and rust; the trades dropping at six in the evening; the smell of something sweet going rotten under the banyans; men in white suits drinking gin on a lawn that ran down to a seawall, agreeing with one another that the mainland understood nothing, that Washington understood less, that the islands were a special case, a fragile equilibrium, a garden requiring vigilance. Cissy Christian, Inez's grandmother, smoking her one daily cigarette in its bamboo holder at half past four exactly. The flat sound of the surf at Waikiki before the hotels went up. The blooded complacency of an American class that believed itself a race.

That book would have had a certain dense Victorian shapeliness, and I gave it up, finally, because it was a lie. The shapeliness was the lie. The Christians of Honolulu did not accumulate their history the way the book would have had them accumulate it, in chapters, with each generation handing the next a heavier and more burnished portmanteau. The Christians, like everyone else in this story, mislaid their history. Carol Christian, Inez's mother, a pretty woman from Stockton who never once in twenty years on the island got the trades and the kona winds straight, mislaid hers on a Matson liner in 1946, waving from the railing in a lei she had bought herself, telling her daughters, Inez was twelve, her sister was ten, that this was not goodbye, this was just mommy needing the mainland for a little while, needing the seasons. Paul Christian, Inez's father, mislaid his over the subsequent decades in increments, in Cannes, in Tangier, in a series of borrowed apartments and unanswered letters, until what remained of it was only grievance, and grievance, as we will see, was the most combustible thing the family owned. I am getting ahead of myself. I said the notes do not march.

Here is a note, verbatim, from a card I have dated 26 March 1975: "I.V. at the airport mtg. the 8 a.m. flight. White ginger someone hands her, she holds it upside down, like a tennis racquet. Says: it's aloha shirts now, even the funeral. Says: you know what we say in the islands instead of saying somebody died. We say somebody went to the mainland."

A word about what Jack Lovett did.

When Inez met him in 1952 he was an Army officer, on the books anyway, attached in some unelaborated capacity to Schofield, a man of about thirty-five who flew out to the atolls on the shot schedules as an observer, his word, along for the ride, his words, there for the show. By 1955 he was out of the Army and describing himself as an aircraft broker. By 1960 he was describing himself as a consultant on agricultural development, Saigon, Vientiane, Jakarta, and there was a period in the middle sixties when the cards he handed people read only J. LOVETT and a telex number in Hong Kong. He was one of those men, and the Pacific in those years was full of them although there were never as many as people later believed, for whom information was the only currency that did not fluctuate. He did favors. He moved things: aircraft parts, rice, currencies, occasionally people. If you asked him a direct question about what he did he would answer with geography. I was in Manila. I was up in Phnom Penh. I had a thing going in Singapore. The geography was the answer. The geography was all the answer there was, and the women in his life, two wives, both of whom described him in the divorce filings with the identical adjective, "absent," never understood that, and Inez Victor understood it the first night she met him and never once asked him a direct question in twenty-three years. This was, I think now, the entire secret of what passed between them. Everyone else wanted Jack Lovett to resolve into a fact. Inez let him remain a climate.

She married Harry Victor in 1955, in New York, four months after she met him at a dinner given by the magazine's publisher. I want to be fair to Harry Victor, who was not a fool and not a villain and who is owed by history a more patient hearing than history is going to give him, but I want also to render exactly the quality he had, which was the quality of a man for whom the world existed chiefly as a series of positions to be taken on it. Harry Victor at twenty-nine had a Coif degree, a clerkship behind him, and the conviction, neither hidden nor obnoxious, simply worn, like a good watch, that the public life was the only serious life. Harry Victor said "the American people" in private conversation. Harry Victor could turn a dinner-table remark about the lateness of the asparagus into a question of agricultural policy, and did, the first night Inez sat next to him, and Inez, who had grown up in a family where nothing was ever discussed and everything was implied, mistook this, she told me once, for openness. "I thought, here's a man who says what he means," Inez said. "It took me about ten years to see it was the reverse. He meant what he said. There's a difference. Don't ask me to explain it."

Congress in 1964. The Senate in 1969. The Alliance for Democratic Institutions, which was the apparatus Harry Victor built for himself after the 1972 nomination did not materialize, and which kept a 727 on charter and Inez in a defined role, the role being Inez Victor, wife. Twins in 1957, a boy and a girl. Inez on the airport tarmacs of two hemispheres in her good navy linen, holding the flowers like a tennis racquet. Inez in eleven hundred hotel ballrooms, smiling, during the applause, at the precise middle distance, because if you focused on an actual face in an actual row the cameras caught you playing favorites. There survives a quantity of film of Inez Victor performing this smile and I have looked at a good deal of it on an editing console and I will tell you what is on it. Nothing is on it. That is the point of it. Inez had worked out for herself by about 1962 the same discipline the Christians of Honolulu had worked out over four generations and the same discipline Jack Lovett had worked out on the atolls, which was: give the observers the event, and keep the yield to yourself.

A reporter from one of the wire services asked her once, Wichita I believe, 1972, the primaries, what she considered the chief drawback of public life. The exchange exists on tape. You can hear the press bus idling. You can hear Inez consider the question, actually consider it, which was her one ineradicable failing as a candidate's wife, and then you can hear her say: "You drop things. You're handed your life back at the end of every day edited, and after a while you can't remember your own cut." The aide traveling with her said immediately that what Mrs. Victor meant was that the family cherished its privacy, and the wire ran the aide's version, and the tape went into a box, and I have the box.

So you see the shape of the thing I am circling.

A man whose business was the unrecorded. A woman whose business was the recorded, and who had lost custody of her own memory in the process. The two of them at a little wet table in a hotel bar in Honolulu in the spring of 1975, while out at Hickam, three miles to the west, the C-141s were turning around around the clock, Saigon, Guam, Hickam, refueled and out again, because the entire American enterprise in the Western Pacific, the enterprise whose first pink dawn Jack Lovett had watched from the deck of a transport off an atoll with no name worth keeping, was at that moment ending, was being, his word for it, audited, and Jack Lovett had business in the audit. Why Jack Lovett was in Honolulu that week instead of in Saigon, where his business mostly was, is a question with a long answer, and the long answer is this book. Why Inez Victor was in Honolulu that week is a shorter answer, and a worse one, and concerns her family, and concerns specifically what her father did on the morning of the twenty-fifth of March with a revolver that had been kept, oiled and loaded and never once in forty years discussed, in the top drawer of a koa desk in the house her grandfather built on Manoa Road; and I find that even now, at this distance, with the principals dead or immune, I keep moving the index card on which that morning is written to the bottom of the stack. Note the evasion. I display it because evasion is the climate of this story, the trades that drop every evening at six, and you will not understand anyone in it, Inez least of all, until you can breathe it.

"You're not asking what I'm doing here," Jack Lovett said.

"No," Inez said.

"Twenty-some years," Jack Lovett said. "You never once asked."

"You'd tell me an island," Inez said. "Why ask."

The bartender brought the two drinks and took away the two glasses, and on the bandstand on the lanai three men in aloha shirts began to play, for the eleven tourists sitting out under the dripping thatch, a slack-key arrangement of a song that had been popular on the mainland the year Inez left the island for school, and Jack Lovett sat with his back to the room, which she remembered too, he had always sat with his back to the wall and his eye on the doors, and watched Inez Victor listen to the song. The rain came straight down through the torchlight. Out at Hickam a transport rotated up off runway eight right, banked west over the reef runway and hauled itself toward Guam, and the sound rolled in across the water and under the music the way thunder gets into a dream without waking the dreamer.

"Wonder Woman Two," Inez said. "That's not a real name. You made that up."

"I never made anything up," Jack Lovett said. "That's the one thing I never had to do."

What I know about that night I know from Inez, who told me about it four years later, in another climate altogether, sitting on a hotel bed in Kuala Lumpur folding and refolding a cable she had no intention of answering, and I am aware that a scene reconstructed at that distance, by that witness, transcribed by this one, has the evidentiary value of coral: an accretion, a reef, built by organisms with no view of the whole. I give it to you anyway. A correspondent for one of the networks, in the islands two days to do a stand-up on the refugee staging at Camp Pendleton West, that was actually said in those weeks, Pendleton West, recognized her across the room, recognized the senator's wife three thousand miles from the senator in a hotel bar with a man in a seersucker jacket no one could place, and came over with his glass, and said, you're a long way from Washington, Mrs. Victor, and Inez performed the smile, the middle-distance smile, and said her family was kamaaina, said she was born a mile from where they stood, said the word "home." And the correspondent looked at Jack Lovett, waiting, the pause that is a question, and Jack Lovett looked back at him with the flat affable nothing he had looked at customs officers and station chiefs and congressmen's wives with for thirty years, and said: "I knew the family." Past tense. Knew. The correspondent went away. The band took its break. The rain stopped the way it does there, all at once, as if a valve had closed, and the smell came up off the wet plumeria and the wet pavement, and Inez Victor sat in a hotel bar a mile from where she was born and smelled the smell of every morning of her childhood, and began, very carefully, in the voice of a woman reading a deposition, to cry.

"I lose things," is what she said. "Jack. I've lost whole years."

"I know you have," Jack Lovett said. He did not touch her. He turned the glass on the wet table, a quarter turn, like a man arming something. "I've got them," he said. "Everything you dropped. I kept it all."

And that, he might have told her, was the other thing about the light at dawn during those Pacific tests, the part he never got to, the part that was not for the bar off the lobby. That after the cloud, after the beauty you were ashamed of and were not ashamed of, the light stayed in your eyes. Not metaphorically. Clinically. For hours you carried a violet smear in the center of everything you looked at, a bruise on the visible world, and you learned to see around it, everybody learned, you read the dials and wrote the logs and played the gin hands seeing around it, and after enough events you understood that this was going to be the permanent condition, not the smear itself, the smear faded, but the seeing around. The accommodation. He was a man who had spent his working life seeing around the brightest thing in the field of vision and never once naming it, and across the little table sat the one person on earth he had ever wanted to name it to, in 1952, in 1975, the pink sky, the wet air, the gardenias, never mind there were not too many flowers around those shot islands.

Something to see, he said. Something to behold.

I tell you these things not because they happened in sequence but because, in the only sense that finally matters, they happened at once.
