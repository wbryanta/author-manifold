He came down to the terminal at North Sydney in the last hour of the afternoon, the ticket folded into his shirt pocket, and on the seat beside him a cigar box that held everything he still had of his brother. Twenty-six postcards. A photograph from 1953 of two boys on the breakwater at Sydney Mines, the older one's arm flung out as if introducing the younger to the sea. A clasp knife with a cracked bone handle that Brendan had left on the kitchen table the last morning anyone in the family saw him, the way another man might leave a note.

Two days earlier a constable named Osmond had telephoned from Port aux Basques. A man had come ashore at Isle aux Morts after the storm of the fourteenth. There was no licence on him, no wallet that had survived the water, only a postcard folded twice in the breast pocket of his oilskin, the ink mostly gone, but the address still legible because it had been pressed in hard with a ballpoint, the way a man writes who has spent his life writing on surfaces that move. *Hugh Castell, RR 2, Margaree Harbour, Cape Breton.*

– We think he may be your brother, Osmond said. We'd ask you to come over and have a look at him.

Hugh had stood in the kitchen with the phone against his ear and watched, through the window, the alders bending along the lane, and had said yes, he would come, and had written down the times of the boat in the margin of a seed catalogue, and afterwards could not remember hanging up, or the hour that followed, only that at some point he had gone out and stood in the yard without a coat.

Now he drove up the ramp into the white steel mouth of the ferry and a boy in a reflective vest waved him forward between the transport trucks until his bumper nearly touched a trailer of frozen fish, and he shut the engine off and sat a moment in the sudden dimness, the cigar box on his knee. Around him the trucks ticked and settled. Men climbed down from their cabs carrying thermoses and paperbacks. The whole deck smelled of diesel and brine and cold rubber, the smell of every working dock he had ever stood on, and he thought, Brendan would have known this smell in his sleep, would have known it the way other men know the smell of their own houses.

He carried the box up the stairwells to the passenger decks.

The boat left at six. He watched Cape Breton go from the after rail, the low hills losing their colour, the lights of the town stringing themselves out and then thinning, and a tern following the wake for a long time as if it had business with them, and then not following. He had crossed this strait only twice in his life. Brendan had crossed everything. The lakes, the gulf, the North Atlantic in winter, the long warm deliveries down to Tampico and back. For thirty years the postcards had come, one or two a year, from Thunder Bay, from Duluth, from Sept-Îles, from Rotterdam where the card showed a windmill Brendan would certainly never have gone to see. Most of them blank except the address. A few with two words. *Still afloat.* Once, in 1977, a phone number with no town attached, which Hugh had kept in his wallet for eleven years and never called, and which he could still recite.

They had grown up four years apart in a house above the colliery wharves, sons of Esmond Castell, who ran the engine of a coal company tug and came home each evening smelling of oil and pipe smoke, and of Marion, for whom the family boat was named. Brendan was the one who taught Hugh the water. Taught him to swim by throwing him off the government wharf in June, standing above him with his hands on his hips, laughing, calling down instructions Hugh could not hear for his own thrashing. Taught him to splice, to read the difference between a mackerel sky that meant weather and one that meant nothing, to row standing up, facing forward, the way the old men did. There is a kind of older brother who is the first country you live in. Everything after is emigration.

The lounge filled with truckers settling into the reclining chairs, draping jackets over their faces. A child ran the length of the cafeteria three times and was caught. Hugh bought a tea he did not drink. Then the island was gone behind them and there was nothing in the windows but the boat's own light thrown back, and he took the postcards out of the box and laid them on the small table in chronological order, two rows of thirteen, the way a man lays out a hand of patience, and looked at them until the words and the postmarks blurred, his cheek dropping toward his shoulder, so that a woman passing with a tray glanced at him as if at someone struck.

When he woke it was past one in the morning and the boat had a long slow roll in it. He went out on deck.

The rift had been over the boat. He could say that now, though for years he had said it was over other things, over Brendan's whole way of being in the world, over the funeral, over money, over their mother's last year. But it was the boat. Their father died in February of 1961 and left the *Marion B* to both of them, half and half, the way he had left them everything else, his cap, his tools, his silences, and that April, while Hugh was up the coast for two weeks helping a cousin frame a barn, Brendan sold her to a man from Chéticamp. Sold her in an afternoon. Signed both their names to the paper, his own and then Hugh's, in that same pressed-down hand, and was gone with the money before Hugh got back, except that he was not quite gone, he was standing on the government wharf with his sea bag at his feet waiting for the bus, as if he had waited there on purpose, as if he needed the thing said to his face. And Hugh had said it. Twenty-three years old, standing in the wind with the bill of sale shaking in his hand, he had told his brother he was dead to him.

Brendan had taken him at his word for thirty years.

What Hugh understood now, and had understood for a long time, in the way that knowledge arrives years after it would have been useful, was that the forged signature had been a kind of mercy. The boat had debts on her their father had hidden. Brendan had taken the shame of the sale onto himself entire, the way he had once taken the blame for everything, and had used the money to pay men in Sydney whom Hugh never knew existed, and had kept perhaps forty dollars for the bus and the boat, and Hugh had learned all of this from an uncle, eight years too late, in a parking lot after a wedding, and had gone home and started a letter that he did not finish, and started another the following winter, and what he finally sent, to a shipping agent's address in Montreal, came back unclaimed. After that there were only the postcards, arriving like soundings taken in a fog. Proof of depth. Proof of nothing else.

The deck was empty except for a woman in cook's whites smoking in the lee of a lifeboat davit, her lighter cupped in both hands like something she was keeping alive.

– Rough enough for you? she said.

– I've seen worse.

– You haven't seen Wreckhouse.

She told him about it the way islanders tell strangers their weather, with pride, as if it were a difficult relative. The funnel of land west of Port aux Basques where the wind comes down off the Long Range and has been clocked, she said, at better than a hundred miles an hour, and used to blow the trains clean off the track, boxcars lifted and laid over in the bog like dominoes. And about the farmer there, Lauchie MacDougall, whom the railway kept on salary for thirty years because he could smell the wind coming. He'd telegraph down the line and they'd hold the train at Port aux Basques until he said it was past.

– A human instrument, she said. They say after he died they put up gauges, machines, the works. The machines weren't as good.

Hugh thought of Brendan at fifteen, standing in the stern of the dory with his face lifted, saying, *We'll go in now,* under a sky that looked to Hugh like every other sky, and the squall coming down on the harbour twenty minutes later as they were hauling the dory up the slip.

– Who do you have over there? the woman asked. Nobody crosses in October for the scenery.

– My brother, he said. Maybe.

She looked at him and did not ask what *maybe* meant, which he was grateful for, and they stood a while in the noise of the wind and the engines, and she finished her cigarette and flicked it over the rail, where the wind took it instantly, a small red thing snatched into the dark, and she said good night and went in, and he stayed.

A strait is not a river. A river is going somewhere. A strait only lies between, water that belongs to neither shore, owned by weather. He had read once, in a book left behind by a boarder, that the mountains of Newfoundland and the mountains of Cape Breton are the same mountains, one range, folded up together four hundred million years ago when the continents closed like a hand, and torn apart again when the hand opened, and the sea came into the tear, and that is the strait. The same stone on either side. The same long green spine going down under the salt water and rising again. So that the crossing is not really a departure, the book said, or he had decided it said. You travel all night over open water to arrive on the far half of your own country.

He slept for an hour or two in a chair with the cigar box held against his chest like a man asleep in church holding his hat, and when he woke the windows were grey and there was land in them, low hummocked rock the colour of gun metal, scoured bare, with the light at Channel Head blinking its last few times before the daylight made it pointless. Port aux Basques came around the headland in pieces. Oil tanks. A church. Houses set down on the rock apparently at random, painted the colours of boats.

Osmond was waiting at the bottom of the ramp, younger than his voice, holding a paper coffee cup in each hand, and gave one to Hugh before he said anything at all, which told Hugh something about the morning ahead of them.

– It's no distance, the constable said. You can follow me up.

The clinic was a low building on the Grand Bay road with a view, like everything in the town, of water. They went down a corridor past a room where a radio was playing very quietly, and through a door into a colder room, and a nurse was there, an older woman who looked at Hugh once, carefully, the way a person checks the sky, and then stood back against the wall with her hands folded.

– I have to tell you, Osmond said. He was in the water some days. The sea's been at him. People expect — the face isn't always how you'd know a man.

– I know how I'd know him, Hugh said.

The body lay under a sheet on a steel table and the nurse folded the sheet down from the face to the chest with two movements, practised, almost liturgical, and stepped away.

It was an old man. That was the first thing, the thing nobody warns you of. The boy who had stood over him on the government wharf shouting instructions down into the green water had been carried away inside this grey-bearded man the way a keel is carried inside a hull, present and invisible, and the face itself told him nothing, the sea having done its slow work on it, having taken the particulars and left a kind of general statement, a man, late sixties, a life outdoors. Hugh stood and looked and felt nothing decide itself in him.

– May I, he said, and did not finish, and reached out and with two fingers moved the wet grey hair off the left side of the forehead.

The scar ran through the eyebrow at an angle, a pale seam, an inch and a half, where no hair grew.

He had put it there himself. Eleven years old, in the dory off Pigeon Rock, a tangled mackerel line, Brendan laughing at him, that particular laugh, and Hugh had swung the oar around without any thought in his head at all and opened his brother's brow to the bone. The blood had been astonishing. It had run between Brendan's fingers and down his wrist and dripped pink into the bilge water, and Brendan had sat there in the stern holding his face and laughing, actually laughing, blood in his teeth, saying, *You've some arm on you, b'y, we'll make a fisherman of you yet,* and had told their mother he'd slipped on the rocks, and had held to that story under direct interrogation for fifty years, and the lie had been a country the two of them lived in alone, a marriage, the first secret of Hugh's life, and here it was, the white seam of it, the signature, the only thing Hugh had ever written on his brother, come back across thirty years and the whole grey strait to be read by the hand that made it.

He looked at the hands too. He made himself. The rope-thickened fingers, the left thumbnail black-ridged where a winch or a hatch had caught it some decade past, and on the forearm, gone soft and grey-green under the skin like something seen through ice, a swallow, the tattoo Brendan had got in Sydney at seventeen, the bird sailors put on themselves because a swallow always finds its way home. Their mother had wept over it. Brendan had worn long sleeves at her table for two years.

– Mr. Castell, Osmond said, after what may have been a very long time.

– Yes, Hugh said. It's him. It's Brendan. It's my brother.

The nurse drew the sheet back up, and Hugh found he had put his hand briefly on his brother's chest through the cloth, the way you steady a thing in a seaway, and then took it away.

In an office down the corridor Osmond brought out a plastic tray. A clasp knife, twin to the one in the cigar box, the same cracked bone handle, so that Hugh almost laughed. A brass boatswain's whistle on a lanyard gone stiff with salt. Sixty-odd dollars dried into ripples. A key on a fob from a rooming house in Burgeo. And the postcard, in a clear plastic sleeve, the picture side a tinted photograph of this very ferry run, the boat small and white on a cobalt strait, the kind of card sold in racks in the terminal for fifty years, and on the back, pressed hard in ballpoint, the address, and a stamp in the corner, and nothing else. Stamped and never sent. Carried in a breast pocket into the water as if the body itself had been pressed into service as an envelope.

– He was cooking on a longliner out of Burgeo, Osmond said. Three seasons. The skipper says he kept to himself but he was well liked. Says he'd been talking, this fall, about going across. He told the skipper he had a brother in Cape Breton.

– Did he say anything else.

– He said his brother had a place on the Margaree and that the swimming was better on that side. Osmond looked down at his notes as if to confirm this was truly what he had written. That's what the skipper remembered. The swimming.

Hugh took the cigar box from under his arm and opened it on the desk and laid one of his own postcards beside the one in the plastic sleeve, Sept-Îles, 1971, and the two addresses sat side by side in the identical hand, the same looping capital, pressed down hard, and Osmond looked at them a moment and said, gently, that it wasn't necessary, the identification had been made, and Hugh said he knew that, and left the two cards lying together a while longer anyway.

He stayed the day in the town. There were papers, calls, the slow machinery by which the dead are given back. In the afternoon, because there was nothing else required of him and he could not sit in the motel room, he drove the few miles east to Isle aux Morts, the Island of the Dead, named for centuries of wrecks, and a man named Hatcher, who had found the body, walked with him down over the rocks and pointed, with an economy Hugh recognized as kindness, to a shelving slope of stone where the kelp lay in heaps, and they stood there together without speaking while the sea did what it does, came in and withdrew, came in and withdrew, neither asking forgiveness nor offering any. There had been a girl from this cove, Hatcher said eventually, back in another century, who rode out through surf like this with her father and a dog to take a line to a wrecked ship, and saved a hundred and some souls, and was given a medal by the King. Seventeen years old. People here had always gone out to the water's dead and the water's living alike, he said. It was just what was done. He said it as though apologizing for the smallness of finding one man, when once they had saved a hundred.

The undertaker said the casket could cross on the Thursday night boat. Hugh booked his passage for the same sailing.

Before he drove aboard he stood at the post box outside the terminal with the postcard in his hand. The plastic sleeve had protected it; the stamp was good, the clerk had told him, a stamp doesn't expire, and Hugh had asked twice to be sure. He uncapped a pen. For a while nothing came, and the wind off the harbour worried the card in his fingers, and then he wrote, beneath the address, in the white space his brother had left empty for him, *Coming home,* and the date, and dropped it through the slot, where it fell out of his hand into the dark with the smallest sound, and would cross the strait some days behind them, in its sack, in the hold of some other boat, and arrive at the house on the Margaree after everything was over, and he would take it from his own mailbox at the end of the lane and hold it, a message from both of them, in one hand from the dead and in the other from the living, and put it in the cigar box, the twenty-seventh.

The night crossing back was calm. Below the passenger decks, below the lounges where the truckers slept under their jackets and the child who runs the length of every ferry in the world ran and was caught, the casket rode lashed among the transport trailers, between the frozen fish going one way and the dry goods going the other, and a seaman had let Hugh come down once after sailing and stand beside it for a few minutes with his hand on the wood, the steel deck humming under them both, and then Hugh had gone back up.

Near four in the morning he went out on the after deck. The wind had gone around to the west and softened. He thought of the man at Wreckhouse who could smell weather, the human instrument, dead now, replaced by machines that were not as good, and he thought that every family has one, somebody put up on the headland of it to read what's coming, and that his had been Brendan, and that the warnings had come for thirty years, blank, postmarked, faithful, and he had held the train and held the train and never let it through. The same mountains on either side, the book had said. One range, torn open, the sea let into the tear. All night you cross open water and arrive on the far half of your own country.

The lights of Cape Breton came up out of the dark a few at a time, low on the water, and under his feet, down through the lit decks and the sleeping truckers and the humming steel, his brother rode the same swell he did, lifted and set down, lifted and set down by the same water, the way they had once stood balancing together in the dory off Pigeon Rock, and Hugh leaned on the rail and began, very quietly, the song their mother used to sing on the wharf in the evenings to call them in, and the wind took it from him a word at a time.
