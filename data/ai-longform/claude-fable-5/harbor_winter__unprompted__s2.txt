The winter the cannery closed for good, my grandfather taught me to read the water, and I have never since been able to look at a river mouth or a harbor or even a pond stippled with wind without hearing his voice telling me what it said. He was seventy-nine that year and I was eleven, and we both believed, in our different ways, that the knowledge was urgent, that there would not be time later, though neither of us said so. He believed it because of his heart, which the doctor in Aberdeen had described to him in terms my grandfather repeated to me only once, flatly, the way he might report a bad forecast. I believed it because everything that winter felt like it was ending — the town, the fishing, my mother's job at the line, the smell of the place itself, which was diesel and brine and the sweetish steam of the retorts, and which I did not yet know I would spend the rest of my life missing.

His name was Eino Maki and for forty-one years he had been a bar pilot, going out in all weather to climb rope ladders up the sides of freighters and bring them across the bar and up the channel to the docks, and then taking the loaded ones back out again, past the jetties, over the shoals where the river quarreled with the sea, and handing them off to the ocean like a man releasing a bird. He had retired before I was born. By the time I knew him he was a widower who lived in a green house on the hill above the boat basin, and from his kitchen window you could see the whole harbor laid out below — the moorage with its fingers of gray dock, the cannery on its pilings with the white plume always rising from the stack, the channel markers, the long dark arm of the south jetty, and beyond all of it the bar, which on bad days you could see breaking even from that distance, a white seam stitched across the river's mouth.

My mother worked the slime line, which is what they called it without embarrassment, gutting and trimming, her hands so cold at the end of a shift that she ran them under warm water and cried sometimes, quietly, with her back to me. My father had gone to Alaska on a crabber three years before and the money he sent had thinned and then stopped, and we did not talk about him much. So it was my grandfather who picked me up from school, my grandfather whose truck I knew by the sound of it, my grandfather who fed me fried smelt and rye bread and decided, that December, without ever announcing it, that I was going to learn what he knew.

The announcement about the cannery had come in November. Everyone had known for years it was coming, the way you know a tide is going to turn, but the letter on the bulletin board by the time clock made it real: the plant would run through the winter season and close in March. The company thanked everyone for their decades of service. My mother brought the news home like a fish she didn't want to clean and set it on the table between us, and we ate dinner around it.

"They'll keep the cold storage," she said. "Maybe. For a while."

My grandfather, who was there that night, said nothing. He had seen the town in 1948, when there were five canneries and you could walk across the basin on the decks of gillnetters, or so he claimed. He buttered his bread. Later, washing dishes while my mother smoked on the back step, he said to me, "Saturday. Wear your boots. The real ones."

That was how the lessons began, though he never called them lessons. We started on the riprap below the coast guard station, where the channel ran close to shore, and he stood with his hands in the pockets of his wool coat and his chin lifted slightly, like a man listening, and said, "Tell me what you see."

I saw water. I said so.

"No," he said. "Nobody sees water. Water is invisible. You see what the water is doing. Look again."

I looked. It was a gray afternoon, windless, the tide somewhere in the middle of doing whatever it did. And because he waited, and because his waiting had a quality to it that made you want to be worthy of it, I kept looking until the gray surface stopped being one thing and became several. There was a band of water near the far shore that was smoother than the rest, almost oily. There was a line, faint as a crease in fabric, running diagonally out from the channel marker, and on one side of it the water had a texture like hammered metal and on the other it was nearly flat. There were small whorls spinning off the end of the riprap below us, dimples that opened and closed.

I described all of this badly, pointing, and he nodded the way he nodded, which was barely.

"The smooth water there, that's the back eddy. The current runs upriver along that shore when the ebb is in the channel. Slack water hides there. A man rowing against the tide stays in the smooth and lets the river fight itself." He pointed with his whole hand, fingers together, a pilot's gesture. "That line is the seam. Two waters that don't agree. Current against current, or current against wind. You see a seam, you ask yourself who's pushing who. The answer tells you what the bottom is doing, and the bottom is the whole story. The surface is just the bottom, telling on itself."

"The bottom," I repeated.

"Everything you see up here is made down there. A shoal, a hole, a ledge, a wreck. The water can't keep a secret. It tries, but it can't. That is the only thing I know that is worth knowing."

He taught me the vocabulary the way you'd teach a language, one word at a time, attached to a thing in the world. Riffle. Chop. Cat's paw — the dark scribble a gust makes coming across calm water, so you see the wind before you feel it. Slick. Boil — the round upwelling mound where current strikes an obstruction and climbs over it, smooth and slightly higher than the water around it, ominous as a muscle moving under skin. Tide rip. Williwaw. He taught me that a shoal makes the swell stand up and steepen long before it breaks, so that you read shallow water by the change in the wave's posture, the way you might read trouble in a person's shoulders across a room. He taught me that the river ran fresh and brown over the heavier salt water in layers, and that the line where they met offshore was visible as a change in color and often marked by a windrow of foam and sticks and gulls, because the seam concentrated the feed, and the birds knew it. "Birds are honest," he said. "Birds can't afford to lie. You see birds working, something is true underneath them."

The cannery, meanwhile, was running hard, the way things sometimes burn brightest going out. The winter season was crab and the boats went when the bar let them, and the whistle blew its shift changes morning and evening, a sound so woven into the town that nobody heard it anymore, the way you don't hear your own heart. My mother worked doubles when she could get them. The company was paying out the season honestly, you had to give them that, and there was a strange almost festive grimness in the town that winter, everyone earning and spending and saying see you tomorrow with a particular weight, because tomorrow was a word with a hole in it.

On Saturdays my grandfather and I walked. The jetty when the weather allowed, the riprap, the dock by the icehouse, the high overlook on the cape road where you could see the whole bar at once. He had a way of standing at the edge of water that I have tried to describe to people who never met him and always failed: not looking at it, exactly, but attending to it, the way a doctor attends to a chest with a stethoscope, knowing already most of what he'll hear and listening for the part he doesn't.

"That ship there," he said one morning, when a log carrier was inbound, a pilot aboard her — someone he knew, probably; he knew them all. "She's drawing maybe thirty-six feet. Tide's flooding, two hours in. Where does he put her?"

"In the channel," I said.

"Where in the channel? The channel is not a hallway. The channel is a live thing. There's more water on the north side of the range right now because the flood sets that way and the dredge hasn't been over the spit since summer. He'll favor the north. Watch."

We watched, and the ship came on, huge and slow and silent at that distance, and she did indeed crowd the northern edge of the buoy line, and my grandfather said nothing further, because the water had confirmed him and he didn't gloat over things that were simply true.

He talked, on those walks, more than I had ever heard him talk, and I understood even then that the talk was the point as much as the water, that he was emptying himself into me deliberately, a man transferring cargo before scuttling. He told me about ships. The Glenbrook, that lost her steering on the ebb in 1957 and went sideways down the channel like a leaf in a gutter while he stood on her bridge saying course corrections to a wheel that answered nothing. The Karin Maersk, whose Danish captain had wept — actually wept — crossing the bar in a February gale, and had then bought my grandfather aquavit in the saloon of his own ship and told him about his farm. The night the pilot boat broached coming alongside in a following sea and Verne Koski went into the water in his oilskins and they got a line on him by luck, by the grace of a single lit match of luck, and Verne piloted for nineteen more years and never once spoke of it. He told me about my grandmother, Aili, who had died when I was four and whom I remembered only as a smell of cardamom and a lap. He said she could hear weather coming in her sleep and would wake him saying it's turning, and she was never wrong, and he had crossed the bar two thousand times trusting instruments and his own eyes but had trusted her hearing over both.

"How did she do it?" I asked.

"Same way you'll do it," he said. "You live next to a thing long enough, honestly enough, it starts telling you things. The water doesn't talk to tourists." He glanced at me sideways. There was a joke in him somewhere, always, buried like a ledge, betraying itself only by a boil at the surface. "You are not a tourist. Your people are buried here. It will talk to you."

In January he took me out in the skiff. He wasn't supposed to — my mother had views, and his heart had views — but there came one of those still bright days that winter sometimes deals off the bottom of the deck, the swell down, the bar asleep, and he called the school sick for me with a steadiness of voice I admired for years afterward, and we went down to the basin and pulled the cover off the skiff, a sixteen-foot lapstrake double-ender he had built with his brother before the war. The motor was a small reliable Evinrude he maintained the way other men keep faith. We ran out past the moorage, past the cannery, where I looked up and saw the lit windows of the line and thought of my mother in there in her apron and gloves, and then we were in the channel and he cut the motor back and said, "Take it."

I steered. He sat in the bow facing me, which meant facing aft, not watching the water ahead at all, watching me watch it.

"Tell me," he said.

And I told him. The seam off the green can, flood against the river. The slick behind the spit where the back eddy was. The way the chop shortened and steepened where the bottom came up by the old pilings, the texture changing from long to crowded the way handwriting gets crowded at the end of a line. He corrected me twice — once about which way the eddy turned, once about the depth over the pilings, which was less than I guessed, "always less than you guess, guess again and then take some away" — and the rest of the time he just listened, and the sun stood low and white over the bar, and the water spoke and I translated, eleven years old, my hand on the tiller of a boat older than my mother, and I think now that I have had a few perfect hours in my life and that was the first of them and possibly still the best.

On the way in he had me bring the skiff alongside the float myself, judging the current's set, and I came in too fast and he fended off with one boot, undisturbed. "Good," he said. "Now you know what too fast feels like. There's no other way to learn it. Anybody tells you they learned it another way, they're lying or they're still learning it."

The storm came at the end of January, two days of southerly gale that put the bar into a state my grandfather called, looking at it from the cape road, "shut. Closed for business." On the second night of it, the Linnea J, a crab boat out of our own basin, forty-two feet, two men aboard — Roy Hanela and his nephew Mark, who had been three grades ahead of me in school until he wasn't in school anymore — failed to come in. They had gone out in the morning lull to pull gear they couldn't afford to lose, and the weather had come back early and harder than forecast, and by dark they were not answering the radio.

I know about that night partly because I was there for some of it and partly from what was told later, the way everything in that town was eventually told. The coast guard had the motor lifeboat out and a helicopter coming down from Astoria, and half the fleet stood in the basin with their engines running because standing there with your engine running was something you could do. And at some point, a coast guardsman not much older than Mark himself drove up the hill to the green house, because somebody at the station had said go ask Eino, the way you might say check the almanac, and my grandfather put on his coat and took his binoculars and went down, and I went with him because my mother was on shift and there was no one to tell me not to.

He stood in the lit operations room with the chart spread under his hands and listened to what they knew: last position, last radio contact, the gear they'd been pulling, the time. Then he was quiet for a while, and I watched him being quiet, and I understood that what he was doing was the same thing he did on the riprap with me, except the water he was reading was in his head, forty years of it, every set and drift and shoal of it, in the dark, in a southerly gale, on an ebbing tide.

"Roy won't try the bar," he said finally. "Not on the ebb, not tonight. He's not a fool, he's just broke, and those are different things. He'll have run north when it came up. He'll get in the lee of the cape and jog. But his engine's tired and if he's lost power—" He moved his finger along the chart, along the shore north of the cape, and stopped it. "The set tonight is onshore and southerly underneath, it hooks around the cape, everybody forgets it hooks. If he's drifting, he's not where the wind says. He's south of where the wind says. He's being carried into the bight, here, and there's a reef at the south end of it that doesn't break until it's too late to matter." He looked up. "Tell the helicopter to fly the bight. South end first."

They found the Linnea J at 11:40 that night, dead in the water, dragging a makeshift drogue Roy had rigged from crab pots, less than a mile north of the reef and closing on it. They took the men off by hoist because of the seas, and the boat went onto the rocks at around two in the morning and was gone by daylight, and Roy Hanela stood in the cannery parking lot the next afternoon with his hands at his sides looking at the water that had taken his boat and spared his life and his sister's son, and people went and stood next to him for a minute or two at a time, not saying much, which was the custom.

Nobody made a ceremony out of what my grandfather had done. A few men nodded to him at the store in a particular way for a few weeks. The coast guard chief sent a letter, which I found years later in a drawer with his pilot's license and a photograph of Aili at nineteen. When I tried to talk to him about it — I was eleven; I wanted it to be a story, I wanted him to be the hero of it — he shut it down gently. "The water told on itself," he said. "Same as always. I just been listening longer than the helicopter."

But something changed in the lessons after that, or maybe in me. I had thought I was learning something beautiful and old and dying, like the Finnish he sometimes muttered, a thing you learned out of love and loyalty. Now I understood it was also true, true the way a knife is true, that the difference between knowing what water in the dark was doing and not knowing was Roy Hanela alive in the parking lot. I started keeping a notebook. He saw it and said nothing, which from him was applause.

The cannery ran its last day on the second Friday in March. There was no event. The company sent no one. The whistle blew the end of shift at five o'clock the way it had blown it for eighty years, and then the line workers came out into the parking lot in the long northern dusk, my mother among them, and stood around in small groups, and somebody had beer in a truck bed, and the plume from the stack thinned and stopped, and that was all. I had thought the whistle would blow something special, long or different, a sound to mark it. It just blew five o'clock. I stood next to my grandfather at the edge of the lot and watched the steam stop, and he watched the water beyond the pilings, the flood pushing upriver, dark coming on.

"What's it saying?" I asked him, because by then it was a thing we said.

He took his time. "It's saying the tide's making," he said. "It's saying that whatever happens up here." He put his hand on my shoulder, which he did rarely, and it weighed what an oar weighs. "Towns are weather," he said. "Water is climate. You understand the difference?"

"Not really," I said, because he had taught me that.

"You will," he said. "Guess again and take some away."

He lived four more years, longer than the doctor had voiced and exactly as long, my mother said, as he had privately intended, which was until I could run the skiff alone in any weather he'd permit and name everything I saw on the water without reaching for the words. The town did what such towns do — shrank, hung on, turned partway into a postcard of itself. My mother moved us to Aberdeen and then I moved myself further, the way you do. The cannery building stood empty a long time and then burned, and people said arson, insurance, kids, and the pilings are still there, and the birds still work the seam off the river mouth because the seam never closed; only the town did.

I live now beside other water, and I make my living in a way that has nothing to do with boats, and still I cannot pass a harbor without stopping. People I'm with grow restless. I stand there and the gray surface comes apart into its parts — the cat's paw arriving before its wind, the slick where the eddy hides, the boil over the thing the water cannot keep secret — and I hear him say tell me, and I tell him. The water doesn't talk to tourists. Your people are buried here. They are; he is, on the hill above the basin, where on a clear day you can see the bar break white across the river's mouth, the bottom telling on itself the way it always has, the way it will go on doing after every whistle on every shore has blown five o'clock for the last time and fallen silent, and somebody's granddaughter, standing at the edge of it, looks again.
