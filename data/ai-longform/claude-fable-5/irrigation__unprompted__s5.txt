The leak had announced itself the way leaks do, which is to say quietly and then all at once, a soft place in the orchard road that had become by Tuesday a glistening seep and by Friday a small brown pond with a killdeer standing in it, and so on Saturday morning Wendell Asher and his brother Roy stood over the wet ground with shovels and looked at it the way men look at a thing they have already agreed, without speaking, is going to ruin most of a day.

"Mainline," Roy said. "Has to be."

"Could be the lateral."

"Lateral's two feet that way. Look where it's coming up."

Wendell looked. The water rose in a slow pulse at the center of the wet, a dark welling like something breathing under the road, and he knew Roy was right and felt the small familiar irritation of Roy being right, which was an irritation older than either of their marriages, older than the trees around them, nearly as old as Wendell himself, who was fifty-six and had been the younger brother for all of it.

It was the second week of June and already hot by eight. The orchard ran away from them on both sides in rows that their grandfather had laid out with a transit and a ball of string in 1947, Red Havens and Sun Crests mostly, peaches that nobody grew anymore because they bruised if you breathed on them, because they had to be picked dead ripe and eaten within days, because the whole apparatus of the world had reorganized itself around fruit that could survive a truck. Half the trees were past bearing well. A quarter were simply past, gray-limbed, leafing out in thin apologetic flushes, and Wendell had marked some of those with orange paint two winters ago intending to pull them and had not pulled them, and the paint had faded to the color of sherbet and the trees stood there still, neither alive enough to keep nor dead enough to make the decision for him.

Roy drove the shovel in at the edge of the wet and turned the first scoop. The soil came up dark and heavy, smelling of iron and rot, and the water moved into the hole he'd made as though grateful.

"We'll want the pump off," Roy said.

"It's off. I shut it off Thursday."

"Then where's this coming from?"

"Line's still draining down from the upper block. It'll weep all day. There's a quarter mile of pipe uphill of us, Roy."

Roy grunted, which was his way of conceding a point without surrendering any territory, and they dug. The water made the digging miserable, every shovelful half mud, the hole filling as fast as they opened it, and after twenty minutes Wendell walked back to the barn for the trash pump and the flat coil of discharge hose, and when he came back Roy was standing in the hole to his shins with his cap pushed back, and behind him the orchard was doing the thing it did in June light that Wendell had never found words for and had stopped trying to, the long aisles of shade going green-gold and infinite, the leaves moving without wind, every row a hallway into the same lit nowhere.

They got the pump running and the hole began, grudgingly, to lose. The little engine clattered and spat its stream down the row, and the brothers dug on opposite sides of the trench in a rhythm they had not had to negotiate since they were teenagers, Roy breaking the soil, Wendell throwing it, the spoil pile rising between them like a small brown argument.

"Hollis called the house again," Roy said. He said it to the trench.

"I figured he would."

"Donna talked to him. He's at one-point-six now."

Wendell threw a shovelful and said nothing. The number sat in the air between them with the gnats.

"That's up two hundred from March," Roy said.

"I can do arithmetic."

"One-point-six, Wen. Split two ways. That's retirement. That's Donna's knees done at a good place, that's Caleb's loans gone, that's you not standing in a hole at fifty-six with your back the way it is."

"My back's fine."

"Your back is famous. People in town ask after your back like it's a relative."

Wendell almost smiled and didn't let it land anywhere Roy could see it. He dug. A foot down the soil changed, went paler, sandier, the river-bottom ground their grandfather had bragged on, soil so good you could plant a fence post and harvest a gate, the old man used to say, and had said, every summer, to every visitor, until the saying of it was a kind of liturgy nobody listened to and everybody would have missed.

The pipe appeared first as a paleness, then as itself: six-inch PVC, forty years in the ground, and along its crown a crack ran like a hair, like a vein in marble, and from the crack the last of the draining water threaded up silver.

"There she is," Roy said, almost tenderly, the way he said it over any broken thing once it had been found, an alternator, a foreleg, their father in the hallway that February morning eleven years ago, there he is, as if the finding itself were a mercy. They widened the hole around the pipe, careful now, spading down along its flanks and scooping underneath by hand so they could get a coupling around it, and the work went quiet and absorbed, and for a while there was no orchard and no offer and no Hollis and no one-point-six, only the cold smooth flank of the pipe and the sucking mud and their eight hands' worth of knowledge between their four hands.

The crack was longer than it looked. They always were. Wendell chased it with his thumb, wiping mud, and it ran fourteen, sixteen inches before it thinned away into sound pipe.

"Need to cut back past it both directions," he said. "Three-foot section at least. You got pipe at your place?"

"I got four-inch. Nobody's got six-inch lying around but you."

"I don't have six-inch."

"You have everything. You have parts for tractors we sold in the nineties."

"I don't have six-inch pipe, Roy."

So that was the trip to town, and it was decided without discussion that Roy would go, because Roy still liked town and Wendell didn't, and because the supply house men would talk to Roy for ten minutes about the heat and the river and the Hollis development east of the highway, the one already framed, the streets already named — Orchard View Lane, Roy had reported once, with a bitterness so pure it was almost admiration, they cut down four hundred acres of trees and named the streets after the view of them — and Roy could carry that kind of talk and Wendell could not. While Roy was gone Wendell cut the pipe. He used the old crosscut saw with the blue handle, kneeling in the mud, and the cutting took a long time and he didn't mind. The PVC dust came off white and clung to the wet. When the bad section came free he lifted it out and laid it on the grass at the edge of the row and looked at it, three feet of pipe with a crack down its spine, and he thought, not for the first time, that there ought to be somewhere you could take such things, the failed parts, the sections cut out, and have them accounted for, entered in a ledger, this is what it cost to keep the rest whole.

He sat down with his back against a Red Haven that he knew without having to think about it had been grafted by his father in 1968 — you could see the graft union still, a healed swelling at knee height, the tree's whole life balanced on that old wound — and he ate half the sandwich Marie had made him and drank water gone warm in the jug and looked down the row. The fruit was sizing well where the trees still had vigor. Another five weeks, maybe six, and the Red Havens would come on, and there would be ten days of the smell that he privately believed was the finest thing his life had given him, better than any single human moment he could name, the smell of a peach orchard at full ripeness in the heat of the afternoon, a sweetness so dense and complicated it was almost grief. You couldn't sell that. That was the whole problem, in both senses: you couldn't sell it, and you couldn't sell it.

Roy came back at noon with two lengths of pipe roped to the lumber rack, two couplings, a fresh can of primer and a fresh can of glue, and a bag of tacos from the place by the tracks, and they sat on the tailgate and ate, and the pump clattered on, and the killdeer ran its broken-wing theater up and down the road pretending they were after its nest.

"Sixty-one dollars for the couplings," Roy said. "Six-inch fittings. Sixty-one dollars."

"Keep the receipt."

"For what? So the orchard account can not pay it like it didn't pay the last one?" Roy ate. He chewed the way he did everything, thoroughly and a little angrily. "I'm going to say it plain, Wen, and then I'll leave it alone. We cleared eleven thousand dollars last year. Both of us together, before we paid ourselves nothing. The year before, we lost four. The pump's original. The well's dropping. Half the trees should've come out under Dad and the other half should've come out under us. And a man wants to give us one-point-six million dollars."

"He doesn't want to give us anything. He wants to buy something."

"Fine. He wants to buy something."

"And what he wants to buy isn't what he'd be getting, because the second the deed records, what he bought stops existing. He's not buying an orchard. He's buying the death of one. There ought to be a different word for that kind of buying."

"There is. It's called a sale. It's how everyone we grew up with got out, and you'll notice none of them moved back."

"Curtis Mello moved back."

"Curtis Mello got divorced and lives over his sister's garage. Don't use Curtis Mello on me."

Wendell laughed before he could stop it, and Roy laughed too, just a snort, and for a moment the argument lay down between them like a dog that belonged to both of them. The pump changed its note — the hole was nearly dry now, slurping air — and Roy slid off the tailgate and went and throttled it down.

The afternoon was the worst of the work. The new pipe had to be measured twice and cut once and then cut again because the ground had shifted the old line a half inch out of true over forty years and nothing was square to anything. The couplings had to be dry-fit, and then the trench had to be widened again on the west end because there wasn't room to flex the line enough to seat them, and the soil there was full of old roots, an entire root system of a tree that had been pulled before Wendell was born, the ghost of it still articulated underground, and they chopped through it with the spade and the mattock, and Roy's shirt went dark down the spine, and Wendell's back announced itself the way the leak had, quietly and then all at once.

"Take a minute," Roy said, not looking at him.


"You went white."

"It's the heat."

"It's the heat," Roy agreed, in the tone of a man agreeing with nothing, and he took the mattock out of Wendell's hands without ceremony and finished the cut himself, and Wendell stood at the trench edge hating it, hating the standing, hating that the hating was childish, hating that his body had become a third party to every job, an unreliable subcontractor.

They primed the pipe ends purple and glued them and made the joints fast, each coupling shoved home with a quarter turn the way their father had taught them — hold it ten seconds or she'll push back out, the glue's alive for ten seconds, count them honest — and they counted them honest, silently, each man's lips barely moving, and then it was done, the new section pale and clean between the old gray runs like a healed place, like the graft union on the Red Haven, and there was nothing to do for an hour while the glue cured but everything else: backfill staged, tools gathered, the trash pump drained and loaded, the cut-out section of bad pipe carried to the truck where it lay in the bed looking smaller and less significant than the day it had cost.

They sat in the shade then, properly, with their backs against two trees in the same row, ten feet apart, facing the trench. It was the geometry they had always argued best in, side by side, no faces involved.

"You think I want it," Roy said. "You've decided I want it, and that lets you be the one who loves the place and I'm the one who'd sell it. That's how you've got it arranged."

"I haven't arranged anything."

"You've had it arranged since we were kids. You were Dad's and I was Mom's, you were the orchard and I was town. And I let you have that arrangement because it never cost me anything until now." Roy picked up a clod and crumbled it and let it run out of his fist. "I was here in '04 when it froze on April ninth and we lost the whole crop and Dad sat in the barn and wouldn't come in for supper. I drove the swamper every August of my life. I buried my hands in this dirt same as you. You don't get to mean it more than me, Wen. You just get to mean it louder."

Wendell looked down the row. The light had begun its long afternoon turn, coming in flatter now under the canopy, and the trunks threw their shadows across the aisles in rungs, so the whole orchard looked like a ladder laid down, going somewhere, going nowhere.

"Then how can you talk about selling it," he said, "if it's in you the same as me?"

"Because it's in me the same as you. Because I look at it honest. You think love is keeping a thing on a ventilator. I watched Donna's mother kept alive for two years past anything that was her, because nobody in that family could be the one to say it, and every one of them calls it love to this day, and every one of them knows better at three in the morning." Roy's voice didn't rise. That was the thing about Roy that people who didn't know him missed; the angrier he got, the quieter, until at his angriest he was nearly gentle. "This orchard is dying, Wendell. It's been dying for twenty years. Not failing — dying. There's a difference. Failing you can fix. We're not undercapitalized, we're done. The varieties are done. The market's done. The water's going to be done — you've seen the well log same as me, eight feet of drop in five years, and Hollis can drill four hundred feet down with money he finds in his couch and we can't drill at all. The question isn't whether it ends. The question is whether it ends with us getting something or ends with the bank getting it, or the county, or our kids getting it as a problem in an envelope. Caleb doesn't want it. Your girls don't want it. Have you asked them? Have you actually asked them, or do you just look hurt in their direction every Thanksgiving?"

Wendell was quiet for a long time. A jay went down the row complaining. Somewhere off east, faint, there was the back-up beeping of equipment, Hollis's equipment probably, the sound that had become the birdsong of the whole valley, and Wendell had gotten so he could hear it in rooms where it wasn't happening.

"I asked Janie," he said finally. "Last Christmas. Out by the propane tank."

"And what'd she say?"

"She said, 'Daddy, I love it the way I love Grandma's house.'" Wendell turned a twig in his fingers. "Grandma's house got sold in 1998."

Roy didn't say anything. He had the decency, or the strategy, not to.

"You know what I keep coming back to," Wendell said. "It's not the money and it's not even the trees. It's that I can stand right here and tell you the history of forty feet of ground. That coupling we just glued is six feet from where Dad cut his hand open in '79 and wrapped it in his shirt and finished the set anyway and needed eleven stitches, and Mom wouldn't speak to him for a day, not because of the hand but because he'd let me see it and I had nightmares. That tree behind you, the one with the lean, Grandad propped it after the '63 wind and said it'd die in a year and it's still here, and he's been gone forty. I'm not telling you anything you don't know. That's the point. You're the only other person alive who knows any of it. We sell, and it isn't that the knowing gets sold. It's that it stops referring to anything. It just becomes two old men's talk. Stuff we say at the coffee shop while the new people wait for us to die."

"It becomes that anyway," Roy said, gently now, fully gentle, which meant they had reached the bottom of it. "Wen. It becomes that anyway. The only question is whether we're sitting in the coffee shop broke."

The hour was up, more than up. They stood — Wendell coming up off the ground in stages, Roy pretending not to watch — and walked to the trench and looked at the joint a last time the way you do, for no reason, since whatever the glue had decided was already decided, and then they backfilled. They shoveled the spoil pile back over the pipe and tamped it in lifts with the flat of the spade and the heel of the boot, and the work was easier than the digging, work that ran downhill, and they finished it in twenty minutes and stood on the new soft ground packing it with their weight, two heavy men stepping in place like some old dance neither would admit to knowing.

"Run the pump," Roy said. "I'm not driving home without seeing it hold."

So Wendell walked up to the pump house in the long light and primed the old pump and threw the switch, and the motor wound up with its familiar reluctance and then its familiar resolve, and he stood there a minute with his hand on the discharge head feeling the water come up, feeling the whole orchard's circulation restart under his palm, the pressure climbing, the pipe thrumming faintly, water that had fallen as snow on mountains he could not see from here, moving now down lines his grandfather had buried, toward roots his father had grafted, every part of the system older than the men running it and dependent on them anyway. He walked back down the row. Roy was standing over the repair with his arms crossed, and the ground there was dry, was staying dry, and the two of them watched it not leak for a good five minutes, which was an absurd amount of time to watch ground do nothing, and was also exactly the right amount.

"Holds," Roy said.

"For now."

"Everything's for now. That's not the insult you think it is."

They loaded the last of the tools. The killdeer had gone quiet, satisfied or exhausted. Down the row the first sprinklers in the upper block were turning, throwing their long ropes of water that caught the low sun and went briefly to fire, and the smell came up off the wetted ground, dust and water, the oldest smell, the one that was both of their childhoods at once, and neither of them said anything about it because saying something about it was not a thing they did.

Roy shut the tailgate. "I'm going to tell Hollis we need until the first of August," he said. "Not yes. Not no. Until the crop's in. He'll wait. He'll grumble and he'll wait, because nobody else is selling him river-bottom ground with a county road on two sides."

Wendell understood what was being offered: not a victory, not a surrender, but a harvest. One more turn of it. Ten more days of the smell. A summer in which the question would hang in the trees with the fruit and ripen at its own rate, and then they would pick the one and answer the other, and maybe by then Wendell would know whether what he felt was love or was only the fear of becoming a man with nothing to repair.

"First of August," he said.

"And you'll think about it honest. Not loud. Honest."

"I'll think about it."

Roy got in his truck. He sat a second with the door open, one boot still on the ground, looking down the row the way their father used to look down it, an appraisal that was also a kind of prayer, and then he said, "Sixty-one dollars for the couplings," shaking his head, and pulled the door shut and drove out, and the dust of his going rose and drifted into the trees and settled on the leaves, where the next irrigation would wash it off, or the one after that.

Wendell stayed until the set was running full. He walked the line as far as the upper block, checking heads, clearing one plugged nozzle with the wire he kept in his shirt pocket for that purpose and no other, and the water went out over the ground in its slow silver wheels, and the orchard drank, indifferent to its own prognosis, and he stood in the last of the light at the head of his grandfather's longest row, in the only place on earth he had ever fully been, and let the question wait. It would keep. Everything held, for now. That was not the insult he had thought it was, either.
