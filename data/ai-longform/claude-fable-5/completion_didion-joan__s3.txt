But the Pacific. The Pacific was another deal altogether. The Pacific was the one place where the thing had a certain beauty to it, I’m not saying beauty exactly, I’m saying you stood on the deck of that transport at first light with the goggles pushed up on your forehead because nothing had happened yet and the sky going that pink and the water flat and warm as a bath, and you could think for a minute that what was about to happen out there was natural. A sunrise. A weather event. Then the count would come over the speakers and you’d pull the goggles down and even through the goggles, even with your eyes shut, even with your back turned, which was the recommended procedure for the larger events, you saw the light. You saw it through your own skull. The bones of your hands if you held your hands up, somebody was always holding his hands up just to see it, you saw the bones, like an X-ray, like a souvenir of yourself. And then the heat on the back of your neck, a flash of heat like an oven door opening somewhere behind you, and then nothing, the long nothing, and then the sound. The sound was always late. The sound was always a disappointment, a kind of dull freight-train thud, after the light. The light was the thing. The light was what nobody ever got right, not the painters, not the photographers, the film always came back looking like a smear, like a mistake at the lab. You had to be there. You had to stand on the deck in the wet air with the gardenia smell and watch the whole eastern sky turn colors that didn’t have names and feel your own bones light up, and then the cloud would start to build and stand up off the water, and I’ll tell you something, Inez, I never told anybody this, every time I watched that cloud build, every single time, I thought of you. I thought of you dancing in that white dress at the Outrigger. Nineteen years old. I was a married man and I stood on the deck of a transport off an atoll nobody can find on a map and watched the end of the world rehearse itself and what I thought about was a girl in a white dress.

Oh hell, Inez, he said.

Harry Victor’s wife.

He said this to her, Jack Lovett said this to Inez Victor (who was born Inez Christian), in the spring of 1975, in the bar of an airport hotel in Honolulu, at a time when the order of things to which they had both been accustomed was visibly coming apart, when the helicopters were lifting off the roofs of Saigon and the C-141s were coming into Hickam loaded with what the manifests called orphans, and when Inez Victor had certain pressing reasons of her own, reasons that will be gotten to, for being in Honolulu and for sitting in the bar of an airport hotel at four in the afternoon with a man her husband’s people had spent eighteen years keeping out of the photographs. I want you to notice the time and the place. Four in the afternoon. The trade winds dropping. A bar with a fish tank and a bowl of macadamia nuts and a television set tuned without sound to the news from the mainland. I want you to notice that Jack Lovett, who never in his life said three sentences about himself to anyone who could write it down, talked for the better part of an hour about pink light over the Pacific, and that Inez Victor, who had spent eighteen years being photographed listening to other people, listened.

This is a hard story to tell.

Let me put it another way. I have been trying to tell this story since the spring of 1975, and I have not yet found the door through which to enter it, although I have found a number of doors that lead somewhere else. The novelist’s usual instruments have failed me here, or I have failed them. I had, when I began, all the materials a novelist is supposed to want. I had a family, the Christians of Honolulu, four generations of them, shipping and sugar and land, missionary stock gone to seed in the sun, a family with a motto (in Latin, on a crest, ordered from a stationer in San Francisco in 1923) and a habit of dying intestate. I had the islands themselves, which is to say I had jasmine and orchids and the smell of something rotting under the plumeria, I had the rainbow showers and the rain moving across the Koolau and the particular light of two o’clock at Punaluu, and I had what every novelist who has ever passed through Honolulu has had, the sense that the place is a tropic of the American imagination, a colony of the mind, the westernmost suburb of the California dream. I had a murder, which will be gotten to. I had a public man, Harry Victor, congressman from New York, senator from New York, member of the Alliance for Democratic Institutions, almost a candidate in 1972, “a voice for the forgotten,” and I had his wife, and I had the other man, the one who flew in and out of places that were about to fall.

What I no longer had, by the spring of 1975, was the confidence of the narrator. The omniscience. The trick of saying “Inez believed” or “Jack Lovett wanted” as if I had been issued a clearance to the interior of other people. The spring of 1975 was a season in which a great many narratives lost their narrators, in which the lines on the maps in the newspapers moved every day and then the maps themselves stopped appearing, and it has seemed to me since that any story I tell about Inez Victor had better be told the way you would file a report from a place whose government has just fallen: provisionally, in the first person, with the sources named where they can be named.

So. Name the source. I first met Inez Christian in 1960, when she was working at Vogue and I was working at Vogue, two girls at the back of the same long room on Lexington Avenue, typing captions. Eight million women want to know. The eye travels up. I remember that she kept on her desk a glass paperweight with a hibiscus suspended in it and that she never once mentioned Hawaii unless someone asked her directly, and that when someone did ask her directly she would say “I grew up there” in the voice of a person closing a drawer. I remember that she was engaged that winter to a man at Sullivan & Cromwell and that she did not marry the man at Sullivan & Cromwell. I remember that she married Harry Victor instead, in 1962, in a garden in Oyster Bay, and that the wire photograph showed her holding her stephanotis at her waist and looking not at Harry Victor and not at the camera but at something outside the frame, and I remember thinking, when I saw the photograph, that I had no idea who she was. I had sat four feet from her for the better part of a year and I had no idea who she was. This was not, I came to understand, a deficiency in my attention. This was Inez. Inez was a woman whom many people described as radiant and some people described as remote and whom nobody, including I think Harry Victor, including possibly Inez, could have described in any detail at all.

Consider the most available footage. January 1972, the snow in Manchester, New Hampshire, Inez Victor in a green wool coat standing behind her husband on the flatbed of a truck, clapping her gloved hands in the rhythm of a song the loudspeakers are playing. June 1969, the tarmac at Jakarta, Inez Victor accepting a sheaf of cane from a child while behind her, just inside the frame, two men in short-sleeved shirts watch the crowd and not the child. August 1968, Chicago, Inez Victor in the lobby of the Hilton with wet eyes, saying to a CBS correspondent that she had not seen anything, that they had come in through the kitchen, that she had not seen anything at all. May 1973, the fund-raiser at the Beverly Hilton, Inez Victor saying into a microphone marvelous, marvelous to be back. In the spring of 1971 a writer from one of the newsmagazines, doing the obligatory piece (HARRY VICTOR: A CANDIDATE IN SEARCH OF A SEASON), asked Inez Victor what she believed to be the major cost of public life, and Inez Victor said: memory. The writer waited for her to go on and she did not go on, and the quote did not run, and I know about it only because the writer told me about it some years later in the bar of the Hotel Inglaterra in Havana, where neither of us was doing anything that could be construed as useful. Memory, mainly, is what I remember her saying, the writer said. You give it up. You drop it overboard, the way you’d lighten a plane. Things that happened to you get replaced by the agreed-upon version of things that happened to you, and after a while you can’t tell the difference, and after a longer while there isn’t any difference. She said it without any visible bitterness, the writer said. She said it the way you’d give somebody a recipe.

I offer that quote as the first hard evidence in this report.

The second piece of hard evidence is this: between December of 1952, when Inez Christian, age seventeen, danced with a thirty-three-year-old married man named Jack Lovett under the banyan tree at the Outrigger Canoe Club and continued seeing him, in the daytime, in borrowed cars and on the windward beaches, for the better part of four months, and the spring of 1975, when she sat with him in the bar of an airport hotel in Honolulu while the order of things came apart, Inez Victor and Jack Lovett were together, by my count, eleven times. Eleven times in twenty-three years. I have the dates. Some of the dates I have from Inez, some I have from flight manifests and hotel registers and one I have from a photograph in the files of the Associated Press, Jakarta, 1969, the tarmac, the child with the cane, the two men in short-sleeved shirts watching the crowd: the man on the left, his face turned three-quarters away, is Jack Lovett. Eleven times, and on none of those eleven occasions, as far as I can establish, did anything occur that Harry Victor’s people, had they known, could have used or feared, nothing but coffee and silences and twice, I believe, tears, and yet I would say, and I have thought about this, that Inez Victor and Jack Lovett were, for those twenty-three years, what would have to be called faithful to each other. I am aware that this is not a fashionable thing to say. I am aware that fidelity at a distance of eleven meetings in twenty-three years is a condition some people would call fantasy, or sentimentality, or worse. I can only report what the evidence suggests, and the evidence suggests that Jack Lovett carried the image of Inez Christian through every venue of his exceedingly opaque career the way other men carry photographs in their wallets, and that Inez, who had given up memory the way you would lighten a plane, kept Jack Lovett. Jack Lovett was what she kept.

About the career. You will want to know, as the congressional investigators wanted to know, as two grand juries wanted to know, what exactly Jack Lovett did. The answer on the record is that Jack Lovett was a businessman. The answer on the record is that Jack Lovett was, at various times between 1955 and 1975, a consultant to an aircraft-leasing concern in Burbank, a partner in a construction company doing harbor work at Vung Tau, the holder of a power of attorney for a Liechtenstein entity called Pacific Era Resources, and a registered representative of nothing at all. He had been, before that, an army officer, and before that the kind of young man from Bakersfield who joins the army because the alternatives have a way of presenting themselves as oil leases that don’t come in. The answer off the record is that Jack Lovett did what the situation required. Situations were his medium. He moved through the gray latitudes where the cargo gets to the airstrip without anyone signing for it, where the end-user certificates name countries that have no air force, where the man meeting the plane is known to everyone at the bar of the Hotel Constellation in Vientiane and to no government on earth. He was registered nowhere and recognized everywhere. He had the operator’s instinct for airports, could read an airport the way other men read a racing form, told me once, in the coffee shop at Kai Tak, that you could tell everything you needed to know about a country’s situation by which flights were delayed and who was standing at the Pan Am counter trying to pay cash. He distrusted theory, distrusted memoranda, distrusted above all the men in Washington who believed, as he put it, that the world was a seminar. The world, in Jack Lovett’s reading, was a deal in progress, a series of weathers, and the only sin was to be sentimental about the weather.

And yet. The gardenias. The pink light no painter could approximate. Thought of you every time, Inez. I ask you to hold both of these things in your mind at once, the man who moved cargo through the gray latitudes and the man who stood on the deck of the transport thinking about a girl in a white dress, because if you cannot hold both of these things in your mind at once you will not understand what happened in 1975, and what happened in 1975 is the only story I have.

Here is what I had instead of a novel.

I had notes. I had, in a folder, the deed history of a parcel of land at Kahala that the Christian family sold in 1959, the year of statehood, for what was then the largest price per front foot ever paid in the Territory, and I had a clipping from the Honolulu Advertiser about the dedication of the Christian Wing at a hospital, and I had a photograph of Inez’s grandmother, Cissy Christian, in white gloves at a luncheon of the Daughters of Hawaii, and I had the text of a toast Inez’s uncle Dwight made at a wedding in 1966: we are blessed, we are blessed in these islands, we have made the desert bloom, an interesting toast in that the islands had never at any point been a desert, but Dwight Christian was a man who ordered his metaphors the way he ordered his cement, in bulk and from the mainland. I had Inez’s father, Paul Christian, who will figure in this story in a way I would have given a great deal to avoid, Paul Christian who came and went, Sydney, Carmel, a sublet flat in Cannes, a single room at the Tropic Palms when the remittances thinned, Paul Christian who described himself on hotel registers as “investor” and described his family, to anyone who would listen, as vipers. I had Inez’s sister, Janet, who stayed. Janet who married Dick Ziegler, who made his money in windward land development, Janet who gave the parties and sat on the symphony board and called Inez twice a year, at Christmas and on their mother’s birthday, collect, a small joke of Janet’s, since the calls were the only inexpensive thing about her. I had the mother herself, Carol Christian, who came out from Stockton on the Lurline in 1934 with all her expectations packed in steamer trunks, and who learned over the next decade what the islands taught haole wives of a certain type to learn, which was the difference between being family and being deck cargo, and who went back to the mainland in 1946 on the first Pan American Clipper she could get space on, leaving two daughters at Punahou and a note that said only: it’s nobody’s fault, the climate never agreed with me. Inez was eleven. Janet was nine. When I once asked Inez what she remembered about her mother leaving she looked at me for a while and then she said, I remember we got leis. Somebody took us down to see the Clipper off and you couldn’t see the gangway, everybody was throwing serpentine, and somebody, I suppose it was Uncle Dwight’s secretary, bought us leis, and afterwards we had shave ice. She said this in the voice of a person closing a drawer. The agreed-upon version. The recipe.

I lay all this out, the deeds and the clippings and the leis and the shave ice, because this was to have been the novel: the fall of the house of Christian, three generations of the Pacific dream curdling in the heat, the bougainvillea and the dry rot, a big and orderly machine of a book in which the murder occurs at the exact center and the meanings radiate outward like spokes. I made charts. I will admit this. I made charts with the names of the characters in boxes and the lines of force between them inked in two colors, and I pinned the charts above my desk, and every morning for some months I sat down beneath the charts and every morning the book declined to begin. The book declined to begin, I came finally to see, because the book was a lie. Not a lie in its facts. The facts held. A lie in its shape. The shape said that events proceed from character and accumulate toward meaning, that houses fall for reasons, that a narrator exists who can stand at a fixed point and watch the radiating spokes. In the spring of 1975 I stopped believing in the fixed point. In the spring of 1975 anyone who read the cables stopped believing in the fixed point. Da Nang fell in a weekend. Men who had spent a decade explaining the war on television explained, in the same reasonable voices, why the war had never been explicable. A planeload of orphans crashed on takeoff and the next planeload took off anyway. In Honolulu, where I happened to be, you could stand at the rail at Hickam and watch the C-141s come in every ninety minutes, and on the first of April 1975, standing at that rail, I saw, although I did not for several days understand what I had seen, Inez Victor and Jack Lovett standing together about forty feet apart from everyone else, not touching, not talking, watching the ramp of a C-141 come down. Inez was holding a clipboard. She had flown out, the wire story said later, as a representative of one of Harry Victor’s relief committees. Jack Lovett was not holding anything and no wire story ever said why he was there, but I can tell you that of all the people at the rail that morning, the colonels and the Red Cross women and the wives with their Instamatics, Jack Lovett was the only one watching not the children coming down the ramp but the cargo doors, the fuel trucks, the perimeter. The operator’s eye. The weather eye. And I can tell you that at a certain point Inez Victor looked over at him, one look, two seconds at the outside, and that I have spent a good part of the years since trying to render the content of that look and have not managed it, any more than the Sunday painter managed the pink sky.

So you see the difficulty. The cards I hold are not the cards the game requires. I have the deeds and the manifests and the wire photos and the eleven dates, and I have the look on the tarmac at Hickam, and I have, from Inez herself, in a hotel garden in Kuala Lumpur in December of 1975, with the rain coming straight down into the swimming pool and a waiter standing under the eaves folding napkins as if the folding of napkins were the last reliable transaction on earth, some hours of talk that I taped, with her permission, and that I have played so many times since that I can hear the rain on the tape before I hear the rain. What I do not have is the connective tissue, the because, the and so. I am going to proceed without it. I am going to give you the islands and the family and the murder, and Harry Victor in the primaries and Jack Lovett in the gray latitudes, and the spring when the maps stopped appearing, and I am going to lay the pieces on the table in the order in which they came to me rather than in the order in which a more confident narrator would deal them, and where I do not know a thing I am going to say that I do not know it.

The light at dawn during those Pacific tests was something to see, Jack Lovett said to Inez Victor in the spring of 1975.

Something to behold.
