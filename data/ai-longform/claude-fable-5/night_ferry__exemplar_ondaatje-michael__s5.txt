He drove onto the ferry at North Sydney in the last hour of light, following the hand signals of a boy in an orange vest who guided him into the steel cave of the car deck as if guiding something blind. Around him the trucks settled and ticked. He turned the engine off and sat for a moment in the half-dark with the smell of diesel and rope, his hands still on the wheel, and then he took the envelope from the seat beside him and went up the metal stairs towards the noise of other people.

The call had come two days earlier, a voice in St. John's that belonged to a constable named Hynes. A man had come ashore in a cove east of Rose Blanche, the voice said, and there was reason to believe. The voice was careful with him, gentle the way men are gentle when they have rehearsed gentleness, and Owen had stood in his kitchen in Glace Bay holding the phone and watching a wasp work the inside of the window, the deliberate fury of it, while the voice said the words *answering the description* and *no positive identification at this time* and *next of kin*. He had not heard his brother's name spoken aloud by another person in four years. Hearing it now was like having a stone returned to him that he had thrown into deep water.

— Brendan Connell, the voice said. We believe.

— Yes, Owen said. And then, because the silence needed something: He's my brother.

Eleven years. There had been one postcard in eleven years, a picture of a lighthouse, no message on the back except a single penciled line of numbers that Owen had finally understood, months later, to be coordinates. He had looked them up in the school atlas. A stretch of empty water south of the island, no land within thirty miles. That was Brendan's idea of a letter. *Here is where I am. Here is where there is nothing.*

In the forward lounge the seats were filling with truckers and families, a hockey team in matching jackets sprawled across three rows already asleep, the boys' faces slack and ancient in the fluorescent light. Owen found a seat by the window. The ferry trembled, and the lights of the town began to slide. He watched the breakwater pass, the last green buoy, and then there was only the strait, six hours of it, dark as a held breath.

His brother had been a diver. Urchins first, in the cold seasons, working off a boat called the *Lila June* out of Louisbourg, coming up with his bags of spines, his lips blue, laughing through the regulator the way no one was supposed to. Then salvage, which paid better and asked more. Brendan went down to wrecks, to lost anchors, to the propellers of draggers fouled with their own gear. He went under the ice in February to find a snowmobile and the man still on it, and he never spoke of that except once, drunk, in their mother's kitchen. *He was sitting up*, Brendan said. *Like he was waiting for the light to change.* Then he laughed his hard laugh and would say no more, and their mother crossed herself at the sink with her back to them.

Owen had stayed on land. That was the shape of it, the simplest way to draw the two of them: one brother who went down into the dark for money and one who kept books for the co-op and married and unmarried and stayed within sight of the house they were born in. Their father had been a welder at the steel plant and then, when the plant began its long dying, a fisherman the way a man becomes a fisherman at fifty, with borrowed gear and bad knees and a boat he loved more than he had ever been able to say to anyone.

It was the boat at the bottom of it. The estrangement. Though estrangement was a word Owen had never used; what they had was more like a wound that had been allowed to heal wrong, the way Brendan's hand had healed wrong after the winch cable took it at nineteen, the two last fingers fused into a single hooked thing he used afterwards like a tool, proudly, holding his cigarettes there, holding cards. Their father had drowned on a Tuesday in October, setting nets alone off Scatarie because Brendan, who was supposed to crew that morning, was sleeping off a night in someone else's bed in Sydney. They found the boat circling, the engine still running, describing its slow loyal loops around the place where he had gone over. At the wake Owen had carried his grief across the room to his brother like a lit thing and put it into his hands.

— You should have been with him.

That was all. Four words. Brendan had looked at him a long time with their father's pale eyes and then set down his glass and walked out of the house, past the cars, past the cousins smoking in the yard, and kept walking, and the next anyone knew of him he was on the island, on the far shore of the strait, and the years began to stack up between them like cordwood.

A woman sat down across from Owen with a thermos and a paperback she did not open. She was perhaps sixty, with the wind-cured face of the outports, and she watched the black window the way other people watch a fire.

— Going home? Owen asked, because the silence had gone on so long it had become a kind of question itself.

— Going home, she said. My sister's after breaking her hip. And you?

— Family, he said. The word arrived in his mouth before he could choose another.

She nodded as though this were the only reason anyone crossed water at night, which perhaps it was. Later, near midnight, she poured tea into the thermos cup and handed it to him without asking, and he drank it, and they did not speak again. There is an intimacy possible only between strangers on a night crossing, he thought, two people suspended over six hundred feet of dark water, owing each other nothing, the tea passing between them like a sacrament neither would mention in the morning.

He could not sleep. He went out onto the deck where the wind took his jacket and shook it on his body, and he stood at the rail and looked for the lights of other ships. There was one, far off to the south, a dragger maybe, a handful of lights low to the water, and he watched it until it was gone. The strait at night gives you nothing. It is not like a river, which carries its country with it, the smell of fields, the bodies of trees. The strait is only itself, salt and distance, and what it has taken it keeps without ceremony.

We are fourteen and seventeen and it is August at the slag quarry behind the plant, the water in the pit so deep and so still it holds the whole sky upside down without trembling. Brendan stands on the high ledge, the forty-footer, where nobody goes. He is showing me how to die without dying, which is what he calls it. You take three breaths, he says, and on the third you keep it, and you go down feet first with your arms at your sides like you're standing at attention for the Queen, and you let the water close over your head, and you keep going until the light is a coin above you, and then a sequin, and then nothing, and that is where you stop. That is the floor of yourself. You'll want to panic, he says. The panic is a liar. The body has more in it than the body believes. Then he steps off the ledge without ceremony, without even straightening, drops the forty feet, and is gone so long I begin to count, and I am still counting when he surfaces at the far side of the pit, blowing, laughing, his chipped tooth white in his wet face, shouting something I can't hear across the water that I understand anyway. Come down. Come down and see.

Owen went back inside near three and slept badly in the chair, and dreamed not of his brother but of the boat, the *Marion C.*, circling its empty circle, the wheel turning itself, and he woke with the lounge lights still humming and the woman across from him asleep with her face composed and dignified as a carving, and through the window the sky had begun, in the east, the first gray business of morning.

Port aux Basques came up out of the fog like something remembered rather than seen — the long rock arms of the harbour, the houses set into the stone hills the way teeth are set into a jaw. He drove off the ferry into a rain so fine it was almost a quality of the air, and the town's one early restaurant gave him eggs and capelin and tea, and the girl who served him looked at him twice, the second time longer, and he understood that she was seeing Brendan in him, the family face, and that his brother had sat in this room, perhaps in this chair, perhaps a hundred mornings.

Hynes met him at the detachment, a big man younger than his telephone voice, who shook Owen's hand and held it a half-second past the shake, which was its own sentence.

— It's not a thing I'd wish on anyone, Hynes said in the car. He'd been in the water some days. I want you prepared for that.

— How many days.

— Nine, we think. Maybe ten.

Owen watched the harbour pass, the stacked lobster pots, a man in yellow oilskins hosing down a deck with the bored thoroughness of ritual.

— Where was he found?

— Cove east of Rose Blanche. Fellow walking his dog. He was — Hynes paused, choosing. The sea had brought him in gently, if that means anything. Laid him up on the kelp like it was setting him down.

The hospital was small and the morgue was in the basement, as morgues are, as if the dead must be kept near the foundations of things. A room of steel and tile and a light that made everyone in it the same colour. The attendant was an old man with rimless glasses who moved with the unhurried tact of his profession, and there was a form on a clipboard, and there were the sentences Hynes was obliged to say, and then there was the drawer, and the long zippered bag, and the sound of the zipper, which Owen knew he would carry for the rest of his life the way you carry a song you never chose.

The sea had worked on the face. He had been warned and the warning was useless; no one can be warned into readiness. What lay there had been a man and was now a proposition about a man, an argument the water had been making for nine days. The hair was Brendan's colour, gone gray at the temples the way their father's had. The frame was right, the long heavy bones of it. But the face would not resolve. Owen stood looking down and felt nothing he had prepared to feel, not grief, not horror, only a terrible attentiveness, as if he were being asked to translate a language he had once been fluent in and had let go.

— Take your time, Hynes said from somewhere behind him.

— The left hand, Owen said. Can I see the left hand.

The attendant drew the bag open further. And there it was. The two last fingers fused into their single hooked thing, the scar tissue pale and smooth as candle wax, the old violence of the winch cable healed into the hand forty years ago and carried down all the years since, through every glove, every deck, every glass lifted in every bar on two shores, and into the water, and out of it. Owen had watched it happen. He had been eleven, on the wharf, and the cable had jumped, and his brother had not screamed, had only looked at his own hand with an expression of profound surprise, as though it had told him a secret.

— Yes, Owen said.

— You're certain.

— That's his hand.

He heard how strange the sentence was even as he said it. Not *that's him*. *That's his hand*. As if the rest of his brother were elsewhere, unaccounted for, still down at the floor of himself where the light was a sequin and then nothing, and only the hand had been sent up, the way a diver sends up a line to say *I am here, I am working, do not haul yet*.

There was an inner forearm too, and on it, blurred blue-green, a compass rose tattooed by a man named Felix in a kitchen in Louisbourg in 1979, badly, the north arrow leaning drunkenly east, so that Brendan used to say he'd spent his whole life navigating fifteen degrees off true and that this explained everything. Owen did not need it. But he looked at it a long time anyway, the way you look at handwriting.

They gave him tea afterwards in an office, and forms, and he signed his name several times, and his name looked strange to him each time, the signature of a man impersonating himself. Hynes told him there would be no inquest, likely. There had been no boat reported missing. Brendan had last been seen on a Friday evening, walking east out of Rose Blanche along the shore path, alone, sober as far as anyone could say, and the weather had come up hard that night from the southwest.

— He'd been living out there three years, Hynes said. Room over the fish plant. Kept to himself, but they liked him. He'd dive for people. Lost gear, fouled props. Wouldn't take money half the time. Took fish, took a dinner.

— That sounds like him, Owen said, though in truth he did not know if it sounded like him. He was learning his brother now from strangers, the way you learn a country you have only seen on maps.

He drove out to Rose Blanche in the afternoon, the road following the coast through a landscape of rock and tuckamore, the stunted trees all leaning inland together away from the salt wind like a congregation that has heard bad news. The fish plant was at the head of the harbour, and the landlady, a Mrs. Osmond, was waiting for him because Hynes had called ahead, and she took both his hands in both of hers at the door and held them.

— He spoke of you, she said.

— Did he.

— He said, my brother keeps the books for the co-op in Glace Bay. He said it like it was a marvel. Like you'd been made a bishop.

The room was at the top of an outside staircase, one room with a sloped ceiling and a window full of harbour. A bed made with military neatness. A chair. A small table with an oilcloth. His diving gear was racked along one wall with the same neatness, the suit hanging like a shed skin, the tanks upright, the regulator coiled with a care that was almost tenderness. Their father had been that way with gear. *Look after the thing that looks after you.* Owen had not thought of the sentence in thirty years and now it was in the room with him.

On the table there was a chart of the south coast, much handled, soft as cloth at the folds, and on it, in pencil, small marks. Dozens of them, scattered along the shore and out into the deep water, each one a tiny circle with a date beside it in Brendan's cramped hand, some of the dates ten years old. Owen bent over it in the window light. They were not fishing marks. Some were too deep for any gear. He understood, slowly, the way you come to understand a poem, that they were dives. Every one. A log kept not in a book but on the water itself, each circle a place his brother had gone down into the dark and come back up, the whole coast annotated with descents, and far out, south of everything, in water with no name, one circle older and darker than the rest, gone over so many times with the pencil that the paper had worn thin, and beside it no date at all. Owen took the lighthouse postcard out of his jacket — he had carried it across the strait without admitting to himself why — and checked the penciled coordinates against the chart, though he already knew. The same place. The place where there was nothing.

— He'd go out there once a year, Mrs. Osmond said from the doorway. She had followed him up with tea she set down and did not pour. Get one of the boys to run him out in October. Always October. Dive alone, which you're never supposed to do. They'd wait on the boat for him. He never said what was down there. Patrick Hatcher asked him once and he said, nothing, my love, that's the point of it. There's nothing down there at all.

October. Their father's month. Owen stood with the chart in his hands and felt the room tilt very slightly, the way a deck tilts, and right itself. There was no wreck out there, no bones, nothing of their father, whose body had come ashore decently at Scatarie within the week and lay now beside their mother in the churchyard at Glace Bay. There was only depth. Only the dark and the cold and the floor of himself, and a man going down into it every October with his lungs full of held breath or bottled air, standing at attention for the Queen in two hundred feet of black water, in the one church he trusted, saying whatever he said there, or saying nothing, which was perhaps the same prayer. *You should have been with him.* Four words, and his brother had spent eleven years being with him, annually, faithfully, in the only place it could still be done.

In the drawer of the table there was a tobacco tin, and in the tin a few photographs. Their mother in a print dress, squinting, a hand raised against the sun, forever about to wave or forever shielding her eyes, no way now to know which. The *Marion C.* at the wharf, white and patient. And one of the two of them, Owen and Brendan, at the quarry, fourteen and seventeen, wet, blinding white in old light, Brendan's arm across his brother's shoulders and the chipped tooth showing, and on the back, in pencil, in the cramped hand, one word. *Down.*

Under the photographs lay their father's watch. Owen had believed it lost, had once, to his shame, believed it taken. It had stopped, of course — no watch survives a fisherman's son — and the hands stood at twenty past four, some afternoon or some morning years deep, a moment preserved without its day. He held it a while. Then, because the body has more in it than the body believes, he wound it, slowly, feeling the old resistance gather under his thumb, and held it to his ear, and it spoke.

He stayed that night in the room, with Mrs. Osmond's blessing, lying on his brother's bed in his clothes with the window open to the harbour sounds, the lines tapping the masts, the water working at the pilings, all the small ongoing diplomacy between the made world and the sea. He thought he would not sleep and he slept.

In the morning the fog had lifted and the strait lay out beyond the harbour mouth, blue-grey and creased, and on the early ferry back, standing at the stern rail with the island shrinking behind him and the watch ticking in his breast pocket against his heart like a second, smaller heart, Owen watched the wake unspool behind the ship, that long white seam the vessel stitches and the water immediately, patiently, begins to unpick. Somewhere beneath the route of every crossing, he thought, lies everything the strait has been given. The two shores do not touch and are never out of each other's sight. A man can spend a whole life fifteen degrees off true and still, in October, find his way back to the one place. Three breaths, and on the third you keep it. The panic is a liar. He stood at the rail until the island was a low blue word on the horizon, and then a syllable, and then nothing, and that is where he stopped.
