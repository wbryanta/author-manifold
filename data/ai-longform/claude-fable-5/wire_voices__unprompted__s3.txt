The board in Calder was an old one, a three-position toll board with cords the color of dried blood and lamps that lit a small dirty amber when a line wanted you, and for eleven years I sat at the middle position on the evening shift, from four o'clock until midnight, with a headset whose band had worn a part into my hair that never grew back right. Number, please. That was the whole of my catechism. Number, please, and then the listening, and then the reaching of the cord up into the jack, and the small click of two distances becoming one. People think of an operator as a woman who talks, but the truth is we were women who heard. A voice would come up out of the dark of the line and I would know things about it before it finished its first sentence — whether it was calling with good news or bad, whether it was drunk, whether it was used to being obeyed, whether it had been crying and had stopped just long enough to dial. You could not help knowing. The voice arrived stripped of its face and its clothes and its automobile and everything else it used in daylight to introduce itself, and what was left was the thing itself, naked as a nerve.

I had come to the work the way most of us did, which is to say sideways, out of some other life that closed. My mother took sick in 1953 and I left the teachers' college in Bowling Green to nurse her, and the telephone company would let a girl work evenings, and my mother died slowly enough that by the time she was gone I had eight years' seniority and a paid-for house and a chair at the board that fit my back like a saddle fits a horse it has ruined. There had been a man once, in the college years, who went to Korea and wrote me letters that got shorter and then stopped, and I have wondered since whether I took the job because of him — whether I sat down at that board the way some women sit down at a window, to watch a road. I never said so to anybody. The other girls on the shift were younger, Donna and Phyllis, and they talked about their boyfriends and their layaway plans, and Mrs. Pell the chief operator sat behind us at her desk like a hen on a clutch of stones, and on slow nights you could hear the whole room breathing, three women and the faint sea-sound of every conversation in the county passing under our hands.

He first came up on my board on a Sunday in January of 1968, a little after nine o'clock, when the Sunday rates were down and the board had gone quiet because decent people were getting ready for Monday. The lamp lit on a local trunk and I answered and a young man's voice said, "Long distance, please, I want to make a person-to-person call to Miss Carol Ann Hodges in Bakersfield, California," and he read the number off slow and careful the way a man reads something he has copied onto paper but knows by heart anyway. The line had the hollow ring of a coin telephone, and I knew which one it would be, because Calder only had four, and only one of them was anywhere a person would stand on a January night, and that was the lighted booth on the sidewalk outside Hartley's Rexall on Main Street. I quoted him the rate, which was a dollar fifty-five for the first three minutes, Sunday rate, and I heard him feeding the coins, and here is something nobody remembers anymore: the coins rang. A nickel struck one bell, a dime struck it twice quick, and a quarter hit a gong, deeper, like a small church. He paid in quarters, six of them and a nickel, six little Sundays going down the slot, and I built the call up through Columbus and Chicago and Denver and got a Bakersfield operator with a voice like warm dust, and we rang, and a girl answered before the second ring finished, the way you only answer when you have been sitting beside the telephone with your hand already lifted.

"I have a person-to-person call for Miss Carol Ann Hodges."

"This is she," the girl said, and the boy in the booth let out his breath, just a little, just enough that I heard it, and I said go ahead please, and I started the ticket and stamped the time and I should have come away off the line then, and I did, mostly. An operator on a coin call has to come back to collect the overtime, that was the regulation and also my excuse, and so every three minutes I dipped back in to say please deposit a dollar fifty-five for the next three minutes, and every three minutes I caught a spoonful of them, the way you catch a phrase of music out a passing car window.

That first night I learned that it was cold in Ohio and that it was not cold in Bakersfield, and that her aunt did not like her tying up the line, and that he said all right then, all right, three more minutes, and fed the gong six more times. The next Sunday he was back at five past nine, and the Sunday after that, and by Lent it was as fixed as the angelus. Phyllis took him once when I was on my relief and I came back and felt something I am not proud of, a small mean stab, as if she had sat in my pew. After that I arranged my relief around him. Twenty past nine I would tell Donna, take your coffee now if you're going, and she would look at me sideways and go.

Over the spring I came to know them the way you know a serial in a magazine, in installments, with the maddening parts left out. He had stayed in Calder because his father was sick — the lungs, I gathered, the cough came through the line some weeks like a third party on the call, the old man hacking somewhere in a farmhouse out the Pike while the boy stood in a glass booth in town and said Pop's about the same, Pop's no worse. Her family had gone west two years before, the whole tribe of them, for work in the packing sheds and then better than that, her father had got on at a place that made parts for airplanes. They had been seventeen and eighteen when the moving van came. Now she was twenty and he was twenty-one and they had been engaged, as far as I could tell, since before they knew the word, and there was no ring because every quarter he had went down that coin slot or into the doctor or into a 1962 Fairlane he was forever getting right. He had a low voice with a hitch of laughter always waiting in it. Hers was quick and light and ran ahead of itself, and when she was trying not to cry it went flat and careful, like someone walking on ice, and I learned to hear that before he did.

I want to set down plainly that I knew it was wrong, the listening. There is a discipline in the work and Mrs. Pell preached it like Scripture: you are a piece of the machine, Vera, she told me my first month, the machine has no ears. But the machine did have ears, that was the whole trouble, the machine was three women in a room above the dry goods store, and that year of all years the machine heard everything. The night in April when Dr. King was shot the board lit up like a grass fire, every lamp at once, and stayed lit till two in the morning, everybody calling everybody, sons calling mothers, and half the calls were just people saying did you hear, did you hear, as if the news were not real until it had traveled down a wire between two voices that loved each other. In June it was the same again, for the Kennedy boy, and all that summer the overseas military circuits ran heavy out of the cities, and once a month or so I would handle a call patched through from some MARS station, a soldier's voice riding the radio from the far side of the earth, saying over after every sentence, I love you, over, and the mother saying I love you too, over, and me sitting between them like a fencepost between two fields. You did not stay a piece of the machine through 1968. Nobody did.

So I listened, in my spoonfuls, and I will not pretend it was only duty. The truth is they had become my Sundays. I had the house, and the radio, and a sister in Toledo who called on my birthday, and on Sunday evenings I had a boy in a phone booth and a girl in California, and between collections I would sit with my hand near the key and watch the little ticket clipped in front of me filling up with time stamps, three minutes, six, nine, some flush weeks fifteen, and I would think: there it is, that is what it sounds like. I was forty-one years old. I had stopped expecting it for myself without ever exactly deciding to, the way you stop expecting a letter, and it did not make me bitter to hear it in others. It made me — I have looked for the word for thirty years — it made me warm at a window, watching a lit house across a field.

Once, only once, I saw him. It was a Sunday in July and the heat had broken and I had walked downtown after church just to walk, and there was the booth outside Hartley's with its light on, in the daytime when the light hardly shows, and a young man in it with his back to the street, one boot up against the glass, his head bent to the receiver. Tall, with sunburned arms and pale hair gone dark with Brylcreem, and a folded piece of paper pressed flat against the glass — a letter, I supposed, or a list of things he meant not to forget to say. He was not making his nine o'clock call; it was barely noon; maybe it was her birthday, or maybe he had sold something, or maybe he just had two dollars and the day had gotten long. I did not slow down. I want that understood. I walked past on the far side of Main Street at my regular pace like a respectable woman going nowhere in particular, and all I took with me was the back of his head and the boot against the glass, and that night at the board when the lamp lit at five past nine I knew his voice the way you know a song after you have finally seen the singer.

His father died the second week of September. I knew because the call came on a Wednesday, off schedule, at four in the afternoon, person-to-person, Miss Carol Ann Hodges, and his voice was scraped down to the boards. The funeral was Tuesday, he told her. There wasn't hardly anybody. Reverend Slocum did it fast on account of the rain. He talked about the rain for a while, what it had done to the cemetery road, the way men will inventory the small damage when the large is unsayable, and she did not interrupt him once, and when he ran out she said, "Sam." Just that. And the line was quiet for so long that I checked my cords thinking we'd lost the connection, and then he said, "I know it. Soon as I square things here. Soon as the car's right." It was the first time I had heard his name. All those months he had been only the Sunday boy, because a person-to-person caller names the party he wants, not himself, and now I had it: Sam. The Tullys out the Pike — I matched it later to the death notice in the Calder Ledger-Gazette, Earl Raymond Tully, 58, foundryman, survived by one son, Samuel.

Through the rest of September and into October the Sunday calls turned into one long piece of arithmetic. The farm wasn't a farm, it was a house and eleven acres and a barn with a swayback, and there was a man who would take it but the bank had paper on it, and there was the auction, the words came through in my three-minute spoonfuls — the disc and the harrow and Pop's tools, a man from Findlay bought the anvil — and underneath all of it ran the one bright wire: he was coming. By spring at the outside, he said in late September. Then it was by Thanksgiving. Then, on the second Sunday of October, with his voice up out of its grief for the first time in a month, he said, "Don't you write me back after this one, Carrie, the letter'd cross me on the road." And she laughed, and her laugh broke in the middle the way ice breaks, from gladness, and he fed the gong twice more, two extra quarters he didn't owe, just to say it over: don't write me back, I'll be the one knocking.

The third Sunday of October he did not call.

I sat at the middle position and watched the local trunk lamps from nine o'clock to nine-thirty the way I have since watched for fevers to break, and I told myself everything sensible. He was on the road. Of course he was on the road; he had said so; you do not stop in Indiana to spend a dollar fifty-five telling a girl you are coming when you can save it for gasoline and be there saying nothing. By ten o'clock I had nearly believed it. The next Sunday I believed it less, because by then he should have been in Bakersfield three or four days, and a boy who had spent every Sunday of a year in a glass booth does not arrive at the very telephone he has been calling and let the hour go by without — without what? Without thinking of me? I caught myself there, and I am ashamed to write what I felt, which was jealousy's poor cousin: I had been left out of the ending. The serial had finished in an issue I would never see. They were sitting on a porch in California with their hands together and no further need of wires, and I told myself that was the happy ending and a happy ending is one where the operator hears nothing ever again, and I worked my board, and I went home, and I did not sleep well.

Then on the first Sunday of November, at five past nine exactly, a lamp lit on the inward toll trunk from the west, and a Bakersfield operator's warm-dust voice said she had a person-to-person call for Mr. Samuel Tully, and read me the number of the coin telephone outside Hartley's Rexall.

I rang it. You could ring a coin phone in those days; it had a bell like any other. I sat in the room above the dry goods store and rang a glass booth on a dark street four blocks away, and I have never in my life listened to anything the way I listened to that ringing. Six rings. Eight. On the tenth, a man answered, out of breath, an older man — Hartley closed Sundays, this was just a passerby the bell had hooked — and I said I had a call for Mr. Samuel Tully, and he said, honey, there's nobody here but me, this is the phone booth in front of the drugstore, and I said thank you and released him, and then there was nothing between Carol Ann Hodges and me but the Bakersfield operator, who said, in the flat ritual, your party does not answer, and went away off the line, and the girl said, "Operator? Operator, are you still there?"

I should have said this is the operator, may I help you, in the machine's voice. I said, "I'm here."

"He calls from that telephone," she said. "Every Sunday, this time, he calls me from that telephone. Are you — would you be the one that puts him through?"

The regulation is in the green binder and I could recite it asleep: information regarding the existence, content, or parties of any communication shall not be disclosed. I sat with my finger on the key and the whole green binder in my throat. "I can't discuss other calls," I said. And then, because her breathing had gone flat and careful, walking on its ice, I said, "He told you not to write because the letter would cross him on the road. Roads take longer than letters, sometimes. That's all I know." Which was a firing offense, every word of it, and Mrs. Pell ten feet behind me, and I would say it again.

"Thank you," she said, very quiet. "Will you — if he calls. I know you can't. But."

"I'm on Sundays," I said. "Four to midnight. I'm always on Sundays."

She tried twice more, the next two Sundays, person-to-person to a phone booth, paying for nothing because an uncompleted person-to-person call carried no charge — the company's one accidental mercy — and twice I rang the empty glass on Main Street and twice the Bakersfield operator said your party does not answer, and the second time Carol Ann did not ask to speak to me, and I understood that hope was being rationed now, that she could not afford to spend it on operators. In between, on a Tuesday on my own time, I did a thing I had never done in eleven years: I walked into Hartley's and bought a tin of aspirin I did not need and asked Earl Hartley, as idle as I could pitch it, whether the Tully boy had ever sold the home place, and Earl said sold it, settled up, packed that Fairlane to the gunwales, and pulled out — middle of October, it was, because he'd come in that last Saturday and bought a thermos and a road map of the western states and stood right there at the register grinning like Christmas. Going to California, Earl said. Girl out there. And I said yes, I'd heard something like that, and walked home with my aspirin past the booth, which stood there lit and empty the way it stood every night, a little glass chapel nobody prayed in anymore.

The call I had been dreading came the second Sunday of December, at five past nine, because she would not give up the hour even then; especially then. The Bakersfield operator said station-to-station this time, and gave the number of the booth, and then Carol Ann's voice came up the line past her, before I had even rung, "Operator — Calder — is that you, the Sunday one?" and I said yes, and the other operator, bless her, took her cord down and left us.

"They found him outside Tucumcari, New Mexico," she said. She said it like a lesson learned by heart, every word set down in order so none of them could move and hurt her unexpectedly. "The eighteenth of October. The car went off the road in the nighttime. The patrol out there didn't know who to tell, because his daddy was dead and there wasn't anybody, and it took them all this while, going through the county records, and they — there were letters in the glovebox. A whole year of my letters, done up with a rubber band. That's how they finally found me to tell me. They mailed them back. I got my own letters back in the mail." She stopped, and the ice held, barely. "He was three days from me. He drove too long because he wouldn't stop, because he told me — he told me —"

"He told you he'd be the one knocking," I said.

"You heard that."

"I collect the overtime," I said. "On a coin call I have to come on the line every three minutes. I heard pieces. I heard a year of pieces." And then I told her the regulation could go hang, and I told her the pieces: the way he breathed out the first night when she answered before the second ring. The two extra quarters in October. The boot against the glass, the folded paper, the back of his pale head on a hot Sunday noon, because I was the only living soul who had both of them, the voice and the glimpse, and she had a right to everything I held.

She was quiet a long while. Out west behind her I could hear a screen door and somebody's television. Then she said, "Will you do something for me? It isn't a proper call. There's nobody to answer it."

"Ask me."

"Ring it," she said. "The one he called from. Just ring it and let me hear."

So I reached the cord up and rang the booth outside Hartley's Rexall, four blocks away on Main Street in Calder, Ohio, at half past nine on a December Sunday with snow beginning, and I held my key open, and the two of us listened. It rings differently into a phone you know is empty; I swear it does; the bell goes out into the glass and the cold and comes back with the emptiness folded inside it. I let it ring a long time. No passerby came; the street was shut down for the night; it was only the bell, and her breathing, and mine, three women if you counted the machine, and at last she said, soft, the way you close a door in a sickroom, "All right." And I took the cord down, and the lamp went dark, and I said there would be no charge, an uncompleted call carries no charge, and she said thank you, Sunday, and was gone west down the wire for the last time.

They pulled the cord boards out of Calder in 1971 and cut us over to dial, and the company offered me Lima and I took my pension money instead, and the booth outside Hartley's came down sometime in the eighties; there is a planter there now. But for three more years I worked that middle position, and every connection I made I made knowing what it was: temporary. That is the work, when you come down to it. You hold two distances together with your own hands for as long as the talking lasts, and then you take the cords down, every one, every night, and the lamps go out one by one across the board, and you hang up your headset on its hook, and you walk home through the town full of houses, all of them wired to each other and all of them dark.
