The leak announced itself the way most things on the place announced themselves now, which was by ruin: a soft spot in the orchard road, a green flush of mallow and bindweed where nothing green had any business being in June, and beneath it, when Lawrence put his boot down, a sucking give like stepping on a sponge. He stood there a moment in the early light with the water rising silently around his sole, then walked back to the house and called his brother.

Davis arrived a little after nine in a truck so clean it looked rented, though Lawrence knew it wasn't, knew his brother washed it himself in his driveway in San Jose on Sunday mornings the way their father had washed the Ford on this gravel every Sunday of their childhood, a chrome of soap running down toward the ditch. Davis got out wearing jeans that had never seen dirt and a T-shirt from a 10K he had run in 2019, and he looked at the orchard the way he always looked at it now, which Lawrence had decided was the way you look at a relative in a hospital bed when you've already begun, privately, to compose what you'll say at the funeral.

"Where is it," Davis said.

"Down the middle road. Past the old Blenheims."

"They're all old Blenheims."

"Past the oldest ones, then."

They loaded the flatbed cart with what Lawrence had laid out in the barn at dawn: two lengths of four-inch PVC, a hacksaw, the blue can of primer and the gray can of cement, couplings, two shovels, a digging bar, the trenching spade with the handle their father had wrapped in friction tape sometime during the Carter administration, a cooler with water and the sandwiches Lawrence had made standing at the counter at six, listening to the well pump cycle on and off in a way it should not have been cycling. The pump was the thing that had told him, really, before the soft spot in the road did. A well pump talking to itself all night is a well pump trying to fill a hole in the ground.

They rode down on the cart behind the little Kubota, Davis sitting on the pipe to keep it from rolling, and the orchard went by on either side of them in its rows. The apricots were ten days from picking, maybe twelve, the fruit blushed on the south sides and still green-gold in the shade of the leaves, and even now, even this year, the smell of them was in the air the way it was every June, a smell Lawrence could not have described to anyone who hadn't grown up inside it — honey was wrong, and so was sugar, it was closer to the smell of sun itself, if sun had a smell, sun and something faintly fermented underneath, the windfalls of last week already cooking into the ground.

"Trees look stressed," Davis said.

"They're fine."

"The leaves are cupping. Third row, look at it."

"They're fine," Lawrence said, though they weren't, though he had walked the third row Tuesday and felt the leaves between his fingers like paper. The water table had dropped eleven feet in four years. The well that their grandfather had driven in 1946 to a hundred and ten feet, and that their father had deepened to two-forty in the drought of '77, now pulled from somewhere down around three hundred, and what it pulled came up harder every year, like the ground itself had grown reluctant, like the ground was reconsidering.

The soft spot had spread since dawn. Water stood in the wheel ruts now, skinned with dust, and a killdeer ran along the edge of it crying its own name. Lawrence shut the tractor off and in the silence they could both hear it: a faint subterranean hiss, the sound of money leaving.

"Shut the pump off?" Davis asked.

"Shut it off at five this morning. That's residual. Line's still draining back."

They stood looking at the wet ground the way men stand looking at wet ground, which is to say longer than the looking required, both of them postponing the shovel. Forty feet away the Blenheims went on doing what they did, holding their fruit up into the light. These were the trees their grandfather had planted, sixty-some years old now, twice the working life anyone gave an apricot, their trunks black and ropey and split, half of them held together at the crotch with bolts their father had run through them in the eighties, threaded rod and washers the size of saucers, the bark grown over the hardware in places so that the trees seemed to have swallowed it, taken the iron into themselves like a grievance.

"Six hundred a tree," Davis said.

"What?"

"That's what a guy told me a heritage Blenheim's worth, if you could move it. Which you can't. They die if you look at them sideways. I'm just saying I looked into it."

"You looked into selling the trees."

"I looked into everything. That's what you do, Lawrence. You look into things." Davis picked up a shovel. "Where do you want to start?"

They started ten feet up-slope of the wet, because the water travels and shows itself downhill of the wound, everyone knew that, their father had taught them that with this same trenching spade in this same dirt, and they dug. The first foot was easy, June-dry and powdery, and then they were into the moist layer and the digging got heavy, the clay coming up in slabs that gleamed where the shovel had sheared them. Lawrence worked without talking. Davis talked.

He talked the way he had talked since he was nine years old, in paragraphs, with topic sentences. He talked about the offer. The offer was four point two million dollars from an entity called Corten Land Partners for the whole forty acres, the house, the barn, the well and its rights such as they were, and Lawrence had the letter in the kitchen drawer under the rubber bands, where it sat like a coal he kept checking to see if it had gone out. Davis talked about per-acre comps in the county, about what the parcel up at Renner's had gone for, fallow ground, no improvements, and how Renner's daughter had moved to Bend, Oregon, and bought a house with a view of three mountains.

"You don't even like mountains," Lawrence said.

"It's not about mountains."

"You said in 1988 that mountains made you feel watched."

"I was fourteen. I'm making a point about Renner's daughter."

"Renner's daughter never grafted a tree in her life. Renner's daughter came out twice a year and complained the house smelled like dog."

"And now she has no debt and a view of three mountains and her father's land is somebody else's problem. That's the point I'm making."

The trench was knee-deep now and Lawrence's shovel rang on something. They both knelt. He cleared with his hands, the wet clay cold around his fingers, and there it was: the mainline, the old gray PVC their father had laid in 1981 when he converted from flood to drip, four hundred feet of it from the pump house to the manifold, and along the top of the pipe ran a crack like a hair, like a hairline fracture in a bone, weeping. But weeping wasn't the sound they'd heard. The hiss was farther on. This was just a sympathy crack, a secondary failure, the pipe gone brittle from forty years of pressure cycles and the slow chemistry of sunless time.

"It's not even the break," Lawrence said. "It's failing everywhere. It's failing in places we haven't found yet."

"That's what I keep saying," Davis said, and he said it gently, which was worse.

They moved down-slope and dug again, six feet of trench this time, and found the true break a foot deeper than the line had any right to run, where the original trench must have wandered, where their father, twenty-nine years old and working alone after dark, had dug deep through a stubborn place rather than around it. The pipe there had split full open, a four-inch wound, and the cavity around it had been hollowed by the escaping water into a smooth-walled room the size of a bushel basket, a small cave the pressure had carved, and in the bottom of it lay a gopher, drowned, neat as a glove.

"There's your culprit," Davis said. "Or your victim. Hard to say."

Lawrence lifted the animal out on the blade of the shovel and laid it on the spoil pile. Its fur was slicked into points. "Probably tunneled along the pipe for the cool," he said. "Undermined it. Pipe sagged into the void and snapped."

"Or the pipe broke first and drowned him in his bed."

"Either way."

"Either way," Davis agreed, and for a moment they were just two brothers contemplating a dead gopher, which was the closest they had come to peace all morning.

They cut the line. The hacksaw went through the old PVC with a dry screech and the cut ends showed the material gone yellowish and crystalline inside, like hard candy, like something you could snap in your hands. Lawrence measured, cut the new pipe, dry-fit the couplings. Then they had to wait — the trench bottom was wet where the line had drained, and you could not cement onto a wet line, so they squared the trench walls and bailed the standing water with a coffee can and waited for the cut ends to dry in the air, and the day came on around them. It was eleven now and the heat had its hand flat on the back of Lawrence's neck. The orchard ticked and rustled. Far off, a labor contractor's bus went by on the county road, headed for the strawberries that had replaced the Olsens' prunes, and beyond that, faint, the eternal sound of the valley now, which was construction, the beep of something heavy backing up.

They ate the sandwiches sitting on the edge of the trench with their boots hanging in it like boys on a dock.

"I want to ask you something," Davis said, "and I want you to actually answer it, not do the thing."

"What thing."

"The thing where you answer a different question that's easier."

Lawrence chewed. The sandwich was ham and the mustard was the coarse kind their mother had bought, the jar still going two years after her, because how often does one man use mustard.

"Go ahead."

"What did the orchard clear last year? Net. Actual."

Lawrence looked down the trench. The water in the bottom had a skin of clay on it the color of milky coffee. "It lost eleven thousand dollars," he said. "After the well work, it lost twenty-six."

Davis didn't say anything, which was a courtesy, and Lawrence understood it as a courtesy, and resented it.

"The fruit's worth more than ever," Lawrence said. "That's what kills me. Blenheims — the dried market, the fancy market, those people in the city, your people, they pay nine dollars a pound for what we used to sell to the cannery for nothing. The fruit was never worth more. It's everything around the fruit that's worth less. The water, the labor, the trucking. I can grow the best apricot in the world and lose money on every one. Explain that to me. You're the one with the spreadsheets."

"I can explain it to you exactly," Davis said. "You're forty acres in a valley where land is worth more as anything other than what it is. The dirt under a Blenheim is worth a hundred times the Blenheim. The world has voted, Lawrence. It votes every day. It's been voting since we were kids."

"The world voted for a lot of things I don't have to go along with."

"You're sixty-one years old."

"I know how old I am."

"You're sixty-one, your knees are gone, you can't hire a crew you can afford, and you're down there in that trench like Dad, exactly like Dad, and you know how that ends, you were there, I was there, we both watched how that ends."

Their father had died in the orchard, which everyone said as if it were a mercy, and maybe it was, but Lawrence had been the one to find him, between the fourth and fifth rows in late July with the props still in the trees, and what Lawrence remembered was not mercy. What he remembered was that the irrigation had been running and had gone on running, that the drip emitters had gone on metering out their gallons-per-hour with perfect indifference, and that the ground where his father lay was dark with water in a spreading circle, the system doing its work around the dead man who had built it, and Lawrence had had to step over him to get to the valve, because that was the order of operations, that was what his father himself would have done — shut the water off first. You always shut the water off first.

"I'm not Dad," Lawrence said.

"No. Dad had Mom doing the books and prices that meant something and a water table at a hundred feet. You have less than Dad had and you're holding harder."

The cut ends of the pipe had dried. Lawrence got down into the trench because the conversation had reached the place it always reached, the place where Davis was right about every number and Lawrence had nothing left but the work itself, and so he made the work the answer. He wiped the pipe ends with the rag. He swabbed the purple primer on, the smell of it sharp and chemical and somehow festive, the smell of every repair of his life, and then the cement, and he shoved the coupling home with a quarter-turn the way you were supposed to, the way their father's hands had taught his hands before his hands knew they were learning, and held it to a slow ten-count while the solvent welded plastic to plastic, two things becoming one thing or failing to.

"Hold the other end up," he said, and Davis got down into the trench too, in his clean jeans, without complaint, and held the old line steady while Lawrence primed and glued the second joint, and for a few minutes there was nothing in the world but the work, the two of them shoulder to shoulder in a grave-shaped hole in their grandfather's ground, doing a thing competently together, and Lawrence felt the old feeling come up in him unbidden, the feeling from when they were boys and the work had been the language, before Davis had gotten good at the other language, the one made of numbers and futures, the one their father had never learned and had therefore mistrusted, and had therefore, in his way, mistrusted Davis.

"He never blamed you for leaving," Lawrence said. The joint was setting under his hands. He hadn't planned to say it.

Davis was quiet for a second. "He never said anything about it at all. Which I notice you've inherited, the not-saying. You people and your monk vows."

"You people."

"You orchard people. You sit on everything like it's fruit you're afraid to bruise." Davis shifted his grip on the pipe. "He blamed me. Don't insult us both. I came home for Christmas in '94 with the job offer and he shook my hand at the door like a customer."

"He shook everyone's hand like a customer."

"He shook yours like a son."

Lawrence counted his ten and let go of the joint and sat back on his heels in the trench. Above them the rim of the dug earth and above that the apricot leaves and above that the sky, hazed white at the edges with heat. From down here the orchard had no horizon. From down here it was the whole world, which, Lawrence thought, was exactly the problem with it, or exactly the truth of it, depending on which brother you asked.

"It has to cure an hour before we pressurize," he said. "More, in this heat. Heat shortens it, actually. Forty-five minutes."

They got out of the trench and Davis walked the row while they waited, and Lawrence watched him do it. He watched his brother stop at the tree with the bolt through its crotch, the big one, the one they had called the Saddle Tree because as kids they had ridden it, one boy on each scaffold limb, urging it into battle, and he watched Davis put his hand flat on the bark over the place where the iron went in. He stood like that for a while. Then he came back and sat down on the spoil pile beside the dead gopher and said, "I'm not the villain here. You've cast me as the villain and I want to say one time, on the record, that I'm not. I lie awake too. You think I want a Corten Land Partners. You think I want forty townhouses called The Apricots at Harlan Creek with a fake water feature where the well was. I grew up here the same as you."

"Not the same as me."

"No," Davis said. "Not the same. You're right. You got it and I got out, and we've both spent thirty years pretending we chose." He picked up a clod and crumbled it. "But here's what I know that you won't say. It's failing everywhere, Lawrence. You said it yourself, an hour ago, in this hole. Not just the pipe. The whole — " he moved his hand at the rows, the valley, the white sky — "the whole system it was plumbed into. The cannery's gone. The dryer's gone. The neighbors are gone. Dad's system worked because there was a world around it, and the world around it is gone, and you're down here gluing couplings onto 1981 like the world's coming back. And every year you don't sell, you don't just lose the eleven thousand or the twenty-six. You lose a year. Of you. And you're the only brother I have."

Lawrence looked at the joint curing in the trench. The purple primer had stained his fingers, would stain them for days; he would see it on his hands at the sink tonight and tomorrow at the coffee. He thought about the letter in the drawer under the rubber bands. He thought about Renner's daughter and her three mountains. He thought about nine-dollar apricots and the people who bought them, who spoke of the Blenheim the way people speak of a language with four living speakers, with that tender museum voice, and how he hated the voice and depended on the people, and how that was the shape of everything now, hating the voice and depending on the people.

"When the fruit comes off," he said. "Twelve days. I'll read the thing properly when the fruit comes off. I'll sit down with you and your spreadsheets and I won't do the thing. That's what I can say today. I'm not saying yes in a trench."

Davis looked at him for a long moment, and Lawrence watched him decide not to push it, watched him fold the victory or half-victory or whatever it was away into himself the way he folded everything, neatly, for later.

"Okay," Davis said. "Twelve days."

When the cement had cured they backfilled the trench halfway, leaving the joints exposed the way you did, and rode the cart back up to the pump house, and Lawrence threw the breaker and the pump caught with its deep electrical groan, pulling from three hundred feet, and they drove back down and stood over the open trench and watched. The line knocked and shuddered as the air worked out of it. Water spat from the flush valve at the row's end, brown, then clearer, then clear. The new joints held. The old pipe on either side of them held too, for now, gray and brittle and forty-three years old, holding the way everything on the place held, provisionally, out of habit, because no one had told it yet that it could stop.

Down the rows the emitters came alive one by one, each tree receiving its measured water, the dark circles blooming on the dry ground under the Blenheims exactly as they had bloomed around their father, and Lawrence stood watching the system run, the system that was the pipe and the pump and the well and also somehow the two of them, the last two fittings in it, solvent-welded a long time ago by hands that were gone.

"Look at that," Davis said quietly. "Still works."

"For now," Lawrence said, and heard, too late, that he had made his brother's argument for him, and saw Davis hear it too, and have the grace not to say so.

They filled the rest of the trench in together as the light went long, the shovels falling into an old shared rhythm, throw and throw, and the clay went back into the ground it had come out of, covering the new pipe and the old, and the dead gopher with it, and when they were done there was only a darker stripe of turned earth in the orchard road, which the dust would take by August, whoever owned it by then. The apricots hung in the last sun, ten days out, twelve, each one turning its slow blind face toward ripeness, asking nothing, knowing nothing, worth more than ever.
