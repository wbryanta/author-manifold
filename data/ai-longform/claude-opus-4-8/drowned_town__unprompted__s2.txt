The water had pulled back from the valley like a sheet drawn off a body, and what it left was gray. Not the green of the hills above the waterline, not the brown of the mud flats fishermen complained about in the lean years, but a uniform gray that had no name Della could find. It was the color of things that had been underwater for sixty years. She stood at the top of what had been Marsh Road with her tripod over her shoulder and her data collector in its canvas bag against her hip, and she looked down at the town where she had been born and could not, for a long moment, make herself walk into it.

The county wanted the streets mapped. That was the job. The drought had dropped Coldwater Reservoir to a level no living engineer had seen, and the old town of Hale was up out of the water for the first time since the Army Corps had flooded the valley in 1961. There were questions about easements, about the original survey, about whether the foundations posed a hazard to the boats that still went out on the diminished lake. There was, beneath all of it, a kind of municipal nervousness, as if the drowned town were a debt come due. They had sent Della because she was the county surveyor and because, though no one said this part, she was the only one who had walked these streets when they were dry.

She had been seven the year they left. She was sixty-seven now. The arithmetic of it stood in her mind, plain and unbearable.

She went down.

The mud had crusted on top but stayed soft underneath, and her boots broke through with each step, releasing a smell she had not smelled in six decades and recognized instantly, the way you recognize a voice on the phone before the speaker says their name. It was the smell of the lake bottom, of rot and minerals and something cold and green that lived where the light didn't reach. It was the smell that had been under everything in her childhood, the lake that wasn't a lake yet, the river slowing as the dam went up, the basements that flooded each spring.

The foundations came up out of the mud like teeth. That was the comparison her mind reached for and would not release. Low rectangles of fieldstone and poured concrete, the houses gone entirely, the walls and roofs and doors and all the wood of them long since floated away or rotted to silt, but the foundations stayed, because men had built them to stay. She set up the tripod at the first intersection and shot the corners and let her hands do the work while her eyes went where they wanted.

Here was the Pruitt place. She knew it the way she knew her own hand, though there was no sign, no number, nothing but a foundation like all the others. She knew it by the shape of the lot, by the way the road bent just past it, by some sense in her body older than memory. The Pruitts had kept geese. The geese had chased her once, all the way to her own door, and her father had laughed and her mother had not. The foundation held a skin of water in its lowest corner, a black pool that had not drained, and in the pool she could see the gray sky.

Della wrote the coordinates. The data collector chimed. She moved on.

She had told them at the county office that she remembered the town, and they had taken this as an asset, a thing that would make the survey go faster, and she had let them think so because it was easier than the truth, which was that she did not know whether she remembered the town or remembered only the photographs and the stories, the few things her mother had carried out and set on shelves in the new house in the dry hills. There was a photograph of the church. There was a photograph of the main street with the awnings out. There was her mother's voice, going on through the years, naming things. The hardware store. The Methodist church and the other church, the one for the people who took it more seriously. The school with the bell. Della had carried these names so long that she no longer knew if the things behind them were hers or borrowed.

But the smell was hers. The smell she had not been told about. No one describes a smell in a story; you cannot carry it out on a shelf. The smell rose up around her boots and told her that some part of this was true, was hers, had happened to the body that was now sixty-seven years old and walking carefully over broken ground with a tripod on its shoulder.

She worked through the morning. The sun, which had caused all of this, the long rainless months, the reservoir falling foot by foot through the spring, hung white and indifferent over the valley. By noon she had mapped the upper end of Marsh Road and the three short streets that ran off it, and she sat on a foundation wall to eat the sandwich she had brought and drink from her thermos, and she looked at the town spread out below her, all of it the same gray, the streets visible now only as the absences between foundations, the negative spaces where people had once walked.

There was a man on the lake. She could see him out past the edge of the exposed ground, where the water still held, a small aluminum boat and a figure in it, not fishing, just sitting, looking at the shore. She had seen others on her drive in. The drought had become a kind of attraction. People came to look at the bones of Hale the way they would come to look at any disaster, slowly, with a guilty appetite. The local paper had run photographs. There had been a man on the radio, an old man, who had wept describing the house he was born in, and the host had let him weep and Della had turned the radio off.

She did not want to weep. She wanted to do the survey. She finished the sandwich and folded the wax paper and put it back in her bag, because even here, even on the floor of a drowned town, she did not litter, and she stood and shouldered the tripod and went down toward the center of things.

The main street was wider than she expected. In her mind it had been narrow, a child's street, a street you could cross in three steps to reach the candy in the window. But it was a real street, a generous street, and she walked down the middle of it where the water still pooled in the wheel ruts that had not been smoothed away in sixty years under the lake, and she found that she was counting. Counting the foundations. Counting the lots. The hardware store would be on the left, her mother's voice said, the big one with the double front. And there it was, a foundation twice the width of the others, and Della stood in front of it and the survey went out of her mind entirely.

She had been inside that store. This was not the church in the photograph or the main street with the awnings. This was a thing that had happened to her, that came up now out of the gray without being summoned: the cool dark of the store on a hot day, the smell of oil and metal and twine, the bins of nails sorted by size, her father lifting her up to see into a barrel of something, seed or grain, and the man behind the counter giving her a peppermint from a jar. She could taste it. Sixty years and she could taste the peppermint, and she stood in the mud where the store had been and she put her hand out as if to touch a counter that was not there, that had been gone since before the men who flooded the valley were themselves old men.

The data collector chimed in its bag, a question it asked when it had been idle too long, and she ignored it.

Her father had died in the new house in the dry hills. Her mother had died there too, years later, in the room that looked out over country that had never been underwater, scrub oak and red dirt, a hard bright land that her mother had hated quietly for thirty years without ever once saying so, because to say so would have been to say the other thing, the thing none of them said, which was that the valley had been taken from them and paid for at a price the Corps had set and they had not been allowed to refuse. Her mother had kept the photographs and named the streets and never said she wanted to go back, because there was nothing to go back to. The town was under two hundred feet of water. You could not be homesick for a place that no longer existed in the dimension where homesickness could reach it. So she had named the streets instead, and Della had listened, and now Della stood on one of them with the names rising around her like water.

She made herself set up the tripod.

She shot the corners of the hardware store. She wrote the coordinates. She gave the building a number in the county's system, a string of digits that would attach to it forever now, that would fix it in the record so that when the water came back, as it would, as the rains would surely come and the reservoir fill and the town drown again, there would be this: a set of numbers that said the hardware store had been here, at these coordinates, to this precision. It was not much. It was what she could do. She was a surveyor. She turned the world into numbers so that it could be argued about and owned and remembered, and she had spent her life doing it without ever once thinking that it was a kind of mourning, and now she thought it, standing in the mud, and the thought did not lift her or comfort her but it sat with her, a companion, while she worked.

The afternoon went long. She mapped the main street to its end, where it had run down to the river before the river became the lake, and she mapped the cross streets, and she came at last to the church.

She knew it from the photograph. It was strange to come to a thing she knew from a photograph after spending the day among things she knew, or did not know, from her own buried memory. The church foundation was larger than the others, with the long rectangle of the nave and the square at the front where the tower had stood, and there were steps, actual stone steps, three of them, leading up to where the doors had been, and the steps had survived. She had not expected the steps. She stood at the bottom of them and looked up at the doorway that opened onto nothing, onto gray sky, and she thought of her mother in the new house naming this church, the Methodist church, where Della had been baptized, where her parents had been married, where her grandparents were—

She turned and looked toward the rising ground at the eastern edge of the valley.

The cemetery would be up there. They had moved the cemetery before they flooded the valley; that was one of the things the Corps had done, one of the few decent things, they had disinterred the dead of Hale and carried them up to high ground and reburied them in rows in a new cemetery that had no town attached to it, a cemetery that sat alone on a hill above the water with a chain-link fence around it and a county sign. Della had been there. Her grandparents were there, moved from the ground here to the ground there, their bones carried up out of the valley in 1960 while they were still bones and not yet only names. But the church where they had been mourned was here. The ground where they had first been laid was here, under her boots, somewhere in the gray, an empty grave now, a hole that had been dug and filled and dug again and left, and then drowned.

She climbed the three steps.

She should not have. There was nothing up there, no floor, no altar, nothing but the same mud at the same level, the foundation walls rising to her knees on either side. But she climbed the steps because they were there to be climbed, because someone had built them to be climbed and no one had climbed them in sixty years, and she stood in the doorway of the church and looked down the length of the nave, the long gray rectangle, and she tried to put it back. The pews. The windows. The light through the windows, which would have been colored, surely, even a small country church would have had some colored glass, and the light would have come through it in the morning and laid itself down across the floor in patches of red and blue, and the people would have sat in the patches of light and the dust would have turned in the colored air, and she had been there, she had been a child in that light, and she could not remember it. She reached for it and it was not there. The peppermint was there and the geese were there and the smell was there but the church was a photograph, was her mother's voice, was a foundation in the mud, and the actual morning light through the actual colored glass had gone where the church had gone, down under the water, and the water had taken it the way water takes everything, slowly and completely and without malice.

She sat down on the foundation wall.

She had not meant to. Her legs simply stopped holding her in the standing position and she sat, in the doorway of the drowned church, with the tripod across her lap, and she looked at the gray and she let herself feel the thing she had driven down into the valley promising herself she would not feel, which was not grief exactly, was not sorrow for the town or for her parents or for the drowned dead, though it was made of all those things, but was something larger and emptier, a grief for the fact of it, for the way the water came up over things and they were gone, the way the years came up over things and they were gone, the way she herself was a foundation now, a set of walls in the mud, with the wood and the roof and the colored light of her long since floated away, with only the structure left, the bare load-bearing facts of a life, my name is Della and I was born in Hale and I am the county surveyor and my parents are dead and the town is gone and I am sixty-seven years old.

The man in the boat had come closer. She saw him when she looked up, his aluminum boat nosed up against the edge of the mud at the bottom of the church lot, fifty yards off, and he was watching her. An old man, older than she was, in a cap. He raised one hand, not quite a wave, more an acknowledgment, the gesture of one person seeing another in a place where they had not expected to see anyone.

She raised her hand back.

He cupped his hands around his mouth. "You from the county?" His voice came across the mud thin and clear.

"Yes," she called.

"They sending you to map it?"

"Yes."

He nodded slowly, as if this confirmed something he had long suspected about the county and its intentions. He did not come closer and he did not go away. He sat in his boat at the edge of the drowned town and looked at her on the steps of the drowned church, and after a while he called out again.

"I was born here."

She did not answer right away. The valley was full of people who had been born here, or whose parents had been, the radio man weeping, the slow guilty traffic on the access road, the whole county leaning down to look at its own buried past. She might have let it pass. She might have said something kind and gone back to her tripod. But she was tired, and she was sitting in the church where she had been baptized, and the smell of the lake bottom was in her clothes now and would not wash out for days, and so she called back the thing that was true.

"So was I."

He went still. Even at fifty yards she could see him go still. Then he worked the oars and brought the boat in until its bow ground up into the mud, and he climbed out, stiffly, an old man's careful climb, and he stood at the bottom of the church lot looking up at her.

"What's your name?"

"Della Voss. Della Pruitt, I was. Pruitt was my—"

"The Pruitts up on Marsh Road. With the geese." He said it flatly, not a question.

The bottom went out of her. "Yes," she said. "With the geese."

He came up the lot toward her, slowly, picking his way over the broken ground, and she watched him come and did not know if she wanted him to reach her or not. He stopped a few feet off and looked at her, an old man's frank long look, reading her face for a child's face the way she had been reading the foundations for the shapes of houses.

"I'm Walter Henning," he said. "We were on Marsh Road too. Three down from you, toward the river. My mother used to send me up to your place for eggs."

"We didn't keep chickens," Della said. "The Pruitts kept geese."

"Goose eggs, then." He almost smiled. "I don't know. I was small. I was sent for eggs and I came back with eggs. I remember the geese. They were—" He lifted a hand to his own height, to a child's height, the geese as tall as the boy. "They were bigger than me."

"They chased me once," Della said. "All the way home."

"They'd do that." Now he did smile, and the smile took years off him and put them back. "They were mean as anything, those geese. My father said your father kept them instead of a dog."

She laughed. It came out of her unexpectedly, a sound she had not made all day, and it surprised them both, a laugh in the doorway of the drowned church, and Walter Henning looked at her laughing and his eyes were wet, and she understood that he had come up out of the boat and across the mud not for the town and not for the church but for this, for someone who had been here, who was here, who was the only other person in the gray valley who remembered the geese.

"I came down to look," he said, after a while. "I knew I shouldn't. My daughter told me not to. She said it would only—" He stopped. "But I had to come and look. Sixty years I've been thinking about it under there. You think a thing is gone and then the water goes down and there it is, and you find out you never stopped—" He stopped again. He looked down the gray length of the nave the way she had looked down it. "I don't know what I thought I'd find."

"This," Della said. "This is what's here."

"Foundations."

"Foundations." She stood, finally, the tripod in her hand. "The wood's all gone. The houses are gone. But they built the foundations to last and the foundations lasted." She heard her own voice doing the thing it did, turning the unbearable into the technical, and she let it, because it was the only way she had ever found to hold a thing like this in her hands. "I'm putting them all in the record. Every one. Coordinates and dimensions. So when the water comes back—and it will come back, this is a drought, the rain comes back—when the water comes back over all this, it'll be in the record. Where everything was."

Walter Henning looked at her. "Why does that matter," he said. It was not a challenge. He wanted to know. He stood in the mud and wanted, genuinely, to know why it mattered, whether it mattered, whether the thing she was doing was a comfort or only a different kind of forgetting.

She thought about it. The sun was going down the western rim of the valley now and the gray was warming, just slightly, the only warmth it would take all day, a thin gold coming into it from the low light.

"Because somebody walked here," she said. "Because there were geese here, and a hardware store with a peppermint jar, and your mother sent you up the road for eggs. And the water took it and it'll take it again. But it happened. It was here. And if it's in the record then it's a fact, it's a thing that can't be argued out of having existed, it's—" She looked at the steps under her feet, the three stone steps that someone had built and that had outlasted everything else, every soft thing, every living thing, the wood and the glass and the people. "It's the steps," she said. "It's building the steps to last."

Walter Henning nodded slowly. He looked like he understood, or like he was deciding to.

"Map my mother's house," he said. "Three down from yours. Toward the river. Will it have a number?"

"It'll have a number."

"Will you tell me the number? After. So I'll have it."

"I'll tell you the number," Della said.

She set up the tripod on the steps of the church, in the last of the gold light, and Walter Henning stood and watched her work, the two of them the only living things in the drowned town, and she shot the corners and wrote the coordinates and gave the church its number, and then she packed the tripod and they walked up Marsh Road together in the dusk, slowly, an old man and an old woman, to find the house where his mother had sent him for eggs, before the water came back over all of it and took it down again into the gray.
