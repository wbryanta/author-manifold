The ferry left at nine, an hour after the last light had gone out of the water, and Halvard stood on the open deck longer than the cold could justify because the cabin smelled of diesel and the bread other passengers had brought, and because he did not yet want to be a man inside a small room with his own thoughts. The strait was thirty kilometers across at this point, narrow enough that on a clear day you could see the far shore from the near one, but tonight there was no far shore, only the place where the lights of the town behind him stopped reaching and the dark began in earnest. He had crossed this water before. As a boy he had crossed it many times, with his mother, with his brother, the two of them pressing their faces to the salt-fogged glass and competing to be the first to name the lighthouse at Grimsby Point, which was not its real name but the name they had given it, after a town in a book neither of them had read past the first chapter.

The body was in a town called Vargøy, which meant Wolf Island though there had not been wolves there in two hundred years, and a woman from the district police had called him on Tuesday. She had been gentle in the way of people whose gentleness is a professional skill, and she had said that a man had been found, that there were reasons to think it might be his brother Anders, that there were no documents, that the face was not such that a photograph would help. He had understood what she meant by this last thing and had not asked her to be more specific. He had said he would come. He had not asked why him, why not their mother, and the woman had not volunteered it, and only later, on the train to the coast, had he remembered that their mother was four years dead and that he was, in the bookkeeping of the family, all that remained on Anders's side of the ledger.

He went into the cabin at last and found his bunk, an upper one in a room of six, the others already occupied by sleepers or by the shapes of sleepers, a man's boots set neatly beneath the lowest berth, a paperback splayed face-down on a folded coat. He lay in his clothes with his shoes still on and listened to the engine come up through the steel, and he thought, the way a man does when he has not slept and does not expect to, about everything at once.

The last time he had seen Anders had been at the funeral, which was the only funeral, their mother's, eleven years ago now or perhaps it was twelve, the arithmetic had grown slippery. Anders had come late and left early, in a borrowed-looking suit, and the two of them had stood at the graveside on opposite sides of the open ground like men staking out a territory. There had been a thing said. Halvard had told himself for years that he could not remember what the thing was, and this was a lie he had maintained the way you maintain a fence on land you no longer farm, out of habit and a vague sense that the boundary still mattered. He remembered it perfectly. Anders had said, you were always her favorite and you didn't even want it, and Halvard had said something about money, about a loan that had not been repaid, which had been true and small and had nothing to do with anything, and Anders had looked at him with an expression Halvard had spent a great part of the following decade trying not to see again, an expression of such complete and weary recognition, as if Halvard had at last confirmed something Anders had always suspected and had hoped, against the evidence, to be wrong about. Then Anders had gone, and there had been no address, and twice a year for a while a card had come without a return, postmarked from places that moved north over the years, and then the cards had stopped, and Halvard had not gone looking, because to go looking would have been to admit that the silence cost him something, and he had not been prepared, in those years, to admit it.

He was prepared now, lying in the dark, and he found that the admission cost him nothing, which frightened him, because it meant the price had already been paid in full somewhere along the way without his noticing, the way you discover a wound has stopped hurting and realize it is because the nerve is dead.

He must have slept, because he came back from somewhere when a man in the lower bunk across from him began to snore, a great industrial sawing, and Halvard lay and hated him with a pure simple hatred that was a relief after the other things. Then the hatred passed too and he felt only a kind of tenderness for the snoring man, who was sleeping so well, who was crossing the water for some reason that was surely his own and surely large to him, a wedding or a job or a death, everyone on a night ferry crossing toward something. He climbed down and went out again into the corridor and up to the deck.

The cold was worse now and cleaner. They were in the middle of the strait, both shores gone, and the sea moved under the boat with the long indifferent swells of deep water. He gripped the rail. He thought about the word identify, which the woman had used. He was being brought across the water to perform an identification, to look at a face that was not such that a photograph would help and to say yes or no, to say that is my brother or that is not my brother, and he understood for the first time, fully, standing in the wind, that he did not know whether he could. Not whether he could bear it. Whether he could do it. He had not seen Anders in eleven years or twelve. A man's face changes in that time, and a dead man's face changes more, and a dead man's face that has been some while in finding has changed past the help of photographs. What did he remember of Anders's face? He tried to summon it and got instead a composite, a brother-shaped blur, the cowlick that Anders had hated and flattened with water every morning, the small white scar through one eyebrow from a bicycle, the way the left front tooth had been turned slightly, just slightly, so that you only saw it when he laughed. The scar would still be there, scars outlast everything. But a scar was not a man. Halvard had a terror, suddenly, that he would be shown a face and would feel nothing, no flicker of recognition, and would have to decide based on a scar and a turned tooth whether to claim a stranger as his blood, and he saw himself standing in some cold tiled room saying yes, yes, that is Anders, out of a desire to give the official woman the answer she wanted, out of an inability to stand there saying I don't know, I don't know, he was my brother and I don't know.

Or worse, the other thing. That he would look and would know with total certainty that it was not Anders, that some other lost man had washed up wearing the wrong name, and that he would feel, beneath the decent relief, a small dropping disappointment, because the alternative to that body being his brother was not that his brother was alive. The alternative was that his brother was still out there in the silence, unaccounted, and that Halvard would have to go on not knowing, would have to climb back onto this same ferry and cross back over this same water carrying the not-knowing home with him like a parcel he had failed to deliver.

A woman came out onto the deck and lit a cigarette in the lee of the funnel housing, cupping the flame, and the small orange point of it was companionable in the dark. She did not look at him and he was grateful. They stood, the two of them, ten meters apart, two people awake on a sleeping ship, and after a while she finished and went in and he was alone again with the engine and the swell.

He thought about the boy he and Anders had been. There had been a summer, the summer Halvard was nine and Anders eleven, when their father was still alive but mostly absent in the way that would shortly become permanent, and the two of them had been sent to their grandmother's house on the coast, not far from here in fact, a few headlands south. There had been a boat, a small clinker-built thing, and they had not been permitted to take it out alone, and they had taken it out alone every day. Anders rowed. Halvard sat in the stern and trailed his hand and gave orders that Anders ignored, and they went out past the skerries to where the water turned from green to a blue that was almost black, and they fished for nothing in particular and caught nothing in particular and came back burned and salt-stiff and savagely hungry, and it was the happiest Halvard had ever been, before or since, and he had not known it at the time, and he wondered if Anders had known it, if Anders, two years older and already carrying whatever it was he carried, had sat there rowing his idiot little brother around the skerries and thought, this is it, this is the good part, remember this. He hoped Anders had known it. He hoped one of them had.

Where had it gone, the thing between them. It had not gone in a single place. That was the trouble with trying to find it, you went back along the line looking for the break and there was no break, there was only a slow fraying, a hundred small withdrawals on both sides, the years when Anders was getting into the troubles that Halvard, the steady one, the favorite, was held up against as a contrast, until the contrast became the whole of their relation, until they could not stand in a room together without each of them being mostly a thing the other was being measured against. Their mother had done some of that, God rest her, she had needed one good son and one bad one the way some people need a clean side of the bed, and she had cast them early and they had played the parts. But Halvard had let himself be cast. That was the thing he could see now, in the dark, that he had not been able to see in the lit rooms of his life. He had liked being the good one. It had cost him almost nothing and it had cost Anders almost everything, and Halvard had taken the dividend year after year without once examining where the money came from.

The sky to the east, when he noticed it, had begun to gray, not toward sunrise yet, only toward the long northern dawn that would take hours to arrive. The shape of the sea became visible, the texture of it, gunmetal and moving. He could make out, far ahead and low, a darker line that was land. Vargøy, or the approaches to it. He found that his hands had gone numb on the rail and he put them in his pockets and discovered there the folded paper on which he had written the name of the hotel and the name of the woman from the police, Lindqvist, and the time of the appointment, ten o'clock, at a place whose name he had also written down and which he now could not read in the half-dark, the ink having run where the spray had got to it.

He went down and drank a coffee from a machine in the empty cafeteria and watched the land come up. By the time they docked the dawn had arrived in its gray completeness and Vargøy was revealed to be a small place, a working harbor, fish sheds and a fuel depot and a steep little town climbing the hill behind, lights on in the windows because it was that kind of latitude and that kind of morning, the people inside drinking their own coffee and putting on their own coats to go to their own ordinary days. He was the seventh or eighth person down the ramp. The snoring man was somewhere behind him, restored to dignity by daylight, a big mild-faced fellow with a suitcase. Halvard wanted to thank him for something and could not have said what.

He had three hours. He walked up into the town and found the hotel and left his bag and could not sit in the room, so he went out again and walked the harbor, and the morning sharpened around him, gulls and the clatter of a winch and the smell of fish and salt and tar that was the smell of his grandmother's coast, of the summer with the boat, so that he had to stop and lean on a bollard. A man mending a net looked up at him and looked away, incurious, a stranger off the ferry being moved by something or hungover, all the same to him.

At a quarter to ten he asked directions of the woman in the hotel and walked to the place, which was not a hospital but a low building beside the small clinic, and Lindqvist met him at the door. She was younger than her voice had been, or the telephone had aged her, and she shook his hand and held it a half-second longer than the handshake required, which he understood to be a kindness and received as one. She took him into a small room with two chairs and offered coffee, which he declined, and then she told him, in a low clear voice, the circumstances. The man had been found by a fisherman, caught in gear off the north of the island, eleven days ago. He had been in the water some time, she said, and she let that phrase do its work without elaborating it. There were no documents. There had been, in a sealed inner pocket, a few things, and a name had come from those things, and the name had led, by a path she did not detail, to Halvard. She wanted him to understand before he went in that the identification might not be possible from the face, that it was permitted to say I cannot tell, that there were other ways, and that he was not to feel he had to give an answer he did not have. She had clearly said this before, to other people, and it was no less kind for that.

There are things, she said, that he was carrying. Would you like to see those first.

Yes, he said. The word came out rough and he cleared his throat and said it again.

She brought a tray with a cloth over it and lifted the cloth. There was a folding knife, corroded, the bone scales of the handle gone soft-looking. There was a coin from a country that no longer used that coin. There was a key. And there was, in a small clear envelope, a photograph, ruined by water to a brown ghost of itself, but he could see, holding it up to the window light with a hand that he watched perform this action as if it belonged to someone else, that it was two boys in a boat. The smaller one in the stern with his hand in the water. The larger one at the oars, looking not at the camera but down at his brother, with an expression you could no longer read because the water had taken his face, but you did not need to read it, Halvard found, you only needed to know it had been there, that on a day forty years gone someone, their grandmother probably, with the box camera she kept in a drawer, had caught Anders looking at him like that, and that Anders had carried it, had kept it dry through how many years and rooms and bad nights, had sealed it in an inner pocket against the very water that had at last got it, had carried the good part with him all this time, remember this, after all.

Halvard sat down. He had not decided to sit down. Lindqvist put her hand on his shoulder and did not say anything, which was the right thing, and he was grateful to her for the rest of his life, for the few minutes she let pass in that small room while he held the brown ghost of the photograph and wept without making much noise, the way men of his kind weep, badly, out of practice.

After a while he said, I don't need to see the face.

She said nothing. She was a careful woman.

It's him, he said. I'd know. He carried this. He kept this the whole time. He looked up at her. It's my brother, he said. His name is Anders. Was. His name was Anders.

I'll need you to confirm, she said gently, in a way that suggested she would help him do whatever was required and that the photograph alone, the carrying of the photograph, would not be enough for the forms, and that he would after all have to go through the door, and he understood that she was right, that he had been trying to escape the room with the face by way of the room with the things, and that there was no escaping it, that he had come across the water precisely to look, and that to refuse to look now would be one more dividend taken, one more letting himself off, one more of the small withdrawals that had cost his brother everything across all the years, and he would not do it again. He stood up.

All right, he said. Let's go.

The face, when he saw it, was past photographs as she had warned, and yet. There was the scar through the eyebrow, white and certain, surviving everything. And there was something in the structure of the thing, in the brow, the set of the bones, that was Anders, that was the boy at the oars and the man at the graveside both, the weary recognition still somehow legible there in the ruin, you were always her favorite and you didn't even want it, and Halvard looked and did not look away, and let himself, finally, want it, want the brother, claim him, too late by every measure that mattered and not too late by the only one left.

Yes, he said. That's him. That's Anders.

There were forms after that, and Lindqvist was patient, and somewhere in the middle of them she brought the coffee he had earlier refused and this time he drank it, and it was very bad and very hot and he was glad of it. There were arrangements to discuss, what would be done, where, the question of bringing him home, and Halvard heard himself asking that the burial be here, on the island, near this water, and could not at first have explained why, and then could. There was the boat. There was the summer. There was a coast that smelled like this one. Let him stay by the water he had been carrying a picture of, where the good part had happened, rather than be freighted back to the town of the graveside and laid by the mother who had needed him to be the bad son. Let him rest, for once, uncast.

When it was finished Lindqvist walked him to the door and out into the day, which had become, in the hours he had been inside, almost bright, a thin high sun on the harbor and the gulls and the climbing town. She shook his hand again. The ferry back was at nine that night. He had a whole day to fill on Wolf Island where there had been no wolves in two hundred years, and he thought he would walk, find the north shore where the gear had caught his brother, stand there a while. He thanked her. She said it was nothing, which it was not, and they both knew it, and she went back in to her careful work and he stood in the street with the small clear envelope she had let him keep, the photograph, two boys in a boat, one of them watching the other with all the love there had ever been between them, before the casting, before the fraying, before the silence and the water and the eleven years or twelve.

He put it in his own inner pocket, against his own heart, where it was dry, and he began to walk down toward the harbor and the sea.
