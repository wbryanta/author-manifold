The card table came first, which seemed wrong somehow, because the card table had always been the most temporary thing in the house. Margaret remembered it being dragged out of the hall closet for canasta nights and folded away again by morning, a piece of furniture that existed only in the conditional. Now a man in a Carhartt jacket was carrying it down the porch steps as though it had always belonged to him, the aluminum legs clicking against each other, and Margaret made a checkmark on the clipboard the estate company had given her and did not say anything at all.

"Six dollars," said the woman from the company, whose name was Donna and who had set up a folding chair and a metal cash box on the kitchen table at eight that morning. "He talked me down from eight. I let him. The folding stuff never moves otherwise."

"It's fine," Margaret said.

"You don't have to stay, you know. People do this. They leave a key, we send a check. It's cleaner."

"I know."

"Some people find it's easier."

"I'm sure they do."

Donna had a way of looking at her that Margaret recognized from nurses and from the woman at the funeral home, a soft professional appraisal that asked, without asking, whether she was going to be a problem. Margaret was determined not to be a problem. She had driven six hours from Pittsburgh the day before and slept in her mother's bed because the guest room had become, in the last years, a storage room for things her mother could not throw away — magazines, a sewing machine that no longer worked, boxes of greeting cards saved for the pictures on the front. She had lain in the bed and smelled the particular smell of her mother, which was Pond's cold cream and something underneath it that had no name, and she had not cried, and she had been faintly alarmed by not crying, the way you press a bruise to see if it still hurts.

The house filled up by ten. They came in the way people come to these things, with a kind of forced casualness, pretending they were only browsing while their eyes moved over everything with quick acquisitive precision. They opened drawers. They turned chairs upside down to read the makers' marks. A young couple stood in the living room arguing in low voices about whether the secretary desk would fit in their apartment, and Margaret wanted to tell them it wouldn't, that her father had built it into that corner practically, that it had taken two men and a lot of swearing to get it in there in 1974, but she said nothing because it was no longer her place to say anything about the desk.

She had thought she would feel the things leaving. That was the part she had braced for. Instead what she felt, standing in the front hall with the clipboard, was a strange administrative numbness, as though she were a clerk processing the dispersal of some stranger's life. The objects, once they were being handled by other people, stopped being hers. They became merchandise. The lamp with the pleated shade that had stood on the piano her whole childhood — the lamp she could have drawn from memory, every fold of it — became, the moment a heavy woman in a purple sweatshirt picked it up and turned it to find the price sticker, simply a lamp, slightly dated, twelve dollars.

"Is this real brass?" the woman asked.

"I don't know," Margaret said. "I think so. It's old."

"Feels light for brass."

"It might be brass plate."

The woman set it down with the small disappointment of someone who had hoped to be cheated in her own favor, and moved on, and Margaret looked at the lamp standing there abandoned on the piano and felt the first thing she had felt all morning, which was a sudden irrational protectiveness, as though the lamp had been insulted. She wanted to take it off the market. She wanted to say, it is real brass, you don't know anything, my mother polished it every Easter. But she didn't, and ten minutes later a man bought it without asking any questions at all, and that was worse.

The piano did not sell. It was an upright, a Baldwin, and Donna had priced it at a hundred and fifty and told Margaret frankly that nobody wanted pianos anymore. "Used to be every house had one. Now they can't give them away. The movers charge more to take it than it's worth." Margaret had taken lessons on that piano for nine years, Tuesday afternoons with Mrs. Heller, who smelled of menthol and rapped your knuckles with a pencil if you looked at your hands. She had hated it. She had begged to quit and her mother had refused, year after year, with a stubbornness Margaret had read as cruelty at the time and understood now to have been something closer to faith — a belief that a girl who could play the piano was a girl who had been given something that could not be taken away. Margaret had quit the day she left for college and never played again. The something had been taken away after all, or had simply atrophied, which was the same thing in the end.

She sat down on the bench while there was a lull. The keys had yellowed unevenly, the ivories warm in color where her mother's fingers must have rested most. Her mother had kept playing long after Margaret stopped, hymns mostly, and the slow popular songs of her own girlhood. Margaret put her hands on the keys and found she remembered the shape of a C-major scale, the thumb tucking under, and she played it very quietly, ascending and descending, and then stopped because Donna had glanced over and Margaret did not want to be a woman playing her dead mother's piano in front of strangers. That was exactly the kind of thing she had driven six hours determined not to be.

"You play?" Donna asked.

"Not really. Not for years."

"My mother made me take accordion." Donna laughed, snapping the cash box shut on a sale. "Accordion. Can you imagine. There's a thing nobody wants either."

Margaret smiled because it was expected and got up from the bench.

In the kitchen the dishes were going fast. There was a particular kind of buyer for dishes, older women mostly, who came specifically for the Pyrex and the Fire-King, who knew the patterns by name and held the mixing bowls up to the light to check for the dishwasher cloudiness that ruined the value. Margaret watched a woman with reading glasses on a beaded chain go through the cabinet where the everyday plates lived, the plain white ones with the thin gold rim, and she remembered eating off those plates ten thousand times, twenty thousand, the gold rim worn to nothing at the centers from forks. The woman bought four of them. Not the set, just four, the four least worn, and Margaret thought about the remaining plates sitting in the cabinet with their set broken and felt that protectiveness again, sharper this time.

"You doing okay?" Donna said. She had come up beside her with a roll of stickers. "You're looking a little pale. There's coffee. I brought a thermos."

"I'm fine. It's just strange. Watching it."

"It is. I won't lie to you, it is. That's why most people don't stay." Donna peeled a sticker and pressed it to the bottom of a casserole dish. "But I'll tell you what I've noticed, doing this twenty-some years. The ones who stay, they think it's going to be the watching that's hard. The strangers and all. And it is. But that's not the real thing."

"What's the real thing?"

Donna considered her, deciding how much to say. "The real thing is the stuff that doesn't sell. End of the day. You'll see. You'll have a houseful of things people picked through and left, and you'll have to decide what to do with it, and that's when it gets you. Because the good stuff, the stuff that sells, at least somebody wanted it. It's the leftover stuff. The stuff nobody wanted." She shrugged. "That's the part. But you got hours yet. Have some coffee."

Margaret did not have coffee. She went upstairs instead, to get away from the foot traffic, and found a man and his teenage son in her old bedroom. The room had not been her bedroom for thirty years; her mother had made it into a sort of sitting room with a daybed, a place to do crosswords in the afternoon sun. But Margaret still thought of it as hers. The man was measuring the daybed with a tape and the boy stood by the window with the boredom of the young, scrolling something on his phone, and they both looked up when she came in.

"This for sale?" the man asked, pointing at the daybed.

"Everything's for sale," Margaret said.

"There's no price on it."

"I'm not — I don't work here. The woman downstairs has the prices."

"You family?" the man asked, and something in his face was kinder than the question, and Margaret found she couldn't lie.

"It's my mother's house. Was."

"Oh." The man lowered the tape. "I'm sorry. We'll get out of your way."

"No, it's fine. Measure what you want. That's what it's here for."

But he had already coiled the tape and was steering the boy toward the door with a hand on his shoulder, that male gentleness that comes out sometimes and surprises you, and at the doorway he stopped and said, "My mom passed in March. We did the same thing. The sale." He said it as an offering, the way you'd hand someone a tool you thought they might need. "It's a hell of a thing."

"It is."

"You get through it." He didn't say how. Maybe there was no how. He nodded and took his son and went, and Margaret stood alone in the room that had been hers and looked at the window where she had sat as a girl watching for her father's car, and the light came through it the same as it always had, the exact same slant of afternoon light, indifferent and gold, falling now on a daybed that some stranger would carry away.

She sat on the daybed. From the window you could see the whole street, the Kowalskis' old house across the way, repainted, and the maple her father had planted that was now enormous, its roots buckling the sidewalk. She had learned to ride a bike on that sidewalk. Her mother had run alongside her holding the seat and then, without telling her, let go, and Margaret had ridden half a block before she realized she was alone, and the realization had made her crash. She remembered the skinned knee and the way her mother had laughed and cried at the same time, proud and apologizing, dabbing at the blood with the hem of her apron. She had been so angry. She had felt betrayed by the letting go, even though the letting go was the whole point, was the gift, was the thing her mother had been trying to give her the entire time and that Margaret had received only as abandonment.

She had let go of her own daughter the same way, more or less, sending Claire off to Oregon with a packed car and a face that worked hard not to cry, and Claire had called less and less, and now they spoke on Sundays in a way that was warm and careful and contained nothing, and Margaret had not understood until this moment, sitting on the daybed, that she had done to her mother exactly what Claire was doing to her, and that her mother had borne it the way you bear weather, and that she had never once heard her mother complain about how rarely she called.

This was the thing she had braced against and it had come from an angle she hadn't covered. Not the lamp, not the dishes. The window. The light. She sat with it for a while and did not cry, but it was closer now, a pressure behind the face, and she understood that the not-crying was not numbness after all but a kind of effort, a holding, and that the holding was getting tired.

Downstairs a small commotion had started. She heard Donna's voice, raised slightly, and then another voice, a woman's, querulous and old. Margaret went down. An elderly woman in a good wool coat, far too warm for the day, was standing in the front hall holding the brass lamp — no, a different lamp, the smaller one from the hall table — and Donna was saying, gently, "Ma'am, you have to pay for that, I can't just let you take it," and the old woman was saying, "But it's mine, I gave it to Eleanor, I gave it to her for her sixtieth, you can ask her, where is Eleanor—"

Eleanor was Margaret's mother.

"She gave it to her," Margaret said, coming down the last steps, and Donna turned, relieved and wary. "It's all right. She knew my mother."

The old woman looked at Margaret and her face moved through several things, suspicion and hope and a terrible groping effort, and she said, "Are you Eleanor?"

"I'm her daughter. I'm Margaret."

"Margaret." The woman said it as though tasting it. "Little Margaret. With the pigtails. You played the piano." And then, brightening, certain of something at last: "Where's your mother? I brought back her cake plate, I've had it for ages, I kept meaning to—" She had no cake plate. Her hands held only the lamp. "I kept meaning to bring it back."

Margaret crossed the hall. Up close the woman smelled of powder and of age, and her eyes were the milky blue of very old eyes, and Margaret did not know her, could not place her among the women who had come and gone through this house, the bridge club, the church, the neighbors, but the woman clearly knew her, or had known her, in the unreachable archive of a mind that was coming apart.

"Mom's not here right now," Margaret said, and Donna looked at her, and Margaret did not look back. "But I know she'd want you to have the lamp. Since you gave it to her."

"It's a good lamp," the woman said, reassured.

"It is."

"Brass."

"Real brass."

The woman beamed. A younger woman appeared in the doorway then, harried, car keys in hand, calling, "Mom? Mom, I told you to wait in the car—" and took in the scene with the quick exhausted reading of a person who spends her life retrieving someone from situations. "I'm so sorry," she said to Donna, to Margaret, to the room. "She wandered off. She does this. We were just driving by and she saw the sign and—" She stopped, seeing the lamp. "Mom, you can't take that. Put it back."

"It's all right," Margaret said again. "She knew my mother. Let her have it."

The younger woman looked at her, and something passed between them, the recognition of one daughter by another, both of them in the business now of managing what could not be managed. "Are you sure? I can pay—"

"No. Please. It would've made my mother happy."

The younger woman's face did a complicated thing. "Thank you," she said quietly. She took her mother's arm. "Come on, Mom. We've got the lamp. We can go now."

"Tell Eleanor I said hello," the old woman called over her shoulder as she was led out. "Tell her about the cake plate. I'll bring it next time."

"I will," Margaret said. "I'll tell her."

The door closed behind them. Through the front window Margaret watched the younger woman fold her mother into the passenger seat of a Camry with a patience that was clearly costing her something, and buckle the belt, and shut the door, and stand for a moment with her hands on the roof of the car and her head down before she got in and drove away.

"That was kind of you," Donna said. "You didn't have to do that."

"It was just a lamp."

"It wasn't just the lamp I meant."

Margaret didn't answer. She went and stood by the window where the lamp had been and put her hand on the empty hall table, and Donna, with a tact Margaret would remember later and be grateful for, went back to her cash box and left her alone.

The afternoon wore down. The crowd thinned to stragglers, the serious buyers having come and gone in the first hours, leaving behind the browsers and the lonely, the people for whom estate sales were a way to spend a Saturday, to walk through a stranger's life and touch the things and put them down. The house grew emptier in a way that was visible now, whole regions of it cleared — the dining room a bare floor with rectangles of un-faded carpet where the table and the hutch had stood, the bookshelves gutted, the records gone. Margaret walked through the rooms and the emptiness had a sound, or rather it took away sound, the way an empty house swallows your footsteps differently than a full one.

In the dining room the un-faded rectangles on the carpet were the exact dimensions of the furniture, ghost-prints, and Margaret stood looking at the bright clean shape where the table had been, the table they had eaten Thanksgiving at, and birthdays, and the ordinary thousands of dinners that you do not remember individually because they are the very substance of a life and you cannot remember the substance, only the events that interrupt it. The table was gone to the young couple who had also, in the end, taken the secretary desk, having measured and reconsidered and decided to make it fit. Margaret hoped it fit. She hoped they would eat at the table for forty years and have children who hated something about it and grew up and stood someday in an empty room looking at the place where it had been.

Donna found her there. "Last ones are clearing out. You want me to start the count? We can settle up. You did good. We did real good, actually, better than I figured. Pianos and the big furniture aside."

"What happens to what's left?"

"Depends what you want. We can do a second day, Sunday, half off everything, that moves most of it. What doesn't go, there's a guy comes with a truck, takes the rest to the donation place, or the dump, depending. You don't have to be here for any of that." Donna paused. "Told you. It's the leftover stuff."

Margaret went into the kitchen. The everyday plates were still in the cabinet, the broken set, the four good ones gone and the rest worn to nothing at the centers, and the casserole dishes nobody had wanted, and a drawer full of utensils, the can opener and the potato masher and the wooden spoons gone gray and soft at the edges from fifty years of stirring. Nobody wanted wooden spoons. She picked one up. It was worn into a shape, the shape of her mother's hand and the curve of a particular pot, and it was nothing, it was worth nothing, it was a stick that had been used to stir food, and Margaret stood in the kitchen holding it and felt the holding in her face finally give way, not dramatically, not the way she had feared, but quietly, the tears just coming and running down without any sound, and she let them, because there was no one left to be a problem in front of, only Donna in the next room counting bills, and Donna had seen it all before and would say nothing.

She kept the spoon. She put it in her purse. She kept the four worn plates too, and the brass lamp from the piano that the man had bought and that she could not get back, so not that, but she kept the pleated shade off another lamp that hadn't sold, and a chipped mixing bowl, and a single canasta deck she found in the drawer of the telephone table, the cards soft as cloth from her mother's hands. She did not keep the piano. She told Donna to give it to anyone who would take it, anyone at all, even if they paid nothing, even if it had to be hauled out for free, because she could not bear the thought of it going to the dump, of those keys her mother's fingers had warmed being broken up in a truck.

"I'll find it a home," Donna said. "I know a church might take it. I'll make some calls."

"Thank you."

"You want to be here Sunday?"

"No," Margaret said. "I think I've done the part I needed to do."

She signed where Donna showed her and took the check she did not look at and walked out onto the porch into the long gold light of the late afternoon, the same light, falling now on the maple her father had planted and the buckled sidewalk where she had learned that being let go of and being abandoned were, after all, the same motion, seen from two sides. She stood on the porch a while. Then she went down the steps her mother had swept ten thousand times, with the wooden spoon in her purse, and got in her car, and sat for a moment before starting it, looking at the house, letting go of the seat, letting it ride on without her.
