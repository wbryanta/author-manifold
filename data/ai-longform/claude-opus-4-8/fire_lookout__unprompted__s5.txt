The smoke came up white at first, which meant it was small, which meant it was nothing, which meant Vera should have turned back to her logbook and recorded the time and the bearing and the estimated distance and gone on with the long slow business of her last summer. White smoke was water still cooking off, green wood, duff and needle and the wet underside of things that had not yet decided to burn. White smoke was a campfire someone would put out with the dregs of their coffee. She had seen a thousand columns of it in nine summers up here and most of them had come to nothing, and the ones that had come to something had announced themselves differently, with a kind of brown intention, a column that thickened rather than thinned.

But she knew the drainage. That was the trouble. She did not need the Osborne fire finder to give her the bearing, did not need to rotate the brass sighting ring and read the azimuth off the map mounted under glass on the table that had stood in the center of this tower since before she was born. She knew where the smoke was because she had spent the better part of her life learning the shape of that fold in the land, the way the ridge stepped down in two benches toward the creek, the way the aspens marked the seep where the spring came up even in August, the dark wedge of doghair lodgepole that her father used to say would go like a torch one day, just go, and take everything below it.

Below it was the cabin.

Vera stood very still in the glass room at the top of the tower, eighty-one steps above the rock, and watched the smoke and did not move toward the radio because she already knew the radio was dead. It had been dead for eleven days. She had reported it dead on the satellite phone the second day, and the dispatcher, a young man named Cody whose voice she had never matched to a face, had said they'd get someone up there, and no one had come, because no one came up here anymore. They were closing the tower. This was the last summer it would be staffed at all, a courtesy to her, a sinecure, a way of letting her finish what she had started without anyone saying out loud that the work itself no longer mattered, that satellites and aircraft and the cameras bolted to other towers did now what she had done with her eyes and a pair of binoculars and the patience that had calcified in her over nine seasons into something she no longer knew how to spend anywhere else.

The satellite phone was in the metal box under the cot. She crossed to it and knelt and worked the latch and took the phone out and pressed the power button and watched the screen wake gray and then blue and then ask her, as it always did, to wait while it found the sky. She carried it to the catwalk door and stepped outside where the antenna had the best chance, and she stood at the rail with the whole country falling away from her on every side, range behind range going blue and bluer until the farthest was only a suggestion, and she waited for the bars.

The smoke had thickened.

It was not white anymore. It had gone the gray of dishwater at the base, and above that a column was forming, leaning a little to the northeast with the afternoon wind, the wind that came up every day at this hour off the warming basin and ran upcanyon until sunset. Northeast. Away from the creek, toward the slope. Toward the doghair lodgepole.

She looked at the phone. NO SERVICE. She held it higher, the way you do, the way everyone does, as if six more inches of altitude might matter to a machine talking to something twenty-two thousand miles up. NO SERVICE. She walked the catwalk to the north corner and tried there. She tried the east. She had lived inside the reliability of this phone for eleven days, had let it stand in for the radio, had let it be the thing that meant she was not entirely alone up here, and now, with the smoke building in the one drainage in all this country she could not bear to watch burn, the little screen gave her nothing, and she understood, in the cold clear way she understood weather, that the satellite that should have been overhead was not, that she was inside one of the gaps in the constellation that Cody had warned her about, that for the next forty minutes, or the next two hours, she was as cut off as her grandfather had been when he built the first tower on this rock with a crosscut saw and a mule.

Eight miles to the road. The truck was at the trailhead, where the truck always was, because you could not drive to a lookout, that was the whole point of a lookout, it stood where the roads did not go. Eight miles down through the basin and across the saddle and down again, two thousand feet of descent on a trail that switchbacked through the talus and then dropped into timber and got bad, got rooty and washed and steep, in the last two miles before it met the old logging spur where the truck waited. Three hours down if she ran the parts she could run. Then the truck, then twenty minutes on the spur to where there was a phone or a radio or a person, twenty minutes she could not shorten, the road being what it was.

By then the smoke would have answered the question of what it was going to be.

She went back inside. She made herself sit down at the fire finder, because that was the discipline, that was what nine summers had built in her, the refusal to act before she knew. She put her eye to the sight and rotated the ring and read the azimuth and wrote it in the log with the time, 2:47, and she got the binoculars and braced her elbows on the table and found the column and followed it down to its source and tried to read it the way she had been taught to read it, by a man who was dead now, her father, who had stood where she was standing and taught her that smoke was a language and that most people only ever learned to read the loudest words.

She could not see flame. The source was tucked behind the first bench, in the dead ground she could never see into, the one blind fold in her whole domain, and she had cursed that blind ground a hundred times over the years because it was exactly where she would have put a fire if she had been a fire and wanted to grow up unseen. The smoke rose out of it gray now, and where it caught the sun it had a quality she did not like, a kind of muscularity, a way of boiling up rather than drifting, and she watched a single darker pulse climb the column like something swallowed by a snake, and she knew that was a tree, that something with pitch in it had gone up all at once, and that meant the fire had found the duff and the duff had found the ladder fuels and the ladder fuels were reaching for the canopy.

She thought about Maddy.

She had not spoken to her sister in four years. The arithmetic of it was simple and the reasons were not, and she had spent enough nights up here turning it over that she had worn the reasons smooth and could no longer feel their edges, only their weight. Their mother's house, sold. Their mother's things, divided badly. Words said in a kitchen with the estate lawyer's letter on the table between them, words that could not be taken back because neither of them had wanted to take them back, had wanted instead for the words to land and stay landed, had each preferred the clean wound of estrangement to the long infection of pretending. Maddy had kept the cabin. The cabin had come down through their father's side and Maddy had wanted it and Vera had let her have it, had said take it, had said it like a curse, take the whole rotten thing, and Maddy had taken it, and that was the last transaction between them, the cabin passing into Maddy's keeping like a coin paid to close an account.

Maddy came up in the summers. Vera knew this the way you know things about a person you have stopped speaking to but cannot stop knowing. She had never seen her sister's truck on the spur, the tower being too far and too high to make out a vehicle, but she had seen, some evenings, a thread of woodsmoke from the cabin's chimney, blue and thin and domestic, rising in the still hour after sundown, and she had stood on the catwalk and watched it and known that her sister was down there, eight miles away and four years away, making her supper, and Vera had never once reached for the phone.

The smoke now was not chimney smoke. The smoke now was the thing her father had pointed at across forty years and said one day. 

She put down the binoculars. Her hands were steady. She noticed that they were steady the way you notice a stranger being calm, with a kind of detached approval. The procedure, when you spotted a fire, was to report it. Bearing, distance, size, behavior, fuels, your name, your tower. You reported it and then you were done, your job was to see and to say, and other people with engines and aircraft and shovels did the rest, and the whole architecture of the thing depended on the radio, and the radio was dead and the phone could not find the sky.

Was there anyone in the cabin.

That was the only question. If the cabin was empty the smoke was a thing to report and not a thing to run toward, because a person alone does not fight a crown fire in doghair lodgepole, a person alone gets killed doing that, and Vera was sixty-one years old and had a bad knee and would be eight miles and three hours from the trailhead the whole way down. If the cabin was empty she should stay in the tower and keep trying the phone and the radio and do the one useful thing she could do, which was to be a witness, to log the fire's growth, to have the bearing ready the instant the sky opened so that when she could finally reach someone she could hand them the fire complete, located, described, ready to be fought.

But if Maddy was down there.

She had no way to know. That was the unbearable arithmetic of it. The thread of chimney smoke she sometimes saw came at evening, and it was not evening, it was the middle of a hot afternoon when a person might be down at the creek or up the slope or gone entirely, the cabin standing empty with its windows full of sky. She had seen no chimney smoke today. That meant nothing. You did not light a stove at two in the afternoon in August. Maddy could be down there reading in the shade. Maddy could be in town buying groceries. Maddy could be anywhere. There was no signal Vera could read from eight miles off through a fold of blind ground that would tell her whether her sister was about to burn.

She tried the phone again. NO SERVICE.

She thought: I could go down.

She thought: I could go down and it could be nothing, an empty cabin, and I could destroy my knee and spend the night lost in the talus for an empty cabin, and the fire I should have been watching could run the basin while I was off the rock, and other cabins, other people, could pay for my running toward the one thing I cannot stand to lose.

She thought: but it is Maddy.

And under that, in a place she did not like to look, she thought: and if I go down and she is there and the cabin burns and I pull her out of it, then we will be even, then the four years will be paid off, then she will owe me her life and I will have given it and we can begin again on that, on a debt, the way we ended on a debt. And she was ashamed of the thought even as she had it, ashamed that some part of her could look at her sister's burning and see in it an opportunity, a door, a way back in. She was ashamed and she did not let the shame change anything, because the shame did not change the smoke.

She watched the column another minute. It had a head on it now, a dirty cauliflower boiling up where the heat punched through into the cooler air above, and it was leaning harder northeast, and the wind had stiffened, she could hear it now coming up through the krummholz below the tower, that dry rushing that was the sound of the whole basin breathing in. A spotting wind. A wind that would carry embers a quarter mile ahead of the front and lay down new fire in the cured grass and the dead-and-down, and the cabin was northeast of the source, the cabin was downwind, the cabin was exactly where the embers would go.

Vera stood up.

She did not decide, exactly. She had spent her life mistrusting decisions, the way they pretended to be made in a single clean instant when really they were the sediment of everything that came before, settling out all at once and calling itself a choice. She stood up and she was already someone who was going down, had perhaps been that person since the smoke first went from white to gray, and the standing up only made it visible.

She put the satellite phone in the cargo pocket of her trousers. She would try it the whole way down, at every high spot, every place the trees opened, and the moment it found the sky she would stop and report the fire and tell them about the cabin and ask, beg, for someone to come, and then she would keep going down because by then she would be closer to the cabin than to the tower and there would be no reason on earth to climb back up. She filled both water bottles from the jug. She put the headlamp in her pocket because three hours down meant she might be coming back in the dark, if she came back. She laced the boots she had broken in over nine summers, that knew her feet, and she took the long-handled shovel from where it leaned by the door because it was the only firefighting tool she had and because her hands wanted something to carry, and she stood for a moment in the doorway of the glass room and looked back at it, the table, the log open to today's date, the cot, the small kingdom of her solitude that she was leaving in the middle of the day with the watch unkept for the first time in nine years.

Then she went down the eighty-one steps, and the country rose up around her as she descended, the long view folding away behind the nearer ground until she was only a woman on a trail, no longer the eye on the rock, no longer the one who sees, just one more small warm body moving through fuel.

The trail through the basin was good and she made time. The wind was at her back, pushing her downhill, and she did not like that the wind was at her back because it meant the wind was going where she was going, toward the cabin, and she could not see the smoke now, the tower's whole purpose being that it could see what the ground could not, and down here in the basin she was blind, she was everyone, she was the people she had watched for nine years walking unknowing toward things she could see and they could not. She stopped at the high point of the saddle, where the trail crested between two knobs of granite and the country opened northeast, and she took out the phone, and the screen searched, and she stood with her chest heaving and watched it search, and she could see the column from here, much bigger, the head of it sheared off and streaming, and the screen said NO SERVICE and then it said, after a moment that stretched out long enough for her to hate it, ONE BAR.

She dialed before it could change its mind. Not 911, which would route her to a county dispatcher who would have to find the forest, but the dispatch number she knew by heart, Cody's number, the line into the world she had been speaking to for nine summers, and it rang once, twice, and the bar held, and on the third ring a voice said the dispatcher's greeting, the unit and the name, and Vera said her tower and her name fast and flat the way you were trained to, cutting through whatever the voice was going to say.

"I've got a fire," she said. "Established, with a column, head's sheared, it's spotting. I make it the Halloran Creek drainage, the lower bench—" and she gave the bearing she had read at 2:47, gave the distance, gave the fuels, doghair lodgepole and cured grass and heavy dead-and-down, gave the wind, upcanyon, stiffening, and the voice was reading it back to her now, all business, the machine engaging, and she heard in the background the particular activity of a room where people had just stood up. And then she said the thing that was not procedure. "There's a cabin in that drainage. Private inholding, lower bench, north side of the creek. There may be someone in it. There may be a woman in it. I'm on foot from the tower, I'm at the saddle, I'm going down to it."

A pause. The voice, Cody, younger than she'd let herself imagine, said, "Ma'am, I need you to stay—"

The bar dropped. The call died not with a click but with a softening, the voice thinning into static and then into nothing, and she looked at the screen and it said NO SERVICE again and she understood that the satellite had gone back behind the curve of the earth or the ridge had eaten the signal or both, but it did not matter, because she had said it, the fire was reported, the bearing was in the room with the people standing up, an aircraft would come, a crew would come, the machine was moving now whether or not she ever spoke to it again. She had done her job. She was no longer the eye on the rock but she had handed off what the eye had seen and now she was free, terribly free, to be only a sister going down a mountain toward a fire.

She put the phone away and she went down off the saddle into the timber, and the trail got bad the way she knew it would, rooty and steep, and her knee began its argument with her on the third switchback and she told it the thing you tell a knee, which is nothing, which is to keep going. The smoke smell reached her here, drifting up the canyon, the unmistakable resinous reek of burning conifer, and it was thicker than it should have been for a fire still that far off, which meant either the fire was bigger than she'd logged or the wind was bending the smoke down into the canyon, pooling it, and she went through a band of it where the visibility dropped and her eyes stung and she pulled her shirt up over her mouth and kept descending, the shovel banging against the rocks, the light coming strange and orange through the haze.

She thought about the last thing Maddy had said to her in the kitchen four years ago, with the lawyer's letter between them. She had thought about it ten thousand times and it had never once helped and she thought about it now. Maddy had said: you were never up there to watch the forest. You went up that tower to get away from all of us and you found a job that would let you call it duty. And Vera had said the unforgivable thing back, the thing about their mother, the thing she would not even now let herself repeat in the privacy of her own skull, and Maddy had gone white and still and had picked up the letter and folded it and that had been the end, four years of silence sealed in a kitchen over the bones of their mother's estate.

You went up that tower to get away from all of us.

Maybe. Probably. And here she was coming down it, finally, at sixty-one, with a shovel and a dead phone and a bad knee, going down into the smoke toward the one person she had climbed it to escape, and she did not know if she was going down to save Maddy or to be forgiven by her or to forgive her or only to stand beside her in the one place that had ever belonged to both of them and watch it burn. She did not know and the not knowing did not slow her. The trail dropped. The smoke thickened. Somewhere below her, through the trees, she heard the sound she had spent nine years watching for and had never once heard from inside it, the low continuous roar of a fire that had found its legs and meant to run, and she went down toward it, toward the cabin, toward her sister, into the loud heart of the thing she had only ever been trained to see from far away and name.
