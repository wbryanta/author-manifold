The price stickers were the worst part, Margaret decided, those little orange dots the estate company had pressed onto everything she had ever loved. They were on the mahogany sideboard ($85) and on the brass lamp with the pleated shade that had stood beside her mother's reading chair ($20) and even, absurdly, on the chair itself ($40), which seemed to her a sum both insulting and unbearable, since she could not separate the chair from the woman who had sat in it, knitting, dozing, occasionally calling out to Margaret across forty years and six hundred miles to ask whether she was eating enough green vegetables.

She had arrived at nine that morning, before the sale began, and the estate woman—Brenda, sturdy and kind, with reading glasses on a beaded chain—had given her a clipboard and a chance to walk through and pull anything she wanted to keep. Margaret had walked the rooms slowly, and the strange thing was how little she could decide upon. She had thought she would want everything. Instead she had stood in the middle of the dining room with the clipboard hanging at her side and felt the way she imagined a person felt in deep water, treading, the bottom nowhere beneath her feet. In the end she had marked the photograph albums and a wooden recipe box and a small painting of nothing in particular—a barn, a field—that had hung in the upstairs hall as long as she could remember. The furniture she had let go. There was no room for it in her condominium in Maryland, and her daughter Claire, twenty-six and living in a studio apartment in Chicago with a man Margaret had met only twice, did not want a mahogany sideboard. No one her daughter's age wanted brown furniture. Brenda had told her this gently, as a fact of the market, the way one might explain that a particular crop had failed that year.

So now Margaret sat in a folding chair in the kitchen, out of the main traffic, drinking coffee from a paper cup, and watched the strangers come.

They came in twos and threes, in windbreakers, women mostly, some with grown daughters of their own, some with husbands trailing behind holding measuring tapes. They had the look of people at a county fair, browsing, faintly excited, alert for the thing they had not known they needed. They picked up her mother's things and turned them over and looked at the bottoms and set them down. A man in a Carhartt jacket spent a long time examining the dining table, getting down on one knee to inspect the legs, and Margaret had to look away, because that was the table where she had done her algebra homework, where her father had carved the Thanksgiving turkey with a knife he sharpened ceremonially each year on a whetstone, where her mother had laid out the casseroles after the funeral, where Margaret herself had sat three weeks ago with the hospice nurse going over the schedule of medications, the morphine in its little brown bottle with the dropper.

"This is solid," the man said to no one. "They don't make them like this."

"Real wood," his wife agreed.

They bought it. Brenda peeled the orange dot and wrote something on her pad, and the man backed his truck up to the front walk, and Margaret watched through the kitchen doorway as he and another man she did not know carried the table out the front door, tilting it to clear the frame, the same maneuver, surely, that the deliverymen had performed in reverse some fifty years ago, before Margaret was old enough to remember. The table left a pale rectangle on the floor where the sun had not reached for decades. It looked like the chalk outline at a crime scene. Then the man laid a moving blanket over the table in the bed of his truck, and tied it down, and drove away, and the table that had been the center of the house, the planet around which the family had revolved, was simply gone, was now an object in a stranger's house in some other town, where it would hold their mail and their car keys and the elbows of people Margaret would never meet.

She had not cried yet. She had expected to and was waiting for it the way you wait for a sneeze that won't come, and the not-crying made her feel monstrous, as though the failure proved something about her, about the cool dutiful efficiency with which she had handled all of it—the phone calls, the death certificate, the obituary she had written in a hotel room composing and recomposing the single sentence about her mother's life, which seemed both too long and far too short. *She enjoyed gardening, reading, and the company of her grandchildren.* As if a life could be reduced to a list of hobbies. As if anyone, reading it in the *Marion Star*, would understand that this woman had once stood at the foot of Margaret's bed at three in the morning during a fever and pressed a cold cloth to her forehead and sung, badly, in a voice Margaret could still hear, a song about a mockingbird, and that this, this small ordinary act repeated across years, was the entire and irreplaceable content of love.

A teenage girl came into the kitchen, trailing her mother, and opened the cabinets.

"Are the dishes for sale?" the mother asked Margaret, perhaps taking her for staff.

"Yes," Margaret said. "Everything is."

The girl reached up and took down a stack of the everyday plates, the white ones with the thin green band around the rim, and Margaret felt something move in her chest, because she had eaten ten thousand meals off those plates, had stood at the sink drying them as a girl while her mother washed, the two of them in the wordless rhythm of the work, the radio playing, the window over the sink looking out onto the backyard where the clothesline ran between two posts her father had set in concrete. She knew the exact weight of those plates in her hand. She knew the one with the chip on the underside that you couldn't see when it sat on the table. She wanted to tell the girl these things and understood that they were precisely the things that could not be transmitted, that the girl would carry the plates home and they would be, to her, simply plates, eight dollars for the set, useful, and this was not a tragedy, this was the way of the world, but it pressed against Margaret all the same.

"They're nice," the girl said, to her mother.

"For your apartment," the mother said. "When you go to school."

And Margaret thought: yes. Let them go to the girl. Let her eat off them in some dormitory or first apartment, let her serve her friends midnight spaghetti off the green-banded plates, let them become the plates of someone's youth again, the way they had been the plates of Margaret's. There was a kind of mercy in it, if she could make herself see it that way. The objects would outlive them all and accumulate new lives, new hands, new meals, and the love that had soaked into them—if love could soak into a thing—would be invisible but perhaps not entirely lost, just unreadable, like writing in a language no one spoke anymore but which had once meant everything.

She did not quite believe this. But she held it, the idea, the way you hold a smooth stone in your pocket.

Brenda came into the kitchen with her glasses pushed up on her head. "How're you holding up?"

"I'm fine," Margaret said, which was what she said.

"You don't have to stay, you know. Plenty of folks just hand us the keys and go. We mail you the check. Sometimes it's easier."

"I know." Margaret looked into her coffee. "I think I want to be here."

"Some people do." Brenda nodded as though this confirmed a theory. "They want to see who gets it. I had a man last spring sat in the corner of his mother's bedroom the whole day, didn't say a word, just watched. By the end he was telling the buyers stories about every single thing. This is the dresser my mother kept her gloves in. That kind of thing. The buyers love it, actually. Makes them feel like they got something special."

"Did it help him?"

Brenda considered. "Hard to say. He cried in the driveway after. But people cry. That's not the same as it not helping." She patted Margaret's shoulder and went back out toward the living room, where someone was asking about the lamp.

Margaret got up and walked, against her own better judgment, toward the front of the house. The living room was busier now, the morning crowd thickening toward noon, and she stood at the edge of it, against the wall, and watched. The brass lamp was gone already. The reading chair still had its orange dot. A woman about Margaret's own age was sitting in it, testing it, leaning back, and her husband said, "Comfortable?" and the woman said, "Oh, it's perfect," and Margaret had to leave the room.

She went up the stairs.

It was quieter up there. A few browsers drifted through the bedrooms, but the buying energy was downstairs, where the good furniture was. Margaret went into her mother's room. The bed had a dot on the headboard. The vanity, the one with the three mirrors that folded so you could see the back of your own head, the one Margaret had sat at as a child watching her mother put on lipstick before church, pressing her lips together against a tissue—the vanity had a dot, and someone had already written on Brenda's pad, because there was a little SOLD tag tucked into the corner of the mirror.

She opened the closet. Most of the clothes had been bagged for the church donation already; the rack was half empty, the hangers exposed, a few garments remaining that Brenda must have judged unsellable. There was a winter coat, navy wool, that her mother had worn for thirty years. There was a housedress. And there, on the floor of the closet, in the corner, were the shoes, a dozen pairs lined up the way her mother had always kept them, toes out, and Margaret crouched down and looked at them and finally, finally, she felt it begin to rise in her, because the shoes held the shape of the feet, you could see where her mother's bunion had pushed against the leather, where the heel had worn, and the body that had pressed those shapes into the shoes was gone, was ash in a box at the funeral home that Margaret was supposed to pick up tomorrow, and the shoes did not know this, the shoes were still waiting, faithful, toes out, for feet that would never come.

She knelt on the floor of her mother's closet and she did not make any sound but the tears came and ran down and dropped onto the backs of her hands, and she let them, and it was not the cleansing release that people promised, that she had half-expected, the way movies promised it. It was just grief, dumb and physical, the body doing what the mind could not, and it went on for a while and then it eased, not because anything was resolved but because the body tired, and she wiped her face with the heel of her hand and stood up, her knees aching, fifty-six years old, an orphan now, which was a ridiculous word for a woman her age and was nonetheless the true one. There was no one above her anymore. She was the oldest generation now, she and her brother in Phoenix who had not come, who had said over the phone, *I just can't, Maggie, I said my goodbyes when she was still herself,* and Margaret had been angry and was still a little angry and also understood it completely.

She took the navy coat off the hanger. It was too big for her and out of style and she would never wear it. She folded it over her arm anyway. She took none of the shoes, because the shoes belonged where they were, with the body that had made them, and would go to the donation pile, and some other woman would wear them out the rest of the way, and that was right.

Downstairs, the recipe box. She had marked it that morning but now she went to find it, suddenly anxious, irrationally certain it would have been sold by mistake. It was where she'd left it, on the kitchen counter, with a torn piece of paper taped to it that said HOLD - FAMILY in Brenda's handwriting. Margaret opened it. The cards were furred at the edges from handling, written in her mother's slanting hand and, on the older ones, in a hand Margaret recognized with a small shock as her grandmother's, a woman dead since Margaret was nine. *Eleanor's Apple Cake. Ham loaf. Refrigerator rolls.* Some had splatters on them, grease, vanilla, the archaeology of a hundred holiday dinners. One card, for a salad involving lime gelatin and cottage cheese that Margaret had hated as a child and would defend now with her life, had a note in the margin in her mother's hand: *Margaret won't eat. Make less.* She had not known her mother had written that. She read it three times. *Margaret won't eat. Make less.* A small accommodation, recorded forty-five years ago, the love of a mother for a fussy child encoded in a recipe, and her mother had kept the card all this time, had never thrown it out, had cooked from this box until the very end, and the note had waited in the dark of the box for Margaret to find it on this day, and it did what the shoes had done, it opened her, and she stood at the counter and pressed the card to her mouth.

The Carhartt man's wife came back in, alone this time, looking for her husband, and saw Margaret and stopped.

"Are you all right, honey?"

"Yes," Margaret said. "I'm sorry. It's my mother's house."

The woman's face changed entirely, the browsing brightness draining out of it, replaced by something older and more careful. "Oh," she said. "Oh, I'm so sorry. We took your table. I didn't—I didn't realize the family was here. We can—" She gestured vaguely toward the door, as though she might call the truck back.

"No," Margaret said. "No, please. I'm glad you have it. It's a good table. I did my homework on it."

The woman looked at her for a moment. Then she said, "I'll take good care of it. We've got grandkids. They'll do their homework on it too." She said it simply, as a promise between two women who would not meet again, and Margaret found that she believed her, and was grateful, more grateful than the moment seemed to warrant, so that she had to press her lips together and nod and could not speak.

The woman touched her arm, briefly, the way Brenda had, the way women of a certain age and region touched each other in moments of feeling, a hand on the arm worth a thousand words none of them would say. Then she went to find her husband, and Margaret heard them leave, the screen door with its particular two-note complaint, the catch and then the slap, a sound she had heard ten thousand times, coming home from school, coming home from college, coming home, coming home.

The afternoon wore on. The crowd thinned after lunch and thickened again toward three, a second wave, bargain hunters now, asking Brenda whether she'd take less. The house emptied around Margaret. The sideboard went to a young couple who were, Brenda reported, restoring an old farmhouse out on the county road and wanted period pieces; they had been delighted, they had said it was exactly right. The reading chair went to the woman who had found it perfect. The lamps, the side tables, the china cabinet, the bed frames, the dishes, the green-banded plates carried out in a cardboard box by the teenage girl, who would not remember this day, for whom this was simply the afternoon she got her dishes for school. Each thing left its pale ghost on the floor or the wall, the rooms reverting square by square to some earlier version of themselves, before the family, the original blankness of a house that is only a house.

Margaret stayed. She found that she did not want to tell the stories the way Brenda's man had, did not want to narrate her childhood to strangers, but she wanted to be present for the leaving, to witness it, to stand at the door as the things went out the way she might have stood at a bedside. It seemed to her the last service she could perform. She had not been there at the actual end—she had stepped out to the parking lot to call her brother, and her mother had died in the four minutes she was gone, slipped out while Margaret was leaving a voicemail, and she would carry that, the four minutes, for the rest of her life. So she stayed now. She would not step out again. She would watch every piece go.

By five the house was nearly empty. Brenda's crew began carrying the unsold items, the leftovers, out to the truck for the donation center—the coat she'd put back, the shoes, a box of mismatched glassware. Brenda came and sat down in the second folding chair beside Margaret in the kitchen and took off her glasses and rubbed the bridge of her nose.

"Good sale," she said. "Better than I expected, honestly. The farmhouse couple alone." She named a figure, the day's total, and it was both more and less than Margaret had imagined, and meant nothing to her, a number, the price of a life's accumulated objects, which she would deposit and which would pay for a fraction of the funeral.

"Thank you," Margaret said. "For—the way you do it. You're kind about it."

"It's people's lives," Brenda said simply. "You can't do this work and not know that." She put her glasses back on. "You staying in town tonight?"

"The Hampton out by the highway. I pick up the—I have an errand tomorrow, and then I drive back."

Brenda nodded, understanding the errand. "Long drive."

"It is."

They sat for a moment in the emptying house, the late sun coming gold through the kitchen window over the sink, falling on the linoleum that Margaret's mother had wanted to replace for twenty years and never had. Outside, the truck doors slammed. The crew called goodbye. Then it was only the two of them, and then Brenda gathered her clipboard and her cash box and stood.

"I'll mail the final check within the week," she said. "You take whatever time you need in here. Just pull the door behind you, it locks itself. The realtor has a key." She paused at the kitchen doorway. "You did good today. Staying. Not everybody can."

And then she was gone too, the screen door's two notes, and Margaret was alone in her mother's house for the last time.

She walked through it slowly. The empty rooms. The pale rectangles on the floors. The dining room where the table had been, where the family had eaten and argued and reconciled, where she had announced she was getting married and, eleven years later, divorced, both announcements received by her mother with the same steadiness, the same setting-down of the fork, the same *Well. All right then. Let's think it through.* The living room with its ghost of the reading chair. The window seat in the front room where she had read whole summers away as a girl, where the radiator had clanked, where she had watched for her father's car. The house held nothing now and so it held everything; emptied of its objects it was finally only itself, the rooms her body knew, the dimensions written into her before memory, the place that would dissolve now into other people's lives and be, within the year, someone else's home, with someone else's furniture making someone else's pale ghosts on the floor.

She climbed the stairs one last time and stood in the doorway of the room that had been hers, smaller than she remembered, as childhood rooms always are, and she did not go in. She had taken what she could take. She had the albums and the painting of the barn in her car, and the recipe box on the seat beside her, the card on top, *Margaret won't eat. Make less.* She had the sound of the screen door in her, which no one could buy, which had no orange dot.

She went down the stairs and through the kitchen and she did not look back at the gold light on the linoleum. At the front door she stopped with her hand on the frame, the way the table had been tilted to clear it, the way she herself had been carried through it as an infant in some forgotten arrival, and she stood there a moment in the threshold, neither in nor out.

Then she stepped onto the porch and pulled the door behind her until she heard it lock.

The street was quiet, the maples she had climbed gone enormous and old, the light long across the lawns. She got into her car. She sat for a moment without starting it. She put her hand on the recipe box, flat, the way you would put your hand on a sleeping animal, or on the chest of someone you loved, to feel them breathe.

Then she drove out of the town, west into the lowering sun, toward the highway and the long way home, and the house behind her stood empty in the dusk, waiting, the way the shoes had waited, for the next life to come and fill it.
