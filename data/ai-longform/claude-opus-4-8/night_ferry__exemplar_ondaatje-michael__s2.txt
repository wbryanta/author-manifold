The ferry left the mainland at nine in the evening, an hour after the light had gone, and Niall stood at the railing on the upper deck where the cold came at him in long pulls off the water. He had been told there would be coffee in the lounge but he did not want coffee. He wanted the cold. He wanted the particular discomfort of standing where the wind could find him, because it kept him inside the present, and the present was the only place where his brother was not yet dead.

There were perhaps nine other passengers. A woman with two sleeping children laid out across a bench like loaves. A man in oilskins who smelled of fish and diesel and who had nodded at Niall once, in the way of someone who crossed this strait often and recognised the faces of those who did not. The northern islands were a place people left. They did not arrive there for pleasure. They arrived to bury someone, or to be told whether or not they had to.

The telephone call had come three days before. A constable with a soft careful voice, the voice of a man who had been trained to deliver such things slowly so they could be absorbed in stages. A body had been recovered from the rocks below the lighthouse at Caill`. A man, between forty and fifty-five. No identification. But there had been, the constable said, a letter in the breast pocket, sealed against the water in a tobacco tin, and the letter had Niall's name on it, and an address that was eleven years out of date.

– We have not opened it, the constable said. – It seemed to belong to you.

– You think it's my brother.

– We think it might be. We have a man who knew him a little. But he is not certain. These things, the constable said, and let the sentence dissolve, because there was no end to it that would not have been a cruelty.

Niall had a brother named Edward who had gone north fourteen years ago and had not, in any sense that Niall could measure, come back. Edward had written perhaps four times. The handwriting had grown smaller in each letter, as though he were trying to take up less and less of the page, until the last one, which was a single line, and the single line had said: I am all right here, do not look for me. Niall had read it standing in the hallway of the house their mother had died in, and had understood that his brother was telling him something other than the words, but he had never been able to name what it was. He had folded the letter and put it in a book and the book had gone into a box and the box had gone, with the years, somewhere he no longer thought about.

The ferry's engine was a thing he felt in his teeth. Below him the water turned over and over in the dark, showing nothing, a long muscled blackness that the boat cut and the boat's wake healed. He thought of his brother walking out along the rocks below a lighthouse. He did not know whether Edward had walked or fallen or been brought there by the sea from somewhere else entirely. He found he did not want to know which. To know was to choose between three different brothers, and he was not ready yet to lose any of the three.

A girl came up onto the deck. She was perhaps twenty, with a scarf wound twice around her throat, and she came to the railing four or five feet from him and looked at the water as though she had expected to see something there and was disappointed.

– You'll freeze, she said, not to him exactly, to the dark in his direction.

– Yes.

– Most people stay inside.

– I know.

She did not go. She stood with her gloved hands on the cold rail and after a while she said, – I do this crossing every month. My grandmother is on the island. She is dying very slowly. I go to watch her not die. That's what it feels like.

– I'm sorry, said Niall.

– Don't be. She is ninety-one and she was unkind to my mother her whole life and now she is sweet to me, which I think is a way of apologising that costs her nothing. The girl laughed without any cruelty in it. – Why are you going?

He did not answer for long enough that an ordinary person would have let the question fall. But she only waited, watching the water, giving him the gift of her not looking at him.

– To find out whether someone is my brother, he said.

She turned then, and the deck light caught the side of her face, and she did not say any of the things people said, the I'm sorry or the oh how terrible. She said: – And if he is?

– Then I'll have lost him.

– And if he isn't?

– Then I'll have lost him a different way.

She nodded slowly, as though he had said something reasonable, something she had already known and was glad to hear confirmed. – My grandmother, she said, – I lose her every visit and find her again the next month. I have been losing her for six years. I think it would be easier if it happened once. He understood that she was offering him this, the way you offer a stranger half of what you are carrying, because the two of you are going the same dark way and will never see each other again. He was grateful for it in a manner that surprised him.

They stood together at the rail without speaking for a while. Then she said goodnight and went below, and he was alone with the wind and the engine and the long black sliding of the water, and he thought, for the first time in years, about the summer he and Edward had built a raft.

They had been boys. Eleven and nine, or twelve and ten. There had been a river behind the house, not deep but quick, and they had spent the whole of one July dragging fence posts and a door someone had thrown out and lashing them together with rope their father did not know was missing. Edward had been the elder and the careful one. He had insisted on testing the raft a hundred times in the shallows before he would let them take it into the current, and Niall had hated him for the caution, had called him a coward, had said terrible easy things the way younger brothers do. And then on the day they finally launched it the raft had come apart in the fast water below the bridge, and Niall had gone under, and it had been Edward, the careful one, the coward, who had come in after him without testing anything at all, who had got a fist into the collar of Niall's shirt and hauled him to the bank and held him there in the reeds while he coughed up half the river, and who had said, when Niall could breathe again, not are you all right but: don't tell. Don't tell them about the rope.

He had not told. That was the first secret he had ever kept, and he had kept it for his brother, and it occurred to him now, on the dark deck, that perhaps every secret a man keeps for someone is a kind of rope, a thing taken that no one knows is missing, holding two people together across a current. And Edward's last letter had been him cutting the rope. Do not look for me. He was untying himself, post by post, so that when he came apart in the fast water there would be no one downstream to come in after him.

Niall had not gone in after him. For fourteen years he had not gone in after him. He went below to get out of the wind.

The lounge smelled of vinyl and old coffee and the particular sweetness of children sleeping. The man in oilskins was playing cards with himself at a table, laying them out in long patient rows. Niall sat across the room and the man glanced up and after a time gathered his cards and came over without being asked and sat down opposite, as though they had agreed to it.

– You're the one for Caill`, the man said. – The constable's been telling everyone. Small island.

– Yes.

– I knew an Edward. The man said it carefully, watching Niall's face the way you watch the surface of water for what's moving underneath. – If it's the same. Came about, oh, a long time. Kept the light for a while when old Murdo's chest went. Lived out past the peats in the green hut. Didn't talk much. The man turned a card over and looked at it and turned it back. – Was a kind man, if it's the same. Mended things. Mended my outboard one winter and wouldn't take money for it. Said he had time and no use for money, which on this run is a thing you only say if you mean it.

Niall found that his throat had closed and he had to wait before he could speak. – Did he ever, he said, – did he ever say he had family.

The man considered this with the seriousness it deserved. – Once, he said. – Once only. We'd had a few. He said he had a brother somewhere who could swim. The man frowned, trying to be accurate, to give Niall the exact words and not the easier ones. – That's all. A brother who could swim. I thought it a strange thing to remember about a person.

Niall put his hand over his mouth. The man looked away to give him privacy, and turned his cards, and after a while he said, gently, to the table, – It might not be him, you know. A letter in a tin's an odd thing. Could be he gave it to someone. Could be a dozen things. I wouldn't, the man said, – I wouldn't go and bury him before you've seen him. He gathered his cards then and went back to his own table and laid them out again in their patient rows, and Niall sat alone and understood that the man had told him the most important thing of all, which was that his brother had not, in the end, untied every rope. A brother who could swim. He had kept that one. He had carried it out past the peats to the green hut and kept it all those years, the one thing that was Niall and not the silence Niall had let stand between them.

He must have slept, because when he opened his eyes the lounge was grey with the beginning of the light and the children were awake and quarrelling softly and the woman was pouring something from a flask. Through the salt-blinded windows he could see, low and dark and growing, the shape of the island, a long spine of rock with the white finger of the lighthouse at its northern end, very small, going round and round, its beam useless now in the coming day but turning anyway, the way the heart goes on with what it was set to do.

The constable was waiting at the pier. He was younger than his voice, with a face that had not yet learned to hide what it felt, and he held the tobacco tin in both hands as though it were warm.

– Mr Doran.

– Yes.

– Thank you for coming. I'm sorry it's for this. The constable held out the tin and Niall looked at it but did not take it. On the lid was the brand of a tobacco their father had smoked, a thing Niall had not seen in twenty years, and he understood that this small detail was either the proof that it was Edward or the proof that the dead carry the same small ordinary things as the living, and that he would not be able to tell which until he had looked at the face.

– I'd like to see him first, Niall said. – Before I open it.

The constable looked at him with something like respect. – The doctor's place, he said. – It's a short walk. We thought it kinder than the boat shed.

They walked up from the pier into the grey morning, the constable a half-step ahead, and the island opened around them in long slopes of wet green going up to the bare rock, and there were sheep on the slopes that lifted their heads to watch them pass, and a smell of the sea and of turf smoke and of something growing that Niall could not name. Out to the east, past a low ridge, he could see the dark stripes of the peat cuttings, and beyond them, he thought, though he could not see it, the green hut where his brother had lived and mended things and taken no money and said, once, after a few drinks, that he had a brother somewhere who could swim.

The doctor's house was white with a slate roof and a garden gone to seed. They went in through a side door into a cold room with a high small window, and there was a table in the room, and on the table a shape under a clean sheet, and the constable stopped at the door and said, – I'll be just outside, and went out, and Niall was alone with it.

He did not go to the table at once. He stood by the door and listened to his own heart and to the gulls outside and to the small sounds of the doctor's house settling in the cold, and he thought, absurdly, that he was not ready, that he had spent fourteen years not being ready, that the whole of his life had been an arrangement designed to ensure he would never have to stand in exactly this room and lift exactly this sheet. He thought of his brother holding him in the reeds. He thought of the rope. He thought: I came north. After all the years, I came in after you. It is too late, it is fourteen years too late, but I came.

He crossed the room and took the top of the sheet in both hands and folded it back.

He looked for a long time.

The face was a man's face, between forty and fifty-five, with a grey beard the sea had matted and then the doctor had combed, and a long nose and deep-set eyes that were closed, and the salt had cracked the lips and the cold had taken the colour out of everything, and Niall looked at it the way you read a letter in handwriting you knew once and have not seen in many years, the letters familiar and strange at once, the hand of someone you loved made foreign by time. He looked for the boy in it. He looked for the careful one, the one who tested the raft a hundred times, the one who came in without testing anything at all. He looked for his brother.

And he could not tell.

That was the thing no one had prepared him for, that he had imagined the moment as a door swinging one way or the other, yes or no, my brother or a stranger, found or lost, and instead there was this third country he had not known existed, this terrible blank country where he stood over a dead man's face and could not say. Fourteen years had taken Edward's face from him as surely as they had taken everything else. He had let his brother go so completely that now, at the last, he was not even able to recognise him. The sea had done less damage than the silence.

He reached out and touched the dead man's hand where it lay along his side. The hand was cold and the fingers were broad and the nails were broken and there was, on the back of the right hand, near the base of the thumb, a small white scar in the shape of a fishhook.

Niall stood very still.

He knew that scar. He had been there when it was made. They had been boys still, after the raft, and they had gone out one dawn to the river with a hook and a line their father did not know was missing, and Edward had set the hook badly into his own hand reaching to free a trout, and it had gone in deep and come out worse, and Niall had had to push it the rest of the way through, the way the older boy at school had told them you had to, and Edward had not made a sound, had only gone white and looked at his brother with absolute trust while Niall did the terrible necessary thing, and afterward there had been a small white scar in the shape of the hook, and Edward had said, don't tell, because their father would have known about the line. Don't tell. Another rope. Another thing taken that held two people together.

Niall held the cold hand in both of his and bowed his head and let the years come up through him like the river coming up through the gravel of the ford, and he wept, at last, for his brother, who had carried out to the green hut a brother who could swim, and a scar in the shape of a hook, and a tin of letters with a name that was eleven years out of date, all the small ropes that he had pretended to cut and had not cut, that had held even across the silence, even across the strait, even across death, holding the two halves of a life together like a map folded along an old crease so that the far places touch.

After a while the constable put his head in at the door and saw, and understood, and came in quietly and stood by him, and did not speak.

– It's him, Niall said. – It's my brother. He has a scar. We were boys.

– I'm very sorry, the constable said.

Niall drew the sheet back up, carefully, the way you cover a sleeping child, and then he was ready, and he turned and took the tobacco tin from the constable's hands and prised off the lid that his brother had sealed against the water. Inside, dry, folded small, was a single sheet of paper, and on it, in handwriting that had grown small over fourteen years and now stopped growing smaller forever, were not the words Niall had braced himself for, not an accusation, not an apology, not an explanation of the rocks below the light. There were only seven words, and they had been written, he understood, not on the day his brother walked out to the sea but long before, on some ordinary evening in the green hut past the peats, and kept ready, kept against the water, against the day a stranger or a brother might come and need to be told the one thing that mattered.

The seven words said: It was never your fault. Come swim.

Niall stood in the cold white room and read them, and read them again, and through the high small window the light from the lighthouse came round and went, came round and went, turning over the grey island in the grey morning the way the water had turned over all night in the dark, showing nothing, healing every wound it made, and he folded the paper along its old crease so that the far edges touched, and put it in his breast pocket, against his heart, where his brother had carried it, and went out into the morning to begin the long business of bringing him home.
