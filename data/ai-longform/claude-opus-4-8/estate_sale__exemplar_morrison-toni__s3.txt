The dishes went first, which Della had not expected. People crowded around the boxes on the porch like the bowls and saucers were warm and might cool if they waited. A woman in a sun hat held up the gravy boat, the one with the chip Mama had hidden by turning the bad side to the wall, and turned it over to read the bottom, and Della watched her thumb find the chip and decide it didn't matter. Two dollars. The woman paid Lonnie at the card table and carried it down the steps cradled against her chest like something she'd birthed.

It was a Saturday in October and the light came thin and yellow through the maple, the one Daddy planted the year before he left and never saw grow. Now it dropped its leaves on the heads of strangers. Della sat in the kitchen on a stool that would be sold by three o'clock and listened to her childhood being carried out the front door in pieces.

She had told herself she would stay in the kitchen. She had hired Lonnie and his daughter to do the selling because she could not bear to name prices for the things, to say a number out loud as if the chifforobe where her mother hung her good dress had a number, as if you could put a number on the smell that came out of it when you opened the door, cedar and powder and the ghost of a perfume nobody made anymore. So Lonnie said the numbers. Lonnie said five and people said three and Lonnie said four and that was that, the chifforobe going sideways through the door, the two men tilting it, one walking backward down the steps with his knees bent, and the smell came loose into the yard and was gone.

Don't, Della told herself. Don't do this all afternoon.

But she did. That was the thing nobody told you about it, that grief was not a wave but a tide, it came in and went out and came in, and you could not predict the hour of it. She had buried her mother in June and felt almost nothing, a clean numb space where the feeling should have been, and had been ashamed of that, had sat in the front pew at Mount Calvary in her navy suit and felt like a woman pretending to be a daughter. And now in October over a gravy boat with a chip in it the whole thing arrived.

A girl came into the kitchen. She was young, maybe twenty-five, with a baby on her hip and a man behind her who would not come all the way in, who stood in the doorway with his hands in his pockets the way men do when they are letting the woman want things.

"This for sale?" the girl said, meaning the table.

"Everything's for sale," Della said. It came out harder than she meant.

The girl ran her hand along the edge of the table and the baby reached for the salt shaker that wasn't there. It was a good table, oak, scarred. There was a burn near the middle from a pot Mama set down too fast the Thanksgiving the gas was nearly cut off, the year of the layoffs, when they ate what they ate and were glad. There was a place at the corner where Della had carved her initials when she was nine and gotten a whipping for it, not for the carving but for the lie she told about it, said her cousin Ree did it, and Ree got blamed and Della carried that small meanness in her for forty years, a splinter that never worked its way out. She put her hand over the initials now without deciding to.

"How much?" the girl said.

"Ask the man on the porch." Della stood up off the stool. "Twenty," she said, because she could not stop herself. "But I'll take fifteen."

The girl looked at the man in the doorway. The man nodded one inch.

"You sure?" the girl said, and Della understood the girl was asking something else, some kindness, having seen Della's face, and Della did not want it.

"It's just a table," Della said.

The girl carried the baby out and the man came in and crouched and ran his palm under the table feeling for the rail, and Della left them to it and went out the back, through the kitchen door into the yard where the clothesline still ran from the post to the corner of the garage, sagging, three wooden pins on it gone gray. She had hung wash on that line. Mama standing in the grass with a mouthful of pins, talking around them, telling Della to mind the hem dragging in the dirt, and the sheets snapping like sails, and the smell of them after, sun-smell, a smell you couldn't buy though they sold it now in bottles and lied about it on the label.

That bitch is dead, Della thought, and was so startled by the word in her own head that she put her hand to her mouth.

But there it was. It had been waiting under the grief the way a root waits under a stone. Her mother had not been kind. People at the funeral said *she was a strong woman* and *she didn't take any mess* and these were the kindest things they could find to say and they were true and they were not love. Mama had a hand that came fast. Mama had a tongue. Mama said to Della at thirteen, looking her up and down in the new dress for the spring program, *Well, you won't get by on your face,* and laughed, and meant it as instruction, meant it as a gift even, the gift of no illusions, and Della had carried that too, had built a whole life on the foundation of *you won't get by on your face,* had been smart instead, had been useful, had been the one who handled things, who paid the numbers, who said the hard true sentence in the meeting that nobody else would say, and had wondered all her life why men's eyes slid off her in a room.

She had not gotten by on her face. Mama had been right. That was the worst of it, that Mama had been right, and being right was not the same as being good to a child, and you could spend fifty years trying to make those two things be the same thing and they would not be.

A man's voice came around the side of the house. "Della? Lady wants the bedroom set."

The bedroom set. Della stood in the yard a moment with the dead clothesline and then went back in.

It was the woman in the sun hat, the gravy boat woman, come back, or never left, standing now at the foot of the stairs with the satisfied stillness of a buyer who has found the thing she came for without knowing she came for it. "The set upstairs," she said to Della. "The dark wood. Is the mirror going with it?"

"The mirror goes with it," Della said.

She went up the stairs ahead of the woman because she could not let the woman go up alone, into the room, her mother's room, where the bed was, the high bed Della had been born in, or so the story went, though Della doubted it, doubted all the stories now, suspected they had been told to her the way you tell a child anything, to make a shape it can hold. The dresser with the swing mirror. The mirror that had held her mother's face every morning of Della's life, her mother sitting at it pinning up her hair, the bone-handled comb, the jar of pomade, the face in the glass not looking at the glass but through it, somewhere past it, at something Della had spent her childhood trying to see and never could.

The woman in the sun hat went straight to the mirror and looked into it and adjusted her hat, and Della watched her mother's mirror hold a stranger's face, and the stranger smiled at herself, pleased, and it was the most ordinary thing in the world and Della wanted to take her by the collar.

"It's a beautiful piece," the woman said. "They don't make the joinery like this. You see how the drawers don't catch?" She pulled one out, slid it home. It was Mama's drawer, the top one, where the handkerchiefs were, and the photographs nobody was allowed to look at, and the gun.

The gun was gone. Della had taken the gun the day after the funeral, a little pearl-handled thing Mama kept wrapped in a stocking, and Della did not know to this day whether it was loaded all those years or whether it had ever been fired or what her mother thought would come up these stairs that she would need to meet with it. She had taken it and driven it out to the reservoir and thrown it in, an act she could not explain to herself, a thing she had needed to do and had done in secret like a crime, and stood at the rail watching the rings go out where it went under, and felt for one moment the loosest and freest she had felt in years.

"How much for the set?" the woman said.

"Make me an offer," Della said.

The woman named a number that was too low and Della took it, took it gladly, because the lower the number the more it pleased some bitter clerk inside her who was keeping the accounts. *You see, Mama,* the clerk said. *You see what your things go for. You see what it all came to.* The high bed for forty dollars. The mirror that ate your face every morning for forty dollars and the dresser thrown in.

She helped the men carry it. She had not meant to but when they got the dresser to the top of the stairs and one of them, the young one, lost his grip and the corner gouged the wall, Della put her own hands under it without thinking, the way you reach for a falling child, and so she was there, walking backward down her mother's stairs with the weight of the dresser pulling at her arms, the mirror swinging on its hinge and throwing the stairwell around the walls, ceiling, floor, ceiling, her own face flashing in it once, upside down, an old woman's face, *you won't get by on your face,* and then they were down and out the door and the dresser was in the bed of the woman's truck and a man was tying it with rope.

Della stood on the porch breathing.

Lonnie came over. He was a decent man; he had buried his own mother and he knew. "You doing all right?" he said.

"I'm doing fine."

"We're moving good. Most of the small stuff's gone. Got people asking about the piano."

The piano. "The piano's not for sale," Della said.

Lonnie looked at her. "It was on the list."

"Take it off the list."

He nodded and did not argue and went back to his table. Della did not know why she had said it. She could not keep the piano. She had no room for it and no way to move it and she did not play and never had; it was Mama who played, Sundays, hymns mostly, but sometimes in the evening when she thought no one heard she played other things, slow things, with her eyes closed, things Della never learned the names of, things that did not sound like any other part of her mother, and Della used to sit on the stairs in her nightgown and listen through the rails and feel that she was overhearing a stranger, a woman who lived inside her mother and only came out at the keyboard, a soft woman, a woman with all the hardness gone out of her hands.

She went in and stood by the piano. She opened the lid over the keys. The ivory was yellow and one key, middle C, had a brown stain on it from a cigarette her mother set there once and forgot, and the smell of the inside of the piano came up, old felt and dust and the metal smell of the strings, and Della pressed the key with the stain and the note came out true, still true after all these years, untuned but true, the note going out into the emptying house and dying.

She sat down on the bench.

She could not play. Her mother had not taught her. Her mother had taught her to be smart and to be useful and to expect nothing from her face, and had taught Ree's mother's children and the children at the church and the children of the woman she cleaned for, taught them their letters and their manners and the way to carry themselves, and had been generous to all of them with the part of herself that was hard and clean and useful, and had kept the soft part, the evening part, the piano part, for no one, for the empty room, for the dark.

*Why,* Della said, not out loud. *Why wouldn't you give me that one. Of everything you had. Why not that one part.*

And she heard, the way you hear a thing you have known a long time and refused, her mother's answer, which was not an answer, which was only the truth: that her mother had not had it to give. That the soft part was so small and so guarded that it could only survive in the dark, in the empty room, that to bring it into the light of a daughter's wanting would have killed it, the way you kill a thing from the deep water by bringing it up too fast. That her mother had loved her the only way she had, which was hard, which was *you won't get by on your face,* which was instruction, which was the truth told to a child to make her strong enough for a world that would not be kind, and that this was love, thin or thick Della could not say, but love, the only kind her mother had, scraped up out of her own childhood that Della knew almost nothing of, a girl come up from someplace south with nothing, with a hardness she had needed to survive and could not put down even for her own child, even for the one person she might have wanted to put it down for.

Maybe she did want to, Della thought. Maybe the piano in the evening was the wanting. Maybe that was the whole of it, all she could manage, to play the soft thing where Della could almost hear it and could never come close enough to touch.

A woman appeared in the doorway. Not the sun hat woman. A different one, older even than Della, in a man's cardigan, holding her purse with both hands.

"Is the piano for sale?" she said.

"No," Della said.

The woman nodded and looked at the piano with a longing Della recognized, the longing of someone who had once had a thing and lost it. "My sister had one like that," the woman said. "Same make. We grew up on a piano like that. She taught me, my sister did." She smiled, apologized with the smile. "Foolish. You can't carry the whole world out of one house."

"No," Della said. "You can't."

The woman stood a moment longer. "You play?" she said.

"No."

"Pity," the woman said, not unkindly, and went away, and Della heard her on the porch asking Lonnie about the lamps.

Della put her hands on the keys. She did not press them. She just laid her hands where her mother's hands had been, ten thousand evenings, and she found she was crying, finally, the tide all the way in now, over the dishes and the table and the dead clothesline and the gun in the reservoir and the mirror in the stranger's truck and the burn on the table from the year of the layoffs and the initials she carved and the whipping she took for the lie and the lie itself and Ree and the splinter that never came out, all of it, the whole emptying house running out of her at last through her two hands flat on the cold keys.

She sat until it passed. It passed. The light moved on the floor.

When she could, she stood up and closed the lid over the keys, gently, the way you close a coffin you have decided not to look in again, and she went out to the porch where Lonnie was making change for the cardigan woman, two lamps in a box at the woman's feet.

"The piano's for sale," Della said.

Lonnie looked up.

"Tell her," Della said, nodding at the cardigan woman. "Tell her she can have it. Whatever she'll pay. Tell her—" Della stopped. "Tell her her sister taught her right. Tell her to play it in the evenings."

Lonnie did not understand and did not need to. He told the woman, leaving out the last part, and the woman put her hand to her chest the way the gravy boat woman had, the way you do when something you didn't let yourself want is suddenly held out to you, and she said a number, a real number, more than the table and the bedroom set together, and Della said yes before Lonnie could bargain her up, said *yes, that's fine, that's plenty,* and went back into the house so she would not have to watch them carry it out.

She stood in the front room. It was nearly empty now. The afternoon was going. Through the front window she could see the maple her daddy planted dropping its leaves on the heads of strangers, and the truck with the bedroom set pulling away, and another truck pulling up, men getting out, looking at the house and seeing not a house but a thing to be emptied, which is what it was, which is what they were all for, finally, the houses, they held you and they let you go and they passed to strangers who could not read a single one of the marks you left in them, the burn, the carving, the chip turned to the wall.

She heard the men come in for the piano. She heard them sizing it up, the grunt and discussion of weight, the appraisal of the doorway and the steps. She did not turn around. She listened to her mother's piano going out the front door of the house her mother was born in or said she was, the legs of it knocking the frame, the strings humming once, low, all together, a sound the piano had never made in tune, a sound like a chord and like a moan, the whole instrument speaking at once as it was tipped through the door, and then the light changed in the room behind her as the doorway cleared, and it was gone, and the room had a new emptiness in it shaped exactly like a piano, an absence so precise she could have walked around it.

The cardigan woman would play it in the evenings. Della had decided that for her, had handed her the soft part of the dead house the way her mother never could hand her anything soft, and it was not the same, it was not her mother's hands at the keys, but it was the closest Della could come to forgiveness, which was to give the one tender thing away to a stranger who would use it tenderly, who would sit in some other small room in some other small town and play the slow evening songs and not know whose grief was in the felt and the wood.

Maybe that was all forgiveness ever was. Not a feeling. A thing you did with your hands. You took the tender thing you had been denied and instead of breaking it you gave it on, to whoever would have it, and the giving was the whole of it, the giving was the only soft part you were able to manage, scraped up out of your own hard childhood and handed across in the failing light.

Della went and stood on the porch. The leaves came down. Lonnie was folding up his card table; the afternoon was nearly over, the crowd thinned to two or three who picked over the last of the small things, a cake stand, a footstool, a box of mismatched silver. The house behind her was almost empty and would be sold next, the house itself, to a stranger, and Della would sign the papers and drive back to the city and that would be the end of it, the end of the long account between her and her mother, the books closed, nobody owing anybody anything anymore.

But the books did not close. That was the lie of it. She knew it standing there. The accounts ran on whether you wanted them to or not, the dead kept their hold, you carried the house out of the house in yourself and there was no estate sale for that, no card table, no man saying numbers. You carried it. *You won't get by on your face,* her mother said, forever, and Della, fifty-three years old on the porch of the emptied house, said back to her at last, out loud, to the maple and the falling leaves and the strangers carrying the last of it down the steps, the thing she had never once been able to say to the living woman:

"I know it, Mama. You were right. I never did."

And then, after a moment, the harder thing, the true thing, the thing that cost her:

"But I wish you'd lied to me. Just the once."

The leaves came down. Nobody answered. Nobody was ever going to answer, and that, Della understood, standing in the last of the light, was the whole of what it meant to be the one left. You said it to the air. You said it to the tree your father planted and never saw grow. And the air took it, the way the reservoir took the gun, the rings going out and out and the surface closing over, smooth, as if nothing had ever gone down.
