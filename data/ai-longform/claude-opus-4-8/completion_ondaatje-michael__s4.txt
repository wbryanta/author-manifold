Anil watched her from the edge of the pit, the trowel idle in her hand. She did not try to speak to the woman. There was nothing useful she could have offered, and besides, the woman had retreated into a silence that seemed less like absence than a kind of fullness, the way a room can be crowded with what is no longer in it. The translator, Ramon, did not approach either. They had learned, all of them, the etiquette of these places, which was not really etiquette at all but a recognition of limits. You did not interrupt a person's last conversation with the dead.

The team resumed its work. Anil knelt at the eastern end of the grave, where the larger of the two skeletons lay, the one they believed to be the husband. The bones had been in the earth long enough to take on the color of the soil, that ochre that stained everything here, that got into the creases of your hands and under your nails and would not wash out. She brushed the dirt away from the orbital ridge with a soft sable brush, the same kind a watercolorist might use, and the skull came clearer, the long architecture of the face that had once held a particular arrangement of muscle and skin, a particular smile the woman behind her had loved.

She was careful. She was always careful, but here, with the woman watching, she was conscious of being watched in a way that changed the quality of her attention. In the laboratory in Oklahoma, in the controlled fluorescence of the examination room, a body was a problem to be solved, a sequence of questions whose answers were written in calcium. Here the body was still somebody. The transition between these two states was the thing she had not yet learned to manage, and she suspected she never would, that no one did, that the people who claimed to had simply stopped looking.

By midafternoon they had exposed both skeletons fully, lying side by side in the position the woman had described without describing it, the position of men sleeping. The smaller one, the brother, had a fracture across the left parietal that Anil noted in her book and photographed from three angles. Blunt force, antemortem or perimortem, she could not yet say. The husband's hands had been bound; the wrists showed the characteristic crossing, and though the rope had long since rotted, the bones held the memory of constraint, the slight rotation that told you a man had died with his arms behind his back. She wrote this down too. She did not look at the woman when she wrote it.

That evening, in the small hotel in the town an hour's drive from the site, Anil sat on the balcony with a beer going warm in her hand and the report half written on her knee. The mountains went blue and then black. Somewhere a dog began barking and others answered it, a relay of dogs across the valley, and then they stopped, all at once, as if a hand had been raised. She thought about the woman's face, which she had told herself she would not forget, and was already aware of the work memory was doing to it, smoothing and abstracting it, turning it into a representative face, the face of grief, when what had struck her was precisely that it had not been representative at all. It had been one woman's, particular and unshareable. The bones could be measured. The face could not. This, she thought, was the whole problem of her profession compressed into a single afternoon.

She had been in Guatemala fourteen months. Before that, Arizona. Before that, the long apprenticeship, the years of school, the slow accumulation of a competence that had taken her, by degrees she had not entirely chosen, very far from the place she had been born. When people asked where she was from she had developed a way of answering that closed the conversation rather than opening it. She said the names of cities. She did not say the name of the island.

The call from Geneva came two weeks later. She took it in the office of the regional coordinator, a Belgian named Clyne who chain-smoked and kept a fan going even in the cool of morning so that the papers on his desk were pinned down by stones, a habit she found she could not see without thinking of the plastic sheeting over the graves. The Centre for Human Rights wanted her. There was a program, jointly run, a forensic component attached to a larger investigation. The government of her own country had, under international pressure, agreed to permit it. Agreed was perhaps too strong a word. Had been unable to prevent it without a cost it was not willing to pay. They wanted someone with her qualifications. They wanted, she understood without its being said, someone who could be presented as both an outsider and a native, a person the government could not dismiss as a foreigner ignorant of local conditions and could not embrace as one of its own.

"It is your country," Clyne said, when she had been silent for a while. He said it without inflection, a statement of fact, and tapped ash into a saucer.

"It was," she said.

He shrugged, which meant he did not see the distinction, and perhaps he was right not to.

She flew out of Guatemala City with the bones of the husband and the brother filed and labeled behind her, their case still open, the woman still presumably moving from excavation to excavation in the highlands, the possibility of her dead everywhere. Anil thought of her on the plane, somewhere over the dark Pacific, and tried to feel that she was leaving one set of obligations for another rather than abandoning the first, but it was not a feeling she could manufacture, and she let it go and watched the small painted plane on the seatback screen creep across the curve of the world toward the island.

She had not been back in fifteen years.

The heat at the airport was the first thing, the heat she had forgotten she knew, that came at her through the doors of the plane before she had even seen anything, a wet weight that her body recognized before her mind did, the way you recognize a smell from childhood and are returned bodily to a room you could not have described a moment before. The officials were polite. They had her name on a list. They knew who she was and why she had come, and their politeness had an edge to it that she catalogued and filed away, the politeness of people performing a cooperation they resented. She was given papers. She was assigned, she learned, a partner from the local side, an archaeologist named Sarath Diyasena who would accompany her and, she did not have to be told, would also report on her. She accepted this. It was the condition of the work. You took the watcher with the access.

Sarath met her at the institute two days later. He was older than she had expected, in his late forties perhaps, with a stillness about him that she could not immediately read. He did not smile when he shook her hand. He had a way of looking at her that was assessing without being unfriendly, the look of a man deciding how much trouble she was going to be. His English was perfect, university English, with an accent that placed him somewhere she could not have placed him, English plus something. He had studied abroad, she learned, and come back, which made him in her eyes either a fool or a man with a reason, and she did not yet know which.

"You worked in Guatemala," he said. It was not a question.

"Yes."

"And before that."

"Other places."

He nodded as if she had told him something. "You will find this place is not those places," he said. "The difficulty here is not the digging. The difficulty here is what the digging means."

She thought he was warning her and she did not yet understand the warning, but she remembered it later, many times, that first dry statement of his that she had taken for an old man's portentousness and that turned out to be the most honest thing anyone said to her in those first weeks.

They were given an archaeological reserve to work in, a government-controlled area in the south where excavations of sixth-century remains were ongoing, a region of caves and old monastic sites. The arrangement was transparent in its cynicism. The reserve was off-limits to the public, guarded, and the government had chosen it precisely because nothing recent could be found there—or so the government believed. Anil understood the logic. By giving the international team a site of ancient burials, the government could demonstrate cooperation while ensuring that the cooperation produced nothing. Let them dig up the dead of fifteen hundred years ago. Let them count the bones of monks. There was no embarrassment in a sixth-century skeleton.

She and Sarath drove south. The road ran along the coast for a while, the sea flat and metallic to their right, fishing boats drawn up on the sand, and then turned inland through paddy and then through scrub, the land drying as it rose. Sarath drove. He did not talk much. When he did talk it was to point out things—a tank, an old irrigation reservoir built by a king whose name meant nothing to her, a rock with a Brahmi inscription that he said no one had properly read. He spoke of the past with an ease and an intimacy that she found she envied, the way he could reach back a thousand years and produce a king, a queen, a date, as if these were members of his family. She could not do this. Her own past, the personal one, the fifteen years she had been away and the eighteen before that, was a country she had emigrated from and could not now re-enter without a visa she did not possess.

The site itself was a complex of caves in a hillside, the rock pale and the drip ledges cut by hand by monks long dead, and below the caves a series of graves the previous teams had begun to open. There were three skeletons exposed, lying in the manner of monastic burials, and a fourth they had not yet reached. They set up. They worked. For days they worked, and the work was good, the careful methodical work she loved, the recovery of the long dead, the reading of the bones. The three skeletons were old. Anil knew it within an hour of examining the first, knew it from the mineralization, the color, the condition of the cortex. These were the bones the government wanted them to find, the safe dead, the dead of an era no living person could be blamed for.

It was the fourth body that was the problem.

They reached it on the sixth day. Sarath was the one who first saw what it was. He had been clearing the soil from above it, working down to the level of the others, expecting the same, and Anil heard him stop, heard the change in the rhythm of his trowel, the silence of a man who has found something he was not looking for. She came over. The bones were there, in the same posture as the others, laid out as a monk would have been laid out fifteen hundred years before, in the same grave, at the same level, surrounded by the same soil.

But these bones were not old.

She knew it the way she had known the others were old, immediately and certainly, before any test, from the color and the condition and a dozen small signs that she could not have enumerated but that her hands and eyes had learned over years until the knowledge had sunk below the level of language. This man had died recently. Within the last five years. Perhaps fewer. The bones were the color of life, not the color of the soil. And he had been buried here, in this protected place, in this ancient grave, among the old dead, where no one would think to look and no one was permitted to look, by people who had access to a government archaeological reserve.

She did not say any of this aloud. She looked at Sarath and Sarath looked at her, and she saw that he had understood it as quickly as she had, and that he understood, too, what it meant, all of it, the layers of it, in a way that she, the outsider, the returned native who was not a native, could only begin to grasp. Someone had used a government-protected site, a site no one could enter without permission, to hide a recent murder. Which meant that whoever had buried this man had had that permission. Which meant the state.

"We should cover it," Sarath said quietly.

"We should examine it."

"We should cover it and decide what to do."

They stood on opposite sides of the small grave with the four skeletons in it, three of them old and innocent and one of them a piece of evidence that, if it was what Anil believed it to be, could not be allowed to exist by the very people who had granted them this site to keep them busy and harmless. The afternoon light came in long under the drip ledge. Somewhere below the hill a bird she did not know the name of called twice and was silent. She thought of the woman in the grave in Guatemala, the grief in that shoulder. She thought that she had crossed an ocean to find the same thing she had left, that the dead were the same everywhere, that the only difference was who had killed them and how much it cost to say so.

"His name," she said. "We have to find out who he is."

Sarath looked at her for a long time. There was something in his face she would spend the next weeks trying to read, a wariness that she took at first for cowardice and later understood was something more complicated, the caution of a man who lived here, who would still live here after she had flown back to her other life, who knew the exact weight of what she was proposing in a way she did not.

"You don't understand this place," he said. It was the second time he had told her this, and this time there was no portentousness in it at all, only a kind of weariness. "You think finding the name is the end. Here the name is the beginning. Do you know what happens to people who become beginnings?"

"Then we don't tell anyone yet," she said. "We move him. We study him somewhere else."

"Move him."

"He's evidence. He doesn't belong in a sixth-century grave. We have a mandate. We can remove remains for examination."

"And if they ask what remains?"

"We're examining four sets of remains from a monastic burial complex."

He almost smiled then. It was not quite a smile but it was the nearest thing she had seen from him, a brief acknowledgment that she was perhaps not as naive as he had assumed, that she had grasped at least the shape of the deception they would have to perform. Three old monks and a fourth body the records would say was old too, and only the two of them would know otherwise, and only the two of them, and whoever had put him there, would care.

They worked until the light went, and then they covered the grave with the plastic sheeting and weighted it with stones, and Anil thought of the families in Guatemala lifting the sheeting at dawn, keeping their vigil, and thought that here there would be no vigil, that whoever had loved this man did not know where he was, would never have thought to look in a sixth-century monastic cemetery in a government reserve, would be like the woman in the highlands traveling from grave to grave with the possibility of her dead everywhere, except that this dead man's family, if he had one, did not even have the highlands to search.

That night they did not stay in a hotel. There was a rest house near the site, a colonial building gone slightly to ruin, and they took two rooms, and Anil sat up late as she had sat up in Guatemala, only this time there was no balcony and no valley of dogs, only a small window with a torn screen and beyond it the dark mass of the hill with the caves in it and the four bodies under their plastic.

She got out the bones she had brought up, the small ones, the ones that could be carried, the hand bones and a section of the man's foot, and laid them on a clean cloth on the table under the single bulb. She did this not because she needed to but because handling them calmed her, because the work was the one thing she trusted, the one language in which she was fluent without ambiguity. She turned the small bones in the light. The man had walked a great deal in his life; the foot told her that, the slight remodeling of the metatarsals, the stress the body writes into itself over years. He had done physical work. He had been, she thought, somewhere between twenty-eight and thirty-five at the time of his death, though she would want the pelvis and the skull to be sure. And there was something else, a thing she had noticed at the site and now confirmed in the lamplight, a small healed fracture in one of the bones of the right hand, old, well-knit, the kind of injury a man might get from a fall or a fight in his youth and carry forgotten for the rest of his life.

It was nothing. It was a detail. But it was the kind of detail that, multiplied, became a person, and a person, unlike a body, could be missed, could be searched for, could be named. She wrote it down. The healed fracture, the right fourth metacarpal, the angle of it, the degree of remodeling. She gave the man, in her notebook, a working name, because she could not keep writing the fourth body, and because to give a thing a name was to begin to admit that it had once had one.

She called him Sailor. She did not know why that word came to her. Perhaps because of the foot, the walking, the going far. Perhaps because she was thinking of the sea she had flown across, the ocean between her two lives. She wrote it at the top of the page. Sailor. And then she sat with the small bones in the lamplight and the moths coming in through the torn screen and circling the bulb and falling, and she thought that she had come back to find one man among the thousands the country had lost, and that this was either the most absurd ambition or the only one that mattered, and that she did not yet know which, and that perhaps the two were the same thing.

In the morning Sarath was already up, drinking tea on the verandah, watching the hill. He did not ask her how she had slept. He had the look of a man who had also not slept, who had also sat up with the thing they had found, turning it over.

"There is a man," he said, without preamble, when she sat down beside him with her own cup. "If we are going to do this. There is a man we might go to."

"What kind of man?"

"He was a doctor. He worked in the field hospitals during the worst of it, in the north and then in the south. He saw more bodies than any forensic team. He left the work. He drinks now, mostly. But he knows things. He knows how the killing was done, in different places, in different years. The methods change. Each group, each period, has a signature, if you can read it. He can read it." Sarath drank his tea and looked at the hill. "If anyone can tell us when your Sailor died, and by whom, it is him."

She noticed that he had said your Sailor, that the name had already passed between them and been accepted, that the fourth body had become, in a single night, a person they were both responsible for. She noticed too that Sarath had decided something while she slept, that the wariness of the day before had resolved, not into trust exactly, but into a kind of commitment, the commitment of a man who has weighed the cost and chosen to pay it for reasons of his own that he had not told her and perhaps never would.

"Then we go to him," she said.

Sarath was silent for a moment. The morning light moved up the hill toward the dark mouths of the caves. Down in the trees the unnamed bird called its two notes again and was answered, this time, by another, so that the call went back and forth across the valley the way the dogs had answered each other in Guatemala, a relay of living things in the early light, indifferent to the dead under the plastic on the hill.

"You understand," Sarath said finally, "that once we know, we cannot un-know. And that knowing here is not safe. In Guatemala the families wanted the truth. Here there is no family waiting at the grave. There is only the government, and the government does not want the truth, and the people are too frightened to want it, and we will be two people holding a thing that everyone with any power would prefer did not exist." He turned and looked at her, and she saw that he was giving her, deliberately and for the last time, the chance to refuse, to declare the fourth body old, to write the report the government wanted and fly home to her other life with her hands clean. "Do you want to know who he was?"

She thought of the woman in the highlands, the grief in her shoulder, the men sleeping side by side on the mat of the grave. She thought that to know a name was the smallest possible act of justice and also, perhaps, the largest, the only one that was ever fully in your power, the one act no government could entirely prevent if you were stubborn enough and the bones survived. She thought that she had not crossed an ocean to write a lie.

"Yes," she said. "I want to know."

Sarath nodded slowly, as if she had confirmed something he had hoped and feared in equal measure, and set down his cup, and stood, and went to make the arrangements, and Anil sat alone on the verandah in the climbing light, watching the hill where Sailor lay among the old dead waiting to be given back his name.
