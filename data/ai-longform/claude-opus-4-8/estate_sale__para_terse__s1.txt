The folding table came from the garage, and Diane set it up just inside the front door because that was where the woman from the estate company told her to put it, though it seemed wrong to her, blocking the entrance like a checkpoint, as if the people coming in to buy her mother's things needed to be processed first. She had a metal cash box that did not lock and a roll of orange stickers and a pad of receipts that nobody would ask for. It was nine in the morning and already the heat was coming up off the driveway in the way it did in late August, the cicadas going in the oaks along Sherman Street, and the first car had parked at the curb before she'd even gotten the door propped open with the brick her mother had always used, the one painted to look like a ladybug by some grandchild's hand thirty years ago, though which grandchild Diane could no longer say.

The estate woman, whose name was Patrice, had done all the real work in the days before. She had gone through the house with her clipboard and her practiced eye and put little prices on everything, on the green glassware and the nesting bowls and the bedroom suite and the cedar chest, on the mahogany dining set that had been Diane's grandmother's before it was her mother's, and Diane had agreed to all of it because she did not know how to disagree, did not know what a thing was worth or what it should cost a stranger to take it away. Patrice had said she would come by around noon to check on things but that Diane would be fine, that estate sales mostly ran themselves, that the dealers came first and early and were rude but quick, and then the regular people came, the ones looking for a bargain or a memory or a side table, and by four it would mostly be over and they could see what was left.

So Diane sat at the folding table with the cash box and watched a man in a fishing vest walk directly to the kitchen as if he had been there before, as if he knew the house, and she heard him opening the cabinets, the particular sound of those cabinet doors that she had not heard in years but recognized instantly, the slight stick and give of the one above the stove that had never hung right. He came back with an armful of the Pyrex, the set with the wheat pattern, and he set it on her table and said, "These all here? The lids?"

"I think so," Diane said. "I don't actually know."

He looked at her then, a quick assessing look, and seemed to understand something, and his manner softened by a degree. "You the daughter?"

"Yes."

"Sorry for your loss," he said, in the way people said it, the words worn smooth as river stones, and he counted out twelve dollars in ones and quarters and carried the bowls out to his truck, and that was the first thing gone, the wheat-pattern Pyrex in which her mother had made the same broccoli casserole for every holiday of Diane's life, the one with the crushed Ritz crackers on top, and Diane had not even tasted it in twenty years and had complained about it when she was young, had called it bland, had wanted the food other families ate, and now it was going down the front walk in the arms of a man in a fishing vest and she had taken twelve dollars for it.

She had flown in from Seattle on Thursday. Her brother Mark could not come; he was in Arizona and his wife was having surgery, a real reason, a good one, and Diane did not begrudge him though some part of her had wanted to. They had spoken on the phone and agreed it made sense to sell, that neither of them was going to move back to Ohio, that the house was the largest asset and the market was decent and there was no sense paying to store furniture that no one wanted. It had all been so reasonable on the phone. Mark had said, "You're better at that stuff anyway," which was not true but which she had let stand because it was easier than the alternative, and now she was the one sitting at the folding table while strangers carried out the furniture of her childhood, and Mark was in Arizona holding his wife's hand in a recovery room, and both of those things were happening at once and she could not decide which of them she would rather be doing.

By ten the house was full of people. They moved through the rooms with a frank acquisitiveness that she found at first appalling and then, gradually, restful, because at least they wanted things, at least the things were wanted. Two women in their sixties went through the linen closet and exclaimed over the embroidered pillowcases, and Diane wanted to tell them that her mother had not embroidered those, that her mother could not sew a button, that the pillowcases had come from her great-aunt and had sat in that closet unused for fifty years, but she said nothing, because what did it matter, and the women paid four dollars for the pair and were delighted, and perhaps they would use them, perhaps they would actually sleep on the pillowcases that her mother had only ever kept for a guest who never came.

A young couple bought the kitchen table. They were maybe twenty-five, and the woman was pregnant, and they stood in the kitchen for a long time discussing whether it would fit in their place, and the man kept measuring it with his hands, spreading his arms, and Diane watched them from her station by the door and thought about how many mornings she had sat at that table eating cereal and watching the snow come down on the backyard, how her mother had stood at that counter in her quilted robe with her coffee, how once, when Diane was sixteen and had said something cruel, something about wanting to leave this town and never come back, her mother had set down her coffee at that table and looked at her for a long moment and said, "You will, honey. That's what it's for," and Diane had not understood then whether her mother meant the town or the leaving or what, and she had never asked, and now she never could.

The couple decided it would fit. The man came up to the table and paid the thirty-five dollars Patrice had marked it at, and he was apologetic somehow, embarrassed by his own good fortune, and he said, "It's a really nice table," and Diane said, "My mother had it forty years," and he said, "Oh," and looked stricken, and she was sorry she had said it, because it was not his fault, it was nobody's fault, it was only that the table was thirty-five dollars and her whole childhood and those two facts were standing in the same kitchen and refusing to reconcile. The man and the woman carried it out between them, the woman walking backward and laughing when she bumped the doorframe, and Diane heard them out in the driveway negotiating it into a pickup with bungee cords, the cheerful improvised physics of young people who own almost nothing.

She drank the coffee she had brought in a paper cup from the gas station because there was nothing in the house to make it with, the coffeemaker having sold the day before, Patrice told her, for two dollars. She watched a man buy her father's tools out of the garage, the whole rolling chest of them, and her father had been dead eleven years and the tools had been sitting out there going to rust and she was glad, genuinely glad, that they would be used, that someone would fix something with them. She watched a teenage girl, dragged along by her mother, suddenly stop in front of the bookcase and pull out a paperback and stand reading it with the total absorption that teenagers gave to the thing they were not supposed to be doing, and the girl bought the book for fifty cents, and it was a copy of Rebecca that had been Diane's, that she had read at exactly that age in exactly the posture, standing up, unable to wait, and she almost said something to the girl and then did not, because some things you should not interrupt.

Around eleven a heavyset man in a polo shirt came in and walked the rooms with a different kind of attention, the dealer's attention, and he stopped in front of the dining set, her grandmother's mahogany set, and he ran his hand along the top of the table and crouched to look at the legs and turned over one of the chairs to read whatever was stamped underneath. He came to her table and said, "The dining set. What's the bottom on that?"

"I'm sorry?"

"The ticket says one twenty-five. I'll give you seventy for the whole thing, table and six chairs and the hutch."

"Patrice marked it at one twenty-five," Diane said.

"Patrice marks high. She always marks high. It's veneer, it's chipped on the one leaf, the market for brown furniture is dead, nobody wants it. Seventy and I take it today, I got my guy with the truck."

Diane looked at the dining set across the room, at the table where she had eaten Thanksgiving for forty-five years, where her mother had sat at one end and her father at the other, where Mark had announced his engagement and where they had sat after both funerals, her father's and now soon, in a way, her mother's, though the funeral was already done, the small service on Tuesday, and this was only the after, the dispersal. One twenty-five was nothing. Seventy was nothing. The difference between them was nothing, fifty-five dollars, less than she would spend on dinner in Seattle, and yet she heard herself say, "No. I think one twenty-five."

The man looked at her, and there was no cruelty in his face, only the patience of someone who did this every day. "It's not going to sell at one twenty-five. I'm telling you that as a courtesy. You'll be sitting here at four o'clock with a dining set and I'll be gone."

"That's all right," Diane said.

He shrugged and gave her a card she did not want and said to call him if she changed her mind, and he left, and Diane sat with her hand flat on the cool metal of the cash box and did not understand why she had done it, why she had defended the table against the only person all day who knew what it was actually worth, which was almost nothing, which was seventy dollars, and she thought that maybe she had wanted him to be wrong, wanted there to be someone who would walk in and see the table the way she saw it, who would understand that it was not brown furniture and dead market and veneer but the actual surface against which her family had occurred, and that no such person existed, that the table to everyone alive was simply a table, and that she herself, if she were honest, did not want it, could not take it, had no room for it and no dining room to put it in, and the truth was she wanted neither to keep it nor to sell it, she wanted it to stay in this house in this town the way it had always been, with her mother coming in to set it, and that was the one thing not for sale, the one thing not on Patrice's clipboard, and so she had said one twenty-five to a man because there was nothing else she could say.

A woman about her own age came in around noon, alone, and moved through the house slowly, and Diane noticed her because she was not shopping, exactly. She was looking. She stood in the living room for a while just looking at the room, and then she came over to the table, and she had not picked up anything to buy, and she said, "Did the Hendersons live here?"

"Yes," Diane said. "That was my parents."

"I thought so. I'm Karen Voss, I grew up over on Linden. I used to come here for piano." She laughed a little. "Your mother gave me lessons. Mrs. Henderson. I was terrible. I came every Wednesday for like three years and I was just terrible, and she never once made me feel bad about it."

"I didn't know she gave lessons that long," Diane said. "I was already gone by then, I think."

"This would've been the eighties. There were a bunch of us. She had this jar of butterscotch candies and you got one after, win or lose." Karen smiled. "I haven't thought about that in years. I just saw the sign and I thought, I have to go in. I'm sorry, I'm not buying anything, I just wanted to—" She gestured vaguely at the house. "She was a really nice lady. I'm sorry."

"Thank you," Diane said, and she meant it, and after Karen Voss left, having taken nothing, having only looked, Diane sat very still for a while, because she had not known about the butterscotch candies, had not known her mother had a thing she did every Wednesday that made a stranger drive in off the street thirty years later, and it occurred to her with a force that surprised her that her mother had been a whole person here, a person to other people, a person who existed in the memories of Karen Voss from Linden Street in a way that Diane, who had left at eighteen and visited at holidays and called on Sundays, did not fully have access to, and that all of that, all of those Wednesdays and butterscotch candies and terrible piano students, were now also being carried out the front door, except no one was carrying them, they were just going, evaporating, and there was no folding table for them and no orange stickers and no price.

Patrice arrived a little after twelve as promised and surveyed the diminished house with satisfaction and told Diane she was doing great, just great, and that the morning take looked good. She straightened some of the displays and moved the remaining glassware to a more prominent spot and said the afternoon would be slower. She noticed the dining set still there and said, "Nobody bit on that?"

"A man offered seventy," Diane said.

"You should've taken it," Patrice said, not unkindly. "Brown furniture. Nobody wants it anymore. Their grandparents had it." She said it the way the dealer had said it, as a known fact about the world, and Diane understood that this was simply true, that there had been a time when a mahogany dining set meant you had arrived, that her grandmother had saved for it and her mother had been proud of it and that the entire meaning of the thing had drained out of it within a single generation, that meaning was not a property of objects but a thing loaned to them by people, and withdrawn.

The afternoon was slower. The sun moved around to the front of the house and came in hot through the windows where the curtains had already sold, and Diane took off her cardigan, and a few people drifted in and bought small things, a lamp, a set of TV trays, the contents of the junk drawer for a dollar, which a man wanted for the parts, the picture hooks and the rubber bands and the dead batteries and the single key to nothing. She sold the bird feeder off the back porch. She sold the welcome mat, which seemed to her the strangest thing of all, that someone would buy the WELCOME off the front step while she was still standing in the doorway, but the woman who bought it was perfectly nice and Diane took the dollar.

At some point she got up and walked through the house, leaving the cash box on the table because there was no one to watch it and she had stopped caring, and she went through the rooms while they were briefly empty, the rooms getting barer by the hour, the rectangles on the walls where pictures had hung, the dents in the carpet where furniture had stood for decades, the particular quality of light in her old bedroom which was now empty entirely, just the closet with the height marks she had not known were still there, pencil lines on the doorjamb with dates, Diane and Mark, going up the years, and the highest one was hers, age fourteen, after which she had stopped letting her mother measure her, had thought herself too old, and her mother had simply stopped and never painted over the marks, had kept them there in the closet of a room no one used for forty years, and Diane stood in the empty room and put her hand on the pencil line at the top, fourteen, and she was fifty-three now and her mother was dead and the line was exactly where she had left it.

She thought she would cry then but she did not. She had cried on the plane, unexpectedly, into a cocktail napkin, and she had cried at the service, and she found now that she was somewhere past it, in the dry country on the other side, where things were very clear and very quiet. She thought about the pencil marks. She could not take the doorjamb. She could not take the room. She had her phone in her pocket and she took a picture of the marks, the flash going off in the dim closet, and the photo when she looked at it was not the same, was nothing, was a picture of some pencil on some wood, and she put the phone away.

The dealer in the polo shirt did not come back. At about three-thirty a different couple looked at the dining set for a long time and Diane watched them with a hope she was ashamed of, and they decided against it, and at four o'clock the dining set was still there, her grandmother's table and six chairs and the hutch, marked at one twenty-five, exactly as the man had predicted, and Diane sat at her folding table and looked at it across the nearly empty room and felt that she had won something and lost something and could not tell which was larger.

Patrice came back to close out the sale. She counted the cash box and wrote Diane a figure on the receipt pad and explained the commission and said the leftover items would be picked up by a service on Monday, donated or hauled, and that Diane didn't need to worry about any of it, the house would be empty by Tuesday and broom-clean for the closing. She was brisk and kind and she had done this a thousand times. She looked at the dining set and said, "You want me to have the haulers take that too? Or—" and she left it open, because she had been doing this long enough to know that sometimes there was an or.

Diane looked at the table.

"No," she said. "I'll arrange to ship it."

Patrice raised her eyebrows just slightly, the cost of shipping a dining set from Ohio to Seattle being, she clearly knew, many times its value, being more than one twenty-five and seventy combined and then combined again, but she only said, "Sure. I can give you the number of a company that does it. They crate it and everything."

"Thank you," Diane said.

And she did not know, even as she said it, whether she would actually do it, whether the table would arrive in Seattle and stand in her condominium that had no dining room, whether she would eat at it or whether it would become a surface for mail and folded laundry, a brown island in a life that had no use for it, and whether in twenty years her own niece, Mark's daughter, would stand at a folding table somewhere and sell it for seventy dollars to a man with a truck, and have to decide whether to say it was a really nice table.

But she could not let it go down the walk today. That was all she knew. The Pyrex and the books and the tools and the welcome mat, all of it could go, all of it was already gone, but not the table, not today, not while she was still standing in the doorway. She signed Patrice's pad and took the check and gathered her cardigan and the empty paper cup, and she carried the ladybug brick in from where it had propped the door all day, and she set it on the kitchen counter where her mother used to keep it, and then she stood for a moment in the kitchen that no longer had a table in it, in the late gold light, and then she went out and pulled the front door shut behind her, and it stuck slightly and gave, the way it always had, and then it was closed.
