The water had quit in the night and by morning the lower trees stood in their dry furrows like men waiting on a verdict and the brothers came down through the rows with the sun not yet over the eastern ridge and the light gray and provisional upon the land.

They walked the line where it ran along the headditch and the older one carried a shovel across his shoulder and the younger one a coil of pipe and a canvas bag of fittings that clinked with his stride. The grass at the ditchbank was wet and they wet themselves to the knee passing through it and neither spoke. The trees went away from them in their long converging files toward the hills and the limbs hung with fruit not yet come to color and some of the trees were dead and stood among the living with their gray arms lifted.

Where the main split off toward the south block the older brother stopped and set the shovel blade in the ground and leaned on it and looked at the dry earth where the water should have stood.

Here, he said.

The younger one set down his coil.

You can hear it when it goes, the older one said. You wake up and somethin aint right and you dont know what and then you know its the quiet. The water makes a sound all night you dont even hear till it stops.

I never heard it.

You never woke up for it.

The younger one crouched and put his hand flat to the soil and it was dry to the heel of his palm and dry under that. He looked off down the rows.

Whole south block went dry, he said.

Most of a day theyll be all right. They been worse.

They been dead is what some of em been.

The older brother didnt answer this. He took up the shovel and stepped down into the ditch and began to clear the silt from where the headgate seated and the silt came away black and stinking of the buried summers and he worked without hurry and the younger one watched him.

You want to know what I think, the younger one said.

I know what you think. You been thinkin it loud enough I dont need you to say it.

Then I wont.

Good.

But he stood there and did not go and after a while he said it anyway. We could sell it. The whole of it. Take what they offer and be done.

The older brother went on digging. The light had come up the eastern ridge now and stood along the tops of the trees and the dead trees and the living all gilded the same and indistinguishable in that first light so that a man could not have told the quick from the dead among them.

The Hartleys offered, the younger one said. They offered good. More than its worth with the well goin and the trees half of em finished. More than itll ever be worth again.

Worth, the older one said.

Worth. Yes. Money is what Im talkin about.

I know what youre talkin about.

He pitched a shovelful of black mud up onto the bank and it lay there glistening. Then he set the shovel aside and got down on his knees in the ditch and reached into the dark water that had pooled at the gate and felt along the iron with his hands like a man feeling the face of someone he could not see.

Hand me that wrench, he said.

The younger one dug in the canvas bag and found the wrench and stepped to the edge and handed it down and the older one took it without looking. He worked at something below the water and the muscles stood in his forearm and his face was turned up toward the sky and set with the effort and at last something gave and he brought up a length of corroded pipe broke clean across at the coupling and he held it up dripping for the other to see.

There, he said. There it is. Right there is your worth.

It rusted through.

It rusted through. Forty years that pipes been in the ground and it finally rusted through and thats all it is. Aint the end of nothin. Aint a sign. Its a pipe.

The younger one took the broken length and turned it in his hands. The metal was eaten thin as paper at the break and flaked red onto his fingers.

You can patch it, he said.

I can do better than patch it. I can run new from the gate to the split. I got the pipe for it in the barn. Take us the day but we can do it.

A day.

A day aint much to ask.

The younger one looked at him a long moment and then he looked off toward the house that stood small and white on the rise above the orchard with the one window catching the new sun like a coin. Their mother in it. The house their grandfather had built and the trees their grandfather had set out with his own hands when he was a young man and the world was different and there was water in the river all year and a man could make a life from forty acres of trees if he was not afraid of work. None of that was true any longer save the work.

He aint comin out, the younger one said.

No.

I went in this mornin to tell him the water quit and he just looked at me.

He heard you.

He looked at me like he didnt know me. Like I was somebody come to read the meter.

He knows you.

I aint so sure.

The older brother climbed up out of the ditch and stood and his trousers were black to the thigh and he wiped his hands down them and looked at the house too.

He knows you, he said again. He just dont have the words to say it no more. Theres a difference between not knowin and not bein able to say.

Maybe there is. It dont look like much of a difference from where I stand.

They went back up to the barn for the pipe and walked the long way through the rows so as to look at the trees and the older brother stopped at one and put his hand on the bark and there was a wound in the trunk where a limb had been taken and the bark had not healed over it and the wood beneath was gray and soft.

This ones goin, he said. Borer got into it.

You see, the younger one said.

I see one tree.

I see a hundred. I see a thousand. I see every one of em goin a little at a time and us out here every mornin runnin from one dyin thing to the next and patchin and propping and prayin and for what. For who. The old man dont know an apple from a stone no more and Ma's wore down to nothin tendin him and you and me out here breakin our backs over forty acres of trees that aint made a dollar in three years.

Two.

Three.

The older brother let his hand fall from the tree.

It made money, he said.

It lost money. There's a difference. Like you said. The accounts dont lie even if you do.

I dont lie.

You lie to yourself. Every mornin you get up and you lie to yourself that this is goin to be the year it turns and every night you lie yourself to sleep on it and I'm the one has to do the books and look at what's real.

The older brother walked on and the younger one came after and they came up out of the rows onto the open ground before the barn and the barn stood weathered and leaning slightly to the south as if it had been pushing all its years against some wind out of the north and was tired now.

They drew the pipe out from where it was stacked along the barn wall under a tarp gone brittle with the sun and they laid the lengths out on the ground and counted them and there was enough and a little over. The older brother gathered the couplings and the cement and the rags and they loaded all of it onto the small flatbed trailer behind the tractor and the older one climbed up and started the tractor and it caught on the third turn with a gout of black smoke and ran rough and then evened out and he sat there a moment letting it warm with his hands on the wheel and the younger one stood below him.

Were you even goin to talk to me about it, the younger one said over the engine.

About what.

The Hartleys. The offer. You aint even said it to my face that the answer's no. You just go on like it was never said.

The older brother looked down at him.

Get on, he said. Were burnin daylight.

I want an answer.

The answer's the answer it's always been.

Which is no.

Which is no.

It aint yours alone to say. It's half mine. It's a third Ma's and what's left the old man's and when he goes itll come to us both and you cant just—

When he goes.

You know what I mean.

The older brother throttled the tractor down so it idled low and quiet and in the quiet his voice came level and hard.

I know what you mean, he said. You been waitin on it. You been doin the arithmetic on a dead man before he's dead.

That aint—

It is. I heard it. When he goes you said. Like you was checkin a calendar.

The younger one's face colored and he looked away.

I love him same as you, he said.

I never said you didn't.

You as good as said it.

I said you was doin arithmetic. And you was.

The younger one stood with his fists at his sides and the tractor idling between them like some third thing that would not take a part. Then he climbed up onto the trailer among the pipe and sat with his back to his brother and the older one put it in gear and they went down through the orchard with the pipe lengths chattering on the bed and the dead trees and the living going past on either hand.

They worked through the morning. They dug the trench from the headgate down along the line where the old pipe ran and they pulled the old pipe up out of the ground in sections and it came up reluctant and trailing roots and the roots had wrapped the pipe and broke into it at the joints seeking the water within and the older brother held up a section choked solid with white root like the pipe had grown a nerve and showed it to his brother without a word and the younger one looked at it and looked away.

The sun stood high and the heat came down and lay on them and the sweat ran and they did not speak save to say pass me this or hold that or here. They cut the new pipe to length with a handsaw and cleaned the ends and swabbed the cement and fit the couplings and held each joint while it set counting the seconds under their breath and the smell of the cement was sharp in the heat and went to the head. Length by length the new line went down white and clean into the black trench and they backfilled as they went and tamped the earth with the flats of the shovels.

At noon their mother came down from the house with a pail and they saw her coming a long way off small among the trees and they stopped and stood up out of the trench. She came on slow and she was not old in years but she moved old and when she reached them she set the pail down and there was bread in it and cold meat and a jar of tea gone the color of the river and she did not say anything at first but looked at the trench and the white pipe and the black mud heaped along it.

You got it, she said.

Most of it, the older one said. Be runnin by evenin.

Good, she said. The lower trees.

We watered the lower trees by hand this mornin from the stock tank. They'll keep.

She nodded. She looked tired in a way that had nothing to do with the walk down. The younger brother watched her.

How is he, he said.

She did not answer right off. She looked back up toward the house.

He had a good spell, she said. After you left. He knew me. He asked after the both of you. He asked was the water fixed and I told him you was fixin it and he said good. He said your daddy always kept after that line. He told me about the time the line broke in the freeze of fifty-two and your daddy and your grandaddy was out all night with lanterns.

The older brother's face changed at this and he looked at the ground.

He remembered that, he said.

Clear as day. He told it like it just happened. He laughed about your grandaddy fallin in the ditch in the dark.

And then he was gone again, the younger one said. Wasnt he.

She looked at him.

He gets tired, she said.

He was gone again. By the time you come down here he didnt know what year it was.

She did not deny it. She bent and took the cloth off the bread.

Eat, she said. The both of you. You're burnin up out here.

They sat in the shade of a tree that yet lived and ate the bread and the meat and passed the jar of tea between them and their mother stood a while and then sat too on an upturned pail and watched them eat the way a woman watches her sons eat that has watched it all their lives and finds in it something that steadies her. The younger brother ate and watched her and at last he set down the bread.

Ma, he said. The Hartleys made an offer on the place.

The older brother stopped chewing.

I know it, she said.

You know it.

Mr. Hartley come up to the house. He's a decent man. He took his hat off and he sat in the kitchen and he told me the number and he said there was no hurry and to think on it.

The younger one looked at his brother and then back to his mother.

What do you think on it, he said.

She was quiet a while. A bird went over and threw its shadow on the dry rows and was gone.

I think, she said, that this land took your daddy's daddy and it's takin your daddy now in its own way, and that I have watched it ask of this family every single thing this family had and give back less and less each year. I think that. And I think your grandaddy set these trees out with his hands and there's not a foot of this ground I could walk that I wouldnt be walkin on something one of you was a part of or your daddy was or his.

That aint an answer, the younger one said, not unkind.

No, she said. It aint. Because I dont have one. I'm too old to have one and too tired and I leave it to the two of you, and that's a coward's way and I know it, but there it is. Whatever you decide I'll abide it. Only.

Only what, the older brother said.

She stood and gathered the cloth and the empty jar.

Only decide it together, she said. Dont let it be a thing that's done to the one of you by the other. I've seen brothers split over less than this and never speak again to the grave and I would not live to see that. I would rather see every tree on this place dead and the ground sold to a stranger than see that.

She took up the pail and stood looking at them both and then she turned and went back up through the rows toward the house and they watched her go small among the trees and into the white house and the door close.

For a while neither of them spoke. The older brother finished the last of the bread and folded the cloth it had been in and set it aside carefully.

Fifty-two, he said.

What.

The freeze. He told her about fifty-two. I'd of been four years old. I dont remember it but I remember him tellin it. He told it every winter when it come a freeze. Him and Daddy out with the lanterns. He'd tell it and Daddy'd say it wasnt as bad as he made out and the old man'd say it was worse and they'd go back and forth on it.

I remember.

You remember.

I remember him tellin it. I remember Daddy sayin it wasnt so bad.

The older brother nodded slowly. He picked up a clod of the dry earth and broke it in his fingers.

That's all it is now, he said. That story. Him out with the lanterns. That's the only place that night still is, is in him, and when he goes it goes with him and then it's like it never was. Him and Daddy out in the dark over this line. This same line. And here we are over it again.

It aint the same line, the younger one said. We just pulled it out. We're puttin a new one in.

The older brother looked at him.

That's a hell of a thing to say.

It's true. The line he was out over in fifty-two rusted through in the night. We pulled it up. It was full of root and rot and it broke when you touched it. That's whats true. The story dont keep the water in the pipe.

No, the older brother said. It dont.

He stood and brushed the dirt from his trousers.

But I'll tell you what does, he said. Us. Out here. Puttin the new in. That's what keeps the water in the pipe. And as long as there's water in the pipe there's a reason for the story, and the day there aint, the story's just an old man's noise in a sold house.

The younger one stood too.

You're talkin in circles, he said. You want to keep the place to keep the story and you want the story to give a reason to keep the place. It's a snake eatin its own tail. There's got to be something underneath it that's real.

The water's real, the older brother said. The trees that's livin is real. The fruit on em is real and it's comin on and in two months it'll be ripe and we'll pick it and somebody'll eat it that's never heard of us nor this place nor the old man nor fifty-two. That's real. You want real, that's as real as it gets.

And the dead trees. The well goin. The accounts. That's real too.

That's real too.

So which real do we go by.

The older brother looked off down the rows toward the dead trees standing among the living.

I dont know, he said.

It was the first time he had said it and the younger one heard it and something in him eased and hardened both at once.

You dont know, he said.

No. I dont know. You want me to say I know and I dont. I know what I want. I want to keep it. I want to die on it like he's dyin on it and I want my boy if I ever have one to die on it after me. That's what I want and I know that clean and clear. But I dont know if it's right. I dont know if it's right to ask Ma to keep tendin him here where every day's a labor or if she'd have an easier time of it somewhere else. I dont know if it's right to keep you here breakin your back on a thing your heart aint in when you could take your half and make a life that's yours and not his and not mine. I dont know that. I'll tell you I dont know it because it's the truth and Ma said dont let it be a thing one of us does to the other and she's right.

The younger one stood with the saw in his hand and the sun on him.

I never said I wanted to leave, he said quietly.

You said sell.

Sellin and leavin aint the same. I'd stay. If we sold to the Hartleys and they kept it in trees I'd come work it for em same as now. It aint the work I'm runnin from. It's the—

He stopped.

It's the what, the older brother said.

It's watchin it kill us slow, the younger one said. That's all. I dont want to watch us go down with it the way the old man's goin down with it. I'd rather let it go while we still can than be drug under holdin onto it.

The older brother was quiet a long moment.

That's an honest thing, he said. That's the first honest thing you said all day. The rest was numbers. That's the thing under the numbers. You're scared.

I aint scared.

You're scared the place'll do to you what it's doin to him. And maybe you're right to be. I dont fault you it.

The younger one looked away.

They stood there in the heat with the new pipe going white into the ground at their feet and the trench yet to be finished and the afternoon stretched out before them.

Let's get the line in, the older brother said. We can settle the rest after. But the trees need the water tonight whatever we decide, and a thing decided in the heat aint decided right. We'll get the water runnin and we'll go up and we'll sit with him while he eats and maybe he'll have a spell and know us and maybe he wont, and tomorrow when it's cool we'll talk it through and we'll decide it together like she said.

The younger one looked at him a while and then he nodded and bent and took up the saw and the next length of pipe and laid it across the trench to measure it.

They worked on into the afternoon. The trench went down the rows toward the split and the new line followed it white and clean and the old pipe lay heaped along the bank red and broken and full of root. The sun came down and the shadows of the trees both quick and dead lengthened across the dry ground and the brothers worked without speaking now in the old rhythm of men who have worked together all their lives and know each other's hands.

Toward evening they made the last coupling at the split and held it while it set and then the older brother went up to the headgate and the younger one stood at the split to watch and the older one called down to him and put his shoulder to the wheel and the gate came up and far off the younger brother heard it before he saw it, a sound come up out of the new pipe like a breath drawn, and then the water came shouting down the line and out at the split and into the dry furrows of the south block, brown and roiling and shining in the last of the light, and it ran out down the rows to the waiting trees the quick of them and the dead alike, and the younger brother stood and watched it go and did not say anything and up the line his brother stood at the gate with his hand still on the wheel watching it too, and neither of them could have said in that moment whether they would keep the place or sell it, only that the water was running, and that was real, and it would have to be enough for the day that was left of the day.
