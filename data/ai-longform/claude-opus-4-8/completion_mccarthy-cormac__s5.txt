He woke again into the bed and the woman beside him breathing and the dark close about them like water. He lay listening. Somewhere out past the warped boards of the wall a dog took up barking and was answered far off by another and then by nothing. He could hear the river too, that low and ceaseless commerce of the water with the land, and he thought of the men he had not seen, the three who would cross his path in a country yet unmade, though he could not have said how he knew of them or why their image came to him riding the underside of sleep.

You was hollering, she said.

I know it.

What was it.

Nothing. Go to sleep.

She turned and the ropes of the bed complained beneath her and beneath the weight she carried that was his and was not yet anything at all but trouble waiting on its hour. He lay with his eyes open in the dark and would not close them for he feared the prophet and the cold square and the upturned ruined faces and the dark that fell like a shout. After a while the gray came up at the seams of the boards and he rose and dressed himself in the cold and went out.

The morning stood gray and without wind over the bottomland. Mist lay in the low places and along the river in long slow rivers of its own that moved against the current of the water beneath. He walked down to the bank and stood and looked at the river going by all brown and swollen with the spring rains far upstream and carrying with it the wrack of distant country, branches and whole trees turning slow in the flood and once the bloated carcass of some animal he could not name turning too and going under and rising and going on. He squatted on his heels and watched it. He was a young man then, not yet twenty, lean and dark with a long jaw and eyes set deep beneath a heavy brow so that he seemed always to be looking out from some recess in himself, some cave wherein a wariness kept watch. He had come into this country out of another and would go out of it into yet another and in none of them would he find what he could not name and did not know to look for.

When he went back the woman had the fire going and cornmeal mush in the pot and she set it before him and he ate without tasting it and she watched him eat.

You aint said two words, she said.

There aint two words to say.

There's something eatin you.

He looked at her. She was not old but she had the look of age on her already, the bones of her face stood close beneath the skin and her hands were the hands of a woman twice her years. She had been pretty once. He could see it the way you can see the shape of a country under snow.

There's nothing eating me, he said.

You had a dream.

A man cant help what he dreams.

No, she said. He cant.

He pushed the bowl away and rose and took his hat from the nail by the door. I'll be back by dark, he said.

Where you going.

Down to Wartons. He's got work if a man'll do it.

She said nothing. He went out and she sat by the fire with her hands folded over the child and after a while she began to rock slowly back and forth and to hum some tune that had no name nor end to it.

He walked the river road south through the bottomland with the mist burning off and the sun coming up red through the trees. Crows went up from a field of dead corn and circled and cried out and settled again. He passed a man hauling shoats to market in a cart and the man raised a hand and he raised one in return and neither spoke. The country he walked through was a poor country and the people in it were poor people and they got their living out of the dirt with their hands and their backs and when the dirt was done with them it took them back and that was all of it. He had known no other.

Warton's place sat on a rise above a creek that fed into the river and the house was a low log house with a dogtrot through the middle of it and a porch sagging on its posts. There were hounds under the porch and they came out baying and circled him in the yard with their hackles up until Warton himself came to the door and called them off.

That you, Warton said.

It's me.

Come on then.

He went up onto the porch. Warton was a big man gone to fat with a red face and small pale eyes and he stood in the door in his suspenders and looked out at the morning.

You wanting work, Warton said.

If you got it.

I got it. I always got it. Question is can you do it.

I can do it.

Warton spat off the porch. We'll see, he said.

The work was clearing a piece of bottomland Warton meant to put in cotton and it was the hardest kind of work there was, grubbing out the stumps of the trees that had been felled the year before and burning the slash and breaking the ground with a single mule and a turning plow that bucked and snagged on roots and threw a man about like a thing of no weight at all. He worked through the morning in the cold and then in the heat as the day warmed and the sweat ran off him and soaked through his shirt and dried white with salt. Warton came down once to watch him and stood at the edge of the field with his thumbs in his suspenders and watched and said nothing and went back up to the house.

At noon a black man came down from the house with a piece of cornbread and a tin of water and set it on a stump and went away without a word and the young man ate and drank and lay back in the new-broke earth and looked up at the sky where buzzards turned on the high thermals, slow and patient, biding. He watched them a long while. Then he rose and took up the plow again.

When the sun was low he came up to the house and Warton paid him out of a leather poke, counting the coins into his palm slow as though it cost him blood.

You'll come back tomorrow, Warton said. It was not a question.

I'll come back.

There's two weeks work in that bottom if there's a day.

I'll be here.

Warton looked at him. You got a wife down at the old Pruitt place.

I got a woman.

She's carrying.

The young man said nothing.

I knowed Pruitt, Warton said. Before he died. That was his place. You squatting on it I reckon.

There wasnt nobody using it.

No, Warton said. There wasnt. He looked off toward the river. Pruitt had a girl, he said. She run off with some no-account when she wasnt but fifteen. That be her?

I dont know whose she be, the young man said. She's mine now.

Warton laughed, a short ugly sound with no mirth in it at all. Yours now, he said. Aint we all just full of what's ours. Go on then. Be here at light.

He walked home in the dusk with the coins in his pocket and his hands cramped and torn from the work and his back a single ache from the nape to the seat of him. The frogs had started up in the low places and the first stars stood out pale in the east where the dark came on. He walked and did not think of anything and that was a kind of peace to him, the walking and the not thinking, the body used up and the mind gone quiet as a stunned thing.

She had supper for him, fatback and beans and the last of the cornbread, and he ate it all and she watched him and did not eat much herself.

You ought to eat, he said. For the baby.

I aint hungry.

You ought to eat anyhow.

She took up a little of the beans then to please him and he saw it and said nothing more. When he was done he sat by the fire and she came and sat near him and after a while she put her hand on his arm and he let it lie there.

You been good to me, she said.

He didnt answer.

I know you didnt have to take me on. With the baby coming and all. I know what folks would say. You could of left me to it.

I aint left you, he said.

No. You aint.

The fire popped and a coal rolled out onto the dirt and he toed it back. The shadows of them stood up tall and black on the boards and moved and were still.

Where'd you come from, she said. You never said. You come walking up that road last fall and you never said where from nor where to.

From up north of here, he said. There aint nothing to tell. My people are dead. I been on the road two years.

Two years. Doing what.

Whatever there was. Cutting timber. Driving stock. There was a war and I was in some of it for a while and then I wasnt.

You been in the war.

For a while.

She was quiet. Then she said, My daddy died in a war. The other one. Before I was born hardly. Mama said he went off and never come back and they never even told her where he was buried at. She used to say he was just somewheres in the ground and the rain fell on him same as it fell on us and that was all the church he ever got.

He looked into the fire. The rain falls on everbody, he said.

I know it. She moved closer to him. After a while she said, You was hollering again last night. In your sleep.

Was I.

You was saying me. Just me, me, like that. Like you was calling out for somebody to fetch you.

He said nothing. The dream came back to him whole and at once, the cold square and the prophet looking down and the sun going black and the howling of the ruined ones, and he felt a thing move in him that was close to fear and he put it down.

It dont signify, he said. A dream's just a dream. It's the day a man's got to live, not the night.

But she had begun to cry, softly, with no sound to it, only the shine of it on her face in the firelight, and he saw it and did not know what to do and so he put his arm around her and she leaned into him and they sat that way a long time and the fire burned down to coals and the dark came up close around them and the river ran on outside in the night going where all the water went and carrying with it all that it had taken up along the way.

He dreamed no dreams that night or none he carried out of sleep and woke before light and rose and went out as before. The mornings ran together after that, the same gray river and the same red sun coming up through the trees and the long walk south and the bottomland that took his work and gave back nothing but more of itself to be worked. Warton paid him out each evening with the same slow grudging count and each evening he walked home in the dusk with the frogs starting up and the stars coming out and the body used and quiet and the mind gone still.

The bottom got cleared. The two weeks ran out and Warton found other work, fencing and a roof to be patched and a well to be deepened, and the young man did it all and Warton paid him and they spoke little. There came a thing between them that was not friendship for Warton had no friendship in him but was something else, a kind of grudging measure that a man takes of another man's work and finds it good and will not say so but lets it stand in the silence between them.

Once Warton said to him, You aim to farm that Pruitt place.

I been thinking on it.

It's poor ground. Pruitt never made nothing off it.

I aint Pruitt.

No, Warton said. You aint. He looked at him with the pale eyes. You got something in you, boy. I dont know what it is. A man works the way you work, he's running from something or running to something, one. Which is it with you.

I dont know, the young man said. And it was the truth and he knew it was the truth as he said it. I dont know which it is.

Warton nodded slowly as though this were an answer that satisfied him. Most dont, he said. Most dont ever know. They just run and call it living.

The spring came on full then and the bottomland greened and the cotton went in and the days grew long and hot. The woman's time drew near. She moved heavy about the cabin now and her face had swollen and there were dark places under her eyes and at night she could not lie comfortable any way at all and he would wake and find her sitting up in the dark with her hands on her belly and her breath coming slow and he would ask was she all right and she would say she was and they would both lie awake in the dark not speaking.

There was a granny woman lived up the river a piece, an old black woman that the folks in the bottom went to for their birthing and their dying and the doctoring that came between, and he walked up there one evening and stood in her yard and called and she came out, old and bent and white-headed with eyes gone milky at the edges, and looked at him.

I got a woman near her time, he said. Down at the old Pruitt place.

I know the place, the old woman said. I knowed Pruitt's girl. That her?

I reckon it's her.

When's her time.

Soon. I dont rightly know. Soon.

The old woman looked at him a long while. You come for me when it starts, she said. Day or night. You come. First ones is hard sometimes. And she's narrow built. I seen her. She's narrow built and that aint good. But the Lord made em and the Lord delivers em and we does what we can in between. You come for me.

I'll come.

She turned to go back in and then she stopped and looked at him over her shoulder. You troubled, she said. It wasnt a question either.

The young man said nothing.

I can see it on you, she said. You got a shadow on you, boy. I seen it when you come in the yard. Some folks carries a light and some carries a shadow and you got the shadow.

A dream, he said. I been having a dream.

Dreams, she said, and she nodded. Dreams is real, dont let nobody tell you different. A dream's a place same as any other place. You go there in the night and you come back and you bring something with you, or you leave something there. She studied him. What you dream.

There's a prophet, he said. And the slow telling of it came out of him then though he had told no one, the cold square and the supplicants and the sun going black and the prophet saying yes I think perhaps you will be cured and then the dark falling and the sun not coming back and the howling of the ruined ones who knew him even in the dark.

The old woman listened and when he was done she stood quiet a long time and the dusk came down around them and the first bats went cutting through the air over the yard.

The cure that dont come, she said at last. That's the worst of all the curses. Worse than the affliction. A man can bear his affliction. He's borne it long. But to be told he'll be cured and then the dark to come and no cure in it and never to come out the other side. She shook her head. That's a hard dream, she said. That's a hard dream to carry.

What's it mean, he said.

It dont mean nothing, she said. Or it means everthing. Aint no difference at the end. You go on and live your life, boy. The dream'll have its way or it wont and there aint nothing you can do to turn it. Go on home to your woman. And come for me when it starts.

He walked home in the dark and the dream sat on him heavier than it had and he could not put it down. The river ran black beside the road and the stars stood thick overhead and he looked up at them and thought of the stars of another season that had hung over the cold square in the dream, the wrong stars, the stars of a year that had not come or had already gone, and he felt himself small under them and walking in a country that did not know him nor care that he walked in it.

The woman's time came in the night three days after. She woke him with her hand on his arm and her breath gone tight and she said it's coming, it's started, and he rose and dressed in the dark and built up the fire and put water on and then he took the lantern and went out into the night and ran the river road north to the granny woman's place and woke her and she came, slow on her old legs but coming, and he wanted to carry her she was so slow but she would not be carried and so he walked beside her with the lantern and she talked to him low and steady the whole way as though to keep him from running out of his own skin.

It's a long business, she said. Birthing. Aint nothing for the man to do but wait and pray if he's a praying man and keep the fire and the water hot. You hear. Aint nothing for you to do. You done your part nine months back. She laughed softly in the dark. Now you just got to wait and learn what waiting is. You'll learn it tonight. Aint no school like it.

When they got to the cabin the woman was on the bed with her knees up and the sweat on her and she cried out when the pains took her and the old woman went to her and put her hands on her and felt of her and spoke to her low and gentle and then she turned to the young man and said, You go on out now. This aint no place for you. You keep the fire and the water and you wait. We'll call you when there's something to know.

He went out and stood on the packed dirt of the dooryard in the dark. The night was warm and still and the frogs were loud in the low places and the river ran on and from inside the cabin came the woman's voice rising and falling and the old woman's voice steady under it like a hand laid on a frightened animal. He stood and listened and could not bear to listen and went down to the river and stood at the bank where he could still hear but only faintly and looked at the black water going by.

He thought of the three men he had dreamed of that first night, the bearded one and the two others, moving west along the river in their ragged duffel, mute and implacable, and he did not know why he thought of them now nor what they were to him nor whether they were real men somewhere in the world walking under these same stars or only shapes that had risen up out of his own dark and would sink back into it. He thought of the prophet and the cure that did not come. He thought of the woman in the cabin crying out in her narrow-built body to bring forth a child that was not his and was his now and would be his, the nameless weight in her belly given a name at last and a face and a cry of its own to add to the crying of the world.

The night wore on. The stars wheeled slow overhead and the moon came up late and small and red over the trees across the river and laid a track of broken light on the water. The woman's cries grew worse and then there was a long while when they did not come at all and he could not tell was that good or bad and he went up toward the cabin and stood near the door but he could hear only the old woman's voice low and steady and could make out no words and he did not go in.

Toward the gray of morning he heard the woman scream, one long scream that climbed and climbed and seemed it would not stop and then stopped, and in the silence after it he stood with his heart in his throat and his hands knotted at his sides and waited for he did not know what, and then there came another cry, thin and high and angry and altogether new in the world, the cry of a thing that had not been and now was, and he sat down where he stood in the dirt of the dooryard and put his face in his torn hands and the gray light came up over the river all around him.

The old woman came to the door. She was wiping her hands on a rag and her face was tired and old in the gray light but there was something in it that was not tired.

You got a boy, she said. He's little and he come hard but he's whole and he's hollering and his mama's tore but she'll mend. You hear me, boy. You got a son.

He could not speak. He looked up at her and she looked down at him sitting in the dirt and her face softened.

Go on, she said. Go and see him. He's yours now whatever he was before. You go and look at him and you tell him welcome. They like to be told it though they cant understand. Aint none of us understands it. We just get told welcome and then we're here and then we go on.

He rose and went past her into the cabin where the fire burned low and the woman lay spent and white on the bed with the child at her breast wrapped in a piece of clean cloth, and she looked up at him and her face was wet and ruined and shining and the most beautiful thing he had ever seen, and he came and knelt by the bed and looked at the small red wrinkled face of the child, and the sun came up then over the trees and through the seams of the boards and laid its first light across the bed and across the woman and the child and the kneeling man, and for a moment they were all of them held in it, in that spurious sanctity of the early light, before the day came on for good and they had to rise and live in it.
