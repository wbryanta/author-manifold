The pump house door had swollen in its frame again, and Dale had to put his shoulder into it twice before it gave. Inside, the air was close and smelled of rust and old grease, the particular mineral dampness he associated with this building more than any other place on earth. The float switch hung where it had always hung. The pressure tank squatted in the corner with its skin of orange scale. He'd been coming in here since he was seven years old, and the smell undid something in him every time, loosened a knot he didn't know he kept tied.

"It's the line out by the third row," Wes said behind him. "I walked it yesterday. There's water coming up where there shouldn't be."

"You walked it."

"I walked it." Wes set the toolbox down on the concrete with a flat clang. "I do come out here, you know. I haven't moved to the moon."

"Bakersfield."

"Same difference, according to you."

Dale didn't answer. He crouched by the pressure switch and tapped the gauge with one knuckle, the way their father had, as if the needle might be persuaded by attention. It read low. It always read low now. The whole system was a confederation of failures held together by the fact that nobody had yet asked it to fail completely at the same time.

"We'll need to dig," Dale said. "If it's coming up at the third row, the break's probably under the old apricot, where the roots are."

"The apricot's dead."

"Roots don't know that."

They carried the shovels out into the light. It was not yet nine and already the heat had a body to it, a presence that leaned against them as they walked between the rows. The orchard ran east off the county road in long aisles, almond mostly, with the apricots their grandfather had planted at the margins as a kind of indulgence, fruit you couldn't ship but could eat off the branch in July, warm and split and dripping. Half the almonds were stressed now, the leaves curling early, and the ones at the far end had simply quit, standing gray and brittle like something drawn in pencil. Dale looked at them and then made himself look at the ground instead.

"You see that," Wes said. He wasn't gloating. That was the thing about Wes; he'd never once gloated, which somehow made it worse. "That's not coming back, Dale. That's a hundred and ten trees right there that aren't coming back, and we're paying district rates to keep the live ones alive, and the price came in under three dollars last fall."

"I know what the price came in at."

"Then you know."

"I know what you think I know."

They reached the third row. Dale found the wet place easily enough, a dark blooming in the dust under the dead apricot where the water had wicked up and spread, the only green for thirty feet a sudden idiot fringe of weeds drinking what wasn't meant for them. He set the point of his shovel and stepped on it and felt the old satisfaction of the blade going in, the give of soaked earth.

"Mark where you're cutting," Wes said. "I don't want to nick the line worse."

"I've done this before."

"I know you've done this before. I'm just saying."

They dug. It was slow because the apricot's roots were everywhere, fibrous and stubborn, and twice Dale had to drop the shovel and go in with the loppers, kneeling in the mud he was making, cutting roots that were thicker than his thumb. Sweat ran into his eyes. Across the little trench Wes worked steadily, methodically, the way he did everything, and Dale found himself watching his brother's hands and thinking that they were their father's hands, the same broad palms and short fingers, and that his own were too, and that this was the kind of thought that got you nowhere.

"There," Wes said. The poly line lay exposed now, black and slick, and where it bent around a root it had split along a seam, a clean tear that breathed water with a small steady pulse. "See? It's the joint. The glue let go."

"It's the line. The line's twenty years old. The glue let go because the line's shot." Dale sat back on his heels. "We patch this, it lets go somewhere else in August. You know that."

"So we patch it now and we deal with August in August."

"That's the whole orchard, Wes. That's the whole argument. Patch it now, deal with it later. We've been dealing with it later for ten years."

Wes wiped his face with the back of his wrist, which only moved the dirt around. He looked older than thirty-four out here, in this light. They both did, Dale supposed. The sun was honest in a way that rooms weren't.

"Let's just fix the line," Wes said. "We can fight after."

"We can fight while. I'm good at both."

That got something out of Wes, not quite a laugh, an exhale through the nose. He went back to the toolbox and came up with the coupling and the can of cleaner and the glue, and they set to it, and for a while there was nothing to say because the work said it. Dale cut out the bad section with the ratchet cutter, the blade closing on the pipe with a soft click. Wes cleaned the ends. They fitted the coupling dry to check it, then primed both faces and glued them, holding the joint a slow ten count while the glue grabbed, their four hands together on the pipe in the wet hole, not touching, almost touching.

"Hold it," Wes said.

"I'm holding it."

"You let go too early last time and it blew."

"That was Dad. That was Dad who let go too early."

Wes was quiet. "Yeah," he said. "That was Dad."

They held it. Somewhere off in the dead end of the orchard a mockingbird was working through its catalogue, robin then jay then something it had heard from a phone, an electronic chirp it had stolen and made its own. The water had stopped pulsing because Dale had gone back and shut the valve at the head, and now the trench just held its little pond, settling, going clear at the top.

"You remember when he had us out here pulling sucker growth," Wes said. "That whole summer. He paid us a penny a sucker."

"Penny a sucker. We thought we were rich."

"You counted yours. You kept a tally on a piece of cardboard."

"I did not."

"You absolutely did. You wrote the number on it every night and you wouldn't let me see it because you thought I'd copy it." Wes shook his head. "You were maybe nine. You had a system at nine."

"Somebody had to."

"That's what I'm saying." Wes let go of the pipe slowly, testing, and the joint held, the glue set now, the two pieces become one piece. He sat back. "That's exactly what I'm saying, Dale. You've got a system. You've always had a system, and the system is the orchard, and I'm telling you the orchard is the thing that's broken and your system can't fix it."

There it was. Dale stayed crouched over the joint a moment longer than he needed to.

"You walked it yesterday," he said. "You said. So you saw the second block. The young trees."

"I saw them."

"Those are eight years old. Those are just coming into their bearing. I put those in myself. I budgeted three dry years to get them established and we're through the worst of it, and they're going to carry, Wes, they're going to be the thing that carries this place when the old block comes out."

"With what water."

"With the water we have."

"We don't have the water, Dale." Wes said it gently, which was the unbearable register, the one that meant he'd decided the thing already and was only being kind about it now. "The district cut the allocation again. You showed me the letter, remember? You're the one who showed me. Forty percent of contract. The well's pulling sand half the days in August. You want to keep eight-year-old trees alive on forty percent of contract, you're going to let the old block die to do it, and then you've got young trees and dead trees and a well full of sand and a balloon payment on the equipment loan in March, and you'll have spent everything to get there."

"You've got the whole thing memorized."

"I had a guy run the numbers."

Dale looked up. "You had a guy."

"An ag broker. A consultant. He does this, valuations, he does the whole valley." Wes had the grace to look away when he said it. "The land's worth more than the orchard, Dale. That's the thing nobody wants to say out loud. The dirt is worth more empty than it is with trees on it. There's an outfit putting in panels on the Hadley place, you saw the panels, and there's two almond operations from down south buying up anything with a well permit grandfathered in, and the number—" He stopped.

"Say the number."

"The number's good. The number is better than good." Wes finally looked at him. "It's enough that you don't have to do this anymore. That's what I keep trying to say and you keep hearing something else. It's enough that you could be done. You could be fifty and not broken. You could sleep."

"I sleep fine."

"You don't. Karen told Lisa you're up at four most mornings doing the books."

"Karen shouldn't—" Dale stood, his knees complaining, and stepped back from the trench. "This is why I didn't want to do this today. I wanted to fix the line. We fixed the line. Now you want to—"

"I want to talk about it because there's never a good day to talk about it, there's just days, and they're running out." Wes stood too. They faced each other across the open ground with the patched line between them like a thing they'd buried and might dig up again. "He left it to both of us, Dale. That was the whole—you know why he did that. He did that so it'd take both of us to keep it and both of us to let it go. He knew you'd never let it go on your own. He set it up so you couldn't."

"He set it up so neither of us could do something stupid alone."

"Maybe. Or maybe he knew you'd dig your heels in and somebody'd have to be the one to say the thing, and he figured it'd be me because it's always been me, because you're the one who stayed and I'm the one who—" Wes made a gesture that took in himself, his clean truck up by the road, the city he'd driven from. "I'm the one who left, so I'm the one who's allowed to want him to be able to stop."

"That's not allowed. That's not what that is. That's you wanting your half."

It came out worse than he meant it, or maybe exactly as bad as he meant it, and he watched it land. Wes's jaw tightened. For a second neither of them said anything, and the mockingbird filled the gap with its robin and its jay and its stolen electronic note.

"Okay," Wes said. "Okay. You think that, then. You think that if it helps."

He turned and walked up the row toward the truck, and Dale let him go, because there was a part of him, an ugly small part he wasn't proud of, that wanted the day to break here so that nothing would have to be decided. But Wes didn't go to the truck. He went to the bed of it and came back carrying the two water bottles they'd left there and one of them was for Dale, and that was the thing about Wes, that was the whole thing, he came back with water for the brother who'd just told him he was greedy, and he handed it over without a word and they stood in the partial shade of a tree that was dying and drank.

"It's hot," Wes said. "We should backfill before it gets worse."

"Yeah."

They knelt again, on opposite sides, and pushed the dirt back in by hand first, packing it around the joint so the backfill wouldn't shift the new glue before it cured, and then they used the shovels, and the wet earth went dark to pale as they turned it, the little pond disappearing under it. Dale tamped the top with the flat of the shovel.

"I'll open the valve slow," he said. "You watch the joint."

"I'll watch it."

He walked back up to the head and put his hand on the valve, the old gate valve with its red wheel gone pink from the sun, and he opened it a quarter turn and waited, and then another, and he could feel through the metal the line filling, the system taking water the way a body takes breath. Down the row Wes raised a hand: holding. Dale opened it the rest of the way.

The orchard drank. Through the buried lines, out the emitters, water went to the trees that were alive and to the trees that were dead and didn't know it and to the eight-year-old block beyond the rise that he'd planted with his own hands in the third dry year, on faith, on a bet that the weather was a thing that turned and came back. He stood at the valve with his hand still on the wheel and looked down the long aisle and tried to see it the way Wes saw it, as a number, as dirt worth more bare, and he could, that was the terrible part, he could see it that way and the seeing was correct, the arithmetic was sound. Wes wasn't wrong. That had never been the difficulty. If Wes were wrong it would be easy.

He walked back down. Wes was crouched by the joint, watching it the way you'd watch something that might still betray you.

"Dry," Wes said. "It's holding."

"Good."

"You did a clean cut."

"I've done it before."

"I know." Wes stood. He didn't pick up the toolbox. He stood there with his hands hanging and he said, not looking at Dale, looking at the dead apricot, "He taught us both the same things. Out here. That's what I don't think you've ever—he taught me all of it too, Dale. The float switch and the glue and the sucker growth and reading the gauge. I know how to do all of it. I just don't think I have to keep doing it to have had it. You think if you let the land go you lose him. I think I carry him whether or not I'm standing in his dirt."

Dale didn't say anything for a while. A breeze came up the row, hot, smelling of dust and the green idiot weeds and, faintly, from somewhere, of fruit, though it was too early and the only fruit was on the one apricot at the far margin that was somehow still alive, that put out its hard green knots every June out of pure stubbornness.

"You smell that?" Dale said.

"What?"

"The apricot. The one that's still going. Down the end."

Wes lifted his head. "I don't smell anything."

"It's early. They're not ripe. But on a hot morning sometimes you can—" He stopped. He felt foolish. "Dad used to say you could smell July coming in June if you knew how to stand still. I never knew what he meant. I think I've been standing out here twenty years trying to smell it."

Wes was looking at him now, and his face had changed, some of the broker's certainty gone out of it, and what was left was just his brother, the kid who'd thought Dale was rich because of a number on a piece of cardboard.

"That's the thing I can't put in the spreadsheet," Wes said quietly. "I know. You think I don't know there's a thing that doesn't go in the spreadsheet. I just—somebody has to hold the spreadsheet, Dale. You won't. So I do. And it makes me the bad guy in this, the one who came up from the city with a consultant, and I'll be the bad guy, fine, but I'm not going to pretend the column adds up when it doesn't, just so you can keep standing here smelling July."

"I'm not asking you to pretend."

"You're asking me to lose money every year for the rest of my life so you don't have to grieve him."

It was the truest thing either of them had said, and it sat between them, and Dale found that he wasn't angry at it. He was tired. He went and sat down on the overturned bucket by the pump house wall, in the shade, and after a moment Wes came and sat on the toolbox, and they sat there like two men at the end of a shift, which they were.

"The young block could make it," Dale said. "If we pulled the old trees this winter. Took out the whole old block, the dead and the dying both, and put everything—water, money, all of it—into the eight-year-olds. They're efficient. New emitters, low-volume. A guy at the co-op walked me through it. We'd be a third the acreage but we'd be solvent on it. We wouldn't be carrying ghosts."

Wes was quiet. "You priced that out."

"I'm up at four most mornings. Karen's right about that part."

"You never said."

"You came in with a consultant and a number. I figured I'd lose the argument before I made it." Dale turned the water bottle in his hands. "I'm not saying it's the answer. I'm saying it's a thing that isn't selling and isn't pretending. There's a version where we keep the well and the young block and we let the rest go back to the bank or to dirt or to whatever, and it's small, and it's not what Dad had, but it's alive, and it's ours, and I could maybe sleep."

"You'd take out the apricots."

Dale looked down the row. "The dead ones."

"The live one."

"That one's at the margin. The new block doesn't reach it. It'd come out with the rest." He heard his own voice do something it didn't usually do. "I know. I thought about that. I lay there at four in the morning and I thought about that one tree, which is insane, Wes, it's one tree, it doesn't even bear enough to—and I thought, that's the price. If I want the part that's real I have to give up the part that's only mine, the smell of July, the one stubborn tree. And I couldn't decide if that meant I was finally being a grown man about it or finally giving up."

Wes didn't answer right away. He reached down and picked up a clod of the backfill dirt and broke it in his fingers, the way men did out here when they wanted their hands occupied, the way their father had.

"Both," he said finally. "It means both. That's what he never told us, the both part. You can do the grown-up thing and have it be a loss. They don't cancel out. He kept the apricots his whole life and they never made a dime and he wasn't being a child about it, he just—he liked the smell of July." Wes let the dirt fall. "We could leave the one tree."

Dale looked at him.

"If we did your thing," Wes said. "The young block, pull the rest. We could leave the one apricot at the margin. It's not in the way. It doesn't have to come out just because the dead ones do. We could run a line to it. One emitter. It'd cost about four dollars a year in water."

"You'd sign off on four dollars a year of pure sentiment."

"I'd sign off on that." Wes almost smiled. "That I can put in the spreadsheet. One line item. 'July.' I'll defend it to the consultant."

And Dale felt the knot that the pump house always loosened loosen the rest of the way, not undone but slackened, the thing he kept tied so tight that he'd stopped feeling it as a thing he was doing rather than a thing that was simply true about him. They hadn't decided anything. That was the strange part. The whole orchard was still in question, the loan was still due in March, the well was still pulling sand, and Wes would go back to Bakersfield tonight and Dale would be up at four, and none of it was settled. But they'd found the one thing they could both stand on, four dollars of water for a tree that didn't bear, and it was small enough to be honest and large enough to hold them, and maybe that was how a thing got decided after all, not in a single turning but in the slow filling of a line, joint by joint, the system taking water until it could carry the weight you put on it.

"Let's check the emitters in the young block," Dale said, standing. "Long as we're out here. Make sure the new ones aren't clogging."

"Long as we're out here," Wes agreed, and picked up the toolbox, and they went together up the row toward the rise, past the dead trees and the one live apricot, into the part of the orchard that was still trying.
