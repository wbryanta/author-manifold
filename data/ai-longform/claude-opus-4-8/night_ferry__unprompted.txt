The ferry left at eleven and would arrive a little after six, which meant the night belonged to him in a way nights had not belonged to him in years. Magnus had booked a cabin out of some instinct toward dignity, though he understood now, standing in the narrow space between the bunk and the basin, that there was nothing dignified about it. The cabin smelled of disinfectant laid over something older, a human smell that disinfectant could mask but never remove. He set his bag on the lower bunk. He had packed a clean shirt for the morning, as if the dead cared what he wore, as if Aksel had ever cared what he wore.

He went back up. The cold on deck was a relief after the cabin's closeness. The harbor lights of the town he was leaving slid backward, orange and indifferent, and beyond them the dark hills he had grown up under, where their father had kept the sheep and their mother had kept her silences. The strait opened ahead, black and faintly luminous, the way water is luminous when it has nothing to reflect but its own movement. He gripped the rail. The metal burned cold through his gloves.

The call had come two days ago from a sergeant in the north with a careful, flattened voice, the voice of a man who delivered news like this often enough to have sanded all the edges from it. A body had come ashore near the fishing station at Strandvik. Drowned, some weeks in the water, hard to say. There were papers in a sealed pouch, water-spoiled but legible in part. A name. An old address that led, after some searching, to a brother. To Magnus.

"We can't be certain," the sergeant had said. "The condition. You understand. We'd ask you to look, if you're willing. There's the matter of the effects, also."

The effects. Magnus had said yes before he had decided to, the way a man steps off a curb without looking and only afterward feels the lurch of his own carelessness. He had not seen Aksel in eleven years. He had not spoken to him in nine. The last words between them had been spoken across the kitchen table after the funeral, their mother's funeral, and they had been ugly words, the kind that cannot be taken back because they are true in the moment and the moment was real.

A woman in a yellow oilskin came along the deck, walking a small dog that stopped to investigate every bolt and stanchion. She nodded at him. He nodded back. The dog sniffed his boot and moved on, finding him uninteresting, and he felt, absurdly, the small sting of rejection, as though even the animal had judged him and found him wanting.

He went to the bar because there was nowhere else to be that was warm and had other people in it. The bar was half a deck wide, lined with windows that showed only the reflection of the room, the bottles, the bored woman behind the counter, the few passengers scattered at tables nursing beer and coffee. He ordered a whisky and took it to a table by the window where he could pretend to look out at the sea and see only himself looking back.

Aksel had been the younger by three years. As a boy he had been the soft one, the one who cried easily and forgave easily and was therefore, in their father's economy, the weak one. Magnus had been the steady son, the one who could be trusted with the gun and the boat and the money. He had carried this trust like a yoke and had resented Aksel for being spared it, for being allowed to be tender, for crying at the slaughter of the lambs and being held by their mother while Magnus stood by the wall and learned to despise his own envy.

Eleven years. A man could become anyone in eleven years. The boy who cried could become a man who drowned in a strait far from home with water-spoiled papers in a sealed pouch, and Magnus would not know him. That was the thing that frightened him, sitting in the warm bar with the whisky going down like a struck match. He was afraid he would look at the dead face tomorrow and not know whether it was Aksel or not. He was afraid the eleven years had emptied his brother out of him so thoroughly that recognition would be impossible, and he would stand in the cold room and shrug at the attendant and say, I can't be sure, and walk away from his own brother for the second and final time.

"You're not sleeping either."

The man who said it had sat down at the next table, a heavyset man of about sixty with a face like weathered tackle. He had a beer and a paperback he was not reading.

"No," Magnus said.

"Bad on these crossings. The engine's just under here." The man knocked on the floor with his heel. "You feel it in your teeth. I've made this run two hundred times and I've never slept once."

"What takes you north?"

"Daughter. Grandchild on the way." He said it without warmth, as a fact. "And you?"

Magnus turned the whisky glass on the table, watching the bottom catch the light. He had not told anyone. He had not had to. There had been no one to tell. He lived alone in a flat above a shop, he kept the books for a fish-processing plant, and his life had narrowed over the years to a thing that required no explanation because no one ever asked.

"I'm going to identify a body," he said.

The man took this in. He did not flinch or murmur the soft useless things people murmured. He nodded slowly, the way men nod when they have buried their own. "Family?"

"My brother. Maybe. They aren't sure."

"Drowned?"

"Drowned."

The man was quiet a moment. Then he said, "My brother went into the sea. Forty years ago now. Off a trawler in weather they had no business being out in. We never got him back. There was nothing to identify." He turned the paperback over and looked at the cover as though it might tell him something. "I used to think that was the worst of it, not having anything. But I've come to think it might be a mercy. You don't have to look. You don't have to carry that picture."

"I haven't seen him in eleven years," Magnus said. He did not know why he was telling this stranger what he had told no one. Perhaps because the man was a stranger, and the night was long, and the sea outside was erasing everything behind them. "We fell out. After our mother died."

"Over money?"

"Over everything. Money was just the shape it took." He drank. "I said things to him. He said things to me. We were both right and that was the trouble. If one of us had been wrong we could have forgiven it."

The man considered this. "And now you'll look at him and you'll have to decide if you ever loved him."

Magnus said nothing. The man had said the thing he had not let himself form into words, and hearing it spoken in another man's mouth made it both more bearable and more terrible.

"I'm sorry," the man said. "That was a hard thing to say. I'm old and I've stopped softening things. It costs too much energy and I haven't got it to spare."

"No," Magnus said. "It's true. That's what it is."

The man finished his beer and stood, gathering his book. "I'll tell you what I learned, for whatever it's worth, which is probably nothing. When my brother went down I spent twenty years angry at the sea, at the skipper, at God, at my brother for being a fool. And one day the anger just—" he made a small flat gesture with his hand, a wave smoothing out "—it went. Not because I forgave anyone. Because I got tired. And what was underneath it, when the anger went, was just the love. It had been there the whole time. The anger was only the lid on the pot." He nodded. "Get some sleep if you can. Don't, if you can't. Either way the morning comes."

He went out, and the door swung shut behind him, and Magnus was alone again with his reflection.

He did not sleep. He lay in the lower bunk with the engine throbbing up through the mattress into his spine, and he let himself remember, which he had not done in a deliberate way for a long time. He remembered Aksel at seven, building a town out of driftwood on the shingle, naming every house, assigning families to them, weeping when the tide came and took it because he had grown to love the people he had invented. Magnus had laughed at him. Their father had laughed at him. And Aksel had stood with the water at his ankles and the wrecked town floating away and he had not understood why the two of them were laughing, and Magnus, even at ten, had felt the wrongness of his own laughter and had not been able to stop because stopping would have meant standing on Aksel's side, and you did not stand on Aksel's side, not in that house.

He remembered the day their father died, the relief of it, the way the relief had been the thing none of them could say. He remembered Aksel coming back from the city for the funeral, grown and strange in a black coat too thin for the weather, and how they had stood at the grave not touching, two men who had once shared a bed in a cold room and whispered to each other after the lamp was out, now unable to find a single word.

And he remembered the kitchen, after their mother. The papers spread on the table. The house, the small money, the boat. Aksel saying he wanted none of it, he wanted only to be free of it, and Magnus hearing in this not generosity but abandonment, the same abandonment as always, Aksel floating away on the tide while Magnus stood with the water rising and held everything together. You always leave, he had said. You leave and you let me carry it and then you come back clean and tell me you want nothing. And Aksel had said, You never let me carry anything. You never once let me. You took it all so you could be the one who suffered. You wanted the weight. And Magnus had stood up so fast his chair fell and he had said the thing, the worst thing, the thing about their mother and who she had loved and who she had merely tolerated, and he had watched it land in his brother's face like a stone in still water, and Aksel had gathered his thin coat and gone, and that had been the end of it.

He had been right. That was what he could not get past. Their mother had loved Aksel best, openly, helplessly, the way you love the child who needs you, and Magnus had spent forty years knowing it and saying nothing, and then he had said it, weaponized it, used the truest tenderest fact of all their lives as a knife. And the cruelty of it was not the saying. The cruelty was that he had wanted to be loved like that and never had been, and so he had taken the one person who had been loved that way and shown him that even this could be turned into a wound.

The cabin was very small. The sea passed on the other side of the steel, inches away, cold and patient. He thought of his brother in that water for weeks, turning in the slow currents of the strait, the papers in the pouch dissolving toward illegibility, the name going soft. He thought: if it is him, he was coming home. The address in the pouch was the old address, the house Magnus had sold years ago. Why would a man carry the address of a house that no longer belonged to anyone they knew, unless he was trying to find his way back to something, unless even after eleven years there was a thread in him that ran toward the place where the wrecked town had floated out on the tide?

Or perhaps it was not him at all. Perhaps it was some other drowned man who had happened to find, by the obscene coincidences the sea was capable of, papers that pointed at Magnus. Perhaps Aksel was alive in the city, in another country, alive and indifferent, and Magnus would go to the cold room and look at a stranger and feel the strange double grief of a death that was both real and not his to grieve.

Toward four he gave up on sleep and went back up on deck. The cold had deepened. The sky in the north had begun, very faintly, to suggest that it was not infinite, a thinning at the edge of the dark that was not yet light but was the rumor of it. The dog-walking woman was gone. The deck was empty. He stood at the rail and watched the black water unscroll and he tried to pray, though he did not believe in anything, and what came out was not a prayer but a kind of address, spoken under his breath into the wind that took it instantly and gave nothing back.

I'm sorry, he said. It was eleven years too late and it was said to the sea, which was not his brother, and it cost him everything to say it and accomplished nothing, and he understood that this was the shape of all real apology, that it is offered into a void that cannot absolve you, and that you make it anyway because the alternative is to carry the unsaid thing into your own grave.

He stayed on deck until the cold drove him in. He drank coffee in the bar where the bored woman had been replaced by a bored man, and he watched the windows go from black to gray to the pale exhausted blue of a northern dawn, and the land came up out of the sea ahead, low and dark and ringed with the lights of the town where the cold room was.

The sergeant met him at the terminal. He was younger than his voice had suggested, with a kind tired face. He drove Magnus through streets just waking, past shuttered shops and a bakery already lit and steaming, and Magnus thought how strange it was that the world went on baking bread on the morning you went to look at your brother's drowned face.

The mortuary was a low building behind the hospital. They gave him a coffee he did not want. A woman in a white coat explained, gently, what he would see and what he would not see, and warned him that the time in the water had altered things, that he should prepare himself, that recognition might come from small details rather than the whole, a scar, the shape of an ear, the hands.

The hands. Aksel had their mother's hands, long-fingered, the nails square. Magnus had their father's, blunt and broad. He had not thought of this in years and now it was the only thing he could think of, that he would know by the hands, that the hands would tell him.

They took him into the cold room. It was very bright and very quiet and it smelled of cold and chemicals and underneath, faintly, the sea. The shape on the table was covered. The woman in the white coat looked at him and he nodded that he was ready, though he was not, and she drew the sheet back from the face.

He made himself look.

The sea had done its work. The face was a face and was not a face; it had the architecture of a human face but the particulars had been softened and swollen and discolored until they were almost abstract, a sculptor's rough draft of a man. He looked at the brow, the nose, the set of the eyes that were closed. He looked for Aksel in it. He looked for the boy on the shingle and the man in the thin black coat, and he could not find them, and his heart began to pound with a terrible despair because the eleven years had won, he could not know, he would have to say he could not be sure, he would fail at this last simple thing as he had failed at everything between them.

"Take your time," the woman said quietly.

He looked at the brow again. There was something. Above the left eye, half-lost in the alteration of the flesh, a small white line, an old scar. And he remembered: they had been boys, on the boat, and the boom had come round in a gust and caught Aksel across the head, and there had been blood everywhere, blood on the deck and in Aksel's eyes, and Magnus, fourteen, had held his brother's head in his lap with his shirt pressed to the wound and had said, over and over, you're all right, you're all right, I've got you, and had felt for the first time in his life the full unbearable weight of loving someone, the terror of it, the way it could be taken from you. They had not told their father how it happened because it had been Magnus at the tiller and Magnus who had not watched the wind. Aksel had carried the scar and the lie for him and had never once, in all the years of their estrangement, used it against him.

"It's him," Magnus said. His voice did not sound like his own. "It's my brother. There's a scar. From when we were boys. I gave it to him."

He had not meant to say the last part. The woman did not react. She drew the sheet up gently and the face was gone, and Magnus stood in the bright cold room and understood that the despair of a moment ago had been a lie his fear had told him, because the recognition had come, it had come from the scar but it had come, and underneath the eleven years and the ugly words and the unforgiven things, the love had been there the whole time, exactly as the old man had said, the anger only the lid on the pot, and now the lid was off and there was nothing to be angry at, there was only his brother on the table and himself standing beside it, the two of them alone at last with no father between them and no mother's love to fight over, only the scar he had given and the lie his brother had carried for him without complaint for thirty years.

They took him to a room and gave him the effects. A wallet, the leather gone soft and dark. A few coins. A key to nothing they knew of. And the sealed pouch with the papers, which they had dried as best they could. Among them, folded small and worn at the creases as though it had been opened and refolded many times, was a photograph. The water had taken most of it. But he could see, in the ruin of it, two boys on a shingle beach, and the suggestion of something between them, driftwood perhaps, a structure, a town.

He had not known the photograph existed. He did not know who had taken it. But Aksel had carried it for eleven years, into the water, into the dark of the strait, and that was the thread, that was the address he had been steering toward, not the house Magnus had sold but the beach where the town had washed away and the brother who had laughed at him and held his bleeding head and lied to their father for him, all of it the same brother, all of it the same love that neither of them had ever once managed to say.

Magnus sat down with the photograph in his blunt broad hands. Outside the window the town was fully awake now, and the light had come, the thin gold light of the north, and somewhere a gull was crying over the harbor in the voice that gulls have, which is the voice of something that has lost something and does not know how to stop saying so. He held the photograph and he did not weep, not yet; that would come later, on the ferry south, alone on the deck with the wind taking it. For now he only held it, and he let himself, for the first time in eleven years, simply love his brother, who was dead, and who had come home.
