The smoke came up out of the Kettle drainage at twenty past two, and Marian knew the look of it before she knew anything else.

She had been watching that country for nineteen summers. She could read the difference between a column that meant a cookfire gone careless and a column that meant trouble the way other people read a face across a kitchen table. This one had the wrong color. Not the thin gray-blue of green wood, not the white of a doused pit still steaming. This was the brown of duff catching, of needle litter going up under a canopy, and it rose without hurry and then with hurry, building a shoulder against the flat afternoon sky.

She set down the coffee she had not finished and stood at the south window with the Osborne fire finder under her hands. Her thumbs found the brass by habit. She turned the ring until the sighting hair crossed the base of the column and read the azimuth and did not need to, because she knew exactly where the smoke was coming from. It was coming from the bench above Kettle Creek where the timber thinned into an old burn and a meadow opened and a cabin sat at the meadow's edge with a green metal roof her sister had hauled up in pieces over three summers, twenty years ago, on the back of a mule she'd named after their mother as a joke nobody else found funny.

The cabin was Eleanor's. Marian had not stood inside it in eleven years.

She reached for the radio out of the same reflex that had moved her thumbs, and her hand closed on the handset and she keyed it and there was nothing. Not static. Static would have been something. There was the particular dead silence of a thing that had given up. The radio had been failing for a week, fading in and out, and she had reported it twice to the district and twice they had said they'd send someone up with the resupply, and the resupply was not due for four more days because this was the last season the tower would be staffed at all and the district had already half forgotten it existed. They were letting Skookum Point die quietly. They were letting her die out of it quietly too, a sixty-one-year-old woman with a bad hip and a pension that wouldn't cover the winter, and the kindest thing they could think to do was not look at her too closely while it happened.

She keyed the radio three more times. She said the station call sign into the dead plastic. She said it the way you say a name at a bedside when the person is already gone, knowing it changes nothing, doing it anyway.

Then she put the handset down and made herself stop and look.

The column had a base now, a dark thickening down low where the fire was working in the heavy fuels, and above it the smoke was beginning to lean. There was wind moving up the drainage, an afternoon upcanyon wind that would push the fire toward the bench, toward the meadow, toward the green roof. She had watched a hundred fires in her life and she knew this one's intentions. It would run uphill. Fire always wanted to climb. It would reach the meadow within the hour if the meadow's grass was cured, and the grass would be cured because it was the middle of August and there had been no rain since the Fourth of July.

Eleanor would be there. Eleanor was always there in August. That had been the shape of her sister's life for two decades, the migration up to the Kettle every summer the way other people went to the same rented stretch of coast, except that Eleanor went alone and stayed for months and spoke to no one, and the cabin was not a vacation but a kind of refusal, a way of being unreachable that she had perfected the way some people perfect a craft. There was no phone up there. There was no road that a car could take, only the same trail the mule had walked. If the fire came for her she would have whatever warning her own eyes gave her and her own legs to carry her out, and Eleanor was fifty-eight and her knees were no better than Marian's hip.

Marian went to the cupboard and got the binoculars and came back and braced her elbows on the windowsill. The meadow was eight air miles off and the glasses could not bring her the cabin clear, only a green suggestion in a fold of dark timber, but she looked anyway, hunting for movement, for the small wrongness of a person where the land should be empty. She saw nothing. She saw the smoke, and the smoke saw her back.

She thought: she might not be up there this year.

She thought: you don't know that. You don't know anything about her year. You don't know anything about her at all anymore, and whose doing is that.

The last thing Eleanor had said to her, in the parking lot of the funeral home in Republic with their father not yet cold in the building behind them, was *you always have to be the one who's right, even now, even about this,* and Marian had said something back, she could not now fully remember what, something about how somebody had to make the decisions while Eleanor was off playing hermit, something cruel and true the way the cruelest things are true, and Eleanor had gotten into her truck and driven up the mountain and that had been the end of it. Eleven years. Birthday cards that stopped after the third year. A forwarded address that went stale. Marian had told herself the silence was mutual, was dignified, was the natural cooling of two people who had never been able to be in a room together without one of them bleeding. She had told herself a great many things up here in the tower where there was no one to argue.

Now there was smoke over her sister's roof and a dead radio in her hand and eight miles of downhill between her and anyone who could do anything about it.

She did the arithmetic that her body had been doing for the last several minutes without telling her. The truck was at the trailhead, eight miles down the lookout trail, in the gravel turnout where she left it all season. If she walked out she could reach it in—she was honest with herself—three hours, maybe more with the hip, and from the trailhead it was another forty minutes of forest road to the ranger station, and then they would have to dispatch, and someone would have to climb a helicopter into the late-afternoon air, and the whole machinery of it would grind into motion long after the fire had already decided what it meant to do. By the time anyone reached the Kettle bench it would be dark, or it would be over.

Or she could go down into the drainage herself.

It was not a plan a sane person made. There was no trail that ran from Skookum Point down into the Kettle; the two pieces of country belonged to different worlds, divided by a ridge and a long talus slope and a band of timber that no one had reason to cross. She would be going down into a drainage that was on fire, alone, sixty-one years old, with whatever she could carry, and the smart thing, the trained thing, the thing she had spent nineteen years teaching to every green seasonal who passed through the tower, was that you did not put yourself below a fire on a slope with the wind at its back. You stayed high. You stayed in the black or you stayed out of it altogether. You called it in and you let the people whose job it was do the job.

But the people whose job it was could not hear her. And the fire did not care about the wisdom she'd spent a life accumulating, and neither, she understood, looking at that green roof through the shaking glasses, did she.

She made herself sit down. She made herself drink water. Panic was a fire of its own and she had watched it burn down better people than herself. She breathed and she thought and she let the thinking go where it wanted, which was backward, which was always backward when you were afraid.

She and Eleanor had built things together once. That was the part nobody who knew them now would believe. There had been a span of years in childhood, before their mother got sick the first time, when the two of them had been a single four-legged animal roaming the property outside Republic, building forts in the lilac, damming the irrigation ditch, lying out on the warm boards of the porch at night naming the few constellations they knew and inventing the rest. Eleanor had been the dreamer and Marian the engineer. Eleanor would say *let's build a tower so high we can see the ocean* and Marian would say *we can't see the ocean, the ocean's six hundred miles away,* and Eleanor would look at her with that particular wounded patience and say *I know that, Mare, that's not the point,* and Marian had spent the rest of her life not understanding that it was not the point. That had been the whole trouble between them, distilled. One of them lived in the world of what could be measured and the other lived somewhere Marian had never been able to follow, and instead of loving her sister for the country she lived in, Marian had stood at the border with her instruments and corrected the map.

The smoke had doubled while she sat there grieving the past. She stood up so fast her hip caught and white light went through her and she gripped the window frame and waited it out.

There was no more deciding to do. The deciding had been done in some older part of her before her mind got involved.

She packed fast and she packed like the professional she was, because that at least the years had given her. Two liters of water in the daypack and a third she would carry in her hand and drink first. A wet bandana for her face. The wool shirt, because synthetics melted onto skin and she had seen the scars on a man who'd learned that. Leather gloves. The headlamp, because she would be coming back in the dark if she came back at all. Her father's old hunting knife. A space blanket, which was the closest thing she had to a fire shelter and was very nearly useless and she took it anyway. She wrote her intention on the log sheet on the table in her square careful hand—*1455 hrs. Smoke in Kettle drainage, est. azimuth 197, vicinity of private cabin. Radio dead. Proceeding on foot to render aid. M. Vesper*—and she dated it and she left it where the resupply crew would find it in four days, and she understood as she wrote it that she might be writing the last sentence anyone would have from her, and she found that she did not much mind. She was sixty-one. The tower was being decommissioned. The summers were ending. There were worse things to spend a life's remainder on than walking toward your sister.

She went down the tower stairs and out into the heat.

The smell of it reached her halfway down the catwalk, faint, the first thread of woodsmoke riding the upcanyon wind, and her body responded to it the way it had responded for nineteen years, with a lift of alertness, with the old animal readiness. But underneath that, today, was something the nineteen years had never put there: a personal fear, located not in the trained mind but in the gut, the fear of a particular green roof and a particular woman who shared her blood and her stubbornness and her mother's stubborn jaw.

The first two miles were the worst because they were the steepest and the most foolish, dropping off the back of Skookum Point through the talus where the rocks shifted under her boots and the hip sang with every long step down. She used a stick she found, a sun-bleached length of subalpine fir, and she went sideways down the worst of it the way she'd been taught a lifetime ago by a packer who said you never face downhill on bad ground because the mountain wants to throw you and you mustn't give it your chest. Below the talus the timber started and the going was easier and darker and she could no longer see the smoke, which was its own kind of torment, because she could smell it strengthening and she could not see what it was doing.

She thought about what she would say to Eleanor if Eleanor was alive and well and merely surprised, if the smoke turned out to be a thing already under control, a slash pile, a lightning snag that would lie down on its own. She thought about Eleanor opening the cabin door and finding her older sister standing there filthy and gasping after eleven years of silence, having descended a mountain that had no trail, and she could not imagine the look on her sister's face and the not-imagining frightened her more than the fire. What did you say. *I saw your roof and I thought.* I saw the smoke and I couldn't—

She lost the thought to the work of crossing a deadfall, a great fallen Douglas-fir she had to go over because going around meant a hundred yards of brush, and she got a leg up on it and swung the other over and her hip betrayed her and she came down hard on the far side, onto her hands and one knee, and knelt there in the duff breathing while a fly investigated her ear. She got up. You always get up. That was the one piece of wisdom she trusted entirely, the only theology she had: you get up and you keep going downhill toward the thing that needs you, because the alternative is to lie in the duff and let the flies have you, and she was not ready for that, not yet, not with smoke over Eleanor's roof.

The light began to change as she came down into the drainage. It went orange and strange, a sick filtered light, the sun shouldering through the smoke that was now spilling across the whole upper basin. The air thickened. She wetted the bandana from her hand-bottle and tied it over her nose and mouth and breathed through the wet cloth and the smoke still found her, scratching down into her chest, and she coughed and kept walking. The wind was at her back now, pushing down into the drainage—no. She stopped. The wind was wrong. It should have been blowing uphill, upcanyon, this time of day; that was what afternoon wind did, that was what she had been counting on, that the fire would climb away from the cabin and toward her descent and that she would meet it coming up and skirt around its flank. But the wind was coming down the drainage now, into her face, downcanyon, which meant the fire's own heat had begun to make its own weather, or the larger pattern of the day had shifted, and either way it meant the fire was no longer climbing predictably toward her. It was being pushed back down toward the meadow.

Toward the cabin.

She came out of the timber onto a rib of open rock that gave her, for the first time since the tower, a view down into the basin, and she saw it all at once and it stopped her cold.

The fire had grown into something with a will. It had a flaming front maybe a quarter mile wide working through the timber on the far slope, throwing up a roiling column that flattened out at the top into a dirty anvil, and beneath the column the light was the color of an old bruise. And there, below it, perhaps a mile off and a thousand feet down, was the meadow, gold in the strange light, and at its edge the dark mass of the cabin's timber and the dull green flash of the roof, and the fire was reaching down the slope toward all of it with long fingers of flame that ran in the cured grass faster than a person could.

And in the meadow there was a figure.

She brought up the binoculars with hands that would not be steady and found the gold blur and dragged the focus until the blur resolved into a person, small and dark, moving. Moving with purpose, back and forth at the meadow's edge near the cabin, and Marian understood after a moment what the figure was doing because she would have been doing it herself: beating at the grass, fighting the small fires that the embers were starting ahead of the main front, trying to keep the cabin's clearing from catching. It was Eleanor. It could be no one else. It was her sister down there in the burning meadow beating at the grass with what looked like a wet blanket or a coat, fighting a fight she could not win, too stubborn or too frightened or too alone to run.

"Get out," Marian said into the wet bandana. "Eleanor, get out, leave it, get out—" and her voice went nowhere, was nothing, was less even than the dead radio had been.

She started down off the rib, fast and stupid now, all her caution burned away by the sight of that small fighting figure, and the slope dropped her through brush that whipped her face and over rock that turned her ankle so that she had to catch herself again and again on the fir stick, and the smoke got thicker as she descended into it until the world was a brown blur thirty feet across and she was navigating by the slope itself, by down, by the simple gravity that pulled her toward her sister.

She came into the meadow's upper edge with her lungs full of fire and her eyes streaming, and the heat was a wall now, a thing with mass, and she could hear it, the freight-train roar that people always said and that was true, the sound of a fire eating a forest, and through the murk she saw the cabin closer now and the figure still beating at the ground, and she filled what was left of her lungs and screamed her sister's name into the roaring air.

The figure stopped. Straightened. Turned.

And Marian saw, in the last clear instant before the smoke folded between them again, that it was not Eleanor.

It was a young man, twenty-five perhaps, soot-blackened, wild-eyed, a stranger, holding a sodden sleeping bag he'd been using to beat the embers. He saw her and his face broke open with a desperate hope, the hope of a person who has been alone with a catastrophe and suddenly is not, and he came toward her stumbling through the grass and caught her by the arms and was shouting something she could not hear over the fire, and then could: "—help me, there's a woman, she fell, her ankle, she can't walk, I couldn't—I couldn't carry her and fight it too—"

"Where," Marian said. "Where is she."

"In the cabin, I got her in the cabin, but the cabin's going to—"

And Marian was already moving, already past him, because she had understood everything in the half-second it took him to say *a woman*, understood that Eleanor had rented her refusal out, or sold it, or given it away, that the cabin had passed to strangers or that these were strangers Eleanor had let in, friends or renters or hikers caught by weather, and that it did not matter, that it had never mattered who was in the cabin, that she had come down a mountain with no trail for a green roof and the green roof held a stranger and Marian was going to get her out anyway because there was a person who could not walk in a building that was about to burn and Marian's whole foolish stubborn life had been arranged, she saw now, precisely so that she could be standing in this meadow at this moment with her body still strong enough to do the one thing that needed doing.

She reached the cabin door. The young man was at her shoulder. The heat off the timbered wall was enormous and the meadow behind them was beginning to run with little snakes of flame in the cured gold grass. She hit the door with her shoulder and it gave and she went into the smoke-dim room and there on the floor by the cold stove was a woman, gray-haired, clutching her own ankle, her face a mask of pain and terror, and she looked up at Marian out of the smoke.

It was Eleanor.

She was older than the woman in Marian's memory by eleven hard years and her hair had gone fully gray and her face was thinner, but it was Eleanor, it was her sister's face, her mother's jaw, and the young man behind Marian was shouting *Mom, I found help, Mom,* and Marian understood the last thing, the thing that struck her harder than the heat, harder than the eleven years: that her sister had a son. That somewhere in the silence Eleanor had made a life with a person in it, a child, a grown child who called her Mom and had stayed to fight a fire for her, and Marian had known none of it, had been corrected off the map entirely, had been the one who left the country and not the one left behind.

Eleanor's eyes found her in the smoke and went wide, and her cracked lips moved, and over the roar of the fire eating the meadow her sister said her name—not a question, not an accusation, only her name, *Marian,* the way you'd say it across a kitchen table, the way you'd say it to someone you had been expecting, all these years, to finally come.

"I've got you," Marian said, and bent, and took her sister's weight onto her ruined hip, and lifted.
