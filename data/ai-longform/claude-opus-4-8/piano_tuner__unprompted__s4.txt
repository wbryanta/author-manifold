The Hutchins place came first on the route, the way it always had, three miles out from town where the road turned to dirt and the mailboxes leaned at the angle of old men listening. Earl Tobin drove slowly because his eyes had begun to fail at the edges, the way a photograph yellows from the border inward, and because there was no longer any reason to hurry. The light fell long across the soybean fields. He had made this circuit for fifty-one years, the same eleven stops, and now there were six, and now there would be none, because his hands had begun to betray him and because the people who had once kept pianos had mostly died or moved their children to cities where nobody owned anything that needed tuning.

Della Hutchins met him at the screen door in a housedress the color of weak tea. She was eighty-six. Her husband had been gone nine years and the piano had been her mother's, a Story & Clark upright with a cracked soundboard that Earl had been nursing along since the Eisenhower administration.

"I knew it was Thursday," she said. "I made coffee."

"You didn't have to do that, Della."

"I know I didn't have to."

He carried his case into the parlor, where the piano stood against the inside wall as it had always stood, away from the windows and the damp. The room smelled of dust and lavender and the particular staleness of a house kept too warm by someone who felt the cold. He set his case on the bench, opened it, and looked for a moment at the felts and the tuning hammer and the little strips of rubber mute, the tools worn smooth by his own grip, and he had the sensation, not for the first time lately, of looking at the instruments of a trade that had outlived its purpose, like a cooper's adze or the tongs a farrier used.

"Does anybody play it now?" he asked, though he knew the answer.

"I touch it sometimes," Della said. "I can still do 'In the Garden' if I go slow. My fingers don't always go where I send them."

"Mine either."

She laughed, a dry sound. "Then how do you tune it?"

"By ear, mostly. The hands just hold on."

He sat and ran his thumb up the keys, and the old wires answered him, some flat, some sharp, the A nearly a quarter-tone off so that the whole instrument sounded as if it had been dreaming of another key. He set the mutes and began with the temperament octave, the way he had learned it from a German named Brubaker in 1958, laying the foundation in the middle of the keyboard from which everything else was reckoned. He tuned the fourths and fifths slightly narrow, slightly wide, listening to the beats, the little pulses of dissonance that told him how close he was, counting them without counting, the way you breathe without deciding to.

Della sat in the wing chair behind him and did not speak, and he was grateful for that. Some people talked the whole time, as if silence in their own house frightened them, and he had learned to tune through it, but he preferred the quiet, the long communion with a single instrument that nobody but himself would hear at its best.

When he finished he played a little of a Bach prelude, the C major, the one everyone knew, and the piano sounded as good as it could sound, which was not good, but was honest.

"There," he said.

"It sounds like it used to," Della said, and he did not correct her.

She paid him in cash from a coffee can, the same as ever, and pressed an extra five into his hand which he tried to refuse and then accepted because refusing would have shamed her. At the door she touched his arm.

"This is the last time, isn't it," she said. It was not a question.

"I'm getting out of it, Della. The hands."

She nodded. "I'll have nobody come round on Thursdays now."

"You could call. I'm not going anywhere."

"No," she said, and smiled. "Neither am I. That's the trouble."

He drove on. The Vance farm next, where the piano belonged to a man named Royce who had bought it for a daughter long since grown and gone to Houston, and who kept it tuned out of some obscure loyalty to a version of his life that had not turned out. Royce was not home; he left the key under the flowerpot as arranged, and Earl let himself in and tuned the spinet in the empty house with the clock ticking and a dog watching him through the glass of the back door, and when he was done he locked up and left the agreed-upon amount written in the little notebook he kept, to be settled later, except there would be no later, and he supposed Royce would mail him a check or not.

The day went on like that. He had loved this work. He wanted to be able to say that he still loved it, but the truth was more complicated and he was too old to lie to himself, having spent so many years lying gently to other people about their pianos. What he had loved was the certainty of it. A piano either was in tune or it was not. The beats either slowed and stopped or they did not. In a life that had refused, finally, to resolve—the marriage that had gone quiet and then cold, Marian dead now four years; the farm sold; the friends thinning out like hair—the piano had remained a problem he could solve. He could walk into a room where everything was wrong and make one thing right.

He stopped at the diner in town for a grilled cheese and a cup of coffee, and the waitress, Brenda, who was sixty and had known him forever, asked if he wanted pie and he said no and then said yes, because why not, and ate a piece of cherry pie alone at the counter while the afternoon emptied out the booths. He thought about the church. He had saved it for last on purpose, the way you save the thing you most want or most dread, which are sometimes the same thing.

It had been twenty-two years.

Daniel had been eleven. He had wanted to play—nobody had pushed him, that was the thing Earl held onto, nobody had made the boy sit at the bench. Marian had said maybe it was too much, with the lessons costing what they cost and the drive into Carthage every week, but the boy had a feeling for it that Earl recognized the way you recognize your own face in a child, the same and not the same. He had a quickness in the left hand. He heard things. Once, when he was nine, he had come into the parlor while Earl was tuning the church's old piano at home—they had loaned it to him for a rebuild that winter—and the boy had said, that note is lonely, and Earl had played it, the E above middle C, and asked what he meant, and Daniel had said it doesn't have anybody, and Earl understood that the boy was hearing the way the unison was off, the three strings of that note not yet brought into agreement, one of them wandering, and he had thought, this child hears what I hear.

He finished the pie and left money on the counter and told Brenda to take care of herself and she said you too, Earl, the way people did, not knowing it was the last time, or maybe knowing, because in a town that size the news of a man getting out of his life's work traveled the way weather did.

The church sat at the north end of town where the houses gave out and the country began again, a white frame building with a steeple that had been struck by lightning in 1971 and rebuilt slightly out of true, so that it leaned a few degrees toward the cemetery, as if bowing. First Methodist. Earl was not a believer, had not been since the war, but he had tuned this piano for half a century and had come, in the way of long association, to feel something for the building that was not quite faith and not quite nothing.

The current minister was a young man named Pruitt who had a key waiting for him in the office and a note in handwriting that sloped uphill: *Mr. Tobin—Thank you for everything. Lock up when you're done. The Lord keep you.* Earl let himself into the sanctuary through the side door.

The light came in slantwise through the tall plain windows, no stained glass, only the wavering old glass that turned the trees outside to water. Dust hung in the beams. The pews waited in their rows. At the front, to the left of the pulpit, stood the piano, a Baldwin grand of considerable age, the only grand on his whole route, donated decades ago by a family whose name was carved on a little brass plate screwed to its side. He had argued for this piano many times. The board had wanted to replace it with an electronic keyboard in the nineties, easier to maintain, never needing tuning, and Earl had stood up in a meeting—he, who never spoke in meetings—and said that an electronic instrument did not make a sound, it made a recording of a sound, and that the difference was the difference between a flower and a photograph of a flower, and they had kept the Baldwin, whether because of him or in spite of him he never knew.

He set his case on the front pew and went to the piano and lifted the lid and propped it open on the long stick, and looked down into it, at the harp and the strings and the dampers, the felts gone a little gray. He could smell the old wood and the wax. He sat on the bench.

The boy had sat here.

It had been June. The church had been full, or full enough—the recital was a joint affair, the piano teacher from Carthage bringing her students out to perform in turn, because the church had the only good instrument and because the teacher, Miss Acheson, had a sentimental streak and liked the idea of children playing in a place built for music that was greater than they were. There had been seven children. Daniel had been fifth.

Earl had tuned the piano that very morning, had risen before light and driven in and made it as perfect as he had ever made anything, leaning over the strings while the church was empty and gray, and he had thought, this is the best thing I will ever do, not the tuning but the reason for it, my son will play this and it will be right, I have made the world right for him for the space of one afternoon.

He had sat in the third pew with Marian. He could not now remember what the first four children played. He remembered Daniel walking out from the side in the gray slacks they had bought him, too long, hemmed up by Marian the night before, and the white shirt, and how the boy had not looked at the audience but had gone straight to the bench as Earl had taught him, sit, find your hands, breathe, begin. He had played a Clementi sonatina and then a piece by Schumann from the album for the young, "The Wild Horseman," and then, because Miss Acheson had let her best students choose a third, he had played a thing he had been working at in secret, a nocturne by Field, which was too hard for him, which everyone knew was too hard for him, and he had played it anyway, with mistakes, with two places where he stopped and had to find his way back in, and it had been the most beautiful thing Earl Tobin had ever heard in his life.

Not because it was good. It was the playing of a gifted child, no more. It was beautiful because his son was making the sound and the sound was true and the piano under those small hands was the piano that Earl had spent his life learning to serve, and the two things had come together in that room, the instrument and the boy, and Earl had understood that everything he had done—every cold morning, every drive down every dirt road, every hour bent over wires in somebody else's parlor—had been, without his knowing it, a preparation for this, a long apprenticeship whose masterpiece was four minutes of a child playing John Field imperfectly in a church that leaned toward the graves.

He had wept. Marian had taken his hand. Daniel had finished and stood and bowed the stiff little bow Miss Acheson had drilled into them, and had looked, for one instant, out into the pews, and found his father's face, and Earl had nodded at him, just once, and the boy had seen it. That was the thing. The boy had seen him nod.

Three years later Daniel had stopped playing. Fourteen, and other things had claimed him, a guitar and then a car and then a girl, the ordinary defection of a boy from the dreams his father had dreamed for him, and Earl had told himself it was right, that you could not hold a child to a path he had not chosen, that the gift was the boy's to spend or keep. He had told himself this and had believed it most of the time. The piano lessons had stopped. The Baldwin had gone on needing tuning for the hymns on Sunday, and Earl had gone on tuning it, and the boy had grown up and the boy had gone to the community college and then enlisted, not in any war anyone could name, just the peacetime army, a job, a way out of a town that was dying, and there had been a helicopter, a training exercise, a thing that should not have happened and that happened anyway, in a field in Georgia, and Daniel had been twenty-one.

That was nineteen years ago now. Earl had kept tuning the Baldwin.

He sat on the bench with his hands in his lap and the afternoon light moving by degrees across the floor, and he was very tired. He had thought, planning this, that he would tune the piano one last time and that this would be the close of something, a door shut gently, a final right thing done in the place where the best thing had happened. But now that he was here he did not know if his hands would do it. They had begun to tremble in the last year, a fine tremor that he could hide from his clients by working slowly, but that he could not hide from himself, and that some mornings made the tuning hammer feel like a living thing trying to twist out of his grip.

He flexed his fingers. He took the hammer from the case and the strip of red felt and the rubber mutes. He set the mutes between the strings of the temperament octave, silencing the outer strings of each note so he could tune the middle one true, and he struck the A and brought it to the fork—he still used a fork, an old steel tuning fork worn bright at the tines, A-440, that Brubaker had given him—and the note rose to meet the fork and the beats slowed and slowed and stopped, and the A was true, and his hands had done it.

He went on. The light moved. He worked his way out from the center, the fourths and fifths, the octaves, the unisons, and the trembling was there but the ear was steady, the ear that did not tremble, that heard the beats and counted them and knew when they ceased, and one by one he brought the strings of the old Baldwin into agreement, into that condition that is not perfection, because equal temperament is a compromise, a way of being slightly wrong in every key so as to be playable in all of them, but that is the closest a piano comes to telling the truth.

He tuned the lonely notes. He brought the three strings of each unison into one voice. He thought, as he worked, of how a piano is not a single instrument but two hundred and thirty strings pulling against an iron frame with twenty tons of force, all of it wanting to fly apart, all of it held in a tension that is itself the music, and how the tuner's whole art is to manage that tension, to make of all that contrary pulling one agreeing sound, and how this had seemed to him, for fifty-one years, like a thing worth doing with a life.

When he reached the top of the keyboard his hands were aching and the light had gone gold and then begun to gray, and he set down the hammer and removed the last of the mutes and sat for a moment with his eyes closed.

Then he played.

He was not a pianist. He could play in the plain way of a man who had spent his life around the instrument without ever studying it, hymns mostly, and the few simple pieces he had taught himself in order to test his work. But he had kept, all these years, the music for the Field nocturne. He had bought a copy after the recital, meaning to learn it slowly, the way the boy had learned it, and he had never gotten very far, and after the boy died he had not been able to look at it. But he had kept it. It was in his case now, the paper soft with age, the corner of the first page torn.

He set it on the rack. He found the opening with his right hand, the simple singing line, and brought his left hand under it, the rocking accompaniment that he had heard Daniel practice a hundred times through the wall of the boy's room, the same six notes over and over until they were even. He played it slowly. He made mistakes. He stopped twice, as the boy had stopped, and found his way back in. His hands trembled and the notes were not always even and the nocturne came out of the old Baldwin halting and imperfect and alive, and the piano was in tune, was as in tune as he could make it, was true under his unsteady hands the way it had been true under the boy's small ones, and the sound went up into the empty sanctuary and the slanting light and was held there, the way the steeple held its bell, the way the frame held the strings, the way a man holds a thing he cannot keep.

He played it to the end. The last phrase descended and came to rest on the tonic, and he let the note ring and held the pedal so that it rang and rang and slowly died, the felts coming back down onto the strings, the sound thinning into the silence of the room until there was no longer any way to say whether he still heard it or only remembered it.

He sat with his hands on the keys.

Outside, a car passed on the road and was gone. The light reddened. He thought he should be feeling something larger than what he felt, some grief equal to the size of the thing, but what he felt instead was very quiet and very small, like the last beat of a unison just before it stops, that final slow pulse you have to hold your breath to hear. He had made the world right for the space of one afternoon. He had done it once when it mattered most and the boy had seen him nod, and he had done it now when it mattered to no one but himself, and these were, he understood, the same act, separated by twenty-two years and joined by the instrument under his hands, which would go on being a piano after he was gone, which would go out of tune again as everything did, and which no one might ever tune again.

He closed the music. He put it back in the case with the hammer and the mutes and the worn felts and the bright steel fork. He lowered the lid of the Baldwin and slid the stick into its bracket and laid his hand flat on the wood for a moment, on the cool old finish, the way you would lay your hand on the flank of an animal to quiet it, or on the shoulder of someone sleeping.

Then he carried his case up the aisle through the graying light, and at the door he turned and looked back at the piano standing alone at the front of the empty church, and he stood there a while. The room was very quiet. He had spent his life in the service of a sound that did not last, that began to die the instant it was made, and he found that he did not mind. The flower was better than the photograph. He had been right about that, in the meeting, all those years ago. A thing that fades is more alive than a thing that doesn't.

He turned off the lights. He locked the door behind him as the note had asked. The evening was coming on, cool, smelling of cut hay, and the steeple leaned its few degrees toward the stones where Daniel lay, and Earl Tobin stood by his truck in the gravel and did not get in, not yet, listening to the country going quiet around him, the last beats slowing, the long unison of the world coming, for the space of one held breath, into something like agreement.
