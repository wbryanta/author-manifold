The reservoir had a smell now that it had never had before, or rather it had a smell that belonged to something else entirely, to the inside of things rather than the outside. Mud and rot and the green breath of algae dying on stones that had not seen air since before the war. Della Pruitt stood at the edge of what had been the water and what was now a long gray flat of cracked silt, and she breathed it in, and her body remembered before her mind did. The back of her throat knew this place. Some animal part of her that had been four years old and had not thought about any of this in fifty-six years stood up inside her and looked around.

She had brought the equipment because the county had asked her to bring the equipment. That was the official reason she was here, the only reason that existed on paper. The drought had pulled Lake Hesper down to a level not recorded since the dam closed its gates in 1961, and the planning commission wanted the old streets mapped while they were visible, wanted the foundations documented, the well casings flagged, the question of structural hazard answered before some teenager broke a leg climbing into a cellar hole. Della was the senior surveyor for the county. It was her name on the work order. Nobody on the commission had thought to check, or had thought it worth mentioning, that the town under the lake was the town where she had been born, that the family on the deed of the parcel marked PRUITT, J.H. on the 1958 plat had been her family, that the J.H. was her grandfather and the small square beside it her father's house.

She had not corrected them. She had signed the work order and loaded the truck.

The bottom of the lake was not what she had expected, though she could not have said what she expected. She had carried an image for half a century and the image was wrong in the way that childhood images are always wrong, scaled to a child's body, lit by a child's certainty. She had imagined streets. She had imagined walking down a street. What lay before her was a country of suggestion, a place where the idea of a town had been pressed into mud and then erased and then half-recovered, like a word written in pencil and rubbed out and written over. The roadbeds were visible as low rises where the gravel had held. The foundations were rectangles of darker stone, some of them, and most of them not even that, just changes in the texture of the silt, depressions, the ghosts of rooms. Trees stood here and there, the trees that had not been cut before the flooding, and they were not trees anymore but black bones, limbless trunks the water had stripped and pickled and stood back up. They leaned at angles. They had a look of having been surprised.

She set up the base station on the boat ramp where the concrete still held, lashed the antenna to the rail, and let it find the satellites. While it acquired she stood with the rover pole resting in the crook of her arm and looked down what had been Front Street toward what had been the center of town, and she made herself be a surveyor. That was the discipline of it. You reduced the world to points. You did not let the world be larger than the points or you would never get any work done. A surveyor's eye took the chaos of the actual and found in it the recoverable: a corner, an edge, a place where two lines met and could be called a thing. She had spent forty years learning to look at land this way and the looking had saved her more than once, on days when the land held something she did not want to feel.

She started down Front Street.

The mud was firmer than it looked where the old road ran beneath it, and softer than it looked everywhere else, and she learned the difference fast, learned to read the sheen of it, the places where her boot would sink to the ankle and the places where it would hold. She walked the centerline as best she could reconstruct it and she shot points as she went, the rover beeping its small satisfied beep each time it fixed a position, and she spoke into the recorder clipped to her collar. Road centerline, she said. Gravel surface, intermittent. Probable culvert, she said, at a place where the rise dipped and a rusted ring of corrugated pipe showed its mouth in the bank. The work made a sound around her, the steady procedural sound of her own voice, and inside that sound she walked into the town where she had been born.

The Hendry store had stood on the corner of Front and Main. She knew this the way she knew the arrangement of her own kitchen, without consulting anything, and when she came to the corner there was the foundation, a real one, mortared fieldstone two courses high, the rectangle of it clean and legible in the mud. She stopped. She had been inside that store. The certainty of it arrived whole and undeniable. She had stood on a wooden floor inside those walls and there had been a smell of coffee and oiled wood and the cold metal smell of the candy case, and her grandfather had lifted her up to see into the case, and she had pointed at something, and the memory stopped there the way memories stop, at the pointing, before the having. She did not know what she had pointed at. She never would. The floor was gone and the coffee was gone and the candy case was gone and the man who had lifted her was sixty years gone, and what remained was two courses of stone enclosing a rectangle of mud that her instrument now reduced to four corners and an area in square feet.

She shot the corners. She spoke the description. Commercial foundation, fieldstone, she said, approximately twenty-two by thirty. She did not say Hendry's store. She did not say I bought candy here. The recorder was county property and the words on it would be transcribed by a clerk and filed, and there was no field on any form for the things she was not saying.

She worked the foundation slowly, walking its perimeter, and at the back corner she found the well. It was a dug well, stone-lined, the kind that had served the whole block before the town had pipes, and the drought had emptied it the way it had emptied the lake, so that she could lean over the ring of stones and look down into a dark shaft that went below the level of the lakebed, below the dead town into the older dark that had been under the town before there was a town. She got down on her knees in the mud and looked into it. A breath came up out of it, cool, mineral, the breath of the actual earth, and she understood that this hole had been here before her grandfather and would be here after the lake came back, that of all the made things in this drowned place the well was the one that reached down past making into the simple fact of water, and the irony of it was not lost on her, a well at the bottom of a lake, a thirst preserved in the middle of all that drowning.

She flagged it. The commission wanted the wells flagged for the hazard they posed. She drove a length of rebar with an orange flag into the silt beside the stone ring and the flag stood up bright and absurd in all that gray, the only saturated color in the whole landscape, and she looked at it and felt something she could not afford to feel and stood and went on.

She had decided, when she signed the work order, that she would not go to the house. She had decided this clearly and she had not examined the decision because examining it would have made it a thing she was struggling with, and she did not want it to be a thing she was struggling with, she wanted it to be a settled matter, an absence on the map of the day. The house was on Cedar Street, two blocks back from Front, and her route did not require her to go up Cedar. She could map the commercial center and the road network and the public infrastructure and write her report and the house would remain where it had been for fifty-six years, underwater in her, and the lake would come back in the fall when the rains came and cover it again and that would be that.

She mapped Main Street. She mapped the church foundation, which was larger than she remembered and which still held the lower steps that had led up to a door that was not there. She mapped the school, where the concrete had been good and where the play yard could still be made out, a flat place, and she did not let herself imagine children in it. She mapped the bank, a small bank, the vault still standing because they had built it to stand, a steel box four feet on a side, its door swung open and silt-choked, emptied of money in 1960 and emptied of meaning since, and she shot its corners and moved on. The sun went over and started down the western sky and the light came across the lakebed almost level now, raking, and in that light the texture of everything sharpened, the ridges and depressions threw long shadows, and the town stood up out of the mud more clearly than it had all day, more clearly, she thought, than it had any right to.

She was packing the rover at the boat ramp, the day's points stored, the work essentially done, when she stopped packing the rover.

She stood with the pole half-collapsed in her hands and she looked back up Front Street toward the corner where Cedar branched off, and she thought about her father.

He had carried her out. That was the thing she had not let herself arrive at all day, the thing under the candy case and the well and the surfaces she had kept her mind moving across. When the gates closed and the water began to rise, slowly, over months, the town had emptied in an orderly way, the families taking what could be taken, the houses sold to the water district for what the water district decided to pay, and it had not been a drama, it had been a long bureaucratic drowning that the newspapers of the time recorded with a kind of pride, the dam a triumph, the reservoir a guarantee against exactly the drought that had now undone it. But for her it had been her father carrying her down the front steps of the house on Cedar Street for the last time, and her looking back over his shoulder at the door, and the door standing open, and inside the door the dark of the hall where the stairs went up, and her father's hand spread warm on her back, and his voice saying something she could not recover, and the smell of him, tobacco and the wool of his coat, and the certainty in her four-year-old body that they would come back, that this was a leaving like other leavings, that the door stood open because they would walk back through it.

They had not walked back through it. He had died when she was eleven, in the next town over, in a rented house that had never smelled like the wool of his coat. And the house on Cedar Street had gone under the water and become a thing she did not visit even in her mind, because the door was open in her mind, it was always open, and she had learned young not to look down the hall.

She put the rover down on the concrete.

She told herself she was going to map Cedar Street because it was part of the road network and the road network was part of the work order, and this was true, and it was also a lie, and she held both of these in her hands the way she had held the contradiction of the well, and she walked back up Front Street into the lengthening shadows with only the rover, leaving the rest of the equipment at the ramp.

Cedar Street ran back from Front between two rows of foundations, the houses of the people she had been born among, the Caudills and the Sextons and the family whose name she had lost, and she walked it slowly, reading the rises, and she did not shoot points now, she had stopped pretending to herself about the points. She walked up the left side because the house had been on the left. She counted the foundations. The first was the Caudill place, she was nearly sure, and the second a smaller house she could not name, and the third—

The third was theirs.

She knew it before she reached it and she did not know how she knew it, there was nothing to know it by, it was a rectangle of mud like the others with a low remnant of foundation wall, but her body walked her to it and stopped her at the front of it where the steps had been, and she stood where her father had carried her down the steps that were not there, and she looked at the place where the door had been.

There was no door. There was no hall. There was the open rectangle of the foundation and inside it the silt lay smooth and gray and undifferentiated, the rooms erased, the walls that had divided the rooms gone to whatever the water takes walls to, and she stood at the front of it and made herself look at the smooth gray mud where the dark hall had been for fifty-six years, and the hall was not there. There was nothing down the hall because there was no hall. There was only mud, lit orange now by the low sun, holding nothing, threatening nothing, a flat and ordinary absence with a snail track crossing it where some creature of the lakebed had made its slow way across the floor of her father's house.

She stepped over the foundation wall.

She had not planned to. Her boot went into the mud inside the rectangle and sank and held, and she stood inside the house, inside where the house had been, in the front room, and she looked back at the gap where the front door had been and she saw it from the inside now, from where she had never been when they left, because when they left she had been looking back over her father's shoulder from outside, from the steps, from already gone. She had never stood inside and watched the door. She stood inside it now. The gap was just a gap. The light came through it and lay on the mud. Beyond it she could see down the rise of Cedar Street to the corner and beyond the corner the flat of the lakebed and beyond that the standing water, far off, low, waiting in its diminished basin for the rains.

She walked back through the house.

She walked from where the front room had been toward where the back had been, and her boots found and lost the firmness of the old floor beneath the silt, and she passed through the place where a wall had stood between the front room and the kitchen, and she felt herself pass through it, felt the absence of it as a kind of permission, and she came to the back of the foundation where the kitchen had been, where her mother—

She stopped.

She did not remember her mother. Her mother had died when she was two, before the leaving, and the woman who had been in this kitchen was a woman she knew only from one photograph and from the shape of an absence, and she had not expected the kitchen to do anything to her and it did something to her. She stood in the back corner of the foundation in the place where a woman she could not remember had stood, and the not-remembering rose up in her with a force that the remembering never had, because you could not stand inside not-remembering, there was no door to look down, there was only the fact of a person who had been in this place and whom she had lost so early that the loss had never even had a shape, had only been the water she swam in, the given condition of her life, a mother-shaped hole so old and so total that she had stopped being able to see it as a hole at all.

She put her hand out. There was nothing to put it on. She put it out anyway into the air of the kitchen where the woman had been, and she held it there, and the low sun came across the lakebed and warmed the back of her hand, and she stood in the drowned kitchen of the drowned house in the drowned town and she let it come, finally, the thing she had not been able to afford all day, and it came up out of her the way the cold breath had come up out of the well, from below the made things, from the older dark, and it was not grief exactly, it was larger and plainer than grief, it was the simple unbearable fact of having been a child here, of having been small and carried and warm against a wool coat, of having believed the door would open again, and of the door not opening, of fifty-six years of the door not opening, and of standing now in the place where the door had been and finding that there was no door, that there had never been a door, that there had only ever been the water rising slowly over everything she had been given and the long bright drought of the rest of her life in which she had learned to reduce the world to points so that it could not get larger than she could bear.

She did not make a sound. She was a person who did not make sounds. But the water came out of her, down her face, into the collar of her work shirt, and she let it, standing with her hand out in the empty kitchen, and the recorder clipped to her collar took it all down, the small wet catches of her breath, and she did not turn it off.

After a while it passed. It passed the way weather passes, not because anything had been resolved but because the body cannot hold a thing at that pitch for long, and she lowered her hand and wiped her face with the back of her muddy glove and left a smear of the lakebed across her cheek, and she stood in the kitchen and she was a sixty-year-old woman again, a county surveyor with work to finish before the light went, standing in the mud.

She took the rover off her shoulder. She turned it on and let it fix a position, and when it beeped she spoke into the recorder, and her voice was steady, was the procedural voice, was the voice that had carried her through forty years and would carry her through the rest.

Residential foundation, she said. Cedar Street. Approximately eighteen by twenty-four. And then she stopped, because the form had no field for the rest of it, and she stood there with the rover beeping its small satisfied beep, and she decided that the clerk who transcribed this could make of it what she would.

Pruitt, she said. The Pruitt house. I was born here.

She shot the four corners. She walked the perimeter of the foundation and stood the pole at each place where the wall turned, and the instrument fixed each corner in the indifferent coordinate system of the state, in latitude and longitude and elevation, in the grid that did not care what had happened in the rectangle it described, and she gave the house to the grid, which was the only thing she had to give it, the only permanence she had to offer against the rains that were coming and the water that would rise again over all of it in the fall and cover the door that was not there and the kitchen that was not there and the well with its orange flag and the bank vault and the church steps and the whole drowned country of suggestion that the drought had lifted briefly into the light.

When she had shot the last corner she stood for a moment at the front of the foundation, at the gap where the door had been, looking out through it down Cedar Street toward the lowering sun.

Then she stepped over the wall and walked back down toward the boat ramp where the rest of her equipment waited, and she did not look back over her shoulder at the door, because there was no door, and because she had already seen it from the inside, and because the seeing had been enough, or as much as she was going to get, which in the end she understood to be the same thing.

The base station beeped on the ramp. The flag stood orange by the well. Behind her the light came level across the floors of the houses, and the shadows of the dead trees stretched long across the streets, and the town lay open to the sky for the first time in sixty years, and would lie open a few weeks more, and then the rains would come.
