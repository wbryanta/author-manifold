The card table came first, which seemed wrong to Diane. It had no business being the first thing to go. It was the cheapest item in the house, a folding table with a vinyl top patterned to look like wood grain, the kind of thing you bought at a drugstore in 1974 and used for forty years of jigsaw puzzles and bridge games and the overflow at Thanksgiving when the dining room couldn't hold everyone. A man in a Carhartt jacket carried it out under his arm like a briefcase, six dollars handed to the woman at the door, and Diane watched it go from the top of the stairs and felt something she couldn't name catch in her throat.

She had hired the estate company precisely so she wouldn't have to do this. Wexler & Daughters, the sign said, and a woman named Pam ran the operation with the brisk competence of someone who had emptied a thousand houses and felt nothing. Pam had told her on the phone that the family member shouldn't be present. It complicated things, she said. People got attached to a number on a sticker, started arguing with buyers, drove the prices up out of grief. Diane had agreed. And then she had driven the four hours from Columbus that morning anyway, telling herself she would only stop in, only check that the good silver had been pulled, and now here she was at eleven in the morning on a Saturday in October, standing at the top of the stairs in her mother's house while strangers moved through the rooms below her, picking up the objects of her life and turning them over to read the prices on their bottoms.

She should leave. Pam had given her a look when she came in, a look that said you're going to regret this, but had been too professional to say it out loud. Diane had retreated upstairs, which was where the smaller items were, the bedroom furniture and the linens and the costume jewelry laid out on a card table—not the card table, a different one, one of Pam's—and she had found that she could not make herself go down again and she could not make herself go out to her car. She was stuck on the landing, on the half-step where the stairs turned, where the runner had a worn patch she had known her whole life, a thin place in the wool where four generations of feet had come down hard rounding the corner toward breakfast.

Below her the house was full of people. That was the strangest part. The house had been so empty these last two years, her mother alone in it with the television going, and Diane visiting when she could, which was not often, which was a thing she would now have the rest of her life to think about. And now it was full. There were maybe thirty people moving through the downstairs, more arriving, the screen door slapping its frame the way it had always slapped, and they were touching everything. A woman in a quilted vest was holding her mother's mixing bowls, the yellow ones, the nesting set, holding the largest one up to the window to check for cracks. A young couple was conferring in low voices over the secretary desk in the hall. Someone laughed in the kitchen.

"You doing okay up there?"

Pam, at the bottom of the stairs, looking up. She had reading glasses pushed into her gray hair and a money apron tied around her waist, fat with bills.

"Fine," Diane said. "I'm fine. I'm just going to—" She didn't finish, because there was no end to the sentence. She wasn't going to do anything. That was the whole problem.

"Take your time," Pam said, in a voice that meant the opposite, and went back to the kitchen to ring someone up.

Diane sat down on the landing. She was fifty-six years old and she sat down on the stairs of her mother's house like a child sent there to think about what she'd done. From here she could see the front door and the slice of yard beyond it, the maple her father had planted the year she was born, gone gold now, dropping its leaves on the walk. The Carhartt man was loading the card table into a pickup. He set it in the bed with surprising gentleness, then a box of something, then he stood for a moment looking at his phone, and then he was gone, and the card table was gone, off to some other house, some other family's puzzles, and Diane understood that she would never see it again and that this was the most ordinary thing in the world and that it should not have been able to do what it was doing to her.

She had not cried at the funeral. People had remarked on it, she was sure, in the gentle vicious way of small towns. Carol Strickland's daughter, dry-eyed through the whole service, you'd think she'd—. Diane had stood at the front of the Methodist church in the good black dress she'd bought for exactly this and she had felt scoured out and clean and absent, watching her own mother's casket the way you watch weather, something happening at a distance, behind glass. The minister had known her mother only slightly and had said the things ministers say. Her cousin Ruth had sung. Afterward there had been ham and scalloped potatoes in the fellowship hall and Diane had shaken sixty hands and accepted sixty condolences and driven back to Columbus that same night, and she had thought, in the dark on the highway, with the radio off: I am all right. I have made my peace. We were not close. It does not hurt the way it would hurt if we had been close.

And now a man in a Carhartt jacket had driven off with a six-dollar card table and she was sitting on the stairs unable to breathe.

The thing was, she had not been able to remember her mother's hands. This had happened to her three weeks ago, in the night, in Columbus, lying next to Gary who slept like the dead and would not have woken if the house caught fire. She had been trying to fall asleep and her mind had drifted, the way it did, toward her mother, and she had tried to picture her—not in the hospital bed, not the last version, but the real one, the kitchen one, the standing-at-the-counter one—and she had found that she could see the dress and the apron and the set of the shoulders but she could not see the hands. The hands had gone. And she had lain there in the dark trying to summon them, the specific knuckles, the wedding ring her mother never took off, the way the skin had loosened over the years, and she could not do it, the harder she tried the more the image dissolved, and she had felt a panic out of all proportion, a drowning feeling, and she had gotten up and gone to the bathroom and run the cold water over her own hands and looked at them under the light. Her own hands were becoming her mother's hands. She could see it starting. The same long second finger, the same way the veins were beginning to stand up. She had her mother's hands, and she could not remember her mother's hands, and these two facts together had seemed to her, at three in the morning, like the cruelest thing she had ever encountered.

She had not told Gary. There were a great many things she did not tell Gary, not because they were secrets but because there was no language for them that survived the trip across the kitchen table. How are you doing with everything, he asked, meaning the estate, the paperwork, the lawyer in town, and she said fine, getting there, and that was true, the paperwork was getting done. The other thing had no place in that conversation.

A child came up the stairs. A little girl, six or seven, holding the railing, climbing with great seriousness, and behind her a man saying Becca, honey, stay where Daddy can see you, we're not supposed to be up there. The girl stopped two steps below Diane and looked at her with frank curiosity.

"Are you selling your stuff?" the girl asked.

"It's my mother's stuff," Diane said.

"Where's your mother?"

"Becca." The father, at the foot of the stairs now, apologetic. "I'm so sorry. Becca, come down here."

"It's all right," Diane said. "She died," she told the girl. "She lived in this house and she died, and now we're selling her things."

The girl absorbed this without alarm. Children took death plainly when you gave it to them plainly; it was adults who flinched. "My grandma died too," the girl offered. "We have her clock."

"That's a good thing to have," Diane said. "A clock."

"It chimes," the girl said, and then her father came up and took her hand and led her down, mouthing sorry over his shoulder, and Diane said it's fine, it's fine, and they were gone into the crowd.

We have her clock. Diane thought about the clock. There was a clock here too, on the mantel, a mantel clock in a walnut case that had been her grandmother's before it was her mother's, and it did not chime, it had never chimed in Diane's lifetime, the chime had broken sometime before she was born and nobody had ever fixed it, so that the clock kept perfect time in perfect silence, and every hour of Diane's childhood had passed unannounced beneath it. She got up. She would go and look at the clock. She did not want anyone to buy the clock. She had told Pam to set the silver aside and the photographs and a short list of things, and the clock had not been on the list, because three weeks ago when she made the list over the phone the clock had not seemed important, nothing had seemed important, she had been so proud of how little seemed important, how cleanly she could let it all go.

She went down the stairs into the crowd. It was strange to be among them, to be one body among many in these rooms. A woman she half-recognized—someone from town, someone from the long ago—gave her a small sad smile of recognition and Diane nodded and moved past before there could be a conversation. The living room had been rearranged by Pam's people into a kind of showroom, the furniture pulled away from the walls and tagged, and it was wrong, all of it was in the wrong places, the sofa angled out into the middle of the room where it had never been, so that the room her mother had kept just so for forty years was now a furniture store, and on every surface were the little white stickers with Pam's careful numbers.

The clock was still on the mantel. Forty dollars. Someone had stuck a forty-dollar sticker on the front of the walnut case, right on the glass over the face, and Diane felt a flush of anger so pure and sudden that it frightened her. Forty dollars. She put her hand on the clock. It was heavier than she remembered. She had not held it in—she could not think when she had last held it, you don't hold the clock, the clock sits on the mantel, you grow up under it and you move away and it goes on sitting there keeping its silent time and you don't think about it at all until a stranger puts a price on it.

"You want that one?" A man beside her, older, in a windbreaker, reaching for the clock. "Go ahead, I was just looking."

"It's mine," Diane said. "I mean—it's not for sale. It shouldn't be out. There's been a mistake."

"Sticker says forty," the man said, not unkindly, but with the small territorial edge of the bargain hunter, a man who had come for clocks and here was a clock.

"I know what the sticker says." She picked it up. She held it against her chest with both arms, the way you'd hold a cat that didn't want to be held, and the man raised his hands and backed off, muttering, and Diane carried the clock through the crowd toward the kitchen, toward Pam, to tell her it had to come off the floor, it had been a mistake, and she was halfway there when she stopped because she had caught sight of the kitchen.

The kitchen was where her mother lived. Not the living room with its careful furniture, that was for company, that was for the minister and the relatives at Christmas. The kitchen was where her mother had actually existed, eight by ten feet of linoleum and laminate, and they were selling it out from under her. The yellow bowls were gone—the woman in the quilted vest must have bought them. The cookie jar shaped like a beehive, gone, only the ring of less-faded wallpaper where it had stood. The kitchen table, the real one, the everyday one with the chrome legs and the boomerang pattern, was being looked at by the young couple from the hall, the woman running her hand along the edge, the man crouched to check the legs, and they were nodding at each other, they wanted it, they were going to take the table where Diane had eaten every breakfast of her childhood, where she had done her homework, where she had sat across from her mother ten thousand times and where, two years ago, she had sat across from her mother and not said any of the things she might have said because there had seemed to be so much time, there had seemed to be a next visit and a next.

She held the clock and watched the young woman touch the table and she thought: I should tell them. I should tell them what that table is. And then she thought, with the next breath, the way the mind turns: it is nothing to them. It is a good mid-century table at a good price and they are young and starting out and they will eat at it and it will become theirs, their breakfasts, their homework, their children, and that is exactly what it is for, a table is for the eating that happens at it, and her mother's eating was over and so the table was free to go and find more eating somewhere else. There was nothing wrong with what was happening. That was the thing she could not get past. Nothing wrong was happening. People were buying used furniture at a fair price on a Saturday in October. The horror was entirely her own, manufactured in her own chest, and she had no right to inflict it on the young couple who were only trying to furnish an apartment.

"Diane." Pam had found her. Pam looked at the clock in Diane's arms and her face did something complicated, professional patience laid over real understanding, and Diane realized that Pam had seen this exact thing a thousand times, the family member clutching the one object, and that she, Diane, who had been so proud of her composure, was the most ordinary griever Pam had ever managed. "You want to put that aside? We can put it aside."

"It was on the floor," Diane said. "It had a sticker on it. It shouldn't have—it wasn't supposed to be for sale."

"Okay," Pam said gently. "We'll set it aside. You want to come back here, let's set it aside in the back room, get it out of the traffic."

She let Pam steer her into the back of the house, into the little den that had been her father's, that was now the staging area, boxes everywhere, the things being held for the family, the silver in its chest and the photo albums in a banker's box and a few framed pictures wrapped in newspaper. Pam took the clock from her—Diane let it go reluctantly, her arms cold where it had been—and set it on the desk, her father's desk, and Diane saw that the desk too had a sticker, sixty-five dollars, and she understood that she could not save the desk, she could not save all of it, she had to choose, that was what this was, an afternoon of choosing what to carry out of the fire and what to let burn, and she had not understood that when she made her list on the phone three weeks ago. On the phone she had said the silver and the photographs and Grandma's quilts and that's really all, we don't need to keep much, and Pam had said are you sure, take your time, and Diane had said no, that's all, I'm sure, sell the rest, and she had felt so light saying it, so free, a woman who knew that things were only things.

"Can I ask you something," Diane said.

Pam waited.

"Does it ever—do people ever buy it all back? After? Do they call you and say they made a mistake, they want the table back, the bowls?"

"Sometimes," Pam said. "They call. But it's gone by then. We don't keep records of buyers, mostly. Cash sale, out the door." She was quiet a moment. "I always tell people, before the sale, keep more than you think you want. You can always let go of it later, at your own pace. You can't get it back once it's out the door." She adjusted her glasses. "You didn't take that advice. Most people don't. It's all right. There's no right way."

Diane sat down in her father's chair. Through the den doorway she could see a wedge of the dining room, people moving across it, an arm reaching for something, money changing hands. The house was being disassembled around her in real time, atom by atom, sticker by sticker, and in an hour or two it would be empty again, emptier than it had ever been when her mother was alive and lonely in it, and then there would be the cleaners and then there would be the realtor and then there would be a family she would never meet, painting over the kitchen, tearing out the worn runner on the stairs, and the house would forget her mother entirely, the way houses do, the way they hold no grudge and keep no faith, and there was nothing wrong with that either.

"I want the table," Diane said. "The kitchen table. The one with the chrome legs. There's a couple looking at it right now, in the kitchen, but I want it. Can you—is it too late?"

Pam was already moving. "It's not too late till the money's in the apron." She went out fast, quicker than Diane would have guessed she could move, and Diane sat in her father's chair and listened, and heard the murmur of it, Pam's voice and the young man's voice, and she could not make out the words but she could hear the shape of it, the apology and the disappointment, the couple being told that the table had been spoken for after all, family wants it, so sorry, and Diane felt a flash of guilt, they were young, they were starting out, and then she felt the guilt burn off into something fiercer and more selfish, mine, it is mine, you cannot have the table where my mother sat. She had the clock and now she would have the table and she did not know where she would put a 1958 chrome-legged kitchen table in her house in Columbus where every room was already decided, already complete, already hers and Gary's and nothing to do with this, but she would find a place, she would put it in the basement if she had to, she would put it in the garage, she would keep it because it could not go out the door, because once it went out the door it was gone and you could not get it back, Pam had said so, and Diane had let so much go already, had been letting it go for two years, for longer, had let the visits thin out and the phone calls shorten and the years go by on the assumption of more years, and her mother had sat at that table alone with the television going and Diane had not come, and now the table was the only place left to put any of it.

Pam came back. "It's yours," she said. "I'll have the boys load it in your car before they take the rest to the truck. The clock too. Anything else?" And she said it carefully, she gave Diane the opening, anything else, and Diane understood that she was being offered a door, that she could stand up now and walk through these rooms and point, the bowls, no, the bowls were gone, but the mantel, the andirons, the quilt on the bed upstairs, the mirror in the hall, that she could take all of it if she wanted, fill her car and Gary's truck both, drive it all back to Columbus and let it sit in the garage rebuking her for the rest of her life.

"No," Diane said. "Just the table and the clock." Her voice was steady. She was surprised by how steady. "That's enough. That's—I'll take those two."

"All right," Pam said, and there was something like approval in it, or maybe just recognition, one woman to another, and she went out to find the boys, and Diane sat a while longer in her father's chair in the disappearing house, holding nothing now, her hands empty in her lap, her mother's hands, and she looked at them, and for just a moment, in the slant of the October light through the den window, she could see them—the other ones, the originals, the knuckles and the ring and the loose freckled skin—she could see them moving at the counter, the way they cracked an egg one-handed, the way they smoothed a tablecloth flat, and then a man laughed somewhere in the front of the house and the screen door slapped and the hands were gone again, and Diane sat very still and let them go, because she had no choice, because you cannot keep the hands, you can only keep the table, and the table would have to be enough.
