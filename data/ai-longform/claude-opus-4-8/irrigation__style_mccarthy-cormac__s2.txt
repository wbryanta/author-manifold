They came up through the orchard in the gray light before the sun with their shovels on their shoulders and the dew darkening the cuffs of their jeans and neither of them spoke. The trees stood in their crooked rows like men assembled for some accounting and the apples among them small and hard and green and bitterly far from ripe. The older brother walked ahead. He was forty and he walked the way their father had walked, leaning into the grade with his head down as though reading the ground for sign.

The younger came behind with the pipe wrench in his free hand and the toolbag swinging from his shoulder. He was thirty-four and there was gray already coming at his temples and he stopped once and looked back down the slope to where the house sat small and white in the bottomland with the kitchen light yellow in the window and then he went on.

The break was up at the head of the line where the main came down off the ridge and split into the laterals. They had found it the day before, a dark seep in the dust that spread and stood and would not dry, and they had shut the valve and let it bleed off through the night and now they came to dig it out.

You see where it's pooling there, the older one said.

I see it.

We dig from the both ends and meet in the middle. Don't put your shovel through the line.

I know how to dig.

The older one looked at him. Then he set down his shovel and shrugged off his bag and knelt in the dirt and put his hand flat to the wet ground as though he might feel the pulse of something running under it.

They dug. The sun came up over the ridge and laid the shadows of the trees long across the rows and the light moved down the slope and into the bottom and the cool went out of the morning slowly and then all at once. They dug to the pipe and along it and the dirt they threw up was black at first and then the dry red clay beneath. The older brother worked steady and without hurry and the younger worked in bursts and stopped to lean on the handle and wipe his face.

You eat anything, the older said.

Coffee.

That's not eating.

I'm not hungry in the morning. Never have been.

The older went on digging. After a while he said, Carla send anything up.

There's biscuits in the bag. She wrapped them in a towel.

The older stopped and reached the bag and unwrapped the towel and took a biscuit and ate it standing there in the trench with the dirt to his knees and he held the towel out and the younger came and took one too and they ate without talking and the biscuits were still warm in the middle and that was a small thing but it sat between them like a kindness neither would name.

They found the break at midmorning. The pipe had split along a seam where the old galvanized had rusted thin and the water had cut a channel in the clay below it the way water will always find the going down. The older brother cleared the dirt away with his hands and ran his thumb along the split and looked at it.

This is the third one this year, he said.

I know it.

The fourth if you count the one down by the pumphouse.

I count it.

He sat back on his heels. The whole line's gone to rust, he said. We patch this one and she'll blow somewhere else come July. There's no end to it. You patch and you patch.

That's what patching is.

The older brother looked up at him. The sun was behind the younger and his face was dark and the older could not read it.

We need to talk about it, the older said.

We're talking.

You know what I mean.

The younger turned and walked back to his end of the trench and took up his shovel. I'm not selling Daddy's place to some outfit out of Sacramento so they can put houses on it, he said. I told you that in March and I'm telling you now and I'll tell you in December.

It's not Daddy's place. Daddy's dead.

It's his place.

He's been dead four years and it's our place now and ours means we got to decide what to do with it and you can't decide nothing by saying no to everything.

The younger drove his shovel into the bank. Then dig, he said. We come up here to dig.

They went down to the truck at noon and ate in the shade of the tailgate with the radio low. Carla had sent up cold chicken and bread and a jar of tea and they ate and the older brother poured the tea into two tin cups and they drank and the dog came down from the house and lay in the dust by the wheel with its tongue out and the older threw it a piece of the chicken and it took it and swallowed it whole and looked at him for more.

You spoil that dog, the younger said.

He works for it.

He don't do nothing but sleep.

He keeps the deer off in the fall.

The dog keeps nothing off. They come right up to the house. I seen six of them at the edge of the orchard last week eating the drops.

There's nothing to eat. Half the crop's dropped already and it green.

The younger said nothing to that. He looked out across the rows. The leaves had a dullness to them, a curl at the edges, and in the heat of the day they hung slack and you could see through the canopy to the hard blue sky in places where there should have been shade. He looked away.

You remember when this was all green, the older said. You couldn't see fifty feet into it. We'd lose the dog in here.

I remember.

That was good ground then. That was when the water table was up and the line was new and Daddy had the both of us and the Mexicans come through in the fall and we'd run two trucks to the packing house a day. You remember that.

I said I remember.

The older drank off his tea and set the cup on the tailgate. Well, he said. It ain't that no more. And it ain't going to be that again. The water's down forty foot from when we was boys. You know what it costs to pump from forty foot deeper than your daddy ever pumped. You seen the bills. I bring the bills to the table and you don't look at them.

I look at them.

You don't look at them.

I look at them and I know what they say and looking at them harder don't change what they say.

That's right, the older said. It don't. That's the whole of it right there.

The younger stood and dusted off the seat of his jeans and walked off into the trees with the jar of tea and he stood among them with his back to the truck and drank and the older watched him go and did not call after him.

They worked through the heat of the afternoon. The older brother cut out the bad section with the hacksaw, working the blade slow through the old pipe and the iron dust falling red into the trench, and the younger went down to the truck for the couplings and the new section and the pipe dope and came back up sweating with the materials in his arms. They fitted the new pipe and the older threaded the couplings on and the younger held the section steady while he worked and for a while there was only the sound of the wrench and the breathing of the two men and the dry rattle of a dove somewhere off in the orchard.

You hold it square, the older said.

It's square.

Hold it.

I'm holding it square.

The older set the wrench and turned it and the threads bit and drew up tight and he turned it until the metal would not give and then he sat back. He wiped his face with the back of his wrist and left a smear of red clay across his cheek.

There was a man come by the house, the older said.

The younger looked at him.

While you was in town Tuesday. Come up the drive in a new truck and got out and walked around like he owned it and I went out and he give me a card.

What man.

A land man. Said he represented some interests. That's how he said it. Interests.

You talk to him.

I took his card. I didn't talk to him beyond hello.

The younger stood. What'd the card say.

It said his name and it said a company. It's in the truck.

You been carrying his card around in the truck.

I been carrying it around. I didn't call him. I just been carrying it.

The younger came up out of the trench and stood over his brother and the older did not look up at him but went on turning the wrench though there was nothing left to turn.

How much, the younger said.

I don't know how much. I didn't talk numbers.

You talked enough to carry his card four days.

The older set down the wrench. He looked up the slope to where the line came down off the ridge and disappeared into the dry scrub at the top of the property where their father had once meant to plant and never had.

They're paying eleven, twelve thousand an acre out the valley road, he said. For ground worse than this. Ground that won't grow goatweed. We got a hundred and forty acres here. You do that arithmetic.

I don't need to do it.

A million and a half. Maybe more, this close to the road, with the water rights, what's left of them. You and me split that. You think on that.

I have thought on it.

You ain't thought on it. You hear it and you put your hands over your ears like a child.

The younger crouched down at the edge of the trench so that he was level with his brother and he looked at him.

What would you do with it, he said. The money.

The older looked at him a moment. Then he looked away. I'd pay off the place first. The note. There's still near sixty on the note Daddy took out the last bad year and we been paying the interest and never touching it. I'd pay that. Then I don't know. Get Carla something. She's wanted to go somewhere her whole life and we never been past Bakersfield. Maybe put some by for the kids.

You'd buy your way out of it.

I'd buy us both out of it. That's the part you don't hear. There ain't no out for one of us that ain't for the both. We're tied to this thing like two men tied to a stone and you want to keep on holding it because Daddy held it and his daddy. And I loved him same as you. But he held it and look what holding it done to him. You was there. You seen him at the end.

I seen him.

He didn't sleep the last two years of his life for the worry of it. He'd be up walking the rows at two in the morning. I'd come out and he'd be standing down there in the dark just looking at it like he could keep it alive by looking. And it killed him. The worry of it killed him surer than the cancer. You know that. You was here. I was in Fresno but you was here and you seen it.

The younger said nothing. He had picked up a clod of the red clay and he was breaking it apart in his fingers and letting the pieces fall.

You was always the one stayed, the older said, and his voice had gone quieter. I went off and you stayed. And I know what that's worth and I ain't saying it ain't worth nothing. But staying ain't the same as keeping. A man can stay with a thing all the way down into the ground with it. That ain't loyalty. That's just being too scared to leave.

The younger stood up sharp.

Don't tell me what I'm scared of, he said.

I'm not telling you. I'm asking you.

You're telling me. You always told me. You was twelve and I was six and you told me then and you're telling me now and you been gone fifteen years and you come back and you got it all figured and you got a man's card in your truck.

The older stood too and they faced each other across the open trench with the new pipe bright between them.

I come back, the older said. That's the thing you keep stepping over. I come back. When Daddy got sick I come back from a good job and a house with a mortgage I could pay and I come back here to bury him and I stayed. I been here four years same as you. So don't you tell me I'm the one that left. I left once and I come back and you act like a man can only do one or the other his whole life and never change.

You come back to sell it.

That ain't fair and you know it.

You come back to sell it. You just had to wait long enough that it'd look like you tried.

The older brother's jaw worked. For a moment the younger thought he would come across the trench at him. They had not fought with their hands since they were boys but the old grammar of it was still in their bodies, the way the older's weight came forward onto the balls of his feet, the way the younger's chin dropped. The dog had come up from the truck and stood at the edge of the trees watching them with its ears up.

Then the older brother let his breath out and turned away and went and sat on the bank with his forearms on his knees and his head down.

Turn the valve, he said. Let's see does it hold.

The younger stood a moment. Then he walked down the line to the valve where it came out of the ground in its rusted housing and he took the wheel in both hands and turned it and it gave hard and then easier and he heard the line begin to fill, the air going out of it in a long sigh and then the water moving and the new section ticking faintly as it took the pressure and the cold. He walked back up. The older was on his knees again at the coupling with his hand on it feeling for a weep.

Holds, the older said.

The younger crouched and put his hand beside his brother's on the pipe. It was cold and it was dry. The water ran through it and on down the laterals and out to the trees and somewhere below them in the orchard the emitters began to tick and drip into the dust at the base of each tree, ten thousand small wettings spreading and sinking into the cracked ground that would drink it and not be satisfied.

They knelt there with their hands on the cold pipe and neither moved to take his hand away.

You know what he told me, the younger said. Daddy. Before he died.

The older waited.

He told me to keep it as long as I could and then to let it go and not to grieve it. He said it just like that. As long as you can and then let it go and don't grieve it. I never told you that.

Why not.

I don't know. I held it back. I wanted to have it just mine, I think. The last thing.

The older said nothing.

So you been right the whole time, the younger said, and his voice had gone strange. Even Daddy said it. Let it go. I been fighting you with his name and the whole time he told me the opposite of what I been saying. As long as you can. And I been telling myself this was as long as I can but it ain't. I can go another year. I can go three. I can pump from sixty foot and watch the trees die one row at a time and call that as long as I can. But that ain't what he meant. He meant let it go while it's still worth something. While there's still something to hand to the next man. Not the bones of it. He didn't mean hold on to the bones.

The older put his hand on his brother's shoulder. The younger did not shrug it off.

I don't want to be the one, the younger said. That's all it ever was. I don't want to be the one that done it. The one they say sold the home place. Granddaddy cleared this ground. There's pictures of him standing where we're standing with a mule and a stump puller. I seen the pictures. And I'm going to be the one signs the paper. You can split it down the middle on paper but it'll be my name they say. The one let it go.

It'll be both our names.

It won't and you know it. You went off. You got a life that ain't this. When this is gone you go back to that life and it closes over the hole. But this is the only life I got. When it's gone I don't have nothing it was instead of.

The water ticked in the line. Far down the slope the dripping had spread and the smell of wet earth came up to them faint on the air, that smell that was the best smell either of them knew, the smell of the ground taking water, and it was a cruelty that it should smell like that today.

You'll have the money, the older said, but he said it without conviction and they both heard the hollowness in it and he was sorry he had said it.

The money ain't a thing, the younger said. The money's just the absence of the thing shaped into numbers.

The older brother looked at him then with something like surprise, as though he had not known his brother could put a thing that way, and perhaps he had not, and perhaps the younger had not known it of himself until the words were out.

They got up off their knees. The older filled the trench. The younger stood and watched him and then he took up his own shovel and helped and they filled it from both ends the way they had dug it and tamped it down with the flats of the shovels and the line ran on underneath it carrying the water out to the dying trees. The sun was off to the west now and going down behind the coast hills and the light coming through the orchard was gold and dust hung in it and the leaves that had looked so poorly in the white light of noon looked almost well in this light, almost green, almost what they had been.

I'll call him, the older said. The land man. I'll do the calling and the talking and the dealing. You don't have to be in the room. And when there's a paper I'll sign it first and my name'll be on top.

The younger leaned on his shovel and looked off into the trees.

They won't keep them, he said. The trees. Whoever buys it. They'll push them up. The first thing they'll do is push them all up and burn them in a pile.

I expect they will.

I'd want to be gone when they do it. I couldn't watch that.

We'll be gone, the older said. We'll be long gone.

The younger nodded slowly. The dog had come and sat at his feet and he reached down without looking and put his hand on its head and the dog leaned its weight against his leg.

Not yet though, the younger said. I want one more harvest. Whatever's left to come off these. I want to run one more truck to the packing house even if it's a half a truck. Even if it don't cover the diesel. I want to do it the one more time and then I'll sign.

All right, the older said. One more harvest.

You'll wait that long.

I'll wait.

You won't call him this week and have it done by August.

I'll wait till the apples are in. You got my word.

They shouldered their shovels. The older took up the toolbag and the younger the wrench and they went down through the orchard the way they had come up, only now the light was at their backs and their shadows went before them long down the rows, two thin dark men stretched out across the watered ground, and the water ran under them as they walked, and the trees on either hand stood in their crooked rows holding up their small hard bitter fruit to a sky that gave them nothing, and the brothers went down without speaking and the kitchen light was yellow in the window below and the smell of the wet earth followed them all the way down.
