The reservoir had given up its dead town slowly, the way a body relinquishes heat. First the church steeple in March, then the courthouse cupola in April, and by the time June arrived without rain the whole of Mercer's Hollow lay exposed under a sky the color of dishwater, its streets laid out in cracked mud like the lines of a hand someone had forgotten how to read.

Nora Vance parked the county truck at the end of the access road, where the asphalt simply stopped, dissolving into a bank of dried silt that had once been a boat ramp. She sat for a moment with the engine ticking. The equipment cases on the passenger seat shifted slightly as she set the brake. Total station, tripod, the data collector, the rod and prism she would have to carry herself across ground no vehicle could trust. She was forty-three years old and she had not stood on this earth since she was nine.

The county wanted maps. That was the simple fact of it. With the water down sixty feet below the spillway, the engineers wanted to know what the silt had done to the old streambed, whether the foundations posed a hazard to the intake structures, whether there was anything down here worth recording before the rains came back and drowned it all again. Nora had not asked for the assignment. She had not refused it either. When her supervisor slid the work order across the desk and said, *Isn't this where you're from?* she had only nodded and taken it, because to say no would have required an explanation she did not have.

She got out of the truck. The smell reached her first—a low, green, rotten smell, fish and mud and something mineral underneath, the smell of a thing that should have stayed covered. The lake bed stretched out before her, vast and gray, the standing water a dull sheet far off to the west where the channel still held. Between her and that distant shine lay the town.

She had seen it in photographs, of course. The historical society had a folder, donated by people who had been moved out in 1961 when the dam was finished and the valley filled. Black-and-white pictures of a feed store, a row of houses with deep porches, men in hats standing in the road. But the photographs had always seemed to her like pictures of somewhere else, a place she could not connect to the sensation she carried in her body, which was the sensation of water. She had been born here. She had lived here until she was nine, in the last house on Cedar Street, and then the engineers had come and the families had gone, and the water had risen over everything, and her mother had driven them to a rented duplex in the county seat and never spoke of the Hollow again.

Nora shouldered the first case and started down the bank.

The mud held her, mostly. In places it had dried to a hard pan that crunched underfoot like crusted snow, and in other places her boots broke through to a gray sludge that sucked at her heels and released them with a sound like a kiss. She had worn her oldest field clothes. Already she could feel the silt working into the cuffs of her trousers, fine as flour. The light was strange out here, flat and shadowless, the overcast pressing down so that the whole expanse had no horizon she could fix on, only the gradual lift of the far hills where the tree line marked the old shore.

She reached the first foundations within twenty minutes.

They were not much. Stub walls of fieldstone and mortar, knee-high, furred with a black-green slime that had begun to crack and curl in the sun. The water had taken the wood—every house in the Hollow had been wood above the first course of stone—so what remained was only the print of where people had lived, the rooms laid out as floor plans, the doorways open to nothing. She stood at the edge of one and looked down into the rectangle of it. A cellar hole, half full of silt, and in the silt the shape of something. She crouched. A bedframe, iron, rusted to lace, its springs collapsed into a tangle. Someone had left it. In 1961 they had taken the photographs and the dishes and the children and left the heavy iron bed for the water.

She did not set up the instrument yet. She walked.

This was not procedure. Procedure said establish your control points, your benchmarks, tie your survey to the known monuments and work outward in disciplined radials. But she found she could not begin. She walked instead down what had been a street, reading the foundations on either side, and slowly the dead geometry began to resolve into a place. Here the lots grew larger; here a wider apron of stone suggested a public building, the church perhaps, or the school. Her feet knew something her mind had lost. She turned left at an intersection without deciding to, drawn by a slope in the land, a way the ground fell off toward where the creek had run, and she understood that she was walking home.

Cedar Street had been the last street, the one nearest the water even before there was a reservoir, because the creek had run along its foot and the houses had backed up to it with their gardens going down to the bank. She remembered the creek. That memory had survived where so much else had drowned. She remembered the cold of it in summer, the smooth stones, the way her father had shown her to turn them over to find crawdads, the small armored bodies darting backward into the murk. Her father. He had died the winter after they moved, of a thing in his chest that the doctors named and could not stop, and her mother had always seemed to blame the moving for it, as if leaving the Hollow had unmoored something in him that could not be set right.

She found the house by the chimney.

Most of the foundations had lost their chimneys—the brick had toppled in the long submersion, scattered into the silt—but the house on Cedar Street still had the base of its chimney standing, a square column of brick four feet high, and Nora knew it the way she would have known a face. She stopped at the edge of the foundation and her chest did something complicated, a tightening and a release, and she found that she had pressed her hand flat against her sternum without meaning to.

It was so small. That was the first thing. In her memory the house had been large, as childhood houses always are, a country of rooms, but the foundation that lay before her was the size of a single modest floor, four rooms perhaps, the partitions still legible as low ridges of stone. She could have crossed it in fifteen steps. She stood at what had been the front, where the porch would have been, where the steps came down, and she looked into the small gray rooms where her life had begun.

She set the case down then. She climbed over the stub wall, careful of her footing, and went inside.

The silt in the rooms was firmer than she expected, dried to a surface that took her weight. She walked from the front room to the back, following the ghost of a hallway. Here would have been the kitchen—she could see the broader chimney base where the stove had stood, the iron of an old wood range still half-buried, its surface a continent of rust. Her mother at that stove. The smell of cornbread, of beans cooking down all day. The window above the sink that had looked out on the garden and the creek. There was no window now, only the open air where the wall had been, and beyond it the gray reach of the lake bed and the far water.

She crouched by the stove and put her hand on the cold iron.

She had not expected the grief. She had told herself, driving out, that this was only work, that the place had been dead and drowned for thirty-four years and whatever she had felt for it had drowned with it. But the grief came up through her hand from the iron, came up through her boots from the silt, an old grief she recognized as her father's, the grief he had carried to the duplex and into the hospital and out of the world, the grief of a man who had been made to leave the only ground he understood. She had been too young to name it then. She could name it now. She stayed crouched by the stove for a long time, and the gray light did not change, and the smell of the mud was the smell of the bottom of the world.

When she finally stood, her knees ached. She made herself go back for the equipment.

The work, when she began it, was a mercy. There was a discipline in it that held her. She set the tripod on the firmest pan she could find and leveled the instrument with the bubble centered and her breathing slow. She established her first control point and recorded its coordinates from the GPS unit, waiting the long minutes for the satellites to fix her position with the precision the county required. She wrote the number in her field book in her small square hand. Then she took up the rod and walked out to the corners of the foundation and held the rod plumb on each one while the instrument, back at the tripod, measured the angle and the distance and stored them, and slowly, point by point, the house came back into existence as a set of numbers, as a polygon she could draw.

This was what she did. She made the world into measurements so that it could be held. She had chosen the work, she sometimes thought, because it was the opposite of memory—because memory was vague and treacherous and made the house large when the house was small, and a survey was exact, was true, did not lie about distances. You could not be sentimental about a coordinate. You could only record it.

But as she walked the foundation with the rod, she found the measurements were not enough. She held the rod on the corner of what had been her own room—she knew it was her room because it was the smallest, the one off the back, the one with the window that had faced the creek—and she stood there longer than the instrument needed, looking at the rectangle of stone, and she thought: I slept here. I lay in this exact place, on a bed that is gone, under a roof that is gone, and I listened to the creek, and I did not know that any of it would be taken. The coordinate did not contain that. The coordinate was 847,231.4 easting and the number meant nothing and held everything.

She worked until the light began to fail.

By late afternoon she had surveyed the house and the three nearest foundations, and the data collector held a small dense constellation of points, and she should have packed up and walked back to the truck before the dark made the lake bed dangerous. But she did not want to leave. She knew that if she left she might not come back—that the rains might come, that the county might lose interest, that the water might rise again over the chimney and the stove and the small room where she had slept, and she would have had only this one day, and she had spent so much of it being a surveyor that she had not let herself be the other thing, the child, the daughter, the one who remembered.

She left the instrument on its tripod and walked down to the creek.

It was still there. Lower than the rest of the lake bed, a channel cut into the silt where the original streambed had been, and in the bottom of it a thread of water ran—the creek, the actual creek, finding its old course through the drowned valley as it had before the dam, as it would after the dam was gone, if the dam was ever gone. She climbed down the bank, slipping in the soft mud, and stood at the edge of the water.

It was clear. That surprised her. She had expected the same gray murk, but the water in the channel ran clear over a bed of pale stones, and she could see the stones, the smooth gray and brown stones, the stones her father had taught her to turn. She crouched. The water was cold when she put her fingers in it, shockingly cold, colder than the day, and she understood that it was spring water, that the creek was fed from somewhere up the valley by a spring the reservoir had never truly conquered, and that this cold clear water had been running down here in the dark under sixty feet of lake for thirty-four years, patient, unceasing, keeping the old channel open against the silt.

She turned over a stone.

There was nothing under it. Of course there was nothing—the crawdads were a memory, a thing from before, and probably they had not lived in the Hollow's creek since the water rose. But she turned over another, and another, working her way along the bank the way she had as a child, and on the fourth or fifth stone something moved. Small and quick and backward, a brown body the length of her thumb, darting into the deeper shadow of the channel. A crawdad. She sat back on her heels in the cold mud and laughed, a single broken sound that startled her in the silence, and then she found she was crying, sitting on the bank of the creek with her muddy hands held out in front of her, crying the way she had not let herself cry at the funeral or after, crying for her father who had turned over these stones, for the small life that had hidden under them and outlasted everything, for the town and the water and the long submerged patience of the spring.

She did not know how long she sat there. When she came back to herself the light had gone gray-blue and the air had cooled and she knew she had to move. She washed her hands in the cold water and climbed the bank and went back up to the instrument, and she packed it carefully, breaking it down into its cases, wiping the silt from the tribrach and the prism, because the equipment was not hers and she was responsible for it. The discipline held her again. She was grateful for the discipline.

But before she left she did one thing that was not in the work order.

She took the rod and she went back into the house, into her own small room, and she drove the point of the rod into the silt at the center of the floor, where her bed had been, where she had slept, and she took a reading on it. She recorded the coordinate. She gave it a point number and in the description field, where she would normally write CORNER FDN or NW PROP, she wrote a single word: HOME. She did not know why. The county would strip the description when they processed the data; it would mean nothing to anyone; it would be deleted or ignored. But she wanted it recorded, once, in the true and exact language of coordinates, that this place had been her home, that it had a position on the earth that could be found again, that even drowned and forgotten it remained at 847,231.4 easting and could be returned to. She wanted it in the record. She wanted it held.

Then she pulled the rod from the silt and shouldered her cases and walked back across the lake bed toward the truck.

The walk back was harder in the failing light. The mud that had held her in the afternoon seemed less certain now, the pans harder to distinguish from the soft places, and twice she broke through to her ankle and had to wrench her boot free. The foundations she passed had gone strange in the dusk, the stub walls catching the last gray light, the cellar holes pooled with shadow. She passed the iron bed in the first foundation she had seen. She passed the wider apron that might have been the church. She thought of all the people who had lived here, the men in hats, the women at their stoves, the children who had slept in small back rooms and listened to the creek, and how they were scattered now across the county and beyond, in duplexes and nursing homes and graves, carrying the Hollow in them the way her father had carried it, an interior country that no longer existed and would not stop existing. She was mapping the foundations. But she could not map that. There was no instrument for it.

The truck appeared at the top of the bank, dark against the dishwater sky. She climbed up to it, her legs trembling now with the effort, and set the cases on the passenger seat and stood for a moment with the door open, looking back.

The town lay below her in the dusk, all its streets exposed, all its rooms open to the air for the first time in thirty-four years and probably for the last. Somewhere down there the creek ran clear over its stones, fed by the patient spring, and somewhere down there a single crawdad had hidden under a rock. Somewhere down there was the chimney she had known like a face, and the rusted stove, and the small room with the coordinate she had named. In a season the rains would come. The reservoir would fill. The water would rise over the steeple and the courthouse and the church, over Cedar Street, over the house, over the room, over the creek, and the Hollow would go back down into the dark where it had lain for most of her life. She would have the data. She would have a map. The county would have its hazard assessment for the intake structures. And the place itself would be gone again, given back to the water, beyond reach.

She thought she should feel that as a loss. And she did. But standing at the truck in the cold blue dusk she found that something else had settled in her alongside the loss, something she had not had that morning. She had walked the foundations. She had put her hand on the stove. She had turned over the stones and found the small life still hiding there, and she had recorded the position of her home in the only language she fully trusted, the language of exact location, so that whatever happened to the water it would remain true that the place had been here, at this easting and this northing, on this earth. The water could cover it. The water could not unmake the fact of where it was.

Her father had not had that. He had been carried away from the Hollow without a map, without a coordinate, with only the failing instrument of his memory, and the grief of it had gone into his chest and stopped his heart. She had thought, all these years, that she had taken up surveying by accident, or by aptitude, because she was good with numbers and liked to be outdoors. But standing at the truck she understood that she had taken it up so that she would never be without a map, so that no place she loved could be taken from her without a record, so that she would always be able to say: it was here, it was exactly here, and here is the number, and the number does not lie.

She got into the truck. She turned the key and the engine caught and the headlights came on, throwing two cones of yellow light across the silt and the nearest foundations, and the dead town leapt up sharp and strange in the beams and then she turned the truck and the light swung away from it. She drove up the access road to where the asphalt began again, and she did not look in the mirror, because she did not need to. She had the coordinate. She had it in the data collector on the seat beside her, written in her small square hand, point seventeen, description HOME, and she would carry it back to the county office and she would not delete it, and even after the rains came and the water rose and the Hollow went down again into the dark, she would know exactly where it was, and that, she found, was enough to drive home on, the long way back through the dusk to the rented rooms of her present life, the headlights steady on the road, the drowned town holding its position behind her, fixed forever at 847,231.4 easting, waiting under the patient water to be found again.
