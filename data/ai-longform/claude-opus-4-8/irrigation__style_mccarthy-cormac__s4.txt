They came up through the orchard in the gray of morning with the dew still on the grass and their boots dark with it and the dog trotting ahead of them through the rows of trees that stood in their failing ranks like old men at some last muster. The older brother carried the pipe wrench and a coil of wire across his shoulder and the younger carried the shovel and a bucket of fittings that clanked with each step he took and the sound of it went out across the still and graying land and came back to them off nothing at all.

The trees were apricot mostly and old. Their bark was checked and gray and many of them had limbs dead and barkless that reached up out of the green crowns like the bones of things drowned and risen. The ground beneath was littered with last year's mummied fruit gone black and the new fruit had set thin and would set thinner still without the water. Beyond the last row the foothills lay brown already though it was only June and past them the mountains held a little snow yet in the high cuts and that snow was the whole of what they had and it was going fast.

Where's the break, the younger one said.

Up by the headgate. Where it always is.

It aint always there.

It's there enough.

They walked on. The dog had flushed something in the grass and went bounding sideways with its nose down and the older brother whistled once without breaking stride and the dog came off it and fell in behind them with its tongue out.

The main line ran along the high edge of the orchard where their father had laid it forty years gone, aluminum pipe set on little concrete piers and feeding down into the laterals, and where it crossed the dry wash there was a joint that had pulled loose in the freeze and the water had been weeping out of it for a week and pooling in the wash and running off down to nothing. They set their tools down in the grass. The older brother knelt and put his hand to the wet pipe and felt along the coupling.

It's the gasket, he said.

You got a gasket.

I got a gasket.

He took from his shirt pocket a black rubber ring and set it on his knee and commenced to working the coupling loose with the pipe wrench, his forearms knotting as he leaned into it, and the younger one stood over him with his hands in his back pockets watching the way a man watches another man work when he has not yet decided to help.

You could get under there and hold it, the older one said.

The younger one squatted down and took the pipe in both hands and held it while his brother worked the nut and when it came free a cup of cold water ran out over both their hands and they each of them felt the shock of it for the water came down off the snow and held the cold of that high country in it still.

There went a week's worth, the younger one said.

It'll fill back.

They worked the old gasket out with a screwdriver. It came in pieces, gone hard and cracked, and the older brother laid the pieces on his knee one by one like a man laying out the parts of something he meant to understand and then he flicked them off into the grass.

Listen, the younger one said.

Dont start.

I aint starting. I'm telling you Halversen made an offer.

I heard about the offer.

You heard the number?

I heard the number.

The older brother set the new gasket into the seat of the coupling and pressed it home with his thumbs all the way around and then he held the two pipe ends together and started the nut by hand. His knuckles were skinned and the skin of his hands was the color and grain of old harness leather and there was dirt worked so deep into the creases of them that no washing would ever take it out and the younger one looked at his brother's hands and then at his own.

It's a good number, he said.

It's a developer's number. That tells you all you need.

What it tells me is somebody's willing to pay it.

For the dirt. Not for the trees. He aint paying for the trees. He'll dozer every one of em the first week and you know it.

The younger one stood and walked a little ways off and looked down the long slope of the orchard to where the house sat small and white against the dark of the cottonwoods along the creek and the barn beyond it with its roof gone the color of ash and one whole bay of it sagged in where the purlin had broke two winters back and they had never mended it.

He aint paying for trees, the younger one said, because the trees aint worth nothing. That's the part you wont say.

The older brother spun the nut up tight with his fingers and then fit the wrench to it.

The trees made us, he said. They made Daddy. They put you through two years of that college before you quit it.

I didnt quit it. I run out of money for it. There's a difference and you know there is.

Same arrival, the older one said. Different road.

He set his weight against the wrench and the nut turned a quarter and stopped and he reset and turned it again and the muscles stood in his neck and a vein there filled and the younger one watched him strain and made no move to help him.

You're going to crack the coupling, he said.

I been doing this since before you could walk.

That aint an answer to will you crack it.

The older brother stopped and sat back on his heels breathing and wiped his forehead with the back of his wrist and looked up at his brother against the brightening sky.

You're going to do it anyhow, he said. Aint you. Whatever I say.

I cant do it without you. It's half yours. That's the only reason we're stood here talking.

You're talking. I'm fixing the line.

The sun came over the foothills then all at once the way it does in that country, not a dawning but an arrival, and the light came across the tops of the trees and down through them and lit the grass and lit the wet pipe and the two brothers and their long shadows ran out before them down the rows toward the house. The dog got up and turned in a circle and lay back down in the new warmth of it.

You want to hear what I think, the younger one said.

Not particular.

I'm going to tell you anyhow.

Figured.

I think you cant let go of it because of him. Not because of the trees. The trees are dying and you know it better than I do, you out here every day of your life trying to keep em alive and them dying anyway, a little more every year, and you cant make it stop no matter what you do. So it aint the trees. It's him. You think if you sell it you've gone and quit on him.

The older brother looked at the wrench in his hands.

That's a lot of thinking for a man who wont pick up a shovel, he said.

The water main needs charging before we can test it, the younger one said. There's nothing for the shovel yet.

The older one allowed that this was so. He fit the wrench again and turned the nut the last of the way and it came tight and true and he tested it with a final pull and then he set the wrench down in the grass.

Go open the headgate, he said. Half a turn. No more. We'll watch the joint.

The younger one walked up the slope to the concrete box where the ditch came in off the lateral canal and he bent and took hold of the wheel that stood up out of it rusted near solid and he put his back into it and it gave with a shriek of metal and he turned it the half turn and no more and stood and watched the dark water climb up the channel and slide into the mouth of the pipe with a sound like a long indrawn breath. He came back down. They stood over the joint together and watched it. A bead of water formed at the seam and hung there trembling and did not fall.

There, the older one said. There it holds.

Give it pressure.

It'll hold.

They stood watching the bead of water that hung at the joint and did not fall and the sun came up the slope and warmed their backs and somewhere off in the trees a meadowlark threw up its long liquid run of notes and stopped and threw it up again.

He never wanted us to fight over it, the younger one said.

He never thought we would.

He never thought you'd dig your heels in like a mule is what he never thought.

The older brother almost smiled. He squatted by the joint and put one finger to the bead of water and it broke and ran down the underside of the pipe and dripped off and a new bead started.

You remember when he set out the back forty, the older one said. The Blenheims.

I was little.

You were little but you remember. He set every one of them by hand. Wouldnt let the hired man do it. Said a tree set by a hand that loved it would outgrow a tree set by a hand that didnt and we all of us laughed at him, Mama laughed at him, and then those trees came up and they outgrew the front orchard by a third and bore heavier every year of their lives. Now you tell me how that works. You tell me the science of that.

It dont work, the younger one said. It's a story. He set em in better ground is all and never knew it.

Maybe.

It is maybe. It's all maybe with you. That's the whole trouble.

The older brother stood. He looked off down the rows to the back of the property where the Blenheims stood, taller than the rest, their crowns full and dark, and even from here a man could see they bore heavier, the limbs of them bent already with the weight of the green fruit, the only trees in all that failing ground that looked like they meant to live.

He's buried back there, the older one said. You know that. Up under the third tree in the fourth row. We put him there ourselves.

We put his ashes there. There's a difference.

Is there.

There's every kind of difference. Ashes aint a man.

The older brother turned and looked at him.

You'd sell the ground he's in, he said. You'd take Halversen's number and you'd watch that man's dozer go up that row and tear out the tree your daddy's under and push it into a pile and burn it and you'd take your half of the money and you'd what. Buy a truck. Move to town.

The younger one's face had gone tight.

That aint fair, he said.

Tell me what part of it aint true.

I'd move his ashes. We'd move em. Before. You think I'd let a dozer do that. You think that low of me.

I dont know what I think of you anymore, the older one said quietly. That's the truth of it. I used to know.

They stood a while and did not speak and the water moved in the pipe with a sound they could feel as much as hear, a long shudder running through the aluminum, and far up the line another joint they could not see took the pressure and the whole system woke and began to breathe. Down in the rows the little sprinkler heads on the laterals began to turn, one and then another and then a hundred of them, ticking and throwing their thin arcs of water out over the dry ground, and the smell came up off the wetted earth all at once, that smell of water on dust that is older than any man and waters something in a man he cannot name.

The younger one watched the sprinklers turn.

It dont matter how good it smells, he said. It's costing more to pump than the fruit'll ever bring. You know the numbers same as me. We're paying to grow apricots that the cannery wont take half of and the fresh market dont want because they're small. Small because of the water. No water because of the snow. No snow because of whatever it is, the climate, the years, I dont know. It all runs downhill to the same place. We're paying every year to lose money slower than we'd lose it standing still and you call that keeping faith with him. I call it throwing good after bad and calling it love.

The older brother had bent to gather his tools and he straightened with the wrench in his hand.

You done, he said.

There's a lateral down in row twelve, the younger one said. Wont charge. I checked it yesterday. Probably a gopher chewed it or a head's broke off. You want to go look or you want to keep talking about Daddy.

They went down through the rows with the sprinklers ticking around them and the morning warming and the ground going dark with the water and the smell of it everywhere now and the dog walking through the spray with its head down enjoying the wet of it. In row twelve they found the lateral dead, the sprinkler heads on it standing still and dry, and they walked it down to where a fountain of water broke up out of the ground and ran muddy off into the row.

There, the younger one said. Told you.

The older brother knelt by it and dug with his hands in the mud and found the poly line where it had split, a long jagged tear in the black plastic, and he held the two edges of it together and the water sprayed up between his fingers and ran down his arms.

Gopher, the younger one said.

No. Look. It split clean. The line's just old. It's all old. We patch it here and it goes somewhere else next week. That's the whole orchard. The whole system. It's forty years old and it's coming apart faster than two men can run.

He let go the line and the water sprayed up again and he sat back in the mud and looked at it.

Go shut the gate, he said. We cant fix it under pressure.

The younger one went and shut the headgate and the system died slowly, the sprinklers slowing and faltering and the arcs of water falling shorter and shorter until they were only dribbling at the heads and then nothing, and the orchard went quiet again but for the dripping of the trees, and he came back down and they cut out the split piece of line with a knife and fit a coupling on each end of the break and a new length between and the younger one held the pieces while the older one worked and for a while neither of them said anything and there was only the work, the small competent motions of their hands, hands that were the same hands near enough, their father's hands divided between them.

You ever think, the older one said, that maybe he'd want us to sell it.

The younger one looked up.

You think that.

I dont. But I make myself think it sometimes. To see can I. The way you press a sore tooth.

And can you.

No, the older one said. I cant. I get the words lined up in my head, he'd want us happy, he wouldnt want us breaking our backs on dying ground, he was a practical man, and all of it's true, every word, and I still cant make it come out the way you'd have it. Because the last thing he said to me. You want to know the last thing he said.

I was there.

You werent in the room. You'd gone for the nurse.

The younger one's hands had gone still on the pipe.

What did he say.

He took hold of my arm, the older one said. He hadnt the strength to lift it but he took hold of my arm and he pulled me down and he said dont let it go to weeds. That's all. Dont let it go to weeds. And then you came back with the nurse and he never said another word that made sense and by morning he was gone.

The younger one looked down at the broken line in his hands.

That aint dont sell it, he said. That's dont let it go to weeds. That's a different thing entire.

Is it.

It's a whole different thing and you know it. A man could sell this place to somebody'd farm it right and that wouldnt be letting it go to weeds. That'd be the opposite. It's us letting it go to weeds. Look at it. Look around you. Half the back orchard's dead. The barn's falling in. You cant keep the water in the lines. We are the weeds, brother. We're what he was afraid of.

The older brother said nothing. He fit the last coupling and tightened it and they had no more to say about it just then because the work would not let them, and they finished the line and packed the mud back over it with the shovel and the younger one tamped it down with his boot. They gathered the cut pieces of old line and the older brother stood with them in his hands looking at them, the brittle sun-grayed plastic that had carried water to these trees for longer than the younger brother had been alive, and then he tossed them in the bucket with the rest.

They went back up to the headgate and the older one opened it the half turn and they walked the line all the way down again watching for breaks and they found two more, small ones, weeping at the joints, and they marked them with sticks driven in the ground to come back to. By then it was near noon and the sun stood high and hot and the snow on the far mountains looked no different than it had at dawn but they both knew it was less, that it went a little every hour of every day now and would not come again until November if it came at all, and that everything they did out here they did against that going.

They sat down in the shade at the end of a row to eat what they'd brought, bread and cold meat and a thermos of coffee gone lukewarm, and the dog lay between them with its chin on its paws and watched their hands move to their mouths. The orchard ticked and hissed around them with the water going through it.

You know what he'd really say, the younger one said.

Tell me.

He'd say you two work it out. He'd say that's why I had two of you, so you'd have each other to lean on, not to push against. He'd be ashamed of us out here. Both of us. You for holding on past sense and me for wanting to let go.

The older brother chewed and looked off down the rows.

Maybe so, he said.

I dont want the money, the younger one said. I want you to know that. It aint the money. I'd give my half to charity, I dont care. It's that I cant stand to watch it. Every year it dies a little more and you grieve it a little more and you go a little grayer and I'm watching my brother bury himself out here one season at a time alongside our daddy and I cant stand it. That's the truth I've been carrying. The number's just the door. It aint the room.

The older brother set down his bread. He sat for a long while and the younger one did not look at him and gave him the room to sit.

Give me this season, the older one said at last. The Blenheims'll bear. You saw em. They'll bear heavy. Let me bring in one more crop off the back orchard, his crop, the trees he set with his own hands, and we'll move his ashes come fall to wherever you like, up to the cemetery in town by Mama, somewhere a dozer wont go, and then we'll call Halversen. I'll call him myself. I'll sign whatever's put in front of me. Just give me the one more.

The younger one was quiet.

You mean that, he said.

I mean it. I'm tired, brother. You think I dont know what you know. I know it before breakfast and I know it after dark. I just couldnt be the one to say it.

The younger one put out his hand and the older one looked at it and then took it and they shook there in the shade of the trees their father set, two men with the same hands, and the dog lifted its head and looked from one to the other of them and then put its head back down.

One more season, the younger one said.

One more.

They sat a while longer and did not talk and then they got up and gathered their tools and went back down the line to the next stick they'd driven in the ground and knelt together in the wet earth and went to work, and the water ran on through the orchard around them, through all its failing and mended lines, out to the trees, and the trees took it up as they had always done, asking nothing of the men who tended them but this, and giving back what they could, which was less each year, and which this year would be the last.
