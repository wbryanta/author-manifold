The Hendersons' piano had not been played in eleven years, but Albert Voss tuned it anyway, because that was the arrangement, and because Margaret Henderson still paid him in cash folded inside a thank-you card she bought specially from the pharmacy in Carrow.

He arrived at nine, the way he always had, his fingers stiff from the cold steering wheel. The house sat back from the road behind a stand of birches that had gone yellow overnight, and as he came up the gravel he saw her at the window, waiting, a small gray shape behind the glass. She opened the door before he reached it.

"Albert," she said. "You're looking thin."

"I'm looking old," he said. "There's a difference, but not much."

She laughed, the way she did, with her hand at her collar. Inside, the house smelled of woodsmoke and the particular mustiness of rooms kept too warm and too closed. The piano stood against the far wall of the parlor, an upright Bechstein that her husband had bought at auction the year they married, and which their daughter had learned on, and which nobody touched now that the daughter lived in Auckland and the husband lived in the churchyard.

"I'll bring you coffee," Margaret said, and left him to it.

He set his case on the side table and opened it and looked for a moment at the felts and the wrenches and the little tuning fork worn smooth by his thumb. Then he lifted the lid and pressed middle C and listened. The note came up to meet him like an old dog, glad and arthritic. He had tuned this instrument perhaps sixty times. He knew its temper, its habit of going flat in the bass over a wet winter, the one string in the upper register that would not quite hold and that he had long ago stopped apologizing for.

He worked. The morning light moved across the floorboards. Margaret brought coffee and set it where he could reach it and stood a while in the doorway, then went away again. He heard her in the kitchen, running water, setting something down. When he struck the chords to test the temperament she stopped moving, and he understood that she was listening, that this was the whole of it, that the tuning was the only music the house received now and she did not want to miss a note of it, even the broken phrases, even the same scale climbed and climbed again.

"There," he said when he was done. He played a few bars of a Schubert impromptu, badly, because he was a tuner and not a player, but enough that the instrument could show what it had. The sound filled the small room and pressed against the windows.

Margaret came and stood by the piano with her coffee cooling in her hand. "Robert used to play that," she said.

"He did."

"You remember."

"I remember most things people played. It's a curse." He closed the lid. "Not the names. The hands. I remember the hands."

She gave him the card. It said *With Gratitude* in gold script and inside were two twenties and a ten. He did not count it in front of her. He never had.

"You'll come in the spring," she said. It was not quite a question.

"If I'm able."

"You'll be able." She walked him to the door. The birches were dropping their leaves into the gutter and onto the roof of his car. "You're not retiring on me, Albert. I won't allow it."

"No," he said. "Not on you."

But he was. That was the thing he had not said, would not say, to her or to any of them. This was the last round. He had three more stops after the Hendersons and then the church, and after the church he would drive home and put the case in the cupboard under the stairs and that would be the end of forty-eight years of going out to the houses of the county and setting their pianos in order. His hands were going. Not the strength, which had never been much, but the hearing in his left ear had begun to fail in a way that made the high partials swim, and a man who cannot trust his own ear has no business charging people money. He had decided this in August, lying awake, and he had told no one, because there was no one in particular to tell.

He drove on. The next house was the Pyotts', where the piano belonged to a boy named Cameron who had been promising and was now nineteen and at university in the city and played, his mother said, only the guitar now, and even that not often. The piano was a Yamaha, bright and forgiving and not yet ten years old, and it held its tune so well it hardly needed him. He tuned it anyway. Mrs. Pyott was not home; she left the key under the boot scraper and a note on the music stand that said *Help yourself to anything in the fridge, A. You're a saint.* He did not help himself to anything. He worked in the empty house with the autumn light and the ticking of a clock somewhere and the photographs of Cameron on the wall, Cameron in a blazer, Cameron with a trophy, Cameron squinting into the sun with his arm around a girl. When he was finished he played a scale and then sat for a moment on the bench with his hands in his lap.

He thought about the way a piano was a kind of promise a family made. You did not buy one for a child who showed an interest the way you bought a bicycle or a baseball mitt. You bought it as a vow. You said, with this money, which is considerable, we are betting on the proposition that something in you is worth cultivating, that you will sit and you will practice and you will become, through suffering and repetition, a person who can make beauty with your hands. And then the child grew, and the beauty did or did not arrive, and either way the piano stayed, taking up its corner of the room, a monument to a particular afternoon when the parents had believed in something. The houses on his route were full of these monuments. He kept them in tune. It was, he had come to think, a kind of tending of graves.

He locked up and put the key back under the boot scraper.

The third stop was the worst, and he had arranged the route so it would not be the last, because he did not think he could end on it. The Larkins lived at the end of a long farm track, and old Tom Larkin had a baby grand in a barn he had converted, a Steinway model A, the finest instrument on Albert's whole route, bought God knew how with farm money in a year the prices were good. Tom had wanted to play. He had wanted it the way some men want a woman they cannot have. He had taken lessons in his forties, his fingers thick as sausages from milking, and he had never been able to make it do what he heard in his head, and he had wept once, openly, in front of Albert, the only time Albert had ever seen a grown farmer weep, and said, *I can hear it, Albert, I can hear exactly how it should go, and my hands won't do it. Why give a man the ear and not the hands?*

Albert had not had an answer. He still did not.

Tom was eighty-four now and his hands had gone the rest of the way, the knuckles swollen, the grip mostly memory, and he could not play even the little he once could. But he kept the Steinway tuned to concert pitch, and twice a year Albert came and made it perfect, and Tom sat in the good chair by the woodstove and listened to Albert test it, and they did not talk much, two old men in a barn with the finest piano in the county between them.

"Albert," Tom said when he came in. He did not get up. Getting up was a campaign now. "You found us."

"I always find you."

"Cup of tea on the side there. Hettie made it before she went to her sister's." He nodded at a thermos. "Pour me one too if your hands are steadier than mine."

Albert poured. They drank. The barn was cold despite the stove and the piano had drunk in the cold and the damp of the place and was flat all over, the way it always was, and Albert set down his tea and went to work bringing it up. It took a long time. The Steinway demanded honesty. You could fudge a tuning on an upright and the room would forgive you, but a great piano told the truth about your ear, and Albert listened to it tell the truth about his, and what it told him was that he had been right to stop. The high notes swam. He had to play them and lean his good ear in close and trust the fork over his own hearing, and he hated that, the not-trusting, the working blind.

"You're slow today," Tom said. Not unkindly.

"I'm thorough."

"You're slow." Tom shifted in the chair. "We're both slow. Whole world's slow now and we're the slowest in it." He coughed. "How's the route?"

"Quiet."

"They all dead or just not playing?"

"Both. Either."

Tom laughed, which turned into the cough again. "When I'm gone," he said, "the boys'll sell this. The piano. They won't know what it is. They'll let it go for a song to some fella drives a van." He said it without bitterness, a man stating the weather. "You should buy it, Albert. You're the only one knows what it's worth."

"I've no room for it. And no hands to play it, same as you."

"No," Tom agreed. "No, you've got the ear, though. You've always had the ear. That's worse. I told you that once."

"You did."

"It's worse," Tom said again, settling it, and closed his eyes, and Albert went on with the tuning, and when he had it as right as he could get it he played, for Tom, the slow movement of a Beethoven sonata, the Pathétique, which he could just about manage if he went carefully and forgave himself the runs. He played it for the old man with his eyes closed in the good chair, and the Steinway gave it back to the cold barn rich and dark, and Albert thought, this is the last time I will touch an instrument this fine, and he made it last, and when he lifted his hands at the end the silence came down soft as snow.

Tom did not open his eyes for a moment. Then he did. They were wet.

"Hands won't do it," he said, "but the ear's still there. Every note. I heard every note of it the way it should go." He wiped his face with the back of a ruined hand. "You play it better than you let on, Albert."

"I play it adequately."

"You play it like a man who knows what it costs." Tom waved a hand. "Go on. You've got your churches and your widows. Go on. Same as always in the spring?"

Albert packed his case. He looked at the old man and the old piano and could not say it. "Same as always," he said, and Tom nodded, satisfied, and Albert went out into the cold afternoon and sat in his car a while before he could turn the key.

The light was already going long and gold when he reached the church. He had timed it without meaning to, the way you time things after fifty years, so that the last stop fell at the end of the day, and the end of the day fell at the end of the year, the leaves down, the fields cut, the whole county tilting toward winter and toward dark.

St. Brendan's stood on a rise above the river with the graveyard sloping away behind it and the yews black against the sky. It was a small church, stone, older than anything else for miles, and it had a piano rather than only an organ because in the seventies a wealthy parishioner had left money and the rector of the day had spent it on a Kawai upright instead of repairing the roof, a decision still spoken of. Albert tuned both the organ, badly, because he was not really an organ man, and the piano, well, twice a year, and the church paid him out of the flower fund and was always a little embarrassed about it.

He let himself in with the key the verger left for him under the third stone of the porch. The church was cold and full of the colored light that came through the west window when the sun was low, red and blue lozenges lying across the flagstones and across the pews and across the lid of the piano, which stood to the side of the chancel where the choir would have been if there had still been a choir.

He did not go to it at once. He sat in the front pew, in the colored light, and let the cold and the quiet settle on him.

His son had played here. Daniel. Thirty-one years ago, when he was fourteen, in the autumn, in light like this. Albert had taught him from the time the boy could sit at the bench, and Daniel had what Tom Larkin had wanted and never had: the hands. The hands and the ear both, the rare double gift, and Albert had known it early and had been frightened by it, because he understood better than anyone what it was worth and how the world ground such things down. He had not pushed the boy. He had been afraid to push and afraid not to. He had tuned the pianos of the county and come home and listened to his son play and felt a thing he had no word for, a joy so close to grief he could not always tell them apart.

The recital had been the rector's idea. A Sunday afternoon, the church half-full, the parents and a few of the faithful and Tom Larkin, who had driven in specially and sat at the back with his ruined hands folded. Daniel had played the very Schubert that Albert had stumbled through that morning at Margaret Henderson's, and a Chopin nocturne, and at the end, because Albert had asked him to, the slow movement of the Pathétique. He had played it the way it should go. Every note the way it should go. Albert had sat in this pew, in this light, and listened to his son make the beauty arrive, fully arrive, the thing the piano was a promise of, and he had wept the way Tom Larkin would weep years later in the barn, silently, helplessly, an old emotion he had no defense against.

He had thought it was a beginning.

It was the only recital Daniel ever gave. Not because the gift failed. The gift did not fail. The boy grew, and the gift grew with him, and the world that Albert had been afraid of came and got him the way it gets everyone, only faster, because the talented are not spared, they are merely more visible when they fall. There had been a scholarship and a conservatory in the city, and the city had been too much, or not enough, or the wrong thing, and there had been the drinking that ran in Albert's own family and that he had prayed past his son and had not, and there had been years that Albert did not let himself walk back through, years of phone calls and silences and money sent and not enough done, and then there had been a winter morning eleven years ago, the same winter Robert Henderson died, when the telephone rang and it was a policeman in the city, very kind, very young by the sound of him, asking was he the father of Daniel Voss.

He was forty-four years old when he died. He had not played in public in over a decade. Somewhere in the city there were people who had heard him at fourteen and remembered, the way Albert remembered hands, the way you remember a thing that was supposed to last and did not.

Albert sat in the pew until the cold came up through the stone into his bones, and then he rose, because the work was the work and the piano had to be tuned, and he went to it and lifted the lid.

The Kawai was in poor shape. The church was unheated except for Sundays and the swings of temperature were murder on it and there was damp in the wall behind it that he had complained about for twenty years to no effect. He pressed the keys and found it flat and sour, several notes badly out, a hammer sticking, the whole instrument neglected and willing, like an old horse that will still pull if you ask it.

He worked. The light through the west window deepened and reddened as the sun went down toward the yews. He set the temperament, careful, leaning his good ear in, trusting the fork. He brought the bass up out of its mud. He freed the sticking hammer with a touch of his old knife. He went string by string through the upper register that swam in his failing ear, and he got it as right as he was able, and he knew it was the last piano he would ever tune, and he did not hurry, and he did not let himself feel it yet, because there was the work, and the work came first, it always had, it was the one thing he had never failed at.

When it was done the church was nearly dark. The colored light had gone gray and then was gone altogether and only the last of the day stood in the high windows. He should have turned on the lamp by the door. He did not. He sat down on the bench in the dimness in front of the instrument he had just made true.

He had not meant to play. He had meant to test it and go home. But his hands found the keys in the dark, and he sat there, an old tuner with a failing ear and hands that had never been able to do what his son's could do, and he began, very slowly, the slow movement of the Pathétique.

He played it the way he played it, adequately, a tuner's playing, the runs simplified, the tempo too careful, a man feeling his way. But the piano was true now, freshly true, every string set right by his own hands, and it gave back what he gave it without complaint, and in the cold dark church the notes went up into the rafters where his son's notes had gone thirty-one years before. He played for the boy of fourteen in the colored light. He played for the man of forty-four in the city in the dark. He played for Tom Larkin who had the ear and not the hands, and for himself who had the ear and not the hands either, and for all the silent pianos of the county standing in their corners like vows nobody had kept, and for the houses where no one played anymore, and for Margaret Henderson listening at her kitchen sink to a tuner climb a scale because it was the only music left to her.

He was not a player. He knew exactly what he was. He had spent his life making it possible for others to make the beauty, keeping the instruments ready for hands better than his, and the hands had come, some of them, his son's among them, and they had made the beauty and then they had gone, and what was left was the old man and the tuned strings and the going light.

He played it through to the end, the long slow theme returning and returning, and on the last phrase he let it ring out into the dark and did not damp it, and the church held the sound the way the church held everything, the prayers and the funerals and the one boy's one recital, and then the sound was gone, soaked into the stone, and there was only the silence and the smell of cold and old hymn books and the river going by below in the dark.

Albert closed the lid. He sat a while longer. Then he stood, his knees protesting, and packed his case in the dark by feel, the felts and the wrenches and the fork worn smooth by his thumb, and he closed the case and lifted it.

At the door he stopped and looked back at the shape of the piano against the last gray window. "There," he said, to it, or to the boy, or to the empty church, the way he said it in every house when the work was done. "There."

Then he went out and locked the door and put the key back under the third stone of the porch, and stood a moment in the churchyard with the yews black around him and his son somewhere up the slope under the cut grass, and the cold coming down, and the whole county going quiet for the winter.

He did not say goodbye to the boy. He never did. He only stood there until the cold moved him, and then he walked to the car and set the case on the seat beside him where it had ridden for forty-eight years, and drove home in the dark with his hands sure on the wheel, the one thing they had always known how to do.
