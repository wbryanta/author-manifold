But the Pacific. The Pacific was something else. The Pacific was where you wanted to be if you had to be anywhere watching the world get remade in the dark. He said this to her. Jack Lovett said this to Inez Victor on a verandah in the spring of 1975, although when I say verandah I am improvising, I am reconstructing, the way I have been reconstructing this whole story for some years now, trying to lay it down straight, trying to make the chronology track, trying to fix a sequence on events that never quite consented to occur in sequence.

Let me establish a few things.

This is a story about a marriage, and about a Pacific, and about the year certain governments came apart, certain illusions came apart, certain people I knew came apart. Inez Christian Victor was the wife of a United States senator who became, briefly, in the years I am describing, a candidate for the presidential nomination. Harry Victor. You will have seen the name. You will have your associations, none of them perhaps quite accurate, all of them perhaps beside the point. Harry Victor was a man who believed, or made you believe he believed, that the moral life consisted of saying the right things in the right rooms, and there were a great many right rooms, and Inez stood in all of them, smiling, wearing the right clothes, holding the right glass with nothing in it.

I knew them all. I want that understood at the outset. I knew Inez and I knew Harry and I knew Jack Lovett, although Jack Lovett was the kind of man you only thought you knew, the kind of man who told you a story about pink skies at dawn precisely so you would not ask him what he was doing at Johnston Island in the first place, what he had been doing at Schofield, what he was doing in Saigon in the last week of April when the whole apparatus came down. He told you about the lobsters and the gin so you would not ask about the cargo manifests. He told you the air smelled like gardenias so you would forget to notice that no one ever quite knew who he worked for.

I notice it now. I am trying to notice everything now.

Cards on the table. I am a writer, which is to say I make a living dealing in fixed ideas, in the imposition of narrative on the unrupturable disorder of what actually happens, and I have spent a good deal of my professional life learning to distrust that imposition, and I distrust it still, and I am going to impose it anyway, because the alternative is to say nothing, and I am not yet prepared to say nothing about Inez Victor. I taught her, in a manner of speaking. That is, I had her in a writing class at a school in the East in the years before she married Harry Victor, a girl from Honolulu with too much money and a way of looking at you that suggested she had already concluded you would disappoint her and had decided, generously, not to hold it against you. She wrote one story that I remember. It was about a woman who set fire to her own house. I gave it a high mark. I did not understand it. I understand it slightly better now.

Consider the Christians.

Inez Christian came from a family that had been in the islands for five generations, which in the islands is a way of saying forever, a way of saying you are not to be confused with the people who came on the boats to work the cane and the pineapple, the people whose grandchildren now ran the unions and the legislature and looked at the Christians with the particular cold patience of those who have learned to wait. The Christians owned land. They owned, at various times, sugar acreage and a dairy and a certain amount of downtown Honolulu and a house in Manoa with deep verandahs and a garden gone to seed and a koa staircase that the termites were taking back inch by inch the way the ocean takes back a sand spit. They owned, you might say, a version of history in which they figured prominently and benignly, and they maintained this version with the serene tenacity of people who have never had occasion to test it.

There was Inez and there was her sister Janet. There was their father, Paul Christian, who in 1975 was living in a single room at a residential hotel in Waikiki and was understood, within the family, to be not entirely well. There was the grandfather, Cyrus, dead by then, who had founded the family's idea of itself. There was an uncle, Dwight Christian, who ran the construction firm, Christian Brothers, which built the runways and the docks and the tank farms across the Pacific that men like Jack Lovett had reason to know about, which is one of the ways this story coils back on itself, one of the ways the family's serene version of history shook hands in the dark with the part of the century that did not get written down on those islands.

I want to get this right and I am aware that I cannot.

Here is what I can do. I can tell you that on a particular evening in March of 1975 Inez Victor stood at a window of her uncle Dwight's house high up on the slope above Honolulu, a house with a lawn that fell away toward the lights of the city and the harbor and the dark beyond the harbor where the carriers were, and she watched the lights and she held a glass with nothing in it and Jack Lovett came and stood beside her and told her about the pink skies at dawn during the Pacific tests, and she let him, and that was the whole of it and it was not the whole of it at all.

They had known each other a long time. That was the thing you needed to understand and the thing no one, least of all Harry Victor, did understand. Inez Christian had met Jack Lovett in 1952, when she was sixteen and he was a man already, a man with no clear occupation and a borrowed car and a way of turning up at the Schofield officers' club on nights when she was there with somebody's brother, and he had driven her home along the Kamehameha Highway with the windows down and the gardenias in her hair, and he had not touched her, that first year or the next, and this restraint, which she had taken at sixteen for indifference, she had come to understand at forty for what it was, which was a kind of patience she had not encountered before or since. Jack Lovett had decided something about Inez Christian when she was sixteen and he had simply waited, the way he waited on those islands for the weather to give the go, the way he waited everywhere for everything, with the bottomless patience of a man who has understood that the things worth having arrive on their own schedule and that the worst thing you can do is hurry them.

He had married, in the interval. So had she, more durably. He had married twice, both times briefly, both times to women whose names I do not have and do not need, women who had been, I gather, blonde and decorative and bewildered, women who had ended by leaving him because there was nothing to leave, because Jack Lovett went where he was sent and told you it was Manila when it was Johnston Island and you could spend a marriage standing at a window waiting to find out where your husband actually was. Inez understood this. Inez had a gift for understanding the men who declined to be understood. It was, you could say, the central fact of her competence, the thing that made her so good in the right rooms: she had married one man whose entire being was performance and she had loved, all along, in the way you love a fixed star, another man whose entire being was the refusal of performance, and she had learned from both of them the same lesson, which was how to stand still in a moving thing and give nothing away.

Let me try to tell it as it happened. Let me at least try.

In the spring of 1975 Harry Victor was in Honolulu because Honolulu was, that spring, a place where things were happening, or rather a place from which the things happening elsewhere could be conveniently observed and commented upon, and Harry Victor was a man whose vocation was commentary. The war that had defined his political life was ending, finally, messily, in a way that confirmed nothing he had said about it and refuted nothing either, and Harry had come out to the Pacific with a small staff and a vague mandate, a fact-finding thing, a presence, a way of being photographed against the right backdrop at the right moment of history. He brought Inez. He brought the children, Jessie and Adlai, although Jessie at that point was not quite where anyone wanted her to be and Adlai was at the age when a boy's principal occupation is the cultivation of his own opinions. He brought Billy Dillon, who managed Harry the way other men manage portfolios, with attention and ruthlessness and a complete absence of sentiment. They stayed at first at the Royal Hawaiian and then at Dwight Christian's house on the hill, and Inez moved among the rooms of her childhood as though she had never left and as though she had never been there at all.

I have a photograph from that spring. Someone gave it to me, later, when I was trying to assemble this. It shows Inez Victor on the lawn at Dwight Christian's, in a white dress, slightly out of focus, looking off to the side at something outside the frame, and you cannot tell from the photograph whether she is happy or unhappy because her face has the particular blankness that years of being photographed will teach a woman, the blankness that gives nothing away because it has learned that everything given away will be used. There are other people in the photograph, at the edges, but they are not in focus and they are not the point. The point is Inez, looking off, and the thing outside the frame, which I have decided, with no evidence whatsoever, was Jack Lovett.

Here is what I know about Jack Lovett's business in the Pacific that spring, which is less than I would like and more than was good for anyone.

He was moving things. He had spent his life moving things, and people, across that ocean, on aircraft that belonged to companies that existed mainly on paper, into countries whose governments were about to change or had just changed or were being encouraged to change, and that spring the things he was moving had to do with the end of the war, with the people who were going to need to get out of certain cities in a hurry, with the materiel that was going to need to disappear before it could be inventoried by whoever inherited the inventory. He had interests, was the word. Jack Lovett had interests. He had, specifically, interests in an air-transport operation and in certain real estate and in the disposition of certain commodities that did not appear on the manifests, and he had, that spring, a particular problem in the shape of a young man who had married into the Christian family and gotten himself entangled in the part of Jack Lovett's business that involved the things that did not get written down.

That young man was named Wendell Omura. No. I am getting ahead of myself, and getting it wrong besides. Let me hold the names for a moment. Let me stay on the lawn.

Inez stood on the lawn at her uncle's house and Jack Lovett told her about the pink skies and what he was actually telling her, under the pink skies, was that he had come to take her away, that the waiting was over, that the weather people had given the go. He did not say this. Jack Lovett never said the thing itself. He said the sky was a pink no painter could approximate and the air smelled like the gardenias she used to wear, and Inez, who had been married twenty years to a man who said everything and meant the saying of it, heard exactly what he was telling her, and she did not answer, and she did not need to answer, and that was the difference, finally, between the two men in her life. With Harry she had to produce a response, a face, a quotable line. With Jack she could stand at a window and give nothing away and be perfectly understood. It was the only place she had ever been able to rest.

Across the lawn Harry Victor was talking to her uncle Dwight and to a man from the consulate and to Billy Dillon, and what they were talking about was the situation, the deteriorating situation, the way the situation was going to play, and Harry's voice carried the way it was meant to carry, and Inez heard it the way you hear weather, as a condition of the air.

"The point," Harry was saying, "the point that keeps getting missed—"

She did not hear the point. She had stopped hearing Harry's points some years before, around the time she understood that the points were interchangeable, that the function of a point in Harry's conversation was not to be true but to be made, to demonstrate that Harry was a man who made points, and that any given point could be replaced by its opposite without altering the demonstration in any way. This was not a thing she held against him. She had married him knowing it, more or less, or knowing enough of it. She had married him in a registry office because Jack Lovett was at that moment in a place he could not tell her about doing a thing he could not tell her about, and because Harry Victor had been there, present, urgent, certain, a man who wanted her in the daylight and said so, and because at twenty-four a woman will sometimes choose the man who is present over the man who is patient, mistaking presence for love and patience for indifference, and by the time she understood the mistake she had two children and a public life and a competence at standing in rooms, and you do not throw those things over, or she had not, not yet, not for twenty years.

"You're not listening to him," Jack Lovett said.

"No."

"You used to listen to him."

"I used to do a lot of things."

Jack Lovett looked at her glass with nothing in it. "You want me to get you a drink."

"No."

"You want me to get you out of here."

She did not say no to that. That was the thing. She looked at the lights falling away toward the harbor and she did not say no, and Jack Lovett, who had spent twenty years reading the weather over that ocean, read the not-saying and understood that the front had finally moved, that conditions were go, that all the years of waiting were about to resolve into an event with a yield he could not yet calculate.

What I am trying to tell you is that they were both, in their way, weather people. They had both learned to wait for the go. And the go, that spring, was coming, not because of anything between them but because the whole apparatus of the world they had moved in was coming down at last, the war ending, the governments falling, the cargo planes lifting off from cities about to be renamed, and in the general collapse of fixed things a man and a woman who had been holding still for twenty years would finally be free to move, the way debris is free to move when the structure that held it in place gives way.

I should tell you about Janet. I should tell you about Paul Christian in his room at the residential hotel, cleaning, that spring, a revolver he had brought home from a war forty years gone, although no one knew that yet, no one knew what he had or what he meant to do with it. I should tell you about Wendell Omura, who was a congressman, who was on a committee, who had said certain things about certain contracts that touched on Christian Brothers and on the runways and the tank farms and on the part of the business that shook hands in the dark with Jack Lovett's business. I should tell you about Janet's husband, who was named Dick Ziegler and who had real estate problems and who was, that spring, in a state of mind that no one was monitoring closely enough. These things were converging. They converged, finally, on a single night, and I have spent years trying to lay the convergence out in a line, cause and effect, this and therefore that, and I want to be honest with you: it does not lay out in a line. It happened the way the weather happens. It happened all at once, out of conditions that had been building unobserved, and when it was over certain people were dead and certain people were gone and Inez Victor had walked out of the only world she had ever known and into the back of an unmarked aircraft on a runway her own family's company had poured, and the official version, the version Billy Dillon constructed and Harry Victor delivered and the papers printed, the official version explained none of it and was believed by everyone, the way official versions are.

But that is the end, or near it, and I have not earned the end. I have to go back.

Go back to the lawn. Hold there.

Hold on the white dress, the empty glass, the lights, the carriers in the dark water. Hold on Harry Victor making a point. Hold on Jack Lovett saying you want me to get you out of here, and Inez not saying no. Hold on the particular quality of that Honolulu evening, the trades coming up the valley with the smell of rain in them, the smell, yes, of the gardenias, because there were gardenias in Dwight Christian's garden, planted by somebody's dead wife, gone leggy and untended but still throwing that smell at dusk, the smell that for Inez Victor was inseparable from being sixteen and being driven home along the Kamehameha with the windows down by a man who would not touch her and would not go away.

She had not known, at sixteen, that you could spend a whole life inside a smell. She knew it now. She stood on the lawn and the gardenias came up out of the dark and forty years collapsed into the single fact of Jack Lovett standing beside her, not touching her, telling her about a pink no painter could approximate, and she understood that she had been, all her life, a weather person, that she had been waiting for the go, that everything she had built and worn and stood inside of was provisional, was a Quonset and a strip of matting rolled out on a sand spit, was something you could leave behind in an afternoon when the weather changed.

"Inez." Billy Dillon had crossed the lawn. Billy Dillon was always crossing lawns, materializing at the elbow, managing. "Harry wants you for the thing tomorrow. The hospital thing. They want a picture."

"All right."

"You'll wear the blue."

"I'll wear the blue."

Billy Dillon looked at Jack Lovett the way he looked at anything that was not on the schedule, which was to say with a brief assessing hostility that resolved instantly into the smooth nothing of the professional. Billy Dillon knew who Jack Lovett was, or knew enough, knew that he was the kind of man you did not want in the photograph, the kind of man whose presence in the background of a photograph would, years later, require explaining. "Jack," he said.

"Billy."

That was the whole of the exchange and it contained, if you knew how to read it, the entire situation: Billy Dillon, who managed the visible world, and Jack Lovett, who worked the dark behind it, and between them, at the window, in the white dress, Inez Victor, who belonged, it was about to turn out, to neither world, or to the seam between them, the place where the two worlds touched and the touching could not be written down.

Billy Dillon went back across the lawn to Harry. Inez watched him go.

"He's good," Jack Lovett said. "Dillon. He's the best one in the room."

"You always say that about people you'd have killed."

Jack Lovett laughed, the short laugh, the one with no give in it. "I never had anybody killed."

"No," Inez said. "I don't suppose you did. I don't suppose you ever had to."

This was, I think, the truest thing she said to him that night, and the cruelest, and he took it the way he took everything, without expression, filing it, and the trades came up the valley and the gardenias came up out of the dark and across the lawn Harry Victor's voice rose and fell, making points, and the carriers sat black on the black water, and somewhere across that water the cities were emptying out and the manifests were being burned and the whole long American adventure in that ocean was folding itself up like a strip of matting being rolled back off a runway, just rolled right back up like a goddamn bathmat, and Inez Christian Victor stood at the seam of the visible and the invisible world and waited, the way she had been taught to wait, the way they had both been taught, for first light. For the pink sky. And then the shot, naturally.
