But the Pacific. The Pacific was something else.

He said this to her in the bar of the airport at Kuala Lumpur, or maybe it was Hong Kong, the time of year you could not always tell, the air-conditioning the same in all of them, the rattan the same, the boys in white coats bringing the same gin and the same bitters, the same overhead fans turning slowly through the same wet heat. Inez Victor remembered the fans. She remembered the heat. She remembered that he had been drinking gin and bitters and that she had been drinking nothing at all, that she had been turning a glass of melted ice in her hands and watching the water bead on the outside of the glass while he talked about the light at dawn during those Pacific tests.

She remembered that she had not seen him in twenty years.

She remembered that she had not expected to see him then.

This is a story about an American woman named Inez Victor, who was born Inez Christian in the Territory of Hawaii in the year 1935, and who in the spring of 1975 left her husband, a United States senator and former candidate for the presidency of the United States, and went to live in Kuala Lumpur. I am telling you this now because I have been telling it wrong, telling it as if there were a beginning, a middle, an end. There is no such story. There is only what Inez Christian told me, in the spring of 1975, sitting in the dim cool of a house on a hill above the harbor, and what Jack Lovett told me, and what I was able to find out for myself, which was not much.

Cards on the table.

I am an anthropologist by training, although I have not practiced the discipline in some years. I once believed in a study of the human community as a body of observable fact. I believed that if I could collect enough data, enough kinship charts and rainfall records and accounts of who slept with whom and who killed whom and who got the breadfruit and who got the taro, I could understand. I no longer believe this. I no longer believe that anything can be understood in that way. I have been keeping notes, these past years, toward a study I will not write, and the notes have a way of arranging themselves not as data but as a story, and the story has at its center a woman I knew slightly and loved without quite admitting it and never understood at all.

So. Let me establish some coordinates.

Inez Christian came from a family that had been in the islands a long time, longer than most, long enough to have stopped counting and to have begun, instead, to forget. The Christians had come as missionaries and stayed as something else, which was the usual progression. By the time Inez was born they owned land on three islands and a house on the Nuuanu Pali side of Honolulu and a certain idea of themselves that did not require examination. They were not the richest family in the islands and they were not the most prominent, but they had a way of behaving as if these distinctions were vulgar, as if the having of money and the wanting of position were things that happened to other people, mainland people, people who had arrived on the Lurline with letters of introduction and a determination to be photographed at the Royal Hawaiian.

Inez's father was named Paul Christian. He was a man of considerable charm and no fixed occupation, a man who had once, briefly, gone to sea, and who referred to this period of his life so often and with such elaboration that it came to seem the only thing he had ever done. He drank. He had ideas. He would come home at two in the morning and wake the children and explain to them, with great tenderness and great urgency, the true nature of the trade winds, the migratory patterns of the golden plover, the betrayal he had suffered at the hands of his brother Dwight in the matter of a land trust. He believed himself to have been cheated. He may have been right. In any case the belief sustained him, gave shape to his days, and when, in 1952, he shot and killed a man at the Honolulu Airport—a man named Wendell Omura, a man Paul Christian had decided was somehow at the center of the conspiracy against him—it surprised the family less than it might have surprised another family. They had been waiting, without knowing they were waiting, for Paul Christian to do something. They had only not known what.

Inez was seventeen.

This is the kind of detail that, in the study I will not write, I would have been obliged to set in context, to surround with qualification, to present as one fact among many. But it is not one fact among many. It is the fact that Inez carried, the way you carry a stone in your shoe, the way the stone shapes how you walk and how far and toward what. She did not speak of it. In all the years I knew her I heard her mention her father exactly twice, and on neither occasion did she mention the airport, or Wendell Omura, or the trial, which was brief, or the verdict, which was insanity, or the place to which her father was committed, which was a hospital on the windward side from which, over the years, he sent letters about the trade winds and the golden plover and the betrayal he had suffered.

Jack Lovett, on the other hand, came from nowhere in particular. He was born in 1922, or 1923, depending on which document you credited, in a part of the country—Tennessee, possibly, or Kentucky—that he never described and to which he never returned. He arrived in the Pacific during the war and never really left it. After the war he held a series of jobs that were not quite jobs, attaching himself to one agency and then another, to one program and then another, always at the edge of things, always present when something was about to happen and gone by the time it had happened. He was, by his own account, an observer. Along for the ride. There for the show.

What he actually did, I came to understand, was move things. Information, mostly, but also money, and sometimes people, and sometimes the heavier and more compromising kinds of equipment. He knew the men who arranged coups and the men who reversed them. He knew which colonels could be reached and at what price and through which intermediary. He knew the schedules of certain aircraft that did not appear on any schedule. He had a way of being in Saigon, or Vientiane, or Jakarta, or Phnom Penh, at exactly the moment when being there meant something, and a way of leaving before it meant something else.

He met Inez Christian in 1952, the year her father shot Wendell Omura. He was thirty, or twenty-nine. She was seventeen. He was married, although not for long, to a woman named Carol who came from San Jose and who would eventually move back there, and Inez was a girl in a white dress at a party at the Outrigger Canoe Club, a girl whose father had just done a terrible thing, a girl who stood very straight at the edge of the lanai and watched the water and did not cry, and Jack Lovett watched her not crying and understood something about her that he would spend the rest of his life trying to name.

He did not name it that night. That night he only got her a drink she did not drink and told her, because he could think of nothing else to say and because it was true, that the light at dawn during those Pacific tests was something to see. Something to behold. Something that could almost make you think you saw God.

She remembered that he said this.

She remembered that she had thought, at seventeen, in her white dress, with her father in a cell and the whole structure of her life gone suddenly transparent and weightless: this is a man who has seen something.

They did not become lovers, not then. There was no occasion for it, and besides she was seventeen and besides he was leaving, he was always leaving, that was the first thing she learned about him and the last thing and the only thing she could ever fully count on. He was leaving in the morning for somewhere he could not name, on business he could not describe, and he would be back when he was back. This was not a line. With another man it would have been a line. With Jack Lovett it was a description of the weather, a statement of how the world was arranged, and Inez, who had just learned how unstably the world was arranged, recognized in his evasions a kind of honesty more reliable than the candor of the men who told her everything.

She married Harry Victor in 1957.

Harry Victor was, in 1957, a young lawyer with the look of a man who would be photographed a great deal, and he was, over the next eighteen years, photographed a great deal. He had been at Harvard. He had ideas, but unlike Paul Christian's ideas, Harry's ideas were the kind that got a man elected, first to one thing and then to another, ideas about justice and opportunity and the responsibility of those who had been given much, ideas that Harry believed, that Harry meant, and that became, in Harry's mouth, over the years, so smooth and so practiced that the believing and the meaning grew harder and harder to distinguish from the saying. Harry Victor was not a hypocrite. This was the thing people got wrong about him. He was something more durable than a hypocrite. He was a man who had arranged his life so that the question of his own sincerity need never arise, not even to himself.

Inez was good at being his wife. This surprised people, who expected the daughter of a man who had shot a stranger at an airport to be unstable, dramatic, a liability. Inez was none of these things. Inez was perfect. Inez stood at the edge of ten thousand lanais, ten thousand receiving lines, ten thousand hotel ballrooms in ten thousand interchangeable cities, and watched the water or the wallpaper and did not cry, and smiled when smiling was called for, and remembered the names of the wives, and got through it. Getting through it was Inez's gift. She had been getting through it since she was seventeen.

What people did not see, what I did not see myself until much later, was the cost. The cost showed up as a kind of absence. Inez was always, in any room, slightly not there. She had developed, over the years of being Harry Victor's wife, a way of being present in body and absent in every way that mattered, a way of conducting the necessary transactions of social life from somewhere just behind her own eyes, a great distance back, in a room she did not let anyone enter. People found her gracious. People found her remote. People did not, on the whole, find her, which was the point, which was how she had arranged to survive.

She saw Jack Lovett, over those eighteen years, perhaps a dozen times. Always by accident. That was their arrangement, although it was not an arrangement, since neither of them had ever proposed it or agreed to it. He would be in a city where she was. He would appear at the edge of a reception, in the lobby of a hotel, in the bar of an airport between flights, and they would talk, and he would tell her about a shot he had observed or a coup he had not exactly observed, and she would tell him almost nothing, because there was almost nothing she allowed herself to tell anyone, and then he would leave. He was always leaving. And each time he left, Inez understood a little better that the great distance back behind her eyes, the room she let no one enter, was a room she was keeping for Jack Lovett, against the day he might want to come in.

He never said he wanted to come in.

He only said, each time, in one form or another, that the light at dawn during those Pacific tests was something to see, and Inez understood that this was the nearest he could come, that this was Jack Lovett's way of saying what other men said with flowers and promises and lies, that the pink sky and the wet air and the smell of gardenias where there were no gardenias was the whole of what he had to offer, the whole of what he had, and that it was, in its way, everything.

In the spring of 1975 the arrangement that was not an arrangement ended, or rather changed into something else, and the reason it changed had to do with the way the world was coming apart in that particular spring, in that particular part of the world, the part of the world where Jack Lovett had spent his life being present at the moment when being present meant something.

I am thinking of the spring of 1975 in Southeast Asia.

I am thinking of the helicopters on the roofs, the embassy gates, the barges in the harbor at Saigon, the C-130s going out heavy and coming back empty, the whole apparatus of an American adventure folding in on itself, the way a building folds when the charges are properly set, all at once and straight down into its own footprint. I am thinking of the men like Jack Lovett, the men who had spent thirty years moving things and reaching colonels and being present, watching the thing they had been present at all those years arrive at last at the place it had been going all along, and understanding, some of them, for the first time, where that place was.

Jack Lovett was in Saigon in April of 1975. He had business there. He always had business there. The business in April of 1975 had to do with getting certain people out, people he had used and people who had used him, people to whom he owed something and people who owed something to him, and it had to do as well with getting certain things out, certain documents and certain valuables and certain individuals whose continued breathing was, to one party or another, worth arranging. It was bad work and it was the only work and Jack Lovett did it the way he did everything, at the edge, present, along for the ride, there for the show, gone before the thing he had set in motion arrived wherever it was going.

And in the middle of it, in the middle of the worst of it, with the city coming apart around him, Jack Lovett did a thing he had never done in twenty-three years.

He called Inez Victor.

She was in Washington. Harry was, that spring, much in the news, much photographed, much consulted, a senator with views on the unfolding catastrophe, views he expressed with great feeling on a number of programs, and Inez was being his wife, standing at the edge of things, getting through it. And the telephone rang, and a voice she had not heard in three years, a voice she would have known after thirty, said her name.

"Inez," Jack Lovett said. "I want you to do something for me."

She did not ask what. This is the thing about Inez that I keep returning to, the thing I cannot get to the bottom of. She did not ask what. In twenty-three years he had never once asked her to do anything, had never once presumed on the room she kept for him, and now he asked, and she did not ask what, she only said yes, the way you say yes to the weather, the way you say yes to the thing you have been waiting your whole life to be asked.

What he wanted, it turned out, was complicated, and it had to do with Inez's daughter.

Inez had two children, twins, a boy and a girl, born in 1958. The boy was named Adlai, after the man Harry admired, and the girl was named Jessie, after no one in particular, after a sound Inez liked. By the spring of 1975 they were seventeen, the age Inez had been at the Outrigger Canoe Club, the age at which the structure of a life can go suddenly transparent and weightless, and Jessie, that spring, had gone to Vietnam.

This was not as strange as it sounds, or it was exactly as strange as it sounds but it was the kind of strangeness that was available to a certain kind of American girl in that particular year. Jessie had ideas, like her father, but Jessie's ideas had a way of attaching themselves to actions, which her father's never did, and one of the actions Jessie's ideas had attached themselves to was a notion that she could be useful, that there was work to be done, that a person of good will and energy could go to Saigon and help, in some unspecified but deeply felt way, with the children. She had gone in March. She had told her parents she was in Seattle. She had been, when Jack Lovett called Inez, somewhere in or near Saigon for five weeks, unreachable, untraceable, a seventeen-year-old American girl with ideas, in a city that was about to stop existing in the form in which she had found it.

Harry did not know.

This was the part Inez could not get past, the part she told me about, much later, in the house above the harbor at Kuala Lumpur, turning a glass of melted ice in her hands. Harry did not know his daughter was in Saigon. Harry believed his daughter was in Seattle. Harry was, that very week, on three programs, explaining to the American people what was happening in Saigon, and his daughter was there, in it, and he did not know, and the reason he did not know was that Inez had not told him, and the reason Inez had not told him was that she had not known how to begin, had not known how to enter the smooth machinery of Harry's certainty with a fact so jagged, and so she had carried it, the way she carried everything, alone, in the room behind her eyes, getting through it.

And then Jack Lovett called.

Jack Lovett knew where Jessie was. Of course he knew. Knowing where people were was Jack Lovett's life. He had come across the girl's name on a list, a list of the kind he was paid to come across, a list of Americans the embassy could not account for, and he had recognized the name, Jessie Victor, and he had made some calls, and he had found her, and now he was calling Inez to tell her that her daughter was alive, that her daughter was working in a hospital in Cholon, that her daughter did not understand what was about to happen, and that he, Jack Lovett, would get her out.

"I'll bring her to you," he said. "Do you understand me? I'll bring her out and I'll bring her to you. But I want you to come and get her. I want you to come to where I tell you to come, and I want you to be there, and I want you to take your daughter and I do not want you to go back."

There was a silence on the line, the particular silence of a great distance, the hiss and pulse of the cable under the ocean.

"Inez," Jack Lovett said. "Do you hear what I'm telling you."

She heard. She had heard, she realized, for twenty-three years. She had been hearing it at the Outrigger Canoe Club when she was seventeen and her father was in a cell and the world had gone transparent, hearing it in the airport bars and the hotel lobbies and the dozen accidental cities, hearing it in the only language Jack Lovett had ever found to say it in, the language of pink skies and wet air and gardenias where there were no gardenias. He was telling her to come in. After twenty-three years he was opening the door of the room she had been keeping, and he was telling her to come in, and to bring her daughter, and not to go back.

"Where," Inez said.

That was all. Not when, not how, not what about Harry, not what about Adlai, not what about the eighteen years and the ten thousand lanais and the whole laborious architecture of getting through it. Just: where.

And Jack Lovett, four thousand miles away, in a city that was burning, in the last week of the only world he had ever wanted to live in, smiled. She could hear him smile. She had been able to hear him smile for twenty-three years.

"Hong Kong," he said. "Or Kuala Lumpur. I'll let you know. Pack light."

He hung up. He always hung up first. He was always leaving. But this time, Inez understood, standing in the bedroom of the house in Washington with the telephone still in her hand and Harry's voice coming up faintly from the television downstairs, this time he was leaving toward her, this time the leaving had a destination and the destination was the room she had kept, and she sat down on the edge of the bed where she had slept beside Harry Victor for eighteen years, and she did something she had not done at the Outrigger Canoe Club when she was seventeen, something she had not done at her wedding or at the funerals or on the nights when Harry came home smelling of someone else or on the morning her father's first letter arrived from the hospital on the windward side, full of trade winds and golden plovers and betrayal.

She cried.

She cried for perhaps four minutes, sitting on the edge of the bed, and then she stopped, and she wiped her face, and she went to the closet and took down a small bag, the smallest she had, and she began, very calmly, to pack light. She packed two dresses and a photograph of her children and a thing Jack Lovett had given her years before, a small souvenir bought off a reconnaissance pilot on Johnston Island, the souvenir he had told her he bought in Manila and had not, and which she had known all along he had not, and which she had kept all those years precisely because it was the only lie he had ever told her, the one small human lie in twenty-three years of terrible truths, and therefore the most precious thing she owned.

Downstairs, Harry Victor was explaining to the American people what was happening in Saigon.

Inez Victor zipped the bag.

This is where the story starts, although I have told you it has no start. This is where Inez Christian, who married Harry Victor and stood at the edge of ten thousand lanais and got through it, stopped getting through it, and walked into the room she had been keeping all her life, and found Jack Lovett there, waiting, the way he had been waiting since she was seventeen, with the only thing he had ever had to offer her, which was the light at dawn during those Pacific tests. Something to see. Something to behold. Something that could almost make you think you saw God.
