They came down through the orchard in the early light with the dew still on the grass and their boots dark with it and the older one carried the shovel over his shoulder and the younger carried the pipe wrench and a coil of wire and they did not speak. The trees stood in their crooked rows going off into the gray and many of them dead and the dead ones gone to gray bones in the morning and standing among the living like things that had wandered in from some other country and died there. A few of the limbs bore fruit. Small hard apples no bigger than walnuts and the bark blackened with some canker the old man had never named.

Where's it broke at, the younger one said.

Down past the standpipe. I marked it.

They went on. The grass was high between the rows and gone to seed and it brushed wet against their thighs. A quail called somewhere off in the brush and another answered it and then there was nothing but their boots in the grass and the older brother's breathing.

You all right, the younger said.

I'm fine.

You sound winded.

I said I'm fine.

They came to the place where a stake stood driven into the ground with a rag tied to it and the older brother stopped and leaned on the shovel and looked at it. The ground there was dark and wet and a thin sheen of water lay in the row and went off downhill in a little thread bright as wire in the new sun.

Right there, he said.

The younger one set down the wrench and the wire and got down on his knees and put his hand in the mud and felt along the pipe. It was old galvanized line the old man had put in forty years gone and it had rusted through in a dozen places over the years and they had patched it each time and each patch outlived its setting and gave way somewhere else. He found the break with his fingers.

It's split, he said. Whole length of it near about.

Then we'll cut it out.

You know what new line costs.

I know what it costs.

He stood up and wiped his hand on his jeans and looked at his brother. The older one had taken off his hat and stood with it in his hand and the sweat already on his forehead though the day was still cool.

We could let it run, the younger said. What's down there to water.

The trees.

The trees is dying, Dell.

The older one put his hat back on. He took up the shovel and stepped down into the row and set the blade and stove it into the wet ground with his boot and began to dig. The younger watched him a while and then he got down and began to clear the mud away from the pipe with his hands.

They dug out a length of the line and the older brother went back to the truck for the hacksaw and the younger sat in the row with his back against the bank and looked off down through the trees toward the house. From here you could see the roof of it and the chimney and the line of cottonwoods along the ditch that the old man had not planted and that had been there before any of them and would be there after. The mountains stood off to the west blue and bare and the sun not yet on them.

The older brother came back with the saw and a length of new pipe under his arm and the fittings in his shirt pocket and he handed the saw down.

You cut it, he said. My hands ain't worth a damn this morning.

What's wrong with your hands.

Nothing's wrong with them. They're just stiff is all.

The younger took the saw and set the blade against the old pipe and began to draw it back and forth and the rust came off red in the water and ran away downhill. He sawed and the older one squatted beside him and watched.

Mac come by yesterday, the older one said.

I heard.

You heard what about.

I heard he come by.

He's offering again.

The younger one did not stop sawing. The blade caught and he worked it free and went on.

Same as before, the older said. Maybe a little better. He didn't say a number but he as good as said it'd be better.

We ain't selling to Mac Stoddard.

I didn't say we was.

You said he come by.

He did come by. A man can come by. I'm telling you what was said is all.

The pipe gave and the cut end dropped into the mud and the younger lifted it out and laid it up on the bank and started in on the far cut. The water that had pooled drained away now through the open ends and the row ran clear and they could see the gravel bottom of it.

You think on it any, the older said.

I don't have to think on it.

That ain't thinking on it. That's just saying you ain't.

I thought on it the first time he asked and I thought on it the second and I'll think on it now and it comes out the same. We ain't selling.

The older brother stood and walked off a little way down the row and stood looking at the trees. There was a thing in the way he stood, a kind of caving in at the shoulders, that the younger marked and looked away from. The old man had stood that way at the last.

It ain't making money, Dell. It ain't made money in nine years. We're feeding it out of what I make hauling and what you make shoeing and that's the truth of it whether you'll say it or not.

I'll say it. It's true.

Then.

Then nothing. A thing don't have to make money.

The older brother turned. What's it have to do then.

The younger one finished the second cut and the freed length of bad pipe came loose and he hauled it up out of the mud and slung it up the bank where it rolled and lay. He sat back on his heels.

It was his, he said.

He's dead.

I know he's dead. I buried him same as you did.

Then it ain't his. It's ours. And we can do with it what we want and what I want is to not stand out here at six in the morning in June patching forty year old pipe to water trees that's going to die anyhow whether we water them or not.

You don't know they'll die.

Look at them.

I'm looking at them.

The older brother came back and squatted again and took up the new length of pipe and held it against the gap to measure it. He marked it with his thumbnail and handed it across.

Cut it there, he said.

The younger set the saw to the new pipe and it cut clean and bright and quick where the old had cut slow and ragged. He cut it and they fitted the couplings on either end and the older brother took the wrench and threaded the first of them on and the younger held the pipe steady against him while he worked.

Granddad cleared this, the younger said. With a team and a stoneboat. Pulled the stumps with a mule named Bell and the old man told it that the mule died in the traces right out yonder past where the dead tree is and granddad set down in the dirt and cried over a mule like a child.

The older brother grunted and threw his weight onto the wrench.

He cried, the younger said.

I heard the story.

A man don't cry over a mule unless.

Unless what.

Unless it stood for something.

The older brother stopped and looked at him. Sweat ran down out of his hatband and along his jaw and dropped.

It stood for a mule, he said. It stood for the price of a mule and a season's work and a man that couldn't afford to lose neither one. That's all it stood for. You want to make everthing into something it ain't. The old man done the same. That's how come he died broke and that's how come we're out here.

You don't believe that.

I believe every word of it.

They worked the second coupling on and the older brother cinched it down and stood and put his hands to the small of his back and bent backward against them and his spine cracked twice loud in the morning quiet.

Open it, he said. See does it hold.

The younger one walked up the row to the standpipe and lifted the lever and they heard the water come knocking through the line and then it came up out of the open ground around the new joint and seeped and then held and the couplings darkened with the wet but did not run.

It holds, the older called.

For now, the younger said.

He came back down and they stood and looked at the joint a while and the water purled away downhill into the rows beyond and went on toward the dying trees.

That's a thousand dollars of line, the older said, between here and the lower end. That we don't got. And it'll go again. It always goes again. That's the nature of the thing. You patch one place and the patch holds and the line breaks a yard further on and that's how it'll be till there ain't no line left to break and there won't be no us left to break it.

The younger one squatted and began to push the mud back into the trench with his hands, packing it down around the pipe.

You member when we was boys, he said. He'd let us ride the headgate boards down the ditch when the water come.

The older brother said nothing.

You'd go first cause you was bigger and you'd shove off and ride her down and I'd stand up top and holler at you.

I member it.

I about drowned one time and you come in after me. You didn't even take your boots off. Just come in boots and all and got me by the collar.

The older brother looked off at the mountains. The sun was on them now and they had gone from blue to a kind of tan and the snow gone off them weeks since.

That don't change the arithmetic, he said.

No. It don't.

Then I don't know what you're telling me it for.

I'm just telling it.

The younger one stood and they gathered the tools and the older one took up the lengths of bad pipe and carried them on his shoulder back toward the truck and the younger followed with the saw and the wrench. They went up through the orchard in the full light now and the dew burning off and the smell of the wet ground rising around them and the apples small and hard and green on the few living limbs.

Halfway up the older brother stopped and stood the bad pipe down and stood breathing.

You sure you're all right, the younger said.

I told you I'm fine. Quit asking me.

You been short of breath all morning.

It's the dust.

There ain't no dust. Everthing's wet.

The older brother picked the pipe back up and went on. The younger watched the back of him a moment and then came on after.

I went to the doctor, the older said. He did not turn around to say it.

When.

Thursday. When you thought I went to Alamogordo for the fittings I went to the doctor first.

What'd he say.

He said a lot of things. Most of it I didn't follow.

What'd he say that you did follow.

The older brother stopped at the truck and swung the pipe up into the bed and it rang against the metal and lay still. He turned around and leaned against the tailgate and took off his hat and held it.

He said my heart's give out about half of it. He said the other half's working overtime to make up for it and it won't keep at it. He said there's a thing they can do but it's down in El Paso and it costs and there's no promising on it.

The younger one stood with the saw and the wrench in his hands and did not set them down.

How long, he said.

Don't ask me that.

How long, Dell.

He didn't put a number to it. They don't like to. He said a year. He said it could be five. He said it could be sooner. He said all of it like he was reading a feed sack.

The younger one set the tools down in the bed of the truck slow and careful as if they might wake something.

That's why, he said.

That's why what.

That's why you want to sell.

The older brother turned the hat in his hands.

You want to sell it so I'll have something when you're gone. So I won't be out here by myself feeding forty year old pipe and watching the trees die and you in the ground with the old man and granddad and the mule.

The older brother didn't answer for a while. A truck went by out on the county road and trailed a plume of dust behind it that hung in the still air and then began to settle.

You'd run it into the ground, he said finally. You and your sentiment. You'd hold it till there wasn't nothing left to hold and then they'd take it for the taxes and you'd have nothing. Not the land and not the money for the land. Nothing but a chest full of stories about a mule.

It ain't your business what I'd do after.

It is my business. You're the only business I got. You and this ground and the ground's a loser. I aimed to get shut of the loser before I went so I'd not leave you the both of them.

The younger one stood at the side of the truck and put his hand flat on the warm metal of the bed and looked down into it at the lengths of rusted pipe lying there.

I'd druther have you, he said.

I know it. But that ain't on the table.

I'd druther have you and the loser both than the money and you in the ground.

The older brother put his hat back on. He looked old in the morning light, older than his years, and the younger saw it and could not unsee it now that he had been told and he understood that he had been seeing it for weeks and had told himself it was the work and the heat and the worry over the money and that it had been none of those things or all of them and the one thing under them.

We don't have to settle it today, the older said.

No.

Mac ain't going nowheres.

No.

I just wanted you to know how it set with me. Why I been at you about it. It ain't that I don't love the place. I do. I just don't see the sense in loving a thing past the point it'll love you back.

The younger one wiped his face with the flat of his arm.

A thing don't have to love you back, he said.

The older brother almost smiled. There you go again, he said.

It don't though. A man loves his brother. His brother's heart's give out half of it and he loves him just the same and worse for it. That ain't a thing that pencils out. You can't put it in the ledger and balance against it.

That's different.

It ain't different. It's the same exact thing and you know it. It's the same as the mule and the same as the trees and the same as this ground and you setting there telling me to be sensible about it. There ain't no being sensible about the things you love. That's how you know them from the things you don't.

The older brother looked at him a long while. Then he looked off at the orchard standing in its rows in the sun with the dead trees among the living and the water running silver in the lower rows where they'd set the new pipe and the mountains beyond going tan and bare into the heat of the day.

Maybe so, he said.

Maybe so nothing.

Maybe so, he said again. But we still got the lower line to do. And it's going to be a hundred degrees by noon.

Then let's do it.

We'll have to come back down. I'm wore out.

We'll come back this evening. When it cools.

All right.

The older brother climbed up into the truck and sat with the door open and his legs hanging out and his breath coming hard from the climb up the hill. The younger stood by the door.

You should of told me, he said.

I'm telling you now.

You should of told me Thursday.

I wasn't sure how to put it.

You put it fine.

The older brother looked out through the windshield at the orchard and the house below it and the cottonwoods along the ditch.

I'll think on it, the younger said. The selling. I'll think on it honest. I won't promise nothing but I'll think on it.

I don't want you to sell it on my account.

That ain't what I said. I said I'd think on it. There's a difference.

The older brother nodded.

There's the doctoring too, the younger said. Down in El Paso. That costs.

We don't have it.

We'd have it if we sold.

The older brother looked at him.

Now you're thinking, he said.

I ain't said I'd sell.

You're thinking is all I'm saying. That's all I ever asked.

The younger one shut the door for him soft and went around the truck and climbed in the other side. He sat with his hands on his knees. The cab smelled of dust and hot vinyl and the rust off the pipe in the bed and underneath it the green wet smell of the orchard coming in the open windows.

You want to drive it down, the older said. Or you want me to.

I'll drive it.

He started the truck and it caught and ran rough a moment and then evened out and he backed it around and they went down the dirt track between the rows with the dead trees going by on either side and the live ones too and the small hard fruit hanging in them, and at the bottom where the track crossed the ditch he stopped and they sat and looked at the water running in it clear and quick over the gravel and on past toward the headgate where as boys they had ridden the boards down the spring flood hollering, and neither one of them said anything, and then he let out the clutch and they went on across and down toward the house where the cottonwoods stood and the morning was already going to heat.

He let her come down evenings now and then, the younger said. The doctoring. We could find it.

We could.

We will.

We'll see.

We will, the younger said. I say we will.

Or you say we will, the older said, and looked out the window at the trees going by, and did not say the rest of it, and the younger did not say it either, and they came on down to the house in the long light with the dust rising up behind them and hanging in the air and slowly settling back to the ground they had not yet sold and had not yet decided whether they ever would.
