The truck wouldn't make it down the access road, so Della left it at the gate and walked the rest of the way carrying the equipment in two trips. First the tripod and the total station in its hard case, then the rest—the rod, the data collector, the cooler with her lunch and the four liters of water the county made you sign for now during a drought. She set everything down at the waterline that wasn't a waterline anymore, just a long curving stain of dried mud where the reservoir had been, and she looked at the town.

She had expected it to be smaller. People always said that about the places they came from, that they shrank, and she had braced for the disappointment of scale, the way a childhood house turns out to be a cottage. But the drowned town was the opposite. It was larger than she carried it. The mud flats ran out toward the low brown water a half mile off, and across that whole expanse the foundations stood up out of the ground like the broken teeth of something enormous, and the streets ran between them, and the streets had names, and she knew the names.

She had been seven when they closed the valves. That was 1962. Her family had been among the last to go because her father had argued every step of it, had stood up in the county meetings and called the engineers liars, had refused the first buyout and the second and taken the third only when the sheriff came to the door with a paper. Della remembered the moving more than she remembered the town. She remembered her mother wrapping the good plates in newspaper at the kitchen table, and she remembered that the newspaper was full of the reservoir, the same story they were living, so that her mother's hands were wrapping the town in news of its own drowning.

Now she was sixty-five and the county surveyor for the third district, and the water had gone down sixty years' worth in a single brown summer, and they had given her the job because of course they had. She had not told them she was born here. It was in her file if anyone wanted to look, but no one looked, and the assignment had come across the desk like any other: *exposed structures, reservoir bed, survey for hazard assessment and historical record.* She had read it twice and then signed the equipment out herself.

She set up the tripod over the first benchmark, a brass disk the state had set into a slab of bedrock back before the valley was anything but a valley. The disk was still good. She leveled the instrument and let the bubble settle and entered the date and the control point, and her hands did the work they had done ten thousand times, and that steadiness was a mercy because nothing else in her was steady.

She shot the benchmark and turned the scope toward the nearest foundation and walked the rod out across the mud. Walking was the hard part. The mud had dried to a crust on top but underneath it was still soft, and her boots broke through in places, and the smell came up where they broke—not rot, she had expected rot, but something mineral and cold, the smell of a place that had been kept from air for a lifetime. It was the smell of the bottom of things. She had not known it would have a smell.

The first foundation was a house. She could tell because the foundations of houses are a particular size and the foundations of barns and churches and stores are other sizes, and she had been reading foundations her whole working life, but that was not how she knew this one. She knew this one because of the steps. There were three concrete steps that had led up to a porch, and the porch was gone and the house was gone but the steps remained, going up from the street to nothing, and at the bottom step someone had once pressed something into the wet concrete, and Della crouched down and brushed the silt away with the side of her hand and it was a horseshoe. Just the shape of a horseshoe, pressed in and lifted out, the ghost of one. The Hendersons. This was the Henderson place, and old man Henderson had shod horses even after there was no money in it, and he had pressed a shoe into his own front step the way another man might have carved his initials.

She wrote *Henderson* in the field book next to the point number. It was not information the county wanted. The county wanted coordinates, elevations, the dimensions of hazards. But she wrote it anyway, in the margin, and then she stood and shot the corners of the foundation, four points, and moved on.

By the middle of the morning she had surveyed eleven structures and the sun was high and white and she had drunk a liter of the water already. She worked the way she always worked, methodically, building the map point by point in the data collector, but the map that was building in the machine and the map that was building in her were two different things and they did not match. The machine knew Elm Street as a line of recorded coordinates. Della knew Elm Street as the place where she had fallen off a bicycle and split her chin, and she put her hand to her chin where the small scar still was, and there was the bicycle and there was the fall, fifty-eight years gone and waiting for her in the mud.

She came to the church around noon. She knew it would be there and still it stopped her. The Methodist church had been the tallest thing in town, and they had argued about it the way they argued about everything, whether the steeple would be a hazard to boats, and in the end the engineers had come and taken the steeple down before the flooding, had unbolted the bell and crated it and the county had promised it would go to a church in the new town and Della did not know if it ever had. The foundation that remained was long and the walls stood higher here than anywhere else, four feet of stone in places, and she walked into the nave through what had been the door and stood in the middle of the floor where the water had left a fine pale silt like a carpet and she looked up at where the ceiling had been and there was only sky, the hard white drought sky, and a single buzzard turning in it.

She had been baptized here. She knew this the way you know a thing you were told rather than a thing you remember, and she stood roughly where the font would have been, the front of the church, and tried to feel it and felt nothing but the heat and the strange cool smell coming up through her boots. Her mother had stood here holding her. Her father had stood here. They were both gone now, her mother fifteen years, her father twenty-two, and they had died in the new town in houses on streets that had never been underwater, and they were buried in the new cemetery on the hill, and Della understood standing in the drowned church that her parents had spent the second halves of their lives as exiles four miles from home. They had never said so. Her father had stopped arguing once the water came. He had taken a job at the feed store in the new town and he had grown tomatoes and he had not, that she ever heard, said one word about the valley again. But he used to drive out to the reservoir on Sunday afternoons. She remembered that now, standing in the church. He would drive out and park at the overlook and sit on the hood of the truck and look at the water, and she had thought, as a teenager, that it was boring, that her father was a boring man who liked to look at a lake, and she had not understood that he was visiting. That under that flat brown water was his whole life, and he had come every Sunday to sit above it.

She had to sit down. She found a place where the stone wall was wide enough and she sat and drank some water and ate half the sandwich she did not want, and she let herself cry for a few minutes, which she had promised herself she would not do, and then she stopped because the survey was not going to do itself and because she was, before anything else, a person who finished things.

She set up over the church and shot it properly, all the corners, the door opening, the line where the steeple base had been. She noted in the margin: *Methodist, steeple removed pre-1962, bell crated.* She did not know where the bell was. She thought she might find out. There was probably a record, somewhere in the county archive, of where the bell had gone, and she found she wanted very much to know, the way she wanted to know things her job did not require, the small destinies of objects, where everything had ended up.

In the afternoon she worked the main street. She had been saving it without admitting she was saving it. The main street ran east from the church for two hundred yards and it had been the whole world once—the store, the other store, the bank that was really just a savings and loan in a brick front, the barber, the café her aunt had run. She surveyed them in order and at each one she wrote the name in the margin and the names were a litany she did not know she still carried. *Pruitt's. The Rexall. Citizens S&L. Vada's.* Vada was her aunt, her mother's sister, and the café had been called Vada's and the foundation of it was small, much smaller than the café had been in her memory, and here at last was the shrinking she had braced for in the morning, here at the one place that mattered most, and it undid her more than the bigness had. The café where she had sat on a stool too tall for her and her aunt had given her the cherry from a Coke, that whole warm vanished world, was a rectangle of cracked concrete twelve feet by twenty.

She crouched in the middle of it. There was a depression in the slab where something heavy had stood, the stove maybe, or the cooler, and she put her hand flat on the concrete and it was cool and damp and gritty and she stayed like that for a while. Her aunt had drowned too, in a manner of speaking. Not literally—Vada had moved to the new town with everyone else, had tried to open a new café and it had failed because the new town was on the highway and people did not stop the way they had stopped in the valley, and Vada had ended her days waitressing at a chain place out by the interstate, and she had been bitter about it until she died, the only one of them who had stayed angry the way Della's father had stayed angry but out loud, who would say at holidays *they drowned us for a swimming pool*, which was not fair, the reservoir was for drinking water and flood control and a little hydropower, but Della understood now that fairness had nothing to do with what Vada meant. They had drowned the place where Vada was somebody. In the valley she had been the woman who ran the café. Everywhere else she was a waitress.

A meadowlark called somewhere out on the flats, the first bird Della had heard besides the buzzard, and she looked up and the light had changed, gone gold and long, and she realized she had been sitting in her aunt's café for nearly an hour and had four more structures to shoot before she lost the day. She got up. Her knees were not what they had been. She gathered the rod and went on.

She found her own house last.

She had not been avoiding it. She had simply worked east along the main street and her house was not on the main street, it was on a lane that ran off behind the church, and she came to the lane in the last hour of usable light and she walked up it and counted the foundations and she knew which one before she reached it. The Crane house. Her own name on the assignment had been Della Crane sixty years ago and was Della Crane still, because she had never married, and she had wondered sometimes if she had stayed a Crane out of stubbornness or out of grief, out of an unwillingness to let go of even the name of the drowned place, and now she stood at the edge of the foundation of the house where she had been Della Crane the child and she did not know the answer to that any better than she ever had.

It was just a foundation. She made herself say that. It was a rectangle of poured concrete with the stubs of pipes coming up where the kitchen had been and a darker rectangle within it that was the cellar, full now of dried silt, and there was nothing here, no horseshoe, no pressed initials, no sign that it had been hers. The houses give up nothing. They are only the cold underneath part, the part that was never meant to be seen, and standing there Della thought about how a foundation is the most honest part of a building and the least personal, the part that holds everything up and that nobody loves. You love the porch and the kitchen window and the room you slept in. You don't love the foundation. And the foundation is the only thing that lasts.

She set up the tripod over the benchmark she'd carried forward and she leveled the instrument and she found that her hands were not quite working right, that she had to do things twice. She made herself slow down. The light was going. She would have to come back tomorrow regardless, she could not shoot the whole town in one day, but she wanted this one. She wanted to put her own house in the record with her own instrument, and she understood that this was the reason she had not told the county, this was the reason she had signed the equipment out herself and driven the four miles alone, so that she could be the one. So that the drowned town would be measured by someone who had loved it and not by a contractor from out of state who would see only rectangles.

She walked the rod to the first corner. She had to walk it herself; there was no rodman, she'd told the office she didn't need one, and so she set the rod on the corner of her own house and then she walked back to the instrument to take the shot, and then back out to the next corner, and this was absurd and slow and she did it anyway, back and forth across the foundation of her childhood, the rod and the instrument, until she had all four corners and the cellar and the pipe stubs, and then she stood at the instrument and looked at the data on the little screen, the points, the coordinates, the cold true numbers, and she thought: this is what I have. This is what I can do. I can write down where it was.

The sun was on the rim of the hills. The flats had gone from brown to bronze to a deep red, and the water out at the far edge had caught the color and lay there like something molten, and Della stood among the foundations in the long light and she could see, for a moment, the whole town as a survey, as a drawing—the streets she had recovered, the structures she had named, the network of it laid out clean and complete. She had spent her life making such drawings. She made the world legible; that was the work; she took the chaos of the actual ground and reduced it to lines and numbers that could be filed and recorded and trusted. And she had never until this moment understood what the work was for. It was for this. It was so that when the thing itself was gone—and the water would come back, the rains would come, the reservoir would fill, this was a drought and not a miracle and the town would go under again, this winter or the next—when the thing itself was gone there would be a record. A true record. Made by someone who knew the names.

She thought of her father on the hood of his truck on Sunday afternoons. He had not been able to make a record. He had not had the instrument or the training or the brass disk in the bedrock to measure from. He had only been able to sit and look at the water and hold the town inside himself, and when he died the town died again, the second drowning, the one that takes the rememberers. Della had no children. When she died there would be no one who had stood on the stool in Vada's café, no one who had fallen off the bicycle on Elm Street, no one who had been baptized in the Methodist church with the bell that went away to who-knew-where. The town would have no one left who carried it. Except that it would have the survey. It would have the lines and the numbers and the names in the margin, filed in the county archive, and someone in a hundred years could pull the drawing and see that there had been a town here, and that it had a main street, and that a café called Vada's had stood twelve feet by twenty at the corner, and that a horseshoe had been pressed into the bottom step of the Henderson house.

It was not the same as remembering. She knew that. A drawing was not a Sunday afternoon. But it was what could be kept, and she was the one who could keep it, and she understood now that this was not an accident of the assignment crossing her desk. It was the shape of her whole life bending around to meet her. She had become a surveyor. She did not know, had never known, exactly why; she had told people she liked being outdoors, she liked the precision, she liked working alone. But here was the reason, surfacing out of the mud like the town itself: she had become a person who could make permanent records of vanished places because she came from a vanished place, and some part of her had spent fifty years getting ready for the summer the water went down.

She packed the instrument as the last light failed. She did it by feel as much as by sight, the way she could do it in her sleep, the total station into its foam bed, the latches, the tripod legs collapsed and strapped. She would carry it out in two trips again. She left the cooler for the second trip and shouldered the instrument and the tripod and started back across the flats toward the gate, and she walked the main street one more time in the dark, past Vada's and the bank and the Rexall and Pruitt's, and she found she was saying the names under her breath as she passed, not for the record now, the record was made, but for herself, the way you say goodnight to the rooms of a house.

At the waterline that wasn't a waterline she stopped and looked back. The foundations were just shapes now against the last gray of the sky, the church a little taller than the rest, the streets running between them invisible but known, mapped, held. The water would come back and take it all. But she had the points. She had the names in the margin. Tomorrow she would come back and finish—there was the east end still, the school, the cemetery they'd moved but not entirely, the lane past her house—and she would shoot every corner and write every name she knew and some she would have to dig out of her memory like the horseshoe out of the silt, and when she was done there would be a true drawing of the drowned town, and she would file it, and her name would be on it, Della Crane, surveyor, third district, and that was a kind of return. That was the only kind of return there was.

She carried the instrument up the dark access road to the truck. The mineral smell of the bottom of things was on her hands and would not wash out for days. She set the case in the bed and went back down for the rest, and the stars were coming out over the valley that had been a lake and was a valley again for one more season, and somewhere out on the flats the meadowlark called once more and went quiet, and Della Crane stood in the middle of her ruined and recovered town and listened to the enormous silence of a place that had finally, after sixty years, come up for air.
