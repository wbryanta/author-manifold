The ferry left at eleven, which meant Aksel had the whole evening to lose before he could even begin to sleep through the crossing. He arrived at the terminal at half past nine with a duffel bag he had not needed to pack so fully, and he sat on a bench beneath a flickering fluorescent panel and watched the few other passengers arrive in ones and twos. A woman with a folding bicycle. Two truckers who knew each other and stood smoking outside the doors, their breath and their smoke indistinguishable in the cold. A family with a sleeping child draped over the father's shoulder like a coat. None of them looked as though they were going north to see a dead man.

He had the letter in his jacket, though it was not a letter, only a printed email he had folded into quarters until the creases had gone soft. A constable named Sundqvist had written it. *We have recovered a body that may correspond to your brother Tomas Lindqvist, last registered at this address as next of kin. We would be grateful if you could attend in person.* That was the phrase that had stayed with him. *Attend in person.* As though it were a function, a recital, something with a printed program. He had read it perhaps forty times and each time the words rearranged themselves slightly, so that now he was no longer certain whether it said *may correspond* or *does correspond*, and he would not take the paper out again to check because he did not want the truckers to see him reading it.

He had not seen Tomas in nine years. He did the arithmetic again on the bench, the way he had been doing it all week, hunting for an error that would make the number smaller. Nine years. The last time had been at their mother's funeral, where Tomas had arrived late and left early and said almost nothing, and what he had said had been about money, about the house, about who would take what, and Aksel had been so disgusted by the timing of it that he had not answered, and the not-answering had become its own permanent thing, a structure they both lived inside afterward, each on his own side of it.

The boarding call came as a recorded voice in three languages. Aksel rose and shouldered the duffel and walked up the ramp into the steel throat of the ship.

His cabin was below the waterline, a windowless box with two bunks, and he was glad to find he had it to himself. He sat on the lower bunk and felt the engines start somewhere beneath him, a deep mechanical shudder that came up through the mattress and into his spine, and then the slow heave as the ferry pushed off and the harbor lights began to slide past the corridor portholes he could see through his open door. He left the door open for a while. He did not want to be sealed in yet.

He thought he would not be able to eat but found, when he climbed the stairs to the cafeteria, that he was ravenous in a shameful, animal way. He took a tray and chose meatballs and boiled potatoes and a lingonberry sauce from a plastic tub, and he carried it to a table by the dark window and ate quickly, watching his own reflection eat. Beyond the reflection there was nothing, only the black water moving and the occasional far light of a buoy or another vessel. He was forty-six years old. His face in the window looked like their father's face, which had also looked, toward the end, like a man considering a question he could not phrase.

There had been a time when he and Tomas were close in the way only brothers near in age can be, which is to say without choosing it, the closeness imposed on them by shared rooms and shared parents and the long boredom of a childhood in a small place. They had built things together. Aksel remembered a raft, a genuine raft, lashed from fence-posts and oil drums, which they had launched on the lake behind the church and which had held together exactly long enough to carry them out past their depth before coming apart with a kind of sigh, the drums drifting off in different directions, the two of them treading water and laughing so hard they nearly drowned. He must have been ten. Tomas eight. The memory had the over-bright quality of a thing examined too often, its colors worn pale at the edges, and he did not entirely trust it anymore. Perhaps it had not been a raft. Perhaps it had been a smaller thing he had inflated in the retelling until it became a raft. But the water he trusted, the cold shock of it, and the way Tomas had grabbed his arm, not from fear but from joy, gripping it to make sure Aksel was seeing what he was seeing.

When had that boy become the man at the funeral? There must have been stages, a slow erosion, but Aksel had not been present for them, and so in his memory the transformation was instantaneous and therefore unjust. He had a younger brother who gripped his arm in cold water, and then he had a stranger who talked about the value of a house while their mother lay in a box ten meters away. The years between were Tomas's own and he had not been invited into them. He knew the outlines only. The drinking, which their mother had spoken of in lowered tones as though it were a relative who occasionally visited. The jobs that did not last. The woman in Trondheim, then no woman. A child, possibly. Aksel was not even sure of that. There might be a niece or nephew somewhere whom he had never met and who did not know he existed, and that thought, surfacing now over the cooling potatoes, struck him with a force out of proportion to the meatballs, and he had to set down his fork.

He bused his tray and went out onto the deck.

The cold was immediate and total, a cold with weight to it, and the wind came across the open water unobstructed and made his eyes stream. He gripped the rail. The deck was deserted. Below him the hull cut the black sea into two white seams that healed instantly behind the ship. He could see the lights of no land in any direction now. They were in the middle of the strait, the deepest part, where the old charts marked depths he could not imagine, cold pressure and darkness and whatever lived down there in it without ever needing the sun. He thought of bodies in water. He could not help it. He thought of the raft coming apart and the two of them in the lake, and he thought of Tomas, whom they had found, the email did not say where but the word *recovered* suggested water, *recovered* was a word for things taken back from water, and he stood at the rail and tried to picture his brother's face and found that he could not. He could picture the boy. He could picture the stranger at the funeral. But the specific present face, the face he might be asked tomorrow to identify, the face that the email called a body that *may correspond*—that he could not summon, and the failure frightened him more than the cold did.

A man came out onto the deck behind him. One of the truckers, the taller one, who nodded and lit a cigarette in the shelter of the bulkhead, cupping the flame. For a while they stood not speaking, the two of them and the wind.

"Bad night for it," the trucker said eventually, raising his voice over the engines.

"Yes," Aksel said.

"You going up for the fishing?"

"No."

The trucker drew on his cigarette and did not ask further, which Aksel was grateful for, and after a few minutes he flicked the butt over the rail, where the wind took it sideways and out, and he went back inside, and Aksel was alone again. He stayed until the cold became unbearable and then a little past that, as though he owed it something, as though discomfort were a form of preparation, and then he too went in.

In the cabin he lay in the lower bunk with his clothes on and the light off and listened to the ship. There was no possibility of sleep. He had known this would be the case and had brought a book, a thick paperback he had read before, but he could not concentrate on it, and so he lay in the dark and let the memories come, since they were coming anyway.

He thought about the last phone call. Not the funeral; after the funeral there had been one phone call, two years later, which he had told no one about and which he had spent six years not thinking about with a discipline that surprised him. Tomas had called late, near midnight, and Aksel had known from the first slurred syllable that he had been drinking, and something in him had closed like a shutter. Tomas had wanted to talk. That was all. He had not wanted money, though Aksel had braced for it; he had wanted, it became slowly and unbearably clear, simply to talk to his brother, and he had not known how to begin, and so he had circled, telling a long pointless story about a man at his work, and Aksel had stood in his kitchen with the phone against his ear and his free hand gripping the edge of the counter, waiting for it to be over. He had said *mm* and *I see* in the places where those sounds went. He had not asked a single question. And at some point Tomas had stopped in the middle of the story and there had been a silence, and then he had said, in a different voice, a sober voice almost, *You don't want to talk to me.* And Aksel had said, *It's late, Tomas.* And Tomas had said, *Yeah. It's late.* And he had hung up.

It's late. He had meant the hour. He had only meant the hour. But the words had carried more than he had put into them, the way words do, and Tomas had received the more, and they had not spoken again. Six years. Aksel had let it stand. There had been a hundred moments since when he might have called back, a number in his phone he had never deleted, and each time he had thought *not now, when things are calmer, when I have the energy for it,* as though reconciliation were a heavy piece of furniture he would move once he had cleared the room. The room had never been cleared. And now there was a constable named Sundqvist and a body that *may correspond,* and the energy he had been saving was of no use to anyone.

He turned onto his side. The engines went on. He thought: it may not be him. He held onto that. The email had been careful. *May correspond.* There were unidentified bodies that turned out to be other men entirely, men with their own brothers, and tomorrow he might walk into a cold room and look at a face and feel the enormous shameful flood of relief that it was a stranger, that it was someone else's grief and not his, and he would get back on the ferry and go home and the number in his phone would still be there and he would, this time, he swore it into the dark of the cabin, he would call it. He would clear the room. If it was a stranger tomorrow he would take it as the warning it was and he would call his brother and say the unsayable thing, which was not even complicated, which was only *I'm sorry it's been so long.*

But he knew, lying there, that it was Tomas. He did not know how he knew. The constable would not have written had there not been strong reason; you did not summon a man across a strait on a guess. Something had been found on the body. A wallet, a tattoo, a scar. Aksel tried to remember whether his brother had any scars and found that he did not know, that he had not known the man's body for thirty years, had not seen him undressed since they were boys swimming in the lake, and the lake came back again, the cold of it, the grip on his arm.

He must have slept after all, because he woke to the change in the engines, the slackening, and the gray light coming through the porthole in the corridor. They were entering the fjord. He lay still and felt the ship slow and the motion smooth out as they passed into sheltered water, and through the porthole he could see, when he sat up, the steep dark sides of the land going up into low cloud, the snow on the high parts, a few houses scattered improbably along the shore with smoke standing straight up from their chimneys in the windless cold. It was beautiful in a way that did not care whether he found it beautiful. He washed his face in the tiny steel basin and looked at himself and again saw his father, and he gathered his bag, which he had not really needed to pack so fully, and he went up to wait by the doors with the truckers and the woman with the folding bicycle and the family, the child awake now and fretful, and the ramp came down with a great hydraulic groan onto the concrete of the northern quay.

The town was small and steep and the police station was not hard to find. He had time; the ferry had docked at seven and Sundqvist had said any time after nine, and so Aksel walked. He drank a coffee standing up in a bakery that had just opened, and the woman behind the counter spoke to him in the dialect of the north which he could follow only with effort, and he ate a cinnamon bun he did not taste. He walked down to the harbor and looked at the fishing boats and the gulls and the gray water that had taken his brother and given him back. He was very calm. He noticed his own calm with suspicion, the way one notices an absence of pain that may only mean the nerve is dead.

At nine he presented himself at the station and gave his name, and a young officer made a call, and after a few minutes a heavyset man of about sixty came out, and Aksel understood from his face that this was Sundqvist, and that Sundqvist had done this many times, and that he was good at it, and that being good at it had cost him something he had decided long ago to pay.

"Lindqvist," Sundqvist said, and shook his hand, and held it a moment longer than a handshake. "Thank you for coming. It is a long way."

"It's no distance," Aksel said, which made no sense, but Sundqvist nodded as though it did.

They sat in a small office and Sundqvist explained. The body had been recovered four days earlier by a fishing crew, some distance out, beyond the mouth of the fjord. It had been in the water some time; he was honest about this without being brutal about it, and Aksel understood that he was being told, gently, that the identification might be difficult, that he should prepare himself. There were personal effects. A wristwatch. And there had been, in a sealed inner pocket, sodden but legible, an old photograph, and Sundqvist slid across the desk a clear plastic sleeve, and Aksel looked at it.

It was a photograph of two boys on a raft.

So it had been a raft. The water came up over him then, all of it, the whole lake, and he sat very still so that it would not show on his face, but Sundqvist was watching him with the patience of his profession and saw it anyway and looked away to give him the privacy of it.

The boys in the photograph stood on a structure of fence-posts and oil drums that had clearly been built by children, lashed with what looked like clothesline, and the larger boy had his arm around the smaller boy's shoulders, and both of them were squinting and laughing into a sun thirty-six years gone. Aksel did not remember the photograph being taken. Their mother must have taken it, from the shore, before the launch, before the coming-apart. He had never seen it. He had not known it existed. And Tomas had carried it, this image of the two of them, in a pocket against his body, sealed against the wet, all the years of the drinking and the lost jobs and the woman in Trondheim and the late phone call and the long silence; he had kept it, this one thing, this proof that there had been a day when his brother's arm was around him and they were both laughing, and he had taken it into the water with him.

"Is this your brother?" Sundqvist asked, very quietly.

"This is both of us," Aksel said. "I'm the one on the left."

Sundqvist nodded and made a small note and waited.

"He kept it," Aksel said. He found he needed to say it aloud to someone. "All this time. He kept it."

"People keep what matters," Sundqvist said. It was the sort of thing the man had surely said before, in this room, to others, and it was no less true for that.

They went down a corridor and then down stairs into the cold part of the building, and there was a room, and there was a steel table, and a sheet, and Sundqvist asked if he was ready and Aksel said yes, and the sheet was drawn back.

The water had done its work. The face was not a face he could have identified by sight alone; it was swollen and dark and the features had gone, and a younger man, a stranger's son, might have looked at it and felt the flood of relief he had imagined, might have said *no, that's not him, that's someone else's grief.* But Aksel did not feel relief. He looked at what the water had left of his brother and he knew it utterly, knew it past the ruin of it, the way you know a word with its letters scrambled, the shape of the whole thing rising up underneath the damage. There was the hairline. There was the set of the brow that was their father's brow and his own. There was the small white line of an old scar through the left eyebrow that he had not known he remembered until he saw it and remembered the day it was made, a thrown stone, an accident, Aksel's own hand that threw it, fifty years ago, both of them six and eight, the blood and the screaming and their mother's terror and then the laughter afterward, always the laughter afterward, the two of them.

"Yes," Aksel said. "That's my brother. That's Tomas."

His voice did not break. He was almost angry that it did not break. He wanted, standing there, to be the kind of man who broke, who fell to his knees, who made the sounds that grief was supposed to make, because that would have been a way of giving Tomas something, of paying something across the table at last. But he only stood, and looked, and said the name, and felt the calm cover him over like ice forming on still water, thin and total, and underneath it everything moving and cold and without any bottom he could find.

Sundqvist drew the sheet back up.

Later there were forms. There were always forms. He signed where the young officer showed him, and he answered the questions, the date of birth he knew and the other dates he did not, and he was given a small sealed bag with a wristwatch in it that he did not recognize, and the photograph, which Sundqvist said he could keep now, it was no longer needed, and Aksel held the plastic sleeve in both hands and looked at the two laughing boys and could not put it in his pocket and could not put it down.

"There will be arrangements," Sundqvist said. "If you wish, we can give you the names of people here who handle these things. Or you can take him home. South. Many people prefer to take them home."

"I'll take him home," Aksel said, and was surprised to hear himself say it, and then was not surprised, because of course he would, of course; he had come all this way to attend in person and he would not now leave his brother in the north among strangers. He would take him back across the strait. They would make the crossing together, the two of them, one of them in the hold in a box and one of them on the deck in the cold, and it was nine years too late and six years too late and it was the only thing left that he could do and so he would do it.

He went out into the steep cold town with the photograph in his hand. The ferry south did not leave until evening. He had the whole day to lose. He walked down to the harbor again and stood at the edge of the gray water where it lapped at the stones, and he took the folded email out of his jacket, the soft-creased paper, *attend in person,* and he held it and the photograph together, the words and the boys, and he stood there a long time while the gulls wheeled and cried over the boats, and at last, because he did not know what else to do with it, he spoke aloud to the water, very quietly, in the dialect of his childhood that he and Tomas had shared and that no one else here would have recognized as theirs.

"It's late," he said. "I know. It's late."

And the water, which had carried the raft and the boys and the man and the photograph and would carry them all home, said nothing, only moved against the stones, patient and cold and without any bottom that he could find.
