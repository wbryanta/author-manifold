The price stickers were the worst part. Not the strangers, not the way they touched things, but the little orange dots I had pressed onto everything the night before, kneeling on the kitchen floor with the roll between my teeth. Four dollars for the cake stand. A dollar for the mixing bowls, the green ones with the white stripe she'd had since before I was born. I had sat there assigning numbers and the numbers were lies, every one. There was no number for the bowl. You cannot put a dot on the inside of a woman's wrist where she rested it against the rim while she beat eggs for forty years.

Now it was afternoon and they came through the door my mother had painted blue against the advice of every neighbor on Sugar Street.

"This solid wood?" A man in a windbreaker tilted the dining chair upside down to look at its underside like a horse he meant to buy.

"It's solid," I said. I didn't know if it was. My mother would have known. She knew the bones of every object she owned, had carried most of them up from nothing, from layaway, from the back of somebody else's truck.

"Wobbles," he said, and set it down hard, and the wobble was something my body recognized before my mind did. That chair had always tilted. I had eaten eighteen years of suppers leaning slightly to the left because of it and I had never once thought to fix it and now this man in nylon was holding it against her like a flaw.

"Three dollars," I said.

"You said two on the tag."

"Then two." I wasn't going to fight a man over the chair I'd grown up tipping in. Let him have it. Let him carry it out into the white light and put it in his garage and never once know that a girl had done her arithmetic homework on it, crying, while a woman stood at the stove and said, without turning around, *You're not stupid, you're just scared, and scared looks like stupid till you stop being scared.*

I had not stopped being scared. I was fifty-six years old and standing in my dead mother's front room and I had not stopped.

"Bathroom working?" A woman with a child on her hip.

"Down the hall."

She went and I heard the toilet, the old complaining run of it, the handle you had to jiggle. I had meant to fix that too. There was a whole house of things I had meant to fix while she was alive enough to thank me for it, and instead I had driven the four hours twice a year and sat with her in the kitchen and told her about my life in the careful, edited way you tell a parent, leaving out the man who left and the money that didn't come and the nights, the long ones. She had let me edit. That was her kindness, the editing she allowed. She knew the full plate and ate only what I served her.

A girl, maybe twenty, was lifting the lid of the cedar chest at the foot of where the stairs began.

"Empty," I said, faster than I meant to. "That one's empty."

It wasn't. It hadn't been empty an hour ago. I had emptied it myself that morning, carrying the quilts up to the car in my arms like a woman carrying water, and the smell had come up out of the cedar and the wool the way it always had, the smell of my mother's particular dark, and I had put my face down into the bundle in the back seat and stood in the driveway of the house I grew up in and made a sound I did not know I owned.

"How much?" the girl asked. She had a sweet face. She had the kind of face that doesn't yet know what a chest like that is for, what gets folded into it, what gets carried out.

"Fifteen."

"Would you take ten?"

"I'd take ten." I would have taken nothing. I would have paid her to leave it. But you cannot pay people to leave your mother's things, you can only watch them decide what your mother is worth and then disagree by a few dollars and lose.

The girl gave me a ten and a folded five and I gave the five back and she looked at me like I was a strange thing, and I was. She lifted one end of the chest and a boy I hadn't noticed lifted the other and they took it sideways through the blue door, knocking the frame, and out into the day. I watched the cedar chest go down the porch steps that my mother's father had built, that my mother had sat on snapping beans, that I had sat on at seventeen telling her I was leaving this town and never coming back, watched her face take it, watched her say *all right* in a voice that meant *I made you to leave, that was the whole point, don't you dare look back at me with pity.*

I had looked back. I'd been looking back ever since.

"You the daughter?" An old woman now, in a cloth coat too warm for the day. She had the look of the neighborhood about her, the look of someone who had earned the right to walk into this house without buying anything.

"I'm the daughter."

"I knew your mother. We sang together. Mount Zion." She let the name sit there like a question.

"I remember the choir," I said, which was not an answer to the question she was really asking, which was: *Where were you. Where were you when she got small. Where were you when the lights in this house went out one by one and she sat in the one room she could still afford to heat.*

"She was proud of you," the woman said. "She told everybody. Said you were out in the world doing something."

Doing something. My mother's phrase for a life she could not picture and would not interrogate. *Out in the world doing something.* It had covered everything—the failures, the rented rooms, the work that came and went. She had taken *something* and made it sound like *everything,* the way she could take a ham bone and three potatoes and feed five children and call it Sunday.

"I should have come more," I said. I don't know why I said it to this stranger in a hot coat. Maybe because she was a stranger. You can't give the full plate to the people who'll have to eat with you afterward.

The woman didn't flinch and she didn't comfort me, which I will be grateful to her for until I die. She just looked at me with her old eyes and said, "Mm." And then: "That her piano?"

"That's her piano."

"You play?"

"No."

"She played."

"She played."

There was a thing happening in the room with the piano in it that I could not stop. The light came in low and yellow through the front window, the window I had broken once with a softball and gotten the only whipping of my whole childhood for, not for the window but for the lie about the window, *don't you lie to me girl, I can stand near anything but I can't stand a lie standing in my own front room.* The light came in and lay across the top of the upright and the man in the windbreaker was back, he had brought a friend, and they were standing in front of the piano talking about whether it would fit in a basement.

"Needs tuning," the friend said.

"Everything needs tuning," the windbreaker said, like a man who knew things.

My mother had bought that piano when I was nine. I do not know how. I have done the math of her life many times since and the piano does not fit anywhere in it; there was no slot for it, no slack. And yet it came, on a Saturday, four men and a hand truck and my mother standing in the doorway in her good dress at ten in the morning for no reason except that a piano was coming into her house and she would meet it dressed. She could play hymns and she could play one piece of ragtime that she'd say she didn't remember where she learned, smiling when she said it, the smile of a woman with a door in her she didn't open for her children. She made me take lessons. I quit. I quit because I was lazy and I told her I quit because Mrs. Furmanek smelled like camphor, another lie, and she had looked at me and not whipped me that time, just looked, and said, *Fine. But you'll be sorry, and being sorry's the most expensive thing there is, you pay for it your whole life on the installment plan.*

I was on the installment plan. I made a payment every month. I would be making one for that piano as long as I lived.

"What you want for it?" the windbreaker asked me.

I had not put a dot on the piano. I had stood in front of it the night before with the roll of orange stickers in my hand and I had not been able to do it. Not because I meant to keep it—there was no room in my life for a piano, no truck, no basement, no someone to play it—but because to put a number on it was to admit that I had let it come to this, that I had let the whole house come to this, the chairs and the bowls and the chest going out the door in the arms of people who would have it longer than I'd had her at the end, longer than the visits added up to, all those Saturdays I could have driven four hours and didn't because of work, because of a man, because of *something,* out in the world doing something.

"It's not for sale," I said.

The windbreaker frowned. "It's in the sale. Everything in here's in the sale, isn't it? That's what the sign says."

"That one's not."

"You can't have a piano in an estate sale and then—"

"I can do whatever I want," I said, and my voice came out of me in my mother's register, the low one, the one that ended things. "It's my house. It's my mother's house. That's her piano and it's not for sale."

The room got quiet the way a room does. The old woman in the coat looked at me and something moved in her face, approval maybe, or memory, or both, the two being most of what's left when you get to be her age, when you get to be my mother's age, which my mother never got past now, eighty-one and stopped.

The windbreaker put up his hands. "All right, lady. All right." And he and his friend drifted off toward the kitchen to price out my mother's bowls.

I sat down on the piano bench. It was the same bench, the seat that lifted up, and inside it was the sheet music she'd kept, the hymnal and the loose yellow pages soft as cloth from her hands turning them. I lifted the seat and there it was, and on top, where I had not put it, where it had been waiting, was the one piece of ragtime, hand-copied in pencil in a young woman's hand that I had never seen, that could not have been my mother's hand because my mother's hand was old as long as I'd known it, but it was her name at the top, her girl name, the name before she was anybody's mother, written by some person on a porch in some year I wasn't alive for, in a town she left to come to this one, with a door in her she kept shut, and behind the door this: a girl who learned ragtime from somebody she never named, and folded it into a piano bench, and carried it through her whole life under the lid, under the hymns, under me.

I sat there with the yellow page in my hands while strangers carried the furniture of my childhood out the blue door into the light.

"You all right?" the old woman asked. She had come close. She smelled of peppermint and the particular powder old women wear, the same powder, I realized, that the cedar chest had smelled of, that my mother had smelled of, that I would now spend the rest of my life smelling on strangers and turning around for.

"I never knew where she learned this," I said, and held up the page, though it meant nothing to her, a page of pencil marks.

"Learned what."

"There was a song she played. She said she didn't remember learning it."

The old woman looked at the page and then at me and she did a thing I did not expect, she laughed, a small sound, a girl's sound coming up out of all that age. "Oh, she remembered," she said. "We all remembered. We just didn't tell our children everything. You don't tell your children everything. They can't carry it and you don't want them to. Some things you carry so they don't have to."

"I would have carried it," I said. "I wanted to carry it. She wouldn't let me carry anything."

"That's the love," the old woman said. "That's the thick kind. That's the kind that won't let you help. It looks like she was keeping you out. She was keeping you safe. There's a difference, but you can't see it from where you're standing. You won't see it for a while yet."

She patted the top of the piano the way you pat the flank of an animal you're leaving in a field, and she went out the door without buying anything, having come, I understood now, only to say that, only to put that into my hands the way the cedar chest had put the smell into the air, and then she was gone down the steps and into the rest of the afternoon.

The house emptied slowly. It does not empty all at once, a house, even with the doors open and the price stickers and the strangers; it empties the way water leaves a thing, the way it leaves wood, going first from the surface so you think it's dry and then for years it keeps leaving from somewhere deeper that you can't see. By four o'clock the dining table was gone, the sideboard, the bed frames, the lamps, the green bowls, the cake stand, the chair that wobbled. A young couple bought the kitchen curtains, my mother's kitchen curtains, off the rod, the woman standing on a chair to unhook them while her husband held her steady, and I watched the window become a bare window, become just an opening with the day on the other side of it, and I thought: *she stood at that sink and looked out that window for fifty years and now it's just a hole in the wall again, the way it was before she came, the way it'll be after.*

I should have hated them, the buyers. I didn't. They were just people who needed curtains, who needed a chair, who would put my mother's things into their own lives and rest their own wrists on the rims of her bowls and never know, and that not-knowing was the thing, that was the whole cold size of it: that her life would go on out into the world in pieces, dispersed, carried in a hundred directions in the arms of people who would call them *a nice old bowl, a solid chair, good curtains, hardly any wear,* and there was no number for it, no dot, no price, you couldn't put it on the table and sell it because what it was could cleave the bone, and the man in the windbreaker would have looked at it and tilted it upside down and said it wobbles, and he'd have been right, and he'd have known nothing.

I stayed on the bench. I had the page. I had not been able to play in forty-five years and I was not going to be able to play now, the *being sorry* had come due and there was no version where I sat down and the music was in my hands the way it had been in hers, that was a story that happens in other people's lives and not in mine, I was not going to be redeemed by a piano on the afternoon I sold my mother's house. But I put my right hand on the keys. They were cool and they had the give I remembered, the slight resistance and then the drop, and a note came out into the emptying room, just one, and it was out of tune, the windbreaker had been right, everything needs tuning, and the note hung there crooked and yellow as the light and I left it. I didn't play the next one. I just let that one note stand in the front room my mother had painted the door of against everyone's advice, in the house she carried up out of nothing, the note she'd left me by leaving me the bench and the page and the door I'd been told all my life to leave through and never look back from.

I looked back. I had spent my whole life looking back at her face in the doorway saying *all right.* And now there was no doorway, or there was, but it was empty, the door open on the porch and the steps and the street and the going light, and through it, finally, with no one left to carry anything out, the afternoon came in.

A last man stuck his head through. "Anything left?" he said. "Sign's still up."

I looked at the room. Bare floor, the lighter rectangles on the walls where the frames had hung, the dust outlining where the furniture of my childhood had stood for fifty years like the chalk around a body.

"No," I said. "There's nothing left."

"Piano," he said, pointing.

"That's not for sale."

He looked at me a second, weighing whether to push it, and decided not to, people can tell, finally, when a thing is past selling, when a woman is sitting on a bench in an empty room with a yellow page in her lap and one out-of-tune note still ringing somewhere in the bones of the house, and they leave it, they have the grace at the end of the day to leave it.

"Sorry for your loss," he said, which is the sentence people say, the one with no dot on it either, the cheapest and the truest thing in the room, and I said thank you, and he went, and pulled the sign up out of the yard on his way past it, the way you do for a stranger, a small mercy, and I heard the stake come out of my mother's ground.

I would call the men about the piano in the morning. I'd find a truck. I'd find a room for it in my life that did not have a room for it, the way she had found a slot in a life that had no slack, because that is what you do, finally, with the one thing you won't put a number on: you carry it, you make the impossible space, you stand in your good dress in the doorway to meet it. I would have it tuned. I would sit down in front of it in whatever rented room held it and I would be sorry, expensively, on the installment plan, and I would try, with my bad late hands, to learn the one piece of ragtime off the soft yellow page in the girl's hand that was my mother's before she was mine, the door in her she never opened, opening now, too late, the only time a door like that ever opens, which is after, which is when the house is empty and the light comes in and there's nobody left to carry anything out, and the thing you couldn't sell is the only thing you have.

I closed the lid over the keys, gently, the way she would have, the way you close a thing you mean to open again. Then I sat in the empty room of my mother's house until the yellow went out of the window and the blue door stood open on the dark, and I did not get up to close it for a long time, because closing it would mean it was over, and I was not done looking back.
