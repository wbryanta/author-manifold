The ferry left the harbour at eleven, an hour late, the lights of the town sliding backwards into the dark water as though the place were being slowly drawn under. Niall stood at the rail with his coat collar up and watched until there was nothing to watch, then went inside where the heat of the lounge pressed against his face like a wet cloth.

He had been telephoned in the late afternoon. A man's voice, official and careful, asking him to confirm that he was the brother of Daniel Brennan, last known to have lived at an address near the northern docks, and Niall had said yes, though it was nineteen years since he had been able to say with any certainty where Daniel lived or whether he was living at all. There is a body, the voice had said. We would be grateful. And then a series of facts about the morgue and the times of ferries and the necessity of a passport for identification, as though Niall would need to prove who he was before he could be asked to prove who Daniel was.

The lounge was nearly empty. A woman slept across three seats with her arm over her eyes. Two men played cards without speaking, laying the cards down with a precision that suggested they had been doing this on these crossings for years, that the game was a kind of weather they moved through. Niall bought coffee from a machine and sat by a window that gave him only his own reflection and the dark beyond it, and he tried to remember his brother's face.

This was harder than it should have been. He could remember Daniel at twelve, throwing stones at the gulls on the breakwater, the way he had of cocking his whole body before the throw like a held breath. He could remember the back of Daniel's neck, sunburned, the hair growing in a whorl there that their mother used to press her thumb against. But the face at the age the body would now be — forty-three, forty-four — he could not assemble. He had a photograph somewhere of the two of them at a wedding, Daniel already drinking, already half-turned away from the camera as if he had heard something behind him, but the photograph was at home in a drawer and he had not thought to bring it, and now he understood that he had not wanted to bring it. He had wanted to arrive empty.

The last time they had spoken there had been money involved, and a woman, and the small terrible inheritance of their father's house, which neither of them had wanted and both of them had fought over with the ferocity of men fighting over a thing precisely because it is worthless. Daniel had said something then that Niall had carried for nineteen years like a stone in the shoe, a thing you forget for hours at a time and then step down on. You were always the one he watched, Daniel had said. You never even noticed you were being watched. And then Daniel had gone north, to the docks and the cold work there, and the letters had stopped, first from Daniel and then, in a kind of answering silence, from Niall.

The ferry shouldered into the swell and the coffee tilted in its cup. One of the card players said something and the other laughed, a single dry sound, and laid down a card.

Niall thought: I will know him by his hands. Daniel had broken the smallest finger of his left hand at fourteen, falling from the wall behind the church, and it had set badly because they had told no one, had splinted it themselves with a lollipop stick and tape, conspirators in the small house of their childhood, and the finger had healed crooked, bent inward toward the palm at the last joint as though it were always beginning to make a fist. Niall held up his own left hand against the window and looked at the small finger, straight, ordinary, and felt the old guilt move in him that he had been the unbroken one.

He slept badly, sitting up, his head against the cold glass. He dreamed of water coming into a room very slowly, rising past the legs of the furniture, and in the dream he was not afraid, he was only waiting for someone to come and tell him whether the water was the sea or the river, because it seemed to him that everything depended on knowing this, that the two were entirely different things though they looked the same and tasted the same and would drown a man the same way. When he woke the woman who had been sleeping was gone and the card players were gone and the lounge had the abandoned brightness of all places that are lit through the night for no one.

He went out onto the deck. The cold took the breath from him. They were crossing the strait at its widest now and there was nothing on either side, only the black water moving and the faint phosphorescence where the hull cut it, and above, when he tipped his head back, more stars than he had seen in years, the whole reckless spill of them, indifferent and exact. He thought of Daniel under those same stars on the docks all those years, loading and unloading the great ships, and he understood for the first time that he had always imagined his brother somewhere warm, somewhere south and golden, that he had given Daniel that life in his mind as a kind of apology, when in fact Daniel had gone north into the cold, into the dark crossing, had perhaps stood on this very deck on this very ferry, watching this same water, thinking of him.

The thought was unbearable and he held the rail and let it be unbearable.

A crewman came out to check a coil of rope and nodded to him, the small companionship of men awake at the wrong hour, and Niall nodded back. You should be inside, the crewman said. It's no night for the deck. And Niall said he knew, and did not go in.

By dawn the far shore was a grey line and then a hardness on the grey, the long low country of the north coming up out of the sea as if it had been waiting under there the whole time and was only now, reluctantly, allowing itself to be seen. He could make out the cranes of the docks, the gantries, the rust-coloured hulls of ships at anchor, and beyond them the town climbing its hill, the houses pale and crowded together against the cold. He tried to find in it the place his brother had lived. He could not. It was simply a town, like the town he had left, like all the towns on all the cold coasts of the world where men go who do not wish to be found, and he understood that Daniel had chosen it for that, had chosen its anonymity the way Niall had chosen to bring no photograph, the way the whole family had chosen, all their lives, to look at the floor and the wall and the cards rather than at one another.

The ferry came into the harbour slowly, the engines reversing, the water churning brown and white between the hull and the dock, and the gulls came out to meet it crying as gulls had cried over the breakwater of his childhood, the same birds or their grandchildren, and Niall watched them and thought of the stones, of Daniel cocking his body before the throw.

A man was waiting for him at the foot of the gangway, holding a folded newspaper as though it were a sign with Niall's name on it. He was young, younger than Niall had expected, with a kind face that had practised, Niall saw, a particular expression for these occasions, neither grief nor cheer but a level grey thing in between, a held door. Mr Brennan, he said. You had a smooth crossing, I hope. And Niall said the crossing was fine, and they walked together up through the cold morning streets, past the shuttered fronts of the harbour shops, the young man talking quietly the whole way about nothing, about the weather and the ferry and the length of the journey, and Niall understood that the talk was a kindness, was the young man's way of carrying part of the weight, and he was grateful for it though he did not listen to the words.

The morgue was a low building behind the hospital, brick, with a flat roof and high windows that let in light but no view. Inside it was very warm and smelled faintly of something clean laid over something that the cleanness could not entirely cover. A woman in a white coat met them and shook Niall's hand and said his name, the wrong way, with the stress on the wrong syllable, and he did not correct her. There were forms. There was a small room with chairs and a low table where someone had set out a jug of water and three glasses, and Niall sat and drank a glass of water he did not want because it gave his hands something to do.

He understood the body had been pulled from the water four days before, that it had been in the water some time, that there was a question therefore about the face. The woman in the white coat explained this without explaining it, in soft circling sentences, the way the young man had talked, the whole north it seemed conspiring to wrap him in a gauze of gentleness, and Niall said, I'll know him by his hands. They looked at him. He has a broken finger, Niall said. The left hand. The little finger. It set crooked. We were boys. The woman nodded and wrote something down and the young man looked at the floor, and Niall realized that he had given them, for the first time, something true, a fact with a child in it, and the room had changed slightly for the giving of it.

They took him down a corridor. The light here was different, harder, and the warmth fell away as they went so that by the door at the end he was cold again, and glad of it. The woman stopped with her hand on the door and said, You can take as long as you need, and there is no shame in turning away, people do, it is nothing to be ashamed of. And Niall said thank you and meant it and went in.

The room was small and very bright. There was a table and on the table a shape under a sheet, and the smell here was stronger, the clean thing and the other thing beneath it, and Niall stood for a moment just inside the door and made himself breathe. The woman had come in with him and stood by the sheet, waiting, her face composed into the grey neutral kindness, and when Niall nodded she drew the sheet down to the chest.

The face was a face that had been in the water. He made himself look at it. It was swollen and the colour was wrong and the features had the blurred, softened look of something half-erased, a drawing left out in the rain, and he could find in it neither Daniel nor not-Daniel. He looked for the whorl of hair, but the hair was matted and dark with water and told him nothing. He looked at the mouth and the mouth was a stranger's mouth and then for an instant, in the set of it, the slight downward turn at one corner, it was his father's mouth, and then it was no one's. He stood and looked and the room was very quiet and he could hear his own blood in his ears, and he thought, with a clarity that frightened him, that he did not want it to be Daniel and did not want it not to be Daniel, that both were unbearable, that he had crossed the whole dark strait precisely so as to be made to choose between two unbearable things in a bright cold room that smelled of soap.

I need to see the hand, he said.

The woman moved to the side of the table and drew the sheet back from the left arm and laid the hand bare. It was a working man's hand, broad, the knuckles enlarged, the nails broken and dark, and Niall bent over it. The little finger lay straight. He looked at it a long time. It lay perfectly straight beside the others, ordinary, unbroken, the finger of a man who had never fallen from the wall behind the church, who had never been splinted with a lollipop stick by a brother's careful hands in a small house full of secrets, and Niall felt the strait open up inside him, the whole black width of it, and he did not know whether what he felt was grief or its terrible opposite.

It's not him, he said.

The woman did not move. You're certain, she said, not unkindly. Niall looked at the straight finger. He thought of how easily he could have said nothing, could have said yes, it is him, and taken the swollen ruined stranger home across the water and buried him under his father's name and ended it, ended the nineteen years, given the silence a body and a grave so that he could at last go and stand over it and be done. The temptation was enormous. It rose in him like the water in the dream, slow and patient, past the legs of everything.

The finger is wrong, he said. My brother broke this finger. It set crooked. This man's finger is straight.

The woman wrote something down. She drew the sheet back up over the hand and then over the face, gently, smoothing it, and the small bright room was just a room again with a shape in it that belonged to someone else, to some other man who had gone into the water for his own reasons, who had his own brother perhaps crossing some other strait at this moment, and Niall felt for that unknown brother a wash of pity so sudden and total that he had to put his hand on the cold edge of the table.

Outside in the corridor the young man was waiting. He read the answer in Niall's face before Niall spoke and his expression changed, the practised grey kindness giving way for an instant to something more ordinary, something almost like disappointment, as though he had wanted, for Niall's sake, the matter to be closed. I'm sorry you came so far, he said. Niall said it was nothing, that it was a journey he had needed to make though he had not known it. The young man did not understand this and did not ask.

There were more forms, and the woman in the white coat shook his hand again and said his name the wrong way again, and then he was outside in the cold morning, and the day had opened up while he was inside, a hard clear day with a high pale sun, and the cranes of the docks stood black against it.

The ferry back did not leave until evening. He had the whole day. He walked down through the town toward the water without deciding to, his feet carrying him, and he came out onto the long stone of the harbour wall and walked out along it to the end where the lighthouse stood, and there he sat down on the cold stone with the sea on three sides of him and the town behind, and he thought about his brother, who was alive.

For that was what the straight finger meant. Somewhere Daniel was alive, or had been alive four days ago, or a month ago, or a year. The body in the bright room was not him and so the world still held him, the cold world, the docks and the ships and the dark crossings, and Niall sat with this and turned it over and could not decide what it weighed. For nineteen years he had carried his brother as an absence, a clean grief with the edges worn smooth, and he had crossed the strait prepared to convert that absence into a death, into something finished, and instead the strait had handed him his brother back, unfinished, somewhere out there under the same pale sun, an old man now, or nearly, with his crooked finger and his sunburned neck and the whorl of hair their mother used to press her thumb against, and the words he had spoken nineteen years ago, you were always the one he watched, still hanging in the cold air between them, unanswered.

The gulls wheeled over the harbour. A ship was coming in, very slowly, the rust on its hull catching the light, and men moved on the docks to meet it, small dark figures, and Niall watched them and thought that one of them might be his brother. It was not likely. But it was possible. It had become possible again, possibility itself had been handed back to him in the small bright room, and he did not know if he had the courage for it, for the possible, after nineteen years of the safe and finished impossible.

He took out the slip of paper on which the official voice's address had been written, the place near the northern docks where Daniel was last known to have lived. He looked at it a long time. The wind came off the sea and pulled at the paper and he held it tightly. He could go there. He could walk up through the town and find the street and knock on the door and a stranger would answer, or no one, or — and here the water rose again, slow and patient — or his brother would answer, an old man in a doorway, looking out at him with their father's mouth, and neither of them would know what to say after nineteen years, and the silence would stand between them in the doorway like a third brother, the one they had made together out of all the things they had not said.

He sat for a long time. The ship came fully in and the men made it fast and the light moved across the harbour as the sun went over. He thought of the dream, the water coming into the room, and how in the dream everything had depended on knowing whether it was the sea or the river, and he understood now that there was no knowing, that the sea and the river merged the way two lives merge, the way a tale told backwards meets a tale told first, and that you walked into the water either way and it closed over you either way and you could not tell, when it had closed, which of the two had taken you.

When the cold became too much he stood up. His legs were stiff. He folded the slip of paper and put it back in his pocket, and he did not yet know whether he would go to the street near the docks or whether he would walk back to the ferry and cross the dark strait home and let the silence stand, let his brother go on living unmet in the cold north, a possibility he could carry now the way he had carried the absence, smooth-edged, safe. He did not know. He walked back along the harbour wall toward the town with the lighthouse behind him and the gulls crying over the water, and the choice walked with him, unmade, and the strait lay between him and his home, dark even now in the bright afternoon, waiting to be crossed in either direction, and he thought that whichever way he went the water would be the same water, and that this was either the cruelty of it or the mercy, and that he would not know which until long after, if he ever knew at all.

He stopped at the end of the wall where the stone met the road. Ahead of him the road went up into the town, toward the docks, toward the street on the slip of paper. Behind him the wall went out to the lighthouse and the sea. He stood at the place where the two met, the road and the water, where the harbour wall came up out of the strait and became a road that climbed into the lives of strangers, and he understood that he had been standing at this exact place his whole life, at the ford where the thing followed becomes the thing imagined, and that the only question, the only question there had ever been, was which one he would let himself walk into now.

He turned up toward the town. He had not decided to. His feet carried him, as they had carried him down to the water, and he let them, and he climbed the cold bright street with the slip of paper in his pocket and his brother's crooked finger held like a small lit thing in his mind, and behind him the gulls cried over the harbour, and ahead of him the town rose pale against the hill, full of doorways, full of strangers, one of whom, just possibly, was waiting.
