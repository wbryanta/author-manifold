The pump house door had swollen in its frame again, and Daniel had to put his shoulder into it twice before it gave. Inside, the air was close and metallic, smelling of old grease and the iron tang of standing water. He found the valve by feel, the way their father had taught him, and shut it down. The pressure died with a shudder that traveled up through the floorboards and into his boots.

"It's off," he called.

Out in the light, Theo was already on his knees in the dirt at the head of the east row, scraping mud away from the coupling with a flathead screwdriver. He didn't look up. He had taken his shirt off and hung it on the low branch of the first peach tree, and his back was already pinking across the shoulders. Forty-two years old and he still wouldn't wear a hat.

"You're going to burn," Daniel said.

"I've been burning since I was six."

Daniel crouched beside him and looked at the coupling. The PVC had split along a seam, a hairline fracture that had been weeping for who knew how long, softening the ground all around it into a black soup that smelled faintly of rot. The split itself was nothing. A two-dollar fix. But the line ran another four hundred feet from here, buried at a depth their father had chosen forty years ago when the soil was different, and there was no telling how many other places it had gone the same way.

"We've got the fittings in the truck," Theo said. "I bought extra."

"You think it's just here."

"I think we fix what we can see and run the pressure back up and walk the rows and listen. You can hear them when they go. That little hiss." Theo worked the screwdriver under the cracked piece and levered it loose, and a slug of brown water bubbled up into the hole. "Dad used to find them by the trees. You'd see one start to yellow at the top and you'd know there was a break upstream and it wasn't getting its share."

"Half of them are yellow."

"Some of them are yellow."

Daniel stood and looked down the row. The orchard climbed a low grade toward the road, peach and a few stubborn apricot, and in the early light it looked, if you let it, like something still worth saving. The leaves caught the sun. The grass between the rows was tall and going to seed and full of the small frantic movement of insects. You could stand here and not see the dead wood in the older trees, the way the bark had pulled away in long gray ribbons, the limbs that put out no leaf at all and stood among the green ones like the bones of a hand.

"You want me to dig out the next length?" Daniel asked.

"Hand me the cement first. Purple primer's in the blue can."

They worked. The morning was the easy part and they both knew it. There was a rhythm to the two of them when they used their hands, a thing that had survived everything else, and for an hour they didn't talk about anything but the work — pass me that, hold this, no, the other one, easy, easy. Theo cleaned the cut ends of the pipe and swabbed them with the primer that stained his fingers the color of a bruise, and Daniel held the new coupling while the cement set, counting in his head the way their father had, thirty seconds though the can said ten, because their father had never trusted anything to set as fast as it claimed.

When it was done Theo sat back on his heels and wiped his forehead with his wrist and left a smear of purple there, above his eyebrow, and Daniel didn't tell him.

"Buyer called again," Daniel said.

Theo capped the can of primer with more force than it needed. "On a Saturday."

"He's motivated."

"He's a vulture. They're all vultures. They circle around until somebody's too tired to keep the land and then they come down."

"He offered four-twenty."

"It's worth six."

"It's worth six to somebody who's going to put twelve houses on it. It's worth four-twenty to somebody who wants an orchard, and there's nobody who wants an orchard, Theo. There hasn't been anybody who wants an orchard in twenty years."

Theo got up. He picked his way along the row to where the next coupling lay buried and got down again and started digging with the trowel, throwing the wet dirt out behind him in dark clots, and Daniel watched the muscles move under the burning skin of his brother's back and felt the old helplessness rise in him, the sense that he was speaking a language Theo had decided not to understand.

"You don't even live here," Theo said, not turning around. "You live three hundred miles away. You come up twice a year and you stand in the rows and you do the arithmetic. It's all arithmetic to you."

"Somebody has to do the arithmetic."

"Dad never did the arithmetic."

"Dad," Daniel said, "is the reason there's a mortgage on a piece of land that's been in the family since 1911."

Theo stopped digging.

For a moment neither of them said anything. A truck went by on the road above, slowing for the curve, and they both listened to it the way you listen to anything that breaks a silence you've made, hoping it will go on long enough to give you an excuse not to speak.

"That's not fair," Theo said finally.

"I know it isn't."

"He kept it through the freeze in '90. He kept it through the years the price of cling dropped to nothing and the cannery closed and every other grower around here tore his trees out and sold to the highball outfits. He kept it because his father kept it. You want to put a number on that, go ahead, but don't pretend the number's the whole thing."

"I'm not pretending that."

"You're acting like it." Theo found the coupling and began to clear around it, and Daniel saw his hands were shaking a little, the way they did when he was angry, a fine tremor that had been there since they were boys. "You sit at that table with your laptop and your spreadsheet and you've already decided. You decided before you drove up. The whole thing's just — what do you call it — due diligence. You're walking me through the slides."

"I came up to fix the irrigation."

"You came up to get me to say yes."

Daniel didn't answer, because it was true, and they both knew it was true, and there was a kind of mercy in not saying so.

The second coupling hadn't split but the gasket inside it had perished, gone hard and crumbly as old caulk, and when Theo got it apart it came away in flakes on his fingers. He held them up.

"See that? That's age. That's not neglect, that's just age. Everything down here is this old. Dad put it in the year I was born." He let the flakes fall. "We replace the gaskets, we get another ten years out of the mainline."

"Or we hit a winter and the whole thing freezes and cracks in forty places and we spend ten thousand dollars trenching it out."

"You don't know that."

"No. But I know that's how it goes. It always goes like that. One thing fails and you fix it and the fixing shows you the next thing, and you fix that, and meanwhile the trees are getting older and the yield's going down and the price you can get for cling peaches wouldn't cover the diesel to truck them to the —" He stopped. "Theo. I'm not the one killing the orchard. The orchard's dying. I'm just the one saying it out loud."

Theo set the dead gasket on the lip of the hole, carefully, as if it mattered where it went. Then he stood and walked off down the row without a word, between the trees, and Daniel let him go.

He went to the truck and got the water jug and drank, the water already warm and tasting of plastic, and he leaned against the tailgate and looked at the house up at the top of the rise. It needed paint. The porch sagged on the east side where the post had rotted out, and their mother's roses had gone wild and climbed the railing and the trellis and the side of the house itself, throwing out long thorned canes in every direction, beautiful and strangling. She had died in that house. Their father in the hospital, but he'd done his dying in the house first, over two slow years, in the front room with the window that looked down the rows so he could watch the trees leaf out one last spring. Daniel had been there for the end of it. Theo had been there for all of it.

That was the arithmetic that never got onto the spreadsheet. Theo had stayed. Theo had given the land twenty years that Daniel had spent somewhere else, building a life that didn't smell like grease and rot, and now Daniel was the one who held the power of attorney, because their father had trusted the son who left to be reasonable about the thing the son who stayed could never be reasonable about. There was a cruelty in that arrangement that Daniel had only understood after the funeral. His father had known exactly what he was doing. He had set his two sons against each other across a fence line and called it stewardship.

When Theo came back he had his shirt on. He walked up and took the jug and drank and handed it back and the two of them stood at the tailgate looking at the rows.

"Remember the fort," Theo said.

"In the windbreak."

"We about killed ourselves dragging that plywood all the way out there."

"You dropped it on my foot."

"You cried."

"I was nine. And it broke my toe."

"It did not break your toe." Theo almost smiled. "You limped around for a week to get out of stacking."

"It broke my toe, Theo. The little one. It's still crooked." Daniel pulled off his boot and his sock and held up his foot, and there it was, the smallest toe canted off at an angle, and Theo looked at it and laughed, a short surprised laugh that he seemed to catch and pull back in.

"Twenty-five years," he said. "And I'm just finding out."

"You never asked."

"You never said." Theo took the jug again and didn't drink, just held it. "That's us though, isn't it. We don't say."

They worked through the heat of the day. They found a third break where a root had grown around the pipe and squeezed it until it cracked — a peach root, fat as a forearm, the tree above it one of the dying ones, putting all its last strength into the thing that was killing the line that fed it — and Daniel knelt in the mud and sawed through the root with a bow saw while Theo held the pipe steady, and neither of them said anything about the obvious thing, that the tree was strangling its own water and would have to come out, that you could not save the tree and the line both.

By two o'clock they had the mainline charged again and they walked the rows the way Theo wanted, slow, listening. The sun was straight overhead and merciless and the cicadas had started up in the windbreak, that long rising drone that Daniel had not heard in years and that opened in him a door he would rather have kept shut, the smell of these summers, the endless flat afternoons, their father's voice carrying down the rows. They found two more weeping joints and marked them with surveyor's flags, little orange flags that stood up bright among the green, and Daniel counted them, six flags now, six places in four hundred feet, and didn't say what the count meant.

"There," Theo said. He'd stopped. He was looking at a section where the water had surfaced, a wet dark patch spreading between two trees, and you could hear it, that hiss he'd talked about, faint under the cicadas. "You hear it?"

"I hear it."

Theo crouched and put his hand flat on the wet ground as if taking a pulse. "This one's bad. This one's a real break, not a joint." He looked up at Daniel and his face had changed, the anger gone out of it, replaced by something Daniel found harder to look at. "We're going to be at this all summer. You know that. This is just the start of it. We fix these six and there's six more by August."

"Yeah."

"And I can't —" Theo stopped. He stood up and put his hands on his hips and looked off down the rows toward the house. "I can't do it by myself. That's the thing nobody says. Dad couldn't do it by himself at the end either, he just wouldn't admit it. There's two hundred trees and I've got a back that's going and a wife who's stopped asking when I'm coming in for dinner because she knows the answer's the dark, and you live three hundred miles away, and the money I make selling fruit to the two restaurants and the farm stand doesn't cover the water, let alone the mortgage, let alone —" His voice did something then, caught and dropped, and he turned away. "I'm tired, Danny. I'm so tired I can't see straight half the time."

Daniel stood there. In all the months of phone calls, the lawyer's letters, the buyer's offers, the careful reasonable conversations across the kitchen table, Theo had never once said this. He had said the orchard was worth six. He had said the buyer was a vulture. He had said their father had kept it through the freeze of '90. He had said everything except the only true thing, which was that he was a man alone in a field that was bigger than he was, holding on with both hands to something that was pulling him under, and he didn't know how to let go, and he didn't know how to keep holding.

"I didn't know it was like that," Daniel said.

"You didn't ask."

"You didn't say."

Theo laughed, that same short caught laugh. "Yeah. Us."

The water hissed up out of the ground between them. Somewhere up in the windbreak a bird Daniel couldn't name was repeating itself over and over, three notes, three notes, and the heat came up off the dirt in waves you could see.

"I'm not trying to take it from you," Daniel said. "I want you to know that. Whatever you think this is — me coming up here with my arithmetic — I'm not doing it to win. I'm doing it because I'm scared of what happens if we don't. I'm scared the bank takes it and we get nothing and you've broken your back for twenty years for nothing. Four-twenty isn't nothing. Four-twenty pays the mortgage and there's enough left over that you could — I don't know. You could rest. You could do something that doesn't put you in the ground ten years early."

"And then what." Theo wasn't asking. "I sell the land Dad kept through everything, I take the money, I rest. Doing what. Who am I when I'm not the guy who keeps the orchard? I've been the guy who keeps the orchard since I was twenty-two. That's the whole — that's what I am, Danny. You went off and became somebody else and I just stayed and became this. And now you're telling me to sell the only thing that makes me anybody."

And there it was, finally, the real fence line, the one that had nothing to do with the price of cling peaches. Daniel felt it land in him like a stone dropped into deep water, the slow sinking of it, the understanding of what he had been asking his brother to do all these months without ever once knowing he was asking it.

"You're my brother," Daniel said. "That makes you somebody. The orchard doesn't make you somebody. You were somebody before there was a single tree in your name."

"That's an easy thing to say from where you're standing."

"It's not easy. None of this is easy."

Theo crouched down again by the break and started picking at the wet dirt with his fingers, not really digging, just moving it, the way a man does something with his hands when the thing in his chest is too big to hold still through.

"Dad gave you the say-so," Theo said. "You know why he did that?"

"Because he thought I'd be reasonable."

"Because he couldn't do it himself. He couldn't be the one to decide to let it go, so he made it so the decision wasn't his. He gave it to you because you were the one who could stand to do it. That's not him not trusting me, Danny. That's him knowing me. He knew I'd hold on till it killed me. He knew that about me and he loved me and he made sure I wouldn't have to be the one with my hand on the —" He stopped. He pressed the heel of his hand against his mouth and his shoulders went, just once, a single hitch, and then he was still.

Daniel got down in the mud beside him. He didn't touch him. They had never been brothers who touched, their family had not made them that way, and he understood that to reach for Theo now would be to make it about comfort, and this wasn't about comfort, this was about something harder than comfort. So he just got down beside him in the wet ground where the water was coming up around both their knees, ruining their jeans, the cold of it a shock against the heat of the day, and he stayed there.

"We don't have to decide today," Daniel said.

"I'll never decide. That's the thing. I'll never be ready."

"Then maybe I decide. Maybe that's the job he gave me. Maybe I decide so you don't have to carry it."

Theo looked at him. His eyes were red but his face had gone calm in a way that was somehow worse than the crying would have been, a kind of exhausted clearing, like a sky after weather.

"You'd do that," Theo said. "You'd be the one. So that I could hate you instead of hating myself."

"If that's what it takes. Yeah."

Theo turned back to the water. He watched it bubble up out of the broken line, the line their father had laid the year Theo was born, and after a long time he reached down and cupped some of it in his hand and let it run out between his fingers.

"Not today," he said. "We finish the line. I want to finish the line first. I want to see it run one more season the way it's supposed to. The whole thing, no flags, no hissing. Pressure all the way to the back row. Can we do that, at least? Can we give it one more season?"

"Yeah," Daniel said. "We can do that."

"And then in the fall we'll —" Theo couldn't finish it.

"In the fall we'll talk."

"In the fall we'll talk."

It wasn't a yes. Daniel knew it wasn't a yes, that it was only a man buying himself one more turning of the year, one more spring of watching the trees leaf out, the same thing their father had done over and over until the doing of it had run out of him entirely. But it was something. It was the first time in all of it that Theo had said the word fall and meant it as a place where the decision lived, instead of a wall to put his back against.

They got up out of the mud. Their jeans were black to the thigh and the wet of it ran cold down into their boots and they stood there dripping and ridiculous in the burning afternoon, and Theo looked down at the two of them and started to laugh, really laugh this time, and Daniel found that he was laughing too, the both of them standing in the ruined field laughing at nothing, at the mud, at twenty-five years, at a crooked toe and a dead man's careful cruelty and the long hopeless beautiful labor of keeping a thing alive past its time.

"Hand me the saw," Theo said, when it had passed. "This one's a real break. We're going to be here till dark."

"I know."

"You're going to miss your drive."

"I'll leave in the morning."

Theo nodded and knelt back down at the head of the break, and Daniel went to the truck for the fittings, and above them the dying tree threw its small shade across the two of them, and the cicadas went on, and the water ran out of the broken line into the ground, into the roots, into the long slow business of a summer that had not yet decided to end.
