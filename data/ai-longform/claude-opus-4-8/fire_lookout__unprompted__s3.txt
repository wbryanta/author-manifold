The smoke came up white at first, which meant green wood or wet duff, something smoldering rather than running. Della saw it the way she saw everything now, through the long habit of forty summers in the tower: not as an event but as a sentence she was being asked to parse. Color, volume, the lean of the column against the afternoon wind. White meant young. White meant she had time, if time was a thing she still had the right to spend.

It was rising from the Calder drainage. She knew the country the way she knew the inside of her own mouth, every ridge and saddle named in her private cartography, and there was no mistaking the fold of land where the smoke originated. Calder drainage, north fork, the bench above the creek where the lodgepole thinned into a meadow. Margaret's cabin sat in that meadow. Had sat there for nineteen years, since the divorce, since Margaret had taken her settlement and bought forty acres of nothing in particular and built a place to be alone in, which was the one ambition the two sisters had ever fully shared and the one thing they had never managed to do together.

Della lowered the binoculars. Her hands had developed a tremor in the last two years, nothing dramatic, a fine vibration like a struck wire, and she had learned to brace her elbows on the map table to steady a sighting. The Osborne firefinder sat in the center of the room on its pedestal, the brass ring she had turned ten thousand times, and out of reflex she crossed to it and aligned the sights on the column and read the azimuth. One hundred and forty-two degrees. She wrote it in the logbook because writing it was what you did, even now, even though the radio on the shelf had been dead since the second week of June.

She had reported the radio. That was the thing she would want someone to understand later, if there was a later, if there was a someone. She had reported it on the landline the first morning it failed, standing in the supply shed at the base of the tower with the old crank phone that connected to the ranger station thirty miles off, and a young man named Petey or Petersen had told her a tech would come up on the next supply run. The supply run was Thursdays. This was the third Thursday since, and no tech had come, and she had stopped expecting one, because this was the summer they were closing the tower for good and a dead radio in a decommissioned lookout was not a priority for anyone but the woman who slept under it.

So she could not call it in. That was the situation, stated plainly. A smoke in the Calder drainage and no way to put it on the air.

She went down the ladder, which she did slowly now, facing the rungs, her knees registering each step as a separate negotiation. The supply shed smelled of dust and old creosote and the particular mineral coolness of a place the sun never fully reached. She cranked the phone. She held the receiver to her ear and heard nothing, not even the ocean-shell hiss of an open line. She cranked again. Nothing. She pressed the receiver hard against her ear as though she could squeeze sound out of the dead Bakelite, and there was only the smell of her own hand and the far-off complaint of a raven somewhere down the slope.

The phone line ran on poles along the access road, and the access road ran eight miles down to the gate, and somewhere in those eight miles a tree had come down, or a squirrel had chewed through, or the whole works had simply given up the way everything on this mountain was giving up this year, all of it scheduled for removal, the tower and the phone and the woman alike. She set the receiver back on its hook with a care that was almost tender.

Eight miles to the gate. From the gate it was another four to the first house with a working phone, the Henstrom place, unless the Henstroms had sold too, she couldn't remember. Twelve miles, then, on foot, downhill, which her knees would punish her for the entire way and which would take her the better part of the afternoon and into the evening, by which time the smoke would have done whatever it was going to do.

Or she could go to Margaret.

She stood in the shed and let herself think it. The trail to the Calder drainage left the tower on the north side and dropped through the burn of '09, where the snags still stood gray and limbless like the masts of a sunken fleet, and then into the unburned timber, and then to the creek, and then up the far bench to the meadow. Four miles, maybe a little more. Half the distance to a phone, but in the wrong direction, toward the fire instead of toward anyone who could fight it. She would arrive with nothing. No pump, no shovel even, unless she carried one, no authority, no radio, an old woman walking into a drainage that might already be burning to tell her sister a thing her sister could surely see for herself, which was that there was smoke.

But Margaret was sixty-eight and lived alone and did not, so far as Della knew, hear well anymore. Margaret slept hard in the afternoons; she always had, even as a girl, dropping into sleep the way other people dropped things, suddenly and completely. If the fire was small and close and Margaret was asleep, then the distance between a walk and a phone call was the distance between a person and a name in a newspaper.

Della went back up the ladder. She did not decide anything on the ladder. She had found, over a long life, that decisions made themselves while she was busy doing the next necessary thing, and the next necessary thing was to look again, to be certain, because forty years of practice had taught her that the first thing you do with a smoke is doubt it.

She put the binoculars on the column again. It had grown. Not alarmingly, but it had grown, and the white was edging toward gray at the base, which meant the fire had found something drier to eat. There was no flame visible, only the column, leaning east-northeast in the afternoon wind, the wind that came up every day at this hour off the heating valley and that she had cursed and blessed for four decades. East-northeast. She turned and found the meadow on her mental map and saw that the wind, if it held, would carry the fire away from the cabin and up toward the rockslide above the bench, where it would run out of fuel against the talus. If the wind held. The wind did not always hold. In the evening it reversed, came downslope, cool air sliding off the high country into the drainages, and an evening wind would push the fire back across the meadow and into the cabin's lap.

She had perhaps four hours of upslope wind. After that, the fire would turn around and come home.

She thought about her sister's face, which she had not seen in person in eleven years. The last time had been at their mother's funeral, in a town neither of them lived in anymore, and they had stood on opposite sides of the grave like the poles of a magnet that only knew how to repel, and afterward in the parking lot Margaret had said something and Della had said something back, and the somethings had been about the house, the childhood house, who had wanted it and who had let it go and what that wanting and letting had meant, but underneath the house it had been about everything, it had been about the whole long ledger the two of them kept, each in her own hand, each certain the other was the debtor. They had not spoken since. Christmas cards, the first few years, signed with first names only, no message, the card itself the message: I am still here and I have not forgiven you and I want you to know I am still here. Then not even cards.

Della had told people, when she told them anything, that her sister lived in the next drainage and they didn't get along. She had said it lightly, the way you'd mention a knee that acted up in cold weather. She had never said the truth, which was that she could stand at her own north window on a clear morning and see, if not the cabin itself, the smoke of her sister's woodstove rising from the trees, a thin gray thread in the cold air, and that some mornings she watched for it, and that the watching was the closest thing the two of them had to a conversation. Margaret's smoke. Della knew the difference between her sister's woodstove smoke and any other smoke on the mountain. She had spent eleven years learning to read it.

This smoke was not that. This was the other kind. And the irony of it, that after all those mornings of reading her sister's small benign smoke she should now be reading a large malignant one from the same patch of trees, was not lost on Della, who had a sense of humor that had narrowed over the years to a single dry channel but still ran.

She filled a water bottle from the cache. She took the fire shovel from its bracket by the door, the long-handled one, and a folding saw, and she put a few things in the canvas rucksack she used for her infrequent descents — the headlamp, because if this went long she would be walking back in the dark, the first aid kit that was mostly aspirin and gauze gone yellow at the edges, a wool layer because the mountain did not care what month the calendar claimed. She wrote in the logbook. She wrote the time, and the azimuth, and a sentence: Smoke in Calder drainage, radio inoperative, phone line down, proceeding on foot to investigate. She wrote it for the record, the record that no one would read because the tower was closing and the logbooks would go into a box and the box would go into a basement, but she wrote it because she had always written it, and because if something happened to her on the trail she wanted there to be a sentence somewhere in her own hand explaining what she had been doing and why.

Then she stood for a moment in the small square room that had been the better part of her life. The bed in the corner with its iron frame. The propane ring where she cooked. The shelves of paperbacks swollen with damp and read to softness. The window glass on four sides through which she had watched more weather than most people see in ten lifetimes, lightning walking the ridges, rain coming up the valley like a gray wall, the long slow turning of the seasons that out here were not four but a hundred, each day its own small season. She had thought she would feel something more, this final summer, some grand swell of valediction. Mostly she had felt the radio's silence and the ache in her knees and a low steady astonishment that it was all really ending, that the Forest Service in its wisdom had decided that satellites and aircraft could do what she did, and that perhaps they could, and that the doing of it had never really been the point of her being here anyway.

She went down the ladder for the last time that day and started north on the trail.

The burn of '09 was hot in the early afternoon, the gray snags offering no shade, the ground between them grown up in fireweed and young aspen, and she walked through it at the pace her body allowed, which was slower than her will. She had fought the '09 fire, or rather she had watched it and reported it and then watched it taken away from her by the crews and the air tankers, and she had stood in the tower while it ran up this very slope and she had thought, that summer, that the tower would burn and she with it, and it had not, the fire had split around the rocky knob the tower stood on and rejoined itself above and left her on her little island of green, and she had felt, when it was over, an emotion she had never been able to name, something like having been spared and something like having been overlooked.

She thought about Margaret as she walked because there was nothing else to do but walk and think, and the thinking, once started, would not be stopped. She thought about how they had been as children, which was inseparable, the two of them a single organism with two heads, sharing a bed in the cold farmhouse and whispering after the lamp was out, inventing countries, assigning each other roles in elaborate ongoing dramas that ran for whole summers. Margaret was the older by two years and so Margaret had been the queen, the captain, the doctor, and Della the loyal subject, the first mate, the nurse, and Della had not minded, had in fact loved the arrangement, because to be needed by Margaret was the central fact of her early life and the source of all its warmth.

And then they had grown up and the needing had not survived the growing. That was all. There was no single wound, no betrayal you could point to and say there, that is where it broke, though both of them had spent years trying to locate exactly such a wound, to give the thing a clean edge. The truth was duller and worse. They had simply become two people who could not be in a room together without each becoming the worst version of herself, defensive, scorekeeping, cruel in the offhand way only a sister can be cruel, knowing exactly where the soft places are because she was there when they formed. Their mother had refereed for decades and then their mother had died and there was no referee and the game had simply stopped, both of them walking off the field in opposite directions, and the silence that followed had been, Della could admit now, walking through the hot snags with the shovel on her shoulder, a relief. A terrible relief. She had been so tired of the game.

But she had kept watching for the smoke. Every cold morning. She had never stopped doing that.

She came down out of the burn into the green timber and the temperature dropped ten degrees and the trail softened underfoot with needle duff and she could smell, now, faintly, the fire. Not woodstove smoke. The broader, wilder smell of a forest beginning to consume itself. She stopped and listened and could not hear it yet, no roar, no crackle, which was good, which meant it was still small, still creeping. She went on, faster now, ignoring her knees, down toward the creek.

The creek was low, late-summer low, and she crossed it on the rocks she knew and started up the far bench, and now the smell was strong and she could see, through the trees, the haze of it hanging in the timber ahead, the light gone strange and orange. She came up onto the bench and into the lower edge of the meadow and saw the fire.

It was burning in the dry grass at the meadow's upper end and in the slash and deadfall along the timber edge, a low front of flame perhaps a hundred yards wide, creeping and flaring, sending up the column she had seen from twenty miles off as a clean white sentence and that was here, in person, a chaos of smoke and heat and small fierce sounds. The upslope wind was holding, pushing the fire up toward the talus, away from the cabin, which stood at the lower end of the meadow with its metal roof and its cleared yard, untouched, for now, the fire eating its way uphill and away. For now.

But the evening would come, and the wind would turn.

The cabin's chimney showed no smoke. Of course it didn't; it was August, no one would have a fire going. But the windows were dark and the truck was parked in the yard, the old green truck Margaret had driven to their mother's funeral, and there was no sign of Margaret, no figure moving, no sense of a person awake and aware in a landscape that was on fire a hundred yards from her door.

Della crossed the yard. Her heart was doing something it should not, a stumbling, and she made herself slow, made herself breathe, and she climbed the two steps to the porch and she knocked on the door of her sister's house for the first time in nineteen years, since before the house existed, since before the meadow was anything but a place the two of them had walked once, long ago, as girls, and decided was the most beautiful place in the world.

There was no answer. She knocked again, harder, and called her sister's name, and her voice came out cracked and strange, the name unfamiliar in her own mouth. "Margaret." And then again, louder, almost a shout, the shout of the whole eleven years, "Margaret."

The door opened.

Her sister stood there in a flannel shirt and reading glasses pushed up into white hair, a book in her hand with her finger still marking the place, and her face when she saw who it was went through a sequence Della would think about for the rest of her life, however long or short that proved to be: blankness first, the not-recognizing, the way you don't recognize a thing too far out of its expected context; then recognition, the features rearranging, the eyes widening; then a flash of something that was almost joy, unguarded, instant, the face of the girl who had shared a bed in a cold farmhouse, present for less than a second before the adult arrived and shuttered it; and then wariness, the old defensive set of the jaw, the scorekeeper coming to the window.

"There's a fire," Della said, before any of the other things could be said, before the ledger could be opened. "In your meadow. The wind's pushing it uphill now but it'll turn this evening and come back down. I couldn't call it in. The radio's dead and the phone line's down. I came over the ridge."

Margaret looked at her for a moment longer, and then she looked past her, at the meadow, at the smoke, and Della watched her sister's mind do the thing both their minds did, the assessment, the reading of the land, because whatever else they were they were both their father's daughters and their father had taught them both to read a fire. Margaret took it in. The width of the front, the lean of the smoke, the distance, the wind. She set the book down on the table just inside the door, spine up, still marking her place, a gesture so ordinary and so like their mother that Della felt something turn over in her chest.

"You walked over the ridge," Margaret said. It was not quite a question.

"Yes."

"With your knees."

So Margaret knew about her knees. Della did not know how Margaret knew about her knees. Some thread of news, some mutual acquaintance, some watching of her own across the divide that Della had not known about. "With my knees," Della said.

Margaret nodded slowly. She looked at the shovel in Della's hand and the saw and the rucksack, and something in her face shifted again, and this time it did not shutter, this time it held, a kind of rueful exhaustion that Della recognized because she felt it too, the exhaustion of two old women who had spent the only sisterhood they would ever get on a quarrel neither of them could any longer fully reconstruct.

"I've got a pump," Margaret said. "Down at the creek. Honda, runs good. And a hundred feet of hose, and another hundred in the shed. And two more shovels and a pulaski." She said it the way you'd lay down cards, evenly, professionally, the fire suddenly the only subject, the eleven years set aside not because they were resolved but because there was no time, because the wind would turn this evening and there was work to do before it did. "If we wet down the grass between the front and the house, and cut a line along the lower timber edge, we might hold it when it comes back down. Two of us might."

"Two of us might," Della said.

Margaret stepped back from the door to let her in, into the small dim room that smelled of woodsmoke and coffee and old paper, her sister's life, the cabin Della had watched from twenty miles off every cold morning for eleven years without ever once setting foot inside. And Della stepped across the threshold, and for a moment the two of them stood close together in the narrow space, closer than they had been since the graveside, close enough that Della could see the broken vessels in her sister's cheeks and the particular brown of her eyes that was the same brown as Della's own eyes, their mother's brown, and neither of them said any of the things, the apology or the accusation, the question or the answer, the whole unspeakable freight of it. There was no time. The wind would turn this evening.

"The pump's heavy," Margaret said. "We'll carry it up together."

"All right," Della said.

And they went out into the smoke to save the house.
