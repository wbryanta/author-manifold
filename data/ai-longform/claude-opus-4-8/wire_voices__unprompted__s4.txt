The board lit the way it always did at six minutes past eight on a Sunday, the small amber bulb under the jack for the Aldous, Nebraska exchange glowing up at her like something patient that had been waiting all week to be noticed. Marguerite knew it before the light finished its first pulse. She knew it the way you know a knock at your own door, the particular weight of a familiar hand. She let her own hand hover a half-second over the cord, not because there was any need to wait but because she liked the small ceremony of it, the inhale before the dive.

"Number, please."

"Aldous four, two-two-one." A man's voice, gravel softened at the edges from use, from saying these same numbers across the same wire every Sunday for the better part of two years. "Calling Sacramento."

"One moment, please." She said it the way she always said it to him, a touch slower than she said it to strangers, and she felt the small private foolishness of that and did not stop herself. She pulled the cord, set the plug, and waited for the distant ringing to begin its long travel west across the dark middle of the country, across the wheat that she imagined lay flat and pale under the Nebraska night, across mountains she had never seen, to a city by a river she had also never seen, where a woman named Eleanor would be sitting by her telephone with her hand already reaching.

The woman picked up on the second ring, as she always did. "Hello? Walter?"

"I'm here," he said. "I'm here, Ellie."

"Go ahead," Marguerite said, the way the procedure required, and then she did the thing she was not supposed to do, the thing she had been doing for the better part of those two years. She did not close her key all the way. She left it cracked the width of a held breath, so that the line stayed open into her headset, low, a third presence on the wire who said nothing and was never named.

It had started by accident. That was how she explained it to herself on the nights she felt she needed explaining-to, lying in her bed above the bakery on Ferrand Street with the smell of tomorrow's bread coming up through the floor. The first time she had simply forgotten to release the key, distracted by a supervisor passing behind her, and by the time she noticed, Walter had been saying something about a calf that had been born wrong-legged and had to be put down, and the way he said it, the plain grief of it laid down flat without decoration, had held her hand still on the key. She had told herself she would release it after the next sentence. She had not. She had listened to a man in Nebraska tell a woman in California about a dead calf, and she had understood from the particular tenderness in the woman's silence that the calf was not really the subject, that nothing they said was ever really the subject, that the subject was always and only the fact of the other one being there, alive, on the far end of a wire, breathing.

She had never learned the whole of it. That was the strange intimacy of her position, that she knew the shape of a love the way you know a country from its coastline, tracing the edges without ever walking the interior. She knew that Walter had a farm and a brother who drank and a mother who was failing slow. She knew that Eleanor had come to Sacramento for reasons that were spoken of only sideways, that there had been a husband once, mentioned the way you mention weather that has passed, and that she worked now in the office of a dry goods concern and did not like the man who ran it. She knew that they had grown up two miles apart outside Aldous and had loved each other at seventeen and that something had come between them that was never said into the wire, some failure that Marguerite suspected belonged to Walter, because of the way he sometimes apologized for things that had no name.

"You sound tired," Eleanor said this Sunday.

"Cutting all week," Walter said. "We got it in ahead of the rain, though. Whole north field."

"That's good. That's good, Walter."

"How's your knee."

"Oh, it's a knee. It does what knees do."

There was a silence then, and Marguerite, twenty-six years old and unmarried and living above a bakery, leaned the smallest degree closer to the board, as if proximity to the apparatus could bring her nearer to the thing itself. She had a long face that she did not think much of and brown hair she pinned up for work, and she had once been engaged to a man named Donald who sold insurance and who had told her, gently, that she was hard to reach. *Hard to reach.* She had thought about that phrase for years, she who spent her days reaching across a continent for other people, plugging the cords that let voices find each other. She had wondered if it was true, and if it was true, whether these two voices on Sunday nights were her way of being near a thing she could not seem to make for herself.

"I been thinking," Walter said.

"Have you."

"Don't say it like that."

"Like what."

"Like you already know what I'm going to say and you've already decided against it."

Eleanor laughed, but it was a soft laugh, not unkind. "I don't know what you're going to say, Walter. I never do. That's the trouble with you. Forty years I've known you and you still surprise me, and a man my age shouldn't be surprising anybody."

"You're not that old."

"I'm old enough that surprising's a luxury."

Marguerite closed her eyes. The room around her hummed with the other operators, the murmured numbers, the click and pull of cords, the supervisor's heels on the linoleum at the far end, but all of that was a country she had left, and she was here, on the wire, in the dark between two states.

"I been thinking," Walter said again, "that there's not so much keeping me here as I always told myself there was."

"Your mother."

"My mother doesn't know me from the postman half the time now. And the half she does, she'd rather it was the postman."

"Don't."

"It's the truth, Ellie. I'm not saying it to be hard."

"And the farm."

"Royce could take the farm. Royce wants the farm. Royce has wanted the farm since we were boys, he just doesn't know how to want a thing without being angry that he doesn't have it yet."

There was a silence on the line that Marguerite had learned to read. It was not an empty silence. It was a silence with current in it, like the held breath before a person steps off a high place or steps back from it, and you cannot tell which from the held breath alone.

"You're saying you'd come out here," Eleanor said finally.

"I'm saying I've got money put by. I'm saying I'm fifty-eight years old and I've spent forty of those years two miles from a house you don't live in anymore, looking at it. I'm saying that's a long time to look at an empty house."

"Walter."

"Just tell me to stop and I'll stop. You always could. One word from you and I'll go back to talking about the weather and the price of feed and we'll go on the way we've gone on, and it's not nothing, Ellie, I want you to know I don't think it's nothing, these Sundays. They're the spine of my week. But I'm asking if maybe there could be more spine than a Sunday."

Marguerite was not breathing. She was aware that she was not breathing, and she made herself breathe, slowly, through her mouth, so that no sound would carry. She had no right to this. She had less than no right. And yet she had carried these two for two years across the dark, she had been the bridge their voices walked across, and she felt, absurdly, that she had earned some small standing on it, that she was permitted to want this for them the way you are permitted to want the ending of a book you have read so long it has become a thing you live inside.

"I have to think," Eleanor said.

"All right."

"I'm not saying no."

"I know you're not. You'd have said it by now. You never could stand to leave a hurt sitting."

"That's not fair, Walter."

"No," he said. "Probably it's not. I've never once in my life been fair to you. That's its own kind of love, I suppose, the unfair kind, the kind where you spend your whole life owing somebody and never paying and never quite leaving either."

The supervisor's heels were coming nearer. Marguerite opened her eyes.

"Next Sunday," Eleanor said. "I'll tell you next Sunday. Give me the week."

"I'll give you the week," Walter said. "I'd give you a good deal more than that."

"I know you would. That's the trouble. You'd give me everything except the years I wanted it."

There was a sound then that Marguerite could not name, a small wet sound, and she understood that one of them was crying, and she could not tell which, and the not-knowing felt like a kindness she had not earned, a privacy she had stumbled into the edge of and ought to back away from.

"Three minutes," Marguerite said, into the line, because it was true and because the supervisor was four feet away, and she heard them both startle the smallest degree, having forgotten as they always forgot that there was a third on the wire, that the bridge had ears.

"We'll say goodnight then," Walter said, his voice gone formal, gathering itself.

"Goodnight, Walter."

"Goodnight, Ellie. Next Sunday."

"Next Sunday."

Marguerite pulled the cords. The amber light died. The supervisor passed behind her, smelling of the violet pastilles she sucked, and said nothing, and moved on, and Marguerite sat with her hands in her lap for a moment that the board did not allow her, before the next light bloomed and she said, "Number, please," to a stranger.

She thought about them all week. This was not new; she always thought about them; but the week between that Sunday and the next had a different quality, a held quality, the quality of the line itself in the silence after Walter had asked his question. She found herself doing sums in her head while she worked. Fifty-eight and what would Eleanor be, near it, fifty-five, fifty-six. Years they had left, if they were lucky, if the knee and the failing mother and the brother who wanted the farm all resolved themselves into the simple arithmetic of two people in one city. She thought about Sacramento, which she pictured wrongly, she was sure, as a place of white light and orange trees, and she thought about a man arriving at a train station there with a single case and his hat in his hand, and a woman with a bad knee waiting on the platform, and she found that the picture made her throat tight in a way that embarrassed her, alone in her room above the bakery, weeping over two people she had never seen for a reunion that had not happened and might not.

She thought, too, about Donald, whom she had not thought of in some time. *Hard to reach.* She wondered if the reason she had let that key stay cracked for two years was that she had needed to know it could be done, the reaching, that two people could be two thousand miles apart and find each other across the dark, that distance was not the thing that kept people from each other, that the thing was something else, something interior, some failure of nerve that Walter at fifty-eight had finally, maybe, set down. She wondered if she had been waiting two years to learn whether a person could change at the last, whether the unfair kind of love could be made fair before the end, or whether some things simply went on the way they had gone on, Sunday after Sunday, the spine of a week and never more.

On Wednesday she nearly told the girl who shared her boardinghouse table, a thin cheerful operator named Patsy who worked the day shift, about Walter and Eleanor. She got as far as *there are these two people I connect* before she understood what she would be confessing, the cracked key, the listening, the long theft of other people's tenderness, and she turned it into something else, a complaint about a difficult party who could never hear the operator, and Patsy laughed and said they were the worst, the deaf ones, and Marguerite agreed that they were the worst and ate her soup and said nothing more.

Sunday came the way Sundays come, slow and then all at once. She was on the board by half-past seven, earlier than her shift required, because she could not have borne to be anywhere else, and she watched the Aldous exchange jack the way a person watches a horizon for a sail. Six minutes past eight had been the hour for two years. It was a thing she could have set a clock by, had set her heart by, the small amber faithfulness of it.

Six minutes past eight came and went.

She told herself there were a hundred reasons. A man cutting all week might be cutting still. A storm might have taken a line down somewhere in the dark middle of the country. A mother might have taken a turn. There were a hundred reasons and she counted them all, and her hands worked the board for the strangers who needed it, the boys calling sweethearts and the sweethearts calling home and the long sad calls that came in the late hours that she had learned not to wonder about, and the whole time some animal part of her kept its eye on the one dead jack, the one light that did not come.

At twelve minutes past eight a light came up two jacks over and her heart leapt up wrongly before she saw it was the wrong exchange, and she connected a man in Iowa to his daughter and did not crack the key, did not want to, wanted only the one wire, the one she could not have.

It was nineteen minutes past eight when the Aldous light finally bloomed, and Marguerite's hand was on the cord before the bulb had finished its first pulse, and she said, "Number, please," and heard her own voice come out steady and was distantly proud of it.

But it was not Walter.

"I need to reach a party in Sacramento," a woman said. The voice was younger than Eleanor's, flatter, a voice with the farm still in it but none of the years. "I don't know how you do this. I've never done this. My uncle, he had a number he called, I found it written down by his telephone, it just says Ellie and a number, Sacramento. I don't know who that is. I thought I should call her. I thought somebody should tell her."

Marguerite's hands had gone still on the board. The room hummed around her, the other girls, the heels, the violet pastilles, the whole apparatus of connection grinding on, and she sat at the center of it gone perfectly cold.

"Tell her what," Marguerite said, and it was not in any procedure, there was no script for it, and she did not care.

"That he passed," the woman said. "My uncle Walter. Thursday morning. His heart, the doctor said, quick, he wouldn't have known much. I'm his niece, I'm Royce's girl, I'm out here seeing to things. And I found this number and it just says Ellie and I thought, well, somebody should know. He didn't have a wife. He didn't have much of anybody, truth told, just us, and we weren't—we weren't close, exactly, my daddy and him didn't—" She stopped. "I'm sorry. You're just the operator. You don't need all this."

"It's all right," Marguerite said.

"Can you connect me? To the Sacramento party?"

Marguerite looked at the jack. She looked at it for a long time, long enough that the woman said *hello? are you there?* into the line, and Marguerite said, "I'm here." She thought of Eleanor in Sacramento with her bad knee, sitting by her telephone, waiting on the second ring that she would answer the way she always answered, *Hello? Walter?*—the name already in her mouth, the hope already in her hand. She thought of that name landing in the room and finding no one there to catch it. She thought: I could do this. I could put this stranger through and let her say it, the niece who wasn't close, exactly, who would say it the way she had just said it, flat and farmed and kind in the way of people doing a duty, *he passed, his heart, Thursday morning*, and Eleanor would hear it from a voice she had never heard, a girl she had never met, the news of the end of forty years delivered by a stranger across two thousand miles of dark.

She thought of the week. *Give me the week,* Eleanor had said. *I'll tell you next Sunday.* And the week had been given to her, and at the end of it she had her answer ready, whatever it was, yes or no, the leaving of the farm or the going-on of the Sundays, and she did not yet know—Eleanor did not yet know—that the question had been taken off the table by a heart that stopped on a Thursday morning in a field that had been cut ahead of the rain. She had her answer in her mouth and there was no longer anyone to give it to.

And Marguerite understood, sitting at the board, that this was the only thing left that she could do for them. Not connect the call. Not put the stranger through to break the thing apart with a few flat true words. She could let Eleanor have her Sunday. She could let her sit by the telephone at six minutes past eight, and wait, and have the light not come, and draw from that silence whatever she drew—and she would draw the truth, Marguerite knew, in time; she would understand what an unanswered Sunday meant; but she would understand it slowly, in her own time, in the privacy of her own knee and her own kitchen, and not have it handed to her by a stranger's voice, and she could keep, for one more hour, the possibility that the answer she had ready was an answer that mattered.

It was not much. It was the smallest mercy in the world and it was a lie of omission and Marguerite had no right to any of it.

"I'm sorry," Marguerite said. "I'm not able to complete that call."

"What?"

"The party in Sacramento. I'm not able to reach them. The line—" and here she did the thing she had done with the key for two years, the small unsanctioned thing, "the line is out of service."

"Out of service," the woman repeated.

"Yes."

"Will it come back? Should I try again?"

Marguerite closed her eyes. "I couldn't say," she said. "I'm sorry. Sometimes they come back. You might write," she said, "if you have the number, you might find the address and write. A letter might be the kinder way. For news like this. A letter she could—she could take in slow."

There was a silence on the line, and it was not the silence she knew, not the silence with the current in it, but it was a silence in which a person was thinking, and Marguerite let it be.

"Maybe you're right," the niece said at last. "Maybe a letter's better. I wasn't looking forward to it, telling a stranger over the telephone. That's an awful thing to do to somebody, isn't it. Just a voice out of nowhere telling you the worst thing."

"Yes," Marguerite said. "It is."

"A letter then. I'll write her a letter. Thank you. You've been—" the woman seemed to search for it, "you've been kind. That's funny to say to an operator. But you have."

"Goodnight," Marguerite said.

"Goodnight."

She pulled the cord. The amber light died.

Somewhere two thousand miles to the west, in a city of white light and orange trees that did not look the way she pictured it, a woman with a bad knee was sitting by a telephone, waiting for a ring that was not going to come, with her answer ready in her mouth. Marguerite would never know what the answer had been. She would never know if Eleanor had decided yes, come, come and let us have more spine than a Sunday, or whether she had decided no, let us go on as we have gone on, it is not nothing, these Sundays. She would carry the not-knowing the way she had carried everything else about them, from the edges, never the interior, a country known only by its coastline.

The next light bloomed on the board. A stranger, an exchange she did not know.

"Number, please," Marguerite said, and reached across the dark for two people she would never meet, and put them through, and closed her key all the way, the way the procedure required, the way she would close it from now on, and gave them their privacy, and let them go.
