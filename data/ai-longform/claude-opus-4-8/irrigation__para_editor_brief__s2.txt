The pipe had split along a seam, and the water came up out of the ground in a slow welling that darkened the dirt in a circle the size of a wagon wheel. Daniel found it first, walking the line in the gray before the sun cleared the eastern ridge, and he stood looking at the mud as if it had done something to him personally.

"Carl," he called toward the house. Then louder, because Carl slept like the dead and always had. "Carl. We got a break."

He heard the screen door slap. His brother came across the yard in unlaced boots, a coffee cup in one hand, his hair pressed flat on one side. At forty-six Carl had gone soft in the belly the way their father never had, but the arms were still there, the forearms thick as fence posts, and Daniel felt the old reflexive thing, that mix of resentment and reliance, watching him come.

"Where," Carl said.

Daniel pointed. Carl crouched at the edge of the wet circle and put two fingers into the mud and worked them down until he found the pipe, then traced its length until he reached the place where the water pulsed up between his knuckles.

"Seam," he said. "Whole length of it's probably going. This line's older than you."

"It's older than both of us."

"That's what I said." Carl stood and wiped his fingers on his jeans and drank his coffee, surveying the orchard the way a man surveys a thing he is preparing to be done with. The trees ran in their rows up the slope, four hundred of them, apples mostly, some pears at the north end where the frost pooled. A third of them were dead or dying, the limbs gone black and brittle, and even the living ones had a starved look, the leaves curling early though it was only June. Their father had planted the oldest of them in nineteen sixty-one. He had been dead two years now and the trees were dying after him as if out of loyalty.

"We'll need to dig it out," Daniel said. "Cut the bad section, sleeve in new pipe. I got couplers in the barn from last time."

"Last time was the well line."

"I got couplers."

Carl finished the coffee and turned the cup upside down and shook the dregs onto the mud. "You know what new pipe costs. You know what a man with a backhoe costs to come trench this whole thing, which is what it needs. You're out here patching a body that's already dead, Danny."

"Get the shovels," Daniel said.

They dug. The sun came over the ridge and the morning cool burned off fast and by eight o'clock the sweat was running down the gully of Daniel's spine. They worked from either end toward the break, prying up the dirt, and the soil was good here, dark and friable, the one thing the place had always had going for it. Carl dug the way he did everything, in hard bursts followed by leaning on the shovel handle and talking, and Daniel dug steadily and said nothing, and between them the trench lengthened.

"Hollis came by again," Carl said. "Tuesday."

"I figured he would."

"He's at four-twenty an acre now. That's up from three-eighty in the spring. He wants the whole parcel, the house too, he said he'd let Mom stay in the house long as she—" Carl stopped. "Long as she's around."

"Mighty kind of him."

"It is kind, actually. He doesn't have to offer that."

"Hollis doesn't do kind." Daniel threw a shovelful clear of the trench. "Hollis does math. He wants the water rights and he wants the road frontage and the house is in his way, so he lets her keep it because moving a dying old woman makes him look like the kind of man who'd move a dying old woman, and Hollis cares what people think because that's how he gets the next farm."

"She's not dying."

Daniel looked up. Carl was looking off down the rows.

"I didn't mean it like that," Daniel said.

"She's not, though."

"No."

But she was, in the slow way, the way that takes a decade and announces itself first as a thing you can almost ignore, a misplaced name, a pot left on the burner. Their mother was seventy-four and still made breakfast every morning, and twice this spring Daniel had found the burner going under an empty pan and once she had called him Walter, which was their father's name, and had not seemed to know she'd done it.

They reached the break by ten. Carl went to the barn and came back with the hacksaw and the couplers and a length of new pipe that Daniel had in fact been right about, and they cut out the split section, the water gone now, Daniel having shut the valve up at the head ditch. The cut ends of the old pipe were thin as paper where the years had worked at them from the inside.

"Look at that," Carl said, holding up the cut piece. "You could push your thumb through it. Whole line's like this. We fix this seam, it'll blow out forty feet up next week."

"Then we'll fix it next week."

"Listen to yourself."

"You listen." Daniel set down the saw. "You want to talk, let's talk. But I'm not going to stand here and let you walk me toward yes one little reasonable step at a time, which is what you've been doing since February. Hollis at three-eighty, Hollis at four-twenty, the pipe's bad, Mom's bad, the trees are bad. You line up the bad things in a row and you call it being practical. I see what you're doing. I've seen you do it my whole life."

Carl set the pipe down too, carefully, which was worse than if he'd thrown it. "And what is it I'm doing, Danny. Tell me. Since you see it so clear."

"You're leaving. You been leaving for thirty years. You left at eighteen and you came back twice a year for the holidays and you let me be the one who—" Daniel stopped himself. The water had pooled in the bottom of the trench, a brown skin of it, and he watched a water strider, impossibly, work its way across the surface, here in this hole they'd dug, miles from any standing water. "You don't get to come back now and be the practical one. You weren't here."

"No," Carl said. "I wasn't. You're right about that."

The agreement took the wind out of him. Daniel had been braced for the fight, the old shapes of it, and he didn't know what to do with a brother who simply said *you're right.*

"I wasn't here," Carl said again. "I went to Spokane and I sold farm equipment to men who were losing farms exactly like this one, and I watched it happen, Danny, hundreds of times, the exact same thing. A man inherits a place his daddy built and the place doesn't pay anymore, hasn't paid in years, the world moved and the place didn't, and the man holds on because holding on feels like loving his daddy and selling feels like killing him. And every one of those men, every single one, held on two years too long and three years too long and then the bank took it and they got nothing. Not a dime. They could've sold standing up and walked away with money and instead they got walked off by a man with a clipboard. I watched that a hundred times. So don't tell me I don't know this place because I wasn't here. I know exactly what this place is. I've seen its future a hundred times over."

Daniel crouched and put his hand in the trench water and felt the cold of it, the well-cold that the sun never reached. "We're not in debt."

"Not yet."

"We owe nothing. Dad paid it off in 'eighty-eight. I was here. I remember the dinner he made of it."

"And what's it worth, owing nothing on a thing that loses money every year? You're not in debt, you're just bleeding out slow enough to call it living."

Daniel didn't answer. He fitted the first coupler over the cut end of the old pipe and tightened the clamp, and his hands knew the work and did it without him, which left his mind free, which was the trouble with this kind of labor. He thought about the dinner in 'eighty-eight. He'd been fifteen. Their father had bought a steak for each of them, four steaks, an extravagance that had felt almost frightening, and he'd cooked them himself, badly, gray all the way through, and they'd eaten them like a sacrament. *We own it now,* their father had said. *Free and clear. Nobody can take it.* And Carl, who was already most of the way out the door, who was seventeen and had a girl in town and a plan, Carl had said, *Take it where, Dad?* and their father had not understood the question and Carl had not explained it and Daniel, fifteen, had understood it perfectly and hated his brother for it.

"You're quiet," Carl said.

"I'm working."

They sleeved the new pipe in. It took both of them, Daniel holding the alignment while Carl seated the couplers, and there was a stretch of maybe twenty minutes where they didn't talk because the work didn't allow it, where they were just two bodies doing a thing that required two bodies, and Daniel felt the old ease come back into it, the thing that had no words, the rhythm they'd had as boys hauling hay or stretching wire, when their father was alive and the orchard paid and the future was a thing you didn't have to think about because it would obviously just be more of the present.

When it was seated, Carl sat back on his heels and they both looked at the join.

"That'll hold," Carl said.

"That'll hold."

"For a while."

"For a while is all anything does."

Carl laughed, surprised. "Listen to you. That almost sounded like wisdom."

"I have my moments."

They didn't fill the trench yet; they'd run the water first and check the join under pressure. Daniel walked up to the head ditch and opened the valve and walked back down the line, watching the ground, and the water came down the pipe and through the new section and the new section held, dry as a bone, and the brown pool in the trench bottom was just the old water, nothing new added to it.

"There," Daniel said.

"There," Carl agreed. He was sitting in the dirt now with his back against an apple tree, one of the dead ones, the bark gone gray and shedding. He had a way of leaning against things that their father had hated, *stand up straight, you're not furniture,* and watching him lean Daniel felt their father so strongly it was like a hand on the back of his neck.

"I'll get the sandwiches," Daniel said. "Mom made them this morning. Before."

"Before what?"

"Before she forgot she made them and made them again. There's eight sandwiches in there, Carl. Same sandwich. Ham and the yellow mustard, cut on the diagonal. Eight of them."

Carl was quiet. "I didn't know it was that."

"It's that."

"How long?"

"A while." Daniel looked up the rows toward the house, white and small against the green of the hill, the one thing on the property that didn't look like it was dying. "She has good days. She had a good week in May, you'd have thought there was nothing wrong. Then she called the propane company to report that someone had stolen the stove. The stove was right there. She was standing in front of it."

Carl put the heels of his hands against his eyes. He was not a man who cried, or Daniel had never seen it, and he didn't cry now, but he sat with his hands over his eyes for a long moment and when he took them away his face was the same but older.

"That's why," Carl said.

"That's why what."

"That's why you won't sell. It's not the trees. You don't give a damn about the trees, half of them are dead and the other half might as well be. It's her. You sell the orchard, you're admitting the thing that's coming. Long as you're out here fixing pipe and pruning deadwood and pretending this is a going concern, you can pretend she's just having a rough patch. The place dying and her dying, you've got them tangled up together, and if you let go of one—"

"Don't," Daniel said.

"—you have to let go of the other."

"I said don't."

But it was said. It sat there in the June heat between them, true the way only a cruel thing said by someone who loves you can be true. Daniel went up to the house and got the sandwiches, four of them, leaving four in the refrigerator for the supper their mother would not remember she'd prepared the makings of, and he checked the burners, all off, and looked in on her where she sat in the front room with the television going and a magazine open on her lap to a page she was not reading, and she looked up at him and smiled and for one terrible held breath he didn't know if she knew him.

"Danny," she said. "You're filthy. What've you been into?"

"Pipe broke. Out on the east line."

"Your father will want to know."

He stood in the doorway. The television laughed at something. "I'll tell him," Daniel said.

"He's down at the Grange till four. Tell him when he's back."

"I will, Mom."

He brought the sandwiches down and gave Carl two and they ate sitting against the dead tree, in its meager shade, looking down the slope at the water moving in the ditches now, silver in the noon light, threading down between the rows to the trees that could still use it. The ham was good. The mustard was the cheap yellow kind they'd grown up on and Daniel had never been able to make himself buy a better one.

"She thinks Dad's at the Grange," Daniel said. "Told me to tell him about the pipe when he gets back at four."

Carl chewed slowly. "What do you say. When she does that."

"I say I'll tell him."

"You don't correct her."

"What's the point. She'd just have to find out he's dead all over again, and she takes it new every time, it's like the first time every time. You want to do that to her twice a day?" Daniel shook his head. "No. I tell her I'll tell him. She's happy. The pipe's getting fixed and Walter's at the Grange and supper's the ham sandwiches, which she made, and the world's in order. I let her have it."

Carl looked at him for a while. "When did you get like this."

"Like what."

"Kind. You weren't kind. You were a mean little kid, you used to—" Carl smiled. "You bit me. You remember that? You bit me to the bone, right here." He showed his forearm, and there was nothing there to see, fifty years gone, but Daniel remembered it, the taste of his brother's skin and the salt and the satisfaction.

"You deserved it."

"I probably did. I don't even remember what for."

"You broke my dam. Down at the creek. I'd spent all day on it, I had it backed up two feet deep, and you came down and kicked it out just to watch the water go."

Carl laughed, a real one, from the belly. "God. I did. I remember the water going. I don't remember you biting me but I sure remember that water going, it was the best thing I ever saw, it just took the whole thing out in about four seconds, all your work, gone." He wiped his eyes, laughing. "You spent all day and I spent four seconds and the water won. The water always wins, Danny, that's the—" He stopped. Heard himself.

"Real subtle," Daniel said.

"I didn't mean it as a—"

"I know what you meant. The water always wins. Hollis is the water. The orchard's my dam." Daniel finished his sandwich and folded the wax paper into a small tight square the way their mother had taught them, *don't waste the paper, we can use it again.* "I get it. I got it in February. I'm not stupid, Carl, that's the thing you never figured out about me. I know the place doesn't pay. I know the trees are mostly done. I know that if we hold on we hold on into debt and then the bank takes it and we get nothing, I know all of it, you don't have to walk me through the arithmetic like I'm Dad in 'eighty-eight who didn't even understand the question you were asking him."

"You remember that."

"I remember everything. That's my whole problem. You're the one who gets to forget, you went to Spokane and forgot, and I stayed and remembered, somebody had to be the one who remembered and it was always going to be me."

Carl set down his second sandwich half-eaten. The water moved in the ditches. Somewhere up the slope a meadowlark went off, that liquid falling song, and they both heard it and neither said anything about it, but it was the sound of every summer of their childhood and they both knew it.

"So sell," Carl said quietly. "If you know all of it. If you know how it ends. Sell now, standing up, and take Hollis's money, and we put Mom somewhere they can watch her, a good place, there's a good place outside Wenatchee, I looked into it, and you don't spend the next ten years going broke and watching her go and burning yourself down to nothing trying to hold a dam that the water's going to take anyway. Sell."

"I can't."

"Why."

"Because." Daniel looked for it. It was hard to find, harder to say. "Because the place is the last thing that remembers her the way she was. You understand? She doesn't remember herself anymore. There's whole years gone for her. But the place remembers. That's her rose bed by the porch, she put it in the year I was born, the place knows that even when she doesn't. She walks out there and something in her settles, I've seen it. She doesn't know why but she knows the ground. You move her to Wenatchee, to a clean good place with people watching her, and she's nobody. She's a woman in a chair who used to be somebody, somewhere else. Here she's still—" His voice did something and he stopped and made it stop. "Here the ground still knows her. That's worth something. That's worth going broke for, maybe. I don't know. Maybe it isn't. But it's the thing I've got and you came back to take it and tell me it's the practical choice."

Carl didn't say anything for a long time. The water moved. The meadowlark went again.

"I came back," Carl said finally, "because I didn't want you to do it alone. Whatever it was. Sell or don't sell. I didn't want you carrying it by yourself anymore. That's the truth, even if it doesn't sound like it under all the Hollis and the arithmetic. I'm no good at the other kind of talking. I do numbers because numbers I can do. But I came back for you, not for the money, I don't even need the—" He stopped. "I came back for you."

Daniel looked at his brother, leaning against the dead tree, gone soft in the belly, the arms still strong, and he believed him, which was the strange thing, after thirty years of not believing much of anything Carl said. He believed him.

"We don't have to decide today," Daniel said.

"No."

"The pipe'll hold. For a while."

"For a while." Carl pushed himself up off the tree, dusting the gray bark from his shirt. "We should fill that trench before it caves."

They filled the trench. They worked it shoulder to shoulder this time, no ends to work from, just the two of them pushing the dark good soil back into the hole they'd made, tamping it with the backs of the shovels, and the water ran in the ditches alongside them down to the trees, and the sun came over and started its long fall toward the western ridge. They didn't talk much. There wasn't much more to say that the work wasn't already saying, the two of them moving in the rhythm they'd had as boys, the wordless thing, filling in the ground.

When it was done and tamped and the trench was just a darker line in the dirt that the grass would take back by August, Carl stood and stretched his back and looked up the slope toward the house, where their mother had come out onto the porch and was standing among her roses, small and white-haired, shading her eyes against the sun to watch her two sons down among the dying trees.

"Walter," she called down to them, or to one of them, or to the both of them, her voice thin and carrying in the still afternoon. "Walter, supper's the sandwiches."

Carl raised his hand to her. "We know, Mom," he called up. "We're coming."

And they stood a moment longer in the smell of turned earth and ditch water, looking up at her, before they shouldered the shovels and started up the long rows toward the house.
