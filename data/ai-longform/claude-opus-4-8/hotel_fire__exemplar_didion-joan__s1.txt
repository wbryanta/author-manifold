The hotel had been rebuilt to look exactly as it had looked before, which was to say it had been rebuilt to look like something that had never existed, a hotel out of an architect's rendering, all white columns and bougainvillea and a porte cochère where the valets stood in their maroon jackets waiting for cars that arrived too seldom in the off season. Marian Pruett had not been back in eleven years. She had not intended to come back at all. She had come because her niece was being married in the garden at six o'clock, and because her sister Dot had said, on the telephone, in the particular voice Dot reserved for occasions when she believed herself to be making a generous gesture, that it would do Marian good to see the place fixed up nice.

"You always did dwell," Dot said.

"I don't dwell."

"You dwell, Marian. Mother dwelled and you dwell."

Marian had not argued the point. She had driven down from Bakersfield on the Friday, alone, with the air conditioning broken in the Buick and her good dress laid flat across the back seat so it would not wrinkle, and she had checked into a Travelodge two miles from the hotel rather than stay at the hotel itself, which Dot had also remarked upon, the staying at the Travelodge, as though it were a further instance of Marian's tendency to dwell.

She had been nineteen the year of the fire. She had worked the third and fourth floors with a girl named Cherokee Bowen, who was not in fact Cherokee but called herself that, and a heavy slow woman named Mrs. Olivas who had worked the hotel since before any of them were born and who did not, that night, get out. There had been eleven who did not get out. Marian could have named all eleven once. She found now, standing in the lobby with her overnight bag, that she could name only seven, and that the seven came to her not as a list she recited but as a sudden flooding, the way the names of the dead will come, unbidden, in the wrong order, attached to nothing.

"You'll want to freshen up," Dot said. Dot had come down in the elevator, which was new, which made no sound. The old elevators had announced themselves the length of the corridor. "The ceremony's at six but Cissy wants pictures at five-thirty, the whole party, you're not in the party but she wants the family in some of them."

"I'm fine," Marian said.

"You drove all that way."

"I'm fine, Dot."

They stood a moment in the lobby. The lobby was where the fire had not reached. The fire had started in the kitchen and gone up the service stair, which was the stair the maids used, which was the stair that in the rebuilt hotel had been moved, Marian noticed, or perhaps not moved but reconceived, so that it no longer rose where it had risen. She had gone up that stair a thousand times with her arms full of linen. She had come down it that night with her dress on fire at the hem and Cherokee Bowen's hand in hers, and Cherokee had let go somewhere around the second floor, had simply let go, and Marian had not known until the parking lot that Cherokee was not behind her.

"This is all new," Marian said.

"They spent eight million," Dot said, as though the figure settled something. "Mr. Aldemann that owns it now, he's the one giving the rate on the rooms, he's known the Tatums for years. You'd never know."

"No," Marian said. "You'd never know."

The Tatums were the family of the boy Cissy was marrying. Marian had met the boy once, at Dot's, a tall pink boy named Brick whose people had cotton land near Corcoran and who called Marian "ma'am" in a way that she understood was meant well and that she nonetheless could not bear. Cissy had been four years old the year of the fire. Cissy did not know about the fire. Dot had seen to it that Cissy did not know about the fire, the way Dot saw to things, and Marian had let her, because it was Dot's daughter and Dot's house and because Marian had stopped, somewhere in the intervening years, expecting to be told that her experience mattered to anyone but herself.

She went up to the room Dot had reserved for her after all, on the fourth floor, because the rate was good and Dot had been unable to comprehend why Marian would pay for a Travelodge when there was a perfectly nice room going. The room was on the fourth floor, which was the floor Marian had worked, and the number on the door was 412, and Marian stood in the corridor a long while before she put the key card in the slot, because 412 had been a room she cleaned, had been Mrs. Olivas's room actually, in the sense that the maids spoke of rooms as theirs, and Mrs. Olivas had complained that the people in 412 left the tub a sight.

The room inside was a room in any hotel. There was a print of egrets above the bed and a coffee maker on a tray and a card explaining how to conserve the planet's water by reusing one's towels. Marian set down her bag and crossed to the window. The window looked down on the garden where they would be married at six, where the white chairs stood in rows on the grass and a man in shirtsleeves was running a cord out to a place where there would be music. Beyond the garden was the parking lot, and Marian saw that the parking lot was the same parking lot, that they had not been able to rebuild the parking lot into something it had never been, and that she had stood down there in her burning dress eleven years ago at a spot she could now, from the window, precisely locate, between the second and third light standards, on the cracked asphalt that they had repaved but that lay in the same place asphalt had always lain.

She did not cry. She had expected, if anything, to cry, and she did not, and she understood looking down at the parking lot that the not crying was itself a kind of dwelling, that Dot was right, that she had carried the parking lot inside her for eleven years precisely so that she would not have to cry when she saw it again.

At five-thirty she went down for the pictures. She wore the good dress, which was navy, and the pearls that had been her mother's, and she stood at the edge of the family group on the lawn while a photographer with a ponytail arranged them and rearranged them and told Brick's mother she had a lovely smile. Brick's mother did have a lovely smile. Brick's mother was a woman named Lavonne Tatum who wore a great deal of gold and who took Marian's hand in both of hers and said she had heard so much, which Marian doubted.

"You're the aunt from Bakersfield," Lavonne Tatum said.

"That's right."

"Cissy thinks the world of you."

Marian did not think this was true either, but she said that she thought the world of Cissy, which was at least closer to true. Cissy in her dress was beautiful in the way all brides are beautiful, which is to say she was made anonymous by it, made into the idea of a bride, so that Marian looking at her could not find the four-year-old who had pulled the heads off Dot's marigolds, could not find anyone she knew, could find only a young woman in white satin holding her bouquet too tightly and laughing at something the photographer said.

"Aunt Marian, stand by Mama," Cissy called.

So Marian stood by Dot, and the photographer told them to think of something funny, and Dot whispered, "Smile, Marian, for God's sake," and the shutter went, and that was a picture that existed now, Marian and Dot on the lawn of the rebuilt hotel, smiling, between the second and third light standards, more or less, of the parking lot beyond.

The ceremony was brief. The minister was a young man who spoke of journeys. There was a moment when the breeze came up off the parking lot and lifted the veil and Cissy laughed and Brick reached to steady it, and the guests made the sound guests make, and Marian, in the third row on the bride's side, found that she was looking not at the couple but at the service door at the side of the hotel, the gray steel door that opened onto the place where the laundry had been brought out, where the men had stood smoking, where eleven years ago she had not gone, because the fire was between her and that door, and so she had gone the other way, up before she could go down, which was the thing she had never told anyone, that she had gone up first, by instinct, the way a cat will climb a tree it cannot climb back down, and had been on the fifth-floor landing before she understood that up was death and had turned and come down through the smoke with her hand in Cherokee Bowen's hand.

After the ceremony there were drinks on the terrace. The terrace was new. The terrace overlooked the garden and beyond it the parking lot and beyond that, faintly, the lights coming on along the highway. Marian took a glass of white wine from a tray and stood at the rail. The waiters were young, younger than she had been, in white shirts and black vests, and they moved among the guests with their trays held high, and Marian watched one of them, a girl, a thin dark girl with her hair pulled back hard, and the girl reminded her of no one in particular, of all of them, of herself.

"It's pretty here," a man said beside her.

He was one of the Tatum connection, a soft-bodied man in a seersucker jacket, holding a bourbon.

"It is," Marian said.

"You'd never know it burned, would you. They had a fire here, you know. Way back. Burned the whole back of it."

"Did it," Marian said.

"People died, the way I heard it. Maids and such. Whoever's back there in a hotel when it goes up, that's who dies. Never the guests." He drank. "They built her right back up though. Insurance, I imagine. Insurance and the Lord." He laughed at this. "You here for the wedding?"

"I'm the bride's aunt."

"Is that right. Fine girl. Brick's a lucky man." He looked out at the garden. "Pretty here," he said again, and drifted off toward the bar, and Marian stood at the rail and thought that the man had told her about the fire, had told her about the maids and such, and that this was the form the dead took now, that they had become a thing a man in a seersucker jacket told a stranger at a wedding, a fact about a building, insurance and the Lord.

She set down her wine and went inside.

She did not decide to find the stair. She would have said, if anyone had asked her, that she was looking for the powder room, and in a sense she was, she had asked a waiter and the waiter had pointed her down a corridor off the lobby, and at the end of the corridor, past the powder room, past a door marked PRIVATE, there was a gray steel door with a small wired-glass window, and a sign that said this door was an exit and must not be blocked, and Marian put her hand on the bar of it and pushed.

The stair behind it was concrete, painted gray, lit by a fixture in a wire cage. It was not the stair she had come down. They had built it in a different place. But it was a service stair, the only service stair, and it smelled of concrete and of the faint chemical smell of new buildings that have not yet been lived in long enough to smell of anything else, and Marian stood on the landing with the door closing slow on its pneumatic arm behind her and the sound of the wedding cut off all at once, and she began to climb.

She climbed to the fourth floor. She did not know why the fourth floor and not the fifth, not the second where Cherokee had let go, except that the fourth floor was the floor she had worked, the floor of 412 and Mrs. Olivas, and when she came out of the stairwell onto the fourth floor she was in the corridor of guest rooms, carpeted in the wine-colored carpet, with the egret prints, and the ice machine humming in its alcove, and no one anywhere, the whole floor empty, everyone down at the wedding or out at the bars or gone home.

She walked the corridor. The doors went by, 408, 410, 412. She stopped at 412. She had her key card; this was her room; she could go in. She stood at the door.

She had cleaned this room. Mrs. Olivas had cleaned this room but Marian had cleaned it too, on the days Mrs. Olivas was out, and she remembered the way the afternoon light came in off the parking lot at a certain hour and lay across the bed, and she remembered finding a child's shoe under the bed once, a single white shoe, and turning it in over to the desk, and she remembered Mrs. Olivas saying that the people in 412 left the tub a sight, and she remembered nothing else about the room at all, which seemed to her now, standing at the door, an unbearable thing, that she should have spent so much of her young life in these rooms and remember of them only a shoe and a tub and the angle of the light.

She did not go in. She stood at the door a while and then she went on, to the end of the corridor, where the corridor turned, and at the turn there was a window, and the window looked down on the garden, where the wedding had moved now to its dinner, the round tables with their white cloths set up on the lawn, the candles in their hurricane lamps, the band beginning to play. She could hear the band faintly through the glass. She could see Cissy moving among the tables in her white dress, and Dot, and the boy Brick, and the man in the seersucker jacket, and Lavonne Tatum's gold catching the candlelight. They were very far below and very small and they did not know she was watching them, and Marian thought that this was where she had always been, above them, behind the wired glass, on the wrong stair, the stair the dead came down.

She heard the elevator open behind her. She turned. A waiter came out, the thin dark girl, carrying a tray of dirty glasses, and she stopped when she saw Marian standing at the window at the end of the empty corridor in her navy dress and her pearls.

"Are you lost, ma'am?" the girl said. "Are you looking for your room?"

"No," Marian said. "I have my room. I was just looking out the window."

The girl came a few steps closer. She was younger even than Marian had thought, sixteen or seventeen, with a tired plainness to her face that Marian recognized, that was the face of a girl who has been on her feet since noon and has hours yet to go.

"It's a nice view," the girl said, doubtfully, glancing at the window.

"How long have you worked here?" Marian said.

"Since it opened. Two years." The girl shifted the tray. "It's all right. The tips are good in the season."

"And in the off season?"

"In the off season it's slow." The girl smiled a little. "But you don't work so hard."

"I worked here," Marian said. She had not meant to say it. "Years ago. Before. I was a maid here."

The girl looked at her, at the navy dress and the pearls, and her face changed, took on the cautious blankness of the young confronted with the past, with the idea that the old were once anything but old.

"Before the fire?" the girl said.

So she knew about the fire. Of course she knew about the fire; they all knew about the fire; it would be told to every new girl her first week, the way such things are told, half warning and half story. Marian wondered how it had been told to this one, whether it had been told with the maids in it.

"Before the fire," Marian said.

The girl was quiet a moment. Then she said, "Were you here. When it." She did not finish.

"I was here," Marian said.

The girl looked at her, and Marian saw that the girl believed her, and that the believing had made the fire real to the girl for the first time, made it not a story told to frighten new hires but a thing that had happened to a woman standing in front of her in a navy dress, and Marian saw the girl's face take this in, the way her own face must once have taken in some piece of the world that could not afterward be put back. The girl's hands had tightened on the tray.

"They say eleven people died," the girl said.

"Yes."

"On the stairs. The old stairs. They say that's why they moved them. They wouldn't build the new stairs in the same place."

Marian had not known that. She had not known that the stair under her, the gray concrete stair she had climbed, stood in a different place because no one would build a stair where eleven people had died. It seemed to her the only true thing anyone had told her since she came back, the only thing that was not insurance and the Lord and you'd never know.

"I didn't know that," Marian said.

"That's what they say." The girl looked down at her tray of glasses. The band was playing below them, and a voice on a microphone was asking the bride and groom to come to the floor. "I have to take these down," the girl said. "There's the dinner. I'm sorry. About before. That you were here."

"It was a long time ago," Marian said, which was what she always said, and which the girl, being young, would believe.

The girl went to the elevator and pressed the button and the doors opened at once, soundless, and she stepped in with her tray, and just before the doors closed she looked back at Marian once, and Marian saw in the look something she had not seen in any face all day, not in Dot's or Cissy's or Lavonne Tatum's or the seersucker man's, something that was not pity and was not curiosity but was a kind of recognition, the recognition of one who serves for one who has served, and then the doors closed and the girl was gone, down to the wedding, down the silent shaft where the old stair had been.

Marian stood at the window a while longer. Below, the bride and groom were dancing. The guests stood around them in a ring, holding their drinks, and the band played something slow, and the candles burned in the hurricane lamps, and beyond the lawn the parking lot lay dark except where the light standards threw their cones of light down onto the asphalt, between the second and third of which she had stood, eleven years before, in her burning dress, alone, having let no one go, having only been let go.

She did not go down by the stair. She took the elevator, like a guest. It made no sound. It set her down in the lobby that the fire had not reached, and she walked out through the lobby and through the porte cochère, past the valets in their maroon jackets, and she did not go back to the garden, where the dancing was, where Dot would be looking for her presently and would say, later, on the telephone, that Marian had disappeared in the middle of the dinner, that Marian had always been one to dwell. She walked out across the parking lot in her navy dress and her mother's pearls, between the light standards, to the Buick, and she got in, and she sat for a moment with her hands on the wheel, in the dark, listening to the band play, very far away, for the strangers dancing on the grass above the place where the stair had been.
