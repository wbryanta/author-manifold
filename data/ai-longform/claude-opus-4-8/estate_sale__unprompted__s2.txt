The card table came first, which seemed wrong to Eleanor, though she could not have said what order would have seemed right. A man in a Carhartt jacket carried it out by himself, folded flat against his chest like a sandwich board, and she watched the green vinyl top with its single cigarette burn pass through the front door and into the gray November light. Her mother had bought that table in 1971 for the bridge club that met on Thursdays. The burn was from Marjorie Pelham, who had died in 1989, who had smoked Pall Malls and laughed in a way that made the windows shudder. The man paid eight dollars for it and did not know any of that, and there was no reason he should.

Eleanor sat at the kitchen table—not for sale, or rather, marked NFS on a strip of blue tape because she had not yet decided, which was its own kind of indecision she recognized in herself and disliked—and she had the cash box open in front of her. The estate company had wanted to run the whole thing, take their thirty-five percent and leave her out of it entirely, and a saner woman would have let them. But she had flown in from Seattle and she had three days and she could not bear the thought of it happening without her, the house emptied by professionals while she sat in a hotel by the airport watching cable news. So she had compromised. The estate woman, Donna, ran the floor. Eleanor ran the money. It gave her something to do with her hands.

"This is fifteen?" A woman held up the cut-glass bowl, the one that had held ribbon candy at Christmas, hard candy that nobody ate, that sat in the bowl going dusty until February when her mother threw it out and washed the bowl and put it back on the sideboard empty.

"Fifteen, yes."

"Would you take ten?"

Eleanor looked at the woman. She was perhaps sixty, in a quilted vest, her reading glasses pushed up into hair the color of dishwater. There was nothing wrong with her. She was doing what you did at these things. You haggled. It was expected; it was almost courteous, a way of participating.

"Twelve," Eleanor said.

"Sold." The woman beamed and dug into a coin purse and counted out the bills and the change, and the bowl went into a canvas tote already heavy with other people's mothers' things, and that was that.

Donna moved through the rooms with a clipboard, repositioning items, talking up the better pieces. She had done this a thousand times. She knew that the secretary desk in the front hall would sell within the first hour because the good furniture always went first, the people who knew furniture coming early with cash and a truck. She knew the linens would sit until the last day and then go for nothing, by the box. She had explained all of this to Eleanor that morning with the gentle authority of a hospice nurse, and Eleanor had understood that Donna had developed, over years, a way of being kind to people on the worst ordinary day of their lives.

The house filled with strangers. That was the part nobody warned you about, that you would stand in your mother's living room and there would be eleven, twelve people you had never seen, opening drawers, turning chairs over to read the makers' marks, holding lamps up to the window. A man in his thirties was going through the bookshelf in the den, pulling out volumes, checking the copyright pages. He had the particular avidity of a dealer. Her father's books, most of them, the Will Durant histories in their matching bindings that he had bought through a mail-order club, a volume a month, and read in the evenings in the brown chair with a glass of something amber. The man bought the whole shelf for forty dollars and Eleanor watched him box them and she thought: my father read those for fifteen years, and they are worth forty dollars, and that is correct, that is what they are worth, and there is no other number that would be more correct.

It was important, she had decided on the plane, not to be sentimental. Sentiment was a kind of dishonesty. The things were just things. Her mother had been the last of them, her father gone six years now, and the house in Ashtabula had become a museum of a marriage and a family that no longer existed anywhere except in Eleanor's head and the heads of a few aging people scattered around the country who would themselves soon be gone. The objects did not contain the people. She knew this. She had read enough, lived enough, to know that the cut-glass bowl was glass and the table was vinyl and particleboard, and that to weep over them was to make a category error, to confuse the vessel with the thing it had briefly, accidentally held.

She believed this completely and it did not help at all.

"Excuse me." A young woman, maybe twenty-five, with a baby strapped to her front in a complicated harness. "The dresser upstairs, the white one with the gold trim? Is that the price on the tag, or—"

"Whatever's on the tag," Eleanor said.

"It's thirty-five."

"Then it's thirty-five."

The young woman bit her lip. She had the look of someone counting money in her head, someone for whom thirty-five dollars was a real number and not an abstraction. The baby gnawed its own fist and stared at Eleanor with the flat unembarrassed gaze of the very young.

"It's a child's dresser," Eleanor heard herself say. "It was mine. When I was a girl." She had not meant to say it. The white dresser with the gold trim, four drawers, a mirror that her mother had hung pictures in the corner of, a snapshot of Eleanor at her first communion, a dried corsage. "There used to be a mirror but it broke years ago. It's good for a child's room."

"It's so pretty," the young woman said. "I have a girl. She's two. This one's the boy." She jostled the baby slightly.

"Take it for twenty-five," Eleanor said.

The young woman looked up, surprised, and something passed between them, woman to woman, mother that was to mother that had been, and Eleanor felt the inadequacy of the gesture even as she made it. What was she doing. Discounting her childhood. As if ten dollars off could transmit anything, could send the dresser into that little girl's room freighted with whatever Eleanor needed it to carry.

"Thank you," the young woman said, and meant it, and went to find Donna and a man with a truck, and Eleanor sat with the cash box and looked at her hands, which were her mother's hands now, she had noticed that on the plane too, the same knuckles, the same way the rings sat.

The afternoon light came in low and brown through the front windows. Ashtabula in November was a place the sun seemed to have given up on. She remembered it as gray, almost entirely gray, the lake throwing its weather inland, the long winters, the snow that came off Erie in walls. She had left at eighteen and not looked back, college in Columbus and then graduate school and then the long migration west, husband, no husband, a career in hospital administration that had given her a competence and a salary and a condominium with a view of water that was nothing at all like this water, and she had come back over the years for the obligatory visits, Christmases that grew shorter, and then for the two funerals, and now for this.

A man was buying the dining room chairs. Six of them, the ladder-backs that her mother had refinished herself one summer, stripping them on newspaper in the garage, the smell of the stripper, her mother in a kerchief and rubber gloves, swearing mildly when it splashed. Forty-eight dollars for the set. The man carried them out two at a time, and Eleanor counted the trips, six chairs, three trips, and she thought about how many dinners. You couldn't count them. Sunday dinners and Thanksgivings and the ordinary Tuesdays that were most of a life, the chairs scraping back, her father saying grace in the perfunctory way he had, her mother already up getting the rolls, Eleanor and her brother kicking each other under the table.

Her brother. She should call Danny. He was in Arizona and had said he couldn't get away, which was probably true and probably also not the whole truth, and she had not pressed him because some part of her had wanted to do this alone, had wanted the suffering of it to be hers, undiluted, unshared, a thing she could hold against him later in the ledger of who had loved their mother more. She recognized this impulse as ugly and indulged it anyway. She would call him tonight from the hotel and not mention the chairs.

"You doing okay, hon?" Donna, materializing beside her with two paper cups of coffee from the machine they'd brought. She set one down by the cash box. "It's a lot. People don't realize. They think the worst part's the funeral but it's this. The funeral, you've got everybody around you. This you do alone, mostly."

"I'm fine," Eleanor said, and took the coffee. It was terrible and she was grateful for it.

"The big stuff'll be gone by four," Donna said. "Then it's the pickers and the box lots. Tomorrow's the real circus, half-price day, but most of what you'd feel something about'll be gone by then." She said this gently, as information, as a kindness, letting Eleanor know that the acute phase had an end, that by the close of today the house would be substantially empty and the worst of the watching would be over.

"I appreciate you running this," Eleanor said.

"It's what I do." Donna looked around the room with a professional's eye and something else underneath it. "I lost my mom in '04. House out in Geneva. I had my brother-in-law do the sale, I couldn't be there. I've always been sorry. Or, no. I've always wondered. Whether I'd've been sorrier to see it or sorrier not to." She shrugged. "You're seeing it. That's worth something, I think. Hard to say what."

She moved off to talk a man up from forty to fifty on a mirror, and Eleanor sat with that, sorrier to see it or sorrier not to, and could not resolve it either.

The afternoon went on. A space heater, three dollars. A set of pots, the Revere Ware with the copper bottoms that her mother had kept gleaming with a special paste, scrubbing the copper to a shine that served no function, that nobody saw, that was simply a thing you did because your mother had done it and her mother before her, a whole genealogy of women polishing the bottoms of pots. Twelve dollars for the set. The hi-fi cabinet, the big console stereo that had played her parents' records, the Mantovani and the Ray Conniff and the one Harry Belafonte that seemed exotic in their house, that long low cabinet that had been the most important object in the living room for two decades, the altar of it—a man bought it for twenty dollars and said he was going to gut it and put a bar inside, a liquor cabinet, and Eleanor said that sounded nice and meant it, because at least it would go on being something, would go on standing in a room where people gathered, rather than to the dump.

That was the thing she kept arriving at. The dump. Everything in the house was on a journey to the landfill, all of it, and the estate sale was simply a way of routing some of it through other people's houses first, a detour that might last years or decades but ended in the same place. The man with the liquor-cabinet plan was buying the console a few more years of being looked at. There was a mercy in that. She decided to think of the whole afternoon that way, as a distribution of small mercies, these strangers each taking custody of an object and granting it a stay.

She got up to stretch her legs and walked through the emptying rooms. Without the furniture the house was smaller, as houses always are. She had grown up here believing it large. The living room where the console had stood now showed a clean dark rectangle on the carpet, the pile uncrushed, a ghost of the cabinet, and the carpet itself was the carpet of her childhood, a gold shag that had been the height of something in 1973 and was now simply old, and would be torn out by whoever bought the house, and she stood looking at the dark rectangle where the stereo had been and felt the closest she had yet come to crying, not at the loss of the stereo but at the shape of its absence, the way it had left a mark, the way everything leaves a mark and then the mark itself is torn out and replaced.

The den still had its built-in shelves, empty now, the dealer having taken the books. There was a water stain on the ceiling in the corner that had been there as long as she could remember, that her father had always meant to fix, that had a particular shape she had stared at as a child lying on the floor in front of the television, a shape like a rabbit, or a continent, depending on the light. She had not thought of the water stain in forty years and here it was, faithful, unsold, unsellable, and it undid her a little, the water stain, more than the stereo had, because nobody would ever buy it and nobody would ever miss it and it had been a companion of her childhood and it would be painted over by strangers without a thought.

"Ma'am?" A teenager, a boy, in the doorway. "Lady out front says to ask you. There's a box of records. She wants to know are they for sale, they don't have a tag."

The records. Her parents' records, the Mantovani and the rest. "Yes," Eleanor said. "They're for sale." Then: "Wait."

She went out front. The woman was perhaps her own age, fifties, going through the box of LPs with the quick practiced flip of someone who collected, and Eleanor watched her hands move through the albums, her parents' albums, and she said, "How much would you give for the box?"

The woman looked up. "All of them? I don't know. Are there any good ones?" She flipped. "It's mostly easy listening. Lounge stuff. The Belafonte's worth a couple bucks. The rest—" She made a face, not unkind. "I'd give you ten for the box. Honestly I'd just take the Belafonte off your hands for two and leave you the rest."

"Take the box," Eleanor said. "Ten is fine."

But she stood there while the woman counted out the money, and she said, "My mother played these. On Sunday afternoons. She'd put on the Mantovani and do the ironing." She didn't know why she was telling this stranger. "The whole house. You could hear it in the whole house."

The woman stopped counting. She looked at Eleanor and her face changed, the dealer falling away from it. "My mother had the same one," she said. "The Mantovani. The strings. God." She pulled the album out of the box and looked at the cover, the lush idiotic romantic cover, and she said, "You want to keep this one?"

"No," Eleanor said. "No, you take it. You'll play it. I'd just—I'd just have it in a box."

The woman nodded slowly and put the album back and finished counting out the ten dollars and pressed it into Eleanor's hand, and for a moment they were two women in their fifties standing in a driveway in Ashtabula holding the same grief from different houses, and then the woman picked up the box and carried it to her car, and Eleanor went back inside.

By four o'clock Donna's prediction had come true. The big pieces were gone. The house had been hollowed out, the rooms echoing now, and what remained was the residue, the small things, the box lots, the items that were really just trash with a dollar sign on them—the half-used candles, the costume jewelry, the kitchen drawer's worth of rubber bands and twist ties and dead batteries that her mother had saved because her mother had been a child in the Depression and you did not throw away a battery just because it was dead, you kept it, in case. Eleanor had found that drawer that morning and had nearly lost her composure over it, the saved dead batteries, the proof of a frugality so deep it had become a kind of faith.

The crowd thinned. The serious buyers had their trucks loaded and gone. What was left were the browsers, the lonely, the people for whom estate sales were a way to spend a Saturday and be among others, the retirees who went to every sale in three counties and bought a coffee mug for a quarter so as to have a reason to have come. Eleanor recognized them now; she'd been watching all day. There was an old man who had been there since opening and had bought nothing, who simply walked the rooms slowly, looking, and she understood that he was not there to buy, he was there to be in a house with other people in it, and that the estate sale was offering him something the price tags didn't name.

She sold a mug for a quarter. She sold a stack of dish towels for a dollar. She sold the saved twist ties, the whole drawer, to a woman who gardened and wanted them for her tomatoes, fifty cents, and she thought, there, Mother, you were right, somebody needed them, you were right to keep them, and she had to turn and look out the window for a moment.

The light was failing. Ashtabula in November gave you a short day and took it back early. Donna started consolidating the leftover small items onto the dining room sideboard—NFS, the sideboard, blue tape, Eleanor hadn't decided—grouping the box lots for tomorrow's half-price assault. The house was almost empty. The dark rectangles on the gold shag carpet were everywhere now, a constellation of absences, here the stereo, here the sofa, here the chair where her father had read his forty-dollar histories.

"You'll want to go through what's left tonight," Donna said. "Anything you want to keep, pull it now, before tomorrow. It goes fast on half-price day. They come like locusts." She said it fondly. "I'll lock up. You take whatever time you need."

The last buyers drifted out. The Carhartt man had been the first and now a woman in a church-sale coat was the last, buying a single juice glass for a dime, one of the set with the oranges printed on them, the breakfast glasses, and Eleanor watched her carry the single glass out into the dusk, wrapped in newspaper, and she thought about how somewhere tonight in some other house in some other kitchen one of her mother's juice glasses would hold someone's orange juice, would catch the morning light, would go on being a juice glass, and that this was as close to continuance as anything got.

Then they were gone, all of them, and Donna gathered her clipboard and her cash apron and said she'd be back at eight in the morning, and asked again was Eleanor all right to lock up, and Eleanor said she was, and Donna left, and the door closed, and the house was empty and silent for the first time all day.

Eleanor stood in the front hall. The secretary desk was gone from where it had stood. She walked through the rooms once more in the failing light, not turning on the lamps because the lamps were sold, and the house went blue and then gray around her. She came to the den and looked up at the water stain on the ceiling, the rabbit, the continent, and she lay down on the gold shag carpet beneath it the way she had as a child, on her back, her hands folded on her stomach, and she looked up at the stain and let the day catch up with her at last.

The carpet smelled of the house, the particular smell she would never be able to describe to anyone and would never smell again after tomorrow, dust and old heat and something underneath that was simply the smell of having lived here, of forty years of meals and arguments and Sunday Mantovani and saved batteries. She lay on the floor of the emptied house and looked at the water stain and did not try to keep from crying, finally, because there was no one to see, and the strangers had carried all of it out the front door into the gray light and paid their dimes and their dollars, and tomorrow the locusts would take the rest, and the day after that she would fly back to her water that was nothing like this water, and the stain on the ceiling would be painted over by people who would never know it had looked like a rabbit, and that was correct, that was how it was supposed to go, and it was unbearable, and she bore it, lying on the floor in the dark, the way you bear the things that are both.
