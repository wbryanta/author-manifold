The board lit up the way it always did at six minutes past eight on a Sunday evening, the small white bulb above the cord pair for the Hartwell exchange glowing steady and sure. Loretta did not need to look at the clock. She had been working the long-distance switchboard at the Beaumont central office for eleven years, and her hands knew the geography of the panel the way her tongue knew the roof of her mouth. But she looked at the clock anyway, because she liked the confirmation of it, the way you might glance at a familiar face across a room just to be certain it had not changed.

"Number, please."

"Yes, hello. I'd like to place a call to Tucson, Arizona." The woman's voice came through with that particular Sunday-evening softness, the edges worn smooth by whatever the week had done to her. "The number is—"

"Four-six-two, two-one-one-seven," Loretta said.

There was a small silence on the line, and then a laugh, warm and surprised, though it should not have been surprised anymore. "You remembered."

"I'd remember if I forgot," Loretta said, which was a thing her mother used to say and which made no sense, but the woman laughed again, so it was the right thing.

"Go ahead and put it through, then. You're better at it than I am by now."

Loretta seated the plug into the jack and began the slow ritual of the connection, ringing down the line, waiting for the relay of operators across two thousand miles of copper and switching stations to hand the call along like a bucket of water passed down a row of men fighting a fire. She did not have to stay on the line once the call connected. The rules said she should drop off. But she had learned, over the months, to keep one ear half-tuned to the cord, the monitor key thumbed just barely, not enough to be listening, only enough to know that the call had gone through and that the two of them had found each other again.

The woman's name was Eleanor. The man in Tucson was named Walter. Loretta knew this not because she had ever been told but because she had connected them every Sunday for the better part of a year and you could not help but learn a thing if it came to you at the same time every week and asked nothing of you but that you let it pass through your hands.

The phone in Tucson rang twice.

"Eleanor." Walter always said her name like that when he answered, not a question, a confirmation, the same way Loretta looked at the clock. As if he too needed the world to keep being what it had been.

"Walter. Hello. Can you hear me all right? There was a storm earlier and I thought the lines might be—"

"I can hear you fine. You sound close."

"I don't feel close."

"I know. I know it."

Loretta took her thumb off the monitor key. This was the place where she always let them go, at the first I know it, because after that it was no longer a connection but a conversation, and a conversation belonged to the people having it. She turned her attention to the rest of the board, to a man in Beaumont wanting his sister in Lake Charles, to a boy placing a collect call to a girl whose father, judging by the voice that answered, would not be accepting the charges tonight or any night. The boy's disappointment came through clear even at the distance of the switching apparatus. Loretta felt it the way you feel weather coming, a pressure behind the eyes.

But she came back to the Tucson cord now and then through the evening, the way you return to a window to check on a child playing in the yard. Not to listen. Only to confirm. And in the confirming she would catch a word, a phrase, the shape of a sentence, and over the months these had accumulated in her like silt at the bottom of a slow river, until she understood things she had never set out to know.

They had been married once. That was the thing she had understood first, and it had surprised her, because she had assumed at the beginning that she was connecting two people who were courting at a distance, two people moving toward each other. It took her some weeks to understand that they were moving apart, or had already moved apart, and that these Sunday calls were not the building of a bridge but the maintenance of one, a man and a woman walking out to the middle of a span every seven days to make sure it had not fallen into the water while they were not looking.

He had gone to Arizona for his lungs. That was another thing. There was a dryness he needed that Beaumont, sitting in its wet heat off the Gulf, could not give him. Loretta had heard him cough, early on, a sound that had since grown less frequent, so that she imagined the desert was doing its work. Eleanor had not gone with him. Loretta did not know why. She had constructed several reasons over the months, the way you might try on coats, and discarded each of them, because none of them quite fit the voices, and the voices were the only true thing she had.

"Did you go to the Pruitts' on Friday?" Walter asked her this Sunday, and Loretta, passing by the cord with her thumb on the key, stopped.

"I did. I almost didn't."

"You should go. You shouldn't sit in that house."

"It isn't sitting in the house I mind. It's coming back to it after."

There was a silence then, and into it Loretta could hear, faintly, the hum of all that distance, the great patient hum of the wire that connected everything to everything, and she took her thumb off the key and let them have the silence, because a silence was the most private thing two people could share and she had no business in it.

She had a husband of her own, once. She did not think about Donald often, which was its own kind of answer to a question she had stopped asking. He had not died. He had simply become a man she used to be married to, the way Walter and Eleanor seemed to be becoming people who used to be married to each other, except that they kept calling, and Donald had not called in six years and would not have known the number to call if he'd wanted to. She wondered, sometimes, whether the difference was love or only habit, and whether there was finally any difference between those two things if you stretched them out long enough over enough years.

At a quarter to nine the Tucson call ended. It always ended around then; the rates changed, and these were not people with money to burn. Loretta heard the click of Eleanor's receiver settling into its cradle, and she pulled the cord and the white bulb went dark, and the connection that had existed for thirty-nine minutes between Beaumont and Tucson ceased to exist, became again two thousand miles of empty copper waiting for the next thing that wanted to cross it.

She thought about them that week, more than she would have admitted to anyone. She thought about them while she ironed Tuesday evening, the radio low, and while she walked to the market on Thursday past the houses with their windows lit, each window a life she would never connect to anything. She had begun, without deciding to, to carry Eleanor and Walter around with her, two voices in a coat pocket, and she took them out and looked at them the way a child looks at a smooth stone, for no reason but the comfort of the shape.

She knew it was not right, this attachment. The supervisor, Mrs. Vance, a woman with a face like a closed drawer, had spoken to all the girls more than once about the impropriety of forming attachments to the traffic. The traffic, she called it, as though the calls were water in a pipe and a girl who let herself care about the water was a girl who would one day let the pipe burst. You are a connection, Mrs. Vance said. A connection has no opinion about what it connects. Loretta had nodded and thought that Mrs. Vance had never sat eleven years at a board listening to the whole catastrophe of human longing pass through her hands, the proposals and the deaths and the I'm sorrys and the come homes, and remained a woman with a face like a closed drawer. Or perhaps she had. Perhaps that was how you got a face like that.

The next Sunday Loretta came in early. She told herself it was because the schedule had shifted, which was true, but the schedule had shifted before and she had not come in early. She settled herself at the board and put on her headset and went through the ordinary evening traffic with half her attention while the other half waited, the way you wait for a particular footstep on the stair.

Six minutes past eight.

The bulb above the Hartwell cord pair lit, steady and sure.

Loretta felt something in her chest unclench that she had not known was clenched. "Number, please," she said, and she could hear in her own voice the warmth she was not supposed to put there.

"Tucson, Arizona," the woman's voice said. "Four-six-two, two-one-one-seven."

"Go ahead and let me put it through for you," Loretta said, and seated the plug, and rang the line down, two thousand miles, the bucket passed hand to hand. The phone in Tucson rang. It rang twice. It rang a third time, and a fourth, and Loretta felt the unclenched thing in her chest begin slowly to close again, finger by finger.

"He's there," Eleanor said. "He's always there by now. He waits for it."

"The lines are slow tonight," Loretta said, which was not true, but it was the kind of thing you said. "Let me try again."

She pulled the cord and reseated it and rang the number a second time, and again the phone in Tucson rang into a room where no one lifted the receiver, rang six times, seven, the sound of it growing somehow lonelier with each repetition, a sound going out into a space and finding nothing to stop it.

"Maybe he stepped out," Eleanor said, but her voice had gone thin. "Maybe he—he might have gone to the corner for cigarettes. He does that. He forgets the time."

"He could have," Loretta said.

"Would you—" Eleanor stopped. "Would you try once more? I'm sorry. I know it isn't—I know you have other calls."

"I don't have anything more important than this," Loretta said, and meant it, and rang the number a third time, and the phone in Tucson rang, and Loretta closed her eyes and willed across two thousand miles of darkness a hand to reach out and lift the receiver. The bulb glowed. The phone rang. No one answered.

"He isn't there," Eleanor said finally, very quietly.

"It might be the line on his end," Loretta said. "Sometimes a storm out there—the desert storms come up fast, I've heard, and a line goes down and the man doesn't even know his phone isn't working. He could be sitting right by it waiting for you to call and not knowing the line is dead."

"Do you think so?"

"I've seen it happen," Loretta said, and this too was not true, or was only true in the sense that anything was possible, that the world was wide and full of dead lines and men sitting beside them.

"All right," Eleanor said. "All right. Then I'll—I'll call again next week. The line will be fixed by next week."

"It will."

"Thank you. You've been—you don't know. Every week you put me through to him and I never even—what's your name?"

The rules said you did not give your name. The rules said you were a connection and a connection had no name. "Loretta," she said.

"Loretta. Thank you, Loretta. I'll talk to you next week."

The cord came down. The bulb went dark.

Loretta sat for a moment with her hands in her lap, which she was not supposed to do, the board live in front of her and bulbs lighting and somewhere a man wanting his sister and a boy wanting a girl whose father would not accept the charges. She thought of the phone ringing in the room in Tucson. She had heard rooms before, through the ringing. You could tell something about a room from how a phone rang in it, whether it rang in a small close space or a large empty one, whether there was carpet or there was not. The phone in Tucson had rung the way a phone rings in a room where the windows are open and the curtains are still.

She went back to the traffic. She connected the man to his sister. She told the boy, when he came on again, that the party did not answer, which was a kindness, because the party had answered and the answer had been no.

That week was longer than the week before. She found that she had begun to construct, in the place where she had used to keep her tried-on reasons for why Eleanor had not gone to Arizona, a new structure entirely, a thing made of dread, and she did not want to live in it but she did not know how to tear it down. A man who waited by the phone every Sunday at six minutes past eight, who answered before the second ring had finished, who said his wife's name like a confirmation of the world—such a man did not step out for cigarettes. Such a man did not forget the time. Loretta knew this the way she knew the geography of the board.

But there was the other possibility, the line down in the desert, the storm, the man sitting beside a dead phone, and she held to it, because the alternative was a thing she could not hold.

She thought about calling Tucson herself. She could do it. She had the number; she had the whole apparatus of the long-distance system at her fingertips; she could ring that phone any hour of any day she chose. But to do it she would have to know what she would say if a voice answered, and to do it she would have to bear it if no voice did, and she found she could not face either thing alone, in the middle of the week, with no Eleanor on the other end to share the silence. So she did not call. She waited. She carried the two voices in her coat pocket, except that now one of them had gone quiet, and she took out the stone and turned it over and over and it was not smooth anymore. It had an edge.

On Wednesday she nearly told Mrs. Vance. She had some idea that Mrs. Vance, with all her years and her closed-drawer face, might know what to do, might have a procedure for it, the way the company had a procedure for everything. But when she stood at the door of the supervisor's office she understood that the only procedure Mrs. Vance had was the procedure for not caring, and that this was a procedure Loretta did not want to learn, even now, even with the edge of the stone cutting into her palm. So she said she had only wanted to ask about the schedule, and Mrs. Vance told her about the schedule, and Loretta went back to the board.

Sunday came the way Sundays come, slowly all week and then all at once.

She was at the board by half past seven. The evening traffic moved through her hands and she barely felt it. She watched the Hartwell cord pair. She watched the small dark bulb that had lit so faithfully every week for the better part of a year, and she found that she was praying, not in words, not to anyone she could have named, only the old animal prayer that is mostly just the body leaning toward what it wants.

Six minutes past eight.

The bulb lit.

"Number, please," Loretta said, and her voice shook, and she did not try to stop it shaking.

"Loretta?" Eleanor said. "Is that you? I asked for you. I asked the first operator if she could get me Loretta and she didn't understand what I meant, but then—is it you?"

"It's me."

"Did you—has the line been fixed? In Tucson? Did they fix it?"

And there it was, the question, handed to her across the board the way the call itself was handed across the country, hand to hand, and Loretta understood that she did not know the answer and that in a moment she would, that she had only to seat the plug and ring the number and the truth would come up out of the wire whether she wanted it or not.

"Let me try it for you," she said. "Let me put it through."

She seated the plug. Her hands knew the way. She rang the number, two-one-one-seven, two thousand miles, the bucket passed down the long row of operators she would never see, all of them connecting things to things across the whole dark breadth of the country, and somewhere at the end of it the phone began to ring in a room in Tucson, and Loretta did not take her thumb off the monitor key. She left it on. She listened. She would not leave Eleanor alone in it this time, whatever it was.

The phone rang once.

It rang twice.

There was a click, the particular click of a receiver lifted, and then a voice, and the voice said, "Eleanor."

Not a question. A confirmation. The world being, again, what it had been.

"Walter." Eleanor's voice broke on it, broke clean down the middle, and Loretta felt her own eyes go hot and full. "Walter, I called last week and you didn't—the operator tried three times and you didn't answer and I thought—"

"I know. I know it. I'm sorry. Eleanor, I'm sorry, I—I was in the hospital. It was nothing. It was the lungs again, they kept me a few days, they wouldn't let me up to the phone and I had no way to—I thought about you sitting there waiting and I couldn't do a thing about it. I'm all right now. They sent me home Friday. I'm all right."

"You were in the hospital."

"It was nothing. It's the dry air, it's supposed to be good for me, and mostly it is. I'm home now. I'm right here."

"You're right there," Eleanor said.

"I'm right here."

Loretta took her thumb off the monitor key.

She sat with her hands in her lap, which she was not supposed to do, and she let the tears go down her face because there was no one to see her do it and because they were not unhappy tears, or not only unhappy, but the other kind too, the kind that come when a thing you were braced against turns out not to fall on you after all. The line down in the desert. The man sitting beside the dead phone. She had been right, or right enough; the man had not stepped out for cigarettes, had not forgotten the time, had only been kept from the phone by his stubborn ruined lungs, and now he was home, and he was right there, and he had said his wife's name like a confirmation of the world.

She thought of all the things she did not know about them and would never know. She did not know what had happened between them that had put two thousand miles in the middle of their marriage. She did not know whether the calls were a bridge they were maintaining or a bridge they were slowly, kindly, taking down a plank at a time. She did not know whether, when his lungs finally gave out, as lungs like that finally do, Eleanor would go to him at the end or whether she would sit in Beaumont in a house she did not like coming back to and wait for a phone that would not ring at six minutes past eight on a Sunday ever again. She did not know any of it. She was a connection. A connection has no opinion about what it connects.

But she knew this much. She knew that for thirty-nine minutes every Sunday, two people who could not be in the same place reached across the whole dark width of the country and found each other, and that her hands were the hands that closed the circuit and made the finding possible. It was not nothing. Mrs. Vance could call it the traffic if she wanted. Loretta knew what it was. It was two voices saying I know it into the patient hum of all that distance, and a third voice, hers, saying go ahead, let me put it through.

At a quarter to nine the call ended, the way it always ended, the rates changing, the receivers settling into their cradles. The bulb above the Hartwell cord pair went dark.

Loretta wiped her face with the back of her hand and turned to the rest of the board, where the bulbs were lighting, all of them, the whole catastrophe and tenderness of it waiting to pass through her hands, and she said, to the next one, in a steady voice, "Number, please."
