They came up through the orchard in the gray light with the shovels over their shoulders and the dog trotting ahead of them through the rows. The trees stood in their long dying with the leaves gone curled at the edges and the fruit small and hard upon the limbs and some of the limbs bare altogether and gone to gray wood that would not leaf again. The older brother walked ahead and the younger came behind and neither spoke. The sun was not yet up over the eastern ridge but the sky there had begun to whiten and the cold of the night still lay in the low places along the ditch.

They stopped at the head of the line where the standpipe rose from the ground rusted and weeping at its joint and the older brother set down his shovel and stood looking at it.

It's froze up or it's broke, he said. One or the other.

It aint froze. Not in June.

I know it aint froze. I'm tellin you what it looks like.

The younger brother squatted and put his hand to the wet ground below the pipe and held the dirt and looked at it and let it fall.

It's broke down below, he said. Down past the elbow.

Then we got to dig it.

We got to dig it.

They dug. The dog lay off in the grass with its head on its paws and watched them and the sun came up over the ridge and lay long across the orchard and the dew steamed off the leaves. They dug down along the pipe taking turns and throwing the dirt up onto a tarp they had spread and when one tired he stepped out of the hole and the other stepped down into it and they did not speak of who would go and who would stop but only knew it by some long custom between them.

When they were down past their knees the older brother stopped and leaned on the shovel and wiped his face with his sleeve.

Pop dug this line in fiftytwo, he said.

I know when he dug it.

He dug it by hand. All of it. He and uncle Dell.

I know that too.

The older brother looked at him. You know everthing this mornin.

I know what you're fixin to say and I dont want to hear it.

You dont know what I'm fixin to say.

I know it.

The younger brother threw a shovelful up onto the tarp and the dirt slid and some of it fell back into the hole. He looked up out of the hole at his brother standing against the white sky.

Then say it, he said. Go on and say it.

The older brother did not say it. He looked off across the rows to where the trees went up the slope and stood ragged against the morning and then he took up his shovel and stepped down into the hole again and the younger brother climbed out.

They found the break at the elbow where the older brother had said it would not be and he did not say anything about it and the younger one did not either. The pipe had cracked along the seam and the water had been running out underground for who knew how long, weeks maybe, and the trees above it standing thirsty all that while with the water passing not three feet beneath their roots and them not getting any of it.

How long you reckon, the younger brother said.

I dont know.

A while.

A while, the older brother said.

They cleaned the break and the younger one went to the truck and brought back the coupling and the can of cement and the file. They sat on the edge of the hole with their feet hanging down into it and the older brother filed the broken ends square while the younger one read the directions off the can though neither of them needed the directions read.

It says let it set up twenty minutes.

We'll let it set up.

It says clean and dry.

It's clean. It aint dry. It aint never goin to be dry down there.

The younger brother set the can down. You think it'll hold if it aint dry.

It'll hold. It held forty year on a hairline crack. It'll hold.

He swabbed the cement on the ends and seated the coupling and held it and the smell of it came up sharp and sweet in the morning air. He held it the twenty minutes by the watch he took from his pocket and laid open in the dirt beside him and the younger brother watched him hold it and watched the watch and watched the dog that had got up and gone to nosing along the ditch after something it could smell and not find.

When it had set the older brother let go of it slow as a man letting go of something he did not trust to stand and they sat and looked at it.

We could get four hundred a acre, the younger brother said.

The older brother did not look at him. We been over this.

We aint been over it. You been over it. You been over it by yourself and decided it and you aint asked me nothin.

I'm askin you to dig a ditch. That's what I'm askin you.

The dog came back and lay down. The sun was up good now and the heat coming into the day and far off they could hear a truck on the county road and then they could not hear it anymore.

Garza offered three eighty, the younger brother said. Last fall. He'd go four. He'd go four and a half if you pushed him.

I aint pushin him nowhere.

You aint pushin him because you wont talk to him.

I wont talk to him.

The younger brother stood up out of the hole and stood looking down at his brother sitting there with the watch in the dirt and the can of cement and the file.

What are we savin it for, he said. You want to tell me that. Look at it. Look at these trees. They're done. They been done. Pop's done, he's been in the ground six years. Who are we keepin it for.

The older brother picked up the watch and wiped the dirt from the crystal with his thumb and put it in his pocket.

We aint keepin it for nobody, he said. We're just keepin it.

That aint a reason.

It's the reason I got.

The younger brother turned away and stood with his hands on his hips looking up the slope at the trees. When he turned back he had got the heat out of his voice and spoke quiet.

I got a family, he said. I got kids comin up. I cant be pourin what little I got into a piece of ground that aint give back a dime in five year. You see that. I know you see it.

I see it.

Then.

The older brother stood up and his knees popped and he stood a moment getting his legs back and then he climbed up out of the hole and stood beside his brother and the two of them looked up the slope together.

You remember when these was good, he said.

I remember.

You remember haulin to Deming. The whole bed of the truck full and us settin up on top of it ridin in and Pop singin that song he sang.

I remember the song.

What was it.

You know what it was.

The older brother smiled a little. I know what it was, he said. I just like to hear you say you remember it.

I remember it, the younger brother said. I remember all of it. That aint what this is about.

They went back to the truck and got the second coupling and the lengths of new pipe that the younger brother had bought at the cooperative in town and they laid them out along the line where the old pipe had gone soft and weeping at every joint and they began to dig out the old line section by section. The work was hard and the day got hot and they took off their shirts and hung them on the limb of a dead tree and the sun came down on their backs and the dirt got in the creases of their hands and dried there pale.

At noon they sat in the shade of the truck and ate the sandwiches the younger brother's wife had made and wrapped in wax paper and they drank water from a jug that had gone warm and they did not talk for a while. The dog came and the younger brother gave it the crust of his bread and it took it gentle from his hand and lay down to eat it.

You ever think about what she'd of wanted, the younger brother said. Mama.

I think about it.

She'd of sold it.

You dont know that.

I know it. She wanted Pop to sell it in seventyeight when the freeze took half the trees. You was there. You heard her.

I heard her.

She said it'd be the death of him. And it was. Wasnt it. Workin it. It wore him down to nothin and it'll do the same to us and for what. For dirt. For a piece of dirt that dont want us on it no more than we want to be on it.

The older brother wrapped the wax paper back around the half a sandwich he had not eaten and set it on the running board beside him.

It wants us on it, he said.

You dont believe that.

I dont know what I believe. But I know a piece of ground knows whose hands has been in it. I believe that.

That's just talk.

It might be. The older brother looked at his hands and turned them over and looked at the palms of them. It might be just talk, he said.

They worked through the heat of the afternoon and the older brother got down in the trench and the younger one fed him the pipe and they coupled the sections and laid them in and the new pipe lay white and clean in the brown earth like something that did not belong there yet, that had not been there long enough to belong. The dog moved with the shade as the shade moved. A pair of doves came and lit in the dead tree and sat there a while and then flew on.

Garza'd run cattle on it, the younger brother said from the lip of the trench. He told me. He'd push the trees and run cattle. It's good water rights. He dont care about the trees.

The older brother fitted a length and did not answer.

It'd be done then, the younger brother said. All of it. He'd dozer it and that'd be the end of it and you wouldnt have to look at it dyin no more. You ever think of that. That it might be a mercy. To not have to watch it.

The older brother straightened in the trench and looked up at his brother against the high blue sky with the sun behind him so that he could not see his face but only the dark shape of him.

A mercy to who, he said.

To you. To both of us.

The older brother bent again to the pipe.

You go on and think it's a mercy if you need to think that, he said. To get it done. I aint goin to argue you out of it. But dont call it a mercy to me. Call it what it is.

What is it then.

It's sellin, he said. It's just sellin. Folks sell things. It aint a sin and it aint a mercy. It's just sellin and a man can do it and live. I just dont want to.

Why dont you.

The older brother stood a long moment in the trench with the pipe in his hands and the water of it still dry and waiting and he looked off down the rows where the orchard ran on past where they had worked to where it gave out at the fenceline and beyond the fence the desert started and ran on flat and pale to the foot of the mountains.

Because there aint nothin else, he said. There aint nothin out there. There's just this. You take this away and what is there. Where do I put my hands.

You'd find somethin.

I aint lookin to find somethin. I had somethin.

He climbed up out of the trench and the two of them stood looking at the half of the line they had laid and the half that was yet to do and the sun had gone over and the light come slanting now and gold across the rows and the dead leaves of the trees glowed in it as if they were not dead at all but only resting.

We aint goin to finish it today, the younger brother said.

No.

We'll have to come back.

We'll come back.

The younger brother sat down on the edge of the trench and the older one sat down beside him and they let their feet hang and they were both of them tired through and the day going off the land slow the way it does in June.

I aint trying to take nothin from you, the younger brother said. I want you to know that. I aint after Pop's place to spite him or you nor nobody. I'm just lookin at the numbers and the numbers dont work. They aint worked in a long time. I been carryin some of it you dont even know about.

I know about it.

You dont.

I know about the note you took. At the bank. To cover the water last summer.

The younger brother was quiet.

How do you know about that.

The teller's Vera's girl, the older brother said. You think a thing like that stays quiet in a town that size.

You never said nothin.

What was there to say. You done it for the trees same as I'd of done it. I just didnt have it to do.

The younger brother looked down into the trench at the new pipe lying there.

I cant keep doin that, he said. That's all I'm sayin to you. I cant keep takin notes against my own house to water a orchard that aint never goin to come back. I got Marie and the kids to think of. I'd carry it forever if it was just me. You know I would. But it aint just me.

I know it aint.

The older brother put his hand on his brother's shoulder and left it there a moment and then took it away.

Let me have this season, he said. That's what I'm askin you. One more. We patch the line and we run the water one more season and we see what these trees do with a full drink in em for the first time in two year. And if they dont come back, if they set fruit and it dont color or it dont size, then come fall I'll call Garza myself. I'll call him and you wont have to. I'll sign whatever's put in front of me.

The younger brother looked at him.

You mean that.

I mean it.

You'll call him.

I'll call him. In the fall. If they dont come back.

The younger brother let out his breath slow and looked off at the mountains gone purple now with the sun behind them.

They aint goin to come back, he said. You know that. A tree this old, you cant bring it back with one season of water. It dont work that way.

Then I lose, the older brother said. And come fall I call Garza and we sell and you get your money out of it and I get mine and we go on. But I want the season. I want to of done it. I want to be able to say I give it the water and it still wouldnt come. I dont want to sell it thirsty. I dont want that on me.

The younger brother nodded slow.

All right, he said.

All right.

One season.

One season.

They sat a while longer and did not say anything and the dog got up and came and lay between them with its chin on the older brother's boot and the light went off the orchard row by row as the sun dropped behind the mountains and the cool came down out of the gap again the way it had come down at the start of the day so that the day was bracketed at both its ends by that same cool wind off the high country.

When it was near dark they got up and gathered the shovels and the file and the can of cement with the lid pressed back on it and they carried the tools to the truck and laid them in the bed. The unfinished trench lay open behind them in the dusk and the white pipe in it caught what light was left and held it.

We ought to of covered that, the younger brother said.

Itll keep till mornin.

Somethin could fall in it. The dog.

The dog aint goin to fall in no ditch he dug himself.

The younger brother almost smiled at that. He shut the tailgate and the latch caught with a sound that carried out across the still orchard and came back faint off the trees.

You comin to the house, he said. Marie's got a roast on.

The older brother stood looking back up the slope at the trees standing black now against the last of the light in the west.

I'll be along, he said. Go on. I want to set here a minute.

You'll set here in the dark.

I'll set here in the dark.

The younger brother stood with his hand on the door of the truck and looked at his brother and there was something he wanted to say and he did not know how to say it or whether it ought to be said and in the end he did not say it.

Dont stay too long, he said. It'll get cold.

I know it.

He got in the truck and started it and turned it around in the lane and the headlights swung across the rows and lit them stark and white for an instant, the trees and the dead leaves and the open trench and the older brother standing there, and then they swung away and the truck went off down the lane toward the house and its lights got smaller and the sound of it died and then there was nothing but the older brother and the dog and the orchard going into the dark.

He stood a while. Then he walked back up to the head of the line where the standpipe was and he reached down and turned the valve and far off down the line where they had coupled it that morning the water came into the new pipe with a sound like a thing waking and he stood and listened to it run and put his hand on the standpipe and felt it shudder and steady as the water took it and held.

It's holdin, he said. To no one. To the dog. To the trees.

The dog looked up at him and then put its head back down.

He stood there in the dark with his hand on the cold pipe and the water running under the ground beneath all those thirsty trees, running and finding the roots in the dark and the dark roots taking it, and he could not see any of it and he knew it was happening all the same, the way a man knows a thing in his hands that he can no longer see, and he stood there a long time and did not go to the house though the cold came down hard off the mountains and the dog whined once and was quiet and the stars came out over the orchard one and another until the whole black sweep of them stood overhead, and still he stood, listening to the water.
