The ferry left at eleven, and by then the harbour lights had become the only certain thing, the rest of the town folding back into the dark hills the way a hand closes. He stood at the stern with his coat collar up and watched the wake unspool behind them, phosphorescent, a long pale road that healed itself almost as soon as it was made.

He had been given a name at the terminal. A woman behind glass with a small brass lamp, the kind you might find in a chapel, had written it on a card and slid it through the gap. *Coroner's office, Vestfjord. Ask for Halvard.* As though the dead required introductions, a chain of acquaintance leading from one stranger to the next until you arrived at the face you were meant to know.

The strait was forty nautical miles. He had crossed it once before, decades ago, going the other way, with Anders asleep against his shoulder and their mother somewhere below in the cheap berths, seasick and praying in the language she only used when frightened. He had been eleven. Anders had been seven, and had drooled on the wool of his coat, and he had let him, because there was nothing else to do at that hour and on that water but let your brother sleep against you.

Now he tried to remember the weight of that head and could not. The body forgets first. It is the mind that insists on keeping things, badly, like a man hoarding photographs of people whose names have left him.

In the lounge a few passengers had arranged themselves around the heaters. A fisherman with hands like tools. Two girls sharing earphones, one wire each, leaning their heads together so the cord would reach. An old woman knitting something long and shapeless, the needles going in the firelight like a small engine that would never stop, that had perhaps been going for years and would carry on after she was gone, the wool feeding itself in from some inexhaustible source below the floor of the world.

He bought a coffee from the machine and did not drink it. He held it for the warmth and watched the black water through the salt-blind windows and thought about how you identify a person. By the face, if the face remains. By the hands. By a scar, a tattoo, the particular crookedness of a finger broken in childhood and never set right. Anders had broken his finger falling from the Pedersens' roof, the little one on the left hand, and it had set itself at an angle so that for the rest of his life — for the rest of the life that he had, however long that turned out to be — he could not lay his hand flat on a table. He used to perform this for people at parties. *Look*, he would say, *look what my brother did*, though it had not been his brother who did it, it had been gravity and the slick autumn shingles, but Anders preferred the version with a culprit in it. Anders always preferred the version with a culprit.

They had not spoken in nine years. He could account for the silence the way you account for a debt, knowing the principal and having long since lost track of the interest. There had been the matter of the house. There had been the matter of their mother's last months and who had been there for them and who had been, as Anders put it in the one letter he sent, *off living your life as though she were a subscription you'd cancelled*. There had been, underneath all of it, the older thing, the thing from the water and the years on the island, that neither of them had ever named because to name it would have been to agree it was real.

He had not answered the letter. That was the truth of it, the small ugly stone at the centre. He had read it twice and put it in a drawer with the warranties and the foreign coins and he had not answered it, and then a year had passed, and then it was impossible, the way it becomes impossible to enter a cold sea you have stood beside too long. You have to go in at once or not at all. He had not gone in.

A man came and stood near him at the rail outside, having followed him out, or having come for his own reasons. He was older, with a face the weather had been at for a long time, and he smoked one of those thin cigarettes that seem to belong to another century.

"You're going for the cold reason," the man said. Not a question. He must have seen it on him, the particular stillness of a man travelling toward a body. Such men recognise one another the way the recently bereaved do, by a frequency the rest of the world cannot hear.

"My brother. Maybe my brother."

"Maybe." The man considered this, drawing on the cigarette, the ember a small red eye in the dark. "There's a mercy in the maybe."

"Is there."

"While it's maybe he's both. Alive and not. You'll have him both ways until the morning." The man flicked ash into the wind, which took it instantly, completely. "Then you'll have him one way. And you'll be sorry you wished for it."

"I didn't wish for it."

"No," the man agreed. "But you wanted to know. Which is the same wish wearing gloves."

He let that sit. The water went by underneath them, ancient and indifferent, the same water that had carried him as a boy with his brother asleep against his shoulder, the very molecules of it perhaps, who could say, water being a thing that does not leave, that only circles, that rises into cloud and falls again as the rain on a roof a child slips from.

"I had a brother," the man said, when the silence had gone on long enough to require either company or solitude, and he had chosen, it seemed, company. "Drowned. Out past the skerries, the year I turned forty. They never found him. So you see I had the maybe forever. No one ever came to me with a card and a name." He said this without bitterness, as a man describes the weather of a region he has lived in all his life and no longer notices. "You're lucky in a way that won't feel like luck."

Then he was gone, back into the lit interior, and the door's brief opening let out a wedge of yellow that lay on the deck and was withdrawn. He stood alone again with the coffee gone cold in his hand and tipped it over the side, the dark liquid joining the darker water without a sound he could hear over the engines.

He went below to find his berth. The corridor narrowed and the ceiling lowered and there were the smells of all such places, diesel and brine and the floral chemical they used to mask them, and under that the human smell of sleep, of bodies surrendering for a few hours their guard against the deep beneath them. His cabin was barely a cupboard. A bunk, a basin, a porthole the colour of the inside of an eye. He lay down in his clothes.

He did not expect to sleep and so of course he slept, and dreamed, and in the dream he was on the island again, the summer island, the one their father had taken them to before their father stopped being a thing that happened to them and became instead a story they told with increasing economy until it was only a sentence, then only a word, then a silence with their father's shape. In the dream he and Anders were on the rocks at the low water and Anders was small, the broken finger not yet broken, the whole catalogue of their estrangement not yet begun, just a boy crouched over a pool with his hair in his eyes, saying *look, look at this one*, and lifting from the water in his cupped hands a thing that glistened and pulsed and that he could not see, however he leaned, however he tried, because in the dream his brother's hands were always turned slightly away, the contents always in shadow, *look*, Anders kept saying, *why won't you look*, and he woke with the word in his mouth and the engines changed beneath him, the great pitch of them dropping, and he knew without rising that they had entered the lee of the far shore and the worst of the crossing was behind.

It was not yet light. He went up. The deck was wet and the air had the iron taste of a country he did not know. Ahead the lights of Vestfjord were strung along the base of the mountains like something washed up, and above them the mountains themselves were beginning to declare their black shapes against a sky that was thinking about grey.

A woman was already at the rail. Not the knitting woman; younger, in a red coat that was the only colour in the whole grey composition of the morning. She glanced at him and away.

"You came up for the harbour," she said. "Everyone does. There's nothing to see but everyone comes up." Her accent placed her here, a local, returning.

"I haven't been here before."

"No. I'd have known you." She said it simply, the way people in small places say such things, the population of the known being finite and the unknown therefore conspicuous. "You're for the town or you're for the islands beyond?"

"The town. The coroner's office."

She nodded slowly and did not ask, and her not-asking was its own kind of kindness, a door left open that he was not obliged to walk through. They watched the lights come closer. A gull found the ferry and rode the air beside it, motionless relative to them, beating against the same wind that carried the ship, so that it hung there as if painted, as if it had always been there and always would be.

"My father did that work," she said after a while. "Not here. Down south. He used to say the hardest ones weren't the strangers. The strangers you could be gentle with, professional, you could give them that. The hard ones were when the family came and looked and you watched them decide." She paused. "Decide whether to know him. He said you could see it, the second where they chose. Some of them looked and you could tell they'd already decided no, before they came in the room, no, it isn't him, and then they had to unmake the no while you stood there. And some came in with the yes ready and had to look anyway because the rules required it, and they hated you for the rules." She turned her face into the wind. "He said the worst was the ones in the middle. The ones who genuinely didn't know. Who looked and looked and couldn't say. He said those ones broke something in the room that didn't go back together."

"And what did he do. For those ones."

"He waited," she said. "He said there was nothing to do but wait. That eventually the body tells you, or your own body tells you, you just have to be still long enough to hear it." She drew the red coat tighter. "He's dead now too. My father. So I suppose someone stood and decided about him. I wasn't there. I was somewhere else, being young." She said it the way one names a country one has been exiled from. *Somewhere else, being young.* "You'd be surprised how much of your life you spend somewhere else, being young, and then it's over and you weren't there for the things."

He thought of the letter in the drawer. *Off living your life as though she were a subscription you'd cancelled.* He thought he might tell this woman in the red coat about the letter, here at the rail in the last of the dark where confession was cheap and would dissolve at landfall like the wake, but the harbour was upon them now, the engines reversing, the great shuddering business of arrival, and the moment for it closed the way all such moments close, gently, completely, without anyone deciding.

The town at six in the morning was a place still deciding whether to be awake. He walked up from the quay past shuttered fronts, a bakery exhaling warmth and yeast through a vent, a single light in a window where someone old was perhaps unable to sleep and was watching the strangers come off the boat as the only theatre on offer. The coroner's office was a low building of grey brick set a little apart, as such buildings always are, as though the town wished to keep its dead at arm's length, neighbourly but not intimate.

Halvard turned out to be a large soft man with a beard going white in patches, who shook his hand with both of his and held it a moment longer than a handshake required, the way doctors do, the way men do who have learned that the body is sometimes the only language left.

"You've come a long way," Halvard said. "You'll want coffee. You'll say you don't but you do."

He took the coffee this time. They sat in a small office with a window onto a courtyard where a single birch stood, leafless, its bark peeling in long pale curls like a thing slowly undressing itself.

"I have to tell you how it is," Halvard said. "Before you see. So there's no shock that isn't necessary. He was in the water some time. The face is — the water takes the face, you understand. It softens it, it takes the things you'd know a man by. So I can't promise you the face. What I can give you is the rest. The hands. The build. Anything you remember that the water can't reach."

"His finger," he said. "His left hand. The little finger. It was broken when he was a boy and never set. It sat at an angle. He couldn't lay his hand flat."

Halvard wrote something on a pad. "Good," he said. "That's good. The bones keep their stories. The bones are honest in a way the rest of us aren't." He set down the pen. "There's something else you should know. He had papers on him, sealed, the kind that survive the water. There was a name. The name is your brother's name. So you understand the position. The name says one thing. But names travel. Men carry other men's papers, men give their names away or lose them or sell them. We've had it before. A name in a dead man's coat is a strong thing but it isn't the thing. You're the thing. That's why you're here. The state can read the name. Only you can read the man."

He looked into the coffee.

"There's no hurry," Halvard said. "He's not going anywhere now. That's the one thing about them. They've given up all their hurry." He smiled, not unkindly, an old joke worn smooth. "You take your time. When you're ready you come and find me."

But he was ready, or he would never be ready, those being the only two conditions on offer, and he said so, and Halvard rose with a grunt and led him down a corridor where the light was the flat shadowless light they keep for the dead, the light that admits of no softening, and through a door, and there it was, under a sheet on a steel table, a shape that had been a man and had a name in its coat that was his brother's name.

Halvard stood at the head of the table with his hand on the sheet and looked at him, asking with his eyes, and he nodded, and the sheet came back.

The face was as Halvard had promised. The water had been at it. It was a face the way a coin too long in circulation is a face, the features there but worn past the point of portraiture, the particular man gone out of it. He looked and felt the room do the thing the woman's father had described, the breaking thing, because he could not say. He looked at this softened ruined face and it was Anders and it was not Anders and it was every drowned man and it was no one, and he understood for the first time fully that he had come all this way not to recognise a face but to find out whether he could, whether nine years and a letter in a drawer had left him able, still, to know his brother, and the answer the face gave him was nothing, was silence, was the grey indifferent water that had made it.

"The hand," he said. His voice did not sound like his.

Halvard moved to the side and drew the sheet from the left arm, gently, the way you uncover a sleeping child you do not wish to wake, and there was the hand, pale and swollen and wrong, the hand of the drowned, and he made himself look at it, made himself look at the little finger.

It lay flat.

It lay flat against the steel, straight, unbroken, a perfectly ordinary finger that had never fallen from the Pedersens' roof, that had never been performed at a party, *look, look what my brother did.* It was not Anders's hand. Whoever this was, whatever name slept in his coat, he was not the boy who had drooled on the wool of a coat on a ferry forty years ago, he was not the brother of the man who stood looking at him now with something rising in his chest that he could not at once identify and that turned out, to his shame, to his enormous unbearable shame, to be relief, a relief so total and so animal that it buckled his knees, and Halvard's hand was on his arm at once, steadying, having seen this before, having seen all of it before.

"It's not him," he said. "The finger. It's straight. His was — it's not him."

Halvard nodded slowly and drew the sheet back over the hand, over the face, restoring to the man his anonymity, his maybe, returning him to the population of the lost, and somewhere out past the skerries another man would carry a maybe forever, would never be brought a card with a name on it, and this one too would be someone's, would be the answer to some other crossing, some other estrangement, some other letter in some other drawer.

"It happens," Halvard said. "Don't be sorry for what your body did. The body's faster than the heart. It knew before you did. That's all that was."

But he was not sorry for the relief. That was the worst of it, the thing he carried back down to the quay through the town that had decided now to be awake, the bakery open, the gulls down off the air and walking the cobbles like small grey citizens — he was not sorry, and the not-being-sorry told him everything about the nine years that the letter in the drawer had only hinted at. Because if it had been Anders, the relief would have been impossible. If it had been Anders he would have had to stand in that flat light and know, finally, completely, that he had let a year pass and then it had been too late, that he had stood beside the cold sea too long. And the relief he felt was the relief of a man who has been spared, at the last second, the knowledge of what he is.

Somewhere Anders was alive. Somewhere a man with a finger that would not lay flat was performing it at a party, or not, was an old man now or a sick one or a happy one, was carrying his own version with the culprit in it, and he was still reachable, still on the living side of the water, still a thing that could be written to. The drawer was not yet a grave. There was still a door, cold as the sea, that he might go into at once or not at all.

He stood at the quay where the ferry would take him back at noon, and he watched the strait, the same forty miles of grey water, and he thought about the man in the night who had said *maybe* was a mercy, who had said you'll have him both ways until the morning and then you'll have him one way and be sorry. But it had not gone like that. He had it neither way and both. He had a brother who was alive and unforgiven and unforgiving, which was its own kind of drowning, its own body in its own cold water, never found, the maybe stretching on past the skerries to the edge of sight.

He would write the letter. He told himself this, standing at the rail of the same ship going the other way, watching the wake unspool and heal, unspool and heal. He would write it and this time he would go into the sea at once. He told himself this with the particular conviction of a man who has been frightened, and who knows, in the part of himself that the body speaks for, the part that is faster than the heart and crueller, that he probably would not, that he would arrive home and put the resolution in the drawer with the warranties and the foreign coins, and that a year would pass, and then another, and the water would go on circling, rising into cloud and falling as rain on roofs that children slipped from, indifferent, patient, keeping nothing and losing nothing, healing every wound it made almost as soon as it was made.
