The ferry left at dusk and he stood at the rail watching the harbour lights detach themselves from the land, one by one, until the town behind him became only a smudge of yellow against the darkening hill. He had not slept the night before. He had driven seven hours north through country he did not know, past lakes the colour of slate, past the burned husk of a barn standing alone in a field, and now he was crossing water he had only ever seen on maps, the strait a black absence between two coasts.

His name was Adrian. He carried a single bag and inside the bag a photograph he had not looked at in eleven years.

The man at the morgue had been careful on the telephone. He had said: we believe it may be your brother, but we cannot be certain, the body has been in the water, you understand. And Adrian had understood, or had understood the words, which were not the same thing. He had written down the name of the town and the name of the dock and the time of the ferry on the back of an envelope and then he had sat in the kitchen for a long while with his hand flat on the table, as though steadying it against some movement only he could feel.

The strait was forty nautical miles. The crossing took the night.

He went below when the cold became too much. The lounge was nearly empty—a woman knitting something grey beneath a fluorescent tube, two men in oilskins asleep with their heads tipped back and their mouths open, a child wandering the aisle with the loose-jointed gait of the very young, touching the backs of seats. The ship's engines made a sound under everything, a low animal sound, and the floor trembled with it, and Adrian sat by a window that showed him nothing but his own face floating on the dark.

His brother's name was Daniel. Was. He stopped himself. He did not yet know which tense the world required.

There had been three years between them and for the first part of their lives that distance had been everything—Daniel older, Daniel ahead, Daniel always at the far edge of the field already doing the thing Adrian was only beginning to imagine. They had grown up in a house near the sea, a different sea, the southern one, warm and bright and indifferent, and their father had been a man who repaired boat engines and their mother a woman who had wanted to be elsewhere and had said so, gently, all her life, without ever leaving. Daniel had inherited the wanting. Adrian had inherited the staying. It was the simplest way to put it and like all simple things it left out almost everything.

He took out the photograph.

It was the two of them on the slipway, summer, Daniel perhaps sixteen, himself thirteen, both of them squinting, both of them brown, Daniel's arm slung around his neck in a grip that was half affection and half the threat of a headlock. Behind them the masts of small boats and the white glare of the water. He could not remember who had taken it. He could remember the heat of that arm, though, the weight of it, the way Daniel had smelled of salt and engine oil and the cigarettes he was not supposed to have. He put the photograph away.

The woman knitting looked up at him and then looked down again.

What he had told people, in the years between, was that they had simply lost touch. This was the word, touch, as though the connection between brothers were a physical thing that could be set down and walked away from and not picked up again. It was not the truth. The truth was a single evening in a rented flat in a city neither of them had been born in, the truth was money, and a woman, and words that could not be unsaid, the truth was Daniel's face going very still the way their mother's used to and then Daniel saying, quietly, all right then, and putting on his coat and going out into the rain, and Adrian standing in the doorway and not calling him back. The not calling him back. That was the thing Adrian had carried for eleven years, smooth and heavy as a river stone, the not calling him back.

He had heard things since. That Daniel had gone north. That he had worked the boats, the real boats, the deep-water trawlers that went out for weeks. That he had married and that the marriage had ended. That he drank. That he had stopped drinking. That he had a daughter somewhere. The information came secondhand, thirdhand, through cousins and old friends, and Adrian had received each fragment and filed it away without ever once lifting the telephone, because to lift the telephone would have been to admit that the silence was a thing he had built and could therefore unbuild, and it was easier, it was always easier, to believe that it had simply happened to them, like weather.

And now the strait. And now the night.

He went back up on deck. The wind had risen and the deck was wet and the lights of the ferry made a small bright bubble in the enormous dark and beyond that there was nothing, only the sense of water moving, the long swells lifting the ship and setting it down. He gripped the rail. Somewhere ahead, on a coast he had never visited, in a cold room, a body was waiting for him to give it a name or to take its name away.

He thought: if it is him, I will have to look at his hands.

He did not know where the thought came from. But he understood it. You could change a face—the water could change a face, the man on the telephone had been delicate about this—but the hands a man carries are his own. Daniel had a scar across the back of his left hand, a white seam, from a winch cable when he was nineteen. Adrian had not been there but he had heard about it, had heard their mother on the telephone saying the word stitches in a voice gone thin with distance. He would look at the left hand. If there was a scar he would know.

It seemed a terrible thing, to have reduced a brother to a scar. To be sailing through the dark toward the question of a scar.

A man came out onto the deck beside him, an older man in a heavy coat, and lit a cigarette with some difficulty in the wind, cupping it, and the brief flare lit his face and then was gone. They stood together for a while without speaking, the two of them, looking at the same nothing.

You're going across for the first time, the man said. It was not a question.

Adrian said that he was.

I can always tell, the man said. People who know the crossing don't stand out here. They know there's nothing to see. He drew on the cigarette. You watch for the lights of the other shore but they don't come up for hours yet. You're better off sleeping.

I can't sleep, Adrian said.

The man nodded as though this too were something he could always tell. He smoked. The ship rolled and they both adjusted their feet without thinking, the body learning the sea faster than the mind.

You have people over there, the man said.

Adrian considered the question. My brother, he said, and the word came out of him strangely, rusted, a word he had not spoken aloud in years.

The man said that was good, to have family. He said it the way people say things they no longer entirely believe but have decided are true on principle. He finished his cigarette and flicked it out into the dark, where the wind took it, a small orange seed flung into the water, and he said goodnight and went below, and Adrian was alone again at the rail.

My brother. He had not lied. There was no lie in it. But he had let the man believe a thing that was not the shape of the truth, had let him believe in a brother waiting on the far shore, a brother to be embraced, when what waited—if it waited at all—was a body on a steel table, and what he carried toward it was not love exactly but something older and more difficult, something for which he did not have a word, the way you do not have a word for the ache in a limb you lost years ago.

He remembered a thing he had not thought of in a long time.

They had gone out together once, the two of them, in their father's small boat, without permission, when Adrian was eleven. Daniel had wanted to reach an island that lay off the point, a low green island with a single ruined cottage on it, and they had taken the boat at first light and motored out across a calm sea and the island had come up slowly the way islands do, refusing to grow nearer for a long time and then suddenly there. They had beached the boat and walked through grass to their waists and Daniel had gone into the ruined cottage and stood in it as though he owned it, in the roofless room with the sky for a ceiling, and he had turned to Adrian and said: this is where I'm going to live. When I'm grown. Right here. And Adrian had believed him, completely, the way you believe your older brother when you are eleven, and he had felt a grief he did not understand, because already in the saying of it there was a leaving, already Daniel was somewhere Adrian could not follow, in a house with no roof on an island off the point, alone, and happy to be alone.

They had eaten bread they had stolen from the kitchen and Daniel had shown him how to find fresh water in a hollow of rock, and the day had gone on and on the way days did then, enormous and slow, and when the wind got up in the afternoon they had been afraid, both of them, getting the boat off the beach, the swell pushing them back, and Daniel had jumped into the water to his chest to shove them clear and Adrian had seen, for one moment, that his brother was afraid, that the fear was not something only younger brothers carried, and that had frightened him more than anything, the fear in Daniel's face, and then it was gone, Daniel was over the side and at the tiller and laughing, his hair plastered to his head, and the island shrank behind them and was an island again, indifferent, green, and they never spoke of it, the fear, but Adrian had not forgotten it.

He wondered if the body would carry that. The fear. Whether you could see it, in the end, on a man's face, the thing he had been afraid of all his life rising up at last to meet him in the water.

The man on the telephone had not said how Daniel died. If it was Daniel. He had said only that the body had been recovered, that there had been no identification on it, that they had reason to believe, and the reason he had not given, and Adrian had not asked. He had not asked because to ask would have been to want to know, and he did not yet know whether he wanted to know, whether knowing was a thing he could survive. He thought of Daniel in the water. He thought of the trawlers, the deep cold water beyond the strait, the long swells he was riding now, and he thought: how does a man who knows the sea so well end in it. And then he thought: how does any of us end anywhere but where we have been heading all along.

He went below again because the cold had got into him.

The lounge had emptied further. The two men in oilskins were gone, off to some bunk or some other deck. The woman with the grey knitting was still there but the knitting had fallen into her lap and she was asleep, her chin on her chest, and the child was asleep too, curled across two seats with a coat over it, and Adrian sat down across the aisle and watched the child sleep, the small chest rising and falling, the hand thrown out, and he thought of the daughter. The daughter he had heard about, the one Daniel was said to have somewhere. He did not know her name. He did not know if she existed. If the body was Daniel's then somewhere there was a girl who would have to be told, a girl who had a father one day and none the next, and Adrian thought that perhaps it would fall to him, the telling, that this was perhaps the true shape of the thing he was sailing toward—not the body but its consequences, the people the body left behind, the whole slow ruin of a death spreading outward like the wake of the ship through the dark water.

He had no children. He had married once and it had ended quietly and there had been no children and he had told himself this was a thing he had chosen, but tonight, watching the child sleep across the aisle, he was not sure he had chosen anything, not the marriage's ending, not the silence with his brother, not any of it; it seemed to him suddenly that he had simply stood in doorways all his life and watched people go out into the rain and had called himself a man who chose staying when in truth he was only a man who could not move.

The not calling him back.

He took out the photograph again and held it under the fluorescent light and looked at his brother's face, sixteen years old, squinting into the sun, that arm around his neck, and he tried to find in that face the man it had become, the man who worked the deep boats and married and unmarried and drank and stopped and fathered a girl and disappeared into a strait, and he could not. The boy was only a boy. The boy on the slipway did not know any of it was coming. He grinned at the camera with his whole self, with the careless completeness of someone who believes the day is endless and the island reachable and the brother beside him a fixed star, and Adrian wanted to reach into the photograph and warn him, or thank him, or simply hold the arm that was around his neck and keep it there, keep it from ever lifting away.

He must have slept. He had not believed he could but the body takes what it needs and he woke with his neck wrenched sideways and the photograph fallen to the floor and the grey light of morning in the windows. The child was gone. The woman was gone. Someone had turned off the fluorescent tubes and the lounge was lit only by the dawn, and Adrian sat up and his mouth was dry and for a moment he did not know where he was, only that the engines had changed their note, had slowed, and that the floor no longer trembled in the same way, and he understood that they were coming in.

He retrieved the photograph and put it away and went up on deck into a cold that took his breath.

The other shore was there. It had come up in the night the way the island used to, refusing and refusing to grow nearer and then suddenly present, a long low coast of dark hills with snow in the high creases of them and a town spread along the water, the houses pale, the harbour full of boats, and gulls already wheeling out to meet the ferry, crying their thin cries, and Adrian stood at the rail and watched the land assemble itself out of the dawn and felt his heart go strange in his chest. Somewhere among those pale houses was the cold room. Somewhere was the table and the man from the telephone and the question, lying there, waiting.

The water in the harbour was very still. The ship moved through it slowly, and the engines reversed, and the gap between the hull and the dock narrowed, men in heavy coats moving on the dock to take the lines, and there was the smell of the place, fish and diesel and cold stone, a smell so like the harbour of his childhood that for a moment the years folded over and he was a boy again, waiting on a slipway for his brother to come back across the water.

He was among the first off. He carried his bag down the ramp and the cold of the new shore came up through his shoes and he stood on the dock not knowing which way to go, and a woman was waiting at the foot of the ramp with a clipboard, not for him, for the freight, and he asked her the way to the town and she pointed up a road that climbed between the pale houses and he thanked her and began to walk.

The town was waking. A man was hosing down the front of a shop. A dog crossed the road ahead of him without hurry. The streets ran uphill and the harbour fell away below him and the strait beyond it, grey now in the early light, the strait he had crossed, looking from here like a thing that could be crossed easily, a short grey distance to the far hills, nothing at all, when he had spent the whole night on it, the whole black night of his life, it seemed to him, getting from one shore to the other.

He found the building because the man on the telephone had described it. A low grey building set back from the road with a flag on a pole, the flag not moving in the still morning. There were steps. He stood at the bottom of the steps for a long time. A car went past behind him. Somewhere a child was calling out, a high happy sound, calling a name he could not make out, and the sound went on for a while and then stopped.

He thought of the island. The ruined cottage with the sky for a roof. This is where I'm going to live. He thought of his brother in the water with the cold coming up around him. He thought of the scar on the back of the left hand, the white seam, and he understood that in a few minutes he would either find it there or not, and that everything depended now on a few inches of skin he had never seen, only heard about, only imagined, across all the years of his not asking and his not calling.

If it was Daniel he would have to look at the hands.

If it was not Daniel then his brother was somewhere still in the world, alive, on some other shore, and the silence between them was still a thing that could be broken, still a door he could walk through instead of standing in, and Adrian was astonished to find, standing at the bottom of the grey steps in the cold morning of the far country, that he did not know which he wanted. That he had crossed the whole strait without knowing. That a part of him, a part he was ashamed of and could not silence, was hoping the body would be a stranger's, so that Daniel might be returned to him not as a corpse but as a possibility, a man he might yet telephone, a man he might yet say to: I should have called you back, that night, I have wanted to call you back for eleven years.

And another part of him, older and quieter, knew that the dead are easier than the living. That a brother on a table asks nothing of you but a name. That a brother alive on a far shore would ask everything, would require the whole slow labor of return, the years of it, the awkwardness and the apology and the building back of what had taken one rainy evening to break, and that this was harder, far harder, than grief, and that he was not certain, standing there, that he was equal to it.

He thought: whichever it is, I have already failed him. The failing is already done. It was done eleven years ago in a doorway in the rain and nothing on the table in that cold room can undo it or complete it. There is only this, now, the going up the steps.

He went up the steps.

At the top there was a door, grey like everything, and a bell, and he pressed the bell and heard it sound somewhere within, far off, in the building's depths, and he stood with his bag in his hand and waited, and the morning was very quiet around him, the gulls gone quiet, the child gone quiet, only the cold and the pale light and the strait below him going on toward the hills, and he waited to be let in, to be taken down into the cold room, to be shown the hands, to learn at last, on this far shore, after the long crossing, which of his brothers he had come all this way to meet—the dead one, who would forgive him by saying nothing, or the living one, who never would.

He heard footsteps approaching the door from the other side.
