The hotel had been rebuilt to look like itself, which is to say it had been rebuilt to look like nothing at all, and this was the first thing Marian noticed when the cab let her off under the porte-cochère: that they had gotten the color of the stucco exactly right, that particular dun that was neither beige nor gray nor the color of anything you could name, the color of a photograph left in a window. She stood with her overnight bag and looked at it. A bellman came toward her with the rolling cart and she waved him off, which she would later understand to have been a kind of refusal, the first of several, though at the time it felt only like not wanting to be touched.

She had not told anyone she was coming. That is not precisely true. She had told the bride, who was the daughter of a woman Marian had known in another life, the life that had a hotel in it, and the bride had said oh you must come, you must, in the way of brides, who believe for a period of months that the world has been arranged for the purpose of their gathering, and Marian had said yes she would come, and then had not thought about it, or had thought about it the way you think about a tooth that has not yet begun to hurt, running the tongue over it, testing.

The fire had been in 1987. It was now a span of years she preferred not to total, because the totaling produced a number that seemed to belong to someone older than she felt herself to be, and she had learned that the discrepancy between the number and the feeling was itself a kind of trap, a place you could fall into and not climb out of. She was forty-nine. She had been twenty-three. The chambermaid she had been was a person she could describe but could not quite inhabit, the way you can describe a house you grew up in, the placement of the rooms, the smell of the hallway, without being able to walk through it.

The lobby was cool and smelled of lilies, which were everywhere, in arrangements taller than children, because of the wedding. She had forgotten that hotels smelled like this now, that there had been a decision made somewhere, in some corporate room, that a hotel should smell like a funeral, and she stood for a moment in the smell and let it do what it did, which was to make her think of her mother, who had died in a different state of a different thing entirely, and then she went to the desk and gave her name.

The girl at the desk was perhaps twenty. She had the high gloss of someone who has been trained, who has been told to make eye contact and to use the guest's name, and she used Marian's name twice in the first sentence, Ms. Holloway, welcome, Ms. Holloway, and Marian watched her fingers on the keyboard and thought about how the desk had been over there, against the other wall, before, and how the woman who had worked the desk before, a Filipina named Dolores who wore a gold cross and kept hard candy in a drawer, had died, not in the fire, she had not been working the night of the fire, but later, of something ordinary, and Marian had read it in a Christmas card from someone and had been surprised by how much it had cost her, the reading of it.

"You're here for the Aldous wedding," the girl said.

"I am."

"It's going to be beautiful. The whole fourteenth floor." The girl smiled. "Have you stayed with us before, Ms. Holloway?"

There was no malice in it. There was nothing in it at all. It was a question on a screen, a prompt, and Marian understood that she was being given an opening, the kind of opening that life occasionally provides and that we almost always decline, and she heard herself decline it.

"No," she said. "Never."

The girl gave her a key in a small cardboard sleeve and told her the room number and told her the elevators were just past the lilies, and Marian thanked her and crossed the lobby with her bag, past the lilies, and pressed the button, and while she waited she looked up at the ceiling, which was new, which was high and coffered and lit from within, and she thought: this is where it was not. This is exactly where the lobby was not, the old lobby, the low one, the one with the popcorn ceiling that had rained down in flakes when the trucks shook the building. The new ceiling floated above the absence of the old one like a thing that did not know what it was floating above.

Her room was on the eighth floor. The fire had started on the ninth. She had been told the room number on the phone, weeks ago, by a reservations agent who could not have known and would not have cared, and she had written it down on the back of an envelope and had not thought to ask for another, and now in the elevator she watched the numbers light and go dark, two three four, and she thought about asking, when she got upstairs, to be moved, and she knew she would not ask, because to ask would be to explain, and to explain would be to become, in the eyes of whoever she explained it to, a woman with a story, and she had spent a great deal of the intervening years not being that.

The eighth floor hallway was carpeted in a pattern of interlocking shapes that the eye could not resolve, a deliberate pattern, she understood, designed by someone who understood that the human eye, given a pattern it cannot resolve, will not look down and see the stains. The old carpet had been red. She remembered the red carpet, and the brass numbers on the doors, and the housekeeping closet at the end of the hall where the linens were stacked and where she had stood, that night, with her cart, when she first smelled it.

She let herself into the room. It was a room. It was every room. The bedspread was the rust color that does not show wear, and there was a painting over the bed of a sailboat, or a thing that suggested a sailboat without committing to it, and there was the desk and the chair and the lamp and the window that did not open more than four inches, a thing they had instituted everywhere, she had read, after a number of incidents, and she set her bag on the bed and went to the window and looked out at four inches of air and at the parking structure across the way and at the slice of hills beyond it, brown, the same brown as the stucco, the brown of everything here, and she stood there a long time.

She had not screamed. This was the thing she returned to, in the years, not often but in the small hours, when she returned to anything. She had not screamed and she had not frozen. She had done what she had been trained to do, which was almost nothing, because the training was a single afternoon and consisted mostly of a man named Gary telling them where the extinguishers were, and she had not used an extinguisher, there had been no question of an extinguisher, but she had done the other thing, the thing that was not in the training, which was that she had gone down the hall knocking on doors. She had gone down the ninth floor hallway in the smoke knocking on doors and calling out in a voice she did not recognize as her own, a flat loud voice, fire, fire, get out, and people had come out, in their underthings, in their hotel robes, a man with shaving cream on half his face, a woman clutching a small dog, and she had pointed them toward the stairs, the stairs she could not see but knew, because she had walked that hallway a thousand times with her cart, and she had gotten to the end of the hall, to 914, and she had knocked, and there had been no answer, and she had knocked again, and the smoke was coming up through the floor now, she could feel it warm through her shoes, and she had not had a key, the master key, she had left it in the closet, and she had stood at 914 and knocked and called and then she had gone down the stairs because she could not stand there any longer, because the body has a vote, the body votes and it cannot be overruled, and she had gone down the stairs.

There had been a man in 914. He had not gotten out. His name had been Wallace Pruett and he had been sixty-one and from Sacramento and he had been, the papers said, a salesman, and she had read everything there was to read about Wallace Pruett, in the weeks after, she had read his name so many times it had stopped being a name, and the coroner had said smoke, not fire, that he had likely been overcome in his sleep, that he had likely not known, and Marian had held onto that, that he had likely not known, the way you hold onto a railing, and she had also known, in the place where you know things you do not say, that the coroner could not have known whether he knew, that the coroner had said it the way the reservations agent had said the room number, as a thing that fit in the available space.

The investigation had found that the fire had begun in a wiring chase, that it was no one's fault, which is to say it was the fault of the building, of the year the building was built and the materials it was built from and the inspections it had passed, and the hotel had settled with the families, with Wallace Pruett's family, his sister, and the hotel had closed, and then after some years the hotel had reopened, rebuilt, and Marian had read about that too, the reopening, in a trade publication she had no reason to read except that she had gone on reading about the hotel the way you go on probing the tooth, and there had been a photograph of the new lobby with its floating ceiling, and a quote from a man who said they were proud to restore a landmark to the community, and Marian had sat with the publication in her lap for a long time, in a different apartment, in a different decade, before she had thrown it away.

She unpacked the dress. It was navy, because she had decided years ago that navy was the color a woman wore when she did not wish to be looked at and did not wish to be seen not wishing to be looked at, a color that asked nothing, and she hung it in the closet, which had no door, only an alcove and a brass rail and the little hangers with the fixed hooks that you could not steal, and she ran a bath, because there was time, the wedding was at six and it was three, and she got into the bath and lay there in the water and listened to the building.

The building made no sound. This was the thing. The old building had made sounds, the old building had been alive with its own dying, the pipes had knocked and the elevators had groaned and the wind had found the gaps in the windows, and you had known, working in it, that it was a thing that had been made by hands and was coming apart by degrees, and this new building made no sound at all, the water came on silently and the air came from somewhere with no sense of effort, and she lay in the water and understood that they had built a building that did not admit to being a building, and that this was the point, that the point of the new building was to be a building that could not burn, that had never burned, that stood on the site of nothing.

She got out of the bath and dressed and did her face in the mirror that was lit too brightly, that showed her everything, and she went down to the wedding.

The fourteenth floor was a ballroom, or had been made into one, with a foyer and then the room itself, and the lilies had migrated up here, and there were people in clothes that cost money standing holding glasses, and a string quartet was tuning, and Marian took a glass of champagne from a tray because it gave her hands something to do and she stood at the edge of it, near the windows, which up here were larger, which went nearly to the floor, and she looked out at the city, which was lit now, the early dark coming on, and she thought about the height. The fourteenth floor. There was no thirteenth floor, of course, there was never a thirteenth floor, and so the fourteenth floor was the thirteenth floor, and she stood on the thirteenth floor that was called the fourteenth and looked down at the city and thought about how high it was, how if there were a fire here you would not knock on doors, there would be no doors to knock on, there would be only this room and the windows that opened four inches and the long way down.

The bride found her. The bride was radiant in the way of brides, which is a real thing, a real radiance, not a figure of speech, the skin of a young woman on the day someone has decided to marry her, and she took Marian's hands and said you came, you came, I'm so glad, and Marian looked at her and saw her mother in her, the mother Marian had known, and felt the thing you feel, the vertigo of time, the daughter standing where the mother stood, and she said of course I came, you look beautiful, and the bride said do you remember when, and told a story about a Christmas, a story Marian half remembered, and then the bride was pulled away, as brides are, into the machinery of the evening, and Marian was alone again at the window.

A man came and stood near her. He was older, gray, in a suit that fit him, and he held a glass of what was not champagne, something brown, and he looked out at the city with her for a while before he spoke.

"Bride's side or groom's side," he said.

"Bride's. An old friend of her mother's."

"Groom's," he said. "Uncle. The uncle nobody knows what to do with." He said it without self-pity, as a fact, and Marian liked him for it, a little. "You from here?"

"I was. A long time ago."

"It's changed," he said, which is what people say, and she said yes, it has, which is what you say back, and they stood there, and then he said, gesturing with the brown drink at the room, at the building, "You know this place used to be something else."

She turned and looked at him.

"This hotel," he said. "Used to be the old Carlton, before that. There was a fire. Big fire, back in the eighties. They tore the whole thing down, built this." He took a sip. "Somebody died, I think. I had a buddy who was a fireman then, used to talk about it. Worst kind of call, he said. The hotel calls."

"Yes," Marian said.

"You remember it? You said you were from here."

And here it was again, the opening, life providing it, holding it out the way the bride had held out her hands, and Marian looked at the man, this uncle nobody knew what to do with, who had said somebody died, I think, who had reduced Wallace Pruett to a clause in a story about his fireman buddy, and she understood that she could say it, that she could say I was there, I was the chambermaid, I went down the hall knocking on doors, and that the man would be galvanized, that he would set down his brown drink and turn to her with his whole body and say my God, and that the story would pass from her to him and become his story, the story of the woman he met at the wedding who had been there, and she would have given it away, the one thing, she would have let it out of her hands and watched it go into the world to be told at other weddings, and she found that she could not, that she would not, that the refusal she had begun under the porte-cochère completed itself here, at the window, fourteen floors up.

"I remember there was a fire," she said. "I don't remember much about it. I was young."

"Right," he said. "Sure. Long time ago." And he drank his drink, and the moment closed over like water, and presently someone called the room to order, and they took their seats for the ceremony.

The ceremony was on the fourteenth floor with the city behind the couple, going dark, the lights coming up across it, and the bride came in on the arm of her father and the words were said and the vows were said and Marian watched and felt the thing you feel, which is grief, though no one calls it that, the grief that lives inside every wedding, the grief of the irreversible, the door closing on all the other lives, and she thought, watching the bride's face, that the bride did not know yet that this was what she was feeling, that it would take her years, that one day in a hotel somewhere the bride would understand that she had been grieving on her wedding day and would not be able to explain it to anyone, and Marian wished she could spare her that, and knew she could not, and the couple kissed and the room rose to its feet and applauded and Marian rose with it and applauded too.

At the dinner she was seated with strangers, people her own age, the friends of the mother of the bride, and they talked about their children and their knees and the drive in, and Marian said the things you say, and ate the chicken, and watched the dancing begin, and then, while the band played and the floor filled, she rose and excused herself and went out into the foyer and to the elevators, and she pressed the button, and she did not press eight.

She pressed nine.

The ninth floor was guest rooms, like the eighth, like all of them, and the hallway was the same unresolvable carpet and the same brass numbers, and Marian stepped out of the elevator into the silence of it and stood, and she understood that the geography would be wrong, that they had not rebuilt the rooms in the same places, that the hallway that lay before her did not correspond to the hallway she had walked, that 914 was not 914, that there was no 914, perhaps, or that it was a closet, or that it lay in the middle of a room where a stranger was at this moment asleep or watching the television, and she walked anyway, slowly, down the hall, past the doors, 902, 904, reading the numbers, and the carpet was soft and gave nothing back, and the air came silently from somewhere, and she walked to the end of the hall where the closet had been, where she had stood with her cart, and there was a door there, unmarked, with a slot for a key card, a service door, and she stood in front of it.

She did not know what she had come up for. She had thought, getting into the elevator, that she had come up to stand where it had been, to mark it somehow, to perform the thing you are supposed to perform, but standing here in the wrong hallway over the new floor she understood that there was nothing to mark, that the place was gone, that it had been carried away in trucks and that what stood here now had never known Wallace Pruett, had never known the red carpet or the popcorn ceiling or the girl she had been, that the building had been absolved by its own destruction, made innocent, and that she was standing in a hotel that had no idea what had happened on the site where it stood, and that this was either a mercy or an obscenity and she could not decide which, had never been able to decide which, in all the years.

She put her hand flat against the service door. The metal was cool. Below her, fourteen floors of building, and then the ground, and then the place where the ground had always been, where the old lobby had been, where the trucks had shaken the flakes from the ceiling, where it had all come down. She kept her hand on the door and she said his name, once, quietly, into the silent hallway, Wallace Pruett, the way you would say it to introduce someone, to bring them into a room, and the hallway took it and gave nothing back, and after a while she took her hand off the door and went back down the hall to the elevator and pressed the button and rode down to the party, where the bride was dancing, where no one had noticed she was gone, and she got herself a glass of champagne and stood at the edge of it and watched, and in the morning she checked out, and gave her name, and the girl at the desk, a different girl, said safe travels, Ms. Holloway, and Marian said thank you, and went out under the porte-cochère into the brown morning, and did not look back at the stucco, which she already knew the color of, which was the color of nothing, which was exactly right.
