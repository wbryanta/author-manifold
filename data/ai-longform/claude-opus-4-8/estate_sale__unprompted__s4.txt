The card table came first, which felt wrong to Margaret, though she could not have said why a card table should go before anything else. A man in a Carhartt jacket carried it out under one arm, the way you'd carry a cookie sheet, and set it in the bed of his truck without folding the legs, so that it rode away looking like an animal lying on its back. Four dollars. She had marked it herself that morning with the little orange stickers from the dispenser, pressing the price onto the underside of the table where it would not show. Her mother had played solitaire at that table every evening for the last six years of her life, the radio tuned to the AM station out of Mansfield that still read the grain prices and the obituaries.

"You doing okay?" said the woman from the estate company. Her name was Donna and she had a lanyard and a fanny pack heavy with change and she had done this, she'd told Margaret, four hundred times. She said it the way other people said they'd been married thirty years, with a weariness that was also a kind of pride.

"I'm fine," Margaret said.

"You don't have to stay. Lots of people don't. They give me the key and they go get lunch in the city and come back when it's over."

"I'll stay."

Donna nodded and did not push it, which Margaret appreciated. She had decided to stay the way you decide to look at a wound. There was a part of her, the sensible part that had paid the property taxes and arranged the hospice and signed for the cremation, that knew the staying was sentimental and probably masochistic and accomplished nothing. But she had driven six hours from Pittsburgh and she was not going to sit in a Bob Evans eating a patty melt while men she'd never met carried her childhood out the door. If it was going to happen she would watch it happen. That seemed like the only honest thing.

The house had opened at nine. By ten the good stuff was already moving — the term Donna used, the good stuff, as if there were a moral grade to objects. The good stuff was the cherry sideboard and the set of Haviland and the two wing chairs that had flanked the fireplace in a room nobody had used except at Christmas. Margaret had taken nothing from the house except her mother's wedding ring, which she wore now on her right hand because it did not fit her left, and a shoebox of photographs she had not yet looked through. Her sister Carol, in Tucson, had asked for the sideboard, and then had not arranged to ship it, and then had stopped answering, and so the sideboard was for sale like everything else, and Margaret had decided not to feel anything about that either.

She stood in the kitchen, which was the room where she felt least like a trespasser, and watched the front hall through the doorway. People moved through the house with a frank acquisitiveness that she found, against her expectations, almost comforting. They were not pretending. A heavy woman in a church sweatshirt was going through the kitchen drawers two feet from her, lifting out the wooden spoons, the meat thermometer, the rubber-banded stack of takeout menus from restaurants that had closed.

"These spoons go?" the woman asked.

"Everything goes," Margaret said.

"How much?"

"I think — there's stickers."

"Ain't no sticker on the spoons."

Margaret looked. There was no sticker on the spoons. "A quarter," she said, inventing it, and the woman gave her a quarter, dropped it into Margaret's open palm as though Margaret were the cashier, which in a sense she now was, and Margaret closed her hand around the warm coin and did not know what to do with it. There was a cash box in the front room with Donna. She put the quarter in her cardigan pocket. It was the first money anyone had handed her directly, and it stayed in that pocket all day, and she found it that night in the motel and set it on the nightstand and looked at it for a long time.

The spoons had been her mother's, but before that they had been her grandmother's, the wooden ones worn pale and concave on one side from sixty years of stirring against the same wall of the same pot. You could not put a sticker on that. You could not even explain it to the woman in the church sweatshirt, who was not unkind, who was only buying spoons. Margaret understood that the worn place in the spoon was visible only to her, that to everyone else in the world it was simply a defect, a thinning, a spoon that had been used and would be used again or thrown out, and that this was the truth of nearly everything in the house. The objects had been saturated with meaning by exactly one family, and that family was now Margaret, standing alone in the kitchen, the last reservoir of the knowledge that the burn mark on the counter by the stove was from the night her father came home from the second shift in 1971 and set the hot pan down without thinking, and her mother had cried about it, actually cried, over a countertop, which Margaret had not understood at the time and understood completely now.

She went out onto the back porch to get away from the spoons.

The backyard was smaller than it lived in her memory. This was the cliché everyone warned you about and it was still a shock. The yard had been the whole world once. There had been a swing set where the bare patch was, and beyond the fence the Hendersons' field, which was now a cul-de-sac of vinyl-sided houses she did not recognize. The clothesline was still there, the metal T-posts her father had set in concrete, though the lines themselves were gone. She remembered the sheets on that line, the specific smell of them, cold and clean and faintly of the green plastic clothespin bag, and she remembered being small enough to walk between the hanging sheets as though through rooms, the white walls billowing, the light coming through them, and how her mother would be on the other side and Margaret would press her hand against the sheet and her mother would press back from the other side, palm to palm through the cloth, and they would laugh.

"There's a guy asking about the freezer in the basement," Donna said from the doorway. "It runs?"

"It runs," Margaret said. "It's full of food."

"He don't want the food."

"No. I don't suppose he does."

"You want me to deal with the food? We can throw it. Lots of times we just throw it."

The food in the freezer. Margaret had opened it that morning, the big white chest freezer that had stood in the basement her entire life, and inside it was her mother's whole economy: the casseroles in foil, labeled in shaking ballpoint on masking tape, BEEF & NOODLE, CHICKEN DIVAN, with dates. The dates went up to about four months ago and then stopped, which was when her mother had gone into the hospital the first time and not really come back out. There were freezer-burned bags of corn her mother had probably bought on sale. There was a turkey. There was, Margaret had discovered, an entire wedding cake's worth of frozen cake in a Cool Whip container, the top tier of somebody's wedding, and Margaret did not know whose. Not hers; she'd never married. Not Carol's; Carol had eloped. Some neighbor's, maybe, some gift, some saved thing, frozen for an anniversary that the freezer had outlived.

"I'll deal with the food," Margaret said. "Tell him he can have the freezer but he has to come back for it. He can't take it now."

Donna looked at her. "He's here now."

"Then he can come back. Tomorrow. I'll be — somebody'll be here." This was not true. The house would be empty and shut by tomorrow, the keys handed to the realtor. But Margaret could not let the freezer go out the door full, and she could not stand in the driveway tonight throwing her mother's casseroles into a dumpster while strangers watched, and so she lied, easily, the way you lie to buy yourself one more night, and Donna shrugged and went to tell the man, who did not, in the end, come back.

Inside, the house was emptying in patches, like snow going off a field. The wing chairs were gone. The space where they had been was marked by two pale rectangles in the carpet and a scatter of crumbs and lint and one bobby pin that had lived under a chair for who knew how long. A teenager and his father were carrying the dining table, tilted on its edge, and the father was saying *easy, easy, you're gonna gouge the jamb*, and they got it through the door and Margaret watched the table — the table where she had done her homework, where the family had eaten every dinner of her childhood in the particular silence that she had thought was normal until she went to college and ate at other people's tables and heard them talk — she watched it go out into the gray Ohio afternoon and be slid into the back of a Honda Odyssey with the seats folded down.

She had thought she would cry and she did not. That surprised her. What she felt instead was a strange lightening, almost a vertigo, as each thing left. It was as if the objects had weight not only in the physical sense but in some other register, and as they were carried out the house grew not just emptier but less dense, less able to hold her, so that she felt she might float up off the floor. She had read once that the body of a dying person was measured to lose twenty-one grams at the moment of death, the supposed weight of the soul, and that it was nonsense, debunked, a single bad experiment from a hundred years ago, but standing in the hollowing house she thought that whoever did that experiment had simply been measuring the wrong thing. It was not the body that lost weight. It was the world around the body. Her mother had died in March and the weight had been going out of the world ever since, and today it was going out all at once, in the arms of strangers, four dollars at a time.

A young couple was in the back bedroom — Margaret's old room, though it had been a sewing room for thirty years — looking at the iron bed frame. The woman was visibly pregnant. They were young, younger than Margaret had ever been, it seemed, and they were talking quietly and the woman ran her hand along the iron rail and said something and the man laughed and Margaret realized they were imagining it in a room she would never see, a baby's room, the bed grown into a guardrail crib or simply waiting until the child was older, and she felt a sudden fierce wish for them to have it, to take the bed, that exact bed in which she had lain as a feverish child and as a sulking teenager and in which, the last summer before she left for good, she had lain awake making the plans that became her whole life away from this town. She wanted them to have it so much that she went in and said, "It's a good bed. My — it's a good bed. The springs are still good."

"How much would you take for it?" the man asked, because that was the only language available, and Margaret looked at the sticker, which said thirty, and said, "Twenty. Twenty's fine."

"You sure?"

"I'm sure. You'll want a new mattress."

"Oh, we know," the woman said, and put her hand on her belly in the ancient way, and Margaret got out of the room before the feeling could finish arriving.

By one o'clock the rooms were ringing. That was the only word for it; an empty room has a sound, a brightness in the upper registers, the sound of nothing soft left to absorb a voice. People's footsteps changed as the rugs went. Donna ate a sandwich standing up by the cash box and offered Margaret half and Margaret said no thank you and then took it, and they stood together not talking, eating turkey on white bread, and it was the most companionable Margaret had felt all day, this stranger and her, two women presiding over a disassembly.

"You grow up here?" Donna asked.

"Until eighteen."

"It's a nice house. Good bones." Donna said it professionally, but not unkindly. "These old ones, they're built. You couldn't build this today for triple."

"My father built the porch," Margaret said. "The back porch. Not the house, but the porch. He poured the footings himself. He used to say he made one mistake on it and he never told anybody what it was, and we'd try to find it, my sister and I. We'd go out and look at the porch and try to find the mistake. We never found it."

Donna smiled. "Bet there wasn't one."

"That's what my mother said. She said he made it up so we'd keep looking at the porch. So we'd appreciate it." Margaret stopped, the sandwich gone tasteless in her mouth. She had not thought of that in years and now it seemed unbearably exact, the whole of her father in it, a man who could not say *look at this thing I made, please love it* and so invented a flaw to make them look. He had been dead nineteen years. She set the rest of the sandwich on the windowsill. "I never thanked him for the porch," she said. "It seems like a stupid thing. But I never did."

Donna did not say anything wise, which Margaret was grateful for. She just nodded and wadded up her wax paper and said, "I'll take the rest of these stickers up to a buck after two. Move what's left."

The afternoon went long and gray. A school bus went by outside, the same route, probably, that Margaret had ridden, the brakes catching at the corner of Walnut and Third with the same exhausted sigh. Children came off it and dispersed into the new vinyl houses. The light through the bare windows was the particular light of an Ohio November, which is not a color so much as an absence of commitment, the sky unable to decide between rain and merely threatening it, and the light came in flat and even and lit the dust that the moving of furniture had thrown up into the air, so that the empty rooms were full of slowly turning galaxies of it, her mother's dust, her own dust, the sloughed skin of everyone who had ever lived in the house, hanging in the light.

She went down to the basement to be alone and found that someone had bought the workbench. The pegboard was still on the wall, the painted outlines where her father had traced each tool so he would know where it went and know when one was missing — a hammer-shaped void, a void in the shape of a hand saw, the outline of a level. The tools themselves were upstairs in a box marked $15. But the outlines were on the wall and could not be sold and would go to the dump with the house if the house came down, or would be painted over by whoever bought it, the young couple maybe, painting the basement a cheerful color for a playroom, rolling white primer over the ghost of her father's hammer without ever knowing it was there. She stood in front of the pegboard for a while. Then she did a thing she would not tell anyone about, which was that she took out her phone and photographed the pegboard, the empty outlines, so that somewhere in the world there would continue to be a record that her father had known where his tools went.

The freezer hummed in the corner. She opened it. The cold breathed up at her. She picked up the Cool Whip container of frozen wedding cake and held it and tried once more to think whose wedding it could have been, and could not, and understood that this was now permanent, that the question would never be answered, that her mother had been the only person who knew and her mother was ash in a box on a shelf in a closet in Pittsburgh, and the not-knowing settled over Margaret as a small clean grief, distinct from the large one, a grief shaped exactly like a Cool Whip container. There would be thousands of these. She was only at the beginning of them. For the rest of her life she would come up against things only her mother had known, the name of the woman who used to live in the green house, the year they got the dishwasher, the reason her mother never ate lamb, and each one would be a little locked door, and the keys were all gone.

She put the cake back. She closed the freezer. She would deal with it. She would come back in the morning after all, she decided; she would not hand the keys over until she had stood in the driveway and put every casserole in the trash herself, BEEF & NOODLE, CHICKEN DIVAN, the labels in her mother's failing hand, and she would do it alone and it would be terrible and it was hers to do.

When she came back upstairs the light had shifted and the crowd had thinned. The serious buyers were gone; what was left were the late browsers, the ones who came at the end hoping for the dollar tables, and they drifted through the stripped rooms picking up the last small things, the ashtrays and the spice tins and the framed print of a covered bridge that had hung in the upstairs hall. A man bought the print. It went out the door. Margaret had hated that print her whole life and now she watched it leave with something close to panic, and she nearly called out, nearly said *wait*, and did not, and the door closed behind it.

"We did real good," Donna said, counting the box. "You did real good. People don't always stay. The ones that stay, sometimes it helps. You think it'll be worse and sometimes it's better. You see it go to somebody. The young couple with the bed, that's nice. That's a nice thing to see."

"Yes," Margaret said.

"They'll take care of it."

"I think they will."

Donna snapped the box shut and stood and put her hand briefly on Margaret's arm, the practiced touch of a woman who had done this four hundred times and had learned exactly how much touch was the right amount, and then she gathered her things. "I'll lock up the front. You take whatever time you want with the back. Just pull the door hard, it sticks." She paused in the doorway, the lanyard swinging. "It's a nice house," she said again, as if it were a thing that needed saying twice, and then she was gone, and her van started up in the driveway and backed out, and Margaret was alone in the house for the first time and the last.

She walked through the rooms. They were not large. She had known they were not large but walking them empty she understood how small they truly were, how a whole life had been conducted in this little box, meals and arguments and Christmases and the long boredom of childhood and her father dying in the front room because he had refused the hospital, all of it in these few hundred square feet, and the smallness was not sad, exactly. It was astonishing. That so much could happen in so little space. That the world could be this size.

She ended in the kitchen, where she had begun. The burn mark was still on the counter, the one from 1971, the one her mother had cried over. It would go with the house. She put her fingers on it. Then she went to the back door, the door that led out to the porch her father had built and never named the flaw in, and she stepped out onto it, and she stood on the boards he had nailed down, and she looked at them in the failing light, and she tried, one more time, the way she had as a girl, to find the mistake. She went down on her knees on the cold concrete steps and looked at the footings. She ran her hand along the rail. The light was nearly gone now and she could not see well and she did not find it, of course she did not find it, there had never been one to find, and she knelt there on her father's porch with her mother's ring on her right hand and the worn spoon's quarter in her pocket, and she said out loud, to the empty yard, to the bare clothesline, to the vinyl houses where the windows were coming on yellow one by one, "It's a good porch, Dad. There's no mistake in it. It's a good porch." And then she stood, and brushed off her knees, and pulled the door hard behind her because it stuck, and went around the side of the house to her car in the dark.
