They came up through the orchard in the early light with the shovels over their shoulders and the dew still gray on the grass and the long rows of trees standing in their ranks like men assembled for some accounting yet to come. The older brother walked ahead. He did not speak. The younger came behind with the spool of pipe and the toolbox and he watched his brother's back and the way the dark stain of sweat had already begun between his shoulders and he said nothing either.

They stopped at the head of the rows where the line came up out of the ground at the standpipe and the older brother set down his shovel and crouched and put his hand to the dry iron of it.

It's plugged somewheres, he said.

Or broke.

Or broke. He looked off down the rows. The trees stood gray and silver in the slant light and beyond them the land fell away to the river bottom and beyond that the dim blue shapes of the mountains stood ranged against the sky. Some of the trees had died standing and their limbs were bare and pale as bone and the leaves of the living ones hung curled and yellow at the edges for want of water.

How far down you reckon, the younger said.

Could be anywhere. We'll have to walk it.

They walked it. They went down the rows with the dead grass whispering at their boots and the older brother bent now and again and put his hand to the ground as if he could read in the dirt the secret history of the water that had ceased to move beneath it. The sun came up over the eastern ridge and stood white and hard above the trees and the dew burned off and the day began to heat.

Here, the older brother said.

He stood over a low place in the row where the dirt was darker and a faint seep stood in the bottom of it black and oily looking in the early light. The younger came up and stood beside him and they both looked down at it.

That's it, the younger said.

That's where it's broke. He looked at the seep a while longer. Get the shovels.

They dug. The dirt came up dark and damp at first and then dry and pale below and they cut down through the roots of the orchard grass and the small white roots of the trees that had come questing toward what little moisture the broken line had given up. The older brother dug like a man at war with the ground. The younger paced himself and stopped to lean on the shovel and wipe his face with his sleeve and he watched his brother work.

You're goin to kill yourself before noon.

I aim to get it done.

It'll keep till we're dead. The whole place'll keep.

The older brother did not answer him. He bent and threw the dirt and bent again and the muscles stood in his forearms and the sweat ran from him into the hole he was digging.

They came to the pipe at the depth of about two feet. It lay in the trench black and corroded and the older brother knelt and cleared the dirt from around it with his hands and they could see where it had split along the seam, a long ragged mouth in the iron through which the water had wept itself away into the ground these many seasons.

Look at that, the younger said.

I see it.

It's rusted clean through. The whole line's like to be that way.

Maybe.

You know it is. You patch this and it'll go someplace else come August. You know that as well as I do.

The older brother sat back on his heels and looked at the split pipe and did not say anything. A jay called somewhere down the rows and was answered and the heat lay over the orchard like a hand. He took up the hacksaw from the toolbox and fitted a blade and bent to the pipe and began to cut.

They got out the broken section. It took them the better part of an hour to cut it free and to thread the new and to fit the couplings, the two of them down in the trench on their knees with the pipe dope on their hands and the gnats coming up out of the damp ground to worry at their eyes. They worked without speaking. When they had the new section in the older brother stood and his knees cracked and he put his hands to the small of his back and looked off down the rows.

Try her, he said.

The younger walked back up to the standpipe and opened the valve and they listened and after a while the water came knocking and shuddering through the old iron and they watched the couplings and the new section held and no water showed and the older brother nodded once.

That's got it, he said.

For now.

For now.

They sat down in the shade of one of the trees to eat. The younger had brought tortillas and cold beans wrapped in a cloth and they ate sitting with their backs against the trunk and passed the waterjug between them. The older brother ate slowly and looked out at the orchard.

Mr. Watkins come by the house, the younger said.

The older brother did not say anything.

He's offerin good money. Better than good. More than this place has cleared in ten years.

I heard what he's offerin.

You didnt say nothin.

There wasnt nothin to say.

The younger brother folded the cloth and set it in his lap and looked at his hands. They want to put houses on it, he said. The whole bottom. He's already got the men comin out to look at the water rights.

I know what he wants.

It's good money, Lee.

The older brother set down the jug and screwed the cap onto it. It aint about the money, he said.

What's it about then.

He didnt answer right away. He looked off at the dead trees standing pale among the living and at the long rows running down to the river and the light lay over all of it soft and gold and merciless.

Daddy set these trees, he said. And his daddy before him cleared this ground when there wasnt nothin here but mesquite and rock. They hauled the water in barrels the first three years before they ever dug the line. You know that. You've heard it same as me.

I've heard it.

Then you know what it's about.

The younger brother picked at the grass between his boots. That was them, he said. That was their ground. They're dead, Lee. They been dead twenty years. It dont mean nothin to the trees who set em.

It means somethin to me.

It's killin you. Look at you. You're forty-one years old and you work this place like a mule and it dont give back what it takes. You said yourself the line's near gone. You patch one place it goes another. The trees are dyin. Half of em are dead already and the other half ought to be.

The older brother said nothing.

We cant make it pay, the younger said. We never could. Daddy couldnt and his daddy couldnt and we cant. The only reason it's worth anything now is them houses Watkins wants to build and once that water goes to the houses it dont come back. You know that. There aint goin to be another offer like it.

The older brother turned and looked at him. You want to sell it then.

I want to live, the younger said. I want to not bust my back on ground that hates me. I want a little money in my hand for once before I'm too old to spend it. Yeah. I want to sell it.

They sat a while in the heat without speaking. Down in the bottom a redtailed hawk came up off a snag and turned once over the river and beat away to the south. The older brother watched it go.

You remember when we was boys, he said. We used to run these rows in the summer. The water'd be runnin in the furrows and we'd run barefoot in it and it'd be cold off the mountain even in July. You remember that.

I remember.

The whole place green then. The trees so heavy with fruit Daddy'd have to prop the limbs. We'd pick all August and they'd come from town to buy and there wasnt a better peach in the valley and everbody knew it.

That was a long time ago.

It was.

It aint that way now and it aint goin to be that way again. That's what you wont see. You keep lookin at how it was. You're livin in a place that dont exist no more.

The older brother stood up. He stood looking down at his brother and the younger looked up at him with the light in his face and squinting.

Maybe it dont exist for you, the older brother said. It exists for me.

He took up his shovel and walked off down the rows. The younger sat a while longer and then he gathered the jug and the cloth and the toolbox and followed.

They worked the rest of the day at the line. There were other low places where the dirt stood dark and they dug them out one by one and found two more splits and patched the one and on the other the older brother only wrapped it and clamped it for there was not pipe enough to do otherwise. They did not speak much. The sun crossed over and stood in the west and the shadows of the trees came long across the rows and the heat began to go out of the day.

Late in the afternoon they came to a place far down toward the river where the line ran under a great old tree that had stood there longer than any of the others, a tree so broad in the trunk that a man could not get his arms half around it, and its limbs spread out gnarled and heavy over the row. But it was dead. It had been dead some years and its bark had gone gray and begun to slough and where the line passed beneath it the roots had heaved and cracked the pipe and the dirt all about was wet and sour smelling.

The older brother knelt and looked at it.

This is the old one, the younger said. The one Daddy called the king tree.

I know what it is.

It's dead.

I can see it's dead.

The younger crouched beside him. The roots had it, he said. Even dead the roots kept comin for the water and they broke the line. Even dead. He shook his head. You see what I mean, Lee. You cant beat it. The ground itself is against you. The dead trees are against you.

The older brother put his hand to the gray bark of the dead tree and held it there a while as a man might lay his hand on the flank of a horse that had carried him a long way and could carry him no further. Then he took his hand away.

Daddy set this one first, he said. Before any of the others. He told me that. He said he stood right here and looked at the ground and figured the rows off of where he set this tree. Everthing else come off of this. He set it the year he married Mama.

The younger brother said nothing.

And now it's dead.

Now it's dead, the younger said gently.

They dug the line out from under the dead roots. It was hard work for the roots had grown thick about the pipe and embraced it and they had to cut them away with the hatchet and the older brother swung the hatchet and the white wood showed pale under the bark and the chips flew. He swung at it like a man at some old grief. The younger stood back and let him do it. When the pipe was clear they patched it and clamped it and stood and looked at it in the failing light.

That's the last of it I can find, the older brother said.

There'll be more.

There'll be more. But that's the last I can find today.

They gathered the tools. The light had gone amber and then red over the mountains and the orchard stood in it black and still and the dead trees and the living were all one color now in that light and there was no telling them apart. They walked back up the rows toward the house with their shovels over their shoulders and the long shadows of themselves going out before them.

Halfway up the younger stopped. The older went on a few steps and then stopped too and turned.

Lee, the younger said.

What.

He stood holding the toolbox in one hand and the spool of pipe in the other and he looked at his brother in the red light.

I'm goin to sell my half, he said. Whatever you decide on yours. I cant do this no more. I'm sorry. But I cant.

The older brother stood looking at him. The light was going fast now and behind him the first of the stars had come out pale over the eastern ridge.

You cant sell half, he said. You know that. He wants the whole place or none. The water rights dont sever. You sell your half you've sold mine.

I know it.

Then you know what you're askin me.

I'm not askin, the younger said. That's just it. I aint got it in me to ask no more. I've asked. I've asked you a hundred times in a hundred ways. I'm done askin. I'm tellin you what I'm goin to do.

The older brother said nothing for a long while. A nighthawk passed overhead with a sound like cloth tearing and was gone.

You'd do that, he said. Against me.

It aint against you. God almighty, Lee. It's for us. For both of us. I'm tryin to save your life and you wont let me.

It aint your life to save.

You're my brother.

That's right, the older brother said. I'm your brother. And this is my daddy's ground and his daddy's. And you'd sell it out from under me whether I will or no.

The younger brother set down the toolbox in the grass. He stood with his hands at his sides.

What would you have me do, he said. Tell me. You tell me how a man lives out here. You show me where the money comes from. The trees are dyin. The line's gone. The market's gone, they buy their peaches from California now in the store, you've seen it. There aint no buyers come from town no more, there aint no town hardly. You tell me how it works and I'll do it. I'll stay till I drop. I swear it. But you cant tell me. There aint no how. There's just you wantin it to be like it was and it aint.

The older brother looked off down the dark rows toward the river. You could go, he said quietly. There's nothin holdin you. Go on and live your life. I'll buy your half from you over time. It'll take years but I'll do it. You dont have to stay.

And watch you die here.

I aint dyin.

You're dyin slower than the trees, the younger said. That's all. You just dont measure it the same.

The older brother did not answer this. They stood in the dark and the cold came down off the gap and moved through the orchard and the leaves of the living trees stirred and went still. Far off a dog barked once and quit.

I cant raise the money on my own, the younger said. I need you to sign. You know I do. And if you wont sign then I'm trapped here same as you, and I'll hate you for it. I dont want to hate you, Lee. We're all that's left. You and me. There aint nobody else.

The older brother turned and started up the rows again. The younger took up the toolbox and came after him. They walked up through the dark orchard in single file and neither spoke and the house showed yellow in its windows ahead of them where it stood on the rise with the one light burning in the kitchen and the smoke standing straight up from the chimney in the still cold air.

At the edge of the yard the older brother stopped. He stood looking at the house.

She used to set in that window of an evening, he said. Mama. You wouldnt remember. You was too little. She'd set there snappin beans and watchin for Daddy to come up out of the rows. Every evening. He'd come up just about this time of day, just dark like this, and she'd be settin there in that light waitin on him.

I dont remember it, the younger said.

No. You was too little.

He stood a while longer.

I'll think on it, he said.

The younger brother looked at him. On sellin?

On signin. I aint sayin I will. I'm sayin I'll think on it. That's more than I've said. He turned and looked at his brother in the dark. I cant promise you no more than that.

That's enough, the younger said. That's enough, Lee. I aint askin more than that.

You said you was done askin.

The younger brother almost smiled. The light from the kitchen window caught the side of his face.

I lied, he said.

They went on across the yard. At the steps the older brother set his shovel against the porch rail and stood scraping his boots and the younger went past him to the door and opened it and the warm light fell out across the boards and the smell of cooking came out into the cold and somewhere inside a radio was playing low.

You comin, the younger said.

In a minute. Go on.

The younger brother went in and the door stood open behind him and the light lay out across the porch and the older brother stood at the rail and looked back down the way they had come, down into the black orchard where the trees stood ranged in their long rows, the dead and the living all one now in the dark, and the river beyond them lost in the night, and the mountains gone, and the only thing visible the few hard stars above the ridge and the one window burning yellow behind him in the house his fathers had built. He stood there a long time. Then he turned and scraped the rest of the mud from his boots and went in and shut the door and the light went out of the yard and there was only the house standing on the rise with its smoke going up and the cold settling down over the orchard and the long night coming on.
