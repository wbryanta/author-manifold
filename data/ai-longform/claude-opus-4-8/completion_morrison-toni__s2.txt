He knew this country well from earlier visits and from the maps engraved in his memory, but the trail had a way of disguising itself with seasons. Spring rains had swollen the creeks and softened the banks, so that twice Regina balked at crossings, ears flat, and he had to coax her with low words and a steady hand. He was in no particular hurry now that the worst of the journey lay behind him. The man he had come to see, a Portuguese planter named D'Ortega, would keep, and keeping a man like that waiting was no bad thing. Jacob had no love for the breed—men who counted their wealth in flesh and acres and thought themselves elevated by the counting. But debts were debts, and money owed had a way of pulling a man across an ocean and through a swamp of golden fog whether he wished it or no.

The sun climbed and burned off what remained of the morning's gold. By midday the trail widened and the trees thinned, and he came upon tobacco fields stretching out in their ragged rows, the plants not yet to full height. Here and there figures bent among the green, and he did not look too long at them, though he marked their number and the way an overseer sat his horse at the field's edge with a coiled whip resting on the pommel like a sleeping snake. Jacob touched his hat to the man, who did not return the courtesy but watched him pass with the dull suspicion of one whose whole life was spent watching.

The road to Jublio—for so D'Ortega had named his place, in the manner of men who fancied a Latin grandeur—ran between two lines of young poplars, planted, Jacob supposed, to suggest an avenue such as European lords possessed. But the trees were spindly and ill-tended, and the grand effect they were meant to produce only emphasized how raw and unfinished the whole enterprise was. At the avenue's end stood the house, and Jacob slowed Regina to take its measure.

It was a vast thing, or meant to be. Brick imported, he had heard, at ruinous expense, the windows glazed, a double stair of carved stone climbing to a portico held up by columns. And yet it sagged. The mortar had a greenish cast where damp had crept in. One of the columns leaned by a degree that the eye could not forgive. The grass before it grew in patches, and a peacock—an actual peacock, dragging its ruined tail through the mud—screamed at his approach with a sound like a woman in distress. Jacob shook his head. He had built his own modest house in the north with his own hands and the help of two hired men, and it stood square and true and asked nothing of any man's eye but honesty. This pile asked for awe and got only pity.

A boy came running to take the horse, a thin black child whose eyes did not rise to Jacob's face. Jacob dismounted and handed over the reins, and because it was his way and cost him nothing, he said, "Walk her cool before you water her. She's been ridden." The boy glanced up then, startled to be spoken to as though he were a stable hand at an inn and not a thing, and nodded once before leading Regina away.

D'Ortega received him in a room that strove for splendor and achieved only excess. There was too much of everything—too many chairs of dark carved wood, too many tapestries gone dim with smoke, a great sideboard crowded with plate that wanted polishing. The planter himself was a man of perhaps forty, running to fat, dressed in a coat of plum-colored velvet wholly unsuited to the heat, so that sweat stood on his brow and darkened the cloth beneath his arms. He smelled of clove and something underneath the clove, a sourness no perfume could mask. His wife sat by the cold hearth, a small pale woman in a gown too fine for the hour, her hands folded, her eyes lowered. She did not speak the whole of the visit, nor was she introduced beyond a wave of D'Ortega's beringed hand.

"Senhor Vaark," said D'Ortega, rising and spreading his arms as though to embrace, though he did not. "You have come a great distance. You will take wine. You will eat. We are not so coarse here as the country makes us seem."

Jacob took the wine because to refuse would have been an insult and because he was thirsty, but he did not sit until invited, and when he sat he kept his satchel near his boot. He had not crossed an ocean and a swamp to be flattered out of what he was owed. He drank, and the wine was good, better than the house deserved, and he said as much, and D'Ortega laughed too long and too loud and called for more.

They came to it slowly, as men of business do, circling the matter through pleasantries about the crossing, the weather, the late troubles in the colony, the price of leaf in Bristol. Jacob let the planter talk. He had learned long ago that a man who liked the sound of his own voice would tell you, without meaning to, the shape of his trouble. And D'Ortega's trouble was plain enough. The man owed Jacob a sum—the residue of a venture gone wrong, a cargo Jacob had financed in part through a chain of obligations too tangled to recount, a debt that had passed into Jacob's hands as such debts do, by the failure of others. The sum was not enormous. To a man like D'Ortega, who lived as though he commanded the wealth of Lisbon, it ought to have been nothing. But Jacob saw within the first hour that there was no money. The house, the wine, the velvet—all of it was a kind of lie the man told himself, and the truth lived in the green stain on the mortar and the leaning column and the unpolished plate.

"You understand," D'Ortega said at last, dabbing at his face with a handkerchief that had once been white, "the misfortune was not mine to command. The ship—" He spread his hands. "God's hand was in it. We lost a third of the cargo to the bloody flux before we made port. A third! Imagine the loss. They sicken, they die, and there is no recompense, no insurance against the will of Heaven."

Jacob said nothing for a moment. He understood now what the cargo had been. He had not let himself think on it before, when the debt was only figures on a page, but here in this room, with the silent woman by the hearth and the boy outside walking his horse, the figures took on weight and breath.

"A hundred and fifty come aboard at the coast," D'Ortega went on, warming to his grievance, "and barely ninety stand to be sold. The waste, Senhor. The waste."

"The waste," Jacob repeated, and something in his voice made the planter look up.

"You are not a man of the trade," D'Ortega said, half question.

"I trade in many things," Jacob said. "Rum. Timber. Furs from the north. I have a notion to plant some acres myself, when the labor can be had honest." He turned his cup slowly. "I have not bought men. The cost runs high and the conscience runs higher."

D'Ortega's smile thinned. "You take a fine view from your cold latitudes. Here a man must use what the country gives, or be ruined by those who do."

"That may be." Jacob set down the cup. "But the country has not given you my money, Senhor, and I have come a long way for it."

The afternoon wore on, and they ate—a heavy meal of mutton and fowl and a fish gone slightly off in the heat, which Jacob ate around—and through it all the matter sat between them like a third guest. After the plates were cleared, D'Ortega proposed a walk in the air, and Jacob, who could not bear the close hot room and the smell of the man any longer, agreed. They went out through the back of the house into a yard, and beyond the yard to the place where the planter kept his stock.

He showed them off as a man might show off horses. They stood or sat in the shade of a long open shed, perhaps a dozen of them, men and women and a few children, and at D'Ortega's approach an overseer roused them to their feet so the buyer might look. Jacob had said he did not wish to buy, but D'Ortega, having no money, had hit upon the only currency he possessed and meant to discharge his debt in flesh.

"Take your pick," he said expansively, as though offering a gift. "Any two. Three, even, and we call the matter settled, you and I, friends. A man of your enterprise, you will see the sense of it. Labor is wealth, Senhor. Labor is the only wealth that breeds more wealth while you sleep."

Jacob walked the line because courtesy and self-interest both required it, but his face was hot and not from the sun. He looked at the men—strong some of them, broad through the shoulder, their eyes fixed on nothing—and he could not. It was one thing to hold a debt on paper. It was another to lead a living man away on a rope and call him paid. He thought of his own two hands raising the beams of his house, and he thought of his wife waiting in the north, the children they had buried, the daughter the river took and the boy the kick of the cow took, and the cradle that stood empty in the corner of the room. He was not a soft man. He had grown up in a poorhouse and made his own way through a world that gave nothing, and he had hardened himself against most things that hardened a man. But he could not lead one of these away.

"I have no use for a field hand," he said. "I work my own land. I'll not feed and clothe a man to stand idle through a northern winter."

D'Ortega's color rose. He had wanted the matter closed, the debt off his ledger, and Jacob's refusal threatened to expose the emptiness of his coffers. "Then what," he said, and there was an edge of desperation under the courtesy now, "what would the Senhor have? I have offered—"

It was then that Jacob saw the woman. She came from the cookhouse with a kettle in her hands, a big-boned woman in a thin shift, and behind her skirts a small girl held tight to the cloth, and at her other hand a boy of three or four years, and pressed to her hip, looking out at the world with great still eyes, an infant. The woman set the kettle down and straightened, and seeing the men she gathered her children closer with a movement so quick and so practiced that Jacob understood it without being told. He had seen mothers do that. He had seen his own mother do that, in another country, before another kind of master.

"That one," D'Ortega said, following his glance, and his voice quickened with relief at having found anything the buyer's eye lingered on. "A good breeder. You see she has whelped three and lost none. Healthy stock. The man, the father, he is not on the place, but you see they take after her—strong, clean of limb. I would let her go, Senhor, though it grieves me. For the debt. Her and one of the children, what you like."

Jacob looked at the woman and the woman did not look at him, but the boy did, the little one, with his hand caught in his mother's, and his eyes were calm and unafraid in a way that struck Jacob to the heart. And the woman, who had been watching the ground, suddenly stepped forward.

He could not afterward have said exactly what passed in her face. It was quick and it was terrible. She let go the infant's weight a little, settling it more firmly on her hip, and she pushed the small girl forward, and she spoke. Her voice was low and her Portuguese was rough but Jacob understood the shape of it, and D'Ortega, frowning, translated with reluctance, as though she had committed some impropriety merely by opening her mouth.

"She asks—" D'Ortega stopped, irritated. "She asks the Senhor to take the girl. The daughter." He waved his hand. "It is nothing. A whim. They do not understand these matters. She means only—"

But Jacob understood. He understood better than the planter, who had never been poor enough or low enough to know what the woman knew, which was that the men were arguing over numbers and that numbers could be made to fall any way, and that here, in this little space between one decision and another, she might bend the falling. She did not want to come herself, or perhaps she did, but she saw that what was being offered her was not herself but a fragment, a chip off the block of her own life, and she had chosen, in the space of a breath, which fragment.

The girl she pushed forward was perhaps eight years old, thin, big-eyed, barefoot in the mud, and she clung to her mother's shift until the mother peeled the small fingers loose with a gentleness that did not match the swiftness of the act. The girl did not cry out. She looked from her mother to the white man and back, and her face had in it something old, something already resigned, as if she had always known the world would come for her one way or another and was only now learning which way.

"Take the girl," the mother said again, or that was what her gesture said, both hands pressing the child toward Jacob now, and her own face a held thing, a clenched thing, and the infant on her hip beginning to fret at the change in her body.

Jacob did not want a child. He had no use for a child of eight in the north, no more than a man, and less—a girl that small could do little, would have to be fed and grown before she earned her keep, and his wife had buried enough children that he did not know how she would bear a strange one underfoot. All this passed through him in the moment. And yet.

And yet he saw that to refuse was to leave the woman with nothing—not even the bitter consolation of having saved a piece of her own from this place. He saw that D'Ortega would mark the debt unpaid and the visit a failure, and that he, Jacob, would ride away through the golden fog with empty hands and a heavy heart and nothing settled. And he saw the girl's face, that old resigned face on the small body, and something in him that he did not examine too closely turned over and said yes.

"She'll have to be clothed for the cold," he heard himself say. "She has no shoes."

D'Ortega seized on it. "Shoes! Of course, shoes, we will find shoes, anything—" He turned and barked at the overseer, who barked at someone else, and a pair of shoes was produced, a woman's shoes, far too large, and the girl was made to put them on, and she stood in them swallowed to the ankle, ridiculous, and Jacob nearly laughed and nearly wept and did neither.

"This," said D'Ortega, "this we count against the debt, then. Her and—"

"Her and nothing," Jacob said. "The girl, and we are square."

"The girl is worth less than the debt, Senhor. Surely—"

"The girl, and we are square," Jacob said again, and there was something in his voice now that ended the matter, because they both knew the debt would never be paid in coin and that Jacob was, in truth, taking less than he was owed and not more, and that the planter ought to be on his knees with gratitude rather than haggling. "Bring me a paper. I'll have it in writing, the debt discharged, your hand and mine."

The mother watched all this with her clenched, held face, and when it was done, when the paper was being fetched and the matter closed, she knelt in the mud before her daughter and took the small face in her two big hands and said something to her, low, urgent, that Jacob could not hear and would not have understood if he had. She said it once and then again, holding the child's eyes, pressing the words into her as if she could plant them where they would not be lost, and the girl nodded, solemn, taking the words in, and the mother kissed her forehead and stood and stepped back and did not reach for her again.

It was the last Jacob saw of the woman's face before he turned to the planter's paper, and it stayed with him, that face, for he could not read it. He thought she was grateful. He thought, in the years that came after, when he had cause to think of her at all, that she had chosen the child a better life and was glad of it. He never knew, and could not have known, what the woman knew—that she had not chosen the girl out of love above the others, though she loved her, but had read in the white man's face the only mercy on offer, and had given up the one she could most bear to lose so that she might keep the baby at her breast and the small son at her hand. He never knew that the words she pressed into the child were not comfort but instruction—about how to carry herself, about the eyes of men and how to slip beneath their notice, about a god the girl had been baptized into in some half-remembered ceremony, about the necessity of being good. He took it for a fond farewell. It was a mother arming a daughter for a war the mother herself was losing.

The paper was signed. D'Ortega pressed wine on him again, and Jacob drank it standing, eager now to be gone, the close hot reek of the man and the house unbearable. The peacock screamed in the yard. The light was going gold again toward evening, the fog beginning to creep back up from the bay, and Jacob did not want to be caught on the Lenape trail after dark with a strange child and a tired horse. He took the girl by the hand—it was cold, the small fingers, though the day was warm—and led her out to where the stable boy had brought Regina round, watered and cooled as Jacob had asked, and he lifted the child up before the saddle, shoes and all, and mounted behind her.

She did not weep. She sat very still, her too-large shoes hanging, her small back straight against him, and as Regina carried them down the spindly poplar avenue away from Jublio, the girl turned her head once to look back at the open shed and the cookhouse and the place where her mother had stood, but the figures had gone in, or been driven in, and there was nothing to see but the leaning house and the patchy grass and the bird dragging its broken splendor through the mud. Then she faced forward and did not look back again.

They came to the trail as the fog rolled up to meet them, and the world turned once more to that thick hot gold Jacob had come through at dawn, so that the trees lost their crowns and the path swam and softened, and the two of them rode through it together, the man and the child he had not meant to buy, into a brightness so complete it was like riding into the heart of something burning. The girl said nothing. Jacob, who was not a talking man, found himself talking anyway, low, the way he had talked to Regina at the creek crossings, telling her about the north and the cold and the snow she had never seen, about his wife who would not be unkind, about the house he had built with his own hands that stood square and true. He did not know if she understood a word. She gave no sign. But once, when the fog thinned and a heron rose out of the swamp grass beating its great slow wings, he felt the small body before him go tense and then ease, and he thought, she is only a child after all, and was both glad and sorrowful in a way he could not name.

He did not yet know what he had done. He thought he had settled a bad debt and acquired, almost by accident, a girl who might in time be useful about the place and easy enough to keep. He thought of her as a small obligation, a thing he had taken on out of a softness he half despised in himself. He did not know that he had reached into another woman's life and drawn out a thread, and that the drawing would not stop with this one pulling, but would run on through years and across a country, snagging on every hard thing, unraveling toward an end that none of them, riding through that golden fog, could have foreseen. He knew only that the day was ending, that the trail was treacherous, that the child before him weighed almost nothing, and that he wanted, more than he had wanted anything in a long while, to be home.

The fog closed over them, and the trail bent north, and they went on.
