He woke again to her hand and the cold mud of morning and a light come gray and grudging at the window of the shack, and he lay a long while with his eyes upon the rafters where the smoke had blacked the wood and listened to her breathing beside him in the dark. The child had not yet come. He could hear the wind off the river working at the boards and somewhere out in the trees a thing crying that might have been bird or beast or neither and he lay and listened to it and did not move until the gray had gone to white and the white to the thin yellow of the sun and then he rose and dressed in the cold and went out.

The country lay about him under a frost. The river ran sullen and brown past the bench of land where the shack stood and the willows hung gray and naked over the water and downstream a heron stood in the shallows like a thing carved and did not move while he watched it. He went to the woodpile and split kindling with the maul and brought it in and built the fire and she stirred and did not wake. He set the coffee on and squatted before the flames and warmed his hands and the smoke rose blue and thin through the chimney hole and out into the morning where it ran flat along the ground in the cold air and tangled in the brush.

When she woke she did not speak of his crying out in the night and neither did he. She rose heavy from the bed and stood at the fire and her belly stood out before her great and round under the shift and she put a hand to the small of her back and grimaced and he looked at her and looked away.

It wont be long now, she said.

He said nothing.

She poured the coffee and they drank it and ate cold bread and after a while he took his hat and his rifle and went out and she watched him go from the door, a thin dark figure passing up the trace toward the timber with the dog rising out of the leaves to follow him and the two of them dwindling into the trees and gone.

He hunted the ridges all that morning and saw nothing he could shoot. The frost burned off and the day came up mild and pale and he sat for a long time on a deadfall with the rifle across his knees and the dog lying at his feet and he watched the valley below where the river ran and the smoke of his own fire rose thin from the trees and beyond it the country opening blue and immense toward the mountains he had come down out of and would not see again. He did not know what he was waiting for. The dog raised its head and looked at him and he put his hand to it and it lay back down.

When he came in at evening he carried a brace of squirrels he had taken off the limbs of a hickory and these he skinned and gutted at the block by the door while she watched him from inside and she made a stew of them with what roots she had and they ate it in the failing light without a lamp for they had no oil and when they had eaten she sat by the fire and he sat across from her and neither spoke. The wind had come up again and it moaned in the chinks of the wall.

You was dreamin again, she said at last.

He looked at her across the fire. The light moved on his face.

I dont remember it, he said.

You was callin out.

I dont remember.

She watched him. The firelight stood in her eyes. After a while she looked away into the flames.

My mother used to say a body that dreams bad is a body with something on him, she said. Something he done or something done to him.

I aint done nothing.

I never said you had.

He got up and went to the door and stood looking out into the dark where the river ran unseen and only the cold smell of it came up to him and the wind. He could hear it going by out there in the night, the great brown weight of it sliding past in the dark toward whatever it was going to, and he stood and listened to it until he was cold through and then he came in and barred the door and lay down on the bed in his clothes and after a while she came and lay beside him and they slept.

In the night the pains took her. He woke to her hand gripping his arm and her breath drawn sharp between her teeth and he sat up in the dark and did not know at first where he was.

Its come, she said. Its started.

He got up and felt his way to the hearth and stirred the coals and fed them tinder until the flame caught and the light came up red and small in the room. She lay with her knees drawn and her face slick and shining and when the pain came on her again she turned her face into the tick and made a sound low in her throat that he had not heard her make before.

What do I do, he said. Tell me what to do.

But she could not tell him for the pain and he stood over her helpless with his hands at his sides and the fire snapping behind him and the wind at the wall. He had seen calves born and foals and once a woman in his own people's house when he was a boy, hid behind the door and watching with his eye to the crack, but he had never done the thing himself nor been the only one to do it and he was afraid.

It went on through the night. The pains came closer and she labored at them and he heated water in the kettle and tore an old shirt into rags and brought them to her and held her hand when she would have it and the rest of the time he could do nothing but stand and feed the fire and listen to her. Toward morning she began to bleed. He saw it on the tick and on the rags and he did not know if it was meant to be so or not and he asked her and she did not answer him for she had gone somewhere past answering, her eyes fixed on the rafters and her lips moving and no sound coming and the sweat standing on her in beads.

The child came near dawn. It came in a rush and a welter and he caught it in his two hands, a small blue thing slick and warm and tangled in its cord, and he held it not knowing and it did not cry. He turned it and cleared its mouth with his finger as he had seen done and still it did not cry and he held it up in the firelight and looked at it and its face was the face of something very old, the eyes shut and the small mouth set, and he breathed on it and rubbed its chest with his thumb and at last it drew breath with a sound like tearing cloth and began to wail, thin and high and outraged in the cold room.

It's a boy, he said. It's a boy.

But she did not answer. She had gone gray as ash and the bleeding had not stopped and it ran from her dark and steady onto the tick and onto the packed earth of the floor and he saw it and a cold went through him worse than the cold of the room. He tied off the cord with a strip of the shirt and cut it with his knife and wrapped the child and laid it by the fire and went to her and took her face in his hands.

Hey, he said. Hey now. Stay with me.

Her eyes came round to him slow and far away. She tried to speak and could not. He pressed the rags to her and they soaked through and he pressed more and they soaked through too and the blood came and came and there was nothing he knew to do to stop it and he knelt there with his hands red to the wrist and the child wailing by the fire and the gray light coming up at the window and he watched her go out of her face the way a light goes out of a window when the lamp is carried into another room. She was looking at him and then she was not, though her eyes did not close, and her hand that had gripped his loosened and lay open on the tick and the breath went out of her and did not come again.

He knelt a long time. The fire burned low and he did not feed it. The child cried itself hoarse and then to a thin mewling and then to silence and slept and still he knelt. The light at the window came full and white and the sun rose somewhere beyond the trees and a bird began to sing down by the water and he heard none of it. After a while he reached and closed her eyes with his thumb and he drew the blanket up over her face and he stood. His knees had gone stiff. He looked down at her shape under the blanket and at the dark stain spread out around her and then he turned and looked at the child by the fire and he did not go to it. He went out into the morning and stood in the cold sun and the river ran by below and he stood there bareheaded with his hands hanging and looked at nothing.

He dug the grave that day on the bench above the river under a lone oak that stood there, working with the spade through the frozen crust and into the softer ground beneath and it took him till past noon for the digging was hard and he was not strong from the night. The child he had brought out and laid in a basket lined with the wool of an old coat and set it in the sun and from time to time it cried and from time to time he went and looked at it and once he picked it up and held it awkward against his chest and it quieted and he stood holding it and looking out over the river until it slept and then he laid it down again and went back to the digging.

When the grave was deep enough he went in and wrapped her in the blanket and bound it with cord and carried her out. She was light. He had not known she would be so light. He laid her in the ground and stood over her and tried to think of words to say and none came to him for he had no learning of such words and at last he said only her name aloud, once, into the still cold air, and then he filled the grave. He mounded the earth over it and set stones upon it against the beasts and he stood the spade against the oak and stood looking at the raw mound and the child cried again behind him and he turned and went to it.

He had no milk to give it. He thought of this standing there in the dusk with the dead woman in the ground and the living child in his arms and the night coming on and no fire built and nothing in the shack to eat but cold bread and a little of the squirrel. The child would die. He knew it would die without milk and he had no milk and the nearest house where there might be a woman with milk or a goat or a cow he did not rightly know but thought it lay some half a day's walk down the river where the trace went and where there was said to be a settlement of sorts, a store and a forge and some few cabins, though he had never been to it.

He built the fire and warmed the child and sat with it through the night and it cried much of the night with hunger and he could do nothing but hold it and walk it back and forth in the small room and once he dipped a rag in water and let it suck at that but it would not be fooled for long. Toward morning he made a thin gruel of bread mashed in warm water and got a little of it into the child off his finger and it took it and quieted some and slept and he slept too, sitting against the wall with the child in the crook of his arm and the fire dying and the cold coming in.

In the morning he made up a bundle of what he would need and he wrapped the child well in the wool and the blanket from his own bed and he took up the rifle and the bundle and the child and he went out and stood a moment by the grave under the oak. The frost was on it. He did not say anything. The dog came up out of the leaves and looked at him.

You stay, he said.

But the dog would not stay and followed him and after a while he let it. They went down off the bench and onto the trace that ran along the river and they went west and south as the river went, the man walking steady with the child held against him and the dog trotting at his heel and the river sliding brown and silent beside them under the gray sky.

The country thereabouts was poor and broken, all cutover hills and second growth and old fields gone to broom sedge and blackberry and here and there the chimney of a burnt house standing alone in a field like a marker over the grave of something. He passed these and did not stop. The day stayed cold and gray and once it spit a little snow that did not hold. The child slept and woke and cried and he stopped and tried to feed it from a little crock of the gruel he had brought and got some into it and went on. By midday he was tired and his arm ached from the carrying and he stopped at a place where a spring came out of the rocks and drank and rested and the dog drank and lay panting and the child woke and cried and he held it and looked down into its small red face and it looked back at him with eyes still milky and unfocused and he wondered what it saw if it saw anything and what it would be and whether it would live to be anything at all.

He came to the settlement in the late afternoon. It was as it had been told to him, a clutch of cabins set about a muddy track with a store at the center of them and a forge from which came the ring of a hammer and the smell of smoke and hot iron. There were a few people about and they looked at him as he came in among them, a gaunt and hollow-eyed man in poor clothes carrying a child and trailed by a yellow dog, and they did not speak to him and he did not speak to them but went to the store and went in.

The store was dim and close and smelled of leather and meal and molasses and old smoke. A man stood behind the counter, heavy and bald with a leather apron, and he looked up when the door opened and the light fell in.

I'm needin milk, the man said. For the child. Its mother is dead.

The storekeeper looked at him and looked at the bundle in his arms.

When did she die, he said.

Yesterday morning.

The storekeeper came round the counter and looked at the child where the man held it and he put back the wool from its face with one thick finger and looked at it.

It's near starved, he said.

I aint had nothing to give it. I had a little bread and water is all.

The storekeeper looked at him a while. Then he went to the door and called out and a name and after a moment a woman came, broad and gray-haired in an apron with her sleeves rolled, and the storekeeper said something to her low and she came and took the child from the man's arms before he rightly knew she meant to and she looked at it and clucked her tongue.

There's a woman down to Hatchers got a babe at the breast, she said. She'll take it. Come on then.

And she went out with the child and the man stood a moment and then followed her and the storekeeper followed him and they went down the muddy track to a cabin at the end of it where a young woman sat by the fire with an infant of her own and the gray-haired woman went in and spoke to her and the young woman looked up and looked at the man in the door and then she held out her arms and the gray-haired woman gave her the child and she opened her dress and put it to the breast and it found the nipple and took it and began to suck and the man stood in the door and watched and something went out of him that he had been holding all that long way and he leaned against the doorframe and shut his eyes.

You can set, the young woman said. You look done in.

He came in and sat on a stool by the fire and the dog lay down in the door and the woman nursed the child and after a while it slept and she held it and looked at the man.

What's its name, she said.

It aint got one yet, he said. There wasnt time.

She looked at him and did not say anything to that. The fire burned and the two children slept and the gray-haired woman had gone and the storekeeper had gone and it was only the man and the woman and the firelight and the gathering dark outside.

You can leave it here, she said. Till it's weaned. I got milk enough for the two. Then you can come for it. If you've a mind to come for it.

He looked at her.

You have got a place, she said. You aim to keep it.

He did not answer at once. He looked into the fire.

I dont know what I aim, he said. I aint thought past gettin it here.

She nodded as though this were a thing she understood. Well, she said. There's time to think on it. It's safe here. You go on and do what you have to do and come back when you can.

He stayed the night in the woman's cabin, lying on the floor by the fire wrapped in his blanket with the dog beside him, and in the gray of morning he rose and looked at the child where it lay sleeping in a box by the woman's bed and he did not wake it. The woman woke and watched him from the bed.

You goin, she said.

I'll come back, he said. When I'm able.

She looked at him and he could not tell what she was thinking. All right, she said.

He stood over the child a while longer. Then he took up his rifle and his blanket and he went out into the cold and the dog rose and followed him and he went back up the muddy track and out of the settlement and onto the trace along the river and he went the way he had come, north and east, into the gray morning and the broken country, and the river ran beside him going the other way and the smoke of the settlement's fires fell away behind him and was lost among the trees.

He did not go back to the shack. He could not have said why. He came to the place where the trace forked, one way running on along the river toward the bench where the shack stood and the grave under the oak, and the other climbing away from the water into the hills toward the high country and the mountains, and he stood at the fork a long while in the cold with the dog sitting beside him watching him, and then he took the way that led up into the hills and away from the river and he went up it and did not look back.

The country rose under him as he climbed. By noon he was up out of the cutover land and into standing timber, great trees dark and silent with the floor beneath them deep in old leaves and no sound in them but the wind moving high up in the tops. He went on through the afternoon and the trees closed over him and the light went green and dim and the trace dwindled to a track and the track to a trace again and then to nothing, only the open floor of the woods running on ahead of him into the gloom, and still he went on. The dog followed. Toward evening it began to snow, the flakes sifting down slow and silent through the trees and settling on the leaves and on his hat and shoulders, and he went on into it with his head down and the rifle in the crook of his arm and the dog at his heel, the two of them moving slow and small through the great dim timber and the falling snow, and the dark came down around them and the snow whitened the ground and filled their tracks behind them as they went so that it was as though they had not passed that way at all, as though no one had ever passed that way, the man and the dog going on into the high country and the night and the snow with nothing before them and nothing behind and the cold come down out of the mountains to meet them.
