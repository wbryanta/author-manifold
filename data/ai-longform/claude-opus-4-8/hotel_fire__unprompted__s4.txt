The taxi let her out at the bottom of the drive, because the driver said the gravel would scratch his paintwork, and so Marta walked the last hundred yards with her shoes in one hand and her invitation in the other, the way she had once walked the service corridor at the end of a double shift, peeling off the day in layers.

The hotel had been rebuilt. She had known it would be. She had read about it in the local paper, which her sister still mailed her in a brown envelope twice a year as though Marta might forget where she came from, and there had been a photograph of the new façade, all pale stone and glass, the architect quoted saying something about honouring the past while embracing the future. She had folded the clipping into the drawer with the others and not looked at it again. But now here it was, the building itself, and the photograph had lied the way photographs do, by being silent.

She stopped at the turn in the drive where the rhododendrons had always been, and they were still there, or new ones planted in the same place, fat and dark and dripping from the afternoon's rain. From here you could not tell that anything had ever happened. The lawns ran down green and even to the ha-ha. The fountain played. A marquee had been raised on the east lawn, white and swollen, and she could hear, faintly, the sound of a string quartet tuning, that scrape and pluck that always sounded to her like furniture being moved in another room.

She put her shoes back on. They were good shoes, the most expensive thing she owned, bought for this, and the heels sank a little into the wet gravel so that she had to walk on her toes, which made her feel like a girl pretending at being a woman, though she was fifty-one and had buried a husband and raised a son who did not call.

The invitation had come from Eleanor Whitby, who had been a child of eight the night of the fire and was now to be married on the lawn of the hotel her father had owned and lost and her husband's family had bought and rebuilt. Marta had not expected to be invited. She had not, in truth, expected to be remembered. But the letter had been handwritten, the ink slightly smudged where a left hand had dragged across it, and it had said: *You carried me down the back stairs. My mother said you sang to me so I wouldn't be afraid. I have wanted to thank you my whole life and I did not know your name until last year, when I found the old staff records. Please come. There is a place for you.*

There was a place for her. She turned the phrase over as she walked. It was the kind of thing people said.

A young man in a waistcoat met her at the top of the drive with a tray of glasses and a smile that had been issued to him along with the waistcoat. "Champagne, madam? Or there's elderflower."

"Champagne," she said, and took it, and her hand did not shake, which she noted with a small private satisfaction, the way you might note that a wound had finally stopped weeping.

"Are you bride or groom?"

"Neither," she said. "I'm an old friend of the family."

He nodded and moved on, and she stood at the edge of the lawn holding the cold glass and looking up at the hotel.

The thing she had not been prepared for was that they had kept the shape of it. The new building stood where the old one had stood, the same broad front, the same three storeys, the same pediment over the entrance, though the windows were larger now and the stone was the wrong colour, too clean, the colour of a tooth that has been capped. She found her eye climbing the façade the way it had climbed it that night from the lawn, when she had stood in her nightdress among the guests in their coats over their pyjamas and watched the third floor go.

The third floor had been staff. The guests slept on the first and second, in the good rooms with the river view, and the staff slept under the roof where the rooms were small and the ceilings sloped and in summer the heat collected like water in the bottom of a boat. There had been eleven of them up there. There had been nine of them on the lawn afterwards.

She drank the champagne too fast and felt it go to her knees.

"Marta?"

She turned. A woman was coming towards her across the grass, holding her hat against the wind with one hand, her dress the colour of the inside of a shell. She was perhaps thirty, with a wide pale face and dark brows, and Marta would not have known her at all except that something in the way she moved, quick and tilting, was the way the child had moved, the child she had carried with that same face pressed wet and hot against her neck.

"Eleanor," Marta said.

"You came." Eleanor stopped a few feet away as though there were a line drawn between them she did not dare cross without permission. Her eyes were very bright. "I wasn't sure you would. I've thought about it every day since I sent the letter, that you'd read it and think, what a strange thing, what does she want from me, and you'd put it in a drawer."

That was so nearly exactly what had happened that Marta laughed, and the laugh surprised her, and surprised Eleanor too, who laughed back, and then they were two women laughing on a lawn and the awful tightness in Marta's chest loosened by a notch.

"I nearly did," Marta admitted. "Put it in the drawer."

"What made you come?"

Marta looked at her. The truth was complicated and not entirely kind, and the truth was that she had wanted to see it, the place, rebuilt, standing, going on; she had wanted to stand in front of it and feel something and know what the something was, because for twenty-three years she had felt a hard knot where the feeling should be and she had wanted to come and dig at it. But you could not say that to a bride on her wedding day.

"You wrote me a beautiful letter," she said instead. "It would have been rude not to."

Eleanor's face did something complicated. "I don't remember the fire," she said. "That's the thing I should tell you. I have to tell you, before — I don't remember it. I don't remember the back stairs or you singing or any of it. I remember being told. My mother told me so many times that it's like a memory, but it isn't, it's a story about me. I was eight but I don't remember." She said this in a rush, as though confessing. "I thought you should know. In case you came expecting — in case I'm not what you. I don't know."

"You don't owe me a memory," Marta said.

"But you have them," Eleanor said. "You have all of them and I have none of them and they're about *me*." She pressed her lips together. The wind took her hat after all and she let it go, and a child in a kilt went chasing it down the lawn, shrieking with delight. "I'm sorry. I'm not making sense. I've had two glasses of this already and it's not even four o'clock. Will you sit with me? Before everything starts. There's a bench by the rose garden, it's the only part of the old grounds they kept, the roses, will you come and sit with me by the roses?"

So they walked, the bride and the chambermaid, around the side of the rebuilt hotel to where the rose garden lay in its low brick walls, and it was true, the roses were old, gnarled and thick-stemmed, and Marta recognised them, or recognised the kind of them, the deep red ones the head gardener had called by a woman's name she had forgotten. They sat on a wrought-iron bench that was wet and soaked through their good clothes and neither of them moved.

"My father never came back," Eleanor said. "Not once. He died in Spain. He couldn't even drive past the road that led to it. My mother used to bring me to the village to see my grandmother and we'd go the long way round, miles out of the way, so we wouldn't pass the gates." She picked at a thread on her dress. "And here I am getting married on the lawn. People think it's strange. My mother thinks it's strange. She's here, she came, but she hasn't gone inside the building, did you notice? She's been on the lawn all day. She won't go in."

"And you?" Marta said. "Why here?"

Eleanor was quiet for a moment. A blackbird worked at something in the wet grass.

"Because I wanted to take it back," she said. "Is that a terrible thing to say? It feels like a terrible thing to say in front of you. But the place took so much from my family, from everyone, from you — and I wanted to come and stand on it and be happy on it. I wanted there to be one good day on this ground. Just one. To set against." She looked up. "Does that seem mad?"

Marta thought about the third floor. She thought about Anya, who had slept in the room next to hers, Anya from Gdańsk with the gap between her teeth and the photograph of her mother she kissed every night, Anya who had not come down the back stairs. She thought about how she herself had got up that night because she could not sleep in the heat and had gone to the window for air and so had smelled it first, the smoke, before the bells, and had run down the corridor banging on doors, and had got to Anya's door and it had been hot, the handle hot, and she had not opened it, she had run on, she had gone down the front stairs, no, the back stairs, she had — and the order of it had come apart in her, the order had burned, and for twenty-three years she had not been able to lay the events of that night down in a straight line that she could walk along and trust.

"I don't know if it's mad," Marta said. "I think you'll find out. After today. Whether it worked."

Eleanor nodded slowly as though this were a thing she had needed someone to say to her plainly. "You're the only person who's spoken to me about it like it's real," she said. "Everyone else talks around it. Even James. My fiancé. His family did this — bought it, rebuilt it — and they're lovely, they really are, but for them it's a hotel they own. A good investment. A pretty venue. They don't — there's no weight in it for them." She laughed, not happily. "I keep wanting to grab them by the collar and say, people died here, do you understand, people *died* in the rooms upstairs, and I don't even remember them, I have no right to feel it, but I feel it, I feel it all the time."

"You have a right," Marta said. "You were here. Memory isn't the same as being here."

"Were they your friends? The ones who — " Eleanor stopped. "I shouldn't ask."

"You should ask," Marta said. "Nobody asks. That's the strange thing, you know, that nobody ever asks. They say how terrible, they say how lucky you were, and then they change the subject because it frightens them, and you learn to change it for them so they won't have to. You learn to carry it quietly so it won't slop over the sides and wet anybody." She turned her glass in her fingers. It was empty. "Yes. They were my friends. There were eleven of us under the roof. Two didn't come out. Anya and a boy called Tomas who I didn't know so well, he'd only started in the spring. Anya I knew. Her room was next to mine."

Eleanor said nothing, but she put her hand over Marta's hand on the wet bench, and her hand was warm, and Marta let it stay.

"I've never said her name to anyone," Marta said, and was astonished to find this was true. "Twenty-three years. My husband knew there'd been a fire, of course, everyone knew, it was in all the papers. But I never said her name to him. Anya. I don't know why I'm saying it now."

"Say it again," Eleanor said.

"Anya."

"Anya," Eleanor repeated, carefully, like a child learning a word, and the saying of it together on that bench by the old roses did something to the knot in Marta's chest that twenty-three years of silence had not done; it did not undo it, but it loosened a single thread, and Marta felt the slow warm shame of tears coming and turned her face away towards the wall.

"I'm sorry," she said. "On your wedding day. This is the last thing — "

"No," said Eleanor fiercely. "No. This is exactly the thing. This is why I wanted you here. Not to thank you — well, to thank you, but not only. I wanted — " She stopped and started again. "When I found your name in the records I felt like I'd found a piece of myself. Does that sound greedy? You carried me out of a burning building and you're the closest thing I have to a memory of the most important night of my life and I don't even have *that*, I have you instead, you're my memory, you're standing in for the eight-year-old I can't reach. I wanted to look at you and feel it. I wanted you to make it real."

Marta wiped her face with the back of her hand. "I'm not sure I can do that for you," she said. "I'm not sure anyone can make a thing real that you don't remember. I think you have to make peace with the not-remembering. That's its own thing you'll carry."

"Tell me about it, then. The night. Tell me what you remember about *me*. So I can have it."

And so Marta told her. She told her how she had smelled the smoke from her window and run down the corridor, and how the back stairs had been clear still, and how on the second floor she had gone along banging doors because the alarm had not yet sounded — the alarm, it had come out at the inquest, had been faulty, the wiring, the owner had cut a corner and a man from the council had signed a paper and both of them had been ruined by it afterwards, though ruin was not death — and how she had come to the Whitby suite and the door had been locked and she had hammered on it and a man's voice, Eleanor's father, had come muffled and angry, who is it, what is it, and she had shouted fire, fire, get out, and the door had opened and there had been the smell of brandy and sleep and the father in his shirt and behind him the mother already up, already pulling the child from the bed, and the child — Eleanor — had been crying, not screaming, just crying steadily with her fists in her eyes, and her mother could not carry her and the bags and pull the husband who had frozen in the doorway, and so Marta had taken the child onto her hip the way you do, the way her body knew how, and she had said to her, what songs do you know, do you know this one, and she had sung — she could not now remember what she had sung, some nonsense, some lullaby in Polish her own mother had sung — and she had carried her down the back stairs and out across the lawn and set her down on the wet grass, and the child had looked up at the building burning and had said, in a small clear voice, *will the fishes be all right?* because there had been an aquarium in the lobby, a great glass tank of orange fish, and that was what the child had thought of, the fish, and Marta had said yes, the fishes will be fine, knowing they would not be, and the child had been satisfied and had put her thumb in her mouth.

"The fish," Eleanor whispered. "There was an aquarium?"

"In the lobby. Orange fish. Big ones."

"I have a — I've always had a thing about — " Eleanor laughed and pressed her hand to her mouth. "I keep fish. James thinks it's a funny little hobby. I have a tank in the flat, I've had one for years, I never knew why, I just always wanted — orange fish. I always buy the orange ones." She was crying now too, openly, her wide pale face crumpling. "I didn't know why. I thought it was just a thing about me. Just a thing I liked."

Marta looked at her, and felt the knot in her chest move again, that single thread, and thought: so there it is. Not nothing. Not a clean burned-away nothing. A child looks up at the end of her whole world and asks about the fish and the asking goes down into her somewhere below memory and grows up through the years into a tank of orange fish in a flat in a city, and the woman keeps the fish and does not know why, and a chambermaid sings a song she cannot remember in a language the child does not speak, and twenty-three years later they sit on a wet bench and find the broken ends of the same thread and hold them up to the light.

"It was a thing about you," Marta said. "It still is. It's just older than you knew."

They sat. The quartet on the lawn had stopped tuning and begun to play in earnest, something light and turning, and there were voices now, more of them, the gathering of the guests, and somewhere a woman laughed too loudly and a cork went off like a small soft shot.

"They'll be looking for me," Eleanor said, not moving.

"Yes."

"I'm not ready to go in."

"You don't have to go in," Marta said. "You're getting married out here. On the lawn. You only have to walk across the grass."

"Will you come?" Eleanor said. "Will you sit at the front? I had them put a chair — there's a chair for you, second row, I had your name on it. Marta. I didn't know your surname so it just says Marta. I hope that's — I hope that wasn't presumptuous."

"Marta is enough," Marta said.

Eleanor stood and shook out her shell-coloured skirts, which were ruined now, soaked and grass-stained across the back, and she looked down at them and laughed and did not care. She held out her hand. "Will you walk me round? My mother won't come near the building and my father's dead and I haven't anyone to — it's silly, you're a guest, you shouldn't have to — but will you walk me round the side, just to where the aisle starts, and then you can go and sit down? Will you do that?"

Marta stood. Her good shoes had sunk into the soft ground and she had to brace herself to pull free, and she took the bride's arm, and they walked together out of the rose garden and around the side of the rebuilt hotel, past the wrong-coloured stone, towards the lawn where the white chairs stood in rows and the people stood among them in their bright clothes, and the marquee glowed, and the river ran on at the bottom of the slope the way it had always run, indifferent, the only thing on the whole estate that had not burned and had not been rebuilt and did not remember anything at all.

At the corner of the building, just before they came into view of the guests, Eleanor stopped and looked up, the way Marta had looked up when she arrived, climbing the façade with her eyes to the third floor where the windows were larger now and the rooms behind them were function suites with names like the Garden Room and the Orchard Room, no one sleeping under the roof anymore, the staff housed off-site, in the village, where they could not smell the smoke first.

"Up there," Eleanor said. "That's where you slept. Where they — "

"Yes."

"Anya," Eleanor said, to the windows.

"Anya," Marta said.

Then they walked out onto the lawn into the turning music and the turning faces, and Marta found her chair in the second row with her one name on a small white card, and she sat down among strangers, and she watched the woman she had carried out of the fire walk across the grass towards her happiness, and the hard knot in her chest, twenty-three years grown, did not break, but it held a little water now, and let a little out, and she sat in the second row and wept quietly for all of them, the living and the dead, while the strangers around her thought, how nice, an old friend of the family, look how moved she is, how she must love that girl.
