The ferry left at eleven, which meant Aaron had four hours to kill in a town he'd never wanted to see, a grey port called Helsø where the houses turned their backs to the water and the gulls had the look of animals who had given up expecting anything good. He found a café near the terminal that stayed open late for the dock workers and ordered coffee he didn't want, mostly to have somewhere to sit, somewhere to put his hands. The waitress was young and tired and asked nothing of him, which he was grateful for. He had spent the day being asked things. His name, his relation to the deceased, whether he understood that identification was not legally required but could be helpful, whether he understood that the condition of the body might be difficult.

The deceased. They had said the word carefully, the way you'd carry something that might still be hot.

He didn't know yet that it was Daniel. That was the point of the crossing. A man had been pulled from the water off the northern shore eleven days ago, and in the pocket of his coat there had been a laminated card with a phone number and the words *in case* written in handwriting that the police on that side could not match to anything. The number was old, a landline that had been disconnected for years but still traced, through some bureaucratic persistence, back to a house their mother had owned, and from the house to a name, and from the name to Aaron, who was the only one left to call.

"We are not certain," the officer had said on the phone, a woman with very good English and a flat, kind voice. "There are reasons to think it could be your brother. There are reasons to be uncertain. The water does things."

He had not seen Daniel in nine years. He was not sure, sitting in the café with the coffee going cold and skinned over, that he would recognise him at all. That was the thing he kept circling, the splinter he couldn't work out. You assumed you'd know your own brother's face the way you knew your own hands. But the last face he had of Daniel was nine years old and furious, lit by the porch light, mouth moving around words Aaron had decided at the time he would never forgive. Then the door. Then the car. Then nine years in which Daniel became a series of absences — a Christmas card that didn't come, a wedding he wasn't at, a voicemail Aaron had listened to twice and deleted because Daniel had been drunk and Aaron had been proud, and pride was cheaper then.

He boarded at half ten. The ferry was old and enormous, a floating hotel that smelled of diesel and frying oil and the particular carpet smell of places that were never quite warm or quite cold. He had a cabin below the waterline, a narrow berth with a porthole that showed only the black flank of the harbour wall and then, once they moved, nothing at all. He lay on the bunk in his clothes and felt the engines find their rhythm, a deep shudder that came up through the mattress and into his spine, and he thought he would not sleep and was right.

After an hour he went up.

The bar on the upper deck was half empty. A group of men in work clothes were playing cards without much joy. A couple in their sixties shared a bottle of wine in silence, the way long marriages allow. Aaron bought a beer and took it to a window, though there was nothing to see but the reflection of the room laid over the dark, his own face hung out there in the water like something already drowned. He looked older than he expected. He always did, lately. He had their father's jaw, which meant he had Daniel's too, and for a moment the face in the glass could have been either of them, could have been the thing waiting for him in the cold room on the far shore.

"You're not a regular," a man said.

Aaron turned. It was one of the card players, an older man who'd peeled off from the game and brought his glass with him. He had a face like weathered rope, all knots and salt.

"No," Aaron said. "First time."

"You don't look like a man on holiday."

"I'm not."

The man nodded as if this confirmed something. He didn't ask, which Aaron appreciated, but he didn't leave either. He leaned against the window frame and looked out at the same nothing.

"This crossing," the man said. "People take it for two reasons. Work, or to deal with something. The ones dealing with something always come up to the bar at two in the morning." He raised his glass slightly, a toast to the category. "I've done this run thirty years. You learn the faces."

"Which were you?" Aaron asked. "Work or dealing with something?"

The man smiled, showing a gap in his teeth. "Both. That's the third kind. The ones who turn the dealing into the work so they never have to stop." He drank. "My wife's over there. Was. We split the difference — I kept the boat, she kept the house. Now I go back and forth across the thing that's between us and I never have to land on either side for long." He said it lightly but it landed heavy, the way confessions do when they've been polished smooth by repetition.

Aaron found he wanted to say it out loud, to this stranger he would never see again, in the middle of the black water where words seemed to cost less.

"I'm going to identify a body," he said. "They think it might be my brother."

The man went still in the way that decent people do when you hand them something real. "Think," he repeated.

"They're not sure. We hadn't spoken in nine years. I'm not sure I'll know him."

"You'll know him," the man said. There was no comfort in it. It was just stated, the way you'd state that the tide would turn. "Whether you want to or not."

Aaron didn't answer. The man finished his drink, set the glass on the sill, and put a hand briefly on Aaron's shoulder — a sailor's hand, hard and warm — and went back to his cards without another word, and Aaron understood that the conversation had been a kindness and that its ending was a kindness too.

He stayed at the window a long time. The crossing was supposed to take nine hours. Somewhere out there was the meridian of the strait, the invisible line where one country's water became another's, and he imagined Daniel's body — if it was Daniel — drifting across that line for eleven days, belonging to no one, owing nothing to either shore. The water does things, the officer had said. He didn't let himself think about what.

What he thought about instead was the fight, because the fight was where Daniel had stopped and the absence had started, and he had spent nine years not thinking about it with a discipline he was almost proud of.

It had been about their mother. Of course it had. Everything in the last decade of her life had been about her, the great gravity of her decline pulling both of them off course. She had been ill a long time, the kind of long that hollows a family out, and Aaron had been the one who stayed. He told the story that way to himself: he had been the one who stayed. He had moved back into the cold house with the disconnected landline that still somehow rang and managed the medications and the appointments and the slow administrative violence of dying, the forms, the carers who came and went, the smell. And Daniel had been the one who left. Who called sometimes and visited rarely and sent money instead of time, as though money were the same currency as showing up, as though you could wire your presence.

That was the story. He'd told it to himself so many times it had worn a groove.

But out here on the black water with his brother possibly cold in a drawer across the meridian, the story wouldn't hold its shape the way it used to. Because there was another version, and the other version had been getting louder lately, the way the truth does when there's no one left to argue it down. In the other version, Aaron had wanted the staying. Had needed it. Had needed to be the good son so badly that he'd made the house a fortress and pulled the drawbridge up behind him, and every time Daniel had tried to come back in — and he had tried, that was the part Aaron had filed away — Aaron had found a reason it wasn't enough. Too late, too little, too drunk, too easy. He had hoarded their mother's suffering like a treasure because it made him the hero, and he had let Daniel believe he wasn't welcome because being needed by a dying woman was the only thing in Aaron's life that had ever made him feel large.

The night of the fight, Daniel had come to take a turn. He'd actually come to help. Aaron had forgotten that for nine years and now couldn't stop remembering it. Daniel had shown up unannounced with a bag, said he'd take the next two weeks, said Aaron looked like death and should sleep, should go somewhere, should be a person again. And Aaron — Aaron had heard an accusation in it. *You can't cope. You're failing. You've made a mess of this and I'm here to fix it.* He'd heard all the things he was most afraid of, and he'd thrown them back as attack. He'd said Daniel didn't get to swan in at the end and play the rescuer. He'd said where were you. He'd said she asks for you and I have to lie. He'd said you don't get to feel good about this. And Daniel, who had come to help, had stood there with his bag and taken it, and then he'd said the thing Aaron had decided to never forgive.

He'd said: *You don't want help. You want a witness. You want someone to see how much you're suffering so it counts.*

And the worst of it, the thing that had made Aaron open the door and stand by it, was that it was true. It was so exactly true that Aaron had had no defence except to make Daniel leave, because if Daniel stayed he would keep seeing, and the seeing was unbearable.

Their mother had died four months later. Daniel had come to the funeral and they had not spoken beyond what the day required. And then nine years of the discipline of not-thinking, which Aaron had mistaken for healing the way you might mistake a held breath for calm.

He went back down to the cabin around three and lay listening to the engines and did not sleep but rested in something adjacent to it, a grey suspension. He thought about the laminated card. *In case.* In case of what — in case Daniel was found, in case someone needed to know who he'd been, in case the last thread to a brother who had thrown him out should ever be needed. The number was nine years dead. Daniel had carried it anyway. He had carried a disconnected line to a house that no longer existed with the name of the brother who had told him he didn't deserve to grieve. He had carried Aaron with him into the water.

That was the thought that finally broke something. Not grief exactly — he couldn't grieve yet, not without knowing — but something structural, a wall he'd been leaning against giving way so that he half fell into the dark of the cabin, his eyes wet and his throat working. He had been so certain he was the wronged one. He had built nine years on it. And the card said otherwise. The card said Daniel had still, after everything, kept a way back.

Or it said nothing. It might not be Daniel. He had to hold both. That was the cruelty of the crossing — it made you grieve a thing you couldn't confirm, so that you arrived already exhausted by a death that might belong to someone else's brother, some other unfound man with an old number in his pocket.

Dawn came grudgingly to the porthole, a thinning of black into iron grey, and then he felt the engines change, the great deceleration, the ship gathering itself to land. He went up on deck for the approach. The far shore came out of the mist in pieces — a lighthouse, a breakwater, a cluster of buildings the colour of old teeth, low hills behind them with snow still in the creases though it was nearly spring. The cold came off the land and met him at the rail. He stood there with the other early risers, the work crowd shouldering their bags, the couple from the bar wrapped in matching coats, and watched the gap between ship and shore narrow until it was only a churn of grey water and then a thudding bump and then ropes, and the meridian was behind him, and he was on the side where his brother might be dead.

The officer who met him at the terminal was the woman from the phone, and her voice was as flat and kind in person as it had been down the line. Her name was Vigdís. She had a car, and she drove him through the waking town with its shuttered shops and its single lit bakery breathing flour-smell into the cold, and she did not fill the silence with anything false. Only once, at a red light, she said, "You did not sleep."

"No."

"Few do." She watched the light. "I want to prepare you. The man we have — the water and the time, eleven days. The face is not as a face should be. We rely on other things. The build, the hands. Any marks you might know. Scars, the teeth. Did your brother have marks?"

Aaron tried to remember Daniel's body and found, with a fresh stab, that he barely could. The body of a child he'd shared a bath with, a teenager he'd wrestled, a young man he'd clapped on the back at a wedding that turned out to be the last good day. "He broke his arm when we were kids," he said. "The left. There was a — the bone set wrong, there was a bump. And a scar on his eyebrow, the right one, he went through a glass door." He hadn't known he remembered that until it was in his mouth. The glass door. Daniel had been six. Aaron had dared him to run through the gap before the door closed and the door hadn't been open, it had been glass, and Daniel had gone through it and there'd been blood on the kitchen lino and their mother screaming and Aaron, eight years old, certain he had killed his brother and that it was the worst thing that would ever happen, not knowing what he'd do at thirty-one with a door of his own.

"Good," Vigdís said gently. "These things help."

The mortuary was a low building behind the hospital, and inside it was warm, which was somehow the most terrible thing — he had braced for cold and got warmth, a clean institutional warmth, radiators ticking, a smell of disinfectant laid over something he refused to name. Vigdís spoke to a man in scrubs in their shared language, soft and quick, and then turned to Aaron.

"You can take your time," she said. "And if you cannot do it, that is also all right. There is no failing here."

*There is no failing here.* He wanted to laugh, or weep. He'd spent his whole life believing the opposite, that there was nothing but failing, that you were the good son or you weren't, that you stayed or you left, that you got it right or you carried it forever. Daniel had tried to tell him there was no failing and Aaron had thrown him out for it.

The room was small. The body was under a sheet on a steel table and the man in scrubs stood at its head, waiting, his hands folded, a kind of priest. Aaron stood by Vigdís at the threshold and made himself breathe.

"When you are ready," she said.

He thought he would never be ready, and then he understood that this was a thing you could not be ready for and therefore had to do unready, the way you did everything that mattered. He nodded. The man in scrubs drew the sheet back to the collarbone with a practised gentleness, and Aaron looked at the face that the water had made, and it was true what they'd said, it was not as a face should be, it was swollen and wrong and the colour of nothing, of meat left out, and he could not have said from the face whether it was his brother or a stranger or no one at all.

He made himself look. The shape of the brow. The set of the jaw — their father's jaw, his own jaw, but every man on these northern shores might have a jaw like that, it proved nothing. He looked at the right eyebrow and there was so much damage there that he couldn't tell, the skin was gone or changed, the scar could have been there or not, the water had taken even that.

"The hands," he said. His voice came out cracked. "Can I see the left arm?"

The man drew the sheet further, lifted the arm with both of his, and Aaron crouched, his knees protesting, and looked at the forearm where the bone had been. There was swelling everywhere; the whole arm was distended and changed. But he ran his eyes along it, and then — because he had to, because looking wasn't enough — he asked, and the man nodded, and Aaron put his own fingers to the cold ruined skin and felt along the bone.

And there it was.

A ridge. A thickening of the bone halfway along the radius, the old break that had set proud, the bump that Daniel had let him touch a hundred times in childhood, *feel it, that's where it knitted wrong, the doctor said it'd never look right,* Daniel almost proud of it, a souvenir of survival. Aaron felt it under his fingers, that small geography of an old injury, and he knew. The water had taken the face and the scar and the colour and the warmth and everything that made a face a face, but it had left the bone, and the bone was Daniel, and Daniel was dead.

He stayed crouched, his fingers on his brother's broken arm, for a long time. He was aware of Vigdís behind him not moving, and the man in scrubs not moving, both of them holding the room steady around him while he came apart in it quietly, without sound, his shoulders going and his breath ragged and his hand not leaving the bone because it was the last warm thing he could do, the last bit of Daniel's body he would ever touch, and he had spent nine years not touching it, not calling, not forgiving, hoarding the wrong like it was treasure, and now there was only this — a ridge of bone in a warm room across a meridian, and a laminated card with his own number on it, *in case*, in case Aaron ever wanted his brother back, and Aaron had wanted him back, he understood now with a completeness that was its own kind of drowning, he had wanted him back every single day and had called it something else, had called it being right.

"It's him," he said, when he could. His voice didn't sound like his. "It's my brother. Daniel. The arm. It's him."

"Thank you," Vigdís said softly, and there was real sorrow in it, the sorrow of a woman who had stood in this room many times and never gotten used to it, which was its own kind of grace. "Take whatever time you need."

He took his hand away at last. The man in scrubs laid the arm down with that same terrible gentleness and drew the sheet back up, over the bone, over the face, and Aaron stood, his knees gone to water, and let Vigdís put her hand on his arm and steer him out into the warm corridor where the radiators ticked.

There would be forms. There were always forms; he knew that better than anyone. There would be questions about what he wanted done, about whether Daniel was to be brought back across the water or kept on this side, about a hundred administrative violences of death that he would have to answer in the coming days. He would have to learn who his brother had become in nine years, the shape of the life that had ended in the strait, whether it had been an accident or a choosing, though he suspected he would never be sure and would have to live in the not-knowing the way he was learning, too late, to live in everything else.

But for now he stood in the corridor of a low building in a country he'd never wanted to see, and through a window at the end he could see the strait, grey and enormous and indifferent, the water that had carried his brother across the invisible line and finally given him back, and he thought: I will take you home. Not because it was owed. Not to be the good son, not to be seen suffering, not because there was a right way to do this that he could finally get right. But because Daniel had kept the number. Because in case had meant in case, all those years, a door left open in a man Aaron had thrown out into the dark. He would carry his brother back across the meridian. It was the only thing left that he could give, and for the first time in nine years he wanted to give a thing without needing to be thanked for it, without needing a witness, with no one watching but the water, which forgave nothing and asked nothing and simply, endlessly, moved.
