The ferry left at eleven, which meant Halvard had the better part of an hour to stand at the rail and watch the lights of the town pull away from him, or to go below and find his cabin and try to sleep, or to do what he actually did, which was buy a coffee from the machine in the lounge and sit at a table bolted to the floor and look at his own face in the black window glass.

He had not slept properly in three days. The face that looked back at him had the loose, papery quality of a man who has been awake too long, the eyes set deep, the cheeks gone soft and gray. He was fifty-one. He looked, he thought, like their father had looked at sixty, which was to say like a man who had given up the argument he'd been having with his own life and had decided instead to simply outlast it.

The call had come on Tuesday. A woman from the district police, her voice careful and unhurried, the voice of someone trained to deliver a particular kind of news. A man had been found. There was reason to believe — she used that phrase, reason to believe, as though belief were a quantity that could be reasoned into existence — that the man might be his brother. There were documents. A name that resembled the name. An old identification card, water-ruined, the photograph gone to a smear. They had found Halvard through the registry, through the thin official thread that still connected two brothers who had not spoken in nineteen years.

Could he come north and look.

He could. He had said so without quite deciding to, the way a man steps off a curb without consulting his legs.

The coffee was terrible. He drank it anyway, holding the paper cup with both hands as though it were cold and he needed the warmth, though in fact the lounge was overheated and smelled of diesel and wet wool and the particular staleness of rooms where people wait. Across from him a young couple had arranged themselves around a sleeping child, the mother's coat folded into a pillow, the father reading something on his phone with the screen turned down low. They had the ease of people for whom this crossing was nothing, a routine inconvenience, a way of getting from one ordinary place to another. He envied them this. He envied them the sleeping child and the folded coat and the unremarkable destination.

When the engines changed pitch and the last of the town's lights swung out of the window and were gone, he went up.

The deck was empty and the wind came hard off the water, colder than he'd expected, with a smell of salt and something mineral underneath it, the smell of deep cold places. He held the rail. Below him the black sea moved in long unhurried swells, and the ferry rode them with a heaviness that he found, against all reason, comforting — the sense of a great mass committed to its course, indifferent to weather, indifferent to him.

Nineteen years.

He tried to do the arithmetic of it and found he could not quite hold the number still. It was easier to count the things he remembered, which were few and which had the over-bright, untrustworthy quality of memories revisited too often. Erlend at the kitchen table with his boots on, their mother telling him to take them off, Erlend not taking them off. Erlend at the lake, swimming out further than was allowed, his head a dark dot against the gray water and Halvard on the shore not calling out, not telling, holding the secret of his brother's defiance like something precious. Erlend's laugh, which was sudden and too loud and which made strangers turn. Erlend at the funeral — their father's — standing apart, not crying, his jaw set in that way he had, and then afterward in the car park the thing he'd said, the thing that had ended it.

You were always his. I was never anyone's.

Halvard had not understood it then and he was not sure he understood it now. It had seemed at the time a piece of cruelty dressed up as grief, a way of making the day about himself, which was the thing Erlend had always done, the thing that had worn through their mother and then their father and finally through Halvard too. He had said something back. He did not remember exactly what. Something about how the world did not owe Erlend an explanation for his own unhappiness. Something cold and reasonable and devastating, the kind of thing Halvard was good at, the kind of thing that closed a door so quietly the other person didn't realize at first that it was shut.

And then there had simply been no occasion to open it again. There is never an occasion. That was the thing nobody told you. The estrangement did not require maintenance; it was self-sustaining. A year passed and then it was strange to call, and two years passed and then it was impossible, and at some point the silence stopped being a thing that was happening between them and became simply a fact about the world, like the distance between two stars.

He'd heard things. The way you hear things. Erlend had gone north. Erlend was working the boats, or had been, or had stopped. Erlend had a woman, then didn't. Erlend was drinking. Erlend was sick. The reports came secondhand and thirdhand, through their cousin Marit mostly, who maintained a stubborn loyalty to both of them and who relayed news of each to the other in a tone of gentle reproach, as though they were children she expected to eventually shake hands and be done with it. Marit had died four years ago. After that the news stopped, and Halvard had not realized until the police called on Tuesday how completely it had stopped, how he had let his brother slide off the edge of the known world without noticing the moment it happened.

A gull went past, very white against the dark, riding the wind beside the ship for a while without seeming to move its wings, and then peeling away.

He found that he was crying, which surprised him. It was not grief, or not only grief. He could not have said what it was. The wind took the tears sideways across his face and he let it. There was no one to see.

After a while the cold drove him below.

His cabin was small and clean and entirely without character, two bunks and a steel sink and a porthole that showed nothing but black. He had paid for it alone. He sat on the lower bunk and took off his shoes and lay back without undressing and looked at the underside of the bunk above him, which was a few feet from his face, and listened to the engines and the sea.

He did not expect to sleep and he did not, exactly, but he passed into a state adjacent to sleep, a gray drifting in which the past came up around him like water. He was twelve and Erlend was nine and they had built a raft from oil drums and planks scavenged from the boathouse, and it had not floated so much as wallowed, half-submerged, the two of them clinging to it and shrieking with the cold and the joy of it while their mother stood on the dock with her hands pressed to her mouth. He could feel the rope in his hands, the rough wet sisal of it. He could feel Erlend's weight pulling against him as the raft tipped, and his own desperate effort to keep them both above the water, and the absolute certainty he'd had then — a certainty he had not felt about anything since — that it was his job to keep his brother above the water, that this was simply what he was for.

When had that ended. It must have ended somewhere. There must have been a particular day, a particular argument or absence or disappointment, after which he was no longer the one holding the rope. But he could not find it. It had eroded rather than broken, the way a coastline erodes, imperceptibly, so that you only notice it is gone when you go looking for the place you remember and find open water.

He thought about what he would see in the morning.

The woman from the police had been gentle about this part too. The body had been in the water some time, she said. He should prepare himself. There were features that could still be identified — she did not specify which — and they would also take a sample, if he was willing, for the laboratory, which was the surer method but which took weeks. The looking was for certainty's sake but also, she implied, for his sake. As though looking would give him something. As though the eye knew things the laboratory did not.

He was not sure he believed this. He was a careful man, a man who trusted documents and procedures, a man who had built a quiet competent life out of not trusting his own impressions. He did not see how looking at a ruined face would tell him anything. And yet he had come. He had gotten on the ferry. Some part of him that he did not fully govern had decided that he needed to be in the room, that there was a debt to be paid in presence, in the simple animal fact of standing beside his brother's body if it was his brother, of being the one who came.

He had told no one he was going. There was no one to tell, really. His marriage had ended amicably and emptily six years ago; his daughter was at university in the south and they spoke on Sundays about nothing in particular and he had not wanted to burden her Sunday with this. So he carried it alone, which was, he recognized, exactly the kind of thing Erlend would have done. There was a symmetry to it that he did not like.

You were always his. I was never anyone's.

The sentence would not leave him. It had a barb in it that he had not felt at the time, numbed as he'd been by the funeral, by their father's terrible diminished face in the open coffin, by the duty of being the one who held it all together. But now, in the dark, with the engines thrumming through the steel, he felt the barb. Because it was not entirely false. He had been their father's. There had been an ease between them, a likeness, the two of them quiet and orderly and contained, while Erlend had been something else, something that the household could not metabolize — too loud, too hungry, too much. Their father had loved Erlend, certainly. But he had not understood him, and he had not tried very hard to, and the not-trying had been a kind of verdict that Erlend had carried his whole life. And Halvard had benefited from that verdict. He had been the good son by the simple expedient of being the one their father could understand. He had not earned it. He had merely been born more convenient.

He had never said this to Erlend. He could have, at the funeral, in the car park. He could have said: you're right, it wasn't fair, I had something you didn't have and I didn't earn it and I'm sorry. That was what a generous man would have said. Instead he had said the cold reasonable thing, because the cold reasonable thing protected him, because to concede Erlend's point would have meant admitting that his whole comfortable sense of himself as the steady one, the responsible one, the one who held the rope, was built on an accident of temperament he had done nothing to deserve.

He turned on his side. The porthole showed black, then a faint paling at its edge, or he imagined it. It was too early for that. It was the middle of the night and they were in the middle of the strait, in the deepest part of the crossing, equidistant from both shores.

He thought: if it is him, what do I do with it.

He thought: if it is not him, what do I do with that.

Because there was a hope in him, ugly and undeniable, that it would not be his brother. That he would look at the ruined face and feel the clean relief of non-recognition and get back on the ferry and go home and resume the life in which Erlend was merely absent rather than dead, in which the door was merely closed rather than walled up. And there was, alongside this hope, its shadow: the fear that he would look and not be able to tell. That the water and the time would have done their work so thoroughly that the face would be no face, and he would stand there searching it for his brother and finding nothing, neither recognition nor its opposite, only an absence shaped vaguely like a man, and that he would have to say I don't know, I can't say, and they would take the sample and he would go home and wait weeks for a laboratory to tell him whether his brother was dead.

That seemed to him the worst outcome. Not the death. The not-knowing. The weeks of suspended grief, the body in a drawer somewhere, neither his to bury nor not his, while the slow machinery of certainty ground forward.

He must have slept after all, because he woke to the change in the engines and a grayness in the porthole that was real this time, the thin reluctant light of a northern morning. His mouth was dry and his neck ached. For a moment he did not know where he was, and then he knew, and the knowing settled over him like a wet coat.

He sat up. He went to the sink and ran the water and put his face in his cupped hands and held it there. When he looked up at the small steel mirror his face was Erlend's face. He blinked and it was his own again. They had not, in life, looked much alike — Erlend darker, broader, his features bigger and more mobile — but exhaustion and bad light had erased the differences and left only the family substrate, the shared architecture of the skull, and for a moment his brother had looked back at him out of the mirror with an expression Halvard could not read.

Up on deck the land was coming.

It came out of the gray as a darker gray, a low line that resolved slowly into hills, into the dark mass of forest, into the scattered white and red of buildings along a shore. Snow lay in the high places. The water here was different, grayer and stiller, the swells gone, and the air had a stillness to it too, a held quality, as though the whole north were waiting to see what he would do.

He had coffee from the machine again and did not taste it.

The town, when they came into it, was small and orderly and shut, the way such towns are at that hour, smoke standing straight up from a few chimneys, a single car moving along the waterfront road. The ferry sounded its horn, an enormous mournful sound that rolled out across the water and came back off the hills, and Halvard felt it in his chest like grief that had not yet found its object.

A woman was waiting on the quay. He knew her by her stillness, the way she stood apart from the small knot of people meeting the ferry, watching the passengers come down the ramp with a steady attention that was not looking for a face it would be glad to see. She was younger than her voice had suggested, dark-haired, in a dark coat, and when his eyes found hers across the diminishing distance she gave a small nod, just enough, and he understood that she had picked him out already, that he was recognizable, that grief or its anticipation had marked him in a way she had learned to read.

"Halvard," she said, when he reached her. Not a question.

"Yes."

"I'm Sunniva. We spoke." She did not offer her hand and he was grateful for it. "You'll have had a hard night."

"I slept some."

"There's no rush this morning. We can get you something to eat first. There's a place that opens early."

"No," he said. "Thank you. I'd rather just — I'd rather do it."

She looked at him a moment, assessing, and then nodded again. "All right."

They walked. The town was very quiet. Their footsteps were loud on the frozen pavement and the air bit at the inside of his nose. She did not fill the silence with talk, which he was grateful for too; she seemed to understand that words, at this point, were a kind of imposition. Only once, as they turned up a street away from the water, she said, without looking at him: "You came a long way. Not everyone does. We have people we can't reach at all, and people we reach who say it's nothing to do with them and won't come. You came."

He did not know what to say to this. "He's my brother," he said. And then, because it seemed dishonest to leave it there: "We weren't — we hadn't spoken. For a long time."

"I know," she said. "It's often the ones who hadn't. They feel it more, I think. The ones who'd kept up, they've already done a lot of their — they've had time. The ones who let it go, they have to do it all at once." She said this without judgment, simply, as a thing she had observed. "Here we are."

It was a low building, modern and unremarkable, set back from the road behind a few bare trees. Inside it was warm and very clean and smelled of disinfectant over something else, something colder. A man met them, older, white-coated, with a kind tired face, and he and Sunniva exchanged a few words too low for Halvard to hear, and then the man looked at Halvard and said, "Whenever you're ready. There's no hurry. And if you need to stop, you say so, and we stop. Do you understand?"

"Yes."

"He's been prepared as well as we could manage," the man said. "But I want you to understand that the water — you should not expect him to look as you remember. The recognition, if it comes, it often comes from something small. The shape of an ear. A scar you might know. The hands. Don't look for the whole face all at once. Let your eye find what it finds."

Halvard nodded. His mouth was very dry.

They went through a door into a smaller room, cooler than the rest, lit with a flat even light that came from everywhere and cast no shadow. There was a table, and on the table a shape under a pale sheet, and the man went to the head of it and looked at Halvard and waited until Halvard gave the smallest nod, and then he drew the sheet back to the shoulders.

Halvard made himself look.

The man had been right. It was not a face he knew. The water had done its work; the features were swollen and discolored and wrong, the proportions altered, the skin gone to something that did not read as skin. He looked at it and felt the first thing he had feared, the absence, the searching that found nothing, and a wave of vertigo went through him so that he had to put his hand on the cold edge of the table. This could be anyone. This could be no one. He searched the ruined face for his brother and could not find him, and the not-finding was so total that for a moment he thought he might be sick, or might laugh, or might simply walk out and keep walking until he reached the sea.

And then his eye, untethered from the face, did what the man had told it to do. It found what it found.

It found the hands. They had brought the arms up alongside the body and the hands lay open at the edges of the sheet, and they were swollen too, and pale, but on the back of the left hand, below the knuckle of the first finger, there was a scar. An old scar, white and raised, in the rough shape of a hook. And Halvard knew the scar. He had been there when it was made. They had been clearing brush behind the boathouse, the summer Erlend was eleven, and the bill-hook had slipped, and there had been a great deal of blood and their mother's white face and the long walk to the doctor in the next valley, Erlend silent and gray with the shock of it, holding the wadded cloth against his hand, and Halvard walking beside him holding his other hand, the good hand, the unhurt one, holding it the whole way and not letting go even when Erlend told him to, even when Erlend said you're hurting me, Halvard not letting go because some part of him understood that the holding was the only thing he had to give, that he could not stop the bleeding or undo the slip of the blade but he could hold his brother's hand the whole long way to the doctor, and so he had.

He looked at the scar.

"Yes," he said. His voice did not sound like his own. "Yes. It's him. That's my brother."

And then he reached out, before anyone could stop him, before he had decided to, and he took the cold ruined hand in both of his, the left hand, the scarred one, and he held it. It was nothing like a living hand. But he held it anyway, the way he had held it on the road to the doctor, the way he had held the rope, and he understood that this was what he had come all this way to do, across the dark water in the night — not to look, not to identify, not to satisfy the law or the laboratory, but this: to be the one who came, to hold his brother's hand at last, too late, the whole long way.

He did not know how long he stood there. The man and the woman let him. The room was very quiet and very cold and the flat light fell on everything without distinction, and Halvard held the hand and did not speak, because there was nothing left to say that the holding did not say better, and after a while he found he was talking anyway, very low, almost below hearing, saying the thing he had not said in the car park nineteen years ago, saying it now to a man who could not hear it, which was perhaps the only way he had ever been going to be able to say it at all.
