The ferry left at nine, and by the time the lights of the harbor had thinned to a few orange smears behind us, I had already forgotten what the man at the ticket counter looked like, though I remembered his hands, which were chapped and red and had pushed my change across the counter as if the coins were something he wanted to be rid of. I went up to the deck because I could not sit. The cafeteria was warm and smelled of frying oil and the seats were the kind that hold the shape of everyone who has sat in them, and I had spent enough hours in rooms like that, waiting, and I did not want to wait sitting down.

The strait was black. There is a particular blackness to northern water at night that has nothing to do with the absence of light; it is a presence, a thing that swallows the wake the ship cuts and gives nothing back. I leaned on the rail and felt the cold come up through the metal into my forearms and I thought, this is what he would have done. He always preferred the cold to the warm room. When we were boys and our mother kept the stove going until the kitchen was a furnace, Tomas would put on his coat and go stand outside in the yard, and she would say through the window, you'll catch your death, and he would not answer, only stand there with his breath going up white into the dark, and I would watch him from inside and not understand him and want to be him.

I had not seen my brother in eleven years. I want to be precise about this because precision is the only thing I have left of him, the only honest currency. Eleven years and four months. The last time was at our father's funeral, which was not really a funeral but a gathering in a rented hall with bad coffee, and Tomas had come late and left early and in between had said almost nothing to me except, once, in the corridor by the coats, you look like him now, and I had said, so do you, and that was the whole of it. We had been close once. That is the thing people do not understand when they hear about an estrangement; they imagine a wall that was always there, but it is never that. It is a closeness that curdles, a thing that was warm and then was not, and the not is so much worse for having had the warm before it.

A woman came out onto the deck and lit a cigarette with some difficulty in the wind, cupping her hands around the flame, and the small light of it showed me her face for a moment, middle-aged, tired, the face of someone who has made this crossing many times and finds in it neither romance nor dread, only the necessity of getting from one place to another. She caught me looking and nodded, the way you do at sea, and I nodded back, and she smoked her cigarette and flicked it over the rail and went back inside, and I was alone again with the water and the engine's vibration coming up through my feet.

The call had come on a Tuesday. A man with a careful, official voice, speaking my language with the slight overcorrection of someone who has learned it well but late. He said there had been a death. He said a body had been recovered—that was the word, recovered, as if the sea had taken it and they had taken it back—and that among the few possessions there had been a card with my name and number on it, and would I be willing to come and confirm the identity, as no one else had come forward. I asked whose body. He said they believed it might be my brother. I asked why they believed that. He was quiet for a moment and then said, the card, and a tattoo, a description that matched a man reported missing some weeks before, and I said I would come, and he gave me the name of the town and the address of the place where the body was being held, and I wrote it on the back of an envelope that I then could not find for two days and that turned up at last in the pocket of a coat I had not remembered wearing.

A tattoo. I did not know my brother had a tattoo. This troubled me more than it should have, standing there at the rail. Eleven years is enough time for a man to put any number of marks upon himself, but I found I could not picture it, could not imagine what Tomas would have chosen to make permanent on his own skin, and the not-knowing opened up in me like a pit, because if I did not know this then what did I know, what was I going north to identify, what face did I think I would recognize when they drew back the sheet.

I tried to remember his face. This is harder than you would think. You believe you carry the faces of the people you love around with you, fixed and whole, but they are not fixed; they slide and blur, they are reconstructed each time from fragments, and the more you reach for them the more they dissolve, like a word said too many times. I could remember particular things. The scar through his eyebrow from the time he went over the handlebars of his bicycle on the gravel road by the church. The way his front teeth crossed very slightly, which our mother had wanted to correct and our father had said no money for that, and so it had stayed, and become part of him, become the thing that made his smile his and not anyone else's. The color of his eyes, which was the same as mine, a sort of gray-green that our mother called the color of the sea before weather. But I could not assemble these into a face. They floated separate. I had a photograph at home, in a drawer, but I had not thought to bring it, and now I cursed myself for that, because I thought, what if I look and I am not sure, what if I stand there and I cannot say yes or no.

I went inside eventually because the cold had gotten into my chest and I had started to cough, the deep cough I have had since the winter, and I bought a coffee from the machine and sat down after all in one of the molded seats, and across from me an old man was asleep with his mouth open and his hands folded over a canvas bag in his lap, and beyond him a young couple were sharing a pair of earphones, one bud each, their heads bent toward each other, and I watched them and felt the great distance between their lives and mine, the way you do at night in transit among strangers, when everyone is suspended between where they were and where they are going and no one is anywhere at all.

The reason for the estrangement is both simple and impossible to explain, which is the case with most of them. There was money, our father's, not much but enough to fight over, and there was the house, and there was the question of who had stayed and who had gone, because I had stayed, I had been the one who drove our mother to her appointments in those last years and sat with her in the waiting rooms and learned the names of her medications, and Tomas had gone, Tomas had been in the south, in the city, doing whatever it was he did, sending money sometimes, which our mother kept in an envelope and would not spend, as if to spend it would be to forgive him for not being there. And when she died he came back and stood at the graveside and afterward there was the matter of the house, and I said things, and he said things, and what was underneath the things we said was older and deeper than the house, it was the whole long account of who had been loved more and who had given more and who had been the disappointment and who the favorite, and none of it could be settled because none of it was true in the simple way we needed it to be true in order to fight about it. He said I had made myself a martyr. I said he had made himself a stranger. Neither of us was wrong. That is the unbearable part. Neither of us was entirely wrong.

I slept a little, despite myself, sitting up, and woke with my neck stiff and a thread of drool at the corner of my mouth and the sense, immediate and sickening, of having been somewhere I could not return to. I had dreamed of the house. Not as it was at the end, but as it was when we were boys, with the long grass behind it that went down to the fence and the field beyond, and Tomas was running ahead of me through the grass and would not wait, would not turn around no matter how I called, and I ran after him and the grass got higher and higher until it was over my head and I could not see him anymore, could only hear him laughing somewhere ahead, and then not even that. I sat in the molded seat with my heart going and the engine droning and the old man across from me still asleep, and I thought, I am going to look at a dead man's face and try to find my brother in it, and I do not know if I want him to be there or not.

Because here is the thing I had not let myself think until the strait was around me on every side and there was no getting off the ship. If it was him, then it was over, the long silence was over in the worst possible way, ended not by reconciliation but by the simple fact of his ceasing, and I would have to carry forever the knowledge that we had not spoken, that the last words between us had been said in a corridor by the coats, you look like him now, so do you. But if it was not him—if I drew back the sheet and saw a stranger's face, some other man who had died alone and carried my brother's card for reasons I would never learn—then Tomas was still out there somewhere, still alive in the world, still possible. And I did not know which of these I was hoping for. I am ashamed to write that, but it is true. A part of me wanted it to be him so that the not-knowing would end, so that the wound would at least be a clean one, a death, a thing with a shape, rather than this endless open question of a brother somewhere out there choosing, every day, not to call.

Toward dawn the sea changed. I felt it before I saw it, a shift in the motion of the ship, and I went back out onto the deck and the sky in the east had gone the color of ash with a thin line of dirty gold along the horizon, and the water was no longer black but a heaving gray, and ahead of us, very faint, was the dark line of the land. The wind had dropped. The same woman was out there again, or I think it was the same woman, smoking, and this time she spoke to me. Cold one, she said, or something like it, in her language, and I understood the meaning if not the words, and I said yes, in mine, and she understood that, and we stood a little way apart at the rail and watched the land come up out of the sea, and there was a strange comfort in it, in standing beside a stranger who asked nothing of me and to whom I owed nothing, who did not know where I was going or why.

The town, when we docked, was smaller than I had imagined and grayer, a fishing town clinging to the edge of the water with the hills rising brown and treeless behind it. I was among the last to leave the ship. I had nowhere I needed to be until the place opened at nine, the place where they were keeping the body, and so I walked. I walked through streets that were just beginning to wake, past a bakery with its lights on and the smell of bread coming out of it, past a man hosing down the pavement in front of a shop, past gulls picking at something in the gutter. I found a café that was open and I drank coffee and ate a roll that I did not taste, and I watched the clock on the wall, and I thought about turning back. I could simply not go. I could get on the next ferry and go home and tell the man with the official voice that I had been unable to come after all, and the body would be buried as an unknown or a probable, and I would never have to look, never have to know. The thought was a great temptation. It sat with me at the table like a companion.

But I went. Of course I went. I had not crossed the strait in the night to sit in a café and turn back. I found the address, which was a low building of pale brick near the hospital, and I gave my name to a woman at a desk, and after a time a man came out, and it was him, the voice, I knew it at once, careful and official and overcorrect, and he shook my hand and his hand was warm and dry and he said he was sorry I had had to come so far. He took me down a corridor that was very clean and smelled of the chemical smell of such places, and we came to a door, and he stopped before it and turned to me and asked if I was ready, and I said yes, though I was not, and he opened the door.

The room was cold and bright. There was a table and on the table a form under a sheet, and the man went to the head of it and looked at me and I nodded, and he drew the sheet back, only to the shoulders, gently, the way you would uncover a sleeping child you did not want to wake.

I looked.

I want to tell you that I knew at once, that there was a flood of recognition or its opposite, that the body on the table either was or was not my brother and that I felt the certainty of it in my own body. But that is not what happened. What happened is that I looked at the face of a dead man and I could not tell. It was a man of about the right age. The hair was the right color, or close to it, gone gray at the temples in a way I could not have known but that seemed possible, that seemed like time having done its work. The water had not been kind; the face was swollen and changed, the lips dark, the skin a color skin should not be. There was a scar through the eyebrow. I saw it and my breath stopped. There was a scar through the eyebrow, the left one, and I tried to remember which of Tomas's eyebrows had the scar and I could not, I could not remember, the fragment had floated free of its place and I did not know if it was the left or the right, and I stood there over the body of a man who might be my brother and I could not remember which eyebrow my brother had cut open falling from his bicycle on the gravel road by the church when he was nine years old.

The man asked me, quietly, is it him.

And I looked at the crossed teeth, but the mouth was closed, I could not see the teeth, I could not ask him to open the mouth, and I looked at the closed eyes and could not know their color, and I stood there in the cold bright room and I felt the whole of my life with my brother rushing through me, the grass and the stove and the funeral and the corridor and the eleven years, and none of it would resolve into a yes or a no, none of it would tell me whether this swollen changed gray-templed scarred dead man was the boy who ran ahead of me through the long grass and would not turn around.

I asked about the tattoo.

The man seemed relieved to have something concrete to offer. He lifted the sheet a little further, on the left side, and showed me the forearm, and there on the pale dead skin was a tattoo, small, a bird, a swift or a swallow, the kind that sailors used to wear, faded and blue-green, and I looked at it and I had never seen it before in my life, I had no memory of my brother having a bird on his arm, and this proved nothing, because eleven years, because he could have gotten it at any time, because I did not know him anymore, I had not known him for a long time, and the not-knowing was the whole truth of us, it was the only thing I could be certain of, that I did not know.

I thought of the swallow. Tomas had loved the swifts that nested under the eaves of the house in summer, had watched them for hours, screaming and wheeling in the evening light, and once had said to me that he would like to be one, that they slept on the wing, did I know that, that they could stay up in the air for months and never land, that some of them never touched the ground from the day they fledged until the day they died and fell out of the sky. I had forgotten this. I had forgotten it completely until I stood over the bird on the dead man's arm and it came back to me whole, his voice saying it, the certainty in it, the longing.

It could be him. The bird could be him; it was exactly the kind of thing he would have chosen, the swift that never lands, and the scar could be his scar, and the gray could be the gray that time would have given him. Or it could be a stranger, a man who had loved birds for his own reasons and cut his eyebrow in some other fall and carried my card for some reason that died with him in the cold water of the strait.

The man was waiting. I understood that he needed an answer, that there were forms, that a thing could not remain unresolved forever in a clean cold building of pale brick, that the world requires us to say yes or no and bury its dead under one name or another.

I looked at the face a long time. I thought, if I say yes, then it is over, and I will go home and grieve a brother I had already lost, and at least the losing will be finished. And if I say no, then somewhere out there is Tomas, still choosing, and I will carry that choosing for the rest of my days, the open question, the silence that is not death but is worse than death because it could end and does not.

And I found, standing there, that I could not lie. Not because of the forms. Because of him, because of whatever he was to me still, under all the years; I could not give him a death that was not his, and I could not deny him one that was. I could only tell the truth, which was the one thing left between us that was clean.

I don't know, I said.

The man looked at me.

I'm sorry, I said. I want to tell you. But I don't know. I haven't seen him in eleven years. I can't be sure. I would be guessing, and you don't want me to guess.

He nodded slowly. He drew the sheet back up, over the bird, over the scar, over the face, gently, and he said that they could do other tests, that there were ways, that I had been right to be honest, that many people in my position said yes simply to be done with it and that this caused worse trouble later. He said it kindly. He led me back down the clean corridor and out into the gray morning, and the air off the sea was sharp and good and I breathed it in, and he shook my hand again and said they would be in touch.

I did not take the next ferry. I stayed in the town, in a small room above a bar, and the tests took some days, and in those days I walked the gray streets and watched the boats and the gulls and thought about my brother, and I found that the not-knowing, which I had thought I could not bear, was in fact only the thing I had been living in all along, for eleven years, and that I knew how to live in it, that I had had a great deal of practice. And in the evenings the swifts came out, even there, even over that gray town, wheeling and screaming under the eaves in the last of the light, and I stood and watched them and thought, he is up there, or he is down there under the sheet, and either way he is somewhere I cannot follow, and I watched them until the light was gone and they were only sounds, and then not even that, only the dark and the sound of the sea working at the stones of the harbor, patient, taking its time, giving nothing back.
