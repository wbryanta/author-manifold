He knew this country well from prior dealings, knew its waters, its forests and the people who lived among them. The journey south he made each spring was rarely pleasant but always necessary, for in the warmer regions a man with no aristocratic blood and no inheritance to speak of might still acquire something of value—a debt collected, a parcel of land traded, a chance for the kind of profit unavailable to him in the colder colonies where he had cast his lot. He had been an orphan, shipped from a Dutch poorhouse to a place where willing hands and a strong back could earn a future. That future had come, slowly, in the form of one hundred and twenty acres deeded to him by a patroon glad to be rid of land too far north to farm profitably. Jacob had not minded the distance or the cold. He minded only being beholden, and so each year he widened his commerce, dealing in goods, in lumber, in whatever changed hands honestly enough to let him sleep at night.

This particular errand troubled him, however, and had troubled him since the letter arrived. A planter named D'Ortega, a man of Portuguese stock holding a Maryland tobacco estate called Jublio, owed him a sum substantial enough to warrant the trip. The man had borrowed against a shipment that had not arrived, or had arrived spoiled, or had been swallowed by some unlucky reef—Jacob never learned which, for D'Ortega's accounts were as slippery as his manners. Now the debt was due, and Jacob meant to collect it, though he suspected D'Ortega had no intention of paying in coin.

The trail wound through stands of oak and chestnut, the canopy still wet with the morning's fog though the gold had thinned to ordinary daylight. Regina moved steadily beneath him, her ears flicking at sounds Jacob could not hear. He let his mind drift, as a man does on a long ride, to his wife waiting in the north, to the farm and the work that never ended, to the children buried beneath the trees in the small plot he had fenced himself with his own hands. Two daughters and a son, all gone before they could walk steady, each loss a stone laid upon his chest that he carried still. His wife, Rebekka, bore it differently. She turned inward, to her Bible and her chores, and some nights he heard her speaking to the dead as though they sat at the table. He did not stop her. A man learns there are griefs that cannot be argued with.

By midday the country opened. Tobacco fields stretched in ordered rows, the broad leaves drinking sun, and here and there figures bent among them, their backs curved like question marks against the green. Jacob had seen such fields before, but they unsettled him each time. There was a wrongness to wealth grown this way, a wrongness he could not always name but felt in his gut like a turned stomach. He was no saint. He dealt in goods that passed through many hands, some of them bloody, and he told himself a man must live in the world as it was made, not as he might wish it. Still, the sight of those bent figures stayed with him longer than he liked.

The road to Jublio was marked by an avenue of trees so deliberately planted that Jacob slowed to admire and resent them in equal measure. At the avenue's end stood the house, a great brick affair with two stories and glazed windows that caught the light and threw it back at him in flashing rebuke. It was, he judged, the largest private dwelling he had ever approached, and it spoke of money in a tongue Jacob could not help but understand even as it offended him. A man who could build such a house could surely pay his debts. That he had not, that he had instead summoned his creditor to travel hundreds of miles to plead for what was owed, told Jacob much about the man he had come to see.

A boy took Regina's reins without a word and led her toward a stable that was itself finer than many a farmhouse. Jacob mounted the steps, his boots leaving wet prints on the pale stone, and before he could knock the door swung open. A servant in a coat better than his own bowed him into a hall hung with portraits of stern-faced ancestors, men and women who had never set foot in this country but whose painted eyes presided over it nonetheless.

D'Ortega received him in a room that smelled of beeswax and tobacco smoke. He was a small man, soft about the middle, with a fastidious manner and hands that fluttered when he spoke. He wore a wig despite the heat, and lace at his throat and wrists, and he greeted Jacob with an effusion of welcome that Jacob distrusted at once. There was wine, there was a plate of fruit and cheese, there was talk of the journey and the weather and the troubles in Virginia, all of it designed, Jacob saw, to delay the matter at hand and to soften him with hospitality so that the asking of money might seem coarse by comparison. It was an old game and Jacob had played it before, though never with such elaborate staging.

He drank the wine, which was good, and ate the cheese, which was better, and waited. He had learned patience in a hard school. When at last D'Ortega could no longer avoid it, when the conversation had circled the debt three times without naming it, Jacob set down his glass and named it himself.

"You owe me, sir, and I have come for payment."

D'Ortega's hands fluttered up like startled birds. The amount, he said, the precise amount, ah—there had been reversals. A ship lost. The loss of the ship had cascaded, as such losses do, and now he found himself, a gentleman of standing, embarrassed before a man to whom he owed a debt of honor he fully intended to discharge, fully, only not, alas, in the manner that had been envisioned.

Jacob said nothing and let the silence work.

D'Ortega filled it, as such men always do. Perhaps, he suggested, an arrangement. A man of Jacob's evident good sense would surely understand that coin was not the only form of wealth, that here, on this estate, were riches of another kind. Land he could not spare, the entail forbade it. But other property—D'Ortega smiled—other property might change hands and satisfy them both.

He meant slaves. Jacob understood this at once and felt the turned-stomach wrongness rise in him again. He had no use for slaves. He had said as much in his letters. His farm was small, his needs were met by hired labor and his own two hands and Rebekka's. He had no plantation to work, no fields stretching to the horizon that needed bending backs. To take a person in payment of a debt struck him as a kind of theft compounded, a wrong he would be folding into his own ledger.

He said this, more or less, though he softened it for the sake of the negotiation. D'Ortega waved away his objections. A man could always use labor, could he not? A strong man could clear land, build, fetch and carry. A woman could cook and clean and mind a wife's household. And if Jacob did not need them himself, why, he might sell them on at a profit and recover his money that way and more. D'Ortega seemed to find this last point particularly clever, as though he had solved a riddle that had stumped them both.

Jacob's distaste deepened, but he was a practical man in a country that left little room for impractical men. The debt was real. D'Ortega's poverty in coin was, Jacob judged after watching him, also real, or at least real enough that pressing would yield nothing but excuses and perhaps an enemy. To leave with empty hands was to lose entirely. He thought of the long ride home, the months ahead, the always-pressing need for money to do the things he meant to do—for he had ambitions, Jacob did, ambitions he confessed to no one, of a house, a real house, perhaps not so grand as this but grand enough, grand enough to silence the small voice that still called him orphan and bondsman.

"Show me, then," he said, and hated himself a little for saying it.

D'Ortega brightened and rose, all flutter and pleasure, and led Jacob out through the back of the house into the heavy afternoon. They walked past kitchen gardens and outbuildings to a yard where, at D'Ortega's command, the slaves were assembled for his inspection. Jacob looked at them and looked away and made himself look again. He was a man who did not enjoy the looking. They stood in a loose line, men and women and a few half-grown children, and D'Ortega moved among them like a merchant displaying bolts of cloth, lifting a chin here, slapping a shoulder there, narrating their virtues in a voice grown suddenly businesslike. This one was strong. This one bred well, had thrown four already, all living. This one knew metalwork. Jacob listened and said little, and the whole proceeding filled him with a shame he could not afford to indulge.

He noticed the women in particular, perhaps because he was thinking of Rebekka and the help she lacked, the loneliness of her days on the isolated farm, the work that broke her down by season's end. There was one woman who held herself apart, tall and silent, with a child at her skirts and a baby on her hip, and something in her face—a stillness, a watchfulness—caught Jacob's eye and held it. D'Ortega saw the direction of his gaze and shook his head quickly. Not that one, he said. She is too valuable, too useful in the house. But—and here his hands fluttered again—but consider others. Consider value. We must arrive at a sum.

They could not arrive at a sum. They returned to the house and argued figures, and the figures D'Ortega proposed were absurd, valuations inflated past all reason, so that the slaves he offered would not have covered half the debt at any honest reckoning. Jacob saw the trick of it: D'Ortega meant to discharge a large debt with a small payment and call it generosity. The negotiation soured. Voices, though kept low by the rules of hospitality, grew edged. D'Ortega's welcome thinned to a brittle courtesy, and Jacob's patience, never inexhaustible, frayed.

It was in the midst of this that the woman from the yard appeared again, the tall silent one, bringing some dish or other into the room at her mistress's quiet bidding. She set it down and turned to go, and as she did Jacob became aware of a small figure that had followed her in and now stood half hidden behind her skirts—a girl, very young, six or seven perhaps, with large frightened eyes and bare feet too big for her thin legs. The girl wore a woman's cast-off shoes, Jacob saw, stuffed with rags to fit, and the sight of those ridiculous shoes on those small feet struck him oddly, struck him in the place where his buried children lay.

The woman looked at Jacob. It was a brief look, a flash of something he could not name, and then it was gone and she was guiding the girl out of the room with a hand on her shoulder.

D'Ortega, irritable now, said something sharp in Portuguese and the woman stopped. There followed an exchange Jacob did not understand, the planter speaking low and angry, the woman answering in a voice so quiet it barely disturbed the air. Then the woman did a thing that Jacob would remember for the rest of his life and would never fully comprehend. She turned to him, to Jacob, the stranger, the creditor, the man with wet boots and a cheap saddle, and she spoke.

"Please, Senhor," she said, in English broken but clear. "Not me. Take her. Take my daughter."

The room went still. D'Ortega's face colored. The girl behind the skirts made a small sound. The woman did not look away from Jacob. Her eyes held his with a force that pressed against him like a hand on the chest, saying something important, saying something he could not entirely hear but felt the shape of. Take the child. Take her away from here. Whatever waits for her here, this is worse, and you, stranger, you with your discomfort and your turned stomach, you are the lesser harm.

Jacob did not know what he was looking at. He thought, in the muddle of the moment, that the woman wished to be rid of the child, that there was some cruelty in her, some absence of feeling. He was a man and could be foolish in the way men are foolish about women's hearts. He could not see, would never quite see, that he was being asked to perform an act of rescue dressed as an act of commerce, that a mother was spending the only currency she had—the chance, the slim chance, that a stranger's hands would be gentler than the hands she knew—and that the spending of it was tearing something in her that would never knit.

He looked at the girl. She was nothing, a scrap, no use to him at all, too small to work, another mouth on a farm where mouths were counted. To take her was to take a loss, plain and simple, to fold a useless child into a transaction already weighted against him. And yet.

And yet there were those shoes. There was Rebekka alone in the cold north, who had buried three and might welcome a fourth that came already breathing. There was the woman's eyes, which would not release him. There was, perhaps, the orphan in Jacob himself, the boy shipped from a poorhouse, who knew what it was to be given away and what it was to be taken in.

"This one," Jacob heard himself say. "I'll take this one. Against the debt."

D'Ortega protested—the child was worth little, surely Jacob wanted value, a grown hand, a worker—but Jacob, who had argued for value all afternoon, now insisted on the opposite with a stubbornness that surprised them both. He did not understand his own insistence and did not examine it. He named a figure, a generous figure for so small a thing, and let it stand against a portion of the debt, the rest to be paid in goods later, in promissory notes, in whatever fiction would let him leave this house and never return. He wanted, suddenly, only to be gone. He wanted the cold north and his small farm and his grieving wife. He wanted to be away from these portraits and this lace and this wrongness that clung to everything D'Ortega touched.

The arrangement was made. The papers were drawn. D'Ortega, recovering his oily good humor now that the matter was settled in a way that cost him little, pressed more wine on Jacob, which Jacob declined. He wanted to leave before the fog of the morning returned to trap him in this place.

The mother was sent for. She came with the child's small bundle, a knot of rag holding a comb, a length of cloth, nothing. She knelt and spoke to the girl rapidly in a tongue that was neither English nor Portuguese, some mix of her own, pressing words into the child's ears as though words could be packed away like provisions for a journey. The girl did not cry. She watched her mother's face with the terrible attention of a child memorizing what she fears she will not see again. The mother put her hand on the girl's head, on her cheek, on her shoulder, touching as if to leave a print that would outlast the touching. Then she stood, and she did not look at Jacob again. She had spent her look. There was nothing left to spend.

Jacob took the child's hand. It was small and dry and it did not grip his back. He led her out through the hall, past the painted ancestors, out into the failing afternoon light where Regina waited, and he lifted the girl onto the saddle before him as though she weighed nothing, which she nearly did. She made no sound. She held the bundle against her chest with both arms and looked back once at the great brick house, at a window where perhaps her mother stood, perhaps did not, Jacob could not tell and did not look closely, for looking closely was a thing he found he could not do.

They rode out along the avenue of deliberate trees, the child silent before him, her thin back against his chest, and Jacob felt the strange weight of what he had done settle onto him. He had come for money and was leaving with a child. He had argued all day against taking a single soul in payment and was carrying one home. He could not have explained it to Rebekka, could not have explained it to himself. He told himself it was charity, and was not sure that was true. He told himself it was foolishness, and was not sure of that either. The girl smelled of woodsmoke and tobacco and something underneath, the particular smell of a child, and it undid him in a way he had not been undone since the last of his own was lowered into the cold ground.

"What's your name?" he asked her, when the house was behind them and the trail had closed around them.

She did not answer at first. He thought perhaps she had no English, or none she would give him. But after a long while, as Regina picked her careful way along the Lenape track and the light went gold again toward evening, the child spoke, so quietly he had to bend his head to hear.

"Florens," she said.

"Florens," he repeated, and the name sat strangely on his tongue, foreign and soft. "I am Jacob. Jacob Vaark. You'll come and live with me and my wife. Far to the north. It is cold there, colder than here. But there is food and there is a roof and no one will—" He stopped. He did not know how to finish the sentence. No one will what? He could not promise her what he wished to promise. He knew the laws of this country, the lawless laws he had named to himself only that morning, riding cautious through the Virginia mess, and he knew that a child like this one was owned now, owned by him, that the kindness he imagined himself doing was built on the same foundation as D'Ortega's cruelty, that he had simply chosen to be a gentler master and that the word master remained.

The thought troubled him and he set it aside, as he had set aside many troubling thoughts that day, for a man cannot carry every weight at once and still keep moving forward. He would think on it later. He would find some way to make it right, or right enough. He would treat the child well. Rebekka would teach her, would clothe her, would mother her if she could find it in her broken heart to mother again. There was Lina already at the farm, the native woman he had bought years before from a settlement decimated by sickness, Lina who was clever and strong and lonely. There was Sorrow, the strange girl found half drowned and half mad whom no one else would take. There was Will and there was Scully, the indentured men he hired from a neighbor. It was an odd household he was building, an accidental gathering of the unwanted and the orphaned and the cast-off, and Florens would be one more thread in that strange weave. Perhaps, he thought, perhaps such a household, made of broken pieces, might hold together where whole and proper families had failed. He had buried his proper family. He would try, then, this other thing.

The fog did not return that evening. The trail ran clear before them through the deepening woods, and the child grew heavy against him as the light failed, her head nodding, her grip on the bundle loosening as sleep took her. Jacob held her steady with one arm and guided Regina with the other, and he rode slow now, not from caution but from something gentler, a wish not to wake her, a tenderness that ambushed him out of the gathering dark. He thought of the woman who had spent her last look on him, who had pressed words into her daughter's ears and a print onto her cheek and then turned away with nothing left. He had taken her child. Whatever he told himself, whatever charity he claimed, he had taken her child and ridden off with her into the night, and the woman would stand in that yard or that window with empty arms and an emptied face, and there was no ledger in the world that could balance such an account.

He did not know that the woman had chosen him not for his kindness, which she could not have seen, but out of a desperation he could not measure—that she had read in some sign or instinct a danger to her daughter at Jublio that she would do anything, surrender anything, to spare her. He did not know what eyes she had seen turned upon the child, what future she had glimpsed and recoiled from. He thought she did not want the girl. He carried that misunderstanding north with him along with the girl herself, and the misunderstanding would lodge in the child too, would burrow into Florens and grow there in the dark like a seed planted in poor soil, the conviction that she had been given away, cast off, chosen against—that her mother had held the little boy's hand and let the daughter go. She would carry it all her life, that wound, and it would shape her hungers and her terrors, the way she watched faces, the way she feared being left.

But that was all ahead, unwritten, in the cold north where they were bound. For now there was only the dark trail, the slow horse, the sleeping child, and the man who held her, riding home through a ruined country toward a farm and a wife and a house he meant someday to build, carrying with him a burden he had not sought and could not put down, the small warm weight of a girl named Florens, asleep against his heart.
