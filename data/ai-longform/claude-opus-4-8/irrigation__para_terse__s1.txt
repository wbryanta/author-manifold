The water had stopped sometime in the night, and by the time Daniel walked the lower rows the soil at the base of the trees had gone the color of old ash. He could feel it through his boots, that particular dryness that meant the drip line had failed again, somewhere between the well house and the far end where their father had run the last extension twenty years ago. The June sun was not yet hot, but it would be. He stood in the gray light with the irrigation map he carried only in his head and tried to guess where the break had come, and behind him he heard the screen door of the house clap shut and then his brother's truck door, and he knew the day had begun whether he was ready or not.

Marcus came down the slope with two thermoses and a roll of new tubing slung over his shoulder, and he handed Daniel one of the thermoses without a word, which was itself a kind of word. They had not been easy with each other since April. Daniel took the coffee and they stood looking at the apricot trees, which were the oldest and the first to show distress, their leaves curling like paper held near a flame.

"It's the main feed," Marcus said. "Has to be. Pressure's gone all the way down."

"Could be the well."

"Pump was running when I checked. I could hear it cycling."

Daniel drank. The coffee was too strong, the way Marcus had always made it, the way their father had, and he wondered if Marcus did it on purpose or if it was just the groove the family had worn into the act of making coffee, a channel that water followed without thinking. "Then we walk it," he said. "Start at the well house, find the break."

"Or," Marcus said, and Daniel knew what was coming because it always came, "we accept that we've been resurrecting this thing for three years and it keeps dying anyway."

"Drink your coffee."

They walked. The orchard ran forty acres up a south-facing slope, apricots at the bottom where the frost pooled and killed them half the years, then the Bartletts, then at the top the old apple varieties their grandfather had planted, names that meant nothing to anyone anymore, Wolf River and Maiden Blush and a russet whose name even their father had forgotten. The main line ran up the eastern edge in a trench their father had dug by hand the summer Daniel was nine, and they followed it now, Marcus carrying the new tubing and Daniel carrying the shovel, and the dew came off the grass and soaked their boots.

"You haven't called the man back," Marcus said.

"No."

"He's not going to wait forever. The offer's good, Danny. It's better than good. You know what land's going for. He doesn't even need it for the trees, he's going to pull them and put in a vineyard, so it's not even about whether the orchard works—"

"I know what he's going to do with it."

"Then you know it doesn't matter that the line's broken again. That's my whole point. We could let it all die today, right now, and it wouldn't change the number on the paper."

Daniel stopped walking. They had reached the first valve, and he knelt and felt the housing, dry as a stone. He did not look at his brother. "Then why are we out here," he said.

Marcus was quiet a moment. "Because you wanted to be," he said, and there was no cruelty in it, only a tiredness that Daniel recognized as the mirror of his own. "Because you can't stand to watch them die slow."

They found the first problem at the second valve, a gopher had chewed through a lateral and the water had been bleeding into the ground all night, which explained the lost pressure though not all of it. Marcus cut out the chewed section while Daniel held the line, and they worked without talking, and there was a rhythm to it that was older than the argument, older than the offer, older maybe than the resentment that had grown up between them like a sucker from a graft, the same way the orchard itself had a rhythm that did not care what they decided.

"Hold it steady," Marcus said.

"I am holding it."

"You're letting it kink."

Daniel adjusted his grip. The sun had cleared the ridge now and he could feel it warming the back of his neck, and he thought about the years he had spent under this sun, the summers home from college that he had resented and the summers after their father got sick that he had not. He had been the one who came back. Marcus had a life in the city, a wife, two children Daniel saw at Christmas, a job that involved spreadsheets and a lanyard. Daniel had a single bed in the house where they had grown up and a dog that had died in March and a checking account that their father's medical bills had hollowed out. He held the line steady.

"There," Marcus said, fitting the coupling. "Open it back up."

Daniel walked back to the valve and opened it, and they listened, and the line firmed but did not firm enough.

"Another break," Daniel said.

"Or ten."

They walked on. The grass between the rows had not been mowed and it dragged at their legs, and grasshoppers went up before them in dry little explosions. Marcus said, "You remember when Dad flooded the whole bottom field because he forgot to close the valve?"

"He didn't forget. He fell asleep."

"He told Mom he forgot."

"He told Mom a lot of things." Daniel found himself almost smiling, and he let it happen because it was easier than the other thing. "She knew."

"She knew everything." Marcus stepped over the trench. "You know she's the one who'd want us to sell."

"Don't."

"It's true, though. She hated this place. She'd have sold it the week after the funeral if it'd been hers to sell."

"It wasn't hers to sell."

"No. Dad made real sure of that." There was the edge again, surfacing. "He made sure of a lot of things. He made sure you'd never be able to leave it, you know that? He knew exactly what he was doing when he left it the way he did. Joint. So neither of us can do anything without the other. So we'd have to fight about it. So one of us would have to be the bad guy."

Daniel drove the shovel into the soft ground where a wet spot had appeared, a dark bloom in the gray. "Here's another," he said.

They knelt together and dug, and the cool earth came up smelling of iron and rot, and Daniel thought his brother was wrong, that their father had not been clever enough or cruel enough to engineer their disagreement, that he had simply loved the place too much to imagine it gone and had not been able to picture a future in which his sons did not love it the same way. That was the failure of imagination that had made him a hard man to live with and a hard man to bury. He had not been able to picture other people's interiors. He had assumed the orchard lived inside everyone the way it lived inside him.

"It's a split in the main," Marcus said. "Look. It's perished. The whole line's perishing. We patch this one, it'll go somewhere else next week."

"So we patch it next week."

"Listen to yourself."

"I'm listening." Daniel sat back on his heels. The break was an ugly thing, the old black poly gone brittle and gray, cracked along a seam where the freeze and thaw of twenty winters had worked at it. "You think I don't know it's failing. I know exactly what it is. I'm the one out here in February when the lines freeze. I'm the one who found the deer in the well house last year. You think I'm romantic about it. I'm not romantic about it. I know what it is better than you ever will because you only come back to argue about selling it."

Marcus's jaw worked. "That's not fair."

"None of it's fair. You said it yourself."

"I come back when I can. I have a family."

"I know you have a family."

"You say it like it's a crime."

"I don't say it like anything." But he did, and they both knew he did, and the thing that lay under it, the thing neither of them said, was that Daniel had stayed and given up the possibility of a family of his own, that he was thirty-eight and lived alone in a farmhouse with a dead man's furniture, and that some part of him had needed the orchard to be worth it, had needed it to be a sacrifice and not merely a place where his life had drained away into the soil like the water bleeding from the broken line. If they sold it, then he had stayed for nothing. If they kept it, he had to keep staying. There was no version where he got back the years.

Marcus seemed to hear all of it. He always had been able to hear the unsaid part; it was the one thing about him that was not city. He cut the broken section out and held the two clean ends and said, more quietly, "I'm not trying to take something from you, Danny. I'm trying to give you a way out. That's what you can't see. The money's not for me. I've got money. I want—" He stopped. "I want you to have a life that isn't this."

"This is a life."

"Is it?"

Daniel didn't answer. He handed his brother the coupling and Marcus fitted it, and they worked the clamps down, and Daniel went back and opened the valve and this time the line firmed and held, and far down the slope he heard the first emitters begin to tick, a sound like rain that wasn't rain, and the dry soil at the base of the apricots began, slowly, to darken.

They sat in the shade of the largest apple tree to drink the rest of the coffee. The tree was a Wolf River, their grandfather's, and it was dying from the top down, the upper branches bare and pale, but the lower limbs had set fruit, hard green knots that would swell through the summer into apples so large a single one could fill a pie. Nobody bought Wolf River apples anymore. Daniel gave them away or let them fall.

"He used to put me up in this tree," Marcus said. "You were too little. He'd put me up in the crotch there and tell me to drop the apples down to him and he'd catch them in his shirt. He had that way of holding his shirt out."

"I remember."

"You couldn't remember. You were two."

"I remember him holding his shirt out. Maybe it was for me."

"Maybe." Marcus looked up into the dying crown of the tree. "You think it knows it's dying?"

"It's a tree."

"That's not an answer."

Daniel considered the bare branches against the hard blue sky. "I think it just does what it does," he said. "Sets fruit where it can. Lets go of what it can't keep alive. I don't think it knows anything. I think we're the ones who know things. That's our whole problem."

Marcus laughed, a short surprised sound. "That's the most you've said in a month."

"Don't get used to it."

They sat. A red-tailed hawk turned over the upper field, and the emitters ticked, and somewhere a phoebe was calling from the well house eaves. Daniel thought about how he would describe this day later, if he ever described it to anyone, and realized there was no one to describe it to, that the inventory of his life had narrowed to this brother and these trees and the dog that was gone, and that the narrowing had happened so gradually he had not felt it the way you do not feel yourself falling asleep.

"There was a girl," Marcus said suddenly. "Wasn't there. Years ago. The one who worked at the co-op. Sarah."

"Sara. No h."

"You did remember her, then."

"I remember a lot of things."

"What happened with her?"

Daniel turned the thermos cup in his hands. "She wanted to go to Portland. There was a thing, a job, for her. She asked me to come." He shrugged. "Dad had just had the first stroke. I couldn't."

"You could've."

"No. I couldn't." He said it without bitterness, which surprised him, because he had spent years saying it with bitterness, in his head, to a version of his brother who was not listening, and now that the real brother was listening the bitterness had somehow gone out of it. "He couldn't get up the stairs. Somebody had to be here. Mom couldn't lift him."

"You never told me that."

"You never asked."

Marcus was quiet for a long time. The hawk drifted off the field and was gone. "I asked you to be the bad guy," he said finally. "When I said sell, all this time. I made you be the one who said no, so I wouldn't have to be the one who said yes. So I could tell myself you were the stubborn one, you were the one keeping us chained to it. But you're not keeping us chained to it. You already gave it everything. You gave it Sara." He pressed the heels of his hands into his eyes. "Christ. I knew Dad was up here falling down and I was in the city worried about whether we'd get the kitchen redone before the baby. I knew and I let you do it. I let you be the one."

"Somebody had to."

"It didn't have to be only you."

"It's done," Daniel said. "There's no use." But he felt something shift in him, some ground that had been frozen a long time, and the feeling of it was not relief but a kind of grief, because the bitterness had at least been his, had at least kept him company, and now even that was being taken.

They got up after a while and walked the rest of the line, and there were two more breaks in the apple section, both of them small, and they fixed them and moved on, and by noon they had walked the whole forty acres and the whole system was holding, the trees getting water for the first time in two days, and the soil all up and down the slope going dark, and the smell of wet earth rising in the heat. They stood at the top by the russet whose name was lost and looked back down the slope at what they had done, and it was good work, and they both knew it was good work that did not matter, that they had nursed a dying thing through one more day for reasons neither of them could fully name.

"It'll go again," Marcus said, but gently now, not as an argument.

"Probably tonight."

"You'd come out and fix it again."

"Probably."

Marcus nodded slowly. He was looking down at the house, small and white at the bottom of the slope, their mother's hollyhocks still coming up along the south wall though she had been gone six years, planted by a hand that no longer existed and persisting anyway, the way the orchard persisted, the way the coffee got made too strong. "When I was driving up this morning," he said, "I had the whole thing planned out. What I was going to say. I had numbers. I was going to wear you down. I'm good at that, it's literally my job, wearing people down with numbers until they agree to a thing." He laughed without humor. "And I get here and you're standing in the lower rows with that look on your face Dad used to get, and I think, I can't do it. I can't be the one to wear him down. Not about this."

"You don't have to wear me down."

Marcus turned to look at him.

"I'll sign," Daniel said. The words came out and he heard them as if someone else had said them, and then he understood that he had known them for weeks, maybe months, that he had walked the lines in February with this decision already made and had only needed his brother to come up the slope with two thermoses and a roll of tubing for him to be able to say it. "Call the man back. Tell him yes. I'll sign."

Marcus did not say anything for a moment, and Daniel saw that he had wanted this and had also, somewhere underneath, not wanted it, that his brother too had needed the orchard to be a thing worth fighting about, because if it was nothing then their father had been nothing, then the whole inheritance of stubbornness and silence and too-strong coffee was nothing, was just a forty-acre liability on a south-facing slope.

"You're sure," Marcus said.

"No. But I'm doing it anyway. That's what sure is, I think. Doing it without being sure." Daniel looked at the dying russet, at the green fruit it had set despite everything, fruit that would ripen in October on a tree that would be bulldozed by November. "I'm tired, Marcus. I'm tired in a way I can't fix with water. I've been keeping it alive so I wouldn't have to figure out what I am if I'm not the one keeping it alive. And I don't know what I am. But I'm thirty-eight and I'd like to find out before I'm Dad up in that tree dropping apples to a kid who's too little to remember it."

Marcus put his hand on the back of Daniel's neck the way their father had done to both of them, the only gesture of tenderness the old man had owned, and they stood like that, two grown men at the top of a dying orchard, not looking at each other, looking down at the white house and the persisting hollyhocks and the dark wet earth, and the emitters ticked all up and down the slope, watering trees that had been condemned by the very decision that had just been made, and there was something in that Daniel could not have explained to anyone, the care they had taken with a thing they had agreed to let die, the good work that did not matter and was maybe the only kind that ever did.

"We should get the rest of the coffee," Marcus said finally, his voice not quite steady. "Before it goes cold."

"It's already cold."

"Then we'll make more."

They walked down through the apple rows, through the Bartletts, down into the bottom where the apricots stood in their dark wet circles, the frost-pocket trees that had broken their father's heart a dozen springs and that he had refused to pull because to pull them was to admit something he could not admit. Daniel stopped at the oldest of them and touched the curling leaves, which had already begun, in the few hours since the water came back, to relax, to uncurl at the edges, reaching after the moisture in the ground. They would green up by evening. They would not know they had been saved for nothing. They would do what they did, set fruit where they could, let go of what they could not keep alive, and in this they were wiser than the men who tended them, who knew too much and could not stop knowing it.

"They're coming back," Marcus said, surprised.

"They always do. Give them water, they come back. They don't hold grudges." Daniel let the leaf go. "It's the one thing I always envied about them."

They went on down toward the house. The June day had become the hot bright thing it had promised at dawn to become, and the well pump cycled in its little house, and the water ran out through the patched and failing veins of the orchard, and the two brothers walked down out of the trees into the open, where their mother's flowers were blooming against the wall of the house their father had filled and emptied, going to make a fresh pot of coffee too strong to drink, because it was noon, and they were thirsty, and there was nothing else, just then, that either of them knew how to do.
