The frost had not yet lifted from the fence wires when Aldous Renn loaded the canvas bag of tools into the back of the Morris and let the dog watch him do it. The dog was old too, a border collie gone milk-eyed at the edges, and she no longer expected to come along. She lay in the doorway of the house with her chin between her paws and observed the ritual of departure as she had observed it for thirteen years, registering the bag, the thermos, the felt hat, and the particular slowness with which Aldous now closed the boot.

"Not today," he told her, though she had asked nothing.

He drove out past the milking sheds where the Loftus boy raised a hand without looking up, past the chapel that had been a chapel and was now a place where a man named Pursglove stored furniture under tarpaulins, and onto the road that climbed toward the high farms. The valley unrolled below him in the grey light, all stone walls and the white breath of cattle, and Aldous drove it the way he had driven it for fifty-one years, knowing where the camber threw the car and where the verge softened in wet weather and where, on the long descent past Three Chimneys, you could put the engine out of gear and coast the better part of a mile if you were the sort of man who wished to save petrol, which he was, or had been raised to be, which amounted to the same thing.

There were five instruments on the route now. There had once been forty.

He thought about this without bitterness, the way he thought about his own diminishment, as a matter of fact rather than grievance. The pianos had gone the way of the farms, sold off or chopped for firewood or left to swell and crack in unheated rooms until their voices became something no tuning could reclaim. The people had gone too, the daughters to the cities and the sons to wherever sons went, and the old ones into the churchyards, and what remained was a scattering of houses where a piano stood against a wall like a piece of inherited weather, a thing nobody touched and nobody could bring themselves to be rid of.

He tuned them anyway. They paid him, some of them, a few coins or a jar of something or nothing at all, and he came twice a year and made the dead instruments speak for an hour and then drove away. He could not have said why they wanted it done. He could not have said why he did it. Perhaps it was that a thing kept in tune was a thing not yet given up on, and to surrender the tuning was to admit a death larger than the instrument's.

The first house was the Bellinghams', though no Bellingham had lived there in a decade. It was kept now by a niece, a heavy quiet woman named Ruth who let him in without speaking and stood in the kitchen doorway while he worked. The piano was a Broadwood upright, walnut-cased, and it had been her aunt's pride. Aldous laid out his tools on the cloth he carried, the tuning levers and the wedges of red and grey felt, the tuning fork he struck against his knee out of long habit though he had a meter now that did the listening for him. He did not use the meter. He had bought it to please his son, years ago, and kept it in the bag like a relic.

He set the wedges and brought the A up to pitch and worked outward from it, the fifths and the fourths, the slow beating of the intervals settling under his hands. The room smelled of damp and lavender polish. Through the window the fells went up and up into cloud.

"Does anyone play it?" he asked, knowing the answer, asking because the silence wanted filling.

"No," Ruth said. Then, after a while: "I sit with it sometimes. In the evenings."

He nodded. He understood that perfectly and said nothing, because to say he understood would have been to make a small thing larger than it could bear. He tuned the upper register, where the strings ran thin and bright, and the notes came clean off his hammer one after another, and Ruth stood in the doorway with her arms folded and her eyes somewhere else.

When he had finished he played a little, as he always did, to prove the work. He played a few bars of a hymn tune, nothing more, his hands stiff at the knuckles now and slower than they had been. The Broadwood answered him richly. It was a good instrument and it had been built by men who were dead and it would outlast Ruth and outlast the house, probably, and go on standing mute against some wall while the world rearranged itself around it.

"There," he said.

Ruth gave him fifty pence and an apple, and he took both with equal gravity, and the dog of the house, a different dog, younger, walked him to the gate.

The second house was Ackroyd's, where the old man himself was still living, ninety-one and deaf as a post, which made the tuning an act of pure abstraction. Ackroyd could not hear the piano and never could have heard whether it was in tune or not, but his wife had played it, and his wife was four years dead, and the piano was tuned twice a year for the same reason the photographs were dusted and her coat still hung by the door. Aldous worked while Ackroyd sat in his chair by the range with a blanket over his knees and watched the fire. They did not attempt conversation. There was no conversation to be had across that gulf of silence, and both men had made their peace with it long ago. When Aldous finished he came and stood in front of the old man so that he could be seen and made the small gesture, hand flat, tipped side to side, that meant *done, it's all right, it's done*, and Ackroyd nodded and lifted one spotted hand from the blanket in something like benediction.

There was money on the dresser, left out for him. He took what was owed and not a penny more and let himself out.

By noon the cloud had come down off the tops and a thin rain moved across the valley in sheets, and Aldous ate his sandwich in the car in the lane above Garsdale with the wipers off and the world dissolving on the glass. He drank tea from the thermos. He thought, because he could not always help it, about the order of the route, and about how he had arranged it, decades ago, so that it ended where it ended, at the church in the bottom of the valley, and how for most of those years the ending had been a matter of geography only, the church simply being the last and lowest point, and how at some indeterminate time it had become something else, a destination he drove toward all day with a tightness under his breastbone that he did not examine.

The boy had been eleven.

Aldous did not think the word *boy* and then correct it to a name, because the name was a country he did not enter lightly. But the boy had been eleven, and it had been a recital, a real one, the children of the district playing in the church on a June evening with the doors open to the warm air, and the boy had played a piece by Schubert that Aldous still could not hear without his throat closing, an impromptu, the one in G-flat, all that flowing right hand over the slow tread of the left, and he had played it without a mistake, without hesitation, with a kind of inwardness that had frightened Aldous because it was not the playing of a child. The other children had banged through their pieces and been applauded and that was right and good. But when the boy played, the church had gone still in a different way. Aldous had sat in the third pew with his wife's hand in his and understood, in the space of three minutes, that his son possessed something that had been withheld from him, Aldous, all his life — that he, the father, could tune the instrument to a hair's breadth and lay every interval clean as a blade and that none of it, none of it, was the same as what the boy was doing, which was making the thing *mean* something, which was speaking through it.

He had wept. He was not ashamed of that. Men wept then, at such things, more readily than they were given credit for.

And afterward there had been the long warm walk to the car, and the boy's hand in his, and the boy had asked, *was it all right, Dad,* in that way children had of not yet knowing what they were, and Aldous had said *yes,* had said *yes, it was all right,* which was the largest word he owned and the smallest possible vessel for what he meant. He had meant to say more. He had thought there would be time to say more.

He finished his tea and screwed the cup back onto the thermos and drove down out of the rain.

The third and fourth houses he did quickly, his hands knowing the work without him, his mind elsewhere. One was a chapel cottage with a piano so far gone that tuning it was a kindness extended to a corpse, the soundboard split, three notes dead and unrecoverable, and he did what could be done and apologised for what could not, and the woman there, who was perhaps sixty and had the face of someone perpetually braced against bad news, said it didn't matter, said it was lovely, said her mother would have been pleased, and Aldous understood that the tuning was for the mother and the mother was dead and the piano was a sort of grave that could be visited twice a year and made briefly to sing.

He was tired by the time he reached the fourth, a Mrs. Heseltine who gave him tea he did not want and talked at him without pause about her hip and her son in Leeds and the price of coal, and he was glad of it, oddly, glad of the noise of a living person who wanted nothing from him but an audience. Her piano was a little better than most because a granddaughter came sometimes and made a racket on it, and Aldous found himself glad of the racket, glad of the worn places on the keys where small hands had been, glad that somewhere a child still hit the notes carelessly and laughed.

"You'll be wanting to get on," Mrs. Heseltine said at last, which was what people said when they themselves wanted you gone, and he stood and gathered his bag.

"I'll be down at the church after," he said, not knowing why he told her.

"Doing the organ, are you?"

"The piano. The little one, in the vestry."

"Does anyone play that?" she asked, and it was the question of the day, the question that hung over the whole route like the low cloud, and Aldous said no, no one played it, and Mrs. Heseltine clicked her tongue at the waste of it and saw him to the door.

The light was going by the time he came down the last hill into the bottom of the valley, where the river ran brown and full under the packhorse bridge and the church stood among its yews with the lych-gate sagging on its hinges. He parked in the lane and sat a moment with his hands on the wheel. The rain had stopped. A blackbird was singing somewhere in the wet trees, that liquid evening song they sang, indifferent and complete.

He took the bag and went in through the lych-gate and up the path between the graves. He did not look at the graves. He knew where they were, all of them, the ones that concerned him, and he had long ago stopped needing to look. The church was open because the church was always open, there being nothing in it worth stealing and nobody to steal it, and he pushed the heavy door and went into the cold stone smell of the place, the smell of hassocks and lime and old hymnals, and stood for a moment in the dimness while his eyes adjusted.

It was smaller than it lived in his memory. They always were, the places where things had happened to us; they shrank to accommodate the ordinary while the event went on swelling somewhere out of scale. The nave ran forward to the chancel and the east window held the last grey light, and the pews stood in their ranks, and the third one on the left was simply a pew, a thing of dark oak with a worn rail, and Aldous looked at it and felt the old pressure under his breastbone and looked away.

The piano was in the vestry, through the low door to the side. It was a small upright, an old Chappell, and it had been there since before Aldous's time, used for choir practice in the years when there had been a choir, and for the children's recitals in the years when there had been children. He had tuned it perhaps a hundred times. He went in and set down his bag and opened the lid and the keys were yellow in the dimness, ivory gone the colour of old teeth, and he stood looking at them.

He had not let the boy practise on this piano. That was a thing he had remembered lately, in the nights, with the particular clarity that came at three in the morning. The boy had wanted to come down and practise in the church before the recital, to get the feel of the instrument and the room, and Aldous had said no, there wasn't time, he had the route to drive, and the boy could practise at home on the Bechstein which was a far better instrument anyway. And the boy had said but it's a different room, Dad, it sounds different, and Aldous had not understood that, had thought it a child's fancy, because to Aldous a piano was a piano and a room was only the box it sat in, and he had not yet learned — would learn it only later, too late, alone — that the boy was right, that the room was part of the instrument, that a note was not a note until it had finished travelling through the air of a particular place to a particular ear. The boy had known that at eleven. Aldous had known nothing.

So the boy had walked into the church on the evening of the recital and sat down at a piano he had never touched, in a room he had never played, and had made of it the thing that closed his father's throat. Without practice. Without the comfort of the familiar. He had simply sat down and listened to the room for a moment with his head tilted, the way he tilted it, and then he had begun, and the room had given him what it had, and he had taken it.

Aldous set out his tools on the vestry table by feel, in the failing light, because he had not thought to bring a lamp and the electric in the church had been off for years. He could tune in the dark. He had tuned in the dark before. The hands knew it; the ear knew it; he did not need to see.

He struck the fork against his knee and set it on the soundboard and let the A swim up out of the wood, and he found the string and brought the note to meet the fork, and the beating slowed and slowed and stopped, and there was that moment, that small clean moment he had spent his life chasing, when two things became one thing, when the interference vanished and the air went smooth and the note stood alone and true. It was the only certainty he had ever fully trusted. Not God, whose house this was; not love, which he had felt and bungled and felt again; but this, the moment when a thing came into tune, when the world consented for an instant to be exactly what it should be.

He worked outward from the A. The light went from grey to blue to nearly nothing, and the church grew cold around him, and the blackbird outside fell silent at last, and Aldous Renn tuned the little Chappell in the vestry of the church where his son had played, his old hands moving over the pins, the wedges going in and coming out, the intervals laying themselves down one after another, clean, true, beating and then not beating, until the whole instrument stood in tune from the bottom to the top, a thing nobody would play, made perfect in the dark for no one.

When it was done he did not pack the tools. He left them on the table. He carried the small stool out of the vestry and into the nave, and he set it down — not at the vestry piano but in the open space before the chancel where the children had stood to play, where the boy had stood, and then he went back and he did a thing he had never done, which was to take hold of the little Chappell, which was not heavy as pianos went, and to walk it, in the awful screeching way one walks a piano, corner by corner across the stone floor, out through the low door and into the nave, into the body of the church, into the room.

It took him a long time. He had to rest twice. His heart did things in his chest that he chose to ignore. But he got it there, into the place where the children had played, where the sound could go up into the high dark roof and come back changed, and he turned it to face down the empty nave, and he sat at it on the stool, an old man in a cold church in the last of the light.

He did not play the Schubert. He could not have played the Schubert; it was beyond his hands and always had been, and besides it was not his to play. It belonged to the boy and to that one June evening and he would not lay his clumsy fingers on it now. Instead he played the hymn tune, the simple one, the one he played to prove his work in every house on the route, and he played it slowly, and he listened — for the first time in his life he truly *listened*, the way the boy must have listened, not to whether the notes were in tune, which he knew they were, which he had made certain they were, but to what the room did with them, to the way each note bloomed and travelled up into the dark and came back softened and joined to the next, the way the church took his small playing and gave it a size it did not deserve, a meaning that was not in his hands but in the air, in the stone, in the place.

And he heard it. At last, at eighty years of age, alone, he heard what the boy had heard. He understood that the tuning was only the beginning, was only the laying clean of the road, and that the music was the journey the sound took afterward through the living air, and that this was what he had never been able to do and what the boy had done without trying, and that the difference between them had never been a deficiency in Aldous but only this: that the boy had loved the sound and Aldous had loved the instrument, and that these were not the same love, and that the second one, his own, was the smaller and the safer and the lonelier of the two.

He played the hymn through twice. The second time his voice came into it, low and cracked, not singing the words, just carrying the line under his breath, and the church held it all and gave it back, and somewhere in the second verse Aldous Renn stopped distinguishing between the boy's playing on that June evening forty years gone and his own playing now, and the two became one thing, the beating slowed and stopped, the interference vanished, and for the length of a held note the world consented to be exactly what it should be.

When the note died he sat with his hands in his lap and let the silence come back into the room. It came back whole, undamaged, the way silence does. Outside it was full dark and the river ran on under the bridge and the dog at home lay in the doorway waiting for the sound of the Morris in the lane.

He would leave the piano where it was, he decided. Let them find it in the nave and wonder, or not wonder, and let it stand there in tune until the tuning failed, which it would, in time, all tunings failing in the end, the strings creeping flat, the air winning as the air always won. But not yet. For tonight it stood true, in the room, in the place, ready, as if someone might still come in out of the dark and sit down and listen to the room for a moment with his head tilted, and then begin.

Aldous closed the lid gently over the yellowed keys. He gathered his tools in the dark, by feel, and he went out through the heavy door into the cold, and he did not look at the graves, and he drove home.
