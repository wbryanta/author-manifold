The ferry left at eleven, and by then the harbour lights had thinned to a scattering, the way a hand scatters seed and walks on without looking back. He stood at the rail with his collar up. Below him the water moved black against the hull, and he thought of how water at night keeps no memory of the day, holds nothing, gives back only the cold.

In his coat pocket was the telegram, folded and refolded until the creases had gone soft as cloth. A body found on the rocks at Þórshöfn. A man of fifty or thereabouts. Effects suggesting Edvard. They had used that word, effects, as though a life could be reduced to the things found in its pockets, a knife, a comb, a photograph water had ruined past knowing.

He had not seen his brother in nineteen years.

The cabin they gave him was below the waterline, a narrow berth with a basin and a single bulb that hummed. He did not sleep. He lay listening to the engines, that great patient labour somewhere beneath him, and he thought how a ship at night is a held breath, all of it forward motion and none of it visible, the destination only a rumour at the far end of the dark.

There had been a time when they were inseparable. This is the thing memory does, it begins with the inseparable so the breaking will cost more.

*

Their father had been a man who mended nets. In the village this was a kind of priesthood, the hands always moving, the wooden needle going in and out, and the two boys learned it sitting on either side of him on the stone step, the green twine running across their knees. Edvard was the elder by two years and the better at it, his fingers quick where the younger boy's were clumsy, and their father would sometimes stop his own work to watch Edvard's hands, and say nothing, and the saying nothing was the highest thing he had.

The younger boy — he was the one on the ferry now, fifty-one years old with grey coming into the stubble of his jaw — had learned early to read the weather of his father's attention. How it gathered around Edvard like light around a window. He did not hate his brother for this. That was the strange part, the part he had tried to explain to a woman once, late, in another country, and failed. You could not hate the thing the light fell on. You could only stand in the place the light did not reach and learn the shape of your own shadow.

*

Somewhere past midnight he went up onto the deck. The wind had risen. The strait here was famous for its violence, a place where two seas met and argued, and the ferry rolled through it with a long sick patience, lifting and settling. He gripped the rail. Spray came over the side, salt on his lips, and he licked it and tasted the whole of his childhood in it, the smell of the drying sheds, the gulls, his mother's hands always chapped and red.

A man came and stood near him at the rail. An old man in oilskins, a crewman perhaps, or a passenger who could not sleep either, and for a while they said nothing, two figures leaning into the same wind.

You are going across for the dead one, the old man said. It was not a question. On a ship this small, news of a passenger's purpose travels like weather.

I am going to see if it is my brother.

The old man nodded slowly, as though this were a common errand, as though half the ship were crossing to look upon the drowned. And if it is, he said.

Then it is.

The old man looked out at the black. The sea keeps them a long time up here, he said. The cold. It does not let them go the way warmer seas do. A man can come up after a week looking as if he only fell asleep an hour past. He had said this to be a comfort, perhaps, or perhaps only because it was true and the true thing was the one nearest his hand.

*

The last time he had seen Edvard it had been spring, and there had been a quarrel over the house. Their father was three months dead and the house stood between them like a third brother, the one nobody loved but everyone wanted. Edvard had wanted to sell. He had a wife by then, in the town, and plans, always plans, his hands still quick but turned now to figures and schemes and the slow accumulation of small advantages. And the younger brother had wanted to keep it, the house, the step, the smell of the drying sheds, not because he loved it but because he could not bear to see Edvard hold the knife to that throat too.

They had said things. He could no longer remember the precise words, only their shape, the way you remember the shape of a blow long after the bruise has gone. He had said something about their father. About the watching. About the light and where it fell and where it did not, and he had seen on Edvard's face for one instant a thing he had not expected, which was not anger but a kind of terrible surprise, as though the younger brother had struck him in a place he did not know he could be struck.

And then Edvard had gone very still, the way he went still, the stillness that was worse than any shouting, and he had said: You think I asked for it. You think I wanted to be the one he looked at.

And the younger brother had said nothing, because the truth was he had never once thought of it that way, that the light might be a weight as much as a warmth, that the watched man might also want, somewhere, to be left alone in his own shadow.

He had let his brother walk out of the house and out of the country and he had not gone after him. The house he sold within the year, to strangers, who painted it a colour he hated and would never see.

*

He must have slept, finally, because he woke to a different quality of engine, a slowing, and grey light at the porthole the colour of an oyster. The sea had calmed. They were coming into the lee of the land.

He shaved in the small basin, watching his own face come clean of foam, and he thought how a brother is a mirror you are given before you know what a mirror is. How Edvard's face had always been the place he checked himself against, even in the years of silence, even across the distance of a continent. He had grown a beard at thirty largely so as not to see, each morning, the line of his brother's jaw rising out of his own.

*

Þórshöfn was a smear of low buildings under a sky that could not decide. A man met him at the quay, a policeman of sorts, young, with a face that had not yet learned what to do in the presence of the bereaved and so did too much, too gently. He carried an umbrella though it was not raining.

You had a long crossing, the young man said. The strait was rough last night.

It was rough.

They walked up from the harbour through the town, which was barely a town, a clinic, a church, a shop with its shutters still down. The young policeman talked, out of nerves, about the man who had found the body, a boy really, out checking his father's lobster pots, and how the boy had not slept since, and how in a place this size a thing like this entered every house at once, like cold through a gap.

We do not know who he is, the policeman said. There was a name in his things. We thought — there are records — we found a man of that name with a brother living abroad. We hope we have not brought you all this way for a stranger.

A stranger, the man on the ferry repeated. And he thought: nineteen years. Was a brother you had not seen in nineteen years a stranger? Was he less of one or more? The word would not settle.

*

The body was in the clinic, in a room at the back that was cold and smelled of the cold, a metal table and a sheet and a window with frosted glass through which the indecisive light came grey and even.

He had thought he would feel something at the door. A reluctance, a held breath, the heart's refusal. But he felt only a great clarity, the clarity of exhaustion, of a night without sleep, of having come a long way to a small room at the end of the world. He had crossed a sea for this. The least he could do was look.

The policeman drew back the sheet to the collarbone and stepped away, out of some sense of decency, to the window, and stood with his back turned.

The man on the ferry looked at the dead face.

The sea, the old man had said, keeps them a long time up here. And it was true. The face was not ruined. The face was a man of fifty or thereabouts, the hair grey and still wet-looking though it must have long dried, the skin gone the colour of tallow, the lips parted very slightly as if about to say a thing it had decided, at the last, not to say.

He looked for his brother in it.

This is the thing no one tells you. That a face you have not seen in nineteen years is a face you have been steadily losing the whole time, that memory is not a vault but a river, that what you carry of a person erodes and silts and changes course, so that the brother he carried in him was a brother of thirty-two, quick-handed, still, surprised, walking out of a house into the spring, and this was a man of fifty whom time had been working on all the years he was not watching.

Was it him. Was it Edvard.

The jaw. He looked at the jaw, the line of it, the line he had grown a beard to escape, and he could not tell. It might have been. It might as easily not. Death takes the particular animation out of a face, the thing that makes it a person and not a likeness, and what is left is general, is everyman, is the bones we all wear under the brief loan of our flesh. He looked at this stranger who might be his brother and he understood that he could not be sure. That the certainty he had crossed the sea to obtain was not on the table in front of him. It was somewhere out in the strait, gone down into the black water that keeps no memory.

There was a scar, the policeman said, not turning from the window. On the left forearm. The doctor noted it. An old scar.

And the man on the ferry went very still.

Because he knew that scar. He had made that scar.

*

They had been boys, nine and eleven, and they had found a gull tangled in a length of fishing line down by the rocks, a great black-backed gull with its wing bound to its body, and it had fought them, of course it had fought them, it did not know they meant to free it. Edvard had taken hold of it, his quick hands, and the boy had come at the line with his father's knife, and the gull had heaved, and the knife had gone not into the line but into the soft of his brother's forearm, a long shallow opening, and the blood had come bright and the gull had torn free at last and beaten up into the air over the water, free, indifferent, gone.

And Edvard had not cried out. That was the thing he remembered. Edvard had looked at the blood and then at his brother's face, which must have been white with horror, and he had said, quite calmly, it is all right, it does not hurt, look, the bird is away — turning the moment, even then, even bleeding, into a gift he could give the younger one, the absolution offered before the guilt could fully form. It is all right. The bird is away.

The scar had healed pale and long, a finger's length, on the inside of the left forearm. He had seen it a thousand times. He had seen it the last day, in the house, on the hand that gestured at the figures, the schemes. He could have found it in the dark.

*

He moved to the table. The policeman, hearing him move, half-turned and then thought better of it and faced the window again, and the man on the ferry took the edge of the sheet and drew it back further, down the arm, the cold arm, and turned it gently, the way you would turn the arm of a sleeping child to look at what it held, and there on the inside of the left forearm, pale and long, a finger's length, was the scar.

He stood for a long time holding his brother's cold arm.

Outside the indecisive sky had decided, at last, on rain, and it came against the frosted window in a soft continuous sound like the sea heard from very far inland, and the young policeman stood with his back turned, giving the man what he imagined a man would want, which was to be unobserved, when in fact what the man wanted, standing there, was to be seen, to be witnessed, to have someone in the room understand that he was holding the arm of the only person on earth who had ever made a gift of his own pain to spare another, and that he had let that person walk out of a house and out of his life and had never once, in nineteen years, written to say the thing he should have said, which was not I am sorry about the house. Which was only: I never thought it was a weight on you. The light. I am sorry I did not see it was a weight.

*

It is him, he said. The voice did not sound like his own.

The policeman turned. You are sure.

The scar, he said. I made the scar. When we were boys.

He saw the young man's face do a complicated thing then, relief and pity at war in it, relief that the long machinery of identification could now turn, pity that what it had turned up was a brother and not a stranger after all. I am sorry, the young man said. It is hard to be the one who is sure.

And the man on the ferry thought: yes. It is hard to be the one who is sure. He had spent his life being the one who was not sure, who stood in the shadow and was not certain even of the shape of it, and here at the end of a sea-crossing he had been handed a certainty at last, pale and long, a finger's length, and it was the worst thing he had ever held.

*

There were papers. There are always papers, even at the end of the world, even with the dead. He sat in a small office with the policeman and a woman from the clinic and he signed where they showed him to sign, and they asked him questions, and he answered them as from a distance, hearing his own voice supply the facts of a life he had not been part of for nineteen years. Where had his brother been living. He did not know. Was there a wife. There had been a wife, once, in the town across the strait, but whether she was wife still or had left or died he could not say. Did he know what had brought his brother to this coast, this far north, to the rocks at Þórshöfn where a boy checking lobster pots had found him.

He did not know. He would never know. That was the part that would not heal. That his brother had come to this place, to this edge, for a reason that had died with him, and that the younger brother had not been anywhere near, had been across the sea in another country growing a beard so as not to see his brother's jaw in the glass, while Edvard came north alone to wherever it was a man goes when he has run out of the things that hold him.

There was no note, the policeman said gently, as though anticipating the question the man could not bring himself to ask. We do not think — there is no reason to think it was anything but the sea. The rocks are bad there. Men are taken off them every few years. There is no reason to think.

And the man on the ferry was grateful for the lie, if it was a lie, and grateful too that he would never know whether it was, that the not-knowing on this one point would be a mercy he could choose, the only mercy left to choose.

*

They asked him about the body. What he wished done. And he understood that this too was now his, that the brother he had let walk out of a house had walked back into his keeping at the very last, that there was no one else, that across all the distance and all the years it came down to him, the younger one, the one in the shadow, to say what should become of the flesh.

Home, he said. I will take him home.

But there was no home. The house was painted a colour he hated and owned by strangers. The village was a place he had not been in nineteen years. The father was bones in a churchyard, the mother beside him. Home was a word as unsettled as stranger had been on the quay that morning, a word that would not hold still.

And yet he had said it. I will take him home. And he found that he meant it, that there was a churchyard, that there were two graves in it with room, surely, for a third, that whatever the village had become it was the ground their father had mended nets above and their mother had reddened her hands in, and that to lay Edvard there was the one thing he could still do, the one gift he could give back, late, nineteen years late, in exchange for it is all right, the bird is away.

*

The return ferry did not leave until the evening. He walked, in the rain, out of the town and down to the rocks where the boy had found him.

The policeman had told him the place, reluctantly, and had offered to come, and he had said no. He went alone. The rain had eased to a fine drift, more mist than rain, and the rocks were black and wet and the sea worked among them with a sound he felt in his chest more than heard. He stood at the edge where the land gave out and the water began and he looked at the place his brother had last stood, or fallen, or stepped, and he understood that he would never know which, and that the strait did not care, the strait that kept no memory, that held nothing, that gave back only the cold and the long-preserved bodies of men with their pale old scars intact.

He thought of the gull. The great black-backed gull beating up off the rocks and out over the water, free, the blood still bright on his brother's arm. He thought of how the bird had not looked back, had not known or cared what it had cost them, the wing torn free into the wide indifferent air. And he thought that this was perhaps what his brother had wanted, at the end, on these rocks, in the cold, after the wife and the schemes and the figures had all come to nothing, to be the bird and not the boys, to beat up off the rocks and out and away and not look back, free at last of the light and the weight of the light, gone into a place where no one watched him and he did not have to give the gift of his own pain to anyone ever again.

It is all right, the man said aloud, to the rocks, to the sea, to the fine drift of rain, to the cold preserved body waiting in the clinic to be taken home. It does not hurt. Look. The bird is away.

And the sea took the words the way it took everything, without acknowledgement, and worked on among the rocks, and somewhere out in the grey a gull lifted off the water and beat north into the mist, and he watched it until he could not tell it any longer from the rain, and then he turned and walked back up toward the town, toward the evening ferry, toward the long dark crossing of the strait he would have to make again, this time with his brother below the waterline beside him, the two of them going home together at last across the black indifferent water, inseparable.
