The hotel had been rebuilt in a style its owners called Mediterranean, which meant that the new wing rose pink and stuccoed against a sky that had never been anything but California, and that bougainvillea had been trained up trellises where there had once been only the service entrance and the laundry chutes and the gray painted door through which Marin had carried, for eleven months in 1962, the folded sheets and the small wrapped soaps and the towels she was instructed to fold in thirds. She had not meant to come. She had told her daughter she would not come, and then she had come anyway, because the daughter of a woman she had known in those years was being married in the garden, and because she had wanted, in some way she did not examine, to see whether the place still stood where the old place had stood, whether the ground itself remembered. Ground does not remember. She knew that. She had been taught, in the years since, to distrust the part of herself that believed otherwise.

The wedding was at five. She had come at three, which was a mistake, because there was nothing to do at three except stand in the lobby and order a drink she did not want and watch the bellmen, who were young and wore the same maroon jackets the bellmen had worn before, although the men who had worn them then would now be old, would now be dead, some of them, three of them at least dead in the fire although the papers at the time had said two. She had known it was three. She had counted. Counting was a thing she did.

“Will you be checking in?” the girl at the desk asked her.

“I’m here for the Vincent wedding,” Marin said.

“The garden,” the girl said, and smiled, and looked back down at her screen, and Marin understood that she had been dismissed, that she was a category now, garden, five o’clock, and that this was a mercy of a kind. She took her drink to a chair near the window that gave onto the pool. The pool was new. The pool had been, in 1962, a parking lot, and beyond the parking lot the kitchens, and it was in the kitchens that the fire had started, or that they had said it started, although there had been talk, the kind of talk that travels through a staff like a low fever, that it had started in 320, in the room of the man from Long Beach who drank and who was not, after, among the registered dead or the registered living, who was simply not, who became one of those absences that a hotel folds back into itself the way water closes.

She had been nineteen. She had been on the third floor at the time, in the linen closet at the end of the hall, because she had been hiding there, which was a thing she had done in those months when the work was slow and Mrs. Cazeau was downstairs, hiding in the linen closet with the door cracked and a magazine she had taken from a checkout room, and she had smelled it before she heard anything, smelled it and thought, the way one thinks the small domestic thought even then, that someone had left an iron on. That was what she had thought. An iron. She had folded the magazine and put it under the stack of pillowcases where she kept it and she had opened the door of the linen closet to a hallway already gone wrong at the far end, the air at the far end moving in a way air does not move, and she had stood there for what she later understood to be a long time, although it did not seem long, it seemed to be no time at all, a held breath, and then she had walked—she had not run, she remembered that she had not run, she had been proud of it later and then ashamed of the pride—she had walked to the service stairs and gone down.

She did not think about it often. She wanted that understood, in the court of whatever judged these things, that she did not sit at her kitchen table in Hayward and think about the fire, that she had a husband who had died of something ordinary and a daughter who taught school and a small clean life that she had built deliberately out of the unburned material that remained to her. But she thought about it now, in the rebuilt lobby, because the lobby insisted that nothing had happened. The lobby was the lie the building told about itself, and she had come, she saw now, to see the lie up close, to put her drink down on the new marble and say to the marble: I know what you are covering.

At four-thirty she went out to the garden. They had set the chairs in rows on the grass, white chairs with white ribbon, and an arch of flowers at the front of the rows, and beyond the arch the view that had always been there, the only true thing, the hills going brown in the slant light and one hawk turning above them. People were beginning to arrive in their pale wedding clothes. Marin took a chair on the aisle near the back and folded her hands in her lap and watched a man in a linen jacket move among the guests with the ease of a man who owned something, and she thought he might be the father of the bride, and then a woman came and sat beside her and said, “You're a friend of Dorothy's,” and Marin said yes, although Dorothy was not a friend, Dorothy was a woman she had folded towels beside, a woman who had been on the second floor that night and who had got out a window and broken her ankle dropping to the awning over the entrance and who had never, in the years since, worked again, had married a man with a hardware store in Modesto and had a daughter, and the daughter was the bride.

“Such a lovely place,” the woman beside her said. “Have you stayed here?”

“No,” Marin said. “I've never stayed here.”

This was true. She had worked here and she had nearly died here but she had never stayed here, had never once slept in one of the beds she made, and the distinction seemed to her suddenly the whole of it, the distinction between the people who slept and the people who folded the sheets in thirds and hid in linen closets, and she understood that the woman beside her was a sleeper, had always been a sleeper, would die a sleeper, and that there was nothing to be done about it and nothing to be said.

Dorothy came down the aisle on the arm of the man with the hardware store. Marin had not seen her in—she counted—in thirty-one years, and Dorothy was heavy now and her hair was a color hair is not, and she was crying in the way mothers cry at weddings, easily and without shame, and she did not see Marin, and Marin was glad. Dorothy sat in the front row. The music changed. Everyone stood, and Marin stood, and the bride came down the aisle on no one's arm, which was the fashion now, alone in a great deal of white, and she was twenty-three or twenty-four and her face was the face of a girl to whom nothing had happened, and Marin watched her and thought: your mother went out a window.

The ceremony was short. The young man at the front had a soft chin and the bride promised him things and he promised her things and the man who married them said that love was a garden, which was either a thing he had thought up looking at the garden or a thing he said at every wedding, and then it was done and there was the small applause that is not quite applause, and the bride and the soft-chinned young man went back up the aisle, and the guests followed, and Marin let them go ahead of her and stood for a moment looking at the empty chairs and the hills and the hawk, which had not moved, or had been replaced by another hawk indistinguishable from the first.

The reception was in the ballroom. She had not wanted to go into the ballroom but the channel of the party carried her, the way the channel of a party does, past the bar set up under the trellis and through the French doors and into the long bright room where there were round tables and a band setting up and waiters in white coats moving with trays of champagne held flat on their palms, the way she had been taught to hold them, the way Mrs. Cazeau had said, flat, you walk with it flat or you walk out of here. The ballroom was where it had been. The ballroom was on the site of the old ballroom, which had not burned, the fire had not reached it, but which had filled with smoke, and they had brought people out through it, and she had a memory she did not trust of standing in the old ballroom while it filled and watching a chandelier through the smoke and thinking the chandelier was the most beautiful thing she had ever seen, which was a thought the mind has, she understood now, when it is deciding whether to live.

“Champagne?” a waiter said.

He was young and his coat did not fit him in the shoulders. She took a glass and did not drink it.

She found a place at a table near the windows with people she did not know, a couple from Sacramento and an older man who said he was an uncle of the groom and a young woman with a baby on her lap who jiggled the baby through the dinner. They asked Marin how she knew the family. She said she had known the mother of the bride years ago. They asked where, and she said at work, and they let it go, because it was a party and there were other things to ask. The food came and she ate some of it. The band played. The young people danced and then the older people danced and Dorothy danced with her husband and with the groom and Marin watched all of it from the table by the windows, watched it the way she had once watched the lobby fill in the hours before a convention, with a professional patience, an understanding that her job was to be present and to be invisible and to make sure the sheets were folded in thirds.

It was during the dancing that the man in the linen jacket came to her table. He was the father of the groom, it turned out, not of the bride, and he had had a good deal to drink, and he pulled out the empty chair beside her and sat in it heavily and said, “You're the only person here I haven't met.”

“I'm a friend of the bride's mother,” Marin said.

“The whole thing,” he said, gesturing with his glass at the ballroom, the band, the trellis beyond the doors, “the whole thing cost me a fortune and I don't know half these people. You ever notice that? You pay for the whole thing and you don't know half the people.”

“I worked here,” Marin said.

She had not meant to say it. She heard herself say it the way one hears oneself say a thing in a dream, with surprise and a kind of slow horror, and the man turned his good-natured drunk face to her and said, “Here? You worked here? At the hotel?”

“A long time ago.”

“What did you do?”

“I was a chambermaid,” Marin said. “On the third floor.”

The man laughed, not unkindly, the way a man laughs who has decided the evening is to be amusing. “Well, isn't that something,” he said. “And now you're a guest. That's the American thing, right there. That's the whole thing right there. You start as the chambermaid and you come back as the guest.” He was pleased with this. He turned in his chair as if to find someone to tell it to. “Hey, Dottie used to work here too, didn't she? The bride's mother. Somebody told me she used to work here.”

“We worked here together,” Marin said.

“There was a fire,” the man said, suddenly, the way a drunk man arrives at a fact, all at once, from a great distance. “Wasn't there? Somebody told me. Way back. They had to rebuild the whole thing. Burned right down.”

“Most of it,” Marin said.

“You weren't here for that.”

She looked at him. He had wet, friendly eyes and his collar had come open and he was waiting for her to say no, she had not been here for that, because it would be easier for him if she had not been here for that, and she understood that she could give him this, that it cost her nothing to give him this, and that to give him this was the gracious thing, the thing a guest did, the thing that kept the party a party.

“No,” she said. “I wasn't here for that.”

The man nodded, satisfied, and patted the table near her hand and got up and went off to find someone to tell about the chambermaid who came back as the guest, and Marin sat with her untouched champagne and looked out the window at the dark, where the hills had gone and the hawk had gone and there was only her own face floating on the glass, and she thought about the man from Long Beach in 320, the one who became an absence, the one the papers did not count, and she thought that she had just done to herself what the hotel had done to him, had folded the true thing back into the smooth surface, had said the gracious thing, had let it close like water.

She had not been able, for years, to remember the last part. The going down the service stairs she remembered, and the door at the bottom, the gray painted door, and the parking lot, and the strange daylight ordinariness of the parking lot with the cars in it and the fire not yet anything you could see from the parking lot, only smelled, only a wrongness in the morning. But between the linen closet and the service stairs there was a thing she did not remember, or remembered only as a shape, a thing she had walked past, and it was years before she let herself understand that the shape was a person, that there had been a person in the hallway, in the wrong-moving air at the far end where she had not gone, where she had turned the other way toward the stairs, and that she did not know and would never know whether the person was someone she might have reached. She had been nineteen. She told herself she had been nineteen. The court of whatever judged these things had been, she believed, lenient on the matter, had ruled that a nineteen-year-old who smells smoke and walks to the stairs has done the only thing the body permits, and she accepted the ruling, mostly, in the daytime, in Hayward, at her kitchen table. But she had come back to the place where the ruling was handed down, and the place said: nothing happened here, look at the bougainvillea, and the band played, and the bride who had nothing happen to her danced with the groom who had a soft chin, and Marin understood that she had come back not to remember but to be told once more that there was nothing to remember, and that she had been told, and that it had not worked, that it had never once worked, that the lie of the lobby was a lie she could see and the lie at the bottom of herself was a lie she could not.

The young woman with the baby leaned across the empty chairs. “Are you all right?” she said. “You look a little pale.”

“I'm fine,” Marin said. “It's warm in here.”

“It is,” the young woman said, grateful, jiggling the baby. “It's so warm. They have all these doors and they keep them shut.”

“They keep them shut,” Marin agreed.

She got up after that. She told the young woman she was going to find some air, and the young woman said yes, good idea, air, and Marin went out through the French doors onto the terrace, which had a railing and a view of the pool, lit now from beneath so that the water was a panel of moving green light, and she stood at the railing and breathed. The band was muffled behind the glass. Down by the pool a waiter was carrying a tray of glasses back toward the kitchens, holding it flat, walking with it flat, and she watched him until he went through a door she could not see, a door that would be, she knew, gray, or some new color that did the work the gray had done, the door between the part of the hotel that was a party and the part of the hotel that made the party possible. She had come out of such a door once into a parking lot full of ordinary daylight. She had stood in such a lot while three men she had folded towels beside, or two, the papers said two, did not come out at all.

Dorothy found her there. Marin did not see her come; she was simply there, at the railing, in her mother-of-the-bride dress, with her ruined hair and her ankle that she would have favored had anyone been watching, and she said, “It is you. I thought it was you. All through the dinner I thought, that's never her, and then I thought, it is.”

“Hello, Dorothy,” Marin said.

They stood at the railing. Dorothy was crying again, easily, and she put her hand on Marin's arm and left it there, and Marin let it stay, and below them the green water moved.

“I didn't think you'd come,” Dorothy said. “I sent the invitation but I didn't think. I almost didn't send it. Bill said, why would she want to come back here, of all places.”

“It's a beautiful wedding,” Marin said. “She's a beautiful girl.”

“She doesn't know,” Dorothy said. “About any of it. I never told her. You don't tell your children a thing like that, what do they need with it.” She pressed Marin's arm. “Do you ever think about it? I can't be here five minutes without. I told Bill, this place, of all the places, and he said it's the nicest place in the valley now, it's not the same place, and I said it's the same ground, Bill.”

“It's the same ground,” Marin said.

“I went out a window,” Dorothy said, with a kind of wonder, as if she were telling it to herself, as if Marin had not been there. “Did you know that? I went out a window onto the awning and I broke this ankle and I have never once since been able to wear a real shoe. Forty-one years and I can't wear a real shoe. And tonight I'm dancing in these things.” She lifted the hem of her dress an inch and they both looked at her shoe, which was flat and soft and not a real shoe. “How did you get out? I never knew how you got out. After, when they had us all on the lot, I looked for you and somebody said she's fine, she went down the stairs, and I thought, the stairs, she just walked down the stairs.”

“I just walked down the stairs,” Marin said.

Dorothy looked at her for a moment with the strange clarity that comes to people sometimes through a great deal of feeling, and Marin thought she would ask the next thing, the thing under the thing, but Dorothy did not ask it, because Dorothy was a sleeper, had always been a sleeper, and the next thing was not a thing sleepers asked. She squeezed Marin's arm again and wiped her face and said the band was about to do the thing where the bride threw the flowers and she had to be there, she had to be there for that, and would Marin come in, and Marin said she would come in in a minute, she just wanted the air.

When Dorothy had gone back through the French doors Marin stood a while longer at the railing. The pool light went off—someone had thrown a switch—and the water went black, and the hills she could not see were out there in the dark holding the heat of the day and giving it back, the way ground does, the way ground gives back what it takes in, which is the only kind of remembering ground does, and is not nothing. Behind the glass the band stopped and there was a gathering and a shriek of young women and then more music, and Marin set her full champagne glass on the railing, where a waiter would find it and clear it and fold the moment back into the smooth surface of the evening, and she went down the terrace steps and along the side of the rebuilt hotel, past the trellis, toward the front, where she had left her car, walking, not running, walking the way she had walked toward the stairs, with the same proud terrible patience, away from the party and the bride and the woman who had gone out a window, out into the parking lot, where it was, as it had been, only ordinary dark.
