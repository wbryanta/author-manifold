He knew this region only slightly, having traveled it but twice before, and both times the journeys had soured him. Yet now there was promise in the errand, or so the letter had implied, and a man without sons must make what alliances he can. The trail wound through pine and the smell of pine, that clean sharp scent he favored above all others, and he let Regina pick her own pace through the shadowed places where roots crossed the path like the backs of buried things. The fog he had quit at the shore had not followed him inland. Here the day was bright and ordinary, the sky a washed blue between the high crowns of the trees, and he was grateful for the change though something in him grieved the gold a little still.

He thought of Rebekka as he rode, as he often did when alone, and the thought steadied him the way a hand on a rail steadies a man on a pitching deck. She would be at the stock now, or at the wash, her sleeves pushed up and her face set in that mild absorption he had loved at once and without understanding why. He had sent for her sight unseen across an ocean, a stranger answering an advertisement, and the gamble had paid better than any he had made with land or pelts or salt cod. That she had buried three children and a daughter taken by accident had not broken her, only quieted her, drawn her inward to a place he could not always reach. He understood that place. He had his own.

The trail opened onto a stretch of cleared land where someone had once meant to farm and given it up. Charred stumps stood in rows like a graveyard of intentions. He passed it without slowing. The sun climbed. By midday he had reached the first markers of the plantation he sought, a fence of split rails running straighter and longer than any he had seen since leaving the north, and behind the fence fields of tobacco standing tall and green and tended, and bent among the rows the dark backs of men and women he did not look at directly, though he saw them, and saw too the small figures moving water down the rows, and the one who straightened to watch him pass with a face he could not read at the distance.

He rode on. The house, when at last it rose before him, took his breath in a way he had not anticipated and did not entirely welcome. It sat upon a low rise above the river, a great brick pile of a thing, three stories and a gabled roof, with chimneys at either end like horns, and before it a sweep of crushed shell drive that crunched beneath Regina's hooves loud as gunfire in the still afternoon. The windows were glazed, every one, glass enough to ransom a town, and the door when he came to it was iron-studded oak tall enough to admit a man on horseback should the master wish it. He sat his horse a moment looking up at the thing, this monument a man had raised to himself out of leaf and the labor of the bent backs in the fields, and he felt the old contempt move in him, and beneath the contempt, shamefully, like a worm in good fruit, a thread of want.

A boy came running to take Regina. Jacob dismounted and stood smoothing his coat, which was travel-soiled and plain, and he was aware of his own plainness against the grandeur of the place and resolved not to mind it. A house servant in livery, an actual livery with brass buttons, appeared at the door and inquired his business in a voice that managed to be both deferential and dismissive, and Jacob gave his name and his purpose and was admitted into a hall floored in black and white stone like a chessboard, with a staircase curving up out of it and portraits of pink-faced men in dark frames staring down with the complacency of the dead who had got what they wanted.

He was made to wait. He waited a long while, which he understood to be a message and chose not to receive as one, standing instead with his hat in his hands turning slowly to take in the wealth of the place, the carved chair rails, the silver sconces, the smell of beeswax and tobacco and beneath it something fouler that no amount of wax could cover, the smell that all such houses had, that he had smelled even in the small holdings of men who owned but two or three souls, a smell he had no word for but knew at once whenever it reached him.

At length he was shown into a room where a man sat at his ease before a cold hearth, a heavy man going to fat, with rings on fingers that had never gripped a plow, and a glass at his elbow though the hour was early for it. This was D'Ortega, the Portuguese, or the man who called himself Portuguese though Jacob privately doubted the whole pedigree. He rose halfway, a courtesy calculated to its last inch, and waved Jacob to a seat, and a servant brought wine Jacob did not want and could not refuse without giving offense he could not yet afford to give.

They spoke of the road, of the fog, of the weather and the crop, circling the matter that had brought them together the way two dogs circle before they decide whether to fight. The matter was a debt. D'Ortega owed Jacob money, a considerable sum, the residue of a venture in which Jacob had ventured capital and D'Ortega had ventured promises, and the venture had foundered when a ship went down off the Azores with all aboard and all owed, and Jacob had come the long way south to collect what he could of what was his. He had not expected to collect coin. He had learned long ago that men who built houses like this one kept no coin loose in them, that their wealth was all tied up in land and leaf and flesh, and that a creditor must take what could be taken or take nothing.

D'Ortega lamented. He was eloquent in lamentation, a man clearly more practiced in describing his losses than in preventing them. The ship, the captain's folly, the want of insurance, the cruelty of Providence to an honest man. Jacob listened with a face he had schooled to patience and watched the rings catch the light when the hand moved, and thought of the glass windows and the liveried servant and the smell beneath the wax, and said little, and let the silence do his arguing for him.

When at last the heart of it was reached, D'Ortega proposed payment in kind. He had no coin to spare, he was himself pressed, his factors in Lisbon were slow, the season had been poor. But he had goods. He had tobacco, though Jacob would have to arrange its transport at his own cost and risk and sell it himself in a falling market. He had no use for tobacco he could not carry. He had a quantity of fine cloth, lately come from England, which Jacob examined and found to be neither fine nor a quantity. And he had, D'Ortega said, lowering his voice to a tone of confidential generosity, men. He had more than he needed. He would sell Jacob the debt's worth in strong young men, healthy, broken in to the work, and Jacob might do with them as he liked, sell them on at a profit in the north where such were dear, or keep them and prosper.

Jacob declined the men. He said he had no use for so many and no means to feed them through a northern winter, which was true, and he did not say the other thing, which was that he wanted no part of the trade in souls and had built what he had built without it and meant to keep on so. He had a low opinion of men who grew rich on the backs of others while keeping their own hands clean and white, and he kept this opinion to himself as a man keeps a knife, sheathed but ready. He had bought labor before, indentures, time-bound and ending, men and women who worked off their passage and went free to fail or flourish on their own, and a girl once from a poorhouse, and he counted these transactions different in kind from what D'Ortega proposed, and perhaps they were and perhaps the difference was one he told himself to sleep at night. He did not examine it closely. He declined the men.

D'Ortega's face, which had worn its lament like a fashionable coat, hardened now into something nearer its true shape. They would walk, he said. Jacob would see the stock and choose, for choose he must, there being nothing else to be had, and a creditor who refused all reasonable satisfaction was no creditor but a thief and a guest who had outworn his welcome. So they walked, out the iron door and across the shell drive and along a path beside the great house, and as they went D'Ortega recovered his expansiveness and pointed out his improvements, the dovecote, the smokehouse, the new wing he was raising, and Jacob looked and said nothing and felt the want in him grow against his will, the want for a house, not this house, but a house, something to set against the leveling work of time, something his name might rest upon when he was gone and had no son to carry it.

The slave quarters lay beyond a stand of trees, out of sight and smell of the great house, a double row of cabins of unbarked log with mud chinking and roofs of bark and rush. Here the smell that the beeswax had masked stood undisguised, the smell of too many bodies in too little air, of waste and woodsmoke and sickness and the particular sour reek of fear that Jacob had smelled in jails and on slave decks and once, long ago, in a room where his mother had died. The people were brought out for him to view. They stood in the trampled yard in the late light, men and women and a scatter of children, and they did not look at him, having learned what looking cost, and D'Ortega walked among them naming their qualities like a horse-coper, this one's strong back, that one's breeding years, and Jacob stood apart and looked at the ground and wished himself anywhere but here and wished he had never ventured a penny with the man and wished the ship had not gone down and wished, uselessly, to be a better man than the one who stood in this yard appraising the price of human beings against a bad debt.

It was then that he noticed the woman. She stood at the edge of the group, not in the front where the prime hands were displayed but back near the cabins, and she held against her hip a child of perhaps two and beside her stood a girl of seven or eight and behind the girl, half hidden, a smaller child, a boy. The woman was not young and not old and had about her a stillness that was not the stillness of the others, which was the stillness of cattle, but a watching stillness, a stillness with a mind behind it. She was looking at Jacob. When his eyes found her she did not look away as the others did. She held his gaze a moment and then, deliberately, she pushed the little girl forward, out from behind her skirts, into the light where he could see her, and she did this with her eyes still on him, and Jacob understood that she was saying something to him, something he could not hear but felt strike him like a hand laid flat against the chest, and he looked away, troubled, and looked back, and she was still looking, still saying it.

D'Ortega had been speaking. Jacob had not heard him. The woman, D'Ortega was saying, following Jacob's eyes, was a good worker, a cook, knew the dairy, but he could not part with her, she was wanted in the kitchen, the master could not be expected to give up his own table's comfort for a debt that was none of his making. But the others, the girl now, the eldest girl, she was nothing yet, an extra mouth, she could be spared, she would grow, she had her mother's hands, she would learn.

The woman heard this. Jacob saw her hear it. He saw a thing move across her face that was gone before he could name it, and then her face closed like a door and she did the thing again, pushed the little girl forward, and now she spoke, the first sound any of them had made, low and quick and urgent, words Jacob half caught, take the girl, take my girl, and D'Ortega struck her, not hard, a backhand cuff such as a man might give a dog that begged at table, and she fell silent and stepped back into her stillness, but her eyes did not leave Jacob and the saying did not stop.

Jacob did not want the woman. He did not want the girl. He did not want any of them. He stood in the foul yard in the gold light that was no longer gold but the ordinary red of evening, and he wanted to be quit of the place and quit of the debt and quit of the smell, and he heard himself say, against all his intention, against the cold sense that told him a child of seven was no fit payment for a man's grown ambition, against the want and through the want both at once, that he would take the girl.

He did not know why he said it. He told himself afterward, riding north, that it was practical, that Rebekka wanted help and a young one would cost little to keep and would grow into use, that it was no more than buying time as he had bought time before, that the girl would have a better life in the north than in this pit, that he had done the child a kindness and the woman a mercy and himself no great injury. He told himself all of this and some of it was even true. But the truth that sat beneath the other truths, the way the foul smell sat beneath the beeswax, was that he had not been able to refuse the woman's eyes, that she had asked him a thing he did not understand and he had answered yes because he could not bear to answer no while she was looking at him, and that he had spent his life refusing to be the kind of man who stood in such a yard and yet here he stood, and the only way he could find to be that man and not be him was to take the child the woman pushed toward him, as though by carrying the girl away he might carry away too the look on the mother's face.

D'Ortega protested. The girl was worth less than the cook, he could not call the debt settled on a child's account, Jacob must take more, must take a man besides, must take the cloth and the tobacco both. They argued. The light failed. The argument was the same argument as before, the creditor and the debtor and the impossibility of squaring what was owed against what was offered, and in the end Jacob, weary of it, weary of the place and of himself, agreed to call the matter closed for the girl and a promissory note against the rest, a note he doubted would ever be honored and did not much care, having got from the transaction the one thing he had not come for and could not explain.

The woman was not there when it was done. She had been sent back to the kitchen, to the master's table and its comforts. So she did not see the girl handed over, did not see Jacob lift the child, who was light as a basket of feathers and stiff with terror, onto Regina before him. The little girl did not cry. That was the thing Jacob would remember, that she did not cry, that she sat rigid against him with her small hands gripping the saddle's pommel and her face turned forward into the dark, not looking back, as though she had already learned at seven that looking back cost more than a body could pay. He felt her trembling through his coat. He spoke to her once, told her his name, told her she was going to a good place, to a woman who was kind, and the child said nothing and did not turn, and he fell silent, ashamed without quite knowing of what.

They rode out through the trees and past the great house, which blazed now with candlelight in every glazed window, gold light spilling out across the shell drive, and Jacob looked at it as he passed and felt the want rise in him again, fierce and clear, but changed now, turned. He would have a house. He would have his own house, raised by his own means on his own land in the clean north, and it would be a fine house, finer than men of his station built, and no liveried servant would keep a creditor waiting in its hall and no foul smell would hide beneath its wax. He did not yet see, riding away from D'Ortega's monument with D'Ortega's child stiff against his chest, that the want the house had planted in him would outlive his contempt for the man who had planted it, that a man may despise a thing and covet it in the same breath and let the coveting win. He saw only the house ahead of him, the house he would build, gold-windowed, and he carried the picture of it north the way he had carried the gold fog up from the shore, as a thing both real and dreamed.

The trail was dark now and he could not hurry. He let Regina find the way. The child slept at last, or feigned sleep, her head dropping against his arm, and her weight changed, became the heavy trusting weight of a sleeping young thing, and Jacob felt it and was moved by it and was frightened by it both. He had buried children. He had stood in the small graveyard he had cleared with his own hands and laid into the cold ground three sons born and lost, and a daughter, his Patrician, five years old, kicked in the head by a horse, and he had thought after that fourth grave that he had done with the wanting of children, that a man could not survive the wanting of them, the way they were given and taken. And here was this child, not his, not wanted, the residue of a debt, asleep against his chest and warm and breathing, and the wanting came up in him unbidden, treacherous, against all the sense he had learned at four small gravesides, and he held the reins in one hand and steadied the sleeping girl with the other and rode north through the dark wood toward the house he had not yet built and the wife who was waiting and the new and unaccountable thing he had done.

He thought again of the mother. He would think of her for years, at odd hours, the way she had pushed the girl forward, the words he had half heard, the door of her face closing after. He had thought, in the yard, that she wanted to be rid of the child, one mouth fewer to feed, and had been faintly disgusted by it as a man is disgusted by a thing he does not understand. It was only later, much later, that another understanding came to him, slow and cold, that she had not been giving the child away but saving her, that she had looked at him, a stranger, a creature of unknown disposition, and had judged him, in the half a minute she had to judge, to be a man into whose hands she would rather deliver her daughter than leave her where she stood, in that yard, in that house, growing toward whatever the master of glazed windows and liveried servants intended for a girl with her mother's hands. She had chosen him. She had looked at the worst of the men available to her and chosen the least worst, and pushed her child toward him with her own hands, and he had taken the child and ridden away thinking himself charitable, and the woman had gone back to the kitchen to serve at the table of the man she was sparing her daughter, and would not know, ever, whether her judgment had been sound, whether the stranger she had chosen would prove kind or cruel, whether she had saved her girl or only sent her on to a different ruin. She had let go of the child's hand and would never know.

Jacob did not understand all this on the dark trail north. He understood only that he was troubled, that the night was long, that the child against him was warm and that her warmth both eased and accused him. He rode on. Somewhere far off a bird called and was answered. The fog had not come this far inland and the sky between the trees was thick with stars, more stars than a man could count in a long life, indifferent and bright, and beneath them the wood and the trail and the man and the horse and the stolen sleeping child, going north, going home, toward the house that did not yet exist and the years that did not yet exist and all that would be built and bought and buried in them, none of it visible yet, all of it coming.
