The price stickers were the worst of it. Marcine had bought them at the drugstore on Route 9, a roll of little orange circles, and now they sat like scabs on everything her mother had loved. Seventy-five cents on the gravy boat. Two dollars on the cedar chest that had stood at the foot of the bed for as long as there had been a bed. A dime—she had not been able to make herself charge more—on the hand mirror with the silver back gone to black, the one her mother used to hold up behind her head so she could see, in the dresser glass, what the rest of the world saw when she walked away.

She stood in the front room and watched a man in a Carhartt jacket lift the cedar chest by one end, testing its weight, and her whole body said *no* without making a sound. The man did not hear it. He set the chest down again and walked off toward the kitchen, and Marcine breathed.

It was October and the light came in low and yellow through the front windows, the same windows her mother had washed every spring with vinegar and newspaper, and the light fell across the floor in long planks the color of butterscotch, and the strangers walked through it picking things up and putting things down. They did not know they were walking through her childhood. They did not know that the worn place in the rug by the radiator was where she had lain on her belly at seven, eight, nine, reading library books with her chin in her hands until her mother said *Marcine, you'll ruin your eyes,* though her eyes had never been ruined, had stayed sharp all these years, sharp enough to read the small print on the orange stickers she had pressed onto everything with her own thumb.

She had not slept. The town she had grown up in, this little fold of Ohio where the river bent and the foundry had closed in 1981 and never reopened, was not a town that forgave a woman for leaving it. She had left it at nineteen with a suitcase and a typewriter and her mother standing on the porch not waving, just standing, one hand at her throat where the cameo used to be before she sold it, and Marcine had gotten on the bus and watched the porch and the woman on it slide backward into the past, and she had told herself she would come back rich, come back with something, and she had come back with this: a power of attorney, a death certificate, a roll of orange stickers.

"Excuse me." A woman was holding the gravy boat. She was young, maybe thirty, with a baby strapped to her chest in a length of patterned cloth, and the baby was asleep with its cheek against her breastbone. "Is this real silver?"

"It's plate," Marcine said. "It was my mother's."

The woman turned it over, looking for the mark, and the baby's mouth worked in its sleep, dreaming of milk. "Seventy-five cents is a steal then," the woman said, and smiled, and Marcine watched her own mother's gravy boat go into a canvas tote bag with a logo on it, the kind they gave away at banks, and she said nothing because there was nothing to say. That was the agreement. You put the sticker on, you took the money. You did not tell the story. The story was not for sale. The story was the only thing not for sale and so the story was worthless, and Marcine understood for the first time, watching the gravy boat disappear into the tote, that worthless and priceless were not opposites but the same country seen from two sides of the same river.

Her mother had been named Odessa. Odessa Pruitt, born in Alabama, brought north as a girl by a father who had heard there was work, and there had been work, for a while, and then there had not been, and Odessa had stayed anyway because by then she had married Marcine's father in the AME church on Fountain Street and had a child and a house, this house, bought with a mortgage that the bank had not wanted to give a colored man even with a foundry job, and Odessa's father had cosigned, and Odessa's father had died, and the house had stayed. Forty-one years it had stayed in her mother's hands. And now in a single afternoon it was emptying like a bathtub when you pull the plug, the water spiraling down and away, faster at the end than at the beginning.

"How much for the lamp?" A teenage boy, slouching, with his mother behind him.

"Dollar fifty," Marcine said. It had a sticker. He could read.

"Will you take a dollar?"

She looked at him. He was not a bad boy. He had a face like a boy who would grow into a man who fixed things, refrigerators maybe, furnaces, the kind of man people called in winter. "Take it," she said. "Take it for a dollar."

The lamp had stood on the table by her mother's reading chair, and her mother had read under it every night of her life that Marcine could remember, the Bible mostly but also the church bulletin and the newspaper and, near the end, the large-print Reader's Digests that the church ladies brought in stacks, and her mother had read these the way she read everything, with her lips moving slightly, with one finger sometimes underlining the words, a woman who had left school in the sixth grade to take in washing and had taught herself, taught herself, to read so well that she read aloud in church on the third Sunday and her voice when she read filled the whole sanctuary so that people closed their eyes to hear it better.

The boy carried the lamp out the door, holding it against his chest the way the woman held the baby, and Marcine watched the lamp go out into the October light and then the door closed and the lamp was gone and her mother's voice reading went with it, somewhere, into a house Marcine would never see, a different table, a different chair.

She had hired the estate company at first. A woman named Brenda had come out from Columbus in a blazer and walked through the house with a clipboard making little sounds, *mm, mm, mm,* and Brenda had said the company took forty percent and would handle everything, and Marcine had said no. She did not know why she had said no. The forty percent was nothing to her; she had money now, finally, late, more money than she knew what to do with, money that had come too late to buy her mother the things her mother had wanted, the trip to see the ocean, the new roof, the operation on her hip. She had said no to Brenda because she could not stand the idea of a stranger in a blazer touching her mother's things and not knowing, and now here she was, and the strangers were touching everything anyway, and at least she was here to see it, at least she was the witness, and she understood that this too was a kind of vanity, that the dead do not need a witness, that her mother was beyond caring who carried out the cedar chest, and that the witnessing was for Marcine and Marcine alone.

A man was at the bookshelf. He had taken down the photo albums, the ones her mother kept on the bottom shelf, fat with black pages and the little corner mounts gone yellow, and he was leafing through one.

"Those aren't for sale," Marcine said, crossing the room faster than she meant to.

The man looked up. He was older, sixties maybe, with reading glasses pushed up on his forehead and a paperback already under his arm that he meant to buy. "Oh," he said. "I beg your pardon. I thought, with everything—"

"They're not for sale." She took the album from his hands, more roughly than she meant to, and he raised both palms and stepped back, an apologizer, a man who had spent his life apologizing for taking up space.

"My mistake," he said. "I collect old photographs, sometimes. Local history. The historical society—" He stopped. "I didn't mean anything by it."

"I know," Marcine said, and she was ashamed, and she did not apologize, and the man bought his paperback for fifty cents and left.

She stood holding the album. She had not looked inside it. She had not been able to. She had carried it from the shelf to the dining room table and set it down on the table among the things she had not yet stickered, the things she could not sticker, the things that would not become money but would have to become something, garbage or memory or the weight in a box in a closet in her apartment in the city where she would not open them for years, if ever.

The dining room table itself had a sticker. Forty dollars, the whole set, table and six chairs, and a woman had already claimed it, had put down a deposit and gone to get her husband and his truck. Marcine had eaten at this table for eighteen years. Sunday dinners after church, her mother's hands moving the platter of chicken, the bowl of greens cooked down with the smoked turkey wing because her mother would not cook with pork after she got the high blood pressure, the rolls her mother made from scratch and the butter going soft in the heat of the kitchen. Her father at the head until her father was not anywhere, until her father went out one morning in 1973 to look for the work that was no longer in the town and did not come back, sent a money order once from Detroit and then nothing, and her mother had set his place at the table for a month afterward, just set it, the empty plate and the fork and the knife, until one Sunday she didn't, and neither of them ever spoke of it, the plate that stopped appearing, the way you stop expecting a tooth your tongue keeps finding the gap of.

The woman with the baby was back. She had put the gravy boat in her car, apparently, and now she was looking at the cedar chest, the one the man in the Carhartt jacket had tested and abandoned. She lifted the lid and the smell of cedar came out into the room, that smell, and Marcine's knees went soft because the smell was the inside of her mother's whole life, the smell of the chest where her mother kept the things that mattered, the quilt her own mother had made, the baby clothes, the letters tied with string, the wedding dress that yellowed and then was sold, the cameo before that was sold too.

"It's empty," the woman said, almost to herself, looking down into the chest.

"Yes," Marcine said. She had emptied it. She had emptied it at two in the morning, kneeling on the floor in her good slacks, taking out each thing and deciding, and the things she could not decide about she had put in a paper bag and the bag was in her rental car and she did not know what was in it now, could not have told you, had done it in a kind of trance with the radio off and the house ticking and settling around her like a thing cooling.

"It smells wonderful," the woman said. The baby stirred against her chest, made a small sound, and the woman swayed automatically, a motion Marcine recognized though she had never had a child, the sway that all the mothers knew, that her mother had known, the body remembering before the mind. "I could use it for blankets," the woman said. "In the nursery."

"My mother kept blankets in it," Marcine said. The words came out before she could put a sticker on them. "A quilt. And other things."

The woman looked up. She had a soft face, an unguarded face, the face of someone to whom nothing very bad had happened yet. "Did she pass recently?" she asked, and the gentleness in it undid something.

"Three weeks ago." Marcine heard her own voice from a distance. "She was eighty-six. She lived in this house forty-one years."

The woman nodded slowly. She did not say *I'm sorry,* which Marcine was grateful for, because everyone said *I'm sorry* and it was a sticker too, a little orange circle they pressed onto your grief so they could move it out the door. Instead the woman said, "My grandmother had a chest like this. We never knew what happened to it. After she died everything just—" She made a gesture, a small opening of the hand, the fingers spreading. "Went."

"Yes," Marcine said. "It goes."

The woman looked at the chest a long moment. Then she closed the lid, gently, and the cedar smell shut back inside it. "I think you should keep it," she said. "I'll find something else."

"It has a sticker on it," Marcine said. "Two dollars."

"I know." The woman smiled, and the baby's hand had worked free of the cloth and lay open against her chest, the impossibly small fingers, and Marcine looked at the hand and then away. "But I think you should keep it anyway."

And then the woman with the baby went on through the house, into the kitchen, where she bought a set of mixing bowls, the nested ones, yellow and green and white, that Marcine's mother had used to make the rolls, and Marcine let those go, let the bowls go into the bank tote with the wad of newspaper the woman wrapped them in, because you could not keep everything, because keeping everything was the same as keeping nothing, was a different kind of estate sale where you sold yourself to the past at forty percent and the past took everything and you kept the clipboard.

By three o'clock the house was half empty. The bones of it showed now, the rectangles on the walls where pictures had hung and the wallpaper behind them was a brighter version of itself, undimmed by forty years of light, so that the walls were a map of absence, here a clock, here a cross, here the framed certificate from the church naming Odessa Pruitt a Mother of the Year in 1968, which Marcine had taken down and which was in the paper bag in the car, or she thought it was, she could not be sure.

The man with the truck came for the dining table. He and the woman who had claimed it carried out the chairs first, one in each hand, four trips, and then the two of them lifted the table itself and angled it through the doorway, turning it on its side to fit, and Marcine stood and watched the table go through the door it had never been meant to leave by, the table that had come in before she was born and would now go out after her mother, and she thought about the dinners and the homework and the place that stopped being set, and she thought, this is the last thing, the table is the last thing, and then she remembered that it was not the last thing, that there were many more things, that the afternoon was not nearly over, that you could not run out of last things because everything was a last thing, the whole house was a last thing being carried out piece by piece through a door too small for it, turned on its side, scraping the frame.

She went into the kitchen. It was the only empty room now, the cabinets standing open, a few cans of food on the counter that no one would buy, soup and cling peaches and a can of the evaporated milk her mother put in her coffee, and Marcine put her hands flat on the counter and bent her head. The linoleum under her feet was worn through to black in front of the sink, worn through by her mother's feet, by the standing and standing, the washing and the cooking and the standing, and Marcine looked at the worn place and thought, that is where she stood, that is the actual place, the ground her mother's body wore down, and someone will buy this house and tear out this floor and put down something new, and the worn place will be gone, the last evidence that a woman stood here for forty-one years, gone, and no one will know, no one will know she stood here, and Marcine knew it, Marcine was the only one alive who knew it, and when Marcine died the knowing would die too, and the worn place was already gone really, was already only in Marcine's head, the floor itself meaning nothing without the head that held the knowing.

She heard the front door open, the bell her mother had hung on it years ago, a string of little brass bells from the dollar store that her mother put up at Christmas one year and never took down, and the bells made their small sound and a voice called out, uncertain, into the half-empty house.

"Marcine? Marcine Pruitt?"

She came out of the kitchen wiping her hands on nothing, on the air. An old woman stood just inside the door, very small, in a good coat, leaning on a cane with a quad base. It took Marcine a moment. The face had folded in on itself the way old faces do, gone soft and creased like a paper bag opened and closed too many times, but the eyes were the same, sharp and dark and amused.

"Mrs. Tolliver," Marcine said.

"You don't have to call me Mrs. Tolliver. You're a grown woman now. Older than I was when I taught you." She looked around the room, at the rectangles on the walls, at the strangers, at the orange stickers, and her mouth pulled to one side. "I heard about your mother. I would have come to the service but I don't drive at night anymore and they had it so late in the day."

"I didn't know you were still—" Marcine stopped.

"Still alive." The old woman laughed, a dry sound. "I'm ninety-one. Outlived my husband and two of my children and most of my students, I expect. Your mother and I used to sit together at the ten o'clock service." She came forward a few steps, the cane clicking, looking at things. "She was proud of you, you know. She kept your books on the shelf. Every one. She made me read the first one. She said, *that's my girl wrote that,* and she made me take it home." The old woman's eyes came back to Marcine's face. "I didn't understand all of it. But I understood she was proud."

Marcine stood very still. The books had been on the shelf. She had seen them there at two in the morning, her own name on the spines, four books across forty years, not enough, never enough, and she had not been able to look at them either, had left them on the shelf for the historical society man to find and not buy, and now they were still there, and her mother had read them, had made Mrs. Tolliver read them, had said *that's my girl,* and Marcine had not known, had thought her mother did not understand what she did, had thought the leaving had cut something that could not be mended, and all this time on the bottom shelf the books had stood beside the photo albums in the cedar-smelling house, and her mother had read them with her lips moving, with one finger underlining the words.

"I didn't know she read them," Marcine said.

"Course you didn't," the old woman said, not unkindly. "Children never know what their mothers know. That's the whole trouble with the world." She reached out and put one hand, light and cool and dry as paper, on Marcine's arm. "Don't sell those books," she said. "Whatever else you sell. Those have her hands on them now."

Then Mrs. Tolliver looked around once more at the emptying house, the strangers moving through the low gold light, and she said, "I just wanted to see it once more. Before." She did not say before what. She turned, the cane clicking, and made her slow way back to the door, and Marcine moved to help her but the old woman waved her off, and the brass bells rang as she opened the door, and the October light came in around her small dark shape, and then the door closed and the bells stilled and she was gone.

Marcine went to the bookshelf. She knelt down on the worn place in the rug, the place by the radiator, the place where she had lain on her belly reading as a child, and she took the four books off the shelf and held them against her chest, against her breastbone, the way the woman had held the baby, the way the boy had held the lamp, and she stayed there on her knees in the emptying house while around her the strangers carried out the furniture of her childhood, and the light came down through her mother's clean windows and lay across the floor in long planks the color of butterscotch, and she did not peel the sticker off the cedar chest, not yet, but she would, before the day was over she would, and she would carry it out herself, through the door, turned on its side if she had to, the smell of it shut inside, into the rest of her life.
