She did not go far. She settled herself at the edge of the cleared earth, beneath the half-shade of a thornbush, and she watched them resume their work as though they were tending something of hers. Anil knelt again over the bodies, the trowel small and exact in her hand, the brush smaller still. She had learned long ago to think of the dead not as the dead but as the evidence, as the assembly of facts that the body holds within it long after the body itself has stopped being a person. A femur could tell you the age within a few years. The pelvis would speak of sex. The teeth would carry the record of childhood hunger, the years of poverty mapped in the bands of disrupted enamel. The bones were honest. They did not negotiate, did not weep, did not refuse to answer. That was the comfort of them, and the horror.

She did not look at the woman again, though she felt her there at the rim of vision, the way one feels heat from a wall. The men in the grave had been shot. She had known this for two days now, from the small starred fractures at the back of one skull, from the way the other had fallen with his arm beneath him as if he had been reaching to break a fall he never completed. She brushed at the soil along the line of a rib and felt, as she always felt at such moments, the thinness of the membrane between her professional certainty and something that had no name and no use.

By late afternoon the light came in low and gold across the highlands and the heat began to loosen its grip. The team packed the finds into labeled bags, photographed the grave from the four cardinal points, drew the site plan with its grid of string and its careful annotations. The woman stayed until they covered the bodies again with the plastic sheeting and weighted the corners with stones. Then she stood and walked back down the path toward the road, and Anil watched her become small against the green of the hills, and then gone.

That was Guatemala. That was years ago.

She thought of it now because she was returning to a country she had not seen in fifteen years, and the thought of the woman in the grave had risen in her without her summoning it, the way certain images surfaced unbidden, attached to nothing she could explain, the mind's own grave-goods. The plane banked over the western coast of the island and she saw the line of surf, white and ceaseless, and the green going inland, and she felt nothing she had expected to feel. She had expected to feel something. She had prepared herself for it the way one braces for a wave. But there was only the strange neutrality of arrival, the hum of the engines changing pitch, the seatbelt sign, the faces of strangers around her composing themselves for landing.

She had been born here. She had left at eighteen, on a scholarship, and had not come back except once, for a few days, for a death, and that visit she remembered only in fragments she did not trust. Now she was returning as a foreigner, which was to say as someone official, attached to a Geneva-based human rights organization, sent at the request of various concerned parties to investigate. The word investigate had been used in the briefing as though it described something clean. She was to work alongside a local archaeologist appointed by the government. She was to examine certain remains. She was to determine, if she could, the nature and approximate date of certain deaths, and whether they suggested what everyone privately suspected and no one would say aloud.

She had a Sri Lankan passport, expired, and a British passport, current, and a green card, and a flat in another country she thought of as home in the way one thinks of a coat. She spoke Sinhala badly now, the language eroded out of her by years of disuse, surfacing in fragments, in the names of foods, in fragments of song, in the rhythm of a sentence she would begin and not be able to finish. She would say a word and the people around her would smile, not unkindly, at the accent that had come back wrong.

The airport at night was bright and underpopulated. There were soldiers, young men with automatic rifles standing in pairs near the doors and along the corridors, and their presence was not a thing anyone remarked on, which told her more than the rifles did. The casualness of it. The way the travelers moved past them without slowing, the way the soldiers themselves looked bored, leaning, talking quietly among themselves, the rifles slung as ordinarily as bags. A country becomes used to anything, she thought. That was the lesson of the work she did. People could become used to anything, and the becoming-used-to was itself a kind of wound, invisible, structural, a fracture in the enamel.

A man held a sign with her name on it, spelled almost correctly. He drove her into Colombo through streets she did not recognize and streets she half-recognized in the way of dreams, the layout familiar but the buildings wrong, the trees larger, the spaces filled in that had once been empty. The city had a curfew. There were checkpoints, sandbagged, lit by single bulbs, and at each one the driver slowed and turned on the interior light so the soldiers could see their faces, and the soldiers looked in at her foreign clothes and her foreign manner and waved them through. The third time this happened she understood that the light was a ritual of submission, of making oneself visible and harmless, and she remembered the miner's song her father used to hum, the one about being invisible as a fly down in the shaft, the safety of being unseen. Here it was the reverse. Here you were safe only when you let them see you, when you offered up your face to be read.

She was taken to a guesthouse, an old colonial building with high ceilings and slow fans and the smell of frangipani and mildew and old wood. She slept badly. She woke before dawn to the sound of crows and to a sound she could not place at first and then understood was the ocean, far off, persistent, the same surf she had seen from the plane. She lay in the dark and listened to it and tried to feel that she had come home and could not, and then she got up and worked, because work was the thing she had instead of feeling, and she had learned not to be ashamed of this.

The archaeologist appointed to work with her was named Sarath Diyasena. She met him on the second day, in a government building where the corridors smelled of disinfectant and the files were yellowing and tied with string, where men moved papers from one desk to another in a vast and patient choreography whose purpose she could not divine. Sarath was perhaps forty-nine, a thickset man with a tired, intelligent face and a way of holding himself slightly apart, watchfully, as though he were always calculating the cost of what he was about to say. He had studied in England, he told her, and in the United States, and he spoke English with the formal precision of a man who had learned to be careful in two languages. She could not tell, in those first hours, whether he was an ally or a minder, whether he had been assigned to help her or to watch her, and she understood that she might never be able to tell, and that she would have to work alongside him in that uncertainty.

"You've been told what is possible and what is not," he said, not as a question.

"I've been told very little. That's usual."

"Yes." He almost smiled. "It is usual." He poured her tea from a thermos, his movements economical. "There are six government archaeological preserves on the island. They are under guard. Access is restricted. No one is permitted to dig there without authorization, and the authorization is difficult to obtain even for those of us who work for the government." He set the cup before her. "Four of them are caves and rock-shelter sites, ancient, of great importance. They contain remains that are six thousand years old. They are guarded because they are precious." He paused. "You understand what I am telling you."

She understood. The preserves were guarded, and the guards kept people out, and in the keeping-out anything might be hidden, because a place no one was permitted to enter was the safest place to put a thing one did not want found. The ancient dead and the recent dead, lying in the same protected ground.

"We have permission for one site," Sarath said. "The cave at Bandarawela. A site of paleontological interest. There are skeletal remains there that are very old. We are to examine them."

"Why that site?"

"Because it is the site for which permission was granted." He looked at her steadily. "I would advise you to be grateful for what is granted and not to spend too much energy on what is withheld. In this country that is the difference between doing some good and doing none."

They drove up into the hills the next morning, into the cool of the tea country, the long terraced slopes green and exact, the women in the rows with sacks on their backs and bright cloths over their hair, their hands moving over the bushes with the speed of a thing memorized past thought. The road wound and climbed. The air grew thin and clean. Sarath drove and said little, and she watched the country open and fold and open again, and she thought she had forgotten how beautiful it was, and then she thought that beauty was the wrong word for what she felt, that it was something more like grief, that the beauty was indistinguishable from the loss, that she could not look at the green hills without thinking of what they covered.

The cave at Bandarawela was reached by a path through scrub forest, and at its mouth a soldier checked their papers and then sat back down on a flat stone and resumed not watching them. Inside, the air was cool and still and smelled of stone and bat and age. Sarath had arranged for lights, a generator humming outside, cables run in along the floor. There were four skeletons there, laid out in shallow graves cut into the cave floor, and they had been excavated already in part, the bones exposed, brown and ancient, curled in the fetal position of the very old dead, the position that meant deliberate burial, that meant someone six thousand years ago had grieved and folded the body of a loved one and covered it with care.

She knelt over them with a feeling that was almost relief. This was the work she knew. These were the dead who could be touched without trespass, the dead whose mourners were themselves long dust, the dead who had passed entirely into evidence and out of pain. She measured and recorded. She and Sarath worked in a companionable silence broken only by the technical exchanges of their trade, the names of bones, the language of measurement, the small confirmations and disagreements of two people doing skilled work together. He was good. She had not expected him to be so good. He read the bones the way she did, with attention and tenderness and a refusal to invent, and watching his hands she began, against her own caution, to trust him a little.

It was on the second day in the cave that she found the fourth skeleton was wrong.

She did not say so at first. She had learned to be quiet about a thing until she was certain of it. But the bone in her hands was wrong, and she knew it the way one knows a false note in music, a thing the body registers before the mind can say why. The three other skeletons were the brown of great age, the color leached and absorbed into them over millennia, the bone itself transformed, mineralized, made one with the earth it lay in. The fourth had that color too, on the surface. But where she had brushed the soil from a fractured edge she saw beneath the brown a different color, paler, and the weight of the bone in her hand was not the weight of six thousand years. And there were marks. On a long bone she found a small cut, clean, the kind of cut a tool makes, and around it a faint burning, a scorching she did not understand until she did.

She sat back on her heels. The generator hummed. Outside, the soldier dozed in the sun.

The fourth skeleton was not ancient. It had been made to look ancient. Someone had taken a recent body, a body perhaps four or five years old, and had buried it among the protected dead, in the one place where no one was permitted to dig, in the safest ground on the island, and had treated it in some way—she could see it now, the scorch marks, the deliberate staining—so that it would pass, at a glance, for one of the ancient ones. And it would have passed. It would have lain there unquestioned for another six thousand years, except that someone had granted permission for this cave, this one cave, and had sent for a foreign forensic specialist, and had assigned a careful man to watch her, and somewhere in the long machinery of the state someone had made a mistake, or had not made a mistake at all but had intended this, had wanted this thing found, and had arranged it so that the finding could not be traced back to any single hand.

She looked up. Sarath was watching her. He had been watching her for some time, she realized. He had known. Of course he had known. He had read these bones before she ever came, he had been into this cave many times, he was the appointed archaeologist of the protected sites, and he had known what lay in the fourth grave, and he had said nothing, and he had brought her here.

"You see it," he said quietly.

"How long have you known?"

He did not answer at once. He came and knelt beside her and looked at the bone in her hands, the small clean cut, the scorch. "It is interesting," he said, in the flat technical voice they used, "that the staining was done with a vegetable dye. And the scorching is uneven. It was done by someone who understood the principle but not the practice. Someone who knew that age changes the color and the surface of bone, but did not know precisely how." He took the bone gently from her and turned it in the light. "Someone with access. Someone permitted into these caves."

She understood what he was telling her, and she understood that he was telling her something dangerous, and she understood that by telling her he had committed himself to something, crossed some line he had been careful not to cross, and she did not yet know why.

"Who?" she said.

"That is your question now. That is the question you have been sent here to ask, whether or not anyone meant for you to ask it." He set the bone down with great care, in its place, as though it might still feel the handling. "We will call him Sailor."

"Why Sailor?"

He almost smiled again, the tired smile. "Because the dead need names. And because if I am to help you, and I think now that I will help you, we will need a name to use that is not his real name, which we do not know and may never know. He worked, I think, on something. The wear on the bones suggests labor. The vertebrae are compressed. He carried weight, repeatedly, over years, beginning when he was young." He looked at her. "A laborer. A miner, perhaps, or a man who carried sacks, or a fisherman who hauled nets. We will give him back a life from these marks, you and I, and then perhaps we will be able to give him back a death, which is to say a truth about how he died and where and at whose hand. It is the only restitution we can offer him. It is small."

"It isn't small," she said.

He looked at her with something she could not read, and for a moment the careful watchfulness lifted from his face and she saw underneath it something exhausted and human and frightened, and then it closed again.

"You have been away a long time," he said. "You think of this as a case. A set of facts to be established. Evidence. Truth. In Geneva, in London, in the places where you have learned your trade, truth is a good. It is wanted. The establishing of it is a service that is paid for, and the world is grateful, and the truth, once established, has somewhere to go." He stood and walked a few steps and stopped. "Here it is not so. Here the truth is a thing that can get you killed. Not metaphorically. Here a man who establishes a truth about how Sailor died may himself become a Sailor, may himself be made to disappear and buried where no one will look, and his family will stand at the edge of no grave, because there will be no grave, because that is the point. You must understand this before we go further. I will help you. But you must understand what you are asking, and what you are asking me."

She was quiet. Outside the cave the afternoon went on, the birds, the wind in the scrub, the soldier with his rifle dozing in the sun, the whole bright indifferent surface of the country under which the dead lay in their protected ground.

"I understand," she said, and did not, not yet, but would.

They worked until the light outside began to fail and the generator's hum seemed to grow louder in the dimming cave. They wrapped Sailor's bones, each one, in cloth and in plastic, and packed them with the labeled samples of the ancient three, so that to any eye watching, to the soldier at the mouth of the cave, to anyone who might examine the records later, they had merely done their authorized work and removed their authorized findings, four sets of ancient remains for study, nothing more. Sarath did this packing himself, with his own hands, slowly, and she understood that this too was a kind of decision, that he was choosing to hide Sailor in plain sight, among the ancient dead, the same camouflage his killers had used, turned now to a different purpose.

"Where will we take him?" she asked.

"Somewhere we can work without being watched. I have a place. It belonged to my brother. It is empty now." Something moved across his face at the word brother and was gone. "We will need equipment we cannot use in the open. We will need to reconstruct the face, if it can be done. We will need a man I know, who does that kind of work, who lives outside the city and outside the war, more or less, as much as anyone can. He is difficult. But he is the best, and he is far enough from the center of things that he may be willing." He fastened the last of the packing and stood, brushing the soil from his trousers, an ordinary gesture in an unordinary moment. "We are taking on something now. You understand. We cannot put it down once we have lifted it. A grave is like that. Once you open it you have to answer for what you find."

She thought of the woman in Guatemala, in the grave, on her haunches, looking down at the two men who had been her husband and her brother, the feminine string between them, and she thought of the woman rising to make room for the scientists to do their work, the courtesy of it, the unbearable courtesy. There are no words she knew that could describe that woman's face. But the grief in that shoulder she had not forgotten, would not forget, still remembered. And it occurred to her now, kneeling in the cave with the bones of a stranger packed beside her, that perhaps that was what all of this was, all the measuring and recording and the establishing of facts that she had told herself was a service to truth—that perhaps it was only this, only a way of standing at the edge of the grave with the bereaved, of saying with the instruments of her trade what could not be said in words, that someone had lived and someone had loved them and someone had taken them away, and that here, at least, in this one cave, in these few bones, the taking would not be allowed to be the last word.

"Anil," Sarath said. It was the first time he had used her name. "You should know that they will be aware of us. Whatever we do, they will know that something is being done. We will not be invisible. The thing is to do enough, fast enough, and to get it out, to get the truth to a place where it cannot be unmade, before they decide that we have become a problem worth solving." He lifted the case of bones, cradling it against his chest. "Do you still want to go on?"

She stood. Her knees ached from the stone. Through the mouth of the cave she could see the last of the light on the hills, the terraced slopes going dark, the long green country folding into evening, beautiful and terrible and hers and not hers. Somewhere out in that dusk a generator was being switched off and a soldier was waking and stretching and lighting a cigarette, and somewhere beyond him a family was lying down in a hut not knowing where their son was, was holding to the possibility of him, which was everywhere, which was in every cave and every river and every field of the island. She thought she would carry that now. She thought she had no choice but to carry it.

"Yes," she said. "I want to go on."

He nodded, once, as if she had only confirmed something he had already known about her, something he had perhaps been sent to find out, or had decided to find out for his own reasons, which she did not yet understand and might never understand. He carried Sailor out into the dusk, and she gathered the last of the equipment and followed him, and the soldier at the mouth of the cave stood and watched them pass with his rifle slung and his cigarette glowing, and waved them through, and they went down the path through the scrub in the failing light toward the vehicle and the road and the city and everything that was now beginning.
