The first house belonged to the Wendlands, and Otto Brauer let himself in through the side door because nobody had locked it in forty years and nobody was going to start now. The kitchen smelled of woodsmoke and the particular mustiness of a house where one old woman lived alone and had stopped opening windows. Greta Wendland was ninety-one. She sat in the front room with a blanket across her knees, and when she heard him she called out, "Is that the piano man?" in the voice of someone who already knew.

"It's me, Greta."

"You came."

"I said I would."

He set his case down beside the upright, a Schimmel that her husband had bought in 1958 from a dealer in the city who was long dead. The wood had gone the color of weak tea. Otto opened the lid and the felts breathed out the smell of every winter the room had held. He pressed middle C and listened to it sag.

"Nobody's touched it," Greta said. "Not since Hans. You know that."

"I know."

"So why do you come? It's a foolishness. An old man driving all this way for a piano nobody plays."

He took out his tuning lever and his strips of felt and laid them in a row on the bench the way he had laid them out for fifty-one years. "Because it goes out of tune whether anyone plays it or not," he said. "That's the thing people don't understand. The strings pull. Twenty tons of pull on a frame like this. It wants to come apart. Every day it wants to come apart a little more, and the only thing holding it is the tuning. So you tune it. Even if nobody plays. Especially if nobody plays."

"That's no answer."

"It's the only one I have."

She laughed, a dry sound, and pulled the blanket higher. "You always talked like a priest. Even as a young man. Hans used to say it. The piano man talks like he's giving last rites."

"Maybe I am."

He worked. The A came up first, and from the A he built the temperament, the careful narrowing of fifths, the widening of thirds, the small impurities you accepted so that the whole would hold together — the lie at the center of every tuned instrument, the compromise that let it play in any key. He had explained this to his son once, long ago, the boy sitting on the bench beside him with his legs not yet reaching the floor. You cannot make everything perfect, Konrad. If you make the fifths perfect the octaves drift. If you make the thirds perfect you cannot play in more than one key. So you spread the error out. You give a little to everyone and nobody gets it all and that is how the thing stays whole. The boy had nodded with the terrible seriousness of a child being told something true.

"How is your son?" Greta asked, as if she had reached into him.

"I don't know," Otto said. "We don't speak."

"Since when?"

"A long time."

She was quiet. Outside a tractor went by on the road, the first sound of another human life in an hour. Otto brought up the second octave, his ear leaning into the beats, counting them slow, slowing them, killing them, until the interval lay flat and clean.

"He was going to be a great one," Greta said. "Everyone said. The recital at the church. I was there. You don't forget a thing like that, a boy playing like that in a country church where we only ever heard hymns banged out by the Fessler woman with her arthritis. I cried. Hans cried and he never cried at anything. We thought, this is a boy who will go to Vienna, to Berlin, who will play for the world. And then—" She stopped.

"And then," Otto agreed.

"What happened, Otto? I never knew. Nobody knew. He played that one time and then he was gone and you never spoke of him."

Otto set down the lever. He looked at the keys, the ivory yellowed and chipped on the F above middle C where Hans Wendland's wedding ring had struck it for thirty years of Sunday afternoons. "Nothing happened," he said. "That's the truth of it. Nothing happened that you could point to. He played the recital. He was fifteen. He played the Schubert, the B-flat sonata, the slow movement, and I have never in my life heard anything like it and I have spent my life listening to pianos. And afterward I told him." He stopped.

"Told him what?"

"What I thought he had done wrong."

The room held very still.

"There was a passage," Otto said. "In the second theme. He rushed it. A little. Nobody else heard it. Maybe nobody else in the world would have heard it. But I heard it, because that is what I am, I am a man who hears the thing that is slightly wrong, it is the only gift I have ever had, and I could not — " His voice caught and he was surprised by it, an old man surprised by his own grief like a stranger come to the door. "I could not let it be. He came to me, he was shining, he was lit up like I had never seen him, and he said Papa, Papa, did you hear, and I said yes, I heard, you rushed the second theme. That was the first thing I said to him. The only thing he remembered, I think. Not that it was beautiful. He knew it was beautiful. He needed me to know it. And I told him about the second theme."

Greta said nothing. There was nothing to say.

"He never played again. Not for me. Not that I know of. He left at seventeen and he does mathematics now, in another country, he has a wife and children I have seen in photographs, and he is by all accounts a happy man, and he has not touched a piano in thirty years, and I do not blame him." He picked up the lever again. His hands were steady; his hands were always steady; it was the one thing time had not taken. "I tune pianos that nobody plays," he said. "You asked me why. There's your answer too, I suppose."

He finished the Wendland piano in silence. When he played the closing chords of the little Bach prelude he always used to test his work, the whole instrument rang true, every interval lying in its bed of accepted compromise, the lie at the center holding the truth together. Greta had closed her eyes. He thought she might be asleep until she said, "Play it again."

He played it again. It was the only music that house would hear until he came back in the spring, if he came back, if she was still here, if either of them was.

"Thank you, Otto."

"It's nothing, Greta."

"It's not nothing. Don't say it's nothing. An old man knows when a thing is not nothing."

He packed his case and would not take her money, and she did not insist, because this had been the arrangement for years now, the fiction that he was still in business, that this was still a trade and not a pilgrimage. At the door she said, "Go and see your son."

"He's across an ocean."

"There are airplanes. There are telephones. You're not so poor and you're not so proud. Go and see him before one of you is in the ground and the other one is standing over it with his lever in his hand wondering why he never said the thing he should have said. I buried Hans. I know what I'm telling you."

"Goodbye, Greta."

"Stubborn old goat."

"Goodbye."

The road ran north between fields gone brown and flat under a sky the color of a held breath. He drove slowly because he had nowhere to be and because the car, like everything he owned, was old and asked to be treated gently. There were five houses on his route and after the Wendlands' there was the Pohl farm, where a piano stood in a parlor that the family used twice a year, and after the Pohls there was the schoolmaster Brenner who had died in the spring so that the house was empty now, but the daughter had asked him to keep tuning the instrument because she could not bear to sell it and could not bear to think of it falling silent and out of true in the cold. He had a key. He let himself in and tuned a piano in an empty house, the dust sheets over the furniture like the shapes of sleeping animals, and when he was done he played the Bach to the empty room and the empty room gave it back to him with a slight echo it had not had when the house was full of living and breathing things.

Then the Achterbergs, where the wife had dementia and sometimes sat at the piano and pressed the keys at random for hours, so that the tuning was for her, for the comfort of sound, and it did not matter if it was in tune except that it mattered to him, it mattered terribly, because she deserved a true sound even if she could not tell the difference, perhaps especially because she could not tell the difference, there being no one left to perform for and therefore no reason except the thing itself.

The light was going by the time he reached the church.

It stood at the edge of the village of Sankt Veit, a small church of gray stone with a squat tower, the kind of church that had stood through wars and plagues and the slow emptying of the countryside, the young going to the cities, the old going to the churchyard, until on a Sunday now you might count twenty heads, all of them silver. Otto parked the car and sat for a moment with his hands on the wheel. He had saved this one for last. He always saved this one for last, the way you save the thing you cannot face until you have run out of other things to do.

The piano in the church of Sankt Veit was a Bösendorfer, a grand, a real one, donated in some flush forgotten decade by a family that had since died out or moved away. It had no business being in a country church and that was exactly why it was there: it had been a gift too grand to refuse and too grand to ever properly use, so it sat to the side of the altar under a cloth, and twice a year someone played it badly, and once, thirty-four years ago, a boy of fifteen had played it as well as it had ever been played anywhere.

The door was unlocked. Churches, like the houses of the old, did not lock in this part of the world. Otto went in and the cold came up off the stone floor and the last light fell in long bars through the tall windows, dust turning in it, and the place smelled of cold stone and old wood and the ghost of candle smoke. He walked up the center aisle the way he had walked up it that day, behind his son, his hand on the boy's thin shoulder, and he had been proud, God forgive him, he had been so proud he could not contain it, and he had let none of it show because he had thought — he had genuinely thought — that to show it would be to spoil it, that the boy needed a clear-eyed critic and not a weeping father, that love and judgment were the same thing, that to hold someone to the highest standard was the purest form of love there was.

He had been wrong about that. He had been forty years arriving at the knowledge that he had been wrong, and it had cost him a son to learn it.

He took the cloth off the Bösendorfer and folded it and laid it on the front pew. He opened the lid and propped it on the long stick and the strings lay there in the failing light, the great copper-wound basses and the bright trebles, the whole gleaming architecture of the thing. He played middle C. It had drifted, but not badly; the church was cold and steady and a cold steady room is kind to a piano. He laid out his felts. He laid out his lever. He began with the A.

While he worked he let himself remember, because there was no one here to see an old man's face do what it would do.

The boy had been frightened. Of course he had been frightened. He had stood in the little room behind the altar where the priest kept his vestments and he had said, Papa, I can't, and Otto had crouched down to him and said, You can. You have done it a thousand times. The piano does not know you are afraid. It only knows your hands. Give it your hands and let the rest go. And the boy had nodded and gone out and sat down at the great instrument that dwarfed him and put his hands on the keys and the whole church had gone quiet, the kind of quiet that has weight, and then he had begun to play, and the Schubert had come out of him, the long singing line of it, the slow movement that is not really slow but suspended, held, time stopped and turned over and examined like a stone in the hand, and Otto, standing at the back by the door, had felt the top of his head come off. He had felt the boundary between himself and the world dissolve. He had thought: this is why. This is why all of it, the years, the work, the lonely roads and the dying pianos and the trade that was already dying around him — all of it had been so that this boy could stand in this church and play like this. He had made a thing that could make this. There was no greater accomplishment available to a human life and he had achieved it and he had stood at the back of a country church and wept without making a sound.

And then it was over and the people had risen to their feet, these farmers and shopkeepers and old women, they had risen as one and the sound of them was like the sea, and the boy had stood and bowed, awkward, fifteen, and looked out over them and found his father's face at the back, and his whole self had been one question, one open and undefended question — was it good, Papa, was I good, did I do the thing — and Otto had been so full, so dangerously full, that he had retreated into the only safe country he knew, which was the country of the small correct observation, the place where he was master and could not be hurt, and afterward in the vestry when the boy came to him flushed and shining and said Papa did you hear, Otto had said: Yes. You rushed the second theme.

He set down the lever in the cold church and put his face in his hands.

He had watched the light go out. That was the thing he could not forgive himself, the thing he had carried up and down these roads for thirty-four years tuning pianos for the dead and the demented and the absent. He had watched the light go out of his son's face. Not all at once. That would have been a mercy, a clean wound. It had gone slowly, over a moment that lasted a year, the open question closing, the shining dimming, the boy understanding something about his father and about the price of his father's love and recalibrating his entire life around that understanding. And the boy had said, after a moment, very quietly, Yes. I felt that. I'll work on it. And he had never worked on it because he had never played it again, never played anything again, had put the lid down on that part of himself and walked away from it into the clean abstract world of numbers where his father could not follow and could not judge and could not, with one small true correct observation, take the whole of heaven away.

Otto sat on the bench in the dark church for a long time.

Then he wiped his face with his handkerchief, the way an old man does, methodically, and he picked up his lever, because the piano was still out of tune and that at least was a thing he could put right. He worked his way up through the octaves. The church grew darker and he worked by feel and by ear, which was how the best of it was done anyway, the eyes only a distraction at the end. He set the temperament and built out from it, the careful web of compromise, and the old Bösendorfer came slowly into true under his hands, every string brought into its right relation, the whole vast tense architecture of it pulled into agreement with itself, the lie at the center making the truth at the surface possible.

When it was done he sat with his hands in his lap.

He should test it. He always tested it. The Bach, the little prelude, to hear the whole of it ring. But his hands would not go to the Bach. They sat in his lap and he looked at the keys gleaming faintly in the last of the light and he understood that the Bach was not the thing his hands wanted, that they had wanted something else for thirty-four years and he had never let them have it.

He began to play the Schubert.

He was not a pianist. He had never been a pianist. He was a tuner, a man whose hands knew the keys the way a surgeon's hands know the body, intimately and without art. He could not play the Schubert as his son had played it. No one could play it as his son had played it. But he knew it; he had heard it that one time and it had engraved itself on him; and he played it now in the dark and cold, slowly, more slowly than it should be played, stumbling, the notes coming wrong and being corrected, the long line broken and rejoined, an old man picking his way through the most beautiful thing he had ever heard, played once, in this room, by a boy he had failed.

And when he came to the second theme he did not rush it.

He took it slow. He took it as slow as it would go. He let each note stand and ring and decay into the cold stone air of the empty church, and he gave it all the time in the world, all the time he had left, all the time he had stolen from his son and could never give back, and the tears ran down into the white stubble of his face and he did not stop to wipe them because his hands were busy, his hands were occupied with the only apology he had, played to no one, in an empty church, at the end of his last round, on a piano that no one would hear again until spring if anyone heard it at all.

He finished. The last chord hung in the air and faded and the church was silent.

He sat until the cold drove him up. Then he put away his felts and his lever, each in its place, and he closed the lid of the Bösendorfer and took the stick away and let it down gently. He spread the cloth back over it. He did not want to. He wanted to leave it open, ready, as if someone might come. But you covered the piano; that was how it was done; you covered it against the dust and the cold and the long waiting.

Outside, the first stars were coming out over the brown fields and the gray tower. Otto stood by his car in the cold and took out his telephone, the small foolish thing his daughter — Konrad's sister, who still spoke to him, who had given him the photographs — had bought him and taught him to use with great patience. He found the number. He had had it for years and never used it. It was very late where Konrad was, or very early; he could never keep the hours straight, the great distance, the ocean and the turning of the earth.

He stood in the churchyard and he pressed the number and he put the small cold thing to his ear and listened to it ring, once, twice, the strange foreign tone of a telephone in another country, in another life, in the house of a man who did mathematics and had a wife and children and had not touched a piano in thirty years.

It rang a third time. A fourth.

Otto looked up at the stars over the church where his son had once made the top of his head come off, and he waited, and he rehearsed in his mind the thing he would say, which was not an observation, and was not a correction, and was not small. It was four words and he had waited thirty-four years to say them and he hoped to God the man on the other end would still want to hear them.

The ringing stopped. There was a silence, and a breath, and a voice he had not heard in years, thick with sleep and wary, said, "Hello?"

"Konrad," said Otto. "It's your father." His own voice broke on it and he let it break. "It was beautiful," he said. "You were beautiful. I should have said so. I'm saying so now."

The silence on the line was very long, and across all that distance, across the ocean and the dark and the turning earth, he could hear his son begin, quietly, to weep.
