The boy who came to take the cannery's measurements wore yellow boots that had never touched mud. Hanna watched him from the window above the kitchen sink, watched him pick his way across the gravel lot as though it might give way beneath him, clipboard held against his chest like a shield. Behind her, at the table, her grandfather did not look up from the chart he was correcting in pencil, though he must have heard the car.

"They've sent someone," she said.

"They send someone every week now." He licked the tip of the pencil, a habit she had told him would poison him, and made a small mark off the headland where the depths had changed since the last survey. The chart was forty years old. He corrected it anyway, the way some men shave each morning whether or not they'll be seen. "Come away from there. You'll learn nothing watching a man who is afraid of his own shoes."

She was twelve that winter, old enough to be useful and young enough to still believe that being useful was the same as being loved, which in her grandfather's house was nearly true. Her mother had gone south in October to a job in a hospital laundry and a man whose name Hanna refused to keep in her mouth, and the arrangement had been made without anyone asking Hanna what she wanted, which was to stay. So she had stayed, and it had been understood, in the wordless way the old man understood most things, that staying meant working.

He set down the pencil. "Tide's turning in twenty minutes. Get your coat."

The town was called Sorgvik, which an ancestor with a grim sense of humor or simple honesty had named for sorrow, and it sat at the throat of a fjord that had fed nine generations of fishermen and was now feeding none. The cannery was the last thing that ran. It had run since before her grandfather was born, the great brick building with its corrugated additions, and the smell of it—brine and fish oil and the sweetish rot of the offal barrels—was the smell of the town itself, so that the people who lived there no longer noticed it and the people who visited could not stop talking about it. In March it would close. Everyone knew. The men from the south had come with their clipboards and their yellow boots to inventory what could be sold and to assess what the land beneath might be worth to someone who would tear all of it down.

Her grandfather had piloted ships into that fjord for fifty-one years. There was no lighthouse keeper anymore, no harbormaster who was not also the man who ran the petrol pump, but there had always, until his retirement, been a pilot, because the fjord was treacherous in a way that did not show itself on charts. The depths were honest enough. It was the water on top of them that lied.

"Look there," he said, when they had walked out along the breakwater to the place where the stones ended and the iron ladder went down to nothing. He pointed, not at the horizon, but at a patch of water perhaps two hundred meters out, indistinguishable to her from the gray water all around it. "What do you see?"

"Water."

"You see water the way a deaf man sees a violin. Look again."

She looked. The wind came hard off the open sea beyond the heads and her eyes watered and she wanted to be home, where the stove was, where she could read the English paperbacks her mother had left behind without anyone telling her she was wasting her sight. But she had learned that the fastest way through her grandfather's lessons was to give herself to them, and so she narrowed her eyes against the cold and made herself see.

The patch he had pointed to was darker. No—not darker. Rougher. The small waves there were closer together, more anxious, breaking against something they could not see.

"It's not the same," she said. "It's choppier. Like there's something under it."

"There is something under it. A shoal. Comes up to four meters at low water, and a ship that draws five will open her belly on it like a fish on a board." He did not look at her when he taught; he looked at the water, as though it were the water he was instructing and she only happened to be standing near enough to overhear. "The chart shows it. Any fool with a chart knows it's there. But charts get left below, and fog comes, and engines fail, and then a man has nothing but his eyes. The water tells you. The water always tells you, if you've learned its language. That texture—we call it the *blinde vann*. The blind water. Because it's where the blind go to die."

He let that sit. He was fond of letting things sit. The cold worked its way through her coat and into the long bones of her arms.

"Now," he said. "The tide's turning. Watch what the shoal does when the water starts to move."

They stood for the better part of an hour. She learned, in that hour, more than she would have believed water could contain. She learned that the turning tide announced itself first not at the shore but in the middle distance, where the surface went briefly slack and confused, like a room full of people who have all stopped talking at once. She learned that the current ran differently over the shoal than around it, that you could see the seam where two bodies of moving water met and argued, a line of small turbulence that wandered and reformed. She learned that a gull sitting on the water was sitting on a current, and that you could read the speed of the water by how fast the bird was carried, and that her grandfather knew the gulls in some private way, could tell her which were feeding and which were only resting and which had found a school of herring that no instrument on any ship would have detected.

"You're cold," he said finally, and it was not a question and not quite an apology.

"I'm all right."

"You're cold. Good. A pilot who minds the cold gets work done. A pilot who minds it too much stays home, and a pilot who doesn't mind it at all is dead by forty. Come. There's coffee."

They walked back. The cannery loomed on its pilings over the black water of the inner harbor, and the boy in the yellow boots was photographing the loading doors, and her grandfather looked at him the way he looked at the blind water, with a kind of weather-eyed contempt that was almost tenderness, as though the boy too were a hazard that simply did not know enough to be ashamed of itself.

That night her grandmother's chair sat empty by the stove as it had sat empty for six years, and her grandfather filled the silence the way he always did, with talk of the water, because it was the only subject on which he was not silent. He told her about the night in 1974 when he had brought a Greek freighter through the fjord in a fog so thick he could not see the bow from the bridge, navigating entirely by the sound of the water against the headlands and the smell of the spruce on the northern shore, which a man could smell, he swore, before he could see the trees. He told her about the pilot before him, old Severin, who had taught him as he was now teaching her, and who had died in his bed at ninety-one still able to name every rock in the fjord by the sound the sea made breaking over it.

"There won't be a pilot after me," he said. He said it without self-pity. "No ships big enough to need one. When the cannery closes there'll be nothing in this water but the little boats, and they know it well enough or they drown, and either way they don't need an old man to tell them."

"You could teach me anyway," she said.

He looked at her then, which he rarely did when they were talking, and something moved in his face that she could not read, because she had not yet learned to read his face the way she was learning to read the water. "I am teaching you anyway," he said.

The weeks went on and the winter deepened. The men from the south came and went and finally stopped coming, having taken whatever measurements satisfied them, and a notice appeared on the cannery door giving the date of the final shift. Hannah's mother called on Sundays from the south and the calls were brief and strained, full of weather reports from two places that could not imagine each other. Her mother asked if she was warm enough, if she was eating, if the old man was treating her well, and Hannah said yes to all of it, and did not say that she had learned to tell the depth of water by its color, the green of the deep channel and the brown-gold of the flats and the strange flat pewter that meant a bottom of mud rather than sand. There was no way to say it that her mother would have heard as anything but the old man filling the girl's head with the past.

But it was not the past. That was the thing Hanna could not have explained. The water was not the past. The water was the most present thing she had ever encountered, more present than the warm kitchen or the paperbacks or the voice on the telephone, because the water was always changing and always demanding to be read again, fresh, in the light of the particular hour. You could not learn it once. You learned it continuously or you did not know it at all. Her grandfather had been reading it for sixty years and he still went out every day to read it again, because yesterday's water was gone and meant nothing, and tomorrow's had not yet arrived.

In February the herring came, late and thin, the last run before the cannery closed. The boats went out in the dark and came back in the dark and the cannery ran around the clock for eleven days, the lights of it burning all night over the harbor, the conveyors clattering, the women in their oilskins and white caps standing at the gutting tables as their mothers and grandmothers had stood. Hanna's grandfather took her down to watch the boats come in, and she saw that he was not watching the boats. He was watching the water in the harbor mouth, the way the incoming hulls displaced it, the wake each one threw, the set of each boat in the chop.

"That one's overloaded," he said, of a boat she could barely distinguish from the others. "Sitting too low astern. Skipper's greedy because it's the last money he'll see from this water for a long time. Watch—he'll touch the bar coming in if he doesn't mind his line."

She watched. The boat came on, its green and white lights pitching, and at the harbor mouth it slewed suddenly, the bow swinging, and there was a shout that carried across the water, and then the boat straightened and came on and was safe.

"He touched it," her grandfather said with satisfaction. "Felt his keel kiss the bar and it scared the greed right out of him. He'll come in light tomorrow." He turned up his collar. "You saw it before he did. Did you notice? You saw he was too low. That's the beginning of it. When you can see the danger before the man who's standing in it—that's a pilot."

She had not, in fact, seen that the boat was overloaded; she had taken his word. But she had seen the slew at the harbor mouth, had felt in her own stomach the lurch of it, and she understood for the first time that what he was teaching her was not a body of facts about a particular fjord. It was a way of standing in the world. It was attention. It was the refusal to be told what the water was doing when you could look and see for yourself.

The cannery closed on a Thursday. There was no ceremony. The notice had given the date and the date arrived and the last shift ended at two in the afternoon and the workers came out into the gray light and stood in the gravel lot in their oilskins, not knowing quite what to do with their bodies, which had known for so long where to go at the end of a shift and now did not. Some of them wept. Most did not. Her grandfather stood with Hanna at the edge of the lot and named them to her, the families, the histories, who had worked there and whose mother had worked there and whose grandfather had hauled the bricks up from the barges when the building was raised in 1898.

"Bjornsen," he said. "His wife's people ran the smokehouse before there was a cannery at all. And the two Lind girls, there—their father drowned on the *Havørn* in '88 and the cannery kept their mother on out of decency though she could barely lift a knife by then. And that's old Mortensen, who I would not trust to find his own front door, but he can fillet a cod in four seconds and now there will be nothing for his hands to do." He was quiet a moment. "A town is a kind of water too. It has its currents and its depths. You learn to read it the same way. Who's sinking. Who's about to run aground. Where the bottom drops away."

"Can you save them?" she asked. "Like you saved the boats?"

He considered this with the seriousness he gave to all her questions, which was one of the things she would remember about him longest, that he had never once given her a child's answer.

"No," he said. "A pilot doesn't save the ship. The ship saves itself, if it listens. The pilot only says what the water is doing. That's all. You point at the blind water and you say, *there, that's where you'll die if you go,* and then it's the captain's hand on the wheel, not yours. Some of them listen. Some of them are proud, or stupid, or tired, and they don't." He nodded toward the workers in the lot. "I can't save them. Nobody can save a town that the water's left. But I can tell them the truth about where they are. That's not nothing. People drown most often because somebody lied to them about the depth."

The crowd in the lot thinned. The cannery's lights went off section by section, someone moving through the great brick building flipping the breakers, and the harbor that had been lit for so long went dark and the stars came out over it harder and closer than Hanna had ever seen them, as though the dark water were pulling them down.

In the last week of March, before her mother was to come north and the question of where Hanna would live was to be decided—a question Hanna had decided privately and absolutely, though no one had asked her—her grandfather woke her before dawn. The fjord was fogged in. She heard it before she saw it, the particular muffled quality of the world, the foghorn that had no keeper now and so did not sound, the silence where it should have been.

"Get dressed," he said. "Warm. This is the lesson I've been waiting to give you."

They walked down to the breakwater in the dark and the fog, and she could not see the water at all, could not see her own grandfather except as a darker shape in the gray, and she was afraid in a way she had not been afraid on any of the clear days. He led her out along the stones by memory, his hand on her shoulder, until they stood at the iron ladder at the breakwater's end.

"Now," he said. "Close your eyes."

"They're already closed. I can't see anything."

"Close them anyway. It helps you stop pretending you can see." She heard him settle, heard the old joints of him creak as he crouched. "Listen. Not for one thing. For all of it. Let it come."

She listened. At first there was only the confusion of it, the wash and slap of unseen water, the drip of the fog from the iron rail, her own blood loud in her ears. But she had learned, that winter, how to give herself to a thing, and she gave herself to the dark and the sound, and slowly it resolved, the way the water had resolved that first cold day into texture and current and seam.

There was the near sound, the small lapping of the harbor water against the breakwater stones, intimate and irregular. And beyond it a deeper sound, a long rhythmic breathing, the swell coming in through the heads and meeting the land—and within that, if she held very still, a change in the breathing, a place where the sound was harder, more broken, where the swell met something that fought it.

"The shoal," she said. "I can hear the shoal. The blind water. It sounds—angrier."

"Yes." His voice was very low. "Now the headland. The north shore. Listen past the shoal."

She listened past it, which she would not have known how to do a month before, sorting the near from the far the way you might pick a single instrument from an orchestra, and there it was, a different breaking, slower and heavier, the swell against the rocks of the northern point, and underneath it, almost imagined, a smell she had not noticed until he had taught her to notice it—the green resinous smell of the spruce on the northern shore, carried on the fog.

"I smell the trees," she said, and heard him exhale, something that might have been a laugh or only the cold.

"Then you'd bring her in," he said. "If you were piloting a ship in this fog and the engines failed and the chart was below and you had nothing—you'd bring her in. You'd keep the shoal on your port hand by its sound and you'd run for the smell of the spruce and you'd find the deep water between, and you'd come home. You could do it now. Today. You're twelve years old and you could bring a ship through this fjord in a fog, and there's not a man from the south with all his instruments who could do the same, because they've never once stood still and listened to where they are."

She opened her eyes. The fog was beginning to lift, the way it did at the turn of the tide, going luminous first and then thinning, and the gray water of the harbor showed itself, and the dark shape of the breakwater, and her grandfather crouched beside her with the fog beaded in his white eyebrows and on the wool of his cap, looking not at her but at the water, always at the water, as though it were the only thing in the world worth the trouble of looking at.

"There won't be ships," she said. "You told me. There won't be any ships to bring in."

"No."

"Then what's it for? What you taught me?"

He stood, slowly, his hand finding the iron rail. The fog lifted further and the heads of the fjord came clear, the two dark headlands, and beyond them the open sea going on to everywhere.

"It was never for the ships," he said. "I thought it was, for fifty years. I thought I was learning the water so I could bring the ships in. But the ships were just the reason. The water was the thing. You understand? The water was always the thing, and the learning of it, and the standing still long enough to be told the truth by something that has no reason to lie to you." He turned and looked at her, fully, the way he almost never did. "They'll close everything here. The cannery's gone and the rest will follow and your mother's right, there's nothing in Sorgvik for a girl your age, nothing they can put on a clipboard. But you'll carry the water out of here in you. You'll stand in some other place, some city, some life I can't see, and you'll know how to be still and read what's in front of you and not be lied to about the depth. That's what it was for. That's what it was always for." He took her hand, which he had not done since she was very small. "Now. Tide's turning. Tell me how you know."

She stood at the end of the breakwater with her grandfather's dry hard hand in hers and she looked out at the water going luminous in the lifting fog, and she found the place in the middle distance where the surface went briefly slack and confused, like a room full of people who have all stopped talking at once.

"There," she said, and pointed. "It's turning there."

"Yes," he said. "It is."

And they stood and watched it turn, the last of the fog burning off the water, the gulls beginning to call, the dead cannery dark and silent on its pilings behind them, and the fjord doing what it had done for nine generations and would go on doing after all of them, after the town, after the name, telling its single unwavering truth to anyone with the patience to stand still and learn to hear it.
