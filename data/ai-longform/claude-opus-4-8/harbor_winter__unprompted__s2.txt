The fog came in low that morning, the way it did most mornings now, sliding off the water like something poured, and Eelo Saarinen stood at the kitchen window watching it erase the breakwater. He had his coffee in the cup his wife had liked, the one with the chipped lip, and he drank from the unchipped side out of an old habit that meant nothing anymore. Behind him the girl was eating cereal too loudly, the spoon clinking, and he listened to that and to the foghorn out at the point and decided the day would clear by ten.

"You'll want your boots," he said without turning.

"It's not even raining."

"It will be wet anyway. The dock's always wet."

Inga was eleven and had been left with him for the winter, which was the polite way of saying it. Her mother — his daughter, Pirkko — had gone down to Seattle to find work and a place to live, and the place she found first was a friend's couch, which was no place for a child, and so the child had come north on the bus with a duffel bag and a library book she had not finished and would not, he suspected, finish, because she was not a finisher. He had recognized that in her the first day. She started things and put them down. He did not hold it against her. He had been the same at her age, before the water taught him otherwise.

She came to the dock in her boots, sulking a little, and he handed her the thermos and took the oars himself because she did not yet know how to feather them and he was not going to teach her in fog. The skiff smelled of bait and gasoline and the particular rot of old rope, and she wrinkled her nose at it the way she did every morning, and every morning he said nothing, because the nose would stop wrinkling eventually. The body learned to forgive a smell. That was one of the first things the water taught.

He rowed them out past the cannery, which stood on its pilings like a great gray animal gone down on its knees in the shallows. The windows were lit. They would be lit until April, and then they would not be lit again. Everyone in town knew it and nobody said it, the way you did not say a name in a sickroom. Forty years Eelo had watched the boats come in to that cannery with their holds silver and heavy, and now the boats were fewer and the holds were lighter and the company in its office somewhere far inland had done the arithmetic that the town had refused to do, and the arithmetic had come out against them.

"Why's it open if they're closing it," Inga said. She was trailing her fingers in the water, which he would have told her not to do if there had been any current to speak of.

"Because there's still fish for now. And men who need the wage."

"Dad worked there."

"He did. Before."

She did not ask before what, and he did not say. Her father was a subject like the cannery's closing, present in every room and spoken of in none. The man had gone south two years ahead of Pirkko and had not come back and had not, as far as Eelo knew, sent much of anything. He kept his opinion of the man behind his teeth where it had lived for two years and where it would die with him if he had any decency, which on the subject of his former son-in-law he was not always sure he did.

"Take your hand out of the water," he said. "You'll want it for the oars soon enough and it does you no good cold."

She took it out.

He shipped the oars when they were past the last of the moorings, out where the harbor opened toward the strait, and he let the skiff sit. The fog was thinning the way he'd known it would, going from milk to gauze, and through it the far shore was beginning to suggest itself, a darker gray laid over the lighter gray, not land yet but the rumor of land.

"All right," he said. "Look at the water. Tell me what you see."

She looked. He watched her look, which was the more interesting thing. She looked the way people looked who had not been taught, which was to say she looked at the surface as if the surface were all there was, as if the water were a floor and not a depth. Her eyes slid across it and found nothing because she did not know there was anything to find.

"Water," she said.

"What color."

"Gray. Like everything."

"It's not all the same gray. Look again. Out there past the skiff, that long streak — see how it's darker? Smoother?"

She squinted. "I guess."

"That's the current. The water moving through there is going faster than the rest, and faster water lies flatter. The little chop gets pulled out of it. So when you see a smooth lane in a rough field, that's where the water's running. You can read it from a mile off if you know to look. A pilot does. A pilot brings a ship up a channel he's never seen the bottom of, in fog, at night, and he does it by reading the top of the water like a page, because the top tells you the bottom. The water can't help telling. It's the most honest thing there is."

She was looking now. He saw the change come into her face, small, the way it came into everyone's, the first time they understood that the surface was a kind of writing. It was the only thing he had to give her, this. He was seventy-one and he had a house with a mortgage paid and a boat that leaked and a pension that the same arithmetic would come for soon enough, and the one thing he owned outright and could pass to a child was the knowledge of how to look at water. So he was teaching her. It seemed to him the last useful thing he might do.

"There," she said suddenly, pointing. "That's smooth too. Is that current?"

He looked where she pointed. She had found a slick out near the rocks, and it was not current, it was the wind shadow off the point, but she had found it by looking, by actually looking, and that was the whole of it.

"That's not current," he said. "That's the wind being blocked by the land. The land stands up and the wind goes over it and leaves a calm patch in its lee. But you saw it. You saw it was different. That's the thing. First you see that it's different. The names come after."

"How do you know which is which?"

"Forty years," he said. "And before that, my father showed me, out in a boat like this, in fog like this. And his father showed him in the old country, on different water, but water reads the same everywhere if you learn the grammar of it." He picked up the oars. "We'll go in. It's clearing and your hands are cold whatever you say about it."

She did not deny that her hands were cold. He counted it a small victory.

The cannery whistle blew as they came back past the pilings, the shift change, and men came out onto the gangway in their oilskins and stood smoking in the thin sun that was burning through now, and one of them lifted a hand to Eelo and Eelo lifted one back. That was Aarne Koski, who had piloted under him for six years and then gone to work on the dock when the piloting fell off, which it had, because the ships came less and the new ones had their machines that read the water for them, sonar and satellites and screens that drew the channel in colored lines so that any fool from anywhere could bring a hull up the strait without ever having looked at the surface in his life. Eelo did not begrudge them their machines. He only thought the machines did not love the water, and a thing you did not love you did not finally understand, and a thing you did not understand would someday, in some fog the machine had not predicted, kill you. But he kept that behind his teeth too. He was keeping a great deal behind his teeth these days. It was where most of what he knew had ended up living.

They walked up from the dock through the town, which was four streets and a church and a store and the cannery, and the houses going gray and the For Sale signs going up faster than the paint could go on. Inga walked beside him with her hands in her pockets and he let her be quiet. He was learning her quiets. There was the sulking quiet of the early mornings and there was the tired quiet of after a meal and there was this one, which was newer and which he thought might be the quiet of thinking, of turning something over, and he did not want to interrupt that one if that was what it was.

"Grandpa," she said. "If the cannery closes, what happens to the town?"

He had asked himself the same and had not answered himself either. "The town gets smaller," he said. "People go where the work is. It's an old story. Towns aren't promised forever. Nothing is."

"Will you have to go?"

"No," he said. "I'm too old to go. I'll stay and watch it get smaller. Somebody has to. It might as well be the ones who can't do anything else."

She frowned at that, and he thought he'd said it wrong, made it sound bitter when he hadn't meant bitter, he'd meant something more like loyal, but the words for that kind of loyalty were hard to find and he hadn't found them. They walked the rest of the way without talking, and at the house he made her cocoa because the cold had gotten into her despite the boots, and she drank it at the kitchen table with both hands around the mug, and he watched her and thought of Pirkko at that age, at that table, with her hands around a mug exactly so, and the thought went through him like cold water through a crack and he turned to the window so the girl would not see his face do whatever it was about to do.

The weeks went on the way winter weeks did up there, short and dark and wet, the daylight a brief gray concession between long nights, and most mornings they went out. Not all. On the bad days the strait stood up white and angry and he kept them ashore, and on those days he taught her on land, with the chart spread on the kitchen table, the soundings inked in their tiny numbers, the channel marked, the rocks named. She learned the names. Black Reef and the Sisters and the Old Man, which was a rock that showed at low water and hid at high and had taken more boats than all the others together because men forgot it was there when they couldn't see it. He taught her that the things that killed you were mostly the things you couldn't see and had forgotten to remember.

"Like the Old Man," she said.

"Like the Old Man."

She got good at the chart. She got good at the water too, faster than he'd expected, faster than Pirkko had, who had never quite cared enough, who had looked at the water and seen only the thing that had taken her grandfather's father — Eelo's grandfather had drowned, though Pirkko had never been told the half of it — and had wanted nothing to do with it. But Inga cared. That was the thing he hadn't dared hope for. She had come up north a child who put things down and she was becoming, out there in the skiff in the cold, a child who picked a thing up and held it. He didn't tell her she was changing. You didn't tell a person that while it was happening. You only watched and were glad and kept it behind your teeth with everything else.

One morning in February she read a tide rip before he did.

They were out past the point in a fair calm, the fog gone for once, the sun actually warm on the backs of their hands, and he was about to say something about the set of the current when she said, "There's something wrong out there," and pointed, and he looked, and a quarter mile off the water was standing in short steep peaks all in a line, a wall of small violence where a moment before there had been nothing, and he hadn't seen it coming and she had.

"That's the rip," he said. "The tide's turned and it's running against the wind. You don't want to be out in that in a boat this size. You read it. How'd you read it?"

"The color changed first," she said. "Before it stood up. It went a different gray right there. I saw the gray change and then it stood up."

He sat with the oars across his knees and looked at his granddaughter, who had read the water turning before it turned, who had seen the warning in the color the way he had learned to see it over years and years and could not have said how, and he felt something he had not felt in a long time, which he eventually recognized, with some surprise, as hope. Not for the town, which was past hoping for. Not for himself, who was past it too. For her. That she would carry this. That the thing did not die with him after all.

"That's right," he said, and his voice did something and he covered it by reaching for the thermos. "That's exactly right. The color goes first. It always goes first. Most men never learn to see that. You saw it the second time out."

"It's not that hard," she said, pleased and pretending not to be.

"It's the hardest thing there is," he said. "You just have the eye for it. Some do. Your great-great-grandfather had it. I have it. You have it. It skips around a family, the eye, it doesn't go straight down. It went around your mother and landed on you." He didn't say the rest, which was that the eye had not saved his grandfather, that having the eye and respecting the water were two different gifts and a man could have the first and lack the second and the water did not care, it took the gifted along with the rest, it was the most honest thing there was and honesty had no mercy in it. He didn't say that. She was eleven. There would be time for the rest, or there wouldn't, and either way it wasn't a thing for a warm morning with the sun on the water and the girl so pleased with herself she was nearly glowing.

They rowed in before the rip could come closer. He had her on the oars now, in the calm, and she feathered them clumsily but better than last month, and he sat in the stern and let her bring them home and corrected her only when she needed it, which was less than before.

In March the company sent a man up to walk the cannery floor with a clipboard, and the men knew what the clipboard meant, and that night there was drinking at the one bar that was left and some shouting that came to nothing, and in the morning Aarne Koski came to Eelo's house with his hat in his hands and asked, in the formal way of a proud man asking for help, whether Eelo knew anyone down south, anyone in the maritime trade, anyone who might have a place for a man who could do most things on a boat and on a dock and had a wife and two children and a mortgage that the cannery had been paying.

Eelo made him coffee and they sat and Eelo went through the names of everyone he could think of, and most of them were dead or retired or in towns as bad off as this one, but there were two who might do, a man in Anacortes who ran a ferry maintenance crew and a man in Bellingham who Eelo had piloted with long ago and who had moved into management and might owe him a favor or might pretend not to remember owing it. Eelo wrote both names on a piece of paper and added the second man's phone number, which he had kept all these years for no reason he could have named, and gave it to Aarne, and Aarne folded it small and put it in his shirt pocket and patted the pocket and said thank you, Eelo, in a voice that was trying not to do anything and not quite managing, and then he left, and Eelo stood in the doorway and watched him go down the gray street between the gray houses with the For Sale signs.

Inga had been in the next room and had heard. She came and stood beside him in the doorway.

"Is everybody going to leave?" she said.

"A lot of them. Yes."

"Are you sad?"

He thought about how to answer that honestly, because he had decided early that he would not lie to her, that the water did not lie and neither would he. "Yes," he said. "But it's the kind of sad that doesn't have anything to do. There's no rip to row away from. The town's just running out the way a tide runs out, and you can't row against a tide that big. You let it go and you watch it and you remember what it looked like when it was full. That's all there is to do with some kinds of sad."

She leaned against the doorframe and against him a little, which she had not done before, and he stood very still so as not to frighten the moment off, the way you stood still when a wild thing came close, and they watched Aarne Koski reach the corner and turn and disappear, and the street was empty after that, and the fog was coming in again off the water, low, the way it did, sliding off the surface like something poured.

"Will you teach me the rest of it?" she said. "Before I have to go back?"

"There's no rest of it," he said. "There's just more of the same thing, deeper. You learn it your whole life and you don't finish. Nobody finishes."

"I don't finish things," she said. "Mom says."

"You'll finish this," he said. "Or rather you won't, but you won't put it down either. There's a difference. Some things you carry exactly because they don't end. This is one of those."

She seemed to think about that. The fog reached the bottom of the street and began to climb it, taking the houses one by one, and Eelo thought that in a few years there would be fewer houses for it to take, and fewer still after that, and someday the fog would come up an empty street and there would be no one to watch it, and the cannery would be a black ruin on its pilings and the channel would be unmarked and forgotten and the Old Man would lie out there at half tide waiting for a boat that no longer came. And it grieved him, all of it, in the way that had nothing to do, the way of a tide running out. But the girl was leaning against him and the girl had the eye, and the eye would go south with her in her duffel bag along with the unfinished library book, and someday she would stand at some other water, warmer water, crowded water full of pleasure boats and machines that read the channel in colored lines, and she would look at it and see the color change before it stood up, and she would not know why she could do that when no one around her could, and then maybe she would remember an old man and a leaking skiff and a cold gray winter and the smell of bait she had stopped noticing, and she would know why.

"Tomorrow if it's calm we'll go out," he said. "I'll show you how to take a bearing off two points so you can find your way home in fog. That's the next thing."

"Off two points," she repeated, filing it.

"Off two points. You line them up and you keep them lined up and the water can do whatever it wants but you'll know where you are." He put his hand on her head, briefly, the way his father had done to him, and then took it away before either of them could make anything of it. "Go in. Your hands are cold."

"They're not."

"They are. They're always cold. You've got the eye but you haven't got the sense yet to stay warm. The sense comes later. Go in."

She went in. He stayed in the doorway a while longer, watching the fog take the last of the street, and he finished his coffee from the unchipped side of the cup out of the old habit that meant nothing anymore, except that now, with the girl's warmth still against his side and gone, it meant a little something again, the way a thing you had set down and forgotten could mean something again when you picked it back up and found it had been waiting for you the whole time, patient, the way the water was patient, the way the Old Man was patient out there under the gray, and he stood and drank and watched until there was nothing left to see, and then he went in too, where it was warm, where the girl was.
