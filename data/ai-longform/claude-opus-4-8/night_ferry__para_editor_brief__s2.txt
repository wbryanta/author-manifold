The ferry left at eleven, which meant Oddvar had the whole of the evening to fill in the town before boarding, and he filled it badly. He walked from the bus station down to the harbor and back twice, looking at the same shuttered fish market, the same chandlery with its window of orange rope and brass fittings, the same café where two old men sat over coffee that must by now have gone cold. He bought a newspaper he did not read. He ate a hot dog standing up, the bun gone soft and sweet in his mouth, and felt obscurely ashamed of eating at all, as though the appetite were a betrayal of the errand.

The errand. He kept reaching for other words for it and finding none that fit. Identification was the official term, the word the woman on the telephone had used, a constable or some clerk attached to the constabulary, her voice careful and flattened by long practice in delivering such things. A body had been recovered. There was reason to believe. The next of kin. She had said next of kin and then waited, and into that waiting Oddvar had said nothing for so long that she asked if he was still there.

He was still there. He was still here. That was the trouble with him; he was always still there, long after the moment had moved on without him.

At the quay the ferry sat lit up against the dark water, white and absurdly large, a floating apartment block, and the gangway swallowed a slow line of foot passengers and a few cars whose headlights swung across the wet asphalt. He showed his ticket. A young man in a reflective vest glanced at it and waved him on without interest, and Oddvar climbed the metal stairs and found himself in a carpeted interior that smelled of coffee and disinfectant and the particular plasticky warmth of enclosed places at sea.

He had booked a cabin. This had seemed extravagant when he did it, a small cruelty toward his own thrift, but now he was glad of it, because the lounges were full of people and he did not think he could sit among people. He found the cabin on the lower of the two cabin decks, a narrow cell with two bunks, a folded towel on each, a round window black with night. He sat on the lower bunk and the ship trembled under him, and then there was the low working sound of the engines and the faint shifting of the floor that told him they had cast off.

He had not seen Hallvard in nineteen years.

It was a number he had carried so long it had stopped meaning anything, the way a word repeated comes apart into sounds. Nineteen years. He could account for them. He had married in the third of them and divorced in the eleventh; he had buried their mother in the seventh and their father in the fourteenth, and at neither funeral had Hallvard appeared, though word had been sent, word had been sent both times to the last address anyone had, a fish-processing town on the far coast where Hallvard had gone after the trouble and from which he wrote, in the early years, postcards of a deliberate and wounding blandness. Weather here is fine. Work is steady. Hope all is well. As if all could ever be well. As if the postcards were not themselves a kind of knife held to the throat of the family's grief, look how little I will say, look how I have made myself into a man who sends weather reports.

Then even the postcards stopped.

Oddvar lay back on the bunk without undressing and looked at the underside of the bunk above him, the wire mesh and the thin mattress sagging into it, and he tried to summon his brother's face and could not. He could summon pieces. The cowlick at the crown that no water would flatten. The chipped tooth, the front one, from a fall off the breakwater when they were boys, a fall Oddvar had caused, or had he, the memory had been handled so often its surface had worn smooth and he no longer trusted it. He could remember Hallvard's hands more clearly than his face, the wide flat thumbnails, the way he held a cigarette cupped against the wind even indoors, a habit from the boats. He could remember his voice, or believed he could, a low voice that went quiet rather than loud when its owner was angry, so that you had to lean in to be insulted.

But the face would not assemble. And this frightened Oddvar, lying in the trembling dark, because tomorrow he would be asked to look at a face and say whether it was that one, and how could he, when he could not even now, with all the will in the world, see it?

He must have slept, because he came up out of something thick and was for a moment unsure where the floor was. The window had gone from black to a deep charcoal that meant either the smallest beginning of dawn or only the lights of some passing vessel. The engines were the same. The ship rolled, gently, a long slow roll that came and went, and he understood they were in open water now, the strait proper, the place where the two seas met and quarreled and where, he had read once, the old people had believed a creature lived that took ships down for sport.

He got up and washed his face in the little steel basin and went out and up to find coffee.

The cafeteria was nearly empty at this hour. A girl behind the counter, very young, with the white headphones of someone half elsewhere, sold him a coffee and a wrapped pastry and gave him his change without looking at him, and he was grateful for it. He took the coffee to a table by the window though there was nothing to see but his own reflection laid over the dark and, far out, three or four lights that were perhaps fishing boats or perhaps the coast they were leaving or the coast they were going toward, he had lost his sense of direction entirely.

A man was sitting two tables away. Oddvar had not noticed him come in, or perhaps he had been there all along. He was older than Oddvar, seventy at least, with a face like a dropped apple, brown and soft and folded, and he was drinking something from a thermos cup, not the cafeteria's coffee, his own. He caught Oddvar looking and nodded, the unhurried nod of a man for whom a night crossing held no novelty.

"Going over or coming back," the old man said. It was not quite a question.

"Going over."

"Mm." The old man drank. "Bad time of year for it."

Oddvar did not know whether he meant the weather or something larger. He said nothing, and the old man seemed content with that, and for a while they sat in the rolling quiet with their two cups and the girl's faint tinny music leaking from her headphones.

"I do it twice a month," the old man said eventually. "My daughter's over there. Was over there. She's there still but she doesn't—" He made a gesture with one hand, a small turning-away. "There was a thing between us. A long time ago. Now I go over and I sit in a café near where she lives and sometimes I see her go past, and she doesn't see me, or she does and pretends not to, and then I take the night boat back. Twice a month. Eleven years."

Oddvar looked at him. "Why don't you speak to her?"

The old man considered this as though it were genuinely new to him, as though in eleven years it had not occurred to him.

"Ah," he said. "If I speak to her, then there's an answer. While I don't speak to her, there's no answer yet. You understand? The answer might be no. As long as I don't ask, the answer is still—" He turned the empty cup in his hands. "Still possible to be yes."

"That's a coward's reasoning," Oddvar said, and was surprised at himself, at the cruelty of it, but the old man only laughed, a wet pleased laugh.

"It is, yes. It's exactly that." He screwed the cup back onto his thermos. "But you'll find out, young man, that a coward's reasoning is most of what gets a person from one end of a life to the other. The brave ones — the ones who go and ask — they get their answer and then what have they got? An answer. A door shut. I'd rather have the corridor." He stood, steadying himself on the table edge against the roll of the ship. "The corridor's a sad place but you can walk up and down it as long as you like."

He went out, and Oddvar sat with the cold coffee and thought, you fool, you have just been talking to yourself.

Because that was it exactly, wasn't it. He had not gone to find Hallvard in nineteen years. Word had been sent at the two funerals, but it was others who had sent it; he himself had not picked up a pen, had not picked up a telephone. He had told himself it was because Hallvard had made his choice, had cut the rope and let the boat drift, and that a man was entitled to be left alone if leaving him alone was what he had so plainly demanded. But that was the coward's reasoning the old man had named. As long as Oddvar did not go to him, there was no final scene, no confirmed estrangement, only the open question, the corridor. Hallvard might be living a life out there in which he thought fondly, even, of his brother. Or he might not. As long as Oddvar didn't ask, he didn't have to know.

And now the asking had been taken out of his hands. Now there was a body, and an answer was waiting at the end of the crossing, and it was the worst answer, the door shut so hard the frame had splintered.

He went back down to the cabin but did not lie down. He sat on the bunk and took out his wallet and found, behind the bank cards, a photograph he had carried so long that the emulsion had cracked into a fine net of lines, like the glaze on old porcelain. Two boys on a slipway. The taller one — himself, he was the taller until they were fifteen and then Hallvard shot past him — squinting into the sun with his arm slung over the smaller one's shoulders, both of them in the shapeless wool sweaters their grandmother knitted, both of them barefoot, a dog out of focus at the edge of the frame, a dog whose name he could not now recall and whose forgetting struck him suddenly as the saddest thing of all, sadder almost than the brother, because the dog had been wholly his to love and had loved him back without complication and he had let even that go.

He looked at the smaller boy's face. There it was. The face. Round and open and laughing, the chipped tooth not yet chipped, the cowlick standing up at the back. He looked at it for a long time and tried to age it nineteen years, thirty-odd years, tried to put weather and drink and labor and the unknown life into it, and could not, and was afraid again, because the face on the slipway and the face that would be shown to him under a sheet or behind glass tomorrow would have nothing to do with each other, and he would stand there and be asked, and he would not know.

He had quarreled with Hallvard, at the end, about money. That was the unbearable smallness of it. It had not been about the girl, though everyone afterward said it was about the girl, and it was true the girl was in it somewhere, the girl Hallvard had got into trouble and would not stand by, or could not, the difference between would not and could not being the whole of the matter and never settled. But the actual quarrel, the words that had been the last words, had been about money. A sum Hallvard owed and Oddvar had paid on his behalf, to a man in the town it was unwise to owe, and then Oddvar had asked for it back, not because he needed it but because — he saw this now, he had not seen it then — because he wanted the leverage of being owed, wanted to be the brother who had saved the other brother, wanted Hallvard small and grateful and beholden. And Hallvard, who could read Oddvar as easily as he read weather, had seen exactly that, and had thrown the money on the table, more than the sum, every note he had, thrown it down with that quiet voice gone quieter still, take it, take all of it, I'd rather owe the devil than owe you, and walked out, and out of the town, and out of the family, and out of nineteen years.

Take all of it. I'd rather owe the devil than owe you.

Oddvar put the photograph away. The roll of the ship had eased; they must be coming under the lee of the far coast. He could not stay in the cabin. He went up again, past the cafeteria where the girl now slept with her head on her folded arms, up and out through a heavy door onto the open deck, into a wind that took his breath and then gave it back colder.

It was almost light. Not light yet but the promise of it, the sky over what he now understood to be the bow gone the gray of a knife blade, and against that gray the land rising, low and black and serrated with the shapes of mountains. A few lights along the shore. A lighthouse, very far off, turning. The smell of the sea was different here, weedier, closer, the smell of a coast rather than open water.

He gripped the rail with both hands. The metal was wet and freezing and he held it anyway, held it until his hands ached, as though the pain were a way of being present, of refusing for once to be the man who was always still there after the moment had passed. He was arriving. In an hour, less, the ferry would dock and he would walk down the gangway into a town he had never seen, and someone would meet him, the constable perhaps, the careful flattened voice given a body, and drive him to a building with a particular smell, and there it would happen, the thing he could not imagine and could not stop imagining.

He thought: it might not be him.

This was the corridor the old man had described, and Oddvar walked up and down it now in the freezing wind. The woman on the telephone had said reason to believe. Not certainty. Belief. There had been papers on the body, or a name known to someone, or some such thread, frayed and uncertain, that led to a town on the far coast and a brother's name and a brother's number. It might not be him. The body might be a stranger's, some other lost man with the same name or no name, and Oddvar would stand in that room and look down and feel the great clean wave of relief, it isn't him, it's nothing to do with me, and he would be free to go home and resume the long not-knowing, the corridor, the brother possibly alive somewhere thinking fondly, possibly, of the slipway and the dog.

And he understood, gripping the rail, that he wanted this. That some part of him was praying for the body to be a stranger's, not out of love for his brother but out of the same cowardice that had kept him home for nineteen years, the desire to keep the door open, the answer not yet given, the corridor with its sad endless pacing room.

And he understood, too, in the same breath, that this was monstrous. Because if the body was a stranger's, then Hallvard was still out there with the quarrel unhealed, the last words still the last words, take all of it, I'd rather owe the devil than owe you, and Oddvar would go home and do nothing, again, would let the relief talk him out of the search the way the fear had talked him out of it before, and the corridor would be no kinder for being longer. Whereas if the body was Hallvard's — and here Oddvar made himself look at it, made himself stand in the cold and look at the worst answer full in the face — if it was Hallvard's, then the door was shut, yes, the corridor ended in a wall, but at least he would have to turn and look at the wall. At least there would be no more pretending the corridor went on forever. At least he would have to grieve, which was a thing he had never let himself do because you cannot grieve a man who might be alive, you can only wait, and waiting is grief's coward cousin, grief with the courage taken out.

He wanted his brother dead so that he could keep not looking for him. He wanted his brother alive so that he could keep not finding him. There was no version of his wanting that did Hallvard any good at all, and he saw this clearly in the gray light, saw the full mean shape of his own heart, and it was not a thing he could have borne to see in any softer light or at any later hour.

The land was closer now. He could make out the breakwater, a harbor arm of piled stone reaching out, and behind it the town climbing the hillside in tiers of small houses, white and ochre and the dull red of old paint, smoke or steam rising from one or two chimneys into the windless dawn. A working town. A fish town. Like the town they had grown up in, like all the towns of that coast, and Oddvar felt the strangest thing then, looking at it: a kind of homesickness, though he had never been here, homesickness not for a place but for a life, the life in which he had not asked for the money back, the life in which the quarrel had been about something that could be apologized for, the life in which he and his brother were two old men now who met for coffee and complained about their knees and remembered the dog's name, which was — and it came to him then, all at once, undammed by some loosening in his chest — the dog's name was Bjørn. Bjørn. A foolish name for a dog, bear, a small brown dog called bear, and Hallvard had named it, Hallvard at six insisting on the grandness of it, and they had laughed at him and he had not cared, he had stood his ground, this dog is Bjørn, and the dog had answered to it, had come bounding up the slipway at the sound of it, and Oddvar stood at the rail of the ferry with the tears coming hot and sudden down his cold face and the name in his mouth, Bjørn, Bjørn, as though if he called it the dog might still come, as though if he called it loud enough his brother might still be six and the money still unpaid and the whole ruinous machinery of their lives still uncranked, still possible to leave alone.

The ferry sounded its horn, a great flat bellow that rolled across the water and came back off the hillside, and the few gulls that had been riding the air above the harbor scattered and cried. Somewhere below him the engines changed their note, slowing, and the gap of black water between the ship and the breakwater began, very slowly, to close.

He wiped his face with the back of his freezing hand. He did not know yet what he would say in the room, or what the face would look like, or whether he would know it. He did not know whether he was hoping or dreading or both at once, twined so tightly he could no longer pull the threads apart. But he knew, watching the town come on, that he had crossed something more than the strait in the night, that the old man with the thermos had been a kind of toll-keeper and had taken his coin, and that whatever waited in the building with the particular smell, Oddvar would not, this time, be the man who was still there after the moment had passed. He would walk down the gangway. He would get into the car. He would go to the room and he would look, and he would say what was true, whatever it was, even if the truth was that he did not know, even if the truth was that he had thrown away the years in which he might have known.

The gangway clanged down. A man in a yellow jacket stood at the bottom of it on the wet stone of the quay, looking up, and though he was too far yet to read, something in the set of him, the official patience of him, told Oddvar this was the one come to meet him. He picked up his small bag. He went down through the warm interior, past the sleeping girl, down the metal stairs, and out into the gray morning and the smell of weed and salt and fish, and the man in the yellow jacket said his name, made it a question, and Oddvar said yes, that's me, and held out his hand, and the man took it, and the day began.
