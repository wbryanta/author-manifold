The trench was Caleb's idea, the way most digging was Caleb's idea, and Daniel followed him down the row with the second shovel because that was the arrangement they had fallen back into without either of them naming it. The morning was already warm. By eight the light came off the irrigation pipe in a hard silver line, and the apricot leaves hung still and dusty, and somewhere up the slope a quail was making its three-note complaint over and over to no one.

"Here," Caleb said. He stood over the place where the water came up wrong, a dark seep that spread out under the trees and never reached the far end of the orchard. He toed the wet ground. "Somewhere under here. We follow the pipe till we find the break."

"We could call Reyes."

"Reyes charges ninety an hour and then he tells you what you already knew." Caleb pushed his shovel into the soft ground with his boot and turned up a black wedge of earth that smelled of iron and rot. "We know where the water's going. It's going right here into the dirt. We just have to find the hole."

Daniel set his shovel beside Caleb's line and started digging. The two of them had dug a thousand feet of trench in this orchard as boys, for their father, who paid them a dollar a day and a sandwich and the right to swim in the canal after, and their bodies still knew the rhythm even though Daniel's body now belonged to a man who sat at a desk in Sacramento and Caleb's belonged to a man who had stayed. You could see it in their hands. Caleb's were a working man's hands, split at the knuckles, the nails rimmed permanently dark. Daniel's were soft and he was aware of them in a way he had not been aware of his hands in years.

They dug without talking for a while. The trench grew along the pipe, and the pipe was older than either of them, galvanized steel gone scaly with rust, patched in places with rubber and hose clamps where their father had patched it, the patches themselves now old.

"You're going to bring it up," Caleb said.

"I wasn't."

"You came up here to bring it up. You drove four hours."

"I came up because you said the trees were dying."

"They're not dying. They're thirsty. There's a difference." Caleb straightened and pressed the back of his wrist to his forehead. He was thirty-eight, two years older than Daniel, and he had their father's heaviness coming into him, the thick neck, the way he planted his feet. "You want to talk, talk. Don't make me feel you circling all day."

So Daniel said it. "Voss came to me again. With a number."

"I know what Voss does. Voss comes to me too. I run him off the property."

"It's a good number, Caleb."

"It's always a good number when you don't want the thing it's buying."

Daniel said nothing. He bent and pulled a rock from the wall of the trench and tossed it up onto the bank. The quail had stopped. Now there was only the sound of the shovels and, far off, the steady push of the highway, which you could not see from here but which had crept closer over the years, the new houses coming up the valley in their beige ranks, each with its little tree staked against the wind.

"How much," Caleb said.

"You don't want to know."

"How much."

Daniel told him.

Caleb laughed, a short sound with no pleasure in it, and bent back to the trench. "That's a lot of money," he said. "That's pay-off-the-house money. That's send-the-kids-anywhere money." He shook his head. "And it would feel like the worst money I ever made for about a week, and then I'd have to live the rest of my life with it."

"You don't know how it would feel."

"I know exactly how it would feel."

They worked. The seep had gotten worse where they were digging now, and the bottom of the trench filled with brown water that they had to bail with a coffee can Caleb had brought, and which slowed everything down. The mud was the heavy kind, clay, the kind that stuck to the shovel and had to be knocked off with every throw. Daniel's shirt was stuck to his back. He thought about the air conditioning in his office, the cool dry hum of it, and was ashamed of the thought and kept digging.

"How's Marie," Daniel asked.

"She's fine. She'd like to see you. You should come for dinner before you drive back, the kids ask about you." Caleb wiped his face on his shoulder. "She thinks we should sell."

This surprised Daniel enough that he stopped. "She told you that?"

"She didn't have to tell me. I can do the books same as her. She does them, actually, she does them better than me, that's how I know she knows." Caleb threw a shovelful up onto the bank. "We're not making it. You want to know the truth, that's the truth. Apricots don't pay. The cannery that used to take the seconds closed four years ago, I told you that. We sell at the farmers market and to two restaurants in the city and a guy who makes jam, and it doesn't cover the water, let alone everything else." He said it flatly, like a man reading a list. "Last year I drove a delivery route November to March. That's what kept us. Driving other people's loads around at night."

"You never told me that."

"What were you going to do about it."

"I'd have helped, Caleb. You know I'd have helped."

"I don't want your help. I want—" Caleb stopped. He stood in the trench with the water around his boots and looked up the rows of trees, and Daniel followed his gaze and tried to see what his brother saw. Daniel saw old apricots, some of them blighted, the bark split, dead wood in the crowns that should have been pruned out years ago and hadn't been because there was no one to do it. Caleb saw something else. Daniel had always known his brother saw something else, and had never been able to see it himself, and that failure was the oldest thing between them, older than the money, older than Voss.

"Found it," Caleb said.

He had hit the break with his shovel, a soft give that was different from the dirt. They cleared around it with their hands, and there it was, the pipe split along a seam of rust for the length of a hand, the water pulsing out of it in a low steady welling. Caleb crouched over it like a man over a wounded animal.

"Look at that," he said. "She just let go. Right along the weld." He put his thumb against it and the water came up dark between his knuckles. "Dad welded this. This is one of his."

"How do you know."

"Because I held the light for him. Summer I was twelve. He was on his back in a hole just like this one and I held the light and he kept telling me to hold it still and I couldn't hold it still because my arm was shaking." Caleb almost smiled. "He patched it then and it held twenty-six years. That's a good patch. Twenty-six years."

They needed to shut the water and they needed a coupling, and they had neither, so Caleb walked up to the pump house to close the valve and Daniel stayed in the trench bailing so they could see what they were working with. He bailed and the water came up almost as fast as he could throw it, and he thought about his father on his back in the dark with the rust raining down on his face, and his brother's arm shaking with the weight of the light, and how that was a thing he had not been there for, because by twelve he had already been the one who read in the house while the other two went out, and no one had made him come, and he had been grateful at the time and was not grateful now.

The water slowed and then stopped. Caleb came back down the row.

"We need a coupling," Daniel said.

"We need a lot of things." Caleb dropped down into the trench beside him, the two of them close now in the narrow cut, hip to hip, and there was something in being down in the ground together that loosened him, the way certain confessions can only be made side by side and not face to face. "I'll go into town. Henley's open till noon Saturdays. You can stay here or come."

"I'll come."

"Suit yourself."

But neither of them got up. They sat on the lip of the trench with their feet in the mud and the broken pipe between them, and Caleb took out a pack of cigarettes Daniel didn't know he carried and lit one and offered the pack, and Daniel took one too, though he hadn't smoked since college, and they sat and smoked in the orchard their grandfather had planted.

"You remember the freeze," Caleb said.

"Which freeze."

"The bad one. Eighty-nine. We were little." Caleb pulled on the cigarette. "Dad had us out here at two in the morning lighting the smudge pots. Whole orchard full of fire. You remember that?"

"I remember the smoke. I remember it got in everything."

"He lost half the crop anyway. But he saved the other half because he was out here all night moving between the pots, and we were running the buckets of oil, and Mom brought coffee out at like four in the morning in the cold." Caleb shook his head slowly. "I think about that night a lot. I don't know why. We were freezing. It was miserable. And I'd give about anything to be back in it."

"That's just the way memory works," Daniel said gently. "It picks the cold night because you survived the cold night. It edits out everything else."

"Don't do that."

"Do what."

"Explain my own feeling back to me like it's a thing you read about." Caleb said it without heat but it landed, and Daniel felt the old shape of himself, the one who had words for everything and the weight of nothing, and he looked down at his soft hands holding the cigarette wrong.

"Sorry," he said.

"It's all right." Caleb flicked ash into the trench. "You always did that. Even when we were kids. Something would happen and I'd be in the middle of it, just in it, and you'd be standing there already telling the story of it. Like it had already happened to somebody else and you were just reporting." He said it almost fondly. "I used to hate it. I don't anymore. I figured out it's just how you stand it. Same as I stand it by being in the dirt. We just stand it different."

Daniel didn't trust himself to answer that, so he didn't. The smoke rose up straight in the windless air. Down the slope a tractor started somewhere on a neighbor's land, ran for a minute, and quit.

"I'm not trying to take it from you," Daniel said finally. "That's what you think. You think I drove up here to take it."

"Didn't you."

"I drove up here because I'm tired of being the one who carries Voss's number around like a secret. Because every time he calls I feel like a thief, and I haven't even done anything. And because—" Daniel stopped. "Because you're driving a truck at night and not telling me. Because you're going to work yourself into the ground for a thing that's already losing, and I'm going to be in Sacramento finding out about it after."

"It's not a thing that's losing. It's a thing that's hard. Those aren't the same."

"For you they're not."

"For anybody they're not." Caleb stubbed the cigarette against his boot heel and field-stripped it, the way their father had, and put the filter in his shirt pocket. "You think I don't know the numbers. I know them better than you, I just weigh them different. You put the land on one side and the money on the other and the money's heavier, so you sell. I put them on the same scale and they don't go on the same scale. The land's not a number. It's where Dad is."

"Dad's in the cemetery in Linden."

"You know what I mean."

Daniel did know what he meant. That was the trouble. He knew exactly what his brother meant and he could not make himself feel it, and the not-feeling was a kind of poverty he had carried so long he'd stopped noticing it, the way you stop hearing a sound that never stops. He had a good apartment with good light and a woman he might marry and a job that mattered to people he would never meet, and none of it was attached to any ground, and when his father had died he had flown up for the weekend and flown back, and had felt the largest part of his grief not at the grave but three weeks later, in a meeting, when someone said the word orchard about something else entirely, and he'd had to leave the room.

"Let me ask you something," Daniel said. "If I sign my half over to you. Right now. Today. So it's all yours. Would you keep it?"

Caleb was quiet.

"That's what I thought."

"Don't put it on me like that."

"I'm not putting anything on you. I'm asking. If it were all yours and the decision was only yours, would you keep going? Driving the truck, losing the crop, Marie doing the books at midnight to figure out how short you are. Would you do that for another ten years."

Caleb looked at the broken pipe. He picked up a clod of clay and worked it in his fingers until it crumbled. "I don't know," he said, and it cost him to say it, Daniel could see that it cost him. "Some days yes. Some days I'm up at four because I can't sleep for the worry and I think, just be done with it. Take the number. Buy Marie a house that doesn't have a bad roof. Sleep." He let the crumbs fall. "And then the sun comes up over the ridge and hits the trees and I think, you would sell *this*. You'd take a check for *this*. And I'm ashamed I ever thought about the roof."

"That's not shame," Daniel said. "That's two true things at once."

Caleb looked at him. "Now you're doing it the right way."

"Doing what."

"The word thing. Sometimes it's the right word." He almost smiled again. "Two true things at once. Yeah. That's it. That's the whole thing, right there."

They climbed out of the trench. Their legs were stiff and Daniel's back had set in the bent position and he straightened it slow, like an old man, and Caleb saw and didn't say anything, which was its own kindness. They walked up the rows toward the truck, past the old trees, and Caleb stopped at one and reached up into the crown and took down an apricot and split it with his thumbs and gave Daniel half.

It was warm from the sun and almost too ripe, and it was the best thing Daniel had tasted in a year. He stood in the dust and ate it and the juice ran down his wrist and he understood, for just a moment, the whole argument his brother had been making with his life, the argument that could not be made in words because words were Daniel's country and not Caleb's, and the moment he tried to put it into words it thinned out and was gone, and he was left only with the taste and the heat and the dead tree behind them that would not bear next year.

"That's a Blenheim," Caleb said. "You can't buy these. They bruise if you look at them. That's why the stores don't carry them, they ship like garbage. The only way to eat a Blenheim is to stand under the tree." He ate his half. "You sell this land, they pull these out in a week. Whole orchard. Bulldozer. They put in those houses and every house gets one tree, some ornamental thing that doesn't fruit, and that's the valley now. That's what we're choosing between. A hundred trees that feed people and one decision, or a thousand trees that feed nobody and a thousand decisions made by people who'll never know what was here."

"I know," Daniel said.

"Do you, though."

"I just ate it, Caleb. Yes. I know."

Caleb looked at him a moment longer and then nodded, like something had been settled that hadn't been settled, only acknowledged, which was maybe all that was available to them.

They drove into town with the windows down. Henley's was a hardware store that had been a hardware store for sixty years and smelled of fertilizer and cut keys and the rubber of new hoses, and the man behind the counter knew Caleb and called him by name and asked after Marie and the kids, and Caleb asked after the man's mother, who was in the home now in Stockton, and the whole exchange took four minutes and would have taken Daniel an hour to learn the steps of. He stood by a bin of bolts and watched his brother be known. That was the thing the money couldn't buy and the thing Daniel had traded away without understanding the price: to walk into a building and be known, to be a man whose father people remembered, whose trench they could picture, whose loss they could feel in their own hands.

They got the coupling and two hose clamps and a tube of pipe sealant and a new section of pipe in case the rust had spread, and on the way out Caleb stopped and bought two cold cans of soda from the machine by the door and gave one to Daniel without asking if he wanted it, and they drank them in the truck in the parking lot before they drove back, not talking, the cans sweating in their hands.

"I'm not going to push you," Daniel said. "On the land."

"I know."

"I mean it. I'll tell Voss to stop calling. Both of us. I'll handle it."

Caleb turned the can in his hands. "And in two years when I can't make the water payment? When the well goes, and a new well's forty thousand dollars I don't have?"

"Then we figure it out then. Together. You tell me when it's bad. You don't drive a truck at night and not tell me." Daniel looked at his brother. "That's the deal. I'll stop bringing the number if you stop hiding the hole. Both of us quit pretending."

Caleb was quiet a long time. Then he put out his hand, his split, dark-knuckled hand, and Daniel took it, and they shook on it there in the cab of the truck in the Henley's parking lot, two grown men shaking hands over a thing their father would have understood without any handshake at all.

They drove back and the day had reached its full heat. They climbed down into the trench again and Caleb cut out the broken section with a hacksaw, working it back and forth with his whole body, and Daniel held the pipe steady, and this time it was Daniel who held it and Caleb who worked, and Daniel thought of the boy holding the light with his arm shaking, and held the pipe as still as he could. The cut piece came free and Caleb handed it up to him and Daniel laid it on the bank, the failed weld and all, twenty-six years of holding and then letting go.

They fitted the new section and threaded the coupling and Caleb worked the sealant into the joint with his thumb and they set the clamps and tightened them down, and it was good work, careful and slow, and there was no arguing in it because the work didn't permit any. Then Caleb went up to the pump house and opened the valve, and Daniel stood over the joint and watched, and for a moment nothing, and then the pipe shuddered and filled and the joint held. No seep. No dark welling in the clay. He put his hand on it and felt the water moving through, cold and fast and going somewhere, all the way down the rows to the far trees that hadn't had a real drink in weeks.

"It's holding," he called up the slope.

He couldn't see his brother from the trench, only hear him, the pump house door banging, his boots on the dry ground coming back. The water ran under Daniel's hand. Up the rows the leaves were the same dusty leaves they'd been all morning, the trees still old, still half-blighted, the orchard still losing in every way that could be counted on a page. None of that had changed. They had fixed one break in one old line, and there would be another break, further down, next month or next year, and a day would come when there were more breaks than the two of them could fix, and they both knew it, and they had shaken hands on facing it together when it came.

But the water was moving now. That was the thing he could feel under his palm, the only thing, the cold pulse of it going down to the far trees, and he kept his hand there and waited for his brother, and decided not to find the words for it. For once he let it be only what it was.
