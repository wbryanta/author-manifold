The ferry left at eleven, which meant Aldous had the whole afternoon to fill in a town that had nothing for him. He walked the harbor twice. He bought a coffee he did not want from a kiosk near the terminal and drank it standing up, watching the gulls work the seam where the tide turned over against the breakwater. The woman at the kiosk had asked if he was traveling for business or pleasure, and he had said neither, and she had not asked anything else, which he was grateful for, because he did not know how he would have finished the sentence.

The call had come on Tuesday. A man named Heikkonen, very correct in his English, with a voice that suggested he had made this kind of call before and disliked it as much each time. There was a body. It had come up in the nets off Sundholm, which Aldous had to look up later on a map, a smear of rock in the strait that did not seem large enough to have a name. Male, the right age, give or take. There had been water damage—Heikkonen used that phrase, water damage, as if describing a ceiling—and the face was not reliable. But there was a wallet in a sealed inner pocket, and in the wallet a card, and on the card a name, and behind the name a brother. Him. Would he be willing to come.

He had said yes before he had decided anything. The yes had come out of him the way a reflex does, the knee jumping under the doctor's little hammer, and afterward he had sat in his flat in the grey light and thought: I have not seen Pell in eleven years. I would not know his face across a room. And now they want me to know it underwater.

The ferry, when they finally let them board, was larger than he expected and emptier. November was not a season anyone crossed for pleasure. He found his cabin, a narrow berth with a porthole the color of slate, and he sat on the bunk and listened to the engines take up their long shuddering note, and he felt the land let go of the hull. There is a moment, leaving a harbor, when the movement is all sideways and uncertain, the ship feeling for the channel, and then it finds the open water and the motion changes, becomes a kind of breathing, a rise and settle that his body remembered from somewhere he could not place.

He had not brought much. A change of shirt. The letter from the coroner's office, folded into quarters, soft now at the creases from his handling it. A photograph he had almost not packed and then had, at the last moment, slid into the back of his passport. It was the only one he had of the two of them together as men, taken at their father's funeral, of all places, the two of them standing apart from each other in dark coats with the church behind, neither smiling, a careful foot of cold air between their shoulders. Pell had his hands in his pockets. Aldous remembered that the photographer was a cousin neither of them liked, and that the only reason they stood together at all was that the cousin had insisted, had herded them with little flapping motions of her hands, and that the moment the shutter went they had stepped apart as if released from a clasp.

He took the photograph out now and looked at it under the cabin's weak light. Eleven years. Pell would be forty-six. The man in the photograph was thirty-five and already going grey at the temple, already carrying the heaviness around the jaw that their father had carried. Aldous had it too, he supposed. People had always said they looked alike, which both of them had resented in the particular way you resent a thing that is true.

He could not have said, now, what the falling-out had been about. That was the shameful part, the part he had not told Heikkonen or anyone. There had been money, yes, but the money had only been the shape the thing took, the vessel it poured itself into. Under the money there had been something older and less nameable, a sense each of them carried that the other had been given more—more of their mother's patience, more of their father's regard, more of the narrow ration of warmth that house had produced. They had divided the inheritance and in the dividing had discovered they no longer had any reason to speak, and so they had stopped, and the stopping had been easy, frighteningly easy, like stepping off a path into deep snow and finding it held you, and then finding, years on, that you could not remember where the path had been.

There had been a card at Christmas, the first two years. From Pell, in his cramped engineer's hand. *Hope you are keeping well.* Nothing more. Aldous had not answered the first and so there had not been a second to answer, and that, too, had been easy. He thought of those cards now with a sensation like pressing a bruise.

He went up to the deck because the cabin had begun to feel like a held breath. The wind came hard off the water and he turned his collar up and walked the length of the ship along the rail, past the lit windows of the lounge where a few passengers sat over their drinks, lorry drivers mostly, men who made this crossing the way other men took a bus to work. The sea was black and the sky was black and the only division between them was a faint disturbance, a texture, where the swells caught what little light the ship threw out. He stood at the stern and watched the wake unspool behind them, white and then grey and then gone, swallowed back into the dark almost as fast as the propellers made it.

A man came and stood a few feet down the rail and lit a cigarette with some difficulty in the wind, cupping it, and the small flare lit his face for a moment, a broad weathered face, before it went out. He nodded to Aldous. Aldous nodded back. For a while they stood without speaking, which the man seemed to understand as the only sane response to that much water in that much dark.

"You go across often?" the man said at last. His English was accented but easy.

"No. First time."

"Ah." He drew on the cigarette. "It is not a beautiful crossing. In the summer, maybe. Now—" He shrugged, a movement Aldous felt more than saw. "Now it is only the way to the other side."

"That's all I need from it," Aldous said.

The man laughed, a short sound torn off by the wind. "A practical man." He smoked. Then, with the directness of strangers in the dark who will never see each other again: "You go to see someone?"

Aldous considered the question. It would have been simple to lie, and he was a man who lied easily about small things, who smoothed his way through the world with the little untruths that keep machinery quiet. But the dark and the water and the engines made lying seem like too much effort.

"My brother," he said. "Maybe. They found a body. I'm to say if it's him."

The man was quiet for a moment. Then he dropped the cigarette and crushed it under his boot with great care, as if it mattered, and Aldous understood that he was giving himself something to do with his hands while he found the right thing to say, and that there was no right thing, and that the man knew this.

"I am sorry," he said finally. "That is a hard errand."

"I don't know if I'll know him," Aldous said. He had not meant to say it. It came out of him the way the *yes* had come out, unbidden. "We haven't spoken in eleven years. I keep thinking—what if I look at him and I'm not sure. What if I say yes and it isn't, or I say no and it is. They put so much weight on it. The man on the phone. As if I'd carry the truth across in my pocket and just—lay it on the table."

The man listened. He did not say what people said. He did not say *I'm sure you'll know* or *blood is blood* or any of the consolations that are really requests to stop talking. He looked out at the water.

"My father," he said after a while, "I did not see him for a long time also. Many years. We were angry men, both of us. He died while I was at sea—I was a sailor then, younger—and when I came back he was three weeks buried. I went to the grave. I stood there." He paused. "And I will tell you something. I did not feel what I thought I would feel. I thought I would feel sorry. I felt—" he searched for the word, "—robbed. Cheated. Like someone had closed a shop before I could go in. The door is locked and the lights are off and you stand in the street and you think, but I was coming, I was on my way, I only needed more time."

Aldous said nothing. The wind worked at them both.

"What I mean," the man said, "is do not worry so much whether you will know his face. The face is not the thing. You will know what you know. It will come from somewhere else." He touched his own chest, briefly, vaguely, and then seemed embarrassed by the gesture and turned it into an adjustment of his coat. "I will leave you alone now. Good luck to you. It is a thing you cannot prepare for, so do not try."

He went, and Aldous heard the door to the lounge open and close, a brief bloom of warmth and yellow light and then the dark again, complete. He stayed at the rail a long time. He was cold all the way through but the cold seemed appropriate, seemed like the correct temperature for the errand, and he did not want to go in.

He thought about the door of the shop, locked and dark.

When at last he went below, he could not sleep. He lay in the narrow berth with the engines a constant pressure beneath him and the porthole showing nothing, only its own faint reflection of the cabin, and he found himself doing the thing he had been avoiding for three days, which was to remember Pell. Not the man in the photograph, the grey-templed stranger at the funeral, but the boy. Because there had been a boy, and Aldous had been a boy alongside him, and for a long stretch of years before everything calcified they had been—he made himself use the word—they had been close. They had shared a room until Aldous was nine. There had been a window in that room that looked onto the back of the house, onto the yard with its single apple tree, and in summer the light came through it late and gold and lay across the floor in a long bar, and they had a game—he had not thought of this in decades—a game where the bar of light was a river and the floor was lava and you could only cross by leaping from the rug to the chair to the bed, and Pell, older, stronger, always made it, and Aldous, younger, always fell short, always landed with one foot in the lava, and Pell would haul him up by the arm onto the safety of the bed, laughing, *you're dead, you're dead, but I'll save you, just this once.*

He lay in the dark and the memory was so vivid and so unexpected that he had to put his hand over his eyes, though there was no one to see.

He had not let himself have these. That was the truth of it. He had built a wall against the good memories specifically, because the good memories were dangerous, the good memories were the ones that could undo a man, and the grievances were safe, the grievances you could hold and turn over and they only ever confirmed what you already believed. He had spent eleven years tending the grievances and letting the apple tree and the bar of gold light grow over with weeds, and now here they came up through the floor of the ship in the middle of the strait, and there was nothing he could do to stop them.

*Just this once,* the boy said, hauling him up by the arm.

Toward four in the morning he slept, badly, and dreamed of nothing he could keep, and woke to a grey that was marginally less than the grey before, the porthole lightening by degrees, and the change in the engines that meant they had begun to feel for the land again. He got up and dressed and went out. The other shore was a low dark line under a sky the color of a coin, and a lighthouse blinked somewhere on a headland, patient, indifferent. The town came up slowly out of the murk, a scatter of lights, the cranes of a small port standing against the paleness like the bones of something. Snow had fallen here. He could see it on the roofs and on the hills behind the town, not deep, just enough to leach the color out of everything, to make the world look like the photograph in his passport, grey on grey, two men in dark coats.

Heikkonen was waiting at the terminal. Aldous knew him at once, though they had never met—a tall stooped man in a grey overcoat, holding no sign, needing none, recognizable by the particular stillness of a person whose job is to wait for the bereaved. He had a long sad face and very pale eyes and he shook Aldous's hand with both of his, which Aldous found he did not mind.

"You had a crossing all right?" Heikkonen said. "It can be rough, this time."

"It was fine."

"Good. Good." He seemed relieved to have the small thing settled. "My car is here. It is not far. Perhaps—you would like coffee first? There is no hurry. He is not—" he stopped himself. "There is no hurry," he said again.

"I'd rather just do it," Aldous said. "If that's all right."

"Of course. Of course." Heikkonen led him out to a small dark car furred with frost, and scraped the windscreen with a plastic blade while the engine ran, his breath clouding, and Aldous stood beside him and looked at the snow on the hills and thought how strange it was to arrive somewhere entirely new on the worst kind of business, to see a whole town for the first time through this particular lens, so that he would forever associate these streets, these grey buildings, this cold clean air, with the inside of his own dread.

The drive was short. The building was low and modern and could have been anything, a clinic, a municipal office, and inside it was very warm and smelled of disinfectant and, underneath that, of something he chose not to name. Heikkonen spoke in their language to a woman at a desk, and there was a form, and Aldous signed where he was shown, his signature looking foreign to him, the hand of a stranger. Then they went down a corridor, and Heikkonen stopped outside a door and turned to him.

"I must tell you again," he said, gently, "about the condition. The water—he was some time in the water. The face you will see is not—it will not look as a face should look. This is normal. It is the water. Do you understand me? Do not be frightened by it. Look past it, if you can, to the things that do not change. The shape of the hands. The—there is a mark, here," he touched his own left forearm, "an old scar, perhaps. These are the things. Take your time."

A scar on the left forearm. Aldous felt something move in him, slow and cold. Pell had a scar on his left forearm. From the apple tree, in fact—the very tree—a fall when he was twelve, a branch that broke, a long gash that had needed stitches and had healed into a pale seam that he had been obscurely proud of all through their boyhood, that he would show to other children, *look, I nearly died,* though he had not nearly died, had only needed eleven stitches and a tetanus shot and a week of being waited on, which Aldous had envied with a fury that astonished him now.

"Yes," he said. His voice did not sound like his own. "All right."

They went in.

The room was cold, much colder than the corridor, and very bright, and there was a shape on a steel table under a sheet, and a technician in a smock who nodded to Heikkonen and took the top of the sheet in both hands and looked at Aldous, waiting, and Aldous nodded though he had not meant to, his head moving on its own, and the technician drew the sheet down.

He made himself look.

Heikkonen had been right; the face was not a face the way a living face is, the way a face in a photograph is. The water had done its work. He understood at once why they had needed him, why a card in a wallet was not enough, and he understood also, looking, that he had been wrong to fear he would feel nothing, that he would stand here numb and uncertain and have to manufacture a response. He did not feel nothing. He felt the floor of himself drop away.

But he did not feel recognition. That was the thing, the terrible thing he had not anticipated in all his anticipating. He looked at the ruined face and he looked for Pell in it and Pell was not there—or might have been there, or might not, the features so altered, so generalized by the water, that they could have belonged to anyone, to any man of the right age and the right heaviness around a jaw that no longer held its shape. He looked and he did not know. The whole weight of the errand came down on him, the thing Heikkonen and the woman on the phone and the law of two countries had decided he could carry, and he could not carry it, because the face would not tell him.

"Take your time," Heikkonen said quietly, somewhere behind him.

"The arm," Aldous said. "You said there was—the left arm."

The technician understood the gesture if not the words, and lifted the sheet at the side, and drew out the left arm, pale and swollen and dreadful, and turned it so the inner forearm showed, and there, running along it, faint but unmistakable, was a long pale seam, an old scar, well healed, the kind a branch makes when a boy falls out of an apple tree and nearly dies and does not.

Aldous looked at it for a long time.

He thought about the bar of gold light across the floor. He thought about being hauled up by the arm onto the safety of the bed. He thought about the Christmas cards he had not answered, the *hope you are keeping well* in the cramped engineer's hand, and how a man writes that to a brother he has not spoken to in years because some part of him is still reaching across the dark water hoping to be hauled up, *just this once,* and how the other man can simply not reach back, can find it easy, frighteningly easy, the not reaching. He thought about the shop with its lights off and its door locked and himself standing in the street saying *but I was coming, I was on my way, I only needed more time.*

And he knew. The man on the deck had been right. It came from somewhere else, not from the face, which could not tell him, but from somewhere lower and older and surer. He knew the scar. He had been there when it was made. He had stood in the garden and looked up into the tree and seen his brother fall.

"Yes," he said. "It's him. It's Pell."

His voice was steady. He was surprised at how steady. Heikkonen made a small sound of acknowledgment and the technician drew the arm back under the sheet, gently, with a care that Aldous found he was grateful for, and then drew the sheet up over the face, and the room held its cold bright silence, and somewhere outside, beyond the warm corridor and the form he had signed and the frost on the car, the strait went on heaving its grey water against the rocks of an island too small, he had thought, to have a name, where his brother had come up in the nets after some time in the water, on his way, perhaps, to somewhere, or from somewhere, reaching across, and no one on the far shore had reached back in time.

"I'm sorry," Heikkonen said. "I am very sorry."

"Thank you," Aldous said. And then, because it seemed important, because there was no one else in the world now who would ever say it: "He saved my life once. When we were boys. He always made it across and I never did, and he'd pull me up. He'd say, you're dead, you're dead, but I'll save you. Just this once." He stopped. The room blurred and he let it. "I never got to do the same for him. That's all. I just never got the chance."

Heikkonen put a hand on his shoulder and left it there, and did not say anything, which was the right thing, the only right thing, and the two of them stood in the cold bright room for a while longer with the sheet and the silence, and then they went out together into the corridor, into the warmth, and Aldous let himself be led.
