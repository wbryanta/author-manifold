The woman with the bad perm wanted to know if the breakfront came apart.

"It's all one piece," Diane said, though she had no idea. She had never once thought about whether her mother's breakfront came apart. It had simply stood against the dining room wall her entire life, a dark looming thing with glass doors that her mother had warned her away from when she was small, behind which lived the good dishes that came out at Thanksgiving and Easter and never otherwise. The dishes were gone now. She'd sold them in a box lot to a dealer that morning for forty dollars, before she understood that forty dollars was a number she would later be ashamed of.

"Because my husband's got the truck," the woman said, "but if it don't come apart we can't get it through the door. We measured."

"You're welcome to try the door."

The woman looked at her, sensing something off in the tone, and Diane made herself smile. It was a quarter past one. The sale had opened at nine. The people had come the way water comes, a trickle and then all at once, parking their cars up and down Linden Street, walking up the driveway her father had poured in 1971, the year before she was born, the year the maple was planted that now shaded the whole front of the lot. They came in and they touched things. That was what she had not been prepared for. The touching. A man had picked up her mother's reading glasses from the side table and tried them on and laughed and put them back down crooked.

"I'll get Dale," the woman said, and went out.

Diane stood in the front hall where she had stood ten thousand times in her life, and she watched a teenage boy carry the kitchen chairs out two at a time, a chair in each hand, swinging them a little, and she thought, those are the chairs. Those were the chairs they had eaten at. The yellow vinyl seats had a particular give to them and a particular sound when you sat down hard, which her mother had not permitted, and her brother Gary had done anyway, every night, just to hear it, until their father said his name in the voice and Gary stopped. Gary was in Arizona now and had not come. He'd called twice during the week of the funeral and once after, to say he trusted her to handle it, that whatever she decided was fine, that he didn't want anything from the house. He'd said it like generosity. *I don't want anything.* As though wanting nothing were the same as helping.

The estate liquidator, a brisk competent woman named Pam who wore a fanny pack of cash and had a system, came through the hall with a clipboard.

"The bed sold," Pam said. "The master. Couple from Mansfield. They want to know can they pick it up tomorrow, they didn't bring anything big enough."

"That's fine."

"You okay?"

"I'm fine."

Pam looked at her the way professionals look at the bereaved, with a practiced compassion that had a clock running behind it. "It's a lot," Pam said. "First sale's always a lot. You did the right thing being here. Some people can't be here, and then they regret it, they wonder what went where."

"I wanted to be here," Diane said, which was true and was also the thing she most regretted.

She had driven up from Columbus that morning in the dark, ninety minutes on roads she could have driven blind, and the closer she got the more she felt herself becoming younger, peeling back, until by the time she turned onto Linden she was not a fifty-one-year-old woman whose own daughter was grown and gone to Seattle, she was nobody's mother and somebody's child, and the porch light was off because there was no one inside to leave it on.

Now a man in a Carhartt jacket was examining the secretary desk in the living room, opening and closing the little drawers. He found the one that stuck. Everyone found the one that stuck eventually; it had stuck for forty years. He worked it open and looked inside and finding it empty, looked disappointed, as though the house owed him a secret.

"Hundred and ten on that," Pam called over.

"You take a hundred?"

"Hundred and ten. It's solid cherry. You won't find that anymore."

The man made a face but he was going to pay it, Diane could see, and she felt a strange flush of pride, the way she might have felt if someone admired her mother's hair. The desk was where the bills had been done. Her mother sitting there in the evening with the checkbook and the long envelopes, the lamp on, the pen on its little chain that she'd gotten from the bank in 1985 and never replaced. Diane had stood in the doorway as a teenager wanting things and learned to read her mother's back, the set of the shoulders, whether it was a night to ask.

"Mom," she said, very quietly, to no one. The man in the Carhartt jacket didn't hear her. He was counting out twenties.

She went into the kitchen to get away from the desk and that was worse, because the kitchen was where her mother had most thoroughly lived. The cabinets stood open, half-emptied, the cheap melamine plates already gone, the drinking glasses going one and two at a time, jelly jars and a set with oranges printed on them and the tall amber ones from a gas station promotion that her mother had insisted were valuable and were not. A woman was holding one of the orange-print glasses up to the window light, checking for chips.

"Those are nice," Diane heard herself say.

"They're cute," the woman agreed. "My grandma had ones like these."

*My mother bought those at the A&P*, Diane wanted to say. *There used to be an A&P out on Route 42 and they gave you a glass when you spent five dollars and my mother went every week and got the whole set and we drank out of them my entire childhood and the orange wore off the one I used most because I held it the same way every time, look, that one, that's mine, the orange is half gone, that's where my hand went.*

"They're a dollar each," she said instead.

The woman bought four. Not the worn one. The worn one she set back on the counter, and Diane waited until she was gone and then she took it and held it and the orange was gone where her thumb had ridden it for eighteen years. She put it in her purse. She had told herself she would take very little. She had a small list, in her head, of things, and she had told herself to be disciplined, because she had watched friends drown in their parents' belongings, renting storage units they paid for monthly and never opened, hauling around the freight of the dead. She would take the photographs, which Pam had set aside in a box because photographs didn't sell. She would take her mother's wedding ring, which was in her purse already. She would take one or two small things. She had not planned on the glass. The glass was not on the list. But the glass had her thumb on it.

Through the kitchen window she could see the backyard, the clothesline still strung between the two metal poles, sagging now, and the spot where the swing set had been, where the grass had never fully come back. Her father had taken the swing set down when she was twelve and she had cried about it, not because she still used the swing set but because taking it down was a statement about time, and she had understood that even at twelve. Her father had been dead nineteen years. The grass remembered him better than most people did. There was a faint rectangle out there, greener, where the dog had been buried, the second dog, Pepper, in 1989. None of these people walking through the house knew about Pepper. They would buy the lawnmower — someone was probably buying the lawnmower right now — and they would mow over Pepper and never know.

"Excuse me." A young woman, mid-twenties maybe, with a baby on her hip. "Is the rocking chair for sale? The one in the back bedroom? It doesn't have a tag."

The rocking chair. Diane's chest did something complicated.

"It might've gotten missed," she said. "Let me look at it."

She led the young woman down the hall, past the bathroom with its pink tile that her mother had loved and everyone else had found dated, past the door to what had been Gary's room and then the sewing room, to the back bedroom that had been Diane's. The wallpaper was the same. Her mother had never redecorated it, had kept it as a kind of shrine or maybe just out of inertia, small yellow flowers on a cream ground, and the water stain in the corner from the roof leak the winter Diane was sixteen, shaped like Florida, that she'd looked at every night falling asleep. The bed was sold. But the rocking chair was in the corner where it had migrated years ago, the chair her mother had nursed her in, and Gary, the chair that had been in the living room when Diane was little and then had been retired back here when it got too wobbly, and her mother had sat in it to read to her, the two of them in this chair, her mother's voice going up for the wolf and down for the grandmother.

"My grandmother nursed all of us in a chair like this," the young woman said, jiggling the baby. "I've been looking for one. They're hard to find that aren't, you know, antique-store priced."

Diane looked at the chair. She looked at the baby, who was looking at her with the frank unembarrassed stare of the very young, a little drool on its chin, fat hands.

"It's a little wobbly," Diane said. "The right rocker. My dad always meant to fix it."

"My husband's handy."

"It's twenty dollars," Diane said, though it wasn't on Pam's list at all and might have gone for more, might have been a thing she kept, and she watched the young woman's face open with the pleasure of it, and the baby reached out toward Diane's purse where the glass was, and Diane stepped back.

"Thank you," the young woman said. "Honestly. My mom passed last year and I — I don't know. It feels good to find something that was loved, you know? You can tell this chair was loved. You can just tell."

And Diane, who had held herself together through the funeral and the lawyer and the listing of the house and the box lot of dishes she'd sold for forty dollars, stood in her childhood bedroom and felt her face come apart, just for a second, just a crumpling she caught and pressed back into place with her hand over her mouth as though stifling a cough.

"I'm sorry," the young woman said, alarmed. "Did I —"

"No," Diane said. "No, it's — this was my room. When I was a girl. You're right. It was loved. Take the chair. Please take the chair. I'm so glad it's you."

The young woman didn't know what to do with this. She had come to a stranger's estate sale to buy a cheap rocking chair and she had walked into the middle of someone's grief, and she did what kind people do, which was to be briefly and awkwardly tender, touching Diane's arm with the hand that wasn't holding the baby, saying *I'm so sorry for your loss* with the particular weight of someone who had recently learned what those words actually cost, and then she carried the chair out tilted against her body with the baby riding the other hip, and Diane heard her in the hall telling Pam she'd take it, and Pam saying she'd carry it to the car for her, and the front door, and gone.

Diane sat down on the floor of her old room because there was nowhere else to sit. She put her back against the wall under the window, under the Florida stain, the way she had sat at fourteen with the phone cord stretched all the way from the kitchen, the way she had sat at seventeen crying over a boy whose name she could no longer summon. The carpet was the same carpet. Industrial beige, low pile, and she put her hand flat on it and it was the same. Some things they hadn't been able to sell and the carpet was one of them; the carpet would go with the house, would be torn out probably by whoever bought it, a young couple Pam said, a couple who would gut the kitchen and paint over the pink tile and the yellow flowers and tear out the beige carpet and put down something gray, the gray everyone put down now, and they would raise their own children here and twenty years from now one of those children would sit on a different floor in a different town while strangers carried out the furniture, and so on, and so on, the whole rolling inheritance of it, and there was no stopping it and no fixing the rocker and no point being angry at Gary in Arizona who didn't want anything.

She thought about her daughter. Megan, in Seattle, who had loved this house in the abstract way of grandchildren who visit twice a year, who had played in this very room, who had been read to in that very chair by Diane's mother just as Diane had been, the same rising and falling voice, the wolf and the grandmother, the unbroken thread. Megan had said on the phone, *Mom, do you want me to fly out, I can fly out,* and Diane had said no, no, it's just a sale, it's not a big deal, I'll be in and out in a day. And Megan had let herself be told that because it was easier, and because Diane had wanted to be told it too. Now she wanted, badly, with a sudden physical hunger, to call her daughter, and she got her phone out of her purse and the worn orange glass was in there knocking against the phone and she got the phone out and held it and didn't dial. It was the middle of a workday in Seattle. And what would she say. *They're taking everything out the front door.* That was the only sentence she had and it wasn't a sentence you could say to your child without becoming, in that moment, a kind of child yourself, and Diane had spent fifty-one years being the one who did not say things like that.

She put the phone away.

After a while Pam appeared in the doorway and looked down at her without surprise, as though finding the deceased's daughter sitting on the floor of an empty bedroom was simply one of the recurring features of her profession, which it probably was.

"The breakfront guy left," Pam said. "Couldn't get it through the door. I told him it doesn't come apart. So that's still here, if you want it, or I can list it for the next sale, sometimes the big pieces I take to the warehouse."

"I don't have anywhere to put it," Diane said.

"Most people don't. That's the thing about the big pieces." Pam came in and lowered herself, with the carefulness of a woman whose knees had opinions, to sit against the opposite wall, facing Diane, and she took off the fanny pack and set it beside her like she was off duty for a minute. "We're slowing down. Couple more hours. By four it'll just be the dealers picking through. You should go home before that part. You don't want to watch the four o'clock part. That's the part where it's just stuff."

"Is it different by four?"

"It's all just stuff by four," Pam said. "Right now you're still seeing it. By four it's been seen enough that it goes back to being objects. That's how it works. You're in the worst part now. The middle."

Diane looked at her. "How long have you been doing this?"

"Twenty-two years. My mother started it. We did her own house, when she went. I had to run my own mother's sale. So." Pam shrugged. "I know where you are. I'm not going to tell you it doesn't matter, because that's a lie people tell. It matters. It's just that it doesn't matter in the way you think it does right now. The stuff isn't the thing. You know that. You knew that before today. It's just hard to keep knowing it with all the strangers around."

"My mother loved this house," Diane said.

"I'm sure she did."

"She kept everything. My room. The wallpaper. She kept the wallpaper for thirty-five years. I used to tell her to update it. I used to be embarrassed by this house, when I was young. The pink bathroom. The orange glasses." She laughed and it came out wet. "I have one of the glasses in my purse. I told myself I wasn't going to take anything I didn't plan on."

"Take the glass," Pam said.

"It's in my purse already."

"Then you already decided. Your body decides before you do. That's why I tell people, walk through first thing in the morning before we open, and whatever your hand reaches for, that's what you keep. Don't think about it. Thinking ruins it. People talk themselves out of the one thing and then they're haunted." Pam stood, recovering her fanny pack, her knees announcing themselves again. "There's a box up front. The photos, and I put a few things in there I didn't think you'd want sold. Your mother's Bible. A little ceramic bird off the windowsill, I don't know why, it caught my eye. Some letters in a shoebox, I didn't read them, but I figured you'd want to be the one to decide on letters. They're yours. No charge on the box. The box is yours."

"Thank you," Diane said.

"You can sit here as long as you want. Nobody's buying the floor." Pam paused in the doorway. "When you're ready, go out the back. Through the kitchen. So you don't have to walk past everybody. Go out the back and around. There's a gate."

"I know there's a gate," Diane said. "My father put in that gate. I'm sorry. Of course you didn't — yes. The gate. Thank you."

Pam nodded and was gone, and Diane heard her up front resuming her voice, the selling voice, *that's solid cherry, you won't find that anymore,* and the front door opening and closing, opening and closing, the breath of the house going in and out.

She stayed on the floor a while longer. She let herself look at the Florida stain. She thought she should feel that she was saying goodbye to the room but she didn't feel that; the room was already not hers, had stopped being hers decades ago, was just a beige-carpeted box with yellow flowers that a young couple would gut. The goodbye had happened a long time ago and she'd missed it, the way you always miss the actual goodbyes, which never announce themselves; you only ever get the ceremonial ones, the funeral, the sale, the standing in the empty room, and those are not the real ones. The real one had been some ordinary afternoon in 1990 when she'd walked out of this room to go somewhere and it had simply been the last time it was hers and she hadn't known to mark it.

She got up. Her own knees had opinions now too; when had that happened. She walked through the house one more time, against Pam's advice, because she was not a person who took the back way out, never had been. She walked through the hall and the dining room where the breakfront stood marooned and enormous, too big for any door, the one thing that couldn't leave, that would have to be broken apart or abandoned, and she put her hand flat on its dark side the way she'd put her hand on the carpet. Through the living room where the secretary desk was gone, a clean rectangle in the dust where it had stood. Into the kitchen, the cabinets gaping, the orange glasses gone but for the one in her purse.

She picked up the box from the front hall. It was lighter than she expected. A whole woman's life, the unsellable part of it, the part nobody wanted, and it weighed almost nothing: photographs and a Bible and a ceramic bird and a shoebox of letters she would read in a hotel that night and would not be able to stop reading, letters between her parents from before she was born, from when they were young and the house was new and the maple was a sapling staked against the wind, but she didn't know that yet. For now it was just a light box.

A man held the front door for her, a stranger, carrying out the floor lamp from the living room, and he stepped aside and said, "Here, let me get that," meaning the door, and Diane walked out through the front door of her mother's house carrying the box, past the strangers and their trucks, down the driveway her father had poured the year before she was born, under the maple that shaded the whole front of the lot, and she did not take the back way, she did not use the gate, she went out the front like everyone else, carrying what was hers, into the bright unbearable afternoon.
