The card table came first, which seemed wrong to Dana, though she could not have said what order would have been right. A man in a Carhartt jacket carried it sideways through the front door, the legs folded against the green particleboard top, and the screen door clapped behind him in the way it always had, two beats, a small one chasing a large. Her mother had meant to fix that door for forty years. Now it would go on clapping for someone else, or no one.

Dana sat at the kitchen table with the metal cash box open in front of her and a roll of stickers she had bought at the drugstore, the small orange dots they used to mark prices. She had spent the previous evening going through the house with the woman from the estate company, Brenda, who wore reading glasses on a beaded chain and had a way of touching things that suggested she had handled the contents of a great many dead people's houses and had arrived at a private accounting of their worth that no living person could dispute. The card table, Brenda had decided, was worth four dollars. The man in the Carhartt jacket had not even haggled.

"You doing all right?" Brenda asked. She had stationed herself near the front door with a clipboard, and she had a folding chair of her own she'd brought from her car, a nicer one than anything in the house.

"Fine," Dana said.

"You don't have to be here, you know. Some people, they give me the key and go to the movies. Come back at five and it's all done."

"I'd rather be here."

Brenda nodded as if this too were a thing she had seen, the people who stayed, and had filed it under its proper heading.

It was the second Saturday in October and the light came in low and yellow through the kitchen window over the sink, the same window her mother had stood at washing dishes for as long as Dana had been alive, looking out at the Hendersons' fence and the line of arborvitae that had grown from saplings to a wall in the time it took Dana to grow up and leave and acquire a husband and lose him and raise a daughter who now lived in Seattle and called on Sundays. The arborvitae had won. They blocked the Hendersons entirely now, and the Hendersons were gone anyway, both of them, the house sold to a young couple who parked two cars in the driveway and had not introduced themselves to anyone, her mother had reported, with the wounded astonishment of a woman who had brought casseroles to strangers her whole life.

A pair of women came through to look at the dishes. Dana had set the everyday dishes out on the counter, the Corelle with the blue cornflower pattern, lighter than they looked, nearly unbreakable, the kind of dish that survived everything including the people who ate off it. The women turned the plates over to check the backs.

"How much for the set?" one of them asked.

"There's stickers on them," Brenda called from the door. "Eight for the whole lot."

The woman considered. Dana watched her consider. There were probably forty pieces, plates and bowls and the saucers that no longer matched any cups because the cups had broken one at a time over the decades, and the woman was deciding whether eight dollars was too much to pay for the plates that Dana had eaten oatmeal off of every school morning of her childhood, her mother standing at the stove with the wooden spoon, telling her to hurry, the bus, the bus.

"I'll give you five," the woman said.

"Six," said Brenda, who had appeared at Dana's elbow with the soundless efficiency of someone whose whole vocation was the conversion of grief into small denominations.

"Six," the woman agreed, and counted out the bills, and her friend began wrapping the plates in the newspaper Brenda had stacked on the counter, the Marion Star, last Tuesday's, so that her mother's dishes went out the door swaddled in the obituaries of people her mother might have known.

Dana made a small mark in the notebook she was keeping. She did not need to keep a notebook; Brenda had her own system and would settle up at the end and take her percentage. But Dana found she wanted the record. Six dollars, dishes. Four dollars, card table. She wrote them down as if she were taking a deposition, as if at some later date she would be called to testify to what had happened here, to account for the dispersal.

She had flown in Thursday from Connecticut. The house had been empty since August, since the morning her mother did not answer the phone and Mrs. Dietrich from the church had let herself in with the key from under the planter and found her in the chair by the window, the television off, a cup of tea gone cold on the side table, gone in her sleep, the doctor said, which was the thing everyone hoped for and no one believed they would get. Dana had come for the funeral and gone home again and come back now for this, the second death, the one nobody held a service for.

"Is the bedroom set for sale?" A young man, mid-twenties, with a girl who was visibly pregnant, the two of them holding hands in the doorway as if they had wandered into a museum.

"Everything's for sale," Brenda said. "Down the hall, first door."

They went down the hall toward her mother's room, and Dana listened to their footsteps on the hardwood, the particular creak of the third floorboard from the bathroom that she could have located blindfolded. She had crept over that board ten thousand times, coming home late as a teenager, stepping wide to avoid it, and her mother had told her years later, laughing, that she'd always heard her anyway, that a mother hears the silence where the creak should be.

The bedroom set was solid maple, a bed and a tall dresser and a vanity with a round mirror, bought by her parents in 1961 from a furniture store in Columbus that no longer existed, a store Dana's father had driven them to in the Plymouth, a whole day's expedition. Her mother had told that story too. Her mother had told all the stories, latterly, the same ones in rotation, and Dana had let them wash over her on the phone while she made dinner in Connecticut, half-listening, saying mm, saying I remember, and now she found she could not remember, not the name of the store, not the price, not whether her father had thought it too dear, and there was no one left to ask. The stories had gone where the woman went. She had been the sole remaining witness to most of Dana's own life, the early part, the part before memory, and that country was closed now, its borders sealed, and Dana stood at the edge of it with a cash box.

The young couple came back. "We'll take the bedroom set," the man said. "Can you—is there a way to hold it? We have to get a truck."

"I take a deposit," Brenda said. "Half down, balance on pickup, pickup by five today."

"How much?"

"Set's marked at ninety. Bed, dresser, vanity."

The girl ran her hand along the edge of the kitchen table, not even aware she was doing it, the way pregnant women touched things, anchoring themselves. She was very young. She looked tired in the way that was about to become a different kind of tired forever.

"Ninety's good," the man said, and Dana understood that to them it was a great deal of money and also a great bargain, that they were furnishing a life out of the leavings of hers, and she wanted suddenly to tell them about the maple, that it would outlast them, that her mother had slept in that bed for fifty-five years and her father for thirty of them before the cancer, that the vanity mirror had reflected a young woman putting on lipstick before a dance and an old woman who could no longer see well enough to find her own eyes. She wanted to tell them: be careful with it, it is not just furniture, it has held us. But that was exactly the thing one could not say, and so she took the deposit, forty-five dollars, and wrote it in her notebook, and the girl smiled at her, a real smile, and said, "Thank you so much, it's beautiful," and Dana said, "It's good furniture. It'll last."

"Did your mom pass recently?" the girl asked, and the man shot her a look, but Dana found she didn't mind.

"In August."

"I'm sorry." The girl meant it. "My grandma passed last year. We got her kitchen table. I think about her every time we eat."

"That's nice," Dana said, and meant it too, and the girl touched the table once more and left, and Dana looked at the kitchen table that the girl had been touching and realized she had not put a sticker on it, that she had unconsciously left it off the inventory, the one piece, as if it might be allowed to stay.

By eleven the house had filled with strangers. They came in twos and threes, retired couples mostly, a few dealers Brenda greeted by name, a woman with a baby strapped to her chest who bought the entire contents of the linen closet, the sheets and pillowcases and the good tablecloth with the embroidered edges that her mother had used at Thanksgiving, ironed each year and put away again. They came and moved through the rooms of the house with the frank curiosity of people in a place that belongs to no one, opening cabinets, lifting lamps to check for cracks, pricing the labor of a life in dollars and discarding most of it as not worth the trouble.

Dana watched a man pick up the ceramic rooster that had stood on top of the refrigerator since before she was born. It was hideous, she had always thought it hideous, red and gold with a comb like a fan, and her mother had loved it for reasons Dana never learned and now never would. The man turned it over, found the seventy-five-cent sticker, made a face, and set it down again, in the wrong place, on the counter rather than the fridge, and moved on. The rooster sat there. Nobody wanted it. Dana thought she might keep it and knew she would not. It would not survive the suitcase, and it would not belong in her life in Connecticut, where the kitchen was clean and gray and nothing sat on top of anything.

"You want to take a break?" Brenda asked. "I can watch the box."

"I'm okay."

"You've been sitting there three hours stiff as a board." Brenda lowered herself into the other kitchen chair with a small grunt and set down her own coffee, in a travel mug she'd brought. "First sale's always the hardest. With a parent. You did the funeral already, that's the public part, everybody comes and says their piece. This here's the private part. Nobody tells you about this part."

"How long have you been doing it?"

"Twenty-two years. My husband and I started it. He passed in '09 but I kept on." She said it without weight, a fact among facts. "You learn things. You learn most of what people keep, nobody else wants. The stuff they fought over, the stuff they wrote it down in the will, who gets the such-and-such. Comes the day and it's a dollar on a table and it sits there." She nodded toward the rooster. "And then some little thing you'd never think, somebody's eyes light up. I had a woman last month cried over a set of measuring spoons. Her mother's. Aluminum, dented all to hell. Cried like a baby." Brenda sipped her coffee. "You never know what's going to be the thing."

"The thing," Dana repeated.

"The thing that gets you. It's never what you think it'll be. People plan for the big stuff. The bed, the wedding china. They steel themselves." She set the mug down. "It's always the little thing comes up from behind."

A man wanted to know if the lawnmower in the garage was for sale, and Brenda heaved herself up to go deal with it, and Dana sat alone at the unmarked table in the kitchen that was emptying around her like a tide going out. She could see into the living room from where she sat. The card table was gone and the recliner where her mother had died was gone, sold within the first half hour to a man who would never know, and the bookshelf was half-empty, the paperback mysteries her mother read and reread going into a box for a quarter apiece. The walls showed where the pictures had hung, rectangles of brighter paint, the ghosts of frames. Someone had bought the framed print of the lighthouse. Someone had bought the mantel clock that had not kept time since the Carter administration and that her mother had wound anyway, every Sunday, out of loyalty to its dead mechanism.

She got up finally and walked through the house while Brenda manned the box. She had not really walked through it, she realized, not slowly, not since she'd arrived; she had moved through it on errands, looking for documents, the deed, the insurance, the bank statements her mother kept in a shoebox in the closet with a rubber band around them. She had been an executor, efficient and dry-eyed, and the house had been a problem to be solved. Now she walked through it as a place.

Her old room was a sewing room now, had been for thirty years, the single bed replaced by a daybed nobody slept in and the closet full of fabric her mother had bought and never used, lengths of cotton in floral prints, a quilt half-finished, the batting still pinned. A dealer was going through the fabric, pulling out the vintage prints, the ones from the seventies that had come back into fashion. Dana stood in the doorway of the room that had been hers and felt nothing she could name, which was itself a kind of grief, the failure of the room to be the room, the way the past did not stay where you left it but kept being lived in, sewn in, repurposed, until it was just space, just square footage, just a daybed and a dealer and the smell of old cloth.

She went into the bathroom and shut the door and sat on the edge of the tub. The bathroom had not changed. The bathroom was the most unchanged room in the house, the pink tile her father had put in himself in 1968, the same tile, pink as a tongue, and the medicine cabinet with the mirror that had a bevel along the edge, and when she opened it her mother's things were still there because nobody buys the contents of a medicine cabinet. The denture cleaner. The aspirin. A lipstick worn down to a nub, a color her mother had worn for forty years, the same tube refilled in her memory though it could not have been, it must have been a hundred tubes over the years all the same shade, and Dana took the cap off and there it was, the smell, faint, waxy, rose, and it was her mother's cheek against hers at the door, leaving for school, leaving for the prom, leaving for college, leaving for Connecticut, leaving and leaving, a lifetime of being kissed goodbye by a mouth wearing that exact color, and Dana sat down on the edge of the tub with the lipstick in her hand and cried.

She cried the way you cry when you have been holding it, which is not gracefully. She pressed the back of her hand against her mouth and her shoulders went and the sounds that came out of her did not sound like her. She thought, distantly, of Brenda. The thing comes up from behind. It was a lipstick. It was a forty-nine-cent lipstick worn down to nothing that no one would buy, that would go in a contractor bag at the end of the day with the denture cleaner and the expired aspirin and the rooster, and it had her mother in it more completely than the maple bed, more than the dishes, more than the house, the whole house, which strangers were carrying out the front door in pieces while she sat on the edge of a pink bathtub and came apart.

She did not know how long she sat there. Eventually the storm passed through her and out the other side, the way they do, leaving her emptied and breathing carefully. She ran the cold water and pressed it to her eyes with her wet hands. The faucet had a squeak too, a quarter-turn of squeak, and she listened to it. She put the lipstick in her pocket. It was hers now. She had not paid for it and would not. She had bought a great deal in this house over the years, in love and worry and obligation, in plane tickets and phone calls and the long slow tending of a woman growing old at a distance, and she would take the lipstick as her commission.

When she came out, Brenda looked at her and said nothing, which was a kindness, and pushed the cash box an inch across the table toward her, restoring her to her post. The afternoon went on. The light moved across the floor and up the wall. The crowd thinned after two and the house was emptier each time Dana looked up, the bones of it showing now, the curtain rods bare, the rooms echoing in the way rooms do when the soft things are gone, the upholstery and the drapes and the rugs that had absorbed the sound of a family for half a century, all carried out, all gone to muffle other lives.

The young couple came back at four with a borrowed pickup and the man's brother, and the three of them took the bedroom set apart and carried it out piece by piece, the mattress, the box spring, the rails, the headboard, the dresser walked out by two of them with the drawers removed and carried separately, and finally the vanity with its round mirror, and Dana watched her mother's face go out the front door, or the place where her mother's face had so often been, the mirror throwing the ceiling and then the doorframe and then the gray October sky as they angled it down the steps. The girl carried the small drawer, the shallow one from the vanity, the one that had held bobby pins and the lipstick's many predecessors. The screen door clapped behind them, two beats, the small chasing the large.

"You folks take care," Brenda called.

"Thank you," the girl called back. "It's really beautiful furniture. Thank you."

Then they were gone, the truck backing out of the driveway her father had poured, and the bedroom was a bedroom-shaped emptiness at the end of the hall, and Dana wrote in her notebook, ninety dollars, bedroom set, and under it she drew a line, because that was the one that mattered, that was the big piece, the heart of the house gone down the front steps in the arms of strangers who would make love in that bed and conceive more children in it and lay their babies across it to change them and grow old in it themselves if they were lucky, and the maple would hold them too, would hold them all, generations of strangers, the way it had held her people, indifferent and faithful, the way furniture is faithful, the way a house is, outlasting everyone who ever called it theirs.

By five it was nearly done. Brenda totaled the box and counted out Dana's share onto the kitchen table, the unmarked table, in soft worn bills and a heavy sleeve of coins, and it came to less than four hundred dollars, the contents of a life, minus Brenda's percentage, minus the cost of the contractor bags for what was left. The leftovers sat in heaps in each room, the unsellable remainder, and a man with a truck was coming Monday to haul it to the dump or the donation center, Brenda would arrange it, it was part of the service.

"You keeping the table?" Brenda asked, nodding at it, the only thing in the kitchen now with no sticker, no buyer, no fate assigned.

Dana looked at it. The Formica top, the chrome legs gone dull, the burn mark near the edge where someone had set a hot pan down sometime in the sixties and her mother had scolded somebody, her father probably, and the chip in the corner, and the place where the pattern had worn away to white from forty thousand wiped-down meals.

"No," she said. "I can't take it back on the plane."

"I can sell it next time. Or it goes Monday."

"It goes Monday," Dana said, and put the lipstick deeper in her pocket, where it was, where it would stay, and signed Brenda's clipboard where the orange sticker showed her where, and walked out the front door of the house for the last time as someone who had any claim on it. The screen door clapped behind her. Two beats. The small one chasing the large, all the way down, until it caught.
