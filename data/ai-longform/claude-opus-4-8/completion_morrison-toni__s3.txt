He knew this region, had visited it twice before, but never in the smothering fog of a coastal morning. He preferred the autumn, when the air was sharp and the trails dry, when a man could see a quarter mile and judge the intentions of whoever shared the road. But the summons had come in late spring, and business did not wait on a man's preferences. The letter, creased and damp from his pocket now, had been brief and oddly formal, written by a clerk in a hand too fine to be the planter's own. Senhor D'Ortega requested the honor of his presence at Jublio to settle a matter of mutual concern. Mutual concern. Jacob turned the phrase over as Regina picked her way along the trail. A debt was what it meant. A debt owed to him by a man too proud to write the word.

He let the horse find her footing while his mind ranged ahead. He had no taste for the South, none for its swagger and its appetites, its men who would not lift a cup to their own lips if a slave stood near enough to do it for them. But he had lent money, and the man had not paid, and a creditor who would not travel to collect what he was owed deserved to lose it. So Jacob rode. The trail wound through stands of pine and live oak hung with gray moss, and here and there the trees opened on cleared land where tobacco stood in ragged rows, the leaves broad and pale green and shivering though there was no wind. He saw figures bent among the plants, dark and stooped, and did not look at them long.

By midday the fog had burned off entirely and the heat came down like a flat hand. He stopped at a creek to let Regina drink and to chew the bread and cheese he carried wrapped in cloth. The water ran clear over a bed of orange clay, and small fish darted in the shallows, quick silver thoughts. He drank from his cupped hands and splashed his face and neck, and felt for a moment almost at peace, the way a man feels when the work he has set himself is half done and the doing of it has gone smoothly. He thought of his farm far to the north, the house he meant to build, the wife he had sent for from England and married barely knowing her, Rebekka of the steady hands and the long silences that he had come, over the years, to understand were not coldness but a kind of waiting. He thought of the child they had buried and the children who had followed it into the ground, and of the daughter alive now, Patrician, five years old and fond of his beard. He did not let himself think long on the dead ones. A man could go mad walking the rows of small graves in his mind.

He mounted again and rode on. In the afternoon the trail widened to a road, rutted by cartwheels, and he passed a wagon drawn by oxen and driven by a white man with a face burnt the color of brick, who raised a hand but did not speak. Jacob raised his in return. The road climbed a low rise, and from the top he saw at last the place he had come for.

Jublio. The planter had named it, Jacob supposed, for jubilation, though it was a strange thing to name a place for joy and then fill it with misery. The house stood at the end of an avenue of trees, white and tall and double-galleried, with windows enough for a church and a great fanlight over the door. It rose out of the cleared land like something dreamed rather than built, and around it spread the fields and the outbuildings and the low huts where the labor lived, and beyond all of it the river, brown and slow, sliding toward the sea. Jacob sat his horse at the top of the rise and looked at the house a long time. He was not a man given to envy, or thought he was not, but something moved in him at the sight of it, some hunger he did not entirely recognize and was not entirely ashamed of. To build a thing like that. To stand at the top of your own avenue and look down it and know that every tree and every brick and every bent back in the fields was yours. He shook the thought off as he might a fly and clicked his tongue at Regina and rode down toward the house.

A boy ran out to take the horse, a slight black child who would not meet Jacob's eye, and another led him up the wide steps and into a hall cool and dim after the glare of the road. The floor was laid in some pale polished stone, and a staircase curved up into shadow, and there were paintings on the walls of pale-faced men and women in ruffs and lace who looked down on Jacob with the same incurious disdain the brick-faced man on the road had shown. He was made to wait. He stood with his damp satchel and his road-stained boots in that cool and elegant hall and waited, and the waiting was itself a message, and he received it, and his jaw tightened.

When D'Ortega came at last he came smiling, arms spread, as though Jacob were a brother long absent rather than a creditor at the door. He was a handsome man gone soft, dressed in green silk and silver lace despite the heat, his hair curled and oiled, a great ring on his finger and a smaller one in his ear. He smelled of orange flowers and beneath that of something less pleasant. "Senhor Vaark," he said, and his English was good, accented and musical. "You honor my house. You have traveled far and you are tired. Come, come, you will refresh yourself, you will eat, and then we will talk of business, yes? Among gentlemen there is always time."

Jacob did not think of himself as a gentleman and did not think D'Ortega thought him one either, but he let himself be led into a room where a table had been set, and he ate, because he was hungry and because the food was good and there was no sense refusing it. There was fish in a sauce the color of saffron, and bread soft and white, and a roasted fowl, and wine that D'Ortega's man poured without being asked and refilled before the glass was empty. D'Ortega talked. He talked of the harvest, of the price of tobacco, of the perfidy of the Dutch and the laziness of the English and the cunning of the natives, of his wife who was unwell and his children who were many, of the new laws and how they would set the country to rights at last. He talked and ate and drank and watched Jacob with eyes that smiled while the rest of his face did, and Jacob ate and listened and watched back and said little, because he had learned long ago that a man who talks much in a bargain has already begun to give ground.

When the meal was done D'Ortega leaned back and laced his beringed fingers over his belly and sighed as though the next thing he had to say grieved him. "Now," he said. "The matter. You will forgive me, Senhor, if I am direct. It is the way of you English and I have learned to admire it. I cannot pay you."

Jacob said nothing. He had expected this and the expectation did not lessen the heat that rose in him.

"The cargo was lost," D'Ortega went on, spreading his hands. "You will have heard. The ship took fever. Of two hundred and forty, we lost half before we made the harbor, and of those who lived many were too weak to sell or sold for a song. Ruin, Senhor. Catastrophe. The insurers cheat me, the captain is a thief, and I am left with nothing but debts and the wreck of a fortune." He shook his head slowly, mournfully, and the great ring caught the light. "I tell you this as a man of honor tells the truth even when it shames him. I cannot give you the money. It does not exist."

"You owe me," Jacob said. His voice came out flatter and colder than he had meant it to, and he was glad of it.

"I owe you, yes. I do not deny it. A debt of honor and I am a man of honor." D'Ortega leaned forward. "And because I am a man of honor I will not see you leave empty-handed. I will pay you in kind. I have land—but land you do not want, so far from your own. I have goods, but goods rot and break. I have"—he gestured at the room, the house, the avenue of trees beyond the window—"a great enterprise, and the means of that enterprise. I will give you a portion of my labor. Strong men, seasoned, worth far more than the trifle I owe you. Come. Let us go down. You will choose."

Jacob's first impulse was to refuse, and the refusal nearly reached his lips. He was not a trader in men. He had built his small fortune in the honest cold of the north, in timber and furs and the careful lending of money and the slow patient improvement of land, and he held a quiet contempt, half-formed and never spoken aloud, for the men of the South and their reliance on flesh. It was not that he thought the practice a sin, exactly. He thought it a weakness, a softness, a way of getting wealth without the labor that gave wealth its meaning. A man who would not work his own land or pay a free hand to work it seemed to Jacob a man already half rotted, like D'Ortega himself, fat and perfumed and unable to pour his own wine.

But there was the debt. And there was the long ride back to nothing if he refused. And there was, he admitted to himself with a small inward grimace, a curiosity he did not like in himself but could not deny. So he rose and let D'Ortega lead him out of the cool house and into the heat, and they walked down past the kitchen and the smokehouse and the stables to a cleared yard of beaten earth where a row of figures stood waiting in the sun, having been made to wait, Jacob understood, as he himself had been made to wait, the whole machinery of the place running on the principle that some must always be kept standing while others sat.

They were men, mostly. Black, of varying ages, stripped to the waist, some bearing the marks of branding and of the lash, all of them sheened with sweat and motionless under the eye of an overseer who stood to one side with a coiled whip and a face that expressed nothing at all. D'Ortega walked the line like a host presenting his cellar, naming the virtues of each. This one a smith, this one good with horses, this one able to read a little and speak three tongues, this one strong as two men though slow of wit. He had the overseer make them turn, made them open their mouths so Jacob could see the teeth, made one lift a sack of grain to show his back did not pain him. Jacob looked, and looked away, and looked again, and felt the heat in his face that was not all the sun.

He did not want any of them. That was the truth of it, and the truth confused him. He had come for money and was being offered men, and the offer revolted him in a way he could not have explained to D'Ortega and did not try to. But to refuse outright was to walk away with nothing, and Jacob Vaark had never in his life walked away from a thing he was owed. So he stood in the yard in the falling light and tried to do the arithmetic of it, to think of these men as figures in a ledger, so much owed, so much repaid, and could not make the figures come out clean, because the figures breathed and sweated and one of them, the slow strong one, was weeping silently, the tears running down through the dust on his face, and would not stop.

"I have no use for field hands," Jacob said finally. "My farm is small. I work it myself. I have one woman to help my wife and a man for the heavy labor. I do not want these."

D'Ortega's smile thinned. "Then what do you want, Senhor? I have offered you the best of what I have."

"I want my money."

"Which I do not have. We have settled this."

They stood looking at each other in the yard, and the overseer watched them both, and the row of men watched the ground at their feet, and the light came slanting gold and long across the cleared land as it had come gold and thick through the morning's fog, and Jacob thought that he had come through one kind of gold into another, and that neither was the kind a man could put in his pocket.

It was then that he saw the woman. She had come from the direction of the kitchen, carrying something, and she stopped at the edge of the yard when she saw the men assembled, and stood holding her burden, which was a child, a small girl, half hidden in the woman's skirts. The woman was thin and her face was set in a stillness deeper than the overseer's, a stillness that was not absence of feeling but the iron suppression of it. And the girl beside her, the little one—Jacob's eye caught and held on her without his deciding it should. She was perhaps seven or eight, too thin, her bare feet wrong somehow, and she looked at the assembled men and at the white men talking and her face was the face of a child who has learned that the world is a thing that happens to her and over which she has no say. But there was something in it too, some watchfulness, some flicker of a self not yet entirely beaten down, that pulled at Jacob in a way he could not name.

The woman saw him looking. Jacob felt her see it. And something changed in her face—not softening, the opposite of softening, a kind of terrible decision. She stepped forward, out of the shade, into the yard where she had no business being, and the overseer turned and made a sound and lifted the whip a little, and D'Ortega snapped something at her in Portuguese, and she stopped but she did not retreat, and she looked at Jacob across the beaten earth with an urgency so naked that he was ashamed to meet it and could not look away.

She held the little girl forward. Just slightly. A small movement of her arm, an offering, and she said a word—he did not know the word, it was no language he knew—but the meaning came through clear as a struck bell. *Take her.* The woman's eyes said it. *Not me. Her. Take this one.*

D'Ortega was angry now, his soft handsome face flushed, ordering her away, and the overseer was moving toward her, and Jacob, who did not know why he did it, who would wonder at it for years and never wholly understand it, heard himself say: "That one. The girl. I'll take the girl."

The yard went still in a different way. D'Ortega turned to him with an expression of frank astonishment. "The child? Senhor, she is nothing. A scrap. She cannot do the work of a grown man, she—you cannot mean it. She is not worth a tenth of what I owe."

"I'll take her against a portion of the debt," Jacob said. "And we'll settle the rest some other way. A note. Goods. I don't care. But I'll take the girl."

The woman had not understood the English but she understood the outcome, and what happened in her face then was the thing Jacob would carry. It was not joy. It was nothing so simple as relief. It was the look of a person who has thrown her whole self against a locked door and felt it give, and who knows that what lies beyond it is unknown and may be no better, but who has done the only thing left to her to do for the one she could not save any other way. She looked at Jacob and there was a charge in the look, a transfer, a burden passed from her hands to his, and he felt it land on him with a weight all out of proportion to the slight body of the child. She had not asked him to save the girl from anything. She had asked him to *take* her, which was a different thing, a more desperate thing, and he understood dimly that the mother saw in him not a rescuer but only a door, a way out, a road leading somewhere that was not here.

D'Ortega protested and bargained and at length agreed, because he had no choice and because the girl truly was worth little to him and the woman's outburst had embarrassed him before a guest. They went back up to the cool house and a note was drawn up by the fine-handed clerk, and Jacob signed it, and D'Ortega signed it, and the debt was reckoned and a portion struck off against the body of the child and the remainder set down as owing. It was full dark by the time it was done. Jacob was given a bed for the night, a fine bed in a fine room with a window onto the river, and he lay in it and did not sleep. He heard the sounds of the great house settling and somewhere far off the sound of a woman singing, low, in that language he did not know, and he did not know whether it was the mother and he did not let himself think on it.

In the morning he rode out early, the child mounted before him on Regina, wrapped in a man's shirt against the cold of the fog, for the fog had come back, sun-fired and golden, and they went down the avenue of trees and onto the road and into the gold. The girl did not weep. She did not speak. She sat rigid within the circle of his arm and looked at the world the fog gave up to her piece by piece, the road, the trees, the brown river when it showed, and Jacob looked at the top of her small dark head and felt the strangeness of the thing he had done settle into him like cold water into cloth, slow and complete.

He had not wanted a slave. He had told D'Ortega the truth: his farm was small, he worked it himself, he had no use for hands. And a child this size was no help to anyone, was a mouth and not a pair of hands, was a thing he would have to feed and clothe and shelter for years before she could earn her keep. By every measure he knew, by the cold honest arithmetic he prided himself on, he had made a foolish bargain. He had let himself be moved. He had looked into a yard full of men he could not use and chosen instead a child he could not use either, and he had done it because a woman with iron in her face had held the child out to him and said *take her* in a language he did not know, and he had not been able to refuse.

Rebekka would understand, he told himself. Rebekka had wanted a girl. Rebekka, who had borne and buried and borne and buried, who walked the small graves in her mind as he did, who would look at this thin dark child and see not a bad bargain but a small life delivered out of that place into hers. Perhaps that was true. Perhaps it was even the reason, the real one, hidden under all the others. He thought of the house he meant to build, grander than the one he had, though never so grand as Jublio—he would not want it so grand as Jublio, he told himself, and was not sure he believed it. A man could build a fine house without becoming the kind of man who sat in it and let another pour his wine. He believed that. He needed to believe it.

The girl shivered and Jacob drew the shirt closer around her, an awkward gesture, the gesture of a man unused to children not his own, and the girl did not flinch from him and did not lean into him either, but held herself in that rigid stillness that was, he would come to learn, her way, the way she had learned in that place. He did not know her name yet. He had not thought to ask and D'Ortega had not thought to say. He would call her something, or Rebekka would, or she would tell them herself when she had words for it. For now she was only a small weight and a small warmth and a small witness, riding north out of the gold and into the rest of her life, which was now bound up with his, the two of them carried forward together by a choice he had made and did not understand and would not, in the time left to him, ever take back.

The fog began to thin as the sun climbed. Ahead, where the road bent, the live oaks loomed and then resolved, and beyond them lay the village and the wharf and the long way home. Jacob clicked his tongue and Regina quickened, and the child's head turned to watch the world come clear.
