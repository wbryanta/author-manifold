The water had quit in the night and by morning the orchard stood in a stillness like the stillness of things abandoned. Dell walked the rows with the dew darkening the cuffs of his trousers and he stopped at the head of the line where the standpipe rose from the dirt like some iron relic of a vanished people and he laid his hand upon it and felt no tremor of water in the pipe and knew that it had failed.

He stood a long while. The trees went away from him in their crooked files toward the eastern fence and beyond the fence the brown hills lay folded one upon another in the early light and there was no wind. The apricots hung small and hard among the gray leaves. A great many of them had not set at all and the limbs where they should have been were bare and the leaves curling at their edges as if touched by some slow fire that gave off neither smoke nor heat.

He went back to the house across the dry ground and his boots raised a fine dust that hung behind him and did not settle. The house sat low and white under the one cottonwood their grandfather had planted and the paint had gone the color of bone on the south wall. He climbed the steps and stood in the kitchen doorway.

Waters quit, he said.

His brother sat at the table with a cup of coffee gone cold before him and a ledger open and the figures in it written in a hand cramped and faint. Carl did not look up.

Quit how, he said.

Quit altogether. Theres no water in the line.

Carl closed the ledger with one finger holding his place. He was the elder by six years and his hair had gone early to gray and his face was burned dark and lined and there was in it a kind of patience that was not patience but its exhaustion.

Pump or the line, he said.

Dont know yet.

We'll have to walk it.

We'll have to walk it, Dell said.

They ate without speaking. The eggs and the bread and the coffee that Carl had made and that was bitter from sitting on the stove. The light came up yellow in the window over the sink and lay across the linoleum and across the worn place by the door where three generations of men had stamped the mud from their boots. When they were done Dell carried the plates to the sink and Carl rose and took his hat from the nail by the door and set it on his head.

You aim to fix it, Dell said.

What else would I aim to do.

Dell said nothing. He dried his hands on the dishcloth and hung it on the oven door and followed his brother out into the morning.

They loaded the truck from the shed. Wrenches and a hacksaw and a coil of new line still bearing the price tag and couplings and a can of solvent and a roll of tape and a shovel each. They worked without words for they had loaded this truck a thousand times and there was nothing to say about it. Carl drove and they went down the lane with the dust boiling up behind and they came to the pumphouse that stood at the low corner of the property where the ditch ran and Carl shut the engine off and they sat a moment in the ticking quiet.

If it's the pump we're done, Carl said.

We've been done before.

Not like this.

They got out. The pumphouse was a structure of corrugated tin gone to rust and the door hung on one hinge and inside it was dark and smelled of grease and of the cool damp of the concrete pad. Carl crouched at the motor and put his hand to it and it was cold. He pulled the cover and looked at the wiring and Dell stood in the doorway with the light behind him and watched.

Bearings, Carl said.

You sure.

I'm sure of the sound it was making. It's been making it three weeks. He sat back on his heels. We can pull it and take it to Espinoza and he can press new bearings in it if he's got them and if he aint we wait on the order.

How long.

Week maybe. Two.

Two weeks no water.

Carl stood and wiped his hands down the front of his trousers. The trees dont care what we want, he said. They want water and we got none to give them and that's the size of it.

They pulled the motor. It was heavy and awkward and they worked it loose from the coupling with the wrenches and lifted it between them grunting and set it in the bed of the truck and stood breathing. The sun was up over the hills now and the heat coming on. Carl took off his hat and wiped his forehead with his wrist and put the hat back.

Espinoza first, he said. Then we'll come back and walk the line. There's leaks in it I'd bet money. There always is.

They drove into town with the dead motor in the bed. The road ran straight along the canal and the canal was low and green and the cottonwoods along it threw a broken shade across the water. They passed the Talley place where the trees had been pushed up two summers back and burned and the ground disced and replanted to almonds for the corporation out of the valley and the young trees stood in their straight rows with the drip line laid black along them and every tree alive and dark green and watered to the inch.

Dell looked at them as they passed.

Talley did all right, he said.

Talley sold, Carl said. There's a difference between doing all right and selling.

He didnt burn them himself.

No. He took the check and let them do it. Stood in his yard I'd reckon and watched the smoke.

Dell was quiet a while. Then he said, There's worse things.

Carl didnt answer.

Espinoza had the bearings. He was a small man with hands like a much larger man and he looked at the motor on the bench and turned the shaft with his fingers and made a sound in his throat and said he could have it by Thursday. Carl asked could he do it sooner and Espinoza said he could do it Thursday and that was sooner than anybody else would do it and Carl thanked him and they left the motor and drove back out along the canal in the white heat of the forenoon.

They did not speak of the trees again until they were back at the orchard and walking the line.

The line ran underground at the head and surfaced where the ground fell away and then it was laid along the rows in the old galvanized pipe their father had put down forty years gone. They walked it foot by foot and Dell carried the shovel over his shoulder and Carl walked ahead with his head bent watching the dirt for the dark stain of seepage. The ground was dry as ash. They found the first leak a hundred yards down where a coupling had rusted through and the pipe there was thin as paper and Carl knelt and dug around it with his hands and then with the shovel and uncovered the joint.

We'll cut it out, he said.

Dell handed down the hacksaw. Carl set the blade to the pipe and drew it back and forth and the rasp of it carried in the still air and the iron filings fell bright into the dirt. He cut on either side of the bad joint and lifted the rotten section free and held it up and looked through it at the sky.

Forty years, he said.

That's a long time for pipe.

It's a long time for anything. He set the section down. Hand me the new piece.

They cut the new line and fit it and Dell held the couplings while Carl wound the tape and they wrenched the fittings down and Dell stood with his thumb over the cut end and felt the cool of the metal and the absence of water within it like a held breath.

That's one, Carl said.

They went on. The sun stood higher and the heat lay on the orchard like a hand pressed down. The leaves did not move. Down the long rows the light fell dappled and shifting through the canopy where there was canopy and fell hard and white where there was not and the dead trees stood among the living ones with their bark cracked and their limbs gray and brittle and reaching.

They found another leak and then another. At the third Carl sat back in the dirt and was a long time getting up and Dell saw it.

You want me to dig the next one.

I can dig.

I didnt say you couldnt.

I can dig, Carl said again, and he got up.

At noon they sat in the shade of a tree that still bore leaves and ate the sandwiches Dell had made and drank water from a jug gone warm. The bread was dry and the meat in it thin. They ate and looked out at the trees and neither spoke for a time and then Dell said it because it had to be said and there was no use waiting on it.

Henley called again, he said.

Carl chewed and swallowed and looked at the jug in his hand.

Did he.

He did. Tuesday. While you were in to the doctor.

What did he say.

He said the same. He said they'll go to nine hundred the acre. He said it dont stay on the table forever. He said they got other ground they're looking at.

Carl nodded slowly. Nine hundred, he said.

That's near three hundred thousand, Dell.

I can do the arithmetic.

It's a lot of money.

It's a lot of money for ground that's been in this family since before there was a road to it. He set the jug down in the dirt. My grandfather cleared this with a mule and a stoneboat. He dug the first ditch with a shovel. You know that. I've told you that.

You've told me.

There's men in the ground at the cemetery in town that this was the whole of their lives. That this was everything they had and everything they made.

Dell looked at his hands. They're in the ground, he said. They cant water trees.

Carl turned and looked at him and his face was hard. What does that mean.

It means they're dead, Carl. It means the dead dont care and we're the ones living and we're the ones out here cutting forty year old pipe out of the dirt because the pump's gone and we got no money to buy a new one let alone replant. It means there's no apricots this year worth picking and there wasnt hardly any last year and the year before that we lost money. We lost money working ourselves half to death. That's what it means.

Carl was quiet.

I'm not trying to be hard about it, Dell said, softer now. I'm not. It's just I look at it different than you. You see what it was. I see what it is.

And what is it.

It's dying, Dell said. You walk these rows and you see it. Half the trees gone or going. The water failing. The ground gone to salt down at the low end where it's been irrigated sixty years. It's tired, Carl. The land's tired. We're tired. There comes a time.

Carl said nothing for a long while. A jay came and lit in the tree above them and looked down at them with its head cocked and then flew off across the rows. The heat shimmered over the open ground.

Where would you go, Carl said.

I dont know.

You dont know.

I'd find something. There's work. There's always work for a man that'll do it.

Doing what. Working for some other man. Driving a truck. Standing behind a counter someplace.

If it come to that.

You'd stand behind a counter, Carl said, and there was something almost like wonder in it, almost like grief. You'd take three hundred thousand dollars and you'd let them push these trees up and burn them and disc this ground under and plant it to almonds for some company in Fresno that's never set foot on it and you'd go stand behind a counter someplace.

I'd be able to sleep, Dell said. That's worth something. You dont sleep. I hear you up nights. You dont sleep and you wont go to the doctor but for the once and then you wont tell me what he said.

Carl looked away.

What did he say, Dell said.

He said I'm fine.

He didnt say you're fine.

He said what he said. Carl stood up and brushed the dirt from his trousers. We got line to walk.

They walked it. The afternoon came on and the heat with it and they found leaks and cut them out and fit new pipe and the new pipe shone among the old like something that did not belong. They worked down the rows toward the low end where the ground went pale with salt and the trees there were the worst of them, half of them dead outright and standing gray and the others struggling with their leaves yellowed and small. Carl knelt to dig at a joint and he was slow getting down and slower digging and Dell watched him and saw the sweat stand out on his face and saw him stop and put his hand flat against his chest and hold it there.

Carl.

I'm all right.

You're not all right.

It's the heat. Carl knelt there with his hand to his chest and his head down and his breath coming slow. Just the heat, he said. Give me a minute.

Dell crouched beside him. He put his hand on his brother's shoulder and felt it rise and fall.

That's what he told you, Dell said. Aint it. The doctor. He told you about your heart.

Carl didnt answer. After a time he took his hand from his chest and picked up the shovel again.

He told you and you come out here and pull a motor and walk a mile of line in a hundred degrees of heat. Dell's voice was not angry. It was something past anger. You'd kill yourself for it. You'd lay down in this dirt and die for it.

There's worse places to die, Carl said.

He said it quiet and he said it without drama and Dell looked at him and understood that he meant it, that it was not a thing said to win the argument but a thing he held to be simply and plainly true, and Dell felt something turn over in him that he could not name.

You really would, Dell said.

This is where I'm from, Carl said. He set the shovel down and sat back in the salt-white dirt and looked out at the dead trees and the living ones and the brown hills beyond. A man's got to be from somewhere. He's got to belong to some piece of ground or he dont belong to anything. I dont know how to be from anyplace but here. I dont know how to want anything but this. You ask me where would I go and I'm telling you true there's no place. There's no place for me that aint this place. You take this and there's nothing left that's me.

Dell sat down in the dirt beside him. The two of them sat there in the heat with the dead orchard around them and said nothing for a long while.

I dont want you to die, Dell said finally.

I know it.

I dont. You're all I got. Ma's gone and Pa's gone and there's no wives and no children for either one of us, we waited too long and worked too hard and now there's just us, just the two of us out here, and I'll be damned if I'll watch you kill yourself for a stand of dying trees. I'd rather have you. I'd rather have you breathing and standing behind some counter someplace and miserable than have you in the ground out here happy. That's the truth of it. That's the whole of it.

Carl looked at him.

You never said that, Carl said.

I'm saying it now.

A wind came up out of the west, the first wind of the day, and it moved through the orchard and the gray leaves stirred and turned and a low sound went through the trees like the sound of water though there was no water in them. They sat and listened to it.

When my grandfather was dying, Carl said, I was a boy. You wasnt born yet. He was in the back room of the house and it was summer like this and they had the windows open and you could hear the irrigation running, you could hear the water in the ditches, it was night and the pump was going and the water running and he could hear it from his bed. And he said to me. He called me in and I stood by the bed and he was near gone, he could hardly talk, and he said you hear that. And I said hear what. And he said the water. He said that's the finest sound there is. He said a man works his whole life so he can lay in his bed at the end of it and hear the water running on his own ground. He said that's all it ever was. That's the whole of what it was for.

Dell said nothing.

And I've thought about that my whole life, Carl said. Every night I've stood out here and listened to the water in the dark I've thought about that. And now there's no water. There's no sound. I laid in my bed last night and there was nothing, and I knew the pump had quit before you ever told me, I knew it in the night because the sound was gone, and I laid there and I thought, this is what the end of it sounds like. It sounds like nothing. It sounds like this.

The wind died and the leaves went still.

We could fix the pump, Dell said. Espinoza'll have it Thursday. We could fix the pump and we could limp it through this season and the next. We could. But it dont change what's coming, Carl. The water table's dropping. You know it is. Henley's people will sink a well three hundred foot if they have to, they got the money to do it, but we dont, and when this well goes dry, and it will, we're done and we got nothing. No trees and no land worth selling and no money. They wont be at nine hundred the acre then. They wont be at anything.

I know it, Carl said.

So.

So, Carl said. He picked up a handful of the white dirt and let it run through his fingers. So you're telling me we sell it while there's something to sell. While there's a man wanting to buy it. You're telling me we take the money and I find someplace to be from for whatever's left of my life and you find some other thing to do with yours.

I'm telling you I dont want to bury you out here.

Carl was quiet a long time. The sun was sliding down toward the hills now and the light going gold and long and the shadows of the trees reaching out across the broken ground.

Finish the line, he said.

What.

The line. We started it. We'll finish it. We'll fix every leak and we'll put the motor back Thursday and we'll run the water one more time. And I want to stand out here at night and hear it. One more time I want to hear the water running on this ground. And then. He stopped. And then we'll talk to Henley. We'll see what he's got to say. I'm not saying yes. I'm saying we'll talk to him.

You mean it, he said.

I mean we'll talk to him. I mean I'll listen. That's as far as I can go today. That's the farthest I can go.

They got up. Carl got up slow and Dell put a hand under his arm and Carl let him and that was a thing he would not have done in the morning and they both knew it. They walked the rest of the line in the failing light and they found two more leaks and they cut them out and fit the new pipe and by the time they finished the sun was gone behind the hills and the sky above them had gone the color of a bruise, violet and gray and a band of dull red low in the west.

They stood at the head of the line by the standpipe and looked back the way they had come, down the long rows into the gathering dark, the new fittings of bright galvanized pipe catching the last of the light here and there along the run so that the line was marked by points of cold fire down the length of the orchard, the places they had mended, the places they had cut out the old dead iron and put in the new, and it ran on like that into the dark until you could not see it anymore.

Thursday, Carl said.

Thursday, said Dell.

They put the tools in the truck and drove up the lane to the house and the dust rose behind them and hung in the last light and did not settle. In the house Dell made supper and they ate it and Carl went to his bed early and Dell sat up at the kitchen table with the ledger open before him and the cramped faint figures running down the page and he looked at them a long time and did not write anything and at last he closed the book and turned off the light and sat in the dark and listened, out of some old habit, for the sound of water on the ground, and there was none, and he sat there in the silence a while longer and then he went to bed.
