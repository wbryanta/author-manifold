They came down through the orchard in the grey light before the sun with their shovels over their shoulders and the dog trotting ahead of them through the wet grass. The trees stood in their rows gone leggy and untended and some of them dead these several years and standing yet with their black limbs against the paling sky like things that had walked out of a fire and stopped there. Dew lay on everything. The older brother carried a coil of pipe slung across his chest and it knocked at his ribs as he walked and he shifted it and said nothing. The younger walked behind. They went down to the headgate where the ditch ran from the creek and the water stood low and brown in it and trash caught against the slats.

Cut off up at the box, the older one said. We'll find the break before we waste the day diggin where there aint nothin wrong.

The younger went up the bank and turned the valve and the standing water in the ditch went slack and then began to draw down into the dark of the line where it bled off through the trees. They stood watching it.

It's somewheres past the second row, the older said. You can see the wet.

They walked the line. The ground along the pipe ran dark and soft where the water had gone up out of the broken place and lay in the low spots and the grass there stood greener and ranker than the grass about it. The dog went to it and drank and the older brother whistled and the dog left off and came on.

Here, he said.

They set their shovels and began to dig. The dirt came up black and heavy with the wet and they laid it back from the trench in a low wall and worked down on either side until the pipe showed pale and slick in the dark earth like something unearthed that had died there. The break was a long split in the old galvanized where the rust had eaten it through and the metal had given and curled back at the edges. The older brother crouched and ran his thumb along it.

This whole line's done, he said. We patch this and she'll go someplace else inside a month.

Then we patch that too.

The older one looked up at him and then back at the pipe. He did not say anything. He took the hacksaw from where he'd laid it in the grass and began to cut back to good metal and the saw rasped in the morning quiet and the filings came down bright onto the black dirt. The younger stood holding his shovel and watching.

You could run pvc through here, he said. Be done with it.

You could. With money you aint got.

I got some.

You got some. The older brother stopped sawing and wiped his face with the back of his wrist. You got enough to do this one line. Then they's the laterals and the main comin off the creek and the box that's half rotted out and the pump that Daddy never did get to run right. You want to do all that you go on. I'll hold your coat.

The younger said nothing. He set the blade of his shovel in the dirt and stood on it and pried at a stone and lifted it out and threw it back into the grass. The sun was coming up now over the eastern ridge and the light came level through the orchard and laid the long shadows of the trees out across the wet ground and where it touched the dew it smoked faintly. The dog had gone off and lay in a patch of sun with its chin on its paws watching them.

You been talkin to that man from Las Cruces, the younger said.

I have.

What did he say.

He said what they all say. He said it's good ground that's been let go and he said it could be made into somethin and he said he'd pay a fair price for the chance to find out.

How much.

The older brother cut the last of the metal through and the cut piece came loose and he set it aside and reached down into the trench and worked the pipe to see how much play was in it. Enough, he said. Enough to clear what's owed on it and have some left over for the both of us. Enough to start somewheres that aint this.

This is where we started.

I know where we started. I was there same as you.

He took up the coupling and the new section of pipe he'd cut to length and he fitted them and worked them home and tightened the bands with the nutdriver and his hands moved over the work without his looking at them the way a man's hands will move over a thing he has done ten thousand times. The younger watched him.

Granddaddy planted these trees, he said.

He did. And he's dead. And Daddy run em near twenty year and lost money sixteen of em and he's dead too. You want to make it three?

That aint fair.

The older brother sat back on his heels. Fair, he said. He looked off down the rows. There aint no fair in it. The water comes or it dont. The price is up or it's down. The trees make or they dont and mostly anymore they dont because they're old and we aint got the money to put in new ones and if we did it'd be seven year before a young tree paid us back a dime and I aint got seven year of throwin good money after bad in me. Neither do you. You just dont know it yet.

They finished the joint and the older one stood and stretched his back and they stood looking down into the trench at the new pipe lying pale among the old.

Turn her on, he said. Let's see if she holds.

The younger went up to the box and turned the valve and came back. They heard the water come down the line with a low rushing and then it reached the place and stood and they watched the joint. A bead formed at the band and hung and fell and another formed.

Snug it, the older said.

He'd already gone down on his knees. He gave the band another quarter turn and the seep stopped and they watched and it held. The water went on past them down the line and somewhere down the rows it would be coming up out of the next bad place but neither of them spoke of that. They began to fill the trench. They shoveled the black dirt back in over the pipe and tamped it with their boots and the work warmed them and they shed their jackets and laid them over a low limb and went on.

The sun climbed. By midmorning it was hot and the dew had burned off and the smell of the orchard came up around them, the green smell of the rank grass and the older deeper smell of the trees and the dirt and somewhere the sweet rot of windfall fruit lying ungathered in the weeds. They worked down the line and found the second break where the older one had said it would be and they dug it out and cut it and fixed it the same as the first and said little while they did it. A jay came and sat in the dead tree above them and scolded and went away.

They went up to the truck at noon and sat in the bed with their legs hanging and ate the sandwiches the younger one's wife had made and drank water from a jug that had gone warm. The dog came and sat below them and the younger gave it the crust of his bread and it took it gently from his fingers and lay down to eat it.

You member when he'd take us out to thin the apples, the younger said.

The older one chewed and looked out across the orchard.

He'd put us each a row, the younger said. And he'd say leave the king and pull the rest. And we never knew which was the king. And he'd come behind us pullin off what we'd left and shakin his head.

I member.

You dont want to member it.

It aint that I dont want to. The older brother folded the wax paper from his sandwich into a small square and pressed the crease of it with his thumb. It's that rememberin it dont change what it is now. You can love a thing and know it's done. Them two aint got to fight each other.

I dont believe that.

I know you dont.

I think if you love a thing you hold to it.

The older one looked at him then. You hold to it, he said. You hold to it past all sense and it takes you down with it. I seen it take Daddy. He held to it and it never once held him back. He set out here on a Sunday afternoon when he couldnt hardly stand and pruned trees that wasnt never goin to fruit again and he done it because his daddy planted em and he couldnt no more let em go than he could let go his own arm. And it didnt make him happy. You think it made him happy?

The younger didnt answer.

It didnt, the older said. It made him bitter and it made him broke and it killed him before his time and the trees dont know the difference. They dont know he's gone. They'd of grew the same for that man from Las Cruces. They'd grow the same for any man cares to water em. They aint loyal. They're just trees.

The younger brother sat with the jug between his hands and looked down at the dog.

You think I dont know all that, he said.

I think you know it and wont say it.

Maybe I aint ready to say it.

The older one nodded slowly. Well, he said. That's honest anyway.

They sat a while. Out past the orchard the land ran off flat and pale toward the mountains and the heat stood up off it in a shimmer so the far ridge swam and trembled like a thing seen through water. A hawk turned high up on the thermals without moving its wings and they watched it go over and out of sight beyond the trees.

I'll tell you what I cant get shut of, the younger said. It aint the trees so much. It's that when it's gone there wont be nothin left that says we was here. You sell it and that man comes and bulldozes them old trees and puts in his pecans or his chiles or whatever it is and in five year there wont be a soul in this valley remembers there was a man named Tillman ever owned this ground or planted a tree on it or buried his wife at the top of it under that big cottonwood. It just goes. Like we was never here at all.

The older brother was quiet a long time.

Mama's up there too, he said.

I know it. I know who's up there.

You sell it that grave goes with it. That's what you're sayin and dont want to say it.

It aint sold yet.

No, the older said. It aint.

He climbed down out of the truck bed and stood and tipped the last of the warm water out of the jug onto the ground and screwed the cap back on and set it in the bed.

Come on, he said. We got the main to look at yet and I want to be off this hill before dark.

They went back down. The afternoon ran long and hot and they worked the main line where it came up out of the creek bottom and found the box rotted as the older one had said and they did what they could with it which was not much and the older brother said as much and the younger didnt argue with him now. Toward evening they stood and looked at it.

It'll get her through this season, the older said. Maybe.

And next year.

Next year aint here yet.

They walked up the rows in the failing light to where the truck stood. The shadows had come around again the other way and lengthened east and the orchard had gone soft and blue in the last of the light and a coolness came down off the gap the way it does in that country when the sun goes even in June. The trees stood as they had stood that morning. The dead ones and the living. The dog ran ahead and stopped and looked back at them and ran on.

At the top of the rise the cottonwood stood by itself bigger than any tree in the orchard with its great pale limbs going up into the dusk and beneath it the two graves with their low stones and the iron fence gone to rust around them and the grass grown up through it. The brothers stopped there the way they always stopped there without saying they would. They took off their hats. The younger one bent and pulled a clump of foxtail that had come up against his mother's stone and threw it aside.

I come up here, the older said. You think I dont. I come up here too.

I never said you didnt.

I know what's here. The older one stood with his hat in his two hands in front of him. I aint cold about it. You think I'm cold about it.

I dont think that.

It's just I can stand here and love em and still know that lettin go of this ground aint the same as lettin go of them. They're not in the ground. The ground's just where we put what was left. You sell the ground we can still come. He'd let us come. I'd put it in the papers that we get to come. Once a year, whenever. He'd not say no to that.

You'd trust that to a paper.

I'd trust it to a paper better than I'd trust this orchard to keep us in a way to come at all. We go broke here, little brother, we lose it anyhow. The bank takes it and they dont put nothin in no paper about who gets to visit. They just take it. And then we lost the trees and the graves both and we got nothin for it but the pleasure of havin held on too long.

The younger brother stood looking at the stones. The light was nearly gone now and the first stars had come out low in the east over the dark shape of the mountains and a bat went over them jerking through the dusk.

Let me think on it, he said.

The older one put his hat back on.

That's all I'm askin, he said. I aint tryin to steal nothin from you. It's half yours and I cant sell it without you and I wouldnt if I could. I just want you to look at it straight. That's all. Look at it with your eyes and not with what you wish was so.

I been lookin at it all day.

I know you have.

The younger pulled his hat down and turned from the graves. The dog had come and sat by the fence and the older brother snapped his fingers and it rose and came to him.

What was the name of that man's place, the younger said. The one he runs down by Mesilla.

The older brother looked at him.

Why, he said.

I just want to know what kind of a man he is. I want to go and look at what he done with that other ground he bought. The one that was a orchard before.

It's pecans now.

I know it's pecans. I want to see is it kept. I want to see does he keep what he buys or does he just take it and let it go like the last man did and the man before him. I'll not sell my mother's grave to a man who lets ground go to ruin.

The older brother stood a moment in the dark and then he nodded though there was scarcely light to see it by.

We'll go Sunday, he said. After church. We'll drive down and look at it.

All right.

It's good ground he's made there. You'll see. He's not a careless man.

We'll see what he is.

We'll see.

They went on to the truck. The younger got in on the passenger side and shut the door and the older stood a moment with his hand on the bed rail looking back down the long dark rows of the orchard he could no longer see, only feel there below him in the dark, the trees standing in their crooked lines the way the old man had set them out with a string and a level fifty years gone and more, and he stood there until the dog whined at his feet and then he opened the door and let the dog up into the cab and got in after it and pulled the door to.

He started the truck and the headlights came on and threw the nearest trees up stark and white and beyond them only dark. He turned the truck in the grass and they went up the track toward the road. In the cab neither of them spoke. The younger brother watched out the side glass at the dark land going by and the older watched the track in the lights and the dog sat between them and panted and now and then leaned its weight against one or the other of them as the truck rocked over the ruts.

When they came out onto the county road the older one stopped and looked both ways though there was nothing for miles either direction and never was.

You want to come eat, the younger said. Sarah's got a roast on.

The older one pulled out onto the blacktop.

I might, he said. I dont want to be no trouble.

It aint no trouble. She set a place. She figured I'd not let you go home hungry after you give me your whole Saturday.

It wasnt nothin to give.

It was somethin, the younger said. Dont say it wasnt.

They drove on. The road ran straight and pale in the lights and the fence posts came up out of the dark and went by and the warm night air came in the windows smelling of creosote and dust and far off the lights of the town stood low and yellow against the black bulk of the mountains.

You know what he'd say, the younger said after a while. Daddy. If he could see us settin here arguin over whether to keep it or sell it.

What would he say.

He'd say we was both fools. He'd say a man holds to his ground come hell or high water and dont ask is it sensible. And then he'd go out and prune them dead trees in the rain and catch his death of it which is just about what he done.

The older brother almost smiled in the dark.

He'd say that, he allowed.

And then the younger said: But he'd of been wrong.

The older one didnt answer right off. They went on down the road and the dog had lain down on the seat between them with its head in the younger one's lap and gone to sleep.

He might of been, the older said. Or he might not. A man can be wrong and right both about the same thing. He was wrong to let it kill him. But he wasnt wrong to love it. I dont reckon a man's ever wrong to love a thing. He's just got to know what it costs him and pay it with his eyes open. That's all I'm sayin. Pay it with your eyes open or dont pay it at all.

The younger brother looked over at him in the dark of the cab where the dashlights caught the side of his face.

When did you get so smart, he said.

I aint smart, the older one said. I'm just tired. They get took for the same thing sometimes.

They came into the edge of the town where the first streetlights stood and the light came washing through the cab pale and then dark and then pale again as they passed beneath them and the dog stirred and resettled and slept on and neither brother spoke again until they turned in at the younger one's house where the windows were lit yellow and warm and a woman's shape moved behind the glass, and they sat a moment in the dark of the cab with the engine ticking before either of them reached for the door.
