The water had stopped sometime in the night and they came down through the orchard in the gray light before the sun with the older brother carrying the shovel across his shoulders and his hands hung over either end of it like a man halfdrawn upon a cross. The trees stood in their rows gone gray and silvered with the want of water and the leaves curled at their edges as if some fire had passed close by them in the dark.

You see where it pooled, the younger one said.

I see it.

They stood at the head of the row where the ground had gone dark and soft and the water lay in a shallow sheet upon it catching the first of the light. Beyond that the furrow ran dry all the way down to the far fence and the dust lay undisturbed in it.

It's broke somewhere up under here, the older brother said. He set the shovel down and toed at the wet ground with his boot. The earth gave and sucked at the leather.

Could be the line itself.

Could be.

They had walked these rows as boys behind their father who walked them behind his own and the trees then were young and the water came when it was called and the whole of it seemed a thing that would go on past any reckoning they could make of it. Now the old man was three years in the ground up the hill under a stone they had set themselves and the trees were dying by the dozen at the dry end of the property where the line could no longer push water and the younger brother had a paper in the truck that a man in town had given him.

The older one knelt and put his hand flat to the mud and held it there as though he might feel through the earth the pulse of whatever bled beneath it.

We dig here, he said.

They dug. The sun came up over the eastern ridge and laid itself long across the orchard and the dew burned off the leaves and the heat came on early the way it did now in these years, coming on in June like it used to come in August. They worked the shovels down through the wet topsoil into the harder clay beneath and the younger brother stopped and leaned on the handle and looked off down the rows.

He'll give us nine hundred an acre, he said.

The older brother did not stop digging.

You hear me.

I heard you.

That's better than we'll see again. Land's not worth that. Not this land. Not anymore.

The older brother brought the blade down and levered up a wad of clay and flung it aside. He says nine hundred now, he said. Wait and see what he says when the papers come round. They always come down off it. Find some fault. Title's bad or the well's no good or there's a lien nobody knew about. He'll come down off it and you'll take it because by then you'll have spent the nine hundred in your head a hundred times over.

I won't.

You will. That's how they do.

They dug on. At a depth of better than two feet the older brother's shovel struck the line and rang dull against it and he scraped away the clay and they looked down at the black pipe lying there in the wet earth like something exhumed.

There she is, the younger brother said.

The older one got down on his knees in the mud and worked his hands along the pipe feeling for the break. The water seeped up cold around his wrists. He worked his way down a length of it and stopped.

Here.

What is it.

Split. Run clean down the side of her. He sat back on his heels and wiped his hands on his jeans. Old PVC. Daddy laid this in what. Eighty-six. Eighty-seven. It's brittle as a eggshell now. You touch it wrong and it'll go someplace else.

The younger brother crouched and looked at the split. A fine line ran the length of the pipe and the water pulsed out of it slow.

So we patch it.

We patch it. The older brother stood. We patch it and it'll hold and then it'll go again somewheres else down the line, and we'll be down here on our knees in the mud again come July. He looked off down the rows toward the dead trees at the bottom of the property. And then again in August.

The younger brother said nothing.

It's all of it like this, the older one said. The whole line. He laid it down forty year ago and it was good for thirty and now it's eaten through and there's not the money to put in new and so we patch it. We patch it and we patch it.

So sell it then, the younger one said.

The older brother looked at him.

You just said it your own self. You said it. We can't keep it up. We can't put in new line. The trees are dying off down there and we're losing money every season we hold it and you stand here and tell me it's all eaten through and then you won't sell. I can't make sense of you.

There's nothing to make sense of.

There's nine hundred an acre.

The older brother bent and gathered up the shovel. They wanted the truck for the patch kit and the primer and the cement and he started back up the row toward where they had left it and the younger one came behind him.

Hold on, he said.

The older brother went on walking.

I said hold on, goddamn it.

He stopped. He turned. The sun was full on them now and the heat lay across the orchard like a hand and the locusts had started up in the trees with their long dry rasping that rose and fell and rose again.

What.

You think I want to, the younger brother said. You think this is something I want.

I don't know what you want. I know what you're fixing to do.

I've got two kids. I've got a note on the house. I drive sixty mile a day to a job I'd as soon walk away from this minute, and at the end of every month I'm scraping. And there's a hundred and sixty acres of dying trees sitting here losing money and a man wants to give us a hundred and forty-four thousand dollars for it, and you want to come down here every June and patch a forty-year-old pipe with your hands in the mud like that's going to bring anything back. The kids are gone, you know. Both of them gone. They don't want it. Yours don't want it any more than mine do. There's nobody coming up behind us to take it on. There's just us and a few more years of patching pipe and then it's the two of us standing in a dead orchard wondering where the time went.

The older brother stood with the shovel and listened to all of it and when it was done he looked off across the trees a while before he spoke.

He's buried up that hill, he said. So's Mama. So's Granddaddy and them. You sell it and that goes too. That goes with the rest of it.

He can't take the graves. There's a thing in the law about it. The cemetery stays. I asked.

You asked.

I asked the man.

The older brother nodded slow. You asked the man, he said. Sat down with him and asked him could he leave Daddy's grave when he comes to push the orchard under and plant it in whatever it is he means to plant. He looked at his brother. What is it he means to plant. Did you ask him that.

Almonds, the younger brother said. He means to put it in almonds.

Almonds.

That's what it is now. That's what's bringing money.

The older brother laughed but there was no humor anywhere in it. Almonds, he said again. Take more water than peaches ever dreamed of and he means to run them in this country with the wells dropping every year and the rivers down to nothing. He'll have the same line in the ground in twenty year and the same split in it and he'll be standing where we're standing. Only it won't be us. It'll be some man we never knew patching a pipe over the top of Daddy's bones.

They went on up to the truck. The older brother set the shovel in the bed and opened the toolbox bolted behind the cab and took out the patch kit and the cans and a hacksaw and a rag and he carried these back down the row and the younger one followed him and neither of them said anything for a while.

At the hole the older brother got down again on his knees and took the hacksaw and began to cut out the split section of pipe. The blade rasped and the water ran out faster now where the cut opened it and pooled around his knees and soaked through the denim and he kept on cutting until the section came free in his hands. He held it up and looked at it. He turned it in the light. The plastic had gone the color of old bone and where it had split the edges were rough and crazed with a thousand small fractures.

You can see it's done, he said. You can see there's no life left in it.

The younger brother crouched on the far side of the hole.

I'm not the enemy here, he said.

I never said you was.

You act it.

The older brother set down the cut section and took up the rag and began to clean the ends of the pipe where they ran into the hole on either side. He worked the rag around them until the plastic showed clean and then he reached for the can of primer and unscrewed the cap and the sharp chemical smell of it rose up out of the hole and hung there in the heat.

You remember the year it froze, he said.

The younger brother looked at him.

The bad freeze. You'd have been what. Nine. Ten. Come down out of nowhere in the spring after the trees had set and Daddy was up all night in the orchard with the smudge pots and the wind machine that wouldn't run and he had us out there both of us with him. You remember.

I remember some of it.

We lost the whole crop that year. Every bit of it. The blooms all blacked and dropped and there wasn't a peach come off this place that season. I remember Daddy standing right about here in the morning when it was done and the trees all stood there ruint and the frost still on them and he didn't say nothing for the longest time. And then he said, well. He just said well. And he went and got the tractor and went on about the rest of it like the trees didn't know any better than to die and so neither would he.

He brushed the primer onto the ends of the pipe with the dauber in the cap, working it on purple and slick, and then onto the inside of the coupling he meant to set there.

He could've sold it that year, the older brother said. Easy. There was men wanting it then and the freeze gave him every reason. Bank would've understood. Everybody would've understood. He held a job in town besides through all of it, did you know that. Drove to the cannery five days a week and worked the orchard the other two and on into the dark every evening so we never knew but what he was just always here. He never said a word of it. I found it out after, going through the papers.

So you're saying.

I'm not saying nothing. I'm telling you he could've quit and he didn't.

That's not the same thing as it making sense.

No, the older brother said. No it ain't.

He took up the can of cement and a fresh dauber and laid it on quick over the primer and pressed the coupling onto one cut end and gave it a quarter turn and held it. The fumes came up thick. He counted under his breath and held it and then he reached for the new length of pipe he'd cut to fit and ran the cement round its end and set it into the coupling the same way, turning it, holding it, his arms down in the hole to the elbow and the water still seeping cold around them.

You got to hold it, he said. Hold it or it'll push itself back out before it sets.

The younger brother reached down into the hole on his side and took the far coupling where the older brother could not reach both ends at once and held it the way he'd seen it done all his life and they knelt there on either side of the open ground each of them holding to the pipe and not looking at one another.

I'm tired, the older brother said.

The younger one waited.

I don't mean of this. I mean of all of it. Coming down here knowing what I'll find. Knowing it's the same thing over and again and there's no getting ahead of it. The trees down low are gone and the ones above'll follow and the price for peaches won't hardly cover the picking anymore and you're right. You're right about all of it. I know you're right.

Then why.

The older brother held the pipe and looked down into the dark of the hole where the water moved.

Because if I let it go I don't know who I am after, he said. That's the whole of it. I been turning it every way I know and that's what it comes to. I been a man with an orchard my whole life. My daddy's orchard. There's not a thing else I ever was. You went off and made yourself into something else, you got the job and the town and the kids that play their ball games and all of it, and that's good, I'm not saying it ain't, but I never did. I stayed. And what I stayed for is these trees. And if they go then I'm just a old man in a empty piece of ground that used to be something. I don't know how to be that. I never learned it.

The younger brother said nothing for a long while. The locusts went on in the heat. A hawk came over low down the line of the trees and tilted and was gone beyond the dead end of the orchard.

You could come work with me, the younger brother said. There's always need where I'm at.

The older brother laughed soft. Doing what.

There's all kinds of things. You're good with your hands. You could—

I'm fifty-three years old and I never punched a clock for another man in my life. He shook his head. No. That ain't it.

They held the pipe. The cement was setting and they could feel it taking, the give going out of the joint as the two pieces became one piece.

I think it's took, the older brother said.

He drew his arms up slow out of the hole and the new section sat there bright and white between the two old yellowed lengths like a clean bone set among rotten ones. He sat back on his heels and the water no longer ran from the line and the bottom of the hole had stopped its welling.

Let it cure a while before we turn it back on, he said. Give it a hour.

They climbed up out of the low place and went and sat in the shade of one of the older trees up the slope where it still held its leaves and the older brother took a bottle of water down off the truck and they passed it between them. The sun stood high now and the day had gone white and hot and below them the orchard ran down in its long rows to the dead trees and the far fence and beyond the fence the road and beyond the road the brown hills going up dry and folded into the haze.

He'd want an answer this week, the younger brother said. The man.

The older one drank and handed back the bottle.

I told him I had to talk to you, the younger said. I told him it was the both of ours and I couldn't say nothing till I'd talked to you.

That was right.

So I'm talking to you.

The older brother leaned back against the trunk of the tree and looked up into the branches where the light came down through the leaves in pieces.

You need the money, he said. It's not a question of wanting. You need it. The note and the kids and all of it. I know that. I'm not blind to it.

I'd give you half. More than half if you wanted it. You could—

I don't want the money.

I know you don't. But you'd have it. You could do anything with it. Go anywhere. Buy a smaller place even, someplace easier, run a few trees just to have them, not this whole—

The older brother shook his head.

It wouldn't be this, he said.

No, the younger one said. It wouldn't be this.

They sat. A hot wind moved up the slope through the orchard and turned the leaves and laid them back the other way so that the whole of it shivered silver for a moment and then went still again.

I'll tell you what I been thinking, the older brother said. And then I'll let it lay and you can do what you have to do, because it's yours as much as mine and I won't fight you on it past today. I'm too tired to fight you and it's not in me to make you choose between this dirt and your own children. That's no kind of brother. So I'll say this and then I'm done.

The younger one waited.

I been thinking we hold it one more season, the older brother said. Just the one. Let me get the crop off the trees up top that's still good, the early varieties, they always bring something, and let me see can I sell the timber off the dead end down there, the wood's still good for something to somebody, and let me put what comes of it toward the line. Not all new. We can't do all new. But the worst of it, the stretch down the middle that's gone the same as this here. And then come fall we sit down again, you and me, and we look at the numbers honest, and if it can't be done then it can't be done and I'll put my own name on the man's paper alongside yours and I won't say a word. But give me the one season. Let me put him down right. Let me not be the one that quit on him when there was still a peach left to pick.

The younger brother sat with the bottle in his hands and looked down the long rows.

The man might not wait, he said.

Then he don't wait. Then there'll be another. There's always another for land. That's the one thing in this world there's no making more of and so somebody'll always want it.

A season, the younger brother said.

One season.

And in the fall we look at it honest.

We look at it honest.

The younger one turned the bottle in his hands. Down at the bottom of the orchard the dead trees stood in their rows holding up their bare gray arms against the white sky and saying nothing.

He'd have been seventy-nine this year, the younger brother said. Daddy.

He would.

I can't hardly call up his voice anymore. That's a thing I never thought would happen. I figured I'd always be able to hear him say a thing. And I can see his face all right but the voice has gone soft on me. Like something underwater.

The older brother looked off down the rows.

I hear it fine, he said. I hear it every time I come down here. He didn't say it to be unkind. He said it the way a man might mention that the well still gave water or that the truck still ran. I hear him fine, he said again, more quiet. That's part of what it is.

They sat a while longer in the shade and the hour came round and the older brother got up off the ground slow with his hands on his knees the way the old man had done it and he went down to the hole and looked at the joint and put his thumb to the seam of it and it held hard and dry.

It's cured, he said.

He walked up the line to the valve at the head of the row where it tied into the main and he put his hand to the lever and looked back down at his brother standing now at the lip of the open hole.

You ready, he called.

The younger brother looked down into the trench at the white pipe lying clean among the old and he nodded though his brother was already turning the valve and could not have seen it. The water came on through the line with a low knock and a shudder and they heard it move down the pipe toward them in the ground and then the new joint stood there holding against it and not a drop came up around it and the water went on past and on down the line toward the dry furrow and the thirsting trees below.

It holds, the older brother called.

It holds, the younger one said, but not loud enough to carry, only to himself, standing over the hole and watching the dark water push down through the ground toward the trees that yet had life enough in them to take it, and the sun standing white and merciless above the both of them and the whole of the burning afternoon yet to come.
