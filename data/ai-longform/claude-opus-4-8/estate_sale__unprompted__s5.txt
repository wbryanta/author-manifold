The card table held the cash box, a roll of stickers in three colors, and a legal pad where Dana had written nothing all morning. She had meant to keep a running tally—blue dots for under ten dollars, yellow for under fifty, the rare red dot for the things that mattered—but the system had collapsed by nine-thirty, and now she simply took the money people handed her and made change from the metal box that had once held her mother's recipe cards.

The recipes were gone too. She'd found them last week in a kitchen drawer, swollen with grease and decades, and she had stood in the empty kitchen reading her mother's handwriting until the light changed, and then she had put them in a box marked KEEP and driven them to the storage unit in Mansfield, where they sat now in the dark with the other things she could not throw away and could not bring herself to look at.

"How much for the dresser?"

A young man in a Carhartt jacket stood at the table, though the dresser was upstairs, in the room that had been hers. Dana knew which one he meant because there was only one dresser left, the cherry one with the mirror that her father had bought for her tenth birthday from a man who refinished furniture out of his garage on Route 13. She remembered the smell of it when it was new, the linseed oil, the way her father had carried it up the stairs himself, refusing help, sweating through his shirt.

"Forty," she said.

"Will you take twenty-five?"

She looked at him. He had a good face, tired around the eyes, a wedding ring. Probably furnishing a first apartment or a baby's room. He didn't know whose dresser it was. To him it was brown wood and a foxed mirror, a thing that would hold socks.

"Thirty," she said, and he nodded and went up the stairs two at a time, and she heard him moving around up there, the scrape of the drawers coming out so he could carry it lighter. She had emptied those drawers herself on Tuesday. There had been nothing in them but a single earring, a gold hoop, and a church bulletin from 1987 folded into a square the size of a stamp. She had not been able to remember why a child would save such a thing. She had thrown the earring away and then taken it out of the trash and put it in her pocket, where it still was, and she touched it now, the little gold circle, while a woman she'd gone to high school with pretended not to recognize her by the bookshelf.

The woman's name was Linda Hasselbach. She was holding a paperback, turning it over as though deciding, but Dana could see she had no intention of buying it. She had come to look. Half the town had come to look. The notice in the *Bellville Star* had said ESTATE SALE and given the address, and the address was enough; everyone knew the Reilly house, white with green shutters, the porch swing, the lilac that Dana's mother had planted the year Dana was born and that was now taller than the second-floor windows. People came to walk through the rooms where a woman they'd known for sixty years had lived and died, to touch her things and put them down and feel, in the touching and the putting down, some confirmation of their own continuing.

"Dana?" Linda said finally, as though it had just come to her. "Dana Reilly?"

"Hi, Linda."

"I thought that was you. I'm so sorry about your mother. She was—everybody loved Ruth. She was just a wonderful person."

"Thank you."

"We missed you at the funeral." A small barb, very gentle, almost not there.

"I was at the funeral, Linda. I gave the eulogy."

"Oh, I know, I just meant—after. At the luncheon. We all wondered where you got to."

Where she had gotten to was the parking lot of the funeral home, in the rented car, with the air conditioning running and her forehead against the steering wheel, for forty minutes, before she could make herself drive to the Methodist fellowship hall and eat ham salad off a paper plate among people who had known her mother better in the last twenty years than she had. She did not say this.

"It was a hard day," she said.

"I'm sure. I'm sure it was." Linda set the paperback down on the wrong shelf and patted Dana's arm and drifted toward the kitchen, where someone was asking about the price of a Crock-Pot.

The house was emptying steadily, the way a tub empties, fast at first and then slower, the water circling. By noon the big things were mostly gone—the sofa, the dining set, the recliner her mother had died in, technically, though she had died in the night and the recliner was just where she'd spent her last evening, watching *Wheel of Fortune*, the TV still warm in the morning when the neighbor found her. Dana had not told the buyer of the recliner any of this. He'd been a dealer, gruff, who'd offered cash for a lot of the furniture sight unseen and then haggled over each piece anyway, and she'd let him have the recliner for fifteen dollars because she wanted it gone, wanted it out of the house before she had to look at it again.

She made change for the dresser. The young man carried it out alone, walking backwards through the front door, the mirror catching the noon light and throwing it across the ceiling, a swimming brightness like water, like the reflection off a pool, and then he was through the door and down the steps and the light was gone and the dresser was a shape in the bed of his truck, and he came back in for the drawers, four trips, and then he drove away. Dana watched the truck to the end of Schultz Street and around the corner. Her tenth birthday. The linseed oil. Gone for thirty dollars to a man whose name she didn't know.

She had thought she was prepared for this. She had told her husband, on the phone, that it would be fine, that it was just stuff, that her mother herself would have said it was just stuff. Ruth Reilly had not been sentimental about objects. She had been sentimental about people, about the church, about the dead, but she would walk through a room and see a thing that had sat in the same spot for forty years and say *I should get rid of that*, and sometimes she did. She had given Dana's childhood drawings to the school for a fundraiser once, framed them and let strangers buy them, and when Dana, home from college, had been hurt about it, her mother had laughed and said *You can always make more art, honey. You're not dead.*

You're not dead. As though that were the only criterion. As though the line between things you kept and things you let go was simply the line between the living and the gone.

A man was looking at the piano.

Dana had been avoiding the piano all morning. It was an upright, a Story & Clark, dark walnut, and it had been out of tune since the Clinton administration. Her mother had played it badly and constantly, hymns mostly, and Christmas carols in December, and a few popular songs from her own youth that she rendered in a stiff, churchy style that drained them of all their feeling. Dana had taken lessons on it for six years, from Mrs. Detwiler, who smelled of peppermint and disappointment, and she had hated every minute and quit at thirteen and had not touched a piano since, and yet the sight of the man's hand on the fallboard, lifting it, exposing the yellowed keys, made something close in her throat.

"This for sale?" the man called.

Everything's for sale, she wanted to say. That's what an estate sale is. The estate of Ruth Reilly, dispersed. Everything she owned, made available, marked down, carried off. The estate of a person is the residue of a life, all the choices made solid—this lamp and not that one, this set of dishes, these towels, this particular brand of coffee still in the cabinet, half a can, which Dana had drunk her way through that week because she could not stand to pour it out.

"Yes," she said. "It's for sale."

The man pressed a key. The note that came out was sour and dead, and he made a face, and his wife, beside him, said, "Oh, it's out of tune," in a tone of discovery, and the man said, "They all are, in a house. You'd get it tuned." He pressed a few more keys, ran his thumb up an octave. "Action's not bad. How much?"

She had no idea how much. The dealer hadn't wanted it; pianos were impossible to move and nobody wanted them anymore, he'd said, kids didn't take lessons, everybody had a keyboard or nothing. He'd told her she'd be lucky to give it away. She'd told him she would not be giving it away, and he'd shrugged.

"Seventy-five," she said.

The man looked at her. "For a piano needs tuning and probably new felts? I'll give you twenty to haul it off your hands. You know what it costs to move one of these?"

"Seventy-five," she said again.

"Honey," the man said, not unkindly, "you're not going to get seventy-five for this. Nobody's going to give you seventy-five. I'm doing you a favor at twenty."

"Then don't do me a favor," Dana said.

The man's wife touched his elbow. They moved off, murmuring, and Dana stood very still behind the card table with her hands flat on the legal pad. She was aware that she had behaved unreasonably. The man was right. The piano was worthless; it would cost more to move than it could ever be sold for; she would, in all likelihood, end up paying someone to take it away, or it would sit in the empty house until the new owners moved in and dealt with it themselves, cursing the woman who'd left it. She knew all this. And she could not let it go for twenty dollars to a man who pressed the keys like he was testing fruit.

She had not played it in forty years. She had hated it. And she stood now guarding it like it was the last thing in the house, which it nearly was.

The afternoon went on. The sun came around to the front of the house and lay in long bars across the floor where the rug had been—the rug was gone, a woman had rolled it up and dragged it out by herself, refusing help, the way Dana's father had refused help with the dresser—and the bare floor showed its history, the dark rectangle where the rug had protected it, the pale paths worn between the kitchen and the living room, between the front door and the foot of the stairs. Forty years of feet. Her mother's feet, going to answer the door, going up to bed. Dana's own, smaller, running. Her father's, before the cancer, and then his slower, and then not at all.

The crowd thinned. A teenager bought the box of records for five dollars. A woman with a baby on her hip bought the kitchen chairs, two trips, the baby fussing. The neighbor who had found Ruth—an older woman named Cheryl, who lived in the gray house across the street—came over around two and didn't buy anything, just stood in the living room looking lost, and Dana made her a cup of tea in one of the mugs that hadn't sold, and they sat together on the front steps because there was nowhere left to sit inside.

"She used to wave at me," Cheryl said. "Every morning. She'd get her paper off the porch and I'd be getting mine and we'd wave. Forty-one years." Cheryl held the mug in both hands. "I keep waving. Isn't that foolish. I went out this morning and I caught myself."

"It's not foolish."

"Your mother was the kindest person on this street. When my Frank passed she brought me a casserole every week for a month. I told her she didn't have to and she said she knew she didn't have to, that's why she wanted to." Cheryl looked at her. "You favor her, you know. Around the mouth. You've got her mouth."

Dana had not known this. She had spent her life being told she looked like her father—the Reilly nose, the Reilly height—and it had never occurred to her that she might also be carrying her mother's mouth around on her face, the mouth that had waved good morning across the street for forty-one years, the mouth that had said *you're not dead* as though that settled it.

"Thank you," she said. "For finding her. For—I know that wasn't easy. I'm grateful it was you. Someone who loved her."

Cheryl's eyes filled. She nodded and looked out at the street and didn't say anything else, and they sat together until the tea was gone, and then Cheryl went home, across the street, to the gray house, and Dana watched her go in and close the door.

By three the house was nearly empty. A few oddments remained: the piano, a card table that wasn't for sale because it was holding the cash box, some kitchen things nobody wanted, a stepladder, a coil of garden hose, a shoebox of photographs that Dana had meant to take to the storage unit and had instead, in a moment she already regretted, set out on a table with a sign that said FREE. She had thought someone might want them—people liked old photographs, used them for crafts, for decoration, those anonymous faces from the past. But of course these weren't anonymous to her, and watching a woman paw through them earlier, holding up a photo of Dana's grandmother as a young bride and saying to her friend *look at this one, isn't she a hoot*, Dana had nearly snatched the box back. She hadn't. The woman had taken three or four photos and left the rest. Dana didn't look to see which ones were gone.

A man came to the door at three-fifteen, the last customer of the day, a big man in a flannel shirt, and he stood in the doorway looking in at the emptied rooms, and then he said, "Is the piano still here?"

"It's still here."

He came in and went to it and didn't press the keys. He lifted the fallboard and looked at the keys without touching them, and then he closed it again gently, and he ran his hand along the top, along the walnut, the way you'd touch the flank of a horse.

"Story and Clark," he said. "These are good old pianos. They built them to last." He looked at her. "How much do you want for it?"

She braced for the haggling. "Seventy-five," she said.

He nodded slowly. "That's fair. That's more than fair, honestly. You could ask more. The case alone—" He shook his head. "My daughter's just starting lessons. She's seven. We've been looking for a real piano, not one of those electric things. Her teacher says you can't learn touch on a keyboard. You need the weight of the keys." He pressed one key, finally, lightly, and the dead sour note came out, and he smiled. "Needs a tuning. That's all right. That's nothing. I'll have my brother-in-law come look at it, he tunes pianos, he'll set it right."

Dana found that she could not speak.

"Seventy-five?" the man said, taking out his wallet. "I've got it right here. You take cards? No, cash, here, I've got cash." He counted out the bills onto the card table, four twenties, and she made him change, and he waved it off—"keep it, keep it"—and she made the change anyway, a five and made him take it, because she could not bear for it to be charity, for any of this to be charity, and he took the five and folded it into his shirt pocket and looked at the piano again.

"My truck's not big enough," he said. "I'll have to come back with my brother-in-law and his trailer. Is tomorrow all right? I don't want to leave you hanging if you've got to be out."

"Tomorrow's fine."

"You're sure. Because I can—"

"Tomorrow's fine," she said. "There's no rush. The house doesn't close until the end of the month."

He nodded. He looked at her, and something in her face must have told him something, because he said, gentle as Cheryl had been, "Your folks' place?"

"My mother's."

"She play?"

"Yes," Dana said. "Badly. All the time."

The man laughed, a warm sound in the empty room. "Well," he said. "My Katie plays badly too, right now. But she's getting better. She practices every day, I make her, twenty minutes, you'd think I was killing her." He put his hand on the piano top again. "It'll get played. I'll tell you that. I'll make sure of it. It won't just sit there. It'll get played every day by a little girl who's getting better."

"Thank you," Dana said, and her voice broke on it, just slightly, just enough, and the man pretended not to notice, which was the kindest thing he could have done.

"I'll be back tomorrow afternoon," he said. "Around two? I'll call first." He'd written his number on the legal pad, she saw, while she wasn't watching, his name too—Dale—in big square letters. "You take care now. I'm sorry for your loss. Sounds like she was a heck of a lady, playing all the time, badly." He smiled again. "That's the way to do it. You don't have to be good at a thing to love it."

He left. His truck pulled away, not big enough for the piano, and turned the corner where the young man's truck had turned, hours ago, with the dresser.

The house was empty now except for the piano and the card table and the FREE box of photographs and the things nobody wanted. Dana sat down on the piano bench—it came with the piano; she'd have to remember to leave it for Dale—and she put her hands on the closed fallboard the way Dale had, feeling the cool of the wood. The light was going amber in the front windows. The lilac outside threw its shadow across the floor.

She lifted the fallboard. The keys were yellowed, the ivory worn through to the wood at the edges of middle C, where forty years of thumbs had rested. She put her right hand on the keys, found the position she had not used since she was thirteen, and she played, very slowly, the first thing her fingers remembered, which was not a hymn and was not a carol but was the little exercise Mrs. Detwiler had started every lesson with, a five-finger scale, do re mi fa sol, up and back down, the most basic thing, the thing you played before you played anything else. The notes were sour and dead and out of tune and the felts were worn and the action stuck on the F, and it was the most beautiful sound she had heard in a week. She played it again. Up and back down. Her mother had heard this, these same notes on these same keys, ten thousand times, drifting up the stairs while she made dinner, while she paid the bills at the kitchen table, while she lay awake at night. Dana playing badly, and quitting, and never touching it again, and her mother keeping it anyway, keeping it tuned for a while and then not, keeping it in the same spot for forty years, playing her own bad hymns on it, the residue of a life made solid, a thing you kept because of what it had held.

You can always make more art, honey. You're not dead.

She played the scale a third time, all the way up, and at the top, instead of coming back down, she let her hand fall and the note hung there in the empty room, sour and pure, fading, until it was gone.

Then she closed the fallboard, gently, and got up, and gathered the cash box and the legal pad with Dale's name and number on it, and she folded the card table and leaned it against the wall where Dale would see it. She took the box of photographs—the FREE sign she crumpled and put in her pocket with the gold earring—and she carried them out to the rented car and set them on the passenger seat, where they would ride with her to the storage unit in Mansfield, into the dark, with the recipes and the other things she could not throw away.

She went back for one last look. The empty rooms held the amber light. The pale paths in the floor ran from door to door, from kitchen to stairs, the record of all that walking, all that going to answer the door and going up to bed, forty years of it, indelible, the only thing the buyers couldn't carry off. She stood in the doorway where Cheryl's mother—where her own mother—had stood waving every morning, and she lifted her hand, foolishly, to no one, to the gray house across the street, and then she turned off the lights and locked the door and left the piano waiting in the dark for the man who would come back tomorrow, and for the little girl who would play it badly, every day, getting better.
