The invitation came on heavy cream paper, the kind that costs more than the meal it announces. Marguerite turned it over twice in the dim light of her kitchen before she understood where the wedding was to be held. The Lanverne. She had to sit down then, lowering herself into the chair the way older people do now, hand finding the table first, the body following after like an afterthought.

Her niece Colette was marrying a man whose family owned three restaurants in Lyon. The reception would be at the Lanverne, which had reopened, the invitation noted in a smaller script beneath the engraved names, after a complete restoration to its original 1890s splendor. Marguerite read that line several times. Original splendor. As though the building had only paused, had merely closed its eyes for a long while and now woken refreshed. As though nothing had burned.

She had not told Colette. There had been no reason to. The fire was forty-one years ago, and Marguerite had been nineteen, a chambermaid in a starched apron, and the people who remembered were mostly dead or scattered. She had moved north afterward, married a man who repaired clocks, buried him, raised no children of her own but had a sister's children to love instead, and Colette was the youngest of those, the one who had crawled into Marguerite's lap at every family gathering and stayed there long past the age when such things were done.

She would go. Of course she would go. You did not refuse the wedding of a girl you had loved like a daughter because of a building.

The train south took most of a day. Marguerite watched the country flatten and then rise again, the vineyards stitching the hills in their tidy rows, and she thought about the smell of the place, which she had not smelled in forty-one years but which she could summon now as easily as her own name. Floor wax and lilies. The lilies had been Madame Sauvigny's particular vanity, great urns of them in the lobby, replaced twice a week regardless of season, flown in from somewhere when the local ones gave out. The smell of the lilies was the smell of the Lanverne, and underneath it, always, the smell of the laundry where Marguerite spent her mornings, the hot wet linen smell, bleach and steam.

She arrived the afternoon before the wedding and took a small room in a pension three streets away, because she could not have slept under that roof, restored or not. From the window she could see the upper floors of the hotel rising above the rooftops, the pale stone, the wrought-iron balconies. They had rebuilt the balconies. She remembered the balconies.

In the morning she dressed carefully. A grey silk dress she had bought for the occasion, pearl earrings that had been her husband's gift on their tenth anniversary. She looked at herself in the spotted mirror of the pension and saw an old woman, neat, composed, her white hair pinned, and she thought: no one will know. No one alive knows. She could walk into that building as any guest walked in, admire the chandeliers, eat the food, watch Colette become a wife, and walk out again, and the building would not know her any more than she would let it.

She was wrong about that, but she did not know it yet.

The ceremony was at the church, and that part was easy, that part had nothing to do with anything. Colette in white, luminous and laughing, the groom red-faced with happiness and nerves. Marguerite wept the ordinary tears of an aunt and felt nothing but the clean grief-joy of seeing a child grown and given away. It was afterward, when the cars came to take them all to the reception, that her hands began to go cold.

The façade had been faithfully reproduced. She saw that as the car pulled up. The same carved garlands over the entrance, the same brass that someone had once spent her mornings polishing — not her, the boys did the brass, but she had watched them — the same revolving door, though that must be new, everything must be new, it only looked old. A doorman in a green coat. There had been a doorman in a green coat. His name had been Émile and he had died in the fire, on the third floor, where no one understood why he had gone, away from his post, up into the smoke. They found him near the linen closet. Marguerite had wondered for forty-one years why Émile had gone to the third floor, and she had her theories, and none of them brought him back.

"Auntie?" Colette's brother had her by the elbow. "Are you all right? You've gone pale."

"The journey," she said. "An old woman's stomach. I'm fine."

Inside, the lobby opened up around her and she nearly turned and walked back out. They had got the lilies right. Whether by accident or by some researcher's diligence, there were urns of lilies on the marble, and the smell came down on her like a hand pressed over her face. Floor wax underneath it. Her knees did something unreliable and she put her hand on the back of a gilt chair to steady herself, and an attendant materialized, young, smiling, asking if Madame would like to be seated, if Madame needed anything.

"No," Marguerite said. "Thank you. I'm only looking."

"It's beautiful, isn't it," the young woman said, with the pride of someone who has been trained to feel proprietary. "People say it's exactly as it was. We have photographs from the original hotel and they matched everything. The plasterwork, the colors. It took years."

"Did they tell you what happened to the original hotel?" Marguerite heard herself ask.

The young woman's smile flickered, recovered. "There was a fire, I believe. A long time ago. But they saved so many of the original drawings, you see, so we could—"

"Yes," Marguerite said. "I see."

She let herself be moved along into the great salon where the reception was laid. Round tables, white cloths, the crystal throwing the light of the chandeliers into a thousand pieces. She found her place card — *Mme. Marguerite Faure*, in the same elegant hand as the invitation — at a table near the back, among the older relatives, which suited her. She sat. She drank a little of the wine that was poured. She tried to be an aunt.

But the building would not let her be only an aunt.

She knew, without being told, that the great salon stood where the dining room had been. The dining room had been where the fire started, or where it was thought to have started, in the kitchens behind, a fryer or a flue, the inquest had never been certain. She knew that the staircase rising at the far end of the room, the grand sweep of it with its red carpet, stood roughly where the old grand staircase had stood, the one the guests used, not the narrow service stair at the back that the staff used, the one she had used, the one that had filled with smoke so fast that two of the girls she worked beside had not gotten down it.

Annette. Sylvie. She made herself think their names because no one else in the world would think them today. Annette had been from the Auvergne, a heavy slow girl who sang while she worked and was teased for her singing, and Sylvie had been Marguerite's particular friend, sharp-tongued, ambitious, saving her wages to open a shop one day. They had been on the fourth floor when it started. Marguerite had been on the second. That was the whole of it. That was the only difference between the living and the dead — which floor, which corridor, which moment you happened to be standing in when the building turned against you. Marguerite had gone down the service stair while it was still passable and Annette and Sylvie had gone up, toward the air, toward the roof, because the smoke was already below them, and they had not come down at all.

"You're not eating, Auntie." Colette had come, in the rustle of her great dress, to crouch beside Marguerite's chair, taking her hand. "I saw you all alone back here. I won't have it. Come sit nearer the front, there's room, I want you where I can see you."

"I'm perfectly happy here, my darling. You mustn't fuss over an old woman on your day."

"It is my day," Colette said, "and I'll fuss over whom I please." She was glowing. Her happiness was a physical thing, a warmth coming off her, and Marguerite loved her so much in that moment that the love and the grief became briefly the same sensation, indistinguishable, a fullness in the chest that was close to pain. "Is something wrong? You've been strange since we arrived. Don't tell me it's your stomach, I know your faces."

Marguerite looked at her niece, this child she had held, and made a decision she had not known she was going to make.

"I worked here once," she said. "When I was young. Before any of you were born. Before this building was this building."

Colette blinked. "Here? At the Lanverne?"

"At the old Lanverne. The one that stood here before. I was a chambermaid. Nineteen years old." She smiled, and felt how unsteady the smile was. "I never told anyone in the family. It wasn't a secret, exactly. It was only — a part of my life that belonged to a different person. A girl I'd more or less forgotten how to be."

"You never said." Colette had sat down properly now, on the empty chair beside her, the dress pooling around them both. "All these years, and you never said. Why?"

"There was a fire," Marguerite said. "I don't know if you know this. The old hotel burned. That's why they had to build it again." She watched the words land. "I was here when it burned. I got out. Some of the girls I worked with did not."

Colette's hand tightened around hers. The glow had gone out of her face, replaced by something older and more careful, and Marguerite was sorry, sorry to have brought it here, into the middle of the girl's joy. But she could not have sat at the back of that room and said nothing. The dead deserved at least to be spoken of, somewhere, by someone who had known them.

"Oh, Auntie," Colette said softly. "And we brought you here. We brought you right back into it. If I'd known—"

"You couldn't have known. I didn't tell you so that you would know. I'm telling you now because—" Marguerite stopped. Why was she telling her now? "Because I think I needed someone alive to know that I was here. That the others were here. That it wasn't always lilies and chandeliers. Once there were two girls named Annette and Sylvie who carried linen up these stairs, and they died here, and no one in this beautiful room knows their names but me."

Colette was crying now, quietly, the careful way a bride cries when she does not want to ruin her face, and Marguerite reached up and wiped the girl's cheek with her thumb the way she had done when Colette was small.

"Don't," she said. "I've spoiled it. I shouldn't have."

"You haven't spoiled anything." Colette caught her aunt's hand and held it against her own cheek. "Tell me about them. Annette and Sylvie. Tell me about you. The girl you were."

And so, in the corner of the great salon, with the wedding going on around them, the laughter and the clink of glasses and someone beginning, at the piano in the corner, to play something light, Marguerite told her. She told her about the dormitory under the eaves where the maids slept four to a room, and how cold it was in winter and how they put their feet against each other's backs to keep warm. She told her about Madame Sauvigny who ran the staff like a small army and was cruel and fair in equal measure and once gave Marguerite an entire afternoon off, unasked, because she had seen the girl was sick with homesickness. She told her about the laundry and the lilies and the boys who polished the brass and flirted with the girls, and about Émile the doorman who used to slip them sweets when Madame wasn't looking. She told her about Sylvie's plans for her shop — a milliner's, it was to be, Sylvie had a gift for hats, could turn a bit of ribbon and a cheap straw into something a fine lady would pay for — and about Annette's singing, the old songs from the Auvergne, sad slow songs in a dialect none of them understood, that made the work go faster all the same.

She did not, at first, tell her about the fire itself. But Colette, who had always been able to read her, said, "And the night it happened. Where were you?"

"The second floor," Marguerite said. "I was turning down beds. We did it in the evenings, turned down the covers, left a sweet on the pillow, drew the curtains. I was in one of the rooms on the second floor when I smelled it. Not smoke, at first. Something else. Hot metal, hot grease, a wrongness in the air. And then the smoke came up through the service stair like water filling a glass, from the bottom, and I knew — I don't know how I knew, but I knew at once it was bad, that it was already too late to be merely bad."

She was quiet a moment. The piano had moved on to something else.

"I went down. The service stair was already thick with it but I was only on the second floor and I held my breath and went down and got out through the kitchen yard, into the air. And I stood in the yard and I watched it. We all stood there, those of us who'd gotten out, and we watched the windows on the upper floors, and we counted who was missing. And the fire brigade came but the building was old and full of wood under all its fine plaster, and it went up so fast. So fast. There was a moment when the whole top floor was just light. Just light, against the night sky. And somewhere in that light—" She stopped. "Annette and Sylvie. And Émile, who'd gone up after them, we worked out later, he'd gone up to bring them down and none of the three came back."

Colette had her free hand pressed to her mouth.

"For a long time," Marguerite said, "I dreamed about it. The light in the windows. I'd wake up with the smell in my nose. And then, slowly, less. You can't grieve at full strength forever, the body won't let you, it's a mercy I suppose. I married your uncle and the dreams stopped almost entirely. But I never came back here. I never came south at all if I could help it. And when your invitation came and I saw where the reception was to be, I thought — well. I thought a great many things. I thought I shouldn't come. And then I thought, I will not miss Colette's wedding for a pile of stone. So here I am."

"Here you are," Colette said. She was holding her aunt's hand in both of hers now. "I'm so glad you came. I'm so glad you told me. I won't ever forget their names, I promise you that. Annette and Sylvie. And Émile."

"And Émile," Marguerite agreed.

They sat a moment longer, and then the new husband came looking for his bride, anxious, smiling, and Colette rose in a rustle of silk and kissed her aunt's forehead and was carried back into the life of the party, the toasts, the first dance. Marguerite watched her go and felt strangely lightened, as though she had set down a bag she'd been carrying so long she'd stopped feeling its weight, only noticing it now by its absence.

But she could not stay in the salon. The room was kind enough but it was not where she needed to be. She waited until the dancing was well underway, until no one would notice one old woman slipping out, and then she rose and made her way toward the grand staircase at the end of the room.

A young man in hotel livery stepped politely into her path. "Is Madame looking for the powder rooms? They're just through there—"

"I'd like to walk up the stairs," she said. "Only to look. I won't be any trouble. I used to know this building, a long time ago."

He hesitated, but she was old and respectable and clearly a wedding guest, and he stood aside.

She went up slowly, her hand on the polished banister. The carpet was thick and red. The walls were hung with reproductions of old paintings, or perhaps real old paintings, she couldn't tell and it didn't matter. At the first landing she paused and looked back down at the salon, the dancers turning, Colette's white dress moving among the dark suits like a moth, and then she went on, up to the second floor, where the corridor stretched away on either side, papered in green and gold, lined with doors.

It was not the same corridor. She knew that. It was a careful reproduction of a corridor that had burned to ash forty-one years ago, every molecule of it new, every door hung in this decade by workmen who had never heard of the fire. And yet standing in it she was nineteen again, with an armful of fresh linen, and Sylvie was somewhere up above her on the fourth floor saying something sharp that would make her laugh, and Annette was singing, faint through the floors, one of her sad songs.

Marguerite walked the length of the corridor. At the end of it, where the service stair would have been, there was a door marked PRIVATE — STAFF. The same place. They had put the service stair in the same place. She stood in front of it and put her hand flat against it.

She thought of the smoke coming up. She thought of how she had held her breath and gone down through it, and how, somewhere in that same shaft of choking dark, a heavy slow girl who sang and a sharp-tongued girl who dreamed of hats had tried to go down and could not, and had turned back, and gone up instead, toward the only air left, and found none. She thought of Émile climbing toward them through that, knowing what he was climbing into, going anyway.

She had spent forty-one years not understanding why Émile had left his post and gone up to the third floor. She understood it now, standing here, an old woman with her hand on a door. He had gone up because the girls were up there. That was the whole of it. There was no mystery. You went toward the people, even into the fire, especially into the fire. She had gone down because down was where the air was and she had been nineteen and wanted to live, and there was no shame in that, none, she knew that, she had always known it — but Émile had gone up, and that was a different kind of thing, and she had carried the not-understanding of it like a stone for four decades when all along it was the simplest thing in the world.

"They've put it in the same place," she said aloud, to no one, to Sylvie, to Annette, to Émile. "The stairs. Isn't that strange. They built it all again exactly the same and they don't even know what they've built. But I know. I know where you were."

The door was only a door. Behind it, she supposed, was a perfectly ordinary modern stairwell, fire-rated, well-lit, with extinguishers on every landing and signs pointing to exits. They would have made sure of that, this time. She did not open it. There was nothing behind it for her. The thing she had come up here for was not behind the door but in front of it, in her, in the standing here and the saying of the names.

She took her hand from the door.

Down in the salon the music had changed to something slower, and she could hear, faintly, the murmur of many voices and one woman laughing in a particular bright way that might have been Colette. Life going on, as it did, as it had gone on for forty-one years, as it would go on after Marguerite too was only a name that someone, with luck, still bothered to say. Colette would say her name. Colette would say all their names. That was something. That was, when you came down to it, almost everything.

She turned and walked back along the green and gold corridor to the grand staircase, and went down it slowly, her hand on the banister, into the light and the music and the smell of lilies, to dance, if anyone would have an old woman, at her niece's wedding.
