The snow came sideways, which meant the wind had swung round to the north, and Mara knew before she looked out the kitchen window that the lambing would go bad before it got better. She'd put the kettle on out of habit, then turned it off again. There wasn't time. The first ewe had been straining since before dawn, and now it was nearly seven and the light through the storm was the color of dishwater.

"Dylan," she called up the stairs. "Get your boots. We've got three close and the weather's turning."

He came down already dressed, which surprised her. Fifteen and most mornings she had to drag him out of sleep like pulling a calf, but lately he'd been up before her, moving through the house with a quiet she didn't trust. She watched him sit on the bottom step to lace his boots, his father's old wool hat pulled down over his ears, and she said nothing about it. The hat had been hanging by the door since November. She'd noticed it gone two weeks ago and had said nothing then either.

Outside the cold took the breath out of her. April. Late April, and the grass had been greening up nice along the south fence, and now this. Eight inches already and coming faster. The path to the lambing shed had drifted over so she went first, breaking trail, her son behind her in the white. The shed sat low against the hill where Tom had built it the year Dylan was born, banked into the slope for warmth, and she could hear the ewes inside before she got the door open, that low anxious murmur of animals that knew their work was upon them.

The lights were on. Good. She flipped the switch by the door from habit and they stayed on, the bare bulbs swaying a little in the draft, throwing the pens into yellow relief. Forty ewes in here, the ones closest to term, and the rest still out in the lower field with the older lambs. The air was thick and warm with the smell of them, of straw and dung and the particular iron tang of birth that she had never minded and that Tom had loved, had actually said so once, drunk and sentimental at a wedding, that the lambing shed smelled like the start of things.

"That one." Dylan pointed. The blackface in the corner pen, down on her side, her flanks heaving. Mara climbed over the rail and knelt by her, ran a hand down the swollen belly, felt the ewe's whole body clench under her palm.

"How long's she been at it?"

"Since I checked at half five."

"You were out here at half five?"

He didn't answer. He'd come round the other side of the ewe, kneeling in the straw, his hands ready. She looked at him a moment, the boy's face gone serious and grown in the bad light, and she thought of all the things she might say and said none of them.

"She's been pushing too long," Mara said. "I'm going in. Hold her head."

She rolled her sleeve and reached in and felt for it, the way Tom had taught her, the way his father had taught him, going back further than anyone living could say. She found a leg. Then another leg. Then, wrong, a third leg, and her stomach dropped because that meant two lambs tangled, two trying to come at once and neither able. She closed her eyes to see better with her hands.

"Twins," she said. "Crossed up. Hold her steady now, she's going to fight me."

The ewe fought her. Mara pushed one lamb back, gently, gently, working against the contractions, against the animal's whole frightened body, and she traced the legs to their bodies and the bodies to their heads and sorted them out in the dark inside, this one yours, this one his, and brought the first one's nose up between its forelegs and pulled with the next push, down and out, and the lamb came in a rush of fluid into the straw, black and slick and still.

"Clear its mouth," she said, already going back for the second. Dylan had the lamb. She heard him clearing the membrane from its face, heard him blow into its nose the way they did, two short breaths, and then the wet sneeze and the first bleat, thin and outraged, and she felt something ease in her chest even as her arm was still inside the ewe finding the second one.

The second came easier. Both of them down in the straw now, the ewe heaving herself up to turn and lick them, the low chuckling sound she made, that mothering sound. Mara sat back on her heels. Her arm was wet to the shoulder and going cold already in the air.

"Good," she said. "That's good. You did good."

Dylan was rubbing the first lamb with a fistful of straw, getting the blood moving. He didn't look up but she saw the corner of his mouth, the thing that wasn't quite a smile but was the nearest he'd come to one in months. Then the lights went out.

Not a flicker. Out, all at once, and the dark came down so total that Mara heard her own breath go ragged before she got hold of herself. The wind outside had risen to a long continuous moan against the north wall.

"Mum?"

"It's the power. Line's down somewhere." She kept her voice flat and easy. "It happens. Hang on." She stood, one hand on the rail, and felt her way along the wall to where the torch hung on its nail. Tom's system. A torch by the door, a torch by the feed bins, a torch in the truck. She'd thought it fussy once. The beam came on weak and yellow but it came on, and she swept it round the shed, found Dylan's face white and wide-eyed over the lamb, found the ewe licking her twins, found the other pens and the other ewes shifting and stamping in the new dark.

"Battery's low," Dylan said.

"There's more in the house. And the camping lantern." She thought. The generator was in the lean-to but Tom had never wired it to the shed, had always meant to, had a coil of cable and a box of fittings sitting on the bench since two springs ago. One more thing on the list of things he'd meant to do, the list that had simply stopped one Tuesday in November when his heart had stopped, no warning, no long goodbye, just Tom face-down in the top field with the dog sitting beside him when she went to see why he was late for his tea.

"How many torches?" Dylan asked.

"Three. And the lantern. And the truck's got a spotlight if it comes to it." She was already counting the night ahead in her head, the way you counted hay through a long winter. Three close. Then the next dozen behind them, days away most likely but you never knew, the cold could bring them on, the stress of it. And no light but what they carried, and no heat for the lamps if a lamb came chilled, and the storm not due to break till morning by the last forecast she'd heard, and that had been yesterday before the lines went.

"Right," she said. "Here's what we do."

They worked. There was nothing else to do but work. She set the lantern on an upturned bucket in the middle of the shed where it threw a circle of light wide enough to manage by, and she sent Dylan back to the house through the snow for the spare batteries and the second lantern and the thermos she should have filled and hadn't. He was gone twenty minutes and she counted every one of them, watching the door, thinking of the drifts and the dark and the way the cold could turn a person around so they walked the wrong way and didn't know it. He came back caked white to the waist, breathing hard, and dumped the bag of supplies and went straight to the blackface's twins to check they'd found the teat, and she let out the breath she'd been holding for twenty minutes.

The second ewe started before the first lambs were dry. A two-year-old, first time, and frightened of it, running the rail and bleating. First-timers were the worst. They didn't understand what was happening to them and they fought it, and this one fought it for an hour, the contractions coming and going, nothing showing.

"She's not progressing," Mara said. The torch was clamped under her chin. "I need to feel what's going on."

But the young ewe wouldn't have her, bolting every time Mara came near, slamming into the rail hard enough to bruise. They cornered her, the two of them, Dylan throwing an arm round her neck and bracing his whole weight against the wall, and Mara got in finally and felt, and felt nothing right. The lamb was backward. Coming tail-first, the worst way, the way that drowned them if you didn't get them out fast once the cord broke.

"Backward," she said. "I have to turn it or get the legs up. Hold her, Dylan, whatever happens hold her."

She worked the lamb back against the heaving of the ewe, found the hind legs folded forward under the body, cupped each tiny hoof in her palm so it wouldn't tear the womb, and brought them back and down one at a time, and it took everything she had, her whole arm aching, her face pressed against the ewe's flank, the smell of her filling everything. The young ewe screamed, the high sound they made, and Dylan said something, she couldn't hear what, and then the legs were back and she had them and she pulled with the contraction, hard, harder than felt safe, because backward you had to be fast.

The lamb came in a slithering rush, and it was limp.

"Down here," she said, hauling it into the lantern light. "Quick now." She swung it by the hind legs to clear the fluid from its lungs, once, twice, the way Tom had shown her, and she laid it down and cleared its mouth and rubbed it hard with straw, and it didn't breathe. "Come on," she said. "Come on, come on." She blew into its nose. Rubbed it. Blew again. A piece of straw she pushed up the nostril to make it cough, an old trick, Tom's mother's trick.

"Mum." Dylan was beside her now, the ewe forgotten, both of them bent over the lamb in the small circle of light with the dark and the storm pressing in all round. "Mum, is it—"

"Rub its chest. There. Hard."

He rubbed. She breathed for it. And she was about to stop, about to feel the thing she'd been holding off all winter rise up in her, the unfairness of it, the way the world took and took, when the lamb shuddered under their hands and coughed and drew a breath of its own, a rattling wet breath, and then another, and then it lifted its head.

Dylan made a sound. She looked and he was crying, just like that, silently, the tears running and his jaw working, and she understood it wasn't only the lamb. She put her bloodied hand on the back of his neck, on the wool of his father's hat, and held it there.

"There," she said. "There. He's alright. Look at him. He's alright."

"It's a he?"

"It's a he." She didn't know, hadn't looked, but she said it, and Dylan laughed, wet and broken, and wiped his face with the back of his wrist and left a smear of blood across his cheek. The young ewe was coming round now, turning, nosing at the thing on the ground that had hurt her so, and the old instinct woke in her and she began to lick, and the lamb bleated, and the shed went on around them, animal and warm and alive.

They got the ewe to take him. That was its own battle, the young mother skittish and unsure, and they penned her close so she couldn't refuse him, and after a while he found the teat and his tail went round and round the way it did when the milk came, and Mara stood and felt the ache settle into her back and knew it would be there for days.

Outside the storm had not let up. If anything it had thickened. She went to the door and opened it a crack and the snow drove in horizontal and stung her face and she shut it again. Past nine now. The yard light, which should have been a far-off comfort, was dark with everything else. No lights anywhere in the whole valley. She thought of the neighbors, the Pryces over the hill with their hundred ewes, old Gwilym alone in his cottage by the ford. Everyone in the dark together, everyone working their own small battles against the same long night.

"Drink this," she said, pouring tea from the thermos into the cup, and made Dylan take it first, watched him drink, his hands shaking a little from the cold or the adrenaline or both. Then she drank, and the tea was scalding and too sweet, he'd dumped sugar in it the way his father had liked it, and she said nothing about that either.

"How many more?" he asked.

"That third one's close. The rest should hold till morning." She paused. "Should."

"What if they don't?"

"Then we do them in the dark, same as these." She looked at him over the rim of the cup. "You sleep. I'll wake you if I need you. Go on, there's straw in the back, it's warm enough."

He shook his head. "I'm not leaving you out here on your own."

She looked at him a long moment. The shadows from the lantern moved on his face and for an instant he was so like Tom at that age—she'd known Tom at that age, they'd grown up three farms apart—that her breath caught. The same set to the jaw. The same stubbornness wearing the shape of devotion.

"Alright," she said. "Then sit. Save your strength. It's going to be a long one."

They sat together against the bales, the lantern between them, the ewes shifting and the new lambs bleating and finding their feet and falling and finding them again. The third ewe lay in her pen breathing hard but not yet ready, and they waited on her, and the wind worked at the shed like something trying to get in.

"Dad never let the power go out," Dylan said after a while. "He was always going to wire the generator up."

"I know."

"There's the cable on the bench. And the box of bits."

"I know it. I've looked at it a hundred times."

He was quiet. Then: "Do you reckon we could do it? In the morning. When it's light. I watched him do the milking shed wiring, I sort of know how."

She almost said no. Almost said it was a job for someone who knew what they were doing, almost said leave it, almost said all the careful protecting things she'd been saying since November, treating him like glass, like she was glass, the two of them moving round the house and the farm like the whole world might shatter if they touched it wrong. But she looked at her son, blood on his face and his father's hat on his head and his hands that had pulled a lamb back from the dead an hour ago, and she heard herself say, "Aye. We could try it. The two of us. If you think you remember."

"I remember some."

"Then we'll work it out. We'll get the book, there's a book in the office. We'll do it proper."

He nodded, and she saw it land in him, the weight of being trusted with it, and she understood she'd given him something. Not the wiring. The other thing. The permission to be more than a grieving boy. The work. Tom would have known to do this. Tom had always known when to hand Dylan a job too big and stand back and let him grow into it, and she'd forgotten, in the long fog of the winter, that this was a thing a parent did, was maybe the main thing a parent did, and she'd been so busy keeping him safe that she'd nearly kept him small.

"Your dad," she started, and stopped. She didn't know what she meant to say.

"It's alright," Dylan said. "You don't have to."

"No. Let me." She turned the cup in her hands. "Your dad would've hated this. The power out, the storm, all of it. He'd have been swearing blue murder. And then about now he'd have said something daft to make us laugh, and we'd all have felt better, and nothing about the situation would've changed at all, but we'd have felt better." She smiled in the dark. "I can't do that part. The daft thing. I never could. That was him."

"You do other things."

"Do I."

"You got that backward lamb out. Dad couldn't always. Remember the one two years back, the big one, he couldn't turn it and we lost it and the ewe. You'd have got it."

She hadn't known he remembered that. She hadn't known he watched that closely, kept that kind of account. "Maybe," she said. "Maybe I would. Smaller hands."

"It's not the hands."

She looked at him.

"It's not the hands," he said again, quieter, like he was embarrassed to have said it once. "You don't give up. Even when it's already dead you don't give up. That's why it lived. The one tonight. You kept going after Dad would've stopped."

She had to look away from him then. She looked at the lantern, at the small flame of it standing against all that dark, and she let the tears come because there was no use fighting them and the dark hid most of it anyway. Five months she'd held it. Through the funeral and the casseroles and the careful sympathetic faces, through the bank and the forms and the long arithmetic of whether one woman and a boy could run a farm that had broken stronger families, through the nights she lay in the bed that was too big now and listened for a breathing that wasn't there. Five months and it took her son telling her she didn't give up to finally, quietly, in a freezing shed in the dark, give way.

He didn't say anything. He shifted closer and put his head against her shoulder, which he hadn't done since he was small, since before he'd decided he was too old for it, and she put her arm around him and they sat like that and the storm howled and the lambs cried and the night went on.

The third ewe lambed at two in the morning. A single, big and healthy, came easy, almost no help needed, as if she'd waited for the worst to pass before doing her part. They got it dry and sucking and Mara checked the rest of the pens by torchlight and they were settled, content, none of them showing yet, and she let herself believe they'd make it to dawn.

"Sleep now," she told Dylan. "I mean it this time. Couple of hours. I'll wake you if it goes wrong."

This time he went, too tired to argue, burrowing into the straw in the back corner near the warmth of the animals, and she watched him until his breathing went deep and slow. Then she sat by the lantern alone, the way she'd sat alone so many nights now, except it didn't feel the same. Something had shifted in the dark between them, some weight redistributed so that she wasn't carrying all of it anymore, hadn't ever needed to carry all of it, had only thought she did.

She thought about the generator. About the cable on the bench and the book in the office and the two of them puzzling it out in the morning light, getting it wrong probably, getting it right eventually. About the season ahead, the dozens of ewes still to lamb, the shearing, the long summer, the whole impossible enormous year of it. She would not be able to do it the way Tom had done it. She would have to do it her own way, with her own hands, her smaller hands that didn't give up. And the boy would do it with her, and he'd do it his way too, whatever that turned out to be, and she found she wanted very much to see what that was.

Toward morning the wind dropped. She felt it before she heard it, the change in the pressure, the easing of the long pushing weight against the north wall. She went to the door and opened it and the snow had stopped. The clouds were tearing apart in the east and there was a paleness behind the hill that wasn't the storm anymore. The yard lay white and smooth and enormous and utterly still, drifted up against the buildings, the fences gone, the whole familiar world remade overnight into something strange and clean.

She stood in the doorway and breathed the cold clean air. Behind her in the warm dark the lambs were waking, calling for their mothers, the small new voices of the season. Soon she'd wake Dylan and they'd start the day's work, the feeding and the checking and then the generator if there was time, the two of them and the book and the box of bits. But for a moment she stood and watched the light come up over the snow, the first morning of the rest of it, and she thought, we made it through the night. We made it through. And there was no one to say it to, and so she said it to the morning, under her breath, to Tom maybe, or to herself, or to the boy sleeping in the straw who would never know she'd said it.

"We made it through."

The sun came up red on the snow and the lambs went on crying for their milk, and she turned back into the shed to begin.
