The card table came out first, which Margaret had not expected to mind. It was the cheap kind, the legs that folded with a metal complaint, the surface a marbled green meant to suggest felt and suggesting instead the inside of a swimming pool. A young man in a Cleveland Browns sweatshirt carried it sideways through the front door, and a woman she didn't know handed him four dollars, and that was that. The card table was gone. Her mother had played solitaire on it for thirty years, the cards laid out in their patient diagonal columns, and now it would go into the back of a hatchback and be played upon, or not, by people who would never know that.

Margaret sat at the kitchen table with the metal cashbox, which she had filled that morning with twenty dollars in ones and fives from her own wallet because she had not thought, until she was unlocking the front door at eight, that an estate sale required change. The cashbox was her mother's too. It had lived in the bottom drawer of the desk in the spare room, and inside it her mother had kept the documents she considered important in a way that did not always correspond to their actual importance: the deed to the house, yes, but also a yellowed clipping about Margaret's high school swim team, a Mass card for a man named Donald whom Margaret had never been able to place, two Indian-head pennies, and a recipe for apple butter in a hand that was not her mother's.

She had taken those things out. They were in a manila envelope in her car. The cashbox now held money that belonged to strangers and would, by the end of the day, belong to her, and this struck her as the truest thing she had understood all morning about what an estate sale was.

"You the daughter?"

The woman asking was perhaps seventy, in a quilted vest despite the warmth, holding a cut-glass bowl that Margaret recognized as the one her mother had used for cranberry sauce, only cranberry sauce, once a year. The bowl had a chip on the rim that Margaret had forgotten about until this exact second, and the forgetting and the remembering arrived together so that she felt, briefly, that her own mind was a house being emptied.

"I am," Margaret said.

"I'm sorry for your loss. This is lovely. Is the price firm?"

The bowl was marked three dollars. Margaret had not marked it; she'd hired a woman named Dell who did estate sales across three counties and who had come Thursday with a roll of orange stickers and a pricing gun and an air of brisk sympathy that Margaret had found, against her expectations, comforting. Dell had priced everything in two hours. She had walked through the house Margaret grew up in and assigned numbers to it, and the numbers were small, smaller than Margaret would have guessed, and Dell had explained, not unkindly, that people did not pay for sentiment. People paid for usefulness and for the rare chance of getting something for nothing. The bowl was three dollars because it had a chip.

"Two," Margaret said, because she did not want to argue and because she suddenly could not bear to think of the bowl going for full price, as though the chip were a flaw being overlooked rather than a thing that had happened in this house, the bowl set down too hard on the counter some Thanksgiving, her father saying *for God's sake, Ruth,* her mother saying nothing.

The woman seemed pleased and handed over two ones, and Margaret put them in the cashbox, and the bowl went out the door under the woman's arm like a small bright moon.

Dell was somewhere in the back of the house, in the bedrooms, where the real money was, she'd said—the bed frames and the dressers, the cedar chest. Margaret had agreed to let Dell handle the back if she could handle the front, the kitchen and the living room, because those were the rooms she could imagine sitting in, and she had wanted, she realized, to be present for the leaving of the things she remembered. She had not anticipated that this would feel like sitting at a wake where the body kept getting up and walking out under the arms of strangers.

The house was a 1953 ranch on a street called Sycamore, in a town whose name appeared on no one's lips outside a forty-mile radius. There were no sycamores on Sycamore. There were maples, and a single catalpa in the Hendersons' yard two doors down that had dropped its long brown beans onto the sidewalk every fall of Margaret's childhood, and that she had assumed, with a child's geographic confidence, was the most ordinary tree in the world until she'd moved to Columbus and found that almost no one had ever seen one. Her mother had lived in this house for fifty-one years. Margaret had lived in it for eighteen and then in pieces—summers, holidays, the long strange month after her father died—and then not at all, except as a visitor, a woman who arrived with a wheeled suitcase and slept in her childhood room under a poster of horses she'd been too sentimental to take down and her mother too indifferent to remove.

The horses were gone now. Dell had taken the poster down and thrown it away because no one buys a poster, and Margaret had watched her do it and said nothing, and that, more than the card table, more than the bowl, was the thing she kept returning to, the way you return to a sore tooth. The poster had cost her four dollars at a horse show in 1979. She had been eleven and had wanted to be a girl who rode horses, and had ridden exactly twice, and had loved the poster more than she had ever loved a horse. It was the kind of love a child could afford. And it had gone into a black trash bag without ceremony because it was worth nothing, and the worthlessness was not an insult, Margaret understood, it was simply the truth, and she had been carrying the truth around all morning like a stone in her shoe.

A man was looking at the recliner.

He was younger than Margaret, mid-forties maybe, with a beard going gray in patches and the cautious, slightly furtive manner of a person at an estate sale who has spotted something he wants and does not want anyone to know how much he wants it. The recliner was her father's. It was a brown corduroy thing that had been the single ugliest object in the house and her father's throne for the last decade of his life, the place where he had sat with the newspaper held up like a wall, the place where he had finally, on an ordinary Tuesday in 1994, set the newspaper down and said he didn't feel right and then never felt anything again. Her mother had not gotten rid of it. Margaret had assumed for years that this was sentiment and had revised the assumption, in her thirties, to inertia, and had revised it again, recently, to something she didn't have a word for, a refusal to rearrange the room around an absence.

"Does it recline okay?" the man asked. "The mechanism, I mean. These older ones, sometimes the spring's shot."

"I don't know," Margaret said. "It was my father's."

She hadn't meant to say it. The man's face did the thing faces did—the small adjustment, the lowering of the negotiating posture. "Oh," he said. "I'm sorry."

"It's fine. He died a long time ago." Twenty-nine years, she did not say. "Try it. Sit down."

The man looked uncertain, but he sat, and he found the lever on the side, and the footrest came up with the exact groan Margaret remembered, the groan that had been the sound of her father settling in for the evening, the groan that had meant, in her childhood, that the day's important business was over and the long quiet hours of adult boredom had begun. The man reclined fully and looked at the ceiling, and for a moment Margaret saw her father's posture exactly, the head back, the hands resting on the corduroy arms, and she had to look at the cashbox.

"Feels solid," the man said, getting up, embarrassed now to have lain down in a dead man's chair in front of the dead man's daughter. "I'll take it. It's—" he checked the sticker—"fifteen?"

"Fifteen," Margaret agreed.

"I've got a truck. I'll need to bring it around, if that's all right."

"Take your time," Margaret said, and the man went out to move his truck, and Margaret sat with the knowledge that her father's chair was leaving and felt, to her surprise, almost nothing, or rather felt the absence of the something she'd expected to feel, which was its own kind of event. She had braced for the chair and the chair had passed through her like weather. It was the bowl that had gotten her, and the poster. There was no predicting it. Grief, she was learning, did not respect the hierarchy of objects. It did not care what things cost or what they had witnessed. It came up through the floor wherever it pleased.

The man came back with a teenage boy, presumably his son, and the two of them carried the recliner out the front door, the man walking backward, the boy steering, and Margaret watched her father's chair go down the front steps her father had built, the steps he had poured himself one summer with a borrowed mixer, swearing, and she remembered standing in the wet concrete in her bare feet before it set, a small rebellion, her mother shrieking, and she thought: the footprints. She got up so fast the chair scraped.

The front steps. She had pressed her foot into them. She was certain. She had been—how old, six? seven?—and she had stepped into the wet concrete near the edge, where it met the grass, and her father had been furious and then not furious, had smoothed most of it but had left, she was suddenly and totally certain, the heel. The heel of a child's foot. It had been there her whole life. She had stepped over it ten thousand times without seeing it, the way you stop seeing the freckle on your own arm.

She went out the front door past the man and the boy loading the chair and crouched at the bottom of the steps and looked. The concrete was gray and pitted and stained with fifty years of Ohio winters and the green ghost of moss in the shaded corner. There was a crack running diagonally that she didn't remember. There were the rust streaks from the iron railing. And there, at the edge, where the concrete met the dirt, half-filled with windblown soil and the dead stalks of last year's weeds—a small depression. A heel. Her heel, from 1974, the print of a foot that no longer existed, that had been replaced cell by cell over fifty years until nothing of the original girl remained except this, a hollow in concrete, the negative space where she had been.

"You all right?" the man asked, coming down the steps with his end of the recliner.

"Fine," Margaret said, standing, brushing her hands on her slacks though she hadn't touched anything. "Watch the bottom step. It's loose."

It wasn't loose. She had no idea why she'd said it. The man nodded and watched the bottom step anyway, and she went back inside.

By eleven the house had been moving through itself for three hours, strangers flowing in the front door and out again carrying pieces of her, and Margaret had developed a kind of professional detachment that frightened her a little with how easily it came. She took the money. She made change. She said *it was my mother's* less and less, having learned that it changed the transaction, made people feel they were taking something they shouldn't, slowed the line. She let the things go nameless. A woman bought the good knives, the ones her mother sharpened on a whetstone at the kitchen sink, drawing the blade toward herself in a motion that had terrified Margaret as a child. A man bought the kitchen clock, the one shaped like a teakettle that had hung over the stove and whose ticking had been the metronome of every meal of her childhood, and Margaret heard, when he lifted it off its nail, the silence it left, a silence that had a shape, the exact shape of the clock.

She thought she had braced for the big things. The dining set, the hutch, the sideboard with the water ring her mother had blamed her for and that Margaret had always privately believed was her father's, from a sweating glass of the gin he was not supposed to be drinking. These went, and she watched them go, and they did not undo her. It was the small things, the cheap things, the things that had no value to anyone but her and barely to her—a juice glass with a faded orange print of a rooster, part of a set that had come free with gasoline in an era Margaret was too young to remember but that her mother had kept, four roosters surviving of an original eight, and a woman bought all four for a dollar and Margaret had to go into the bathroom and run the water and press a paper towel to her face.

The bathroom still smelled like her mother. That was the thing no one could buy and no one would. The house had a smell, a layered, specific smell of this particular soap and this particular mustiness and something underneath that was just the smell of the people who had lived here, and it would persist, Margaret thought, until the next family painted the walls and put down new carpet and cooked their own food, and then it would be their smell, and her mother's smell would be gone from the world entirely, having existed nowhere but here, having been recorded nowhere, the smell of a woman who had lived eighty-one years and would soon not even leave a scent behind.

Dell knocked on the bathroom door. "You okay in there? We got a question about the cedar chest."

"Coming," Margaret said.

The cedar chest was in the master bedroom, at the foot of the bed that was already gone, sold to a couple who'd come at opening with the focused look of people furnishing a first apartment. The chest was the one thing Margaret had told Dell she might keep, and then hadn't decided, and now a woman wanted it, a heavy woman with kind eyes who ran her hand along the lid and said her grandmother had had one just like it.

Margaret opened it. She didn't know why; the woman hadn't asked. She lifted the heavy lid and the smell of cedar came up, sharp and clean and unchanged in fifty years, the one smell in the house that had not aged, and beneath the cedar the things her mother had kept there: a wedding dress gone the color of weak tea, folded in tissue. A baby blanket. A bundle of letters tied with a shoelace because her mother had never owned ribbon, had thought ribbon was a waste. A man's wristwatch, stopped.

"Oh," the woman said. "I'm sorry, I didn't mean—if there's things in there—"

"No," Margaret said. "I'll take these out."

She knelt at the chest and began removing the contents, and the woman, decent, stepped back and made herself busy looking at the closet doors, giving Margaret the privacy of pretended distraction. Margaret lifted out the wedding dress and laid it on the bare mattress ticking of the bed that was no longer there, on the floor where the bed had been. She lifted out the baby blanket, yellow, and did not let herself wonder whether it had been hers or her brother's, her brother who had died at four days old in 1961, two years before Margaret, the brother who was the reason, Margaret had always suspected, that her mother had been the way she was, careful and braced and unable to fully give herself to the daughter who lived, as though loving her too much would be tempting something. They had never spoken of him. Margaret had learned of him from a cousin at a wedding when she was thirty. The blanket might be his. The blanket might be the only soft thing that had ever touched him.

She set it down very gently.

She lifted out the letters, and the watch, and at the bottom of the chest, under everything, flat against the cedar, was a single sheet of paper that turned out to be a drawing. A child's drawing, in crayon, of a house. A box of a house with a triangle roof and a yellow sun in the corner with lines coming off it and four stick figures in front, two big and two small, and Margaret looked at it for a long time before she understood that one of the small figures had been drawn and then scribbled over, hard, in black, by the same hand, a child's hand, her hand, she knew it was her hand, she could feel in her own fingers the pressure of the scribbling, though she had no memory of it at all. Four figures. Two big, two small. She had drawn her family the way it should have been. And then she had crossed out the brother she had never met, or her mother had told her to, or—she would never know. The drawing had been at the bottom of the cedar chest under the wedding dress for sixty years, and her mother had kept it, the drawing with the scribbled-out child, had kept it where she kept the dress and the blanket and the watch, in the place where she kept the things that were too much to look at and too much to throw away.

"I'll take the chest," Margaret said.

"I'm sorry?" The woman turned.

"It's not for sale anymore. I'm sorry. I'm going to keep it."

"Of course," the woman said. "Of course, honey. You keep it." And she touched Margaret once on the shoulder, a stranger, and went out of the room, and Margaret heard her say something to Dell, and Dell came and stood in the doorway but didn't come in.

"You want me to mark it sold-removed?"

"Yes."

"You want help getting it to your car?"

"Not yet," Margaret said. "Leave it. I'll deal with it."

Dell went away. Margaret sat on the floor of her parents' bedroom, in the rectangle of cleaner carpet where the bed had stood, among the things from the chest, and held the drawing in both hands. Outside she could hear the sale continuing, the murmur of strangers, the screen door's spring and slap, the sound of her childhood being carried out into the spring afternoon. A car started. A car door closed. Somewhere in the house someone laughed, a stranger's laugh, easy and unbothered, a person who had found a good deal on a lamp.

She thought she would cry and she didn't. She had cried in the bathroom over four rooster glasses and now, holding the proof that she had once known a grief too large for her small hand and had pressed it down hard in black crayon and her mother had kept the pressing-down for sixty years, she found herself dry-eyed and very still. There were griefs you could cry about. They were almost a luxury, those ones. And there were the others, the ones too far down, that you could only sit with on the floor of an emptying house and hold in your two hands.

She put the drawing in the manila envelope in her mind, the one in her car, with the deed and the Mass card and the apple butter recipe and the swim team clipping. She would put it there literally, soon. For now she let herself hold it.

After a while she got up. She put the dress and the blanket and the letters and the watch and the drawing back into the cedar chest, in order, the way her mother had kept them, and she closed the lid, and the cedar smell shut itself away again into the dark. She would carry it to the car at the end of the day. She would take it back to Columbus and put it at the foot of her own bed and not open it for a long time, and then one day she would, and her own daughter, who was twenty-four and lived in Chicago and called on Sundays, would someday open it after Margaret was gone and find these things and a drawing of a house with four figures and one scribbled out and would not know, would never know, what any of it meant, and would have to decide, alone, what was worth keeping.

That was the whole of it, Margaret thought. That was the entire arrangement. You spent your life accumulating the freight of meaning, and you stored it where you could, and at the end strangers came and carried most of it out the door for a dollar, and the rest you handed forward to people who would not be able to read it, and that was not a tragedy, or it was, but it was also simply the shape of the thing, the only shape it came in.

She went back out to the kitchen. A man was waiting at the table with a stack of her mother's mixing bowls and a patient expression.

"Sorry to keep you," Margaret said, and sat down at the cashbox, and made change, and the screen door opened and slapped, opened and slapped, all the long bright afternoon, until the house was nearly empty and the light came in low and gold across the floors where the furniture had stood, lighting the clean rectangles, the unfaded squares, the map of everything that had been there, that you could only see now that it was gone.
