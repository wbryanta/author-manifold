The pump house door had swollen in its frame again, and Daniel had to put his shoulder to it before it gave with the sound of something tearing. Inside, the air was cool and smelled of rust and old water, the particular mineral dampness that had not changed in thirty years. The pressure gauge read low, as he'd known it would. He stood looking at it the way their father used to, hands on hips, as if the right posture might coax an explanation out of the equipment.

"It's the line again," Wade said behind him. He was standing in the doorway with two thermoses, blocking the light. "Same as last summer. Probably the same crack, working open a little more."

"You don't know that."

"I know it the way you know it. We just don't want to dig the whole thing up."

Daniel took one of the thermoses. The coffee inside was already going lukewarm, which meant Wade had filled them an hour ago, before the drive out, back when the morning had still seemed like it might hold. They had agreed to fix the line. That was the agreement. They had not agreed on anything else, and both of them were carrying the unspoken parts around like the tools they hadn't picked up yet.

Outside the sun was coming over the eastern ridge and laying itself across the rows. The orchard ran downhill from the pump house in long curving lines, the trees set out by their grandfather in a pattern that followed the contour of the slope so the water would not run too fast. Apricot, mostly, with a block of cherries at the bottom that hadn't borne worth selling in years. From here the trees looked healthy enough, full-leafed, the early fruit set hard and green among them. You had to walk in close to see the ones that were dying from the roots up, the limbs going gray and brittle, leaves curling before their time.

"We should start at the manifold and work down," Daniel said. "Check the pressure at each zone valve. Find where we lose it."

"That's a whole day."

"You got somewhere to be?"

Wade didn't answer that. He set his thermos on the concrete lip of the pump house foundation and went to the truck for the shovels and the spool of poly pipe and the box of fittings, and Daniel watched him go, his brother's gait still the loose, rolling walk of a tall man who'd been a tall boy too soon and never quite organized himself around it. Wade was forty-one. There was gray coming in at his temples that Daniel kept being surprised by, as though some part of him had filed his brother away at nineteen and never updated the record.

They walked the first zone together, following the buried line by the slight depression in the soil where it ran, by the heads that ought to have been weeping and weren't. The grass between the rows was already drying to the color of straw. June, and it should have been green another month at least. The wells were lower every year. Their father had drilled the second well the summer Daniel left for the coast, and it had been a kind of accusation, that well, a way of saying we will keep going whether you stay or not. Now both wells together gave less than the one used to.

"Here," Wade said. He'd crouched at a low spot where the dirt was darker, faintly muddy, the only damp ground for fifty feet in any direction. "Feel that."

Daniel knelt and pressed his palm to the soil. Cool. Wet, when everything else was dry. The water was coming up here instead of going where it was meant to go.

"There's your crack," Wade said. "Same as I told you."

"Maybe. Could be a fitting let go."

"Could be. Either way we dig."

They dug. The shovels bit through the dry crust and into the heavier clay beneath, and the work settled into them, that old rhythm of two people who have dug together before, one going down while the other clears, trading without discussion. The sun climbed. Daniel felt the sweat start at the small of his back and then everywhere, felt the particular ache come into his forearms that he didn't get from any work he did in the city, a deep honest soreness he half welcomed. For a while neither of them said anything that wasn't about the hole.

It was Wade who broke it, because it was always going to be Wade.

"Marcus Tilden made an offer," he said. He didn't look up. He drove the shovel in and levered out a wedge of clay and laid it on the pile. "Through his agent. Came to the house Tuesday."

"How much."

"Enough that you'd take it seriously. More than the orchard's worth as orchard, anyway. He wants it for the water rights and the road frontage. He'd plant houses."

"He'd plant houses." Daniel sat back on his heels. The ditch was thigh deep now and they could see the black line of the pipe running through the bottom of it, the poly gone dull and chalky with age. "Forty years Dad spent making sure this water went to trees and Marcus Tilden wants to run it through a hundred toilets."

"Dad's been dead two years." Wade said it without heat. "And the trees are dying, Danny. You can see it same as me. That whole upper block, the old Blenheims, they're done. Root rot's into them. We lost nine last winter and we'll lose more this one."

"So we replant."

"With what money?"

"There's the equipment loan—"

"Which we're behind on. Which I've been behind on since March, while you were—" Wade stopped himself. He'd never been good at stopping himself; it was new, that he could. He bent and brushed the soil away from the pipe, tracing it with his fingers until he found the place, a hairline gone soft and weeping, exactly where he'd said it would be. "There. You see it now?"

Daniel saw it. He didn't say anything.

They cut the pipe on either side of the failure with a hacksaw, and the water that had been pooling in the line ran out into the bottom of the ditch and turned the clay to a slick brown soup that they had to bail with a coffee can before they could work. Daniel did the bailing. Wade dry-fit the new section, two couplers and a foot of fresh pipe, and they let the cut ends dry in the sun while they drank the rest of the coffee sitting on the spoil pile with their boots in the hole.

"You think I haven't thought about it," Daniel said finally. "Selling. I've thought about it."

"You've never said so."

"Because thinking about a thing and wanting it aren't the same."

Wade turned the can of primer in his hands, not opening it. "I'm not saying I want it. You think I want to be the brother who sold Granddad's orchard to a man who'll bulldoze it for a cul-de-sac called Apricot Hollow? You think that's a thing I want carved on me?" He laughed, short and without much in it. "But I'm the one who's here, Danny. I'm the one who drives out every morning and looks at it. You get to keep it pretty in your head. You get to have it be the place we were kids. I've got to have it be the place that's failing, every single day."

It was true, and the truth of it sat between them and neither could move it. Daniel had left at eighteen and not looked back the way you're supposed to not look back, and the orchard had become for him a thing made mostly of light: the smell of the bloom in March, the way the irrigation set in the evening would throw rainbows up the rows when the sun was low, their mother carrying out a pitcher of tea to where the men were working. He had not been here for the bad parts. He had not been here for the part where their father got too proud to ask for help, the part where the second well came in weak, the part where Wade quietly took out a loan in his own name to keep the place from going under and never told anyone, which Daniel had found out about only because the bank statement had come to the house while he was staying there after the funeral.

"You should have told me about the loan," Daniel said.

"Why? So you could have felt guilty from four hundred miles away? You'd have sent money. I didn't want money. I wanted—" Wade shook his head. "Never mind what I wanted."

"Tell me."

"I wanted you to come home and you weren't going to, so." He cracked the primer at last, and the chemical smell of it cut sharp through the morning. "So I quit wanting that. It's a thing you can quit."

They glued the joints. The primer first, purple and volatile, swabbed onto the pipe and into the couplers, and then the cement over it, and the quick firm push and quarter-turn that their father had taught them both with the same words, twist it home, hold it like you mean it, count to ten. Daniel held the joint and counted under his breath and felt, absurdly, that he might cry, and didn't, and the moment passed the way they do.

"It'll need an hour to set before we charge it," Wade said. "You hungry?"

They ate in the shade of the pump house with their backs against the cool concrete. Wade had brought sandwiches, and they were good sandwiches, and Daniel understood that his brother had made them that morning, standing in the kitchen of the house they grew up in, and the understanding made his throat tight. There was a whole life out here he had absented himself from. Wade in that kitchen every morning. Wade driving these rows. Wade carrying it all alone while Daniel sent a card at Christmas and called on birthdays and told himself that distance was just geography.

"You ever think about Mom," Daniel said. "Out here. What she'd say."

"All the time."

"What do you think she'd say."

Wade considered it, chewing. From here they could see the whole lower orchard and beyond it the valley going hazy in the heat, the highway a far silver thread, and on the far ridge the first of the new houses that had crept up out of town in the last decade, white and identical, like teeth. "I think she'd say the land doesn't owe us anything," Wade said. "And we don't owe it anything either. She wasn't sentimental about it the way Dad was. The way you are. She liked it because it was ours and we were here. The trees were just trees to her. It was the us part she cared about."

"That's a convenient thing for her to think, if you want to sell."

"I'm telling you what I think she'd say. You asked." Wade balled up the wax paper from his sandwich. "And anyway it's not convenient. It cuts both ways. If it's the us part that matters, then the orchard's already gone, isn't it. Because there's no us out here. There's me. There's me and a bank and a man named Marcus Tilden."

A red-tailed hawk came up off a fence post down the rows and rode the warming air without beating its wings, and they both watched it, because it was the kind of thing you watched. Their father had loved the hawks. Had let the gophers run a little, almost, so the hawks would stay. Everything out here was connected to everything else if you'd lived in it long enough to see the connections, and Daniel realized that this was a thing he had lost by leaving, this seeing, and that Wade had it and was tired of it, was tired in a way Daniel had no right to be tired.

"What would you do," Daniel said. "If it sold. What would you actually do."

Wade was quiet a long moment. "I don't know. That's the truth. I've been doing this so long I don't know what I am if I'm not doing it. Maybe that's the worst reason there is to keep going. Maybe that's exactly Dad's reason." He turned his head and looked at Daniel directly, which he hadn't done much all morning. "What would you do. With your half. Be honest."

And Daniel found that he didn't have a clean answer either. Pay down the mortgage on the condo, sure. Put something away. The money would disappear into a life that was comfortable and a little empty, and he would tell people at parties that he'd grown up on an orchard, in the past tense, and it would become a story, a charming origin, the place that explained why he liked his fruit a certain way and got quiet in the spring. He would carry the orchard around as a thing he used to be from. That was what selling meant. Not the loss of the land. The conversion of the land into a story about himself.

"I don't know either," he said.

"Well." Wade pushed himself up, dusting his hands on his jeans. "There's a comfort. Neither of us knows a goddamn thing." But he said it gently, and he put out a hand and pulled Daniel to his feet, and for a second they stood close together in the doorway of the pump house, two grown men, the same height nearly, with the same forearms and the same way of squinting, and Daniel thought of all the times their father had stood in this exact spot and felt the years stack up and lean.

They charged the line slowly. Wade went up to the pump house and cracked the main valve a quarter turn while Daniel walked the zone with his hand near the new joint, watching for any weep. The water came on with a soft surge, the pipe filling, the chalky old line going taut and cool. At the first head the water spat air and then steadied into a fine spray, and at the second, and the third, and Daniel walked the row with the spray drifting over him in the heat and was glad of it, the way you're glad of a thing that works, that you fixed with your hands. He bent at the new joint. Dry. Holding.

"It's holding," he called up the slope.

Wade came down to see for himself, because that was a thing about Wade, he had to see for himself, and he crouched at the joint and felt around it and nodded. "Good glue-up," he said. "Dad would've found something wrong with it."

"He'd have said we used too much cement."

"He always said that."

"Even when we used too little."

"Especially then." Wade smiled, and it was the first real one of the day, and it changed his whole face, made him for a moment the boy who'd taught Daniel to drive the old tractor down these very rows, both of them too young, their mother yelling from the porch and their father pretending not to see. "You remember he had that one speech. About the water."

"Which speech. He had nine."

"The one where he'd get going about how the water came down from the snowpack and into the river and the river into the canal and the canal into our ditch and out of the ditch into the line and out of the line into the tree and into the fruit and into us. The whole sermon. The water becomes the apricot, boys. The water becomes the apricot becomes the man."

"And then he'd eat one right off the tree to prove it."

"Juice all down his chin. Eighty years old, eating fruit like a kid." Wade stood. The spray drifted between them. "I think about that one a lot. The water becoming the thing. Because the thing is, it's true, isn't it. It's not just a pretty saying. That's actually how it works. We're mostly that water. We grew up drinking it and eating what it made. There's apricot in us, Danny. Literally. Down in the cells. And if we sell, that just—stops. The water goes to the houses instead. To strangers. They become the place and we stop being it."

Daniel didn't say anything. The hawk had come back, or another one, and was working the same thermal.

"That's the sentimental version," Wade said, almost angry at himself. "Listen to me. That's exactly the kind of thing that'll keep us out here losing money until we're old men with nothing, telling ourselves we've got apricot in our cells. That's how he died, basically. He drilled a second well rather than admit the first one was failing. And I'm out here gluing pipe and quoting his sermons and you're—" He stopped. "I don't know what you are. You came back to argue with me about a thing you left."

"I came back to help fix the line."

"You came back because you can't stand to be the one who said sell. You want me to say it so you can hate me a little and feel clean." Wade said this flatly, and it landed flatly, the way true things sometimes do, without drama, just settling into place. "It's all right. I'll say it. I'll be the one. I've been the one for everything else."

They stood there with the water going around them. Daniel wanted to deny it and couldn't, because it was at least half true, and the half that was true was the half that mattered. He had wanted to come out here and lay his hand on this old line and feel something rise up in him, some clear instruction, the land speaking, and the land had not spoken. The land had just been dry, and dying in patches, and beautiful in the way that things are beautiful when you've stopped being able to afford them.

"What if we didn't decide today," Daniel said.

"Tilden's offer has a clock on it. Thirty days."

"Then we decide in thirty days. But not today. Today we just—" He gestured at the line, the spray, the green hard fruit on the trees. "Today we fixed it. Let it be fixed for a day. We can sell a thing that works as easy as a thing that's broken. Easier."

Wade looked at him for a long moment, and something in his face eased, not agreement exactly, but the willingness to set the weight down for an afternoon. "Easier," he agreed. "Tilden won't care if the line holds. But I'd know. There's that."

"There's that."

They walked the rest of the zones together while the system ran, checking heads, clearing the ones the gophers had undermined, and at the bottom of the orchard they came to the cherry block that hadn't borne in years, the trees gone leggy and wild, and stood looking at it. A few cherries hung in the high branches, too few to pick, too high to reach, going from green to gold to the deep red that the birds would have before any person could. Their grandfather had planted these. Their grandfather, whom neither of them remembered well, a photograph mostly, a man in a hat standing where they were standing now.

"You know what's funny," Wade said. "He didn't grow up here either. Granddad. He came out from Oklahoma with nothing. This wasn't his land, it was just land, and he made it his by working it. So this whole idea we've got, that it's ours by blood, that it's some sacred inheritance—he wouldn't have understood that. To him it was a thing you could buy, that he did buy, with money he saved cutting other men's hay. He bought it from a stranger. We'd be selling it to one. There's a symmetry."

"You've thought about this a lot."

"I told you. I'm the one who's here." Wade reached up and pulled down a low branch and there was a single cherry on it, ripe, improbable, and he picked it and looked at it and then handed it to Daniel. "Here. Apricot's not ready but the cherries are early. Have some inheritance."

Daniel ate it. It was warm from the sun and almost too sweet, that overripe edge to it, and the juice was exactly as he remembered and he hadn't remembered it in years. He spat the pit into his palm and looked at it, the hard pale stone of it, the next tree folded up inside, and he thought about putting it in his pocket and didn't. He dropped it on the ground at the base of the old tree, where it had as much chance as anything.

"All right," he said. "Thirty days."

"Thirty days." Wade clapped him on the shoulder, left his hand there a second longer than the gesture required. "Come on. We've got to shut it down and bleed the line or it'll hammer. And then I'll buy you a beer in town and we can not talk about it some more."

They walked back up the slope toward the pump house, the long rows falling away behind them, the water still running, the hawk gone over the ridge, and the day went on the way June days do, indifferent and golden and far too short, over a piece of ground that had been a stranger's once and would be a stranger's again, but was, for one more afternoon, theirs to keep alive.
