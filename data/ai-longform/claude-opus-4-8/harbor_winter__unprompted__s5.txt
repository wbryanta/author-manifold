The water told you things if you let it, and Eli Vance had spent fifty-two years learning its dialects. Now he stood at the end of the breakwater with the girl beside him, the two of them watching the gray harbor breathe, and he tried to find the words to give her what the water had given him.

"There," he said, pointing with a hand gone knotty as driftwood. "See where it goes dark and smooth, like somebody laid a ribbon across it?"

Nora squinted. She was eleven, small for it, wrapped in a coat that had been her father's and still carried the smell of him faintly in the wool. "I see it."

"That's the current running. Deep water there. The smooth part lies to you—looks calm, looks safe. But that's where the channel is. That's where a boat wants to be when the tide's against her."

"Why does it look calm if it's the dangerous part?"

He almost smiled. It was the right question, the one nobody asked anymore because nobody needed to. "Because the danger and the safety are the same thing, mostly. Depends what you're trying to do."

The wind came off the water with the smell of salt and diesel and, under that, the cannery smell, the iron-and-fish reek that had soaked into the town so long ago that people only noticed it when it threatened to stop. And it was threatening now. The notice had gone up in October, taped to the cannery's office window in a plastic sleeve to keep the rain off, and everyone in Cordell Reach had read it standing in the cold. End of operations, March. They thanked the community for its years of partnership. They used the word *consolidation* the way a doctor used a word you had to look up later, alone, when the news had already done its work.

Nora's mother worked the line. Had worked the line eight years, since the divorce, her hands so scarred from the knives that she couldn't feel the difference between hot and warm anymore. She'd told Eli this at Thanksgiving, holding her coffee, and there'd been no self-pity in it, only the flat report of a fact, the way Eli might have told her the bar was shoaling at the river mouth.

"Granddad." Nora tugged his sleeve. "What's that one?"

A patch of water out past the green can buoy had gone strange, a ruffled stretch like the nap of velvet brushed wrong. He felt the old pleasure of being asked, the pleasure of having an answer.

"Wind against tide," he said. "Tide's running out, wind's pushing in. They argue with each other and that's what the argument looks like. You'd feel it in a boat—chop with no rhythm to it, water coming at you from two directions. You learn to read it so you're not surprised. Surprise is what drowns men. Not the sea. The surprise."

She nodded slowly, filing it away with the seriousness she brought to everything, a seriousness that troubled him sometimes because it seemed too old for her, a thing she'd been issued early like the coat.

They walked back along the breakwater, Eli setting his feet carefully on the wet granite blocks. His knees had opinions now that they'd never had at sea. Behind them the harbor opened its gray throat to the gray sky, and the few boats still working swung at their moorings, fewer every year, the *Marguerite* and the *Donna K* and old Pelletier's *Sea Hound* with its hull the color of a bruise. When Eli had piloted, there'd been ninety boats in this harbor. He'd brought the big draggers in over the bar on the worst nights of the year, standing in the wheelhouse beside men who trusted him with their lives because he could read what they couldn't, the set of the swell, the way the water stacked up over the shallows, the single dark seam that marked safe passage through the breakers. He'd been the town's eyes. Now the town was going blind and it didn't matter, because there was nothing left worth seeing it home.

"Mom says I'm not supposed to bother you about the boat," Nora said.

"You're not bothering me."

"She says you're tired."

"Your mother worries." He stopped, looked down at her. "Do I look tired?"

She considered this with her grave attention. "A little. Around the eyes."

He laughed, surprising himself, the sound rusty. "Around the eyes. All right. The eyes have earned it."

The boat was the *Pilot*, a twenty-eight-foot wooden lobster boat he'd kept long after retiring, kept and maintained and taken out on calm days for no reason but to feel the water move under him. Caroline, his wife, had called it his church, and like a church it was a thing he tended past all reason, sanding and painting and replacing planks, because to stop would have been to admit something he wasn't ready to admit. Caroline was three years gone now. The boat remained. He'd promised Nora he'd take her out before the winter closed down hard, and the promise sat between them like a held breath.

"Saturday," he said. "If the weather holds. I'll take you out and show you the water from the water. It's a different thing entirely, seeing it from down low."

Her face did something he hadn't seen it do in a while. It opened. "Really?"

"Really. Ask your mother. Tell her I'll have you back by noon and that I haven't drowned anyone yet."

"That's not funny, Granddad."

"It's a little funny."

She was fighting a smile, and losing, and he counted the loss as a victory.

Saturday came clear and cold, the sky scrubbed to a hard pale blue, the kind of winter day that fooled you into thinking the cold was a small thing until it got into your bones and stayed. Eli was at the float before her, going over the engine, his breath ghosting in the cabin. He heard her before he saw her, her boots quick on the ramp, her mother's slower tread behind.

Delia stood at the top of the float with her arms crossed, not against him, he understood, but against the cold and the morning and the general drift of her life. She'd been a pretty girl. She was a handsome woman now in the way of women who'd stopped having time to be pretty. The cannery had done that, the early shifts and the cold rooms and the standing.

"You'll keep her in the harbor," Delia said. It wasn't quite a question.

"In the harbor. Maybe just outside if it stays flat. I want to show her the bar."

"Dad."

"Flat as a table out there today, Delia. I checked the marine forecast and I checked the water, and the water doesn't lie even when the forecast does." He wiped his hands on a rag. "She's safe with me. Safer than anyplace."

Something moved across his daughter's face, some old grief he couldn't name, and she looked away from him, out at the harbor. "I know that," she said quietly. "I always knew that." Then, to Nora: "Life jacket on the whole time. Listen to your grandfather. Do what he says, when he says it, no arguing."

"I never argue," Nora said.

"You're arguing now."

The girl climbed down into the boat with the unconscious balance of someone who'd grown up near water, and Eli felt a small fierce pride in that, in the genetic memory of it, the sea passing down through the blood even as the town that the sea had made was dying around them. He started the engine. It caught on the second try, settled into the low chuckle he knew the way he knew his own heartbeat, and the smell of exhaust and cold sea filled the cockpit.

They idled out through the moorings. Eli kept his hand light on the wheel and let Nora stand beside him in the open wheelhouse, the wind pulling at her hair.

"Now," he said. "Same water you saw from the breakwater. But watch how it changes when you're in it. Watch the surface ahead of us, not right under the bow. Always ahead. You read the water you're coming to, not the water you're already in. The water you're in, it's too late to do much about."

She gripped the rail and stared forward with her whole body. He watched her watch it.

"See that line there, the color change? Dark to light?"

"Yeah."

"That's the shoal coming up. Light water's shallow. Sand and gravel under it, reflects the light back up. Dark water's deep. You keep us in the dark today, but the dark's not always safe, remember, it depends—"

"On what you're trying to do," she finished.

"On what you're trying to do." He felt something tighten in his chest, the pleasure and the ache of it together. "You're going to be good at this," he said. "You've got the eye. Some people never get it. They look at the water their whole lives and all they ever see is water."

"Did Mom have the eye?"

He hesitated. "Your mother never wanted it," he said finally, which was the kindest true thing. "She wanted away from here. Can't blame her for that. There wasn't going to be anything here for her." He turned the wheel a few degrees, lining up the channel. "Wasn't supposed to be like this. The town. When she was your age there was work for everybody, more work than people. The boats and the cannery and the trucks coming in and out all day. You couldn't find a parking spot on Water Street." He shook his head. "Funny what disappears. You think a thing's permanent because it was there when you were born. But everything that's here was made by somebody, and what's made can come unmade."

He hadn't meant to say so much. The water did that to him, loosened him, the way it loosened the men he used to pilot, who'd tell him things in the dark wheelhouse on the long approach that they'd never tell another soul. He glanced at Nora to see if he'd frightened her, but she was watching the water, jaw set, taking it in like everything else.

"They're closing the cannery," she said. "I know about it. Mom thinks I don't but I do."

"I figured you did."

"Is Mom going to be okay?"

There it was. The real question, the one underneath all the other questions, the channel running deep and dark beneath the calm. He could have lied. He was good at the comfortable lie; he'd told plenty of them across the years, to wives waiting on the dock, to men who needed to believe the bar was passable. But the girl had the eye, and you didn't lie to someone with the eye, it was an insult to the gift.

"I don't know," he said. "That's the truth. I don't know what your mother's going to do, or what any of them are going to do. Some'll leave. Some'll stay and figure it out. Your mother's tougher than this place, I'll tell you that much. Tougher than me." He let out a breath. "But I won't tell you it'll be fine, because I don't know that it'll be fine, and you'd know I was lying anyway."

She was quiet. The boat moved through the dark water and the shoal slid past on the starboard side, the light water hissing faintly.

"Thank you," she said.

"For what?"

"For not lying."

He had to look away from her then, out at the bar where the swell came in long and gentle, building over the shallows and breaking white and tumbling itself out, the eternal patient work of it. His eyes stung and he told himself it was the wind.

They reached the mouth where the harbor opened to the bay, and he throttled back and let the boat rock easy in the swell. Out here you could see the whole town laid along the shore, the gray houses climbing the hill, the white spire of the church, the long brick bulk of the cannery with its loading docks and its tall chimney. Smoke still rose from the chimney. Until March it would keep rising. Then it would stop, and the cold would move into the building, and the rust would have its way, and in ten years or twenty the harbor would be a place where people came to look at the picturesque ruin of an industry and wonder, vaguely, what it had been like.

"That's the bar," he said. "Where the river meets the sea. Where I did my work. The water piles up here because it's shallow and because the river's pushing out against the tide pushing in. On a bad day, a storm day, this is the worst place on the whole coast. Waves that'll stand a boat on her end. I've seen the bar break clear across, no way through, men sitting outside it in the dark waiting for slack water and praying." He pointed. "But you watch it long enough and you learn it. Every bar has a slot. A place where the water's just a little deeper, where the waves don't build the same way. You find the slot and you ride it in on the back of a swell and you don't look behind you. You never look behind you on the bar, because if you see what's coming you'll lose your nerve and turn, and turning's what kills you. You commit and you go."

"You commit and you go," Nora repeated softly.

"That's the whole of it, really. Not just the bar." He laughed at himself. "Listen to me. Old man making speeches."

"I like your speeches."

"You're the only one." He put his hand on her shoulder, the small bones of it under the heavy coat. "Look there now. Watch the swell come in. Count it. They come in sets, you'll see—a few small ones, then the big ones, then small again. You time your run for after the big set. Watch."

They watched together. The swells came in, and she counted under her breath, and after a while she said, "There's a pattern. Little, little, big, big, big, little."

"That's it. That's exactly it. You see it." He felt absurdly moved, his throat thick. "Took me a season to see what you just saw in five minutes."

"You're just saying that."

"I don't just say things. You'll learn that about me, if I'm around long enough for you to learn it."

The words hung there, and he regretted them, because her face changed and he saw that she'd heard the thing under them, the *if*, the way she heard everything. She was looking at him now instead of the water.

"You're around," she said. A statement, almost a command.

"I'm around." He squeezed her shoulder. "I'm just an old man who likes the sound of his own voice. Don't pay me any mind. Now—you want to take the wheel?"

That distracted her, as he'd known it would. He positioned her in front of him, her hands on the wheel under his, and he showed her how to feel the boat answer, the lag between the turn and the response, the way you had to think ahead of the vessel the same way you thought ahead on the water.

"Bring her around slow. Point the bow at the church spire. Use it as your mark. Pilots always run on marks—a steeple, a water tower, two points lined up. The water shifts and the buoys drag but the land stays put."

She brought the boat around, overcorrecting, then finding it, her tongue caught between her teeth in concentration. The bow swung to the spire and steadied.

"There," he said. "You're piloting. You're a harbor pilot now, third generation. Don't tell your mother."

"Why not?"

"Because she'll worry, and because some things are just for us." He let go of the wheel, slowly, and let her have it, standing close behind in case, but she had it, she held the boat steady on its mark, and he stood in the cold winter sun with his hands free at his sides and watched his granddaughter steer them home, and he thought his heart might come apart in his chest with the pride and the grief of it, the two of them the same thing, the danger and the safety, depending on what you were trying to do.

They came in through the moorings and he took the wheel back for the landing, brought the *Pilot* alongside the float as soft as setting down a sleeping child, and Nora jumped out with the bow line and made it fast the way he'd shown her, a clove hitch and two half hitches, her cold fingers clumsy but getting it. Delia was waiting. She'd been waiting the whole time, he understood. She'd never left.

"Mom! I steered. Granddad let me steer, I steered us all the way back from the bar, I found the slot in the waves and I—"

"The bar." Delia looked at Eli over the girl's head. "You took her to the bar."

"To look at it. Not over it. We never crossed it." He climbed up onto the float, his knees protesting. "She's a natural, Delia. I mean it. She read the swell sets in five minutes. She's got the eye, the real thing, I've only seen it a handful of times in fifty years and—"

"There's nothing for the eye anymore, Dad." Delia said it gently, but it landed like a slap, because it was true, and because she'd said the thing he'd been refusing to say to himself the whole morning. "What's she going to do with it? There won't be a boat in this harbor in ten years. There won't be a town."

He stood there on the swaying float, an old man with cold hands, and he had no answer, because she was right. He'd given the girl a gift that the world had already decided it didn't want. He'd taught her to read a language that was going extinct. He'd handed her a map to a country that was sinking.

But Nora was looking up at her mother with her face still open, still lit, and she said: "It's not about a job, Mom." She said it with the certainty of a child who hasn't yet learned that being right doesn't always matter. "It's about how to see. Granddad says you read the water you're coming to, not the water you're in. He says you commit and you go and you don't look behind you. He wasn't just talking about the boat."

Delia went very still. She looked at her daughter, and then she looked at Eli, and something moved across her face that he hadn't seen since she was young, since before the divorce and the cannery and the long erosion of her life—something that might have been the lifting of a weight she'd carried so long she'd forgotten it was there.

"He wasn't just talking about the boat," she repeated.

"No," Eli said. "I wasn't."

Delia knelt down on the cold wet float, heedless of her one good pair of jeans, and she gathered the girl in and held her, and over Nora's shoulder she looked at her father with her eyes wet, and Eli understood that he had finally, after all these years, told his daughter something she could use. Not in words to her—he'd never been able to find the words for her—but through the girl, the gift passing down through the blood, skipping the generation that hadn't wanted it and landing where it could take root.

The smoke rose from the cannery chimney. It would rise until March and then it would stop. The harbor would empty. The town would do what towns did when the thing that made them went away—it would shrink and gray and finally forget itself. None of that had changed. He couldn't pilot them through it. There was no slot in this bar, no back of a swell to ride in on; this was the kind of weather where you sat outside in the dark and waited, and some boats didn't make it in at all.

But the girl had the eye. And whatever happened to the town, wherever the water took her, she would know how to read it. She would look ahead and not behind. She would know the calm could be the deep part and the deep part could be the dangerous part and the danger and the safety were the same thing, depending on what you were trying to do. She would commit and she would go.

"Come on," Delia said, standing, taking her daughter's hand and, after a moment's hesitation, her father's arm. "Both of you. You're frozen through. I'll make soup."

They went up the ramp together, the three of them, slow because of Eli's knees, into the cold bright town, and behind them the *Pilot* swung at her line on the falling tide, and the water in the harbor went on telling its long story to whoever still knew how to listen, which now, for a little while longer, was two.
