The taxi let her out at the wrong end of the drive, and so Marguerite walked the last hundred yards under the limes, her shoes already complaining. They were new shoes, bought for the wedding, the kind of shoes a woman buys when she means to be looked at and then spends the evening regretting. The drive had been gravel once. Now it was some pale resin aggregate that did not crunch, and she found she missed the crunch, that small announcement of arrival, the sound of a person being expected.

The hotel rose at the end of it the way a thing rises in a dream when you have been told it burned down. She had been told it burned down. She had been there when it burned down. And yet here it was, four storeys of honey-colored stone with white sash windows and a portico that had not existed before, a great pillared mouth where the old revolving door used to be. They had kept the name. The Lindenhof. They had kept the limes, or planted new ones, she couldn't tell which, and she stood under them with her invitation in her bag and her shoes already betraying her, and she thought: I should not have come.

But Hana was getting married, and Hana was the daughter of Pavel, and Pavel had pulled her out.

That was the arithmetic of it. You did not refuse the wedding of the daughter of the man who had carried you down a smoke-filled stairwell with your apron over your face. You did not say, I would rather not go back to the building. You bought shoes.

A young man in a waistcoat met her at the top of the steps. "Welcome to the Lindenhof," he said, in the bright unmarked English of the international hotel, and for a moment she wanted to tell him that she had said those exact words ten thousand times, in three languages, that she had stood where he stood and watched the faces come up the steps and learned to read in the first three seconds which ones would be kind and which ones would leave the room as they found it and which ones would not. But she only smiled and gave Hana's name and said she was a guest of the bride.

"Of course. The ceremony's in the orangery, the reception in the Mirror Room. There's time for a drink in the bar first, if you like."

The Mirror Room. So they had rebuilt that too, or named some new room after the old one. The Mirror Room was where it had started. She knew this the way she knew few things; it had been established at the inquiry, read aloud, an electrical fault in the service corridor behind the Mirror Room, and she had sat in the public gallery in a coat too warm for the season and listened to a man in glasses explain how she had nearly died.

"I'll find the bar," she said.

Inside, the lobby was wrong and right at once. The proportions were the same — they must have had the old plans — but everything that filled the proportions was new. The floor was the same pattern of black and white marble, but the marble was unworn, no soft trough by the desk where a century of feet had stood waiting to be given keys. The chandelier was a clever modern thing pretending to be the old one. She stood in the middle of the lobby and turned a slow circle, and a couple coming in behind her had to step around her, and the woman gave her a look, and Marguerite moved on.

The bar was where the writing room had been. She remembered the writing room: the smell of it, ink and beeswax and the particular dust of old paper, and the desks with their little brass lamps, and old Mr. Frenkel who had come every winter and written letters all day to people Tereza said were all dead. She had liked the writing room. She had liked dusting it, the only room in the hotel where the dusting felt like tending rather than erasing. Now it was a bar, very dark, with bottles lit from beneath like specimens, and a young woman polishing a glass who would not have known a writing desk if she fell over one.

Marguerite ordered a glass of white wine and took it to a low table by the window and sat with her back very straight, because the chair was the kind that wanted to swallow you, and watched the light go gold over the lawn where the orangery stood. The orangery was genuinely new; there had been a kitchen garden there in her day, runner beans on canes, and the head gardener Bohu who would not let the kitchen staff touch his tomatoes and then left baskets of them outside the staff door anyway, as if the tomatoes had walked there themselves.

She was forty-nine years old. She had been twenty-six when the hotel burned. The arithmetic again. Twenty-three years, and a marriage, and the marriage's quiet dissolution, and a daughter now at university in another country, and a job in an office that had nothing to do with hotels, and here she was with a glass of wine watching strangers in good clothes drift across a lawn toward an orangery, and she could not have said whether the thing she felt was grief or only the strange vertigo of finding a place you had memorized rebuilt slightly off-true, like coming home to find your mother had rearranged the furniture while you slept.

"Marguerite?"

She looked up. A woman, perhaps a few years older than herself, in a dove-gray dress, with a face that took a moment to resolve and then resolved all at once, the way a face does.

"Tereza," Marguerite said.

"I wasn't sure," Tereza said. "I saw you come in and I thought, no, it can't be, and then I thought, who else sits like that, like the manager's about to walk past."

And Marguerite laughed, surprised into it, because it was true, she had never lost the chambermaid's posture, the readiness, the way of occupying a chair as though she might at any moment have to give it up to a paying guest.

Tereza sat down without being asked, which was also true to her. They had shared a room on the top floor, the staff floor, the floor that was gone now, replaced by what the young man would no doubt call suites. They had not seen each other in — Marguerite did the arithmetic and then stopped doing it.

"You came for Hana," Tereza said.

"Pavel asked me. How could I not."

"Pavel." Tereza shook her head. "Pavel cried at the rehearsal. You should have seen him. Great big man, blubbering. His little girl." She had a glass of something amber and she drank from it. "He's done well, you know. He owns three restaurants. Did you know that? After everything, he ended up the success of all of us."

"I didn't know."

"And you?"

"I work in an office," Marguerite said. "Insurance claims, if you can believe it. I assess them. I decide what people are owed."

Tereza laughed, a real laugh, a bark of it. "You do not."

"I do."

"After all this." Tereza gestured at the room, meaning the room and not the room, meaning the building and the fire and everything. "You spend your days deciding what people are owed for things that burned."

"It isn't usually fires," Marguerite said. "It's mostly water damage. Burst pipes. Roofs. People are very careless about their roofs."

But she was thinking, as she said it, that Tereza had named something she had never let herself name, and that perhaps this was why she had taken the job when it was offered, all those years ago, the dull good job with the pension; perhaps she had spent two decades sitting in judgment over other people's losses because no one had ever sat in judgment over hers. The inquiry had judged the building. The building had been found to have failed. No one had been able to find a person to blame, which had felt, at the time, like a second injury — that the thing had simply happened, that the smoke had come up the stairwell out of a fault in a wall, indifferent, and that there was no face to put to it.

"Do you want to see it?" Tereza said.

"See what?"

"The room. They've a little — they made a thing of it. A memorial, sort of. In the corridor by the orangery. I'll show you."

Marguerite did not want to see it. She wanted to finish her wine and watch the light and then sit in the orangery and watch Hana marry whoever Hana was marrying — a man named Tomas, she gathered, something to do with software — and then she wanted to eat and drink and go to bed in a room that had no history because she had never set foot in it, and leave in the morning. She had planned the whole thing as a kind of careful surgery, a way of repaying Pavel without opening anything.

"All right," she said, and stood up, because Tereza was the kind of person you followed.

They went down a corridor she half-knew. The geography was the same even where the surfaces lied. Here was the turn, here was the long run toward the back of the building where the service rooms had been, except now it was carpeted in something soft and patterned and there were framed photographs on the walls. She slowed. The photographs were of the old hotel. Someone had gone to an archive, or to the families, and gathered them — black and white and sepia and the bad color of the seventies — and here was the lobby with its worn marble trough, and here was the dining room with its long table and the chandelier, the real one, and here, her heart did something complicated, here was the writing room, with Mr. Frenkel in it, or a man who might have been Mr. Frenkel, bent over a desk by a brass lamp.

"They're good, aren't they," Tereza said. "I gave them some. I'd kept some. From the staff parties."

"You're in some of these?"

"Look." Tereza pointed. Further down, a photograph of the staff, two dozen of them on the back steps in summer in their off-duty clothes, squinting into a sun that had set forty years before any of these wedding guests were born. "There's me. And there." Her finger moved. "There you are."

And there she was. Twenty-six, or twenty-five, in a yellow dress she had completely forgotten owning, with her hair longer than she had worn it in twenty years, laughing at something out of frame, her hand up against the sun. She did not remember the photograph being taken. She did not remember the day. But she remembered the dress now, remembered buying it at the market with her first month's wages, remembered Bohu the gardener saying she looked like a daffodil and Tereza telling him to mind his business, and she stood in the soft-carpeted corridor of the rebuilt hotel and looked at a girl who did not yet know that the building she stood in front of would try to kill her, and she found she could not speak.

"Come on," Tereza said gently. "It's just here."

The Mirror Room had been rebuilt as a ballroom, and they had not put the memorial in it, which Marguerite understood — you could not seat a wedding dinner in a room with a plaque about the dead. They had put it in the corridor outside, a modest bronze panel set into the wall at eye level, with names on it. Six names. She knew all six. She had known them before they were names on bronze, when they were people who left their cups in the sink and borrowed her cardigan and complained about their feet.

Anna Krejčová. Marie Dvořáková. The Bauer brothers, who had worked in the kitchen and shared a room and a temper. Old Mr. Frenkel, who had not gone down, who had been found by the firemen still at his desk, though they had moved the writing room by then to a smaller room at the back and he had been writing his letters there. And Sister Beatrix, who had not even been staff — a guest, an elderly nun traveling alone, who had been on the third floor and whom no one had reached.

"I didn't know about her," Marguerite said. "Sister Beatrix. I didn't — I knew the others. I didn't know there was a guest."

"You were already out," Tereza said. "By the time anyone knew about the third floor, you were on the lawn. They told you that. At the inquiry. You couldn't have done anything."

"I know."

"You were nearly one of these yourself."

"I know, Tereza."

They stood in front of the bronze. Behind them, somewhere, a string quartet had begun to tune. The guests were gathering in the orangery. There would be a wedding in twenty minutes. Marguerite read the six names and the date, which was her date too, the date she carried, the night she had woken in the dark with the smell already in the room and Tereza shaking her, and the two of them had gone out into the corridor where the smoke was coming up the back stairs like something alive, like a tide, and they had gone the wrong way first, toward the back stairs, and the heat had turned them, and they had gone instead along the top corridor toward the front, banging on doors, shouting, and somewhere in the banging she had become separated from Tereza and had not known it, had gone on banging and shouting alone in a corridor she could no longer see, and had at some point sat down. That was the part she did not tell people. She had sat down. The smoke had taken the choice out of her, gently, the way it apparently does, and she had sat down on the carpet outside room nine with her back against the wall, and she had thought, very calmly, that she would just rest a moment, and that was where Pavel had found her, Pavel who had no business being on that floor, who had come up the front stairs against everyone's sense, and had hauled her up by the apron and put it over her face and got her down.

"He shouldn't have come up," she said. "Pavel. He could have died. For me."

"And he didn't," Tereza said. "And you didn't. And his daughter is getting married today in the orangery, and you're going to come and watch, and afterward you're going to dance with him, because I happen to know he's hoping you'll dance with him, and it's the least you can do for a man who carried you down two flights of stairs."

"He's married, isn't he? Pavel."

"Widowed. Two years. Don't get ideas. I only mean a dance."

But Marguerite was not getting ideas. She was looking at Mr. Frenkel's name and thinking about the brass lamps and the smell of the writing room and the way she had liked to tend it, the only room she dusted as if it deserved tending, and she was thinking that they had moved the writing room to the back, to a worse room, the year before the fire, because the bar — the original bar — had wanted to expand into the good room, and that if they had not moved it Mr. Frenkel would have been at the front of the building, and might have got down. The arithmetic of disaster. She had done it before. There was no end to it once you started. If the wall had not been faulty. If Anna had not gone back for whatever she went back for. If the night porter had checked the panel. If, if, if. She had spent years learning not to do this and it took her four seconds, in front of the bronze, to be doing it again, fluently, as though she had never stopped.

"I want to sit down," she said. "Before the ceremony. I want to sit down for a minute."

There was a window seat at the end of the corridor, and she sat in it, and Tereza sat beside her, and through the glass they could see the orangery filling, the white chairs, the guests in their colors, a man she took to be Tomas standing very stiff in a dark suit with another young man beside him, the two of them laughing at something to cover their nerves.

"Do you ever dream about it?" Marguerite asked.

"Used to. Not for years now." Tereza considered. "I dream about the hotel. But not the fire. I dream I'm working, that I've a whole floor to do and not enough time, and I can't find the linen cupboard, and the bell keeps going. The ordinary nightmare. The work nightmare. Not the fire."

"I dream I'm sitting down," Marguerite said. "In the corridor. Outside room nine. And in the dream I know I have to get up, and I know exactly how to do it, I just have to put my hand flat on the floor and push, and I can't. I can't make myself want to. That's the part. In the dream I don't want to get up. I'm so comfortable. And I know that's the smoke, I know it's the smoke making me feel that, but knowing it doesn't help, and I sit there, and I wait." She had never told anyone this. Not her husband. Not the counselor she had seen for a year afterward, who had been kind and useless. "And then I wake up. I never get to the part where Pavel comes. The dream doesn't have Pavel in it. I just wake up still sitting there. So I never — I'm never saved. In the dream. It just stops."

Tereza was quiet. Through the glass, the quartet had settled into something soft, the prelude before the prelude, and the guests were finding their seats.

"You came," Tereza said finally. "That's the thing. You came back to the building. People talk about it like it's nothing, coming back to a place, but it isn't nothing. I've seen people who can't drive past a road where something happened to them. You walked in the front door in new shoes."

"How do you know they're new?"

"You keep flexing your foot. You've a blister starting on the left."

Marguerite laughed, and it loosened something, the laugh, it broke the surface tension of the afternoon, and she found her eyes were wet, and she let them be, because Tereza was the one person in the world she did not have to manage her face for.

"I thought I'd feel nothing," she said. "Or I thought I'd feel everything and have to leave. I planned it like a — like a procedure. Come, congratulate Hana, eat the dinner, dance the one dance with Pavel, sleep in a room with no history, leave. I didn't plan for this." She gestured vaguely — the corridor, the bronze, the photographs, the girl in the yellow dress laughing at the sun.

"For what?"

"For it to be — tender," Marguerite said, and the word surprised her, but it was the right one. "I thought it would be a wound. And it is a wound. But it's a — they kept the limes. They kept the name. There's a girl in a yellow dress on the wall and she doesn't know anything yet and I want to — I want to protect her, but I also know she gets out. I'm the proof that she gets out. I'm sitting here. She's me, and she gets out, and I never let myself think of it that way. I only ever thought about the part where I sat down. I never thought about the part where I'm still here, twenty-three years later, in stupid shoes, at a wedding."

Tereza put a hand over hers. Out in the orangery a door must have opened, because the guests rose all at once, a soft mass standing, and turned, and the quartet lifted into the processional, and somewhere out of sight Hana was beginning to walk, and somewhere near her Pavel would be, weeping probably, the great big man, walking his daughter into the glass room full of light at the back of the building that had once tried to take them all and had been built again anyway, board by board and stone by stone, by people who had decided, against all the arithmetic, that the place was worth having in the world.

"We should go in," Marguerite said. "I don't want to miss it."

"There's time. They always take longer than they think. The bride always stops at the door."

But Marguerite was already standing, smoothing the wedding dress she had bought to be looked at, ready to give up the window seat to whoever might need it, ready in the old way, the chambermaid's way, the readiness she had never lost and saw now she might keep, that it was not only fear, that it could also be this: a person prepared, at any moment, to rise and go toward the light at the back of the house where the living were gathering, and to take her seat among them, and to watch a man she owed her life to give his daughter away in a room that should not have existed, and to be glad, simply and without arithmetic, that she was there to see it.

She put her hand flat against the wall, and pushed, and stood, and went in.
