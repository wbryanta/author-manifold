That was the last excavation Anil worked in Guatemala before her contract ended and she was sent elsewhere. She remembered it now, on the plane, because the memory was the kind that returned without permission, the way certain smells returned, or the particular quality of light in a room where something had happened. She was flying east, over an ocean she could not see in the dark, and below her the water was a thing she only knew was there.

She had been gone fifteen years. She had left at eighteen to study in London and then, on a scholarship she had never quite believed she deserved, she had gone to America, to Oklahoma and then Arizona, where the desert taught her something about bone, about how the earth could preserve and reveal at the same time. She had married and unmarried. She had taken on the work of the dead in Guatemala and the Congo and, briefly, in a country she was not permitted to name, where the bodies came up out of the ground in numbers that made the word *site* seem absurd, a euphemism, a way of not saying *field* or *pit* or simply *here*.

Now she was returning to the island where she had been born, and she carried with her the credentials of an outsider. She was arriving as a forensic specialist on a human rights commission, sent under the auspices of a Geneva organization, to work for seven weeks alongside a local archaeologist appointed by the government. The arrangement had the careful symmetry of a thing designed not to function. The government wanted to be seen to cooperate. The organization wanted to be seen to investigate. Anil wanted, she told herself, only to do the work.

She had not been back since she was a girl. The name she had been given at birth she had traded years ago, in a transaction she still considered the cleverest of her life. *Anil* had been her brother's unused second name, and she had wanted it from the time she was twelve, had bartered for it and bribed for it and finally, when her brother grew bored of the negotiation, won it outright for a hundred rupees and a pen set and the promise to do his anatomy homework for a month. By the time she left the island she was Anil to everyone, and she had carried the name into all her later lives the way one carries a single, deliberate object out of a fire.

The plane began its descent through cloud. She had drunk two small bottles of liquor and slept badly and now her mouth was dry and she could feel the island coming up to meet her, could feel it in the heat that seemed to seep through the fuselage even before the doors opened, a wet heat that was nothing like the dry desert heat she had grown accustomed to, a heat that was full of water and growth and the memory of rain.

At the airport the officials were courteous and slow. A young man named Mr. Gunesena, sent by the Ministry, held a sign with her name spelled almost correctly. He took her bag, would not let her carry it, and drove her into the city through the grey light of a morning that could not decide whether to rain. She looked out the window at the things she had forgotten she knew. The way the bullock carts moved at the edge of the road as if the road belonged to them. The thin dogs. The colors of the buses, which were the colors of the buses she had been carried on as a child. The smell, when they slowed, of diesel and frangipani and something burning that was not anger and not catastrophe but only the ordinary burning of leaves and refuse that went on everywhere, all the time.

"You have been away long, madam?" Mr. Gunesena asked.

"Fifteen years."

"Ah." He nodded as if this confirmed something he had suspected. "It is changed."

"Is it?"

He considered. "No," he said finally, and laughed a little, and she understood that he was a man who would tell her almost nothing, and she liked him for it.

---

She met Sarath Diyasena on the second day, in an office at the Archaeological Department, a colonial building with high ceilings and slow fans and the smell of old paper that had been damp and dried so many times it had become a substance unto itself. He was perhaps forty-nine, careful, with the stillness of a man who had learned that stillness was the safest condition in which to exist. He was an archaeologist by training and inclination, a man whose true love was the sixth century, who had spent years among the rock fortresses and the painted caves and the buried monasteries of the island's past, and who had been assigned to her, she understood at once, not because he believed in her mission but because he could be trusted by the people who did not believe in it either.

"You have read the brief," he said. It was not a question.

"I've read the brief."

"Then you know we are to examine recent remains, recovered from sites that are designated as government archaeological reserves. Which means," he said, and here he paused, choosing the words, "that anything we find on government land will be the responsibility of the government. And anything found on government land will be presumed to be of archaeological interest until proven otherwise."

"Until proven recent."

"Until proven recent," he agreed.

She studied him. There was nothing hostile in his manner, but there was nothing open in it either. He had the watchfulness of a man who had survived years in which watchfulness was the difference between living and not.

"You think this is a charade," she said.

He smiled slightly, the first crack in the surface. "I think," he said, "that we should go to lunch."

They ate at a place near the museum, rice and curry served on a banana leaf, and he was easier there, away from the office, away from the files. He told her about the sites they would visit, about the geology of the central provinces, about the difference between graves of the twelfth century and graves of last year, which lay, sometimes, in the same hillside, divided only by depth and by the particular cruelties of soil chemistry. He spoke about bone with the same affection she felt, and she realized that whatever else he was, he was a man who loved the dead, who treated them with a care that did not depend on when they had died.

"There are four sites," he said. "Three of them are uninteresting. Old graves, monastic burials, the bones of monks who died in the natural way of dying. The fourth—" He stopped.

"The fourth."

"The fourth is in a government zone. A protected archaeological preserve, a cave system, near Bandarawela. Bodies have been recovered there. They are, officially, ancient. Six hundred years old. Buried, perhaps, during the period of the Sinhala kings."

"And unofficially?"

He folded the banana leaf neatly, the way one folds a thing one is done with. "Unofficially," he said, "I do not know. That is why you are here. To tell us what is official and what is not."

She felt then the precise shape of what they had given her. Among four pits, one was a question. And the question had been placed where no civilian could reach it, where only a government archaeologist and a government-sanctioned investigator could go, in a zone sealed and guarded under the laws that protected the nation's heritage. They had hidden a recent body, if it was recent, inside the protection of the ancient. It was, she thought, a kind of genius. You could not dig in a place like that without permission, and the permission was the trap.

---

They drove out to Bandarawela three days later, up into the hill country, through the tea estates that lay across the slopes like green corduroy, through the cool mist that gathered in the valleys and burned off by midmorning. The road wound and climbed. She watched the temperature drop on the small dial Mr. Gunesena had on the dashboard. They passed waterfalls and the small towns clustered along the road, and once, where the road was being repaired, they sat for half an hour while men with hand tools broke stones into smaller stones, the oldest labor in the world, and she thought of the miner's song her mother used to half-sing, the one about going down into the earth at Bogala, seventy-two fathoms deep, and how a man's life was only safe when he came back up. *Invisible as a fly,* the song went, *not seen from the pit head.* That was what they were doing, she and Sarath. Going down into the earth to make the invisible seen.

The cave preserve sat above a village whose name she did not record. There was a checkpoint, soldiers in green who were very young, who looked at her papers and at Sarath's papers and made telephone calls and finally waved them through. A government rest house had been arranged. Sarath had brought equipment, and there was an old laboratory of sorts in a disused wing of a building that had once, he told her, been a college, before the troubles closed it.

The cave was real. That was the first thing she understood, climbing up to it through a path slick with leaf mould. It was an actual archaeological site, the floor of it scattered with the genuine evidence of antiquity—pottery sherds, the dark smoke stains of ancient fires, the layered sediment that took centuries to lay down. There were burials here that were certainly old. She knelt at the first of them and felt the truth of it in the bone, in the way the marrow cavities had been emptied by time, in the mineralization, the way the bone had begun the slow journey toward becoming stone. This was a body that had lain here for six centuries or more. It told her nothing except that it was honest.

There were four skeletons in the cave that had been catalogued by an earlier survey. Three of them were old. She knew it within an hour of examining them, knew it the way she knew her own hands. But the fourth.

The fourth had been found in the deepest chamber, in a niche that had been sealed and then, at some point, opened and resealed. It had been catalogued with the others, given a number, assigned the same date, the same provenance. *Sixth century. Monastic burial.* But when she lifted the long bones to the light of her lamp, when she ran her thumb along the surface and felt the texture of it, when she looked at the epiphyseal lines and the absence of the deep mineral change she had felt in the others, she knew.

"Sarath," she said.

He came and crouched beside her. She did not say anything yet. She handed him the femur and watched his face. He turned it in his hands. He was an archaeologist; he knew bone too, knew it in his own way.

"This is not old," she said.

He said nothing.

"This bone has been in the ground perhaps four years. Five. Not six hundred. Look at the surface. Look at the colour. There is still organic material. It hasn't begun to mineralize. Someone put this body here, in a protected zone, among old bodies, and dated it old, and filed it away where no one could ever come and dig."

Sarath set the femur down with a gentleness that she would remember. He looked at it for a long moment.

"You are certain," he said.

"I'll run the tests. But yes. I'm certain now, and I'll be more certain later."

He stood up and walked to the mouth of the cave and looked out over the valley, where the mist was rising, and she watched the line of his back and could not read it. She did not know yet whose man he was. She did not know whether she had just shown a treasure to a thief or a friend.

"We should take it to the laboratory," he said at last, without turning. "All four. Properly documented. Official chain of custody. Everything correct." He turned then, and his face had decided something. "If we are going to do this, we will do it so carefully that no one can say it was done improperly. That is the only protection there is. Do you understand? The work must be perfect. The work is the only weapon we have."

She understood that he had just made a decision that might cost him a great deal, and that he had made it in the language of procedure because the language of procedure was the only language in which such a decision could be made and survived.

"We'll call him Sailor," she said.

He looked at her.

"The unidentified ones. In the field, we give them names. So they aren't only numbers. So we remember they were someone."

"Sailor," Sarath repeated, as if testing it.

"After the song. The miner's song. Going down into the earth. He went down into the earth and someone has to bring him up so his life is safe."

Sarath did not answer that, but he did not object to the name, and from then on the skeleton was Sailor, and Sailor was a person to them in a way the case file would never allow him to be.

---

They worked through the following week in the makeshift laboratory, the old college wing with its smell of mildew and its windows looking out onto an overgrown courtyard where a flame tree had grown wild and dropped its red blossoms onto the cracked stone. They set up the four skeletons on long tables and worked over them in the cool of the hill-country mornings and the warmth of the afternoons, and slowly the truth of each of them came forward.

Three were old men. Monks, almost certainly, given the burial position and the location and the absence of any trauma. They had died in old age, of the ordinary failures of the body, and they had been laid in the cave by people who loved or honoured them, and they had become, over centuries, almost geological. They had nothing to tell the present.

But Sailor had a great deal to tell, if they could learn to listen.

She began with the things she always began with. Sex: male. Age: she put him between twenty-eight and thirty-five, working from the cranial sutures and the pubic symphysis and the wear on the teeth. Height: she calculated it from the long bones, the regression formulas she carried in her head, and arrived at a figure she wrote in the margin. He had been a small man, by Western standards. A working man.

It was the working she found first, in the bones themselves. There were marks of labour on the skeleton, the kind of marks that took years to make. The joints showed wear consistent with squatting, with carrying, with the repetition of some particular set of motions over a long time. She studied the hands. There was a peculiar pattern there, a development of certain muscle attachments, that she recognized but could not immediately place, and she set it aside in the part of her mind that worked on its own.

Then there was the matter of how Sailor had died, and the matter of what had been done to him.

There was evidence of burning. Not the burning of cremation, which would have been complete, but a partial, careless burning, as if someone had tried to destroy the body and then given up or been interrupted. The bones in places had the characteristic colour and cracking. There was trauma to the skull—she found it on the second day, a wound that had not healed, that could not have healed because it had been part of the dying. And there was something else, something that turned the case from a discovery into an accusation.

The body had been moved.

She found it in the soil that still clung to the bones, in the particular minerals it contained, which did not match the soil of the cave. Sailor had been buried somewhere else first, in a different ground, and then dug up and carried and reburied in the protected zone, among the old monks, to hide him inside the law's protection of the ancient. He had been killed in one place and concealed in another, and the concealment itself was the confession. No one transports a six-hundred-year-old monk. You only move a body that someone wants not to be found.

"This changes things," Sarath said, when she showed him the soil analysis. His voice was very quiet.

"It proves intent. It proves the state was involved, or someone who could move a body into a sealed government zone was involved. A civilian couldn't get past the checkpoint. We couldn't get past the checkpoint without papers."

"Yes," he said. "It changes things." He was looking at the skeleton on the table, at Sailor, and there was something in his face she had not seen before, a kind of weariness that went down a long way, seventy-two fathoms, perhaps, into the dark.

"Sarath. If we can identify him—if we can give him a name, a real name—then he becomes evidence that one person, at least, was killed and hidden by the state. One name. One face. They can disappear thousands when the thousands are anonymous. But one name they have to answer for."

"And how," Sarath said, "do you propose to give a name to a skeleton, in a country where there are no records, where the people who would have reported him missing are themselves afraid, where to ask after the dead is to invite your own death?"

She did not have the answer yet. But she had the hands.

---

That night she could not sleep. She lay in the rest house and listened to the rain begin, the heavy hill-country rain that came down in a roar and then softened and then came again, and she thought about the marks on Sailor's hands, the development of muscle and bone that spoke of a single repeated labour, and somewhere in the rain it came to her, the thing her mind had been working on by itself.

A painter. Not a painter of pictures—a painter of walls, a plasterer, perhaps. Or a miner. Or—she sat up in the dark. There was a trade in the hill country, an old trade, that left exactly such marks. The men who worked the rock. The men who carved, who chipped, who held a tool against stone all day, every day, for years. Quarrymen. Or the men who worked in the gem pits, or the men who—

Or the men who, in another life, in the sixth century that Sarath loved, would have been the painters and carvers of the cave temples themselves. The artisans. There were villages, even now, even today, where the old trades survived, where men still carved stone and painted the eyes onto statues in the ancient way, where the knowledge passed from father to son and left its signature in the very bones of the men who carried it.

If she could find the trade, she could find the village. And if she could find the village, perhaps she could find the man.

She thought of the woman in Guatemala, the one who had sat in the grave among the bones of her husband and her brother, who had been the feminine string between them, who had risen and stepped back to give the scientists room. *There are no words I know that can describe the woman's face.* Somewhere on this island, she thought, there was such a face, waiting, attached to the name of the man who lay on the table in the old college laboratory. Somewhere a woman, or a mother, or a child, kept a vigil that had no body at its centre, that had only an absence, an empty mat in the afternoon, an unanswered question that had hardened over the years into a grief without a shape.

She would give them the shape. That was the work. That was the only thing she had carried home that was worth carrying. Not the credentials, not the contract, not the careful neutrality of Geneva. Only this: the conviction that the dead were owed the truth of their dying, and that a single name, recovered and spoken aloud, was a kind of resurrection. *Blessed be the chain attached to the life wheel.* You went down into the dark and you found the man and you brought him back to the surface so that his life, even after death, even in death, could be safe—could be known, could be counted, could be mourned by its own name.

In the morning she would tell Sarath. She would tell him about the hands and the trade and the villages, and she would watch his face to see whether he was her chain or her cut rope, because she still did not know, and the not-knowing was its own kind of descent. She would have to trust him to go any deeper, and trust, in this country, in this time, was the most dangerous tool in the kit.

The rain came down again, harder, and she lay back and listened to it fill the courtyard and run off the broken stones and find its way down into the ground, where it would seep through the hillside, through the tea roots and the leaf mould and the layered centuries, down to where the old monks lay in their honest sleep and where Sailor had lain, dishonestly, in his borrowed antiquity, until two people had come up the slick path with lamps and trowels and lifted him into the light. She thought of him as a man now, fully, the way she had taught herself to think of all of them, not as evidence but as someone who had once stood at the surface in the morning before going down into whatever pit had claimed him. Someone who had eaten lunch a wife had made. Someone who had slept an hour in the afternoon heat. Someone whose shoulder, in some last embrace, had held a grief of love that no words she knew could describe.

She would find him. She had come fifteen thousand miles and fifteen years to find one man among the disappeared of an island that did not want him found, and she understood, lying there in the roar of the rain, that she had been moving toward this all her life, through all the deserts and all the other graves, that the work she did everywhere had only ever been a rehearsal for the work she would do here, at home, in the one place she had spent her whole life leaving.

Tomorrow they would begin to give Sailor back his name.
