The ferry left the harbour at eleven and he stood at the rail until the lights of the town were no more than a smear behind them, a wound of orange against the dark hills. He had not slept. He did not intend to sleep. Below him the water broke white against the hull and then closed over itself, healing, indifferent, and he watched it the way a man watches something he has decided to trust.

His name was Aaron and he had not seen his brother in nineteen years.

The letter had come on a Tuesday, official, with the seal of the coroner's office in a town he had to find on a map. A body had been recovered from the rocks below the lighthouse at Skarholm. There were effects. There was a name sewn into the collar of a coat, half-eaten by salt, and the name was their name, the family name, and below it in fainter thread the letter said there might be the first letter of his brother's given name though the coroner would not commit himself to this in writing. Effects. He had said the word aloud in his kitchen, to the cold coffee and the window full of rain, and the word had seemed to him then like the only honest thing in the whole document, the way it gathered up a life and made of it a small heap of objects that could be photographed and filed.

He did not know if it was Daniel. That was the thing he carried onto the ferry with him, heavier than the bag on his shoulder. He did not know, and a part of him, the worst part, the part he could not look at directly, hoped.

In the cabin a man was already asleep in the upper bunk, one arm hanging down into the dark like something forgotten, and Aaron sat on the lower bunk without turning on the light and listened to the engines come up through the steel and into his spine. He had been given the cabin by a woman at the ticket counter who had looked at him too long, who had seen something, the way they always did, the bearers of small official kindnesses. There is a category of grief that announces itself before its cause is confirmed, and she had recognized it, and she had not charged him for the cabin, and he had not been able to thank her properly because his throat had closed.

Nineteen years.

He tried to remember Daniel's face and could not assemble it whole. He had the parts. The wide forehead, the eyebrow with the scar through it from the summer they had built the raft and it had come apart under them in the middle of the lake, the two of them treading water and laughing while their mother screamed from the dock, the laughter and the screaming both reaching them across the flat water at the same time so that for years afterward he could not separate the two sounds. He had the hands. Daniel had their father's hands, broad and patient, hands that could fix anything, that had once held a sparrow that flew into the window and held it gently and completely until it remembered how to be alive and left them. He had all the parts but he could not make the face. It came to him only sideways, in profile, turning away, which was how he had last seen it.

They had quarrelled over nothing. That was the thing he could not forgive himself, not the quarrel but the nothing at its centre, the way it had hollowed out and become about everything, about the house and the mother and the years of who had stayed and who had gone, until there was no original grievance left at all, only the structure of grievance, standing on its own like a house after a fire when the walls have burned but the chimney remains, pointing at the sky.

He went out onto the deck again. The wind had risen. He was alone there now and the strait was wide and black and he could not see the other shore or the shore behind, only the running lights of the ferry making their small claim on the dark, red and green, and below them the churning. A northern strait in October. The cold came through his coat and he welcomed it because it was something to feel that was not the other thing.

He thought of the body on the rocks below the lighthouse. He had been told it had been in the water some days. He understood, without being told, what this meant, what it did to a face, and he understood that he was being asked to identify something that no longer had a face to identify, that he would be asked to look instead at the shape of a jaw, at the set of bone, at a scar perhaps, at the hands, and to say from these whether this was his brother. As though a man were the sum of his scars. As though you could read a life in what had marked it. And perhaps you could. Perhaps that was exactly what you could read and nothing else.

A woman came out onto the deck. She was older, in a heavy coat, and she lit a cigarette with difficulty in the wind, cupping it, turning her whole body, and when it caught she leaned against the rail a little distance from him and smoked and looked out at the same black water. They did not speak. There is a companionship that requires no speech, that is in fact ruined by speech, and they had it for a while, the two of them at the rail, and he was grateful to her. After a time she finished the cigarette and dropped it and it went out before it reached the water, and she said, without looking at him, You are going across for someone.

It was not a question. He said, My brother. Maybe my brother.

She nodded as though this were the most ordinary cargo a ferry could carry. We all go across for someone, she said. That is what the boat is for. And she went inside and left him alone again and he never saw her again, not on the boat and not after, and for years he would wonder if she had been real or whether grief had simply furnished him with the company it needed, the way the body manufactures heat.

He thought of the last summer. He had been twenty-three and Daniel twenty-six and their mother had been dying slowly in the front room of the house and the dying had taken so long that it had begun to seem like a kind of permanence, a third thing living in the house with them, demanding to be fed. Aaron had stayed. He had stayed and washed her and lifted her and learned the chemistry of her medications and Daniel had come and gone, Daniel who had work in the city, Daniel who arrived with flowers and good cheer and left after two days having charmed her back into something like life, so that she would say to Aaron after he had gone, Isn't he wonderful, your brother, and Aaron would agree, would say yes, he is wonderful, while he carried out the basin.

It was unfair. He had known even then that it was unfair, that the staying was a kind of choice he had made for his own reasons, that the resentment he built up like sediment in those months was a poison he was brewing for himself. But knowing a thing is unfair does not dissolve it. It only adds the shame of knowing.

When she died Daniel arrived too late, by an afternoon, and stood in the doorway of the room and Aaron, who had sat with her through the last night, who had held the hand that had become a bird's hand, had looked up at his brother in the doorway and had said the thing. He did not even remember the words now. He had said the thing that could not be taken back, and it was not even the true thing, it was a smaller crueller thing wearing the costume of the true thing, and Daniel had not argued. That was the worst of it. Daniel had not argued. He had only looked at his brother for a long moment with their father's patient eyes and then he had gone out to the car and driven away, and the flowers he had brought were still in their paper on the kitchen table, and Aaron had thrown them out and then taken them out of the bin again an hour later and put them in water, and they had bloomed for a week in the empty house, and he had hated them.

He went below. He could not stay on the deck and not jump, not because he wished to die but because the water seemed to want him to test it, to lean a little further, and he was tired and the tired part of a man will agree to things the rest of him would not. In the corridor the lights were dim and the carpet absorbed his footsteps and the boat moved under him with a long slow roll that was almost tender, like being carried. He passed a row of seats where people slept, slumped against the windows, against each other, strangers leaning their heads on the shoulders of strangers in the unconsciousness of travel, and he envied them their oblivion.

In the cabin the man in the upper bunk had stopped snoring. Aaron lay down in his coat and shoes and looked at the underside of the bunk above him, the stained mattress, the metal frame, and the ferry rolled and the engines droned and somewhere far below the water moved past at the speed of their crossing and he thought, I will know in the morning. By morning I will know. And the not knowing, which had been agony in the town, became now in the dark and the rolling something he wished he could hold on to a little longer. Because in the not knowing Daniel was still alive somewhere, in some city, with his charm and his good hands, and there was still time, still the possibility of the letter he might have written, the phone call, the version of the future in which they were old men together and laughed about the raft. The not knowing was the last room in which his brother could still be living.

He must have slept. He had said he would not but the body has its own intentions and he slept and dreamed of the lake, of the raft coming apart, of the planks separating one from another and floating off in different directions on the flat black water, and of treading water and looking around for Daniel and not finding him, only the planks drifting, each one a thing they had built together coming loose, and the screaming from the dock and the laughter coming to him across the water at the same time so that he could not tell, in the dream, which of them was making which sound, or whether the sounds came from the same throat.

He woke when the engines changed.

There is a moment on a ferry near the end of the crossing when the engines drop their pitch and the whole boat seems to gather itself, to remember its purpose, and the change of sound wakes everyone who is sleeping, as though it were a bell rung in a house. Aaron sat up. The porthole was grey. Not light yet but the promise of it, the dark thinning at its edges, and he stood and went out and up onto the deck and there it was, the other shore, low and dark, a line of land with two or three lights on it and above the lights, on a black headland, the slow regular sweep of the lighthouse, turning, throwing its long arm of light out over the water and pulling it back, out and back, the way a man counts something he is afraid to finish counting.

That is the lighthouse, he thought. That is where they found him. Below that turning light, on the rocks at its foot.

The shore came toward them slowly. He could see the harbour now, the breakwater, a few boats, a town smaller even than his own, a place at the end of things. The light grew and the colour came into the land by degrees, brown and grey and a dark green of pines coming down almost to the water, and gulls appeared from nowhere and hung in the air beside the ferry, riding the wind off it, turning their heads to look at him with their flat indifferent eyes. He stood at the rail and watched the town assemble itself out of the dark and he thought that he had come to the end of something whether or not it was the end he feared, that even if the body was not Daniel he would never again be the man who did not know, that the crossing had changed him as crossings do, and that on the far shore, this far shore now drawing close enough to throw a rope to, there waited a small heap of effects and a sheet to be drawn back and a single word he would have to say.

A man was waiting on the dock. Even from the water Aaron could see him, a heavy figure in a dark coat standing apart from the few cars waiting to come aboard for the return crossing, and he understood that this was the coroner or the coroner's man, that he had been described, that they were expecting the grief to walk off the boat and be recognizable, and he straightened a little, ashamed to be so easily read.

The ferry came in against the dock with the long groan of rope and rubber and the slow shudder of the engines reversing, and Aaron went down to the car deck though he had no car, walked down through the cold echoing space that smelled of diesel and the sea, and stood at the door waiting for it to drop, and when it dropped, when the ramp came down with a clang onto the concrete of the far shore, he saw the man more clearly, the grey face, the kind eyes, the way the man had taken his hands out of his pockets to receive him.

He walked down the ramp.

Mr. Aaron, the man said. It was his given name. They had only his given name, then, from somewhere, from the form he had filled in. He took the hand that was offered. It was cold and dry and the man held his a moment longer than was needful, and in that moment Aaron understood that the man knew, that the man had seen many of these mornings, that whatever waited under the sheet the man already knew how it would go, the looking and the words, and that there was in him a great fatigue and a great gentleness, the two of them grown together.

I'm sorry you've had such a journey, the man said. He had a soft way of speaking, a way of letting the ends of his sentences fall.

I slept, Aaron said, surprising himself. I didn't think I would. But I slept.

That's good, the man said. That's good. You'll have had no breakfast. We can stop. There's no hurry. He is not going anywhere now, this one. He said this last with no cruelty at all, only with the flat truthfulness of a man who has stopped pretending death is anything other than what it is, and Aaron found that he was grateful for it, that he could not have borne softness, that the bald fact was the only thing he could hold.

They walked up from the dock to where the man's car was parked. The town was waking around them, a light coming on in a window, a dog somewhere, the smell of woodsmoke laid down flat by the cold air, and the lighthouse still turning out on its headland though the day had come now and its light was lost in the daylight, the great lamp wheeling uselessly, throwing its beam out over a sea that no longer needed it, a habit the land could not break.

They drove through the town and out the other side and the man did not talk and Aaron was grateful for that too. He looked out the window at the houses, low and weathered, the salt in everything, the boats hauled up in the yards for the season, and he thought of Daniel choosing this. If it was Daniel. He thought of his brother, who had been so full of the city, of light and movement and the admiration of strangers, ending at the foot of a lighthouse at the end of the world, and he could not make the two men be the same man, and then he thought that perhaps that was the whole of a life, the distance a man travels from the version of himself that others can love to the version that walks out alone onto rocks above a dark sea, and that the distance was not a betrayal of the first self but only its long completion, and that he, Aaron, had no idea, no idea at all, what his brother's life had become in nineteen years, that he had kept in his mind a young man who no longer existed and would not have existed even if the body on the rocks were a stranger's, that the brother he had lost he had lost long before the water.

The building was low and ordinary, brick, with a sign he did not read. They went in through a back door, into a corridor, into the cold of it, a different cold from the cold outside, a manufactured cold with a smell under it, and the man stopped at a door and turned to him.

There's no rush, he said again. When you're ready. And I'll tell you, it isn't easy, after the water. You look at the shape of things. You'll know or you won't. Some people know straight away. Some people need to sit with it. There's no wrong way.

Aaron nodded. He found he could not speak. He thought of the raft, of the planks drifting apart, of the sparrow held in the patient hands until it remembered itself and flew. He thought of the flowers blooming in the empty house. He thought, This is the last room in which he is still alive, this corridor, these last few steps, and then I will know, and the not knowing will be over, and whatever I say in there I will have to live inside for the rest of my life.

He thought of the woman on the deck. We all go across for someone. That is what the boat is for.

I'm ready, he said, though he was not, though no one ever is, and the man opened the door.

The room was small and very bright and very cold. There was a table and on the table a shape under a green sheet and beside it a metal tray, and on the tray, laid out, the effects: a watch, stopped; a few coins gone dark; a knife with a bone handle; and the coat, the salt-eaten coat, the collar with its sewn thread, laid out flat as though it were the man himself, flattened, read, filed. Aaron went to the tray first because he could not yet go to the table, and he looked at the coat, at the name in its faded thread, and at the single letter below it that the coroner would not commit to in writing, and he could not make it out either, the salt had eaten it, it might have been a D or it might have been anything, the beginning of any name in the world.

Then he looked at the knife with the bone handle.

He had given it to Daniel. He had forgotten, completely, until this moment, in this cold bright room, with the sheeted shape beside him and the dead watch and the dark coins, he had forgotten that on Daniel's twenty-first birthday, two years before the quarrel, before the long dying and the cruel small word, he had given his brother a knife with a bone handle, and Daniel had turned it over in his good hands and run his thumb along the spine of it and said, This is a thing a man keeps, and had put it in his pocket, and Aaron, twenty-one years old himself and not yet ruined, had been so glad.

And now he stood and looked at the knife on the tray and did not need to lift the sheet at all, did not need the shape of the jaw or the scar through the eyebrow, did not need anything the water had left or taken, because the knife was the man, the knife was the whole of him, the giving and the keeping and the nineteen lost years, and he reached out and picked it up and held it in his hand the way you hold a thing that has come back to you, and the coroner's man behind him said nothing, only waited, and outside on the headland the lighthouse went on turning its useless light out over the sea, out and back, out and back, counting and counting what could not be finished.

It's him, Aaron said.

He did not turn around. He held the knife and looked at the sheet and said the word again, more quietly, to the brother under it, to the boy in the water, to the man on the rocks, to all the versions at once.

It's him. It's Daniel. I've come.
