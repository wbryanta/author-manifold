The ferry left at eleven, and Halvard had been told the crossing would take the better part of the night, depositing him on the far shore sometime after six, when the light would still be the grey of unwashed wool. He had bought a cabin, a small one, because the woman at the booking office in Tromsø had looked at him over her glasses and said, you'll want to lie down, even if you don't sleep, and something in the way she said it suggested she had sold this same crossing to others on similar errands and knew what the body required of itself when the mind would not cooperate.

He had not eaten since the morning. There had been a sandwich at the airport, dry, the bread curling at its edges, and he had taken three bites and wrapped the rest in its paper and left it on the table, and now in the lounge of the ferry he felt the absence of food as a kind of clarity, a thinning. The sea beyond the windows was not yet visible, only the lights of the harbour sliding backward, the cranes and the warehouses, and then the breakwater with its single green beacon, and then nothing, the glass giving him back his own face, older than he expected, the face of a man of fifty-three who had slept badly for a week.

The message had come from a police district he had never heard of, a name he could not pronounce on the first attempt, somewhere on the coast across the water where the land broke into islands and the islands into skerries and the skerries into the open sea. A man had been found. There were reasons to think the man might be Knut Halvardsen Berg. There was identification, but the identification was old and the photograph in it did not match well, the face having suffered from immersion, and the next of kin in the records was a brother, Halvard Berg, residing in Tromsø. Would he be willing to come.

He had read the message four times and then he had called the number and a woman had answered in a voice so gentle that he understood at once she had been trained in this, in the delivery of such things, and she had said the words again, more slowly, and he had heard himself say yes, of course, I'll come, as though there were no question of it, as though twenty-two years had not passed in which he had not spoken to his brother and had not, if he was honest, expected to.

Now on the ferry he carried the not-knowing like a stone in the coat pocket. He went down to the cabin and found it smaller than the woman's description had prepared him for, a bunk and a folding sink and a porthole the size of a dinner plate through which he could see the black water moving. He sat on the bunk. He did not undress. He took out his phone and looked at the photograph he had kept all these years without quite admitting he kept it, the two of them at the cabin at Senja, Knut sixteen and himself thirteen, both of them squinting into a sun that must have been low and hard, their father's shadow falling across the foreground because their father had taken the picture and had not known or had not cared that his shadow reached almost to their feet.

Knut had been the elder by three years and the larger by more than that, larger in every way that counted to a boy, louder, quicker to laugh and quicker to anger, the one their mother worried over and their father struck. Halvard had been the watcher. He had learned early that there was safety in the corners of a room, in stillness, in being the one nobody thought to blame. He had watched Knut take the blows that might otherwise have come to him and he had not always been grateful, had sometimes despised his brother for being so loud as to draw the lightning, and only later, much later, had he understood that Knut had drawn it deliberately, had made himself the target, had stood in the open of the kitchen and said the thing that would turn their father's face purple precisely when he saw that Halvard had done something small and forgivable that their father would not forgive.

He had never thanked him. There had been no language for it between them. By the time Halvard was old enough to have found the words, Knut was gone, first into the merchant marine and then into the long unspooling of a life that touched theirs only glancingly, a postcard, a phone call at Christmas growing rarer, a borrowed sum of money never returned, and then the final thing, the thing that had ended it, which Halvard now could barely reconstruct, so much had it been a matter of accumulation rather than event. Their mother's funeral. Knut arriving drunk. Words in the churchyard, in the grey, with the other mourners pretending not to hear. Halvard had said, you were never here, and Knut had said something back, and the something had been the kind of thing that cannot be unsaid, and they had not spoken since.

He lay back on the bunk with his shoes on and listened to the engines. They had a depth to them that he felt in his sternum, a vibration that was almost pleasant, almost the memory of being driven somewhere as a child in the back of a car, half asleep, trusting that the adults knew where they were going. He thought he would not sleep and then he slept, and woke with the disorientation of a man who does not know what hour it is or which way the sea is running, and got up and went out into the corridor and up to the deck because he could not stand the cabin any longer.

The wind on the deck was cold enough to be an assault, and he gripped the rail and felt it leach the warmth from his hands and was glad of it. The ferry was crossing the strait now in earnest, the land gone on both sides, and above him the clouds had torn open in places to show stars, hard and remote, the stars of the high latitudes that seemed to have nothing to do with the warm familiar constellations of picture books. He had read once that the light from some of them was older than the human species. He did not know if this was true and standing there he did not care whether it was true; he wanted only to be a small thing under a large sky for a while, to be reduced, to have his particular griefs and failures rendered negligible by scale.

A woman came out onto the deck a little way along from him and lit a cigarette with some difficulty, cupping the flame, and the smell of the smoke reached him on the wind and he found he did not mind it. She was perhaps sixty, in a heavy coat, her hair whipping loose from whatever had held it. She nodded to him without speaking and he nodded back and they stood for a while at their separate stations along the rail like two people keeping a vigil they had not coordinated.

"You don't sleep either," she said at last, in Norwegian, raising her voice over the wind.

"No," he said.

"Most people sleep. The young ones sleep." She drew on the cigarette. "Where are you going?"

He told her the name of the place, the one he had not been able to pronounce, and she pronounced it for him, correcting the stress, and said, "I'm from near there. What takes you?"

He found he could not lie to her and could not, equally, tell her the truth, and so he said only, "Family."

She looked at him then with more attention, and he saw her understand something, perhaps not the specific thing but the weight of it, the way the word family can be made to carry a coffin. "Ah," she said, and she did not ask anything more, and he was grateful to her for that as he had been grateful to the woman on the phone, grateful to all the women who had been trained or had trained themselves in the management of other people's sorrow.

"It's a hard crossing in winter," she said after a while. "My husband worked these boats. Thirty years. He used to say the strait had a temper. You'd think the sea was the sea, the same everywhere, but he said each stretch of it had its own character, and this one was a sulker. Quiet for days and then it would turn on you." She dropped the cigarette and put her foot on it. "He's gone now," she said, not sadly, only as information. "Four years."

"I'm sorry."

"Don't be. We had a long time." She pulled her coat tighter. "That's the thing they don't tell you. A long time is the most you can ask for. People want a happy time, a peaceful time, but those come and go. A long time is the only mercy." She looked at him again. "You and your family. Have you had a long time?"

The question undid something in him. He gripped the rail harder. "No," he said. "No, I don't think we have."

She nodded as if this confirmed something she had long suspected about the world. "Then I'm sorry for you," she said. "That's the harder kind." And she said goodnight and went back inside, and he stood alone again with the stars and the temper of the sea, and after a while he found that his face was wet and he did not know whether it was the spray or himself.

He had not cried when the message came. He had noted this about himself with a kind of clinical detachment, the way he noted most things about himself, from the corner of the room. He had not cried and he had wondered whether this meant that he had already, long ago, completed the grieving for Knut, had buried him alive twenty-two years before in the churchyard and held the funeral in private over the slow years since, so that there was nothing left to feel now but the administrative residue, the obligation to identify, to sign, to arrange. But standing on the deck he understood that this was not it, or not all of it. What he felt was not the absence of grief but the terror of its arriving late, of its having waited all this time to ambush him, and the worse terror underneath that one, which was the possibility that it would not be Knut at all.

Because that was the thing he had not let himself examine. If the body was Knut's, then it was over, the long not-speaking sealed forever, the words in the churchyard left to stand as the last words, no chance now of revision. But if the body was not Knut's—if he crossed this sea and looked at a stranger's ruined face and shook his head and said no, this is not my brother—then Knut was still out there somewhere, alive, breathing, capable of being telephoned, written to, found. And Halvard did not know which of these outcomes he was hoping for, and the not-knowing of his own heart was worse than the not-knowing of the body's identity, because the second was a fact that would resolve itself by morning and the first was a thing about himself that he might never resolve at all.

He went back down. He did not sleep again but lay in the dark of the cabin with his eyes open, feeling the boat ride the swells, listening to the water against the hull a hand's breadth from his head, only the steel between him and the cold deep, and he thought about how Knut had loved the sea and he had feared it, how that had been one of the dividing lines between them from the beginning. Knut had gone toward the thing that frightened him; Halvard had built a life precisely calculated to avoid it. He had become an accountant. He had married a careful woman and they had been carefully unhappy and had carefully divorced, and he had a daughter who called him on Sundays out of duty and whom he loved with a love he had never found the courage to express, because expressing love had not been modeled for him, because the only person who had ever shown him love had done it by standing in the open and taking blows, and that was not a language Halvard had been able to learn.

Toward morning the engines changed their note. He felt the boat slowing, the great mass of it gathering itself, and he got up and washed his face in the folding sink and put on his coat and went up. The light was as the woman in Tromsø had promised, grey, unwashed, the colour of the inside of a shell. The land was there, low and dark against the lightening sky, the islands he had read about, and the boat was threading among them now toward a harbour he could just make out, a scatter of buildings, a church spire, the orange of a navigation light. Gulls had appeared from nowhere and rode the air above the stern.

A man was waiting on the quay. Halvard knew him at once for what he was, not by his clothes, which were ordinary, but by his stillness, the way he stood apart from the small crowd of those waiting for arrivals, not scanning for a face he loved but waiting for a duty to disembark. When Halvard came down the ramp the man stepped forward and said his name as a question, and Halvard said yes, and the man introduced himself as a sergeant of the district and said he had a car, and asked whether Halvard would like to go to the hotel first, to rest, before—

"No," Halvard said. "Let's go now. I won't rest."

The sergeant nodded as though he had expected this. They drove through the waking town, past the church, past a shop with its lights just coming on, and out along a road that followed the water, and the sergeant did not make conversation, for which Halvard was grateful, and only said, once, "It's a small place. We don't get many. The last one was a fisherman, two years ago. It's not—" He stopped. "It's not something we're used to. I want you to know that whatever you need, there's time. There's no hurry today."

"Thank you," Halvard said.

The building was low and modern and might have been a clinic of any kind, and inside it was warm, too warm after the deck, and smelled of disinfectant and something underneath the disinfectant that the disinfectant was meant to cover. A woman in a white coat met them, a doctor, and shook his hand and said the things that were said, and asked whether he understood the condition of the body, that immersion did things, that he should prepare himself, and Halvard said he understood, though of course he did not, no one could understand a thing they had not seen, the words were only a railing to hold as he went down the stairs of it.

They took him into a room. The body was under a sheet on a steel table and the room was very cold, colder than the deck had been, a deliberate engineered cold, and his breath did not show in it though it felt as if it should. The doctor stood across the table and the sergeant stood by the door and Halvard stood at the head and the doctor looked at him and Halvard nodded and the doctor drew back the sheet to the collarbones.

For a long moment he could not make the face mean anything. It had been changed, as they had warned him, swollen and discoloured, the features coarsened, the lips parted, the closed eyes sunk. It was the face of no one, a face the sea had been at work on, and his first feeling was relief, savage and immediate—this is not Knut, this is a stranger, I am free of it, Knut is alive somewhere and I can find him. And then he made himself look, properly look, the way he had not let himself look at anything in years, and he saw above the left eyebrow a small white seam where the hair did not grow, an old scar, and he knew it, he knew it the way he knew his own hands, because he had been there when it was made.

They had been on the rocks below the cabin at Senja. Knut had been showing him how to jump from the high ledge into the deep green water of the channel, and Halvard had been afraid, and Knut had jumped first to show him it was safe, and had misjudged it, or the water had been lower than he thought, and had struck his head on the rock below the surface and come up with the blood running into his eye, bright, shocking, and Halvard had screamed, and Knut had laughed, had actually laughed, treading water with the blood sheeting down his face, and called up, it's nothing, it's nothing, come down, don't be a coward your whole life. And Halvard had not jumped. He had run for their mother instead, and Knut had carried the scar ever after, a thin white line above the brow, and had told people he got it in a fight, because that was a better story, because the truth was only a boy showing his little brother that the world could be survived.

Halvard's legs went. The sergeant was there suddenly, a hand under his arm, and there was a chair, and he was sitting in it, and the doctor had drawn the sheet back up, and he heard himself saying yes, it's him, it's my brother, it's Knut, and the saying of it was the thing that broke him finally, the grief arriving not late after all but exactly on time, the moment it had been waiting twenty-two years to be sure of, the moment there was no longer any version of the future in which the phone might ring.

He wept in the chair in the cold room with the two strangers standing by, and they let him, and he was ashamed and then he was past shame, because there was no one left to be the watcher for, no one in the corner of the room observing and judging the unseemly display of a man's heart; the only person who had ever stood in the open for him was on the table under the sheet, and he understood that he had spent his whole life learning the wrong lesson from his brother, learning to hide when what Knut had been trying to teach him, that day on the rocks and every day after, was the opposite—to jump, to stand in the open, to say the true thing and take the consequence, to let the blood run down your face and laugh.

Afterward there were papers. There always were. He signed them at a desk with a pen that the sergeant lent him because his own had run dry, and the doctor explained what would happen, the arrangements that could be made, the questions of where the burial would be, and Halvard heard himself saying he would take his brother home, back across the water, back to the place where their mother was buried, and the words surprised him because he had not known he would say them, had not known until that moment that he had decided anything at all.

They asked him if he wanted to know how it had happened, and he said yes. Knut had been living on one of the outer islands, the sergeant said, alone, in a fisherman's cottage he rented by the month, had been there nearly a year, sober, by all accounts, working a little, keeping to himself. He had gone out in a small boat in weather he should have known better than to go out in—he was an experienced man, the sergeant said, that was the strange thing, he knew the sea—and the boat had been found drifting and then, three days later, the rest. There was no note. There was nothing to suggest it was anything but the sea doing what the sea did to those who went out in it, the temper of the strait turning on a man who had trusted it once too often.

But Halvard, signing the last of the papers, thought of his brother sober and alone in a cottage on an island at the edge of the world for the better part of a year, a year in which he might have written, in which the number had not changed, in which a card would have reached Halvard's door and been answered, he was almost sure now that it would have been answered—and he thought that perhaps the sea had not needed to turn on Knut at all, that perhaps a man could go out into weather he knew was wrong because he had run out of reasons to come back in, and that he, Halvard, watching from the corner of the room all these years, had been one of the reasons he might have had and had failed to be.

He did not know which it was. He would never know. It was the last not-knowing, and it would not resolve by morning or by any morning, and he carried it out of the building into the grey unwashed light, where the gulls were still riding the air over the harbour and the ferry he had come on was already loading for its return.
