The chair went first, the one with the cane seat that had sprung loose in 1971 and never been fixed, so that anyone who sat in it sank a little, the way you sink into a memory before you know you are remembering. A man in a denim jacket carried it out by its arms, holding it away from his body like something that might leak, and Delphine watched the back of his head and thought, you do not know that my mother sat in that chair and named me. Named her three other children too, but Delphine she named in that chair, big as a barrel and sweating in August, fanning herself with a church program, saying the name out loud to the room before the child was even born, as if to call her down out of wherever the unborn wait.

Eleven dollars. The chair had a sticker on it that said eleven dollars and the man had not even haggled.

She stood in the doorway of the kitchen because the kitchen was the one room she had decided not to sell out of, though there was nothing in it worth selling, only the table and the chipped enamel pots and the calendar from the funeral home still turned to the wrong month. People moved through the front rooms the way water moves through a house that has lost its walls, finding the low places, pooling at the good furniture, eddying around the lamps. They had come from three counties. The advertisement had run in two papers and the woman from the estate company, Brenda, had put little orange flags down the county road so nobody would miss the turn, and nobody had.

"You doing all right, hon?" Brenda said, passing through with a cash box under her arm like a hymnal. Brenda had a way of asking that did not require answer, which Delphine appreciated, being a woman herself who had spent fifty-three years answering questions nobody truly wanted answered. She nodded. Brenda went on.

The house was small and had always been small, but emptied of the things that made it her mother's it grew, the way a coffin grows around the body it holds, the way the absence of a person is always larger than the person ever was. She had not expected this. She had expected grief to come the way it had come for her father, in a single wave that knocked her flat in the parking lot of the hospital and then receded and left her standing, wet and emptied and able to drive home. But this was not that. This was the slow draining of a bathtub, the long sucking gurgle at the end, and she was the thing left circling.

A young couple was examining the sideboard. The woman was pregnant, holding the underside of her belly with both hands the way they do, as if the child might at any moment require carrying, and the man was crouched looking at the legs of the sideboard, running his thumb along the joint where the wood met. Checking for rot. Smart man. There was no rot. Her mother had oiled that sideboard every Easter and every Christmas with a rag and a tin of something that smelled of oranges and turpentine, had made the children do it some years, Delphine and Carla on their knees with the rags while the boys were let outside to do nothing because they were boys, and the unfairness of it had seemed at eight years old to be the whole architecture of the world.

The sideboard had held the good dishes. It had held the dishes that came out twice a year and were washed by hand in water so hot it turned your fingers the color of the dishes, and dried with the towels kept only for that, and put back, and the door closed, and that was Christmas, that was Easter, that was the whole of the ceremony her mother could afford to give them. The pregnant woman opened the top drawer and looked inside and found it empty, because Delphine had emptied it, had taken the cloth napkins yellowed at the folds and not been able to decide what to do with them and so had put them in a paper bag in the trunk of her car where they sat now, in the heat, going on yellowing.

"How much for the sideboard?" the man asked, standing, and he asked Delphine because she was the only one in the room who looked like she belonged to the house.

"You'd have to ask Brenda," Delphine said. "The lady with the box."

"It's a nice piece," he said, not to her exactly, to the room, to his wife, to himself, justifying. "They don't make them like this anymore."

"No," Delphine said. "They don't."

He found Brenda. Money changed hands. The man and another man — there was always another man at these things, summoned from nowhere, materializing to lift — carried the sideboard out tilted on its side, and one of its feet caught the door frame and gouged a pale line in the paint, and Delphine felt that in her own body, low, a wince, though the paint was nothing, the door frame was nothing, would belong by Monday to whoever bought the house, some other family, some other children who would put their hands where her hands had been on the wood and never know it.

Her mother had not wanted her to sell. Or rather her mother had not said, in life, anything about it one way or the other, had said only, when the subject of the future arose, you all will do what you want with it, said it with the particular flatness she used for the things that hurt her most, the flatness that the children had learned to read as a closed door, a do-not-enter, a here-be-grief. Delphine had three siblings and they had all agreed, on the phone, in the careful rotating way of grown children dividing a parent, that the house should be sold, that none of them could keep it, that Carla in Atlanta and the boys in Columbus and Delphine herself only forty minutes off but unable, simply unable, to live in it. They had agreed so quickly. That was the thing she kept turning over. How quickly they had agreed, as if they had each been carrying the decision already made, waiting only for permission to set it down.

A girl of maybe ten was standing in front of the bookshelf, which was not for sale, which had a sheet of paper taped to it that said NOT FOR SALE in Brenda's blocky hand, and the girl was reading the spines with her head tilted, the way children read, with their whole bodies leaning into the words. Delphine watched her. The books were going to Carla, or were supposed to go to Carla, who had asked for them, but Carla had not come, Carla had sent a check toward the cost of the company and her love and a long explanation about her work and the children's school, and so the books would go to Delphine after all, into the trunk with the napkins, into the dark with the things she could not yet refuse.

"Do you like to read?" Delphine asked the girl.

The girl looked up, startled, the way children are startled to be spoken to as if they are real. "Some of them are in another language," she said.

"French," Delphine said. "My mother taught French. At the high school. For thirty-one years."

The girl considered this. "Did she speak it at home?"

"No," Delphine said, and the no surprised her, came out of her with more force than she meant. "No, she didn't. She spoke it at school and she came home and she spoke English like everybody else." Like everybody white, she did not say. Her mother had been one of two colored teachers in that high school for the first nine years and the only one for three more, and she had come home each day and taken off the French along with her stockings and her good shoes and become again the woman who said y'all and lord have mercy and what did I tell you about that screen door. The French had been a costume she could not afford to wear in her own house, where it would have looked like putting on airs, where her own husband would have squinted at it, where the children would have laughed. So she folded it away each afternoon. And Delphine, who had grown up listening to her mother conjugate the verbs of a country she would never see, had not learned a word of it. Had refused to. Had heard the language as the sound of her mother going away to a place none of them could follow, and had punished the language for it.

"You can have one," Delphine said. "A book. Any one you like."

The girl's face did a complicated thing, wanting and suspecting. "The sign says not for sale."

"It's not for sale. It's free. To you." Delphine crossed the room and pulled down a small one bound in faded green, *Le Petit Prince*, which her mother had read to no one, which had simply lived on the shelf the way certain books live in certain houses, less read than kept, household gods. She held it out. "This one's good. Even if you can't read it yet. There's a fox in it."

The girl took the book in both hands. "Thank you," she said, and then, because she was ten and the dead were a kind of celebrity to her, "I'm sorry your mom died."

"Thank you," Delphine said. The girl went to find her own mother, the book held against her chest, and Delphine thought, there, that's one thing that goes out of this house to somebody who will be glad of it, and the thought was so close to peace that it frightened her and she had to go and stand in the kitchen again.

In the kitchen the calendar from the funeral home hung on its nail. November, it said, though it was July, a picture of a stand of birch trees in some country the funeral home had never visited, the white trunks lined up like the bones of something. Delphine took it down. The nail had been in the wall so long that the calendar had worn a shadow into the paint around it, a faint rectangle of cleaner color, and beneath that, where the older calendars had hung in years past, the ghosts of other rectangles, fainter, layered, a palimpsest of months. Her mother had gotten a calendar every December from the funeral home and the insurance man and the church, three calendars, and had hung only one, the funeral home's, because she said it had the best pictures, which was a joke, because the funeral home's pictures were always trees or barns or empty roads, never people, the funeral home being in the business of the spaces people leave. Her mother had laughed at her own joke every year. Best pictures. And every year Delphine had not understood it was a joke until she was much older, until she was old enough to know what a funeral home was for, and then she had understood it all at once, the way you understand a thing your mother said long ago, arriving late, the light of a star already dead.

She folded the calendar in half and then could not throw it away. She set it on the kitchen table. The table she would keep. She had decided this without deciding it, had simply not put a sticker on it, had told Brenda the kitchen was off limits and Brenda had not argued, Brenda having seen this before, Brenda having a kitchen of her own somewhere with a table she would not be able to sell when the time came.

Through the doorway she could see them carrying out the lamp. The good lamp, the one with the milk-glass shade, the one that had stood on the table by the front window so that anyone coming up the road after dark could see it burning and know the house was a house where someone was awake and waiting. Her mother had left it on for them, all of them, every night of their growing up, and later, when they were grown and gone and came home only at holidays, she had left it on still, so that turning off the county road you saw it first, that one warm eye, and knew you were almost there, knew you were expected. Delphine had driven that road in the dark four nights ago, after the hospital called, and the lamp had not been on. The house had been black against the blacker trees. And she had sat in the car in the driveway for a long time, unable to go in, because a house with the lamp off was a house that had stopped waiting, and she had not been ready to learn what that meant.

Now a woman she did not know was carrying the lamp out into the white afternoon, the shade wrapped in a dish towel, and the cord trailing behind it like something that had been cut.

"That one okay to go?" Brenda asked, suddenly at her elbow. "The lamp. I wasn't sure on the lamp."

"It's fine," Delphine said.

"Because we can pull it. I can catch her, she hasn't paid yet."

"It's fine, Brenda."

"All right, hon." Brenda touched her arm, just briefly, a touch that knew exactly how much it was allowed and took not one ounce more, and was gone.

The afternoon wore down. The rooms emptied. It was a strange mathematics, a house growing larger as it held less, and Delphine moved through it as the buyers thinned, the serious ones gone first with the good pieces, then the browsers, then the bargain hunters picking over what remained, the costume jewelry and the kitchen things and the box of her father's old tools that the boys had not wanted, that nobody had wanted, that went finally to a man who paid two dollars and seemed to feel he had robbed her. By four the orange flags down the road would be the only sign anything had happened here, and Brenda would pull those too, on her way out, rolling them up in their wire stems and dropping them in the back of her van for the next sale, the next mother, the next house draining slow into the hands of strangers.

Delphine sat down at the kitchen table. The chair under her was steady, an ordinary kitchen chair, no cane to sink through, and she put her hands flat on the table the way her mother used to do it, the way her mother sat when she was thinking or praying or only resting, the two of them sometimes sitting like that across from each other in the last years saying nothing, two women out of words and not needing them, the refrigerator humming, the clock on the stove keeping its small machine time.

Her mother's hands had been on this table ten thousand times. A hundred thousand. There was no oil of her left in the wood, no warmth, the wood did not keep her, wood keeps nothing, that was the lie of all of it, the keeping, the not-for-sale, the napkins in the trunk. You could not keep a person in a table. You could not keep a person in a lamp or a sideboard or a green book about a fox. The person was gone out the front door already, had gone out of the body in the hospital with Delphine holding the hand that did not hold back, and everything else was just furniture, just objects pretending to be more than objects because the alternative was to admit there was nothing left to hold.

But she did not believe that either. That was the trouble. She sat at the table not believing the comfort and not believing the despair, suspended between them the way the cane seat of that chair had suspended whoever sat in it, halfway down, never resting, and she thought about her mother naming her in the August heat, the church program going back and forth, the name said out loud to a room that held no one but her unborn child. Delphine. Where had she gotten it. Some French girl in some French book, probably, some name her mother had carried home from school and not been able to set down with the stockings, the one piece of that other country she had smuggled into the house and hidden in plain sight, in a daughter, in a living girl who would say the name aloud her whole life and never know she was speaking French, never know she was the one word of it her mother had refused to fold away.

Delphine put her face in her hands.

She did not cry. She had expected, finally, to cry, sitting there, but the crying did not come, only a great stillness, a held breath that went on and on, the house holding it with her, the empty rooms, the pale rectangles on the wall where the calendars had hung, the gouge in the door frame, the dust standing in the long bars of light from the front windows where the lamp no longer stood. The dust turned slowly in the light. It had always done that. She had watched it do that as a child, lying on the floor in the afternoon, the motes turning gold and weightless in the slant of sun, and her mother had told her it was the house breathing, told her every house breathed if you watched the light, and Delphine had believed her, had believed the house was alive and watching over them, and had only later learned it was dust, only skin, the sloughed-off skin of everyone who had ever lived there, hanging in the air, going nowhere, the dead and the living mixed together and turning gold in the sun.

So it was both. That was what she came to, sitting there, her face in her hands and the dust turning. It was both. There was nothing of her mother in the table and there was everything of her mother in the table. The objects kept nothing and the objects kept it all. The strangers carried out furniture and the strangers carried out nothing, because the thing the furniture had held had never been in the furniture, had been in the looking, in her own looking, and would go out of the house only when she did, would go down the county road in the trunk of her car with the napkins and the books, would live as long as she looked at it and die when she stopped, and she would stop, one day, somebody would carry her own things out into the white light and not know, and that was not a tragedy, she decided, that was only the breathing, the gold dust, the house taking its long slow breaths whether anyone watched the light or not.

"That's the last of them," Brenda said from the doorway. The front rooms behind her were bare. "I'll lock up if you want to head out. Unless you want a minute."

"A minute," Delphine said.

"Take all you need, hon. I'll be in the van doing the paperwork." Brenda hesitated. "You did real good today. I know it don't feel like it. But you did."

"Thank you," Delphine said, and meant it, and Brenda went, and the screen door banged behind her the way it had banged ten thousand times, a hundred thousand, and her mother's voice rose up out of nowhere, out of the wood, out of the dust, what did I tell you about that screen door, and Delphine laughed, a small wet broken sound that was almost the crying and was not quite the crying, and she stood up from the table.

She left the calendar where it was. She left it folded on the table in the empty house. November in July, the white birches like bones, the best pictures. Let the next family find it. Let them wonder. She picked up nothing else. She walked through the bare front rooms where her whole childhood had been carried out the door at eleven dollars and two dollars and free to a girl with a green book, and she stood for a moment where the lamp had been, where the warm eye had burned every night of her life telling her she was expected, and then she went out into the afternoon and pulled the door shut behind her, and the latch caught, and the house was dark inside even in the daylight, a house that had stopped waiting, and she found, walking to the car, that she could bear it. That was all. Not that it was good. Only that she could bear it, the way her mother had borne things, the way the women before her mother had borne things, folding the unbearable away with the stockings, hiding what they loved in plain sight, in a name, in a daughter, in the gold turning dust of an emptied room.

She got in the car. The napkins were yellowing in the trunk and the books and the boy's fox-girl was somewhere on the county road going home with her green prince, and Delphine started the engine and did not look back at the house, looked instead down the road where the orange flags still stood, marking the turn, telling the world that here, today, something had happened, and she drove past them one by one and they fell behind her and then there were no more of them and the road was just the road, going home.
