The ferry left at eleven, and Aksel stood at the stern rail until the lights of the harbor town thinned to a smear of orange against the black water. He had not meant to watch the land go. He had meant to find his cabin and sleep, because the crossing was eight hours and the woman at the terminal had said the morning identification could not happen before nine, and there was nothing to be gained from being awake. But he found he could not stop looking at the place he was leaving, even after it had stopped being a place and become only a quality of the dark.

The strait was famous for its weather. He had read this on the timetable, of all things, a small printed paragraph apologizing in advance for delays. Tonight the water was nearly flat, a long oily swell that lifted the ferry and set it down again with the patience of something breathing in its sleep. He gripped the cold rail and let it lift him. He was forty-six years old and he was going to look at a dead man's face and say either yes or no, and he had not decided yet which answer he wanted to be true.

His name was Marius, the dead man. Or the man might be Marius. The police in the north had a body and a wallet and a name in the wallet, but the face, the woman had said carefully, was not in a condition to be matched to a photograph, and there were reasons, which she did not specify and he did not ask about, why the usual means of identification were not available. So they had gone looking for family. And they had found Aksel, because Aksel was the only one left, and because somewhere in a database his name sat beside his brother's like a pair of shoes nobody had worn in years.

"You were close?" the woman had asked on the phone, not unkindly, the way people ask things they need to write down.

"No," Aksel had said. "We were not close."

She had waited, and he had not filled the silence, and eventually she had told him about the ferry.

He went inside. The lounge was nearly empty: a couple asleep against each other under a coat, a young man with headphones watching something on a phone, a woman in a yellow raincoat doing a crossword with a pen, which he respected. The lights were the flat institutional white of all places that operate through the night, places that have given up the pretense of belonging to any particular hour. A vending machine hummed. He bought a coffee from it that tasted of the machine and not of coffee and carried it to a window seat where he could watch his own reflection laid over the dark.

He had not seen Marius in nineteen years. He could do the arithmetic precisely because the last time had been at their mother's funeral, and their mother had died in the autumn that Aksel's daughter was born, so the two events were stapled together in his memory: the small purple face of the baby and the gray closed face of his brother across the open grave. They had not spoken at the funeral except for the necessary words. Marius had come late and left early. He had worn a coat too thin for the season and had stood with his hands jammed in the pockets as if to keep himself from offering them to anyone. Afterward, at the house, he had eaten nothing and drunk a great deal and then had gone, and that was the last of him.

There had been a reason, of course. There was always a reason, and the reason had a shape Aksel could no longer entirely reconstruct, which troubled him now more than the estrangement itself. He remembered the heat of it but not the fuel. Money, partly — there had been money after their father died, not much but enough to argue over, and they had argued over it with a viciousness that had surprised them both, as though the money were a language in which they could finally say the things they had wanted to say for thirty years. Marius had accused him of something. Of being the favored one, the steady one, the one who had stayed when Marius had to go. And Aksel had said — he remembered this, the one clear line surfacing out of the murk — Aksel had said, *You didn't have to go anywhere. You chose to go. Don't dress it up as exile.*

And Marius had looked at him with an expression Aksel had spent nineteen years trying not to understand, and had said nothing, and had gone.

The ferry rose and fell. Aksel drank the machine coffee.

The trouble with a brother, he thought, is that he is the only other person who was there. Their parents were dead. The house was sold. The town had changed its shops and its faces. There was no one else alive who had stood in that particular kitchen on that particular morning when their father had — but here the memory closed itself, the way a sea anemone closes when you touch it, and Aksel let it. There were memories he kept in that condition, sealed, and he had built a tidy life around the sealed places the way a city builds around a ruin it has agreed not to excavate. His wife knew there had been a brother. His daughter, nineteen now, knew she had an uncle somewhere, the way she knew there were countries she would never visit. He had made the estrangement into a fact of geography, something settled and far, and now the geography was failing him, because the brother had washed up at the top of the map and was requiring to be looked at.

He must have slept, because he woke with his cheek against the cold window and his neck full of pain and the coffee gone scummy on the little table. The lounge had not changed. The woman in the yellow raincoat had finished her crossword and was asleep with the pen still in her hand. The dark outside was absolute now; they were in the middle of the strait, the place from which neither shore is visible, and the ferry seemed to be standing still in a void, only the engine's drone and the slow heave of the deck proving they moved at all.

He got up to stretch his legs and found himself walking the length of the ship, down stairs that smelled of diesel and salt, along corridors lined with cabin doors behind which strangers slept their way north. He had not taken a cabin. It had seemed an extravagance for eight hours, and besides he had not wanted to lie alone in a small dark room with the thing he was traveling toward. Out here, moving, he could keep a half-step ahead of it.

On the car deck — he wasn't supposed to be there, but a door had been propped — his rental car sat among the lorries and the campervans, ticking as it cooled, looking absurd and personal in that cathedral of freight. He stood beside it. The cold down here was different, a wet metallic cold that got into the teeth. Water sloshed somewhere in the bilges. He put his hand on the roof of the car the way you might touch a horse, for the comfort of touching a thing that was his, and he thought about turning around. He thought about how he might, at the far terminal, simply not get off the ferry. Stay aboard, ride it back, telephone the careful woman and say he had been mistaken, he was the wrong man, there was some other Aksel who owed this dead person a face.

But there was no other Aksel. That was the whole of it.

A man came down a steel stair, one of the crew, in a boiler suit, and looked at him without much interest. "You're not to be down here," he said, but tiredly, a rule recited rather than enforced.

"I couldn't sleep."

The crewman nodded as though this were the most natural reason in the world for a man to be standing in the hold at four in the morning touching his car. He was older than Aksel, with a gray mustache stained yellow at one corner and eyes pouched from a life of nights. He took out a packet of cigarettes, considered the NO SMOKING sign, and put the packet away again with the resignation of a man who has lost this argument with himself many times.

"Crossing for business?" the crewman asked.

"No."

"Holiday?"

Aksel almost laughed. "No."

The crewman looked at him more carefully then, and seemed to read something, because he did not ask a third time. Instead he leaned against a lorry's wheel arch and folded his arms and said, "Bad water tonight, under the calm. There's a current here runs the wrong way to the wind. Makes a swell with no chop on it. Worst kind for the stomach, the long slow kind. People think flat means easy." He shook his head. "Flat doesn't mean easy."

"No," Aksel said. "I suppose it doesn't."

They stood a while in the ticking, sloshing dark. Then the crewman pushed himself off the wheel arch and said, "Go up and get a cabin. Tell them Edvard sent you, they've empties tonight. You don't want to arrive looking how you look." And he went off up the steel stair, and Aksel, obscurely grateful, went up too, and did as he was told, and a bored steward gave him a key, and he lay down in a narrow bunk in a cabin the size of a coffin and the size, he thought, of a confessional, and the swell with no chop on it lifted him and set him down, lifted him and set him down, and he gave up at last the half-step lead he had been keeping and let the thing catch him.

What caught him was not grief. He noted this with a kind of clinical surprise, lying in the dark. He had braced for grief and what arrived instead was an enormous, aching curiosity, a hunger he was ashamed of. He wanted to know. Where had Marius been all this time, in what rooms, beside what people. Whether he had married. Whether there were children somewhere who shared the family chin. Whether he had been happy even once, even briefly, in the nineteen years; whether he had stood on a beach somewhere with the sun on his face and thought, this is good, this is enough. Aksel found he wanted that for him so fiercely that his throat ached. He wanted his brother to have been happy in the places Aksel had not been allowed to see. And underneath the wanting, like the current under the calm, ran the older thing, the thing he had sealed: the morning in the kitchen, their father at the table, and Marius — Marius who was older by three years, Marius who had stepped between — and Aksel made himself stop, but the anemone did not close this time. It stayed open. He was too tired to make it close.

Their father had been a man of weather, all calm or all storm, and you learned to read the barometer of him or you suffered. Their mother read it well. Aksel learned to. But Marius never could, or never would; Marius walked into storms as though daring them, and took what came, and one morning when Aksel was eleven and Marius fourteen their father had risen from the table with the particular slowness that meant the worst, and Aksel had felt his whole body go to water, and Marius had stood up and put himself between, just put his body in the space between the man and the boy, and said, in a voice Aksel had never forgotten, *Not him. Me.*

And it had been him. For years it had been him, and Aksel had been the steady one, the favored one, the one who got to stay a child because his brother had volunteered to stop being one. That was the fuel. That was what he had not been able to reconstruct in the lounge and could not now stop reconstructing in the bunk. *You didn't have to go anywhere*, he had said over their mother's grave. *You chose to go.* As though Marius had not been going somewhere his whole life, walking out of that kitchen door step by step for thirty years, and Aksel had watched him go and called it a choice because it was easier than calling it what it was.

He wept then, finally, but it was not for the dead man at the top of the map. It was for the boy in the kitchen, both boys, the one who stood up and the one who didn't, and the weeping was ugly and brief and left him empty and clear, the way a storm leaves the air.

He slept after that, properly, and woke to a change in the engine's note and a gray light leaking around the edge of the cabin's blind. They were coming in. He raised the blind and there was the northern shore, low and dark and dusted with the first snow of the year, a line of it along the hills like salt on the rim of a glass. The town was small. A church, a fuel depot, ranks of fishing boats, a single tall crane standing motionless against the white sky. He washed his face in the steel basin and looked at himself in the steel mirror and saw, for one disorienting moment, his brother — the family chin, the deep grooves their father had bequeathed them both, the eyes that had always looked older than they were. We carried him in our faces, he thought, all this time. There was never any escaping it.

The careful woman was not the one who met him. It was a young policeman, very young, with a soft face he had not yet learned to harden, and he met Aksel on the dock with an umbrella though it was not raining, holding it furled like a baton, plainly nervous. "You had a good crossing?" the young man asked, and Aksel said the crossing was fine, and they got into a small police car that smelled of pine air freshener and drove the short distance through the waking town to a low brick building behind the church.

"It is not far," the young man kept saying. "It is not far." As though distance were the difficulty.

They went in through a side door. There was a corridor and a smell Aksel knew from his mother's last weeks, the smell of a place that fights decay with chemistry and only ever fights it to a draw. A different person met them there, a woman in a white coat, older, with reading glasses pushed up into gray hair and the steady, courteous, slightly removed manner of those who work daily with the dead. She shook his hand. Her hand was cool and dry.

"Thank you for coming so far," she said. "I know it is a great deal to ask."

"It's no trouble," Aksel said, and heard how absurd the words were, and she had the grace to let them pass.

She explained what would happen. She explained, gently, the condition, the time in the water, the reasons photographs were not enough; she explained that he would see the face and only the face, that the rest would be covered, that he should take as long as he needed and that there was no wrong response, that uncertainty was permitted, that many people were uncertain and that was all right. She asked if he understood and he said he understood. She asked if he was ready and he said yes, though he was not, though no one could be, and she led him through a door.

The room was cold and clean and very bright. There was a shape on the table under a pale green sheet. The woman in the white coat stood at the head of it and looked at Aksel and waited until he nodded, and then she drew the sheet back to the collarbones with a slow respect that Aksel would remember for the rest of his life, the way you remember the few genuinely careful acts you witness, and there was the face.

Aksel made himself look.

The water had done its work and the chemistry had done its work against the water, and what lay between the two was a face that had been a man's. The features were softened and wrong, the color was wrong, the stillness was a stillness no living face achieves, and for a long moment Aksel could not read it at all; it was a face the way a flooded field is a field, recognizable as a category but not as a particular thing. He looked at the brow. He looked at the line of the nose, what was left of it. He looked at the chin.

He had been so certain, in the cabin mirror, that he would know. That the family chin would settle the matter, that blood would call to blood across the cold table. But the chin told him nothing. It might have been Marius's chin. It might have been any of ten thousand men's chins. The face withheld itself; it had passed beyond the reach of recognition, into a country where it belonged to no one, or to everyone, or only to the water that had taken it.

"Take your time," the woman said quietly.

He took his time. He looked and looked, and the longer he looked the less he saw, and a panic rose in him, because he understood that he was going to have to choose, that no certainty was coming to rescue him from the choosing. He thought of the wallet, the name. He thought of the database that had set their names side by side. He thought: it would be so easy to say yes. To say yes and sign the form and take the brother home and bury him beside their mother and close the geography for good, this time properly, with a grave you could visit and a date you could carve. To make the estrangement into a thing with an ending. People wanted endings. He wanted one. The dead man on the table, whoever he was, had earned the right to be someone's, to be claimed, to not be left to the indifferent state.

But it was not Marius.

The knowledge arrived without drama, the way the true things do. He could not have said how he knew, since the face told him nothing; perhaps that was how he knew, that a face which was truly his brother's would have told him something even ruined, would have reached him under the swell of his own wanting. This face did not reach him. It lay there politely and was not his brother. The relief and the disappointment came together so violently that they canceled, and he was left standing in the cold bright room feeling nothing at all, which was, he understood, its own kind of answer.

"It's not him," Aksel said.

The woman in the white coat did not seem surprised. She drew the sheet back up, the same slow respect, covering the face, and the room felt larger when she had done it. "You're certain?"

"As certain as I can be." He cleared his throat. "It's not my brother."

She nodded and made a small note and asked him a few more questions, careful questions, whether there were marks he knew of, scars, an old break, and he answered them as best he could, and none of his answers matched, and at the end of it she said that they would continue to try to find the family of this man, that the name in the wallet might not be the man's name at all, that wallets were sometimes stolen or sold or simply lost and found by the wrong drowned person. She said this without cynicism, as a simple fact of her trade. There were the named dead and the unnamed dead, and her work was to move them, where she could, from the second category to the first, and sometimes she could not, and she had made her peace with that long ago.

"I'm sorry to have brought you so far," she said again at the door, "for nothing."

"It wasn't for nothing," Aksel said, and found he meant it, though he could not have explained it to her, and did not try.

The young policeman drove him back through the town. The snow had begun, real snow now, fat slow flakes that melted as they touched the windshield. "I'm sorry," the young man said, who was sorry about everything, "that it was not — that you did not get—" and gave up.

"It's all right," Aksel said.

The return ferry did not leave until evening. He had the whole gray day to fill in a town the size of a held breath. He ate a meal he did not taste in a café by the harbor and walked out along the breakwater in the falling snow with his collar turned up, and watched the boats, and the crane that did not move, and the strait stretching away south into the weather, the way he had come.

His brother was alive. That was what he had learned, crossing the cold water to look at a stranger. Marius was alive somewhere, in some room, beside some person, with the family chin and the deep grooves, walking out of a kitchen he had left thirty years ago. The thought should have meant nothing — it changed nothing, he had no address, no thread to follow — and yet it changed everything, because Aksel had crossed the strait believing in his bones that he was going to look at the end of his brother, and instead he had looked at the end of someone else's, and had been handed back, unasked, the dangerous fact of a living man with whom things were not finished.

He took out his phone there on the breakwater, in the snow, and the cold made his fingers stupid, and the signal was poor, but he found the old number — he had never deleted it, in nineteen years he had never been able to delete it — and he looked at it for a long time. He did not call. The number was certainly dead; numbers do not survive nineteen years. But he did not delete it either. He put the phone away and stood with the snow gathering on his shoulders and looked south across the swell that had no chop on it, the long slow swell that fooled you into thinking the water was easy, and he understood that flat did not mean easy, that nothing was finished, that the brother was out there under the same falling snow, and that this — this terrible reopened wanting — was what he was carrying back across the strait instead of a body.

It was, he thought, getting heavily into the rental car for the wait until the evening crossing, more than he had brought.

It was, God help him, almost hope.
