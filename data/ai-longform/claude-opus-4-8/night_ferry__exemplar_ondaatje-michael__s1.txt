The ferry left at eleven and there was nothing to see, only the lights of the harbour falling behind and then the dark that was the strait, the dark that was the country to the north, no difference between them. Devlin stood at the stern rail with the cold coming up off the water and he thought of nothing for a long while, which was its own kind of mercy. The engines made the floor of the boat tremble. A man in a yellow slicker was coiling a rope and he did this slowly, methodically, the way a man does work he has done ten thousand times, and Devlin watched him because it was easier than watching the water.

He had been telephoned at four in the afternoon. A voice from the constabulary on the island, careful and tired, asking was he the brother of Joseph Devlin, and he had said he supposed he was, which the voice did not know what to do with. There is a body, the voice said. We would like someone to look at it. We think it may be your brother. And Devlin had stood in the hallway of his house with the receiver and the late light coming through the glass of the door and laid out in stripes across the floor and he had not been able to remember his brother's face. Not the whole of it. He could remember Joseph's hands, the right thumb that had been broken at the joint and healed crooked so it would not lie flat against a table. He could remember the back of his neck. But the face would not come, and this frightened him more than the news, that twenty years could erase a face the way water erases a road.

– You'll want to sit, a woman said. She had come up beside him at the rail without his hearing. She was older, in a grey coat, and she carried a thermos. – It gets worse in the middle. The crossing.

– I'm all right.

– Suit yourself. She unscrewed the cup of the thermos and poured something into it and the steam came off it and was gone. She did not offer him any and he was grateful for that, that she let him stand there inside his own quiet, and after a while she went in and he was alone again with the man in the slicker who had finished his rope and gone somewhere Devlin had not noticed.

They had not spoken in twenty years. There was a wedding, his own, and Joseph had come to it drunk and said a thing to the bride that Devlin had never asked her to repeat, and after that the brothers had passed in and out of each other's countries without ever crossing. Their mother died and they stood at the grave on opposite sides of it like two men holding the ends of something neither would let go of and neither would pull. That was the last. Twelve years ago, that was. Joseph in a black coat with the crooked thumb hooked into his belt, and the rain coming down on the both of them, and the priest's voice going up into it and being lost.

Now they wanted him to look at a face.

In the cabin the lights were too bright and there were rows of orange seats bolted to the floor and a handful of people sleeping across them with their coats for blankets. A child slept with its mouth open. A man read a newspaper and turned the pages too loudly. Devlin found a seat by the window and the window gave him back only his own reflection and behind it the dark, so that he seemed to be sitting suspended over the strait, a man hung in the black air with the orange seats floating around him. He closed his eyes.

He thought of the summer they had built the boat. He had not thought of it in years and it came to him whole, the way a thing will when you have stopped looking for it. They were boys. Their father had a length of cedar he had been saving for something and never used and the two of them stole it out of the shed across two nights and carried it down to the slough behind the Macreadys' and there they built a boat, or what they believed was a boat, a flat-bottomed thing caulked with their father's roofing tar, and it had taken them the better part of a month and Joseph had done the figuring because Joseph could see a shape in his head and hold it there, and Devlin had done the cutting because his hands were steadier, and between them they had made the thing.

It floated. That was the miracle of it, that it floated. They had carried it down at dawn and set it on the brown water expecting it to fill and sink and it sat there on the surface light as a leaf, and Joseph had laughed, a sound Devlin had not heard since and could hear now, exactly, the pitch of it, his brother's laugh going out over the slough in the grey light, and they had got into it both at once which they should not have done and it had rocked and held and they had floated out into the middle of that small water like two kings.

He opened his eyes. The window. His own face.

He had forgotten the boat. How could a man forget a thing like that and remember the wedding, remember the words said to the bride, remember the rain at the grave. The mind keeps the wrong things. It keeps the wounds and lets the boat sink. He thought of his brother's hands holding the figuring in his head, the shape that would float, and he thought that perhaps a face is the same, that you carry the shape of it somewhere below the place where you can reach, and that when they pulled back the cloth he would know, the knowing would come up out of him whether he wanted it or not.

The boat was lost the same summer they built it. A storm took it off its mooring and they never found it, only a length of the cedar washed up a mile down with the tar still on it, and they had stood over it the way men stand over a thing that is theirs and is broken, and Joseph had said nothing, and Devlin had said nothing, and they had walked home apart along the road. Even then. Even as boys they had not known how to stand close to a grief.

The woman in the grey coat came back through the cabin with her empty thermos and saw him and stopped.

– You're going for someone, she said. It was not a question. – I can tell. The ones going for someone always stand at the back of the boat.

– My brother. Maybe my brother.

She nodded as if this were ordinary, which to her perhaps it was. – I lost a husband to this strait, she said. – Not the way you think. He didn't drown. He just went across one day and didn't come back, and I've made the crossing every month for nine years looking, and that's a kind of losing too. She said it lightly. – You'll want to be ready for the cold of it. The room they keep them in. They keep it very cold.

– Yes.

– And the face won't be the face you remember. Whatever's happened to it, and whatever the time has done. You'll have to look past it.

– I don't remember the face, Devlin said. He had not meant to say it. – That's the thing of it. I can't bring it up. I've been trying the whole crossing.

She looked at him a moment. – Then maybe it isn't him, she said. – Maybe your body knows before your eye does. She moved off down the aisle with her thermos and her grey coat and he watched her go and thought that she had said the truest and most frightening thing anyone could have said, that the not-remembering was itself a kind of answer, though he did not know which answer it was.

He must have slept. When he woke the engines had changed their note and the boat was slowing and there was grey at the window now, a low grey that was the first of the morning, and beyond it the shape of land, the island, black against the lightening sky with the lights of the town coming on along its edge like a string pulled taut. The child was awake and crying somewhere. The man with the newspaper had folded it and slept at last. Devlin sat up and his neck was stiff from the angle of the window and he thought of the boy at the wedding, his brother drunk and ruined, and he found that he did not feel the old anger when he thought of it. He felt only that it was a long time ago and that they had both been young and stupid in their separate ways and that one of them might now be lying in a cold room waiting for the other to come and say a name over him.

They docked. The slap of the boat against the pilings, the chains, the ramp coming down with its iron sound. He went off with the others, the woman in the grey coat ahead of him, and he watched her go down the gangway and along the wet quay and turn at the end of it and lift her hand to him, once, not waving, only lifting it, and then she was gone among the buildings and he never saw her again.

The constabulary was a low building of grey stone near the harbour and a man met him at the door who was the voice from the telephone, younger than the voice had sounded, with a careful soft face. – Mr Devlin. Thank you for coming over. It's a hard thing, a long way to come for a hard thing.

– Where is he.

– There's no need to rush at it. There's tea. You've been all night on the boat.

– I'd rather look. If I'm to look I'd rather do it now.

The young man nodded and led him through the building and down a stair and the cold came up to meet them as the woman had said it would, a cold that was not the cold of weather but a deliberate cold, a kept cold, and Devlin felt it go through his coat and into him. The room at the bottom was white and lit and there was a steel table and a shape on it under a sheet, and a doctor or a man who did the doctor's work stood by it in a rubber apron and did not look unkind, only weary, the way the man with the rope had looked weary, men who handle the same heavy thing all their lives.

– In your own time, the young constable said.

Devlin stood at the foot of the table and looked at the shape of the sheet and could not for a moment make himself go up to the head of it. He thought of the slough at dawn. He thought of the figuring his brother could hold in his head, the shape that would float. He thought: I will know him by his hands. They will not have changed the hands. And he went around the table to where the right hand would be and he said to the man in the apron, – Can I see his hand. Not the face yet. The hand.

The man looked at the constable and the constable nodded and the man drew the sheet back from the one arm only, carefully, and laid the hand bare, and Devlin looked down at it.

It was a hand. It was a man's hand, pale and stilled and not young, with the nails grown a little the way they say the nails grow after, though that is not true, it only looks that way, the flesh drawing back. And the thumb. The right thumb lay flat against the steel of the table. It lay perfectly flat. It had never been broken. It had never healed crooked. It was a whole and ordinary thumb and it lay there in the cold light flat against the steel, and Devlin stood and looked at it for a long time.

– It's not him, he said.

The constable came closer. – You're certain. Without the—

– His thumb was broken. When he was a boy. It healed crooked, it wouldn't lie flat, he could never lay his hand flat on a table his whole life. This man's thumb is whole. It isn't him.

The man in the apron drew the sheet back over the hand, gently, and Devlin felt something go out of him that he had not known was holding him up, and he put one hand on the edge of the steel table though it burned with the cold of it, and he stood there.

– I'm sorry to have brought you all this way, the constable said. – There was an identification card. We thought it was good enough cause. People lose cards, they're stolen, they're sold. I'm sorry, Mr Devlin. It's a long way to come for nothing.

– It isn't nothing, Devlin said.

He did not know what he meant by it and the constable did not ask. They went back up the stair into the lesser cold of the building and the young man made him tea after all and Devlin drank it and his hands shook around the cup, not from the news, the news was good news, the news was that his brother was somewhere alive in the world with his crooked thumb hooked into his belt, but his hands shook all the same, and he understood as he drank the tea that he had crossed the strait in the night believing his brother was dead and had made his peace with it somewhere over the dark middle of the water, had laid the anger down, had remembered the boat. He had come to the island ready to claim a body and stand over it and say at last the things you say. And the body was a stranger's. And his brother was alive. And he did not know, sitting there in the grey stone building with the tea going cold in his shaking hands, how to take back the peace he had made. It had cost him the whole crossing to make it. It seemed a thing he should not have to make twice.

– Will you go back on the morning boat, the constable asked.

– I suppose I will.

– There's nothing else keeps you here.

– No, Devlin said. – Nothing keeps me.

He went out into the morning. The light was full now over the harbour and the boats were working and the gulls were down over the water and the town went up the hill in its grey terraces with the smoke beginning from the chimneys. Somewhere a man alive carried the shape that would float in his head and could not lay his hand flat on a table. Devlin walked down to the quay and the boat was there that would take him back, the same boat, the man in the yellow slicker coiling the same rope or another like it, and the strait beyond it grey now and ordinary in the daylight, only water, only the distance between one place and another.

He had a sister once. Joseph would not know that he sometimes thought of writing to her, the sister, who had married and gone south, and asking after Joseph, where he was, whether he was well, and he had never done it because to do it was to admit that he wanted to know, and to admit that he wanted to know was to admit that twenty years of silence had been a thing he chose and could un-choose, that the wall was a wall he had built and could climb if he had the will. He stood on the quay and the wind came off the strait and he thought: I will write to her. The body had not been his brother and so his brother was alive and a living man can be written to, a living man can be stood close to at last, the figuring held between them, the shape that floats. He had crossed the water to bury his brother and was crossing it back to find him. The road and the river, he thought, though he did not know where the thought came from, the road sunk under the river, a tale told backwards. They had built a boat once and it had floated and that was the truest thing he knew about the two of them, that once they had made a thing together that floated, and the storm had taken it but it had floated first, it had held them both on the brown water in the grey light like two kings, and you do not forget that, whatever the mind keeps and whatever it lets go, the body keeps the floating somewhere below the place a man can reach.

The horn sounded. He went up the gangway with the others, the few going back, and he found a place at the stern again and stood at the rail and watched the island fall away behind him into the grey, the terraces, the smoke, the grey stone building near the harbour where a stranger lay in the cold under a sheet, somebody's brother, somebody who would come or would not come across the water to claim him. He hoped someone would come. He stood at the back of the boat the way the woman in the grey coat had said the ones who go for someone always stand, and he understood that he was still one of those, that he was going for someone, only the someone was alive now and somewhere ahead of him in the world and the going would be longer and harder than a single crossing of a strait, a going that might take what was left of his life and might come to nothing, might break upon a closed door or a turned back or another grave, but a going all the same, begun now, here, in the grey morning, with the cold coming up off the water and the island sinking behind and the country to the north no longer dark but lit, lit all along its edge by the risen sun.

The man in the slicker finished his rope. The engines took up their trembling. Devlin held the rail and did not go in though the cold was in his hands and his neck was stiff from the night, and he watched until the island was only a low grey line and then was nothing, was water, was the place where the water met the sky and you could not say which was which, as if from a life lived to a life imagined, and he thought of his brother's laugh going out over the slough in the dawn, the pitch of it exact, kept all these years below the place he could reach, and he found that the face came too, now, unlooked for, came up whole out of the water of him: his brother's young face turned to him in the boat that floated, laughing, the both of them riding the brown water like kings, before the wedding, before the grave, before the twenty years, the face he had not been able to bring up the whole crossing over, here it was, given back to him by the one body that was not his brother's, and he held to the rail and let it come.
