The pipe had been leaking for three weeks before either of them did anything about it, and by the time Daniel drove out from the city the water had cut a brown channel down the center row, exposing the roots of the Granny Smiths like the knuckles of a buried hand. He found Russell already there, of course. Russell was always already there. He stood at the head of the row in his canvas pants and the gray T-shirt gone translucent under the arms, holding the broken section of line up to the light as if he could read something in it, and when Daniel's car door slammed he didn't turn around.

"You're late," Russell said.

"It's seven in the morning."

"You said you'd come Thursday."

"It is Thursday."

Russell let the pipe drop and finally looked at him, squinting against the sun that was just clearing the eastern ridge. He had their father's face now, Daniel saw, the same heavy brow and the deep grooves bracketing the mouth, though Russell was only forty-one. Living out here did that. The land took the softness from a man's face and gave back something carved and weathered, and Daniel touched his own cheek without meaning to, feeling the city smoothness there, the indoor skin of a man who sat at a desk.

"Coffee's in the truck," Russell said, which was as close as he came to hello.

They drank it standing in the row, looking at the damage. The orchard ran forty acres down a gentle south-facing slope, eleven hundred trees in their ordered rows, and from where they stood it looked, in the early light, like something that still worked. You had to walk into it to see. You had to see the bare patches where trees had died and not been replaced, the cankers blooming gray on the older trunks, the way half the irrigation had simply been abandoned, the lines capped off because there was no money to fix what broke and no point in watering trees that wouldn't earn back the cost of the water.

"How bad is the main line," Daniel said.

"Bad enough. We dig it up, we replace the section here where it split, and then we'll see. Might be the joint up by the pump too." Russell crushed the empty cup in his fist. "I bought the fittings already."

"With what?"

Russell didn't answer that. He went to the truck and came back with two shovels and handed one to Daniel, handle first, and there was something in the gesture, in the assumption of it, that made Daniel want to set the thing down in the dirt and walk back to his car. But he took it. He took it because they were brothers and because the orchard had been their father's and his father's before him, and because eight months ago when the old man died Daniel had stood at the graveside and said nothing, had let Russell handle all of it, the casket and the priest and the women from the church with their casseroles, and this, the shovel, was the bill come due for that silence.

They dug. The dirt was the dirt Daniel had grown up in, that fine pale clay that packed under your nails and didn't wash out for days, and his hands remembered the work even when the rest of him had forgotten. For a while there was only the sound of the blades going in and the soft collapse of the earth they lifted out, and a meadowlark somewhere down the slope running through its phrases over and over, and the trees ticking faintly as the heat came into them.

"I talked to a man," Daniel said.

Russell kept digging.

"A developer. He came to the office. They're putting houses up on the Petrie place, you know that, the whole eighty acres, and he says—"

"I know what he says."

"You don't, actually. You don't know the number." Daniel straightened, pressing the heel of his hand into the small of his back. "It's a good number, Russ. It's a number that would set us both up. You could buy a place anywhere. You could buy a place that isn't dying under you."

Russell threw a shovelful of dirt against the side of the trench. "It's not dying. It's neglected. There's a difference."

"Is there."

"You take a thing that's neglected and you tend it and it comes back. You take a thing that's dying—" Russell stopped, leaning on the shovel, and for a moment Daniel thought he might actually finish the thought, might say something true. But he just shook his head and bent back to the work, and Daniel understood that the sentence had no end, that Russell had walked himself up to the edge of it and looked over and stepped back.

They reached the pipe by midmorning. It lay there in the bottom of the trench, the old galvanized main, and the split in it was uglier than Russell had let on, a long tear where the metal had corroded through and the pressure had finally finished it. Russell knelt down in the dirt and ran his thumb along the break, and Daniel watched his brother's face and saw that he hadn't known either, not really, how far gone it was. They had both been pretending. Russell had been pretending the orchard could be saved and Daniel had been pretending he wanted nothing to do with it, and the pipe lay between them in the cool dark of the trench telling the truth to both of them.

"It's the whole run," Russell said quietly. "If it went here it'll go everywhere. This metal's eighty years old."

"Then it's a bigger number than the fittings in your truck."

"I can see that."

"It's a number you don't have."

Russell stood up. His knees were caked white and he didn't bother to brush them. "Dad ran this place forty years," he said. "Forty years, on nothing, on less than nothing some years, and he never once talked about a number. You know what he'd say if he heard you out here, first thing, before we've even got the line cut, talking about a number?"

"He's dead, Russ. He doesn't get a vote anymore."

It came out harder than Daniel meant it, or maybe exactly as hard as he meant it, and he saw it land. Russell's jaw worked. The meadowlark had gone quiet. Down the slope the heat shimmered above the rows and the whole orchard seemed to hold its breath, and Daniel thought, not for the first time, that the dead governed the living far more than the living ever admitted, that his father was here in every row, in the spacing of the trees and the angle of the rows to the sun and in Russell's bent stubborn back, and that selling the land would be a kind of second death, the real one, the one that took.

"We need to cut the line," Russell said. "Get the saw."

So they cut it. Russell worked the hacksaw through the old pipe while Daniel held it steady, and the rust came off on both their hands, orange and fine, and the smell of it was metallic and old, the smell of the well house when they were boys, where their father had kept the tools on a pegboard with their outlines painted behind them so you'd know what was missing. They had played in there on rainy days. They had built things out of the offcuts of pipe and the scrap lumber, dams in the runoff ditch, forts in the wind-row of poplars along the north edge, and at night their father would come in smelling of this same rust and this same clay and sit at the table and not speak, worn down to the bone by the day, and their mother would set the food in front of him and the boys would watch to see what kind of mood the land had sent him home in. There had been good years. Daniel made himself remember that there had been good years, the trees heavy and the pickers laughing in the rows and the bins filling and his father almost light, almost young, walking the orchard at dusk with a hand trailing along the branches like a man among his children.

But there had been the other years too, more of them as time went on, the late frosts and the cheap imports and the water getting dearer, and the gradual terrible arithmetic of a small farm in a world that no longer had any use for small farms. Daniel had left for the city at eighteen and had not once regretted leaving, or had told himself he hadn't, and Russell had stayed, and that single divergence had made them into two men who could barely speak to each other across a trench.

"You hold it there," Russell said. "I'll thread the coupling on."

They fit the new section in. It was PVC, white and smooth and obscene against the old gray metal, and it would never corrode and never split and would outlast them both, and somehow that was the saddest thing Daniel had seen all morning, that bright new pipe joined to the dying old line, a transfusion into a body that was already letting go.

They broke at noon. Russell had brought sandwiches, and they sat in the bed of the truck in the shade of a big old tree at the head of the rows, one of the originals, a tree their grandfather had planted, too old now to bear much but too venerable to cut down. The leaves moved overhead. Russell handed him a sandwich and a warm can of soda and they ate without talking, and Daniel felt the strange peace that comes after physical work, the body satisfied even when nothing is resolved, and he thought that maybe the work was the only language they had left, the only place they still agreed.

"Becca's pregnant," Russell said.

Daniel turned. "What?"

"Three months. We weren't going to say anything yet." Russell looked out at the rows, not at his brother. "But I figured you should know. Before you decide. There's going to be somebody. Somebody after us." He took a bite and chewed it slowly. "I always thought there'd be somebody to give it to."

And there it was, Daniel thought, the whole argument in a sentence, the thing he had no answer for. He set the sandwich down on his knee.

"That's not fair," he said.

"I'm not trying to be fair."

"You think I want to sell it. You think I drive out here and I look at all this and I see a number." Daniel shook his head. "I see Dad. I see us. I see the well house and the poplars and the time you broke your arm falling out of the cherry tree we're not even allowed to talk about because it didn't earn its keep. You think I don't see it." He was surprised to feel his throat tighten, and he stopped, and breathed. "I see it more than you do. Because you live in it. You've got so close to it you can't see the whole of it anymore. You're down in the trench, Russ. You can't see that it's failing."

"It's not—"

"It is. You said it yourself. The whole line's gone. We patch this and another section blows next month and you're out here again, spending money you don't have to keep alive a thing that doesn't pay. And now there's going to be a kid, and you want to hand him this? You want to hand him forty acres of debt and a pump that's older than Dad was and the same forty years of going home worn to the bone with nothing to say at the table?" Daniel heard his own voice rising and couldn't stop it. "That's not a gift. That's a sentence. You'd be doing to him exactly what was done to you."

Russell had gone very still. The wind moved in the old tree. Somewhere a tractor was running on a neighbor's land, the sound coming and going on the air.

"What was done to me," Russell repeated.

"You know what I mean."

"I don't think I do." Russell set down his food and turned to face him, and his eyes were wet, which Daniel had not expected, had not seen since the funeral and not even really then. "You think staying was the thing that ruined me. You've always thought that. You went off and you made your money and you look at me and you see a man Dad trapped here, a man too dumb or too scared to leave." His voice was steady but it cost him. "But I stayed because I wanted to, Danny. I stayed because there's nothing in your city I ever wanted. You think I went home worn to the bone with nothing to say. I went home tired because I'd done something with my hands that I could see. Every row I walk, I know what's wrong with it and what's right. I know which trees Dad and I grafted and which ones his father set out. You can't buy that. You can't get that number from your developer." He wiped his face roughly with the back of his rusted hand, leaving a streak across his cheek. "I'm not a thing that happened to me. I chose this. And you've spent twenty years needing to believe I didn't, because if I chose it then maybe you ran from something instead of toward something."

Daniel didn't speak. He looked down at the half-eaten sandwich on his knee and the can sweating in his hand, and below them the orchard ran down to the road in its long failing rows, and he understood that his brother had said a true thing, the truest thing said between them in years, and that there was no good answer to it because it was true.

"I'm tired, Russ," he said finally, and it was not what he had planned to say. "I'm tired of being the one who left. I'm tired of feeling it every time I drive out here. You don't know what it's like to be the one who left. You get to stay and be the good son and I get to be the one who—" He stopped. "Whatever I am."

Russell looked at him for a long moment.

"You think I don't envy you," Russell said. "You think I haven't sat out here in February with the frost killing the buds and thought, he got out, the smart one got out." He almost laughed. "We've each spent our whole lives thinking the other one got the better deal. That's a hell of a thing, isn't it. For brothers."

They sat with that. The tractor sound faded. The shade had moved while they talked and the sun was on Daniel's boots now, warming the leather, and he thought about his father in this same truck bed, in this same shade, eating whatever their mother had packed, alone probably, because their father had always eaten alone in the field, had wanted it that way, a private communion with the work that no son was invited into.

"I can't carry it," Russell said quietly. "That's the truth I've been not saying. I can't carry it alone. Becca's been doing the books and it's worse than I let on. The line's the least of it. There's two seasons of back taxes and the equipment loan and the trees are old, Danny, half of them are past bearing and replanting eleven hundred trees is—" He shook his head. "I've known for a while. I've known and I kept fixing things because fixing things is the only thing I know how to do. You fix what's in front of you. You don't look at the whole of it because the whole of it'll stop your heart." He looked at his brother. "You said I can't see it because I'm in the trench. Maybe. But you've been up on the ridge your whole life, looking down, seeing the whole of it, and from up there it's just a shape on a map. A number. You don't smell it. You don't know which row floods. You don't know the trees." His voice broke a little. "Somebody has to know the trees. When it's gone, nobody'll know the trees anymore. That's what I can't—that's the part I can't—"

He didn't finish. He didn't have to.

Daniel reached out and put his hand on his brother's shoulder, and felt the heat coming off him and the tremor in the big muscle there, and they sat like that, two men in a truck bed under their grandfather's tree, and neither of them said anything for a long time because there was nothing to say that the day hadn't already said for them.

After a while they got down and finished the work. There was no decision in it, no formal turning toward the developer or away from him; there was only the unspoken understanding that they would fix this last thing properly, would make the line whole one more time, because it was in front of them and it could be fixed and a thing that can be fixed should be. They glued the joints and let them set and then Russell walked up to the well house and threw the breaker on the pump, and they stood over the trench and listened to the pump catch and groan and then settle into its old rhythm, and water moved into the line. They watched the new white section. It held. They walked the row together looking for leaks and found none, and the water ran out at the head of the row and into the dry channel and the dust drank it down, and then it pooled, and then it ran clear and steady down the center of the trees the way it was meant to.

"There," Russell said.

"There," Daniel agreed.

They stood and watched the water find its way down the row. The exposed roots of the Granny Smiths darkened as the moisture reached them, and Daniel imagined he could feel the trees taking it up, the long capillary pull of it from root to leaf, the orchard drinking after weeks of thirst, and he thought that whatever they decided, this much they had done, they had given it this one more drink, and there was something in that he didn't have a word for, something between mercy and apology.

"We'll get someone out," Daniel said. "An appraiser. Just to know. Just so we're not deciding blind." He looked at his brother. "And we'll bring in someone who knows orchards. A real assessment of the trees, what could be saved, what it'd cost. Both numbers, Russ. The sell number and the save number. And then we sit down, you and me and Becca, and we look at them together. No deciding from the ridge. No deciding from the trench."

Russell was quiet. The water ran.

"Both numbers," he said.

"Both numbers."

"And if the save number's impossible."

"Then it's impossible, and we'll both know it, and it won't be me doing it to you." Daniel watched the water. "And if it's not impossible, then I'll—" He stopped, surprised at himself. "Then maybe I come out more. Weekends. I don't know how to do any of this anymore but I used to. My hands remembered today." He held them up, rust-orange and clay-pale, the hands of a boy who'd grown up here. "I'd want to know the trees too. Before it's gone. Whichever way it goes. I'd want to know them once."

Russell looked at him for a long moment and then nodded, once, and turned back to the water, and Daniel understood it was the most his brother could give and that it was enough, that something had shifted between them in the heat of the day that would not shift back, some old account finally opened and laid out plainly so that at last they could read what was actually written there.

They walked the rows until the light went amber and long. Russell named the trees as they passed, this one grafted the year Daniel was born, this one the last their father set out before his hands gave up, this gnarled survivor at the corner that had borne through the freeze of '79 when everything around it died, and Daniel listened, and learned them, taking each name into himself the way the dry ground was taking the water, and the orchard around them was failing and beautiful and not yet gone, and the two brothers walked it together as the day burned down, not having decided anything, having decided everything, the water running on behind them in the dark of the trench toward the roots of the patient and indifferent trees.
