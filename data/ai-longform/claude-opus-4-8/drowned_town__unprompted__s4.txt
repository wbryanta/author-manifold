The water had been going down all spring, but it was June before the county sent her out to walk it.

Della parked the truck at the end of the access road, where the asphalt simply stopped at a chain-link gate someone had cut years ago and never repaired. Beyond it the land sloped away in terraces of cracked mud, pale gray and fissured like old pottery, down to where the reservoir still held its diminished shape. She sat for a moment with the engine ticking and looked at it. Sixty years, the memo had said. Lowest level since they filled it. She had read the words in the county office without feeling anything in particular, and then she had seen her own name on the assignment line, and then she had understood that she would have to drive out here and walk across the bottom of the place where she was born.

She got the equipment from the bed of the truck. The total station in its hard case, the tripod, the data collector, the prism pole that telescoped down to almost nothing and that she had carried up so many hillsides she no longer felt its weight. She slung the field bag over her shoulder and stood there in the heat. The reservoir gave off a smell she had not expected, a low organic rot, the smell of things that had been underwater a long time meeting air again and not knowing what to do with it.

The valley had been called Coldwater. She had been seven when they moved her family out, and the town had gone under the year she turned nine, so that her last memories of it were already the memories of a place being abandoned — windows boarded, the Methodist church stripped of its pews, the men from the power authority going door to door with their forms. Her mother had refused to watch the water come up. They had been living in the new house by then, the one in Harmon forty miles east, and her mother had kept the curtains drawn on the side that faced the valley as though the flood might be visible across all those hills if she only looked.

Now Della walked down through the terraces with the tripod over her shoulder, and the mud held her boots and let them go with a sucking reluctance.

The town did not appear all at once. That was the strange thing. She had imagined, driving out, that she would crest a rise and see it laid out below her like a model under glass, the streets in their grid, the foundations like the cells of a honeycomb. Instead it assembled itself slowly as she descended, the way a face assembles out of static. First the road. She was walking on it before she knew it was there — a stripe of different texture underfoot, the mud thinner over a hardness, and then a curb, that unmistakable right angle that nothing in nature makes. She stopped and crouched and put her hand flat against it. Concrete. The curb of a street. She followed the line of it with her eyes and saw how it ran straight for forty feet and then turned, and at the turning there was a square of poured foundation with the stubs of pipes standing up out of it, copper gone green, and she knew without any map that this was the corner of Lyle Street and the county road, and that the foundation was the gas station her uncle had run.

She set up the station there. It was easier to work than to stand. She leveled the tripod and mounted the instrument and turned it on and let it find itself, and she punched in the control point she had occupied that morning up on the dam, and the machine did its small calculations and told her where she was in the world, in the flat coordinates of the state plane, in feet north and feet east of an origin that meant nothing. She wrote it in the book. Sta. 1, she wrote. NE corner conc. found., approx. former gas station. Then she stood holding the prism pole and looked at the green copper pipes and could not make herself shoot the first point.

She had not told anyone in the office that she was from here. It was on no form. She had moved away as a child and gone to the state college and come back to the county as a surveyor twenty-two years later, and in all that time she had never once mentioned that the reservoir they crossed on the highway, the one that supplied half the eastern part of the state, lay over the bones of the town where her grandmother had died. It was not a secret exactly. It was only that there had never seemed to be a way to say it that would not sound like a request for something — sympathy, or interest, or some allowance she did not want.

She shot the gas station foundation. She walked the prism pole around its perimeter and shot the corners, and the data collector accepted each point with its small confirming tone, and she wrote in the book the things the machine could not know. That her uncle Ferris had kept a coffee can of orange soda money under the counter. That there had been a dog. The dog's name would not come to her, and she stood there in the heat trying to retrieve it, and the failure felt like a small betrayal, as though she had been entrusted with the dog and lost it.

By midmorning she had mapped two blocks. The work steadied her, as it always did. There was a town in the data now, a town being born again as a scatter of coordinates in the gray box at her belt, and she found that if she attended to the instrument and the book and the slow geometry of the streets she could keep the other thing at a distance. The other thing was not grief. She had been waiting to feel grief and it had not come. It was something more like vertigo, a sense that the ground she walked on was not the present at all but the past surfacing, and that if she were not careful she would fall through it.

She came to Lyle Street and walked down its center. The road was wide here, wider than the streets in Harmon, because Coldwater had been laid out in the optimistic years when the railroad spur was supposed to come and the town was supposed to grow, and the road had been built for a traffic that never arrived and then was drowned. On either side the foundations stood at intervals, square and rectangular, the open cellars half-filled with silt. Some had collapsed. Some held the geometry of rooms, the lines of interior walls still legible in the slab, so that she could read a house the way you read a blueprint — here the small square of a bathroom, here the longer rectangle of a living room, here the back step down into a kitchen.

She knew the house before she reached it. She had been counting foundations without realizing she was counting, her body keeping a tally her mind refused, and at the seventh lot on the left her feet slowed and stopped, and she was standing at the edge of her grandmother's house.

It was smaller than she remembered. They always said that and it was always true and the truth of it was no less strange for being expected. The foundation was perhaps thirty feet by forty. She could have walked its length in twelve strides. In her memory the house had been vast, a country with provinces — the front porch, the dim parlor with the piano nobody played, the kitchen with its iron stove, the back bedroom where her grandmother had lain that last winter with the curtains drawn against the cold light off the snow. All of it had fit in this rectangle. She stood at the edge and looked at it and felt the vertigo come up strong, the past pressing against the underside of the present like water against a door.

She did not set up the instrument. She left it on its tripod twenty feet back and walked into the foundation on foot, stepping over the low remnant of the front wall, and stood in what had been the parlor. The silt was thick here, a soft gray bed of it, and things showed through. She crouched and brushed at it with her fingers and uncovered a square of linoleum, the pattern of it still faintly visible, a yellow-and-white check gone the color of old teeth. She remembered the linoleum. She had lain on it as a small child on summer afternoons because it was cool, and she remembered the smell of it, and now here it was, and she put her palm flat against it the way she had put her palm against the curb, as though touch could verify what the eyes refused to fully grant.

She found the hearth. The chimney was gone, taken down before the flooding like everything tall, but the firebox remained, the brick of it, and on the brick there was a darkness she at first took for silt and then understood was soot. Sixty years underwater and the soot still held. Her grandmother had burned applewood. The smell of it was the smell of winter in that house, and Della knelt at the cold hearth and looked at the soot and the smell came to her, conjured out of nothing, applewood and the iron stove and her grandmother's hands, which had been enormous to her then and were probably no larger than her own hands now.

The dog's name was Rex. It came to her there at the hearth, unbidden, the way the linoleum had come. Rex. Her uncle's dog, a yellow shepherd mix that had slept under the gas station counter and that someone had taken when they moved, she did not know who, did not know what had become of him, and she knelt in her grandmother's drowned parlor and grieved suddenly and absurdly for a dog dead fifty years, because the dog was a thing she could grieve, a thing small enough to hold, when the rest of it was too large.

She stayed at the hearth a long time. The sun moved. When she finally stood her knees had stiffened and the gray silt had printed itself on them, two pale ovals, and she brushed at them and went back to the instrument because the instrument was what she had come for and the instrument did not care what she felt.

She mapped the house. She shot the corners of the foundation and the lines of the interior walls, and as she moved the prism pole from point to point she found she was narrating to herself, silently, the rooms as she crossed them. Bedroom. Hall. The bathroom with the claw-foot tub her grandmother had bathed her in, and she shot the corner of it and wrote bath in the book and then crossed out bath and wrote SE int. corner because the book was a public document and would be read by people who did not need to know about the tub. But she left a small mark in the margin, a thing of her own, and only she would know what it meant.

When she had finished the house she sat on the front wall and ate the lunch she had packed, a sandwich gone warm in the bag and an apple, and she looked out across the drowned town. From here she could see how much of it there was. The water had pulled back to a narrow channel down the middle of the old valley, where the creek had run before they dammed it — the creek was still there, she realized, it had been there all along under the reservoir, patient, and now the reservoir had shrunk back to almost the size of the creek and you could see what the water had only ever been borrowing. On either side of the channel the town lay exposed in the hard light, block after block of foundations, the streets between them, and here and there a thing that had survived: a set of concrete steps that climbed to a porch that was gone and a door that was gone, leading up into the air; the round drum of a cistern; the truncated cone of what might have been a fountain in the little park by the church. Birds had come down to it already. Killdeer ran on the mud and cried their two-note cry, and a heron stood in the channel with the absolute patience of herons, and somewhere a frog was going, and the place that had been dead under all that water was busy with a new and provisional life, a life that knew the water would come back and lived anyway, urgently, in the meantime.

She thought about her mother. Her mother had been dead eleven years and had never come back to look, not when they lowered the reservoir for the dam repairs in the eighties, not ever. Her mother had been a girl in this town, had been baptized in that drowned church, had married Della's father in it, and had walked away from it without looking and kept the curtains drawn for forty years. Della had judged her for that, privately, the way children judge their parents, had thought it weakness, an unwillingness to face a thing. She was not sure now that she had been right. There was a kind of strength in refusing to look, in deciding that a place was finished and would not be allowed to go on hurting you. Della had not refused. Della had become a surveyor and come back with instruments to measure the wound precisely, and she did not know if that was courage or only a more elaborate form of not letting go.

She finished the apple and put the core in the bag because you carried out what you carried in, even here, even into a town that was going to be drowned again by autumn. Then she stood and shouldered the prism pole and went on with the work, because the light was good and there was a town to map and she had been sent to map it.

In the afternoon she came to the church. It stood at the head of the main street where the land rose a little, on the highest ground in the valley, which was why its foundation was the most complete thing left — the water had been shallowest here, the silt thinnest. The church had been brick and they had not torn it all down. The lower courses of the walls still stood, three and four feet of brick in places, so that the floor plan was not a slab but a room, an open roofless room with its walls half-grown, and when Della stepped through the gap that had been the door she felt the difference at once. The other foundations were diagrams. This was a place. She stood in the nave with the brick walls around her to the height of her chest and the sky for a ceiling and the gray silt for a floor, and she understood that she had been baptized here, though she did not remember it, and that her mother had stood here in a white dress, and that the whole town had come here on Sundays and sat in the pews that were carried out and sold or burned, and that the dead of the town had been prayed over here before they were carried up the hill to the cemetery that, she realized with a small shock, must still be up there, above the waterline, unflooded, intact.

The cemetery. They had moved some of the graves before the flooding — she remembered that, vaguely, the controversy of it, the families who came forward and the families who could not be found, the graves disinterred and carried east and the graves left behind. Her grandmother was in the new cemetery in Harmon; they had moved her, Della had been to the grave. But the old cemetery on the hill would hold the ones nobody claimed, and it had never gone under the water at all, and in all the years she had lived forty miles away she had never once driven out to climb that hill and see it.

She did not map the church right away. She walked its length first, the way she had walked her grandmother's house, letting her boots find the floor. At the east end, where the altar would have been, the silt was mounded a little higher, and something showed in it, a curve of pale stone, and she crouched and uncovered the lip of the baptismal font. It had been too heavy to move, or no one had wanted it, and it had stood here through the filling and the sixty years of dark water and the slow uncovering, and it was full now of gray silt to its brim, a stone bowl full of the bottom of the lake. She knelt at it. She had been dipped in this. Her own head, her infant head that she could not remember, had been wetted from this font by a minister whose name she did not know, in front of a congregation now scattered or dead, in a town now drowned, and the water that had been used had come from the creek that still ran down the middle of the valley, patient, outlasting everything built on its banks.

She put her hand into the silt in the font. It was cool and fine and held the shape of her fingers when she withdrew them, and she did not know why she did it except that it seemed a thing that ought to be done, a touch returned across sixty years, and then she wiped her hand on her trousers and stood and went back for the instrument because she was a surveyor and the light was going.

She mapped the church in the long gold light of late afternoon. She shot the corners and the door and the line of the apse, and she shot the font, and in the book she wrote font, stone, in situ, and she did not cross it out, because a font was a fact a town might want to know it had, and there was no shame in the word. The shadows of the brick walls grew long across the silt. The heron lifted from the channel and flew the length of the dead street and was gone over the western hills. The killdeer quieted. She worked until the sun was a hand's breadth above the ridge and the light had gone from gold to amber to a deep failing orange, and then she stood in the roofless church and looked at the data collector and saw that she had recorded four hundred and eleven points, four hundred and eleven facts about a drowned town, enough to draw it, enough to make a map that someone could lay a hand on and say here, this is where it was, this is its shape, these are its streets.

The map would go in a drawer. She knew that. There was no plan to do anything with this — no plan to restore the town, which could not be restored, no plan to keep the reservoir low, which served too many people in the dry counties to the east. The water would come back. The autumn rains would fill the creek and the creek would fill the reservoir and the reservoir would rise up the terraced hillsides and cover the curbs and the foundations and her grandmother's hearth and the font full of silt, and Coldwater would go under again and stay under, perhaps for another sixty years, perhaps forever, if the rains were good. The map she was making was a map of something that would not exist again in her lifetime. It was a record made for no use, an act of pure attention, and she understood now why the county had not been able to find anyone but her to do it, and why she had not told them she was from here. Some things you did not do for the county. You did them because you had been sent, and you had been sent because no one else would go, and you went because the place had a right to be looked at squarely once before the water took it back.

She broke down the instrument in the last of the light. She collapsed the tripod and cased the station and telescoped the prism pole down to nothing and slung the field bag over her shoulder, and she stood for a moment in the doorway of the church looking back down the main street of the drowned town, all those open foundations filling with shadow, the streets running gray and straight between them toward the channel where the creek still moved.

She thought she should say something. There ought to be words for this, for the leaving of a place you would not see again, but she did not have them and she distrusted the ones she could imagine. So she only stood in the door of the church where she had been baptized, in the town where she had been born, and let herself look, fully, the way her mother had never been able to, and then she turned and began the climb back up the cracked gray terraces toward the truck, the equipment heavy on her shoulders, the killdeer crying once more behind her in the dusk.

At the top, before she got in the truck, she turned and found that from here, in the failing light, the town was no longer visible at all. It had sunk back into the gray of the lakebed, indistinguishable, a field of mud like any other. Only she knew it was there. Only the map knew, the four hundred and eleven points in the gray box, the shape of a place held now in numbers, waiting for the water.

She drove the access road in the dark, and she did not take the highway home. She took the county road east and then the gravel turning that climbed, and she found the old cemetery on the hill above the waterline, where it had been all along, and she sat in the truck with the headlights on the iron gate for a long time before she went in.
