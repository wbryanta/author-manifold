The auctioneer's table was set up in the front room where the piano used to be, and Cordelia watched a man in a green cap turn over her mother's cake stand, the cut-glass one with the chip on its rim that nobody had ever been allowed to wash in hot water. He held it to the window light like he was looking for something inside it. There was nothing inside it. There had never been anything inside it except cake, and once, when Cordelia was nine, a dead wren that her brother had hidden under the glass dome to frighten her, and it had worked. She had screamed and her mother had come running with flour on her hands and that was the last time anyone laughed in that house for a season, because her brother got the strap for it and the bird got buried by the porch and her mother got quiet, which was worse than the strap, the way her mother got quiet.

The man set the cake stand down. He did not buy it.

"You the daughter?" A woman with a clipboard had come up beside her, and Cordelia had not heard her cross the floor, which was strange because the floor in that house announced every footstep, had announced footsteps for sixty years, the loose board by the radiator that her mother never fixed because, she said, it told her when company was coming. The woman with the clipboard had crossed it silent as smoke.

"I'm the daughter."

"We're moving good today. People come out for the old houses. They like the old wood." The woman wrote something down. "You want to keep anything back, you tell me now, before it's spoken for. After it's spoken for it gets ugly."

"I told you already. The Bible. The photographs in the hall."

"Got those set aside." The woman's pencil scratched. "Everything else is everything else?"

Everything else is everything else. Cordelia turned the phrase over the way the man had turned the cake stand, looking inside it for what was not there. Everything else was the bed her mother died in, which two boys were carrying out the door right now, tilted sideways to fit through the frame, the headboard banging the jamb so that a curl of paint came off and fell, and nobody bent to pick it up, and why would they, paint was paint. Everything else was the kitchen table with the burn mark shaped like Florida where Cordelia had set down an iron once at fourteen, distracted, dreaming, and her mother had said nothing, only looked at the mark and then at her, and the look did the work the words would have done and did it deeper. Everything else was the mirror in the hall that had held her mother's face going from young to not-young to gone, a mirror that did not keep any of those faces, that gave them all back to the room and kept none for itself, selfish glass, and now a fat man was carrying it out wrapped in a blue moving blanket like a body.

"Everything else is everything else," Cordelia said.

She had flown in from Detroit on the Tuesday. The town was the same size it had always been, which is to say it was smaller, because the things that had made it large to her as a child—the church with the red door, the drugstore with the spinning stool seats, the school—had all shrunk to their true dimensions, and a place that does not grow when you are not looking is a place that is shrinking, there is no standing still in this. She had driven past the school. The kindergarten building was gone. There was a parking lot where the kindergarten building had been, and the parking lot was full, and she had felt nothing looking at it, which she noted, the way you note a symptom, the way you note that the pain you expected has not come and wonder what that means about the wound.

Her mother had been Pearl. People said it like it was a description and not a name. *That Pearl.* As in: that Pearl never missed a Sunday. As in: that Pearl could make a dollar do the work of five. As in, when Cordelia was small and had done something, anything, the wrong thing: *you watch yourself, that Pearl don't play.* And she didn't. Pearl did not play. Cordelia had spent fifty-three years learning the difference between a mother who did not play and a mother who did not love, and she had learned it too late, she had learned it on the phone three weeks ago when the hospice woman held the receiver to Pearl's ear and Pearl, who could no longer make words, had made a sound, and the sound had no language in it but it had Cordelia in it, her name was somewhere down inside that sound, broken up, and Cordelia had heard it and known it and that was the whole of what she got, a sound with her name drowned in it, after fifty-three years.

"How much for the chairs?" A young couple stood by the dining set. The wife was pregnant and had her hand on the small of her back the way pregnant women do, holding up the future. The husband ran his thumb along the chair rail.

"Tag's on the leg," the clipboard woman said.

The husband turned the chair over. Cordelia watched him do it. That chair had a name. They had all had names once, the dining chairs, named after the people who sat in them, so that the chair at the head was Daddy's chair long after Daddy was dead and gone, and you did not sit in Daddy's chair, and the chair by the window was Grandmother's chair, and Cordelia's chair was the one nearest the door because she was the one always wanting to leave, even at six, even at six she had positioned herself by the exit, and her mother had let her, had given her that chair without a word, which Cordelia had taken at the time for permission and understood now, only now, with the man turning her chair over in his hands to read the price, to have been something else. To have been her mother saying: I see you wanting to go. I will not hold the door. But I will not open it either. You will have to do that part yourself.

She had done that part herself. She had gone to Detroit at nineteen and come back for funerals and Christmases that thinned to funerals only, and now to this, the last thing, the carrying-out.

"It's a good chair," the husband said to his wife.

"It's a good chair," Cordelia said, and they both looked at her, surprised, because the daughters of dead women are not supposed to speak at these things, they are supposed to stand by the wall and let the house empty out around them like water leaving a tub. "It'll outlast you," she said. "It outlasted everybody so far."

The wife smiled the nervous smile of a person who has heard more than she paid for. "We'll take four," she said to the clipboard woman, and they did, they took four, and Cordelia watched her chair go out the door under the arm of a man who would never know its name, who would put it at a table in a kitchen she would never see, and a child not yet born would sit in it and want to leave or want to stay and the chair would not tell, chairs do not tell, that is the mercy and the cruelty of them both at once, they hold what you give them and give it back to no one.

The roaring in the house was the roaring of strangers. They moved through the rooms touching things. A woman lifted the lid of the deep freeze in the back porch and looked in and let it fall. A man stood a long time in front of the bookcase reading the spines, and Cordelia wanted to go and tell him that her mother had not read those books, that those books had belonged to a cousin who died, that Pearl kept them because you do not throw away a dead person's books, but you do, evidently, you do throw them away, or you sell them at three for a dollar, it just takes longer, it takes until you yourself are dead, and then a stranger reads the spines and decides.

"Cordelia? Cordelia Mayfield?"

The voice came from the hall. A woman about her own age, heavy, leaning on a cane, in a church dress though it was a Thursday, and Cordelia knew her before she knew her, knew the eyes before the name climbed up out of the years to meet them.

"Bernice."

"I had to come. I heard and I had to come." Bernice took both Cordelia's hands. Her hands were dry and warm and there was a lifetime in them. "I didn't come to buy nothing. I came to say I'm sorry. Your mother." She shook her head. "Your mother."

"My mother," Cordelia agreed, because what else was there to put after it. *Your mother* was a complete sentence in this town. It did not require a predicate.

"She fed me," Bernice said. "You probably don't remember. The year my daddy was sick and Mama was at the plant. Your mother fed me at this table every day after school. Every single day. Bread and butter and a glass of milk, and on Fridays a piece of that cake, the one she made in the glass stand." Bernice's eyes went to the front room, found the cake stand on the auctioneer's table, and held it. "There's the stand. Lord. There's the very stand."

Cordelia looked at the cake stand. She had not known. In fifty-three years she had not known that her mother fed Bernice at that table every day, bread and butter and a glass of milk and cake on Fridays, while Cordelia sat in the chair by the door wanting out, and she felt the not-knowing open up under her like the loose board by the radiator, that board that told you company was coming, except no board had told her this was coming, this knowledge, this stranger's gift of her mother handed to her now, today, at the very last, when the table was sold and the chairs were sold and the cake stand sat chipped and unbought on a folding table waiting for nobody.

"I didn't know that," Cordelia said.

"Why would you? You was little. You was in your own world. Children are." Bernice patted her hand. "But I never forgot it. You don't forget who fed you. Forty years I been eating, child, and I can taste that bread right now standing here. That's a thing your mother did. That's the kind of thing." She let go of Cordelia's hands to wipe her eyes with the back of her wrist. "She didn't make a show of it. That wasn't her way. She just set the plate down and went on about her business. Like it was nothing. Like feeding a hungry child was nothing. But it wasn't nothing. I knew even then it wasn't nothing."

The clipboard woman was hovering. There was a man wanting to ask about the bedroom set, the price of the whole bedroom set, and the clipboard woman's eyes were on Cordelia the way the waiter's eyes go to the one who is paying.

"You go on," Bernice said. "You got to see to all this. I just wanted to say it. I wanted you to know somebody remembers." She looked around the emptying room. "Somebody besides you remembers."

She went out through the front door past the men carrying the bedstead, and at the door she stopped and laid her hand flat against the frame, against the very wood where the paint had curled off, and she stood that way a second with her hand on the house like you would lay a hand on a horse, or a coffin, and then she went down the steps to the street and was gone.

Cordelia did not see to the bedroom set. She let the clipboard woman see to it. She walked instead to the front room, to the auctioneer's table, and she picked up the cake stand, and the chip on the rim found the same place under her thumb it had always found, the way a key finds a lock, and she held it up to the window light the way the man in the green cap had held it, looking inside it.

There was nothing inside it.

There had never been anything inside it except cake, and a dead wren once, and the Fridays. The Fridays were inside it. Forty years of a woman tasting bread she ate as a child were inside it. Her mother's silence was inside it, the silence that was not absence, the silence Cordelia had spent her one wild life mistaking for a closed door when all along—she saw it now, she saw it the way Paul D must have seen the thing he'd been kept from seeing, suddenly, all at once, too late and not too late because there is no too late for seeing, only for doing—when all along the door had not been closed. The door had been left exactly as it was. Open enough to leave through. Open enough to come back through. And she had only ever gone the one direction, out, while behind her the table got set, the plate got laid down, the bread got buttered for a hungry child who was not her, who was a neighbor's child, because her own child had not been hungry, her own child had been fed, her own child had had the chair by the door and had not even known it was a gift, had thought it was a sentence.

"You keeping that?" the clipboard woman said. "It's tagged. Somebody might've already—"

"I'm keeping it."

"You said the Bible and the photographs."

"And this." Cordelia held the cake stand against her chest the way the pregnant woman had held her back, the way you hold the thing that is going to outlast you. "I'm keeping this."

The clipboard woman looked at her, and something in the look was tired, was older than clipboards, was a woman who had stood in a hundred emptying houses and watched a hundred daughters and sons reach at the last minute for the one wrong thing, the chipped thing, the thing worth nothing, while the good furniture, the worth-something furniture, went out the door under strangers' arms. "Suit yourself," she said. "It's your sale."

It's your sale. The phrase had no inside to it either. Cordelia did not look for one.

The afternoon went on the way afternoons go, which is to say it emptied. The men carried out the highboy and the cedar chest and the radio that had not played since Eisenhower and the mirror was already gone and the bed was gone and the chairs were gone, all four of them, including hers, the one by the door. By four o'clock the front room had its bones showing, the lighter rectangles on the wallpaper where pictures had hung for so long the sun had aged everything around them but not them, so that the wall now wore the ghosts of its own pictures, paler, younger, the wall remembering in those pale shapes what the rooms had looked like before the rooms looked like nothing. Cordelia walked through with the cake stand against her chest. She stepped on the loose board by the radiator and it sounded, the only true sound left in the house, and she stood on it and rocked her weight to make it sound again, to hear it announce her, the only company coming, herself, fifty-three years late, announced at last by the one board her mother never fixed because it told her when somebody was coming home.

She had not cried at the hospice. She had not cried on the plane. She had not cried at the church with the red door three weeks ago when they put Pearl in the ground beside Daddy and Grandmother and the brother who had hidden the wren, all of them in their row, all of them with their names. She did not cry now. But she stood on the board and rocked and made it speak, and the speaking went down through the empty house and came back up to her, and she understood that this was the conversation, this was the whole conversation, this knock of wood was what she had instead of words, the way that sound on the phone had been what Pearl had instead of words, and that between the two of them, the mother and the daughter, there had passed in fifty-three years a great deal that had no language in it but had each of them in it, drowned down inside, broken up, present.

*Your love is too thin,* she might have said to her mother, if her mother were there to say it to. *You fed the neighbor's child and gave me a chair by the door.*

And her mother might have said—she could hear her say it, in the quiet that was her way—*Thin love. Love is or it ain't. I kept the door open. What more was I to do, follow you to Detroit? You got two feet. I gave you the chair you wanted. I gave you the going. That was the gift. You just took it for the wrong thing all your life.*

And it had worked. That was the part Cordelia stood on the board and could not get around, the way Sethe could not be gotten around, would not be moved off the one true thing she had done. It had worked. Cordelia had gone. She had her life in Detroit, her own walls with their own ghosts of pictures, her own table where no child had been fed because she had had no children, had wanted none, had wanted only the door and the going through it, and she had gotten exactly that, exactly and only that, and it had worked, and standing in the emptied house she could not say whether working was the same as being right, whether her mother's love had been too thick or too thin or whether those were even the words for it, whether love came in thicknesses at all or only in directions, the giving direction and the taking direction, and Pearl had spent her life giving in the only direction Cordelia would let her, which was away, the gift of away, the buttered bread of going, and Cordelia had eaten it for fifty-three years and only now, with the chair sold and the stand against her chest and the board sounding under her foot, tasted what it was.

The clipboard woman came to the door of the front room. "We're about wrapped up," she said. "I'll bring you the figures tomorrow. You staying at the Comfort Inn out by the highway?"

"Yes."

"I'll bring them by." The woman looked at the empty room, at the pale rectangles on the wall, at Cordelia standing on the loose board with a chipped cake stand held to her chest like an infant. "It's hard," she said, and she said it the way Bernice had said *your mother*, as a complete sentence, requiring no predicate, because everybody in this work knew what *it* was and there was no need to name the rest. "It gets easier."

"No," Cordelia said. "I don't think it does. I think it just gets carried out the door a piece at a time until there's nothing left to carry, and then you call that easier because there's nothing left to be hard."

The clipboard woman did not argue. She had stood in a hundred houses. She knew which daughters could be argued with and which could not, and she folded her clipboard against her own chest in an unconscious mirror of the cake stand and said, "Lock up when you go. Leave the key in the box on the porch." And she went out, and the men went out behind her carrying the last of it, the kitchen step stool, the ironing board, the things at the bottom of the sale, the things worth almost nothing, the going-out of which made the same sound on the floorboards as the going-out of the good furniture had made, because the floor did not know the difference, the floor announced every footstep equally, the loved and the unloved, the kept and the sold, the coming and the going, all of it the same knock of wood saying *here is somebody, here is somebody passing through.*

Cordelia stayed after the trucks pulled away. The light through the bare window had gone long and gold and lay across the floor in the shape of the window, a bright cross on the boards where the piano used to be, where the auctioneer's table had been, where now there was nothing, only the light, which nobody could carry out, which would lie there until the sun moved and then it too would go, the way everything went, out the door, down the steps, into a kitchen she would never see, and a child not yet born would stand in that light or not, would want to leave or want to stay, and the light would not tell. That is the mercy of light, the same as the chair, the same as the stand. It holds what you give it and gives it back to no one.

She stepped off the board one last time and the house went silent, and she stood in the silence that was not absence, that had never been absence, that she had spent her one life mistaking, and she held the cake stand, and she went out the door, and she did not lock it for a long minute, she stood on the porch with the door open behind her, open enough to leave through, open enough to come back through, before she put the key in the box and went down to the rented car and drove out past the parking lot where the kindergarten used to be, full of cars, the future parked over the past, and felt, this time, looking at it, the thing she had expected and waited for, the pain that meant the wound was real, arriving at last, fifty-three years late, announced by no board, carrying itself in.
