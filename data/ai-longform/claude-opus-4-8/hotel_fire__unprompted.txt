The invitation came on heavy cream paper, the kind that holds an embossment like a scar holds the memory of the blade. Marguerite ran her thumb across the raised letters of the hotel's name before she had read the rest, and her body knew before her mind did. The thumb remembered. There is a way the skin recalls a thing the years have agreed to forget.

The Hôtel Belmont, the card said. Reopened, restored, risen. Her niece Camille was to be married in the rooftop garden where, twenty-two years ago, there had been no garden, only tar and pigeons and a low parapet Marguerite used to lean against on her breaks, smoking the cigarettes she could not afford, looking out over a city that did not know her name.

She had not told Camille. There had been no reason to. The girl had been born four years after the fire, into a family that spoke of it the way families speak of weather that has passed—an event, a misfortune, a thing survived. Aunt Marguerite was burned once, in the hotel, before you were born. That was the whole of the story as Camille had received it. It explained the shine on the inside of her aunt's left forearm, the way the skin there caught light differently, like the surface of a pond gone still. It explained nothing else.

She might have refused. She turned the invitation over in her hands for the better part of a week, propping it against the sugar bowl in the morning, reading it again with her coffee, and each day she told herself she would write back her regrets. Each day she did not. On the seventh day she understood that she was going to go, and that she had perhaps always been going to go, and that the not-deciding had only been a way of pretending the choice was still hers.

She bought a dress. Slate blue, with sleeves to the wrist. The saleswoman, a kind young thing, suggested something with more shoulder, given the season, and Marguerite said no, the sleeves were the point of the dress, and the girl, hearing something in her voice, did not argue.

The train south took four hours. She watched the country flatten and yellow, the vineyards giving way to the scrubbier coast, and she thought, against her will, of the first time she had made this journey, twenty-four years ago, twenty years old and certain she was escaping something rather than walking toward it. She had been a girl from a town with one church and one butcher, and the Belmont had seemed to her then a kind of palace, a place where wealth was a climate you could live inside. She had worn a uniform with a white collar that chafed. She had learned to fold a sheet so the corner was sharp enough to cut, to leave a room as though no one had ever breathed in it, to become a thing that passed through the lives of strangers without disturbing them, like air, like light, like the help.

The hotel, when the taxi delivered her to it, was not the hotel.

She had prepared herself for this and was undone by it anyway. The building was the same shape—they had kept the bones, the long pale façade, the iron balconies, the great doors of dark wood—but everything had been made new, scrubbed and gilded and lit from below by hidden fixtures, so that the stone glowed amber against the dusk. There was a doorman in a uniform that was not the uniform she remembered. There were potted olive trees flanking the entrance, silver-leafed and severe. There was a smell of money and lilies and something underneath, faint, that she told herself was not smoke, that was only her own mind laying its old map over a new country.

"Madame?" The doorman was holding the door.

"Yes," she said. "Thank you." And she went in.

The lobby took her breath. Not with its beauty, though it was beautiful—a long room of marble and brass, a chandelier like a frozen explosion, a desk of pale stone behind which two young people in immaculate black stood ready to receive her—but with the violence of its difference. She had expected to feel the old room beneath the new one, the way you feel the structure of a face beneath its expression. Instead she felt nothing. The room had been so thoroughly remade that it had no memory in it at all. They had not buried the fire here. They had abolished it. They had built a place that had never burned, that could not conceive of burning, and standing in the middle of it Marguerite felt for a moment as though she herself had been disproved.

"I'm here for the Lascaux wedding," she said to the young man at the desk.

He found her name and gave her a key—a real key, brass, heavy, a touch of the antique they had decided to preserve—and told her the room number, and where the rooftop ceremony would be held at six the following evening, and whether she would like assistance with her bag. She would not. She took the elevator up alone, watching the floors tick past in their new numbers, and she got out on the fourth floor because that was where her room was, and she stood in the corridor a long moment because the fourth floor was where it had happened.

Not in these rooms. These rooms did not exist before. But somewhere in this air, in this volume of space twenty-two years deep, there had been a corridor full of smoke, and she had been in it.

She found her room. She did not unpack. She sat on the edge of the bed, which was vast and white and so soft it felt faintly obscene, and she put her hands in her lap and waited for something to arrive. Grief, perhaps, or fear, or the particular vertigo of a returned place. What came instead was a memory so precise it had the quality of a photograph she had not known she possessed.

A child. There had been a child.

She had not let herself think the words in years, and now they arrived whole, and with them the weight of the small body, the heat of the corridor, the way the smoke had not been black like in the films but a kind of brown, a dirty curtain, descending. She had been on the fourth floor changing the linens in 412 when the alarm went, and she had done what she had been trained to do, which was to leave, to go down the service stair at the end of the corridor, quickly and without panic. She had been halfway to the stair when she heard the crying.

She had not wanted to go back. This was the thing she had never told anyone, not the inquiry, not her mother, not the man she had nearly married, not Camille. In the version the family kept, Marguerite was burned in the fire, and the passive voice did all the necessary work; it made the burning a thing that happened to her, an accident of proximity. In the truth, she had stood at the top of the service stair with safety below her, the cool clean stair going down into the air she could already taste, and she had heard the crying, and she had not wanted to go back, and she had gone back anyway, hating it, hating the crying, hating the child she did not yet know, and she had found the door of 419 and it had been hot to the touch and she had opened it.

She did not, sitting on the white bed twenty-two years later, remember much of what came after. The mind is merciful in its erasures. She remembered the shape of the woman on the floor, the mother, already gone, and she remembered the child against the far wall, and she remembered carrying it, and she remembered the corridor had become a thing of fire by then, that the fire had come up the wall to her left in a single breath, that she had turned her body to put herself between it and the child, and that this was how her arm had been given to the flames, the left arm, the inside of it, where she had cradled the small head against her chest.

She remembered the stair. She remembered the door at the bottom. She did not remember the air outside or the people or the ambulance. The family said she had walked out under her own power and handed the child to a fireman and then collapsed, and that this was very brave, and that they were all proud of her. She had let them say it. She had never corrected the part where it was brave, because she did not know what to call a thing you did while hating it, a thing you did because the alternative was to be a person who had not done it.

The child had lived. This much she had been told. A boy, two years old, named—she had never known his name. He had been taken to relatives. The hotel had given her money, a sum that was generous and also, she had understood even then, a transaction, a closing of accounts. The newspapers had used the word heroine for a week and then forgotten her. The skin grafts had taken. She had healed as well as such things heal, which is to say the body knit itself a story over the wound and the story was permanent.

She had never come back. There had been no occasion. And now there was an occasion, and she was here, and the room had no memory in it, and she had brought all the memory herself, in her body, in her arm, like contraband.

She did not sleep well. In the morning she walked the new corridors and found nothing, and this absence was its own kind of haunting. She had lunch alone in a café two streets away because she could not bear the hotel's restaurant. In the afternoon Camille found her in the lobby and embraced her and exclaimed at the dress, the lovely sleeves, and Marguerite held her niece and felt the girl's whole living warmth and thought, this is why, this is the only reason, and resolved to say nothing, to be a guest, to give the day to the young.

The rooftop at six was a transformation. Where there had been tar there was now a garden, real and improbable, with hedges in clipped geometry and roses trained up trellises and small trees in enormous pots, and strings of warm lights, and rows of pale chairs facing an arbor twined with flowers. The city spread out beyond the parapet, golden in the lowering sun, and Marguerite stood at the edge of it all and recognized, with a lurch, the parapet itself. They had kept it. The low stone wall where she had smoked on her breaks. It was the only thing on the entire roof she knew, and they had hidden it behind a row of potted lemon trees, but she knew its height, the height of her own hip, and she went to it while the guests were finding their seats and put her hand on the stone, and the stone was warm from the day, and it was the same stone, she was nearly certain, the actual stone, and she stood there with her hand on it until a young man in a server's uniform approached.

"Madame? The ceremony's about to begin. Can I show you to a seat?"

"Thank you," she said. "I know the way."

She sat near the back. She watched her niece married. The girl was radiant in the foolish, irreplaceable way of brides; the groom wept openly, which Marguerite found she liked him for. The officiant said the usual things and they were not diminished by being usual. The sun went down behind the buildings and the strung lights came into their own. There was applause, and a kiss, and the recessional, and then the chairs were spirited away by the staff with the silent efficiency Marguerite remembered in her own hands, the way good service makes the labor invisible, and the garden became a place for a party.

She drank a glass of champagne. She spoke to her sister, Camille's mother, who cried and clung to her and said it had been so long, why was it always weddings and funerals, and Marguerite agreed that it was always weddings and funerals. She admired the cake. She let the photographer arrange her in a cluster of relatives and she smiled and was documented. The band played and the young people danced and the older people watched the young people dance and remembered dancing, and Marguerite stood at the edge near the lemon trees, near the parapet, with a second glass of champagne she was not drinking, and she thought she had survived it, the return, that she would go home tomorrow and the place would release her, that she had paid whatever debt the visit had demanded.

A man approached her. He was perhaps in his late twenties, which she registered without yet doing the arithmetic. He held a glass of something and he had the careful, slightly formal manner of a young man at a wedding where he knows few people. He had a good face, open, a little anxious.

"You're Marguerite," he said. "Camille's aunt."

"I am."

"I'm sorry to—I asked which one you were. I've been working up to it all evening, and then I thought, if I don't do it now I'll lose my nerve." He laughed, embarrassed. "I'm Théo. I'm a friend of the groom's, that's how I'm—that's why I'm here. But that's not why I wanted to meet you."

"No?" she said. Something had begun in her chest, a slow turning.

"This is going to sound strange." He set his glass down on the low parapet, on the warm stone, and he pushed up the sleeve of his jacket, and then the sleeve of his shirt, baring his right forearm to the light, and there, on the inside of it, was the unmistakable smoothness, the pond-still shine, the skin that caught the light differently. A graft, healed, decades old, grown with him from a wound he could not have remembered receiving.

"I was in a fire," he said. "When I was two. In this hotel. The old one, before they rebuilt it. My mother died." He said it simply, a fact he had carried so long it had worn smooth. "I don't remember any of it. But I was told—the family that raised me told me—that a woman who worked here carried me out. A chambermaid. She was burned doing it. They never knew her name. There was money sent, and the newspapers, and then nothing. She vanished. And I always—" He stopped. He looked at her arm, at the slate-blue sleeve that came to her wrist. "When the groom said the bride's aunt had been burned in a hotel fire, years ago, before she was born, I thought. I did the—I'm sorry. I did the arithmetic. I shouldn't have. I don't even know if it's the same fire. It's probably—there must have been other fires, other hotels. I'm sorry. This is insane."

Marguerite found that she had set her own glass down. She did not remember doing it. The garden had gone very quiet, though the band still played; it was that her hearing had narrowed to the sound of his voice and the sound of her own blood.

"What was your mother's name," she said. It was not the question she had meant to ask. There was no version of the answer she could have verified. But it was the question that came.

"Hélène," he said. "Hélène Carrère. I don't remember her either. I have one photograph."

The name meant nothing to Marguerite. She had never known the woman's name; the woman had been a shape on the floor, already past names. But the not-knowing did not stop the turning in her chest, which had become now a kind of slow capsizing.

She pushed up her left sleeve.

She had not done it in public in twenty-two years. She wore sleeves to the wrist; the dress she had chosen had had the sleeves as its point. She had built a life in which this skin was private, was hers, was a thing she did not show even to lovers without warning. And now she pushed the slate-blue fabric up over her elbow and turned her arm to the warm light of the strung bulbs and showed him the inside of it, the long shine, the pond gone still, the place where the fire had been given what it wanted in exchange for him.

He looked at her arm. He looked at his own. They were not the same wound; they could not be, the fire had touched them in different places, his arm and her arm. But they were the same kind of wound, the same vintage, the same shine, two halves of a single transaction that had happened in a corridor that no longer existed, on a floor that had been rebuilt so thoroughly it had forgotten them both.

"I didn't want to go back," she heard herself say. She had never said it aloud. "I was at the stair. I could have gone down. I heard you crying and I didn't want to go back. I hated it. I hated the crying. I went back hating it."

She did not know why she was telling him this, the one shameful thing, the thing she had protected the family from, the truth that the brave version had been built to hide. Perhaps because he was the only person alive to whom it could possibly be owed. Perhaps because she had carried it this long and he was the only one who could be trusted with it, having been the reason for it.

Théo did not flinch. He looked at her with an expression she could not at first read and then understood was grief, but a strange grief, grief turned inside out, grief for a thing that had ended well.

"You went back though," he said. "That's the only part that—you went back."

"I went back."

He made a sound that was not quite a laugh and not quite the other thing. He reached out, slowly, the way you would approach an animal you did not wish to startle, and he touched her arm. He laid two fingers against the smooth skin on the inside of her forearm, very lightly, and she let him, though no one touched her there, though she had spent two decades ensuring no one could. His fingers were warm. The skin there had little feeling—the nerves had not all come back—but she felt the pressure of him, the fact of him, the weight of a hand on the place where she had once felt the weight of his head.

"Thank you," he said. It was an absurd thing to say, twenty-two years late, two words for a life, and they both knew it was absurd, and there were no other words, and so he said it.

Behind them the band finished a song and the guests applauded and someone called for the bride and groom to dance, and the ordinary glittering machinery of the wedding rolled on, indifferent, joyful, unaware. Camille was somewhere in it, alive and married, born four years after a fire she would never have to understand. The city lay golden and enormous beyond the parapet. The lemon trees screened them both from view.

Marguerite put her own hand over Théo's hand, over the fingers on her arm, and she found that she was weeping, without sound, the tears coming as if from the old wound itself, as if the smooth skin had been holding them all this time and had only now been given permission to let them go. She did not wipe them. She let him see.

"You grew up," she said.

"I grew up."

"Good," she said. "That's. Good."

The stone of the parapet held the day's warmth under their hands. Below them the new hotel held no memory at all, having abolished it, having built a place that had never burned. But up here, where they had kept the old wall, two people stood with their wounds bared to the warm light, remembering for the building, remembering for the dead woman on the floor and the brave version and the true one, remembering the corridor of brown smoke and the heat of the door and the small head against the chest, holding between them the whole weight of the thing that had happened, which no one else would ever carry, which was theirs.
