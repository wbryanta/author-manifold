The pump house smelled of rust and old water, a mineral dampness that had soaked into the concrete forty years ago and never left. Daniel crouched beside the intake valve with the wrench loose in his hand, listening to the silence where the motor should have been running. Outside, the heat was already coming up off the gravel, and he could hear his brother's truck door slam, the particular sound of it, a complaint of metal that meant Carl had arrived and would be in no mood.

"It's the foot valve again," Daniel said when the light from the doorway changed.

Carl stood there a moment letting his eyes adjust, a travel mug in one hand. He'd dressed for work, real work, in the canvas pants that were stiff at the knees and the boots he kept by the back door of his house in town. That meant something. It meant he'd decided to commit to the day, or at least to look like he had.

"You check the breaker?"

"It's not the breaker. It's the valve, or it's the line. Probably the line. I had it primed last night and it bled out by morning."

Carl set the mug on the shelf where their father had kept his coffee, in the ring stain their father's mugs had made, and crouched beside Daniel without being asked. For a moment they were just two men looking at a problem, and Daniel let himself feel the old ease of it, the way they could be wordless together over a machine. It never lasted. But it was there for a moment in the cool of the pump house, and he took what he could get.

"You want to dig the whole line up," Carl said. It wasn't a question. He could read the morning off the tools Daniel had already laid out on the tailgate, the shovels and the couplings and the can of primer purple.

"The blue section past the third row. That's where it's losing pressure. I marked it last fall."

"You marked it last fall and it's still in the ground."

"Because last fall you said we were selling."

Carl stood, his knees popping, and Daniel heard the small sound of him not answering. Through the doorway the orchard went down the slope in its rows, the peach trees their grandfather had planted and the newer apples their father had put in when the peaches started to go, and all of it the same dusty green that meant June and meant thirst. Forty acres of trees that wanted water and a line in the ground that wouldn't carry it.

They went out into the light. The heat took hold of them and Daniel felt his shirt stick almost at once. He handed Carl a shovel and they walked down between the rows to the place where Daniel had hammered a stake with orange tape last October, the tape now faded to the color of an old bruise. The grass was high here, gone to seed, because Daniel hadn't run the mower, because the mower needed a belt, because everything needed something and there was only one of him.

"Right here," Daniel said, and put his shovel in.

The first foot came easy, topsoil that their family had been turning for three generations, loose and dark and smelling of itself. Then the clay, and the work got honest. They dug without talking. Daniel was forty-four and Carl was forty-one, and they had dug together as boys under their father's narration, and there was a rhythm their bodies remembered even when their minds had gone separate ways. Daniel threw the dirt to his side and Carl threw it to his and the hole widened between them until the gray plastic of the lateral line showed at the bottom like something unburied.

"There's your crack," Carl said. He was right. A hairline along the top of the pipe, weeping, the soil beneath it darker than the rest. "Tree root, probably. Or it just got old."

"Everything gets old."

"That a comment?"

Daniel didn't answer. He got down on his knees in the dirt and cleared around the pipe with his hands, feeling for the extent of the damage, and found that the crack ran longer than he'd hoped, a foot and a half at least, the plastic gone brittle. They'd have to cut out the whole damaged section and couple in new. He sat back on his heels and wiped his face with his forearm and looked up at his brother standing against the white sky.

"We can fix this," he said.

"I know we can fix it. That was never the question."

"Then what's the question."

Carl looked away down the row, toward the bottom of the property where the trees petered out and the county road ran past, where there was a sign now, not theirs, a developer's sign with a phone number on it that had gone up on the Hendricks place in April. "The question is how many times we fix it," he said. "The question is whether I want to spend my Saturdays for the next twenty years lying in a ditch."

"Nobody asked you to."

"You ask me every time you call."

Daniel got the hacksaw from the bucket and fitted it against the pipe and began to cut, and the rasp of it filled the space where his answer might have gone. It was true and it wasn't. He called Carl because there was no one else to call. Their father had been dead two years and their mother lived in the assisted place in Visalia where she knew them about half the time, and there were no cousins who'd want this, no sons of their own — Carl had a daughter but she was twelve and lived mostly with her mother and did not, as far as anyone could tell, dream of peaches. So Daniel called Carl. And Carl came, most times, and they fixed the thing that was broken, and afterward Carl would stand in the yard and say it again, that they should sell, and Daniel would say not yet, and Carl would drive back to town.

The pipe came free in two cuts. Daniel pulled the damaged section out and laid it in the grass, and they both looked at it, the long weeping crack, the proof of decay. Carl took it up and turned it in his hands.

"You know what this is," he said. "It's a ninety-three. Look." He pointed to the faded ink stamped on the side, the manufacturer's date. "Dad put this in the year I graduated. He stood right here and glued these laterals in and he told me — I remember it — he told me this stuff'd outlast him. And he was right. It did. By two years." He set the pipe down. "Everything Dad put in is at the end of its life all at once, Danny. The trees. The pump. The lines. The roof on the barn. It's not one thing. It's all of it coming due in the same five years, and you want to chase it one ditch at a time."

"The trees aren't dead."

"The peaches are three-quarters done. You know that better than me. You're getting half the yield off them you got ten years ago, and the apples were a mistake, Dad knew they were a mistake, the chill hours aren't there anymore, it's getting too warm in the winters and it's only going one way." Carl crouched again, urgent now, the words coming faster, and Daniel recognized that his brother had rehearsed this, had said it to himself driving up the valley, maybe said it to his wife. "I ran the numbers. I actually sat down and ran them. We're losing money every year we hold it. You're losing money. You're working a full-time job in Fresno and then coming out here and putting your own paycheck into a hole in the ground. For what?"

"For this." Daniel gestured around them, at the rows, at the trees, at the brown hills beyond. He knew it wasn't an argument. He knew it was the opposite of an argument, the place where argument failed. "For this, Carl. I don't know how to say it better than that."

Carl looked at him for a long moment, and there was no contempt in it, which was worse. There was something like grief. "I know," he said quietly. "That's what scares me. That you can't say it any better, and you won't sell anyway."

They cut the new section to length. Daniel measured twice and Carl held the pipe while he marked it, and the work pulled them back from the edge of it, gave them something to do with their hands instead of their hearts. Daniel cleaned the cut ends with the rasp and wiped them and uncapped the can of primer and the sharp purple smell of it rose up between them, a smell that meant their father, that meant a hundred repairs over a hundred summers, and Daniel saw Carl's face change at the smell of it, saw something soften and then close.

"He'd want us to sell," Carl said. "You know that, right? He told me once. Near the end. He said don't let it bury you. He said the land's not worth a man's whole life."

"He didn't say that to me."

"Because he knew you wouldn't hear it."

Daniel painted the primer on the pipe ends and the inside of the couplings, his hands moving carefully, and he did not look up. He didn't know if it was true. It might be true. His father had been a hard practical man who loved the orchard the way you love a difficult animal, with no illusions, and it was possible he'd said that to Carl in some honest dark hour. It was also possible Carl had built the memory out of his own want, the way people did, the way Daniel knew he himself built memories the other direction, his father's hand on his shoulder in this row, his father saying this is yours now, son, when what his father had probably actually said was something about the spacing of the trees.

"Hand me the glue," Daniel said.

The cement set fast in the heat, so they had to work quick, no second chances. Daniel swabbed the joint and pushed the coupling home with a quarter turn the way their father had taught them, hold it ten seconds, let the chemicals weld it into one thing, and he held it and counted in his head and felt the bond take. Carl held the other end. They worked the new section into the gap and Daniel pushed the second coupling on and held it and counted, and then it was done, a length of bright new pipe spliced into the old, gleaming where everything else was dull, and they both sat back and looked at it.

"That'll hold," Daniel said.

"That'll hold," Carl agreed. "Till the next one."

They didn't backfill right away. They had to let the joints cure before they pressured the line, an hour at least in this heat, and so there was nothing to do but wait, and they climbed up out of the row and walked back toward the pump house and the shade of the big walnut their grandfather had planted for shade and nothing else, the one unproductive tree on the whole property, planted purely so a man could stand under something. Carl got his coffee from the shelf and Daniel got the water jug from the truck and they sat on the tailgate with their boots in the dust.

"How's Mom," Carl said.

"Same. She thought I was Dad on Tuesday. Talked to me about the frost like it was 1985." Daniel drank. "She asked about the peaches. Whether we'd thinned them yet."

Carl was quiet. A hawk turned over the far rows, riding something invisible. "You go every week?"

"Most weeks."

"You don't have to do that alone either."

"You're an hour and a half away. I'm twenty minutes."

"That's not what I —" Carl stopped. He set the coffee down. "I'm trying to say I see it. What you carry. I'm not — I know you think I just want the money. I don't need the money, Danny. Christ. I want you to have a life that isn't this." He swept his hand at the orchard. "I drive up here and you've lost weight and you've got that line in your forehead Dad had, and you're forty-four and you're not seeing anybody, and you spend every weekend keeping a dying thing alive, and I think, my brother's going to die in this orchard. He's going to drop dead in a ditch one June afternoon and I'll have to come find him. That's what I think about. That's why I run the numbers. Not because I don't love it. Because I do, and it's killing you, and I can't watch."

Daniel turned the water jug in his hands. Far off the hawk dropped and rose again with nothing. He found that his throat had tightened and he was angry at it, angry at the easy way his body wanted to give in to his brother's tenderness, which he distrusted more than the arguing, because the arguing he could fight.

"You left," he said. It came out lower than he meant. "That's the thing nobody says. You left for school and you didn't come back and that was fine, that was right, I'm not saying it wasn't. But I stayed. I came back from State and I stayed because somebody had to, and Dad was getting old, and I stayed. And now you want to come up here in your town boots and tell me the staying was the mistake. That the whole thing I did with my life was the wrong call. And maybe it was. But you don't get to be the one who tells me that."

Carl took it. Daniel watched him take it, watched it land, and was sorry and not sorry. The hawk was gone over the ridge.

"You're right," Carl said. "I don't get to tell you that." He picked at the seam of his canvas pants. "But somebody's got to say the true thing, and there's only the two of us. There's nobody else. So I'm saying it and you can hate me for it. The land's going to win, Danny. It always wins. It outlasted Grandpa and it outlasted Dad and it'll outlast you, and the only thing that changes is whether it takes everything you've got first, or whether you walk away with something." He looked at his brother. "I don't want to be standing at your grave thinking you gave your life to a piece of dirt that didn't love you back. Dirt can't love you back. That's the part you won't hear."

"It's not about being loved back."

"Then what's it about."

Daniel didn't answer at first. He looked down the slope at the rows, the peaches heavy and small and many of them already dropping, the harvest that would barely pay for the water to grow it. He looked at the gap in the earth where the new pipe lay curing, the bright thing in the old line. He tried to find the words and they weren't words, that was the trouble, they were a smell of primer and the particular green of June and his father's voice flat and certain over a machine and his own boy-hands learning the quarter turn, and the hawk, and the walnut planted for nothing but shade, and his mother asking had they thinned the peaches yet in a year that no longer existed.

"It's the only place I know who I am," he said finally. "Out there I'm a guy at a job. I'm a guy alone in an apartment. I come out here and put my hands in this and I'm — I'm somebody's son. I'm part of something that was here before me. You go to your house in town and you've got Megan and you've got Katie and you're somebody's husband and somebody's dad, you've got the next thing, you've got people that come after you. I don't have that. This is what comes after me. These trees. So when you say sell, you're not asking me to sell a thing. You're asking me to be nobody."

Carl didn't say anything for a long time. The heat sat on them. Somewhere a quail called and another answered.

"I didn't know it was like that," Carl said.

"I know you didn't."

"Katie could —" Carl started, and stopped himself, because it was a lie before he finished it, and they both knew it. Katie was not going to inherit the orchard. Katie was going to grow up and go somewhere with sidewalks and never look back, and that was right too, that was as it should be, and it left Daniel exactly where he was, the last man standing in a row of trees his great-grandfather had walked.

"Let's pressure the line," Daniel said, and slid off the tailgate.

They went back to the pump house. Daniel primed the pump from the jug, pouring water down into the housing, and threw the breaker, and the motor caught and ran rough and then evened out, and they listened to it the way you listen to a heart. Daniel watched the gauge. The needle climbed and trembled and held.

"It's holding," he said.

They walked down the row to be sure, and at the open ditch the new joint was dry, the bright pipe holding the pressure their father's old pipe couldn't, and beyond it Daniel heard the laterals begin to weep and then to flow, the drip emitters at the base of each tree coming alive one after another down the row like something waking, a small sound, the sound of water finding the roots. He stood and listened to it. It was the best sound he knew. Carl stood beside him and listened to it too, and Daniel thought, he hears it. Whatever else, he hears it.

"I'll backfill," Daniel said. "You don't have to stay for that."

"I'll stay."

They filled the hole together, the clay first and then the dark topsoil, tamping it down with their boots, and the bright pipe disappeared under the earth and the line was whole again, invisible, the orchard drinking. When it was done Daniel raked the grass back over the disturbed ground and you almost couldn't tell, in a week you wouldn't tell at all, in a year it would be as if the break had never been.

They carried the tools up to the truck. The sun was past noon now and the day's heat was the real heat, the heat that meant summer was no longer coming but here. At the tailgate Carl wiped his hands on a rag and handed it to Daniel and looked out over the rows one more time, and Daniel saw him doing the thing they both did, the looking that was also a kind of accounting, a goodbye held in reserve.

"I'm not going to push it this year," Carl said. "The harvest. Whatever it is, it is. We'll get through harvest." He folded the rag and set it on the tailgate. "But after. We talk about it after. Really talk. Not me at you and you at me. Both of us at the table with the numbers and Mom's situation and all of it, and we decide together, like brothers. Not you deciding alone and me complaining. Together. Can we do that."

Daniel looked at the place where the hole had been. The water was down there now doing its quiet work, and the trees would take it up and make their small late peaches, and the year would turn, and the chill hours would or wouldn't come, and his mother would live in 1985 and Katie would grow up toward sidewalks, and the developer's sign would stay up on the Hendricks place until somebody bought it or didn't.

"Yeah," he said. "We can do that."

It wasn't a yes to selling. They both knew that. But it was a yes to the table, to the both of them, and Carl took it the way you take a thing that's offered, with both hands, not looking too closely at the size of it.

"Okay," Carl said. He picked up his coffee, which had gone cold hours ago, and didn't drink it, just held it. "Okay."

He got in the truck. The door complained. He started the engine and lifted two fingers off the wheel, their father's wave, and backed around and went down the gravel toward the county road, the dust rising and hanging in the still air behind him and settling slow.

Daniel stood in the yard until the sound of the truck was gone. Then he walked back down the row, between the trees, to where he could hear the water again, the small living sound of it, every emitter ticking, the whole line breathing at last. He crouched at the base of the nearest tree and put his fingers in the wet dark soil at the root, and held them there, and felt the orchard drink.
