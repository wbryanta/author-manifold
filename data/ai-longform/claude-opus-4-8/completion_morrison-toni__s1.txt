He knew this country well enough, having traveled it twice before, but each time the land had changed its face. New fences, new clearings, a tavern where last year there had been only forest. The trail wound through stands of pine and oak, the morning sun finally burning off the last of the fog so that the world stood plain and ordinary, dappled with light. Birds quarreled overhead. Somewhere a woodpecker drummed against bark. Jacob let Regina pick her own pace where the path was narrow and pressed her gently when it widened.

He was riding to settle a debt, or rather to collect on one, though he expected little joy from the errand. A man named D'Ortega owed him money and had summoned him with a letter so flattering and so vague that Jacob suspected trouble in it. He did not like the Portuguese, did not like papists generally, and liked least of all the trade by which men like D'Ortega grew fat. But money was money, and a debt was a debt, and Jacob Vaark was not a man to leave coin uncollected merely because the man who owed it offended him. He had built what he had by attending to small things, by refusing to despise a profit because it came wrapped in unpleasant company.

By midday he reached a creek and let Regina drink while he ate the last of the bread and cheese he had carried from the sloop. The water ran clear over stones, and he crouched beside it and drank from his cupped hands, the cold of it shocking and good. He thought of his own land far to the north, the patch of it he had cleared and the house he meant someday to raise upon it, a proper house, not the rough cabin he and Rebekka shared now. He thought of Rebekka and felt the familiar mix of gratitude and ache. She had come to him across an ocean, a stranger purchased like cargo though he never thought of her so, and she had proved a marvel: strong, plainspoken, uncomplaining through losses that would have broken a lesser woman. They had buried children. Three of them now, and a fourth taken before it could be named. The thought sat in him like a stone he had learned to carry without letting it show.

He mounted again and rode on. The trail opened at last onto a road of sorts, rutted and wide, and the country grew tamer—tobacco fields stretching brown and stubbled where the leaf had been cut, slaves bent in the rows even now in the cooling year, gathering what could still be gathered. He passed them without looking too long, though they looked at him, and he felt the looking. A white man on a fine horse was a thing to be noted, measured, judged for danger or mercy. He gave them neither. He kept his eyes on the road and rode.

D'Ortega's holding announced itself a long while before he reached its gate. First the fields, then the fences, then a double row of young poplars planted to line a drive that did not yet exist, for the trees were saplings and the drive a muddy track. At the end of it stood the house, and Jacob slowed Regina to look at it.

It was a folly. That was the word that came to him. A great pile of brick and stone, three stories, with chimneys enough for a town and windows of real glass winking in the afternoon light. It sat upon the land like a crown upon a beggar, for all around it the work of building lay unfinished—piles of brick gone green with weather, a wall half-raised and abandoned, scaffolding gray and rotting. The man had built beyond his means and beyond his sense, and now the house mocked him from its own front lawn. Jacob felt a contempt rise in him that he knew was not entirely fair and did not trouble to suppress. He had worked for everything he owned. This man had borrowed and bought and bound others to the doing of it, and the result was this, a monument to appetite that could not pay its debts.

A boy ran out to take the horse. Jacob dismounted, his legs stiff from the long ride, and handed over the reins with a word of care for Regina—water, walking, no oats until she cooled. The boy nodded and led the mare away.

D'Ortega received him in a room too large for comfort, hung with cloth that had once been fine and was now fading, the windows letting in light that showed the dust on everything. He was a handsome man gone soft, dressed in clothes that strained their seams, and he greeted Jacob with an effusion of warmth that Jacob trusted not at all. There was wine, and there was a long meal Jacob did not want and ate sparingly of, and there was talk—endless talk—of everything but the debt. D'Ortega spoke of his troubles with a frankness meant to seem like confidence and intended, Jacob understood, to soften him. A ship had been lost. A cargo had sickened in the hold and died, and the loss had been ruinous. The man's wife appeared briefly, pale and bored, draped in lace, and withdrew. Through it all Jacob waited.

When at last the matter could no longer be avoided, D'Ortega came to it sideways, with apology and explanation, and the truth emerged: he could not pay. He had not the coin and did not expect to have it within any season Jacob could name. He spread his hands and looked grieved, as though the failure were a sorrow visited upon him rather than a thing he had made. Jacob felt his temper climb and held it. He had ridden a long way and crossed a bay in fog for this, and he would not leave with nothing.

"Then we must find another reckoning," Jacob said.

D'Ortega brightened, for this was the conversation he had wanted. He had goods. He had property. He had, he said, leaning forward, slaves—fine ones, healthy, and he would settle the debt in their flesh and Jacob might choose. He said it as a man offers a gift, generously, and Jacob felt his stomach turn even as his mind, the cold practical part of it that had built his small fortune, began to calculate. He did not want slaves. He had said so to himself many times and would say it again. He kept few and used them as he would use any hand, working beside them, and he believed—he had decided to believe—that this made him a different sort of man from the one who sat across the table spreading his soft hands.

But a debt was a debt.

They went out to look. D'Ortega led him across the yard to a row of low buildings, and there the slaves were gathered, having been roused from their work for this purpose, and made to stand in the failing light. Jacob looked at them and did not want to look. They were men mostly, and a few women, and they stood with the stillness of people long practiced in being looked at, in standing for inspection, in showing nothing. D'Ortega walked among them naming their virtues like a horse trader—this one strong, this one skilled at smithing, this one bred from good stock—and lifted a lip here to show good teeth, gripped an arm there to show the muscle in it, and Jacob followed and said nothing and felt the whole transaction settle on him like a sickness.

He would not do it. He decided that, and unsaid it, and decided it again. He would take goods instead—tobacco, what little coin there was, a promissory note he could press at law. He opened his mouth to say so.

And then he saw the woman.

She stood a little apart, near the doorway of the cookhouse, a woman of perhaps middling years though it was hard to tell, worn as she was, and she was looking at him. Not the way the others looked, with the practiced blankness, but directly, urgently, with an intention so plain it stopped him. At her skirts stood a child—a girl, very small, perhaps seven or eight years old, thin as a reed, her feet bare and her eyes enormous in her narrow face. The woman's hand was on the girl's shoulder.

D'Ortega saw where Jacob looked and made a dismissive gesture. The woman was a cook, he said, and a good one, but he would not part with her, she was wanted in the kitchen, his own wife depended on her. As for the girl—he shrugged. The girl was nothing, would fetch nothing, too small to work properly, a half-grown thing.

The woman stepped forward then, which she ought not to have done, and spoke, which she ought not to have done either, and Jacob saw D'Ortega's face darken with the offense of it. But she spoke quickly, in a low pleading rush, her eyes never leaving Jacob's face. Take the girl, she said. Take my daughter. Take the little one. The words tumbled out of her, and Jacob did not know whether to be moved or appalled, for she was offering her child to a stranger as a thing to be carried off, and she was doing it as though she begged for the child's life rather than bartered it away.

He understood, after a moment, that she did beg for it. That she had looked at him and seen in him some difference, some chance, and was spending everything she had—her place, her safety, perhaps her body's small comforts—to seize it. She had seen, where Jacob had not yet seen it in himself, that he was a man who might be persuaded to mercy if mercy could be made to look like profit.

He did not want the child. What would he do with a child, a girl too small to work, a thing that would need feeding and clothing for years before it gave back anything? It made no sense. It was a poor bargain even as charity, and he did not think of himself as a man given to charity, which he regarded as a kind of weakness, a softness men used to feel well about themselves.

But Rebekka.

The thought came to him whole and sudden. Rebekka, who had buried their children, who moved through the house now with a quiet that frightened him sometimes, who needed—he saw it clearly, standing in that muddy yard with the smell of the slave quarters in his nose—needed a thing to tend. A girl about the house. Not a daughter, he would not have called it that, he had no daughter, the daughters he had made were in the ground. But a girl. Help for Rebekka, and company, and something living and small in a house that had grown too quiet around its losses.

"That one," Jacob said, and pointed.

D'Ortega protested. The girl was worth nothing, he repeated, but Jacob understood the protest as its own kind of bargaining, and he understood too that D'Ortega was relieved, for the girl truly was worth nothing to him and to be rid of her against a debt was a fine outcome. The protest was theater. Jacob let him perform it and then named his terms: the girl, and the difference made up in tobacco and the note, and the debt called settled.

The cook—the mother—made a sound. Jacob did not look at her. He had learned long ago that to look too closely at the cost of a thing was to risk being unable to do it, and he wanted this done. The papers were drawn up that evening by candlelight, D'Ortega's hand making elaborate flourishes, and Jacob signed his own name plain beneath, and the girl was made his.

In the morning he prepared to leave. The fog had not come again; the day was clear and cold. He had Regina saddled and his satchel packed, and the girl was brought to him in the yard. Someone had put shoes on her feet, ill-fitting shoes too large, and she stumbled in them. She had a bundle no bigger than a loaf of bread, all she owned in the world. She did not cry. She looked at Jacob with the same enormous eyes and did not cry, and he thought she must be in a kind of shock, too young to understand what was happening to her, and he was almost glad of it, for a wailing child would have been more than he could manage.

The mother was there. She came to the girl and crouched and spoke to her, fast, in a voice pitched low, words Jacob could not follow and did not try to, and she pressed something into the girl's hands—he saw later it was a pair of shoes, a woman's shoes, far too big, that the girl carried clutched to her chest the whole long way—and she held the child's face between her hands and looked into it, and then she stood and stepped back, and her own face when she straightened was a thing Jacob made himself not see. She looked at him over the girl's head. She said something to him then, though it was not for him, he understood that even as she said it, it was for the girl and for herself and for some accounting beyond him. He did not know the words. He nodded as though he did, because there seemed nothing else to do, and he lifted the girl up onto the horse before him and felt how little she weighed, how she was all bone and breath, and he turned Regina toward the gate.

The girl did not look back. He noticed that and did not understand it then, that she kept her face forward and her eyes down and did not turn once to see her mother standing in the yard growing smaller. The mother watched, he supposed; he did not look to see. He rode out between the rotting scaffolds and the green-grown brick, down the muddy track lined with its hopeful poplars, and onto the road, with the child held in the circle of his arm, and he felt strange in himself, lighter and heavier at once, a man who had come to collect a debt and was riding away with a daughter that was not a daughter, owning her, having paid for her, not knowing what he had done.

They rode the day mostly in silence. The girl held her bundle and her shoes and made no sound. Once Jacob spoke to her, asked her name, and she said a word so soft he did not catch it and did not ask again. He shared his bread with her at the creek and she ate it in small careful bites, watching him the whole while as though he might at any moment change his nature and become a thing to fear. He could not blame her for the watching. He did not know what he was either.

The fog was gone but the bay when they reached it was rough, and the sloop when it came for them rode hard at anchor. The sloopmen looked at the child and said nothing, being men paid to carry whatever Jacob brought, and they helped her down into the boat and she sat in the bottom of it with her bundle clutched and her too-big shoes held against her, and she was sick over the side once, quietly, and Jacob held her shoulder while she heaved and then wiped her mouth with his own sleeve, surprising himself. The water was gray and the sky was gray and the far shore came up slowly out of it, and Jacob watched the girl watch the world go by and thought, against his will, of his own buried children, of the small graves on his land, and of Rebekka's face when he should ride up the track with this living thing before him on the horse, and he did not know what that face would do, but he hoped—he found that he hoped hard, with a force that surprised him—that it would soften, that it would open, that the quiet he feared in his wife would break before this small unasked-for gift the way the fog had broken before him among the live oaks, into a thick and golden and blinding mercy.

The journey home took the better part of two weeks, by sloop and then by horse, north through country that grew colder and harder as they went, the trees going bare, the rivers running dark and fast. The girl grew used to him by slow degrees. She did not speak much—he came to think there was something in her speech she was ashamed of, or some loss in it, for she seemed to have words and to hold them back as a person holds back coin they fear to spend. But she stopped flinching when he lifted her. She ate what he gave her. At night when they sheltered in some tavern or under some tree, she slept curled small with her bundle for a pillow and her mother's shoes, those great absurd shoes, held tight against her body, and Jacob did not take them from her, though they were no use to her, though they would not fit her for years. He understood, dimly, that they were not shoes to her. They were her mother. He let her keep them.

He thought sometimes, riding, of the cook's face, and of the thing she had done in giving up her child, and he turned it in his mind trying to make it into something he could carry without weight. He told himself it was mercy he had done. The girl was better off with him, in his house, in his cold clean north with its hard work and its plain food and its God who at least did not require a man to grow fat on the misery of others. She would be raised, not as a daughter, he would not say that, but raised, fed, kept, taught perhaps her letters if Rebekka had the patience for it. It was a better life than the one she would have had in that house of folly and debt. He believed this. He needed to believe it, and so he did, and the believing of it grew firmer in him with each mile he put between the girl and the place he had taken her from, until by the time he saw his own land rising before him he had nearly convinced himself that he had performed a good deed, that the child was lucky, that her mother would understand and be grateful if she could see how the girl would be cared for.

He did not let himself wonder long what the mother understood. He had seen her face for only a moment in the yard, when she straightened from holding the child, and he had made himself not see it, and he was glad now that he had not, because a man could not do necessary things if he stopped to read every face that the doing of them marked. This was a thing he had learned long ago and lived by. He did not examine it.

His house came up out of the gray afternoon, the rough cabin he meant someday to replace, smoke rising from its chimney, and there was a moment as he rode the last of the track when his heart rose up in him almost painfully, with hope and dread mixed, and the girl before him sat up straighter and looked, for the first time in days she lifted her eyes and looked ahead, at the house, at the smoke, at the new world he was carrying her into, and Jacob could not see her face but he felt her go still and alert against his arm.

The door opened. Rebekka came out, drying her hands on her apron, her face turning up the track to see who came, and Jacob watched her see him, and watched her see what he carried, and he reined Regina to a walk and could not for his life have said what he expected, or what he wanted, only that everything seemed to him to hang upon the next moment, the way the whole golden world had hung suspended in the fog on the morning he had waded ashore, before the live oaks split it open and let him through.

Rebekka stood very still. Then she came down the track toward them, and Jacob could not read her at first, and he held the child and held his breath, and the girl pressed back against him as though she would hide in him, the shoes clutched to her chest.

His wife stopped before the horse and looked up at the child a long while, saying nothing, and the girl looked down at her, and the two of them regarded each other across the cold air, the woman who had buried her daughters and the daughter who had been carried from her mother, neither of them anything to the other yet, both of them made suddenly into something by the crossing of Jacob's path and his cold quick reckoning in a muddy yard a thousand miles to the south. And then Rebekka raised her arms.

She raised them up to the girl, to lift her down, and her face when she did it was not the quiet that Jacob feared. It was open. It had come open the way he had hoped it would, and there was in it a hunger and a grief and a kind of terrible tenderness, and the girl, after a moment's stillness, after a long moment in which the whole thing might have gone one way or the other, leaned down into those arms and let herself be taken, and Rebekka caught her against her body and held her, the child and the two great shoes and the little bundle all gathered up together, and stood holding her in the cold yard with her eyes shut.

Jacob got down from the horse. He stood beside them, his wife and the girl who was not his daughter, and he felt the rightness of it settle over him, and beneath the rightness, far down, in a place he did not look into, he felt the other thing, the unread face in the southern yard, the words he had not understood, the cost of the bargain he had made. But that was far down, and the evening was coming on cold, and there was a fire in the house, and his wife was holding the child as though she would never set her down. He led Regina toward the barn. Behind him he heard Rebekka speaking, low, the first gentle words, and he did not turn to look, but he listened, and the listening was sweet to him, and he told himself again, as he would tell himself for years, that he had done a kindness, that the girl was lucky, that all of it had worked out for the best.
