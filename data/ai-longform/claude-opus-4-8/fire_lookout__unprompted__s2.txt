The smoke came up at quarter past two, and Della knew the drainage before she knew anything else.

She had been cataloging the day the way she catalogued every day—wind out of the southwest at eight miles an hour, humidity dropping toward the single digits she had learned to dread, the sky that hard enamel blue that meant no relief was coming. She had logged the morning's nothing. She had eaten half a tin of sardines standing at the window with the fork, and she had been thinking, in the loose unspooling way her mind worked up here, about whether she would miss any of it after September. The tower was being decommissioned. Forty-one years of someone sitting in this glass box on top of Cantle Peak, watching for the first thread of trouble, and then the satellites had gotten good enough and the budget had gotten thin enough and now it was just Della and one last summer, a courtesy posting, a way of letting the old girl down easy. Both of them. The tower and the woman.

So she had been thinking about that, and about nothing, and then there was the smoke.

It rose out of the Pinto Creek drainage in a column that for the first ninety seconds looked almost decorative, a gray feather standing straight up in the windless pocket the ridges made down there. She watched it the way you watch a thing you are still pretending might be something else. A slash pile someone hadn't permitted. A vehicle. The light playing tricks off the talus. She had the Osborne fire finder already, her hands moving to it before the rest of her caught up, sighting the column through the sighting hole, reading the azimuth off the brass ring, and the number she read was a number she had read a thousand times in the wrong direction, a number that meant the lower forks of Pinto Creek, which meant—

Renata's cabin sat in the lower forks of Pinto Creek.

Della stood with her thumb still pressing the cool brass and felt the whole architecture of the afternoon rearrange itself around this single fact. Her sister's cabin. The cabin their grandfather had built and their father had let rot and Renata had bought back, eleven years ago, for almost nothing because nobody wanted land that far up a bad road. Renata had spent a decade restoring it. Renata, who had not spoken to Della in six of those years, was down there in the smoke, or her cabin was, or both.

She did the next thing without deciding to. She crossed to the radio and keyed it.

"Lookout to dispatch. Lookout Cantle to Coldwater dispatch, I have smoke." The set hissed at her. The little needle did not move. She had reported it dead nine days ago—a power supply problem, they thought, a part on order—and they had told her to use the cell signal she could sometimes catch on the south catwalk if she held the phone over her head and stood very still. They had told her, gently, that it didn't much matter, that the tower wasn't really the front line anymore, that the satellites would catch anything she would. They had been kind about it. The kindness had felt like a hand on the shoulder of someone being walked toward the door.

She keyed it again anyway, the way you try a light switch in a room you know has no power. "Anybody. Anybody on this frequency. I have a column out of Pinto Creek, the lower forks, azimuth two-seventy-one, this is not a drill, please respond." Nothing. The hiss had a texture to it, almost a patience, as if the dead radio were waiting for her to be done.

She took the phone and went out onto the south catwalk. The heat hit her, the dry blowtorch quality of it, and below her the world fell away in folds of pine and rock to the valley she could not see the bottom of. She held the phone up. One bar wavered into being and then thought better of it. She moved three feet to the left, the boards warm through her socks—she had come out without her boots—and tried again. The bar came back. She dialed 911 with her thumb and held the phone over her head and watched the call try to leave her, the little spinning thing, the searching, and then it died, and she brought it down and saw No Service, and the breath went out of her in something that was not quite a word.

The road was eight miles downhill. She had driven it that morning in her head, the way she did every morning, an old habit—the switchbacks, the washout at mile three they'd graded badly, the cattle guard, the long traverse where the drop fell away on the passenger side and you didn't look. Eight miles to the bottom and then how far on the county road to anyone, to a phone, to a person who could send the thing that needed sending. Her truck sat at the base of the tower's mountain, at the trailhead, two miles down the foot trail from where she stood. The math assembled itself coldly. Two miles down on foot to the truck, eight miles down the mountain in the truck, and by then the column out there would have done whatever it was going to do.

She looked back at it. In the four minutes she'd spent fighting the radio and the phone it had thickened. The gray had a brown underbelly now, and the top of it had begun to lean, finally, eastward, which meant either the wind had found the drainage or the fire had made enough of its own heat to start reaching for sky. She knew what brown smoke meant. Brown meant it had moved off whatever it started in and into the duff, into the heavy dead-and-down that a wet decade and then this killing drought had laid up under the trees of the lower forks like kindling under a grate.

She went back inside and got her boots and put them on sitting on the edge of the cot, and she was aware, lacing them, that her hands were doing the job competently while the rest of her hovered somewhere above her own head, watching. This happened to her now and then. It had happened the day their mother died, her hands signing forms, her hands accepting a paper bag of effects, while she watched from the ceiling. It had happened the last time she'd seen Renata, in the gravel lot outside the lawyer's office, the day the estate finally closed, the day they had said to each other the things that could not be unsaid. Her hands had been very calm that day too. They had held her car keys. They had not reached out.

She tied the second boot and stood and looked at the column again and thought: I could go down. I could run the trail and drive the road and be at the Helms' place in forty minutes, fifty, and use their phone. And she knew, even as she thought it, the shape of the next thing, which was that forty minutes from now the column would be a plume and the plume would be a thing with a name, an incident, a designation, and crews would come from somewhere, but they would come for the fire, the public fire, the acres-and-values fire, and not for a single woman in a single cabin who might already be in her truck on the road out, who might already be standing in her yard with a garden hose pointed at the eave, who might be anywhere, who might be the one who lit it, burning slash she shouldn't have, watching it get away from her right now.

Or who might not be there at all. Renata spent August in town some years. Della didn't know anymore which years. That was the thing the silence had taken from her without her noticing—she no longer knew the simple operational facts of her sister's life. Whether she was at the cabin in August. Whether she still kept the dog. Whether she had, in the six years, found someone, married, divorced, learned to be happy, learned anything Della would want to know if they were the kind of sisters who knew things about each other. The smoke didn't care about any of that. The smoke only posed the one question, which was whether Della was going to do something or watch.

She had spent a career watching. That was the joke of it, standing there. Twenty-six summers in towers, this one and two others before it, twenty-six summers of being the one who saw and reported and then stood aside while other people, braver or just younger or just employed to do it, went down into the country and did the work. She had made her peace with that long ago. A lookout's whole virtue was distance. You stayed up where you could see, and you didn't go down, because the one thing worse than a fire was a fire and a missing lookout, a search-and-rescue inside an evacuation. Her usefulness was her stillness. She had told herself that for twenty-six years and most of those years it had been true and now it sat in her chest like a stone she had swallowed.

She made herself look at the column with the part of her that knew fire and not the part of her that was someone's sister. The lower forks. The fire was in the bottom, in the creek bottom, where it would have water and cool air and shade on its side, which was good, except the bottom was choked, she knew it was choked, she had walked it as a girl when it was their grandfather's and it had been thick even then. And the wind, the new lean of the smoke, the southwest wind that had been at eight all morning and that the afternoon would build—the wind would push it up the north-facing slope, up toward the saddle, and if it crowned out and got into the saddle it would have the whole upper drainage, and the upper drainage ran out onto the bench where three or four other places sat, the Okories' and the rental and the old Vanos place. So it was not only Renata. It was never only one thing. But it had started as Renata, in her mind, and she could not make it stop being that.

She thought about her grandfather, who had built the cabin and who had been, before any of it, a lookout himself, in a tower two ranges east that was gone now, torn down in the seventies. He used to say the mountain told you what it wanted if you held still long enough. He had been a still man. Their father had not been still and had not been able to hold the land or the marriage or himself, and Renata had inherited the not-stillness and Della had inherited the other thing, the watching, and somewhere in the long division of a family the two of them had ended up on opposite sides of a stillness that looked, from far enough away, almost like the same grief.

The fight had been about money, which was to say it had been about everything. The estate, what little there was, the cabin Renata had already bought back with her own money but which their father's will had muddied with a clause no one could parse, a clause that seemed to give Della a claim she had never wanted and could not seem to renounce in a way the lawyer would accept. Renata had believed Della wanted the cabin. Della had believed Renata believed the worst of her, which was its own injury, the injury of being misread by the one person who should have been able to read you. They had stood in the gravel and Renata had said You always think holding still is the same as being innocent and Della had said nothing, because it was the truest thing anyone had ever said to her and she had no defense against the truth. And then she had gotten in her car. Her calm hands on the keys. She had driven up to her tower for the season and she had let the season become a year and the year become the rest of it.

The column leaned harder east. The brown was winning.

She went inside and got the binoculars and came back and found the cabin's location in the folds, where she knew it would be even though she couldn't see the structure for the trees. The smoke was rising from just upslope of it, or just downslope, she couldn't tell, the foreshortening of the country making liars of all the distances. She watched for movement on the thread of road that came up the drainage. A vehicle. Dust. Anything. The road was empty. That meant nothing. The road was empty most of the time; that was the whole nature of the place.

She thought: if I run down now and drive out, the soonest help arrives is two hours, probably three, and they will stage at the bottom and scout before they commit anyone up that road because that's the protocol when you don't know what you're driving into, and meanwhile the only person on this mountain who can actually see the fire, who can call the azimuth and the rate of spread and the fuel and the lean of the smoke, who can be the eyes, will be in a truck on a switchback seeing nothing. The lookout's argument. The argument for staying. It was a good argument. It had the shape of duty, which was the most seductive shape a refusal could take.

And the other argument had no shape at all. It was just her sister's name.

She stood on the catwalk with the binoculars and she did the thing she had not let herself do in six years, which was to imagine Renata specifically, not the idea of her but the actual woman, sixty-one now, gray probably, she'd gone gray early like their mother, standing in the yard of the cabin she had saved with her own two hands, hearing the fire before she understood it, the way fire announced itself first as a sound, a freight-train sound people always said, a sound Della had heard once at a distance and never wanted to hear close. Renata in the yard. The garden she kept. The dog, if there was still a dog. The single old woman and the wall of fuel coming up the slope and the bad road her only way out and the very real possibility that the bad road was already cut, that the fire had crossed it below her, that she was standing in a yard that had become an island.

The image was unbearable and Della made herself bear it, because it was the thing the stillness had always let her avoid, the specific imagining, the standing-inside-it. She had spent her life at the right distance to keep from having to do that. And the cost of the right distance, she understood now, with the smoke leaning east and the radio dead behind her, was that you were never inside anything. Not the fire and not the family and not your own one life.

She went in and tore a page from the logbook and wrote, in block letters, in case it mattered later, in case there were a later in which someone needed to know what she had known and when: SMOKE PINTO CREEK LOWER FORKS AZ 271 OBSERVED 1415. WIND SW BUILDING. SMOKE BROWNING/EASTWARD 1430. RADIO DEAD. CELL FAILED. and the date, and she signed it, her full name, the way you sign a thing you mean. Then she packed the day pack that hung by the door—it was always packed, the fire shelter and the water and the headlamp and the first-aid kit and the leather gloves—and she filled the two extra bottles from the cistern tap and she stood for one second in the middle of the little glass room that had been her whole world for twenty-six summers and looked out at the country in every direction, the watching room, the still place, and she said goodbye to it without quite knowing that was what she was doing.

She would run the trail to the truck. She would drive the eight miles down. And then she would not turn onto the county road toward the Helms' and the phone. She would turn the other way, toward the Pinto Creek road, toward the drainage, up the bad road into the thing everyone with sense was about to drive away from, because she had spent her life being the one with sense and she was done, she was finished with it, she would rather be a fool in the smoke with her sister than a wise woman alone on a peak watching her sister's house make weather.

She knew it was wrong. She knew it the way she knew the wind speed and the fuel moisture and the lean of the column, knew it as data: a lone civilian driving into an active uncontained fire on a single-lane road with no radio and no one knowing her plan was exactly the thing that got people killed, exactly the additional casualty the protocols existed to prevent. She knew that if she could call it in, the dispatcher would order her to stay put and would be right to. She knew her grandfather, the still man, would have stayed in his tower and called the azimuth and let the system work, because the system was bigger than any one cabin and one old woman's blood. She knew all of it.

And she went down the stairs anyway, the metal treads ringing under her boots, down and down the spiral inside the tower's legs, out the door at the bottom into the full hammer of the heat, onto the foot trail that dropped off the back of the peak through the krummholz and then into the first real trees. She ran where she could and fast-walked where she couldn't and the pack rode her back and the country closed over her, the open all-seeing height of the tower giving way to the blind green tunnel of the trail, and it was strange and awful to lose the view, to go down into the place where you couldn't see, where the smoke was somewhere ahead and above and you only caught it in pieces through the canopy, a smear of brown, gone, then back. She had given up the one thing she was good at. She was a body on a trail now, a fifty-eight-year-old body with a bad left knee, and she was no use to anyone as eyes anymore, and the realization should have stopped her and didn't.

Halfway down, at the spring where the trail crossed the seep and the air went briefly cool and smelled of wet stone and mint, she stopped, not to rest but because something made her stop. She stood in the cool pocket and listened. And under the ordinary sounds—her own breath, a Clark's nutcracker complaining somewhere, the tick of the heat in the dry grass—she heard it. Far off. Low. A sound with no edges, a sound that was less a sound than a pressure, a sense of the air itself being used. The fire. She had heard it once before, years ago, and had never forgotten it, and here it was, the thing itself, the freight-train sound people always said, faint with distance but unmistakable, the sound of the lower forks beginning to burn in earnest.

She thought of turning back. The thought arrived fully formed and reasonable. She could go back up and stand in the tower and be the eyes after all, be useful, be safe, be the still woman her whole life had trained her to be. The tower was right there, a thousand vertical feet above her, the place she belonged. And Renata was down there in the sound, or was not, and Della would not know which until she went and saw, and going and seeing was the thing, finally, that she had been built not to do and was going to do anyway.

She drank from the spring, the water shocking and clean, the way it had been when she was a girl and her grandfather had cupped it to her mouth in his hands. She filled her cupped hands now and drank again and wet the back of her neck. Then she stood and shouldered the pack and started down again toward the sound, toward the truck and the bad road and the drainage, toward the cabin and whatever was or wasn't standing in its yard, leaving the height and the long view and the years of watching behind her on the peak, walking down out of her own stillness for the first time in her life, into the smoke, toward her sister, because the road was eight miles and the radio was dead and there was no one else, and because she had finally understood, too late to be wise and just in time to be a person, that being able to see a thing from very far away had never once been the same as being there.

The trail bent. The trees took the tower from her. She did not look back at it. She went down.
