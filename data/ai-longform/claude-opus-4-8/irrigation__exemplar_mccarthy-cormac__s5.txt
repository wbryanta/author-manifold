They came down through the orchard in the early light with the shovels over their shoulders and the dew still on the grass and the long rows of trees standing gray and twisted in the mist. The older one walked ahead and he did not speak. Behind him the younger carried a coil of pipe slung across his back and a sack of fittings that clinked with his stride and he stopped once to set the pipe down and shift it and then he came on.

The trees were old. Some of them had not borne in years and the dead limbs of them stood up out of the green in pale forks and the wormwood grew thick in the rows where nothing had been disced. Past the lower end of the orchard the land fell away to the river and the river was low and brown and moved without sound through the cottonwoods.

Yonder's where she's broke, the older one said. He pointed with his chin.

The younger one looked. Where the ground was dark.

I see it.

It's been seepin a week or more. Daddy never would of let it go like that.

Daddy's dead, the older one said.

They came to the place and set down their tools. The water had welled up out of the buried line and stood in a black sheet among the trees and the grass there was the deepest green in all that wasted ground and the mosquitoes rose off it in a thin smoke when they walked through. The older one toed the mud with his boot.

We'll have to dig her out, he said.

Reckon.

Get to where she's cracked.

They dug. The clay came up heavy and blue and they laid it back from the trench in long wet slabs and the water ran into the hole as fast as they could bail it and they bailed it with a coffee can passed between them and still it filled. The sun came up over the eastern rim of the valley and burned the mist off and the day grew hot. By midmorning they had stripped to their shirts and the sweat stood out dark on their backs and the younger one straightened and put his hand to the small of his spine and looked off down the rows.

How much you reckon Halford would give for it, he said.

The older one did not look up. He was down in the trench on his knees and he had his hands in the water feeling along the pipe.

I dont know, he said.

You talked to him.

I never talked to him about no price.

But you talked to him.

The older one came up out of the water with his hands black to the elbow. He's the one come to me, he said. I never went to him.

The younger one nodded. He was looking off down the orchard to where the river was.

It's a fair piece of land, he said.

It aint a fair piece of land. It's a wore out piece of land that aint made a dollar in six year. That's what it is.

It made a dollar when Daddy had it.

Daddy had help. Daddy had four men and water rights that wasnt cut and a market for what he growed. We aint got none of that. We got two of us and a busted line and a river the state's fixin to take half of and a bank that calls ever third week.

The younger one squatted at the edge of the trench. He took up the coffee can and bailed for a while without speaking and then he set it down.

I just hate to be the ones, he said.

The older one looked at him.

To let her go. After all that time. Granddaddy planted half these trees.

And he's dead too, the older one said. And it dont trouble him one way or the other what we do.

He found the crack with his fingers. A long split in the iron where the frost had got into it some winter past and swelled and let go and he traced it and then he sat back on his heels in the water and wiped his face with the back of his wrist and left a smear of mud across his cheek.

She's split the whole length of this joint, he said. We'll have to cut her out and put in new.

You got enough pipe?

I got enough.

They worked through the noon and did not stop to eat. The older one cut the line with a hacksaw and the blade caught and bound in the wet iron and he cursed it softly and worked it free and went on. The younger one held the new section while his brother threaded the fittings and they daubed the threads with dope from a can and turned them up tight with the pipe wrenches and the wrenches slipped in their greased hands and barked their knuckles and they bled into the water and the water carried it away pale and gone.

When they had the new joint set they sat on the bank of the trench with their boots hanging down in the hole and they passed a jar of water between them. The cottonwoods along the river shifted in a wind that did not reach them. Out over the trees a hawk hung and slid and hung again.

You ever think about what you'd do, the younger one said.

The older one drank and handed back the jar.

Do when.

If we sold. Where you'd go.

I'd not go nowhere. I'd stay right here in this county.

And do what.

The older one looked out at the orchard. The trees stood in their long rows and the heat shimmered up off the ground between them and the dead limbs hung gray against the blue.

Work for somebody, he said. Same as anybody.

You'd work another man's ground.

It's all somebody's ground. Wasnt for us bein born onto this it'd be another man's to us too. It aint holy just cause Granddaddy stuck a shovel in it.

The younger one shook his head. He took up a clod and broke it in his fingers and let it fall.

I dont see it that way, he said.

I know you dont.

A man ought to have somethin's his. Somethin he can stand on and say this here is mine. My daddy's and his daddy's. You sell that and what are you. You're just a man workin. You could be anybody.

Maybe I am anybody, the older one said. Maybe that's all there ever was. He capped the jar and set it in the grass. Maybe the bein somebody's the lie they sold us so we'd break our backs holdin onto dirt that dont want us. You ever think of that.

No.

Well think of it.

I dont want to think of it.

The older one stood up out of the trench. He stood looking down at his brother and the sun was behind him so that the younger one had to squint to see him and he was just a black shape against the white sky with the shovel handle rising past his shoulder.

You think Daddy laid easy at the end, he said. You think he went out peaceful lookin at his trees. He didnt. I set with him. You was off at the school and I set with him three months and he didnt talk about no trees. He talked about the note. He talked about what he owed and who to and how he never could get out from under it. That's what this land done for him. It eat him alive over thirty year and never give back what he put in it and at the end it had him layin in a bed countin debts in his head like a man tellin beads. That's your somethin's-his. That's what it come to.

The younger one looked down into the trench. The water had cleared some and stood dark and still over the new joint and you could see the joint down there pale and clean among the old black iron.

He never told me none of that, he said.

No. He wouldnt of. He was proud.

The younger one was quiet a while.

That dont mean we got to do the same, he said. We dont have to let it eat us. We could work it different. Lease some of it out. Put in somethin that dont want the water these trees want.

With what money.

There's ways.

There's no ways. The older one bent and took up the coffee can and pitched it across the trench to where his brother sat. Bail her out, he said. Let's see has she took.

The younger one bailed. The water came down slow and then faster as the line below began to draw and they watched the level fall and the new joint stood up out of it dripping and held. The older one knelt and ran his hand along it and felt for any weep at the threads and there was none.

She's tight, he said.

The younger one nodded.

They backfilled the trench in the long heat of the afternoon and tamped the clay down with the flat of their shovels and the water that they could not get out of the ground stood in the boot tracks and shone. The trees there had drunk what they could and would drink no more for a season and beyond them the dry rows ran off to the fence and the trees in those rows were the worst of all, half of them dead and the other half dying, and nobody to cut them and nobody to plant in their place.

They walked back up through the orchard when it was done with the shovels over their shoulders and the empty pipe coil and the sack of fittings and they did not speak for a long time. The shadows of the trees lay out long and blue across the rows. At the top of the orchard the older one stopped and looked back down the way they had come and the younger one stopped with him.

It's a pretty piece of ground, the older one said. I never said it wasnt pretty.

You said it was wore out.

It's both. A thing can be both.

The younger one looked down the rows to the river and the cottonwoods stood gold now in the low sun and the water ran among them brown and bright.

I'd like to keep it, he said. I aint got no good reason. I know all you said is true. I just would like to keep it.

The older one was quiet. A killdeer ran along the head of the orchard crying and stopped and ran again.

I know you would, he said.

You think I'm a fool.

No.

You do.

No, the older one said. I dont think you're a fool. I think you're young and you think a thing can be saved just by the wantin of it bad enough. And it cant. That's all I think.

What if it can.

It cant.

But what if it can.

The older one shifted the shovel on his shoulder. The light was going off the high ridges and the valley filled with a blue dusk that came up out of the ground itself it seemed and not down from the sky.

Then we'll be the two old fools that broke theirselves keepin a dead orchard alive, he said. And folks'll point at us. And we'll have nothin but the land and the land wont feed us. And one of us'll set with the other one dyin same as I set with Daddy and count out the debts in our head. That's what happens if it cant. You want to bet thirty years on if it can.

The younger one did not answer.

They went on up to the house. Halford's truck was not there. The older one had half thought it might be. The brother who would sell looked at the dooryard a long time and the brother who would keep went in and lit the lamp and through the window the older one watched him moving about the kitchen and after a while he set the shovel against the wall of the house and stood out in the dark with the orchard gone black below him and the first stars out over the ridge and he listened to the water somewhere down in the trees running through the line he had mended, running on through the dark to ground that would take it and give back nothing, or little, or maybe one more season, and he could not have said himself which it was he hoped for, and he stood a while longer and then he went in to his brother and the lamp.

They ate without much talk. The younger one had warmed beans and there was cornbread two days old and they ate at the table their father had eaten at and their grandfather before him and the older one looked at the grain of it under the lamp and at the dark places worn into it by the elbows of the dead and he did not say what he was thinking and neither did the younger one and when they were done the younger one took up the plates and washed them in the basin and the older one rolled a cigarette and smoked it sitting with his boots crossed at the ankle.

I'll call Halford tomorrow, the older one said. Tell him we aint decided.

The younger one stood at the basin with his back to him.

You believe we aint decided, he said.

I dont know what we are.

The younger one dried his hands on the rag and turned around.

If we sell I'll go along with it, he said. I want you to know that. I aint goin to fight you on it. You been carryin the worst of this since Daddy went and I aint blind to it. If you say sell, we'll sell.

The older one looked at the cigarette burning down between his fingers.

I know you would, he said.

But if there's any way, the younger one said. Any way at all you can see to keepin it. I'd take the hard part of that. I'd take more than my half of it. I mean that.

The older one did not say anything. The smoke went up off the cigarette straight and then bent in some draft from under the door and broke apart.

We dont have to decide it tonight, he said.

No.

It'll keep till the morning.

It'll keep, the younger one said.

He went on to bed. The older one sat up a while longer with the lamp turned low and he heard the bedsprings give in the back room and then it was quiet save for the small sounds the house made cooling in the night and somewhere far off a dog and farther off than that a truck shifting down on the county road and then gone. He thought about the line down in the orchard that they had mended and how it would hold now for a year maybe two and how there was thirty more joints just like it down there waiting on the frost and there was no mending all of them, no two men could do it, and he thought about his brother taking the hard part and how there was no part of it that was not hard and how the boy did not know that yet but would learn it, would learn it the way you learn the heft of a thing only by carrying it a long way, and he thought about his father in the bed counting his beads of debt and he wondered was there any difference at all between a man broke holding on and a man broke letting go, whether the one was a fool and the other a coward or whether they were the same man at different hours of the same long night, and he could not see it, and he put the cigarette out in the bean can and sat in the dark and did not move for a long while.

In the morning he was up before his brother and he went out into the gray light and walked down through the orchard. The dew was on the grass again the same as the day before and the trees stood the same gray and twisted shapes in the mist and the patched place in the line showed dark where the ground had not yet dried. He walked the rows. He went down past the dead trees and the dying ones and he came at last to the lower fence where the orchard gave out onto the broken ground above the river and he stood with his hands on the wire and looked out at the water moving brown in the cottonwoods.

His grandfather had planted the trees behind him and the trees were dying and there was no help for it that he could see. But the river would run whether they kept the land or sold it. The river did not know who held the paper on the ground it ran through. The river ran the same for Halford as for them and it had run the same before any of them and would run after, and there was a kind of comfort in that and a kind of terror too and he could not have separated the one from the other any more than he could separate the keeping from the letting go.

He heard his brother come down through the orchard behind him. He did not turn. The boy came up and stood at the fence beside him and put his own hands on the wire and they stood there together in the early light looking at the river.

You're up early, the younger one said.

Couldnt sleep.

The boy nodded.

I been thinkin, the older one said.

About what.

About maybe we work it one more year. Just the one. See can we get the line holdin and a crop off the trees that's still got it in them. And if it dont pay, we sell. End of the year. No argument.

The younger one looked at him.

You mean that, he said.

I dont know if I mean it. I'm sayin it. We'll find out do I mean it.

One year, the boy said.

One year. And it's likely a fool's year. I want you to know that goin in. It's likely we'll be sellin next June anyhow and out a year's work besides.

I'll take that chance.

I know you will. The older one took his hands off the wire. That's the trouble with you, he said. But there was no heat in it and the boy heard there was none.

They stood a while longer and then they turned and went back up through the orchard together with the mist burning off ahead of them and the sun just then coming over the eastern rim and laying its light down the long rows so that the dead trees and the living stood all of them gilded and aflame for one moment alike and indistinguishable, and the two brothers walked up into it toward the house and the day's work and the year they had decided on, the one of them believing and the other not believing and neither one saying which was which, and the water ran on below them through the mended line and through the rows toward the dry ground at the end of the orchard, going where it had always gone, asking nothing, promising nothing, only running, the way the river ran, the way the blood ran in them both, down toward whatever ground would have it.
