The price tags were the worst of it. Not the strangers, not the way they touched things, but the little orange stickers Cora had pressed onto everything that morning, the gummed ovals that said this is worth and this is worth, as if a thing could be told what it was worth by a woman with a roll of stickers and a felt-tip pen she'd bought at the drugstore on Main for ninety-nine cents.

Two dollars on the butter dish. Her mother had carried that butter dish to church suppers for forty years, the lid cracked once and mended with a glue that yellowed, and now it said two dollars in Cora's own slanting hand, and a man in a windbreaker the color of a robin's egg was turning it over, looking at the bottom the way they all did, looking for the name of the maker as if the name could tell him whether his two dollars was being spent wisely.

"It's just a dish," Cora said, and the man set it down and moved on, and she was sorry, because two dollars was two dollars, and because the dish wanted to go, she could feel it wanting to go, the way a child at the door wants out into weather its mother has forbidden.

The house was full of people she did not know and would never know. They came in twos and threes through the front door, which she had propped with the cast-iron rooster that had held it open her whole life, the rooster her mother had named Reverend because it crowed nothing and stood for nothing and yet presided. They came and they spread out into the rooms like water finding the low places, and they spoke in the hushed voices people used in the houses of the dead, voices pitched between reverence and appetite, and they ran their hands over the surfaces of her childhood and turned the orange stickers up to the light.

She sat at the card table by the door with a metal cash box and a roll of bills she'd gotten from the bank, ones and fives, because nobody at an estate sale ever had the right change, and she made change and she said thank you and she watched her mother's house leave itself by the armful.

A girl bought the cookie jar shaped like an owl. Cora had hidden coins in that owl's hollow head when she was nine, dimes mostly, the dimes her grandfather gave her for nothing, for being, and she had forgotten the dimes for so many years that they had become a kind of secret kept from herself, and now the girl was carrying the owl out into the October light with her thumbs hooked under its wings, and Cora wanted to call out wait, there might be money in there, but there was not, of course there was not, she had spent those dimes on something in 1971, on candy or comic books or the small necessary stupidities of a child, and the owl was empty and the girl paid four dollars and was gone.

She had not meant for it to be like this. When the lawyer in Columbus had said estate sale she had pictured something orderly, something with a roped-off quality, a man in a vest who knew the value of things calling out numbers while serious buyers raised paddles. But that was an auction, and that was for people whose mothers had owned things worth auctioning, and her mother had owned a butter dish worth two dollars and an owl worth four, and so it was an estate sale, which meant Cora standing at a card table while the town her mother had outlived came to pick the bones.

She knew some of them, after all. That was the thing she had not braced for. She had been gone thirty-one years, gone to Cleveland and then to Chicago and then back to Cleveland, gone into a marriage and out of it, gone into a life that her mother had visited four times and never understood, and she had thought thirty-one years was enough to make her a stranger here too. But Dorothy Pruitt came in at noon and stood in the doorway and said, "You're little Cora Mae," and it was not a question, and Cora was fifty-four years old and somebody's grandmother by marriage and a woman who managed forty accounts at a bank, and she was little Cora Mae standing at a card table selling her mother's things to Dorothy Pruitt, who had taught Sunday school in the church basement and smelled then and apparently still of peppermint and the particular sweetness of old women who had decided to be kind.

"I'm sorry about your mother," Dorothy said. "She was a fine woman. She held that choir together with both hands."

"Thank you," Cora said.

"I always wondered what became of you."

"I'm in Cleveland," Cora said, which was an answer to a question Dorothy had not exactly asked, and Dorothy nodded as if Cleveland explained everything, as if Cleveland were a country from which people did not return, and perhaps it was.

Dorothy bought a set of embroidered pillowcases for a dollar fifty and held them against her chest and did not look at the price the way the others did, and Cora understood that for Dorothy the pillowcases were not a bargain but a relic, a way to carry a piece of Geneva Wright's hands out of this house, the hands that had pulled thread through cloth in the lamplight, and Cora took the dollar fifty and was grateful, suddenly and enormously grateful, that some of the things were going to people who knew whose hands had touched them.

But most of them did not know. Most of them were the antiquers, the ones who drove the county roads on weekends with empty trunks and a hunger that had nothing to do with need, and they came into the house and saw not a life but an inventory. A woman in good shoes spent a long time in the kitchen with a man Cora took to be her husband, the two of them conferring in low tones over the Hoosier cabinet that had stood against the kitchen wall since before Cora was born, the cabinet with the flour sifter built into it that her mother had used to make the biscuits, the biscuits, and the woman in good shoes was saying it would clean up nice and the man was saying for what they're asking it ought to, and the orange sticker on the Hoosier cabinet said sixty-five dollars, which was the most expensive thing in the house, and Cora had put that number on it herself and now hated herself for the meanness of it, sixty-five dollars for the cabinet that had fed her, that had stood like a sentinel through every supper of her girlhood, sixty-five dollars and they thought it was too much.

"Would you take fifty?" the man called across the rooms, and Cora looked at him, and looked at the cabinet behind him, and the biscuits were in it still, she could smell them, the whole house smelled of them though no one had made a biscuit in that kitchen in two years, since her mother's hands had stopped knowing how, since the morning Geneva Wright had stood at the cabinet with the sifter in her hand and could not remember what it was for, and had called Cora in Cleveland to say, in a voice gone small and strange, I have the thing in my hand and I don't know its name, and that was the beginning, that was the first stone of the avalanche, the sifter her mother could not name.

"Fifty," Cora said, and the man looked pleased, and his wife looked pleased, and they did not know they had bought a thing that could not be bought, they had bought a wooden cabinet for fifty dollars and they would put it in a sunroom in Worthington and fill it with their own lives, and the biscuits would not be in it, the biscuits stayed, the biscuits were Cora's and she could not have sold them if she'd tried, she could not have priced them at all.

She made change for a fifty out of the metal box and there was not enough and she had to go into her own purse for it, and she gave the man two ones from her own wallet and did not say so, and he and his wife went out into the afternoon to bring their truck around, and the Hoosier cabinet sat against the wall in the slanting light and waited to be taken, the way the old wait, the way her mother had waited, patient and emptied, for the thing that would come and carry them off.

There was a rhythm to it after the first hour, and the rhythm was the thing that let her bear it. The door, the footsteps, the murmur, the sticker turned to the light, the question that was always the same question phrased forty ways—will you take, would you go, is this your best—and then the cash box, the ones and the fives, the thank you, the door again. She found she could perform it. She had performed harder things. She had performed her own composure at the funeral, in the church where Dorothy Pruitt's choir, what was left of it, had sung her mother into the ground, and she had performed a daughter's grief in a manner she could not entirely vouch for as genuine, because the grief had not arrived on schedule, the grief was late, the grief was a guest who had said it was coming and then did not come, and Cora had stood at the graveside performing its absence as if it were presence, and only here, only now, at the card table with the cash box, did she feel the first true edge of it, and the edge was this: that the house was being disassembled around her and she could not stop it and did not want to stop it and would grieve it forever.

Because she had wanted it gone. That was the shameful thing, the thing under the thing. When the lawyer had said the house had to be sold, that there was no other way to settle what her mother owed the nursing home, the two years of the home that had taken the house in the end as surely as if they'd lifted it brick by brick, Cora had felt, beneath the dismay, a low and secret loosening, a relief like the relief of setting down a weight you had carried so long you'd forgotten it was a weight. She would not have to come back here. After today she would not have to come back here ever, to this town that had watched her leave and would now watch her leave again, to this house where her mother had loved her in the only way she knew, which was a way that had felt, to a girl, like a fist closed around a bird.

A young man bought the record player, the console stereo as big as a coffin that had played Mahalia Jackson on Sunday mornings and, late at night when her mother thought she was asleep, played other things, played Dinah and played Billie, played the music her mother kept for herself, the music that belonged to the woman Geneva Wright had been before she was anyone's mother, before the church and the choir and the closed fist. Cora had crept to the top of the stairs more than once to listen to that other music and to watch, through the banister, her mother dance alone in the front room with her eyes shut and her arms around no one, swaying to Billie Holiday in the blue light of the television she'd turned on for company and turned down to nothing, dancing with a man who was not there or had never been there or had been there once and gone, and Cora had never asked, had never in fifty-four years asked her mother who that was, the man she danced with in the dark, and now she could not ask, and the record player was going out the door for twenty-five dollars on the shoulder of a young man who would put his own music in it.

"There's records in the bottom," Cora said. "In the cabinet part. You can have those too."

"Yeah?" The young man set the stereo down in the doorway and crouched and opened the little doors and pulled out the records in their soft worn sleeves, and he flipped through them with the quick assessing fingers of someone who knew records, and Cora watched him find the Billie Holiday, watched his eyebrows go up, watched him say, "These are nice. These are real nice. You sure?"

"I'm sure."

"You don't want to keep these?"

And there it was, the question, the only honest question anyone had asked her all day, you don't want to keep these, and the answer was that she did, she wanted to keep all of it, the records and the cabinet and the owl and the butter dish and the sifter she'd already wrapped in newspaper and packed in the one box she was taking, she wanted to keep the whole house and carry it to Cleveland on her back, and she wanted just as much to be free of it, to walk out the door at five o'clock with one box and never smell biscuits again, and both of these were true and neither would let the other be, and she said, "I have a record player. I'll keep a few. Take those."

The young man took them, the Billie too, all of it, and he was grateful in the offhand way of the young who do not yet know that everything is the last time, and he carried the music of her mother's secret self out into the street and put it in a hatchback, and Cora made change for a twenty and a five and said thank you and the door swung and Reverend the iron rooster held it.

In the lull that came around two o'clock, when the morning people had gone and the afternoon people had not yet come, Cora got up from the card table and walked through the rooms of the house while it was briefly hers and empty. The rooms had a stripped look already, gaps in them like missing teeth, the pale rectangles on the wallpaper where pictures had hung, the clean squares on the dusty floor where furniture had stood. She went into the front room where her mother had danced and it was nearly bare now, the stereo gone, the good chair gone, the side table gone, only the sofa left because no one wanted a sofa with the cushions gone soft and the floral gone gray, and Cora sat down on the sofa that no one wanted, in the room that no longer contained the thing that had made it the room, and she put her hands in her lap and she waited for the grief.

It did not come the way she expected. It came not as flood but as inventory, item by item, the way the buyers had come through the house. It came as the smell of the place, which was her mother's smell, Jergens lotion and coffee and the church-clothes smell of mothballs and beneath all that a smell that had no name, the smell of the body that had made her, gone now into the ground but lingering here in the fibers of the sofa no one wanted, and Cora put her face down into the gray softness of it and breathed in and there she was, there was Geneva, and Cora made a sound she did not plan to make and was glad no one was in the house to hear it.

She thought of the fist and the bird. She had used that, all her life, to explain herself to herself, to explain Cleveland and Chicago and the thirty-one years and the four visits—her mother's love had been a fist closed around a bird, too tight, too frightened, a love that held by holding down, and Cora had been the bird, and Cora had gotten free. She had told this to a therapist in Chicago who had nodded and written it down. She had told it to her husband, before he was not her husband, and he had nodded too. It was a good story and it was even true. But sitting in the empty front room with her face in the sofa, she understood the other half of it, the half she had never told anyone because she had not let herself know it: that the fist had been closed because the hand was afraid, that her mother had been a woman who danced alone in the dark with a man who wasn't there, that she had loved Cora the only way she could because the way she could was the only thing she had, and that holding too tight is what people do when they have already lost too much and cannot say so, when the losing has no name, when you stand in the kitchen with the thing in your hand and you cannot remember what it's for.

She did not cry, exactly. The tears came but they were not the point. The point was the inventory, the going-through, the way she sat in the emptying house and let it fill her one last time before the strangers came back to carry the rest of it away.

The door opened. She heard it, heard Reverend scrape, heard the footsteps, and she lifted her face from the sofa and wiped it with the flat of her hand and stood up and was a woman who managed forty accounts at a bank, was composed, was little Cora Mae no longer, and she went back to the card table and sat down and the afternoon people had begun.

They came steadily now until four. A church group, Dorothy must have sent them, bought up the kitchen things in a lot, the pots and the chipped plates and the glasses with the painted oranges that her mother had gotten free with gasoline in some decade Cora could not place, and they paid more than the stickers said because they knew whose kitchen it had been, and Cora let them, and was grateful again, and the gratitude was almost worse than the grief because it kept catching her unprepared.

The man and his wife came back for the Hoosier cabinet and brought a dolly and two sons to lift it, and Cora stood out of the way in the doorway to the kitchen and watched them tilt it and strap it and wheel it across the floor where it had stood her whole life, and she did not say the biscuits are mine, she did not say my mother could not remember its name, she said be careful of the floor, there's a board there that catches, and the man said thank you and they got it through the door without catching the board, and the cabinet went down the front walk in the four o'clock light tilted back on the dolly like an old woman in a wheelchair, like her mother going down the walk that last time, going to the home, looking back at the house with a face that did not know it was the last time, and Cora stood in the doorway and watched the cabinet go and did not weep, because she had wept already, in the front room, into the sofa no one wanted, and a person could only do that once a day.

By five the house was a husk. What was left was what no one would take: the sofa, a lamp with a cracked base, a box of her mother's church bulletins going back forty years, a coat in the hall closet, a calendar still turned to a month two years gone. Cora counted the cash box. Two hundred and eleven dollars, less the change she'd put in from her own purse, against a house she could not keep and a debt that would swallow whatever the house brought at the real sale, the sale of the house itself, next month, to people she would never meet who would put their own lives in these rooms and never know the biscuits, never know the dancing, never know that a woman named Geneva Wright had stood in the kitchen with a sifter in her hand and lost the names of things one by one until she lost the name of her own daughter and then, mercifully, lost everything else.

Cora put the money in an envelope and the envelope in her purse. She picked up the one box she was taking, the box with the sifter wrapped in newspaper, and the butter dish she had taken back off the table at the last minute, two dollars unpaid, and a few of the records, the Billie she had told the young man to take and then asked him, at the last, to leave her one, and he had, the worn one, the one she remembered most, and he had not asked her why.

She carried the box out onto the porch and set it down and went back in for the rooster. Reverend was heavy as a held breath. She lifted him and the door, with nothing to hold it, swung slowly shut behind her on the empty rooms, on the pale rectangles and the clean squares and the smell that was her mother and was, already, beginning to fade. She did not lock it. There was nothing in there worth locking up, and anyway the house was not hers to lock, was no one's, was waiting now, patient and emptied, for the thing that would come and carry it off.

She put the rooster in the trunk with the box and got in the car. The light was going gold and long across the small town, across the church and the drugstore and the road out to Cleveland, and Cora Mae Wright sat for a moment with her hands on the wheel and did not start the engine, listening, in the silence the strangers had left, for biscuits.
