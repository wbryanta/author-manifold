The strait was black and the ferry moved through it like a thought a man cannot quite finish.

He had taken the late crossing because the woman at the office in town had said it was the only one until morning, and morning seemed a thing he could not wait for, though he could not have said why. There was no one to meet on the other side. The body would wait. The dead are patient in their drawers of steel, lying under the small white tags the living attach to the wrists of those they cannot name. And yet he had hurried. He had run the last hundred yards to the dock with his coat unbuttoned and the wind getting into him, and only when he was aboard and the gangway pulled up behind him did he understand that there had been no need.

His name was Elias. He was fifty-one years old. He had a brother, perhaps, in a building near the water on the far shore, and perhaps the brother was only sleeping there in the cold the way the dead sleep, which is not like sleeping at all.

He found a seat by a window though there was nothing to see. The glass gave him back his own face, lit yellow by the cabin lights, and behind the face the dark, and now and then the white seam of a wave the ship turned up and discarded. The other passengers were few. A woman with a child asleep across her knees. Two men in work clothes who did not speak. A young couple who held hands as though the holding were a task assigned to them, something they must complete before the crossing ended. He watched them and then he did not watch them.

He had not seen Daniel in nineteen years.

That was the figure he arrived at, sitting there, counting backward through the houses he had lived in, the cities, the two marriages, the one that took and the one that did not. Nineteen years. It was the kind of number you could not feel. You could feel a year. You could feel the gap of a single winter. But nineteen was an abstraction, a length of rope coiled in the dark of him, and he could not tell, pulling on it, where it was tied.

The last time had been in their mother's kitchen, after the funeral, with the relatives gone and the casseroles cooling under their foil. Daniel had said a thing. Elias had said a thing. Neither of them, now, could have repeated the words, but the words had done their work, the way a key does its work and is forgotten while the door swings. Daniel had gone out to the car. Elias had stood at the window with a dish towel in his hands, watching, and he had thought, *He will turn around.* And Daniel had not turned around. He had backed the car down the drive and the brake lights had flared once at the road like two coals breathed upon, and then he was gone into the country of the gone.

There is a photograph Elias carries, not in his wallet but in the deeper place. The two of them on a dock in summer, boys, brown as nuts, Daniel a head taller and laughing at something off to the left, out of frame, so that for all the years Elias has held the picture he has never known what his brother was laughing at. Their father took it. Their father is also dead. The dock is gone, the lake drained for a road. Only the laughing remains, aimed forever at a thing outside the picture's edge.

A man came down the aisle selling coffee from a steel urn on a cart, and Elias bought a cup he did not want, for the warmth of it in his hands, and the man said something about the weather and Elias agreed though he had not heard. The coffee tasted of the urn. He drank it looking at the black window and his own face floating there, and he thought how a man's face at fifty is a thing assembled out of everyone who has touched it, the slap and the kiss, the wind off a hundred waters, and somewhere under all of it the boy on the dock, brown as a nut, looking up at his brother to see what the brother was laughing at, wanting in on the joke, always wanting in.

The telephone call had come on a Tuesday.

A man's voice, official, careful in the way of those who must say such things often. A body had been found. The strait, the cold months, the currents that bring things in along the eastern rocks below the lighthouse. There were no documents. There was a name written on a slip of paper in a pocket, water-ruined, and the name, when they had teased it apart letter by letter under their lamps, had resolved into something close to Elias's own. The surname, anyway. And an address that was no longer his, a house he had sold a decade before, so that the dead man had been carrying him as he was a decade ago, an old self folded in a stranger's pocket, going into the water.

*We are trying to locate the next of kin,* the voice said. *We wonder if you might be able to assist us.*

He had said yes before he understood what he was assisting. He had a brother. He had not heard from his brother in—and here, on the telephone, he had said *some years*, unwilling to count, the number too naked a thing to hand to a stranger. The brother had lived, last he knew, in the north, in one of the fishing towns, where a man could disappear into the work and the drink and the long darkness of the winters and not be missed by anyone who had a telephone. It was possible. The voice agreed it was possible. The voice was sorry. The voice gave him the name of the office and the hours and said that of course there was no certainty, that he should prepare himself for the likelihood that it was someone else's grief entirely, a stranger's brother, and that he was free to come or not to come as he saw fit.

He had hung up the telephone and stood in his kitchen, which was clean and quiet and had in it nothing that was not his own, and he had felt the old rope tighten in the dark of him, tied at last, he thought, to this.

There is a thing that happens to brothers. Elias had thought about it on the train down to the coast and he thought about it now on the water. When they are small they are one creature with two bodies. They share the bath, the bunk bed, the language no one else can speak. Daniel had taught him to swim by the simple method of rowing them both out and putting him over the side, and Elias remembered the green underwater silence and then his brother's hand, when he came up, fisting in his hair and hauling him to the gunwale, and both of them laughing, the terror already converted into something they could keep. *You won't drown,* Daniel had said. *I won't let you.* That was the contract. That was the whole of it, written in a language of hands.

And then they are men, and the one creature tears itself in two, and each half goes off to be a person, and the tearing is so slow that neither notices it as tearing. It feels like growth. It feels like becoming oneself. Only later, in a black window on a ferry, does a man see that what he called becoming himself was also a long unbecoming, a setting-down of the other, finger by finger, until the hand that would not let him drown was just a hand, attached to a man in a car backing down a drive, the brake lights flaring once at the road.

He must have slept. When he opened his eyes the cabin was dimmer, someone having killed half the lights, and the woman with the child was gone, and the young couple slept against each other with their mouths open, no longer holding hands, the task abandoned or completed. His coffee had gone cold on the sill. The ship had taken on a deeper motion, a long slow roll that meant they were in the middle of the strait now, past the shelter of the headlands, out where the two dark masses of land released their grip on the water and let it be itself.

He went out onto the deck.

The cold was immediate and total, a hand laid flat against his whole body, and the wind tore the breath sideways out of his mouth. He gripped the rail. The sea below was not visible so much as sensed, a great breathing blackness, and the foam where the hull cut it glowed faintly, the only light, a green-white phosphor that rose and curled and went out. He thought of his brother in the water. If it was his brother. He thought of the cold going into a man, the way it had gone into him as a boy over the side of the boat, the green silence, except this time no hand. This time the hair floating loose and no fist to take it. This time the contract void, both signatories scattered, one to a drawer of steel and one to a rail in the wind with cold coffee behind him on a sill.

*You won't drown. I won't let you.*

But you let me, he thought, and was surprised at the bitterness, surprised that it was there at all after nineteen years, surprised it had not frozen with the rest. Or I let you. One of us let the other. We took our hands away.

There had been letters, early on. After the kitchen. Daniel had written once, a year or two after, a short letter from a town Elias could not now name, and the letter had been an offering, he understood that now, though at the time he had read it as an accusation. *I think of how it was with us,* Daniel had written, *and I do not understand how we came to this.* And Elias, who was in the middle of the marriage that did not take, who was raw and proud and certain that the fault, whatever it was, lay across the water in his brother's hand, had not answered. He had meant to answer. He had set the letter on the corner of his desk where he would see it, and he had seen it for a month, and then one day he had not seen it because it was no longer there, and he never learned what became of it, whether he had thrown it out or filed it or lost it in a move, and the not-answering had hardened over the months into a thing that could no longer be answered, a silence with a skin on it.

A second letter never came. Why would it. You offer your hand once across the water. If it is not taken you draw it back. That is only sense. That is only a man protecting what is left of himself. Elias understood it. He would have done the same. He had done the same.

The deck pitched and he held the rail with both hands and felt the spray come up cold and fine over the back of them, and he thought: I am going to look at a face and say a word. Yes or no. Him or not him. The whole of nineteen years narrowed to a syllable he would speak to a man in a white coat in a cold room, and the man would write it down, and that would be the end, the absolute end, of the one creature with two bodies that had shared the bath and the bunk and the language no one else could speak.

And he found, gripping the rail, that he did not know which answer he wanted.

If it was Daniel, then Daniel was dead, and the nineteen years could never be unspent, the letter never answered, the hand never taken back across the water. The door that the forgotten words had swung shut would be locked now from the far side, the key dropped in the strait. But if it was not Daniel—if it was some other man, some stranger carrying Elias's old name and old address in his pocket like a curse he'd picked up off the ground—then Daniel was alive somewhere, in the north, in the dark of the winters, and the door was not locked, and a man could still cross water, could still write the second letter that the first had earned, could still put out the hand.

But he would not. He knew that he would not. The not-answering had a skin on it now too thick to break. If the body was a stranger's he would feel relief, and ride the morning ferry home, and the relief would be a lie he told himself, because the truth was that he wanted it to be Daniel. He wanted the door locked. He wanted the choice taken from him forever, so that he need never sit again with the rope in the dark of him and feel that it led somewhere, that something at the other end might still be pulled toward him, might still answer, might still, against all the silence, turn around.

This was the thing he had crossed the strait to learn, and he had not known he carried it until the wind tore it loose. That he would rather his brother be dead than be a man he had failed and might fail again. That the grief he was preparing was easier than the hope. That he had been, all these years, not mourning a lost brother but defending himself against a living one, against the labor of love, which does not end, which asks and asks.

He was ashamed. The shame was warmer than the cold and he stood in it.

A figure came out onto the deck behind him, one of the workmen, and stood a little way off lighting a cigarette with difficulty in the wind, hands cupped, the small flame jumping and catching and the man's face leaping out of the dark and falling back. He did not speak to Elias and Elias was grateful. They stood, the two of them, strangers at the rail in the middle of the black strait, each with his own freight, the cigarette's coal brightening and dimming with the man's breath like the brake lights of a car going down a drive into the country of the gone.

Elias thought of his mother's kitchen. Of the dish towel in his hands and the words he could not now repeat. He had stood at that window certain his brother would turn around, and he saw now that his certainty had been a kind of laziness, a faith that the love between them was a standing structure that did not need to be tended, that would be there when he troubled to look for it, the way the dock had been there every summer until the summer it was not, the lake drained for a road. You assume the thing will hold. You set the letter on the corner of the desk. You count on tomorrow to do the work of today, and the tomorrows accumulate into nineteen years, into a number a man cannot feel, into a body in a drawer that may or may not wear your brother's face.

The workman finished his cigarette and flicked it over the rail, where the wind took the coal and scattered it, and he nodded once at Elias, a nod that acknowledged nothing but their shared exposure, two men out in it together, and then he went back inside and the door sucked shut behind him and Elias was alone again with the breathing dark.

He stayed out until the cold had gone all the way through him, until he could no longer feel his hands on the rail and had to look at them to know they were still gripping. Then he went in. The cabin's warmth was a violence after the deck. He sat in his seat and the cold came out of him slowly in long shudders, and across the aisle the young couple slept on, and he watched them and thought how they did not yet know what they would do to each other, how no one ever knows, how love is the only contract we sign without reading and then spend our lives discovering the terms.

Toward what must have been morning, though the window gave no sign of it, the engine note changed. A deepening, a slackening. He felt the ship begin the long curve that meant land, the navigator somewhere above him bending the great vessel toward a harbor light he could not see. The other passengers stirred. The woman with the child had reappeared from wherever she had gone, and the child was awake now and crying thinly, and she rocked it and murmured to it the way you murmur to the very young and the very ill, sounds without words, the old language, the one no one else can speak. The men in work clothes stood and stretched and gathered their bags. The young couple woke and looked at each other with the particular blankness of those returning from sleep to a thing they had forgotten they were inside of, and then remembered, and one of them smiled, and the hand-holding resumed.

Elias did not move. He sat looking at the window where the black was thinning now, not to light exactly but to a charcoal gray, a suggestion that the world had not ended in the night, that there was still a far shore and it was coming. He thought he could make out, low down, a scatter of lights. The town. The office with its hours. The cold room and the man in the white coat and the drawers of steel. The face he would look at, and the word he would say.

He took out the photograph then. Not from his wallet but from the deeper place, which is to say he closed his eyes and brought it up. The two of them on the dock in summer, brown as nuts, Daniel a head taller and laughing at something off to the left, out of frame, and for the first time in all the years he had carried it Elias tried, deliberately, to see what his brother was laughing at. To turn his own head, in the picture, and follow the line of that laughter to its source. And of course he could not. The frame held. The thing outside it stayed outside. But in the trying he felt something give a little, some seized and rusted part of him turn a quarter turn, and he understood that this was what he had to do. Not learn the joke. Only keep trying to turn his head. Only refuse, finally, to accept the edge of the frame as the edge of the world.

If it was Daniel in the cold room he would stand over the face that was no longer laughing at anything and he would say yes, that is my brother, and he would let the door lock, and he would carry the lock the rest of his life, and the carrying would be his and just. And if it was not Daniel—if it was a stranger with his old name in his pocket—then he would not ride the morning ferry home and call his relief by another word. He would go north. He swore it now, in the warm cabin, watching the gray come up over the strait, knowing how easily a vow made on water dissolves on land. He would go north into the fishing towns and the long winters and he would find the building near the water and he would knock on the door, and whatever swung open or did not, he would have crossed. He would have put the hand out across the water. He would have answered the letter at last, nineteen years late, the only way that was left to him, with his body in a doorway in the cold.

The ship came in. He felt it touch, the long shudder of the hull against the fenders, the engines reversing, the whole vessel groaning and settling like a creature lying down. Through the window the town assembled itself out of the gray: the dock, the slick black road, the low buildings with their few lights, and above them, on a far point, faint now in the coming day, the lighthouse, turning, turning, throwing its arm of light out over the eastern rocks where the currents bring things in.

He stood. He buttoned his coat. He joined the slow line of the living moving toward the gangway, toward the cold and the morning and the answer, whatever it would be, and he found that he was no longer hurrying, and that this, the not-hurrying, was the first thing he had done right in a very long time.

The dead are patient. He would come to them slowly, as a man comes to his brother. He would cross the last small distance on his own two feet.
