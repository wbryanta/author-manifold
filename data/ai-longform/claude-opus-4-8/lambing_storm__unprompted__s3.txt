The snow started before dark, fine as flour at first, the kind that looked harmless against the kitchen window. Maren had been watching it sideways while she rinsed the supper dishes, telling herself it would pass east of the valley the way the forecast promised. By the time she'd dried her hands and gone out to the porch the air had thickened, and the flakes had grown teeth, and the wind off the ridge was already beginning the long low complaint it kept up all night.

"It's coming," she said when she came back in.

Eli was at the table with his physics homework spread out, not doing it. He'd been not doing it for an hour. He looked up at her and she saw Tomas in his face, the way the bones were starting to come forward under the boy-softness, the jaw squaring. It still caught her sometimes, an ambush in her own kitchen.

"How many we got left?" he asked.

"Eleven that I'd call close. Maybe more. The two black-faces are bagged up."

He pushed the textbook away as if it had given him permission. "I'll get my boots."

She wanted to tell him to stay in, finish the homework, sleep. He had a test Thursday and she didn't know yet whether there would be school, and she had managed lambings alone before, sort of, in the years before he was old enough, when Tomas was alive to share them. But that was the thing the word *before* did now. It walled off a country she couldn't get back to. She had eleven ewes and a storm coming and one pair of hands besides her own, and the hands belonged to her son, and the truth was she needed them.

"Dress warm," she said. "Two pairs of socks."

They went out together into the blue dusk with the wind shoving at their backs, the dogs slinking low at their heels then peeling off toward the barn. The lambing shed sat down the slope from the house, a long timber building Tomas's father had raised, banked into the hill on its north side so the earth took the worst of the cold. Maren had strung lights along the rafters years ago, bare bulbs in wire cages, and when she threw the switch inside they came on yellow and steady and the ewes turned their heads in the bedding, their eyes catching the light, green and unbothered.

The warmth hit her first. Forty animals breathing in a closed space made their own weather, and the smell of them — lanolin and ammonia and the sweet rot of spent hay — was the smell of every spring she had lived on this ground. She stood a moment in it. Eli went down the alley checking water, his shadow long and then short under each bulb.

"This one's started," he called from the far pen.

She came down to look. It was the older Suffolk ewe, the one with the torn ear, restless, pawing at the straw and turning to nose her own flank. Maren watched the contractions ripple along her, counted the space between them.

"She's got time," she said. "First waters not even broke. Let her work."

They settled in to the waiting that was most of what lambing was. Eli forked fresh straw into the empty jugs, the small pens where they'd put a ewe and her newborns to bond. Maren mixed a bucket of warm molasses water for the ones who'd already lambed, carrying it pen to pen, and the ewes drank with that greedy ridiculous urgency, foam on their muzzles. Outside the wind found a loose sheet of tin somewhere on the roof and began to work it, a rhythmic banging that would go on, she knew, until one of them climbed up in the morning to nail it down, or until it tore free entirely.

It was full dark and the snow horizontal across the one window when the power went.

The bulbs didn't flicker. They simply stopped, all at once, and the dark came down so complete that Maren heard Eli's breath catch. The wind seemed louder instantly, as though the light had been holding it off.

"Mum?"

"It's all right. Stand still a second, let your eyes come."

She had known it might. She'd known it the way you know weather in your joints. There were two storm lanterns on the shelf by the door and a box of the long matches and she walked to them by memory, hand out, finding the cold shelf edge, the curve of the glass chimney. Her fingers were steady. She was almost proud of how steady they were. The match flared and the small flame steadied and grew and threw her own enormous shadow up across the rafters, and the ewes had gone quiet at the change, only their eyes moving.

"Take this one." She handed Eli the second lantern lit. "Go check the Suffolk."

He went, the light swinging, and she heard him say, low, "Oh," and then, "Mum, there's feet."

She came down the alley. In the lantern light the ewe was down on her side now, straining, and at her tail two small hooves had appeared inside the glistening sac, pointed down, the way they should be. Maren knelt in the straw and waited through a contraction and saw the nose come, tucked neat between the legs, and she let her breath out.

"That's a good one," she said. "That's how it's meant to look. You watch. She'll do this herself."

And the ewe did, heaving, and the lamb came in a rush of fluid onto the straw, and Maren cleared its nose with two fingers and rubbed it hard with a fistful of hay until it shook its head and sneezed and bawled, that thin first complaint, and the ewe lurched around to answer it, nickering low in her throat, already licking.

Eli was grinning. She could see it in the lantern light, the whole of him lit up. "Look at it go."

"There'll be another. She's big." Maren felt along the ewe's flank. "Yep. Give her a minute."

The second came breech, which she'd half expected from the size of the ewe, and that one she did have to help, easing it as the ewe pushed, hooves first and pointing up, the wrong feet but coming all the same, and it came slick and limp and didn't breathe.

"Mum—"

"Hay. Quick."

She swung the lamb by its back legs once, twice, to clear the fluid, the way Tomas had taught her with his big hands around her smaller ones the first year, *like this, not too hard, you're a milkmaid not a slinger*, and then she had it down in the straw and was rubbing, rough, at the chest, at the ribs, pressing and releasing, her thumb in the small ribs, and Eli was kneeling across from her not breathing himself, and the lamb gave a cough, a real cough, and water ran from its nose and it pulled a breath that shuddered the whole small body.

"There," she said. "There you are."

She didn't realize she'd said it the way Tomas used to until she heard it in the air. *There you are.* He'd said it to every lamb and to Eli when he was born, she'd been told, though she had no memory of that hour, only the after. She set the second lamb up by the first where the ewe could reach, and the ewe accepted them both, and they got the two of them into the jug with the molasses water and a flake of good hay, and stood back in the lantern light watching the lambs find their feet, knees buckling, that drunken first stagger toward the udder.

"That's two," Eli said. His voice had something new in it.

"That's two." She looked at him. "Well done."

He shrugged it off but she'd seen it land.

They moved the lanterns to hang from two nails on opposite posts so the light was spread, and the shed became a place of brown shadow and gold pools, the cold pressing in from the corners. Without the heaters the temperature was already dropping. Maren could feel it. The newborns were the danger now — a wet lamb in this cold could chill down and die in an hour, faster, and a dead lamb in a cold barn was a thing that started small and ended in a ewe screaming for what she'd lost, and a season's number short, and the bank.

"We'll need the box," she said. "Bring it down from the house. And the lamp from my room, the battery one. And the kettle — fill the big flask while there's hot in the tank. And Eli. Put more wood in the stove before you come back or the pipes'll go."

He nodded, all business now, and went out into it. She watched the lantern of him climb the slope and dissolve into the white. The wind took the door from her hand when she shut it behind him and she had to lean her whole weight to latch it.

Alone in the shed she did her round again, slower, touching each ewe she passed. The blackfaces in the corner pen were close but not yet, the older one chewing cud with the steady working jaw of an animal in no hurry. A young ewe Maren didn't trust, a first-timer, paced the fence line and would not settle. Maren stood with her a while, talking the nonsense you talked, *easy now, easy, you don't know what's coming but I do, easy.*

She thought about the year Tomas had pulled a lamb that wouldn't come, both of them up to their shoulders in it, the ewe failing, and how he'd worked an hour and saved the lamb and lost the ewe and had sat afterward in the straw with the orphan inside his coat and wept, quietly, the only time. She'd loved him so fiercely in that moment she'd had to leave the shed. He felt every loss like a personal failing, like the animals were his to keep alive by sheer want. *They're not yours to save,* she'd told him once, sharp, scared for him. *You just give them the chance.* He'd looked at her like she'd said something in a language he refused to learn.

The heart had taken him in October, in the high field, fixing fence. He was fifty-one. He'd had no warning and neither had she, and the doctor said it was the kind of thing, the kind of thing, as though *the kind of thing* were an explanation and not just the shape of the hole. She had found him because the dog came back without him. She did not let herself go to that field in her mind. She had built a wall there too.

The door banged and Eli came in white to the knees, the box under his arm, the flask, the camping lamp which he flicked on and which threw a hard white circle on the floor, ugly and bright after the lanterns.

"Snowing like the end of the world," he said. "Can't see the house from the gate."

"Stove?"

"Loaded it up. It's roaring." He set the box down. It was the lambing box, the warming box Tomas had built, plywood lined with an old wool blanket and a lid that lifted, made to take a chilled lamb and a hot water bottle and bring it back from the edge. They hadn't used it yet this season. It still had Tomas's pencil on the lid where he'd marked measurements he never sanded off.

Eli ran his thumb across the pencil line. He didn't say anything. Neither did she.

The young ewe lambed badly. Of course she did — first-timers and bad weather, the lambs seemed to know. Maren had her down in the straw and a single big lamb stuck at the shoulders, the head delivered and swelling already, the tongue going thick. She had her hand in to the wrist, feeling for the leg that was back, the elbow locked against the pelvis, and she could not get purchase, and the ewe had stopped pushing, exhausted or giving up, and time was the thing now, time was the whole thing.

"I need you to hold her head," she told Eli. "Sit there. Knee against her spine. Don't let her thrash."

He got down and did it, and she felt him steady the animal, and she went back in, eyes shut, working by the feel of it the way Tomas had taught her, *see with your fingers, the eyes lie in there anyway*, and she found the trapped leg and cupped the small hoof in her palm to keep it from tearing the womb and walked it forward, forward, the ewe groaning, and then the leg came up alongside the other and the lamb shifted and she said "Now, push, come on, push," to the ewe as if it could understand, and the ewe found one last contraction from somewhere and Maren pulled with it, down and out in the arc of it, and the lamb slid free all at once, huge and blue and still.

It was not breathing. Its head was swollen from the long press, the tongue out and dark.

"Box," she said. "Hot bottle. Move."

She cleared its mouth, swung it, rubbed it, did the chest, and it would not. She put her own mouth over its cold nose and mouth and breathed, two short puffs the way she'd seen the vet do once, and rubbed, and breathed, and Eli was beside her with the bottle wrapped in the blanket and his face was the face of a child again, all the new manhood gone out of it, and he said "Is it—" and she said "Not yet, not yet," not to him.

She breathed for it again. She rubbed until her arm ached. The lamb's eye stayed flat and unlit. She knew the moment was passing, had perhaps passed already, knew it the way she'd known the snow, in her body, and still she breathed and rubbed because the other thing, the stopping, was a country she would not enter in front of her son, not tonight, not in this dark with the wind screaming and the lanterns guttering and Tomas's pencil line on the box.

"Come on," she said. "There you are. Come on. There you are."

And the lamb coughed.

It coughed and the flat eye filmed and rolled and woke and it pulled a wet terrible wonderful breath and another, and she got it into the box on the hot bottle with the blanket over and only its head out, and it lay there breathing, just breathing, the swollen head, and the ewe was up now and calling and Maren let her smell it through the slats but kept the lamb in the warmth a while longer.

She sat back on her heels. Her hands were shaking now. Now they shook.

Eli was looking at her. The hard white lamp lit half his face.

"I thought it was dead," he said.

"It nearly was."

"You didn't stop."

"No."

He was quiet. The wind. The tin banging. The new lamb's breath in the box, fast and shallow but there.

"Did he teach you that," Eli said. "The breathing."

"No. The vet, years ago. Your dad taught me most of the rest." She wiped her face with the back of her wrist, the cleaner part. "He could pull a lamb in his sleep. He had hands like — he could feel things in there I couldn't. He'd say it was like reading. Said I was getting good at it." She hadn't meant to say so much. The lantern light made it possible somehow, the dark and the work and the boy across from her with his father coming up through his face.

"You are good at it," Eli said. "That was — I couldn't have done that."

"You held her. I couldn't have done it without somebody on her head. Your dad and I, that's how we did the hard ones. One on the head, one on the business end." She almost smiled. "I never thought I'd be glad it was you on the head end and not him."

It came out wrong, or right, she couldn't tell. Eli looked down. His jaw worked.

"I miss him," he said, to the straw, in the voice of someone confessing a crime.

"I know."

"It's stupid. It's been — it's months. I thought it would — I keep thinking I'll get a day where I don't, and then I don't get it."

Maren reached across the lamb in its box and put her hand on the back of her son's neck, the way she had when he was small, when he was sick, the gesture older than words between them. His neck was hot and damp under her cold hand.

"You won't get that day," she said. "I have to tell you the truth because nobody told me. You don't get the day where you stop. You get days where it's quieter. You get a day where you're forking straw and you think about him and it doesn't take your legs out from under you. That's the most of it. That's what gets better. Not the missing. The missing stays. You just get bigger around it."

He turned his face away but didn't pull from her hand. She felt him breathing, ragged, getting hold of himself, the way the lamb was getting hold of itself in the box between them, and she thought, not for the first time, that the whole of living was just this, the long stubborn refusal, the breathing for the thing that wouldn't breathe until it did, the rubbing of the cold chest in the dark with no guarantee.

"The blackfaces are going to go tonight," she said after a while, to give him somewhere to put himself. "Both of them, probably, the way they're bagged. We should eat something. There's bread in my coat and the flask. We'll need it."

They sat against the straw bales and ate the bread and drank the sweet tea from the flask cap passed between them, and the lamb in the box breathed, and slowly the swelling in its head went down, and after an hour Maren lifted it out and dried it properly and stood it up and it stood, swaying, on legs it didn't trust, and she steered it to the young ewe's udder and held the ewe still and got the lamb latched, and the ewe stood for it, and that was the thing decided then, the ewe would keep it, they would not have to graft it or bottle it, and Maren felt a tiredness go through her that was almost like peace.

The first blackface lambed at what she guessed was two in the morning, clean, twins, no trouble, as though to apologize for the young one. Eli did most of it himself, kneeling in the straw, clearing the noses, rubbing them up, and Maren stood back with the lantern and let him, only saying a word here and there, *good, that's good, get him to the teat now while he's keen,* and watched her son's hands move, not Tomas's hands, his own, but learning the same language, reading the same book.

The second blackface waited till near dawn. By then the wind had begun to ease — not stop, but ease, the long screaming pulling back to a moan, and the banging tin slowed and finally caught on something and held. Through the small window the white had stopped flying sideways and begun to fall, just fall, the way snow was supposed to, fat and slow and almost gentle, and the dark outside was going gray.

The last lamb was an easy single and it came as the first real light was in the window, and Maren delivered it and set it down and it shook its head and bawled before she'd even reached for the hay, and she laughed, surprised out of herself, exhausted, her back a single bar of pain, her hands cracked and aching.

"That's all of them," she said. "That's the lot."

Eli was sitting against the bales, gray-faced with tiredness, a smear of birth fluid dried on his cheek that he hadn't noticed and she didn't tell him about, because it was Tomas's badge, that smear, the thing he'd come up to the house wearing every lambing dawn for twenty years.

"How many?" Eli said.

She counted them in her head, pen by pen, the night laid out behind her like a country she'd crossed. "Nine ewes lambed. Fourteen lambs. Lost none." She heard her own voice catch on it. "We didn't lose one."

They turned off the camping lamp first, then went round and blew out the lanterns one at a time, and the gray dawn light came up in the shed soft and even and enough to see by, and the new lambs lay curled against their mothers in the straw, and the steam rose off the warm bodies of them into the cold, and the whole shed breathed.

Maren stood in the open doorway looking up at the house through the falling snow, the dark windows, the stove smoke going straight up now in the stillness, and she thought she should be more tired than she was, and she thought she should feel the hole more than she did, and what she felt instead, against everything, was the morning, plain and cold and arriving, the way it did, the way it would, whether she was ready for it or not.

"Come on," she said to her son. "I'll make eggs. Then we sleep."

And they went up through the snow toward the house together, the dogs falling in behind, and behind them the lambs kept breathing in the half-dark, all fourteen, every one.
