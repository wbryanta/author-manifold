Anil watched her go, and then she knelt where the woman had been. The earth was warm there, holding the shape of her body the way water holds the memory of a stone dropped through it. She did not look up at the others. She picked up her brush and began again, the small careful strokes that revealed bone the way light reveals a face emerging from shadow. The two men in the grave had been bound at the wrists. She could see it in the way the wrist bones lay, the radius and ulna of one arm crossed over the other, the posture forced and unnatural even in death. She made a note. She always made the notes, even when her hand did not want to write the words, because the notes were the only thing that could not be argued away later.

The heat pressed down. Somewhere a dog barked twice and then stopped, as if it had thought better of it. The woman stood at the edge of the field now, near the others of her village who had come, and she did not weep, and that was the thing Anil could not forget afterward, the not-weeping, the dry composure of someone who had already given everything she had to give and had only this left, the watching.

That was Guatemala. That was four years before, or five. The years had begun to fold into one another the way the dead did, the way one grave resembled another grave until the small particular detail surfaced—the petal, the footprint, the bound wrists, the cheap watch still ticking on a skeletal wrist when they uncovered it in the Congo, the watch that had outlived the man and gone on counting minutes in the dark. These were the things that kept the work from becoming only work. These were the things that kept her, some nights, from sleeping.

Now she was returning to the country she had been born in, and she found she could not yet think of it as returning. She had left at eighteen and had not been back in fifteen years, and in that time she had become a person her family would not have recognized, a person she herself sometimes did not recognize. She had a forensic specialization and an American education and an English accent that had worn smooth over the harder syllables of her childhood. She had changed her name, which was a thing nobody in the family ever spoke of directly, though it had cost her, the buying of it from her brother, the trade. She had wanted the name Anil and her brother had owned it, an unused second name, and she had bought it from him when she was twelve, with savings and with two weeks of doing his garden chores and finally with a Japanese pen she loved. After that she would answer to nothing else. She had erased the rest. She had made herself, letter by letter, the way she now uncovered the dead, bone by bone, until the shape was undeniable.

The plane came down over the island at night and she pressed her face to the window and saw the lights scattered along the coast, the dark interior, the long curve of surf made visible only as a faint phosphorescence where it broke. She felt nothing she had expected to feel. She felt the way she felt on every flight into every assignment, a tightening in the chest, the professional readiness, and underneath it something else she did not name.

She was here as part of a human rights investigation, sent by an organization in Geneva, attached to a local institution, paired with a Sri Lankan archaeologist whom she had not yet met. The arrangement had been delicate and was, she understood, a kind of compromise. The government had agreed to the investigation because to refuse would have looked worse than to allow it, and it had insisted on the pairing, a local man, someone the authorities could account for, someone who could keep the foreign expert within sanctioned channels. She did not resent this. She had worked under stranger constraints. She knew how to do the work inside whatever box she was given and how to find, sometimes, the small gap in the box's corner through which the truth could be passed.

At the airport a man in a damp short-sleeved shirt examined her papers for a long time, longer than the papers required, turning them as if there were something written on the back. He asked her the purpose of her visit. He asked her where she was born. When she told him, he looked up and studied her face with an expression she could not read, and then he stamped the documents and slid them back across the counter without a word, and she understood that the not-saying was itself a kind of saying, that here, now, in this country, silence had become an entire language and she would have to learn its grammar all over again.

Sarath Diyasena met her two days later at the institution, a low colonial building with ceiling fans that turned the heat around without dispelling it. He was perhaps in his late forties, careful in his movements, with a stillness to him that she would learn to read slowly over the weeks. He did not waste words and he watched her, she felt, the way she watched a grave site, looking for the thing that did not belong.

"You studied here as a girl," he said. It was not a question. He had read her file too.

"A long time ago."

"You don't sound as if you did."

"No," she said. "I suppose I don't."

He nodded as though she had confirmed something, and she could not tell whether the something was good or bad. He poured her tea from a flask without asking whether she wanted it, and she drank it because to refuse would have been the wrong move, and it was too sweet, the way she remembered tea being, the way she had stopped taking it years ago, and the sweetness opened something in her that she pushed quickly closed.

They drove out the next morning before light. Sarath drove. He drove the way he spoke, economically, his eyes moving constantly to the rearview mirror, and she noticed this and said nothing. There were checkpoints. At the first one the soldier was young, his rifle held loosely, and he glanced into the car and waved them through with a kind of boredom that was its own information. At the second the soldier was older and made them get out and stand on the road while he searched the vehicle, and Sarath stood with his hands easy at his sides and his face composed and answered every question in a low steady voice, and Anil stood beside him and looked at the trees and the red road and the soldier going through their equipment cases and she thought: this is the country now. This is what it has become. A place where the dead are evidence and the living are suspects and the line between is wherever a man with a rifle decides to put it this morning.

The site they had been granted access to was a government archaeological preserve, a series of caves in a protected area where, the authorities had explained, a number of ancient burials lay undisturbed, sixth-century interments, monks of an early monastic settlement. This was the official reason for the investigation—the study of ancient remains—and Anil understood the irony of it, that she had been brought to a country drowning in fresh dead and pointed firmly at the dead of fifteen hundred years ago, as if the years between had been empty.

But the caves were government land. They were sealed and guarded. And it was precisely this, Sarath had told her quietly on the drive, his eyes on the road, that made them interesting. A guarded place where no ordinary citizen could go was the perfect place to hide what you did not want found. Among the ancient skeletons, all of them genuinely ancient, all of them genuinely from the sixth century, there might be one that was not. There might be one that had been carried in and placed among the others, hidden in plain sight, hidden in time itself, a recent body disguised as an old one because no one would think to test the age of bones in a protected archaeological site.

"You think this," Anil said.

"I think it is possible," Sarath said. "I think it is worth looking."

They worked the caves for three days. The skeletons were laid out in the old way, in stone niches, and the dust of centuries lay on them, and the air was cool and mineral and smelled of stone and bat and the particular dryness of very old death. Anil moved among them with her instruments. She was meticulous. She had been trained by a man named Larry Angel and a woman in Oklahoma who could read a femur the way a scommuter reads a newspaper, and she had spent her twenties learning to make bone speak, to extract from it age and sex and trauma and labor and disease and the long story of a life and the short story of its ending. The ancient skeletons told their ancient stories. The wear on the teeth, the markers of a particular diet, the calcification, the way the bone had mineralized over a thousand years. She read them and they were old, all of them old, and she felt the first creep of something between disappointment and relief, because if there was nothing here then she could write a report and the report would say nothing and nothing would be required of her.

And then there were four.

There were four skeletons that had been brought up from a deeper chamber, a sealed natural pocket below the main caves, and Sarath had requested their exhumation from the authorities and the request had taken two weeks and three offices and a degree of patience Anil had not known she possessed, and finally they had been granted it, and the four were laid out now in the back of the building on long tables, and Anil went over them one by one in the failing light of late afternoon with the fans turning overhead.

Three were old. She read them quickly. Three were of a piece with the others, sixth century, monastic, the long sleep of fifteen hundred years on them.

The fourth was not.

She knew it almost at once, the way you know a wrong note in a melody before you can say which note. The mineralization was wrong. The bone had the wrong density, the wrong color in the wrong light. There was lead in it, she would establish later, traces of lead and other things that did not belong in a sixth-century monk, that belonged to the modern world, to its paints and its pipes and its petrol and its industry. And there was something else. There was a small mark on the bone, a nick on a rib, the kind of mark a forensic anthropologist learns to recognize the way a sailor learns weather, and it was perimortem, it was made at or near the time of death, and it was made by something with an edge.

She did not say anything that first evening. She covered the fourth skeleton and she sat with what she knew, alone, in the heat, with the lizards on the wall above her motionless and watchful, and she let the knowledge settle into her the way silt settles in still water until the shape of the bottom becomes clear.

The fourth skeleton was modern. Four or five years dead, no more. It had been carried into a guarded government archaeological site, into a sealed chamber where it had no business being, and laid among the ancient dead, and the only reason to do such a thing was the reason Sarath had named on the drive, the only reason there could be, which was to make it disappear into time, into history, into the safe distance of a thousand years, so that if it were ever found it would be misread and waved past, an old bone among old bones, nothing to see, move along.

But it was a murdered man and he was recent and he had been put there by people with the keys to government land, which meant by the government, or by people the government protected, which in the end was the same distinction the soldier on the road had been making with his silence, the distinction that did not exist.

She told Sarath in the morning. She told him plainly, laying out the evidence the way she would lay it out for any court, the mineralization, the lead, the density, the perimortem mark. She watched his face as she spoke. He did not look surprised. He looked, if anything, tired, the tiredness of a man who has had a thing confirmed that he hoped against hope would not be.

"You're certain," he said.

"I'm certain of what the bone tells me," she said. "The bone doesn't lie. People lie. Bone doesn't."

He was quiet for a while. Outside a vendor was calling something in a long musical line that rose and fell, and the fan turned, and the four skeletons lay under their covers on the tables.

"You understand what you're saying," Sarath said at last. "If this is true, then someone with authority over this site brought him here. This is not a place a villager can enter. This is not a place even I can enter without three signatures."

"I understand."

"No," he said, and there was no unkindness in it, only a kind of weary precision. "I don't think you do. Not yet. You've worked in Guatemala, in the Congo, in places where the war was over, or far away, or where the world had agreed to call it a crime. Here the war is not over. Here the people who did this are not in another country writing their memoirs. Here they may be the people who gave us permission to do this work. Here they may be reading the report you have not yet written. You understand the bone. I'm not sure you understand the room the bone is in."

She held his gaze. "Then tell me about the room."

He almost smiled. It was the first time. "That," he said, "will take longer than three days."

They gave the man a name. They could not give him his name, the one his mother had called across a courtyard, the one a woman somewhere might still be saying in the dark before she made herself stop. But they gave him a working name so that he would not be only a number, so that the work would remember he had been a person. Sarath named him. He named him Sailor, for no reason Anil could discover, except that it gave the bones a person to hang on, a someone, the way the woman in the Guatemalan grave had been a someone, had been the feminine string between two men, had been a whole life and not a category. Sailor. The name was a small act and Anil understood it as a small act of defiance, a refusal to let the man dissolve back into the anonymity that his killers had so carefully arranged for him, the anonymity of time, the safe deep grave of a thousand years.

Because that was what they had tried to do. Not only to kill him. Anyone could kill. The thing they had done that turned Anil's stomach colder than the killing was the hiding of him in history, the calculated theft of his death itself, so that even his murder would never be a murder, would never be a fact, would only be an absence, a man who had gone out one evening and not come back, a rumor, a thing a family could be told they had imagined. They had not only taken his life. They had tried to take the very evidence that a life had been taken, to fold him so deep into the past that no future could reach back and pull him out and say: here, look, this happened, this man, this date, this edge that made this mark on this rib.

And now Anil had reached back and pulled him out.

She sat with Sailor in the evenings. She measured him and remeasured him. She established his age—a young man, mid-twenties, no older. She established his sex, his probable height, the marks of his labor. There were stress markers in the bone, in the places where muscle had pulled against bone over years, and she read them the way she had been trained to read them, and she began to construct a life from them, a man who had done physical work, work with the hands and the back and the legs, perhaps a man from the mines, she thought, remembering the song, the men who went seventy-two fathoms into the dark and came up only to find their lives waiting at the surface, blessed be the chain attached to the life wheel. Perhaps a miner. Perhaps a laborer of another kind. She could not say. But she could say he had worked, that he had used his body, that he had been alive in the most physical sense, and that someone had ended that and tried to make the ending disappear.

There were three killings of him, she thought, lying awake in the small hot room they had given her. The first was the killing of his body. The second was the hiding of his body, the killing of the fact. And the third would be the killing of the truth, which would happen if she failed, if the report said nothing, if Sailor went back into the dark with the ancient monks and the years closed over him again. She would not allow the third. The bone did not lie and she would not let the report lie. She had come a long way and changed her name and built herself out of nothing to become the person who stood in this room with this knowledge, and she understood, lying awake, that this was the work she had been built for, that every grave in Guatemala and the Congo had been leading her here, to this man on this table, to this single skeleton out of place in time.

In the morning she would begin the reconstruction in earnest. She would need a place that did not lie. She would need to keep Sailor safe, the way the Guatemalan families had kept their dead safe, covering them with plastic in the night, weighting the plastic with stones, watching so the evidence would not be lost again. She thought of the woman in the grave, the woman who had been the string between two men, and she thought: I am the string now. I am the thing that will bring this man back into the company of the living long enough for them to know him, to know what was done. She did not know his name but she would give the world enough of him that someone might supply it. She would establish where he had come from, what soil his bones had grown in, what diet, what region, and she would narrow it and narrow it the way she narrowed everything until the shape became undeniable, and somewhere a woman would hear it and say a name in the dark and this time not make herself stop.

Sarath came in early, before light, the way they had begun on the first day. He carried two cups of the over-sweet tea and set one beside her and she took it and this time she did not push closed the thing the sweetness opened. She let it open. She was home, in the country that had made her, in the country that was killing its own and hiding them in its own past, and she had work to do.

"There's a place we can take him," Sarath said. "Where we can work without being watched. It belonged to my teacher. It's far from here. It's safe. As safe as anything is."

"Why are you helping me?" she asked. She had wanted to ask it for days. She watched his face in the gray light. "You said yourself the people who did this may be the people who gave us permission. You have more to lose than I do. I can leave. I have a passport from somewhere else. This is your country. You have to stay. Why are you helping me?"

He was silent for a long moment, and she thought he would not answer, that he would fold the question into the great national silence and pass it back to her unanswered. But then he said, looking not at her but at the covered shape of Sailor on the table:

"Because the bone doesn't lie. You said it yourself. Everything else here lies now. The newspapers lie, the radio lies, the men at the checkpoints lie with their faces, I lie, every day I lie, to live, to keep working, to keep my mother safe, I lie. And then I came into this room and there was something that could not lie, this man, these bones, this mark on this rib. And I found I could not bear to let it be made into one more lie. That's the whole of it. That's the only reason I have. It's not a good enough reason to risk your life. But it's the only one I've got."

Anil looked at him, this careful, still, watchful man who had been measuring her since the first morning, and she understood that he had not been measuring her to betray her. He had been measuring her to see whether she could be trusted with the thing he had been carrying alone, the thing he had hoped and feared they would find, the thing that had no place in the official version of anything. He had been waiting to see if she was the kind of person who would write the report that said nothing, or the kind who would not.

"All right," she said. "Take us to your teacher's house. Let's bring him back."

And outside, the country was waking, the war going on in its different forms, the vendors calling, the soldiers at the roads, the families somewhere still keeping their vigils over half-revealed forms, refusing to let the evidence be lost again. And in the back of a low colonial building with the fans turning, a young man four or five years dead, given the name Sailor by a man who would not let him disappear, lay covered on a table, waiting to speak, waiting to be carried out of the deep grave of time and laid, at last, among the recent and acknowledged dead, where a death could still be called a death, and a crime a crime, and a man a man.
