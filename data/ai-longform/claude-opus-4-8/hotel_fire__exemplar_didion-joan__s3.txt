The hotel had been rebuilt in 1962, two years after the fire, and Dolores had read about it in the Sacramento Bee, a small item on the business page noting that the Hotel Senator would reopen under new management with a renovated ballroom and ninety-six rooms, forty fewer than before. She had clipped the item and kept it in the drawer with her marriage license and the children's birth certificates and a photograph of her mother taken in Lodi before the war, and she had not thought about it again, or had told herself she had not thought about it again, until the invitation came. Her niece Christine was being married in the ballroom of the Hotel Senator. There would be two hundred guests. The reception would begin at six.

"You don't have to go," Frank said. He was reading the paper at the kitchen table and he did not look up. "You could say you weren't feeling well. Nobody expects you to feel well, your sister's the one with the daughter getting married, not you."

"It's Christine," Dolores said. "I held Christine the day she was born."

"I'm only saying."

"I know what you're saying."

She had been twenty-three the year of the fire, and she had worked at the Senator for fourteen months, making beds on the fourth and fifth floors and emptying the ashtrays the men left in the rooms, the men who came up from the legislature for the session and the men who came for the cattle auctions and the men who came for no reason she could ever determine, who sat in the rooms in the afternoons with the shades drawn. She had not minded the work. She had liked the long corridors in the early morning before anyone was awake, the carpet absorbing her footsteps, the brass numbers on the doors. She had liked the linen closets that smelled of bleach and the way the sheets came back from the laundry folded into perfect rectangles, and she had liked, although she would not have said so to anyone, the feeling of being inside a place where important things were understood to be happening, where the men in the lobby spoke in low voices and the women wore hats and the bellmen knew which guests to favor.

The fire had started in the kitchen, they said afterward, in the grease behind the broilers, although there were people who said it had started in one of the rooms on the third floor, a cigarette in a wastebasket, and there were people who said it had been set, although they could never say by whom or why. Four people had died. Dolores had been on the fifth floor when the alarm came, and she had gone down the service stairs with two other girls, Ramona and a girl named Patsy whose last name she could no longer remember, and they had come out into the alley behind the kitchen and stood there in the cold not knowing what to do, and Ramona had been crying, and Dolores had not been crying, and that was the thing she remembered most clearly, that she had not cried, that she had stood in the alley watching the smoke come up black against the morning sky and felt nothing she could name.

"What are you going to wear," Frank said.

"The blue."

"The blue's fine."

She had bought the blue dress for Frank's mother's funeral and worn it twice since, and it still fit, more or less, although it pulled across the back now in a way it had not in 1958. She took it out of the closet the night before the wedding and pressed it on the board in the spare room and hung it on the door, and she stood looking at it for a while, the blue dress hanging on the door in the light from the hall, and then she turned off the light and went to bed.

They drove down from Roseville on Saturday afternoon. Frank wore his gray suit and Dolores wore the blue dress and her mother's pearls, which were not real pearls but which her mother had worn to church every Sunday of her life and had left to Dolores and not to her sister, a fact that her sister had never mentioned and never forgotten. The traffic on the highway was heavy with people going somewhere and Frank drove with both hands on the wheel and the radio on low and neither of them said anything until they reached the city limits.

"It's a different building," Frank said. "You know that. They tore the whole thing down."

"I know that."

"It's not the same place."

"I know it's not the same place, Frank."

But it was the same corner. They had not moved the corner. The Senator stood where it had always stood, at Twelfth and L, across from the Capitol park, and when Frank pulled the car up to the entrance and the valet came around to open the door, Dolores looked up at the building and saw that it was the same and not the same, the same corner and the same gray stone facade, or stone that looked like the old stone, but the windows were different, larger, and the door was glass now where it had been brass and glass before, and there was no awning. There had been a green awning. She remembered the green awning and the way the rain had run off it onto the sidewalk and the doorman who had stood under it, a man named Otis who had died in the fire, who had gone back in for reasons no one understood and not come out.

"You all right," Frank said.

"I'm fine."

The lobby had been changed completely. She had expected this and still it took something out of her, the way the lobby had been changed, the low ceiling where there had been a high one, the recessed lights, the carpet that was orange and brown in a pattern that meant nothing, where the old carpet had been red with a border of gold. The front desk had been against the far wall and now it was to the left, and there was a fountain where there had been no fountain, a small fountain set into the floor with a light in it that turned the water blue. She stood in the middle of the lobby and Frank stood beside her with his hand under her elbow and she looked at the fountain and could not understand why anyone had wanted a fountain there.

"The Fayard wedding," Frank said to the girl at the desk. He had the invitation in his hand. "We're with the Fayard wedding."

"The ballroom's on the mezzanine," the girl said. "You can take the elevator or there's the stairs just past the fountain."

They took the stairs. Dolores held the railing and went up slowly in the blue dress and at the top of the stairs there were people, her sister's people and Frank's people and people she did not know, people in dark suits and bright dresses standing with drinks in their hands outside the doors of the ballroom, and her sister Annette came toward her through the crowd with her arms out.

"Dolores. You came. I told Christine you'd come."

"Of course I came. Where's Christine."

"She's getting dressed, she won't come down till six, you know how it is. Don't you look nice. Is that Mama's pearls."

"It's Mama's pearls."

"They look nice on you," Annette said, and something passed across her face and was gone, and she took Dolores by the arm and steered her toward the doors. "Wait till you see the ballroom. They did it all over. It's gorgeous, Dolores, you wouldn't know it."

The ballroom. Dolores had not known there would be the ballroom. She had thought, in the way that one thinks things without quite admitting to thinking them, that the wedding would be somewhere she had never been, some new room, and that she would be able to stand in it without the past coming up around her. But it was the ballroom. They had put the wedding in the ballroom, and Annette was taking her into it, and there was nothing to do but go.

The ballroom on the fifth floor had been where they held the banquets and the political dinners and once, that she remembered, a dance for some governor's inauguration, and she had cleaned it the mornings after, the floor sticky with spilled drinks and the long tables still set with the dishes the waiters had not cleared, and she had found things in that room, a man's watch once, and a woman's shoe, and a letter she had read standing by the windows in the gray morning light and then folded and left on the table because she had not known what else to do with it. The fire had not reached the fifth floor. The fire had been put out before it reached the fifth floor, and the ballroom had not burned, but they had torn it down anyway, torn the whole building down, and built a new ballroom on the mezzanine, and Annette was taking her into it.

It was a different room. She saw that at once and held onto it. It was a different room, lower and wider, with chandeliers that were not the old chandeliers and a parquet floor that was not the old floor, and round tables set for the dinner with white cloths and centerpieces of white flowers, and a bandstand at the far end where four men in white jackets were setting up their instruments. There was nothing in the room she recognized. She stood just inside the door and looked at the room and there was nothing in it she recognized and this was, she understood, exactly what she had wanted, and she found that it gave her no comfort at all.

"Isn't it gorgeous," Annette said.

"It's beautiful, Annette."

"Two hundred people. Bob's family came from Stockton, all of them, and his mother's people from Modesto. You'll sit with us, you and Frank, we're at the family table, naturally." Annette lowered her voice. "I had to put the Pellegrinos at the back, by the band, where you can't hear yourself think, but what could I do, you can't have everybody up front."

"Where's the bar," Frank said.

"By the windows. Go on, get yourself a drink, both of you, you've been driving." Annette squeezed Dolores's arm again. "I'm so glad you came," she said, and there was the thing across her face again, and Dolores understood it now, understood that Annette knew, that Annette had always known, that Annette had asked her to the wedding and put it in the Senator and had known the whole time what she was asking, and was glad she had come, and was sorry, and would never say either thing.

Frank brought her a glass of white wine and she held it without drinking it and stood by the windows looking down at the park. The light was going. Across the street the Capitol was lit up white against the darkening sky and the palm trees stood black along the walks, and below her on the corner the cars came up to the entrance and let people out, and she watched them, the people getting out of the cars, the women holding their skirts as they stepped to the curb, and she thought about Otis under the green awning, opening the doors of the cars, and Patsy whose last name she could not remember, and Ramona crying in the alley.

"They tell me this is a real nice hotel now," said a man beside her. He was one of Bob's people, from Stockton, a heavy man with a red face who had been at the bar. "Top to bottom. My brother-in-law stayed here in the spring, said it was real nice. Course it burned down, you know. Years back. Whole thing burned right to the ground."

"I'd heard that," Dolores said.

"People died in it," the man said with satisfaction. "Four people, I think it was. Or five. They never did find out how it started. My brother-in-law told me the whole story. They built it all back up, though. You'd never know."

"No," Dolores said. "You'd never know."

"Sure is a nice hotel now," the man said, and drifted back toward the bar.

She drank some of the wine. It was cold and slightly sweet and she drank some of it and looked down at the corner and then she set the glass on the windowsill and said to Frank that she was going to find the powder room.

"You want me to come with you?"

"To the powder room?"

"I only meant."

"I'll be right back," she said.

She did not go to the powder room. She went out the doors of the ballroom and past the people standing in the corridor and down the stairs past the fountain with its blue light, and she crossed the lobby and went down a hallway off the lobby where there was a sign for the restrooms and the telephones, and at the end of the hallway there was a door marked NO ADMITTANCE and beside it another door, unmarked, that stood open onto a service corridor, and she went through it.

The service corridor was the same. This was the thing she had not expected and could not have expected, that they would have built the new hotel with a service corridor exactly like the old one, narrow and gray and lit with bare bulbs, the walls scarred where the carts had hit them, the floor bare concrete painted gray, and she stood in it and the smell came up around her, the bleach smell and under it something else, the smell of the laundry and the kitchen and the years, and she understood that it was not the same corridor, that it could not be the same corridor, that everything that had been here had burned, and still she stood in it and her hands were shaking and she put one hand against the wall to steady herself.

A girl came down the corridor pushing a cart. She wore the gray uniform with the white collar, the same uniform, or near enough, and she was young, eighteen or nineteen, with dark hair pinned up under a white cap, and she slowed when she saw Dolores standing there in the blue dress with her hand against the wall.

"You lost, ma'am? The party's up the stairs. You're not supposed to be back here."

"I worked here," Dolores said. "A long time ago. Before."

The girl looked at her without understanding.

"In the old hotel," Dolores said. "The one that burned. I made the beds. The fourth and fifth floors."

"I didn't know there was an old hotel," the girl said. She had stopped the cart and stood with both hands on the handle, polite, wanting to go. "I just started in the spring. They never said anything about a fire."

"It was 1960," Dolores said. "In the winter. February."

"Before I was born, I guess," the girl said, and smiled a little, not unkindly, and Dolores saw that she was very young, younger even than she had thought, and tired, the way you got tired by the middle of a long shift, the way she had been tired, standing in the corridors in the early morning with the whole day ahead.

"It's hard work," Dolores said. "The beds. The corners especially."

"You get used to it," the girl said.

"You do," Dolores said. "You get used to it."

The girl waited another moment and then she said she had to take the linens up, they were short on the third floor, and Dolores stepped back against the wall to let her pass, and the cart went by with its smell of clean sheets, and the girl went down the corridor and turned the corner and was gone, and Dolores stood alone in the gray light and listened to the cart until she could not hear it anymore.

She had not cried in the alley. That was the thing. She had stood in the alley with Ramona crying beside her and Patsy holding onto her arm and she had watched the smoke and felt nothing, and afterward people had said how brave she was, how calm, and she had let them say it, and she had never told anyone that it was not bravery, that she had felt nothing at all, that she had stood there watching four people die, or the place where four people were dying, and felt nothing, and that the nothing had frightened her more than the fire, and had stayed with her, the nothing, for two years and more, so that she had not been able to look at the clipping in the drawer or drive past the corner or say the name of the hotel out loud, not because of what she had felt but because of what she had not felt, the great blank nothing where the feeling should have been, the proof, as she had come to think of it, that there was something wrong in her, something cold and wrong that she had carried since the alley and never been able to name or put down.

And now she stood in the corridor that was not the same corridor and could not be the same corridor, and she found that she was crying. She was crying the way she had not cried in the alley, the way she had not cried at her mother's funeral in the blue dress, the way she had not cried at any of it, and she put both hands over her face and stood against the wall and cried, and she did not know if she was crying for Otis or for Ramona or for the girl with the cart or for the twenty-three-year-old who had stood in the alley feeling nothing, and it did not matter, she understood, which of them she was crying for, it had never mattered, the feeling had been there the whole time, it had only been late, it had been two years and more late in coming, and here it was at last in the gray corridor of the new hotel that stood where the old one had burned.

After a while she stopped. She took a Kleenex from her bag and pressed it under her eyes, careful of the mascara, and she stood up straight against the wall and breathed until her breath came even, and then she went back through the unmarked door and down the hallway past the telephones and across the lobby past the fountain with its blue light, and she went up the stairs holding the railing, slowly, in the blue dress, and at the top of the stairs the people were going in to dinner.

"There you are," Annette said. "We're sitting down. Christine's coming down in a minute, they're going to announce her. You all right? You look like you've been crying."

"It's the air conditioning," Dolores said. "It dries my eyes."

"I know it, it's freezing in there. Come on, you're next to me."

They went in. The band had begun to play, something soft, and the candles were lit on the tables now and the white flowers shone in the candlelight and the two hundred guests were finding their seats, and at the door of the ballroom there was a stir and a turning of heads, and Christine came in on her father's arm in her white dress, Christine whom Dolores had held the day she was born, and the whole room rose, and Dolores rose with them.

She stood at the family table with her hand on the back of her chair and watched the girl come across the room she did not recognize, the room they had built where the other room had burned, and she thought that she would tell Christine, sometime, not tonight, sometime when they were alone, that she had worked here once, in the old hotel, before the fire, and that she had been happy here, which was a thing she had never said and had not until this moment known to be true. She had been happy here. She had liked the corridors in the early morning and the smell of the linens and the brass numbers on the doors, and she had been twenty-three, and she had been happy, and then it had burned, and she had carried the not-feeling out of the alley like a stone in her chest and set it down at last, tonight, in the gray corridor, and she could say now what she had not been able to say.

"Doesn't she look beautiful," Annette whispered.

"She looks beautiful," Dolores said, and she meant it, and the band played, and Christine came on across the room in her white dress through the candlelight and the risen guests, and Dolores stood with the rest of them and watched her come.
