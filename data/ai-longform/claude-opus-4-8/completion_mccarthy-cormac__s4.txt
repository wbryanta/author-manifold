He woke to her hand upon him and the gray of morning at the window like ash settled out of some far conflagration. She had risen on one elbow and her face hung pale above him and he could not for a moment recall who she was nor where this was nor what had brought him to it. Then it came back to him all of a piece, the room and the woman and the child swelling in her, and he lay back and put a hand across his eyes.

You was hollering, she said.

I know it.

What was it.

Nothing. Go back to sleep.

She lay back down beside him and he heard her breathing slow and lengthen and at last grow even in the way of sleepers but he did not sleep again. He lay and watched the window go from gray to a sickly white and listened to the first cart wheels in the road below and the calling of some bird he did not know the name of. When the light was full he rose and dressed in the cold and went down the stairs and out into the street.

The town lay along the river in a kind of stupor, the buildings of unpainted board gone silver with weather and the mud streets rutted and dried into a frozen tumult of wheeltracks. Smoke stood up from chimneys here and there without a wind to move it. He walked down toward the water where the boats lay tethered and idle and a man sat upon an upturned skiff mending net with his fingers blue and clumsy in the cold.

Morning, the man said.

He nodded and went on. At the water's edge he stood and looked out across the river to the far shore where the trees stood black and dripping and a heron stalked the shallows on legs like reeds. The river moved past brown and silent and bore upon its surface a slow traffic of leaves and twigs and once the bloated carcass of some animal that turned slowly as it passed and showed a stiffened leg and was gone around the bend. He watched it out of sight. The cold came up off the water and into him and he turned his collar and stood there yet a while longer with his hands pushed down into his coat.

He had come to this place three years gone with nothing and had got little since. He had hired out to whoever would have him, had cut timber and dug wells and laid stone and shoveled the dead horses out of the streets after the sickness came through, and had taken the woman in the second year because she had nowhere else and because the loneliness in him had grown to a thing he could no longer carry alone, and now there was to be a child and he did not know what manner of man he was to be its father nor whether he had it in him to be any kind at all. He thought of the dream and of the dark that had fallen and of the faces turned against him and he did not understand it but it sat in him like a swallowed stone.

When he went back up the street the man on the skiff had gone and the net lay folded on the boat with the needle stuck through it. He passed the store and the smith's and came to the place where they boarded and went up and the woman was at the stove with a fire going and the small heat of it filling the room.

I'll go up to Coyle's, he said. See is there work.

It's Sunday, she said.

He stopped. He had lost the count of the days. He stood in the door with his hat in his hand and felt the foolishness of it come up in him.

Then I'll go anyhow, he said. He's a heathen same as me.

She did not answer him. She set a plate on the table, cornbread and a little fatback, and he sat and ate it and she watched him eat with her hands folded under the burden of her belly.

You ought not curse so in your sleep, she said. The child'll hear it.

It can't hear nothing yet.

It hears, she said. It knows your voice.

He looked up at her. There was a thing in her face he could not name, some old patience worn into it like the worn silver of the boards outside, and he looked away again and finished what was on the plate and rose.

I'll be back before dark, he said.

Coyle had a place two miles up the river where he ran a few hogs in the bottom and kept a still back in the canebrake that everyone knew of and no one spoke of, and the man walked out to it in the cold with the sun trying to burn through the overcast and failing and the road wet under his boots. Coyle was at his porch when he came up, a big slow man with a face like a side of meat, and he watched him come without rising.

You're working today, he said.

If there's work.

There's always work. He spat off the side of the porch. They's a hog dead in the bottom and I want it drug out and buried fore it taints the rest. You can do that.

What killed it.

I don't know what killed it. Dying killed it. You want the work or no.

He went down into the bottom where the hogs rooted in the black mud among the cane and found the dead one lying on its side already swelling and with its small eye filmed and a froth dried at its mouth. He stood over it. The other hogs had come to nose at it and he kicked them off and they ran squealing and came back. There was no telling what had killed it. He fetched a length of rope from the barn and got it around the hind legs and dragged the thing up out of the bottom with the mud sucking at it and his boots sliding and the carcass leaving a wide smeared track behind it.

He buried it in the higher ground beyond the cane, digging in the cold clay with the pick and then the spade until he had a hole and rolled the hog into it and stood breathing in the cold with the sweat going chill on him. The hole was not deep enough and the hog lay half exposed with one foreleg standing up stiff out of the clay and he looked at it a while and then began to throw the dirt back and it would not cover that leg however he threw it and at last he broke the leg down with the flat of the spade and heard it crack and the thing folded into the hole and he covered it over. He stood on the mound and tamped it with his boots.

When he came back up Coyle paid him in coin and gave him a jar of the clear liquor besides and he took both and started back down the road toward the town with the light already failing and the cold coming on harder than before. The overcast had broken in the west and there was a band of yellow light low along the trees and the rest of the sky gone a deep slate that promised freezing, and he walked into the last of the light with the jar swinging in his hand and the coins warm in his pocket where his fist held them.

He had not gone half the way when he came upon the three.

They were strung along the road ahead of him afoot and moving slow, three men in ragged duffel with their packs upon their backs, and he knew them at once though he had never seen them, knew them the way you know a thing dreamt before, and his step faltered and then went on because there was nowhere to go but on. The bearded one walked foremost and the other two behind, and when he came up on them the bearded one turned his head and looked at him out of the side of his face without slowing.

Evening, the man said.

The bearded one did not answer. They walked. The road was narrow and the man fell in behind them and there was no help for it and he walked behind them with the jar in his hand and watched their backs. They went without speaking. The light died out of the west and the cold deepened and the first stars came out hard and white above the trees. The three made no sound but the soft fall of their feet in the road and the creak of their packs and there was about them a stillness that was not peace.

After a time the bearded one spoke without turning.

You live in that town.

I do.

How far is it.

A mile and a half maybe.

They make a man welcome there.

He did not answer at once. The bearded one turned his head halfway and waited.

Some they do, the man said. Some not.

The bearded one made a sound that might have been a laugh. They walked on. The town showed at last as a scattering of lamps far down along the river and the man felt a relief at the sight of it though he could not have said why he should, walking behind three men on an open road. The bearded one slowed and stopped and the other two stopped with him and the man stopped too because he had to or walk up into them.

You got drink in that jar, the bearded one said.

I do.

Let's have a pull of it.

He stood holding the jar. There was no want in him to give it over but there was less want to refuse and he held it out and the bearded one took it and uncorked it and drank long and passed it back to the others and they drank in their turn and the man stood and watched his liquor go down their throats in the starlight. When the jar came back to him it was a third gone and he corked it and did not drink himself.

Where you bound, the man said.

West, the bearded one said.

What's west.

The bearded one looked at him a long moment in the dark. Same as is everywhere, he said. More of it.

They went on. When they came into the town the streets were empty and the lamps burned in the windows and the river lay black between its banks with the lights laid out trembling upon it. The three stopped in the square before the church, which was no more than a meeting house of board with a small bell hung in a frame beside it, and they stood there and looked about them and the man would have gone on past to the boarding house but the bearded one spoke to him again.

Where's a man eat in this place.

There's the inn. Yonder. He pointed. They'll feed you for coin.

We're shy of coin, the bearded one said. He smiled. His teeth in the lamplight were small and brown. We been traveling rough.

The man stood. He had the coins in his pocket and the jar in his hand and he wished himself away from there but the three stood looking at him and there was a thing in their stillness that held him.

I can spare you a little, he said, and did not know why he said it.

He brought out a coin and held it and the bearded one took it from his fingers and looked at it and put it away somewhere in his rags.

You're a Christian man, the bearded one said.

No, the man said. I ain't nothing.

The bearded one nodded slowly as if this were the answer he had looked for. Nobody is, he said. They just don't know it. He turned to the others. Come on, he said, and they went off across the square toward the inn and left the man standing there alone with the jar and the lighter weight of coin in his pocket and he watched them go and felt that he had paid out something more than money though he could not have named it.

He went up to the room. The woman was abed but not asleep and she sat up when he came in.

You're late, she said.

There was a thing I had to do.

He set the jar on the floor by the wall and took off his boots and his coat and lay down beside her in his clothes and she put her hand on his chest.

You're cold as the grave, she said.

I buried a hog today.

She did not ask him what he meant by it. After a while she said: There was three strangers come through. I seen them from the window. Going to the inn.

I seen them too.

I didn't like the look of them.

He lay and looked at the ceiling where the lamplight from the street below moved faint upon it. No, he said. Nor me.

He did not sleep for a long time and when he slept he did not dream, or dreamt nothing he could carry back across the wall of waking, and he woke in the gray of morning the same as before with the woman warm against him and the child between them like a secret kept.

In the days that followed the three did not leave. He had thought they would go on west as the bearded one had said but they did not go, they took up at the inn and then when their welcome there wore thin they took an empty shed at the edge of town that had been a smokehouse and they lived in it and were seen about the streets in the cold morning standing and watching and saying little. The town did not know what to make of them and so made nothing, only went around them the way water goes around a snag, and the man went around them too but he could not put them from his mind. He saw them everywhere. He would come round a corner and they would be there, the three of them, and the bearded one would look at him out of the side of his face and nod once and the man would nod back and go on with something turning over cold in his chest.

There came talk in the town. A hog gone missing from a pen, then another. A girl said three men had stood outside her window in the night and looked in at her and her family had set a watch and seen nothing. A man named Edmonds said he had been cheated at cards by the youngest of the three and showed the bruise where he had been struck for protesting it. The talk grew and ran along the streets like the smell of the river and gathered to itself an anger that wanted only a shape to pour into.

The man heard the talk and held himself apart from it. He worked when there was work, dug and hauled and split, and at night he came home to the woman and the swelling child, and the cold held the country in its fist and the river ran low and black between rims of ice that grew out from the banks a little farther each morning. The child would come with the spring, the woman said. She had it counted. She sat at the window with her sewing and looked out at the gray street and he watched her and tried to see ahead to what was coming and could not. The dream came back to him sometimes in the dark of his mind, the sun blacking out and the dark falling like a shout and the faces turned against him, and he did not know what it meant or whether it meant anything, but he could not stop himself from setting it alongside the three strangers and the cold and the child coming, as though these were the pieces of some figure he was meant to read and could not.

It was a hard month and then it was harder. The river froze across and the boats sat locked in it and there was no traffic and no work to be had and the food got dear in the store. The talk in the town turned bitter and turned at last toward the three as the cause of every want, and one night a barn burned at the edge of town, old Hutto's barn, and the fire could be seen for miles standing up red against the frozen dark, and in the morning Hutto stood in the square with his face black and his hands burned and said the three had done it though he had seen no one, and the town gathered to him and the anger in it had found at last its shape.

They went out to the smokehouse at the end of town in the gray cold with their breath smoking and clubs and a few of them with guns, and the man went among them, not because he willed it but because the town moved and he moved with it the way a man caught in a current moves. He did not know what he meant to do. He went with his hands empty and his heart cold and he came up at the back of the crowd as they gathered before the shed and Hutto beat upon the door with the flat of his burned hand and called out for them to come forth.

The door opened. The bearded one stood in it. He was not afraid. The man could see that across all the heads of the crowd, that there was no fear in him, only a kind of waiting, and behind him in the dark of the shed the other two stood and watched.

We come for you, Hutto said. We know what you done.

You don't know nothing, the bearded one said. His voice was easy. You never did.

You burnt my barn.

The bearded one looked out over the crowd slow, his eyes going from face to face, and they came at last to the man at the back and stopped there, and the man felt them on him like a cold hand laid against his neck.

I never burnt nothing, the bearded one said, still looking at the man. A man's barn burns it's because there was fire in it waiting. I only stood by. I only ever stood by and watched it come up out of them that wanted it.

The crowd stirred and muttered. Hutto raised his club.

Tell them, the bearded one said, and he was speaking now only to the man at the back, his eyes fixed and pale across the distance, and the crowd turned, some of them, to see who he spoke to. Tell them you was cured, he said. Tell them the sun come back.

The man stood. He felt the cold go all through him and out of him and leave him hollow. The faces were turning to him now, more of them, the whole crowd coming slow around like a thing on a turning wheel, and he saw in their faces the beginning of a question and behind the question the old readiness to fall upon whatever was set before them, and he understood that the bearded one had named him, had marked him out and handed him to them, and that it was the dream come round again into the waking world, the sun gone black and the faces turning and himself caught up among the ruined and the howling with nowhere left to hide.

I don't know him, the man said. His voice came out of him small. I never seen him before he come on the road.

But he heard the lie in it even as he spoke it and so did they. Their faces did not change back. The bearded one smiled his small brown smile and stepped out of the doorway into the cold and the two came out behind him and the three of them stood before the shed and the crowd did not move against them, did not move at all, held there by some weight the bearded one had laid upon them, and he came down through them and they parted for him and he walked toward the man at the back with the other two behind him and the crowd turning and turning to watch him come.

He stopped before the man.

You said you was nothing, he said. You recall it. You said you wasn't no Christian and wasn't nothing.

I recall it, the man said.

That's the truest thing you'll ever say, the bearded one said. He put his hand on the man's shoulder, light, almost gentle, and the man could not move under it. There ain't none of us nothing, he said. We just the dark waiting on the sun to fail. He leaned close and the man could smell him, the rags and the smoke and something under it like cold earth turned over. The sun ain't coming back, he said low, so that only the man could hear it. You knew that in your sleep. That's why you hollered.

Then he took his hand away and walked on past, and the two went after him, and the three of them went up through the town and out along the frozen river to the west, and the crowd stood in the gray cold and watched them go and did not follow, and the man stood among them with the cold in him like a swallowed stone and watched them dwindle and go small against the white world and at last drop down under the curve of the river bank and out of sight altogether, gone west the way they had said, into more of it, into the everywhere, and the sun stood low and red and gave no heat and the man turned at last and went back up through the streets alone to the room and the woman and the child that was coming whether he would or no.

She was at the window when he came in. She had watched it all from above, the crowd and the three going out, and she turned to him and her face was full of the unspoken thing.

They're gone, he said.

Will they come back.

He stood in the doorway with his hat in his hand. No, he said. Not them. He came in and shut the door against the cold. But there's always more of them, he said. They just go on west and there's more come behind.

She put her hand on her belly. What'll we tell it, she said. When it comes. About the world.

He did not answer her for a long time. He went and sat on the edge of the bed and looked at his cold and empty hands and outside the light was failing again over the frozen river and the smoke stood up unmoving from the chimneys of the town.

We won't have to tell it nothing, he said at last. It'll know. They all of them know. They just don't say it.

And he lay back on the bed in his clothes and put his arm across his eyes and the dark came down over the town like a shout drawn out and held and held and never let go, and somewhere out west under the cold stars of another season the three went on, single file and slow, with their long shadows going before them on the snow.
