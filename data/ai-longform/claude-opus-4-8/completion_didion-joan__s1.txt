But the Pacific. The Pacific was something else. The Pacific was where you wanted to be if you had to be anywhere watching things go off, he said. He said this to Inez Victor in the bar of the Borja Garden Hotel in Kuala Lumpur in the spring of 1975, although neither of them would have been able to tell you, had you asked, why he was telling her this, or why she was listening, or what either of them was doing in Kuala Lumpur in the spring of 1975. There were reasons. There were always reasons. Jack Lovett had reasons for being in most places at most times, and the reasons were never the obvious ones, and Inez Victor had learned a long time ago, in another life, the life in which she had been Inez Christian on the lanai of her grandfather's house in Honolulu watching the rain come down over the Koolau Range, not to ask him what they were.

So she did not ask him now. She sat with her drink going warm between her hands and she listened to him talk about the light at dawn during those Pacific tests, and she thought, although she did not say this either, that she had known him for twenty-five years and she had never once heard him talk this long about anything. Jack Lovett did not as a rule narrate. Jack Lovett as a rule extracted. He listened, he watched, he filed things away, and what he gave back was always less than what he took in, always edited, always weighed. That was the deal you made with someone like Jack Lovett, the deal Inez had made when she was nineteen and dancing with him at the Outrigger and her father did not yet know that the man she was dancing with was the kind of man her father would not have wanted her dancing with. You got the watching and the listening and the filing away, and you did not get the rest, and you understood that the rest was the cost of doing business.

And now here he was, in Kuala Lumpur, telling her about gardenias.

"You're not listening," he said.

"I'm listening."

"You're thinking about Harry."

"I'm not thinking about Harry."

She was not thinking about Harry. Harry Victor was in Jakarta, or Harry Victor was on his way to Jakarta, or Harry Victor had been in Jakarta and was now somewhere else, in any case Harry Victor was being met at airports by men from embassies and being driven in cars with small flags on the fenders, and Inez was not thinking about him. Harry Victor was the kind of man you did not have to think about because there were so many people thinking about him already, aides and advance men and the woman named Billy Dillon who had run Harry's life for fifteen years and who had a way of materializing in hotel corridors with a sheaf of cables and a look that said the schedule had changed. Harry Victor was a United States Senator with a national reputation and a wife who had been described in a national magazine as cool and a daughter at Berkeley and a son named Adlai, and Inez was the wife, the cool one, and she was sitting in a bar in Kuala Lumpur listening to a man she should not have been sitting with talk about the way the air used to smell on islands that no longer existed.

"Some of those islands aren't even there anymore," Jack Lovett said, as if he had heard her. "You understand that. We took the tops off some of them. Just—" He made a flat gesture with his hand, a smoothing gesture, a gesture you might make to indicate calm water or a made bed. "Gone."

"Elugelab," Inez said.

He looked at her.

"You told me once," she said. "A long time ago. Elugelab. You said there used to be an island called Elugelab and then there was a hole in the ocean a mile across where the island used to be."

"Mike shot," he said. "November of fifty-two. You were what, fifteen years old."

"I was sixteen."

"You were sixteen years old and I told you about Elugelab."

"You told me about a lot of things."

He turned his glass in a slow circle on the wet ring it had made on the table, and for a moment he did not say anything, and outside the rain had started, the way the rain started everywhere in that part of the world, all at once and without apology, drumming on the broad leaves of the plants by the terrace and on the corrugated roof of the cabana by the empty pool, and Inez thought how strange it was that the sound of rain in Kuala Lumpur was exactly the sound of rain in Honolulu, that you could close your eyes and not know where you were, that the whole equatorial world was joined by this one sound and she had spent her life moving through it from one place to another without ever quite arriving anywhere.

"I shouldn't have told you about Elugelab," he said.

"Why not."

"Because now you remember it. And there's no percentage in remembering things. There's no future in it." He said this without heat, the way he said everything, as a piece of information he was passing along, and then he looked at her and something in his face changed, opened slightly, the way the sky he had been describing must have opened, and he said: "You want to know why I'm telling you all this."

"I didn't ask."

"You never ask. That's the thing about you, Inez. You're the only person I ever knew who never asked."

That was not entirely true, and they both knew it was not entirely true, but it was true enough for the purposes of a bar in Kuala Lumpur at six o'clock in the evening with the rain coming down, and Inez let it stand, the way she had let so many things stand, the way you learned to let things stand if you wanted to keep on going from one place to the next.

What had happened, although it would be some days before Inez understood the sequence of it, was that things had begun to come apart. Not loudly. Not in any way that would have been visible to the men from the embassies or to Billy Dillon with her cables. But Jack Lovett had been in the business of knowing when things were coming apart for a long time now—it could fairly be said that this was his only business, the various other businesses he was understood to be in being merely the cover stories for this single underlying competence—and he had known for some weeks now that this thing was coming apart, the thing in the country to the north, the thing that was at that moment filling the airports of the region with people who wanted to get out and the harbors with ships that could not take them, and he had come to Kuala Lumpur because Kuala Lumpur was a place where a man in his line of work could wait and watch and make certain arrangements, and he had found Inez Victor in the bar of the Borja Garden Hotel because Inez Victor was traveling with her husband the Senator, who was in the region on a fact-finding mission for a subcommittee, finding facts being the official designation for what Senators did when they wanted to be photographed looking concerned in front of refugees.

He had not come to Kuala Lumpur to find Inez. That was important to him, that she understand he had not come to find her, although in fact he had known she would be there, had known the Senator's schedule down to the half hour because knowing such schedules was a part of what he did, and had factored the schedule into his own arrangements in a way he did not examine too closely.

"There's going to be some weather," Jack Lovett said.

Inez understood that he was not talking about the rain.

"How bad," she said.

"Bad enough. It's going to go fast now. Faster than the people in Harry's subcommittee think it's going to go. They're all sitting around writing position papers about an orderly transition and there isn't going to be any orderly anything, there's going to be people hanging off the skids of the last helicopter, that's what there's going to be." He paused. "You should think about getting Jessie out."

Jessie was the daughter. Jessie was at Berkeley, or Jessie was supposed to be at Berkeley, and Inez felt the small familiar contraction she always felt at the mention of her daughter's name, because Jessie was nineteen and Jessie had a problem, the problem being one Inez did not allow herself to name even silently because naming it gave it a shape, and the problem had taken Jessie out of Berkeley some months ago and the problem was the reason Jessie was not where Jessie was supposed to be.

"Jessie's in California," Inez said.

"Jessie's in Saigon," Jack Lovett said.

The rain went on. Somewhere in the hotel a telephone was ringing and not being answered, and a houseboy crossed the terrace with a tray held over his head against the rain, and Inez sat very still and looked at Jack Lovett and did not say anything at all, because there was nothing to say, because the thing she had not allowed herself to name had now been named, not the problem itself but its most recent and most spectacular consequence, which was that her nineteen-year-old daughter was in a city that was about to fall, a city Jack Lovett had just told her would empty out with people hanging off the skids of the last helicopter.

"How do you know," she said finally.

"I make it my business to know."

"How do you know about Jessie."

"Same answer."

"You've been watching her."

"I've been watching a lot of things, Inez. Jessie's one of them. She got a job out there. Some outfit catering to the personnel, you know, the contractors, the embassy people, runs a kind of a dining room operation. She wanted to do something useful. That's what she told the people who hired her, she wanted to do something useful." He said the words without irony, which was worse than if he had said them with it. "She's been there since January. She uses the name Jessie Christian."

That stopped Inez. Christian. Her own name, her maiden name, the name of the house on the rainy slope below the Koolau and the grandfather who had owned the sugar and the cousins and the aunts and the whole vanished apparatus of the life she had left when she married Harry Victor. Jessie had taken her mother's name to go to a war.

"She didn't want anyone to know whose daughter she was," Inez said.

"Looks that way."

"So Harry doesn't know."

"Harry," Jack Lovett said, "is finding facts."

It was not unkind, exactly, the way he said it. It was simply accurate, the way a barometer is accurate, and Inez recognized in it the particular quality that had drawn her to Jack Lovett a quarter of a century before and that had kept her, against all the evidence of her better judgment, coming back to the fact of him through three decades and four continents and a marriage and the births of two children and the slow accretion of a life she sometimes felt she was watching from a window. Jack Lovett did not deal in the things people told each other. He dealt in the things that were actually the case. And the thing that was actually the case was that her daughter was in Saigon under an assumed name and the city was about to fall and her husband did not know and her husband, even if he had known, would have been in his hotel room in Jakarta drafting a statement about the importance of a measured American response.

"You can't tell Harry," Jack Lovett said.

"I have to tell Harry."

"You tell Harry, and Harry tells Billy Dillon, and Billy Dillon tells him it's a problem, which it is, it's a hell of a problem, the Senator's daughter in a city the Senator's been on record about—and then it becomes a thing that has to be managed. They'll want to manage it. And managing it is the one thing that'll get her killed, because managing it means doing it through channels, and the channels are the first thing to go." He leaned forward. "Let me get her out."

Inez looked at him.

"Quietly," he said. "My way. I can be in there and out before anybody in Harry's operation even knows there was a problem. But I can't do it if it's official. I can do it as a favor to you. I can't do it as a service to the Senate."

The remarkable thing, Inez would think later, was that she did not hesitate. She had spent her adult life hesitating, weighing, considering the angles, the way you learned to do when everything you said might be quoted and everything you did might be photographed and the smallest gesture might be construed; she had built an entire self out of the act of not committing herself, the cool wife, the one who did not make scenes, and now, presented with the single decision of her life that actually mattered, she did not hesitate at all.

"Do it," she said.

"All right."

"Jack." She put her hand over his on the table, the first time she had touched him in eleven years, and the houseboy with the tray was gone now and the rain was the only sound. "If anything happens to her—"

"Nothing's going to happen to her."

"You don't know that."

"No," he said. "I don't know that. But I know the alternative, which is doing nothing, and doing nothing is worse. Doing nothing is always worse. That's the one thing thirty years in this business teaches you. The people who get hurt are the people who wait for the situation to clarify. The situation doesn't clarify. The situation just gets darker and then it's over and you're standing there having waited." He turned his hand under hers, so that they were palm to palm, and he looked at their two hands on the table as if they belonged to other people, as if they were exhibits. "I waited once," he said. "With you. I'm not going to tell you that was a mistake because telling you that wouldn't do either of us any good at this late date. But I want you to know I think about it. The waiting. I think about it more than I'd like to."

She did not answer that. There was no answer to that, or there were too many answers, and they were sitting in a bar in Kuala Lumpur with a war coming apart two countries away and her daughter in the middle of it, and this was not the time. It was never the time. That was the whole history of Inez Christian and Jack Lovett, that it was never the time, that they had met at the wrong moment and missed each other by a margin so small it could be measured in the particular set of a girl's father's mouth at the Outrigger Canoe Club in 1952, and they had spent the rest of their lives in the wide country on the far side of that margin, glimpsing each other across it.

"Tell me about the light again," she said.

He understood that she was asking him to stop, to go back, to give her a few more minutes before the thing began that could not be stopped once it began. And he was a man who gave very little, but he gave her that.

"The light," he said. "Okay. The light at dawn. You'd be out there in the transport, three, four in the morning, everybody's quiet because there's nothing left to say, all the stories are used up, and you'd watch the east. And there'd be this moment—you couldn't predict it exactly, it depended on the cloud, the moisture—there'd be this moment where the black went to gray and the gray went to this color I'm telling you no painter ever got, this pink, but it wasn't pink like a color, it was pink like a—" He stopped. He was a man unaccustomed to reaching for words and the reaching cost him something. "It was pink like the inside of something," he said finally. "Like the inside of a shell. Like something that wasn't supposed to be looked at from the outside. And the whole sky would be that, the whole eastern sky, and the water underneath it would go the same color, and for about a minute and a half you'd think you were the first person who ever lived or the last one, you couldn't tell which, and it didn't matter which, and that was the thing about it. That was the thing you couldn't explain to anybody who wasn't there. It didn't matter which."

"And then the shot," Inez said.

"And then the shot," he agreed. "And the shot was nothing. The shot was just physics. The shot was a bunch of guys who'd been on an island too long getting a number they could take back to a committee. The light was the thing. But you couldn't put the light in a report."

He took his hand out from under hers, not abruptly, but with a kind of finality, the way you put away a thing you have finished using, and he stood up and dropped some bills on the table, Malaysian money, soft worn notes, and he stood looking down at her in the dim bar with the rain coming down behind him.

"I'll need a day," he said. "Two at the outside. I'll need a number where I can reach you that isn't a number Harry's people answer."

She gave him the number of the house she still kept in Honolulu, the number that rang in the empty rooms below the mountain where no one had lived for years, where the phone would ring and ring against the koa wood and the long shadows, and she gave it to him because it was the one number in the world that belonged to her and to no one else, not to Harry, not to Billy Dillon, not to the cool wife or the Senator's family or any of the apparatus, and Jack Lovett wrote it on the inside of a matchbook and did not look at it again, which meant he had already memorized it, which meant the writing was a courtesy, a way of letting her see him take it down.

"Inez."

"Yes."

"She's all right. Right now, today, this minute, she's all right. I want you to hold onto that. Whatever you have to do in the next two days, sitting through whatever you have to sit through with Harry, smiling at whoever you have to smile at—you hold onto the fact that she's all right and I'm going to keep her that way. Can you do that?"

"I can do that."

"You're a good liar," he said. "It's the one thing this life gave you. Use it."

And then he was gone, out across the terrace in the rain without seeming to hurry and without seeming to get wet, a trick she had watched him perform in weather all over the world, and Inez sat alone in the bar with the two soft worn banknotes and the warm drink and the sound of the rain on the broad leaves, and she did not move for a long time.

She was thinking, although she had told him she would not, about Elugelab. She was thinking about the island that was no longer there, the hole in the ocean a mile across, and she was thinking that this was the thing Jack Lovett had given her when she was sixteen years old, this image of a place that could simply cease to be, and that she had carried it with her ever since without understanding why it had seemed so important to keep. Now she thought she understood. The whole world was full of Elugelab. The whole world was full of places that had been there and then were not there, lives that had been one way and then were another way, girls who had been Inez Christian on a rainy lanai and were now Inez Victor in a bar in Kuala Lumpur, daughters who had been at Berkeley and were now in a falling city under their mothers' maiden names. The water just closed over. You could not even see, from a ship, where the island had been.

When Billy Dillon found her, half an hour later, she was still sitting there, and the drink was still untouched, and Billy Dillon, who noticed things, noticed both of these facts and filed them away in the manner of a man whose business it was to notice and file, but he did not ask about them, because the schedule had changed and there was no time, the schedule had changed and Harry was coming back from Jakarta a day early and there was a thing in the morning, a breakfast, a photograph, a delegation, and Inez was needed, Inez was always needed for the photograph, Inez the cool one, Inez who did not make scenes.

"You look like you've seen a ghost," Billy Dillon said.

"It's the rain," Inez said. "The rain in these places. It does something to me."

"Drink your drink, then. Harry's plane gets in at nine."

She picked up the glass and drank, because it was the thing to do, because Billy Dillon was watching, because the deal she had made a long time ago required that she pick up the glass and drink when Billy Dillon told her to, and the gin was warm and flat and tasted of nothing at all, and she set the glass down empty and stood up and smoothed her dress and arranged her face into the face that would be photographed in the morning, the cool one, the one the national magazine had described, and she followed Billy Dillon out of the bar and across the lobby with its potted palms and its men reading newspapers in languages she could not read, men who might have been anyone, men who might have been watching her, and she thought, as she walked, that somewhere to the north her daughter was lying down to sleep in a city that was going to disappear, and that a man she had loved for twenty-five years was making the only arrangement that mattered, and that she could not say a word about any of it to anyone, and that this, finally, was what her life had prepared her for: not the photographs and not the dinners and not the position papers, but this single act of holding still, of carrying the knowledge of Elugelab across a crowded lobby with her face arranged, and giving nothing away.
