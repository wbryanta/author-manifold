The frost had come early to the valley, and Albert Henning felt it in the knuckles of both hands as he loaded the canvas bag into the back seat of the Morris. The bag held what it had always held: the tuning hammer worn smooth at the grip, the felt strips and rubber wedges, the mutes, the small electronic box he had bought eleven years ago and never quite trusted, preferring the fork and his own ear. He set the bag down with the care he would have given a sleeping child, though there was no one left to wake.

He did not need to consult the list. He had not needed to consult it for thirty years, but he kept it folded in his breast pocket all the same, the names in his own hand gone faint as breath on glass. Mrs. Calloway, the Pevehouse farm, the Tindalls, the school that was no longer a school, the church. Five stops, and after the church there would be nothing, because after the church he had decided there would be nothing. He had told no one this. There was no one to tell who would understand it as anything but the complaint of an old man, and he was tired of being understood that way.

The car started on the third try. He let it warm while he looked at the house behind him, the curtains Eleanor had hung, faded now to the color of weak tea. He had not changed them. He had changed very little since she went, four winters back, and the house had settled into a kind of held breath around him, every object exactly where her hand had left it. He put the car in gear and went down the lane.

Mrs. Calloway's was first, as it had always been first, the white house at the bend with the laburnum that dropped its yellow chains over the gate each June. She met him at the door in her dressing gown though it was past ten, and he understood without asking that she no longer dressed for the day because the day no longer asked anything of her.

"Albert," she said. "You've come."

"I said I would."

"You always do." She stood aside to let him in. The hall smelled of damp and of the lavender water she had used as long as he had known her, which was the better part of his life. The piano stood in the front room where it had stood since before her husband died, a Broadwood upright with a candle bracket gone crooked on one side. Nobody had played it since her granddaughter moved to Bristol, and that was years now, but Mrs. Calloway had it tuned each autumn as her mother had had it tuned, because a piano left to go sour was a kind of admission, and she was not yet ready to admit it.

He opened the lid and ran his thumb across the keys, lightly, the way you might test the temperature of bathwater. The ivory had yellowed and one of the naturals had chipped at the corner, sharp enough to catch a fingernail. He sounded the A and listened. Flat, of course, and the whole instrument had drifted with it, the way a family drifts apart, each note going its own direction from the center.

"Will you have tea?" she asked.

"After," he said. "Let me do this first."

He worked for the better part of an hour. She did not stay in the room; she never stayed in the room. She brought the tea when the unisons were clean and the octaves had stopped beating against each other, and she set it on the little table and sat with him while he drank it, and they did not say much, because there was not much that needed saying between two people who had reached the same place by different roads.

"I won't be coming next year," he said, when the cup was empty.

She looked at him. Her eyes were pale and wet at the rims, as old eyes are, but there was nothing slow in them. "No," she said. "I don't suppose you will."

"It's the route," he said. "There's hardly anyone left on it. It doesn't make sense to keep it going for—" He stopped.

"For the dead," she said.

"I didn't say that."

"You didn't have to." She gathered the cups onto the tray. "I'm not offended, Albert. It's the truth. You tune a piano nobody plays. I keep a piano nobody plays. We're the same foolishness, you and I." She stood with the tray and looked down at him with something he could not name, something between affection and pity, and he thought perhaps the two were closer than people supposed. "Will you do something for me, then, since it's the last time?"

"If I can."

"Play something. I've never heard you play. All these years you've come and made it ready and I've never once heard it sound the way it's meant to sound."

"I'm a tuner," he said. "Not a player."

"Play something anyway."

So he sat again and played the only thing his hands remembered without his asking them, the opening of a Schubert impromptu, the one in G-flat, the one that goes up and up like water finding its level. He played it badly. His fingers were stiff and the chip on the natural caught at him and he lost the thread halfway through and let it fall away into silence. But Mrs. Calloway stood by the door with the tray in her hands and her eyes closed, and when he stopped she did not open them for a moment, and when she did she said only, "Thank you," and showed him out.

The Pevehouse farm was four miles on, up the long hill where the hedgerows had grown wild and unkempt, no one left to lay them. Young Pevehouse—he was sixty if he was a day, but Albert had known his father and so he was young Pevehouse and always would be—met him in the yard with a dog at his heel and mud to his knees.

"Wasn't sure you'd make it up the hill in that," he said, nodding at the Morris.

"It's made it every year."

"Aye, well. Every year there's a last one." He said it without weight, the way a farmer says everything, having watched more births and deaths than any doctor. He led Albert into the kitchen where the piano stood against the wall by the range, the only warm room in the house and the worst place in the world for an instrument, the heat and the cold of it fighting through the wood season after season. Albert had told the father this thirty years ago and the father had laughed and said the piano could suffer along with the rest of them, and Albert supposed that was the philosophy of the whole valley in a single sentence.

The piano was a cheap one, a German factory thing from the twenties, and it had never held a tune well and never would. Pevehouse's mother had played it. Albert remembered her at it on a Sunday, a heavy woman with a light touch, hymns mostly, and sometimes when she thought no one was listening, the dance tunes of her girlhood. She had been dead twenty years. The piano stood where she had left it because to move it would have been to move her, and no one in that house was sentimental enough to say so but no one was hard enough to do it either.

"Does anyone play it now?" Albert asked, though he knew the answer.

"My granddaughter, when she visits. Picks at it. They learn it on the computers now, you know. Press the keys and the screen tells them if it's right." He said this with the wonder of a man describing a foreign country. "Doesn't sound like music to me. Sounds like a machine apologizing."

Albert worked. The piano fought him as it always had, the pins loose in their holes, the notes refusing to stay where he set them. He did what he could, which was less than he wanted and more than the instrument deserved, and when he was finished it was passable, which was all it had ever been. Pevehouse paid him in cash, the same notes folded the same way as every year, and walked him out to the car.

"You've a tired look about you, Albert," he said.

"I'm an old man."

"We're all old men." He whistled the dog up. "My father thought the world of you. Said you had the best ear in three counties. Said you could hear a thing going wrong in a piano before the piano knew it itself."

"Your father was generous."

"My father was a mean old bastard who never gave a compliment he didn't mean." Pevehouse grinned, showing the gaps in his teeth. "Mind the hill on the way down."

The Tindalls were not home, which Albert had half expected; they spent their winters with a daughter in Spain now and left a key under the boot scraper, and he let himself in and tuned their grand in the cold front room with his coat on and his breath showing, the dust sheets pulled back and folded over the bench. It was a good piano, a Bechstein, the best on his route, and it was the saddest of all of them because it deserved better than this, deserved hands and music and warmth, and got instead an old man once a year keeping it ready for a recital that would never come. He tuned it with particular care, as one tends the grave of a stranger more carefully than one's own, out of a kind of guilt at not having loved it enough in life. When he was done he played a few chords just to hear it, and the room was so still and the sound so full that he had to stop, because it was too much, the beauty of it in that empty house, and he could not bear it.

He locked the door and put the key back under the scraper and sat in the car for a while before he could drive.

The school came next, though it was not a school anymore. They had closed it fifteen years ago when the last families left, and the building stood empty for a time, and then a trust of some kind had taken it on and made it into a hall that could be hired for meetings and the occasional wedding, and they kept the old upright in the corner of the main room more out of inertia than intention. Albert had a key to this too. He let himself into the cold high-ceilinged room where he had sat as a boy himself, in the days when the valley was full of children, and he could still see, if he let himself, the rows of small desks and the map of the Empire on the wall and the master with his cane and his chalk. All gone now. The room held a stack of plastic chairs and a folding table and a noticeboard with a single yellowed flyer pinned to it, advertising an event that had passed two summers ago.

He tuned the piano. It did not matter and he knew it did not matter and he did it as well as he had ever done anything, because the not-mattering was precisely the point, because if he did not do it well then he would have to admit that none of it had mattered, the whole long life of it, the route and the ear and the careful hands, and that was a thing he could not admit, not yet, not while there was still one stop left.

The church was the last.

It stood at the bottom of the valley where the road crossed the stream, a small grey Norman thing with a square tower and a yew in the churchyard older than the church itself, older than anything, a tree that had been ancient when his grandfather was a boy. The services had dwindled to one a month, then one a quarter, and now Albert was not sure there were any at all; the diocese sent a priest who covered six parishes and could give this one only the leavings of his attention. But the church was kept. The flowers were changed by a rota of three women who would not let it go, and the brass was polished, and the piano—not the organ, the organ had failed years ago and the cost of mending it was beyond them—the piano that stood to the side of the chancel was tuned once a year by Albert Henning, who had tuned it for fifty years and would not tune it again.

He parked by the lychgate and sat looking at the church through the windscreen, and the frost lay white on the grass of the churchyard, and the yew was black against the grey of the sky, and he did not want to go in. He had not wanted to go in for four years, since they had buried Eleanor there, in the plot beside the one that already held their son, and he had stood at the graveside and felt the cold come up through the soles of his shoes and thought: that is the end of it, then. That is the family, gone into the ground of this valley, and only I am left above it, and not for long.

But he had a piano to tune. So he took the bag from the back seat and went up the path between the graves, past the stone he did not look at, and let himself in through the heavy door that had not been locked in eight hundred years and was not locked now.

Inside it was colder than out, the deep stone cold of a building that holds the winter in its walls all year. The light came grey and slantwise through the windows, the plain glass and the one stained panel above the altar, a Lamb of God gone dim with age. The piano stood where it had always stood, a black upright, good quality, given to the church in memory of someone whose name was on a small brass plate screwed to the front, a name no one now living remembered.

Albert set down his bag and opened the lid and did not begin.

He had stood in this place on a June evening forty years ago and watched his son walk out to this piano in a jacket bought specially for the occasion, too big in the shoulders because the boy was meant to grow into it and never did. David. He had been eleven. He had practised for months, in the cold front room at home, the same Schubert that Albert's hands had stumbled through that morning at Mrs. Calloway's, the G-flat impromptu, too difficult for him by half, and Albert had said so and Eleanor had said let the boy try, and the boy had tried.

The church had been full that evening. It seemed to Albert now, looking back, that it was the last time the church had been full, that something had begun to empty out of the valley from that night onward, though that was only the trick the mind played, finding endings where it wanted them. But the church had been full, the families still here then, the children still being born, and David had sat down at the piano that his own father had tuned that very afternoon, tuned with a care that bordered on prayer, every unison perfect, every octave clean, the action regulated and the dampers true, the best work of Albert's life poured into that instrument so that his son would have, for once, a piano that gave back exactly what was given to it.

And David had played. Not perfectly. He had stumbled in the same place Albert had stumbled that morning, the rising figure in the middle that goes up and up, and Albert had felt his own hands twitch in his lap, willing the notes right, and the boy had faltered and then—this was the thing, this was the thing he had carried all these years—the boy had not stopped. He had found his way back into it. He had let the wrong notes fall away and found the line again and gone on, up and up, to the end, and when the last chord faded into the stone of the church there had been a silence and then the people had stood, all of them, and Albert had wept, openly, not caring, because his son had done a thing that could not be undone, had made a beauty that had not existed before and now existed and would exist, in some sense, forever, in the memory of everyone in that room.

He had thought it was the beginning of something.

It was the only recital David ever gave. He lost interest in the piano the way boys lose interest, turned to other things, to bicycles and then to motorcycles, and the piano in the front room went quiet, and Albert told himself there would be time, the boy would come back to it, they always came back to it. And then there was a wet road on a November night and a bend taken too fast and a boy of nineteen who did not come back to anything at all.

Albert had buried him in the churchyard outside, twenty paces from where he now stood. And every year since, when he came to tune this piano, he had stood here for a moment in the cold and let himself remember the June evening, the too-big jacket, the figure that goes up and up, the silence and then the standing. And every year he had then done his work and packed his bag and driven home, and that had been enough, the remembering had been enough, it had been the thing that let him bear it.

But it was not enough anymore. That was what he had come to understand in the long nights of this last winter, sitting in the house with Eleanor's curtains and Eleanor's chair empty across from his. The remembering was not enough because he was nearly the last who remembered. When he was gone, the June evening would go with him. Mrs. Calloway might recall it, and one or two of the others, but they were going too, all of them, the whole valley sliding quietly under the grass, and soon there would be no one alive who had heard David Henning play the Schubert impromptu in this church, no one to know that it had happened, that a boy had made that beauty and then died before he could make any more.

He had thought, all these years, that the beauty was permanent. That was the comfort he had told himself: it existed and would exist forever. But it was not so. Beauty lived only as long as someone carried it, and the carriers were dying, and when the last one died the beauty died too, finally and completely, as if it had never been.

He sat down on the piano bench. The wood was cold through his trousers.

He should tune it. That was what he had come to do, the last stop on the last round, and he should do it well, as he had done all the others, and then drive home. But he found he could not lift the hammer. His hands lay in his lap like two tired animals, and he looked at them, the swollen knuckles, the brown spots, the hands that had heard a thing going wrong in a piano before the piano knew it itself, and he thought of all the pianos they had set right over fifty years, all the instruments made true so that people could make music on them, and how few of those people were left, and how the music had gone out of the valley like water out of a cracked basin, slowly, and then all at once.

He put his hands on the keys.

The piano was flat, of course, drifted a whole year from where he had left it, sour and dull, every note a little wrong, the whole instrument pulling away from its own center. It was exactly the piano it should not be, the piano he had spent his life preventing, and he did not tune it. Instead he began to play.

He played the Schubert. The G-flat impromptu, the one that goes up and up. His hands were stiff and the piano was out of tune and he was not a player, he had never been a player, he was only a tuner, only a man who made things ready for others. But he played it anyway, in the cold church, alone, the wrong notes ringing against the stone, the unisons beating against each other like quarreling birds, and when he came to the rising figure in the middle, the place where the boy had faltered and where he had faltered that morning, his fingers stumbled, and he stopped.

He sat with his hands on the keys.

And then, because his son had not stopped, because his son at eleven years old in a jacket too big for him had found his way back into it and gone on, Albert did not stop either. He let the wrong notes fall away. He found the line again. He played it through to the end, up and up, every note sour and every note true, until the last chord faded into the stone, and there was no one to stand, and no one to weep, and the silence came down like snow over the empty church, over the valley, over the graves outside where the frost lay white on the grass and the old yew stood black against the sky, holding, as it had always held, everything that had ever happened beneath it, for as long as it could, which was not forever, but was something, was nearly enough.

He closed the lid. He did not tune the piano. He took up his bag and went out into the cold, and he did not look at the stone as he passed it, because he did not need to. He carried it with him. He carried all of it with him, down the path and through the lychgate and into the small cold car, and he sat there a moment in the gathering dark, and then he drove home, the long way, past all the silent houses, for the last time.
