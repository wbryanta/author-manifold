The board lit up at 7:14 on Sunday evenings, as reliable as the church bells across the river, and by the third week Marguerite had stopped pretending she didn't wait for it.

She worked the long-distance positions at the exchange on Delphine Street, a room the color of weak tea, with twelve other women seated in a row before the great honeycomb of the switchboard. Their headsets pressed a permanent groove into their hair. Their fingers moved the cords with a speed that visitors mistook for indifference, plugging and unplugging, the small jacks sliding home with a sound like a knuckle cracking. Mrs. Pelletier walked the line behind them with her hands folded, listening for slang, for the personal, for any softness in the voice that might suggest a girl was treating a caller as something other than a number to be reached.

Marguerite had been there nine years. She was thirty-four and unmarried, which the younger girls discussed in the powder room as though it were a medical condition, and she had long ago perfected the operator's voice, that bright neutral chime that gave nothing and asked nothing. *Number, please. One moment. I'm ringing them now.* She could keep a hundred connections in her head, the geography of the country spread before her in trunk lines and toll tickets, Chicago to Denver, New Orleans to a farmhouse outside Bismarck, a current of human want running through her ten fingers.

The two voices belonged to a station-to-station call placed every Sunday from a number in Sacramento to a number in Galveston.

The first time, she had connected them like any other. The man in Galveston answered on the second ring, his voice rusty, careful, an old man's voice that had once been a younger man's and remembered it. "Hello? Hello, is that—"

"Go ahead, please," Marguerite said, and meant to pull the cord into monitoring and move on, because there were three other calls stacked and blinking.

But the voice from Sacramento said, "It's me, Pop. It's Daniel," and something in the way the word *Pop* came out — not a child's word but a grown man's, a word worn smooth from being turned over — made her leave the key open a half-second too long.

"Daniel," the old man said. Just the name. As if it were enough to be going on with.

She unplugged and forgot them, mostly, until the following Sunday at 7:14 when the same two numbers lit the same two jacks, and she recognized them the way you recognize a song from its first two notes.

After that she listened. It was against the rules, and she knew the rules, and she had spent nine years being the kind of woman who knew the rules and kept them, which was perhaps why she did it. She told herself it was only for a moment, only to be sure the connection held. The truth was she had become, without intending to, a third party to a conversation that did not know she existed.

They were father and son. The son, Daniel, was somewhere in his forties; she built him from the timbre of his voice, gave him a wife who was sometimes mentioned (Carol, who sent her regards, who had planted tomatoes) and two children whose report cards and broken arms and school plays arrived in installments. The father lived alone in Galveston in a house Daniel had grown up in. There had been, Marguerite gathered, years when they had not spoken. She never learned the cause. She came in after the war was over, into the long demobilization of two men learning to be gentle with each other across fifteen hundred miles of copper wire.

"You eating?" Daniel would ask.

"I eat."

"That's not what I asked."

"I had a chop yesterday. Mrs. Kowalski brought a pie."

"Mrs. Kowalski's sweet on you."

"Mrs. Kowalski is seventy-one."

"So are you, Pop."

"I'm seventy. Don't bury me early."

And the laugh — both of them at once, the old man's a dry wheeze, the son's full and surprised, as if his father's wit caught him off guard every week, as if he had spent so many years expecting the other man's silence that the jokes still arrived like gifts.

Marguerite did not laugh. She sat very still with her face composed in case Mrs. Pelletier passed behind her, and she let the warmth of it move through her like the first sip of tea on a cold morning, and when the call ended she said, into the dead line, very quietly, the things she could not say to them. *Goodnight. Take care of yourselves. I'll be here next week.*

She had a life outside the exchange, or the architecture of one. A room in a boarding house on Ursulines with a window that looked at a brick wall and, if she pressed her cheek to the glass, a sliver of the street where on Saturdays a man sold pralines from a cart. She had a mother in Lafayette who wrote letters in a hand grown spidery, asking when Marguerite would come home, when she would settle, using the word *settle* the way you might speak to a horse. She had two dresses for church and one for everything else. She had a library card and a habit of arriving early everywhere, so that she spent a good portion of her life waiting in vestibules and on benches, composed, her gloved hands in her lap, a woman so practiced at being unobtrusive that she had begun to wonder whether she still made a sound when she moved through a room.

The voices were hers in a way nothing else was. She had not chosen them, had not earned them, would never meet them; they belonged to her precisely because they asked nothing of her. She could love them — she let herself use that word, alone, in the dark of her rented room — without the love being tested, without it being required to survive contact with a real face, a real disappointment. It was a clean love. It cost her nothing and so it was the only love she could entirely afford.

In the fourth month she learned the father's first name. Daniel said it: "Walter Brennan, will you please go to the doctor like Carol asked you to." And the father said, "Walter Brennan's a movie actor," and Daniel said, "Pop," and the father said, "Fine. Fine. I'll go."

So the old man was Walter. She had a name now to hang the voice on, and that night, brushing out her hair before the spotted mirror, she said it aloud, "Walter," and then "Daniel," and the names sounded strange and intimate in her own mouth, like words from a language she'd studied but never spoken to a native.

Did Walter go to the doctor? The next Sunday she learned he had. "Blood pressure," he reported, with the contempt of a man for whom the body's failures were a personal embarrassment. "They want me on a pill. Little white pill, every morning. I'm a regular invalid."

"You're not an invalid. You take the pill."

"I take the pill."

"Every morning."

"Every blessed morning, yes, warden."

But there was something underneath it that week, a thinness in the old man's voice, a place where the breath ran short before the sentence ended, and Marguerite heard it the way she imagined a mother hears a cough through a wall. She wanted to tell Daniel: *listen to him. Listen to what's under the words.* But she was a voice on a board, a switch, a function. She plugged and she unplugged. She rang them and she let them go.

Spring came up the river warm and rotten-sweet. The girls at the exchange got engaged, one after another, like a row of lights coming on, and there was cake in the break room and the slow flash of rings held up to the window. Marguerite admired them. She meant her admiration. She went back to her position and put on her headset and waited for Sunday.

It was a Tuesday in May that Mrs. Pelletier called her into the small office behind the supply room and asked her, not unkindly, whether she was quite well.

"I'm well, ma'am."

"You've seemed far away." Mrs. Pelletier had a face like a closed purse, but her eyes, when she chose to use them, were not cruel. "Mrs. Arsenault tells me you've been holding connections past their completion."

"I check the line. To be sure it holds."

"We don't pay you to be sure it holds. We pay you to make the connection and move to the next." She folded her hands. "There are a great many people waiting to be connected, Miss Hébert. If you linger with one, the others wait longer. That is the arithmetic of it." She paused. "Do you understand the arithmetic?"

"Yes, ma'am."

"It isn't personal. None of it is personal. The moment it becomes personal you are no use to anyone, least of all yourself." And here something flickered across the closed purse of her face, some draft from a room Marguerite couldn't see into, and Marguerite understood that Mrs. Pelletier had once held a connection too long, had once let a key stay open, and had built the rest of her life around making sure no girl under her ever paid for it the way she had.

"Yes, ma'am," Marguerite said. "I understand."

She went back to the board and for two Sundays she connected Walter and Daniel and pulled the key the instant the old man said hello, and she sat in the noise of the room with her face composed, and she did not listen, and she was so lonely in those two weeks that she felt it as a physical thing, a stone swallowed and lodged below the breastbone.

On the third Sunday she listened again. She couldn't not. She told herself it was the last time, knowing it wasn't, knowing she'd tell herself the same thing next week. Walter sounded better. The pill, perhaps. He was teasing Daniel about the tomatoes, about Carol's tomatoes specifically, which had apparently come up leggy and pale. "You overwatered," Walter diagnosed from fifteen hundred miles away. "You always overwater. You loved a thing to death your whole life, son. You'd water a stone."

A silence on the line. Not the silence of a bad connection — Marguerite knew every kind of silence the wire could make — but the other kind, the human kind, where something true has been said and both parties have to decide what to do with it.

"Yeah," Daniel said finally, low. "Yeah, I guess I would."

"I didn't mean—"

"No. You're right. You're right, Pop."

"Daniel."

"I'm here."

"I'm glad you call," the old man said, and it came out of him with difficulty, the words pushed uphill. "I want you to know I'm glad you call. Every week. I sit by the phone from six. Carol thinks I'm — your mother used to say I couldn't say a thing straight to save my life. So I'm saying it straight. I'm glad."

"I know, Pop."

"All those years."

"I know. Me too. I'm glad too."

And Marguerite, at her position on Delphine Street, with three calls blinking and Mrs. Pelletier somewhere behind her in the long room, put her hand over her mouth so that no sound would escape her, and let the tears stand in her eyes without falling, because a falling tear had to be wiped and a wiped tear could be seen.

That was the best Sunday. She knew it even as it was happening, the way you sometimes do, that you are inside the good thing and it will not come again exactly like this. She walked home through the warm dark with the river smell in her nose and the praline man already packed up and gone, and she felt almost unbearably full, brimming with a happiness that had nothing to do with her and was therefore the safest happiness she had ever known. She loved two men who would die without ever knowing her name. There was no way for it to go wrong. She had at last found a love that could not be taken from her because she had never been permitted to hold it in the first place.

The next Sunday the board lit at 7:14.

But only one jack.

She felt it before she understood it, a wrongness in the pattern, the way you feel a missed step in the dark before your foot finds the absence of the stair. One light. The Sacramento number, calling. She plugged the answer cord and heard Daniel's breathing, quick, and she rang Galveston.

It rang. It rang. The far end of the wire opened onto a room she would never see, a phone she had made ring forty times now, beside a chair where an old man sat from six o'clock on. She let it ring eight times, nine. In her ear Daniel waited, and she could hear in his breathing that he was counting too.

"I'm sorry," she said — she had to say something, she was the operator, she was the voice that bridged the silence — "there's no answer at that number, sir. Shall I try again?"

"Yes," Daniel said. "Yes, try again. He's there. He doesn't move fast, he — try again, please."

She tried again. The pulse of the ring went out across the country into the warm dark of a coastal evening. Marguerite saw it, the house she'd invented, the chair, the phone on its little table, the receiver lifting itself in her mind and no hand lifting it. Ten rings. Twelve.

"Try the operator down there," Daniel said. His voice had changed. It had a child in it now, a child climbing up through the man. "Can you — is there a way to send somebody? To check on him? He's seventy. He's got a heart thing. He takes a pill. Can somebody go?"

"I can place a request with the Galveston exchange," she said, and her own voice astonished her, how steady it was, how it kept its chime, how nine years of training held the floor of it firm beneath the buckling of everything underneath. "They can send word to the local operator, who may be able to reach a neighbor. It will take a little time."

"How much time?"

"I don't know, sir."

"Okay. Do it. Please do it. I'll hold. I'll hold here, I don't care how long."

"It may be quite a long while."

"I'll hold."

She put the request through. She spoke to the Galveston operator, a young-sounding woman named Coreen, and gave her the address — she had the address, of course, it was on the ticket, it had been on the ticket every Sunday and she had never once let herself imagine the house at the end of it, and now she imagined nothing else. She asked, against every rule, against the arithmetic, whether someone could be sent to the house. Coreen said she'd see what she could do. There was a Mrs. Kowalski sometimes listed at the adjacent number; Marguerite, who knew about Mrs. Kowalski, who was seventy-one and sweet on Walter and brought pie, said yes, try her, try Mrs. Kowalski, and heard her own voice say the name as though she had a right to it.

Then there was nothing to do but wait, and keep the connection, and work.

Because the other lights were blinking. The whole board was alive with Sunday-evening longing, the whole country reaching across itself, and Marguerite had to plug and unplug, ring and connect, *number please, one moment, go ahead,* while in the corner of her board one jack held open a line to a man fifteen hundred miles away who was sitting in his kitchen in Sacramento with the receiver pressed so hard to his ear it would leave a mark, listening to the long-distance hum, waiting for her to come back to him with news.

She came back to him between calls. "I'm still here," she'd say. "We're still trying."

"Thank you. What's your name?"

She wasn't allowed to give it. "We're still trying, sir."

"You've got a kind voice," Daniel said. "Has anybody told you that? You've got a real kind voice."

She had to pull the key then, for a moment, and connect a call from Memphis to Spokane, two strangers whose business was nothing to her, and when she came back Daniel was still breathing on the line, still there, still holding, and she loved him so much in that moment that she thought she would come apart at the seams of her composure, this man she had eavesdropped into being, this stranger who had just told her she had a kind voice without knowing he'd been speaking to her every Sunday for half a year, without knowing she had memorized the sound of his laugh.

Mrs. Pelletier was coming down the line. Marguerite felt her approach like weather. She would see the open jack, the held connection, the toll ticket running and running. She would do the arithmetic.

"Miss Hébert." The voice at her shoulder. "That connection has been open eleven minutes."

"There's a welfare check in progress, ma'am. Galveston exchange. The party's father isn't answering and there's a medical concern."

"That isn't our function."

"No, ma'am."

"Pull the connection. Give him the toll desk to arrange a callback when there's news."

And Marguerite, who had said *yes, ma'am* her whole life, who had been raised on it and trained on it and built her safety out of it, turned in her chair and looked up at Mrs. Pelletier's closed-purse face, and she didn't pull the connection.

"He's holding for his father," she said. "His father has a heart condition. He lives alone. There's no one but a neighbor and we don't know yet if the neighbor's home. If I pull the connection and his father is — if something has happened, he'll be alone in Sacramento hearing it from a stranger he has to call back and ask for. Let me keep it. Take it out of my pay. The toll, take it all out of my pay, I don't care, but let me keep it open. Please."

The room had not gone quiet — the room was never quiet — but a small radius around them had, the nearest girls keeping their eyes on their boards with the particular stillness of people listening hard. Mrs. Pelletier looked at her for a long moment. The flicker again, the draft from the unseen room.

"Keep it open," she said. "And keep working." And she walked on down the line with her hands folded, and she did not look back, and Marguerite understood that she had been forgiven for something Mrs. Pelletier had never been forgiven for herself.

It was nineteen minutes before Coreen came back on the line.

"Operator? I've got a Mrs. Kowalski. She went over. She's got a key."

Marguerite's hands had gone cold on the cords. "Yes," she said. "Go ahead."

"He's all right." Coreen's voice was bright with the relief of it, the borrowed relief of a stranger who got to deliver good news. "He fell. In the bathroom, this afternoon. Couldn't get up, couldn't reach the phone. He's bruised up and he's been on the floor a few hours and he's mad as a hornet about it but he's all right. Mrs. Kowalski called the doctor. He's sitting up. She says he wants to know if his boy's still on the line, he's worried his boy is waiting."

Marguerite pulled the key into the Sacramento jack. Her vision had blurred and this time she let it; let one tear go, felt it run, did not wipe it.

"Sir," she said. "Daniel."

"I'm here." Instantly. He had not moved in nineteen minutes. "Is he—"

"He's all right. He had a fall, this afternoon, and he couldn't reach the telephone, that's all. He's bruised but he's all right. The neighbor's with him. The doctor's been called. He's sitting up and he's—" her voice did the impossible thing, it held— "he's asking whether his boy is still on the line. He didn't want you to be waiting."

The sound Daniel made was not a word. It was the sound a man makes when a thing he has been holding up with both arms is finally set down, the sound of the whole weight of it going out of him at once. She heard him say, away from the phone, to Carol, *he's okay, he fell but he's okay, he's okay.* She heard a woman's voice rise up behind his. She heard, faintly, what might have been a child.

"Can I talk to him?" Daniel said. "Can you connect me — to the neighbor's, can I—"

"I'll arrange it now."

"Wait. Operator. Wait." A breath. "I don't know your name. I know I'm not supposed to ask. But you stayed on. You stayed with me the whole time. I want you to know — you'll never know what you—" He stopped. "Thank you. Thank you for not leaving me on the line by myself."

Marguerite sat with her kind voice and her ruined eyes in the long tea-colored room full of the click and slide of all the other connections, the whole reaching country, and she said the only true thing she was permitted to say, the thing she had been saying into dead lines for months, alone, in the dark, to two men who had never known she was there.

"You weren't by yourself," she said. "Now hold the line, please. I'm connecting you."

And she did. She slid the jack home — that small sound, knuckle-crack, the sweetest sound she knew — and bridged the last of the distance between them, and pulled her key, and let them go, and turned to the next light blinking on the board, where someone else was waiting to be reached.
