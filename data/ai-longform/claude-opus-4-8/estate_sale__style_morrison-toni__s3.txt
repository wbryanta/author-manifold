They came for the piano first, two men with a quilted blanket between them like pallbearers who had forgotten the body, and Cordelia stood in the doorway between the kitchen and the front room and let them.

The piano had never been played, not properly, not by anyone who could be said to know how. Her mother had bought it the year Cordelia turned nine, secondhand from a church in Xenia that was closing its doors, and it had sat against the east wall catching the morning like a thing that knew it was decorative. Sometimes her mother dusted it. Sometimes, on the long Sundays after church when the house went quiet and the heat pressed down on the town like a wet hand, her mother would sit at the bench and press one key, just one, middle C, and let it ring out and die, and then she would close the lid and stand and go on about her day. Cordelia had asked her once, only once, why she had bought a piano nobody could play. Her mother had looked at her the way she looked at the weather. *Because nobody asked me to,* she had said.

Now two white men in work gloves were carrying it out the door, and one of them said *easy, easy on the threshold,* and the other one grunted, and the piano made a small interior sound, a settling of its strings, almost a chord, as if it were clearing its throat to finally speak and then thought better of it.

Forty dollars. The woman with the clipboard had written it down. Cordelia had watched her write it, the careless loop of the four, the round zero like a mouth, and she had said nothing because there was nothing to say. The house was full of things and the things had to leave, and a number was only a way of opening the door.

She had hired the woman with the clipboard. That was the thing she kept returning to, standing there in the doorway with her arms crossed over the front of herself the way her mother used to stand. She had paid this woman, Marsha, a sharp-faced woman from Springfield with reading glasses on a beaded chain, twenty percent of everything, to come into her mother's house and put numbers on her mother's life and let the town walk through it. *An estate sale,* Marsha had said on the telephone, *is really a kind of service. People want to feel they're taking something home that mattered.* As if the wanting were a kindness. As if the carrying-out were a form of honor.

The front room emptied by inches. First the small things, in the early morning before Cordelia had finished her coffee — the cut-glass dishes, the figurines of shepherdesses her mother had kept in the breakfront, the embroidered runners, the brass candlesticks that had only ever held candles twice, at two funerals, her father's and then the aunt's. People came through the door with that particular hunger, the polite ravenous look of strangers in a dead woman's house, and they touched things, lifted them, turned them over to read the bottoms, set them down. A young couple bought the breakfront itself for sixty dollars and a heavy older man with a sweating face bought the dining table where Cordelia had done her homework for twelve years, where her mother had laid out the good cloth on the days that called for it, where the casseroles had come after the funeral covered in foil that wept.

She watched it all go. She had told herself she would stay in the kitchen, that she would not stand sentinel over the leaving, but she could not help it. The kitchen was its own museum. She had grown up in this kitchen, on this linoleum the color of weak tea, and the linoleum at least was not for sale, was nailed down, was part of the house, and the house would be sold separately, later, by a different person, a realtor named Dale who wore a yellow tie and had already put a sign in the yard. So she stood in the doorway, in the country between the kitchen and everything else, and she watched.

The men came back for the bench. One of them looked at her, a young one, not unkind, and said, *Ma'am, you want we should leave the bench? It wasn't marked.*

It had not been marked because Cordelia had not been able to mark it. Inside the bench, under the hinged seat, there was sheet music nobody had ever played, and there were also, she knew, because she had looked that morning before the people came, three things her mother had kept there for reasons Cordelia could not name: a child's mitten, red, single, the mate long lost; a postcard from Niagara Falls with nothing written on the back; and a photograph of a man who was not Cordelia's father.

"Leave it," she said.

The young man nodded and set the bench down where it was, marooned in the middle of the emptying room, and went out after his partner, and the screen door clapped behind him the way it had clapped ten thousand times, the spring stretched and singing, and Cordelia felt the sound in her teeth.

She had not cried. She kept waiting to and she did not. People said you would, people at the church and the woman next door, Loretta, who had brought a pound cake the day after and stood in the kitchen with her hat still on saying *you go on and cry now, baby, don't you hold it.* But Cordelia had not held anything. There was simply nothing there to be held. She had stood at the casket in the front of the church her mother had attended for sixty years and she had looked at her mother's face, which the man at the funeral home had arranged into an expression her mother had never once worn in life, a kind of placid sweetness, lips slightly pursed as if about to say something forgiving, and Cordelia had thought only: *that is not how your mouth went.* Her mother's mouth had gone in a straight line, a level, a thing you could lay a ruler against. Her mother's mouth had been a closed door her whole life, and now in death they had cracked it open and what came out was a lie.

A girl of perhaps seven was standing at the bench. She had appeared without Cordelia noticing, the way children did, materializing at the edges of grown business. She had two thick braids and a gap where a tooth should be and she was looking at the bench with her hands behind her back the way a child looks at something she has been told not to touch.

"Is this for selling?" the girl said.

"No," said Cordelia.

The girl considered this. "It's just sitting there."

"Some things just sit there," Cordelia said, and heard her mother in her own voice, the flatness of it, the way it closed a subject like a lid coming down.

The girl's mother called her from the front porch — *Tanya, come on now, leave that lady be* — and the girl went, looking back once, and Cordelia watched her go and felt something move in her chest, low down, a stone turning over in deep water.

When she was that girl's age she had hidden under the dining table during her parents' fights, which were not loud fights, which were the worst kind, the kind conducted in a register below shouting, two adults pressing words at each other thin and hard as coins slid under a door. She would sit under the table with her knees against her chest and put her hands over her ears, and through her hands she would hear the low pressing of their voices and the sound of her father's chair scraping back and the front door — that same screen door, that same singing spring — and then nothing. Her father had left enough times that the leaving had stopped being an event and become a kind of weather, and then one time he left and the weather did not change back. She had been eleven. Her mother had said, *Your father's gone to Cleveland,* in the voice you use for the grocery list, and had not said anything else about it, not that day and not in the forty years that followed, except once, near the end, in the hospital, when her mother's mind had loosened from its moorings and she had grasped Cordelia's wrist with a strength that did not belong to the wasted arm it came from and said, *I should have gone with him, but somebody had to keep the house.*

The house. Cordelia looked around the front room of the house her mother had kept. The wallpaper was the same, faded now, a pattern of small blue flowers gone gray at the seams. There was a darker rectangle on the wall where a picture had hung so long the wall around it had aged differently, the way skin does under a ring you finally take off. The picture was already gone; a man had bought the frame for it that morning, not for the photograph, which was a print of a country lane in autumn, but for the frame, which he said was real walnut and they didn't make them like that anymore. He had slid the print out and handed it to Cordelia without ceremony, *here, you'll want this,* and she had taken it because her hands had opened on their own, and it was on the kitchen counter now, the country lane, leading nowhere into orange trees.

Marsha came in from the porch with her clipboard, her glasses down on her nose. "We're doing real well," she said, in the satisfied way she had, as if the doing-well were a thing they had accomplished together. "The bedroom set's spoken for. The Maytag too, though they won't haul it till Tuesday, I hope that's all right, I told them you'd be — " She looked up and saw Cordelia's face and did not finish. She had a businesswoman's instinct for the limits of things. "You holding up okay, honey?"

"I'm fine," Cordelia said.

"You don't have to stand here and watch. Lots of folks, they go to a movie. Come back when it's over."

"I know."

Marsha looked at her another moment, then made a small mark on her clipboard as if Cordelia too were inventory, as if she had been priced and found to be holding up okay, and went back out to the porch where a line of people was gentling in.

Cordelia went into the kitchen because she could not stand the bench any longer, marooned there, refusing to be the thing she had decided it would be.

The kitchen at least was hers. She did not mean that she owned it; she meant that it had not yet been broken into pieces and labeled. The clock above the stove still ran, the one shaped like a cat with a tail that swung, that had hung there since before Cordelia could remember and that had terrified her as a small child for reasons she could not now reconstruct. The canisters still stood in their row, flour sugar coffee tea, though the tea one had been empty for years because her mother had stopped drinking tea after the aunt died and Cordelia had never asked why, had filed it with the other unasked things, the great archive of silence between them that she had inherited as surely as she had inherited the house, the silence being in fact the larger estate.

She opened the refrigerator out of habit and there was nothing in it but a box of baking soda and a jar of pickles and a single egg in the door, and she shut it again. She had been clearing the house for three weeks, coming up from Columbus on the weekends, and she had thought she had cleared everything, but a house did not give itself up all at once. It kept producing. She would empty a drawer and a week later the drawer would have something in it again — a rubber band, a button, a receipt from a store that no longer existed — as if the house were grieving in the only language it had, the slow secretion of small abandoned things.

Through the window over the sink she could see the back yard, the clothesline still strung between the two iron poles, the line itself gone gray and slack, and the garden plot her mother had worked until two summers ago gone to weed and volunteer, a single sunflower standing in it crook-necked and enormous, looking down at the dirt as if it had dropped something.

Her mother in the garden. That was a thing she could see clearly, more clearly than the face in the casket, more clearly than the face in the hospital. Her mother on her knees in the dirt in a man's old shirt with the sleeves rolled, a straw hat shading her face, her hands in the earth up to the wrist, and the particular sound she made, not singing exactly, a kind of low humming, almost a moan, a sound with no tune to it, that Cordelia had heard her whole childhood and never once thought to wonder about until now, standing at the window, fifty-four years old, with strangers carrying her past out the front door. What was that sound. Where did it go down to, that sound, into what well. Her mother humming in the dirt, her mother pressing one key of a piano nobody could play, her mother saying *because nobody asked me to.* There was a whole woman in those gestures that Cordelia had lived beside for eighteen years and visited for thirty-six more and had never once met, a woman who had kept a postcard from Niagara Falls with nothing on the back, who had kept a stranger's photograph in a piano bench, who had said *I should have gone with him* and then closed her eyes and never said another word that made sense, and was now arranged in the ground out past the edge of town in a dress Cordelia had chosen because she could not think of one her mother had actually liked.

She put her hands flat on the edge of the sink. The porcelain was cool. There was a chip in it, near the drain, that had been there forever, and she put her thumb on the chip the way she had as a child, the small rough absence, the place where the white gave way to the dark iron underneath.

She heard, from the front room, the men come back for the bench after all. She heard the young one's voice — *I thought she said leave it* — and the older one's — *new tag on it, look, lady at the desk says it goes* — and she came out of the kitchen fast, faster than she meant to, her hand still wet, and she said, "No."

The two men stopped. The young one was holding one end of the bench and the older one had the seat lid lifted, looking inside, and Cordelia saw him look at the red mitten and the postcard and the photograph of the man who was not her father, saw him take them in with the flat incurious glance of a man whose whole working life was other people's left-behind things, and she crossed the room and put her hand on the lid and brought it down, gently, because she would not slam it, she would not be a woman who slammed things, and she said again, quieter, "No. Not this one. There's been a mistake."

"Marsha tagged it," the older man said, not unkindly, holding up the little orange sticker as evidence. *$15.*

"Then Marsha made a mistake," Cordelia said.

She peeled the sticker off the bench. It came up sticky and curled and she pressed it onto the back of her own hand because there was nowhere else, and she stood there with her hand on the lid until the two men, after a glance between them, shrugged and went back out, and the screen door sang and clapped, and she was alone in the room that was nearly empty now, the picture-shadow on the wall and the dust dancing in the light from the east window and the piano gone from where it had stood for forty-five years, leaving four round depressions in the floorboards, four little graves where its feet had been.

She lifted the lid of the bench again.

The mitten was very small. It would have fit a hand smaller than the gap-toothed girl's. It might have been Cordelia's own; she could not tell; her hands had been other hands once and she had no memory of them. The wool had gone stiff with age and there was a darning near the thumb, careful tiny stitches in a slightly wrong color, her mother's work, her mother sitting up under a lamp mending a mitten for a hand that had already grown out of it, or for a hand that had never worn it, she would never know which, the woman was in the ground.

The postcard. Niagara Falls in colors too bright to be true, the water a chemical green, the spray a white that no spray was, and on the back, the lines for the message, the lines for the address, the place for the stamp, all of it blank, all of it waiting sixty years for words that never came. Her mother had never been to Niagara Falls. Cordelia was almost sure. Her mother had been to Cleveland once, for the funeral of a cousin, and to Cincinnati for reasons Cordelia did not know, and otherwise had stayed in the small grid of this town her whole life, keeping the house, pressing the key, humming in the dirt. So where had the postcard come from. Who had stood at the falls and bought it and not sent it, or sent it empty, or carried it away in a pocket meaning to write something and never finding the words, and had it then traveled, hand to hand, the way small objects travel, until it came to rest in a piano bench in Ohio with nothing on it but the manufactured roar of all that falling water.

And the man. She held the photograph in the light. He was young in it, younger than Cordelia had ever been, in a hat and a good coat, standing in front of a brick wall somewhere with his hands in his pockets and a half-smile on his face, the kind of smile a man gives a person he likes who is holding the camera. He was not her father. She knew her father's face, the few photographs of it, the long jaw and the wary eyes. This was another face entirely, an open face, a face that the camera had caught in the middle of being glad, and on the back, in pencil, in handwriting she did not recognize, faded almost to nothing, were the words: *To E. — I'll keep mine if you keep yours. — R.*

Her mother's name had been Eunice. E.

Cordelia sat down on the bench, in the middle of the empty room, the lid open beside her, the three things in her lap, and she understood that she would never know. That was the whole of it, the entire and final inheritance. There had been a man named R., or whose name began with R., who had stood at Niagara Falls or had not, who had kept his, whatever his was, and had asked Eunice to keep hers, and her mother had kept it, had kept it for fifty or sixty years in the dark under the seat of a piano she had bought because nobody asked her to and could not play, and had said nothing, had hummed it down into the dirt instead, had pressed it once into a single ringing note on a Sunday afternoon and then closed the lid and gone on about her day.

*Somebody had to keep the house.*

And Cordelia, who had not married, who had kept her own apartment in Columbus very clean, who had a job she could describe in one sentence and no children and a way of standing with her arms crossed over the front of herself that she had not known until today she had taken whole from this woman like a key handed across a doorway — Cordelia sat in the emptying room and felt the stone in her chest finish its slow turning and come to rest in a new position, and from the new position something rose.

It was not crying. Loretta would have been disappointed. It was lower than crying and it did not come from the eyes. It came up out of the chest, out of the well her mother had hummed into, and it had no tune, and Cordelia heard it leave her own mouth into the empty room, into the four little graves in the floorboards, into the light, and she understood, hearing it, that she had heard it before, that she had been hearing it her whole life from the other side of a closed door, and that it was not grief exactly and it was not love exactly but was the third thing those two make when they are kept too long in the dark together with nobody to ask the right question.

Out on the porch Marsha was saying a number to somebody. The screen door sang. The sunflower in the back garden bowed its enormous head to the dirt.

Cordelia closed the lid. She kept the bench.
