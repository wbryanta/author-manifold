The board lit up at 6:42 every Sunday evening, the same jack, the same long-distance line running from Galesburg, Illinois, to a number in Sacramento, California, and Marlene had learned to expect it the way she expected the freight train that passed behind the building at seven, the one that made the coffee cups tremble in their saucers and the younger girls look up startled.

She had been at the switchboard for nine years. Long enough that her hands knew the work without consulting her, the cords pulled and seated, the keys thrown, the little dance of plugging and unplugging that the supervisors timed with stopwatches when they thought no one noticed. Long enough that she had developed the operator's particular intimacy with strangers, the way you came to know a city by the calls that passed through it—who was sick, who was dying, who was in love, who owed money. You were not supposed to listen. You were supposed to make the connection and withdraw your attention as cleanly as you withdrew the cord at the call's end. But attention was not a cord. It did not pull out so easily.

The Sunday call she had been connecting for fourteen months now.

The first time, she had thought nothing of it. A young man's voice, asking for the rate to Sacramento, person-to-person, asking for a Miss Eleanor Voss. He had the careful diction of someone counting his money as he spoke, and she had told him three dollars for the first three minutes and he had said all right, all right, like a man stepping off a high place. She placed the call. A woman answered, and there was that small held breath, that pause, before the young man said her name—Eleanor—and the woman said his—Daniel—and Marlene pulled her attention back and let them have the line.

But three minutes is not long. At the two-minute mark she was obliged to break in, to tell them their time was nearly up, and she heard them both go quiet and quick, heard them try to fit whatever they were carrying into the last sixty seconds, and the woman said *I'll write you, I'll write the long things*, and the man said *Sunday, same time*, and Marlene had cut the line and sat with her hands in her lap longer than the supervisors would have liked.

They had kept it. *Sunday, same time.* Fourteen months of it.

She knew their voices now better than she knew her neighbors'. Daniel's had a way of dropping at the ends of sentences, a midwestern flatness gone soft, and when he laughed it came out of him sideways, surprised, as though laughter were a thing that happened to him rather than something he did. Eleanor's voice was lower than you expected, a voice that seemed to be smiling even when the words were ordinary, and she had a habit of saying *now* at the start of her sentences—*now, don't you worry about that*—the way an older woman might, though Marlene had decided she was young, twenty-three perhaps, twenty-four.

She had built them out of three minutes a week the way a person builds a whole season out of one good afternoon.

She knew, because she could not help knowing, the shape of the thing between them. They had met somewhere—a wedding, she thought, from something Eleanor had said early on, a cousin's wedding in the spring of '66—and then Daniel had gone home to Illinois and Eleanor had stayed in California, and what had begun as a courtesy had become a Sunday, and the Sunday had become the hinge on which both their weeks turned. They could not afford more than three minutes. Marlene knew this the way she knew the train schedule. She had heard Daniel hesitate over the price more than once, heard him do the arithmetic of his wages against the cost of her voice, and choose her voice.

She was not in love with either of them. She wanted to be clear with herself about that, because she was a married woman of forty-one with a husband who came home from the foundry smelling of heat and metal and a son in the Army whose letters from someplace she was not allowed to name in conversation came less often than she would have liked. She was not a romantic. She had been a romantic once, briefly, a long time ago, and had put it away with her other girlhood things. What she felt for the Sunday voices was something else. It was the thing you feel for a window across an airshaft, the lamp that comes on at the same hour, the life that proves itself by its regularity. They did not know her. They would never know her. She was the silence between them, the wire, the held breath. And yet she had come to feel that she was, in some way she could not have defended, necessary—that she was keeping something alive by the simple act of plugging in a cord and pulling it out three minutes later, that the thread between Galesburg and Sacramento passed through her and that she had become, without anyone's permission, its keeper.

She had begun to do small things. She was not proud of them. When the call came in she would sometimes let the connection run a few seconds past the three minutes before she broke in—ten seconds, twenty, a gift she could give them that cost the company almost nothing and them nothing at all, because she logged the call at three minutes regardless. She told herself this was generosity. She suspected it was something closer to theft, that she was stealing the seconds for herself, to hear another sentence, another sideways laugh, to stay in the room with them a little longer.

In the autumn of '67 the calls had changed. There had come into Daniel's voice a new pressure, a thing held down. And then one Sunday in November he had said it, fast, before the line was warm: *I've had my notice. I report in January.* And Eleanor had not said anything for a moment, and Marlene, who was not listening, had stopped breathing along with her. *Now,* Eleanor had said finally, and her voice was steady the way a hand is steady when it is being made to be. *Now, we knew it might.*

Marlene had let that call run a full minute over. She had not cared who saw.

After that the Sundays had a clock inside them. Marlene felt it ticking under everything they said. They did not speak of it much—they spoke of small things, of a coat Eleanor was making, of a car Daniel was trying to keep running, of whether she might come east in the spring, of whether he might be stationed somewhere a person could get to. But the clock was in the room, and Marlene heard it, and she found that she had begun to dread the turning of the year on their behalf, as though January were coming for her own.

He had reported. She knew because there had been a Sunday in early January when the call came not from Galesburg but from a number she did not recognize, a base exchange somewhere, and his voice had a different quality, thinned and surrounded by other voices, and he had said he didn't have long, the line was a courtesy and others were waiting, and Eleanor had said *I'm here, I'm right here,* and that had been the whole of it, almost, *I'm here,* repeated, as though presence were the only thing left worth saying when there was no time to say anything else.

Then the base calls stopped, and for three Sundays there was nothing, and Marlene had sat through those Sundays with a hollowness in her chest, watching the jack that did not light, and she had understood from the silence what the silence meant. He had shipped. He was somewhere on the far side of the world where the Sunday call could not reach, where there was no operator to plug in a cord, where the thread ran out into the dark and she could not follow it.

She had thought that was the end. She had grieved it a little, privately, the way you grieve the cancellation of a program you had come to depend on, and she had been ashamed of the size of the grief, which was out of all proportion to her claim. They were strangers. She had no claim. She told her husband nothing, because there was nothing to tell that would not sound like the confession of a woman with too little in her own life, and perhaps that was exactly what it was.

But in March the call came again.

Not from Daniel. From Eleanor. A Sunday evening, 6:42, the same hour, and the request was for a number Marlene did not know, an overseas number, a routing she had to look up and arrange through three other operators down the long chain of the world, San Francisco to Honolulu to somewhere Marlene was not permitted to say aloud, and at the end of it, after waitings and clickings and the strange undersea echo of the very long distance, a man's voice came on the line, and it was Daniel, and he was alive.

Marlene had pressed her free hand flat against the edge of the board.

It cost a fortune, that call. She saw the rate and knew that Eleanor had saved for it, that this was not three dollars but many times three dollars, that the girl had put away coins and bills for weeks for the privilege of four minutes of undersea echo. And the calls became monthly then, because that was what could be afforded, the first Sunday of the month, and the quality was terrible, the voices coming and going under the noise of all that water and distance, and yet Marlene found she lived for them more than she had lived for the weekly ones, because now there was something at stake that she could name, now there was a real dark on the other side of the line and a real voice coming out of it, and her small generosities—the extra seconds, the careful routing, the patience with the failing connection—felt at last like something more than theft. Felt like care.

She learned the rhythm of the new arrangement. The first Sunday of the month, Eleanor calling, Marlene placing the long chain of the call, the two of them shouting their small tendernesses across the curve of the earth. *Can you hear me. I can hear you. Say it again.* So much of it was *say it again.* The distance ate their words and they fed it more words, patiently, the way you feed a fire that keeps going out. Daniel's voice was changed. Marlene heard that. There was a flatness in it now that was not the old midwestern flatness, a careful evenness, the voice of a man choosing not to say certain things, the voice of a man who has decided that the woman on the other end must be protected from the place he is calling from. Eleanor heard it too. Marlene could tell she heard it by the way she pressed against it, by the brightness she put into her own voice to answer his flatness, the two of them performing for each other an okayness that neither believed and both needed.

In April Eleanor said she'd had a letter, that he'd written her about a river, about how green everything was, how it was the greenest place he'd ever seen and he wished she could see the green of it without the rest. And Daniel, when he came on the line, said *did you get the—* and the water ate the rest, and Eleanor said *I got it, I got it, the green, you wrote me about the green,* and there was a silence then that was not the line failing but the two of them simply holding on, and Marlene held on with them, and let the call run, and did not break in.

In May the call was short and bad. The connection failed twice and she rebuilt it twice, San Francisco patient on the other end, and when it held at last there was time only for him to say he was all right, he was all right, not to worry, and for Eleanor to say she loved him, plainly, without the *now*, without the brightness, just the words, and Marlene heard him receive them across all that distance and say them back, and then the line went down a third time and would not come up, and Eleanor's voice came back to Marlene alone, small and asking, *are they—can you get them back,* and Marlene tried, she tried for eleven minutes that she did not log, but the line was gone, swallowed, and at last she had to say, in the voice they were all trained to use, the voice that gave nothing away: *I'm sorry. I'm not able to complete your call.* And Eleanor had said *thank you* in a way that broke something in Marlene, *thank you,* as though Marlene had done her a kindness, and hung up.

June, then. The first Sunday in June. 1968.

Marlene came in early. She had not meant to but she did, the way you arrive early for a train you are afraid of missing, and she took her place at the board and arranged her things and waited, and the other girls talked around her about the things they talked about, a wedding, a dress, the man who had been shot in California, the second one now, the brother of the first, and the country going strange around them, and Marlene listened to none of it because she was waiting for 6:42.

At 6:42 the board did not light.

She told herself it was nothing. The chain of the call was long and the lines were unreliable and the connection might be slow in coming. She watched the jack. At 6:50 it had not lit. At 7:00 the train went by behind the building and the cups trembled and a girl named Sandra looked up startled the way the younger ones always did, and the jack had not lit. At 7:15 Marlene's hands had gone cold in her lap.

She did the arithmetic she did not want to do. There were reasons a call did not come that had nothing to do with the worst thing. Eleanor might be ill. Eleanor might be away. Eleanor might not have the money this month—but no, Eleanor always had the money, Eleanor saved for this the way other people saved for a funeral, the way you save for the one thing you cannot do without. The money was not the reason. There were reasons. She made herself list them. A clock wrong in Sacramento. A line down on Eleanor's end. A flu. A car that would not start, keeping her from wherever she placed the call from, because Marlene had never known whether the girl called from her own home or from somewhere else, a booth perhaps, a neighbor's, she had never known and now the not-knowing sat in her like a stone.

But underneath the list of reasons was the other reason, the one she would not put into words, the one that the whole country seemed to be putting into words this spring, in the newspapers, on the radio, the long lists of names read out, the green place he had written about, the greenest place, the river. If the worst thing had happened to Daniel, then there would be no call from Eleanor, because the call from Eleanor existed only to reach Daniel, and if there was no Daniel to reach then the call had no destination, the thread ran out into the dark with nothing on the far end of it, and the silence on Marlene's board was the silence of a thread cut, and she would never know, because she was only the operator, she was only the wire, and no one tells the wire anything.

That was the cruelty of it, she understood, sitting there at 7:30 with the board lit up with other people's Sundays. She had given them fourteen months and then this spring of undersea calls, she had given them her attention and her stolen seconds and her cold hands and her real, foolish, unaskable love, and she was not owed the ending. She was owed nothing. She would connect ten thousand more calls in the years she had left at this board and she would never learn whether Daniel had come up out of the green or stayed down in it, whether Eleanor was a widow before she was a wife, whether the silence on this particular Sunday was a flat tire or a folded flag. The arrangement that had let her listen had never once asked whether she could bear what she heard. It had only ever asked her to make the connection, and to withdraw.

She sat through her whole shift. She did not have to—she could have asked Sandra to watch her board, could have gone to the washroom and pressed cold water to her face—but she sat through all of it, every call, plugging and unplugging, throwing the keys, saying the careful sentences, *I'm sorry, I'm not able to complete your call,* *one moment please, I'll connect you,* *that will be three dollars for the first three minutes,* and all the while one quarter of her mind stayed fixed on the jack that did not light, the way you keep your eye on a door that someone you love has gone through, in case they come back, knowing they will not come back, watching anyway.

At ten she was relieved. She gathered her coat and her things and she did not go home directly. She walked instead the long way, down to where she could see the freight yard, the lights of it, the cars uncoupled and recoupled in the dark by men she could not see, the whole machinery of distance grinding away under the few stars that Galesburg's sky allowed. She thought about the green place. She thought about a young man who said her name once a month into a roar of water and asked, *can you hear me,* and a young woman who answered, *I'm here, I'm right here,* and she thought that this was perhaps the whole of what any of them ever did for each other, across any distance, the wire humming between, *I'm here, can you hear me, say it again*—and that she had been allowed, by some accident of the switchboard, to overhear two strangers doing it, and that the overhearing had been a gift she had not deserved and would now have to give back without knowing how the story ended.

She came in the next Sunday. And the one after. She came in every Sunday of that summer at 6:42 and watched the jack, and it did not light. By August she had stopped expecting it and only kept the vigil out of a loyalty she could not have explained, the way you go on setting a place at the table for a while after, not because you believe, but because the not-setting would be its own admission.

She never learned. That was the truth of it, and she carried it. The board did not tell her. The newspapers did not tell her. The men in the freight yard, grinding the distance smaller every night, did not tell her. She came at last to live with the not-knowing the way she lived with the train behind the building, a thing that passed through her at a regular hour and shook the small contents of her and went on.

But she did a thing, that August, that she had never done in nineteen years at the board and would never do again. On a slow Sunday evening, with no supervisor near, she placed a call on her own authority, to the number in Sacramento, the original one, the home number from before, the one she had connected a hundred times. She let it ring. She told herself that if a voice answered—any voice, Eleanor's low smiling voice or another's—she would know at least that much, that someone was there, that the thread had an end. She let it ring eleven times, twelve, the way she had once held the failing line open for eleven minutes she did not log.

No one answered. The number rang into a room she would never see, in a city she would never visit, in a life she had built out of three minutes a week and had no right to. She held the cord. She listened to it ring. And then, because she was the operator, because she was the wire, because the work did not care what she could bear, she pulled the cord, and the ringing stopped, and the line went dark, and she sat in the small electric quiet of the board with her two cold hands in her lap, the keeper of a thread that ran out now into a silence she would carry the rest of her life, having given everything the work allowed her to give, which was a connection, which was attention, which was the held breath between two voices, and which was, she understood at last, never once hers to keep.
