The snow started a little after three, fine as flour, the kind that didn't look like much until you noticed you couldn't see the far gate anymore. Marion had been watching the sky go that flat pewter color since noon, and she'd known. You learned to know, after twenty-six years on a place like this. The weather had its own way of announcing itself, and this one had been clearing its throat all morning.

"Get the lamps down from the loft," she told Daniel. "The propane ones. And check the batteries in the headlamps."

He looked up from the ewe he'd been watching, a big Suffolk cross that had been off her feed and standing apart from the others, pawing at the straw. "We losing power?"

"We might."

"We might isn't the same as we are."

"No," she agreed. "But the lamps don't cost anything sitting full and ready, and they cost everything sitting empty when you need them."

That was the kind of thing James used to say, and she heard it come out of her mouth in something close to his cadence, and she saw Daniel hear it too. His face did a thing it had been doing for four months now, a kind of flinch that wasn't quite a flinch, more like a door pulled almost shut. He turned and went for the ladder without another word.

The lambing shed was an old bank barn, stone on the lower course, board-and-batten above, and it held its warmth the way old things did, slow to take it and slow to give it back. They had thirty-one ewes left to lamb out of the ninety they'd bred. The early ones were already out in the near pasture with their lambs, but these late ones had been bred late on purpose, James's idea, to spread the work, to catch a better market. He hadn't planned on not being here for it. Nobody plans on that. The truck had gone off the Ridge Road in December on black ice that nobody could have seen, and that was the whole of it, the entire mechanism of it, ice and a curve and a guardrail that gave. There was no lesson in it and no meaning and Marion had stopped looking for one around February.

She knelt by the Suffolk cross and ran her hand down the ewe's flank, feeling the shift and roll under the skin. The ewe turned her head and looked at Marion with that yellow rectangular eye, incurious, ancient. "Not yet," Marion said. "But soon. You take your time. We've got all night, looks like."

Up in the loft Daniel was knocking things over, the particular clatter of a sixteen-year-old who did everything as though he were angry at it, which, lately, he mostly was. She heard the lamps come sliding to the edge of the boards and she went and stood under and took them as he lowered them down, one and then the other, the glass globes cool and slick in her hands.

"Batteries are good in two of the headlamps," he called. "The third one's dead and we don't have the right kind."

"Two's enough. You and me."

He came down the ladder with the slow heaviness he'd put on this last year, six feet now and still growing, all of it knees and wrists and a kind of furious shyness about his own size. He'd been a small boy. James used to carry him on his shoulders out to check the flock at dusk, and the boy would fall asleep up there sometimes, and James would carry him in like that, and Marion would lift him down. None of them were those people anymore. The boy on the shoulders was gone as surely as the man under them.

The lights flickered at four-forty. Just a stutter, the bulb in its wire cage over the lambing pens dimming and surging, and then it held. Daniel looked up at it.

"Here it comes," Marion said.

It went for good at five. No drama to it. One moment there was the dull yellow light and the hum of the stock tank heater and the radio murmuring from the shelf where she'd left it, and the next there was nothing, a silence so complete it had a pressure to it, and the dark coming up out of the corners of the barn like water. Outside the wind had found its full voice and was working the boards, and snow was driving sideways past the one window in a steady silver stream.

"Headlamps," Marion said, and they put them on, and the world narrowed to two cones of light that swung and crossed wherever they looked.

"What about the house," Daniel said. "The pipes."

"They'll keep till morning. I left the taps dripping. The animals come first tonight."

She'd called it right that the work would come all at once. That was how it went when the pressure dropped; something about it brought them on, James had had a theory about it, barometric, the same as it brought on his mother's headaches. The Suffolk cross went down into the straw at half past five and started in earnest, and at the same time a yearling two pens over, a little Border Leicester they'd kept back, began to circle and nose the ground and make the low private sounds of a ewe going into it for the first time, frightened of the thing happening to her body.

"You take the yearling," Marion said.

"By myself?"

"I'll be right here. You've watched it a dozen times. You've had your hands in it. You know what it looks like when it's right."

"And if it's not right?"

"Then you holler and I'll come. But it'll likely be right. First-timers fuss but they mostly get it done. Just be calm for her. She'll take her calm from you."

He went, the light of his headlamp bobbing across the dark of the barn, and Marion turned back to the Suffolk and forgot about him the way you had to, because there was a thing in front of her that needed all of her. The ewe was straining, her sides heaving, and Marion watched the back end of her and saw, after a while, the first wet gleam of the water bag, and then the small pale points that were two hooves, and that was right, that was the way it should be, two hooves and then the nose riding just above them like a swimmer.

"Good girl," she said. "There you are. There you go."

The ewe rested between the contractions, lying flat out, her breath steaming in the cold. The barn was cooling fast without the heater. Marion could see her own breath now, and the lamb when it came would come into a cold world, and that was the danger, not the birth itself but after, the wet small body and the air at twenty-some degrees and dropping. She had towels stacked ready, and she had the box she'd made up that morning out of some instinct she was glad of now, the cardboard box lined with an old wool blanket, and a hot water bottle she'd meant to fill in the house. The house with no power, no way now to heat the water. She felt the first edge of the night's real fear go through her then, cold as the air. There would be lambs that needed warming and she had no warmth to give them.

She didn't let herself stay with that. You couldn't. You did the thing in front of you and then the next thing. James had been good at that, better than her, he had a stillness in a crisis that she'd married him partly for, and she'd spent four months trying to find where he'd kept it, that stillness, whether it was a thing she could learn now from the memory of him or whether it had gone into the ground with him on the cold day in December when half the valley stood around the hole in their good black coats.

The Suffolk gave a long groan and bore down and the lamb came in a rush, the whole slick weight of it sliding out onto the straw, and Marion was there with the towel, clearing the membrane from its nose, swinging it gently to clear the lungs, rubbing hard at the small ribcage. For a terrible second it didn't move. Then it shook its head, a tiny violent gesture, and drew a breath, and bleated, thin and indignant, and the ewe answered it with that deep guttural nicker that came from somewhere primal, and turned and began to lick.

"There," Marion said, and her voice cracked on the word, and she was glad of the dark and the wind that covered it. "There you are. Welcome to it."

She heard Daniel call her then, over the wind. Not panic in it, but uncertainty. She left the Suffolk to her work — she'd be all right, she'd done this before, three times — and went to where the boy's light was.

The yearling was down and straining and there was something at her vulva but it wasn't right, it wasn't two feet and a nose, it was one foot and then a darkness behind it, a kind of bulge, and Marion knew that shape and didn't like it.

"What is it," Daniel said. "It's been like this, she's been pushing and pushing and nothing's coming, it's just stuck like that."

"Leg back," Marion said. "There's one leg coming and the other's folded back behind. It's caught up on it. We have to go in and find the other leg and bring it forward."

"We?"

"You. Your hands are smaller than mine and she's a small ewe. I'll talk you through it." She saw his face in the crossing lights, the way it went pale and stiff. "Daniel. Look at me. She'll die and the lamb with her if it stays like this. You can do this. I've done it and James did it and his father before him and it's just a thing you do with your hands, it's not magic. You go in slow and gentle and you follow what's there and you find what you're looking for."

"I can't."

"You can. You're going to. Wash your hands in the bucket, the iodine one, push your sleeve up. Go on."

He did it. His jaw was working and his hands were shaking and he did it anyway, and she felt something in her chest that she didn't have a name for, that wasn't pride exactly, that was bigger and more frightening than pride, watching him kneel down behind the laboring ewe with his thin arm and his frightened face.

"Now," she said, kneeling beside him, putting her hand on the ewe's flank to feel the contractions coming. "When she pushes you can't go in, you'll hurt her and you won't get anywhere. You go in between, when she rests. Feel for the contraction stopping. Now — go now, slow, cup your hand, follow the leg that's there back to where it joins."

He went in. She watched his eyes go distant the way everyone's did, the way the eyes did when the hands took over and the mind was riding along behind them, reading the dark inside. "I feel the leg," he said. "I feel — there's the shoulder. I feel the chest."

"Good. Now down and back from the shoulder, there'll be the other leg folded up, you'll feel the knee bent. You have to find the foot and cup it in your hand so the hoof doesn't tear her, and bring it round and forward. Gentle. You've got time. She's resting."

"She's pushing again."

"Wait it out. Keep your hand where it is, don't lose your place, just wait."

He waited. The wind howled and threw a bucket of snow against the window and somewhere behind them the Suffolk's new lamb bleated and was answered. Marion watched her son's face and saw him be braver than he knew he could be, and she thought, absurdly, of James saying once that the boy had no grit, said it fondly, worriedly, the way fathers said such things, the winter Daniel was eleven and cried over a dead barn cat for three days. He had grit. It had only been waiting for something to need it.

"Okay," Daniel said. "She stopped. I'm — I found the knee. I'm following it down. I've got — I think I've got the foot."

"Cup it. Hood it in your palm so the hoof points into your hand. Now bring it up and around in an arc, toward you, like you're sweeping it forward. Slow. If it won't come easy, push the whole lamb back in a little first to make room."

"It's coming. It's — there." His face changed. "It's coming round."

"Now you should have two feet and the nose all together. Feel for the nose."

"Two feet. And — yeah. The nose. It's all there now, it's all lined up."

"Then back out and let her do the rest, but be ready to help her pull on the next contraction, downward, toward her hocks, with the push, not against it."

The yearling, as if she felt the change, gave a great heave, and the feet appeared, two of them now and the nose riding above, and Daniel got his hands around the slick little legs and Marion said "Now, with her, down and out," and they pulled together, the boy and his mother, and the lamb came free into the cold and the dark and the swinging light, and it was alive, it coughed and shuddered and shook its ridiculous ears, and Daniel made a sound that was half a laugh and half something else, and Marion thrust a towel at him.

"Rub it. Hard. Get it breathing good and get it dry. Quick now, it's cold, the cold's the enemy now."

He rubbed at the lamb with a kind of ferocity, and the ewe twisted around and the lamb bleated and the ewe answered and began to lick at it, and Daniel sat back on his heels in the straw with the lamb steaming between his hands and his face was wet, and he didn't bother to hide it now, and Marion didn't say anything about it. She put her hand on the back of his neck the way she used to when he was small, and he let her, which he hadn't in a year, and they stayed like that a moment in the cold with the two new lives breathing around them.

Then there was no more time for it, because the Border Leicester in the far pen was down, and a ewe Marion didn't even have her eye on had her water break, and the night closed over them.

It went on like that for hours. They lost track. The cold deepened and the wind never let up, a steady freight-train roar against the boards, and the snow piled against the lower window until it blotted out the last gray light and there was only the dark and the two moving cones from their headlamps. They worked the pens. Most of the births were easy, were right, came the way they were meant to, and those they only watched and dried and helped the lamb to find the teat. Two more were hard. One was a true mess, a set of twins tangled together, two heads and a confusion of legs, and that one Marion did herself with Daniel holding the light steady and the ewe's head, because it took more strength than the boy had and more knowing than she could talk him through in time, and she got one lamb out dead, already gone, blue and still, and the second one living, and she had no time to grieve the dead one, she set it aside in the straw and turned back to the living and that was the whole brutal arithmetic of it.

Daniel looked at the dead lamb. "Why'd that one die."

"Got hung up too long. Couldn't get air. It happens. It's no one's fault, it's just how it sometimes is." She wiped her forehead with the back of her wrist, the only clean part of her. "You save the ones you can save. You can't save them all. Nobody can. Your father lost lambs every year and good ewes too and it never stopped hurting him and it never stopped him."

She hadn't meant to say his name. It came out of her with the work, the way the work loosened things. Daniel was quiet, holding the light.

"I keep thinking he's going to come in," Daniel said. The wind nearly took it but she caught it. "The door. I keep thinking the door's going to open and he's going to come in stamping the snow off and asking how many we've got."

Marion held the living twin against her chest, rubbing it. "I know."

"Does it stop?"

"I don't know," she said, because she wouldn't lie to him, not in here, not tonight. "It's gotten so I don't expect him as often. That's all I can tell you. The expecting gets further apart. But I had it just now too, when the first one came, I thought, I'll have to tell James, and then I remembered."

"It's not fair," Daniel said, and his voice broke on it, sixteen and furious and undone, and the lamb in his arms struggled and he held it. "It's stupid and it's not fair, it was just ice, it was just a piece of road —"

"I know it. I've said all that. I've said it to the dark a hundred nights. It doesn't answer." She moved closer to him in the straw so their shoulders touched, the two lambs between them steaming. "But I'll tell you what's true. We just pulled a lamb out of a ewe that would've died, you and me, in a blizzard with no power, and it's alive in your hands right now. That's true too. Both things are true. The road and this. You have to hold them both or the first one swallows you. Your father knew that. He buried his own father in February of a hard year and lambed out the flock that same week, alone, and I asked him how, and he said you do the next thing. You just do the next thing that needs doing and the doing of it is how you stay in the world."

Daniel didn't answer, but he leaned into her, the great heavy weight of him, the boy who used to ride on the shoulders, and she took the weight, and they sat there a minute longer than they could afford.

Then they did the next thing. And the one after.

Toward what she guessed was four in the morning, the storm began to break. She felt it before she saw it, the wind dropping by degrees, the freight-train roar gentling to a moan and then to gusts with silences between, and the silences lengthening. They had eleven new lambs and ten ewes still to come, and they'd lost the one, and they'd saved, by her count, three that wouldn't have made it without hands, and the cold had taken none of the living, because they'd dried each one hard and fast and got it nursing, got that first crucial colostrum into it, the heat coming from inside the lamb where it mattered. The fear from early in the night, the box with no hot water bottle, had not come to pass. They'd outworked it.

Marion stood and her back screamed at her and her knees had set up like green wood, and she went to the window and scraped at the frost on the inside of the glass with her thumbnail and looked out. The snow had stopped. The wind had laid down. The sky was clearing from the west and a few stars showed in the gaps, hard and bright the way they only were after a storm, and the snow lay over everything blue and unbroken and the near gate was just a hump in it. It was very beautiful and very cold and her husband was four months dead and her son was asleep sitting up against the wall of the lambing pen with a lamb tucked inside his coat against his chest, and the lamb's small head poked out under his chin, and both of them were breathing slow.

She didn't wake him. There were ten ewes still to come but none of them were close, she'd checked, and the boy had earned an hour. She stood in the window with the cold coming off the glass and she watched the dawn think about beginning, the eastern sky going from black to the deepest blue, and she let herself, for one moment, feel all of it, the whole unbearable weight of the thing, the road and the ice and the empty side of the bed and the lamb that died blue in the straw. And then she let herself feel the other thing alongside it, the way she'd told her son to, the eleven living and the boy's brave hands inside the laboring ewe and his face when the lamb came round, and the warmth of him leaned against her in the dark, and James's voice coming up out of her own mouth all night long, which meant he wasn't all the way gone, which meant some of him was in the work and in the boy and would stay there.

You do the next thing. The next thing was to let her son sleep, and to fill the lamps that were burning low, and to wait for the light, and then to walk out across the unbroken snow to the house and see about the pipes, and then to come back and do it all again tomorrow and the day after, through to the end of lambing, through to grass, through to shearing, through to whatever came, one thing and then the next thing, which was how you stayed in the world.

She filled the lamps. Outside, the sky went gray, then pearl, then the first gold touched the top of the ridge where the road ran, and the new lambs stirred and bleated, and one of the ten remaining ewes lay down in her pen and began, quietly, to push.

Marion went to her.
