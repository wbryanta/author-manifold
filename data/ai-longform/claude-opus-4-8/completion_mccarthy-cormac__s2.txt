He woke gasping in the cold and she had her hand upon him and he could feel the small ferocity of her, her terror and her anger both, and beyond the thin wall of boards the wind moved over the country and through the seams of the cabin so that the single candleflame she had lit guttered and lay flat and stood again as if breathing.

It aint nothin, he said.

You was callin out things.

What things.

She didnt answer. She drew back from him and pulled the quilt about her shoulders and sat with her knees up and the swell of her against the dark. He could not see her face. The candle stood on the floor beside the bed in a saucer of its own grease and the light came up from below and made of her something carved, something out of old wood gone gray and split with weather.

I dont talk in my sleep, he said.

You done it tonight.

He lay back. The roof above him was bare poles chinked with mud and in places he could see through to a star or two where the daubing had fallen away and these he watched as a man might watch a fire and after a while she lay down again though she kept her back to him and he could feel that she did not sleep. Toward morning the wind died and a great stillness came over the land and in that stillness he heard the river going by below the bluff with a sound like something patient and without end.

When the light came he rose and dressed in the cold and went out. The world lay gray and wet and the grass was bent with dew and the smoke of other fires stood up here and there from the cabins along the bottomland thin and blue and unmoving in the windless air. He went down to the river and knelt and drank from his cupped hands and the water was so cold it made his teeth ache and he stayed there a time looking at the brown swirl of it and the things it carried, a branch, a board, once the bloated carcass of some animal turning slowly in an eddy and going on. He thought of the dream but it had begun already to leave him the way such things will and he was left only with the cold dread of it lodged somewhere beneath his breastbone like a swallowed stone.

He worked that day in the field of a man named Eller who paid him in cornmeal and a piece of side meat and at evening he carried these home wrapped in a rag and the woman took them from him without a word and set about the cooking. They ate in silence by the light of the fire. She was big now and moved with care and held herself sometimes with one hand pressed flat to the small of her back and her face when the firelight crossed it was the face of one much older than her years. She was not yet twenty. He did not know her age exactly and neither did she.

You reckon it'll come soon, he said.

She looked at him across the fire.

The young'n, he said.

I reckon when it comes it'll come.

He nodded. He had nothing further to say and there was nothing further she would say and so they sat until the fire burned low and then they lay down together in the dark and he listened to her breathing change and grow long and even and he knew she slept. He lay awake a long while for he feared the dream and feared to call out and wake her and have her look at him so. But it did not come that night and he slept and woke to the gray light again unremembering and rose to go to the fields.

So passed the winter. The child was born in the cold dark of January with a midwife come down from the head of the hollow, an old woman bent near double and smelling of snuff and woodsmoke who shooed him out into the night where he stood by the door and heard the woman cry out and heard at last the thin reedy wail of the thing come into the world and when they let him in there was blood on the rags and blood on the old woman's hands and the woman his wife lay white and spent and the child lay against her wrapped and red and squalling.

A boy, the old woman said. A fine big boy.

He looked at it. It seemed to him a strange and unfinished thing, the head misshapen yet from its passage and the eyes screwed shut against the light and the small hands working at the air as if it would pull itself up out of the very dark it had been delivered into. He did not touch it. The woman watched him and her eyes were dark and unreadable in her white face.

Aint you goin to hold him, she said.

I aint had no practice, he said.

The old woman laughed a dry rattling laugh and took the child up and thrust it at him so that he had no choice but to take it and he held it stiff and away from him and looked down into its blind crumpled face and felt nothing he could name. It quieted somewhat in his hands. Its breath came in small shudders. He thought of the dream then, the first time in months he had thought of it whole, the prophet and the buckling sun and the dark that fell like a shout, and a coldness moved through him and he handed the child back to the old woman quickly as a man hands off something hot.

He's a strong'n, the old woman said. He'll do.

They named the boy nothing for a long while. The woman called him son and the man called him nothing at all and the months went by and spring came up green and sudden along the river and the dogwood bloomed white in the bottoms and the man worked the fields of other men and the child grew. By summer it could sit and watch the world with grave dark eyes and reach for things and laugh sometimes a sudden bright laugh that seemed to come from somewhere else entire and the woman's face would soften when it did and she would lift it and press her mouth to the soft place at its temple.

He had no money. The work he got was day work and uncertain and there were weeks they ate little but greens cooked with a scrap of fat and cornbread baked in the ashes and he grew lean and hard and dark from the sun and his hands cracked and bled at the knuckles. He had come down out of the mountains to the east years before with nothing and he had nothing yet and he saw no road by which he might come to have anything and this knowledge sat in him not as despair, for he had never expected otherwise, but as a kind of weather he lived in, constant and without remedy.

There was a man came through that country in the late summer driving a wagon of pots and pans and bolts of cloth and patent remedies in brown bottles and he stopped at the cabins along the bottom and the man went down to see what he had for he had a few coins put by from a stretch of work cutting timber. The peddler was a fat man with a soft white face that the sun had burned to a peeling pink and he wore a hat with a wide brim and he sat his wagon seat and looked down at the gathered people with the eyes of a man appraising stock.

I got needles, he said. I got thread and I got cloth and I got a tonic here cure the ague and the dropsy and the rheumatism and the female complaints. I got knives. I got a knife here.

He held it up. It was a long blade with a haft of bone and it caught the light and the man who had come down stood looking at it among the others and something moved in him at the sight of it though he could not have said what.

This here knife, the peddler said, was carried by a man went all the way to Mexico and back in the war and killed I dont know how many with it. Greasers and Indians both. He give it me himself for a debt. Look at the steel on it.

How much, the man said.

The peddler looked at him.

Dollar, he said.

The man had a dollar. He had a dollar and forty cents and it was all he had in the world and the child needed shoes against the coming winter and the woman needed cloth for there was little enough of her own dress left that was not patch and mend and he knew all this and stood and looked at the knife and the want of it rose in him like a thirst. He did not understand it himself. It was a fine knife and a man ought to have a knife but he had a knife already, a worn beltknife ground down narrow with years of whetting, and he did not need another. Yet he stood there.

I'll give you eighty cents, he said.

The peddler laughed. Dollar, he said. It's worth three.

The man stood a while longer and then he reached into his shirt and brought out the coins and counted out a dollar into the peddler's soft pink hand and took the knife and turned and walked back up toward the cabin with it and the people watched him go.

The woman was at the door with the child on her hip when he came up and she looked at the knife in his hand and then at his face.

What's that, she said.

A knife.

I can see it's a knife. What'd you give for it.

He did not answer her. He went past her into the cabin and she followed him with her eyes.

How much, she said.

A dollar.

She said nothing for a moment. The child reached toward the bright blade and made a sound and she drew it back. We needed that dollar, she said.

I know it.

The boy's got no shoes for the winter.

I know it, he said again, and his voice was low and hard and she heard in it something that made her hold her tongue. He sat down on the edge of the bed and turned the knife in his hands and looked at it and the light came through the open door and lay across him and across the blade and the woman stood in the doorway with the child and watched him and her face had on it now not anger but something nearer to fear, for she had seen him do a foolish thing for no reason she could find and she did not know the man who would do such a thing and she had thought she knew him.

You aint right, she said quietly.

He looked up at her.

Somethin in you aint right, she said. It wasnt right when I took up with you and it aint right now and it'll be the ruin of us, you and me and that child both.

He looked at her a long moment and then he looked back down at the knife and said nothing and she turned and went out into the yard with the child and he heard her speaking low to it, the words he could not make out, only the murmur of them, and he sat in the dim cabin and held the knife and did not know himself why he had bought it any more than she did. Only that the want had come on him and he had no defense against it. He had never had any defense against the wants that came on him. They came up out of the dark in him the way the dream came, unbidden and without cause, and they took him and he went where they led and it had always been so.

He thought of the dream again. He thought of it more and more that fall. It came back to him sometimes whole and clear and sometimes only in pieces, the prophet's lifted arms, the puckered stumps and blind upturned faces, the sun going black at its rim and the cold that came down. And the worst of it, the part that woke him sweating in the night with the woman stirring beside him and the child fretting in its box by the wall, was the part where the sun did not come back. Where they waited in the dark and it did not return and they turned on him as the one to blame, knowing him even there, falling on him with their ruined hands. He could not say why it was always him. In the dream he had only asked to be cured like the rest of them. He had only put up his hand. And for that they fell on him as though he had brought the dark down himself, as though by asking he had broken something that held the sun in its place.

He told the woman none of this. There were no words in him for it and even had there been he would not have given them to her, for she watched him now with that wariness and he would give her no further reason. The winter came down hard out of the north and the river froze along its margins and the wind came through the cabin walls and they huddled close to the small fire and the child grew thin and coughed in the night a deep wet cough that frightened the woman though she did not say so. There were no shoes for it. The man had bought the knife and there was no money for shoes and the child went barefoot in the cold or the woman wrapped its feet in rags and held it close and the man watched and said nothing and the knife hung at his belt where he had taken to wearing it though it had no use he could find.

In the dead of that winter the child sickened in earnest. The cough deepened and a fever came on it and it lay in its box and burned and the woman sat over it through the nights with a wet rag and bathed its head and the man fetched water and wood and sat helpless against the wall and watched. The old midwife came again, bent and snuffsmelling, and looked at the child and shook her head.

It's in the chest, she said. The lung fever. You got to keep it warm and pray.

We aint got much wood, the woman said.

Then get more, the old woman said, and looked at the man where he sat.

He went out into the cold and the snow lay on the ground blue under a high white moon and he went into the timber along the river and cut wood by the moonlight until his hands were numb past feeling and he carried it back armload by armload and built the fire up until it roared in the small space and the cabin grew warm for the first time all that winter. But the child did not mend. It burned and coughed and grew weak and on the third night of the fever it lay quiet at last and its breathing came shallow and far apart and the woman held it and rocked and made no sound and the man sat across the fire and watched and there was nothing in the world he could do and he knew it.

Toward dawn the child died. The woman knew the moment of it though it made no sound and she went stiff and then she bent over the small body and a sound came out of her that the man had never heard, low and tearing, an animal sound, and she rocked and held it and would not give it up. The man rose and came around the fire and stood over her and did not know what to do with his hands. He put one out and laid it on her shoulder and she struck it away without looking at him.

You done this, she said. Her voice was strange and flat. Her face when she lifted it was wet and ruined and her eyes were not her eyes. You done this. The shoes. The fire. We had no wood account of you and your knife and now look. Look at him.

I cut wood, he said. I cut all the wood there was.

Too late, she said. Too late and not enough. You and your knife.

He looked at the knife at his belt and then at her and then at the small still thing in her arms and the cold came down in him the way it came in the dream, the sun going black, the dark falling like a shout, and he understood for the first time and all at once and with a terrible clarity what the dream had been telling him all along. That he was the one. That the dark came where he went and they were right to know him and right to fall on him for it. He had only put up his hand. He had only wanted, the way men want, and the wanting had brought it down.

He went out of the cabin into the gray dawn. The snow had stopped and the sky to the east was paling and the river ran black between its white banks and the cold lay over everything still and complete. He stood in the yard and looked at the country, the bare timber, the smoke standing up from the other cabins where other men woke and went about their living, and he heard behind him the woman's voice rising and falling in the small dark room with her dead child and he could not go back in. He stood and he did not weep for there was no weeping in him. There was only the cold and the certainty.

After a while he walked down to the river. He took the knife from his belt and held it and looked at it, the long blade, the bone haft worn smooth, the thing he had given his last dollar for and could not say why. The light was coming now and it touched the blade and the blade was bright. He thought of the peddler's soft pink hand. He thought of the man who had carried it to Mexico and killed he did not know how many. He drew back his arm to throw it into the river, into the black running water, to be rid of it and the want it stood for, and he held it there a moment with his arm drawn back and then he did not throw it. He could not. The want held him even then. He lowered his arm and looked at the knife and then he put it back in his belt and stood by the river and the sun came up at last over the eastern hills, red and cold, and laid its light across the snow and across the water and across the man standing there, and it did come back, the sun did come back, and that was somehow the worst of it. That it came back and shone on him as on everything else and made no distinction and judged him not, and he was left to do that himself, standing in the new light with the knife at his belt and the dead behind him and the whole long country opening out before him gray and white and indifferent to the end of time.

He went back up to the cabin when the sun was well up. The woman had quieted. She sat against the wall with the child in her lap and she had wrapped it in the one good piece of cloth there was and she did not look at him when he came in. He took the spade from the corner and went out and found a place on the rise above the river where the ground was a little softer for the sun upon it and he dug there through the morning, the spade ringing on the frozen earth, breaking through inch by inch, his breath standing white about him, until he had a hole deep enough. Then he went in and the woman would not give him the child and he did not force her but waited and at last she rose and carried it out herself and they went up to the rise together and she knelt and laid it in the ground and the man stood by with the spade and when she had done and risen and stood back he filled it in. She watched him. Neither of them spoke any words over it for they knew none and there was no one to say them.

When it was done they stood looking at the mound of fresh dark earth on the white hillside.

I cant stay here, she said.

He said nothing.

I cant stay where you are, she said. Her voice was empty now, scraped clean. I dont blame you. Maybe I do. I dont know. I cant tell no more. But I cant stay where you are. Theres somethin follows you and I dont want it followin me no further.

He looked at her. Where'll you go, he said.

My people are over the mountain. I'll go back to them.

It's winter.

I'll go when it breaks.

He nodded. There was nothing in him to argue it. She was right and he knew she was right and he had known it the day she said somethin in you aint right and he had bought the knife anyway, the way he had done everything, helpless before the wanting. He looked out over the river and the country beyond it running west, the way the three men had gone in the gray of that first morning a year and more ago, strung out in silhouette and dropping into the blue fold of shadow, and he felt the pull of it in him now, that west, that going, the dark that fell where he went and that he carried with him and could not put down.

I'll go on, he said. I'll go on and youll go to your people and that'll be the end of it.

She did not answer. She turned and went back down to the cabin and he stood a while longer by the small grave and then he followed her down. And so it was settled between them with no more words than that, two people standing on a winter hillside above a black river with their child in the ground between them, and beyond them the country lay white and silent and waiting, as it had waited from the beginning, indifferent and without mercy and without end.
