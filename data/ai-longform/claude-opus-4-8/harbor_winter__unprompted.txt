The cannery whistle still blew at noon, though there was less and less to call the men back to. Otto Lindqvist heard it from his kitchen, where he sat with his coffee gone cold and the window fogged white with his own breathing, and he counted the seconds the way he had counted them for fifty years, waiting for the long second blast that meant the morning catch was in. It came, faint and apologetic, as if the whistle itself were ashamed to be heard. Then nothing. He drank the cold coffee anyway. Waste was a sin his mother had beaten into him before the war, and the habit had outlived her, outlived the war, outlived nearly everything that had ever mattered.

His granddaughter Mira was upstairs. He could hear her moving, the particular weight of a nine-year-old who walked as though the floor might be persuaded to forgive her. She had been with him since October, when her mother — his daughter, Ann — had gone down to Seattle to find work, or to find herself, or to find some man Otto had never been introduced to and did not intend to inquire about. Ann called on Sundays. The calls were short and full of the brittle cheer of a person who has decided that everything is fine and dares the world to disagree.

"She eating?" Ann would ask.

"She's eating."

"She doing her schoolwork?"

"She's doing it."

"You're not letting her run wild down at the water."

To this he never answered directly, because the answer was that he was teaching her the water, which was the only thing he had left to teach, and he did not think Ann would understand it as anything other than an old man's foolishness, a danger, one more reason to wonder whether she had been right to leave the girl with him at all.

Mira came down in her socks, dragging the wool sweater that had been Otto's and before that had been no one's, bought too large and worn into the shape of whoever held it longest. It hung to her knees. Her hair was the pale Lindqvist color, the color of dune grass in winter, and she had Ann's wary eyes, eyes that measured a room before entering it.

"It's not raining," she said. This was her way of asking.

"It's never raining the way you'd want it to," Otto said, which meant yes.

They walked down Harbor Street, past the Nystroms' house with its plywood window, past the Lutheran church that now shared a minister with two other towns and so held services only on alternating Sundays, past the laundromat that had become a place where men sat in the warmth and did not wash anything. The town was named Kalsett, after a chief or a captain or a cannery man, depending on who you asked, and it had been dying for so long that the dying had become simply the weather of the place, a thing nobody remarked on anymore. The young people left. The boats that came back came back lighter. And in November the cannery's owners, a company headquartered in a city none of them had ever seen, had announced that the plant would close in the spring, when the last of the season's contracts were filled.

Otto had not worked in the cannery. He had been the harbor pilot, the man who took the big boats through the channel, who knew where the sand had shifted and where the rock waited just under the surface like a held breath. For forty-one years he had climbed the pilot ladder in all weathers and brought them in. There was no harbor pilot now. The boats that still came were small enough to find their own way, and the big ones did not come at all.

"Tell me what you see," he said, when they reached the end of the public pier.

Mira looked. She had learned, in two months, that this was not a question to be answered quickly. She had learned that the wrong answer was to say *water*, the way she had the first time, and that the right answer was something he would not give her, something she had to find by looking until the looking changed into seeing.

"It's flat," she said. "Out past the buoy it's flat and dark. But here close it's all — wrinkled."

"Wrinkled." He nodded slowly. "Why is it wrinkled here and flat there?"

She frowned. The wind came off the water and pushed her hair across her face and she pushed it back with the impatience of someone who had more important things to attend to. "The wind?"

"The wind's blowing the same everywhere. You feel it the same here as there?"

"Yes."

"So it's not the wind that's different. It's what's under." He crouched, his knees complaining, so that his eyes were level with hers and they looked out together along the same low line. "The water tells you what the bottom's doing. Where it's shallow, where the current scrapes the sand, it gets nervous. It wrinkles up. Where it's deep it can afford to be calm. A man who knows how to read that doesn't need a chart. The water *is* the chart. It's drawing the bottom for you, all day long, free of charge, and most people walk right by it and see nothing."

Mira looked at the wrinkled water and the flat water and Otto watched her face and saw the moment the two things separated for her, saw the bottom of the channel rise up into her understanding, and he felt something he had not felt in a long while, a kind of warmth that had nothing to do with the cold coffee or the cold morning, the particular pleasure of handing a thing across the gap between a life ending and a life beginning and feeling it caught.

"Like a map that draws itself," she said.

"Like that."

They stood a while longer. A single boat was coming in, the *Margrethe*, owned by Pers Halvorsen, who was sixty-eight and fished alone now that his son had gone to work on the rigs in the Gulf. The boat came slow, riding low, which meant Pers had done all right, or all right enough. Otto raised a hand and Pers raised one back, two old men acknowledging each other across the water in a language that needed no words and had no future, because there was no one to learn it from them, or there had not been until October.

"Watch how he comes in," Otto said. "He's reading it too. See how he swings wide there? He's not being careless. There's a bar that builds up off that point every winter, the river pushes the sand down and it piles. He knows it without thinking. His hands know it. He's been coming in over that bar for fifty years and his hands turn the wheel before his head even asks the question."

"Did your hands know it?"

"My hands knew everything," Otto said, and it was not a boast, only a fact, and a sad one, because hands that knew everything and had nothing left to know were a kind of grief he had no name for.

They walked home along the shingle, where the storms had thrown up a tangle of kelp and a single rubber boot and the white knuckle of a log worn smooth by a thousand miles of ocean. Mira collected things. Her pockets, by the time they reached the house, held two crab shells, a green bottle-glass pebble, and a flat stone with a hole worn through it, which Otto told her was a lucky stone, a hag stone, that the old fishermen used to hang in their boats to keep the witches off the catch.

"Did it work?"

"The catch came or it didn't," he said. "But the stone gave them something to do with their fear. That's worth more than you'd think."

That night he made fish and potatoes, because it was what he made, and Mira ate it without complaint, which was itself a kind of progress, because in October she had pushed it around her plate and asked for the cereal Ann let her have. Across the table Otto watched her eat and thought about the spring, when the cannery closed and the last reason for the town's existence finally gave out, and he thought about Ann's Sunday calls and the question underneath them, which was when, and the answer he did not have.

"Grandpa," Mira said. "What happens to the water when the cannery closes?"

He set down his fork. It was the kind of question children asked, that went sideways into the truth of things while the adults were still circling.

"The water doesn't care about the cannery," he said. "The water was here before the cannery. It'll be here after. The bar will build off the point and the channel will wrinkle and the tide will come and go whether there's anyone to read it or not."

"But then why learn it?"

He thought about that for a long time, long enough that she went back to her potatoes.

"Because the water doesn't need you to read it," he said finally, "but you need to read the water. You understand the difference? It's not a thing you do for the ocean. It's a thing you do for yourself. So you're not just — floating on top of your own life, blind, taking whatever comes. So you know what's under you."

She nodded as though she understood, and perhaps she did, in the way children understand things before they have the words to hold them, and Otto thought that this might be the only inheritance he had to give her, this and the house, which was worth nothing now, and the lucky stone with the hole worn through it.

The winter deepened. The days shrank down to a few gray hours bracketed by dark, and the storms came up the coast one after another, each one announced by Otto reading the sky and the barometer he kept in the hall, a brass instrument his own father had owned, its glass face crazed with age. He taught Mira to read it too, the slow fall before weather, the way the needle's drop told you not just that a storm was coming but how hard and how soon.

"The water remembers the weather before the weather gets here," he told her. "A swell will reach you a day ahead of the storm that made it, way out at sea. You learn to feel that long slow lift under a boat, that's the ocean telling you something's coming. It's already happened, somewhere far off, and the news is traveling toward you across the whole face of the deep."

"Like light from a star," Mira said. She had learned this in school, that the stars you saw might already be dead, their light still crossing the dark.

Otto had not thought of it that way, and it stopped him, because it was exactly right and he had needed a nine-year-old to say it.

"Yes," he said. "Just like that."

She started bringing her notebook to the pier. The teacher had given them notebooks for nature observations, and Mira filled hers with drawings of the water, the wrinkled places and the flat places, arrows for the current, the shapes the eddies made off the pilings. She labeled them in her careful round hand. *Calm. Nervous. The bar. Where the river comes.* Otto looked at these drawings and saw that she was making her own chart, the chart that lived in his hands and had never been written down because there had never been any need, the knowledge passing from old hands to young ones in the climbing of ladders and the turning of wheels, and now passing instead into a school notebook in green crayon, because the old way was closing like the cannery was closing, and a notebook was better than nothing. A notebook was, in fact, a great deal.

In February the worst storm of the winter came up the coast. Otto read it three days out, the barometer beginning its long slide, the swell coming in heavy and slow so that even from the kitchen window he could see the harbor lifting and settling like a sleeper's chest. The radio talked about it. The few boats still working stayed in. Pers Halvorsen tied the *Margrethe* with extra lines and came up the hill to check on Otto, the way the old men checked on each other now, because there was no one else to do it.

"You set?" Pers asked at the door, not coming in, water running off his oilskins.

"I'm set."

"The girl?"

"She's set too." Otto paused. "You want coffee."

"I want coffee," Pers admitted, and came in, and the two of them sat in the kitchen while the wind began to find its voice in the eaves, and Mira sat on the stairs out of sight and listened to them talk, the way children listen, learning the shape of a world they are not yet permitted to enter.

"They're really closing it," Pers said. "Karlsen got his letter. April."

"April," Otto agreed.

"Forty years I been bringing fish to that dock." Pers turned his cup in his hands. "What's a man supposed to do. Drive two hours to sell to the plant at Anacortes, lose the money in the gas. There's no — " He stopped. He was not a man who finished sentences that hurt to finish.

"No," Otto said, which was agreement with all of it, the whole unfinished thing.

"You're lucky you got out when you did. Pilot pension. You don't have to watch it the way the rest of us do."

Otto did not say that he watched it every day, that he had watched the harbor empty out boat by boat for twenty years, that being retired from a job that no longer existed was not the same as being spared. He did not say that the worst part of watching was knowing the water didn't care, would go on wrinkling and flattening over a bottom no one read, that the channel he had given his life to would forget him entirely and lose nothing by the forgetting. He poured more coffee instead.

The storm came in that night with a violence the season had been promising. Otto woke at two and lay listening to it, the house shouldering into the wind, the rain coming sideways against the glass like handfuls of gravel. He got up and checked the barometer out of habit, though there was nothing to be done with what it told him, and he stood in the dark hall in his long underwear, an old man who had once been needed and was now only awake, and he heard Mira's door open.

"Grandpa?"

"I'm here. Go back to bed."

But she came and stood beside him, and they looked together out the hall window, down to where the harbor was a churn of black and white, the boats jerking at their lines, the streetlight at the foot of the pier swinging so that the whole scene lurched in and out of vision.

"Is the *Margrethe* going to be okay?"

"Pers tied her well. She'll be okay."

"How do you know?"

"I don't know," Otto said, because he had decided, somewhere in these months, that he would not lie to her about the water, that the water was the one place where he would always tell her the truth. "I think she'll be okay. The lines are good and she's in the lee of the breakwater. But the ocean does what it wants. You can read it, you can know it better than you know your own face, and it can still take everything from you in a night. That's the bargain. You learn it not so it'll be safe. You learn it so you'll have a chance."

Mira was quiet, watching the boats fight their lines.

"That's scary," she said finally.

"Yes," Otto said. He put his hand on her head, on the dune-grass hair. "It's the most honest thing there is. Everything else will lie to you about how much it can hurt you. The water never does. The water tells you exactly what it is, every minute, if you know how to look. There's a comfort in that, even when it's terrible. Especially when it's terrible."

They stood there a long time, and at some point Mira leaned against him, her weight settling against his hip, trusting and heavy with sleep, and Otto stood very still so as not to disturb her, his bad knees aching, the storm roaring on, and he felt that he would have stood there until morning, until the storm blew itself out, rather than move and lose the warmth of her against his side.

The *Margrethe* held. In the gray clearing after the storm they walked down and found Pers already on the pier, surveying his boat, and he raised a hand to them and there was something in the gesture that was almost lightness, the relief of a man who has kept the one thing he has left to keep. The harbor was littered with storm-wrack, planks and floats and a whole section of someone's dock that had torn loose upcoast and come to rest against the breakwater. The water was the flat exhausted color of aftermath.

"Look there," Otto said to Mira, pointing past the point. "See how the bar's changed."

She looked, and Otto watched her look, and he saw her see it — the wrinkled water had moved, the storm had pushed the sand into a new place, the river had cut a new channel through it, and the chart of the bottom had been redrawn overnight.

"It's different," she said, with wonder. "It moved."

"Every storm moves it. That's why you can't just learn it once. You have to read it new every day. The man who thinks he knows the water, who stops looking because he's sure — " Otto shook his head. "That's the man it takes."

Mira took out her notebook, there on the cold pier, and began to draw the new bar, the new channel, her tongue between her teeth, her crayon moving in the careful arrows. Otto watched her chart the changed bottom and felt the thing pass between them again, the catch across the gap, and he understood that whatever happened in April, whatever happened with Ann and Seattle and the closing and the dying town, this had happened, this was real, the girl had learned to read the water and the reading was hers now, was in her hands and her notebook and her dune-grass head, and the water could not take it back, and neither could the cannery, and neither could he.

In April the whistle blew for the last time. They walked down to hear it, the whole town that was left, fifty or sixty people standing along Harbor Street in the thin spring sun, and the whistle blew its noon blast and then, after a pause, blew again, the long second blast that had always meant the catch was in, that Otto had counted the seconds for across fifty years, and then it blew a third time, which it had never done, a long held note that ran out across the water and over the empty boats and up the green hills behind the town, and went on and on until it died, and did not blow again.

Some of the men wept. Pers Halvorsen stood with his cap in his hands. Otto did not weep, but he felt Mira's hand find his and hold it, and he held it back, and they stood there together listening to the silence the whistle had left, which was deeper than the silence before it had blown.

"Grandpa," Mira whispered. "It's quiet."

"Yes," Otto said.

"What's going to happen now?"

He looked out past the point, where the water wrinkled over the bar the spring tides had built, the chart drawing itself as it had drawn itself before the cannery and would draw itself after, indifferent and honest and free of charge to anyone who knew how to look. Ann was coming in June. She had a job now, a real one, an apartment with a second bedroom, and she would take Mira south to the city where the stars were drowned in light and no one read the water, and Otto would stay in the house that was worth nothing, in the town that had run out of reasons, and that was how it would go, because that was how it went, and there was no whistle that could call any of it back.

But the girl knew the water now. She would carry it. Wherever she went she would not float blind on the top of her own life; she would know what moved beneath her, would feel the long slow swell of trouble coming a day ahead across the deep, would understand that the things that could hurt you most were the ones that told you the truth. He had given her that. It was the only chart he had, and he had handed it across, and it was caught.

"Now," Otto said, "we read it new. Every day. Same as always."

And he led his granddaughter down to the water to see what the storm had changed.
