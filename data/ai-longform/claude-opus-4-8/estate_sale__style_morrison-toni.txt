The first thing they took was the piano nobody could play.

Cordelia watched the two men angle it through the doorway she had been carried through as a newborn, the doorway she had been pushed through at seventeen with a suitcase and her mother's voice trailing behind like smoke that wouldn't catch, and she thought: that piano never once made music in this house. Her mother had bought it from a church that was closing, bought it the way some people buy mirrors, to have a thing in the room that suggested a self larger than the one occupying the chair. It sat in the front room for thirty-one years collecting the dust of three generations and the fingerprints of children who pressed the keys to hear if it was real, and now a white woman in a quilted vest was paying forty dollars to carry it into a truck and call it a find.

"You sure about the price?" the woman asked, not because she wanted to pay more but because she wanted to be told she had done well.

"I'm sure," Cordelia said.

The men grunted. The piano groaned on its casters, a sound almost like a voice, almost like her mother saying *mind the threshold*, and then it was over the lip of the door and into the light and gone, and the front room had a rectangle in it now, a paler place on the floor where the sun had been kept out for three decades, and Cordelia looked at that rectangle the way you look at the place a tooth used to be.

She had come up on the bus from Cincinnati. She could have driven but she did not want the car here, did not want the means of leaving sitting in the driveway tempting her every hour, because she knew herself, knew that if there had been a car she would have gotten in it by noon and let the whole sale collapse into the hands of the auction company and their forms. So she came on the bus and the bus would come back at five and that was the shape of the day, that was the box it came in, and inside the box was the house, and inside the house was everything.

People moved through the rooms with the particular reverence and greed of strangers handling what the dead had loved. They held things up to the window. They turned the saucers over to read the bottoms. A man in a Cleveland Browns cap was crouched at the bookshelf running his thumb along the spines, and Cordelia knew without looking that he would find the Bible with the family written into it, the births and the one death her mother had recorded in her own hand before there was no one left to record hers, and she had meant to take that out, had meant to and had not, and now she crossed the room quickly, too quickly, and the man looked up.

"This for sale?"

"No," she said, and took the Bible from the shelf, and the leather was soft as a worn cheek under her fingers. "That's not for sale."

"Wasn't marked."

"It is now."

He shrugged and went back to the spines, and Cordelia stood holding the Bible and feeling foolish, feeling the heat come up her neck the way it had when she was a girl and her mother caught her in a lie, that particular heat, the body's confession before the mouth could be made to speak. She opened the cover. There they were. *Joseph Lyle Burch, b. 1911.* *Vesta Mae Burch, b. 1914.* Her grandparents, names she knew the way you know the names of rivers, having crossed them but never lived along their banks. And then her mother, *Della Rose, b. 1937,* the ink a different black, a later black, and her own name, *Cordelia Ann, b. 1949,* and her brother who had drowned at nine, *Tertius James, b. 1952, d. 1961,* and her mother had written the death in the same hand, the same pen probably, kept all that time in the drawer of the secretary that a young couple was at this moment examining in the dining room, opening and shutting the little doors, exclaiming over the secret compartment that was not a secret to anyone who had lived here, that her mother had used to hide nothing more dangerous than rubber bands and stamps.

She put the Bible in her bag.

The house was small and full of light. That was the thing she had forgotten, that her mother's house was full of light, because in memory it was always evening in this house, always the lamp lit and her mother's silence stretched across the rooms like cloth left out to dry that nobody comes to fold. But it was an afternoon house. It faced southeast and the morning came in the kitchen and by midday it had moved through to the front, and Cordelia, who had not been here in the daytime in twenty years, who had come only for the funeral and that had been a December funeral, grey and indoor, was startled by how the light fell, by how it lay along the floorboards in long warm planks, and she thought: she lived in all this light and I never knew it. I thought she lived in the dark because I left her in the dark in my mind so that I would not have to think of her living.

A woman approached with a casserole dish. Pyrex, the kind with the blue cornflowers, and Cordelia had eaten out of that dish more times than she had eaten out of any other vessel on earth, macaroni and the tuna thing and the apple brown betty that her mother made when there was money for apples, and the woman held it up and said, "Is this fifty cents like the sticker says or is the sticker old?"

"Fifty cents," Cordelia said.

The woman beamed. She had won. She carried the cornflowers away pressed against her stomach and Cordelia thought of all the church suppers that dish had been to, riding in the back seat wrapped in a dish towel, her mother's hands at ten and two, her mother's good coat, her mother's lipstick that she wore to church and nowhere else, *Fire and Ice*, a color too bold for her face, a color she had chosen for who she had wanted to be and worn against the face she actually had, and Cordelia had hated that lipstick as a child, had been embarrassed by it, the way it announced a want, and now she would have paid every dollar in her purse to see her mother's mouth in it one more time.

But you didn't come, she said to herself. You didn't come and now you want.

There was a girl on the stairs. Cordelia hadn't seen her come in. A Black girl, maybe ten, maybe eleven, in jelly sandals and a yellow shirt, sitting on the fourth step from the bottom with her chin in her hands, watching the strangers the way you watch fish in a tank, with a flat and patient curiosity, and Cordelia did not know whose she was. There were a few families in town she might belong to, the few that were left, the few that hadn't gone north to Cleveland or further north to nothing, and Cordelia thought she should ask, should say *who do you belong to*, the way grown people always said it to her when she was that age, *who do you belong to, baby, whose are you,* as if a child were a thing that issued from a known source like water from a known spring.

"You waiting on somebody?" Cordelia asked.

"My granny's looking at the dishes."

"Your granny got an eye for dishes?"

The girl considered this. "She got an eye for everything," she said, with the loyalty of a child who has decided her people are the standard against which the world is measured, and Cordelia laughed, the first laugh of the day, and it surprised her, came up out of her like water out of a pump that's been worked, brown and then clear.

"This your house?" the girl asked.

"It was my mother's house."

"She dead?"

"Yes."

The girl nodded slowly, taking the information in the way children take in the largest facts, sideways, without ceremony, filing it next to the price of candy and the names of the dead presidents. "My granny say everybody in this town used to know each other," she said. "She say you couldn't sneeze without three people saying bless you and one of them being mad about it."

"That's true," Cordelia said. "That's exactly true."

"You sneeze and somebody be mad?"

"Somebody always be mad," Cordelia said. "About the sneeze or about something. The sneeze just give them a place to put it."

The girl smiled, and her smile had a gap in it, a tooth gone the natural way that teeth go at that age, the body making room for itself, and Cordelia thought of the rectangle on the floor where the piano had been, how the body of the house was making room too, how it was losing its teeth one at a time to the people who came with their fifty cents and their quilted vests, and how there was something almost right about it, almost merciful, that the house should be emptied this way, by hands, by the hands of strangers carrying things out into the light, rather than all at once by the bulldozer that she knew was coming, that she had signed the paper for, because the company that bought the lot did not want a house on it, wanted only the ground, the flat fact of the ground under the house, the way the bank had not wanted her mother but only the labor of her mother's hands for forty years and then, when the hands could no longer, had wanted nothing.

The girl's grandmother appeared at the foot of the stairs. Cordelia knew her. The knowing came up slow, a face surfacing through the years like a body through water, and then it broke and there it was: Pearl Ann Tilghman, who had sat two rows ahead of her at Second Baptist, who had a voice that could lift the roof and an opinion about everything that lived, and who was old now, was a grandmother now, was carrying a stack of saucers wrapped in newspaper against the front of her dress.

"Cordelia Burch," Pearl Ann said. "I heard you was up here."

"Pearl Ann."

"Look at you." Pearl Ann looked at her, frankly, the way the women of that church had always looked, taking inventory, weighing, the look that said *I knew your mother and so I have a claim on the right to assess you,* and Cordelia stood under it the way you stand under rain you've decided not to run from. "You favor her," Pearl Ann said. "More now than you used to. You got her around the mouth."

"I always heard I favored my daddy."

"Your daddy." Pearl Ann said the word and put it down, set it on the floor between them and stepped over it. "Well. You got Della in you. It comes out as a person ages. The face gives up trying to be its own and goes on back to the people it came from." She shifted the saucers. "These was your mother's good ones. The Sunday ones. You want them?"

And Cordelia opened her mouth to say no, because she had a small apartment in Cincinnati and a cabinet that was already full and no one to set a table for, no Sundays that required a good saucer, no occasion in her whole foreseeable life that called for cornflowers or gilt edges, and what she said was, "How much are they."

"Honey, I'm not paying you for your mother's dishes."

"They're for sale, Pearl Ann. That's what today is."

Pearl Ann looked at her again, and this time the look was different, this time the look went past the face to the thing behind the face, and Cordelia felt herself seen the way she had not been seen in years, seen all the way down to the place where the grief sat curled and unfed, and Pearl Ann said, gently, the way you speak to someone standing closer to an edge than they know, "You didn't come up to see about her at the end."

It was not a question and Cordelia did not answer it.

"I'm not saying it to hurt you," Pearl Ann said. "I sat with her. Me and Vinette and a few others, we took turns. She wasn't alone, if that's the asking on your mind."

"It wasn't," Cordelia lied.

"It was," Pearl Ann said. "It's the asking on everybody's mind. That's the only asking there is, at the end. Was I alone. Will I be." She set the saucers down on the step beside the girl, who had gone still, who had the instinct of children for the moment when grown talk turns into something with weight. "She asked after you," Pearl Ann said. "Not in a sorrowing way. Don't carry it like that. She'd say, *Cordelia's down in Cincinnati, she's got her own life,* and she'd say it proud. Like your life away from her was a thing she made. Like you was the dish she sent to the supper."

Cordelia's hand found the strap of her bag and held it. Through the canvas she could feel the hard rectangle of the Bible, the family written into it, the death of her brother written into it in her mother's hand, and she thought about her mother sitting at the secretary the year Tertius drowned, dipping the pen, writing the date, the way you would record a sale, a fact entered into a ledger so that it could not be argued with later, so that it would stand. *d. 1961.* The river had taken him in August. He had gone with the older boys to the gravel pit where the water was cold and deep and lovely and forbidden, and he had wanted so badly to be where the older boys were, to be counted among them, and the water had counted him among the things it kept. Their mother had not cried that anyone saw. She had gone to the secretary and written the date and then she had gone to work the next morning because the people whose floors she cleaned did not stop needing their floors clean on account of a colored woman's drowned son, and she had cleaned the floors, and she had come home, and she had not cried that anyone saw, and Cordelia had been twelve and had hated her for it, had built across the whole of her childhood a case against her mother's dry eyes, had carried that case to Cincinnati and lived inside it.

And only now, fifty-five years old, standing in the emptying house with a stranger's child watching her and Pearl Ann Tilghman bearing witness, did Cordelia understand that the eyes had been dry because the weeping had gone somewhere else, had gone down into the body and made a country there, a private country with no roads in, and that her mother had lived in that country every day of her life after August of 1961, had cooked from it and cleaned from it and gone to church from it and worn the bold lipstick from it, a flag planted in a land of grief, *Fire and Ice,* and that Cordelia had looked at the flag and thought it was the whole country.

"I'll take the saucers," she said. "Just—give them to me. You're right. Take them."

"I'll take two," Pearl Ann said. "For sitting with her. The rest you keep. A woman ought to have her mother's Sunday dishes whether she's got a Sunday or not. The Sunday comes back. You'll see. You get to a certain age and the Sundays come back on you whether you asked them to or no."

A man came through wanting to know about the chest freezer in the basement and Cordelia sent him down with a flashlight and the price, and a couple wanted the kitchen table and she watched them carry it out, the table where she had done her homework and eaten her suppers and where her mother had sat alone for the last twenty years eating from the cornflower dish, one place set, the radio on for company, and the table went out the door tilted on its side, its legs in the air like an animal, and the couple was happy, they had a baby on the way, the woman said, they were setting up, and Cordelia thought: good. Eat your suppers at it. Do your homework at it. Set one place or set six. The table didn't care. The table had only ever wanted to be useful, the way her mother had only ever wanted to be useful, the way usefulness had been the only kind of love the world would let her mother practice openly, and so she had practiced it ferociously, on floors and on tables and on the bodies of strangers' children that she diapered and fed, and had not known, perhaps, that her own child standing in the doorway with the suitcase had wanted not to be useful to her but to be wept over, to be the cause of dry eyes going wet.

The light moved. It was the kind of afternoon light that does not seem to move while you watch it and yet between one looking and the next has crossed a whole room. It had left the front and was deep in the dining room now, lying gold along the empty place where the secretary had been, where the young couple had carried it out exclaiming, and Cordelia stood in the gold of it and felt the day tipping toward five o'clock, toward the bus, toward the end of the box.

The girl had come down off the stairs. She was standing in the front room looking at the rectangle on the floor where the piano had been.

"What was here?" she asked.

"A piano."

"Could you play it?"

"No," Cordelia said. "Nobody could play it. It just sat here."

"Then why'd y'all have it?"

And Cordelia thought about how to say it, how to say to a child what it had taken her fifty-five years to come near, that you keep some things not for what they do but for what they say about who you mean to be, that her mother had kept a piano nobody could play the way she had worn a lipstick too bold for her face, as a statement made to an empty room, a flag, a saying of *I am more than the floors I clean, there is music in me even if no one ever hears it, there is a brighter mouth on me than the world allows,* and that this was not foolishness, was the opposite of foolishness, was the very thing that had kept her mother alive and upright through the drowning and the leaving and the twenty years of one place set at the table, and that Cordelia, who had judged the piano and judged the lipstick and judged the dry eyes, had been judging all along the only weapons her mother had, the small bright insistences a cornered woman makes against the size of what's cornering her.

"We had it because it was beautiful," Cordelia said. "Even though it didn't do anything. Some things you keep because they're beautiful and they remind you that you could be too."

The girl looked at the rectangle. "It's gone now," she said.

"Yes."

"You sad?"

"Yes," Cordelia said. "I'm sad."

The girl nodded her sideways nod and went to find her grandmother, who was at the door now with the two saucers, and Pearl Ann turned back and looked at Cordelia across the emptying room, across the planks of moving light, and she did not say goodbye, because the women of that church did not say goodbye, they said *I'll see you,* they said it like a promise and a threat and a benediction all at once, *I'll see you,* and what Pearl Ann said was, "You come up for the church anniversary. Second Sunday in May. You bring something. You bring that brown betty if you remember how she made it."

"I don't remember how she made it."

"You'll remember," Pearl Ann said. "You get the apples in your hand, you'll remember. The hands remember what the head lets go." And she took the child and went out into the light, and the saucers caught the sun, two small cornflowered moons going down the porch steps and out of the house and into the rest of their used and useful lives.

The men came back for the bookshelf. The man in the Browns cap had bought it, the spines and all, the whole brown bank of her mother's reading, and Cordelia let it go, let all of it go, and stood in the front room as the afternoon emptied of objects and filled with light, the light coming in now without the piano to throw a shadow, the light reaching all the way to the back wall for the first time in thirty-one years, and she stood in the middle of it, in the pale rectangle, in the place the piano had kept dark, and she put her hand into her bag and felt the Bible there, the names there, the births and the one recorded death and the woman who had recorded it, and she thought: I will write yours. There is a place under your name and I will write it. *Della Rose, b. 1937, d.* — and the year, and her hand, the hand that remembered, the hand that would remember the betty too, would get the apples in it and remember, and she stood in her mother's light until it was nearly five and then she gathered her bag and went out the door she had been carried through, minding the threshold, into the rest of it, whatever it was, and left the house standing open and full of evening for the little while it had left.
