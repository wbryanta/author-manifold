The ferry left at eleven, and by then the harbour lights had thinned to a scattering behind them, each one held briefly in the black water before it slid out of reach. He stood at the stern with his coat collar up and watched the wake fold open and close, the way a wound does in the moment before it heals or refuses to. There were perhaps twenty people aboard. A woman with two sleeping children stretched across three orange seats. A man in coveralls reading a paper folded into a tight square the size of his palm. The crossing took the night. They would dock at six in the grey, and someone from the coroner's office would meet him at the terminal with a clipboard and a face arranged for these occasions.

His name was Niall. The body, they said, might be Eamon's.

He had not seen his brother in eleven years. He could say the number cleanly now, the way you can say the depth of a well by dropping a stone and counting, though the counting tells you nothing of the water at the bottom, only the distance to it.

The phone had rung in the late afternoon. A constable's voice, careful, reading from notes. A man found on the rocks below the lighthouse at Carraig, no identification, but a letter in the inside pocket, water-ruined, the only legible thing an address that was Niall's address, and beneath it, in a hand he had not seen since they were boys but recognised instantly the way you recognise your own cough in a recording, the single word: *brother*.

So he was the line to him. He carried that as he carried the small overnight bag, the toothbrush, the clean shirt folded by his own clumsy hands. He thought: he must have left his shadow on me. Why else would they call. Why else would the dead reach back along eleven years of silence and put a name on me as the one who would come.

In the cafeteria a girl in a hairnet was wiping down the steel counter though there was nothing to serve. He bought tea he did not want and sat by the window where the glass gave him back his own face laid over the dark, and beyond the face nothing, the strait, the long invisible motion of it.

They had grown up on a farm above a different water, a lough, brown and still, that their father said had no bottom though their father said many things to make the world larger than it was. Eamon was older by three years. Niall remembered him mostly in profile, always looking at the thing ahead and not the thing beside him, walking out across the fields towards the line where the land gave up. He had a way of throwing a stone that Niall had spent whole summers failing to imitate, a low flat skim that touched the lough five, six, seven times before it consented to sink. Niall's stones went up and came down and were gone. Eamon would not teach him. *You hold it wrong,* he said, and that was all, as if the wrongness were a fact of Niall's hand he was not responsible for.

The trouble between them, when it came, was about money and then about their mother and then about nothing, which is to say about everything, which is to say it was about the long arithmetic of being two boys in one house, the unequal portions of love and grievance and the inheritance not of land but of a particular silence their father had practised and passed down like a tool worn smooth by use. There had been a night. There were words. Eamon had stood in the doorway with his bag, the same kind of bag Niall now held between his feet, and had said something Niall had spent eleven years not being able to remember, only the shape of it, the sense that it was unforgivable and that he had not forgiven it, had built a small house around the not-forgiving and lived in it comfortably enough.

The ferry rolled and the tea slid in its cup and steadied. A man came and sat at the next table without asking, the way men do at night on boats, and unwrapped a sandwich from waxed paper.

— Bad night for it, the man said.

— It is.

— You going over for the work or coming back from it.

— Neither, Niall said. To see someone.

The man nodded as though this were the most common answer in the world, which perhaps on that crossing it was. He ate his sandwich and looked at the window and the two of them sat together in the way of strangers who have agreed, without words, to be alone in each other's company.

When the man had gone Niall took out the photograph. There was only one. He had searched the house before he left, going through the box of papers his mother had kept and that he had inherited without ever opening, and at the bottom under the gas bills and the funeral cards he had found it. The two of them on the wall by the byre, Eamon maybe twelve, himself nine, both squinting, both holding something the camera had not bothered to make clear. Eamon's arm was around him. That was the thing he could not account for. The arm around the shoulder, the easy weight of it, the hand hanging loose. He had no memory of being held like that and yet here was the evidence, fixed in silver, that he had been, once, before the counting started.

He had been the one their mother sent to find Eamon when Eamon went out across the fields and did not come back at the proper hour. He remembered that now, standing on the dark boat with the photograph going soft at the edges from his thumb. The two of them coming back in the last light, not talking, the lough behind them holding the day after the sky had let it go. He had been the one sent to bring his brother home. And now here he was again, eleven years and a strait away, sent once more, and his brother once more out past the edge of things where Niall would have to go and find him.

He slept eventually, badly, his head against the cold glass, his coat over him like a man who has slept in his clothes because the night came faster than he could prepare for it. He dreamed of the lough. In the dream he threw a stone and it skipped without end, on and on across the brown water, never sinking, and he understood that this was terrible, that a stone that does not sink is a stone that cannot rest, and he woke with his neck wrong on his shoulder and the woman with the children watching him from across the cabin with the frank pity of the sleepless.

The grey came as promised. He went out to the rail. The land was a darker grey within the grey, a low coast with a white tooth of lighthouse far to the north, and he thought: that is where they found him, below that, on the rocks the colour of old iron. He could not see the rocks. He could see the light still turning, pale and pointless now against the coming day, sweeping the water and the boat and his face and away again, on its old patient errand of warning men off the very thing it illuminated.

A car was waiting at the terminal as they had said. A woman this time, not a man, in a green anorak, with a clipboard held against her chest and her hair pulled back hard from a face that had learned to be kind without being soft.

— Mr Brennan.

— Niall. Yes.

— I'm sorry you've had the journey. She did not say *for nothing.* She had been trained not to promise. — It's a half-hour to the mortuary. There's tea there. Did you sleep?

— A little.

— You won't have.

She drove. The road came up out of the terminal and bent inland along a river that disappeared for a while under a confusion of stone and showed itself again further on, the water arguing its way through. He watched it. He had the box of his own life on his lap, the bag with the toothbrush and the clean shirt, and on top of the bag the photograph, and he held it the way Clara had once held a clipping in a library a country and a lifetime away, as if it were the thread that led into the labyrinth and might also be the thing that hanged you.

— Did you know him, the woman asked. Your brother.

— Once.

— That's a hard thing. To be asked, with all that time gone.

— How do you know there was time gone.

She was quiet a moment, watching the road. — The letter, she said. The one in his coat. There was more of it than they told you on the phone. Some of it dried out enough to read, after. I shouldn't say.

— Say.

She drove a while longer. The poplars stood along the river in a long thinning line.

— It was to you, she said. Or about you. He'd written your name three times. As if he was practising. As if he wasn't sure of the spelling, though it's not a name you'd misspell. And then below it the word, the one they mentioned. And below that — she paused at a junction, looked left, looked right, pulled out — below that it said: *tell him I held it wrong.*

The river went under the road and Niall did not see the place where it went under, though he felt the car cross it, the small change in the sound the tyres made. *I held it wrong.* The stone. The throwing of it. Eamon standing on the shore of the lough with his back to him, telling the small brother that the fault was in his hand, and all the years afterward Niall believing it, building his house of not-forgiveness on the foundation of a wrongness that was, it now appeared, his brother's to carry and not his at all. *Tell him I held it wrong.* He did not know which wrongness was meant. The stone, the night in the doorway, the eleven years, the whole inheritance of silence — perhaps Eamon had not known either, perhaps that was why he had practised the name three times and got no further than the one true confession before the water came up over the road of his life and sank it.

The mortuary was a low building behind the hospital, painted a colour that was meant to be calming and was only the colour of nothing. They gave him tea. He drank it this time. A man in a white coat came and spoke to the woman in the anorak in a low voice and then turned to Niall with the practised gentleness of his profession.

— Whenever you're ready. There's no hurry. People think there's a hurry. There isn't.

— I'm ready, Niall said, and was not, and went anyway, because being ready had never once in his life been the thing that decided whether he went.

The room was cold the way rooms are cold when the cold is the point of them. There was a shape on the table under a sheet and the man in the white coat stood at the head of it with his hand on the cloth, and he looked at Niall, and Niall nodded, and the cloth came back.

He had prepared himself for his brother's face. He had spent the whole crossing assembling it from the photograph and from memory, ageing it eleven years in his mind, putting the lines in, the grey in the hair, the loosening of the jaw. He had got it ready so that the dead face would have something to be measured against, so that he could say yes or no with the cleanness the clipboard required.

The face on the table was a stranger's.

He looked at it for a long time. He looked at it longer than he needed to, because he understood, standing there, that he had wanted it to be Eamon. That under all the dread of the crossing, beneath the cold tea and the bad sleep and the photograph going soft under his thumb, he had wanted to come to the end of the strait and find his brother, even like this, even silenced and salt-ruined on a steel table, because a found brother, even a dead one, is a brother who has been at last located in the world, fixed, no longer out past the edge of the fields where the small boy is forever being sent to look for him. And this man was not Eamon. This man had a wider face and a different set to the brow and a scar on the chin that Eamon never had, and the relief and the grief came up in him together so that he could not have said which was which, the way the river and the road had come together back on that flooded ford and you could no longer say which was the thing flooded and which the thing that flooded it.

— It's not him, he said.

— You're certain.

— I'm certain.

The man drew the cloth up with great care, as if the stranger could still be disturbed, and that small courtesy to a man who would now go unnamed into whatever they did with the unnamed undid Niall more than the face had, and he had to put his hand on the cold edge of the table.

— I'm sorry, the man said. We had the letter, you see. It pointed to you.

— It pointed to me, Niall said. Yes.

The woman in the anorak drove him back. The day had come up properly now, a thin sun over wet fields, and the poplars threw their long early shadows across the road so that the car passed in and out of the bars of them, light and dark, light and dark.

— He had your brother's letter on him, she said after a while. So your brother — somewhere your brother is.

— Somewhere, Niall said.

— That's something. Isn't it.

He did not answer at once. He was watching the river come back to the road, the two of them running alongside each other now, neither one giving way.

— My brother wrote a letter to me, he said. And never sent it. And carried it. And then this man, whoever he was, came to have it. And carried it. And drowned with it. And the letter came across the water to find me, the way it was always meant to, only it came in a stranger's coat. He stopped. He found he was almost laughing, a dry thing with no joy in it but no bitterness either, something cleaner than either. — He held it wrong, he said. We both did. The whole thing. We held the whole thing wrong from the start, and it skipped along eleven years and never once sank, and here it is, still going.

She let him talk. She had learned that too, he supposed.

— Where will you look, she said. For him. Now.

— I don't know that I'll look.

— No?

— He's alive, Niall said. A man's letter doesn't get into a dead stranger's coat without the man being alive somewhere to lose it. That's enough. He drowned a stranger and not my brother. He's out there past the edge of the field, the same as ever, and I'm the one sent to find him, the same as ever. He looked at the river. — But I'm tired of being sent. Maybe this once he can come back on his own.

The ferry did not leave until evening, so she dropped him at the terminal and shook his hand with both of hers, a thing he had not expected, and said something he didn't keep the words of, only the warmth, and drove off in the green anorak into the rest of her day of clipboards and the cold room and the bad colour on the walls.

He had hours. He walked. The town gave way to a road and the road gave way to a path and the path went up along the coast towards the lighthouse he had seen from the boat at dawn, the white tooth, and he went towards it without deciding to, his arms beginning to move in a kind of swimming motion through the long wet grass that nobody had cut in a generation, the seedheads breaking against him and the small insects springing up where his legs disturbed them.

He came out at last on the rocks. They were the colour he had thought, old iron, black and ledged and going down in great broken steps to where the strait worked against them, white and then green and then white again. Somewhere here. They had found the stranger somewhere along this edge, the man with his brother's letter in his coat, the man who had stood between Niall and his grief and taken it on himself, unknowing.

He took the photograph out. The two boys on the wall by the byre, the arm around the shoulder. He thought he would throw it into the water and was surprised at the thought, and then understood it, and did not throw it. Instead he looked for a stone. He found one, flat, the right weight, the size that fits the curve of the hand as if hands had been made first and stones after, to fit them. He stood at the edge with the strait moving below him and he held the stone the way he had held it as a boy on the shore of the bottomless lough, and he heard Eamon's voice across the years saying *you hold it wrong,* and he turned the stone in his fingers, and he understood that he had never once, in all his life, tried holding it any other way. He had taken the verdict and kept it. He had made a fact of his hand out of a thing his brother said in a bad temper on a brown shore one afternoon thirty years gone.

He changed his grip. He let the stone lie along the inside of his finger, against the curl of it, low. He brought his arm back and swept it flat and let the stone go out almost level with the surface of the strait, out over the grey water that no man could skip a stone across, that no stone could cross, that was a strait and not a lough and would take whatever you gave it without an argument.

The stone touched once. Twice. A third time, a small leaping silver thing impossibly far out, and then a fourth, and then the swell took it and it was gone, sunk, at rest, on the bottom of the strait between the two countries, between the two lives, the one lived and the one imagined, the tale told first and the tale told backwards, and Niall stood on the iron rocks with his arm still out from the throwing of it and his eyes stinging in the wind off the water, and he could not have said, and did not try to say, whether the thing under his eye was the wind's doing or his own, a sun tear he was unaware of, a small fragment of water he would not have known was there had he not, in that moment, reached up with the back of his hand and found it.

Then he turned and went back through the long grass towards the town and the evening boat, swimming with his arms through the grass that closed behind him, leaving the lighthouse and the rocks and the stranger and the place where the letter had finally arrived, carrying his box of notes, his bag, his clean unworn shirt, his photograph of the arm around the shoulder, his brother somewhere out past the field still and alive and not yet found and for the first time in eleven years no longer his to find.

The crossing back was calm. He slept the whole way and did not dream of the lough, or dreamed of it and did not keep it, and when the harbour lights came up out of the dark ahead, each one held briefly in the black water before the boat carried him on to it, he was at the rail to meet them, watching the bow now and not the stern, looking for once at the thing ahead and not the thing behind, the way his brother always had, walking out across the fields towards the line where the land gave up, the way you do when you have finally, after all the years, learned to hold the thing the way it was meant to be held, and let it go.
