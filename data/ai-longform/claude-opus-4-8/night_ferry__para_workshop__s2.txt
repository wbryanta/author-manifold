The ferry left at eleven, and by then the snow had stopped, which Halvard took as neither good nor bad, only as weather. He had driven six hours to make the crossing and had eaten nothing since a gas station sandwich outside Mosjøen, the bread of which had tasted faintly of the refrigerator it had lived in. Now he stood at the rail with his coat zipped to the chin and watched the lights of the town pull away, smearing across the black water, and he thought about how a town looked smaller the moment you left it, as though leaving were a kind of correction.

The strait was not wide. In summer the crossing took the better part of a night because the ferry called at three islands on the way, unloading mail and machinery and the occasional car, but in winter two of the stops were closed and the boat ran nearly straight across, and still it took until morning, because the captain went slow through the dark and the ice that came down from the fjords in pans the size of rooms. Halvard had been told all this by the woman who sold him the ticket, who had looked at him a moment too long, the way people do when they are deciding whether to ask.

She had not asked. He was grateful for that.

He found he could not go inside yet. Inside there was a cafeteria with fluorescent light and a vending machine and a television bolted high in a corner playing a football match from somewhere warm, and there were the other passengers, a dozen of them, truckers and a family and two old men playing cards, and he did not want to be looked at, and he did not want to be a man who had come to look at a body. Out here he was only a shape at the rail. The cold was clean. It went into his lungs and scoured something.

His brother's name was Aksel. Had been. He did not know yet whether it was past tense, which was the whole reason for the ferry, and he turned the grammar of it over while the wake unspooled white behind the boat. *Is Aksel. Was Aksel.* Six years since they had spoken, and in those six years he had several times imagined Aksel dead — not wished it, he told himself, only imagined it, the way you imagine the house burning when you leave for a trip, a rehearsal of a grief you do not actually want delivered. So that now, called north by a policeman with a soft voice and a careful manner, he did not know what he felt, and the not-knowing felt obscene to him, a failure of some basic human equipment.

"There is a man," the policeman had said on the telephone. "We found him on the rocks below Kvalvika. He had been in the water. There are reasons we believe he may be your brother. But the — condition. We would need someone."

"What reasons," Halvard had said.

A pause. "Things he was carrying. And a description, from people in the village. We don't want to say more until we are sure. You understand."

He had understood. He had not understood. He had written down the name of the town and the time of the ferry and had stood in his kitchen afterward holding the pen as though it were the heaviest thing in the world.

The boat shuddered through a pan of ice. He heard it grinding along the hull beneath him, a long low complaint, and then it was past. The lights of the town were gone now. There was only the dark and the colder dark of the water and, far off to the north and very faint, a greenish wash on the underside of the clouds that might have been the aurora or might have been a trick the cold played on the eyes.

He went inside.

The warmth hit him like a hand. He bought a coffee from the machine and sat at a table by the window with his back to the television and held the paper cup in both hands and did not drink it. The two old men were still at their cards. The family had bedded down across a row of seats, the children asleep under coats, the parents speaking in the low voices people use over sleeping children, and Halvard watched the woman smooth the hair of the smaller child without seeming to know she was doing it, and he felt the old loneliness come and sit beside him like a dog that knew his scent.

He and Aksel had been close once. This was the thing nobody who knew them now would believe, because they had become a story people told — the brothers who didn't speak — and a story flattens everything, takes the long slow particular betrayal and makes it a single event with a single villain, and the villain in the telling was usually Aksel, because Aksel had been the one to leave, and leaving looked like the act, when in fact the leaving had only been the last in a long sentence of acts, most of them Halvard's. He knew this. He had always known this, in the part of himself he visited rarely.

There had been a girl. Of course there had been a girl; there was always a girl, or money, or a house, the worn brass coins men used to buy their estrangements. Her name was Marit and she had married neither of them in the end, which Halvard had come to see as a judgment passed on them both. But before that, before the years of silence, there had been the thing Halvard did, which was to tell their father — who was dying then, who had a month perhaps, who wanted before he went to believe his sons were good men — to tell their father that Aksel had stolen from him. It was not even true. That was the part Halvard had never been able to set down. Aksel had borrowed money, yes, and had been slow to return it, but he had returned it, all of it, and Halvard had known this and had said *stolen* anyway, had stood in the hospital corridor and watched his father's face do something terrible, and had felt, in the moment, a satisfaction so pure and so shameful that he had had to go into the toilet and run the water and stare at himself until the feeling passed.

Aksel had not been told what was said. But he must have felt it, the cooling, in the last weeks; their father had died with a coldness toward his younger son that no one could explain to Aksel and that Halvard would not, and Aksel had stood at the graveside white and bewildered and then, slowly, over months, had stopped calling, and Halvard had let him, and that was the whole architecture of it, a lie told to a dying man, a silence permitted to grow.

He drank the coffee. It was bitter and lukewarm. The aurora, if it had been the aurora, was gone.

A trucker came and sat at the next table with a tray of waffles and ate them methodically, folding each one in half, and nodded at Halvard, and Halvard nodded back, and the small ordinary exchange steadied him. The man's hands were enormous and scarred across the knuckles. He ate as though eating were a job he had been given to do well.

"Long way to go," the trucker said, not really a question.

"Yes."

"Family?"

"My brother. Maybe."

The trucker considered this, the *maybe*, and did not pry, only nodded again, slower, as though he had heard worse and would hear worse still before the night was out. "Cold one," he said, meaning the crossing, and Halvard agreed that it was a cold one, and the man finished his waffles and gathered his tray and went off toward the seats to sleep, and Halvard was alone again with the window and the dark behind it and his own face hanging in the glass, lit from inside, looking back at him with what he thought was an expression of mild accusation.

He had been forty-one when his father died. He was fifty now. He had spent the decade well by most measures — he had a business that did not fail, a small clean apartment, a woman he saw on Thursdays who did not want more than Thursdays — and in all that time he had not once tried to find Aksel, though he could have. Aksel had not hidden. He had gone north, as men in their family went north when they wanted to disappear without the trouble of actually disappearing, and he had taken work on the boats, and someone had told Halvard once, at a wedding, that Aksel was living on an island and had a woman and maybe a child, and Halvard had filed this away the way you file away a thing you have decided not to think about, and now the island had given his brother back to the rocks, perhaps, and Halvard was on a ferry crossing a strait to look at a face that might not even be a face anymore.

*The condition*, the policeman had said.

He stood and walked the length of the cabin and back, the way you do on a boat to feel that you are still in possession of your legs. He went out onto the deck again. The cold took him gratefully. He gripped the rail and looked north and tried to make himself feel the thing he was supposed to feel, and what came instead was a memory, uninvited and very clear, of the two of them as boys on a different boat, their father's old wooden boat, hauling a net in summer light that never ended, and Aksel had been maybe nine and had fallen in, just stepped backward off the gunwale into the black-green water, and Halvard had gone in after him without thinking, without deciding, his body simply following his brother into the cold, and he had got a fist in Aksel's collar and hauled him to the side and their father had hauled them both aboard and they had lain in the bottom of the boat among the fish gasping and laughing, both of them, laughing until they cried, and their father had stood over them shaking and furious and relieved and had not been able to speak.

He had gone in without thinking. That was the boy he had been. He could not now reconcile that boy with the man in the hospital corridor who had said *stolen* and felt that pure clean satisfaction. They were the same person. That was the thing you were never told as a child, that the one who goes into the water for his brother and the one who drowns him with a single word are the same animal, housed in the same skin, taking turns.

The deck was rimed with ice and he held the rail with both hands. Somewhere below him the engines worked. He thought: if it is him, I will have to identify a body my own lie helped put in the water, because if I had not said what I said, he would not have gone north, and if he had not gone north he would not have been on those rocks. He knew this was not strictly true. He knew that a man's death belonged to a thousand causes and that to claim it was self-flattery dressed as guilt, a way of making himself central to a story in which he had in fact been only one early figure who walked offstage. But the knowing did not help. Knowing rarely helped. He had spent his whole life knowing things and being unimproved by the knowledge.

A door opened behind him and the woman from the family came out, the mother, drawing on a cigarette she had clearly come out to smoke in secret, and she startled at the sight of him and then relaxed and offered him one without speaking and he took it, though he had quit years ago, and she lit it for him cupping the flame against the wind, and they stood and smoked and did not talk, and it was the most companionship he had felt in a long time, two strangers on a freezing deck in the middle of the night burning small fires in their hands. When she was done she crushed the cigarette under her boot and picked up the butt and put it in her pocket, conscientious even here, even now, and she said, "It will be light around eight. It's worse in the dark. The waiting."

"Yes," he said.

"Whatever it is," she said, not asking what it was, "it will be more bearable in the light," and she went back inside, and he believed her, because she said it like a woman who had tested it.

He smoked the cigarette down and it made him dizzy and he was glad of the dizziness. He thought about what he would say to the policeman with the soft voice. He thought about the form there would surely be, the document, the box he would tick, and how strange it was that the law required a face to be matched to a name by someone who had loved it, as though love were a kind of credential, as though only love could be trusted to read a face truly — when in fact love was the worst reader of faces, love saw what it needed, love had kept him from seeing Aksel's plain decency for the whole of the lie. Maybe that was the point. Maybe they wanted someone who would be undone by it, because only someone undone could be trusted not to lie about the dead.

He did not know if he had loved Aksel. He had loved the boy in the boat. He suspected that what he had felt for the man, in the years before the silence, was something more like the love you feel for a limb — unconsidered, structural, noticed only in injury. And now the limb was perhaps on a steel table in a cold room in a town he had never seen, and he was crossing the dark to look at it and say *yes* or *no*, and the *no* he found he wanted desperately, the *no* that would let him drive home and resume his life of Thursdays — that *no* was monstrous to him, because it meant he wanted his brother to be some other man's brother, wanted the grief to be carried by some other family across some other water. And the *yes* — the *yes* was unbearable in the other direction, because the *yes* closed the book forever on the conversation he had never had, the conversation in which he told Aksel the truth and Aksel did whatever Aksel would do, forgave him or struck him or laughed, the conversation he had been saving, he realized now, the way a miser saves money he will never spend, taking comfort merely from its existence, the knowledge that it was there, that there was still time. The ferry was carrying him toward the end of time.

He went back inside before he froze. He drank another coffee. He did not sleep, though he closed his eyes, and behind his closed eyes the boat rocked and the years rocked and he saw his father's face doing the terrible thing it had done, and he saw Aksel at the graveside, and he saw the green water, and he saw, for the first time in years, with great clarity, Aksel's actual face, the particular face, the slightly crooked tooth and the scar through the eyebrow from a bicycle and the way he had of looking just past you when he was thinking, as if the thought were standing a little to your left. He held the face in the dark behind his eyes and was afraid he would have to give it up, hand it over, exchange the living remembered face for whatever the water had made.

The crossing went on. The engines droned. At some point he slept after all, briefly, and woke with his neck stiff and his mouth sour, and the window had gone from black to a deep blue, and the blue was lightening, and he understood that the woman had been right, that it was nearly eight, that they were close.

He went out a last time. The cold had softened, or he had stopped feeling it. To the east the sky was doing something extraordinary and almost violent with color, a band of orange laid down hard against the blue, and the water had gone from black to pewter, and there were the islands, low and white and humped like sleeping animals, and one of them, growing, was the one. He could see the harbor now, the breakwater, a cluster of red and ochre buildings, the white church on its rise. Smoke stood straight up from chimneys. People were waking in those houses and lighting stoves and putting on coffee and one of them was perhaps the woman who had been Aksel's woman, and one of them was perhaps a child with a crooked tooth, and Halvard gripped the rail and looked at the town coming and did not feel ready and understood that he would never feel ready, that readiness was not a thing the situation offered.

The ferry slowed. The note of the engines changed. The water broke white and easy around the bow. A man in an orange coat stood on the quay with a rope coiled in his hand, looking out at the approaching boat with the patient boredom of a man who has met every boat for thirty years. Beyond him, a police car, dark and silent, and a figure leaning against it who must be the policeman with the soft voice, and Halvard looked at the figure and the figure looked at the boat and there was no way yet to know anything, the distance still erasing all detail, both of them only shapes, the way he had wanted to be only a shape at the rail in the night, and he saw the cruelty of it now, the wish to be unidentified, when somewhere ahead of him a man had perhaps lost his face to the sea and had to be identified by the only brother he had, who had not earned the office.

He thought: I will say yes if it is yes. He thought: I will not look away. He thought: that is the smallest thing I can do, the only thing left to do, to look at the face, whatever the water has done, and not flinch, and say the name, and claim him, and carry him home across this same water and put him in the ground beside the father I poisoned against him, and stand at the grave I did not let him understand, and say the true thing finally to the only one who can no longer hear it.

And if it is not him — he made himself think this too, made himself stand inside it — if it is some other man, some stranger pulled from the rocks, then he would drive south and he would not resume his life, he had decided this somewhere in the night without noticing the decision being made, he would find the island where Aksel actually lived, he would knock on the actual door, he would stand on the step in the cold and wait for the door to open on his brother's living face and the crooked tooth and the scar, and he would say the true thing while there was still a mouth to receive it and a face to do whatever the face would do. He had been given, perhaps, a reprieve disguised as a summons. He would not waste it twice.

The ferry touched the quay with a long groan of fenders. The man in the orange coat threw his rope and another man caught it and made it fast. The ramp came down with a clang that echoed off the breakwater. The policeman pushed off from the car and came forward, and Halvard could see him now, young, younger than the soft voice had suggested, with a face that was trying to be composed and not quite managing it, a kind face, a face that did not want to be doing this either. They were the same in that, at least.

Halvard picked up his bag. The other passengers were gathering at the ramp, the truckers, the family, the woman who had given him the cigarette, and she caught his eye and gave him a single small nod, and he nodded back, and then he was walking down the ramp into the bright cold morning with the snow blinding white on the hills and the smoke standing straight up from the chimneys and the policeman coming toward him with his hand already half-raised in greeting or in sorrow or in both.

"Halvard," the policeman said. Not a question. "Thank you for coming. It was a long way."

"Yes," Halvard said.

"He's at the clinic. It's just there, the white building. Whenever you're ready. There's no hurry."

"There's no hurry," Halvard repeated, and almost laughed, and did not, and looked at the white building on its rise near the white church, and felt the whole long night fold up behind him like a wing, and he found that his legs were carrying him forward, up the road, toward the door, his body simply following, the way it had once followed his brother off the gunwale and into the black-green water without waiting for him to decide.
