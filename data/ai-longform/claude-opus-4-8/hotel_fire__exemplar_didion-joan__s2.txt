The hotel had been rebuilt in 1952, and although the new building stood on the same lot, faced the same stretch of dry foothills, and bore the same name lettered in the same gilt across the porte-cochere, it was not the same hotel and everyone knew it. Marian Castro knew it perhaps better than anyone, although she had told no one she was coming, had in fact accepted the invitation under a name that was no longer entirely her own, and now stood in the lobby of the Esperanza with a small wrapped box in her hands, looking at the place where the registration desk had been and was now again, in approximately the same location but in a different wood, a paler wood, a wood that had never absorbed anything.

She had been seventeen the summer of the fire. She was thirty-four now, which seemed to her neither old nor young but simply a number she carried, the way she carried the box, the way she carried the invitation in her handbag in case anyone should question her presence, although no one would. They had stopped questioning her presence in such rooms some years before, when she married Howard Castro and acquired the handbag and the kind of suit, gray and unremarkable, that allowed a woman to move through a lobby without being seen at all.

“You’re here for the Latham wedding,” the girl at the desk said. It was not a question. The girl was perhaps nineteen, with the smooth unmarked face of someone to whom nothing had happened.

“I am,” Marian said.

“The ceremony’s on the south terrace. Reception in the Mariposa Room.” The girl smiled. “Isn’t it a lovely day for it.”

“Lovely,” Marian agreed, and did not say that the Mariposa Room had been, in the old hotel, the linen room, that she had spent the better part of three summers in the linen room counting sheets, that the linen room had been where the fire was thought to have started although no one had ever proved it, no one had wanted to prove it, the proving of it would have required someone to admit that the wiring in the linen room had been condemned in 1946 and never replaced. The Mariposa Room. They had named it for butterflies. Marian thought this was probably the most truthful thing anyone had said about the new hotel: that where the dead linen had been, where the smoke had first curled up through the ceiling and into the rooms above, they had put butterflies.

She had not intended to come. The invitation had arrived in March, forwarded twice, and she had set it on the kitchen table and looked at it for a long time the way one looks at a letter from a person believed dead. Susan Latham. She had not thought of Susan Latham in fifteen years, had assumed, when she thought of her at all, that Susan Latham was one of the people who had died, although there was no reason to assume this, Susan Latham had been a guest and the guests had mostly gotten out, it was the staff who had been on the upper floors at two in the morning, the staff who had been sleeping in the rooms with the painted-shut windows. But the name had come back to her attached to an image, a child of perhaps four in a yellow swimsuit standing at the edge of the pool, and Marian had understood that the child was the bride, that the years had passed in exactly the way years passed, silently and entirely, and that she was being asked to a wedding by a woman she had last seen as a child she had once, on an afternoon she had forgotten until that moment, pulled dripping from the shallow end and wrapped in a towel from the linen room.

The Lathams would not remember her. There was no reason they should. She had been the chambermaid who had been kind to the child, if she had been kind, if pulling a child from a pool counted as kindness or merely as the avoidance of catastrophe, which was perhaps the same thing. The invitation had been a clerical accident, a name on an old list, the kind of list Edith Knight kept in scrapbooks, the kind of list that outlived everyone on it. Marian Bell, the invitation had said, using her maiden name, an address from before her marriage that the post office had somehow still connected to her. Marian Bell, who had not existed for eleven years.

She had come anyway. She could not have explained why to Howard, who believed she had gone to visit a cousin in Bakersfield, and she could not have explained it to herself, except that she wanted to see the place rebuilt, wanted to stand in it and feel nothing, wanted to confirm that the new wood had absorbed nothing and would absorb nothing and that she too was new wood now, pale and unmarked, a woman in a gray suit at a wedding.

The south terrace had been the staff parking area. Now it was paved in flagstone and set with white folding chairs in two banks divided by an aisle of rose petals, and Marian took a seat in the last row, on the bride's side, because she had been, in whatever sense she had been anything to these people, the bride's. The other guests were the kind of people the Lathams knew, ranchers' wives and the daughters of ranchers' wives, men in the linen suits men wore to weddings in that part of the state, and they nodded to her with the vague cordiality reserved for someone they could not place but did not wish to insult. She nodded back. She had learned the nod. It was the nod of a person who belonged, and she had practiced it the way she had once practiced folding a fitted sheet so that the corners disappeared into themselves and left no seam.

The string quartet was tuning. Beyond the terrace the foothills rose brown and indifferent, the same foothills, the only thing about the place that had not been rebuilt, the foothills did not burn and did not rebuild, they merely waited, and Marian looked at them and felt the first thing she had felt all afternoon, which was a kind of recognition, not of the hotel but of the hills, the hills had seen the fire, the hills had stood exactly so while the old Esperanza burned and eleven people died, four of them girls she had shared a room with, girls whose names she could still recite the way the child from Tupelo recited her poems, low and wretched and with her eyes shut: Dolores, Anita, Rose, and the new girl whose last name no one had learned, the new girl who had been there nine days.

“Is this seat taken?”

A man had sat down beside her, an older man, sixty perhaps, with a brown ruined face and pale eyes and the particular stillness of a man who has spent his life waiting for things to begin so that he can decline to be impressed by them. He had not waited for her answer. He held a glass, although the bar was not officially open, he had gotten a drink somewhere, men like this always got a drink somewhere.

"It's taken now," Marian said.

He laughed, a dry laugh, a forced laugh, the laugh of a man who found the world funny in a way that gave him no pleasure. "I'm Susan's uncle. The disreputable one. Every wedding has one and I'm theirs." He looked at her sideways. "I don't know you."

"No," Marian said.

"That's refreshing. I know everyone here and I dislike most of them. You're not from the valley."

"I was, once."

"Once." He turned the word over the way Walter Knight had turned over his daughter's married name, holding it up to the light to see what was wrong with it. "Once. We're all from somewhere once. I was from Pasadena once. Now I'm from nowhere, which suits me, a man should aspire to be from nowhere." He drank. "How do you know Susan?"

It was the question she had prepared for and the question for which she had no answer she was willing to give. She looked at the foothills. "I knew her when she was a child," she said. "She wouldn't remember."

"Children remember everything," the man said. "That's the trouble with them. They look like they're not paying attention and then thirty years later they tell you exactly what you did. Where did you know her?"

"Here," Marian said. "At the hotel."

The man went still. Something passed behind the pale eyes, some calculation, and Marian understood with a sinking certainty that this was a man who knew the history of the place, who had perhaps even been here, who collected such histories the way Edith Knight collected clippings, because they confirmed his view that the world was a disreputable business beneath its linen suits.

"At the hotel," he said softly. "The old hotel or the new one."

"There's only one hotel."

"There are two," he said. "There's the one that burned and the one they built so they wouldn't have to think about the one that burned. You knew it before."

"I worked here," she said. She had not meant to say it. The words seemed to hang unnaturally between them, the way the married name had hung between the father and the daughter, and Marian felt her face grow warm, felt the gray suit become suddenly what it was, a costume, felt herself revert in the man's pale eyes to the chambermaid she had been, the girl on the upper floor, the girl who had not died only because she had been awake, only because she had been folding sheets at two in the morning to make up the hours she had lost that afternoon pulling a child from the pool.

"My God," the man said, and there was something almost tender in it, almost greedy. "You're one of them. You were here when it burned."

"Yes."

"They never talk about it. The family. You'd think it never happened. They rebuilt and gave the rooms new names and pretend the place sprang up out of the ground in 1952 fully formed." He leaned toward her. He smelled of bourbon and bay rum, the smell of a certain kind of man, the smell of Warren Bogart, the smell of a man who has made cruelty into a kind of manners. "Tell me. I've always wanted to know. They say eleven died. They say it started in the linen room. They say the girls upstairs couldn't get the windows open because somebody had painted them shut."

The string quartet had begun to play. At the front of the terrace a young minister had appeared, and the groom, a sandy boy who looked frightened, and the guests were settling, turning toward the aisle, composing their faces into the expressions of pleasure that weddings required. Marian looked at the man beside her and understood that he wanted her to perform, the way Warren Bogart wanted the girl from Tupelo to recite, that he wanted her to stand up in the ruined linen room of her memory and say the names, Dolores, Anita, Rose, the new girl, that he would listen with his head tilted and his eyes half closed and afterward he would say something about how terrible it was, how the world was a terrible business, and he would be improved, he believed, by the hearing of it, the way he believed everyone present would be improved by hearing 'Snowbound.'

"I have nothing to tell you," Marian said.

"Of course you do. You have everything to tell me. You're the only one here who knows what this place is."

"I know what it is," Marian said. "It's a hotel where my friends died. That's not a story. That's just what it is."

The bride had appeared at the top of the aisle. Susan Latham, on the arm of her father, in white, with a face that was the face of the child in the yellow swimsuit grown into a woman's face, the same face, the years had passed and changed it and left it the same, and Marian felt her breath go out of her, because she had not expected it, she had not expected to recognize the child in the woman, she had told the man at the desk that the bride would not remember her but she had not considered that she might remember the bride, might remember everything, the wet child, the towel from the linen room, the afternoon sun on the pool that no longer existed because the new pool was in a different place, they had moved the pool, they had moved everything, but the child had not moved, the child had simply grown into this woman walking down an aisle of rose petals on her father's arm.

"There she is," the disreputable uncle said. "Little Susan. I held her at her christening. I dropped her, actually, just an inch, nobody saw." He laughed his dry laugh. "She wouldn't remember."

The bride passed close to the last row. For a moment, the briefest moment, her eyes moved across the assembled guests, the practiced sweep of a bride taking in her witnesses, and they passed over Marian and did not stop, there was no reason they should stop, and then they stopped. They came back. The bride was perhaps ten feet away and she looked at Marian Castro, née Bell, who had pulled her from a pool twenty years before and wrapped her in a towel, and something moved in her face, not recognition exactly, recognition was too much to ask, the child had been four, but something, some disturbance, the way water moves when something passes beneath it, and then she was past, she was at the front, she was giving her father her cheek to kiss, and the ceremony had begun.

"She saw you," the uncle said. He was watching Marian's face with the wariness of Warren Bogart fingering his cigar. "She looked right at you. You said she wouldn't remember and she looked right at you."

"She didn't remember anything," Marian said. "She saw a woman she didn't know. That's all."

But she was not certain that was all, and the man beside her knew she was not certain, and he settled back in his chair with the satisfaction of a man who has found, at a tedious occasion, one genuinely interesting thing, one thing that is not linen and roses and the expressions of pleasure that weddings require, one thing that is real, and the real thing was Marian's face, and he intended to watch it for the rest of the afternoon.

The minister was speaking of permanence. He was speaking of the home the young couple would build, the foundation, the structure that would shelter them through the years, and Marian listened to the words the way she had listened to the names of the dead, low and wretched, with her eyes nearly shut, and she thought that the minister did not know what he was saying, that no one who spoke of foundations had ever stood in a building while it burned, had ever understood that the foundation was the part that remained, the part that did not burn, the part on which they would build the next thing and name its rooms for butterflies. The hills had not burned. The foundation had not burned. Everything that lived had burned, Dolores and Anita and Rose and the new girl whose name no one had learned, and what remained was the thing you could build on, the bare and indifferent thing, and they were building a marriage on it now, in white, in June, with a string quartet.

When the ceremony ended and the guests rose and moved in their slow festive current toward the Mariposa Room, toward the linen room, the disreputable uncle put his hand on Marian's arm. It was not a gesture of intimacy. It was a gesture of detention.

"You'll come to the reception," he said. "I want to introduce you to people. The chambermaid who survived the fire. They'll be fascinated. It's the only interesting thing that ever happened in this family and they've spent twenty years pretending it didn't. I'd consider it a public service. I'd consider it educational." His pale eyes were bright. "There's nobody in that room wouldn't be improved by meeting you."

Marian looked at him. She thought of the girl from Tupelo standing miserably, reciting 'Crossing the Bar' with her eyes shut, looking at the back of the man who could save her and would not, and she thought that there was a kind of man who could not bear a thing to exist privately, who needed everything brought out and made to perform, who mistook this need for honesty, for a courage other people lacked, when it was only appetite, only the appetite of a man who had made himself from nowhere and required other people's somewhere to feed on. She had married Howard Castro and acquired the gray suit and the handbag precisely so that she would never again be required to perform her own life for the improvement of strangers. She had folded the corners of it into themselves until there was no seam.

"No," she said. "I don't think I'll come to the reception."

"You came all this way."

"I came to see the building," Marian said. "I've seen it."

She stood, and gathered the small wrapped box, which she had brought without knowing what she would do with it, a piece of silver, a thing one gave, and she walked not toward the Mariposa Room but back through the new lobby with its pale unabsorbing wood, past the girl at the desk to whom nothing had happened, and she set the box on the corner of the registration desk where the gift table should have been or where it would be, she did not know and did not care, and the girl said, "Aren't you staying for the reception?" and Marian said, "I'm afraid I can't," and the girl said, "Well, isn't it a lovely day for it," and Marian said, "Lovely," and went out through the porte-cochere into the afternoon.

The disreputable uncle had followed her as far as the lobby. She could feel him behind her, in the cool gilt-lettered space, watching her go, and she knew that he would tell them anyway, that at some point in the long afternoon, when the champagne had loosened the linen and the daughters of ranchers' wives had grown restless with pleasure, he would say, you'll never guess who was here, the woman in gray, in the back row, she was one of the girls from the fire, the chambermaid, and they would say, what fire, and he would tell them, he would have his recitation after all, he did not need her present for it, he had never needed her present, he had only wanted her to stand up and slouch and speak the words in her own low wretched voice, and she had declined, and he would do it himself, he would do it better, he would not let the corners disappear.

Let him, Marian thought. Let him tell it. It was his to tell now; she had handed it to him the moment she said she had worked here, the way you handed a man like that anything by letting him see it mattered. She had been careless. She had been seventeen for a moment, in the back row, with the foothills the same and the linen room renamed, and she had let it show on her face, and a man whose whole life was the reading of faces had read it. There was no taking it back. But there was the going. There was the gray suit and the handbag and the rented car in the lot where the staff used to park their nothing, where she had once parked nothing because she had owned nothing, and there was the road down out of the foothills, and she would take it, she would drive down out of the hills that did not burn and did not rebuild, and she would tell Howard about the cousin in Bakersfield, the cousin who did not exist, the cousin she had invented because there were some things you did not bring out and make to perform, some things you folded into themselves until there was no seam, and the not-telling of them was not a lie but the last thing you owned, the only thing the fire had not taken and the rebuilding had not named.

She got into the car. In the mirror the Esperanza stood gilt and pale against the brown hills, full of people improving themselves, and the bride somewhere inside it the wet child grown, dancing now perhaps, and Marian Castro put the car in gear and did not look again, because she had seen the building, she had stood in it and felt very nearly nothing, which was what she had come for, and if it was not nothing, if there had been the one disturbance when the bride's eyes came back, if there had been the names rising up low and wretched and the foothills bearing their old indifferent witness, then that was hers, that was the seam, and she would keep it folded all the way down the mountain and into the rest of her life.
