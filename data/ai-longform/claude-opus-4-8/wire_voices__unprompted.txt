The board was a wall of small lights, and Marian knew most of them by the hour they came alive. The early ones were business, men in offices calling other men in offices, their voices flat with the particular boredom of money. By noon the lonely ones began, wives calling their mothers two states away, the long silences between sentences that Marian had learned not to fill. She sat with the headset pressing its warm shape into her hair and she made the connections and she did not listen, not in the way that would have shamed her, but she heard. You could not help but hear. The voices came through her like water through a pipe and some of them left a residue.

The two she waited for came on Sunday evenings, between seven and eight, Pacific time. She had connected them so many times now that her hands knew the route before her mind named it — the cord to the jack, the ring, the wait, the second ring caught up somewhere in the wire over the Rockies and the long flat middle of the country until it landed in a kitchen in Ohio. She had decided it was a kitchen. There was a sound behind the woman's voice sometimes, a small domestic clatter, a pan set down, and Marian had built the rest of it: linoleum the color of weak tea, a window over the sink, a clock with a loud second hand.

The man called from a base in California. He never said which, and Marian was not permitted to ask, but she knew the prefix and she knew the flatness of certain government exchanges, the way the line had a particular hollowness as though the wire ran through a long cold building. He was young. You could hear youth in a voice the way you could hear weather. He called himself Del when he gave his name to her, and the woman he called was Ruth, and Marian had connected Del to Ruth eleven times now and she thought of them as something that belonged to her, the way a song on the radio could belong to you though thousands of others were hearing it in their own kitchens at the same hour.

"Long distance," Marian said. "I have your party."

"Thank you," Del said. He always thanked her. Most of them forgot she was there the moment the other voice came on, and that was correct, that was the job, to disappear into the click of the connection. But Del thanked her first, a small courtesy, and then he was gone into Ruth.

"Hey," he said.

"Hey yourself," Ruth said, and Marian, who should have pulled her attention back to the board, to the lights waiting, let the cord stay live a moment in her ear.

That was the first Sunday Marian let herself listen on purpose. She told herself it was nothing, that the board was quiet, that she was tired and the chair was hard and the listening cost no one anything. But she knew it was a thing she was doing, the way you knew when you took the second cookie that it was a decision and not an accident.

"How's the heat," Ruth said.

"Hot. You wouldn't believe it. The asphalt out by the hangars, you could fry a thing on it."

"Don't fry a thing on it."

"I won't fry a thing on it."

It was nothing. It was two people who had run out of new things to say and were saying the old things instead, which was its own kind of intimacy, Marian thought, the willingness to be boring with someone. Her own marriage, before it had stopped, had been full of fresh interesting talk right up until the end, and she had come to understand that the freshness had been the problem, that they had been performing for each other because there was nothing underneath to rest on. Del and Ruth could be boring. They had earned it.

"Mom asked about you," Ruth said.

"What'd you tell her."

"I told her you were hot."

Del laughed, and the laugh came through the wire warm and a little staticky and Marian felt it in her own chest, an absurd thing, a borrowed gladness. She pulled the cord. She put her attention on the board where a light was blinking, a man wanting San Francisco, and she made the connection and she did not listen to him because his voice was the color of money and there was nothing in it for her.

She did not know what they were to each other at first. She had assumed husband and wife, but there was no ring in the way they talked, no settled weight of years, and the mother Ruth mentioned was Ruth's own, which a wife might mention but which a girlfriend would mention more. By the fourth or fifth Sunday she had decided they were not yet married and were going to be, and she had built that too, into the kitchen with the tea-colored floor — she had put a ring on Ruth's finger and a date on a calendar, June maybe, when Del's time was up and he came home, if home was Ohio, which she thought it was.

What she could not place was the war. It was 1968 and the war was everywhere, in the paper, on the television set in the corner of her sister's living room where the numbers came up each week like a weather report, and Del was a young man on a base in California, and the arithmetic of that was not hard to do. But they never spoke of it directly. They spoke around it, the way you'd walk around a hole in the floor without looking down. Del talked about the heat and the men in his unit and the food, which he hated, and Ruth talked about her mother and the diner where she worked and a dress she was thinking of buying and then not buying. Neither of them said the word. Marian understood. To say it would be to look down the hole.

"Six weeks," Del said one Sunday, in October, and that was as close as he came.

"Six weeks," Ruth said.

"And then."

"And then."

There was a silence on the line, and it was not the empty silence of two people who had nothing to say. It was full. Marian held the cord and held her breath and the silence went on, three seconds, four, the most expensive silence in the world, costing them by the minute, and neither of them filled it because they did not need to. Then Ruth said, "Okay," very softly, the way you'd say it to a child you were putting to bed, and Del said, "Okay," and they hung up almost at the same moment, and Marian sat with the dead cord in her hand and found that her eyes had gone hot.

She was forty-one years old. She had a small apartment with a sofa that folded out, which she had never folded out because no one came to stay, and a cat named Buttons who was indifferent to her, and a sister in the next town who invited her for Sunday dinner and was relieved when she said she had to work. She worked a great deal. She had asked for the Sunday evening shift specifically, had traded for it with a younger girl named Dolores who wanted her Sundays free for a boy, and Marian had felt a small private shame in the trade, as though she were buying something disreputable, which in a way she was.

The supervisor was a woman named Mrs. Pruett who walked the rows behind the operators with her hands folded in front of her and her heels making a sound that meant straighten up. Mrs. Pruett had a rule, unwritten but absolute, that an operator did not stay on a line past the connection. You connected and you released. The line was not yours. The voices on it were not yours. They belonged to the people who paid for them, and to listen was a kind of theft, though no one called it that; they called it unprofessional, which was worse, because it meant the thing you were doing was not even interesting enough to be a crime.

Marian knew the rule and she broke it only on Sunday evenings and only for the eight minutes or so that Del and Ruth were on the line, and she told herself this was a small thing, a thing that hurt no one, a thing the size of a thumbnail in a life otherwise scrupulously kept. She told herself she would stop. She did not stop.

What she could not have said, if Mrs. Pruett had stopped behind her chair and asked, was what she got from it. It was not the content. There was no content; they talked about heat and dresses and diners. It was something underneath the content, the way a hand laid over another hand is not about the hands. It was the proof that two people, far apart, in a hard year, were holding on to each other across a distance that should have made holding on impossible, and that she, Marian, was the wire they held on through. She was the route. Without her the signal did not cross the Rockies and the long flat middle. She had come to feel that she was not eavesdropping but officiating, that she was a small necessary part of a thing that mattered, and there was no one in her life who needed her to be a necessary part of anything, and so she took it where she could get it, in eight-minute increments, on Sunday evenings, between seven and eight, Pacific time.

In November the calls changed. Del's voice had a thing in it that had not been there before, a held tightness, the sound of a man not saying something. Ruth heard it too; Marian could tell by the way Ruth started doing more of the talking, filling the air, the diner and the dress and the mother, talking the way you talk to keep someone from talking, and Del let her, and made the small sounds that meant he was listening, and at the end he said, "I love you," which he had not said before, not in Marian's hearing, in eleven Sundays, and Ruth went quiet and then said it back, and there was the silence again, the full one, and Marian pulled the cord too fast and lost the end of it.

She knew what the change was. She had known the moment she heard the tightness. Six weeks had become four weeks had become whatever it was now, and the and-then they did not name had arrived, and Del was not coming home to Ohio in June with a ring; Del was being sent. That was the thing they walked around. That was the hole in the floor. She had been wrong about the kitchen and the calendar and the dress on a body in a doorway; she had built a wedding and the truth was a troop ship or a transport plane and a country with a name that came up on her sister's television set each week with a number after it.

She did not sleep well that week. She lay on the sofa she had never folded out and she thought about a young man named Del whose face she did not know and whose last name she did not know and whose only fixed location in the universe was a prefix on a phone line and an hour on Sunday evening, and she thought, this is insane, you have built a person out of a voice, you have made a son or a brother or a husband out of eight minutes a week of a stranger talking about asphalt, and she knew it was insane and she could not stop, because the heart is not a switchboard, you cannot pull the cord on it, the line stays live whether you want it to or not.

The next Sunday she was early to her chair. She had her headset on at six forty-five and she watched the board and she waited, and at seven-twelve the light came alive that she knew was the California exchange, and her hands moved before her mind, the cord and the ring and the second ring caught up over the Rockies, and Ruth answered in the kitchen on the first ring as though she had been standing over the phone.

"Long distance," Marian said. "I have your party."

But there was no thank-you. There was a small sound, a breath, and then a man's voice that was not Del's, older, careful, the voice of a man trained to say hard things. "Ruth," the voice said. "Ruth, it's—"

And Marian understood, in the half-second before she should have pulled the cord, that this was not Del calling from the base. This was someone calling from the base. This was the call that came after the other calls stopped, the one that used the same wire and the same exchange and reached the same kitchen, and she had connected it, her own hands had connected it, she had laid the cord into the jack that carried it.

She should have pulled out. She did not. She sat with the live cord in her ear and she listened to a thing she had no right to hear, and she would carry it the rest of her life, the small sounds a person makes when the floor they have been walking around opens under them. There were no words from Ruth for a long time. Just the breath, gone ragged, and then a single sound that was not a word, that was below words, and Marian's own face was wet and her hand was over her mouth so that no sound of her own could go down the line and betray that she was there, that there was a third person on this most private wire in the world, a stranger, listening.

The careful man was saying things. Marian could not have repeated them. They were the standard things, she supposed, the things they trained the careful men to say, and they fell into the breathing on the other end and were absorbed and made no difference. At some point Ruth said, "When," just the one word, and the man told her, and Marian heard the date and it was a Tuesday, four days ago, while Marian had been lying on her sofa thinking she had built a person out of a voice. He had been gone four days. The Sunday before, the last Sunday, when he had said I love you and Ruth had gone quiet, he had already known. That was the tightness in his voice. He had been saying goodbye and Marian had heard it and had not let herself know what she was hearing.

She pulled the cord. She could not listen anymore; it was past bearing, and it was also, finally, not hers, it had never been hers, she had stolen something and now the thing she had stolen had turned in her hand and cut her, which was only just. She sat at the dead board with her headset on and her face wet and Mrs. Pruett's heels came down the row behind her and stopped.

"Marian."

"Yes, Mrs. Pruett."

"Are you unwell."

"No, Mrs. Pruett."

A silence. The heels did not move. Marian kept her eyes on the board, where the lights came alive and waited and she did not connect them, not yet, she could not, her hands would not. The lights waited. Somewhere men wanted San Francisco.

"Your eyes are red," Mrs. Pruett said.

"It's the dry air," Marian said. "The heat in here."

The heels stood another moment. Marian thought she would be written up, or sent home, or finally asked the question she could not answer, what is it you get from this, and she was almost glad, because to be caught would have been a kind of relief, a hand on her shoulder, a person noticing that she was a person. But Mrs. Pruett only said, after a while, in a voice that had something underneath its flatness that Marian had never heard there before, "Pull yourself together. There's a board to run." And the heels moved on.

Marian ran the board. She connected San Francisco and Chicago and a woman in tears who wanted Reno and a man who was angry that it took so long. She made the connections and she released them, and she did not listen, she had cured herself of listening, the cure was the worst medicine in the world but it had taken. The Sunday evening hour passed and there was no second call from California, there would never be a second call, the route was closed, and Marian understood that she would sit in this chair every Sunday between seven and eight for as long as she worked here and there would be no light she was waiting for, and that this was correct, this was the order of things restored, an operator did not own the lines.

She thought about Ruth all that week, the way she had thought about Del the week before, except that now there was no building to do, no kitchen to furnish, because the truth had come down the wire and the truth needed no decorating. She thought about the kitchen with the tea-colored floor that she had invented, and how Ruth was in it, and how the phone on the wall in that kitchen was now a thing Ruth would have to walk past, the instrument that had brought her both the voice and the end of the voice, and how you could not pull the cord on that either, you had to live in the house with it.

She wondered if she should write to her. It was a mad idea and she knew it was mad. She had the exchange; she could, against every rule there was, find the number, find the address, send a letter. And say what. I am the operator who connected your Sunday calls. I listened to you when I should not have. I built a wedding for you in my head. I am sorry for your loss, which I had no right to witness. There was no letter that was not a violation worse than the listening. There was no way to tell a stranger that you had loved her, in your fashion, from inside a wire, that you had been the route her gladness traveled and then, once, her grief, and that it had been the most that had happened to you in a long time, and that you were ashamed of that, that her catastrophe had been your event, that you had warmed your cold hands at the fire of someone else's house burning down.

She did not write the letter. She went to her sister's for Sunday dinner the following week, traded the shift back to Dolores, who took it gladly because the boy had not worked out and her Sundays were empty again. Marian sat at her sister's table and ate pot roast and listened to her sister's husband talk about the snow they were due, and the war did not come up, and the television in the corner was off, and Marian thought, this is a life, this is what people have, they have rooms with other people in them and the talk is boring because there is something underneath to rest on. She helped with the dishes. She held her sister's baby for a while, a fat indifferent baby not unlike the cat, and the baby grabbed her finger and held it with the whole strength of its hand and would not let go, and Marian sat very still so as not to break the connection.

When she went back to work she did not ask for the Sunday shift again. She took whatever they gave her. She connected the voices and released them and did not listen, and the residue that other people's lives left in her grew lighter, the way a smell fades from a room if you stop noticing it. But on Sunday evenings, wherever she was, at her sister's or on her unfolded sofa with the cat, between seven and eight she would feel the hour go by like a train she was no longer waiting for at a station she still happened to be standing in, and she would think of a kitchen in Ohio with a phone on the wall, and she would not build anything onto it, she had given up building, she would only hold it for a moment in the place where the listening used to be, and then she would let it go, the way you had to, the way the job had taught her, connect and release, the line is not yours, it was never yours, you are only the wire.
