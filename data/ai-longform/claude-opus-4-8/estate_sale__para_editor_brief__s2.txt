The card table came first, which Marlene hadn't expected. She'd imagined the big things would go—the dining set, the breakfront, the maple bed her mother had slept in for forty-one years—but it was the folding card table with the faux-leather top, gone soft and cracked at the corners, that a young man in a Carhartt jacket carried out under one arm at nine-forty in the morning, having paid four dollars for it.

"You sure that's all you want?" Marlene asked him, because she didn't know what else to say, and because the table had lived in the hall closet her whole childhood and emerged only for bridge nights and jigsaw puzzles and the assembly of Christmas cookies on wax paper.

"Got a guy does woodworking," the man said. "He likes the legs on these old ones." And then he was gone, the screen door clapping behind him the way it always had, and Marlene stood in the front hall of her mother's house holding a roll of masking tape and a fistful of orange price stickers and understood that this was how it would go all day. Not the wholesale erasure she'd braced for. A slow subtraction. A house emptied one stranger at a time.

She had hired a woman named Donna to run the sale, a heavyset woman with reading glasses on a beaded chain who had done this, she said, two hundred times, who moved through the rooms the night before with a clipboard and an unsentimental eye, attaching values to objects the way a coroner might. Donna had wanted to know if Marlene was going to be all right being there. Some family members weren't, she said. Some preferred to go to a movie, come back when it was over and the check was written.

"I'll be fine," Marlene had said. She was fifty-six years old and had buried both her parents now and had managed, in the wider sense, her own life across two states and one divorce and a son who called on Sundays from Denver. She could manage a Saturday.

But she had not slept well in the motel out by the highway, the only motel, where the air conditioner ran like a small aircraft and the bedspread smelled of someone else's cigarettes, and she had come back to the house at seven to find Donna already there, the lights on, the price tags blooming across every surface like a rash. Three dollars on her mother's church shoes. A dollar on the rolling pin. The whole of the kitchen—the Pyrex, the melamine, the percolator no one made coffee in anymore—lotted out across the counters under a sign that said EVERYTHING IN THIS ROOM .50 UNLESS MARKED.

"You'd be surprised what goes," Donna said, watching her face. "The little stuff moves. People like the little stuff."

They came at the stroke of opening, a line of them on the porch that Marlene watched form through the front window the way you'd watch weather. Dealers, Donna said, you could tell the dealers, they came early and they came in pairs and they went straight for the back bedrooms and the basement without so much as a glance at the parlor. They had the eyes of people reading a language Marlene didn't speak. They turned plates over to look at the maker's marks. They opened drawers. One man in a fishing vest crouched in front of the breakfront for a long time with a penlight, examining the joinery, and then stood and said to no one in particular, "Repro," and walked away, and Marlene felt absurdly defensive of a piece of furniture she had never especially liked.

She had given herself a job, which was to sit at the dining room table with the cash box and make change, because Donna's helper—a teenage girl, her granddaughter, who chewed gum and ran the calculator on her phone—couldn't be everywhere. It was a good job. It kept her hands busy and gave her a reason to be in the house that wasn't grief. She sat with the metal box and the rolls of quarters and watched her mother's life go out the door in pieces, and she made change, and she said thank you, and she said come again, which was idiotic, but the words came out of her by reflex, the way they had when she'd worked the register at the Rexall the summer she was seventeen.

"This was your mother's?" a woman asked her, holding up a cut-glass bowl. The woman was Marlene's age, maybe older, with kind tired eyes and a quilted vest.

"It was, yes."

"It's beautiful. They don't make this glass anymore. The real stuff, I mean." She turned it in the window light and the bowl threw small fractured rainbows on the wall. "You're selling it for two dollars?"

"That's what the tag says."

"It's worth more than two dollars."

"You can have it for two dollars," Marlene said, and the woman looked at her for a moment with something like understanding and put two crumpled bills in the box and carried the bowl away wrapped in a sheet of the Marietta paper, and Marlene thought: my mother served ambrosia salad in that bowl every Thanksgiving of my life and now it will sit on a shelf in a house I will never see, and there is no version of this where that doesn't happen, and the only choices are two dollars or the dumpster.

That was the thing nobody told you. That an estate was not a museum and could not become one. That the love a person poured into a house over four decades did not transfer, did not convey, was not a lien against the property that the next owner assumed. It simply evaporated at the moment of death, leaving behind only the objects, which were just objects, which were worth what a stranger would pay and not one cent more.

By ten-thirty the parlor had thinned considerably. The two club chairs were gone—a young couple, just starting out, the girl visibly pregnant, and Marlene had knocked five dollars off the pair without being asked because of the way the boy kept doing math in his head and coming up short. The chairs had flanked the front window since before Marlene could remember. Her father had read the paper in the one on the left, the springs eventually conforming to him so completely that after he died her mother could not sit in it, said it felt like sitting in his lap, which she did not want, she said, not because she hadn't loved him but because she had.

"Where do they go," Marlene said aloud, to Donna, who was passing through. "The chairs. Do you know where they go."

"Apartment over in Belpre, sounded like." Donna had a way of answering the literal question. "He said they're fixing up a place."

"My father sat in that chair for thirty years."

Donna stopped. She had two hundred sales' worth of practice at this particular moment, and she did Marlene the courtesy of not rushing it. "My husband passed in February," she said. "I did my own house in March. Couldn't have somebody else do it, and couldn't watch somebody else do it, so I did it myself, which everybody told me not to." She adjusted the glasses on their chain. "You want to know the truth, the chairs are the easy part. It's the stuff that doesn't sell that gets you. The stuff nobody wants. That's what tells you."

"Tells you what."

"What it was all worth," Donna said. "To everybody but you." And she went on into the kitchen, where a dispute had arisen over whether a chipped gravy boat was a quarter or fifty cents.

Marlene got up from the cash table—the girl could watch it—and went down the hall, past the spots on the wallpaper where pictures had hung, the rectangles brighter than the field around them, the ghost gallery of a family. She had taken the photographs down herself, the week before, had boxed them and shipped two boxes to Denver and kept one for herself and there was still, she knew, a fourth box she hadn't decided about, in the trunk of her rental car in the driveway, because some decisions you carried around with you for a while before you made them.

The back bedroom had been hers. It was empty now of everything but the bed frame, which a man was disassembling with a power drill, the headboard already leaned against the wall, and Marlene stood in the doorway of the room where she had become whoever she was and watched a stranger take a socket wrench to it.

"This your room growing up?" the man said. He didn't look up. He had a tool belt and a kind, distracted manner.

"How did you know."

"They always come stand in the door." He backed out a bolt and caught it in his palm. "I do a lot of these. You get a feel. People walk through the rest of the house fine and then they get to one room and they stand in the door like there's a rope across it." He set the bolt in a coffee can. "This is a nice bed. Solid maple. You don't want it?"

"I don't have room," Marlene said, which was true, and also wasn't the reason, and she didn't know the reason, only that when Donna had asked her the night before to walk through and mark anything she wanted to keep, anything at all, no charge, Marlene had walked through the whole house with the clipboard and marked nothing, and had stood in the empty front hall afterward frightened of herself, of the woman who could mark nothing, who could let it all go for quarters.

"My daughter'll like it," the man said. "She's eight. She's been wanting a big-girl bed." And Marlene felt the first thing she'd felt all morning that wasn't an ache, a small unexpected gladness, the image of a girl she would never meet sleeping in the bed where Marlene had slept, dreaming whatever eight-year-olds dreamed, and she said, "Tell her—" and then didn't know what to tell her, and the man waited, and finally she said, "Tell her it's a good bed. I had good dreams in it," which was not entirely true either, there had been the years of her mother's voice through the wall, the arguments, the long silences that were worse, but the man nodded as though she'd said something worth passing on, and she supposed she had.

In the kitchen Donna had resolved the gravy boat. The percolator sold while Marlene watched, to a man who collected them, who had nineteen, he said, who lit up describing the mechanism, the little glass dome on top where the coffee bubbled, and Marlene remembered the sound of it on Sunday mornings, the percolating, a sound that no longer existed in the world, that her grandson in Denver had never heard and never would, and she let the man have it for a dollar and felt she'd done a small good thing, sending it to a house where it would be understood.

Around noon there was a lull. Donna sent the granddaughter out for sandwiches and the house went quiet in the heat, the early rush spent, and Marlene walked through the rooms that were emptying like a tide going out. The dining set was gone. The breakfront, repro or not, was gone, bought in the end by a woman who wanted it for her sewing things. The hall closet stood open and bare. In the kitchen the half-price signs presided over a counter grown sparse, the Pyrex picked over, the orphans remaining: a single salad fork, a juice glass with a faded cartoon on it, a meat thermometer, a tin of buttons.

The buttons stopped her.

It was a Royal Dansk cookie tin, the blue one, the kind every house of a certain era had, and it had never in Marlene's lifetime contained cookies. It contained buttons. Her mother had kept it in the sewing drawer and it was full to the brim with buttons of every kind, the spares that came pinned inside new shirts, the salvage from garments worn past mending, buttons cut from her father's work shirts and her own outgrown coats and her mother's church dresses, a whole archaeology of the family's clothing rendered down to its fasteners. Donna had priced it at a dollar. A dollar for the tin.

Marlene picked it up and the buttons shifted with the sound she knew, a sound like rain, like something poured, and she stood in the kitchen and pried off the lid and looked at them, hundreds of them, thousands maybe, and she could not have said why this was the thing, why the chairs and the bed and the bowl had gone out the door and left her standing and the buttons stopped her cold, except that no one would ever know what these buttons were. The bed was a bed; the next girl would sleep in it as a bed. But the buttons were only buttons to anyone who hadn't watched her mother sit at the kitchen table on a winter evening sewing a button back onto a coat, the tin open beside her, sorting through it with one finger, holding two up to the light to match them, saying this one, this is close enough, no one will know but us.

This is close enough. No one will know but us.

She put the lid back on. She carried the tin out to the dining room and set it beside the cash box and when Donna came back through Marlene said, "I'm keeping this one," and Donna said, "Course you are, hon," without asking what it was, and Marlene understood that Donna knew, that there was always a tin, that every house had its tin and every sale had its moment when the family member came out of the kitchen holding something a stranger would have paid a dollar for, and Donna had seen it two hundred times and would see it two hundred more and it was, she seemed to feel, the most natural thing in the world.

The afternoon wore on. The light came around to the west side of the house and lay in long bars across the floor where the furniture had been, illuminating the dust, the impressions in the carpet, the four round dents where the legs of the sofa had stood. The sofa itself was gone by two, hauled out by two men to a pickup, and after it left, the parlor was nearly empty, just the curtains and the lamp Donna said wouldn't go, it never did, lamps never went, and a single straight-backed chair against the wall that no one had wanted.

Marlene went and sat in the chair, in the empty parlor, in the bars of western light.

She had not lived in this house for thirty-eight years. She had left it at eighteen and come back only for visits, for holidays, for the funerals, and in the last years for the slow business of her mother's decline, the doctors in Marietta, the question of the stairs, the morning she'd come and found her mother on the kitchen floor not hurt but unable to get up, just sitting there, embarrassed, saying she'd only meant to rest a minute. She had not lived here and yet the house was inside her in a way no place she'd actually lived had ever been, the dimensions of these rooms the template against which she'd measured every room since, so that her own apartment in Columbus, perfectly nice, had always felt subtly wrong, the ceilings a few inches off, the light coming from the wrong direction, because it was not this.

And now it would be gone. Not the house—the house would stand, would be bought by the young family or the investor, would be painted and updated, would have its kitchen gutted and its wallpaper steamed off and its character, as the realtors said, preserved or removed according to taste. But the house as it was inside her, the house that was a feeling and not a building, that would go when she went, and there was no tin for that, nothing she could carry out to the car and keep.

"You all right?" Donna stood in the doorway, and Marlene realized she'd been sitting in the empty room for some time, in the single chair, with the cookie tin of buttons in her lap, holding it the way you'd hold a cat.

"I'm all right."

"We're slowing down. Couple more hours, maybe. You want, you can go on. I'll lock up, drop the key where you said." Donna came in and sat, with some effort, on the floor against the wall, the way a younger woman would, and Marlene loved her suddenly and entirely for it. "I always think it's like a wake," Donna said. "The sale. Everybody comes through and handles the things and says something nice or doesn't, and then they leave, and you're sitting there at the end with what nobody took." She looked around the bare parlor. "My husband collected these little ceramic birds. Wrens, mostly. Had probably sixty of 'em. You think those went? Not a one. Nobody wants another person's birds." She laughed, a little. "I kept the birds. Whole box of birds in my spare room. I don't even like 'em. But I couldn't sell my husband's birds for a quarter each to people who didn't know they were his." She shrugged. "So I get it. The tin."

"It's buttons," Marlene said. "The tin. It's just buttons."

"I know what it is," Donna said.

They sat a while. The granddaughter rang up a last few sales at the dining room table, a wind chime, a stack of National Geographics, a casserole dish. The screen door clapped and clapped. The sun moved.

"Can I ask you something," Marlene said. "How do you do this two hundred times. How do you walk into people's houses and put prices on—all of it. And not—" She didn't finish.

Donna thought about it. She was not a woman who answered quickly. "Because somebody has to," she said finally. "And because I'd rather it was somebody who knows what it is. You get people in this business treat it like clearing out a storage unit. Junk, junk, junk, price it and move it. I'm not that." She pushed her glasses up. "Every house I walk into, somebody loved everything in it once. Maybe not now. Maybe by the end it was just stuff piled up and the love wore off, that happens too. But once, somebody stood in a store and picked out that exact bowl because they liked it better than the one next to it. Somebody chose all of it." She gestured at the empty room. "I figure the least I can do is know that, while I'm carrying it out the door. Somebody chose it. That's all anybody gets, in the end. Somebody chose them, once, on purpose."

Marlene didn't trust herself to answer.

"Anyhow," Donna said, and put her hands on her knees, and got up the way you get up when you're sixty and have been on the floor, in stages, with sound. "You go on. You don't need to watch the last of it. Nobody needs to watch the last of it."

But Marlene did watch the last of it. She couldn't have said why. She stayed in the straight-backed chair while the afternoon emptied out, while the final customers drifted through and bought their dollar treasures, while Donna and the granddaughter began the after-work of folding the tables and stacking the unsold into boxes for the charity pickup—the lamp, the orphan dishes, the meat thermometer, the ceramic birds of some other dead man's collection that had ended up here, Marlene realized, from some other sale, traveling the circuit of unwanted things. She watched the man with the tool belt carry out the headboard and then the rails and then the slats of her childhood bed and load them in his truck with care, with the unhurried competence of a person who fixed things, and she thought of his daughter, eight years old, and lifted her hand when he waved from the cab.

At five Donna wrote the check. It was less than Marlene had imagined and more than the contents of the rooms had seemed, sitting there, to be worth, a number that bore no relation to anything, that could not be converted back into the dining table or the percolator or the smell of her mother's kitchen, that was just a number, that she folded and put in her purse next to the motel receipt.

"You did good," Donna said. "Staying. Most people can't."

"I don't know that I did good. I just couldn't leave."

"That's the same thing," Donna said. "Mostly that's the same thing."

Marlene carried the cookie tin out to the car herself. The light was gold now and going, the long Ohio summer evening coming on, the cicadas starting up in the maples that her father had planted as saplings and that now shaded the whole front of the house, too big, she'd been told, the roots getting into the foundation, the next owner would probably take them down. She set the tin on the passenger seat. In the trunk the fourth box of photographs still waited, the one she hadn't decided about, and she stood with the trunk open looking at it in the last light and then she closed the trunk without taking anything out, because some decisions you carried around a while longer, and that was allowed, and she got in the car with the buttons beside her and sat for a moment in the driveway looking at the house, lit up gold, emptied, still standing, no longer hers and never, she understood now, entirely anyone's, and then she backed out into the street and drove past the houses of the people she'd grown up among, the porches, the flags, the one stoplight, and out toward the highway, the tin shifting and whispering on the seat beside her every time she turned, a sound like rain, like something poured out, like buttons, like nothing else on earth.
