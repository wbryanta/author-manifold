The clock went first. A man in a windbreaker the color of pond water carried it out cradled like a baby, and I thought, that's the clock that never kept right time, that ran eleven minutes fast my whole childhood so we were always early to church, always early to the bus, always standing on corners waiting for a world that hadn't caught up to us yet. Eleven minutes. Mama set it that way on purpose and never told anybody and let us all run our lives by a lie she'd built into the gears, and now it was going out the door in the arms of a stranger who paid four dollars for it.

I didn't say anything. That was the thing I'd promised myself in the car that morning, sitting in the driveway with the engine ticking down. Don't say anything. Let them carry it. You can't keep a house by holding its furniture, and you sure can't keep a mother that way, and the storage unit out by the highway was already costing more than the whole haul would bring. So. Let them carry it.

There were maybe thirty people moving through the rooms, and I sat at the card table by the front door with the metal box of bills and the roll of stickers, and I watched them touch things. That was the part nobody warns you about. The touching. They picked up Mama's saucers and turned them over to read the bottoms. They opened her drawers. A woman with a baby strapped to her chest stood in front of the bookshelf running her finger down the spines like she was checking them for dust, and I wanted to tell her those were read books, every one of them, that my mother was not a woman who put a book on a shelf she hadn't been all the way through, but I didn't, because what would that mean to her. A read book and an unread book cost the same. Fifty cents. I had stickered them myself.

"This work?" A man held up the radio.

"It works."

"Don't look like it works."

"It works," I said again, and he set it down with the look they all had, the look that said they knew better, that they'd seen a thousand dead women's houses and knew exactly what worked and what didn't, what was worth the asking price and what you could talk a grieving daughter down on if you stood there long enough looking unconvinced.

I let him talk me down. I let all of them. By eleven the morning was bright and ordinary and the lawn was wet still in the shade by the porch, and I had taken in maybe sixty dollars and watched the front room go from a room my mother had lived in to a room with holes in it. You don't notice a room is made of objects until the objects start to leave. Then you see the shape of the air where the armchair was. You see the rectangle on the wallpaper, lighter than the rest, where the picture of the praying hands hung for forty years, and you understand that the wallpaper has been waiting all this time underneath, keeping its true color in the dark, and that the room around it faded while that one square stayed young.

My sister Della was supposed to come. Della said she would come and then Della called Thursday and said the drive was too much with her hip and the children and Marcus's schedule, and I said all right, Della, I'll handle it, the way I have handled all of it, the hospital and the home and the papers and the man from the funeral place who called my mother "your loved one" so many times I started to think that was going to be carved on the stone. *Your Loved One. 1931 to now.* I didn't say that to Della. I said all right.

So it was me at the card table.

A girl came in around noon, college age, with another girl, and they went straight for the kitchen and I heard them in there laughing low the way young women laugh when they're somewhere they think is funny, an old woman's kitchen, an old town, a sad cheap morning's worth of somebody's whole accumulated life. They came out with the Pyrex. The yellow bowl and the green one and the smallest one that was the color of a bruise gone soft.

"How much for the set?"

"Three dollars."

"For all three?"

"For all three."

The girl looked at her friend and the friend shrugged and the girl gave me three soft worn ones from a little cloth wallet, and I took them, and I thought of my mother's hands in that yellow bowl, the heel of her palm pressing the dough, her wedding ring she'd worked off her finger and set on the windowsill so as not to lose it in the flour, and how I'd stood on a chair beside her and she'd given me a fist of dough to ruin while she made the real biscuits, and how that yellow bowl was, in some way I could not have explained to the girl without frightening her, the inside of a feeling I had not had a name for until right that second, watching it go out my mother's door in the arms of a child who would put cut fruit in it, or earrings, or nothing at all, and never once know.

"You want a bag?" I said.

"We're good," the girl said.

You're good, I thought. You're good. Lord knows you're good.

The thing about it is that I had not lived in that house since I was eighteen and I had not wanted to. I want to be honest, because Della tells the story one way and I have always told it another, and the way Della tells it our mother was a sweet woman whom we loved and the way I tell it our mother was a sweet woman whom we loved, but in mine there is a winter when I was fourteen and she did not speak to me for nine days because of a thing I will not put down here, nine full days, passing me in the hall, setting my plate down without a word, and on the ninth day she set the plate down and said *pass the salt* like nothing, like nothing at all, and I passed the salt and we never spoke of it and I think she believed she had taught me something. She had. She had taught me that a person can be made to not exist by another person who loves them, that love is not the opposite of that, that they sit in the same chest side by side like a good dog and a bad one, and I have spent forty years of my own marriage and my own motherhood unlearning the salt and not all the way doing it.

I did not say any of this to anybody. I sat at the card table.

A heavyset man in suspenders wanted the dining table.

"It's spoken for," I said, which was not true. Nothing was spoken for. But I looked at that table, the long oak table with the leaf you cranked in for Thanksgiving and the burn mark near the head where my father set down a pan once in 1968 before he left us, a man I barely remember except as a smell of motor oil and a way of laughing that made the table shake — I looked at that table and I could not. Not yet. Not before noon. So I lied to the heavyset man and he frowned and moved off toward the bedroom where two women were already going through the closet, holding Mama's church dresses up against themselves in the long mirror, turning side to side.

That mirror. I knew that mirror. I had stood in front of that mirror at sixteen in a yellow dress Mama made me and she had stood behind me with pins in her mouth and looked at us both in the glass, her face over my shoulder, and she'd taken the pins out one at a time and said, *you are going to be a problem for somebody, you,* and smiled, and it was the closest thing to a blessing I ever got from her, that smile in the glass, that word, *problem,* which in her mouth meant something a regular word couldn't carry, meant beautiful, meant trouble, meant mine. And now two strangers were turning side to side in it, and the mirror was doing for them exactly what it had done for me, holding their faces back to them with that same patient honesty, and I understood the mirror did not care, had never cared, would hold any face that came before it and let it go just as easy, and that this is the thing about objects that the people carrying them out the door will never feel: the object does not grieve. Only the watcher grieves. The yellow bowl is fine. The clock is fine, eleven minutes fast in a stranger's hallway, and it does not miss us at all.

By one o'clock the light had moved off the porch and come slanting in through the front windows the way it used to do on Sunday afternoons, and I had to get up and walk through the house because if I sat at that table one more minute in that one o'clock light I was going to do something Della would hear about for the rest of her life.

The house was emptying. That is what an estate sale is, though they don't call it that — they call it a sale, like it's a thing you're doing to gain, when it is purely a thing you're doing to lose, lose it down to the walls, lose it down to the shape of the rooms, and I walked through my mother's losing house with my arms wrapped around my own self and went room by room saying goodbye to the air where things had been.

In the back bedroom a man was on his knees in front of the cedar chest.

It was open. He had it open. The lid was up and he had his hands in it, down in the linens, and I stood in the doorway and watched the back of his neck, red and creased, his collar, his big hands going through the things my mother had kept folded down in the cedar for fifty years, the things she kept for *good,* the things that were too good to use, which is its own kind of madness, to own a beautiful thing and decide you are not good enough to use it, to fold it down in cedar against a day that is always tomorrow and then to die with the day still tomorrow and leave it for a stranger on his knees.

"That's not for sale," I said.

He didn't turn around fast. He finished looking at what he was looking at, a tablecloth, white, with the cutwork around the edge that Mama's mother made, my grandmother whom I never met, who died in Georgia before I was a thought, and whose hands were in that cutwork the same way Mama's hands were in the bowl, hands and hands and hands going back down into a dark I came up out of and never saw the bottom of.

"Says here everything in the house," the man said. He still had it in his hands. "Lady at the door, she said everything's got a price."

"That doesn't."

"How much, then." He was a reasonable man. I could see he was a reasonable man and that this was a thing reasonable men do, they find the number, every grief has a number if you stand there patient and keep your voice low, and he was being patient, and his lowness of voice was the lowness you use on a dog that might bite. "I'll give you twenty for the chest and what's in it."

And here is what I want to set down. Here is the thing.

I almost said yes.

I stood in that doorway and I felt the whole weight of the day come down on me, the storage unit and the highway and the drive home in the dark and Della not coming and the man from the funeral place and *your loved one* and the nine days of silence and the salt, the salt, and I thought, take it, take it all, take the chest and the cutwork and my grandmother's hands and the day that was always tomorrow, take it for twenty dollars and let me go home, because I am fifty-six years old and I am tired in a place that twenty dollars or twenty thousand cannot reach, and what is a tablecloth, what is cedar, what is any of it but the things she kept instead of the things she might have given, folded down in the dark for a good that never came.

I almost said yes.

Then I saw, down in the chest, under where he'd moved the tablecloth, a smaller thing. A flat thing wrapped in tissue gone yellow-brown at the folds. And I knew what it was before I crossed the room and took it out of the chest with him still kneeling there, I knew by the size and the weight of it, and I unfolded the tissue right there standing over him and it was the yellow dress.

The dress she made me. The one from the mirror. Sixteen. *You are going to be a problem for somebody, you.* She had kept it. She had folded it down in the cedar with the good things, with her mother's cutwork, with the things too good to use, and I had not known. In forty years I had not known she kept it, had thought it long gone, given away or worn to rags by some other girl in some other house, and the whole time it was here, an arm's length from where she slept, folded down in the dark against the day. Against this day. Against me, fifty-six and standing in her emptying house, finding out at the very end what she could not say in life, which was that she had thought I was a problem worth keeping, that the smile in the glass was true, that she had taken the dress off my sixteen-year-old body and never let it out of the house again.

"Ma'am?" the man said.

"Get up," I said. Not unkind. I didn't have unkind left in me. "Get up off the floor, please."

He got up. He had bad knees, I could hear them, and he stood there in his reasonable shirt looking at the dress in my hands and I think he understood, in the way that even reasonable men who go through dead women's chests for a living must come to understand something, that he had put his hands down into a place he had no business, that there are things in the cedar that are not for sale at any number, that some of what we fold down in the dark we fold down for the daughter and only the daughter, sent forward through fifty years like a letter with no stamp, to arrive on exactly the afternoon when she has finally, finally given up on hearing from us.

"Sorry for your loss," he said, which was the first true thing anybody had said to me all day, truer than my own sister, and he went on out of the room and I heard him say something to the woman I'd hired at the front and I heard the screen door and his truck, and I sat down on the bare edge of the bed with the dress across my knees.

It still smelled of cedar. Under the cedar, nothing of her, nothing of me, only the long clean nothing that cedar makes, that keeps a thing the way the wallpaper keeps its color in the dark behind the picture. I held it up. It was small. I had been small. The yellow had not faded, folded down all those years out of the light, the yellow was still the yellow, butter and sun and the first forsythia, the loud unembarrassed yellow she had chosen on purpose because she was not, whatever else she was, a woman who believed her daughter should go quiet into a room.

I did not put it on. I want to say I put it on but I am too honest and besides it would not have gone over my arm. I held it against me the way the women had held the church dresses, and I went out into the front room where the long oak table still stood, the one I'd lied about, and the one o'clock light had gone to two o'clock light and lay across the burn mark from 1968, and I stood at the head of the table where my father had stood, where my mother had stood, where I had never once been allowed to sit as a child because the head of the table was for the parent, and I sat down in it. I sat down at the head of my mother's table with the yellow dress in my lap in the emptying house and I let the strangers carry the rest of it out around me. The bed. The bookshelf, fifty cents a book. The radio that worked. The lamp. The good chair and the bad chair, side by side, the way love keeps them.

A woman touched my shoulder near three o'clock. The one I'd hired.

"We're 'bout cleared out," she said. "You want me to put a sticker on this table or you keeping it?"

I looked down the long length of it, all the leaf cranked out for a Thanksgiving that wasn't coming, set for nobody, and I thought about the storage unit and the highway and the drive home in the dark, and I thought about Della's children who had never sat at it, and I thought about how a table is only a table and the love does not live in the wood, the love never lived in the wood, the love lived in my mother and my mother is gone where the wood cannot follow, and keeping the table would not keep her any more than the bowl kept her or the clock, eleven minutes fast forever in a stranger's hall.

"Sell it," I said.

And then, because I am my mother's problem after all, kept and folded and sent forward fifty years to say the thing she couldn't:

"No. Wait." I put my hand flat on the wood, on the burn, where the pan went down in 1968 the year before the laughing man left and the table stopped shaking. "I'll take it."

"It's a big table," she said gently. "Just you?"

"Just me," I said. "For now. But I've got a sister with a hip and some grandchildren of my own coming up, and you never know who's going to need a place to sit."

She gave me the look they all give, the knowing look, but hers had something else under it, and she nodded and peeled the sticker off and stuck it to her own sleeve so I could see it gone.

I stayed past the last of them. I stayed until the woman locked the box and counted out my afternoon into my hand, a hundred and forty dollars and some change for the whole of it, the saucers and the books and the Pyrex and the clock, and I folded the bills the way Mama folded everything, the way her mother folded the cutwork, down small, down into the dark of my pocket against a day. Then I went through the empty rooms one more time in the going-down light, and they were just rooms now, just air and faded paper and the shapes of what had stood there, and the house was not my mother and had never been, and I think I had needed all afternoon and all those strangers' hands to learn it.

I carried the yellow dress out to the car myself. I would not let them touch it. I laid it on the seat beside me, smoothed it flat, and sat a minute in the driveway with the engine ticking up to warm in the cold, looking at the house going blue in the dusk, and the table I'd have to come back for with a truck, and the wallpaper square that was, somewhere in there in the dark, still keeping its true young color where the praying hands had hung.

Eleven minutes fast my whole life, and here at last I had time. I had all the time there was. I sat in it. Then I put the car in reverse and backed out slow into the small Ohio evening with my mother's dress beside me, folded down no longer, riding home in the light.
