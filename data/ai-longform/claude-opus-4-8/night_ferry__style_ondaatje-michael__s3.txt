The ferry left at eleven, and by then the harbour had emptied of everything but the gulls and the slow machinery of departure. Niall stood at the rail with his coat collar turned up and watched the lights of the town slide backward into the water, each one doubling itself, breaking, reassembling. He had not slept properly in two days. The letter was in his breast pocket, folded into quarters, the official seal already broken by his thumb on the train. A man answering this description. We would ask that you attend. The word attend had stayed with him, as if the dead required an audience, as if his brother in death had finally arranged the appointment he would never keep in life.

He thought: it may not be him. He held to this the way a man holds to a rope in fog.

The strait was sixty miles of black water between the mainland and the island where they had pulled the body from the rocks below the lighthouse. In summer the crossing took four hours in daylight and the tourists photographed the seals. But it was October now, and the night ferry ran half-empty, carrying freight and the occasional traveller with a reason that could not wait for morning. Niall had a cabin he did not want. He stayed on deck until the cold drove the feeling from his hands, and then he went inside.

There is a particular quality to the inside of a ship at night. The hum that enters the bones. The corridors that lean, almost imperceptibly, so that walking becomes a kind of negotiation. He found the bar still open, a steward polishing glasses with the patience of a man who had given up expecting custom. Niall ordered whisky and the steward poured it without comment and went back to his polishing, and the two of them existed for a while in the suspended grammar of strangers who will not speak.

Eamon had gone to sea at seventeen. That was the first leaving, the one their mother never forgave, though she dressed it as forgiveness, which was worse. He had sent postcards from ports whose names Niall had to find in the atlas — Valparaíso, Murmansk, a place in the Aleutians whose letters he could not pronounce. The cards said nothing. Weather fine. Ship sound. As though the world were a thing to be reported on in the language of insurance. And then the cards stopped, and there had been the years of silence, broken once by a phone call at three in the morning, Eamon's voice thick with drink or distance, saying only, Niall, are you there, are you there, and Niall, half-asleep and angry, had said yes, what is it, what's wrong, and the line had gone dead, and he had told himself it was a wrong number, a drunk, anyone.

That had been six years ago. He had not called back. He had not known the number to call.

The whisky did not help and he did not expect it to. He carried the glass to a window and watched the dark go by, which was no different from watching nothing, and he found in the nothing a kind of company. Somewhere below the waterline the engines turned. A child cried briefly in a distant cabin and was hushed. He thought about the body. He had been told very little. Male, forties to fifties, no documents, found at the base of the cliff three days after a storm. The sea does things to a man it has held for a while. He understood that he might not recognise the face. He understood that recognition might be a matter of other things — a scar, the architecture of the hands, the small white seam above the left eyebrow where Eamon had gone through the greenhouse glass at nine years old, chasing him, both of them running, and Niall the faster, always the faster, and the sound of the glass and then his brother's astonishment more than his pain, the blood threading down into his eye while he stood there saying nothing, looking at his own red hand.

Niall had run to get their mother. That was what he remembered. He had not stayed. He had run.

He went out again onto the deck because the cabin felt like a coffin upended, and the wind had risen, and now the sea was visible in long pale collapses where the swell broke open and showed its underside. The lights of the mainland were gone entirely. There was only the ship and the cold and the suggestion of immensity that the dark held back. A woman stood at the rail not far from him, alone, and he was surprised, because he had thought himself the only one foolish enough to be out in it. She wore no hat. Her hair went sideways in the wind and she did not seem to mind.

"You'll freeze," he said, because the silence between two people at a rail at midnight asks something of them.

"I've been colder," she said. She did not turn. "You're going across to identify someone."

He looked at her then. "How would you know that."

"It's the only reason to take this boat in October with a face like yours." She said it without unkindness. "I've seen the face before. I worked the crossing for eleven years. You learn to read the manifest in people's shoulders."

"And which is yours," he said. "The manifest."

Now she turned. She was older than her voice, perhaps sixty, with a face that the weather had worked at the way it works at the figureheads of old ships, taking nothing essential, only the soft excess. "I'm going to bury my husband's ashes," she said. "He wanted them in the water off the north head. He was a difficult man and I loved him and I have carried this tin around for two years working up to it." She patted the bag at her side, and Niall heard, faintly, the shift of something granular within. "So. We are both of us couriers of the dead tonight."

"I don't know yet that he's dead. My brother. The one I'm going to see."

"No," she said. "But you came."

The remark sat in him. He had not thought of it as a thing he had done by choice. The letter had arrived and he had got on the train and now the boat, and it had seemed less an act than a current he had stepped into, the way water finds the channel cut for it. But she was right. He had come. There were others who might have gone — a cousin in the city, the wife Eamon had left in Halifax, if she was still alive, if she was still anything. The official letter had found Niall because Niall's name was the one in the old address book they had recovered from a hostel where the man had stayed. The dead man, if it was a man and not a guess. Niall's name and a number long disconnected. So that even at the end, if it was the end, it was him his brother had carried, the brother who ran to fetch their mother and did not stay.

"I left him," Niall said. He had not meant to say it. The wind took half of it. "Years ago. There was a falling-out. Money, but not really money. You know how it is. Money's only the shape the other thing takes."

"I know exactly how it is."

"He went to sea. He kept going. And I stayed, and I married, and I let the silence happen because silence is easy, it's the easiest thing in the world, you don't have to do anything to keep it, it keeps itself." He was talking too much. The whisky, the cold, the woman who would be gone by morning and to whom therefore everything could be said. "And now there's a body on an island and they want me to attend."

"To attend," she repeated, and she heard in it what he had heard. She nodded slowly. "My husband used that word. He was a religious man toward the end, which surprised us both. He said dying was the one appointment a man keeps without trying."

"My brother kept very few."

"Then maybe this is the first time he's waited for you."

He had no answer to that. The ship rose and fell beneath them in its long breathing. After a while she said good night and went inside, the tin shifting in her bag as she walked the leaning corridor, and Niall stayed out alone until the cold was no longer something he felt against his skin but something he contained, a temperature of the self.

He slept, finally, in the narrow bunk, and dreamed in the way of overtired men, in fragments that did not cohere. The greenhouse. The sound of the glass. But in the dream he did not run. In the dream he stayed and pressed his own hand to his brother's eyebrow to stop the blood and Eamon looked at him with that same astonishment, and said, you stayed, and Niall woke with the word in his mouth and the ship still moving through the dark and his face wet, and he lay for a long time not knowing whether he had wept in sleep or whether it was only the closeness of the cabin, the condensation, the sea finding its way in as it always did, by increments, by patience, into every sealed thing.

Toward dawn the engines changed their pitch. He felt it before he heard it, the deceleration, the great animal of the ship beginning to gather itself toward arrival. He dressed and went up and the island was there, low and dark against a sky going grey at its eastern hem, the lighthouse on the north head still turning its slow beam though the night was nearly spent, throwing its light out over water that no longer needed it. There was the harbour, small, a scatter of houses on the hill behind it, smoke standing up straight from one or two chimneys in the windless cold of first light. A place his brother had come to die, or be killed by the simple machinery of weather and rock, three days before anyone found him. Niall tried to imagine the man choosing this island and could not. But then he had never been able to imagine any of his brother's choices, which was perhaps the whole of the failure between them, that Niall's imagination had a wall in it precisely where his brother began.

The woman was at the rail again as they came in. She had slept, he thought; she looked more rested than he felt, or perhaps simply more reconciled. She did not speak but stood beside him as the ferry made its turn and the harbour swung toward them, and the gulls lifted off the breakwater in a single sheet and resettled, and the lines were thrown, and men below caught them and made them fast with the bored competence of those who do a thing ten thousand times. The gangway came down with a clang that echoed off the hill.

"The north head's a walk from here," she said. "Two hours, if the path's not washed out. I'll do it this afternoon, when the light's better."

"I hope it goes as you want it to."

"It won't," she said, and smiled. "Nothing does. But it'll go." She put her hand briefly on his forearm, a thing strangers do not do, and then she had picked up her bag with its quiet cargo and was gone down the gangway, and he watched her go up the road toward the houses until a turning took her, and he never saw her again, and he thought of her sometimes for the rest of his life, the courier of ashes, the reader of shoulders, with a gratitude he could not have explained to anyone.

The man who met him was young, an official of some kind, a constable perhaps, in a coat too large for him. He held a clipboard against his chest like a shield. "Mr. Doherty," he said. "You'll have had a rough crossing. There's tea up at the station. The doctor said there's no hurry, you're to take your time."

"I'd rather not take my time," Niall said.

The young man looked at him and seemed to understand something and nodded. "Right," he said. "It's not far."

They walked up through the village, which was waking, a shop unshuttering, a dog crossing the road on its own business, two women talking in a doorway who stopped to watch them pass with the frank attention of a place where strangers were an event and a stranger going to the mortuary was an event with a meaning everyone already knew. The young man talked to fill the silence, about the storm, about how the body had been found by a man checking his lobster pots, about how they didn't get many of this kind, washed up, the sea around here mostly kept what it took. Niall heard about a third of it. He was watching the island assemble itself around him, the stone walls, the wind-bent trees all leaning the one way like a congregation, and he was thinking that his brother had walked this same road, perhaps, had seen these same walls, had come to the end of all his crossings here, on this small hard place in the middle of the cold water, and had not called, and had not written, and had carried Niall's dead name and number in a book to the last.

The mortuary was a low building behind the surgery, breeze-block, recently built, with the smell that such places have everywhere, the cold chemical smell underneath which is the other smell that the chemicals are meant to answer and never quite do. The doctor was a woman near retirement with kind tired eyes who shook his hand and held it a moment longer than the shake required.

"Take whatever time you need," she said. "And if you're not sure, that's all right. It happens. Uncertainty is an honest answer. We have other ways — we can take samples, send away, it takes weeks but it's sure. You don't have to decide anything today with your eye alone."

"I want to see him."

"Of course."

She led him through. The young constable stayed behind. There was a room and in the room a table and on the table a shape beneath a sheet, and the doctor stood at the head of it and looked at Niall to see that he was ready, and Niall, who was not ready, who had not been ready since the letter, who had perhaps not been ready since the greenhouse, nodded, and she drew the sheet back to the collarbone.

The sea had been at the face. He had been told to expect this and the telling was nothing, the telling was a country described to a man who must now live in it. He made himself look. He made himself do the thing he had come sixty miles of black water to do, which was to take this ruin and lay it against the template he carried, the brother who ran behind him through the long grass, the boy with the red hand, the man whose voice had said into the phone are you there, are you there. He looked at the brow. He looked for the white seam above the left eye, the greenhouse scar, the one certainty he had brought with him across the strait like a coin to pay the ferryman.

It was not there.

The brow was unbroken. Whatever the water had done, it had not invented a scar and it had not erased one cleanly enough to leave the skin that smooth, that whole. He looked and he looked. He asked the doctor, his voice not quite his own, whether there might have been damage there that healed, whether the sea, and she said gently no, there was no scarring of any kind in that region, she had examined him closely, the only old scar on the body was on the right forearm, a long one, surgical perhaps, an old break pinned.

Eamon's arm had never been broken. Niall would have known. He had inventoried his brother's body the way younger brothers do, knew every mark of it, the chickenpox crater at the hairline, the scar, the crooked little finger from the door he'd slammed it in. This man had none of these. This man had a pinned forearm and an unbroken brow and was someone, was certainly someone, was certainly some other family's appointment kept, some other brother's black crossing yet to come.

"It isn't him," Niall said.

The doctor exhaled, and he understood the exhalation, the small professional relief of a wrong put right, a body that could now go toward its true name. "You're certain."

"There's a scar he should have. Here." He touched his own brow, the place. "Since he was nine. It isn't here. And the arm. His arm was never broken." He found that his hand was shaking and he put it down. "It isn't my brother."

She drew the sheet back up, gently, over the face the sea had taken, and for a moment Niall stood looking at the shape of the stranger under the white, and felt rise in him something he had no name for and did not expect, which was grief. Not relief, or not only. Grief, plain and total, for this man he had never met, this courier of someone else's loss, lying nameless on a table on a hard island while somewhere a woman or a brother or a child did not yet know to begin the long business of mourning. And underneath that grief, indivisible from it, the other thing — that his own brother was alive, or at least was not this, was somewhere still under the same grey sky drawing breath, and Niall did not know where, and had not known where for years, and had let the silence keep itself, and the silence was not death after all but only silence, which could still be broken, which was the harder thing, because death asks nothing of us but mourning and silence asks everything, asks that we cross the water ourselves and go and stand at the door and say the name.

"Thank you," he said to the doctor, which made no sense, and she seemed to know it made sense anyway.

He went out into the morning. The constable rose from a bench, his face a question, and Niall shook his head, and the young man's shoulders dropped and he said something kind that Niall did not hear, because he was looking past him to the harbour and the water and the lighthouse on the north head still turning, paler now in the risen day, almost invisible against the brightening sky, and he was thinking of the woman climbing the path that afternoon with her tin of ashes, going to give her difficult husband to the sea off the head, keeping the one appointment, and he thought: I have not kept mine. The man I came to mourn is the wrong man. My own is out there still.

He sat on the bench the constable had warmed and he took the letter out of his breast pocket, the folded official paper, a man answering this description, and he held it and did not read it because he knew it now by heart and it had been a letter about the wrong man all along. He thought about the address book they had found, his name in it, the disconnected number. His brother had carried him. Across how many ports, how many years, how many crossings of how many cold straits, Eamon had carried the name and the number of the brother who ran to fetch their mother and did not stay, who let the silence keep itself, who had not called back. The book was somewhere in this building, evidence, soon to be released to the dead man's true family along with the body. But the book was not the dead man's. The book was his brother's, surely; how else would Niall's name be in it. There had been some confusion, the hostel, two men's belongings, the years made everything porous. Somewhere his brother had lost an address book with Niall's name in it and somewhere a stranger had died with it, and the dead lent the living their names and the living went on losing one another across the water in the old way, the only way, the way that could still, even now, be refused.

The ferry back did not leave until evening. He had the whole day on the island, the unlooked-for grace of it, and he decided he would walk to the north head after all, on the chance, on the small hard chance of seeing her again, the woman, at the water's edge with her tin, two couriers of the dead met once more by accident on the cliff. And if she was not there he would stand on the head himself and look north over the grey heaving sea toward all the ports his brother might be in, and he would begin, in his own slow and inadequate way, to work out how a man goes about finding a brother who is not dead. The path, the constable had said, might be washed out. Niall thought he would chance it. He had chanced the water. He could chance the path. He stood, and put the letter back in his pocket, against his chest, where it had ridden all this way, and turned his face toward the head, and started up.
