The break was where Daniel said it would be, of course, forty feet down the eastern line where the apricots gave way to the last good row of peaches, and Caleb stood over the muddy seep in the dirt with his hands on his hips and felt the old irritation of his brother being right rise up in him like heartburn.

"Told you," Daniel said. He set the toolbox down and it sank a little into the soft ground. "You could see it from the truck. The grass is greener all along here."

"Everything's greener here. It's an orchard."

"Greener than it should be." Daniel crouched and pressed two fingers into the mud, and they came away dark and slick, and he held them up like evidence in a courtroom. "Saturated. The line's been weeping for a week, maybe more. Probably why the pressure dropped on the upper trees."

Caleb said nothing. He looked up the slope toward the house, white and small against the brown hill, the paint going the way of everything else, peeling in long curls under the eaves where their mother used to set the hummingbird feeders. The hills behind it were the color of a lion in June, that gold that meant the rains were four months gone and four months off, and somewhere in the dry grass a single quail was calling, that three-note question that sounded like a door that needed oiling.

"We're going to have to dig it out," Daniel said.

"I know how it works."

"Just saying." Daniel stood, knees cracking, and for a moment Caleb saw their father in the gesture, the way he'd brace a hand against his own thigh to rise, and it gave him a strange vertigo, like looking down a well and seeing not water but the sky. Daniel was thirty-eight now. Caleb was forty-one. Their father had been dead nineteen months and the orchard had been dying for longer than that, dying so slowly that you could pretend it was just resting, just having a hard year, the way you could tell yourself about a person that they were just tired.

They dug. The shovels bit into the wet ground and then into the dry beneath it, two different soils a few inches apart, and the dry came up in clods that broke against the blade. The sun climbed. By nine it was already the kind of heat that made the air over the rows shimmer, and Caleb took off his shirt and hung it on a low branch and felt the burn start almost immediately on his shoulders, the white winter skin of a man who worked indoors now, who sold software to dentists and lived in a city two hours south where the orchard was a thing he carried around inside him like a stone he'd swallowed.

"You should keep your shirt on," Daniel said. "You'll cook."

"I'm fine."

"Suit yourself."

They found the pipe, the old black poly line their father had run himself in the eighties, brittle now, and the crack was a long one, eight inches, where the plastic had simply given up under the pressure of years. Daniel ran his thumb along it.

"This is the third one this summer," he said.

"So patch it."

"I can patch it. I've got a coupling. But Caleb." He sat back on his heels and looked up, squinting, and the look on his face was the one Caleb had been dreading since the drive up, the one that meant they were going to have the conversation again, the one that had been happening in pieces for nineteen months, over the phone, in the truck, at the kitchen table at midnight with the bottle between them. "The whole line's like this. All of it. I patch this and it breaks somewhere else in August. I'm out here every week. I'm out here instead of doing my actual work, instead of—" He stopped. "The whole system needs to be replaced. Forty acres of drip line. You know what that costs?"

"So we replace it."

"With what money."

Caleb threw a clod of dirt off into the row, harder than he needed to, and it burst against a tree trunk. "We've got the money from Dad's life insurance."

"That's gone. That paid the back taxes and the loan against the harvest that never came in. I told you that in March. You were on the call. You said you understood."

"I understood the words."

Daniel exhaled through his nose. He pulled the coupling out of the toolbox and set it on the lip of the trench, and got the knife out, and began to cut the bad section of pipe away with the patience of a man who had learned patience the hard way, alone, out here, every weekend for nineteen months while his brother sold software to dentists.

"Maddie wants to know if she can have the trampoline at her birthday," Daniel said, not looking up. "I told her I'd ask you because it's coming up on the same weekend you said you might come."

"I'll come."

"You said that for Easter."

"Something came up for Easter."

"Something's always coming up." Daniel said it without heat, which was worse than if he'd shouted, and he fitted the first end of the coupling and Caleb watched his brother's hands, the brown forearms, the wedding ring he still wore though Sarah had been gone three years now, gone to Sacramento with a man who sold insurance and didn't smell like diesel and dirt at the end of the day. Daniel had stayed. Daniel always stayed. That was the difference between them and it had been the difference between them since they were boys, when Caleb would be the one to climb the water tower on a dare and Daniel the one who'd stand at the bottom holding the flashlight, ready to run for help, ready to catch.

"I think we should sell," Caleb said.

There. He'd said it in daylight, with the sun on them and the quail calling and the broken pipe between them in the dirt, and it hung in the air and did not dissipate.

Daniel kept working. "I know you do."

"The Hartmanns sold last year. You know what they got? Per acre? Vineyards are coming up the whole valley, they're buying everything, they'll buy us, they'll pay—"

"I know what they'll pay."

"Then you know it's enough that you don't have to do this anymore. You could—" Caleb gestured at the trench, at the brittle pipe, at the whole forty acres of it. "You could do anything. You could buy a house in town. You could send Maddie to college without—"

"Don't." Daniel's voice changed, just slightly, a hardening at the edge of it. "Don't bring her into it like you know what she needs. You see her three times a year."

"That's not fair."

"None of it's fair." He fitted the second end and tightened the clamp, and his hands were steady but Caleb could see the muscle working in his jaw. "It's not fair that you got to leave and I didn't. It's not fair that I'm the one who promised him. You weren't there."

"I was at the funeral. I gave the—"

"Not the funeral. Before. The week before. When he knew." Daniel looked up now and his eyes were wet, and Caleb had to look away, up the hill, at the house. "He held my hand. He could barely talk by then. And he said, don't let them take the trees. He said it twice. Don't let them take the trees, Danny. And I said okay. I said okay, Dad."

Caleb was quiet for a long time. A red-tailed hawk turned in the white sky above the eastern ridge, hunting the dry grass, and the quail had gone silent, knowing.

"He was on morphine," Caleb said finally. "He didn't know what he was saying."

It was the cruelest thing he could have said and he knew it as it left his mouth, the way you know a glass is falling the instant it leaves your hand, and Daniel stood up out of the trench in one motion and walked off down the row without a word, and Caleb stood there with his sunburned shoulders and his stone of an orchard inside him and hated himself in the particular way that only a brother can hate himself, completely and from a great and intimate distance.

He let him go. There was no following Daniel when he was like that; they had learned this as boys too. You let him walk it off. He'd go down to the end of the row and stand under the old fig their grandfather had planted, the one tree on the whole property that wasn't trying to die, and he'd put his hand on the trunk and breathe, and then he'd come back. Caleb picked up the shovel and began to fill in the trench around the patched pipe, tamping the dirt down with the flat of the blade, and the work was good because it was simple, because it asked nothing of him but his back and his hands.

When the trench was filled he walked up to the truck and got the water jug from the cab and drank, the water gone warm and tasting of plastic, and he poured some over his head and stood dripping in the dirt of the turnaround where they'd parked, where their father had parked, where their father's father had parked the old Ford that was still up behind the barn on flat tires with a manzanita growing up through the engine block. Three generations of trucks in the same patch of dirt. He thought about that and then he made himself stop thinking about it because that was exactly the kind of thinking that Daniel wanted him to do, the kind that wasn't thinking at all but feeling dressed up as thinking, the kind that would keep them both chained to forty acres of dying trees until they were old men.

He went and turned the water back on at the valve and walked the eastern line to check the patch. It held. The drip emitters down the row began to tick, one after another, that small sound he'd known his whole life, the orchard drinking, and despite everything something in him eased at the sound, some animal part of him that didn't sell software to dentists.

Daniel came back up the row after twenty minutes. He'd washed his face somewhere, at the spigot by the pump house probably, and his eyes were clear. He didn't apologize and neither did Caleb because they didn't do that, hadn't done it since they were small, but Daniel picked up the second shovel and they stood together looking at the line of ticking emitters and the equilibrium between them was restored, fragile and real.

"It's holding," Daniel said.

"It's holding."

"For now."

"For now."

They stood. The heat pressed down. Up in the apricots a mockingbird ran through its whole repertoire, jay and finch and the rusty-hinge cry of a scrub-oak titmouse, showing off for no one.

"You hungry?" Daniel said.

"Yeah."

They walked up to the house. The kitchen was the same as it had always been, the same yellow curtains their mother had hung before she died, before either of them was old enough to remember her except as a smell and a feeling, lemon and warmth, and the same Formica table with the cigarette burn at the corner from their father's one bad year. Daniel made sandwiches the way their father had made them, thick, with too much mustard, and they ate standing up at the counter because sitting felt like committing to something.

"I looked at the numbers again," Caleb said, with his mouth full, because it was easier to say it that way, casually, like it was nothing. "Last night. I couldn't sleep. The crop loan, the equipment, what we owe the Bertinellis for the custom work. Even if we have a good year—and we won't, the trees are too old, half the peaches didn't set—even if we have a good year we clear maybe twelve thousand. Twelve thousand dollars, Danny. For both of us. For a year of your weekends and your weeks. That's nothing. That's not even a thing you can call money."

"I know the numbers."

"Then how do you—" Caleb set the sandwich down. "Help me understand. Because I've been trying for nineteen months to understand and I can't. It's not a business. It hasn't been a business since Dad got sick. It's a—" He searched for the word. "It's a grave you keep digging."

Daniel chewed. He looked out the window over the sink, down the slope to the rows, the trees going silver-green in the heat, the patched line he couldn't see but knew was there.

"You remember the flood year?" Daniel said. "Ninety-five. You'd have been—what, sixteen."

"I remember it rained."

"It rained for two weeks straight. The creek came up over the lower five acres and Dad was sure we'd lose the whole orchard, the roots would rot, the trees would just topple. He didn't sleep. He went out every night with a flashlight in his slicker to check the trees like checking on a sick kid, all night, walking the rows. And you slept right through it because you were sixteen and nothing touched you yet. But I went out with him one night. I couldn't sleep either. And we walked the whole orchard in the rain, the two of us, and he didn't say anything for the longest time and then he said—" Daniel stopped. His voice had gone rough. "He said, your great-grandfather planted the first of these in 1911. He came out from Missouri with nothing. Nothing, Danny. And he put these trees in the ground because he believed there'd be people here after him to eat the fruit. That's faith, he said. Planting a tree you'll never sit under. And he put his hand on my shoulder and we stood in the rain and I was twelve and I thought—I thought I understood something. About what it was for."

The kitchen was very quiet. Somewhere in the house a clock their father had wound every Sunday for forty years ticked, though neither of them had wound it since, so it must have been Daniel, Caleb realized. Daniel had been winding the clock.

"That's a beautiful story," Caleb said, and he meant it, and he also meant the thing he said next, which was, "but it's not an argument."

Daniel laughed, a short surprised sound. "No. I guess it's not."

"He was a romantic. Dad. He loved this place like a person and it never loved him back, it just took, year after year, it took his health and Mom's—" Caleb stopped. They didn't talk about that. Their mother had died of a cancer that some people in the valley whispered came from the spraying, the old chemicals, the stuff they used in the seventies that was banned now, and no one could prove it and their father had never forgiven himself for the not-knowing. "It just took. And now it's taking your weekends and your marriage and it's going to take Maddie's college and what does it give back? Twelve thousand dollars and a story about rain."

Daniel was quiet. Then he said, "It gives back the story about rain."

"That's not enough."

"It might be." Daniel finished his sandwich and brushed the crumbs off his hands into the sink. "It might be exactly enough. That's what you've never understood, Cal. You think the story's the thing that's left over after you subtract the money. Like it's a consolation prize. But what if it's the other way around? What if the money was always just the thing that let us keep the story going? What if the trees were never the asset. What if the trees were the point."

"You can't eat a story."

"You can't eat twelve thousand dollars either, the way you'd spend it. It'd be gone in a year and you'd have nothing. At least this way the nothing has trees on it."

Caleb pushed away from the counter. He was suddenly furious, the old childhood fury, the one that had nothing to do with the argument and everything to do with being the older brother who was supposed to be the one who knew things and instead was always, somehow, the one being taught. "You promised a dying man something he had no right to ask. That's not noble, Danny. That's a trap he set for you because he couldn't stand to let go himself. He put it on you because you were the one who'd carry it. He knew I'd leave. He knew you'd stay. And he used that. He used the best thing about you to chain you to a dying orchard and you're so—you're so loyal you can't even see it."

Daniel turned around slowly. For a moment Caleb thought he'd swing, and some part of him wanted it, wanted the clean simple fact of a fist, but Daniel just looked at him with an expression Caleb couldn't read, sorrow or pity or something older than both.

"You think I don't know that?" Daniel said quietly. "You think I haven't lain awake knowing exactly that? That he was scared and he handed me his fear and called it a legacy? I know what he did, Cal. I'm not stupid. I'm not a romantic like you think." He shook his head. "But here's the thing you can't understand because you left. Knowing it was a trap doesn't make me want out of it. That's the part you can't get to from where you're standing. I know he was wrong to ask and I'd do it again. I'd promise him again right now, today, knowing everything. Because the promise is mine now. It stopped being his the second he died. It's mine. It's the realest thing I have." His voice broke and he didn't try to hide it. "Sarah left because I wouldn't go. Did you know that? Not really because of the orchard. Because I was the kind of man who wouldn't leave it. And she was right that it cost me. And I let her go rather than be a different man. So don't tell me I don't see the trap. I see it fine. I walked into it with my eyes open and I'm not sorry and you flying up here three times a year to tell me I'm a fool is the loneliest thing in my whole life."

Caleb had no answer. The clock ticked. Outside the heat went on, the trees drank, the patched line held, the hawk hunted the gold hill.

"I don't think you're a fool," Caleb said finally.

"You do."

"I think—" Caleb's throat was tight. "I think you're braver than me and it makes me crazy. It's always made me crazy. I climbed the water tower because I couldn't stand at the bottom and hold the light. That's not brave. That's just—loud. You were the brave one. You were always the brave one, standing there ready to catch me, and I couldn't stand it, so I left." He laughed, miserable. "I sell software to dentists, Danny. I have a condo with a view of a parking structure. I have a girlfriend who doesn't know I have a brother because I never talk about any of this because it hurts too much, it hurts like a—and you're up here winding Dad's clock and walking the rows in the rain and I tell myself you're trapped because if you're trapped then I'm free, but I'm not free, I'm just gone, those aren't the same thing, those aren't the same thing at all—"

He stopped because he was crying and he hated it, hated it in front of his brother, and Daniel crossed the kitchen and put his arms around him, smelling of diesel and dirt and sweat and mustard, the smell of their father, the smell of the place, and Caleb held onto him and shook and the two of them stood in the kitchen where their mother had hung the curtains and their father had burned the table with his one cigarette and the clock that neither of them owned and both of them had kept going ticked off the long seconds of the June afternoon.

After a while they let go. Daniel wiped his face with the back of his hand and Caleb wiped his and they did not look directly at each other for a minute, out of mercy.

"We don't have to decide today," Daniel said.

"No."

"There's a break in the upper line too, probably. The pressure's still low. We should walk it before you go."

"Okay."

"You could stay tonight. There's steak. We could—I don't know. We could not talk about it."

Caleb looked at his brother. "Yeah," he said. "I'd like that. I'd like to stay."

They went back out into the heat. The afternoon had tipped over into its long golden decline, the light coming sideways now through the rows, lighting up the dust that hung in the air, and the trees threw their shadows east, long and reaching. They walked the upper line together, two men in a dying orchard their great-grandfather had planted in faith, looking for the next break, and they would find it, and they would patch it, and it would break again, and somewhere down the line, this year or next, they would have to decide. But not today. Today there was the work, and the light, and the smell of warm dirt, and the mockingbird going through all its borrowed songs, and the two of them walking the rows the way their father had, the way his father had, checking the trees like checking on something sick and beloved, something that took and took and gave back only this: the walking, the checking, the being there together in the failing light, which was, Caleb thought, maybe enough, maybe exactly and only enough, and he kept the thought to himself and walked on beside his brother into the long gold end of the day.
