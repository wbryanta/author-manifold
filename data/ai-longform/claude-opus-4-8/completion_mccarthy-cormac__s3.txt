He woke to her hand upon his shoulder and the dark unaltered and lay for a long while listening to her breath go even again beside him while outside the wind moved in the bitterbrush and somewhere far off a dog took up a barking and quit. He did not sleep. When the gray came he rose and dressed in the cold and went out.

The yard lay dim and frozen, the ruts hard as iron underfoot, and the mule in its pole pen had turned its tail to the wind and stood with its head hung low. He pumped water into the trough and broke the skim of ice with the heel of his hand and the mule came and drank without looking at him. He stood watching the country take shape out of the dark, the low hills coming up gray and the river off below them somewhere unseen but felt, a vast cold presence in the land, and the sky paling and going the color of dirty wool with no sun in it anywhere that a man could name.

She had the fire going when he came in and she did not look up from it. He sat at the table and she brought him cornbread and what coffee there was, which was little and weak and made of things that were not coffee, and he ate without speaking and she stood by the hearth with her hands folded under the swell of her belly and watched him eat.

You was hollerin again, she said.

I know it.

What is it you dream.

He looked at her. He did not answer.

You ort to tell it, she said. They say it leaves you when you tell it.

Who says.

She didnt say. She turned and took up the poker and stirred the fire and the light moved over the rough boards of the wall and over the few poor things they owned, a tin of buttons, a daguerreotype of nobody either of them knew that had come with the house, a Bible neither read. The child she carried was the third and the first two were buried out back under fieldstones with no marks on them, and they had not spoken of this either in a long time, and he watched her at the fire and thought she looked older than her years and that he likely did too and that they had been worn down together like two stones in a creek and would go on so until one or the other of them stopped.

I got to see the man about the bottomland, he said.

You been to see him.

I aim to go again.

He aint goin to give it to you.

I never asked him to give it. I aim to work it for a share.

She said nothing. He rose and took down his coat from the peg and his hat and stood a moment in the doorway with the cold coming in around him and she did not turn from the fire and he went out and shut the door.

THE TOWN SAT in a bend of the river where the road forded it, a scatter of frame buildings gone gray and silver in the weather and a few of brick that had pretensions and a church with a steeple that had been struck by lightning and never rebuilt so that it stood truncate against the sky like a thing bitten off. He came in on the mule in the middle of the morning with the cold gone out of the day some and the sun finally showing pale and watery through the overcast and the street a churn of frozen mud with the ruts thawing at the top so that the mule went slipping and the muck sucked at its hooves.

There were men in front of the mercantile and they watched him come and he nodded to them and they nodded back and he tied the mule and went in. The store was warm and dim and smelled of leather and coal oil and the sweetness of molasses and dried fruit. The storekeeper was a fat man named Hobbs with a green eyeshade and sleeve garters and he looked up from his ledger.

I come to see Mister Eldridge, the man said.

He aint here.

Where's he at.

Hobbs set down his pen. He's gone to Memphis on business. Be back of a week or better.

The man stood with his hat in his hands. Outside the door the men talked low among themselves and one of them laughed.

I'll come back, the man said.

He moved among the goods as if he meant to buy something though he had nothing to buy with, looking at the bolts of cloth and the kegs of nails and the rows of bright cans on the shelves with their labels of fruit that did not grow in that country, and Hobbs watched him do it with the patience of a man who has seen poverty walk his floor a thousand times and knows it has no money in its pockets.

You want somethin, Hobbs said. Not a question.

No, the man said. I thank you.

He went out. The men by the door had grown to five or six and there was a stranger among them now, a tall man in a black coat sitting a good horse, and he had drawn the others to him the way iron draws filings, and he was talking and they were listening. The man stopped on the porch boards to put on his hat and could not help but hear.

—seen them at Buena Vista, the stranger was saying, and at Saltillo, and I tell you there is land down there a man could ride three days and never see the end of, and it's there for the taking by them that has the stomach for the taking of it. The greasers cant hold it. They never could hold it. God never meant for it to be held by such a people.

What's down there, one of the men said.

What aint, the stranger said. Gold and silver in the mountains. Cattle running wild by the ten thousand head with no man's brand on them. Women. He grinned and the men grinned with him. There is opportunity down there, friends, for the bold. The governor of Sonora pays a bounty.

A bounty for what.

The stranger looked at him. For Apache hair, he said. A hundred dollars American for a buck, fifty for a squaw, twenty-five for a child. They take the scalp for proof.

The man on the porch stood very still. The stranger turned his head and found him there and looked at him a moment with eyes that were pale and flat and gave back nothing and then turned away again.

A hundred dollars, one of the men said softly.

In gold, the stranger said. In Mexican gold or American, as you like it. I have seen men come out of that country with a mule loaded so heavy with silver it could scarce walk. I have seen it with these eyes.

The man came down off the porch and untied his mule and the men paid him no mind, all of them leaned in toward the stranger now like flowers toward the sun, and he got up on the mule and turned it and rode back out of the town the way he had come, and behind him the stranger's voice went on, low and even and certain, and he could hear it for a long way until the road bent and the wind took it.

HE DID NOT go straight home. He turned the mule down toward the river and rode along the high bank where the water ran brown and swollen and cold below him, and he stopped the mule and sat looking at it. The far bank was timber, black and tangled, and beyond it the land he had come to think of as his though it was not his and never would be unless Eldridge had a mercy in him that no man had ever yet found there. Bottomland. Black dirt that would grow anything a man put in it. He had walked it in the fall and taken up the soil in his hand and smelled it and it had smelled like money, like food, like the future, and he had stood there a long time with the dirt in his hand and felt something he had not felt in a long while which was hope and he had not liked the feeling because he had learned to be afraid of it.

A hundred dollars in gold.

He sat the mule a long time. The light moved on the water. He thought of the two stones out back of the house with no names on them and the third thing coming that would maybe lie under a third stone, and he thought of his wife's face gone gray and old by the fire, and he thought of the dream that came to him in the night, the prophet and the dark sun and the multitude falling on him with their howls, and he did not understand it and did not want to and he turned the mule's head and rode home in the failing light.

She had a stew of something going when he came in and the smell of it filled the little house and she looked at him from the fire.

Well, she said.

He's gone to Memphis. Be back of a week.

She turned back to the pot.

There was a man in town, he said.

She waited.

Says there's a bounty down in Mexico. For fightin the Indians. A hundred dollars in gold for a man.

She did not turn around. Down in Mexico, she said.

That's right.

How far is that.

He set his hat on the table. I dont know exactly, he said. A long way. Months maybe.

And what about the bottomland.

He did not answer her. He sat down at the table and put his hands flat on it and looked at them. They were big hands, cracked and scarred and the nails black, and they had worked hard all his life and got him nothing.

Eldridge aint goin to give you that bottomland, she said. She turned now and looked at him and her face in the firelight was not angry but tired past anger. You know he aint. He's got it promised to his wife's people or he's goin to sell it or he's just goin to set on it the way men like that set on things. He aint goin to give it to you for a share. We been beggin off these people three year now and where has it got us.

I never begged, he said.

What do you call it.

He stood up. The chair scraped on the boards. They stood facing each other across the little room with the fire between them and the child she carried between them too, unborn and unnamed, and outside the wind came up and worried at the eaves.

A hundred dollars in gold, she said. And how many men come back to claim it.

I dont know.

You dont know. And me here with this. She put her hand on her belly. And the two out back. You'd go off to Mexico and leave me here.

I'd come back with money, he said. I'd come back and buy land. Our own land. Not Eldridge's. Not any man's but our own.

Or you'd not come back at all.

He had no answer for that and they both knew it and the silence sat in the room with them like a third presence and after a while she turned back to the fire and stirred the pot and he sat back down and they ate their supper without speaking and the fire burned down and they went to bed in the cold dark and lay each apart from the other with the weight of the thing unsaid and unsettled between them.

He did not dream that night, or if he did he did not remember it.

IN THE GRAY of the morning he lay awake and listened to her breathing and thought it through again the way a man worries a sore tooth with his tongue. He was nine and twenty years old. He had been a farmer and the son of a farmer and his father had died with nothing and his father before him and the land they had worked had belonged to other men always, and he could see the whole of his life laid out before him like the road through the town, straight and plain and going nowhere, and at the end of it the fieldstones with no names. And he thought of the stranger's voice, low and certain, and the gold, and the women, and the land a man could ride three days and not see the end of, and something turned over in him that he could not put down.

He rose before light again and went out. The morning was clear and bitter cold and the stars still showing in the west and a hard frost silvering all the world. He fed the mule and pumped the water and broke the ice and stood in the cold looking at the country and it gave him back nothing it had not given him every other morning of his life.

When he came in she was at the fire.

I'm goin, he said.

She did not turn around. He could see the line of her back stiffen.

I'll come back, he said. Fore the spring even, maybe. Fore the child comes if I can.

You wont, she said. Her voice was very quiet. You wont come back.

I will.

She turned then and looked at him and her eyes were dry. That's what they all say, she said. Every man that ever left a woman. I'll come back. And then there's a stone in the yard with no name and a woman that's old before her time and a graveyard full of waiting.

He went to her and put his hands on her arms and she stood stiff under them.

I cant set here and watch us starve, he said. I cant do it. There's nothin here. You know there's nothin here.

There's me, she said. There's this. She put her hand again on her belly. That aint nothin.

It aint enough, he said, and the words were out of him before he could stop them and he saw them land on her like a blow and he wished them back but a thing said cannot be unsaid any more than a thing done can be undone.

She pulled away from him. She went to the hearth and stood with her back to him and the firelight came up around her and threw her shadow long and trembling on the wall.

Go on then, she said.

He stood a while longer. Then he gathered his few things, his coat and his hat and a knife and a flint and steel and what little there was, and he wrapped a heel of cornbread in a rag, and at the door he stopped.

I'll send money, he said. Soon as I have it. I'll send it back to you.

She did not turn around. He stood looking at her back a moment longer and then he went out and shut the door behind him and the cold took him.

HE RODE THE mule into town and the street was empty in the early light with the smoke standing straight up from the chimneys and the frost not yet off the shadowed sides of the buildings. He did not know where the stranger had got to or whether he was still about at all, and he sat the mule in the empty street feeling foolish, a poor man on a poor mule with no money and nothing but his two hands and a thing he could not name driving him.

He found them at the livery. The stranger and four others, mounted, and a pack mule loaded, and they were making ready to ride out with the morning. The stranger turned in his saddle and watched him come and his pale flat eyes took him in, the mule, the coat, the hat, the whole poor sum of him, and he did not smile.

You was on the porch, the stranger said.

Yes sir.

You want somethin.

I heard you say there was a bounty. I heard you say a man could come out of that country with money.

He could. If he lived.

I aim to come.

The stranger looked at him a while. The others looked at him too, hard men all of them, lean and unshaven and armed, and one of them spat in the frozen mud and another laughed low.

On that mule, the stranger said.

It's what I got.

You got a rifle?

No sir.

A pistol?

No sir.

The stranger shook his head slowly. The others were grinning now. You aim to fight Apaches with what, the stranger said. Your two hands? Your good intentions?

The man sat the mule and his face burned but he held the stranger's eye. I can get a gun, he said. I'll work for it. I'm a hard worker. I aint afraid of nothin.

Every man's afraid of somethin, the stranger said. The ones that say they aint are the first ones to run. He gathered his reins. We dont take charity cases, friend. This aint a church.

I aint askin charity. I'll earn my share.

The stranger looked at him a long moment more and something moved behind the pale eyes that might have been amusement or might have been calculation or might have been nothing at all.

You got a wife? he said.

The man did not answer.

You do. I can see it on you. A wife and likely a young one coming. The stranger nodded slowly. And here you are. He looked off down the empty street and then back. You know what I have learned about men in this life, friend? I have learned there is two kinds. There is them that the world has its hooks in and they will set in one place till they die because they are afraid to bleed. And there is them that has tore loose. He smiled now for the first time and it was not a thing a man would want turned on him. You have tore loose, he said. I can see it. You just done it this morning. It's all over you.

The man said nothing.

Come on then, the stranger said. We'll get you a gun off the first man we kill or the first man amongst us that gets killed, whichever comes first, and it dont much matter which. Keep up or get left. We dont wait on no man.

And he turned his horse and rode out of the livery into the street and turned it west toward the ford and the others fell in behind him and the man sat his mule a moment in the cold with his heart going hard and then he turned the mule and followed.

THEY FORDED THE river with the brown water coming up cold to the bellies of the horses and the man's mule balking and lunging until he beat it across, and on the far side they took up the road west and the town fell behind them and was gone. The man rode last in the line and no one spoke to him and he spoke to no one. The country opened out before them, the hills falling away to a great rolling distance going brown and gray to the rim of the world, and the sun stood up at last clear of the overcast and laid their shadows long and black on the frozen ground before them, pointing the way they went.

He did not look back at first. He had told himself he would not. But when they had gone some miles and the road climbed a long rise he turned in the saddle at the top of it and looked back the way they had come and there below him was the river bright and winding and the town small in its bend and beyond it the country where his house stood though he could not see it, only knew it was there, and her in it by the fire with her hand on her belly and the two stones out back and a third place waiting, and he looked at it a long time.

You'll wear your neck out, the stranger said. He had reined up and sat his horse beside him though the man had not heard him come. Lookin back.

The man turned forward again.

It dont do no good, the stranger said. A man that's tore loose has got no business lookin back. There's nothin back there for you. There never was. He said it gently, almost kindly, and that was the worst of it. He nudged his horse and went on and the man followed and did not look back again.

They rode west into the falling sun and the land took them in and the night came on and they made camp in a fold of the hills and built a fire and the men sat around it with their guns and ate what they had and the stranger talked, low and even and certain, of the country ahead and the gold in it and the blood that would buy the gold, and the man sat at the edge of the firelight and listened and his shadow and theirs moved on the dark behind them in a black and nameless dance, and far above them the stars wheeled cold and indifferent in their courses, the stars of that season and no other, and he thought of the dream and the dark sun that did not return and the multitude falling on him in the pit of the dark, and he understood that he had walked into it now of his own accord and there would be no waking from it and no prophet to cure him and that the sun, when it set on him at the last, in whatever far place, would not rise again.

He slept that night and did not dream, or dreamed and did not wake, and in the morning the stranger kicked him out of his blankets into the cold and they rode on west, and the river was behind them, and the town, and the woman, and all the green country of his birth, and ahead of them lay only the brown distance and the mountains beyond it gone blue with farness and the long road down into that other country where the sun is white and merciless and the dust drinks blood and gives back nothing, and they went on toward it strung out single file against the morning light, small and dark and going on, and the land swallowed them, and they were gone.
