The snow had started as something almost gentle, fat flakes drifting past the kitchen window while Maren washed the supper dishes, and she had stood there for a moment with her hands in the gray water and thought it would not amount to much. April did this. It threw a last cold tantrum and then remembered itself and melted into mud and green. She had told Eli as much when he came in from the evening check, stamping his boots, his cheeks bright with it.

"Two more bagging up," he said. "The black-faced one and the old gummer."

"They'll wait till morning." She handed him a dish towel. He took it without being asked anymore, which was new, which had come this winter like a tooth pushing through. He dried while she washed, and neither of them said anything about the empty chair or the way the wind had begun to find the corners of the house and lean on them.

By nine the gentleness was gone. The flakes had turned small and hard, ticking against the glass, and the wind came down off the ridge the way it did in February, a long unbroken push that made the stovepipe moan. Maren went out to the porch and could not see the barn. The yard light was a smear of gold suspended in nothing. She came back in and put on Carl's coat without thinking about it, the canvas one that still hung by the door because she had not been able to make herself take it down, and only when she had her arm through the second sleeve did she realize what she'd done. She stood with it half on. Then she shrugged it the rest of the way up because it was warm and it was here and the cold did not care whose grief it was.

"I'm coming," Eli said.

"You don't have to."

"The black-faced one was pawing."

So he knew, then. He'd watched the way his father had taught him, or the way she had, it was hard to say now whose lessons had gone into him. He was fifteen and tall in the surprising way of boys who shoot up overnight, all wrist and Adam's apple, and he had Carl's hands, the same square palms, and she had to look away from them sometimes.

They went out together into the white roar. The path to the lambing shed was a hundred feet and they walked it leaning sideways, and the snow was already to their shins, heavy and wet, the worst kind, the kind that brought down power lines and broke the backs of old barns. Maren kept one hand on Eli's shoulder, not to steady herself but to know where he was, and he let her.

Inside the shed the lights buzzed yellow and the air was warm with bodies and the green smell of silage and the iron smell underneath it. There were forty ewes in here, the ones close to their time, separated off from the main flock in the far barn. The black-faced ewe was down in the straw at the end pen, her sides heaving, and when she saw them she got up and turned in a circle and lay down again and pawed at the bedding.

"There," Maren said. "You were right."

Eli flushed with the small pleasure of it and went to wash his hands in the bucket by the door, and Maren knelt by the ewe and ran her hand along the great drum of her belly and felt it tighten under her palm like a fist closing. The ewe groaned. Outside the wind hit the metal roof and the whole shed shuddered, and the lights flickered, came back, flickered.

"Don't," Maren said to them, to the wind, to the night.

The lights went out.

The dark in the shed was total for a moment, a black so complete it had weight, and Maren heard Eli go still by the water bucket and heard the ewes shift and murmur, and she breathed out slowly through her mouth.

"Mom?"

"It's all right. Stay where you are a second, let your eyes come." Her own were already finding the gray rectangles of the windows, the snow giving back what little light there was. She got her phone from her coat pocket and turned the flashlight on and the beam carved a cone out of the dark, full of drifting chaff. "The generator's no good, it needs the part. We'll do it the old way. There's lanterns in the tack room and the headlamps on the shelf. Go on."

He went, the boy-shape of him crossing the lit cone and vanishing into the dark beyond it, and she heard him bang his shin on something and swear, quietly, a word he wouldn't have said in front of her a year ago, and she let it pass. The ewe strained again. Maren set the phone upright against a hay bale so its light pointed up at the rafters and spread a thin general glow, and she peeled Carl's coat off and rolled her sleeves and dipped her hands in the cold water and waited.

Eli came back with two headlamps and the big battery lantern, and the lantern's light was white and steady and made the shed into a room again, walls and corners and the long row of pens. He fit a headlamp over his stocking cap without being told and handed her the other.

"Power'll be out all night with this," he said. "Probably the whole valley."

"Probably." She listened to the ewe. The contractions were coming closer. "She's getting on with it. Watch now."

He crouched beside her. This was the part he still went quiet for, the part that no amount of doing had made ordinary to him, and she was glad of that, glad it hadn't gone ordinary, because the day it did would be the day something in him had closed up. The ewe pushed and a slick gray bulge appeared and within it the pale points of two tiny hooves, and then the small dished face lying along the legs the way it should, nose to knees, the diver's pose, and Maren let out a breath she hadn't known she was holding.

"Right way round," she said. "Good girl. Good girl."

The ewe did the work herself. Two more pushes and the lamb came in a rush of fluid and Eli caught it the way he'd been taught, easing it down into the straw, and it lay there steaming and still and yellow-slick, and for a second none of them moved, and then Maren cleared the membrane from its nose with her finger and the lamb shook its head, a tiny furious shudder, and sneezed, and began to breathe.

"There he is," Eli said, and his voice cracked in the middle of it and he laughed at himself.

They set the lamb up by its mother's head and she began to lick it, her sides still heaving, and Maren felt along her belly again and there was another in there, there usually was with this one, she threw twins like clockwork. The second came ten minutes later, smaller, a ewe lamb, and she was slow to start, and Maren rubbed her hard with a fistful of straw, rubbed the chest in rough circles, blew into the small wet nose, and the lamb gasped and kicked and lived.

"Two," Eli said. "She's done?"

"She's done." Maren sat back on her heels. Her knees ached. The cold had got into them this winter and not left, and she was forty-six and felt sometimes like sixty and sometimes like a girl, and just now she felt every year of it. The two lambs wobbled and butted at the ewe, finding their way to the udder, and the ewe stood patient and let them, and the white lantern light fell over all of it, and outside the storm went on without them, indifferent, vast.

"That's how Dad did it," Eli said. "The rubbing thing. With the straw."

"It is."

"I forgot he showed me that until just now."

Maren said nothing. She watched the lambs nurse. She had learned this winter that grief was not a wave the way people said, was not a thing that came and crested and receded, but was more like weather, like this snow, a condition you lived inside of, and you could forget about it for an hour while your hands were busy and then you'd turn your head and there it was, filling the whole sky. She had learned to let the small mentions of him come and go without snatching at them. If she snatched, Eli stopped saying them, and she could not bear that more than she could bear the saying.

"He was better at the backwards ones than me," she said. "He had the patience for it. I get in a hurry."

"You're not in a hurry."

"I get in a hurry inside." She stood, her knees cracking, and went to the next pen, where the old gummer ewe stood in the corner with her head low. This one had no teeth left to speak of, hence the name, an ancient broad-bodied ewe they kept because she was a good mother and threw strong lambs and because Carl had liked her, had scratched her poll every time he passed and called her old woman. She was bagged up tight and her vulva had loosened and reddened but she wasn't pushing yet, only standing there with that inward look they got. Maren watched her a moment.

"Not yet," she said. "Soon, but not yet. Let's get the first two ear-tagged and weighed while we've got a minute."

They worked. The cold made everything slower, the antiseptic thick in its bottle, the iodine they dipped the navels in half-frozen so she had to warm it in her hands. Eli held the lambs while she did the tags, and they cried their thin new cries, and the black-faced ewe stamped and called back to them, anxious, and they wrote the weights and the times in the notebook that hung from a nail by the door, the same notebook, Carl's handwriting at the top of the page from back in March before, and then her own underneath, and now Eli's, because he'd offered to keep it tonight and she'd let him.

His writing was bad. Carl's had been worse. Hers was the only legible hand in the family and she felt absurdly proud of that and then absurdly bereft, both at once, weather, all of it weather.

The wind rose another notch. There was a sound from the roof, a long metallic shriek, and they both looked up.

"Loose panel," Maren said. "North corner. It's been working loose since the thaw." If it tore off, the snow would come in, would drift down onto the pens, and they'd lose lambs to the wet and cold the way you could lose them so fast, the heat going out of those small bodies like water out of a cracked cup. She thought about it. She thought about going up on the roof in this, and dismissed it, because that was how you died, and Eli had buried enough this year. "It'll hold or it won't. Nothing to do about it now."

"We could move the pens. The far ones. Under the loft, where the panel can't reach."

She looked at him. It was a good idea. It was the idea Carl would have had, or that she would have had in twenty more minutes once she'd worked round to it, and the boy had got there first, ahead of her, and she felt the strange vertigo of it, of being overtaken, of the generations sliding past one another in the dark of the shed.

"Yeah," she said. "Yeah, let's do that. The lambed ones first, they're the ones that can't afford a chill."

They moved the black-faced ewe and her twins into a clean pen under the loft, carrying the lambs low so the mother would follow them, the ewe bawling and pushing at Eli's hands to keep her nose on them. They forked fresh straw, deep and dry. They shifted the old gummer too, and three others that looked close, herding them down the alley by lamplight while their shadows swung huge on the walls. It took an hour. By the end of it Maren's back was a single hot bar of pain and Eli was sweating through his shirt despite the cold, and the north corner of the roof was screaming steadily now, the panel lifting and slapping down, lifting and slapping, and a fine sift of snow had begun to come through and lie glittering on the empty pens they'd abandoned.

They stood under the loft and caught their breath. The lantern was on a barrel between them. In its light Maren could see how tired the boy was, the gray look around his eyes, and she thought of sending him in to sleep and knew he wouldn't go.

"Eat something," she said instead, and went to the coat and dug the foil packet of bread and cheese out of the pocket, the supper she'd wrapped without quite deciding to, some animal foresight, and they sat on a hay bale and ate it. The cheese had gone hard with cold. It was the best thing she'd ever eaten. Eli wolfed his and she gave him half of hers because he was fifteen and made of hunger, and they sat there chewing while the storm tried to take the roof off and the ewes breathed around them in the dark.

"You remember the year of the big one?" Eli said. "When I was little. The drift over the truck."

"You were six."

"Dad pulled me through the yard on the sled because I couldn't walk in it. The snow was over my head."

"He nearly lost you. You went off the sled and into the drift and for about ten seconds we couldn't see you at all. Your dad went in up to his shoulder feeling around for you."

Eli looked at her. "I don't remember that part."

"No. You thought it was funny. You came up laughing." She brushed crumbs off her knee. "He didn't laugh for about an hour. He was white. He carried you the rest of the way against his chest, wouldn't put you on the sled again."

The boy was quiet, holding this, this piece of his father she'd handed him, a thing he hadn't had a minute ago and had now, and she understood suddenly that this was what was left to her to do, this was the work, harder than the lambing and longer—to keep handing him the pieces, all of them, for as long as she had them, because when she was gone the man would have nothing but what she'd given him now. The thought was so large she had to set it down.

The old gummer groaned.

She'd started. When they came to her pen she was down in the straw and straining hard, the ewe lamb that came so easily out of her long experience already presenting, and Maren knelt and saw the trouble at once. One leg. Only one small hoof showing where there should be two, the other leg bent back inside, and the head coming with it, crooked, jammed.

"Head's turned," she said. "Leg back. I have to go in."

She scrubbed her hand and arm to the elbow in the cold antiseptic and the ewe strained against her as she worked her fingers in, and it was hot inside the animal, shockingly hot, the only warm place in the whole frozen night, and she found the head turned hard to the side and the second leg folded under, and she had to push the lamb back against the ewe's pushing, against everything the ewe's body wanted, to make room to bring the leg around. The ewe screamed. Eli held her head down, his knee against her spine the way he'd been shown, talking to her, low, old woman, old woman, easy now, his father's words in his father's voice almost, and Maren bowed her head and shut her eyes and worked by feel, the way you had to, the way the eyes only got in the way of, and the storm hammered the roof and the panel shrieked and she got two fingers around the folded leg above the knee and walked it forward, cupping the little hoof in her palm so it wouldn't tear the wall as it came, easing it up and around and out.

"Two legs," she said. "Now the head." Her arm was cramping. She found the small wet skull and worked her fingers along the jaw and turned it, gently, gently, into the channel, and felt it seat, felt the whole shape of the lamb come right under her hand like a key turning in a lock.

"Now pull when she pushes," she said. "Watch me. When she—now."

They pulled together, Eli's hands over hers on the slick legs, and the ewe pushed, and the lamb moved, and stuck, and moved, and then came all at once in the slithering rush, and Maren went over backward into the straw with the weight of it across her chest, the lamb, blue-white and still.

It wasn't breathing.

She was up on her knees with it before she knew she'd moved. She cleared the nose, swung it gently to drain the fluid, rubbed it, the hard circles on the chest, blew into the slack nose, rubbed, blew. Nothing. The little ribs under her hands. The heat already leaving it into the cold air. Beside her Eli had gone rigid.

"Come on," Maren said. "Come on. Come on."

She'd lost them before. You always lost some. She knew the moment when you stopped, when it was past, when the rubbing was only for yourself and not for the lamb anymore. She knew it and she didn't reach it, wouldn't reach it, she rubbed and blew and her own breath was ragged and the tears were on her face hot and then cold, and she wasn't crying for the lamb, or not only, she was crying for all of it, for the chair and the coat and the white sky that went on forever, and she pressed the small chest and felt the bird-bones of it and thought, no, not this one, not tonight, you don't get this one—

The lamb gasped.

It was the smallest sound. A catch, a flutter, and then the ribs moved under her hands on their own, and the lamb shuddered and shook its head and gave a thin bleat, and Maren bent over it and laughed, or sobbed, the sound came out of her she didn't know which it was, and Eli's hand came down on her shoulder, gripping, and she could feel him shaking too.

"You got it," he said. "Mom. You got it back."

They set the lamb by the gummer's head and the old ewe turned and began to lick it, unbothered, ancient, as if nothing at all had happened, as if it were the most ordinary thing in the world to go down into the dark and come back, and maybe to her it was. She'd done it a dozen springs. Maren sat back in the straw, her arm bloody to the elbow, her face wet, and looked at the lamb finding its mother's voice, and at her son's face in the lantern light, wet too, and unashamed of it, and the two of them sat there in the wreck of the night and the warm animal smell and did not speak.

The panel tore off the roof sometime after three. They heard it go, a great ripping shriek and then a bang as it cartwheeled off into the dark, and the snow came down through the gap in a white column, but it came down over the empty pens, the ones they'd cleared, and the ewes and the lambs were dry under the loft, and Maren looked at that snow falling harmlessly into the abandoned corner and then at Eli, and Eli looked back at her, and something passed between them that did not need saying and could not have been said anyway.

By dawn the gummer's twins were both up and nursing, and two more ewes had lambed in the night without trouble, easy births, gifts, the kind that made up for the hard ones, and the wind had begun at last to drop. The light came gray and then pale and then, impossibly, gold, the sun breaking under the trailing edge of the storm and laying itself across a world made strange and round and white, the fences gone, the truck a soft dune, the ridge a sweep of unbroken light. Maren stood in the open doorway of the shed with Carl's coat around her shoulders and a cup of coffee Eli had walked through the drifts to make, and she watched the sun come up on the first morning of the rest of it, and behind her in the warm dark the new lambs cried, alive, all of them alive, and her son came and stood beside her in the doorway, as tall as she was now, taller, and they drank their coffee and watched the light come, and did not go in.
