The snow had started at three in the afternoon, fine and dry as flour, and by six it had thickened into something with weight to it, falling so heavily that when Maren looked out the kitchen window she could no longer see the fence line. She stood with her hands wrapped around a mug of tea gone cold and watched the white come down and thought of the forecast, which had promised flurries, an inch maybe, nothing to fret over. The forecast had been wrong about a great many things this year.

"We should bring them all in," Eli said behind her. He was sixteen and had grown four inches since October, so that his wrists hung out of the cuffs of his father's barn coat, which he had taken to wearing without asking and which she had not yet found the words to mention. "The ones still out. The eight in the lower field."

"They'll have lambed by morning, some of them."

"That's why."

She turned from the window. He was right, of course, and she disliked how often he was right these days, how he had taken to filling the silences her husband used to fill with the same plain competence, as though there were a quota of practical observations a household required and he had appointed himself to meet it. John had been dead five months. The ground had been too frozen to dig properly and they'd had to wait, and the waiting had done something to both of them she couldn't name.

"Get the lamp," she said. "The good one. And your gloves, the wool ones, not the ones with the holes."

They went out into it. The wind took the breath from her the moment she stepped off the porch, and the snow was no longer falling so much as moving sideways, a horizontal river of it, stinging the exposed skin of her face. Eli went ahead with the lamp, a yellow smear of light, and she followed the dark shape of him down toward the lower field with the dogs slinking low at their heels, unhappy, ears flat. The cold was the kind that got into the joints of your fingers and the roots of your teeth. April. It was nearly May. The lilac by the gate had budded out a week ago and would be dead by morning, blackened, and she felt an absurd grief about the lilac that she understood was not really about the lilac at all.

The ewes were huddled in the lee of the stone wall, eight of them, heavy-bellied and miserable, their fleeces caked white. Eli moved among them the way his father had, slow and murmuring, and they let him. He'd inherited that. Maren had always been the one the sheep distrusted, the one whose hurry they could smell. She took the rear and the dogs took the flanks and together they pushed the small flock up the slope toward the shed, the animals reluctant, stopping, the wind shoving everyone sideways. One ewe—Maren knew her by the notch in her ear, an old girl, six seasons—stood and would not move, and when Maren got behind her she saw the water bag had broken, a glistening rope of it hanging, and the ewe's sides heaving.

"This one's started," she called, but the wind ate the words. She got her shoulder against the ewe's haunch and shoved, and the animal staggered forward, and somehow they got all eight into the shed where the others already were, forty-odd ewes and the lambs already born, the air thick and warm with the smell of them, ammonia and lanolin and the sweetness of hay, and the sound—that low constant murmur of a flock under cover, the bleating and the answering bleats, mothers and lambs calling each other across the pens.

Then the lights went out.

Not flickering, not warning. One moment the bare bulbs strung along the rafters threw their hard light down over the pens and the next there was nothing, a darkness so total that Maren put her hand out and could not see it. The lamp Eli held became the only light in the world, a small trembling globe of it, and around its edge the dark pressed in and the storm howled against the tin roof.

"Power's gone," Eli said, unnecessarily, and she heard in his voice that he was frightened and trying not to be, and she felt the old instinct rise in her to make it all right, to be larger than the situation, the way you did for a child. But he was not a child, and the situation was large.

"The generator," she said.

"It won't start. Dad never—" He stopped. "It hasn't been run since last winter. It needs the fuel line bled. He was going to show me."

He was going to show me. The unfinished business of a dead man, lying everywhere about the place like tools left out in the rain. John had been forty-four. A blood vessel in his brain, the doctor said, something that had likely been there his whole life waiting, a flaw built into him at the start, and he had been standing at the kitchen counter buttering bread and then he had been on the floor and then, two days later in the hospital with the machines breathing for him, he had been gone. There had been no warning and no time to learn the things he carried in his head, the bleeding of fuel lines and the weather signs and which ewes were trouble and which fence post was rotten at the base. He had carried all of it and now it was scattered, and she and the boy were trying to gather it up piece by piece in the dark.

"All right," she said. "All right. We don't need the generator. We've lambed by lamplight before. Your grandfather did it his whole life. We'll manage."

"It's cold, though. The lambs—"

"We'll manage," she said again, more sharply than she meant. She heard him go quiet.

The old ewe with the broken water bag had taken herself to a corner and lain down, and Maren went to her, taking the lamp from Eli's hand. By its light she could see the ewe straining, the contractions rippling along her flank, and she crouched and waited and watched. This was the part you could not hurry. The ewe knew her work. But after a while Maren saw that something was not right—no nose appeared, no two small hooves, only the ewe's pushing and pushing and nothing coming, and the animal's breathing growing ragged.

"Hold the lamp," she said. Eli took it. She pulled off her right glove and pushed up her sleeve and the cold bit immediately at her bared arm. She reached in. The ewe groaned. Maren felt past the heat and the slickness, her eyes closed, reading with her fingers the way John had taught her years ago, the only one of his skills she had fully made her own. A leg. Another leg. But the wrong way—she felt the little hocks bending backward, the way a hind leg bends, not a front. A breech. And then she felt the second body behind the first, the tangle of them, two lambs trying to come at once and neither able.

"Twins," she said. "And one's backward. I have to sort them."

"Can you?"

"I have to."

She worked by feel in the dark of the animal, pushing one lamb gently back to make room, finding the hindquarters of the breech one, hooking her fingers around the hocks. The ewe strained against her and she strained back, sweat starting on her forehead despite the cold, her arm aching to the shoulder. Beside her Eli held the lamp steady, and she was grateful that he held it steady, that his hand did not shake, or if it shook she could not tell.

"Talk to her," Maren said. "Keep her calm."

And Eli began to talk, low, the way his father had, nonsense really, *there now, there now girl, easy, easy*, and the ewe's ear turned back toward the sound of him, and Maren got the legs and pulled with the contraction, timing it, easing the lamb out backward into the world, the slick rush of it, and she cleared the membrane from its face and swung it gently and it coughed and shook its head and was alive. She set it down in the straw and the ewe, even mid-labor, turned to lick at it.

"One," Eli said.

"One more." And the second came easier, head and forelegs together the right way, sliding out into her hands, and this one too coughed and breathed and lived, and she set it beside its sibling and sat back on her heels in the straw, her arm steaming in the lamplight, her whole body trembling now that it was done.

"You did it," Eli said. There was something in his voice. Wonder, maybe. Or relief so large it had nowhere to go.

"We did it," she said. "Get the towels. We need to dry them before the cold takes them."

They worked over the two lambs with sacking, rubbing them down, and the ewe nosed and licked, and within minutes the lambs were trying to stand, lurching up on impossible legs and falling and trying again, and one of them found the udder and began to suck, the tail flicking, and Maren felt the thing she always felt at that moment, the thing she could never explain to anyone who hadn't seen it, the absolute insistence of it, life simply refusing not to happen.

But there was no time to sit with it. Across the shed another ewe had gone down, and another was pawing the straw and circling, and the night was going to be long.

They lost track of the hours. The storm did not let up; if anything it worsened, the wind finding every gap in the old walls and driving snow through in fine drifts that gathered in the corners of the shed. The lamp burned and they did not dare let it go out, and Maren rationed her attention to it, turning it down when they did not need it, up when they did. Lamb after lamb came. Most came easily, the ewes doing their own work, and Maren and Eli only there to clear a nose or dry a body or, twice, to carry a chilled lamb to the warming box—except the warming box ran on electricity and was dead, and so instead they did the old thing, the thing from before electricity, and Maren stripped off her coat and her sweater and tucked the chilled lamb against her own bare stomach under her shirt, the cold wet weight of it, and held it there until its shivering eased and its body found its own heat again.

"Dad told me about that," Eli said, watching. "I didn't believe him. I thought he was having me on."

"He wasn't. His mother did it, and her mother. You'd put the worst ones in the bottom oven of the range, in the old days, with the door open. My granny lost more than she saved that way but the ones she saved she'd never have saved otherwise."

"Why didn't anyone ever tell me any of this?"

The question was not really a question. It hung in the cold air between them. Why didn't anyone tell you: because there was supposed to be time. Because the telling was supposed to happen slowly, across years, a father and a son walking the fields in ordinary weather, the knowledge passing between them so gradually that neither would notice it had passed. There was supposed to be time and there was not time, and now the knowledge had to be wrenched across in a single night by lamplight, incomplete, with the gaps showing.

"I'm telling you now," Maren said.

He nodded. He took the lamb when its shivering stopped and carried it back to its mother, and the ewe took it, and Maren pulled her sweater back on over her chilled stomach and did not let him see her face.

Around what she guessed was midnight—her phone was dead, the clock in the shed was dead, time had come unmoored—they sat on a hay bale and shared a thermos of soup she had thought, mercifully, to bring out. It had gone lukewarm but it was wet and it was salt and they drank it in turns from the cup. The dogs lay at their feet, recovered from their earlier misery, content now in the warmth of the flock. The storm raged on but inside the shed there was a kind of peace, the deep animal peace of bodies keeping each other alive.

"I keep thinking he's going to walk in," Eli said.

Maren said nothing. She had thought it too, all night—at the edge of every task she half-expected the door to scrape open and John to come in stamping snow off his boots, taking the difficult ewe off her hands, knowing without being told which lamb needed what. The expectation was a kind of madness and she lived inside it the way you live inside a house you know is going to be torn down.

"At the funeral," Eli went on, "everybody kept saying he'd want us to carry on. Be strong. All that. And I wanted to—" He stopped. His jaw worked. He was so much like his father in the lamplight, the same heavy brow, the same way of choosing words as if they cost money. "I wanted to ask them how. How do you carry on when you don't know how to do half the things he did. When the generator won't start and you don't know why. It's all well to say carry on. Carry on what. With what."

"With this," Maren said. She gestured at the shed, the pens, the steaming bodies, the lambs. "This is what. You carry on with what's in front of you. You don't carry on with everything at once. You can't. It's too big. You just do the next thing. The next ewe. The next lamb. And then the one after that. And in the morning the sun comes up whether you've grieved properly or not, and there's stock to feed, and you feed it. That's the whole secret. There isn't any other secret. People want there to be but there isn't."

He was quiet a long while. The wind shook the door in its frame.

"Were you scared," he asked, "when you put your arm in. With the twins. Were you scared you'd do it wrong."

"Yes."

"You didn't look scared."

"Neither did you, holding the lamp. We were both scared and we both didn't look it. That's most of being a grown person, if you want to know. You're scared and you do the thing anyway and you keep your face still so the people next to you don't catch it off you like a cold." She handed him the cup. "Your father was scared half the time. He just never showed it. I only knew because I slept next to him thirty years. He'd lie awake over the accounts, over the weather, over you. Scared. And then in the morning he'd get up and feed the stock."

"I didn't know that."

"There's a lot you didn't know about him. That's not your fault. There's a lot I didn't know. You don't get to know all of a person. You get to know some of them and then they're gone and you spend the rest of your life finding out the rest in pieces. You'll be sixty and you'll learn something new about him from some old man at a market who knew him as a boy, and it'll be like getting a letter from him. That's how it goes."

Eli wiped his eyes with the heel of his hand, roughly, angry at himself, and Maren looked away to give him the privacy of it, and across the shed a ewe began the low groaning that meant her time had come, and Maren stood up, her knees protesting, and went to her.

The night wore on. They lost one. A small one, a single, that came too early and too weak and would not breathe no matter what Maren did, no matter how she cleared its airway and swung it and breathed into its small nose and rubbed at its ribs with the sacking. It simply did not catch. There was a moment when she thought it might—a gasp, a flutter—and then nothing, the small body going slack in her hands, and she knelt over it in the straw and the lamplight and felt the familiar hard descent of it, the failure that was not failure, that was only the way of things, the percentage that you could not save no matter what you knew or didn't know. She had lost lambs with John alive and well beside her, both of them doing everything right. It still felt like a personal accusation.

"Couldn't we have—" Eli started.

"No."

"But if the warming box—"

"No. It was too early and too small. The box wouldn't have changed it. Sometimes they're not made to live and there's nothing in it. You'll want to find the reason. Don't. Some nights there's no reason and looking for one will hollow you out." She wrapped the small body in the sacking and set it aside, out of sight of the ewe, who was already nosing the straw, confused, looking for what should have been there. "She'll cry for it a day or two. We'll graft an orphan onto her if one comes. There's always an orphan."

Eli looked at the wrapped shape in the corner. "Does it get easier? Losing them?"

"No," Maren said. "But you get better at carrying it. Those aren't the same thing. People will tell you it gets easier. They mean well. They're wrong. What happens is you get stronger, so the same weight doesn't bend you as far. The weight's the same. You're just more able to bear it." She put her hand on his shoulder, briefly, the first time she had touched him in tenderness in weeks—they had become careful with each other, since John, formal almost, two people sharing grief like a house too small for both of them. He didn't pull away. "Your father's death isn't going to get lighter. You're going to get bigger. That's the only mercy on offer. It's enough. It has to be."

Toward morning the storm broke. Maren noticed it first as an absence—the wind, which had become so constant she had stopped hearing it, simply stopped, and into the new silence came the small sounds of the flock, the contented ones, the murmur of mothers and the high answer of lambs. She straightened from the last ewe of the night, a young one who had taken her time but delivered cleanly, a fine single ram lamb already up and sucking, and Maren felt her own exhaustion arrive all at once, as if it had been waiting politely for a gap.

"It's stopped," Eli said.

They went to the door and pushed it open against the drift that had built there and stepped out into a transformed world. The snow had ended and the clouds had torn open and there were stars, hard and bright in the black, more than she had seen in months, and over the eastern ridge the first grey of the day was just beginning to lift the dark. The snow lay deep and unbroken over everything, the fences buried, the lilac surely dead, the whole farm made strange and clean and silent. The cold was savage and the sky was beautiful and both things were true at once, the way she was learning everything was two things at once now, grief and ongoingness, loss and the lambs that kept coming whether you were ready or not.

She counted in her head. They had brought in thirty-one lambs through the night. They had lost one. Thirty alive, steaming in the shed behind her, and one wrapped in sacking in the corner, and a boy beside her who had held the lamp steady all night and not let it go out.

"We should start the morning feed," Eli said. "The ewes that lambed need extra. Dad always upped their ration after."

"He did," Maren said. "We will."

Behind them the shed breathed its warm animal breath into the freezing dawn. Somewhere down the valley a tractor would be out soon clearing the road, and the power company would come, eventually, and the generator would still need its fuel line bled, and there would be a thousand things neither of them knew how to do that they would have to learn the hard way, in the dark, by feel. But the sun was coming up. There was stock to feed. She had told him that was the whole secret and she had meant it, and standing in the cold with her son in the new light she believed it more than she had believed anything in five months.

"Come on," she said. "Let's get them fed before it warms and the snow turns to slop. Then I'll show you the generator. We'll figure it out together. It can't be that hard. He figured it out and he was no genius, God rest him."

Eli laughed—a short, surprised sound, the first she'd heard from him since the funeral, and it undid something in her chest, some knot she hadn't known was there. They went together toward the feed store, breaking the unbroken snow with their boots, the dogs bounding ahead now, released into the morning, and the day came up grey and then gold over the buried fields, and the lambs cried out behind them, alive, insistent, refusing not to happen.
