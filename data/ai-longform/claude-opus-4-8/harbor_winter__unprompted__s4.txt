The cannery whistle still blew at six, though there was less and less for it to summon. Viggo heard it from his kitchen the way he heard his own heartbeat, which is to say not at all until he stopped to listen. Then there it was, thin and flat over the rooftops, a sound that had measured out his whole life and now measured out its ending.

Across the table his granddaughter Mette pushed cold porridge into shapes with her spoon. She was eleven and serious in the way of children who have learned that grief lives in a house and must be walked around quietly. Her mother was up north for the season, gutting fish at a plant that still paid, and so Mette had come to live with him in October, when the days were already shortening like a tide going out.

"Eat that," he said. "Not paint with it."

She ate it. He watched her do it and felt the old helplessness of a man who has spent sixty years reading water and has never learned to read a child.

Outside the window the harbor lay grey under a grey sky, the boats fewer than he remembered, the ones that remained sitting low and patient at their moorings. Beyond the breakwater the sea was doing what it did, which was everything, all the time, and saying nothing about it.

"Get your boots," he said. "We'll walk down."

They walked down most mornings now. It had started as a way of filling the hours before her school bus and had become something he could not have named if asked. The path went past the net sheds, half of them empty, their doors swinging, and past the Hansen place where no one lived, and down to the stone pier where Viggo had stood as a boy beside his own father and learned that the sea was not one thing but a thousand things wearing the same coat.

"There," he said, stopping where the pier met the water. "What do you see?"

Mette looked. He had asked her this every morning for a month and at first she had said nothing, or said the obvious—waves, boats, a gull. Children answered what they thought you wanted. It had taken weeks to teach her that he wanted only the truth of her own two eyes.

"The water's darker by the point," she said.

"Good. Why?"

"I don't know."

"That's all right. You saw it. The why comes after. Always the seeing first, then the why." He pointed with his chin, not his hand—a pilot's economy he had never lost. "Darker means deeper, or it means the wind's combing it a different way, or it means weed underneath. Today?"

She studied it. The cold had brought up the color in her cheeks and she had her mother's frown, the vertical line between the brows that meant a person was working.

"There's no wind," she said. "So it's deep."

"It's the channel," he said. "The deep water runs there, in past the point and along the breakwater and out. A boat coming in at night, in fog, with no lights to guide her—where does she go?"

"The dark water."

"The dark water. Always the dark water." He felt something settle in his chest, a satisfaction he distrusted because it was too large for the moment. "That's how your great-grandfather brought boats in before there were lights on the breakwater. He read the dark."

Mette looked up at him. "Did you?"

"Forty years," he said. "Every boat too big to find its own way in, I went out in the little launch and brought her over the bar and into the harbor. Tankers. The cannery ships when they were still big enough to need it. Once a navy frigate that had lost her instruments in a storm." He stopped. He did not want to become an old man telling stories on a pier, though he supposed he already was one. "It's a thing you can learn. That's all I'm saying. The water isn't a mystery. It's a language. Most people just never learn to read it."

She turned back to the harbor and looked at the dark channel, and he saw her trying to read it, and his throat did something complicated.

That winter the news about the cannery came the way bad news always came in the town, sideways and slow, in the post office and the one shop that still sold more than bait. Viggo heard it from Aksel, who had worked the cutting floor for thirty years and now stood outside the shop with his hands in his pockets like a man waiting for a bus that wasn't coming.

"Spring," Aksel said. "They'll close it in spring. They told us this week. Buy our fish cheaper from the Chileans, freeze it on the boats, run it through the big plant in the city." He said the city the way other men said the grave. "Spring."

"How many?" Viggo asked.

"All of us. Everyone." Aksel looked down the street toward the cannery, its long low buildings, the rust working its way up from the waterline like a slow tide of its own. "My father worked there. I worked there. There's nothing else here, Vig. Without it there's nothing else."

Viggo did not say anything, because there was nothing to say that Aksel did not already know. The town had been built around the cannery the way a pearl is built around grit, and now the grit was being taken out, and what was left would not hold its shape.

He thought of Mette's mother, gutting fish up north because there was no work here, and of Mette in his kitchen pushing porridge, and of the school bus that came each morning with fewer children on it than the year before. The town was emptying the way the harbor emptied at low tide, and unlike the tide it would not come back.

"I'm sorry," he said finally, which was useless, and Aksel nodded as if it were not.

He did not tell Mette. She was eleven and she would learn the shape of the world soon enough; he did not need to hand it to her in the dark of winter. But that evening, as they sat by the stove and she did her sums at the table, he watched her and felt the closing of the cannery in his chest like weather coming, and he thought: there is one thing I can give her. One thing that is mine to give.

So he taught her the water. Not the way you teach a child a poem, by rote, but the way the sea had taught him, which was slowly and by attention and without mercy. Each morning they walked down to the pier and he asked her what she saw, and each morning she saw a little more.

"The tide's turning," she said one day in December, and he had not prompted her.

"How do you know?"

"The boats are swinging. They were pointing out and now they're swinging round." She pointed at the moored boats, all of them slowly rotating on their lines like compass needles finding north. "The water's pushing them the other way now."

"The water always tells you," he said. "It can't help it. Everything it does, it tells you. The boats, the weed, the birds. The way the light sits on it. You only have to watch."

She watched. She was good at it, better than he had been at her age, and he wondered if that was the child or the teacher or only an old man's pride dressing itself up. She had a stillness in her that the work rewarded. Her mother had it too, that capacity to wait and look. He had married it, in her grandmother, dead now eleven years and not a day of it easier.

"Grandfather," she said one morning. The fog had come in overnight and lay on the harbor thick as wool, so that the boats were ghosts and the far shore did not exist. "How did you bring boats in when it was like this?"

"Like this is when they needed me most," he said. "When they could see, they didn't need a pilot. It was the fog and the night that I was for."

"But you couldn't see either."

"No." He looked out at the white nothing where the harbor mouth was. "I didn't see. I knew. There's a difference. I'd done it so many times that my body knew the water even when my eyes couldn't. The set of the current against the hull. The change in the sound when you came under the lee of the point. The smell, even—the harbor smelled different from the open sea, weed and oil and fish, and you'd come into it like coming into a room with a fire lit." He stopped. The fog beaded on Mette's hat in tiny jewels. "You learn it so deep that you don't need your eyes. That takes a lifetime. You won't learn that from me. But the rest of it—the seeing—that you can have."

She was quiet a moment. Then she said, "Will you teach me to bring a boat in?"

He laughed, surprised. "There's no boat to bring in. And no fog you'd survive me teaching you in."

"In the launch. In the daytime. When it's clear."

He looked at her. She was serious; the frown was there. And he thought of the launch, his launch, sitting under its tarpaulin in the boathouse where it had sat for three years since his hip had told him he was done. He had kept it. He had told himself he kept it because a thing like that should not be sold to strangers, but he had kept it the way you keep a photograph of someone gone.

"We'll see," he said, which she knew meant no, and her face closed a little, and he was sorry, but the launch was three years of disuse and his hip was bad and the winter sea was no place for an old man and a child.

Christmas came and went, small and quiet. Mette's mother called on the line that crackled and faded, and Mette held the receiver with both hands as if she could hold her mother through it, and afterward she went to the window and looked at the dark harbor for a long time and Viggo let her.

The cannery closing became real in January when the first men were let go, the ones with the least seniority, sent home to wonder what came next. Aksel was not among them yet but his face had the look of a man counting days. The shop talked of nothing else. Some said the town was finished; some said it would find another way, tourists in the summer, perhaps, people from the city who wanted to see a fishing town, though what they would see was a town that no longer fished. Viggo listened and said little. He had piloted boats over the bar for forty years and he knew that you could not argue with the tide. You could only read it and act accordingly.

He thought about the launch.

It was a foolish thing, he told himself, lying awake with his hip aching and the wind working at the windows. The girl wanted to learn the water and he had given her the watching, which was the real thing, the deep thing. The boat was nothing. The boat was vanity. And yet he could not stop thinking of her face at the window, holding the dead receiver, looking at the dark.

In February there came a stretch of clear cold days, the kind that the winter sometimes gave like an apology, the wind dropping and the sky going hard and blue and the sea lying almost flat. On the third such morning Viggo stood in the boathouse with his hand on the tarpaulin and his hip aching in the cold and his heart doing something it had no business doing at his age.

He pulled the tarpaulin off.

The launch was smaller than he remembered, the way everything from the past was smaller. But she was sound. He had kept her sound. He checked the engine and the fuel and the lines, and his hands remembered what to do though his hip protested every time he bent, and by the time Mette came down the path with her schoolbag he had the launch in the water at the foot of the slip, idling and faithful, the smell of her exhaust mixing with the harbor smell he had spent his life inside of.

Mette stopped on the path. She looked at the boat, and then at him, and the frown came and went and something else replaced it, something he had not seen on her face since October, since before the porridge and the dark window and the dead receiver.

"No school today," he said. "I telephoned. I told them you were sick."

"I'm not sick."

"I know that. Get in the boat."

She got in the boat. He showed her where to sit, low and forward, and he took the tiller and felt the old weight of it in his hand, the small living tremor of an engine pushing against water, and he brought the launch out from the slip and into the harbor in the hard blue cold of the February morning.

"Now," he said. "You've watched it all winter. Now you're in it. It's different in it."

It was different. He had forgotten how different, or he had not let himself remember. From the pier the water was a thing you looked at. From the boat it was a thing that held you up, that pushed and pulled and conferred and conspired, that you sat inside of like a thought inside a mind. He took her out slow, naming things as he went, the way the current set across the harbor mouth, the place where the deep channel ran and you could feel the boat ride easier, the chop that started up past the breakwater where the open sea began.

"Feel that?" he said. "The boat tells you too, not just your eyes. She leans into the current. She sits up when the water shallows. Put your hand on her."

Mette put her hand flat on the gunwale. He watched her feel it, the small motions, the boat reading the water and passing the reading up through the wood into her palm.

"I feel it," she said. "She's leaning."

"That's the channel pushing. That's the dark water you saw from the pier, all those mornings. Now you're on it." He brought them about, slow and easy, just inside the breakwater where the swell came in long and gentle on a day like this and lifted the launch and set her down again like a hand rocking a cradle. "When I brought the big boats in, this is the water I read. Right here. Get this wrong and you put a tanker on the rocks. Get it right and you bring her home."

Mette looked at the breakwater, at the harbor mouth, at the open sea beyond it lying flat and enormous and blue. He saw her taking it in, the size of it, the way the harbor was a small held thing and the sea was everything else.

"Now," he said. "You bring her in."

She looked at him.

"Come back here. Take the tiller. I'll tell you, but you'll do it."

She came back and he moved over, his hip screaming, and he put her hand on the tiller and his hand over hers, lightly, just there.

"Push it that way to go this way," he said. "Always backwards. The boat does the opposite of what you tell the tiller. That's the first thing and the hardest thing. Now. See the slip? See where we came from? Bring her there. Slow. Aim for the dark water and let it carry you."

She pushed the tiller the wrong way and the boat swung wrong and she gasped and corrected too hard and the boat swung the other way, and Viggo laughed, and after a moment she laughed too, the frown breaking up like fog burning off, and he felt the thing in his chest that was too large for the moment and let it be large.

"Slow," he said. "The water's not in a hurry. Neither are you. Feel where it wants to take you and let it, then nudge. Don't fight her. Read her."

She found it. He felt her find it, the moment when she stopped fighting the tiller and started listening to it, when the boat stopped lurching and settled into a line, the dark channel water taking her gently and Mette's small adjustments riding on top of it like a voice over a melody. She brought the launch in across the harbor, slow and wandering but true, aimed at the slip where the dark water led, and Viggo took his hand off hers and let her do it alone.

"I'm doing it," she said, not believing it.

"You're doing it."

She brought the launch to the slip and he reached out at the last to take the throttle down and catch the piling, because that part needed a grown man's hand and an old pilot's timing, but the bringing-in was hers, the long read across the harbor was hers, and they both knew it.

She turned to him with her whole face open, the way it had been before October, before the dark winter, and she said, "Can we do it again?"

They did it again. They did it four times that morning, until his hip would not let him stand at the slip another time, and each time she was better, each time she fought the tiller less and read the water more, and by the fourth time she brought the launch in with a calm that frightened him a little, because it was his own calm, the pilot's calm, the thing he had thought died with the boats too big to need him.

They tied up at the slip and sat in the launch a while, not talking, the engine off, the harbor very quiet around them. The cannery whistle had blown at six and would not blow again until tomorrow. Smoke went up from a few chimneys. The boats swung slowly on the turning tide.

"Grandfather," Mette said. "Is the cannery really closing?"

He looked at her. So she had heard. Of course she had heard; she was eleven, not deaf, and the town talked of nothing else. He had thought he was protecting her and he had only been protecting himself, the way old men did, telling themselves their silences were a kindness.

"Yes," he said. "In the spring."

She nodded slowly, taking it in the way she took in the water, the seeing first and the why coming after. "Will we have to leave?"

He did not know. That was the truth, and she was old enough for the truth now, here in the boat, on the water she had learned to read.

"Maybe," he said. "I don't know yet. Your mother and I will have to talk. There may not be a way to stay."

She looked out at the harbor, at the boats and the breakwater and the open sea beyond. He watched her face and he could not read it, his own granddaughter, after all this; a child was still a water he had never learned. But then she said something that he carried with him afterward, through the spring and the closing and everything that came of it, through the leaving when it finally came and the new place that was not home and never would be.

"I'll always know how to read it," she said. "Even if we go. Even somewhere else, there'll be water. And I'll know how to read it." She turned to him. "You taught me. That doesn't close."

"No," he said. His voice was not steady. "That doesn't close."

She put her hand on the gunwale again, flat, the way she had out in the channel, feeling for the small motions of the boat at rest, and he understood that she was not feeling for anything now, that the boat was still and there was nothing to read; she was only keeping her hand on the water's messenger because she had learned to love it, the way he loved it, the way it could be loved without ever being mastered or kept.

The tide turned all the way and began to come in. He felt it lift the launch a little against the slip, the harbor filling again the way it did twice a day, faithful, indifferent, complete. The cannery would close. The town would empty. The whistle would blow its last and the men would scatter to the cities and the boats would be sold to strangers. He could read all of that as clearly as he could read the dark channel water; it was coming the way weather comes, and you could not argue with it, you could only read it and act accordingly.

But the girl beside him had her hand on the water and the water in her now, deep, where it would not wash out. He had given her that. It was the one thing that was his to give and he had given it, and it would go with her wherever the closing tide carried her, into whatever room with whatever fire lit, and she would always, always know how to come home across the dark.

"Come on," he said. "Help me up. My hip's no good in the cold."

She helped him up. They stood on the slip in the hard blue morning and looked at the launch riding the rising tide, and then they walked up the path together toward the house, past the empty net sheds and the Hansen place where no one lived, into the last of the winter, into whatever came after.
