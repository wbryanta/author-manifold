The pump house smelled of rust and old water, a mineral dampness that Caleb had known his whole life and never once thought to name until now, standing in it again at thirty-eight with a wrench in his hand and his brother crouched over the manifold like a man praying to something he no longer believed in.

"It's the gasket," Daniel said. "Same as April. I told you it would go again."

"You told me a lot of things."

Daniel didn't answer. He had a way of not answering that was itself a kind of statement, a silence shaped exactly like the argument he was choosing not to have, and Caleb felt it settle over the small room the way the heat was settling over the orchard outside, thick and total and without mercy. It was not yet nine in the morning. By noon the thermometer nailed to the pump house door would read past a hundred, and the trees—what was left of them, the Galas and the few stubborn Winesaps their father had grafted himself the year Caleb was born—would stand in the white light with their leaves curling like hands closing.

"Hand me the flathead," Daniel said.

Caleb found it in the toolbox, the one with their father's initials burned into the lid, R.M., Robert Maddox, dead now four years and somehow more present in this room than either of his living sons. He passed the screwdriver. Daniel took it without looking, his fingers finding it by the feel of it, and Caleb thought how that was the difference between them, finally, the whole thing in a gesture: Daniel knew the weight and shape of every tool here without needing his eyes, and Caleb had to look.

"You want to talk about it or you want to fix the line," Caleb said.

"I want to fix the line."

"We can do both."

"No," Daniel said. "We can't. You always think we can do both. We can't do both, Cale."

He worked the old gasket free, a black ring crumbling at the edges, and held it up to the light from the doorway, and the way he held it—turning it, examining it, the small frown of his concentration—was their father's exactly, and Caleb had to look away. Out the door the irrigation lines ran in their straight rows down the slope toward the dry creek, black poly pipe sun-cracked and patched with tape, and where the water should have been pulsing through the emitters there was only the tick of expanding plastic in the heat. Forty acres. Four hundred trees, give or take, of the eleven hundred their father had kept alive. The rest stood dead in their rows like a thing photographed and then abandoned, gray wood and the ghost-shapes of crowns that hadn't leafed in three summers.

"The Henderson offer's still good," Caleb said. "I talked to her Thursday. She'll hold it till the end of the month, but after that she's looking at the Pruett place instead, and if she takes that, we're—"

"I know what the Henderson offer is."

"Do you? Because you keep acting like it's something I made up to spite you."

Daniel set the old gasket on the concrete floor, very carefully, as though it might still be of use, and reached for the new one in its paper sleeve. "I know exactly what it is," he said. "It's four hundred and ten thousand dollars and a clause that lets her plant grapes."

"Wine grapes. It's good ground for it. The slope, the drainage—"

"It's apple ground."

"It was apple ground."

Daniel looked up then, and for a moment Caleb saw the boy in him, the older brother who had carried him on his shoulders down these same rows when the trees were heavy and the creek ran cold even in August, who had taught him to find the ripe ones by the give of the fruit and not the color, who had stood between him and their father's belt more than once and taken what came. There was that boy in his face, and there was also a man Caleb didn't entirely know anymore, a man who had stayed when Caleb left, who had watched the water table drop and the wells go shallow and the price of fruit collapse under the weight of a thousand acres of better-funded orchards two valleys over, and who had stayed anyway, every year, fixing the gasket in April and again in June, holding the dying thing together with tape and stubbornness and a refusal Caleb had stopped being able to read as love.

"You don't get to come back and call it the way it was," Daniel said. "You left. You got to leave. You went and did your thing in the city and you sent money at Christmas like that made you part of it, and now Dad's gone and the deed's got both our names on it and suddenly you've got opinions about apple ground."

"That's not fair."

"None of it's fair, Cale. That's not an argument."

He fitted the new gasket into the seat, pressing it down with his thumb, and the precision of it, the gentleness, was a rebuke. Caleb leaned against the doorframe. The wood was warm through his shirt. Out past the dead rows a hawk turned in the white sky, riding something Caleb couldn't see, and he watched it because watching it was easier than watching his brother's hands.

"You know what the numbers are," Caleb said. "I'm not making them up. We lost money last year. We lost money the year before. You're carrying forty thousand in debt on equipment that's worth maybe eight, and the only reason you're not underwater is you don't pay yourself and you don't fix the truck and you eat out of the garden like it's nineteen thirty-two. That's not farming. That's a slow way of dying that you're calling principle."

"And selling's a fast way."

"Selling's a way of being able to breathe."

Daniel began threading the manifold cap back on, his jaw set. "You want to breathe," he said. "Go breathe. Nobody's stopping you. Sign your half over to me and go breathe in the city with all your air."

"It doesn't work like that and you know it. The bank won't carry the note on your income alone. They told you that. They told us both. It's both of us or it's neither."

"Then it's neither."

"Daniel."

"It's neither, Cale. I'd rather it die than sell it to somebody who'll tear out Dad's trees and put in grapes for rich people in San Francisco to drink while they talk about terroir." He said the word like something spat. "You think he'd want that? You think he stood out there in the freeze burning smudge pots at three in the morning so some woman from Marin could plant Pinot on his grave?"

"He's not buried here."

"You know what I mean."

"I don't, actually. I don't know what you mean. Because what I see is a man who worked himself into the ground keeping something alive past the point it wanted to live, and what I don't want is to watch you do the same thing because you can't tell the difference between him and the trees."

Daniel went still. The wrench was in his hand. For a moment Caleb thought he might throw it, and part of him almost wanted it, wanted the clean violence of it, the thing finally said with the body that the mouth kept circling, but Daniel only set it down on the manifold with a small deliberate click and stood up and walked past Caleb out into the light.

Caleb followed.

The heat hit him like a wall. The cracked pipe ran down toward the creek and Daniel walked along it, stooping now and then to check a fitting, and Caleb walked behind him through the rows, past the living trees and into the dead ones, where the silence was different, deeper, a silence with no insects in it because there was nothing for them to live on. The dead trees had been beautiful once. Caleb remembered them in bloom, the whole slope white and humming in April, the smell of it coming through his bedroom window, and his father standing on the porch with a cup of coffee gone cold in his hand just looking at it, not saying anything, because there was nothing to say, because it was everything that could be said.

"There," Daniel said, crouching. "Here's the break. See it?"

Caleb crouched beside him. The poly pipe had split along a sun-crack, a clean dry mouth in the black plastic, and around it the ground was pale and powdery, no sign that water had moved through this section in some time.

"This isn't from this morning," Caleb said.

"No."

"How long's this been like this?"

Daniel didn't answer.

"Daniel. How long's this section been dry?"

"Couple weeks."

"You've got fifty trees on this line."

"I know how many trees I've got."

"They're—Daniel, they're past saving. Look at them. You can't bring these back from—" He stood and turned in a slow circle and counted the curling leaves, the trees on the lower line gone the gray-green of stress, the soft new growth at the tips already dead. "You let these go. You let a whole line go and you didn't tell me."

"I didn't let anything go. The pump's only got so much pressure and the wells are down and I had to choose, so I chose the uphill lines because the soil holds better up there, and that's what farming is, Cale, it's choosing what you can't save so you can save what you can, and you wouldn't know that because you've never had to choose anything you couldn't afford to lose."

The words went into Caleb and stuck. He stood in the dead row and felt them stick, and he wanted to say something back, something that would land as hard, but the thing about Daniel was that he was usually right in the way that hurt and wrong in the way that mattered, and Caleb had never figured out how to answer that, not at fifteen and not now.

"You should've told me," he said finally. "About the wells. About how bad it is."

"Why? So you could put it in your column? Forty thousand in debt, fifty trees dead, time to sell? I didn't need the data, Cale. I'm living it."

"So am I. It's mine too."

"It's not, though." Daniel stood, brushing the pale dust off his knees. "That's the thing you can't see. It's got your name on the deed but it's not yours, because you don't live in it. You don't wake up to it. You don't lie awake doing the math on it. You inherited a half of something you never carried, and now you want to liquidate it, and you've convinced yourself that's the responsible thing, the grown-up thing, and maybe it is. Maybe you're right. But don't stand in my dead row and tell me it's yours."

He walked back toward the pump house to get the coupling and the pipe cement, and Caleb stood alone among the dead trees with the hawk still turning overhead, and after a while he reached out and put his hand on the trunk of the nearest one. The bark came away under his palm in a dry flake. The wood beneath was gray and split. He thought of his father's hands on this same bark, grafting, pruning, the small surgeries of a man who believed that attention was a form of love and maybe the only one he had, and he understood, standing there, that Daniel had inherited that belief whole, had it in his hands the way he had the weight of the tools, and that Caleb had inherited something else, some watchfulness, some need to stand back and see the shape of the thing, and that these two inheritances could not be reconciled because they were not opinions. They were the men themselves.

When Daniel came back they worked.

This, at least, they could still do. Daniel cut out the split section with the pruning saw and Caleb held the pipe steady, and they fitted the coupling and brushed the cement on, blue and sharp-smelling in the heat, and held the joint while it set, neither of them speaking, their four hands on the black pipe in the white light, and for the length of it Caleb felt the old rhythm come back, the way they had worked as boys, no need for words because the work was the words, and he thought maybe this was the only language they had ever really shared and maybe it was enough and maybe it wasn't.

"Hold it," Daniel said. "Thirty seconds."

Caleb held it. He counted in his head. Somewhere up the slope a meadowlark let go of its long falling note, and then another answered, and the heat pressed down, and the cement set, and Daniel let go and sat back on his heels and wiped his face with his forearm, leaving a streak of pale dust like warpaint.

"It won't hold pressure till tomorrow," Daniel said. "We can't open the valve till morning."

"Okay."

"It won't save these. The fifty. It's too late for these."

"I know."

"I just wanted them to die wet." Daniel said it quietly, not looking at Caleb, looking down the row at the dead trees. "Stupid. I know it's stupid. But I figured if I could get the line fixed I could run it down here a day or two and at least they wouldn't go in the dust. He'd have done that. Dad. He'd have wanted them to go in the wet ground."

And Caleb understood then that they had not been repairing the line to save anything. There was nothing on this lower section to save. Daniel had brought him out here, had spent the morning on his knees in the heat fitting a gasket and cutting pipe and breathing cement, in order to give fifty dead trees a decent death. It was the least practical thing a person could do. It was money and labor and water—water, the scarcest thing they had—spent on the already lost, and it was the most their father in him, and Caleb felt something turn over in his chest, some hard thing he'd been carrying since he got the call about the will, and he sat down in the dust next to his brother and the two of them looked down the row at the trees their father had planted and could not save.

"I'm not trying to dishonor him," Caleb said. "You know that. That's not what the sale is."

"I know."

"I think about him out here too. I think about him all the time. I just—I think he'd look at the numbers, Daniel. I think that's the part you don't want, but I think he was a practical man and he'd look at the numbers and he wouldn't want us going down with it. He wouldn't want us spending the rest of our lives doing the math on a thing that can't be saved just because he loved it. He'd want us to live."

"Maybe."

"You don't think so."

Daniel was quiet a while. The meadowlark sang again. "I think," he said slowly, "that he'd look at the numbers and he'd know exactly what they meant and he'd keep going anyway, because the numbers were never the reason he did it. You think it was a business. It was never a business. It lost money most years he ran it too. He kept it because it was the thing he made with his hands and his father made before him, and a man's got to have one thing he doesn't run the numbers on, Cale, one thing he just keeps, or he's nothing but the numbers, he's just a column adding himself up till he dies." He picked up a flake of bark from the ground and turned it in his fingers. "I'd rather keep one thing I can't afford than be a man who can afford everything and keeps nothing."

"That's a nice speech."

"It's not a speech. It's just true."

"It can be both."

Daniel almost smiled. It was the first crack in him all morning, the corner of his mouth going, their mother's smile, the one good inheritance neither of them had earned. "Yeah," he said. "It can be both. You always say that."

They sat. The sun climbed. The dust was warm under Caleb's hands and the dead trees stood over them, and Caleb thought about the four hundred and ten thousand dollars and what it would do, the debt cleared, the equipment sold, Daniel free, both of them free, money in the bank and the long ache of it finally over, and he tried to feel the relief he'd been promising himself and felt instead only the dry mouth of the broken pipe, the pale ground where the water had stopped reaching.

"What if we kept the upper forty," Caleb said.

Daniel turned his head.

"I don't know if it works. I'm just—I'm thinking out loud. If we sold the lower section, the dead ground, the part Henderson really wants for the drainage, the grape ground—if we sold her that, just that, twenty-five acres, maybe she pays enough to clear the equipment debt, and we keep the upper forty with the live trees and the better wells, and you run it. Smaller. Something you can actually water. Something you can keep."

"She won't want just the bottom."

"Maybe not. Probably not. But maybe. I'd have to run the—" He stopped. "I'd have to look at it."

Daniel was watching him now with something Caleb couldn't read, something careful, the look of a man who has been disappointed enough times that hope itself has become a kind of risk he assesses before taking. "You'd do that," he said. "You'd come back and run the numbers on keeping it instead of running them on selling it."

"I'd run them on what's actually there. Both ways. And then we'd look at them together. That's all I ever wanted, Daniel. I never wanted to sell it out from under you. I wanted you to look at it with me. You wouldn't look. You just kept fixing the gasket."

"Because looking felt like giving up."

"Looking isn't giving up. Looking is the opposite of giving up. It's the only way you ever save anything."

Daniel let out a long breath, and his shoulders came down from where they'd been up around his ears since April, since before April, since the day they'd buried their father probably, and he looked down the row at the fifty dead trees and then up the slope at the living ones, the Galas and the stubborn Winesaps, the four hundred that were left, and Caleb watched him do the math, a different math, the math of what could be kept.

"The upper wells are better," Daniel said. "It's true. If I wasn't trying to water all forty acres I might actually be able to—" He stopped himself. "I don't know. I'd have to look at it."

"So we'll look at it."

"Both ways."

"Both ways. Honest. I won't cook it toward selling and you won't cook it toward keeping. We just look."

Daniel nodded slowly. He picked up the pruning saw and stood, and reached down with his free hand, and Caleb took it, and Daniel pulled him up out of the dust, and for a moment they stood close in the dead row holding on, their father's hands, their mother's smile, the two halves of the one thing, and then Daniel let go and turned and looked at the trees.

"They'll be gone by August anyway," he said. "These fifty. Even wet."

"I know."

"But I want to run the line down here tomorrow. After the cement sets. Just for a few days."

"Okay."

"It's a waste of water."

"It's your water," Caleb said. "Run it where you want."

They walked back up the slope toward the pump house through the rows, the dead behind them and the living ahead, and the heat lay on the orchard like a hand, and the hawk had gone, and somewhere down by the dry creek the meadowlark sang its falling song one more time and then was quiet. At the pump house door Daniel stopped and looked back the way they'd come, down the long rows to the dead ground at the bottom where the new coupling held the line together with its blue seam, waiting for morning and the water that would come too late for the trees but would come, and he stood there a moment in their father's stance, looking, not saying anything, because there was nothing to say, because it was everything that could be said, and then he went inside to put the tools away.
