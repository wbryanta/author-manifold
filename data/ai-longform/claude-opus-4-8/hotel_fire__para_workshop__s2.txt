The valet took her keys without looking at her, which was the first kindness the place offered. Margit handed them over and stepped back, smoothing the front of a dress she had bought specifically and would never wear again, and watched the boy drive her small car down the ramp into the dark beneath the building. Then she had nothing to do with her hands. She put them at her sides. She looked up.

They had kept the name. That surprised her more than anything, more than the new glass, the new awnings the color of dried blood, the doorman in his gray coat holding the door for a couple who went through laughing about something that had happened in the car. The Adlerhof. The same gold letters, or letters made to look the same, set above the entrance where she remembered them. As if nothing had happened here. As if a building were only a building and could not be guilty.

She had told Ilse she would come. That was the whole of it. Ilse's daughter was marrying a man Margit had never met, in the ballroom of a hotel Margit had sworn in the back of an ambulance she would never set foot in again, and Ilse had called her in March and said please, you're the only one left who remembers Theresa as a baby, and Margit had said yes before she understood what she was agreeing to. By the time she understood, the yes was already old and had hardened into a fact.

"Madam," the doorman said. He was young. They were all young now, the people who worked here. He did not know what the floor under his polished shoes had once been.

"Thank you," she said, and went in.

The lobby was warm and smelled of lilies and of something else underneath, a clean chemical smell, carpet shampoo or the particular nothing of air that has been conditioned and filtered and breathed by no one for a long time. She had braced herself for smoke. She had genuinely thought, on the drive, that she might smell smoke, that the body keeps these things and produces them when you least want them, and she had prepared a small private collapse to be had in the car if necessary. But there was no smoke. There was a string quartet somewhere off to the right, tuning, and a young woman at a lectern with a tablet, and a great deal of marble that had not existed before because the marble that existed before had cracked in the heat and been carried out in pieces.

"I'm here for the wedding," Margit said. "The Brandt wedding."

"Of course." The young woman smiled with her whole face. "Ceremony's at four in the Rosensaal, just up the staircase and to your left. There's champagne in the foyer now if you'd like."

The Rosensaal. They had named it. In her day it had been the large function room and it had no name, it was just the large function room, and on the morning of the fire there had been a pharmaceutical conference setting up in it, three hundred chairs and a banner, and she had carried towels past its open doors at seven and thought nothing of it. She thanked the young woman and did not move toward the staircase. She stood a moment longer in the lobby, getting her bearings, which was a strange thing to need to do in a place you had walked five hundred times. The geometry was wrong. The desk was where the desk had been. But the corridor that had run behind it, the service corridor, the one she had—

She stopped that. She had decided, in the car, in lieu of collapsing, that she would not go down those corridors today, not in her mind and not on her feet. She was a guest. Guests do not know where the service corridors are. She took a glass of champagne from a tray to give her hands their occupation and went up the stairs.

She was fifty-eight. She had been thirty-one. It seemed to her, climbing, that the distance between those two numbers was not twenty-seven years but a wall, a partition, and that the woman of thirty-one was on the other side of it doing the things she had done, would always be doing them, carrying the towels, hearing the alarm, and that Margit at fifty-eight was only a person who had heard about her, a relative, someone who had been told the story so many times she had begun to mistake it for memory.

"Margit." Ilse was on her before she reached the top, Ilse in violet, heavier than she had been, her face wet already though nothing had happened yet, the ceremony not begun. They held each other. Ilse smelled of hairspray and of her own particular self, unchanged, the one constant in the building. "You came. I didn't think— I knew you'd come. I told Bernd, she'll come."

"I said I would."

"I know what it cost you." Ilse pulled back and held her by the upper arms and looked at her the way you look at someone you are checking for damage. "Coming here. I know."

"It's a room," Margit said. "It's a nice room."

It was a nice room. They had done it beautifully. White and gold, tall windows giving onto the square, chairs in rows with ribbon on the aisle ends, and at the front an arch of white flowers under which Theresa would shortly stand and promise her life to a man called Andreas Brandt who was, Ilse had said on the phone, in logistics, which Margit had not asked her to clarify. The light came in slantwise and golden and fell across the empty chairs. It was the kind of room that asks nothing of you but that you sit in it and be moved.

"Sit with me at the front," Ilse said. "With the family."

"I'll sit at the back. I'm not family."

"You're more than—"

"Ilse. Let me sit at the back. I like to see everyone's faces."

This was a lie and Ilse knew it was a lie and let her have it, because Ilse was the kind of friend who knew when a lie was load-bearing. She squeezed Margit's hand and went to do the thousand things a mother of the bride must do, and Margit took a chair in the last row, on the aisle, near the doors. She had not thought about it consciously. She noticed it only after she had done it, that she had taken the seat nearest the exit, and she allowed herself a small dry inward smile at the predictability of her own animal.

The room filled. People she didn't know, the families and friends of a young couple whose lives had unspooled entirely in the years since she had last breathed this air. They were dressed up and pleased to be there. A baby was carried through, and an old man with a cane was helped to a seat in the front, and two girls in matching dresses chased each other until a mother caught them. The quartet had relocated and began to play, something by Bach, careful and bright. The doors at the front opened and Andreas came in with the officiant and stood under the flowers looking terrified and happy, a tall young man with his hair combed wet, and Margit thought, you have no idea where you're standing. Forgive me. You have no idea and you shouldn't have to.

Where the arch stood now, she was fairly sure, was where the second emergency stairwell had been. The one that didn't open. She had not meant to work this out and she worked it out anyway, her body doing the arithmetic of the old floor plan against the new one, and the answer came up that the flowers stood roughly where the door had been, the door with the bar across it that you pushed and it didn't give, that twelve people had pushed and it didn't give, because someone, some manager whose name had been in the papers and then in the indictment, had ordered it kept locked against the conference delegates slipping out without paying for their lunches. She was very sure of this. She did not want to be sure of it and she was sure of it. The flowers were beautiful. The young man stood under them and waited for his bride.

The music changed. Everyone stood and turned, and Margit stood and turned with them, and there was Theresa on her father's arm, Theresa whom Margit had last held as a toddler with jam on her face, now a tall pale young woman in white walking very slowly the way they teach you to, smiling and frightened, her bouquet trembling slightly. She came up the aisle and passed Margit's row close enough to touch and did not see her, saw no one, saw only the front of the room and the man under the flowers, and Margit felt her throat do something complicated. The girl was so alive. That was the whole of the feeling and it was enormous. The girl was so alive and she was walking toward the locked door and she didn't know and would never need to know, because it wasn't locked anymore, because it wasn't even there anymore, because they had torn the whole thing down to the steel and built it again, and the only person in the room carrying the door was Margit, in the back, in a dress she would never wear again.

The ceremony was brief and in the modern style. There were vows they had written themselves, which made people laugh and then made them cry, and Margit found that she could not follow the words and instead watched the light move on the wall. At some point the light reached the place where she believed the stairwell door had been and lay on the white-painted plaster as if pointing. Don't, she told herself. You promised yourself in the car. She looked at her hands.

There had been a girl called Annika. Younger than Margit, nineteen, from somewhere in the east, who had been on the housekeeping staff for six weeks and whom Margit had been training, which mostly meant Annika followed her around the upper floors with a cart and they talked, or Annika talked, about a boy and about her plan to go to nursing school, and Margit listened the way you listen to someone whose whole life is still a list of intentions. On the morning of the fire they had been on four. The alarm had gone and they had both stopped and looked at each other, and Margit had said it's a drill, because they did drills, because it was always a drill, and then they had smelled it.

She made herself come back to the room. The officiant was saying something about the binding of two lives. Theresa was crying and laughing at once. Andreas held both her hands.

After four, the corridors had filled. Smoke came up the stairwells the way water fills a glass, from the bottom, except it filled from the bottom and you were above it, which was wrong, which made no sense to the body, and you went up to get away from it and that was exactly the wrong thing and also the only thing. She had Annika by the wrist. That part she remembered with a clarity that had not faded in twenty-seven years and would not fade, she thought, until everything faded. She had Annika by the wrist and they had gone to the second stairwell because the first was already a chimney, and the door had the bar across it and she had pushed it and it had not given. She had put her shoulder to it. Annika had put her shoulder to it. Other people had arrived behind them, the conference delegates in their lanyards, and everyone pushed, and the door held because it was a good door, a fire door, a door built to hold, and someone had locked it.

"You may kiss," said the officiant, and the room erupted, and Margit stood with everyone else and clapped, her eyes streaming, and the people around her thought she was crying for the wedding and she let them think it.

They had gone back. That was the thing she had never told anyone, not the inquiry, not Ilse, not the therapist she had seen for three years on Wednesdays. They had gone back from the locked door, she and Annika, and the smoke had been at the top of the stairs by then, coming up, and Margit had thought of the linen chute. The linen chute on three. A wide aluminum tube that ran down to the basement laundry, and she had used it a hundred times to send sheets down, and she had thought, illogically, brilliantly, in the way the mind works when it has decided it wants to live, that it went down, that down was away from the smoke, that she could fit. She was small. Annika was not small. Annika was a tall girl, broad through the shoulders, a swimmer's build, she had said so, she had been on a swim team.

"I'll go first," Margit had said, "and catch you," which made no sense, you cannot catch someone who has fallen three floors down a metal tube, but they were past sense. She had climbed into the chute. She remembered the cold of the aluminum and the dark and the roar that was the fire somewhere being itself, and she had let go, and she had gone down in the dark hitting the sides, and she had come out into the laundry where there was no smoke yet and a fireman had found her four minutes later wandering between the great machines saying a name.

Annika had not fit. Or had not tried. Or had tried and stuck, or had gone back for the door again, or had simply stood at the top of the chute in the smoke and not been able to make herself climb into a black hole in the wall and drop. Margit did not know. That was the hole in the center of it, the thing that had no bottom. She had gone down and Annika had not come down after her and Margit had stood in the laundry saying her name into a chute that brought down nothing, no one, only, after a while, a kind of heat, and then the fireman's hands on her.

Eleven people had died. She knew the number the way you know your own birthday. Annika had been one of the eleven. They had found her, the report said, on the fourth floor, not far from the stairwell. Not in the chute. Margit had read that sentence many times. Not in the chute, which meant Annika had not even tried, which Margit had decided meant Annika had been afraid, which Margit had decided meant that if she had stayed, if she had not climbed in herself, if she had stayed and held the girl's wrist and said we go together, we go down together, you can do it, I'll be right behind you, then maybe. The maybe was the whole of the rest of her life. The maybe had a wingspan that covered everything.

The wedding party was processing out now, the couple first, radiant, then the parents, Ilse on Bernd's arm both of them undone, and Ilse found Margit's face in the back row and mouthed something, thank you, or you came, or simply Margit's name, and Margit nodded and smiled and the party swept out toward the foyer and the champagne and the photographs, and the room began to empty after them, row by row, everyone talking now, released.

She stayed in her seat. She let them all go past her. A few people glanced at her, the older woman alone in the last row, and looked away, the way you do. Then the room was empty and the quartet was packing up their instruments at the front and one of the catering staff came in to begin moving chairs, a young man in a white shirt, and Margit got up at last and instead of going out toward the foyer she walked up the aisle to the front, to the flowers.

"Ma'am, we're just going to start resetting for the dinner," the young man said, not unkindly.

"I'll only be a moment."

She stood under the arch of white flowers. Up close they were real, she could smell them, roses and something green and bitter, eucalyptus maybe. She put out her hand and touched the painted wall behind them, the plaster, cool under her palm. This is where the door was. She was almost certain. She held her hand flat against the wall where the door had been, where the bar had been that didn't give, and she stood like that, an old woman in a good dress with her hand on a wall, and the catering boy decided to leave her alone and went to the other end of the room to stack chairs.

She had expected, if she ever came here, which she had sworn she never would, that the building would accuse her. She had carried it as an accusation for twenty-seven years, the locked door, the chute, the going down alone. She had thought the place itself would know what she had done and not done. But the wall under her hand was just a wall. It was warm now where her hand had warmed it. The building did not know anything. The building had been carried out in pieces and a new one stood in its place and it had no memory and no opinion, it had a name they had kept and a room they had named Rosensaal and an arch of real flowers, and it did not care what Margit had done, and there was, she found, standing there, no one in the room to forgive her and no one to accuse her, there was only herself, who had been doing both jobs for twenty-seven years and was tired.

The girl had not tried the chute. Margit had decided, long ago, that this meant Annika had been afraid. But standing here she let herself think the other thing, the thing she had not let herself think, which was that Annika had not tried the chute because Annika had gone back to the door. To help the others. The delegates, the old ones, the slow ones. That a nineteen-year-old who was going to be a nurse had turned away from the black hole that meant her own life and gone back down the corridor toward the people pushing at the door, and that this was not a failure of nerve but the opposite, and that Margit, who had gone down, who had saved herself, had spent twenty-seven years calling that girl a coward in the privacy of her grief so that she would not have to call herself the other thing.

She took her hand off the wall.

She did not know which it was. That was the truth and she made herself hold it: she did not know whether Annika had been afraid or brave, whether she had frozen or chosen, and she would never know, and she had filled the not-knowing for twenty-seven years with the version that let her keep hating herself, because hating herself had at least been a way of staying with the girl, of not leaving her there alone on the fourth floor, of going back, again and again, in the only way she had left. She saw that now. She had been climbing back up the chute every day for twenty-seven years. She had never once let herself come all the way down.

"That's a beautiful spot for it," she said to the catering boy, who looked up. "The flowers. The arch. It's beautiful."

"The bride picked it all," he said. "Took her months, the planner said."

"She did well."

She walked back down the aisle, between the rows of chairs that were already being broken from their formation and stacked, the wedding's brief geometry dismantling behind her, and out through the doors. The foyer was loud and bright. There was the couple being photographed against a window full of afternoon, there was Ilse laughing with a glass in her hand, there were the two little girls in matching dresses asleep now against their mother, and the noise of it all came at her warmly and she let it. A waiter offered her champagne and she took it.

"You disappeared," Ilse said, arriving, breathless, flushed. "I lost you. Are you all right? Was it—" She lowered her voice. "Was it terrible?"

"No," Margit said, and was surprised to find it was nearly true. "It was a wedding."

"You're crying."

"A little. It's allowed at a wedding."

Ilse looked at her, the old checking-for-damage look, and seemed to decide that whatever she found there was within the range of the bearable. She put her arm through Margit's. "Come and meet Andreas. He's in logistics, you know. I have no idea what it means but he's a sweet boy and he adores her."

"I'd like that," Margit said.

She let herself be led across the loud bright foyer, past the window where the light was going gold and then golder, toward the young man with his hair combed wet who was marrying into a family that had loved her for thirty years, and as she went she thought of Annika, nineteen, on a swim team, going to be a nurse, who had followed her around the upper floors talking about a boy. She thought: I'll let you be brave now. I should have let you be brave a long time ago. It cost her nothing and it cost her everything, and she did it walking, between one step and the next, the way you finally put down a weight you've carried so long you've stopped feeling your own arms.

The young man turned and saw them coming and smiled, the easy smile of someone to whom nothing terrible has yet happened, and put out his hand.

"You must be Margit," he said. "They've told me all about you."

"Not all," she said, and took his hand, and shook it, and the day went on around her, full of people, full of light, in a building that remembered nothing and stood, for now, for them.
