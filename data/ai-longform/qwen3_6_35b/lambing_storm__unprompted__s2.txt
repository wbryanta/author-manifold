The wind did not howl so much as grind, a continuous tectonic sigh that pressed the shed walls inward and shook the corrugated roof until the nails screamed. Outside, the world had been erased. Snow fell not in flakes but in horizontal sheets, driven by a pressure system that had rolled down from the ridge lines with the arrogance of something that owed no one anything. Inside, the air was thick with the warm, animal stink of birth and damp wool, lanolin and iron, the sour tang of swallowed amniotic fluid, the sweet rot of hay that had gone slightly mild in the corners. Maren wiped her hands on her thighs, leaving streaks of grease and dried fluid across the dark denim of her jeans, and watched the ewe shudder. The lamb slid out, slick and dark, followed by a gush of fluid that hit the straw with a wet slap. She reached in, her arms up to the elbows in warmth, and turned the small body, guiding the shoulders, feeling the stubborn resistance of the ribcage giving way under practiced pressure. When it emerged, it came with a gasp that sounded like tearing canvas. She rubbed it vigorously with a handful of coarse straw until its lungs inflated, until its legs kicked against her forearms, until its head lifted and its eyes blinked open, dark and blind and alive. She lifted it to the mother’s nose. The ewe broke, collapsing forward with a guttural sigh, licking the lamb’s flank with rough, desperate strokes. The shed exhaled. The wind still ground against the roof.

Leo stood by the headlamp rack, adjusting the strap on his own unit, his shoulders hunched against the cold that seeped through the floorboards. He was seventeen, all sharp angles and unyielding bone, his hair still growing out fast enough to tangle at the nape despite the clippers. He watched his mother’s hands, the way they moved with an economy that had taken decades to learn. He remembered when those hands used to hold his father’s. The thought came unbidden, as they always did now, not as a wound but as a phantom limb: a persistent, hollow ache that flared when the light hit the empty hook by the door, when the coffee pot sat on the wrong burner, when a shadow crossed the yard and his breath caught before he could correct it. He looked away, fixed his eyes on the metal shelves, the bins of iodine, the coils of rope, the cardboard boxes of colostrum supplement. He turned back to the lamp, clicked it on, felt the weight of it settle against his skull, and walked toward the next pen.

The storm had taken the power sometime after dusk. They had been arguing, or trying not to, over the generator’s fuel line, over the price of diesel, over whose turn it was to shovel the drifts piling against the south door. Maren had reached for the wrench with a patience that felt like a performance, and Leo had felt it, the carefulness of it, the way she held her grief in her throat like a stone and swallowed it when the sheep needed feeding. Then the lights had died. The hum of the refrigerator in the lean-to cut out. The overhead fluorescents guttered and went dark, leaving them in the gray, shifting light of the headlamps and the single kerosene lantern that Maren lit with a long match. The dark had felt like a door closing. Now they worked by the narrow cones of their lamps, the beams cutting through the dusty air, illuminating floating motes, the backs of ewes, the pale ribs of lambs, the frost that was already creeping up the interior of the shed walls in crystalline fractures.

They had been doing this for three days. Four. Time in the lambing shed was measured in contractions, in stretches, in the slow pooling of fluids, in the quiet between the ewes’ low calls. It was their first season without him. Not the first winter, not the first spring, but the first lambing. The shed felt different. Or maybe it was just that the routines had cracked, leaving gaps where his presence used to be. He had been the one who knew which ewe would drop her lamb at dawn and which would wait until night. He had been the one who could read the tension in a hip, the slight dip of a back, the way a mother’s ear would flick before the water bag broke. He had been the one who kept the coffee hot and the blankets warmed and the records in the leather-bound book on the workbench. Now the book sat open, its pages fogged with condensation, its margins filled with her tight, utilitarian script. Leo filled in the blanks when he could, drawing shapes that looked like numbers until he caught himself and started over.

Another ewe was crowning. Maren was on her knees in the straw, her palms flat on the ewe’s flanks, feeling the muscles bunch and release beneath her. Her boots were soaked through, the leather darkened by moisture and salt. She didn’t seem to notice. She never did. Her attention was a focused thing, a beam that excluded everything but the birth, the animal’s breathing, the rhythm of her own pulse in her wrists. Leo approached with a fresh bucket of warm water and a clean towel. He set them down without speaking. She didn’t look up. When the lamb slipped free, it was small, its legs splayed, its head tucked under its chest. It didn’t cry. It lay still, breathing in shallow, frantic gasps. Maren’s hands moved faster. She cleared the nostrils, rolled it onto its side, pressed its back against her thigh to warm it, rubbed it hard enough to raise the skin. Leo held the towel, watching the way her jaw worked, the way her brow furrowed, the way her hair had come loose from its knot and stuck to her temple with sweat. The lamb’s legs twitched. Its mouth opened. A sound came out, thin and reedy, then stronger. Maren exhaled, a long, slow release, and let her hands rest for a moment on the ewe’s shoulder. The mother licked, then lifted her head, her eyes finding Leo’s beam, then his face. He nodded. She nodded back, a slight, almost imperceptible dip of her chin. No words. There never were, when the work demanded it.

They worked in shifts, but not by clock. By need. Leo would take the north pens while Maren managed the south, then they would swap when the ewes grew restless, when the floor needed mucking, when the kerosene supply grew low. The storm kept pace with their labor. It pressed harder as the night deepened, rattling the metal siding, throwing against the doors with impacts that sounded like falling trees. The temperature inside the shed dropped anyway. They could see their breath in the lamp beams, thin white plumes that curled and vanished. They wore their thickest layers, the flannel shirts layered under the canvas work coats, the wool socks pulled over their ankles, the scarves wound tight around their necks. Maren’s gloves were worn through at the palms, the leather cracked and patched with tape. Leo’s were new, stiff with factory finish, but he took them off when the work demanded it, his fingers going numb in the cold, then pink, then red, then numb again. He didn’t complain. He had learned that complaining was a luxury that belonged to a different life, to a different season, to a different set of hands that would never be called to do this.

At one point, after delivering a triplet that clung to its mother’s teats with desperate suction, Leo stepped out to check the generator. The wind hit him like a wall, stealing his breath, filling his collar with snow that melted instantly against his skin. The shed door groaned shut behind him, sealing out the light, sealing in the dark. He knelt by the machine, its housing caked with ice, its exhaust pipe choked with drifts. He cleared the snow with his hands, feeling the metal bite into his palms, the cold leaching into his bones. He cranked the starter. It caught, then died. He wiped the spark plug, checked the line, cranked again. It sputtered, coughed, caught. The light inside the shed flickered, then died again as the wind tore a branch across the power line somewhere beyond the fence. The hum returned for three seconds, then vanished. Maren’s voice cut through the storm, sharp and clear, calling his name. He turned back to the shed, the door swinging open in his face, the wind howling as he stumbled across the threshold, the warmth of it hitting him like a physical thing. She was already on her knees again, her headlamp casting a bright circle on the straw. Another ewe. This one was struggling. Leo wiped his face with his sleeve, kicked off his boots, stepped into the pen.

The ewe’s labor was failing. Her contractions were shallow, her breathing rapid and panicked, her eyes wide with a terror that transcended species. The lamb was stuck, its head and one foreleg visible, the other pulled tight against the body. Maren’s hands were inside, her arms braced against the ewe’s rump, her knuckles white, her breath coming in short, controlled bursts. Leo didn’t ask. He stepped behind her, placed his hands over hers, felt the tremor in her arms, the tension in her shoulders. He didn’t try to take over. He added his strength, his weight, his stillness to hers. Together, they guided the leg, eased the shoulder, felt the resistance give. The lamb slid free, heavier now, its body slick and dark, its mouth already working at the air. Maren pulled back, her hands shaking, her forehead pressed against the ewe’s flank for a moment. Leo rubbed the lamb, turned it, supported the head, watched the color return to its legs, the first true cry that filled the small space. The ewe broke, her legs buckling, her head dropping into the straw. Maren sank back onto her heels, her breath coming hard, her eyes closed. Leo handed her a bottle of warm water. She drank, then handed it back. Their fingers brushed. Neither pulled away.

They didn’t talk about him. Not then. Not ever, not directly. Grief on a farm like this was not spoken in sentences. It was in the way Maren left his side of the workbench clear, not out of sentiment but out of habit, until the space filled with her tools and her papers, and she didn’t notice. It was in the way Leo reached for the heavy wrench when lifting the feed bins, then stopped himself, remembering the reach, the angle, the easy lift that had been his father’s domain. It was in the way they both knew which ewe had been pregnant when he died, how she had been tended, how she had dropped her lamb on a Tuesday in March, how the lamb had lived while he hadn’t, how the irony sat in their mouths like bitter grain. It was in the storm. The blizzard was late, unseasonal, violent, a thing that should not be. Like his absence. Like the way the calendar kept turning while the world remained stuck in the gap. They worked through it. They worked because the work was the only thing that asked nothing of them but obedience. They worked because the ewes would not wait. They worked because the lambs needed them, because the cold would take them otherwise, because the alternative was sitting in the kitchen with the radio on and the silence pressing against their eardrums until it became unbearable.

By morning, the wind had not lessened. The snow had risen to the windowsill of the shed, burying the lower half of the door, piling against the walls in hard, wind-packed ridges. They had not slept. They had drunk coffee from a thermos, eaten hardtack and cheese, shifted lambs to foster mothers, dried out wet newborns under heat lamps that now ran on the generator’s intermittent bursts, mucked straw, refilled water troughs, watched the ewes rest, watched the lambs nurse, watched the light change from black to gray to the pale, directionless white of a storm that had swallowed the sky. They had delivered seven lambs in the night. Two had not survived. They had wrapped them in towels, laid them in the feed room, would bury them when the wind allowed. Maren had not cried. Leo had not cried. They had simply done what needed doing, and the doing had become a kind of prayer, a repetitive, physical liturgy that held the chaos at bay.

The power stayed dead. The generator ran on fumes now, coughing, sputtering, demanding attention. They took turns watching it, shoveling snow from the air intake, checking the line, cranking it back to life when it died. Each time it failed, the shed grew darker, colder, more confined. Each time it returned, the lamps brightened, the air felt marginally less hostile, the work felt marginally less impossible. Maren moved through the shed like a ghost who had forgotten she was dead, efficient, quiet, her movements precise, her voice low when she spoke. Leo mirrored her, his body learning the rhythms, his hands learning the language of muscle and bone and fluid, his attention narrowing to the task at hand. They were not alone in the shed, but they were isolated by the storm, by the work, by the unspoken weight that hung between them like a third presence. It was not heavy. It was just there, a fact, like the cold, like the snow, like the ewes breathing in the dark.

Around noon, or what passed for noon in the whiteout, a new ewe went down. She was a late grouse, bred late, carried late, dropped late. She had been pacing, restless, her breathing shallow, her eyes glassy. She lay on her side, her legs kicking weakly, her udder swollen, her belly taut. Maren knelt beside her, her hands pressing gently against the flank, feeling the hard ball of the lamb’s head push forward, then back, then forward again. It was not progressing. The lamb was large, the ewe had been exhausted by days of poor feeding, by the storm’s toll, by her own failing strength. Maren’s voice was calm. Leo’s was steady. They worked in silence, their headlamps overlapping on the straw, their breath mingling in the cold air, their hands moving in sync, their shoulders brushing, their knees pressing into the damp floor. When the lamb finally emerged, it was born still. Maren’s hands froze. Leo’s breath caught. For a moment, the only sound was the wind, the wind grinding against the roof, the wind carrying the snow like ground glass against the walls. Then Maren began to rub. Fast, hard, relentless. Leo held the lamb’s head, cleared the nostrils, pressed its back against his chest to warm it, whispered nothing, because there was nothing to say. The lamb did not move. The ewe stirred, her legs twitching, her head lifting, her nose pushing against the lamb’s flank. Maren’s hands did not stop. She worked the air into the lungs, she rotated the body, she pressed, she rubbed, she begged the silence to break. Leo’s throat tightened. He looked at Maren’s face, at the set of her jaw, at the way her eyes were fixed on the lamb, at the way her shoulders shook, not with sobs but with the sheer physical effort of refusing to let go. The wind howled. The storm pressed in. The lamb’s chest gave a single, shallow rise. Then another. Then a shudder. Then a cry, thin and broken, then stronger, then full. The ewe collapsed with a sound that was almost a sob. Maren’s hands fell. She sat back on her heels, her forehead resting against her knees, her breath coming in ragged gasps. Leo put a hand on her shoulder. She leaned into it, just for a second, then pulled away, wiped her face with the back of her glove, stood up, her joints popping, her boots finding the straw, her voice steady when she spoke.

“Wrap it,” she said. “Keep it warm. Let her nurse. We’ll move it to the heat lamp when the generator runs.”

Leo nodded. He wrapped the lamb in three towels, held it to his chest, felt the rapid thump of its heart against his ribs, felt its weight, felt its life. He carried it to the corner where the kerosene lamp burned steady, where the heat lamp glowed orange, where the air was marginally less hostile. He laid it down. It stirred. It found its mother’s scent. It began to nurse. Maren watched. She did not smile. But her shoulders lowered. Her breath evened. The shed held them. The storm raged. The lambs lived.

They had been doing this for days. The storm did not end. The power did not return. The ewes kept coming. They came because they were bred, because their bodies knew the turning of the season, because the instinct that had driven them for millennia did not recognize the calendar, did not care about the wind or the cold or the absence of men with wrenches and strong backs. They came because life demands repetition, because birth is not a single event but a river, because the ground will not stop asking even when the sky breaks. They worked. They rubbed. They turned. They warmed. They watched. They fed. They buried the ones that did not make it. They celebrated the ones that did, not with words but with quiet nods, with shared glances, with the passing of a warm bottle, with the clearing of a straw pile, with the resetting of a headlamp, with the refueling of a generator, with the shoveling of a drift, with the closing of a door against the wind.

At dusk, or what passed for dusk in the whiteout, they sat on an upturned feed bin in the corner of the shed, their backs against the wall, their knees drawn up, their thermos between them. The lambing had slowed. The ewes rested. The lambs slept in piles of straw, breathing in unison, their flanks rising and falling. The generator purred steadily now, fueled by a fresh tank Leo had dragged in from the shed out back, his arms burning, his breath pluming in the cold, his boots leaving prints that the snow would fill by morning. The storm had not lessened, but it had changed. It no longer pressed in with the same fury. It whispered now, a long, steady sigh that sounded almost like breathing. Maren opened the thermos. She poured coffee into the tin cup. She handed it to Leo. He drank. It was bitter, strong, hot. He handed it back. She drank. She set the cup down. She looked at her hands. They were swollen, cracked, stained, trembling slightly. She flexed her fingers. They obeyed. She looked at Leo. He looked at her. There was no need to speak. The shed was full of the things that could not be said. The lambs breathing. The ewes sleeping. The storm outside. The generator inside. The heat lamp glowing. The straw on the floor. The book on the workbench, its pages still fogged, its margins still filled with their script. The empty hook by the door, now holding a rope, a towel, a pair of gloves. The weight in the room, not as a burden but as a foundation.

She reached out, not to touch him, but to adjust the strap of his headlamp, to tighten a loose buckle, to correct a angle that had shifted in the work. Her fingers brushed his jaw. He did not flinch. He did not pull away. He sat still, let her do it, felt the cold metal of the buckle, the warmth of her gloves, the quiet certainty of her touch. When she finished, she leaned back against the wall, closed her eyes, let her head fall forward just slightly, let her breath slow. He watched the steam rise from the coffee, watched the dust swirl in the lamp light, watched the snow drift against the wall in slow, heavy sheets. He thought of his father’s hands, not as a wound but as a map, as a set of directions that had been passed down, not in words but in calluses, in routines, in the way a wrench was held, in the way a lamb was turned, in the way a storm was met without flinching. He thought of the ewes coming, the lambs arriving, the work continuing, the days stacking like straw, like snow, like years. He thought of the future, not as a destination but as a season, as a cycle, as something to be worked through, one contraction at a time, one breath at a time, one lamb at a time.

The storm would not break tonight. The power would not return. The ewes would keep coming. They would keep working. They would keep breathing. They would keep standing in the shed, in the dark, in the cold, in the quiet, in the weight, in the work, in the life that refused to stop, that demanded their hands, their strength, their patience, their grief, their endurance, that asked nothing but presence, that gave nothing but continuation, that moved through them like the wind through the eaves, like the snow against the glass, like the blood in their veins, like the breath in their lungs, like the lamb’s first cry in the dark, like the ewe’s sigh of relief, like the quiet between the contractions, like the steady hum of the generator, like the unbroken line from then to now to always.

Maren opened her eyes. She looked at the lambs. She looked at the ewes. She looked at the door. She looked at Leo. She nodded. He nodded back. She stood. He stood. They walked toward the pens. They adjusted their headlamps. They stepped into the straw. The storm continued. The work continued. The lambs continued to breathe. The sheep continued to come. And they continued to meet them, hands ready, breath steady, hearts heavy but open, standing in the shed, in the dark, in the cold, in the quiet, in the weight, in the life, in the work, in the season, in the storm, in the aftermath, in the beginning, in the middle, in the always, in the never-ending, in the unbroken, in the true, in the real, in the necessary, in the good, in the hard, in the right, in the only thing left to do, in the only thing ever to do, in the only thing worth doing, in the only thing that matters, in the only thing that remains, in the only thing that is, in the only thing that is, in the only thing that is.
