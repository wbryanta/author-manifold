The strait did not break but unspooled, a dark ribbon drawn between hills that held their breath in the low summer light. The ferry cut through it with a sound like cloth being pulled slowly over stone, a steady friction that vibrated through the soles of his shoes, up through the steel floor, into the bones of his ankles. He stood on the upper deck, hands in the pockets of his coat, watching the water separate and close behind them. The wake was already fading, leaving only a long pale scar that the wind erased before he could decide whether it had been there at all. Cold air moved across the deck, carrying the taste of iron and kelp and something older, the damp exhale of rock worn smooth by centuries of tides. He kept his collar turned up, though the cold did not bite so much as settle, a familiar weight against his ribs. He had not slept since the call came, since the voice on the telephone had spoken the words with the flat, careful tone of someone reading a form, and then said: *It may be him. It may not be.* He had nodded, though no one was there to see it, and packed a single bag with nothing that felt essential. Now the ship rolled gently, lifting him an inch, setting him down, lifting him again, as if testing the balance of his body against the water beneath.

Inside, the restaurant was empty except for three couples and a man reading a newspaper with the top folded down. The fluorescent lights hummed at a frequency just below hearing, casting everything in a pale, slightly greenish wash. The windows were black mirrors now, showing him only his own reflection: the line of his jaw, the shadow under his eyes, the way his shoulders slanted forward as though carrying a load he had not yet set down. He walked to the bar. The bartender was polishing a glass with a circular motion, his eyes on the rag, not on the room. *Coffee,* he said. *Black.* The man nodded, set the cup down without looking up, and moved to the sink. He took the cup to a table near the window. The ceramic was warm against his palms, the steam rising in a thin, wavering line that caught the light like a thread. He did not drink it immediately. He watched the glass. He had not seen his brother in fourteen years, not since the argument on the porch, not since the silence that followed and did not end. Or did not end until it did, without ceremony, without a door closing, without a word that could be returned to the table and set back in its place. He had thought he would feel nothing. He had thought the estrangement would leave a clean space, like a room cleared of furniture, ready for whatever would be brought in next. Instead, the space had filled with water, slowly, without sound, until it reached the throat.

A woman in a yellow raincoat sat across from him, unzipping her bag with careful fingers. She pulled out a sketchbook, a charcoal stick, and began to draw the curve of the window frame. Her hand moved without hesitation, the stick scratching softly against the page. He watched the line emerge, then fade, then return. He looked out at the strait. The water was darker now, the sky pressing down from all sides, the hills reducing to a single shadow along the horizon. The ship’s engine thrummed through the floorboards, a constant pulse that matched the beat in his wrists. He thought of the brother’s hands. Long fingers, always stained with ink or soil or something he never named. They could hold a book open without bending the spine, could turn a key with a quarter twist, could grip the edge of a table until the knuckles went white and the tendons stood out like wires. He remembered the way those hands used to rest on his own shoulders, heavy and certain, as if anchoring him to the ground. Then the hands had stopped reaching. The distance between them had become a geography of its own, mapped in glances, in missed calls, in the slow accumulation of unsaid things that hardened into habit.

He finished the coffee. It had gone cold, the surface dull, a thin film of oil at the center. He carried the cup to the sink, set it down, watched the water from the faucet hit the ceramic with a quiet splash. A crewman in blue overalls walked past, carrying a stack of folded blankets. His boots made no sound on the deck. The man turned down the corridor toward the lobby. There were more people here, a few sleeping in the armchairs, one woman staring at her phone without scrolling, a young man tracing the condensation on a glass with his fingertip. The light shifted slightly, dimming as the ship moved into a deeper channel. He sat. He closed his eyes. He did not sleep. He let the darkness move through him, let it settle in the hollows of his face, the curve of his back, the spaces between his fingers. When he opened them, the deck was full of rain, fine and slanting, catching in the wind to form a mist that clung to the railings and dripped from the awnings in steady drops. The water outside was no longer visible, only the blackness and the white of the spray against the glass.

He walked to the lower deck, down the narrow stairwell, the handrail cool and smooth under his palm. The air here was heavier, warmer, smelling of diesel and damp wool and the faint sweetness of spilled fruit. A woman was sweeping the floor, the broom moving in long, even strokes, gathering dust and cigarette ash and the small debris of transit into a neat pile. She did not look up. He passed through into the garage level. The space was vast, empty save for a few cars parked in orderly rows, their windshields fogged, their hoods reflecting the low ceiling lights. The air vibrated with the ship’s motion, a deep, resonant hum that made his teeth ache slightly. He walked to the edge, looked over the rail at the water below, churning against the hull, breaking into white foam that vanished as quickly as it formed. He thought of the body. The words on the phone had been careful, measured, leaving room for the possibility of mistake, for the chance that the name would be called and the answer would come back wrong. He had imagined the scene: a room of concrete and steel, a steel table, a sheet pulled back just enough to reveal a face, a hand, a mark. He had imagined turning away. He had imagined not turning away. Both possibilities had felt equally certain.

He returned upstairs. The rain had eased to a drizzle. The sky had lightened at the edges, a pale gray bleeding into the black, the first hint of dawn pulling at the horizon. The woman with the sketchbook was gone. The newspaper man had folded his paper and was watching the water. The bartender was restocking, lining bottles by size, wiping the counter with a cloth that came away gray. He walked to the bow. The wind was stronger here, pulling at his coat, tugging at his hair. The ship pitched forward, lifting him, setting him down, lifting him again. He gripped the rail. The metal was cold, rough with pitting, salt crystallizing in the grooves. He looked out. The strait was narrowing, the hills rising on either side, cloaked in fog that moved like a slow tide. A bird cried, sharp and sudden, then fell silent. He thought of the brother’s voice. It had always been low, careful, as though speaking too loudly might shatter something fragile. They had argued about money, about land, about a woman neither of them had loved, about the way their father had looked at the elder son with something like relief and the younger son with something like pity. They had not spoken the real things. The real things had lived in the spaces between words, in the pauses, in the way they avoided each other’s eyes, in the way the brother had once reached out and then pulled back, as if remembering a rule neither of them had agreed to but both had known.

The ship began to slow. The vibration changed, deepening, settling into a lower frequency. The wake grew calmer. Land appeared through the fog, first a line of trees, then the suggestion of buildings, then the dark shapes of cranes and warehouses along the shore. The man stood straighter. His hands unclenched from the rail. He walked back inside. The lobby was filling. People were gathering coats, checking watches, speaking in low voices that blended with the hum of the engines. He stood near the exit, watching the doors. A uniformed officer approached, holding a clipboard. *Mr. Vance?* he said. The man nodded. *This way, please.* He followed. The corridor was long, the floor tiled, the walls painted a pale blue that had yellowed at the seams. The air was colder here, carrying the sharp, clean scent of antiseptic and metal. They passed a set of double doors. Through the glass, he could see a room, sterile and bright, a table in the center, a chair against the wall, a sink in the corner. The officer opened the door. The man stepped inside. The door clicked shut behind him. The silence was absolute.

The room was exactly as he had imagined it and nothing like it. The light was too bright, casting everything in a flat, shadowless glare. The table was stainless steel, smooth and unmarked, reflecting the ceiling lights in a pale ellipse. The sheet was folded at the foot, the edges precise. The officer stood near the door, his posture relaxed, his eyes on the man’s face, waiting. The man walked forward. His shoes made a soft sound on the floor. He reached the table. He pulled back the sheet just enough to reveal the face. The features were familiar and not. The jaw was the same, the line of the cheekbone, the set of the brow. The eyes were closed. The skin was pale, waxy, the lips slightly parted, the mouth relaxed into a shape that belonged to neither anger nor sorrow but to something older, something that had long ago left the surface. He reached out. His fingers touched the man’s forehead. The skin was cool, smooth, slightly firm. He traced the line of the brow, the curve of the temple, the hollow beside the eye. He looked closer. The nose had been broken once, he remembered, in a fall from a ladder, the bridge crooked but not broken, healing into a shape that had become part of the face. He looked at the hands, resting at the sides, the fingers long, the nails clean, the skin marked with faint white lines across the knuckles, old scars from glass, from wire, from work that had been done without complaint. He looked at the chest, rising and falling, slowly, steadily. The heart was beating.

He stepped back. The officer shifted his weight. *He’s alive,* the man said. His voice was quiet, almost lost in the hum of the ventilation. The officer nodded. *Washed ashore near the eastern cove. No identification. He’s in stable condition. The hospital will process him tomorrow. You can wait outside if you like.* The man turned. He walked to the door. He opened it. The corridor was empty. He walked back through the lobby, past the waiting passengers, out into the morning. The air was clear, the fog burned away by a pale sun that hung low over the water. The strait lay ahead, calm and wide, the surface reflecting the sky in long, shimmering lines. He sat on a bench near the dock. He watched the ships come and go, the ferries cutting through the water, the small boats moving slower, the cranes lifting containers with a grace that seemed almost gentle. He thought of the brother breathing. He thought of the breath he had not taken for fourteen years. He thought of the space between them, how it had grown not from distance but from the weight of things left unsaid, how it had become a room he had refused to enter, how now it had opened without a key. He closed his eyes. He did not sleep. He let the light move across his face, let the sound of the water fill the spaces between his thoughts, let the certainty of breath replace the certainty of absence. When he opened them, the sun was higher, the water was gold, and the strait did not break but unspooled, a dark ribbon drawn between hills that held their breath in the quiet morning. He stood. He walked to the ferry. He boarded. He sat near the window. He watched the land recede. He did not know what he would say when he saw him again. He knew he would not need to. He knew the space between them had already filled with water, and he had already learned how to breathe in it.
