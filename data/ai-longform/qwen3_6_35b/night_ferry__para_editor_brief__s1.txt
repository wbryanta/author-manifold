The strait did not divide the world so much as it suspended it in a state of permanent holding. To the west, the mainland was a smudge of charcoal against a sky the color of wet slate; to the east, the island was nothing but the roar of water and the occasional flash of a lighthouse cutting the dark like a needle. Ewan sat by the porthole of his cabin, the glass frosted with a lacework of salt and condensation that mirrored the veins in his own hands. The ferry shuddered beneath him, a deep, rhythmic thrumming that traveled up through the soles of his boots and settled in his teeth. It was the sound of the engines fighting the tide, the sound of progress made by force.

He checked his phone again. No signal. The device was a black mirror reflecting the dim amber of the cabin light, his own face looking back at him with a hollowed-out quality that belonged to someone he barely recognized. Three days ago, the phone had rung at 4:00 a.m. The voice on the other end had been bureaucratic, stripped of inflection, reciting coordinates and a name that tasted like ash in his mouth. *We have an unclaimed male. Approximately forty years. Found near the drift line at Blackcrag. The strait claims what it can. We need identification.*

Forty years. He was forty-two. The gap was wide enough to be plausible, narrow enough to be a knife.

Ewan rubbed his face, his fingers catching on the stubble that had grown in unevenly over the last two days. He hadn't slept in his own bed since the call. He had thrown a bag into the back of the car, driven north until the roads narrowed and the signs warned of ferries, and then he had walked the deck of the vessel until his lungs burned with the brine air. Now, in the cabin, he felt the sway of the ship as a physical pull, a seduction to lie down and let the dark water take him, to become part of the suspension rather than the observer.

He closed his eyes and the memory came, unbidden and sharp, cutting through the diesel smell of the upholstery. They were twelve and thirteen. The summer the river had flooded the lower meadows, and the mud had sucked the boots off their feet. Liam had laughed, a sound like stones tumbling in a dry creek bed, and grabbed Ewan's wrist to haul him out of the muck. Ewan remembered the grip, the calluses on Liam's palm, the frantic energy of him, the way his brother was all motion and heat, constantly turning away from stillness. *Don't let go,* Liam had shouted over the roar of the water. *You let go, the current takes you.*

Ewan had held on. They had made it to the bank. But later, in the car, with the mud drying into cracks on their skin, Liam had said, *You're always watching, Ewan. You're never in it.* It was the seed. The first crack in the dam. Over the years, the watching had become the default, and the doing had become the stranger's domain. Liam moved to the islands for fishing work, then to the north for construction, then to places without maps. The letters stopped coming first, then the calls, then the silence. The silence had become a third person in the house, sitting in the armchair opposite Ewan, breathing slowly, waiting for one of them to speak. But neither did. Pride was a cold stone they both swallowed, and it sat heavy in the gut, calcifying over time.

The ferry banked hard, throwing Ewan against the bulkhead. He gripped the edge of the table, knuckles whitening. The strait was rough tonight. The captain must be pushing to make the window. He looked down at his palm. In the dim light, the skin looked thin, translucent. He thought of Liam's hands. The scar running from the knuckle to the wrist, a burn from a pipe in the garage when they were eighteen. The missing tip of the left ring finger, lost to a hydraulic press on a rig in '08. These were the facts. The anchors. If the body was him, these would be there. If they weren't, then Liam was still out there, drifting in some other stretch of water, or the name on the paper was a mistake, and Ewan had come here for nothing, only to face the possibility that the silence was permanent and absolute.

The doubt was a parasite. It whispered that it might be better if it weren't him. If it were just a man who looked enough like Liam in death to fool the clerk, then the story would end with a signature, a nod, and a return to the mainland. The estrangement would remain intact, a closed loop. But if it were him, the loop would break. The silence would be answered by the final, irrevocable noise of death. Ewan feared the identification not because he was afraid of seeing a dead man, but because he was afraid of the relief that might follow, or the guilt that might never leave. He was afraid of the moment when the stranger became his brother again, and the brother became a ghost he could no longer outrun.

Hours later, the sky began to lighten, turning the black water to a bruised purple. The ferry slowed, the engines groaning in protest as they approached the slipway. The sound changed from a deep thrum to the metallic clanking of machinery, the scream of winches, the heavy thud of anchor chains. Ewan stood up, his legs stiff. He gathered his coat, his hands trembling slightly. He hadn't touched the coffee in the cabin's little tray; the cup sat there, the liquid cold and skin-forming on top, a monument to neglect.

He stepped out onto the deck. The air was colder, biting at his cheeks. The island rose up ahead, dark and jagged, wreathed in mist. Other passengers were trickling onto the deck, stretching, checking watches, huddling in groups with the wary politeness of people who share a journey but nothing else. Ewan stood apart, looking toward the gangway where the red lights of the terminal pulsed in the gloom. A steward passed by, collecting empty cups, and glanced at Ewan.

"First time to the island, sir?"

"No," Ewan said. His voice sounded raspy, unused.

"Coming back soon, then?"

"I don't know," Ewan said.

The steward nodded, a noncommittal dip of the head, and moved on. Ewan wanted to grab the man by the lapels and ask if he knew, if he felt the weight of what he carried, but he let him go. The stranger knew nothing. He saw a man in a worn coat with a tired face, just another node in the flow of commerce and travel. He didn't see the thread stretched tight between the car in the parking lot and the cold storage unit inland.

The ferry ground to a halt. The groan of metal on metal vibrated through the soles of Ewan's feet. The gangway lowered with a hydraulic hiss, and the doors to the terminal slid open. A wave of warm, stale air washed over the deck, smelling of diesel exhaust, frying grease, and damp wool. Ewan joined the stream of people moving toward the exit. He kept his head down, focusing on the rhythm of walking. *Left, right. Left, right.* If he stopped, he would collapse.

The terminal was a cavern of concrete and corrugated steel, lit by harsh fluorescent tubes that hummed with a frequency that set his teeth on edge. The air was thick with the noise of conversation, shouting men, the squeak of brake pads, the distant wail of a siren. Ewan moved through it all as if underwater, the sounds muffled and distant. He found a payphone on the wall, its cord coiled like a sleeping snake. He didn't have coins; the clerk behind the glass had pointed him to the clinic on the way out, given him a slip of paper with a name and a number. The paper was in his pocket, folded into a small square that felt as thick as stone.

He walked out into the car park. The rain had started, a fine, needle-sharp drizzle that soaked through his coat instantly. The mainland was a smear of grey in the distance, the strait now a churning grey expanse reflecting the low clouds. The water looked indifferent. It didn't care about brothers or silences or the weight of the past. It just took, and gave back what it was given.

The clinic was a low building painted white, the paint peeling in long curls to reveal the rotting wood beneath. It sat on a hill overlooking the harbor, a solitary structure that seemed to resist the wind. Ewan parked the car in a lot marked *Staff Only*, though no one questioned him. He walked up the ramp to the entrance, his breath pluming in the air. The door was heavy glass, and inside he saw a reception desk and a corridor leading back. He pushed the door and walked in.

The receptionist was a woman with grey hair pulled back in a severe bun and glasses perched on the end of her nose. She looked up as he approached, her expression shifting from boredom to professional alertness.

"Can I help you?"

"I'm here about the intake," Ewan said. He placed the slip of paper on the counter. "From the ferry. Last night."

She took the paper, her eyes scanning it. "Number four. You'll have to sign in. And wait in the viewing room. The orderly will be with you in a moment." She slid a clipboard across the counter. "Name?"

"Ewan."

"Surname?"

He hesitated. Why did he have to say it? As if the name connected him to the dead, as if it absolved or condemned him. "Vance. Ewan Vance."

She typed it in, the keys clacking loudly. "Vance. Right. Take a seat, Mr. Vance. The doctor is reviewing the files now. There might be a wait."

Ewan sat. The waiting room was furnished with plastic chairs bolted to the floor, their surfaces scratched and worn. A magazine rack held copies of local papers and medical journals. The walls were the color of old teeth. He stared at the door marked *Restricted*, the sound of a radio murmuring behind it. He thought of the house he had left. It felt a thousand miles away, though it was only an hour's drive. The rooms there would be empty, the furniture covered in sheets, the dust settling on the places where Liam's things used to be. Or maybe he had cleaned them out. Maybe he had sold the workshop. Maybe he had let the garden go to seed. Ewan couldn't remember. The estrangement had erased the routine, the daily erosion of the mind that kept the past accessible. Now, the past was a locked room, and he held the key, but he wasn't sure he wanted to open it.

An hour passed. Then two. The rain intensified, lashing against the windows. Ewan stood up and walked to the window. The harbor was a grid of lights bobbing in the dark water. A fishing boat rocked violently, its crane swaying. He watched it for a long time, wondering what it was like to be out there, alone in the dark, holding onto something while the world tried to take it away.

Finally, the door to the back corridor opened. A man in scrubs poked his head out. "Mr. Vance?"

Ewan followed him down the corridor. The air grew colder, the smell of antiseptic sharp and clean. The floor changed from linoleum to polished concrete. The orderly, a large man with a gentle face and hands that moved with practiced efficiency, gestured to a heavy steel door.

"This is the cold room," he said. "We've got the body on the slab. Dr. Aris is in the examining room. She'll go over the findings, and if you're ready, we can proceed with the identification. Take your time. There's no rush."

Ewan nodded, his throat tight. He walked into the examining room. It was small, tiled, with stainless steel tables and cabinets. A body lay on a central slab, covered by a white sheet. The doctor, a woman with tired eyes and a kind mouth, stood by a counter, reviewing a chart. She looked up as Ewan entered.

"Mr. Vance," she said. "I'm Dr. Aris. I've reviewed the preliminary notes. The man was found by a coast guard patrol. No ID, no wallet. He was in the water for some time. The strait preserves in its own way. You have a photo?"

Ewan pulled a wallet from his pocket and produced a picture. It was taken years ago, a candid shot of Liam laughing, his head thrown back, sunlight catching the grime on his neck. He looked alive. He looked like he was about to tell a joke, to grab Ewan by the shoulder and pull him into the moment. Ewan's hand shook as he handed it over.

Dr. Aris took it. She studied the photo, then the body. "I will show you. But I warn you, Mr. Vance, the strait is unkind to the face. The water breaks down the features quickly. You may have to rely on other markers."

Ewan stepped forward. The cold from the slab radiated up through his legs. He looked at the covered form. It was the size of Liam, the build similar. The sheet was pulled back to the waist, revealing legs clad in torn trousers and boots caked in grey mud. The feet were swollen, the skin macerated. Ewan looked away, his stomach turning. He couldn't. Not yet.

"May I?" Dr. Aris asked softly.

Ewan nodded.

She reached down and lifted the sheet, starting from the chest. She pulled it up slowly, revealing the torso. The skin was pale, waxy, marred by abrasions and the dark bloom of bruises. Ribs visible beneath the surface. The water had done its work. Ewan felt a wave of nausea. He gripped the edge of the table to steady himself.

"Look at the hands," Dr. Aris said.

Ewan forced his eyes to move. He focused on the hands lying by the side of the slab. They were swollen, the fingers curled inward like claws. The skin was white and shriveled. He counted the fingers on the right hand. Five. He moved to the left. Five. But the tips were different. He leaned closer, squinting. The left ring finger... the tip was gone, just a smooth scar tissue. And there, on the back of the right hand, a jagged line of scar tissue, white against the pale flesh, curving from the knuckle toward the wrist.

Ewan's breath hitched. The world narrowed to that scar. The burn from the pipe. The year the garage caught fire and they pulled the welding mask off his hand, screaming. Liam had held Ewan's hand through the night in the hospital, his own hand steady, his voice low and steady, telling Ewan it was okay, that it was just a hand, not his life.

"Does that match?" Dr. Aris asked.

Ewan couldn't speak. He nodded, a jerky movement. "The scar. And the finger. He lost the finger in '08. On the rig."

Dr. Aris made a note. "There is another thing. On the left shoulder, a tattoo. A compass rose, black ink. It's faded, but the shape is distinct. We didn't get to examine it yet due to the condition of the skin, but if you recall..."

"The compass," Ewan whispered. "He had a compass tattooed on his shoulder. He said it was to remind him of the north. That he always needed to know where he was going, even if he didn't want to go back."

He looked at the body again. The face was still covered, but he didn't need to see the face. The body was speaking. The scars were a language he had forgotten how to read, and now the words were flooding back. The tension in his shoulders dropped. The stone in his gut shifted, not vanishing, but changing shape. It was no longer the stone of doubt; it was the stone of reality. It was heavy, immovable, final.

"He's Liam," Ewan said. The name came out as a rasp, foreign and familiar all at once. "He's my brother."

Dr. Aris covered the sheet back down. "I'm sorry, Mr. Vance. There's nothing more to say about the identity. I can provide the paperwork for the coroner. You can arrange for repatriation if you wish, or local burial. We have a form for next of kin."

Ewan stood there for a long moment. The doctor waited, her hand resting on the chart, giving him space. The silence in the room was different from the silence in the house. This silence was full. It held the weight of the body, the history, the strait, the years of silence that had led to this moment. It wasn't an absence; it was a presence.

Ewan looked at the covered form. He wanted to reach out. He wanted to touch the scar on the hand, to confirm the touch, to bridge the gap one last time. But he knew that touching would be a violation, or perhaps a intimacy he wasn't ready for. He stood back.

"Repatriation," he said. His voice was steadier now. "Take him home. To the plot by the river. The one he bought for both of us."

Dr. Aris nodded. "We can coordinate that. Is there anything else? Food? A place to sit?"

Ewan shook his head. "No. I just... I need to go to the car."

He turned and walked out of the room. The orderly met him at the door. "You handled that well, sir."

Ewan looked at the man. "How many do you bring in like this?"

"Enough," the orderly said. "Every strait has its toll. You want to sign out?"

"Yes."

Ewan signed the log. He walked back through the corridor, past the cold room, up the ramp to the reception. He took the slip of paper from the clerk, his signature scratching loudly on the line. He didn't look at her face. He walked out into the rain.

The rain had stopped, leaving the world washed and gleaming. The sky was beginning to clear, the clouds breaking to reveal patches of blue high above. The wind was still biting, but it felt clean. Ewan walked to his car. He sat in the driver's seat for a long time, his hands on the wheel. He looked at the slip of paper in his hand. It was just a form. It meant nothing. He crumpled it and tossed it on the passenger seat.

He started the engine. The heater blasted warm air, fogging the windows. He sat there, listening to the idle of the motor, feeling the vibration of the car, the solidity of the seat, the reality of the machine around him. He had found him. The search was over. The silence was broken. But as he put the car in gear, he realized that the silence wasn't gone. It was just different. It was the silence of a shared ending, a story that had finally closed its cover. There would be grief, he knew. There would be anger at the years lost, at the pride that had been a wall. But there would also be the image of the scar, the hand that had held him when they were boys, the laugh that sounded like stones in a creek.

Ewan drove down the hill toward the slipway. The strait lay ahead, grey and endless. He would cross back. He would have to. But for the first time in twenty years, he didn't feel the urge to run. He felt the weight of the brother in the cold room, a weight that anchored him. He watched the water churn in the wake of the ferries, the white foam breaking and disappearing, replaced by the dark flow behind. The current took what it could, but it also brought things back. Or it gave them to those who had the courage to wait.

He drove to the terminal, parked, and walked out to the railing. The ferry was boarding, the gangway lowered, the red lights pulsing. He watched the first cars roll on. He waited until the boat was full, until the doors were sealed. He didn't get on. He stood there, hands in his pockets, watching the vessel push off from the dock. The propeller churned the water, sending out a spray of mist that caught the light. The boat moved away, growing smaller, the wake widening and fading.

Ewan stayed until the strait was empty, until only the water and the sky remained. The cold seeped through his coat, but he didn't move. He felt Liam's hand in his memory, callused and warm. *You let go, the current takes you.*

Ewan tightened his grip on the railing. He hadn't let go. He had held on, through the years, through the silence, through the doubt. And now, holding on was enough. He turned and walked back to the car. The drive home would be long, and the house would be empty, and the dust would settle on the armchair where the third person had sat. But Ewan would drive. He would get to the river. He would dig the hole. And he would sing, if he had to, or just stand in the quiet and let the stones fall.

He got into the car and started the engine. The heater hummed. He pulled out onto the causeway, the tires crunching on the gravel, the sound steady and sure. The strait fell behind him, the water dark and indifferent, already claiming the next passenger, the next story. Ewan didn't look back. He drove toward the mainland, toward the road, toward the silence that was no longer a wall, but a bridge. The sun broke through the clouds for a moment, illuminating the wet road ahead, the way stretching out, grey and glistening, leading home. He accelerated, the car surging forward, crossing the divide between the water and the land, between the past and the rest of the life, carrying the weight, steady and unbreaking, until the road dissolved into the horizon and the world opened up around him.
