The wind off the strait carried the particular grit of thawing ice and distant diesel, the kind of cold that settles in the hollows of the collarbone and refuses to be shaken. I held the ferry ticket between two fingers as if it might dissolve, the paper already softening from the damp, the ink bleeding at the edges where the condensation gathered. It was a single crossing, one way to the mainland port, and then another ticket would be bought, one way to the coast guard station, one way to the cold room. I had not planned the sequence in advance; it had simply arranged itself in the days since the phone call, since the voice on the other end spoke of a John Doe, of recovered garments that might match a description, of a strait too treacherous in the grey season, of a body that could be anyone and therefore might be him. I stood on the boarding ramp with my coat pulled tight, watching the ferry exhale steam into the morning air, its iron ribs breathing against the water. The gangway groaned under the weight of luggage and boots, the rhythm of it keeping time with the pulse in my throat. I was not sure why I had come so quickly, why I had not waited for the sun to rise properly, for the light to clarify what the dark had blurred. Perhaps because the strait does not wait, because the tide pulls in and out with an indifference that mirrors nothing, because I needed to be moving before the hesitation could harden into something I could not unmake.

The ferry’s interior smelled of wet wool, floor polish, and the faint sour tang of old coffee. I found a seat by a narrow window, the glass beaded with salt, and watched the vessel push off from the quay. The engines engaged with a vibration that traveled up through the soles of my shoes and settled in my ribs. I did not take off my coat. I did not unclench my jaw. I let the ship carry me into the water, into the narrow corridor of landless sea that separates one coast from another, one past from another. The strait was wide, but narrow in the way that matters—narrow enough to see the opposing shore, narrow enough to feel the pull of both, narrow enough that a man could drown in the space between. I had not crossed it in twelve years, not since the last time I saw his face in profile, turned away, the way he always turned away when the conversation grew heavy. He was the one who stayed, who kept the house by the water, who answered the phone, who let the silence accumulate without breaking it. I was the one who left, who took the bus to the city, who never looked back, who wrote letters and tore them up, who learned to speak to him through intermediaries and then through neither. Estrangement is not a single event; it is a series of small abandonments, each one justified by the last, each one leaving a hollow that fills with the weather of regret.

I closed my eyes and listened to the hull cut through the swell. The sound was not loud, but it was constant, a metronome for the thoughts I had been running from. Thomas. Even in my mind, the name felt heavy, like a stone turned over and over in the pocket of a coat I no longer wore. He had always been the larger presence, the one who filled rooms, the one who worked with his hands, who spoke less but meant more, who remembered the small things—how I took my tea, which shelf held the books I preferred, the exact moment the old birch tree in the yard had begun to lean. I had forgotten those things, or told myself I had, and in doing so, I had forgotten him. The body on the ice might be him, or it might be a man who wore the same boots, who had the same scar on his thumb, who had the same habit of tilting his head when he listened. I had been given a photograph, blurry, taken from a distance, and a list of descriptors that could have been copied from any of us. The coast guard man had spoken with the careful neutrality of someone who has done this many times, who knows that grief arrives in layers, and that the first layer is always denial, even when the body is lying in a room thirty miles away. I had said nothing. I had simply taken the ticket.

The sea outside the window was the colour of old slate, broken only by the white caps that rolled past with no particular urgency. The ferry listed slightly, then righted itself, the motion so gradual I almost missed it. I watched a gull ride the wake, its wings steady, its cries swallowed by the wind. I thought of the last time we had spoken properly. It was not a fight. It was the absence of one, the slow withdrawal of a man who stopped asking, who stopped waiting, who learned to live with the quiet. We had been standing by the old boat shed, the wood bleached grey, the tide out, revealing the mussels and the black rocks beneath. He had pointed out a line of barnacles, how they grew in ridges, how each ridge marked a year, a season, a stretch of time that could be read like a record. I had said something about the weather, about the sky, about anything that kept the conversation on the surface. He had looked at me then, not with anger, but with a kind of tired clarity, and said, “You never stay to look at the shore, Eli.” I had walked away. I have never returned to that shed.

The ferry’s dining car was empty except for a few passengers hunched over tablets or staring into mugs of tea. The staff moved with the efficient rhythm of people who know the water can turn at any moment. I did not go. I stayed where I was, where the light was dim, where the vibration of the ship matched the frequency of my thoughts. Memory is not a film; it is a series of fragments that surface when the water is still enough. I saw him as a boy, knee-deep in the shallows, holding a crab in his palm, showing me its claws, his eyes bright with the simple triumph of capture. I saw him at nineteen, standing on the dock with his sea boots on, saying he was leaving, that the city was calling, that he would return in the spring. He had not returned in the spring. I had stayed. We had both stayed, in different ways. The strait is a boundary, but it is also a bridge, and we had spent years pretending it was a wall. I told myself I had left to find myself, to escape the gravity of a life that felt predetermined, the weight of expectation, the quiet suffocation of a town that remembers every mistake. But the truth is simpler, and more shameful: I left because I could not bear the silence he kept, because his presence was a mirror I did not want to face, because I loved him and feared that love would become obligation, and obligation would become resentment, and resentment would become the very thing that drove him away.

The sky darkened as the afternoon bled into evening. The ferry’s lights came on, casting a sickly yellow across the corridor. I walked to the railing and leaned out, the wind immediately finding the gaps in my coat. The strait stretched out in both directions, a grey expanse that offered no landmarks, no direction, only the forward pull of the current. I thought of the body again. I had not asked for details. I had not wanted to know. To ask was to admit the possibility, to give it weight, to let it settle in the chest. Better to float in the uncertainty, to let it be a question rather than an answer. The mind plays tricks in these moments, constructing scenarios, rehearsing speeches, imagining the official’s face, the plastic sheeting, the cold metal table. I saw Thomas’s hands. I knew they would be stiff, the knuckles swollen from years of work, the cuticles ragged, the left thumb missing half its nail from a slip with a chisel. I saw his face. I knew it would be pale, the features softened by water, the eyes closed, the mouth slightly open, the way it always is when a man dies without preparing. I had not prepared for this. I had not prepared for anything. I had prepared for distance, for the slow erosion of memory, for the eventual forgiveness that time promises but rarely delivers.

A passenger walked past, a woman with a dog on a leash, the animal sniffing at the floor grates, unbothered by the motion of the ship. I watched them disappear into the stairwell, and I felt a sudden, sharp pang of envy for the ordinary. The ferry continued its course, cutting through the water with a mechanical certainty that felt almost cruel. I thought of the phone call again, the way the voice had paused before saying, “He might be Thomas.” The pause was not hesitation; it was protocol, the space needed to separate fact from fear, to let the words settle before they were offered. I had said, “I’ll come.” I had not asked when, or how, or what if it was not him. I had simply said, “I’ll come,” and in that word was every unspoken thing, every letter torn up, every visit avoided, every time I had chosen the city over the shore, the road over the water, the noise over the silence.

Night fell fully, and the strait became a black mirror broken only by the ferry’s wake. I returned to my seat. The window was opaque with condensation now, the outside world reduced to a smear of grey and black. I pressed my palm against the glass and felt the chill seep through. The ship’s navigation lights blinked in the distance, another vessel moving in the same direction, or perhaps the opposite, who could tell? Distance is relative on the water. Two ships can pass without seeing each other, their paths crossing at angles that only the charts know. I thought of Thomas again, and the boat shed, and the barnacles, and how time marks everything, even the things we try to erase. He had left notes, once, in the old radio cabinet, folded pieces of paper that smelled of tobacco and salt. I had read them and put them back, telling myself I would return them, that I would call, that I would sit with him and let the silence do its work. I never did. The notes dried out, the edges curled, and eventually I threw them away, telling myself they were worthless, that they held nothing but the past, that the past was a country I had no passport for.

The ferry began to slow as it approached the northern coast. The water changed colour, lightening slightly, the swell growing less regular, the current more visible in the way it pulled against the hull. I stood and walked to the corridor window. The shore was a dark line against the sky, punctuated by the occasional light of a building, a lighthouse, a fishing boat at anchor. I did not know what I was expecting to see. A recognition? A sign? A sudden certainty? The sea does not provide such things. It only offers movement, distance, the quiet insistence of the tide. I thought of the identification room, the cold floor, the official’s voice, the way my hands might shake, the way I might look away, the way I might say his name and have it feel like a betrayal, or like a homecoming. I did not know. I only knew that I had come, and that coming was a kind of answer, even if the question remained.

The ferry docked with a thud that traveled through the deck and into my bones. The gangway lowered, the ramp secured, the doors opening to a wind that carried the smell of wet stone and distant smoke. I stepped onto the quay, the ticket still in my hand, now crumpled, now irrelevant. The harbour was quiet, the water dark, the lights of the town blinking in the distance. I walked toward the coast guard station, my footsteps echoing on the wet concrete, my breath visible in the air. The building was small, unmarked, functional. Inside, a man in a uniform nodded, showed me down a corridor, opened a door. The room was cold, the walls lined with cabinets, the air smelling of antiseptic and metal. In the centre, on a table covered with a white sheet, lay the body. I did not approach immediately. I stood in the doorway, my hand on the frame, the cold seeping through my coat. The man beside me spoke quietly, explaining the procedure, the forms, the confirmation. I did not listen. I watched the sheet, the way it draped over the shoulders, the way it fell away at the feet, the way it held the shape of a man who had been pulled from the water, who had been dressed in borrowed clothes, who had been brought to this room to be known or left unknown.

I stepped forward. The floor was smooth, cold, unyielding. I reached out, my fingers trembling, and lifted the edge of the sheet. The face was pale, the features softened by water and time, the eyes closed, the mouth relaxed. I did not see the scar on the thumb immediately. I did not see it. I looked away, my throat tight, my breath shallow. Then I looked again, and there it was, the thumb, the missing nail, the knuckles swollen, the skin blue-grey, the hand lying where I had seen it a thousand times, holding a tool, pointing at a tree, resting on a table, reaching for mine. I did not cry. I did not speak. I stood there, the cold of the room seeping into my bones, the weight of the strait behind me, the weight of the years in front of me. The body was Thomas. Or it was not. It did not matter. The question had been answered not by the face, but by the hand, by the thumb, by the way the fingers curled slightly, as if still holding something, as if still reaching, as if still waiting for a reply to a conversation that had never been finished.

The man beside me said something, his voice soft, respectful, already turning away, already giving me space, already knowing that some things cannot be rushed, cannot be resolved, cannot be spoken. I nodded, though I was not sure he saw me. I let the sheet fall back into place, covering the hand, covering the face, covering the man who had loved me in the only way he knew, who had stayed when I left, who had waited in the silence, who had not asked me to come back, who had simply left the door open, even when the wind blew cold. I turned and walked out of the room, down the corridor, up the stairs, into the harbour air. The night was clear now, the stars sharp, the water dark and still. I walked to the edge of the quay and looked out at the strait, at the water that had taken him, or would take him, at the distance that had separated us, at the bridge that had never been crossed, at the shore that had never been reached. I did not know if I would return to the city. I did not know if I would stay. I only knew that the crossing was complete, that the ticket was gone, that the wind had shifted, that the water had settled, that the silence was no longer a wall, but a space, a place where something could finally be said, or left unsaid, with the same grace, the same quiet, the same inevitability as the tide. I turned and walked toward the town, my footsteps slower now, my breath steady, the cold no longer a weight, but a companion, the strait behind me, the shore ahead, the long road, the shorter one, the one that led home, the one that had never truly ended, only waited, only breathed, only continued, as all things do, as all things must, as the water does, as the wind does, as the man who left and the man who stayed both do, when they finally meet, when they finally see, when they finally know.
