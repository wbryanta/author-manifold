The ferry cuts through the strait like a blade through cold oil, its hull whispering against water that refuses to hold the sky. I stand at the stern, collar turned against the wind, watching the wake unfurl behind me in a pale, churning scar that dissolves before it can claim the dark. Salt crusts my eyelashes. The air tastes of diesel and brine and something older, something that has waited here long before we arrived. I came because the alternative was to let the water keep whatever it has taken, or to pretend it never held anything at all. The letter from the coast guard station read plainly, clinically: *Possible identification required. Subject recovered near Blackwater Shoals. Please present in person. Files available upon request.* It did not say whose. It said the name on the manifest matched a surname we shared, a partial dental record, a boot size. That is how far the distance between us has stretched: to a ledger of calcium and a piece of damp paper. I do not know if I want the certainty. Certainty is a door that does not close. It only frames what lies beyond. The engine hums beneath my boots, a steady, mechanical pulse that matches the rhythm of my own breath. I press my palm against the wet railing. The metal is cold enough to burn. Beyond the glass, the water is a black mirror reflecting nothing but the sky I have not yet earned the right to look at. I am moving toward a room that will either confirm a truth or fracture it further. I have rehearsed the words I will say to the corpse. I am sorry. I am here. You were wrong to leave. You were right to leave. But the rehearsal ends where language fails. What is left is the body of the man, and the body of the memory, and the long silence between them.

I walk the length of the corridor. The carpet is worn to a flat gray, patterned with stains that resemble old coastlines. I pass a row of cabin doors, some ajar, some closed, each one a throat swallowing the quiet of the night. A woman hums in Room Twelve. The melody is folk, round as a river stone, carried through the vents in fragments. It reminds me of the porch we shared when we were boys, when the house still smelled of pine resin and wet wool and the kind of woodsmoke that clings to your clothes for days. We used to sit there with our knees almost touching, the board groaning under our weight, listening to the gulls argue over the breakwater. Leo always claimed he could tell the wind’s direction by the sound of its own voice. I called it nonsense. He called it survival. That is how we were: two men speaking different dialects of the same loneliness. When he left the family house, he took only his radio, his work boots, and a tin of polaroids. I stayed to pay the mortgage. I stayed because someone had to anchor the house to the earth. I told myself it was responsibility. I know now it was fear. Fear of the silence he left behind. Fear of admitting that I loved him more than I allowed myself to show. Fear that if I acknowledged the love, I would have to acknowledge the cost of letting it go. The ferry lists to starboard. I catch the bulkhead. The cold seeps through my gloves. The sea does not care about my grief. It only cares about motion, about the slow, indifferent accumulation of water against steel. We are all cargo here, waiting to be unloaded. I think of the polaroids. One showed us standing knee-deep in the river, holding up a trout that thrashed between us. His hand was on my shoulder. The photo is faded now, the color drained to sepia and doubt. When was the last time I saw his face clearly? Not the idea of him, not the silhouette I carry in my mind like a compass needle pointing true north, but the actual texture of him. The scar above his right eyebrow from the bicycle fall when he was fourteen. The way his left eye watered in the cold, though he never admitted it. The faint tremor in his voice when he lied, or when he told the truth and wished he hadn’t. Memory is a curator that edits without permission. It removes the awkward pauses, the sharp words, the times he turned his back in the hallway, the phone calls I let ring until the answering machine swallowed them. It leaves only the polished fragments, the ones I can afford to keep. I watch my breath fog the window. In the reflection, my face is older than I feel. Leo was thirty-six. He looked older, even then, with his habit of staring at the horizon as if it owed him answers. He always had that look, like he was listening to a frequency only he could hear. I stopped listening years ago. I told myself I was protecting myself. I was not. I was pruning a tree until it could no longer bear fruit. The deck lights flicker. A man in the observation lounge reads a newspaper, the ink smudged by damp hands. He does not look up. No one does. We are all suspended in the same quiet, carrying our own private archives of loss. I close my eyes. I try to hear his voice. Not the last one, which was a voice message that lasted eleven seconds and ended with a click. I try to hear him at sixteen, arguing about music, about the shape of the future, about how the world was too big and too small all at once. He wanted to sail. He wanted to map coastlines no one else had charted. I wanted to stay, to build, to anchor. We both thought we were being brave. Now I see it was just fear wearing different clothes. One wanted to flee the weight. The other wanted to bear it. Neither knew how to carry it together. The ferry groans. A warning chime sounds, low and distant. I open my eyes. The sky is the color of wet slate. The strait narrows ahead, flanked by ridges of basalt that rise like broken teeth from the water. The lighthouse on the headland flashes once, twice, then goes dark. I think of the identification procedure. They will show me a body. They will say, *Is this him?* And I will look. And the face may not match the face I keep in the vault of my head. That is the cruelty of it: the dead do not wear their living masks. Rigor mortis stretches the skin. The water plumps the features or leaches them. Death is a sculptor with no regard for proportion. What if I look and see a stranger? What if I look and see him, but the recognition feels like a betrayal, as if acknowledging him means admitting the years between us were real? I press my forehead to the glass. The cold seeps into my bones. Grief is not a storm that passes. It is a current that carries you. You do not fight it. You adjust your sails. You learn to navigate by dead reckoning, by the memory of where you were told you came from. The announcement crackles over the speakers, static-laced, about the estimated arrival time. I do not listen. I listen to the water against the hull, the rhythm of the propeller, the deep, mechanical breath of a machine designed to carry bodies across water. I am one of them. We are all waiting to be unloaded.

I sit in the observation lounge. The glass is streaked with condensation. I draw a line with my finger, clear and deliberate. Beyond it, the water is calm, almost glassy, reflecting the first hints of dawn like a bruise along the horizon. I think of the house. The way the floorboards sighed when he walked past my door. The way he left his coffee cup on the sill every morning, a dark ring blooming on the wood. The way I never washed it away. I told myself I was giving him space. I was giving him an exit. I know now I was giving myself an excuse to wait. To hope. To prepare for a door that would never open again. Estrangement is not a single act. It is a thousand quiet withdrawals, each one a coin dropped into a well. You stop hearing the splash. You forget the depth. Until one day you lean over the edge and realize you cannot throw anything back. I stand. My legs are stiff. I gather my coat, my hands, my thoughts. The corridor is quieter now. The woman has stopped humming. The man with the newspaper is gone. I follow the signs: *Morgue – Authorized Personnel Only*. The door is steel, heavy, with a push plate worn smooth by countless anxious palms. A woman in a navy uniform nods at me. Her name tag reads M. Voss. She does not ask why I am here. She only hands me a clipboard. *Sign here. Step inside. Do not touch. Look, and tell me what you see.* I sign. The pen scratches loudly in the quiet room. I step through. The air is different here. Colder. Cleaner. It smells of antiseptic and salt and something metallic, like old coins left in a drawer. The room is long, lined with cabinets that slide open on quiet rails. One is ajar. Inside, a body lies on a stainless steel table, draped in a gray sheet. Only one foot is exposed, clad in a plastic shoe. The toes are pale, slightly swollen. The skin has the waxy translucence of things preserved in deep water. I stand at the end of the table. I do not pull back the sheet. Not yet. I breathe. The room hums with the building’s ventilation, a low, even sound. I think of Leo’s feet. He had wide, flat feet, the arches collapsed from years of walking on uneven ground. He always complained about the cost of boots. He wore them until the soles wore thin. I remember him tracing the grain of the porch boards with his toe, saying the wood remembered every foot that had ever crossed it. I look at the toes. The nails are short, unevenly cut. There is a scar on the big toe, a pale ridge. I remember him slipping on the dock, tearing it on a rusted bolt. He laughed until he cried. He said the scar was proof he still played rough. I look at the sheet. My hands tremble. I reach out. The metal is cold. I lift the sheet slowly. The face is turned slightly toward the ceiling. The eyes are closed. The jaw is slack. The hair is matted, dark, plastered to the scalp. The features are stretched, relaxed, unfamiliar. But the bone structure is there. The high cheekbones. The slight asymmetry of the brow. The ear, with its notch at the lobe, exactly as I remember from the polaroid where he stood behind me, grinning like a fool. I lean closer. The skin is cool, but the shape is unmistakable. It is him. Or it is the architecture of him, left behind after everything else was stripped away. I do not weep. Not yet. I stand there, breathing the cold air, feeling the weight of the decade collapse into the space between us. I reach out and touch his forehead. The skin is firm, cool. I say, *Leo.* The word leaves my mouth like a stone dropped into a well. I wait for an echo. There is none. Only the hum of the room, the distant thrum of the ferry, the quiet acceptance of a truth that does not need to be spoken to be real. I pull the sheet back over him. I sign the clipboard again. I walk out. The corridor is the same. The glass is the same. The sea is the same. But I am not. I climb back onto the deck. The sky is lightening, a pale bruise along the horizon. The strait is narrower now, the headland closer. I do not look back at the morgue. I look at the water. It is calm, almost glassy, reflecting the first hints of dawn. I think of the polaroid in my pocket, the one I finally took out of my wallet when the identification was confirmed. I think of the words I will say at the grave, or at the ashes, or at the empty space where he should be. I will not say I’m sorry. I will say I remember. And that will have to be enough. The ferry docks. The gangway lowers with a metallic groan. I step onto solid ground. The air is wet, cold, alive. I do not hurry. I walk. The sea follows me in my mind. The brother is no longer a ghost. He is a fact. And facts, however heavy, can be carried. I cross the pier. The wind shifts. The sky opens. I keep walking. Behind me, the ferry rests at its moorings, breathing slowly, waiting for the next crossing. Ahead, the town wakes. Lights flick on in windows. A dog barks. A gate opens. The day begins with the same indifference it always has. I pull my coat tighter. I breathe in. I do not look back.
