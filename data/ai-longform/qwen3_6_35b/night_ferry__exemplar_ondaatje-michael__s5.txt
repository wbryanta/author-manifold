The ferry moved through the strait like a blade drawn slowly through cold water, and the man stood at the stern railing with his hands in his coat pockets, watching the black surface yield and close behind the wake. It was past midnight, and the sky had the color of unbleached wool, thin and heavy at once. The wind off the water carried the smell of kelp and diesel and something older, something that belonged to the bedrock beneath the waves. He had not slept since leaving the mainland, though he had tried in the hard seat of the waiting room, with the fluorescent hum and the ticking of a wall clock that had lost a hand. Sleep had come in fragments, like stones dropped into a well, each one sinking past the light before the darkness took it. Now he stood on the ferry, on the upper deck where the air was damp and the lights from below bled upward through the glass panels, painting the deck in patches of sickly yellow and steel blue. He could feel the vibration of the engines in the soles of his shoes, a steady pulse that seemed to sync with his own breath. He had been told to come. The letter had arrived on a Tuesday, typed on thick paper, the margins narrow, the handwriting of the typist deliberate and impersonal. There was no photograph, only an address, a time, and the instruction to bring identification, though he knew what he would be asked to do. He knew the shape of the room where the body would lie. He knew the way the light falls on the face of the dead, how it flattens the planes, how it leaves the eyes open but empty, like stones in a dry creek bed. He had not spoken to his brother in seven years. Not since the winter the roads closed and the phone lines went dead and the silence between them grew so thick it felt like a wall they had built themselves, brick by brick, out of pride and misunderstanding and the slow erosion of language. Now that silence waited for him on the other side of the strait, in a sterile room with linoleum floors and stainless steel tables, in the folded stillness of a body that might be his brother's, might be anyone's, might be nothing at all but the ghost of a name repeated too often until it lost its edges.

He walked to the railing and rested his knuckles against the cold metal. Below, the water was dark and unbroken, save for the silver track of the wake and the occasional flash of something reflective, a piece of plastic, a fish, the sky itself falling into the deep. He thought of the river behind his childhood house, how it used to run clear in early summer and brown and swollen in late fall, how he and his brother would sit on the bank with their legs dangling, skipping stones until their arms ached, until the sun dipped below the pines and the light turned the water to hammered copper. They had not spoken much that day, not because they wanted to be quiet, but because some things do not need language to be held. Now he stood alone, and the quiet was not a shared thing but a weight. He pulled his coat tighter and looked up at the sky. A single star hung low and pale, indifferent to the ferry, to the strait, to the man waiting for a face to tell him who he was.

Inside, the passenger lounge was nearly empty. A few people slept in the padded chairs, wrapped in blankets or coats, their faces turned toward the walls. A woman in the corner read a paperback with the cover torn at the spine, her glasses sliding down her nose. A man in a cap stared at the floor, his hands clasped over his knees like a man in prayer. None of them spoke. The hum of the ship was the only sound, punctuated occasionally by the crackle of the intercom, the clank of a bulkhead, the distant call of a horn from a freighter miles out. The man sat down in the chair opposite the woman, keeping his distance, keeping his silence. He did not look at her again. He looked at the window instead, at his own reflection superimposed over the dark water. He looked older than he felt. The face that looked back was unfamiliar, the lines around the eyes deepened by years of not speaking, the mouth set in a line he had worn so long it had become his own architecture. He thought of the brother he had left behind, or who had left him, it no longer mattered which. He thought of the last time he had seen him, standing in the driveway, the snow up to their ankles, the car packed with boxes they would never unpack, the words they had chosen carefully and then chosen poorly, how language can be a tool and a weapon depending on how it is held. He had turned and walked away. The brother had not followed. That was the shape of it. A door closing. A key turning. A road widening until it could no longer be crossed.

The ferry pitched slightly as a wave caught its hull. The woman lowered her book and looked at him. For a moment they were both still, two strangers bound by the same slow journey, by the same unspoken question hanging in the air like the smell of salt and diesel. He nodded once. She nodded back. No words were offered. Some acknowledgments are enough. She closed her book and turned toward the window. He closed his eyes and let the ship carry him.

He had read the reports. He had called the offices that handled the dead. He had signed the forms. He had packed his bag with the careful precision of a man preparing for a visit he did not want to make. The body had been found near the headlands, where the strait narrows and the currents twist and pull, where the rocks are slick with algae and the wind comes down the cliffs like a blade. They said it had been there for three days. They said it had been washed in from the open water, or pushed, or fallen. They said the identification would be done in the morning, after the coroner had finished, after the photographer had stepped back, after the questions had been asked and answered and filed. They said there was a possibility of mismatch, of confusion, of the dead wearing the living's clothes like a borrowed coat. He had nodded. He had said yes. He had signed another paper. He had boarded the ferry. Now he sat in the lounge, listening to the ship cut through the night, feeling the weight of the unmade decision settle in his chest like a stone dropped into a well.

A steward passed through the lounge, pushing a cart with cups and a thermos. The man did not move. The steward paused, looked at him, offered a cup. He shook his head. The steward nodded, moved on. The coffee would be bitter, the cups thin, the liquid hot and useless for sleep. He had learned long ago that some things are not meant to be warmed, not meant to be smoothed over by routine. He thought of the brother's hands, how they used to be steady, how they could fix anything, how they could also break anything with the same casual grace. He thought of the way they had shared a kitchen for twenty years, the quiet routines, the unspoken agreements, the way they had stopped speaking altogether and let the silence do the work of a bridge or a wall, depending on the season. He had not known which it was until it was too late to ask.

The intercom crackled again. This time a voice, calm and measured, spoke of arrival times, of customs procedures, of the need to remain with all identification. The man stood and walked to the window. The sky was lighter now, the wool of night thinning to gray, the stars fading one by one as if embarrassed by the approaching day. The water below was no longer black but the color of wet slate, the surface rippling with the ferry's passage, the wake stretching out behind them like a scar that would never heal, only fade. He pressed his palm to the glass. It was cold. He let it stay there until his hand ached.

Sleep came later, in the lounge, in the chair, with the coat over his lap and his shoes on. He dreamed of the river again, but it was not the river behind the house. It was a different river, wider, deeper, the banks steep and lined with birch trees that stripped themselves in the wind. His brother stood on the opposite bank, calling his name, but the wind took the words and scattered them across the water. He tried to cross, but the current was too strong, the ice too thin, the distance too vast. He woke with a start, his heart pounding, the taste of cold water in his mouth. The lounge was empty now, the chairs arranged in neat rows, the floor polished by the passage of boots and wheels. A single light burned above the emergency exit sign, casting a green glow that pulsed slowly, like a heart. He stood and walked to the aft deck again. The strait was narrowing. He wrapped his arms around himself and leaned against the railing. The wind had dropped. The water was still. The ferry hummed. He felt the exhaustion in his bones, the kind that comes not from lack of sleep but from the weight of anticipation, from the mind running in circles, from the body bracing for a blow it cannot see.

Downstairs, the crew moved with the quiet efficiency of people who know the rhythm of the sea. He heard boots on metal stairs, the clink of a coffee pot, the murmur of voices in a language he did not speak, the low thrum of the engines changing pitch as the ship adjusted its course. He had always liked ferries. They were neither here nor there, suspended between departure and arrival, between what was left and what awaited. They held their passengers in a kind of temporary peace, a shared anonymity that allowed for the unspooling of thought, for the surfacing of memories that had been kept below deck. He thought of the brother's face as a boy, all elbows and knees and sunburn, running along the shore with a net in his hand, catching minnows and setting them free, catching them again, setting them free, caught in the endless loop of a child's wonder. He thought of him as a man, taller now, quieter, his hands rough from work, his eyes narrowed against the wind, the way he would stand at the edge of the dock and watch the boats come in, his face unreadable, his posture suggesting both invitation and departure. He had not known how to reach him. He had not known how to be reached. Language had failed them. Time had failed them. Pride had failed them. Now only the water remained, and the ferry, and the long quiet walk to a room where a body waited.

The first light came as a thinning of the dark, a graying at the horizon, a softening of the edges of the world. The man did not notice it immediately. He was too busy counting the hours, or not counting them, letting them pass like stones through his fingers. When he finally looked up, the sky was the color of a bruised peach, the water reflecting it in streaks of pink and gray, the cliffs on the far side growing sharper, more defined. A bell sounded, low and resonant, calling passengers to prepare for disembarkation. The man did not move. He watched a gull circle above the water, its wings caught in the early wind, its cry sharp and clear. He thought of the brother's laugh, how it used to sound like water over stones, how it had stopped altogether, leaving only the echo. He thought of the letter again, the typed words, the address, the time. He thought of the room where the body would lie, the stainless steel, the light, the silence, the question that would hang in the air like a thread: who are you to me? He had no answer that would satisfy the dead. He had only the weight of memory, the shape of a name, the possibility that the face beneath the sheet might be his brother's, might be the brother he had loved and lost, might be the brother he had never known, might be no one at all, a stranger washed up on the same shore, a mirror reflecting a face he could not bear to see.

He walked back inside. The lounge was filling now, passengers shuffling toward the exits, their faces pale and uncommitted, their coats buttoned to the chin, their bags zipped and strapped. A few looked at him, then looked away. He understood. Some silences are contagious. Some journeys are too heavy to share. He followed the flow of people toward the lower deck, where the gangway would be lowered, where the ramp would meet the dock, where the real work would begin. The air smelled different now, less like open water, more like wood and concrete and distant smoke. The light through the windows was brighter, harsher, stripping the night away and leaving the day exposed, unadorned, inevitable. He felt a strange calm settle over him, the kind that comes not from resolution but from surrender, from the acceptance that some things cannot be changed, only witnessed, only carried.

The ferry slowed. The engines reversed. The hull groaned against the water. The man closed his eyes and listened to the sounds of arrival, the clank of metal, the hiss of hydraulics, the murmur of voices, the call of birds, the rustle of coats and bags and shoes on deck. When he opened his eyes, the land was there, close and solid, the dock stretching out like a hand reaching for him. He did not step off immediately. He waited until the rest had moved, until the deck was empty, until the ship rested against the pilings with a soft thud. Then he walked to the edge, looked down at the water below, the dark and the light mingling, the wake still spreading, the ferry's reflection broken and reforming with each pulse of the hull. He stepped down onto the gangway. The wood groaned under his weight. The air was cold and clean. He walked forward, past the customs desk, past the waiting car, past the man in uniform who asked for his papers and then looked at him with eyes that had seen too much to ask questions. He took the papers back. He walked out onto the dock. The strait was behind him now, the water calm and endless, the sky light and clear, the day beginning with no fanfare, no ceremony, only the steady rhythm of footsteps on concrete, the smell of salt and pine, the quiet promise of what waited ahead. He did not look back. He kept walking. The body would wait. The silence would wait. The question would wait. He would answer it, or not answer it, with the same quiet gravity with which he had crossed the water. Some bridges are not crossed. Some are simply acknowledged, from a distance, with the hands in the pockets, with the breath steady, with the eyes open to the light. He walked toward the building where the dead were kept, toward the room where the sheet waited, toward the face that might be his brother's, might be no one's, toward the end of the crossing, toward the beginning of the return. The water did not care. The sky did not care. The ferry had done its work. The man did his. He stepped through the door. The light fell on the floor. The silence waited. He walked forward.
