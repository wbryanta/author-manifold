The sun hung white and heavy over the failing orchard and the air was thick with the dust of centuries and the brothers moved among the rows of trees that stood like the ribs of some great beast that had been flayed and left to bake in the noon. The bark was gray and cracked and the leaves were brown and curled and the fruit hung small and hard upon the branches and the water in the irrigation line moved with a sound like the whispering of snakes and it was thin and brown and tasted of copper and decay and the elder brother wiped his face with a rag that was stiff with grease and he looked at the younger brother and his eyes were the color of the dry creek bed and his face was smudged with dirt and his hands were raw and cracked and he spat into the dust and the spit fell like a small black stone. Here pass the wrench, the elder said. His voice was low and rough and it did not echo. The younger brother did not move and he looked at the line of pipe that lay upon the ground like the gut of a serpent and he bent the metal with his hands and the metal groaned and he looked up at the sky and the sky was the color of a bruised plum and the sun was a blister in the center of it. The metal is hot, the younger said. It will cool when the sun goes down, the elder said. Or it wont cool at all, the younger said. He spat again and the spittle fell upon the roots of a tree that had not borne fruit in ten years. You think the water will save us, he said. The water is gone. It has always been gone. We are digging graves for ghosts. We hold the line, the elder said. We hold the line as long as the line holds the world holds together. For what, the younger said. For the dust? For the wind that takes the topsoil and carries it to the sea? The deed is in the name of a creditor in Santa Fe who has not seen this place in thirty years and cares less for it than he cares for the price of a mule. He paced along the trench and his boots left deep prints in the hardened earth and he stopped and he looked at the elder brother and he saw the man's face hardened by sun and want and the lines of the face were deep and the eyes were hollow and the mouth was set in a line of iron. The land is in our blood, the elder said. You would sell your bone? I would sell the bone to buy bread that is not ground from the dust we eat now, the younger said. I would sell the house where the dead sleep and live in a town where the streets are paved and the wells run clear and the air is clean. There is no God here, the elder said. Only the land. The land is the measure of all things. You would trade the measure for the coin? The coin is a fiction, the younger said. The coin is a promise made by men who are dead and who do not know the arithmetic of thirst. The land gives life or it takes it. The coin gives nothing but the promise of bread and the bread may be stale and the water may be poison. The land is true. The land is the only truth. The younger brother laughed and the sound was sharp and dry and it fell to the ground and did not rise. The land is true, he said. The land is true until it kills you. And then the land is dust and the man is dust and the dust is the end of all things and the beginning of all things and there is no truth in the dust. He picked up the hammer and he struck the nail into the flume and the sound rang out sharp and clear in the stillness and the bird that had been perched upon the branch dropped away and flew east and did not look back. They worked in silence for a time and the heat rose in waves from the ground and the light was white and harsh and the shadows were black and deep and the trees stood like sentinels of a forgotten war and the brothers bent their backs and they hauled the sections of pipe across the parched ground and they set them into the trench and they packed the earth about them with their boots and they wound the wire about the joints and the wire bit into their flesh and the blood ran red and mixed with the dust and formed a crust upon their hands and they did not feel it. The surveyor came yesterday, the younger brother said. He stood by the old oak and he measured the ground with his glass and he spoke of roads and of lots and of water rights that are worth more than the fruit and he said the valley is changing and he said the city will come eventually and he said we could walk away with enough to start over. The city is a disease, the elder said. It eats the world and leaves only stone and shadow and the people are hollow and their eyes are empty and they do not know the names of the trees and they do not know the taste of the rain. They buy bread in packages and they drink water in bottles and they do not know that the earth is the body of God. The earth is not God, the younger said. The earth is just the earth. It is the ground beneath our feet and the ground beneath the feet of the dead and the ground that will cover us when we are gone. There is nothing sacred in the dirt. There is only the work and the end. The work is the prayer, the elder said. The work is the worship. We tend the land and the land tends us and if we turn away the land turns its face and the dark comes and the dark is the death of the soul. The soul is a story, the younger said. The story is told by the living and the dead do not care. The dead are gone and the land remembers nothing. The land is a mirror, the elder said. It shows us what we are. And what we are is nothing. We are dust and breath and we are the dust that knows itself and we are the breath that sings and the song is the only thing that matters and the song is the work and the song is the holding of the line. The younger brother stopped and he wiped his brow and he looked at the elder brother and he saw the man's chest heave and he saw the sweat run down the man's neck and he saw the man's eyes wet with a light that was not the sun. He felt a pity that was terrible and without solace and he felt a love that was like a wound and he knew that he could not argue with a man who had made a covenant with the dust. You fear the poverty, the elder said. Not the land. You fear the poverty that will find you in the town. You have not the stomach for it. You fear the death, the younger brother said. Slow death. The death of the spirit that comes from watching the roots dry while you stand by and do nothing. I love this place. That is why I say we must go. To love it is to let it go before it kills us. Is that not so? Or do you love only the possession of it? The pride? The memory of your father walking these rows? My father walked because he had nowhere else to go, the elder said. And I have nowhere else to go. And you have nowhere. We are bound to this soil. You cannot sell your shadow. I can sell the ground beneath my feet and buy a pair of boots to walk on another path, the younger brother said. The path is the same, the elder said. It ends in the same place. The ground is the only thing that is real. The rest is a dream. The ground is a dream of stone, the younger brother said. We are dust. We return to dust. The orchard will return to dust. The only thing that matters is what we do with the breath we have. We can use it to fight the wind or we can use it to build a fire. You choose to fight the wind and die standing. I choose to build a fire and burn. The elder brother looked at the line of water and he spoke softly. Words are things, he said. The words of the land are written in the bark and the root and the water. You cannot bargain with words. You can only obey them or ignore them. And if you ignore them the land will turn its face away and the dark will come. You speak of fires and of cities but you do not hear the silence. The silence is the voice of the land and it says leave. It says leave before the debt of blood is called. We have taken too much. The trees are bitter. The fruit is small. The land is tired. Do you not feel the weariness? It is in the air. It is in the bones. It is the weariness of ages. I feel the weariness, the younger brother said. And I tell you the weariness is a lie. It is the lie of the old to keep the young in chains. The land gives if you ask. We just do not know the words anymore. The words are dead, the elder brother said. They died with the rain. There are no words left. There is only the work and the end. They hauled the final section of pipe and they set it in place and they tightened the bolts and the metal groaned and the bolts bit and the pipe held and the water ran through and it was weak and it was slow and it was brown but it ran and the sound of the water was the sound of the world moving on without them and the brothers stood and they regarded the line and the line regarded them with a silence that was older than the mountains. The sun began to sink and the light changed and the shadows lengthened across the rows and the trees stood black against the purple sky and the air grew cool and the dust settled and the brothers sat upon the flume and they drank from a canteen and the water was warm and it tasted of metal and they passed the canteen and they did not speak. The wind picked up and it blew across the valley and it rustled the leaves of the dead trees and it carried the scent of sage and of dry earth and of the distant smoke of a fire that was not theirs. Will you sign the paper, the younger brother said. He looked at the elder brother and he saw the man's face illuminated by the dying light and he saw the lines of the face and the hollows of the eyes and the set of the mouth and he felt the weight of the years and the weight of the land and the weight of the blood. I will sign nothing, the elder brother said. I will hold the line until my hands fall off. And when they fall off you will hold it. And when you are gone the land will hold the rest. The land holds nothing, the younger brother said. It takes. It takes and it gives nothing. It is a mouth. It is a mirror, the elder brother said. It shows us what we are. And what we are is nothing. But we are the nothing that knows itself. That is all. The younger brother nodded and he looked at the trees and the trees stood silent and the wind moved through the branches and the sound was like the whispering of the dead. Tomorrow we dig the well, the elder brother said. Tomorrow, the younger brother said. Tomorrow we dig. They rose and they shouldered the tools and they walked back to the house and the house stood dark and silent and the windows were closed and the door was heavy and the wood was gray and the roof was sagging and the porch was rotting and the steps were broken and the brothers climbed the steps and they opened the door and they entered the house and the house was cold and the house was empty and the house smelled of dust and of decay and of the ghost of the woman who had died in this room and the brothers did not speak and they set the tools upon the table and they sat and they watched the dark come and the dark was absolute and in the dark the land waited. The night descended upon the orchard and the trees stood like sentinels of a forgotten war and the water in the line made a sound like a slow heartbeat and the brothers slept and the sleep was restless and the dreams were of the sea and of the green valleys and of the dead and the land did not care. The moon rose and it hung pale and replica in the sky and it looked upon the orchard with a gaze that was indifferent and ancient and the light fell upon the rows of trees and it revealed the cracks in the earth and the grayness of the bark and the small hard fruit and the broken flume and the brothers lying upon the floor of the house and the dust settling upon their faces and the dust was the memory of the land and the future of the dust and the dust was the only truth and the dust was the end of all things and the beginning of all things. The elder brother woke and he lay still and he listened to the sound of the water and he listened to the sound of the younger brother's breathing and he listened to the sound of the wind and he listened to the sound of the land turning in the dark and he knew that the argument was over and the argument would begin again and the cycle would turn and the years would pass and the orchard would fall and the dust would rise and the dust would be the witness of their folly and the dust would be the witness of their love and the dust would be the witness of the nothing and the nothing would be the witness of the everything and the words would be things and the things would be words and the land would be the land and the brothers would be the brothers and the line would hold and the line would break and the water would run and the water would stop and the sun would rise and the sun would set and the day would be June and the month would be June and the year would be the year and the time would be the time and the hour would be the hour and the minute would be the minute and the second would be the second and the breath would be the breath and the blood would be the blood and the bone would be the bone and the flesh would be the flesh and the spirit would be the spirit and the spirit would be the dust and the dust would be the spirit and the spirit would be the word and the word would be the work and the work would be the end and the end would be the beginning and the beginning would be the end and the brothers would stand and the brothers would fall and the land would stand and the land would fall and the land would remain and the brothers would be forgotten and the dust would remember and the dust would speak and the words would be words and the words would be things and the things would be nothing and the nothing would be everything and the everything would be the orchard and the orchard would be the world and the world would be the dust and the dust would be the end. The younger brother woke and he lay still and he listened to the sound of the water and he knew that the elder brother was awake and he knew that the elder brother was listening and he knew that the elder brother was waiting and he knew that the elder brother would hold the line and he knew that he would not sign the paper and he knew that the paper was wind and the ink was lie and the ground was truth and the truth was dust and the dust was the only thing that remained and the brothers would dig the well and the well would be dry and they would dig again and they would dig until their hands were bone and their bones were dust and the dust would be the witness and the land would be the judge and the judgment would be the silence and the silence would be the word and the word would be the work and the work would be the end and the end would be the beginning and the beginning would be the end and the brothers would be one and the land would be one and the dust would be one and the nothing would be one and the everything would be one and the one would be the orchard and the orchard would be the world and the world would be the dust and the dust would be the end. The sun rose and it was white and heavy and the sky was a hard and unyielding blue and the brothers rose and they shouldered the tools and they walked to the orchard and the trees stood silent and the wind blew and the dust rose and the brothers bent their backs and they dug and they dug and they dug and the line held and the line broke and the water ran and the water stopped and the day was June and the month was June and the year was the year and the time was the time and the hour was the hour and the minute was the minute and the second was the second and the breath was the breath and the blood was the blood and the bone was the bone and the flesh was the flesh and the spirit was the spirit and the spirit was the dust and the dust was the spirit and the spirit was the word and the word was the work and the work was the end and the end was the beginning and the beginning was the end and the brothers were the brothers and the land was the land and the dust was the witness and the witness was the truth and the truth was nothing and the nothing was everything and the everything was the orchard and the orchard was the world and the world was the dust and the dust was the end.
