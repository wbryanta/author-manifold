The sun came down upon the orchard like a hammer upon an anvil and the earth drank what it could and still grew thirsty. The sky was a polished sheet of pale steel and the air hung still and dry as cured leather. Silas wiped his brow with the back of a hand that was mapped with old cuts and new blisters and looked down the long gray trench that cut through the root zone like a wound. The pipe lay in sections beside him heavy and blackened by years of sun and frost and the slow accretion of dust. He struck it with a length of iron rebar and the sound rang out sharp and hollow and was swallowed by the heat. Ewan knelt across the trench wiping his palms on his knees and watching his brother with the flat unblinking gaze that men wear when they have long ago stopped expecting pity from the world. The older brother stood and drove the rebar into the dry clay and the earth gave way reluctantly and then held fast and he pulled it out again and let the dust fall back in.

They had come to the orchard before dawn when the air still held the memory of night and the shadows stretched long and cold across the rows. The trees stood like broken men in their shirtsleeves the lower branches stripped bare by wind and the upper branches heavy with a sparse and trembling canopy. The leaves were the color of old coin and they hung limp and silent. Somewhere beyond the fence line the highway hummed a flat and endless note but here there was only the crackle of dry grass and the distant cry of a hawk riding the thermal currents above the ridges. Silas carried the coupling wrench in one hand and a coil of rubber hose in the other and Ewan followed with a canvas sack of fittings and bolts and a hammer whose handle had been worn smooth by his father's grip. They laid out the new sections of pipe along the trench and began the slow work of joining metal to metal and rubber to iron.

The ground was hard as bone and every swing of the pickaxe sent shock up through the arms and into the teeth. Silas worked in long measured strokes his shirt dark with sweat at the shoulders and his breath coming steady and even. He did not speak until the sun had climbed high and the heat began to press down upon them like a physical weight. Ewan paused and leaned on his shovel and looked out over the rows of apple trees that had stood since the first frost of the last war and before that since his father's father had driven a stake into the soil and marked the ground with a wooden cross and said this shall be ours and no other's hands shall turn the soil here.

You think the water will hold in the spring if you mend the pipe it will be the same as mending a shirt with thread when the fabric is rotted through. Silas did not look up from the coupling he was tightening. The water holds if the line holds. You want to talk of selling you can do it with the wrench in your hand or you can do it later when your hands are clean and you pretend you never knew the feel of it. Ewan spat into the dust and turned his face from the sun. The land is dying Silas. You can call it by any name you like but it is dying. The roots go deeper every year searching for something that is not there and they pull up and they crack the soil and they leave behind dry skeletons and you and I are just the ones who have to sweep up the bones.

He drove the shovel into the earth and the blade struck rock and sent up a spark and a ring of iron that echoed across the row. Silas set the wrench down and wiped his hands on his thighs and walked over to the younger brother and stood beside him and looked down the trench toward the far end where the irrigation head sat like a rusted sentinel. The water pump sat beside it a green beast with peeling paint and a fuel tank that had long ago gone dry. Behind it the gravel drive wound out past the stone wall that had been stacked by hands that were now dust and past the barn whose roof sagged like a tired back and past the gate whose hinges had rusted shut years ago and beyond that the fields of wheat that had turned to scrub and the scrub to sand and the sand to the flat and endless horizon.

It is not the land that is dying Ewan it is the keeping of it. You think men were born to hold ground the way a man holds a handful of water. You can close your fingers tight and it looks like you have it but it leaks out between the knuckles and soon you are holding nothing and your hands are empty and your wrists are raw from the effort. Ewan turned and looked at his brother and the silence between them was thick and hot and full of things unsaid. The older brother's face was lined with sun and wind and the lines were not wrinkles but cracks in stone. His eyes were the color of weathered iron and they held a depth that was not anger but something older and heavier than anger.

Your father would have worked this land to his last breath. He would have sat in the dirt and he would have coughed up the dust and he would have died with his hands in the soil and he would have been happy. Silas struck the ground with his boot and the heel dug into the clay and came out clean. Your father was a man who knew what he was doing and he knew the price of things. He did not plant trees for sentiment. He planted them because the soil was good and the water was true and the market was steady. We are not living in the time of steady markets or true water. We are living in the time of trucks and contracts and men who sign papers with pens that cost more than a year's harvest.

Ewan walked away from the trench and down the row and the dust rose in his wake and settled on the lower branches of the trees. He ran a hand along the bark of a trunk and the wood was rough and cracked and cold in the shade. He touched the place where a lower limb had been removed years ago and the callus was thick and hard like a scar. He did not look back until he heard the sound of the wrench against metal and the steady rhythm of the pickaxe. He stood there in the heat and the silence and the memory came over him like a wave and he closed his eyes and saw his father standing at the same tree in the spring of the last year he could walk and he saw the man's hands open and close and open again and he heard him say the soil remembers what you do to it and it will answer you in kind and if you give it nothing it will give you nothing back.

He opened his eyes and the tree was still there and the branch was still bare and the ground was still dry. He walked back to the trench and climbed down into it and picked up the hammer and began to tap the coupling into place and the metal groaned and settled and held. Silas worked beside him and the two of them moved in the old rhythm that had been drilled into them by years of shared labor and unspoken understanding. They did not speak again until the pipe was laid and the joints were sealed and the trench was filled in with the clay that had been pulled out and packed down with the heels of their boots.

You think the bank cares about memory Ewan. Silas stood and brushed the dirt from his knees and looked at the sky which was beginning to lighten at the edges with the approach of evening. The bank cares about numbers and the numbers do not change for grief or for pride or for the weight of a name on a deed. They will take the land and they will sell it to a man who will plant almonds or avocados or something that grows fast and sells fast and he will bleed the soil until it is dust and he will leave and he will not look back. We can take what they offer us and we can go somewhere else and we can live in a house with a roof that does not leak and a door that does not stick and we can sleep at night without hearing the wind in the eaves and the creak of the trees and the dry rattle of the branches.

Ewan set the hammer down and leaned against the pipe and looked at his brother. His voice was low and flat and carried the weight of long years of carrying things alone. You think I do not hear the wind in the eaves. You think I do not wake in the night and count the ways we are failing. I hear it every day. I see it in the groves and the wells and the fences and the silence of the market. I sit at the kitchen table and I look at the bills and I look at the maps of the neighboring farms and I see them gone or sold or leased to men who do not know the taste of the soil and I wonder if we are fools or if we are just men who are too stubborn to admit that the world has moved on and left us behind.

Silas did not answer immediately. He walked to the head of the line and ran his hands over the rusted casing of the pump and felt the cold metal beneath the heat. He opened the fuel cap and looked inside and saw only dust and a few insects that had crawled in and died. He closed it and turned back to the trench and looked at the filled earth and the pipe beneath it and the rows of trees beyond. The sun was lower now and the shadows stretched long and thin across the orchard and the light turned the leaves to silver and the sky to the color of old bronze.

The world has not moved on Ewan. The world moves in circles and it turns and it wears down and it builds up and it breaks again. The men who come and take the land think they are moving forward but they are just running in place. They plant and they harvest and they sell and they buy again and the soil grows tired and the water turns sour and the trees weaken and the cycle begins again. We are not running from anything. We are holding the line. We are keeping what is ours and we are giving it to the earth and the earth will decide what to do with it. If it takes it back we will not fight it. If it lets us keep it a little longer we will keep it. Not for pride. Not for ghosts. For the work itself. For the knowing that you dug the trench and laid the pipe and turned the water and watched it soak into the ground and you know that you did it and no man can take that from you.

Ewan looked at his brother and he saw the lines in his face and the strength in his hands and the quiet certainty that did not need to be proven or defended or explained. He felt the weight of the years between them and the weight of the years before them and the weight of the years that would come. He did not speak. He walked to the edge of the trench and pulled himself out and stood beside his brother and they looked out over the orchard as the light faded and the heat began to lift from the earth. The trees stood in the dusk like shadows of themselves and the wind stirred the lower branches and the leaves rattled softly like dry bones turning in a grave.

They went back to the pump and Silas poured a measure of fuel into the tank and Ewan pulled the starter cord and the engine coughed and caught and ran rough and then steadied and the sound echoed across the orchard and into the hills and beyond. Silas opened the main valve and the water came out of the line in a hesitant trickle and then a steady stream and it ran down the trench and soaked into the clay and disappeared into the dark. They stood and watched it go and the water moved slowly and quietly and without fanfare and it was enough.

You think it will be enough. Ewan's voice was soft and carried across the quiet. You think this one repair and one season and one more year will be enough to keep it alive. Silas wiped his hands on his shirt and looked at the water disappearing into the soil. It will be enough for as long as it is enough. It will not be enough forever. Nothing is. But it is enough for now. And that is all we are given. The now. The work. The keeping. Not the ending. Not the selling. Not the running. Just this. The line. The trench. The water. The trees. The ground. The day.

Ewan nodded once and turned and walked back down the row and picked up the shovel and began to fill in the remaining pockets of earth and Silas followed and they worked in silence until the sun was gone and the sky was dark and the stars came out cold and clear and the air grew cool and the orchard settled into the night and the water continued to flow beneath the surface and the roots drank and the land held and the brothers stood side by side and did not speak and the day was done.
