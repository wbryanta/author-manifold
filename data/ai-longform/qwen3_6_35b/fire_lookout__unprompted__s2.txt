The tower complained every time the wind hit the south face, a wooden spine stiffening against the sky, the timbers groaning in the register of dry heat and expansion. Mara knew the language of these complaints, had spent four summers learning the difference between the stress of a storm front and the settling of the foundation, but this was the fifth summer, the final one, and the complaints sounded like goodbyes now. The commissioning order had come down six weeks ago, printed on crisp government stock that felt alien in hands calloused by creosote and splintered pine. The satellites were enough, the drones were faster, the world had moved on to data streams and algorithmic predictions. Mara was left with the cupola, the brass binnacle, the iron stove that smoked when the damp returned, and the endless, vertiginous view that had become her lungs.

She was packing the last crate when the smoke appeared. It didn't rise like the wispy ghosts of dry grass burning off in the sun or the white plumes of lightning strikes in the high alpine. This was a column, thick and bruised purple against the blue, heaving upward with a slow, muscular ambition. It started from the throat of the drainage where the map faded into ink and rumor, from the low-lying bowl of pines and cedar that caught the cold air like a cup. The Blackwood Draw. Where the red dirt turned to clay and the wind died. Where the coordinates pointed to a smudge of coordinates that Mara had memorized long before she could read the topography.

Where Kael's cabin sat.

Mara killed the switch on the radio and set her hand on the dial for a long moment, feeling the cold metal cool her palm. She had killed the power three days ago, when the static became a roar that no amount of tuning could tame, but the gesture remained. The radio was a dead thing, a hollow throat. She had tried the hand-held backup, the battery swelling with age, and gotten nothing but a whine that died in the receiver. The silence was absolute, a sealed jar. Below her, the road was a scar of gray gravel winding down through the trees, eight miles of switchbacks and exposed drop-offs that led to the county highway. Eight miles of climbing back up after the sun set, eight miles of gravel that would shred tires and chew legs. No truck. Just boots, a pack, and the weight of a body that had grown thin on salt pork and pine needles.

She grabbed the binoculars and leaned out over the catwalk, the iron grating singing a sharp note under her weight. The heat rose off the tower, radiating through the soles of her boots, making the air shimmer. She focused on the drainage. The smoke was thickening, curling as it hit the thermal layers, beginning to spread into an anvil head. Through the lenses, the world compressed to a manageable lie. She saw the tops of the firs, snapped like matchsticks. She saw the gray tongue of ash licking the canopy. And there, half-hidden by a stand of lodgepoles that had survived the burns of decades, she saw the cabin.

It was smaller than she remembered. Or maybe the distance was a liar. The blue paint, once bright as a robin's egg, was now the color of a bruised sky, peeling in long strips. The roof line sagged slightly to the east, where the heavy snows of the previous winter had groaned against the eaves. There was no movement. No figure standing on the deck with a shovel, no dog barking, no dark smoke venting from a chimney to signal a life lived. Just the stillness of a structure waiting to be consumed.

Mara lowered the binoculars and pressed her forehead against the warm glass of the cupola. The reflection of her own face ghosted over the view: hair shorn close to the scalp, skin mapped with the fine wrinkles of squinting and wind, eyes the color of the iron stove. She looked like the tower itself, weathered, stripped of ornament, functional to the point of erosion.

"Eight miles," she whispered. The sound of her voice was startling, raw and unused. It scratched against the silence of the room, against the hum of the insects in the eaves. Eight miles was a lifetime. Eight miles was the distance between being a witness and being a participant.

The radio had been the line. For four summers, the daily check at 0800 and 2000 had been her tether to the world, a mechanical pulse of voices from the district office, the reports of wind shifts, the banter of a ranger named Miller who complained about his divorce. Now the line was cut. The isolation was total. And yet, the irony burned in her chest sharper than the stove. For months, she had told herself she wanted the silence. She had told herself the tower was a sanctuary from the noise of a life that had fractured beyond repair. But silence, she was finding, was not empty. It was a mirror. And the mirror showed her the drainage, the smoke, and the shape of her sister standing alone in the kitchen, staring at a phone that would never ring.

The estrangement had not come as a shout. It was not a scene of thrown plates and slamming doors. It was a slow withdrawal, a silencing of the frequency by which they had always tuned to one another. It had started with the funeral, the way they had stood over the grave of their mother without touching, each building a wall of grief that kept the other out. Then came the house, the will, the lawyer's letters that arrived like invasive species. Kael had stayed, as she always did, the anchor in the mud. Mara had left, as she always did, running up the mountain to find elevation where the air was thin and the view was clean. Kael had written. Mara had not answered the letters, though she kept them, bundled in a tin box under her bunk, the paper softening with humidity, the ink bleeding into Rorschach tests of apology she couldn't bring herself to write.

*I'm watching you burn,* she thought, the words forming with a terrible clarity. *I'm watching you burn and I am doing nothing.*

A gust of wind hit the cupola, rattling the brass fittings, and the smoke shifted. The thermal was strong today, the air unstable. The column leaned, then righted itself. The wind was blowing from the north, pushing the smoke away from her, toward the distant ridgeline. But the smell reached her anyway, riding the currents, a heavy, resinous perfume of burning pine and charred cedar. It was the scent of the forest digesting itself, the smell of release. It smelled like the day Kael had burned the dog by accident, howling in the brush with a spark trapped in her fur, the way they had both dug her grave with their bare hands, the soil warm and wet. It smelled like the kitchen in the cabin, where Kael had hung drying herbs from the rafters, the air thick with sage and rosemary, where they had cooked together without speaking, the only communication the passing of the knife, the nod of the pot.

Mara turned away from the window and paced the length of the room. The tower felt too small now, the walls pressing in. She moved to the desk, her hand hovering over the logbook. The final entry waited. *July 14. 0900. Visibility good. No activity. Fire risk elevated.* She could write it. She could document the smoke, the direction, the intensity. It would be a professional record, a testament to her diligence until the end. But the pen felt heavy, useless. The logbook was for the service that had ended. The service was over. What remained was just a woman in a box of wood, watching her sister's life turn to ash.

She went back to the window. The sun was climbing, the light hardening, bleaching the color from the world. The smoke was now a vast gray cloud, casting a shadow over the mountains to the west. The shadows of the clouds moved across the valley like the slow passage of time. Mara imagined the heat down there. The roar that must be growing, a sound like a freight train trapped in a canyon. The crackle of trees snapping as their sap boiled. She imagined Kael hearing it, the way her sister would lift her head, her face going slack with that specific, terrifying recognition that the world was ending at the edges. Would Kael run? Kael, who had planted a garden that year, who had bought a new roof, who had said in the last letter, with a sharpness that cracked like ice, *I'm fixing the porch. I'm staying. Let the world rot.*

Mara closed her eyes. She remembered the last time she had been at the cabin. It was three winters ago. The wind had howled around the eaves, threatening to tear the roof off. She had come down with firewood, with whiskey, with words she had rehearsed for the drive up. *We need to talk. Things are hard. It doesn't have to be this way.* She had climbed the steps, her hand on the railing, and stopped. She had looked through the window and seen Kael sitting in the chair by the fire, a book in her lap, her eyes closed, her face turned away from the door. The stillness of the figure had been a wall more impenetrable than any storm. Mara had turned around. She had walked back down the steps, her heart hammering against her ribs, and she had driven away. The porch remained un-fixed.

The radio crackled.

Mara jerked back, her heart leaping into her throat. She scrambled to the console, her fingers fumbling with the dial. For a second, the static was clear, then a voice broke through, thin and wavering, buried under the interference. *...repeat... check in... tower five... signal lost...* It was Miller. The voice was ghostly, distant, as if coming from the bottom of a well. Mara keyed the mic, pressed it to her lips. "This is Tower Five. I copy you. Smoke in the drainage. North slope. Kael's cabin. Do you read?"

She held her breath. The voice came back, fragmented. *...Five... read... copy... smoke... we know... resources... diverted... hold... tower...* Then the static swallowed the rest, rising and falling like a tide. The voice was gone.

Mara released the button. We know. Resources diverted. Hold.

Hold. As if holding was something she could do from here. As if holding meant anything when the world was burning. The radio had spoken its last word before dying completely, the needle dropping to zero with a soft *thwip*. The silence returned, heavier now, charged with the unspoken command. Hold.

She looked at the cabin again. The smoke was obscuring it now, the view reduced to gray and white. She could not see if the fire had reached the structure. She could not see if there was movement, a figure darting between trees, the flash of metal. Panic, cold and sharp, pricked at her veins. She wanted to shout, to throw the window open and scream until her voice tore, to warn the air itself. She wanted to jump the catwalk, slide down the pole, hit the road, run until her legs gave out.

But the road was eight miles. The fire didn't wait for legs. The fire was faster than her, faster than fear, faster than regret. It consumed everything with a democratic indifference, the pines and the cabins, the lies and the truths. It would take the blue paint and the tin box of letters with equal ease.

Mara sat on the bunk, the springs groaning. She pulled the tin box from beneath her and set it on her lap. She opened the lid. The letters were there, bundled in rubber bands, the edges soft as cloth. She didn't open them. She didn't need to. She knew the cadence of Kael's hand, the way the slant of the letters would shift depending on the mood, the sharp cross of the T's, the loop of the K's that looked like a noose. She had read them once, in the beginning, when the guilt was fresh and the silence was a wound that bled. Now she knew they were there. Their existence was the tether she refused to cut, even though she refused to pull.

The heat in the cupola intensified. The iron stove radiated warmth even though the fire was banked low. Mara wiped her forehead with the back of her hand. Her skin was hot, dry. She felt dehydrated, not just from the air, but from the withholding of herself. She had spent four summers giving the view to the sky, her body to the job, her heart to the work, and she had kept her sister at the distance of a mountain ridge. She had thought she was protecting herself. She had thought she was protecting Kael too, by not dragging her into the vertical world, the world of departure and distance.

*You watch everything from up there,* Kael had written in one of the letters, the ink pressed so hard the paper had torn. *You stand in your glass box and look down on everything. You see the whole picture and you do nothing. You see the fire and you write it down. You see the pain and you write it down. You never reach out. You never come down.*

The words echoed in the small space. Mara looked at her hands. They were stained with oil, the nails filed to the quick, the knuckles swollen. These hands had chopped wood, hauled water, repaired the roof, signed the logbook. These hands had pushed Kael on the tire swing, had held a flashlight while Kael stitched a wound, had waved goodbye at the trailhead. They were capable hands. They were just afraid.

The wind shifted again. The smoke pulled back, revealing the cabin once more. It was surrounded by a ring of blackened earth, the trees around it skeletal and standing. But the roof held. The smoke was curling over it, not engulfing it. The fire was in the draw, moving along the valley floor, following the duff and the dry grass, skirting the higher ground where the cabin sat on a shelf of granite. The cabin was safe. For now.

Mara let out a breath she didn't know she was holding. Her shoulders slumped. The relief was instant, followed by a wave of exhaustion so profound she felt her bones dissolve. She leaned her elbows on the sill and rested her head in her arms. The tower rocked gently, a slow pendulum motion, as if the mountain itself was breathing.

She stayed like that for a long time, the sun moving across the sky, the light changing from white to gold to the deep orange of late afternoon. The smoke began to thin, dispersing at the higher altitudes, becoming a haze that dulled the horizon. The fire would burn itself out or be contained by the creek or the rock bands. The immediate threat had passed. The cabin held. Kael was likely alive, standing on that porch, watching the gray cloud, her heart hammering, her hands shaking, wondering if the sky was falling.

Mara straightened up. She looked at the radio. She looked at the door. The idea of the eight-mile climb was no longer a barrier; it was a path. The fear of the road, the gravel, the distance, it all seemed trivial compared to the silence she had been living in. The radio was dead, but the line wasn't. The line was blood. The line was memory. The line was the fact that if the fire had taken the cabin, she would have carried the ash in her throat for the rest of her life. She would have watched the rest of her life from the tower, from every high place, always looking down, always seeing too much, always doing too little.

She moved to the desk and picked up the pen. Her hand shook slightly as she wrote.

*July 14. 1600. Smoke column in Blackwood Draw. Fire contained by geology. Cabin status undetermined but likely intact. Radio failure at 0915. Signal lost. No further comms possible.*

She paused, then added a line below, her handwriting jagged, the ink flowing dark and decisive.

*Final patrol scheduled. Departure delayed. Awaiting road conditions. Priorities reassessed.*

It was a lie, and Kael would know it. It was the first true thing she had written in three years. Priorities reassessed. The words sat on the page like a stone dropped into a well, creating ripples that would reach the bottom and return.

Mara capped the pen and set it down. She went to the stove and added a stick of pine, the resin sizzling and popping. The smell of burning wood filled the cupola, familiar and grounding. She moved to the supply closet and began to pull out the heavy pack, the one she used for trail inspections. She checked the straps, the buckles, the water bladder. She loaded the first aid kit, the knife, the compass. She packed the tin box of letters.

She worked with a methodical energy that chased away the lethargy. The tower, which had felt like a cage, was now a launchpad. The wood that groaned was not complaining; it was singing a song of departure. The view was still beautiful, still vast, but she no longer needed to stand in it to understand it. She had understood the view. Now she had to understand the ground.

Night fell, and the tower became a lantern against the dark. The smoke had dissipated into a low layer of clouds that hid the stars, but the fire was visible on the ridge lines, glowing like embers in a hearth miles away. Red flashes pulsed in the darkness, silent and rhythmic. Mara watched them from the catwalk, the night air cool on her face, the smell of smoke clinging to her hair. She could see the road now, a dark ribbon winding down into the gloom. Eight miles. It was just a number. It was just wood and stone and air.

She thought of Kael on the porch, the light of the fire reflected in her eyes, the way she would stand and point at the sky and say, *Look. It's coming. We're still here.* Or perhaps Kael was inside, making tea, checking the windows, her face set in that mask of independence that was really just fear in disguise. *I'm fixing the porch. I'm staying.*

Mara touched the railing, the iron rough under her fingers. "I'm coming down," she whispered to the dark. "I'm coming down."

The words felt strange in her mouth, but they held a weight that the silence never had. They were an anchor dropped in the deep water of their estrangement. They were a bridge built of muscle and will. She knew Kael might not open the door. She knew the porch might be broken, the paint might be gone, the house might be gone. She knew she might stand at the bottom of the stairs and find only ashes. But she would stand there. She would climb down.

The final summer was ending. The service was ending. But something else was beginning. The decomposition of the tower, the shedding of the old life, it was all part of the process. She was not leaving the world; she was leaving the distance. She was coming back to the place where the red dirt turned to clay, where the wind died, where the coordinates pointed to a smudge on a map that meant everything.

Mara went back inside and turned off the lights. The cupola was dark, save for the glow of the fire in the ridge. She lay on the bunk, the pack at her feet, the radio silent, the road waiting. Her body ached, her mind buzzed with the images of the smoke, the cabin, the face in the window. She closed her eyes and saw the path of the eight miles not as a descent, but as an approach. Every switchback was a step toward the cabin. Every mile was a word spoken aloud. Every hour of darkness was a breath held before the knock.

The tower creaked, a long, low sound like a sigh. Mara smiled in the dark. It was not a complaint. It was a release. The wood was settling, making room for the wind. The structure had held her, watched over her, let her be the ghost in the sky. Now it was time to go back to the earth, to the heat of the day, the cold of the night, the fire that burned clean, and the sister who waited below.

She slept, and in her dreams, the radio crackled to life, not with static, but with a voice she knew, clear and warm and steady, saying, *I see the light. I've got the door open. Come on down.*

Mara woke before dawn, the sky turning the color of wet slate. The air was still, the fire in the valley extinguished, leaving only the smell of wet ash and the promise of green. She swung her legs off the bunk, the boots already at the door. Her hand went to the radio one last time, her fingers brushing the dead dial. "Thank you," she said softly. "For the years. For the silence. For the warning."

She picked up the pack, shouldered it, and walked to the trap door. The iron pole gleamed in the dim light. She didn't use the door; she used the pole, sliding down the thirty feet of descent with the rush of air against her skin, the friction warming her palms, the speed dissolving the last vestiges of hesitation. She hit the ground running, the gravel crunching under her boots, the trail rising from the tower like a road of pine needles and earth.

Eight miles. She took the first step, and the mountain held her, not to keep her, but to give her footing. She looked up once, at the tower standing tall against the gray sky, a sentinel of the past. Then she turned and walked down, her breath steaming in the cold air, her heart beating a rhythm that matched the drums of the descent, louder and faster, until it was the only sound in the world, a voice saying *down, down, down, to her.*

The smoke was gone. The road was clear. The summer was ending. And for the first time in years, Mara was not watching the fire; she was walking toward it, ready to face the heat, ready to find Kael, ready to burn the distance between them until only the ash of what was true remained. The final summer would be the first of a new season. She stepped onto the switchbacks, the gravel biting, the climb ahead, and she did not look back.
