The invitation had arrived on heavy cream stock, the embossed lettering catching the afternoon light like a small, polite defiance. It had been fourteen years, I calculated, since the smoke had come down the stairwell like a slow, grey tide, since the fire doors had held, since the sprinklers had failed, since I had stood in the hallway in my grey uniform, the wool heavy with damp, and listened to the glass in the lobby window bow inward and shatter in a sound that seemed to come from the earth itself. They had called it an electrical fault. They had called it a tragedy. They had called it a opportunity for reinvention. I had called it nothing. You do not name the thing that strips you down to the bone and leaves you standing in the street in your socks while the sky turns the color of old ash. You simply count the steps back to the curb, you count the breaths, you count the seconds until the sirens stop and the silence returns, heavier than before.

Now I drove past the wrought-iron gates, past the new fountain that poured water into a basin of polished stone, past the landscaping that had been planted with the careful indifference of a corporation that understood aesthetics but not soil. The hotel stood as it had, or rather as it had always been intended to stand, all limestone and wrought-iron balconies, all symmetry and measured proportions, as if the fire had been a temporary miscalculation, a momentary lapse in the building’s long memory. The new awning was the color of champagne, the new canopy taut and unyielding, the new porte-cochère wide enough to swallow three limousines and still leave room for the illusion of space. I had been issued a key card, not a key, and a map, and a schedule that listed the rehearsal dinner, the ceremony, the cocktail hour, the reception, the breakfast. I had attended the wedding of a cousin once, four years before, and remembered it as a series of correct gestures performed in a room that felt too large for the people in it, as if the architecture had been designed to absorb the joy and excrete the discomfort.

I checked in without speaking much. I had not planned to come. The invitation had arrived by email, forwarded by a friend of a friend, and I had not opened it for three days. I had worked there for eleven months. I had been twenty-two. I had needed the job. The fire had come on a Tuesday. Tuesdays were for deep cleaning. Tuesdays were when the east wing was shut down for carpet shampooing. Tuesdays were when the boiler room was inspected.
