The strait did not sleep; it exhaled in great, gray riffs of brine and diesel, a lung of cold water filling and emptying against the ribs of the iron hull. Elias stood at the rail of the third-deck promenade, the wind plucking at the wool of his coat until the fabric flapped like a wounded bird, and watched the darkness unspool behind them. The overnight crossing was a ritual of suspension, a twenty-two-hour purgatory measured in the groan of gears and the shudder of the propellers that threw the ship's wake in froth toward the north, toward the island, toward the place where the water grew shallow and treacherous and where the police had left a message in his voicemail three days ago, their voices flattened by static and procedure, asking him to identify a body.

*We have found a vessel adrift,* the officer had said. *No salvage rights claimed. We have a body in the infirmary. You are the emergency contact listed for the last address. Please come to the terminal in Skärgården.*

The words had sat in his throat like a stone swallowed whole, traveling down to his stomach and hardening there. He had dialed the number back, his hand shaking enough that he dropped the receiver twice, but the line was silent. Dead. The silence that had filled the space between them for eighteen years, sudden and absolute as a door slamming in a drafty house, was now echoing from the future, from the end of the sea.

Elias turned back from the rail, his reflection ghosting in the porthole glass, a pale smudge superimposed over the black water. He looked at his face, searching for the bone structure that he had spent a lifetime trying to deny. His father had the heavy brow, the nose that kinked at the bridge like a broken branch, the jaw that squared with stubbornness. Elias had the jaw. His brother had the brow and the nose. Now, somewhere in the dark belly of the ship, a man lay stripped of name and dignity, his features perhaps preserved, perhaps ruined, bearing the ghost of that kinked nose.

He walked to the stairwell, his boots finding the rhythm of the deck plates. The ferry was a metal ecosystem of indifference. In the mess hall, a handful of passengers slept with their heads lolling against portholes, their coats draped over the table like shrouds, while a woman in a nurse's uniform hummed softly to herself, wiping a tray with methodical precision. No one knew. No one cared. The world moved on, a vast, rolling continuum of life that did not pause for the possibility of a reunion with a ghost. The indifference was a relief, and a cruelty. If the world knew, if the ship knew, it would feel like a judgment. As it was, Elias felt he was carrying a secret that might destroy the vessel itself.

He returned to his cabin, a cramped box of peeling laminate and a narrow bunk that smelled of industrial lemon and old sweat. He sat on the edge of the mattress, the springs squeaking a protest, and took the ticket from his pocket. The paper was damp. He ran his thumb over the printed details: *Passenger: E. Vinter. Destination: Terminal 4, Skärgården. Cabin 302.* Einar Vinter. The name on the ticket was his own, but the weight behind it belonged to the other. They had shared a womb, a roof, a father's wrath, and a silence that had grown so thick it became a wall, brick by brick, year by year, until neither could remember who laid the first stone.

The estrangement had not been a single event but a slow erosion. It was the summer they were twenty-two and the boat had broken down in the fog, and Elias had laughed when Einar's hands were bleeding from the rope, and Einar had stopped speaking. It was the winter their father died, the call Elias received while he was on a conference call in Stockholm, the way he left the meeting early but still missed the funeral, the way Einar wrote a letter that arrived empty except for a pressed leaf from the oak in the yard. It was the phone calls Elias never made, rationalizing that it was too soon, too late, too awkward, too painful. He had told himself he was giving Einar space, that Einar needed solitude, that Einar was strong enough to weather any storm alone. He had built a life of polite distances, of weekend visits to a city he barely knew, of relationships that ended when he realized he had nothing left to give that wasn't measured in absence.

The ship pitched, a violent lurch that sent Elias's coffee cup rattling in its saucer. The sky above the strait was a heavy bruise, purple and black, and the waves were mounting, slapping the hull with the wet slap of fists demanding entry. The strait was known for its quicksands and its squalls, a throat of water that swallowed trawlers and pleasure craft alike. Einar had lived on the island since childhood. He knew the water. He could read the wind in the rigging of the old fishing boats that still bobbed in the coves. If Einar was out there, if he had gone out in a storm, he would have known. That thought was a knot tightening in Elias's chest. It meant there was no accident. It meant intention.

He closed his eyes and saw the island. The red cottages clinging to the granite slopes, the lighthouse that swept its beam across the water like a slow, indifferent eye. He saw the dock where they had built the raft as boys, the splinters and the sweat and the way Einar's shoulders moved under his shirt, lean and wiry, always ready for action, always running toward the sea while Elias stayed on the shore, watching, calculating, fearing the depth. Einar was the one who dove; Einar was the one who climbed the highest ridge to watch the horizon; Einar was the one who loved with a ferocity that frightened them both. Elias was the one who stayed safe. And now he was sailing across the water to find out if Einar had finally met his match.

Time in the cabin was viscous. He drank water from the tap and watched the digital clock tick through the hours. Sleep was impossible. Every creak of the ship was a footstep; every groan of the timber in the bulkheads was a voice. He thought of the body in the infirmary. What would he find? The sea could be merciful, smoothing the features with the patience of a sculptor. Or it could be cruel, bloating the flesh, turning the face into a mask of alien distortion. He had never examined a corpse before. He had no frame of reference for the reality of death, only the abstract concept he had avoided like the tide itself. He imagined the hands. Einar's hands were scarred, the knuckles swollen from years of handling nets and engines, the left index finger crooked from a fracture that never set right. He would look for the finger. He would need to look for the finger.

Around four in the morning, the rain began. It hammered the deck above, a thousand distinct strikes that blended into a roaring roar, drowning out the engine's thrum. Elias lit a cigarette he had found in the ashtray of the compartment, inhaling the smoke until his lungs burned, a sharp pain to anchor him in the present. Through the porthole, the world was reduced to a swirling gray mist. The ferry cut through the darkness, a steel needle stitching the night, but Elias felt unmoored, drifting in the space between memory and expectation.

He remembered the last time he saw Einar. It was ten years ago, in Stockholm, at a café near the water. Einar had come down with a map, his eyes bright and desperate, talking about a deal, about buying a plot of land, about starting over, about how the island was dying and the taxes were killing it and he needed Elias to sign, to co-sign, to be the brother who could make the numbers work. Elias had looked at his watch, at the emails on his phone, at the polished shoes of the man he had become, and he had felt the walls rise. He had said no, gently, firmly, using words that were polite and hollow. Einar had looked at him then, really looked at him, with an expression that wasn't anger but a profound, weary disappointment. *You're just like him,* Einar had said, his voice low. *You're just a man who hides behind doors.* Einar had left the map on the table, folded neatly, and walked out. Elias had never called him back. The map remained, and with it, the silence.

The rain eased as dawn approached, leaving the sky a bruised peach bleeding into pale blue. The ship slowed, the vibration in the soles of Elias's feet changing pitch as the water shallowed. They were approaching the Skärgården. He checked his reflection again. He had shaved, he thought he had, but his face felt rough. His eyes were sunken, rimmed with the red irritation of no sleep. He looked like a stranger. He looked like a man who had come to close a door.

He stood and moved to the window, pressing his palm against the cold glass. In the distance, the island emerged from the mist, a dark shape against the brightening sky. The lighthouse stood sentinel on the headland, its beam swinging, indifferent to his arrival. The ferry banked, turning toward the concrete arms of the terminal, and Elias felt a sudden, irrational urge to turn back, to run to the lifeboat, to surrender to the sea rather than face the morgue. The fear was not just of the body; it was of the confirmation. If the body was not Einar, he would have to sail back to the city, to his quiet life, to the slow death of regret without the release of grief. If the body was Einar, he would have to carry the weight of the end, the finality of the estrangement, the truth that his brother had died alone and unvisited.

The ship docked with a shudder of metal on metal, the mooring lines thrown with the practiced grace of the crew, snapping taut and humming. The gangway extended, a steel tongue reaching out to the quay. Passengers began to stir, packing bags, zipping cases, the mundane rituals of life resuming. Elias waited until the crowd thinned, until the terminal gates were open. He moved through the crowd, his gait stiff, his hands buried deep in his pockets, fingers clutching the ticket and the small object he had brought. A silver flask. Einar's flask. They had drunk from it as boys, mixing aquavit with the sea water for a joke, a prank that had made them both sick. Elias had kept it all these years, a talisman of a time before the walls, a piece of Einar he had never surrendered.

He exited the terminal into the biting air of the harbor. The dock was bustling with trucks, with the shouts of stevedores, with the cry of gulls fighting for scraps. The police car was waiting near the administrative building, a blue box of authority in the graying light. Two officers stood by the entrance, one smoking, the other checking a watch. As Elias approached, they straightened. The one with the cigarette flicked it away.

"Mr. Vinter?"

"Yes."

"Please come with us."

They led him through the service corridors of the terminal, past the loading bays, through a heavy door marked *Authorized Personnel Only*. The air here was cooler, smelling of wet concrete and disinfectant. They walked down a hallway lined with doors, the sound of their footsteps echoing sharply. At the end of the hall, a nurse in scrubs waited by a door. She looked at the police officer, then at Elias. Her expression was professional, unreadable.

"Mr. Vinter? I am Ingrid. I am sorry to bring you here."

"Is it him?" Elias heard his voice crack, a dry rasp. He cleared his throat. "Is it Einar?"

"We have recovered a body from a boat identified by registration as belonging to Einar Vinter. The condition is poor. We cannot confirm identity without your view. We are prepared to show you."

"Show me."

Ingrid unlocked the door and stepped aside. The room was small, tiled in white, lit by fluorescent tubes that hummed with a sickly buzz. In the center stood a stainless-steel table. On it lay the figure, covered from the neck down by a blue cotton sheet. The face was visible, turned slightly toward the door.

Elias stopped in the doorway. The nurse's hand was on his elbow, a light, steadying grip. He pulled away, gently, and walked forward. The floor was cold through his soles. He stood beside the table, and the world narrowed to the rectangle of white tiles and the shape beneath the cloth.

The face was swollen, the skin gray-green, the lips pulled back to reveal teeth that were dark with age and decay. The hair was matted with salt and debris. It was a stranger's face, a mask of rot. Panic flared in Elias's chest, hot and sharp. *It isn't him. It's just a body. It's just a body.* The relief was immediate and nauseating, followed instantly by a wave of guilt so strong he had to lean against the wall. If it wasn't Einar, then Einar was alive. The thought was a lifeline, but it was also a trap. If it wasn't Einar, why had the police called him? Why the registration?

"Look for the scars," the nurse whispered. "The hands."

Elias looked down at the hands. They were resting on the sheet, the fingers curled inward, the nails black. The sea had taken much, but it had left the hands. The skin was leathery, contracted, but the structure was there. The left hand was splayed, palm up. The knuckles were swollen, the joints enlarged. And there, on the index finger, the bone jutted out at a sharp angle, the crooked finger from the trap accident.

Elias stared at the finger. The air left his lungs. The noise of the room faded, replaced by the sound of the wind in the rigging, the creak of wood, the laughter of boys on a dock. He reached out, his hand trembling, and touched the cold flesh of the knuckle. The skin was hard, unyielding. It was real. It was Einar.

He looked back at the face. He forced himself to look past the decay, past the swelling, to the bone structure beneath. The heavy brow, the kinked nose, the jaw that squared with stubbornness. He saw the scar above the left eyebrow, a white line from the time he had fallen from the apple tree and Einar had stitched it with fishing thread. He saw the way the ear was notched at the lobe, a mark of birth that he had seen in every mirror he had ever stood before.

It was him. It was Einar.

A sound escaped Elias, a low keening that he couldn't suppress. He bent over the table, his knees buckling, and caught himself on the edge of the steel. The metal was freezing. He reached into his pocket and pulled out the flask. His fingers fumbled with the cap, the metal cold and slippery. He twisted it open, the sound loud in the quiet room, and brought the silver to his lips. He didn't drink. He just held it there, the taste of the old aquavit rising in his memory, the salt of their foolishness, the warmth of a time when they were two halves of a single heart.

"It's you," he whispered. The words were thin, useless against the silence of the dead. "It's you, Einar."

He looked at the flask, then at the face. "I have it. I have the flask."

He thought of the map on the table in the café. He thought of the land he didn't buy. He thought of the letters he didn't write. He thought of the years he spent telling himself he was protecting Einar from the world, when in truth he was protecting himself from the truth that he had no life of his own, no depth, no sea. Einar had gone out into the storm. Perhaps he had sought the boat, sought the water, sought the only thing that could hold him. Perhaps he had found the island, the land, the struggle, and it had broken him. Or perhaps he had just come to the end, and the sea had taken him, and the police had done their job, and Elias had done nothing until the call came.

The nurse made a soft noise of sympathy but said nothing. The protocol was silent. There was nothing to do but look.

Elias straightened up. His body felt heavy, anchored by the gravity of the truth. He looked at the flask in his hand. He reached out and opened the small cloth pouch that lay on the table beside the hands. It was part of the evidence bag, containing the personal effects retrieved from the boat. He poured the contents into his palm. A compass with a cracked glass face. A pair of spectacles with one lens missing. A crumpled photograph of a woman with laughing eyes. A small knife. And the flask.

Einar had come back. He had brought the flask. He had brought their past with him to the end.

Elias closed his fingers over the objects. The flask was cold, but the metal warmed slowly under his grip. He looked at his brother's face one last time. He saw the pain etched in the tightness of the jaw, the exhaustion in the set of the eyes. He saw the man who had loved the sea more than he could ever understand, the man who had stayed behind and withered in the silence Elias had created. Elias had thought the silence was his own creation, his shield. But looking at Einar, he realized the silence had been a shared thing, a vessel they had both sailed in, drifting further apart with every passing day, until the strait between them was not water but time and regret.

"I'm sorry," Elias said. The words were inadequate, a pebble thrown into a well. He bent and touched the forehead of the dead man, his fingers brushing the matted hair. "I'm here. I'm here now."

He took the flask and the photo and the compass and put them in his pocket, against his heart. He picked up the crumpled map that lay near the hand, the edges soft with salt. He smoothed it out on the edge of the table. It was the map from the café, the one he had left behind. It was folded tightly, wrapped in plastic, a part of the belongings. Einar had kept it. All these years, he had kept the map. He had kept the hope that Elias would sign, that they would fix it, that the bridge would hold. The hope had died with him, or perhaps it had sustained him until the water became the only reality.

Elias looked at the map, then at Einar. The grief arrived not as a wave but as a tide, rising slowly, filling the hollows of his chest, the spaces he had left empty. It was a grief for the brother he lost, for the years wasted, for the safety he had prized over love. It was a grief that would never subside, that would sit in him like the strait beneath the ship, dark and deep and endless.

He turned away from the table. His legs felt like wood, but he forced them to move. He nodded to the nurse, who looked at him with eyes full of a shared humanity. He followed the police officer back through the corridors, out to the light of the harbor.

The sun was up now, burning off the mist, revealing the red cottages on the island, the green roofs, the smoke rising from the chimneys. Life went on. The ferry was disembarking, its belly emptying of the living. The world did not stop. But Elias was changed. He carried the flask against his side, the weight of it a compass pointing true. He had identified the body. He had closed the door. But as he walked toward the car park, toward the ferry that would take him back to the city, he knew he was returning with a piece of the sea, with a map that was no longer blank, with a brother who had waited, finally, for the end.

The strait lay behind him, vast and breathing. He would cross it again, not as a fugitive from memory, but as a man who had looked into the dark and found the face he had tried to forget. The estrangement was over. The silence was broken. There was only the road ahead, the weight of the flask, and the long, slow work of learning how to carry the water without drowning.
