The wind did not so much blow as scour, dragging the last of April’s promised thaw back into the sky in thick, bruising ribbons of cloud. Snow came down not in flakes but in horizontal sheets, struck against the tin roof of the lambing shed with a sound like loose gravel poured from a moving truck. Inside, the air was thick with the smell of damp wool, iodine, and the iron-sweet tang of birth. Elara knelt on a folded burlap sack, her knees pressing through to the packed dirt floor, her hands buried in the cradle of a ewe’s hindquarters. The ewe trembled, then stilled, then gave a long, low shudder that ran through her entire frame. A head emerged, wet and dark, followed by two forefeet tucked beneath a nose already gasping. Elara guided without thinking, her wrists adjusting to the angle, her thumbs pressing where they needed to, her breath counting the seconds between contractions. Behind her, the shed stretched into rows of wooden pens, each holding a ewe waiting her turn, each carrying the quiet, desperate mathematics of spring. Seventeen hours in. Her shoulders felt wired tight, her lower back a dull, grinding ache that had long since stopped being pain and become simply the shape of her body. She cleaned the lamb’s face with her sleeve, rubbed its ribs until it sneezed, then placed it in a heated box lined with straw and a borrowed hot-water bottle. It cried, a thin, reedy sound that cut through the wind’s drone. Elara exhaled, wiped her palms on her trousers, and turned to the next ewe.

Liam was across the aisle, knee-deep in his own work. At sixteen, he had Thomas’s jawline and his mother’s hands, though his were already broadening, already learning the calluses that would map their own territory. He moved with a careful, deliberate economy, as if each motion had to be weighed before release. He was delivering his second lamb of the night. The ewe was a veteran, her teats stained with the history of past seasons, but the lamb was breech, its hips wedged tight against the birth canal. Liam’s face was pale under the sickly yellow glow of the kerosene lamp he had lit an hour ago, when the overhead fluorescents had flickered once, twice, then surrendered to darkness. Now there was only the lamp’s glass globe, the wind’s relentless drumming, and the low, rhythmic bleating of the flock. He wiped sweat from his brow with his forearm, leaving a smear of lanolin and dirt. His gloves were gone, discarded hours ago, his fingers now bare and raw from the cold and the repeated scrubbing of antiseptic. He did not complain. He never did. That was what frightened Elara most.

The power had died at seven. She had been at the sink, washing out the delivery tub, when the lights went out with a soft click, as if the house itself had exhaled. For a moment she had stood in the dark, waiting for the generator to kick in, waiting for the hum of the water pump, waiting for the familiar, grounding reality of modern farm infrastructure. But there was only the wind, the sudden silence of the fridge, and the distant, panicked cry of a ewe in the north pasture. Then she had found the kerosene, the matches, the spare bulbs. Then she had walked out into the shed, through the snow-driven doorway, into the cold, into the work that did not stop for blackouts or grief. The storm had arrived without warning, a late-season tempest that the forecaster had called a zero chance of. It did not care about forecasts. It did not care about the calendar, or the fact that April was supposed to be thaw, or the fact that Thomas was not here to check the weather radio, or to laugh at the absurdity of snow in lambing season, or to put a hand on her shoulder and say, We’ll manage. We always do.

She never said his name aloud. Not here. Not now. The shed was too full of him. His worn leather apron still hung on the peg by the door, its leather soft from years of bending, its pockets holding the ghosts of twine, pocket knives, and dried apple slices. His thermos sat on the shelf, still warm from the tea he had made an hour ago, before he had left to check the storm shutters and never came back to drink it. She kept it there because moving it felt like erasing the last physical evidence that he had been in this room, in this season, in this life. She worked around the absence the way she worked around a stubborn stone: by adjusting her pace, by learning its shape, by never pretending it wasn’t there. Grief, she had learned, was not a wave that broke and receded. It was a current, pulling at the edges of every task, every breath, every moment when her hands reached for a tool he had used yesterday, or when she heard a sound that might have been his boots on the gravel drive. There were no more such sounds. Only the wind, the lambs, the ewes, and the slow, relentless arithmetic of survival.

Liam shifted his weight, grunting softly as he adjusted his grip on the lamb’s haunches. His breathing was shallow, tight. He was pushing now, not just pulling, using his own body weight, his legs braced against the wooden pen. Elara watched him, her own hands still inside the next ewe, feeling the amniotic sac give way, then tear, then slip past her knuckles. She could see the strain in his neck, the way his jaw clenched, the way his shoulders hunched forward as if carrying the weight of the lamb outside of himself. He was trying to be him. That was the unspoken thing between them, hanging in the lamplight like dust motes. Liam was trying to be the man who knew how to handle a breech without panicking, who knew when to stop and wait, who knew that some lambs were born with the strength to push themselves out, and that forcing them could break bones or drown them in fluid. Thomas had taught her that. He had also taught Liam. Thomas had stood exactly where Elara stood now, hands steady, voice calm, saying, Watch the shoulders. Don’t rush the shoulders. Let them find their way. And when Liam had asked how he knew, Thomas had smiled, that quiet, crooked smile that had made her heart ache even in the daylight, and said, Because I’ve done this too many times to count. Because the sheep will tell you if you listen. Because you don’t fight the work. You follow it.

Elara followed the work now. She worked the lamb out inch by inch, her forearms burning, her breath coming in short, controlled bursts. When the body slipped free, she caught it before it hit the floor, supported its weight, rubbed its back vigorously until the lungs expanded, until the first real cry came out, loud and insistent. She cleared the nostrils with her thumb, checked the umbilical cord for bleeding, then placed the lamb in the box beside its sister. They began to nurse immediately, their little mouths searching, their bodies steaming in the cold air. Elara stepped back, her knees protesting, her hands trembling slightly from the adrenaline and the cold. She wiped her face with the back of her wrist, leaving a streak of white wool fat across her cheekbone. She did not look at Liam. She looked at the next ewe, who was already crowning, her head tilted back, her eyes wide and dark. The work did not wait. The sheep did not care about grief. They only cared about the next breath, the next step, the next life. And if you stopped, the cold would win. The storm would take the fences, the feed, the trust of the land. The bank would not take a season of losses. Neither would the soil. So she moved forward.

Liam finished with his lamb, wrapping it in a dry towel, tucking it beside its mother, then standing slowly, his joints popping. He pulled the kerosene lamp closer, tilting the flame so it cast longer shadows across the dirt floor. His hands were shaking now, he could feel it. Not from fear. From exhaustion. From the long hours of holding back tears, of swallowing words, of pretending that the empty space beside him was just space, not a wound. He had not slept properly in three days. Not since the storm warning came. Not since he had woken to the silence where his father’s breathing used to be, and walked down the hall, past the closed door, past the tools hanging on the wall, past the calendar with the last visit circled in blue pen. He had not cried. He had packed his bag, filled the truck with hay, driven to the shed, and started working. That was what you did. That was what his father would have wanted. That was what his mother needed. Even if she did not ask. Even if she never said it out loud. He knew the look in her eyes when she thought he wasn’t watching. The tightness around her mouth. The way her shoulders pulled forward, as if bracing against a weight only she could feel. He had learned to read her the way he had learned to read the weather: by the pressure in the air, by the shift in her voice, by the sudden stillness that meant the storm was inside her now, and he had to be the wall.

The wind picked up, hitting the side of the shed with a force that made the wooden walls shudder. Snow piled against the eaves, then slid in a sudden avalanche, slamming against the door with a heavy, muffled thud. The door frame groaned. Elara pressed her back against the pen to steady herself, her breath catching. The shed was old. It had survived forty years of seasons, of winters that buried the tractor to its axle, of summers that cracked the clay, of births and deaths and everything in between. But it was not built for this. Not for snow that fell like hammer strikes. Not for wind that screamed through the cracks. Not for a power line that had likely gone down miles back, taking the pumps, the heaters, the generator’s fuel feed with it. She did not know how long the cold would last. She did not know how many ewes were stuck in the lower pasture. She did not know if the feed bales were exposed, if the water troughs had frozen solid, if the lambs in the far boxes would survive the night without the heat lamp. She knew only this: they would keep working. They would keep pulling lambs, cleaning them, feeding them, waiting for the storm to pass. And if it did not pass, they would adapt. They always did.

Liam moved to the next ewe without speaking. He did not need to. The rhythm of the work was enough. It was a language they had both learned, not through words, but through repetition, through shared silence, through the way a hand could rest on a shoulder for three seconds and say everything. He knelt beside the ewe, his knees cracking, his breath pluming in the cold. The lamb was coming quickly this time, as if the storm had rushed its own calendar. Liam worked with a new urgency, his movements sharper, less careful, as if time itself was compressing. He felt it in his bones. The way the floor tilted. The way his hands moved before his mind could catch up. He reached inside, felt the amniotic sac, the slick slide of fluid, the firm push of shoulders. He gripped, pulled, held, released. The ewe pushed. He pushed back. The head emerged. The shoulders. The body. He caught it, turned it, rubbed it, placed it. It was over in minutes. In his previous season, it would have taken longer. He would have waited. He would have listened. He would have trusted the process. Now he only trusted speed. He did not know if that was courage or desperation. He did not care. The lamb cried. The mother licked it. He stepped back, wiped his face, and moved to the next pen.

Elara watched him from across the aisle. She saw the way his hands trembled when he thought no one was looking. She saw the way his breath hitched when a lamb cried too loudly, or when the wind hit a certain pitch. She saw the way he avoided the space where Thomas’s stool still sat, empty, waiting for hands that would not return. She wanted to tell him to rest. She wanted to tell him to drink the tea. She wanted to tell him that he did not have to carry this alone. But the words caught in her throat, thick and heavy, like wool wrapped around stone. Instead, she said, Check the umbilical on the third pen. Watch for the white cord. Tell me if it’s bleeding. Her voice was flat, practical, stripped of everything else. It was the only way she knew how to speak without breaking. Liam nodded, already moving, already absorbed in the work. The work was safer than grief. The work did not ask for permission. The work did not mourn. The work only asked you to keep going.

The night deepened. The lamplight flickered as the kerosene ran low. Elara refilled it, her fingers numb, her movements slow. She did not look at the calendar on the wall. She did not look at the photo of Thomas in his work boots, grinning in the summer field, his arm around her shoulders, his thumb wiping a smudge of dirt from her cheek. She looked at the ewes. She looked at the lambs. She looked at the snow piling against the windows, thick and relentless, as if the sky were trying to bury the shed, the sheep, the memory of them. She did not pray. She did not make promises. She only worked. She worked the way Thomas had taught her, with patience, with precision, with a quiet respect for the life unfolding between her hands. She worked the way a mother works, with a fierce, unyielding love that did not need to be spoken to be felt. She worked the way the land demanded, with a stubbornness that had outlasted droughts, and floods, and winters that had taken more than they should have. She worked because there was no other option. She worked because stopping would mean admitting that the storm had won. She worked because Liam was watching, because he needed to see that she could still stand, could still hold her ground, could still be the woman who knew how to bring life into the world, even when the world tried to take it away.

Liam’s voice broke the silence. It was quiet, strained, barely audible over the wind. Mums. The ewe in pen four. The lamb’s head’s stuck. Not the shoulders. The head. Tilted. I can’t get it. Elara did not hesitate. She wiped her hands on her trousers, crossed the aisle, knelt beside him, and placed her hands over his. Her fingers were cold, but steady. Let me see. He stepped back, gave her the space, did not argue. She reached inside, her knuckles pressing against the ewe’s flesh, feeling the tilt, the angle, the wrongness. It was a posterior presentation. The lamb’s head was turned back, its nose against its own back. It would not birth itself this way. It would drown in the fluid before it could breathe. She had seen it before. Thomas had corrected it a dozen times. She had done it herself. She would do it now. She shifted her angle, found the head, guided it forward, then down, then out, feeling the moment when the bones aligned, when the path cleared, when the lamb slipped free with a sudden, fluid motion. She caught it, turned it, rubbed it, placed it. It cried. The mother licked it. Elara exhaled, her shoulders dropping, her breath coming in a slow, steady rhythm. She did not look at Liam. She did not need to. He knew. They both knew. The work continued.

The storm began to change. Not dramatically. Not with a sudden stillness or a parting of clouds. But with a subtle shift in the wind’s pitch, a drop in the pressure against the roof, a softening of the snow’s impact. Elara felt it in her teeth, in the soles of her boots, in the way the lamplight no longer trembled as violently. She did not look at the window. She did not check the weather. She only listened. The wind was still there, but it was no longer fighting. It was moving past. The snow was still falling, but it was no longer driving. It was settling. The storm was breaking. Or at least, it was yielding. She did not celebrate. She did not breathe easier. She only worked. She worked because the lambing was not over. She worked because there were still ewes in labor, still lambs to clean, still boxes to heat, still hours until dawn. She worked because the land did not pause for storms, or for grief, or for the sudden, quiet relief that something might finally stop. She worked because life did not.

Liam moved to the next pen without speaking. He did not need to. The rhythm was back, slower now, steadier, as if the shed itself had exhaled. He knelt, reached in, felt the familiar slide of fluid, the firm push of shoulders, the moment of release. He caught the lamb, turned it, rubbed it, placed it. He did not look at his hands. He did not look at his mother. He only looked at the work. The work was enough. The work was all there was. And in that moment, in the yellow lamplight, in the cold air, in the quiet between the wind’s moan and the lambs’ cries, he understood something he had not been able to name. Grief was not a wall. It was a current. It pulled at you, yes. It made you heavier. It made you slower. But it did not stop you. It only changed the way you moved. It taught you to adjust your balance, to find your footing, to keep going. It taught you that you did not have to be strong. You only had to be present. And presence was enough. Presence was everything.

Elara refilled the lamp. The flame steadied. The shadows lengthened. She did not look at the photo. She did not look at the calendar. She only looked at the ewes. They were slowing now. The frantic energy of the night was giving way to a quieter, deeper rhythm. The lambs were nursing. The mothers were resting. The shed was warm with breath and life. Outside, the snow continued to fall, but it was no longer a weapon. It was a blanket. It covered the fields, the fences, the tracks, the evidence of the day’s work. It would melt. It would soak into the soil. It would feed the grass. It would make the next season possible. Life did not end with storms. It only endured them. And endurance was not a virtue. It was a practice. A daily, relentless, unglamorous practice. A practice of getting up. A practice of reaching in. A practice of pulling life into the world, again and again, even when your hands were raw, even when your heart was heavy, even when the sky tried to bury you.

Liam stood, stretching his back, wincing slightly. He walked to the sink, filled a cup with water from the bucket, drank it slowly, then poured the rest over his hands. The water was cold. It burned. He did not flinch. He dried his hands on a towel, then walked to the shelf, picked up the thermos, unscrewed the cap, and poured two cups of tea. He handed one to Elara. She took it, her fingers brushing his, her breath catching for a fraction of a second. She did not look at him. She only drank. The tea was hot. It burned her tongue. It grounded her. She set the cup down, wiped her mouth, and turned to the next ewe. Liam did the same. They worked in silence. They worked with the quiet certainty of people who had stopped fighting the current and learned to swim with it. The storm outside faded to a whisper. The lambs slept. The mothers rested. The shed held them. And somewhere, in the space between the work and the weight, in the space between the grief and the grace, they found their balance. Not perfect. Not complete. But enough. For now. For always.
