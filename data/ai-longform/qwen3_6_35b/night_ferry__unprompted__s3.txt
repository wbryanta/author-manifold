The hull complained first, a low groan of rivets and steel plates sliding past one another, before the sea began its slow, deliberate work of pulling the ferry through the strait. Elias stood at the porthole, his forehead resting against the cold curved glass, watching the water darken from the bruised grey of late afternoon into the flat, ink-black of the northern night. The window fogged at his touch, then cleared as he wiped it with his sleeve. Out there, beyond the thin pane, the current moved with a quiet insistence, dragging kelp and foam and the occasional floating crate past the ship’s flank. Somewhere in that water, a body might be waiting. He told himself he was going to verify a name. A case number. A set of dental records. He told himself it was administrative. The truth was heavier, settled in his sternum like a stone, and it did not care about administrative categories.

The ferry was an older vessel, bred for cargo and short passages, its interior corridors narrow and utilitarian, lit by fluorescent tubes that flickered when the deck plates vibrated. Elias had paid for a cabin, but he had not slept. He had walked the length of the ship twice, watched the deckhands secure the cranes against the swell, listened to the engine’s monotonous thrum that somehow synchronized with the pulse at his temple. He carried a manila envelope in his coat pocket, sealed with tape, containing a photograph, a medical examiner’s cover sheet, and a folded map marking the coastal facility where the body had been recovered. He had not opened it since the police officer had handed it to him at the precinct door, his voice stripped of ceremony, his eyes fixed on something past Elias’s shoulder. *We don’t know for certain,* the officer had said. *It’s been in the water four days. Identify when you’re ready.*

Elias had said nothing. He had nodded, taken the envelope, and driven north through rain that never quite fell, only hovered, threatening, before dissolving into the exhaust of his own breath. Now the strait lay ahead, a channel of water so cold it seemed to slow time itself. The radio crackled intermittently, voices flat and professional, reporting depth, visibility, current speed. The ship pitched slightly, rolling through a swell that made the glasses in the mess hall chime against their stands. Elias watched the water and thought of Julian.

They had not spoken in seven years. Not since the argument in the kitchen that had ended not with shouting, but with a terrible, final quiet. Julian had left a coffee cup on the counter, half-full, already cold. Elias had walked out without locking the door behind him. That was the pattern: escalation into stillness, stillness into absence, absence into geography. They had lived in different provinces, then different countries, until distance became a substitute for conversation. The last time Elias had seen him, Julian had been wearing a jacket that didn’t fit, his hands trembling slightly, his eyes darting to the window as if measuring the exit. Elias remembered thinking, foolishly, that he could fix it. He had said the wrong thing. Or the right thing, at the wrong time. It didn’t matter now. The space between them had hardened into something unbreakable.

The ferry’s lights dimmed slightly as the crew adjusted for the night passage. Elias pushed off from the porthole, his coat damp at the shoulder, and walked toward the rear of the ship. The corridor narrowed, the walls lined with fire extinguishers and emergency manuals. His footsteps echoed, then were swallowed by the engine noise. He passed a cabin door cracked open, spilling yellow light into the hall. Inside, a woman slept under a wool blanket, her mouth slightly open, one hand curled around a paperback. He closed the door softly behind him.

On the open deck, the air hit him like a physical thing. It was cold enough to taste, metallic and clean, laced with salt and diesel. The sea was rougher out here, the waves climbing in slow, deliberate arcs before collapsing against the hull with a sound like tearing canvas. Elias gripped the railing, his fingers numb through his gloves. He looked toward the horizon. There was nothing but water and sky, merging in the dark, separated only by a faint, wavering line of light from a distant buoy. The strait was narrowing. He could see the shape of the far shore now, a low silhouette against the faint glow of a town he would never visit. The ferry would dock in the morning. Or late night. The schedule said zero three hundred. Time no longer meant anything except the space between heartbeats.

He thought of the day he decided not to answer the phone when Julian’s name flashed on the screen. He had been in a grocery store, standing in the dairy aisle, comparing prices on milk, and the phone had buzzed in his pocket like a trapped insect. He had let it ring out. He had told himself it was better this way. Julian had always been the storm, Elias the shelter, but shelters eventually crack under constant pressure. Julian wanted everything at once: forgiveness, presence, understanding, without offering the small, daily things that make those things possible. Elias had grown tired of being the steady one, the one who absorbed the fallout, who remembered birthdays, who showed up with tools and tea and patience. Julian had called it pity. Elias had called it love. Neither had been entirely right.

A deckhand moved past him, dragging a coiled rope, his boots loud on the wet steel. The man glanced at Elias, nodded once, and disappeared into the shadow of the superstructure. Elias continued to stare at the water. Somewhere below them, a body was suspended in the cold. Or it wasn’t. The letter said *may or may not be*. The uncertainty was a kind of torture he had never considered. Grief he could manage. It had edges, rituals, a shape. Uncertainty was formless, seeping into every corner, turning ordinary moments into minefields. If it was him, Elias would have to look. If it wasn’t, he would have to live with the relief, which might be worse. Relief required gratitude, and gratitude required a future he wasn’t sure he deserved.

He pulled his coat tighter and walked to the railing near the stern, where the wake spread behind them in a churning, phosphorescent trail. The water glowed faintly where the hull cut through it, tiny organisms reacting to the disturbance. It looked like stars fallen into the sea. He remembered a summer they had spent at a lake house, both of them young, both of them reckless. Julian had taught him how to skip stones, his wrist snapping with a precision that belied his usual chaos. *You have to let it go,* Julian had said, releasing the stone, watching it dance across the water. *If you hold on, it just sinks.* Elias had never quite learned how to let things go. He held onto apologies, onto grievances, onto the precise tone of voice from an argument years ago, replaying them until they wore smooth as river stones.

The ferry banked slightly, turning into a channel flanked by dark, tree-covered hills. The strait was narrowing. The ship’s horn sounded, a low, mournful note that rolled across the water and was swallowed by the cliffs. Elias closed his eyes. In the dark behind his eyelids, he saw Julian’s face as it was at twenty-four: all sharp angles and restless energy, a scar above his eyebrow from a bicycle accident, a laugh that came too loud in quiet rooms. Then he saw him at thirty: thinner, quieter, the edges of his charm worn down by whatever had been eating at him from the inside. He saw the last time they had been in the same room: standing in the hallway of a rental house, both of them packing, neither willing to be the first to speak, the silence between them so thick it felt like weather. Julian had walked out. Elias had stayed. He had told himself it was the stronger choice. Strength, he was learning, could be its own kind of cowardice.

He opened his eyes. The deck was empty now, the wind sharper, the ship moving faster. The lights from the far shore were closer, a scattered constellation of yellow and white, reflecting on the water in long, trembling lines. The radio announced in a flat, automated voice that they would reach the terminal in forty minutes. Elias did not feel relief. He felt the weight of the envelope in his pocket, the tape peeling slightly at the corners, the photograph inside turning the paper soft at the edges. He thought about opening it. He did not. Some thresholds are crossed only when you stop hesitating.

He walked back inside, following the corridor toward the lower deck. The air grew warmer, smelling of coffee, damp wool, floor wax. A few passengers dozed in the lounge, wrapped in blankets, faces relaxed in the false comfort of enclosed space. Elias found a chair near the window and sat. The glass was fogged again, but he did not wipe it this time. He watched the condensation spread, then trickle downward, distorting the lights outside into smears of gold and red. He thought of the morgue. He had never been in one. He had watched it on television, on documentaries, in procedural dramas where the camera lingered on faces like masks, where the medical examiner spoke in calm, clinical tones, and the grieving family member collapsed into sobs that seemed to belong to someone else. None of it matched the hollow, aching quiet that lived in his chest now. This was not television. This was not drama. This was a man who had once shared his first bike, his first fear of thunder, his first kiss, his first betrayal. This was a brother who had existed in his periphery for so long that his absence had become a kind of presence, a constant, low-grade vibration in the architecture of his life.

A waitress pushed a cart down the aisle, offering tea and sandwiches. Elias shook his head. She nodded, moved on, her shoes silent on the carpet. The cart clinked softly as it disappeared around the corner. Elias closed his eyes again. He imagined the body. He tried not to. He failed. He saw cold skin, waterlogged fingers, the distortion of features pulled by currents and time. He saw the impossibility of recognition, the way water erases certainty, the way death strips away the stories we tell ourselves about the dead. He had read somewhere that the body remembers nothing of how it died. It only knows the cold. He wondered if Julian had been cold. He wondered if he had been alone. He wondered if he had thought of him at the end. The questions were useless. They were just the mind reaching for footholds in a landslide.

The ship gave a sudden lurch. Elias opened his eyes. The lights flickered, stabilized. Through the window, he could see the terminal now, a long concrete pier stretching into the water, lit by tall, industrial lamps. Boats bobbed at the docks, their hulls groaning against fenders. The strait was behind them. The channel was opening into a wider basin. The ferry was slowing, the engine pitch dropping, the vibration easing into a gentle, rolling motion. They were approaching the berth.

Elias stood. His legs felt heavy, as if the walk back up the stairs had cost him more than it should. He checked his coat, his pocket, the envelope’s position against his ribs. He did not know what he would do when he got there. He did not know how he would walk into that room, how he would speak to the officer, how he would stand in front of a sheet-covered form and decide, once and for all, whether to pull it back. He imagined the sound of the zipper, the rustle of canvas, the first glimpse of a face that might be familiar, might be unrecognizable, might be nothing at all. He imagined his own voice, steady or shaking, saying the words that would either close a door or open one he had kept locked for seven years. He realized, with a quiet shock, that he was not afraid of the identification. He was afraid of what came after. The aftermath of closure, the architecture of a world without him in it, the silence that would follow, deeper and more permanent than the silence between them. He had survived absence. He was not sure he could survive its confirmation.

The ferry bumped against the pontoon. Metal groaned. Ropes were thrown. Boots sounded on the deck above. The announcement came again, softer now, human: *Ladies and gentlemen, we have arrived. Please gather your belongings and disembark via the lower gangway.* No one moved at first. Then the murmurs began, the rustle of coats, the clatter of bags, the collective exhalation of people who had crossed water and were now stepping back onto land. Elias waited. He watched the passengers file out, their faces tired, their steps quick, their eyes already on the world beyond the terminal. He did not move until the last of them had passed. Then he unbuttoned his coat, adjusted his collar, and walked toward the stairs.

The gangway was steep, sloping down to the pier. The air hit him first, colder, cleaner, smelling of wet concrete and distant smoke. He stepped off the ferry, his boots ringing on the metal walkway, then on the concrete. The terminal was a low, functional building, all corrugated steel and frosted glass, lit by harsh fluorescent panels. A single officer stood at the desk inside, looking up as Elias approached. The man’s face was neutral, professional. He handed over a clipboard, a pen, a wristband. *Right room at the back,* he said, tapping a form. *Medical examiner will brief you. Wait outside until called.* Elias nodded, signed, took the wristband, and followed the signs down a long, echoey corridor. The walls were painted a pale, institutional green. The floor was polished concrete, cold through his shoes. He could hear his own breathing, steady now, regulated by habit. He had walked through airports, through courthouses, through hospitals. This felt different. This felt like standing at the edge of a room he had spent years avoiding, only to find the door was open, and he had to walk through it.

He sat on a metal bench in the waiting area, knees together, hands flat on his thighs. He stared at a poster on the wall, advertising ferry safety regulations, its edges peeling, its colors faded. He thought of the lake house again. He thought of the way Julian used to collect sea glass, polishing it on his jeans until it was smooth, then giving it to him with a crooked smile. *For your window,* he had said. *So you always have a piece of the sea.* Elias had kept it. It sat on his desk now, in a drawer he rarely opened, a small, frosted fragment of something that had been shattered and survived. He wondered if Julian had kept anything of his. He wondered if it mattered.

The door at the end of the hall opened. A man in a white coat stepped out, holding a folder. He was in his fifties, his expression tired but not unkind. He looked at Elias, nodded. *Come with me.* Elias stood, followed him down the corridor, past closed doors, past the hum of refrigeration, past the smell of antiseptic and cold metal. The medical examiner’s office was at the end, a small room with a desk, a computer, a filing cabinet, and a window looking out onto the pier. The examiner closed the door behind them. He did not offer tea. He did not make small talk. He placed the folder on the desk, opened it, and slid a single sheet across. *This is the case,* he said. *Recovered from the water yesterday evening. Partially submerged, current brought it into the channel. We’ve done what we can with initial processing. You’re here for positive identification. The body is in Room Two. You may go in. You don’t have to do anything. You just look. I’ll be outside. When you’re ready, come find me.*

Elias did not speak. He took the sheet, folded it, slipped it into his pocket. He followed the examiner to another door, heavier, steel-framed, with a small window at eye level. The examiner unlocked it, stepped aside. *Whenever you’re ready,* he said again, and left, the door clicking shut behind him.

Elias stood in the corridor. The air was cold, sterile, moving in quiet currents. He could hear his own pulse, slow and steady. He walked to the door, pushed it open, and stepped inside.

The room was small, lit by a single overhead lamp that cast a pale, diffused glow. In the center was a stainless-steel table, its surface smooth, unmarked except for a faint sheen of moisture. On it lay a figure covered by a heavy cotton sheet, pulled up to the shoulders. The face was visible. The hands were visible. The water had done its work. The skin was pale, bloated, the features softened, the edges blurred. The lips were parted. The eyes were closed. Elias did not move. He did not breathe. He stared at the face, and the face stared back, not with recognition, but with the absolute, unyielding neutrality of the dead.

He stepped closer. The floor was cold beneath his shoes. He reached out, his hand trembling, not from fear, but from the weight of the moment, the accumulation of years, the silent conversations he had never had, the apologies he had never spoken, the love he had never known how to show. He touched the sheet at the shoulder, then pulled it back, slowly, deliberately, until the face was fully visible. The water had taken the color, the texture, the life. But the structure remained. The jawline. The brow. The scar above the eyebrow, faint but there, a pale line against the pallor. The set of the mouth, familiar in its stubbornness, its silence. Elias did not cry. He did not speak. He stood there, in the cold light, in the quiet room, and he recognized him. Not with a gasp, not with a collapse, but with a quiet, devastating certainty that settled over him like snow, covering everything, muffling the world, leaving only the truth: yes, it was him.

He did not look away. He did not turn off the light. He stood there until the door opened behind him, until the examiner’s voice, soft and careful, broke the silence. *Thank you,* the man said. *I’ll take it from here.* Elias nodded, stepped back, let the sheet fall, let the room close, let the years fall away, and walked out into the corridor, where the ferry would sail again, where the strait would hold its secrets, where the sea would continue its slow, deliberate work, and where he would finally, for the first time in seven years, begin the long, quiet work of letting go.
