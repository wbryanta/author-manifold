The wind came down the ridge like a blade honed on stone, throwing flakes of ice and old snow against the corrugated iron roof of the lambing shed until the whole structure sang with a hollow, rattling fury. April should have been softening, the ground exhaling from winter’s hold, the willows along the creek pushing out green fingers of hope. Instead, the sky had bruised purple by midday, and by two in the afternoon the world narrowed to this: the swaying kerosene lamps, the smell of wet wool and iron and birth-slick, and the endless parade of ewes arriving with their bellies low and their eyes wide with the animal certainty of what was demanded of them. Clara wiped her hands on the inside of her canvas apron, leaving a smear of amniotic fluid and straw dust on the faded green fabric. Her shoulders ached with a familiar, deep-bone fatigue that had nothing to do with the cold and everything to do with the calendar. It was only their second lambing season without him, but the absence had a weight that settled into the floorboards, the feed bins, the way the wind now whistled through a gap in the north wall he’d meant to seal. He was good with storms, he used to say, or maybe she had said it, maybe she had imagined it, but the truth was that the gap was just a gap now, and the storm was just a storm, and the ewes did not care about grief or geography or the fact that the ground was frozen hard enough to crack a boot heel.

Mama? Eli’s voice cut through the rumble of the wind, thin and uncertain. He was kneeling beside a crossbred ewe, his hands trembling slightly as he worked the delivery boot over the animal’s vulva. At sixteen, he had grown tall and lean, all knees and elbows, his hair the color of damp straw, his face still softening into the hard angles of the man he was trying to become. He didn’t look up when he spoke. The heater’s making that clicking sound again. It’s fine, she said, already moving toward him. Just the thermal relays tripping. Leave it. Get your weight behind your hips, Eli. Not your shoulders. Your legs do the work. Let your spine be the line between you and the lamb. He adjusted his stance, breath pluming in the cold air seeping through the cracked window panes. The ewe groaned, a low, wet sound that vibrated in Clara’s ribs. She knelt beside them, her own knees meeting the packed earth floor with a familiar thud that had never lost its sting. She placed a hand on the ewe’s flank, feeling the violent contractions ripple through muscle and hide, then reached for the sterilized towel draped over her knee. Her fingers moved with the automatic grace of ten thousand repetitions, adjusting the angle of the delivery boot, guiding the lambs’ shoulders through the birth canal with a pressure that was firm but never forceful. The ewe pushed again, a guttural cry tearing from her throat, and then the lambs were there, slick and shuddering, one after the other, tumbling onto the straw. Clara worked quickly, clearing the nostrils, wiping the amniotic fluid from their eyes, rubbing them with the coarse towel until they gasped and kicked. Eli watched, his mouth slightly open, his hands still hovering in the air as if afraid to touch what he didn’t yet understand. Good, she said, not looking up. They’re solid. Lift the first one, get it upright, let it nurse. The second one can wait five minutes for the colostrum. Don’t let them lie flat on their sides. They’ll drown in their own lungs if you do. He nodded, swallowed, and carefully lifted the newborn lamb, cradling it against his chest like it was made of glass. It immediately rooted at his sweatshirt, finding the teat through the wool, and began to suckle with a frantic, rhythmic desperation. Eli exhaled, a sound that was half relief, half awe. Clara turned to the next ewe, already pacing toward them down the narrow aisle, her legs trembling with the need to push, her udders swollen and tight. She wiped her hands again, grabbed the delivery sleeve from the hook behind her, and slipped her arm inside. The rubber was cold, then warm, then slick with the ewe’s own secretions. She worked fast, feeling for the lambs’ legs, straightening them, timing her pushes with the animal’s contractions. It was a dance she had learned from her father, then from him, then from the quiet repetition of years, and now she was teaching it to Eli in fragments, in corrections, in the spaces between the screams of the wind and the grunts of the stock.

The generator outside coughed, sputtered, and died. Clara didn’t stop working. She felt the sudden drop in air pressure, the way the shed seemed to contract, the way the fluorescent strips overhead flickered and went dark. The emergency battery lamps clicked on with a pale, clinical glow, casting long, jagged shadows across the straw-littered floor. But the heat died with it. The forced-air unit gave one last shuddering sigh, and then the shed began to cool in earnest. Clara pulled her arm free, wiped it on her apron, and stood. Her breath came out in white plumes. Kerosene lamps, she said, her voice calm despite the sudden chill climbing her spine. Bring me the cans from the supply box. The red ones. And check the fuel lines on the backup. If it’s got gas, it’s got fuel. If not, we work by the lamps and hope the wind doesn’t find the drafts before morning. Eli was already moving, his boots slipping on the wet straw, his shoulders hunched against the cold. He returned with two heavy brass lamps, their glass chimneys already stained with years of soot. Clara lit the wicks with a striker, the flames catching with a soft hiss, and hung them from the exposed beams. The shed filled with a warm, amber light that made everything look older, deeper, more intimate. The wind seemed to notice the shift. It pressed harder against the walls, rattling the tin, sending a draft through the crack in the north wall that whistled like a warning. They worked in shifts now, in silence broken only by the sounds of labor and the occasional muttered correction. Clara’s hands moved with a precision that bordered on violence, her mind parsing each ewe’s readiness, each lamb’s position, each drop of blood, each breath. Eli’s hands were slower, less sure, but learning. He watched her fingers, the way she used her thumb to feel the lambs’ cartilage, the way she timed her pressure to the ewe’s exhale, the way she never rushed what couldn’t be rushed. He asked no questions. He just watched, and did, and learned. It was the first time he’d worked a full night in the shed alone with her, and the distance between them felt like a physical thing, thick and quiet, made of unsaid things and the ghost of a man who used to stand in this exact spot, wiping his hands on his jeans, saying, You’re doing fine, Clara. Just trust the process.

By midnight, the storm had settled into a steady, relentless drone. The snow fell in sheets, blinding the world beyond the shed doors, sealing them in. The ewes kept coming. Some were old, their teeth worn down, their bodies tired, but their instincts sharp as flint. They came with their heads high, their eyes bright, their bellies low, and they gave birth with a quiet dignity that made Clara’s throat tight. She delivered twins to a highland cross, triplets to a marino, a single, massive lamb to a ewe who’d been bred late and carried too long. Each birth was a small crisis, a small victory, a small reminder of the relentless, indifferent machinery of life. She didn’t have time to think about the cold. She didn’t have time to think about the generator. She didn’t have time to think about the fact that her husband’s favorite pair of rubber boots was still sitting by the door, still waiting for a man who would never walk them again. The boots were scuffed at the toes, their laces frayed, their soles worn smooth by years of walking frozen ground and wet grass. She had never thrown them out. She had never moved them. She had left them there as if he might return, as if he might kick them on, step out, and ask why the north wall hadn’t been sealed. But he wouldn’t return. The boots remained. The farm remained. The work remained. The sheep remained. She remained. That was the shape of it now. Not a circle. Not a line. A square. Four walls. A floor. A roof. A door that opened onto snow.

Mama, Eli said, his voice rough. The third ewe. She’s not pushing. Clara was on her knees beside him before he finished the sentence. The ewe was lying on her side, her legs twitching, her breathing shallow. Her udder was heavy, her teats distended, but there was no rhythm to her contractions, only the occasional spasm that did nothing. Clara’s fingers found the lambs inside, tangled, one leg back, the other bent at the knee. The head was high, tilted against the cervix. She’s exhausted, Clara said. And the first lamb’s leg is back. If we don’t correct it, she’ll tear. Or she’ll stop pushing altogether. Eli’s face went pale. What do we do? You hold the tail. I’ll reach in. Keep her calm. Talk to her. Tell her about the spring grass. Tell her about the creek. Tell her anything but her pain. He swallowed, nodded, and leaned close to the ewe’s ear, his voice trembling but steady. Hey. Hey, it’s alright. You’re alright. Just a little longer. The grass is coming. The green is coming. You can feel it, can’t you? Just below the snow. I can smell it. It’s sweet. Like wet stone and clover. Just push. Please. Just push. Clara slipped her arm back into the sleeve, the rubber stretching over her elbow, her shoulder, her bicep. The ewe’s heat was a furnace against her skin, the smell of birth and blood and amniotic fluid thick in the lamp-lit air. She felt the tangled legs, the cartilage, the resistance of the cervix, the way the lambs shifted against her forearm. She worked slowly, deliberately, straightening the back leg, guiding the head down, timing her movements to the ewe’s weak contractions. It took twenty minutes. It felt like hours. It felt like years. But then the lamb slid free, slick and silent, and then the second one, gasping, kicking, rooting at her wrist. Clara pulled her arm out, wiped her hands, and immediately set to work clearing the nostrils, rubbing the lambs, turning them upright. Eli held the first lamb against his chest, his hands shaking, his eyes wide with a fear that was slowly giving way to wonder. Good, Clara said, her voice quiet. They’re coming. They’re strong. You did good, Eli. You held her. You kept her calm. That’s half the work. The other half is just knowing when to let go. He nodded, unable to speak. He just watched the lambs nurse, his breathing slow, his shoulders dropping, the tension in his face easing into something softer, something real. Clara felt a strange tightness in her chest, a mix of pride and grief and the quiet, relentless ache of watching her boy grow into the shape of his father without ever becoming him. She turned away, pretending to check the next ewe’s station, pretending not to notice the way his hands trembled as he wiped his face, pretending not to hear the wind sigh against the walls.

The hours bled into each other. The lambs multiplied. The straw grew damp, then sodden, then a thick, sweet-scented carpet of spent life. Clara’s hands were raw, her knuckles split, her apron stiff with dried blood and wool and amniotic fluid. Her back ached with a deep, throbbing pain that she’d long ago learned to carry without complaint. She didn’t sleep. She didn’t stop. She didn’t let herself look at the clock. She only worked, and watched, and corrected, and delivered. Eli worked beside her, his movements growing steadier, his voice quieter, his presence more solid. He didn’t ask for help. He didn’t complain. He just did, and learned, and stayed. At some point, the generator outside gave a weak cough, then a stronger one, then roared to life. The fluorescent lights flickered on, bright and harsh, stripping the shed of its amber intimacy, leaving only the cold, clinical reality of the work. The heat returned, slow and grudging, warming the air, drying the straw, making the lamps feel suddenly redundant. Clara turned them off one by one, their flames guttering out, leaving only the hum of the generator and the crackle of the space heaters. She didn’t feel relief. She felt only the familiar, hollow exhaustion that came with survival, the quiet acknowledgment that they had made it through another night, and that tomorrow would demand the same, and the next day, and the next, until the lambing was done, until the lambs were weaned, until the ground thawed, until the world remembered it was spring. Mama, Eli said, his voice rough with fatigue. I think I’m done. She looked at him. His hair was matted with sweat and straw, his cheeks flushed, his eyes red-rimmed but clear. He stood straighter than he had all night, his shoulders squared, his hands clean for the first time in hours. He was still her boy. He was still learning. But he was also something else now. Something he hadn’t been before the storm, before the power died, before the ewes kept coming. He was a man who had held a dying ewe’s ear and told her about spring grass. He was a man who had worked until his bones ached and his hands bled and still kept going. He was a man who had stayed. Good, she said. Go upstairs. Take a shower. Eat something. The next shift starts at four. I’ll take the early ewes. You rest. He nodded, turned, and walked toward the stairs, his boots heavy on the wooden steps. He paused at the top, looked back at her, then at the shed, at the lambs sleeping in their straw nests, at the ewes resting with their bellies full and their eyes half-closed. He didn’t say anything. He just nodded, once, then turned and went up, his footsteps fading into the quiet of the house.

Clara stayed. She wiped down the stations, swept the straw, refilled the water buckets, checked the lambs, made sure the ewes were comfortable, the air was warm, the doors were shut. She worked in the hum of the generator, in the brightness of the fluorescents, in the quiet of a shed that had survived another night. She didn’t think about the past. She didn’t think about the future. She only thought about the next ewe, the next lamb, the next hour. She worked because it was what she did. She worked because it was what he needed. She worked because the ground was still frozen, the sky was still gray, and the sheep were still coming. When four o’clock came, she was ready. She didn’t look at the clock. She only looked at the door, at the snow piling against the wood, at the wind finally beginning to tire, at the sky lightening at the edges. She lit the kerosene lamps. She put on her apron. She picked up the delivery sleeve. She stepped into the shed, and the ewes were already there, waiting, breathing, ready to begin again. The first one was a dry nurse, her teats empty, her eyes dull, her udder flaccid. She didn’t need assistance, only patience. Clara sat beside her on a wooden stool, her back against the wall, her hands resting in her lap, her breathing slow, her mind quiet. She watched the light grow, watched the snow settle, watched the wind die, watched the world outside the shed begin to wake. She didn’t feel relief. She felt only the quiet, hollow ache of survival, the same ache that had lived in her chest for two years, the same ache that had never fully faded, the same ache that she had learned to carry without complaint, without fear, without hope, without despair, without anything but the work.
