The diesel exhaust of the crossing hung low over the water, a gray shroud that clung to the rail and seeped into the wool of his coat as if the strait itself were breathing it in. He stood at the stern, hands buried in his pockets, watching the wake fracture into foam and then dissolve into the black heave of the channel. The ferry groaned beneath his boots, a slow, rhythmic exhalation of pistons and water, and with every pitch and roll the cold found the gaps in his gloves, the cuffs of his shirt, the hollow behind his knees. He did not shiver. He had come too far to be undone by the weather, or too weary to care. The letter had arrived on a Tuesday, typed on official letterhead, plain paper, no header, no seal, just the clinical certainty of a man who had never had to ask twice. A body recovered from the shallows near the southern headland. Worn boots, faded jacket, a rusted pocket watch. No wallet. No identification. The postmaster had known the name, had known the history, had known better than to call it a coincidence. He had packed a bag that night, left a note on the kitchen counter that said only I will come, and driven through the sleeping towns until the asphalt gave way to gravel and the gravel to the ferry terminal. Now the strait stretched ahead, three hours of water and sky, a boundary that had never felt wider until it carried him toward a man he had not spoken to in eleven years. He had not spoken to him because the silence had become a room he could not leave, filled with things unsaid and hands withdrawn, with a door closed so suddenly it still echoed in the architecture of his chest. He told himself he was going to confirm a fact, to close a ledger, to draw a line that had been left frayed and bleeding for more than a decade. But the truth, or the part of it he dared acknowledge, was that he needed to know whether the man in the water had remembered him at the end, whether the distance had been a choice or a gravity, whether the body would look like him or like someone else entirely, like a stranger who had borrowed his brother’s face. The ferry’s interior lights bled through the stairwell doors, yellow and sickly, casting long shadows across the polished deck. Inside, passengers slept in plastic chairs, wrapped in blankets they would not return, or sat with phones turned to black, staring at nothing. The air smelled of wet wool, coffee grounds, and the faint metallic tang of rust. He walked back toward the cabin, took the narrow staircase down, and found his berth, a narrow box with a fold-down bunk and a window the color of bruised glass. He did not lie down. He sat on the edge, knees bent, listening to the engine’s steady pulse, counting the seconds between each thud of the propeller against the water. He had not visited the terminal in over a decade, not since the winter the power went out and the pipes froze and the last phone call had been a series of monosyllables that hung in the static like smoke. Silas had been the one to leave, though he had not walked out so much as unraveled, stepping backward through rooms, through years, through the careful scaffolding of their father’s house, until there was nothing left but an address that stopped routing and a number that disconnected. Elias had told himself it was relief. He had told himself the house felt smaller when Silas was gone, that the air was easier to breathe, that the silence was cleaner. He had told himself a great many things, and most of them had been lies, or at least partial truths, the kind that serve as insulation rather than explanation. The water outside the cabin window was a dark sheet, broken only by the occasional flash of bioluminescence where the wake churned it. He thought of the boy who had fallen into the quarry when they were twelve, who had screamed until his voice cracked, who had looked up at the rim with eyes wide and wet and trusting. He had stood there, frozen, watching the water rise, watching his brother’s mouth form words that never came out, watching the surface close over the face he had shared with blood and childhood summers. He had not jumped. He had run to the house, shouted until the neighbors came, until the men with ropes and ladders and lanterns reached down and pulled him out, until the boy coughed water and laughed and bared his teeth and said nothing about the long moment of standing above the drop. They had never spoken of it. They had buried it under homework and chores and the slow accumulation of growing up, under the careful choreography of a house that taught them to avoid certain rooms, certain questions, certain silences. But the quarry had never disappeared. It had waited, shallow and still and dark, and now it had taken his brother instead, or someone else, or perhaps not taken anyone at all but offered a shape in the water that asked to be recognized. The ferry’s lights flickered, just once, and the engine pitch shifted as the rudder caught a deeper current. He stood, walked to the window, pressed his forehead against the glass. The cold burned. He did not move. Dawn would come, gray and thin, followed by the familiar geometry of the mainland: the concrete terminal, the floodlights, the man in the navy coat waiting by the cargo doors, the form that would ask him to step forward and look into a stainless steel room and say yes or no. He had practiced saying yes. He had practiced saying no. He had practiced standing in front of a mirror and telling himself that it did not matter either way, that the body was just meat and bone and salt water, that the brother was gone whether the face matched or not, that grief was a currency that spent itself whether verified or not. But the body in the water had boots that could have been his, a jacket the color of his mother’s kitchen curtains, a watch that had stopped at 3:14, the hour he had woken in the night to find the other bed empty, the hour the phone had gone dead, the hour the silence had become permanent. The ferry banked, and the wake shifted from white to green to a dark, oily blue. He thought of the last time they had stood in the same room, not speaking, just existing in the same air, the way Silas had looked at the ceiling fan, at the dust on the mantel, at his own hands, as if searching for a word that had lost its shape. He had wanted to reach out. He had not. He had watched him leave, watched the door close, watched the key slide into the lock from the outside, watched the sound of footsteps fade until the house was just a shell and the weather moved through it. He had told himself he was protecting his own peace. He had told himself he was not afraid of the quiet. He had told himself he did not care. The words had been useful until they were not, until the letter arrived and the quiet became a weight and the peace became a room with no doors. The night deepened. The strait widened. The water moved with a slow, indifferent patience. He returned to his berth, sat again, pulled his coat tighter. The ferry’s radio crackled, then fell silent. Somewhere above, a door closed. Someone snored. The engine hummed. He closed his eyes and saw the quarry again, not as it had been, but as it might have been if he had jumped, if he had gone into the water, if he had found the boy there, if he had held him, if he had not let go. He opened his eyes. The window was a mirror now, showing him a face he barely recognized, gray skin, hollow cheeks, eyes that had seen too many years of not looking. He touched the glass. It was cold. It would not answer. The hours passed in increments of vibration and sound, in the slow shift of the ship’s balance, in the way the air grew damp and heavy, in the way his own breathing slowed to match the engine. He thought of the letters he had written and never sent, stacked in a drawer, yellowed at the edges, addressed to a man who had stopped receiving mail, stopped returning calls, stopped leaving traces that could be followed. He thought of the phone numbers he had memorized and unmemorized, of the addresses he had crossed out and redrawn, of the way he had learned to live with the absence as if it were a weather system, something to be worn, something to be expected, something to be survived. He thought of the body. He thought of the identification. He thought of the word yes, and how it would sit in a room, how it would change the shape of a room, how it would make the walls closer, the floor harder, the air thicker. He thought of no, and how it would float, weightless, how it would refuse, how it would leave him standing in the doorway with his hand on the frame, with the choice still suspended, with the question still hanging in the water. The ferry’s horn sounded, long and low, vibrating through the floorboards, through his ribs, through the quiet of his chest. He stood, walked to the window, looked out. The horizon had softened. The water had taken on the color of polished steel. The strait was ending. The mainland was beginning. He did not feel relief. He did not feel dread. He felt the heavy, simple fact of motion, of crossing, of arriving at a point where the road ends and the body must be met. The terminal lights came into view, white and square and unblinking. The ferry slowed. The water grew shallow. The hull groaned as it kissed the channel bed. He packed nothing. He did not need to. He walked up the stairs, through the quiet cabin, out into the cold air, down the ramp onto the concrete, past the waiting man in the navy coat, past the paperwork, past the questions that did not need answers, into the back of a van with a refrigerated compartment and a man who did not look at him and said only follow the light, and into the terminal, where the fluorescent lamps hummed and the floor was worn smooth by decades of shoes and urgency and grief, and into a corridor that smelled of antiseptic and salt, and into a room with a door that closed behind him and a table that held a single form and a pen and a man who stood waiting with a face that had learned to hold still. He signed his name. He stepped forward. He looked into the stainless steel room. He saw the body. He saw the boots. He saw the jacket. He saw the watch. He saw the face. He did not speak. He did not move. He simply stood in the space between recognition and doubt, between confirmation and the quiet erosion of certainty, between the brother he had known and the man the water had returned, or not returned, or returned only in the way the sea returns what it takes, slow and indifferent and final. He nodded. He did not nod. He stood. He breathed. The man in the navy coat asked a question he did not hear. He heard the water instead, the strait, the quarry, the long silence between rooms, the weight of a door left shut, the cold of glass, the hum of lights, the rhythm of a ferry that had carried him across a distance he could not measure, to a room he could not leave, to a body he could not unsee. He turned. He walked out. He stepped into the morning. He did not look back. He did not need to. The strait was behind him. The body was where it would be. The silence was his.
