The strait opens like a wound that has forgotten how to scar. From the starboard rail, the water is not black but a deep, bruised violet, the colour of old ink left too long in the cup, of the space between stars when the moon refuses to show itself. We are moving through it at eight knots, a slow exhalation of diesel and salt, and I hold the railing until my knuckles become the colour of unpolished bone. The ferry hums beneath my feet, a low frequency that travels up through the soles, into the calf, settles in the throat like a swallowed note. I have not slept. Sleep is for those who know what they are returning to. I know only that there is a room below deck where the air is kept at four degrees, where a man lies on a steel table, wrapped in polythene the colour of weak tea, waiting to be named or unnamed.

The officer who gave me the paperwork did not look at me when he handed it over. Paperwork, he said. Your brother’s name is on the manifest. There are procedures. The words were flat, administrative, as if language itself could contain a body. I signed where the pen required it, though I do not remember signing. The pen moved. The paper accepted. Now the form is in my coat pocket, stiff with condensation and the oils of my thumb. I do not need to read it to know what it says. I know what the body will say, or what it will refuse to say. Bodies are the only honest archivists. They do not lie. They only withhold, slowly, like ice retreating from a shore.

I walk the deck. The wind is from the north, carrying the smell of kelp and diesel and the faint, metallic tang of the strait itself, a place where fresh water and salt water collide and neither surrenders. There are other passengers. A woman in a grey coat, eyes closed, breathing in the measured rhythm of someone who has already made peace with the dark. Two men in work boots, talking in low tones about engines and ice charts. A young girl sleeping against her mother’s shoulder, her mouth slightly open, the soft pink of childhood untouched by the weight of crossing. I watch them and think of water as a membrane. What passes through it? What remains suspended? I once tried to capture the strait in a drawing, charcoal on gessoed board, but the lines kept falling into the grain. The paper drank the gesture. I tore it up. Leo would have said it was the paper’s choice, not mine. He always trusted the medium to have its own will.

Leo. The name arrives like a stone dropped in a well. I did not expect it tonight. I came to confirm absence, to stand in that cold room and say: it is not him. It is not him. But the mind is not a ledger. It is a wet page, and every word I try to write is already bleeding into the next. We were twenty years apart. That is a long silence to fill with photographs, with the occasional card, with the heavy, carefully blank space of a marriage he did not attend and a childhood I barely remembered. Yet when the phone rang, when the voice on the other end spoke of a body found near the basalt columns, of waterlogged trousers, of a sketchbook pulled from the surf with pages fused into a single block of pulp, I felt the old, familiar narrowing of the throat. Not grief. Recognition. The terrible recognition that the distance between us was not distance at all, but a shared gravity we had both pretended not to feel.

I go inside. The dining lounge is half-empty, tables arranged in neat rows, a television playing a game show with the sound off. I take a chair by the window. The glass is cold against my forehead. Outside, the strait is a field of disturbed silk, each wave a temporary architecture that collapses and reforms. I think of how Leo used to stand in his studio, a converted fish-curing shed in North Sydney, and watch the light change on the water. He would say the water has no memory but it has weight. He would dip a brush in turpentine and let it pool on the palette. He painted what he called the anatomy of absence: the negative space around a chair, the shadow beneath a door, the way a room looks when someone has just left it. I was a conservator. I restored what was broken. I thought restoration was an act of preservation. Leo thought it was an act of violence. We argued once, in the winter of ’98, over a watercolour he was trying to save from a flood. I wanted to flatten it, to dry it between blotters. He wanted to leave it warped, to let the paper remember the flood. We did not resolve it. We simply stopped speaking. The silence became a structure we both lived inside, each in our own room, each listening to the other’s footsteps through the floorboards.

The ferry turns. The engines pitch. I close my eyes. In the dark, the body presents itself not as flesh but as an archive of coordinates: the callus on the right index finger where the brush handle rested, the scar on the left knee from the fall off the boathouse in ’74, the specific way the collarbones meet the sternum, a geometry I have traced with my eyes across a hundred photographs. I have spent my life learning to read surfaces. A crack in varnish, a flake of pigment, the grain of paper, the tension in a tendon. I know how to tell when something has been handled, when it has been held too long, when the fingers of time have left their salt and heat on the edges. The body will be a text. I will read it. But what if it is not his? What if I am preparing to mourn a ghost, or worse, what if I am denying a brother who has finally, after forty years of silence, returned in a form that demands to be named?

I walk to the stern. The wake is a white scar that closes behind us, the water swallowing its own trace. The air here is sharper. I can taste the iron in it. There is a medical compartment below, accessed by a stairwell that smells of antiseptic and old paint. The officer showed me the door. It is keyed. I have the key. It sits in my pocket, a small brass disc that feels heavier than metal should. I do not go down yet. I wait for the light to change. I wait for the mind to stop preparing and simply begin.

The strait widens. Distant lights appear on the horizon, the shape of the mainland, the curve of the coast, a church bell I cannot hear but feel in the teeth. I think of how Leo and I drew as boys, sitting on the dock with pencils on our knees, sketching the gulls, the fishing boats, the way the light broke on the water. He drew the water. I drew the boats. He drew the negative space. I drew the hulls. We were already speaking different languages. I asked him once why he never signed his work. He said signatures are anchors. They fix a thing to a moment. The water does not want to be fixed. The water wants to move. I did not understand. I thought he was being romantic. Now I think he was being precise.

I return to the lounge. A cup of coffee has gone cold. I drink it anyway. The bitterness is useful. It grounds the body in the present. The present is this chair, this glass, this slow movement through a strait that connects two lands but divides two men. I think of the body’s hands. Hands are the most honest parts. They forget how to hold a pen after the fingers stiffen, they forget how to trace a contour, they become tools of grip, of release. I will see them first. The police report will mention the hands. I will know before I see the face. The face is a map that can be altered, aged, distorted by water and time. The hands are the original manuscript.

I close my eyes again. The ferry’s engine becomes a metronome. I count the seconds. I think of the studio. The smell of linseed oil and damp plaster. The way the light fell through the north-facing window in the afternoon, turning the dust into suspended gold. Leo would stand there for hours, not painting, just watching. He said watching is the first layer of paint. I would come in with my brushes, my solvents, my careful plans for intervention. I would say the work is deteriorating. He would say the work is becoming itself. We were both right. We were both wrong. Conservation is a negotiation with entropy. Art is a surrender to it. We never learned how to speak the same dialect of surrender.

The lights grow closer. The mainland. The strait is narrowing, the water shallowing, the current becoming turbulent against the unseen rocks. I open my eyes. There is a child in the aisle, tracing the condensation on the window with a finger. The path it leaves is immediate, transparent, gone as soon as it appears. I want to tell him to watch carefully. I want to tell him nothing is permanent. I want to tell him that the body he may see is not a loss but a translation. But I say nothing. I am a conservator. I do not interfere. I only stabilize.

I stand. The deck is cold through my shoes. I walk to the stairwell. The key is in my hand. Brass is warm from the pocket. I descend. The stairs are narrow, painted with chipped green enamel, the handrail smooth from decades of grip. I pass the galley, the crew quarters, the engine control room with its glass window glowing blue. I reach the door. It is unmarked. I insert the key. It turns with a click that sounds too loud in the quiet. I push. The air that meets me is cold and dry, scented with ozone and stainless steel. There is a hallway, a single door at the end. It is closed. Behind it, the four-degree room. I have the form in my coat. I have the name in my throat. I have the brother in my bones.

I do not open it yet. I lean against the wall. The metal is cold. I close my eyes. I think of how Leo once told me that to identify a body is not to name it but to witness it. Witnessing requires distance and proximity at once. You must be close enough to see the truth, far enough to bear it. I have spent my life standing at that exact point. I have stood before waterlogged manuscripts, before paintings where the pigment has lifted, before sculptures where the stone has cracked. I have learned to read the stress, the decay, the way time leaves its fingerprints on every surface. The body will be no different. It will be a surface. It will be a text. It will ask me to read it without flinching.

I open the door.

The room is small, white, lit by fluorescent tubes that hum at a frequency just below hearing. There is one steel table, centered in the room, with a plastic sheet laid across it. The sheet is taut, slightly wrinkled, the colour of weak tea. Behind it, a sink, a cabinet with glass doors, a chair. There is no one else. The air is still. I step inside. The floor is cold. The door closes behind me, latching with a soft mechanical sound. I am alone with the table.

I approach slowly. The plastic sheet is folded at one end, as if waiting. I do not pull it back immediately. I wait for the waiting to pass. Then, with two fingers, I lift the edge. The sound is a whisper of polythene. I draw it back.

The body lies on its back, arms at the sides, head slightly turned to the left. The skin is pale, almost translucent, the veins darkening the surface like river maps on old parchment. The hair is wet, matted, clinging to the scalp. The face is swollen, the features blurred by water and time, the mouth slightly open, the eyes closed. The hands are at the edges, fingers curled, the nails darkened. The right hand is missing the tip of the index finger. The left hand rests on the chest, the knuckles white, the tendons visible beneath the skin. The posture is not relaxed. It is arrested. As if the water took the breath but not the intention.

I step closer. I do not touch. I read. The callus on the right index finger is gone, but the bone is shorter, the joint thickened, the scar on the knuckle the exact shape of the fall off the boathouse in ’74, the one where Leo twisted his knee and I carried him home through the snow. The collarbones meet the sternum at the same angle, the same narrow, deliberate geometry. The skin on the neck is pale, but there is a faint, brownish mark near the collar, the shape of a birthmark I have seen in photographs, in childhood, in the space between us. The hair is dark, but there is a streak of grey at the temple, the exact streak that appeared the year he stopped signing his work. The hands. The hands are what I look for. The left hand rests on the chest. The fingers are long, the joints slightly enlarged, the thumbnail cracked exactly as it was after he dropped the brush stand. The right hand is turned inward, the missing finger tip the scar from the glass in the studio in ’82, the year we tried to fix the flood-damaged painting and it broke the bottle. I know these things. I know them because I have held them, because I have traced them, because I have loved them and not known how to say so.

I do not weep. I do not speak. I stand there, reading the body as I would read a manuscript, as I would read a painting, as I would read the strait itself. The body is not a loss. It is a translation. It has moved through the water, through the dark, through the silence, and arrived here, on this table, in this room, waiting to be witnessed. I place my hand on the steel edge of the table. The metal is cold. I breathe. The breath leaves my body in a slow, steady stream. I do not look away. I do not flinch. I witness.

I step back. I fold the plastic sheet back over the body. I do so slowly, carefully, as if arranging a page for drying, as if preserving a gesture. I turn off the light. The room goes dark except for the green exit sign. I open the door. I step into the hallway. I do not go upstairs. I wait. The ferry hums. The strait moves. The night holds.

I return to the deck. The wind has shifted. The water is smoother now, the colour of slate. The mainland is behind us, or ahead, I cannot tell. The lights are distant, blurred by the cold and the dark. I sit on a bench. I take the form from my coat. I sign it. The pen moves easily. The paper accepts. I do not need to read what I have written. I know what it says. I know what the body said. I know what I have done.

I look at the water. I think of how Leo used to say the water has no memory but it has weight. I think of how I used to say the paper has no will but it has grain. I think of how we were both right. I think of how we were both wrong. I think of how the strait connects two lands but divides two men. I think of how the ferry crosses it every night, carrying bodies, carrying names, carrying silence. I think of how the water does not remember but it holds. I think of how I have spent my life preserving what I love, and how Leo loved what he loved simply by letting it move. I think of how I am tired. I think of how I am not.

The strait is a place of crossings. Not just of water and land, but of time and memory, of presence and absence, of the living and the archived. I watch the wake close behind the hull, the white scar smoothing into the violet dark. There is a geometry to this crossing, a slow subtraction of distance, a gradual addition of return. The ferry’s prow cuts through the water, parting it, then leaving it to heal itself. I think of how Leo once painted a series of bridges, not the bridges themselves, but the water beneath them, the way it flowed around the pylons, the way it held the reflection of the steel, the way it never quite mirrored it, never quite caught it, only suggested it through layers of pigment and glaze. He said bridges are for those who want to cross without being changed. The water changes those who cross it. It takes a little of your salt, a little of your breath, a little of your time, and leaves you slightly less of what you were when you stepped onto the deck. I am less now than I was an hour ago. I am more present, more hollow, more precise. The body is no longer a question. It is a fact. And facts, like water, do not argue. They only move.

The engines pitch again. The course adjusts. I can feel the shift in the hull, the slight lean to port, the way the water deepens beneath us. There is a lighthouse somewhere to the west, its beam sweeping the dark in slow, deliberate arcs. I have seen it before, in photographs Leo sent when I was still writing letters, when the silence between us was still fresh, still tender. He wrote once about the light, how it cut through the fog like a scalpel, how it revealed the water’s surface only to hide what lay beneath it. I told him I preferred the fog. I told him clarity was overrated. He replied that clarity was a myth, that all light casts shadow, that to see is always to select, to exclude. I did not understand then. I understand now. I selected the body. I excluded the doubt. I selected the brother. I excluded the years. I selected the truth. I excluded the rest. It is all the same arithmetic.

I stand. The bench is cold through my trousers. I walk to the rail. The air is thinner here, closer to the sky, which is slowly lightening at the edges, a faint, coppery wash bleeding into the violet. I think of the studio again, of the way the light fell on the canvases, the way it caught the dust, the way it made the room feel suspended, as if time had paused to watch the work. Leo would stand before a painting for hours, not painting, just watching, his hand in his pocket, his feet planted wide, his breath steady. He said the painting watches you back. He said it asks what you are willing to leave behind. I used to think he was being mystical. Now I think he was being practical. Art is not about adding. It is about subtracting until the thing you want remains. I have spent my life adding layers, varnishes, consolidants, fills, until the damage was hidden, until the past was smoothed, until the object looked as it might have on the day it left the artist’s hand. Leo never wanted to hide anything. He wanted the damage to speak. He wanted the water to have its say. We were both conservators of silence, but we spoke different dialects of it.

The ferry slows. The strait is narrowing. The water is calmer, shallower, the colour shifting to a pale, greenish grey. There are rocks visible now, black and jagged, topped with salt-resistant grasses, seagulls perched on them like punctuation marks in a long, unfinished sentence. I think of how Leo and I used to walk these shores as boys, collecting stones, turning them over in our hands, testing their weight, listening to the way they rang against each other. He would say some stones remember the fire that made them. I would say stones are just cooled time. He would smile, that slow, sideways smile that made his eyes disappear, and say, then let them cool. We never finished the sentence. We never had to. The silence between us was not empty. It was full of everything we had not said, everything we had done, everything we had become. Now it is full of a body on a steel table, and a signature on a form, and a brother who has finally stopped walking away.

I do not go down again. I do not need to. The identification is complete. It was not in the face, not in the hands, not in the scar or the birthmark or the cracked nail. It was in the way the body held itself, the way it waited, the way it refused to be anything other than what it was. I have spent my life trying to make broken things look whole. I have learned that wholeness is a lie we tell the living to spare them the truth of fracture. Leo knew this. He painted the fractures. He painted the water that seeped through them. He painted the way the light caught the edges, the way the shadows pooled in the cracks, the way the damage became the subject, the way the object refused to be anything other than itself. I am tired of making things look whole. I am tired of varnishing over the grain. I am tired of pretending the past can be smoothed. It cannot. It can only be witnessed. And I have witnessed it.

The lights of the port appear on the horizon, a cluster of amber and white, scattered across the dark like spilled coins. The ferry turns toward them. The water grows shallower, the current stronger, the hull vibrating against the waves. I think of how Leo once told me that to cross a strait is to admit you cannot stand on one bank. It is to admit the distance between you and what you want is too wide to jump, too narrow to ignore. He crossed the strait every day, by ferry, by boat, by foot when the water was calm. He crossed it to the mainland for supplies, for books, for the occasional visit to the gallery in Halifax that never hung his work. He crossed it to the islands to sketch the basalt columns, the tide pools, the way the light broke on the water at dawn. He crossed it to the studio to watch the light change. He crossed it to the shore to stand at the water’s edge and listen to it. I crossed it once, in ’91, to tell him I was leaving, that I had accepted a position in Ottawa, that I could not stay in a town that smelled of fish and paint and silence. He did not ask me to stay. He did not tell me to go. He simply handed me a cup of tea, bitter and hot, and said, drink it while it’s warm. I drank it. I left. I have not crossed it since. I crossed it tonight in a different way, not by boat, but by presence, by willingness to stand in the cold room, to look at the body, to say the name in my head, to let the silence between us finally break, not with words, but with witness.

The ferry docks. The ramp lowers with a groan of metal and hydraulics. The air changes instantly, warmer, heavier, scented with diesel and wet concrete and the faint, sweet rot of the harbour. Passengers begin to move, stretching, gathering coats, adjusting bags, stepping toward the light. I do not move. I watch them go. I watch the young girl wake, blink at the light, press her face into her mother’s shoulder. I watch the woman in the grey coat stand, adjust her coat, walk toward the ramp without looking back. I watch the men in work boots nod to each other, step down into the dark. I watch the water lap against the hull, the waves breaking softly, leaving foam that dissolves before it reaches the deck. I watch the strait hold what it has given, keep what it has taken, move forward without looking back. I know now why Leo loved it. Not because it was beautiful. Not because it was peaceful. But because it was true. It did not pretend to be anything other than what it was. It did not try to hide its depth, its currents, its rocks, its silence. It simply was. And in its being, it asked only to be witnessed.

I step to the rail. I take the form from my coat. I look at the signature I have written. It is steady. It is final. I do not tear it up. I do not fold it. I let it rest in my hand, the paper warm now from my skin, the ink dry, the name clear. I do not need to say it aloud. I do not need to speak to the air, to the water, to the dark. I have said it in the cold room. I have said it in the silence. I have said it in the way I read the body, in the way I witnessed it, in the way I let it be what it was without trying to change it, without trying to fix it, without trying to make it fit into the story I had written for us. The story was wrong. The body is not a chapter. It is the margin. It is the space where the water meets the land, where the ink meets the page, where the brush meets the canvas, where the silence meets the sound, where the brother meets the brother, where the past meets the present, where the loss meets the witness. It is enough. It is always enough.

I step back from the rail. I turn toward the ramp. I do not look at the water again. I do not need to. I have seen it. I have felt it. I have crossed it. I have witnessed what it brought. I carry it in my bones, in my breath, in the cold that settled in my throat, in the quiet that now lives where the doubt used to be. I walk toward the light. I step down the ramp. I do not run. I do not hurry. I walk slowly, steadily, into the harbour, into the night, into the life that waits on the other side, into the water that holds us all, into the silence that speaks, into the brother who is no longer a question, into the body that is no longer a ghost, into the strait that is no longer a divide, into the crossing that is no longer a distance, into the witness that is no longer a duty, into the truth that is no longer a burden, into the water that remembers nothing but holds everything, into the light that reveals nothing but shows enough, into the night that does not ask for anything but gives itself, into the brother who is gone but not forgotten, into the silence that is not empty but full, into the strait that is not a wound but a pathway, into the ferry that has carried us all, into the water that has taken us all, into the breath that has left me all, into the body that has become the text, into the margin that holds the page, into the ink that has dried, into the paper that has accepted, into the hand that has signed, into the name that has been spoken, into the water that holds, into the strait that connects, into the crossing that completes, into the witness that remains.
