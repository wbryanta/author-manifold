The hull groaned, a low-frequency groan that vibrated up through the soles of Elias's boots and settled deep in his teeth, a constant reminder that he was suspended in the grey indifference of the strait. The ferry moved with the reluctant gravity of a tectonic plate, shearing through water the color of old lead, churning it into a froth of white foam that died instantly against the iron ribs of the ship. Inside the cabin, the air was thick with the smell of boiled cabbage, diesel, and the damp wool of a hundred travelers huddled in their sleeping bags, their dreams churning in unison with the swell. Elias had not slept. He sat by the porthole, his forehead resting against the cold glass, watching the blackness beyond, waiting for the strait to end, or perhaps dreading the moment it would, because the end of the water was the beginning of the answer.

The call had come three days ago, not from Liam, but from the harbor master at Krossvik, a man whose voice carried the gravelly resignation of someone who read obituaries for a living. There had been a body. A John Doe. Washed out from the basalt ribs of the northern skerries, where the strait narrowed to a throat of jagged teeth and the currents pulled with the malice of a riptide. The identification papers were already stamped, waiting for a name, and the harbor master knew the family, knew the silence that had fallen over the house at the end of the pier where Elias's father had once worked the lines. The harbor master had said, *You're the only one left to ask, Elias. Come and decide.*

Elias had driven south from the city where he had built a life out of glass and steel, far from the salt and the stoicism of the north, only to find himself here, on a twenty-hour crossing, clutching a phone that had gone dead weeks ago, yet still buzzing in his pocket with the phantom weight of his brother's absence. He told himself he was going for the closure, for the bureaucratic necessity of signing a paper that would allow the body to be buried, or burned, or returned to the sea, depending on what remained of it. But the truth was a colder knot in his gut: he was going because he needed to know if the lie he had told himself for twenty years was true. He needed to know if Liam was gone, or if Liam was still there, in some form, and Elias was the one who had died instead, the one who had walked away.

The ferry lurched, throwing the few remaining passengers in the cafe car against the railings. Elias pushed himself up, his knees popping, and walked toward the galley. The crew moved with efficient brutality, ignoring the humans who drifted like debris in their wake. He ordered coffee from a woman whose face was a map of deep fissures, her eyes flat and unseeing. The cup came black, steaming faintly, tasting of copper and burnt beans. He drank it standing, watching the water rush past the portholes, a stream of darkness punctuated by the occasional glint of bioluminescence, like eyes blinking in the deep.

He remembered the last time he had seen Liam. It was not with a face, but with the architecture of the moment: the kitchen table scarred by the knife marks of fish cleaning, the smell of roasting coffee, the way the light from the high window fell across the floorboards, illuminating the dust motes dancing in the stagnant air. Liam had been standing by the door, his coat on, his hands shoved deep in his pockets, shoulders hunched against a wind that didn't exist inside the house. Elias had been standing by the stove, a mug in his hand, saying words that felt heavy and wet in his mouth, words about responsibility, about the city, about the suffocating nature of staying in a place where the air felt like it was slowly turning to stone. Liam had not shouted. Liam never shouted. His anger was a frozen river, silent and lethal. He had just looked at Elias, his eyes the color of the strait at dawn, and said, *If you leave, you don't come back. I won't answer the door.* Elias had waited for the catch, the apology, the hand extended across the gap. There was only the wind howling at the eaves and the slow click of the lock as Liam turned the key from the outside.

That was twenty years ago. The estrangement had calcified over time, becoming a fossil in the strata of Elias's life. He told himself it was better this way. The north was a place for people who could endure the long dark, who found comfort in the repetition of the tide, in the language of nets and gulls. Elias was a creature of light and noise, of screens and schedules, of a love that demanded too much explanation. He had married a woman who spoke in paragraphs and laughed with her whole body, and he had buried them both in a suburb where the grass was cut weekly and the silence was polite. When Liam's wife died, when the heart attacks began, the calls stopped. Elias told himself he was respecting the boundary Liam had drawn. He told himself that to reach out would be to violate a sacred treaty. He was a coward, and he built a city to hide his cowardice.

Now the strait was asking for a reckoning. The harbor master's words echoed: *May or may not be your brother.* The uncertainty was a hook in his chest, reeling him in. If it was Liam, Elias would have to face the finality of the brother he had rejected. If it wasn't Liam, Elias would be relieved, yet haunted by the possibility that he had gambled on a ghost and lost. The body on the slab was not just a corpse; it was a mirror. Elias feared what he would see reflected in the dead face.

He walked back to the deck, escaping the claustrophobia of the lower decks. The wind here was a physical blow, salt spray stinging his face, soaking through his jacket. The night sky was clear, the stars sharp and cold, indifferent to the drama of the ferry. The ship's lights cut a path through the dark, illuminating the swells that rose and fell like the breathing of a leviathan. Elias gripped the railing, his knuckles white, and scanned the horizon. He felt a foolish impulse that Liam might step out of the water, dripping wet, his face set in that familiar line of disapproval, to ask why Elias had come, why Elias had waited until the water took him to come.

The ferry slowed as it approached the outer anchorages of the mainland port. The lights of the town flickered on the distant cliffs, amber smudges against the black. Elias went below to the luggage car, then back up to the main gangway area as the horn sounded, a mournful note that seemed to vibrate in his bones. The docking was a shudder of metal on rubber, the groan of release, and then the stillness. The world stopped moving. The silence that followed was heavy, pressing against his eardrums.

He disembarked into the cold air of the quay. The port was a skeleton of cranes and containers, lit by floodlights that made everything look bleached and unreal. A police car waited with its lights off, a single officer standing by the open door, checking a watch. Elias walked toward him, his boots echoing on the concrete. The officer nodded, a sharp motion, and gestured to a nondescript van.

"You the brother?" the officer asked. His voice was flat, bored. He didn't offer a name.

"I'm Elias," Elias said. His voice sounded thin, swallowed by the vastness of the port. "My brother is..."

"Inside. We got the paperwork from Krossvik. You're the next of kin. You can go up now. Don't take too long."

The van ride was short, a bump over the gangplank, into the belly of the industrial complex that housed the mortuary annex of the regional hospital. The air changed as they entered, scrubbed clean of salt and diesel, replaced by the sharp, chemical sting of antiseptic and the faint, sweet-rot scent of decay that Elias could never quite place. It was the smell of endings, of biology surrendering to chemistry.

The woman behind the desk didn't look up from her computer. She slid a folder across the counter. Inside were photographs. A body bag zipped halfway up, revealing a mass of dark, water-swollen flesh. A face turned to the side, the features obliterated by the bloating, the skin waxy and grey, the eyes gone. A tag on the toe: *Unknown Male. Approx. 40s. Trauma consistent with blunt force and immersion.*

"Sign here," the woman said, extending a pen. "And here. This acknowledges the body as property of the estate for disposition."

Elias looked at the photographs. He felt a wave of nausea, the kind that comes not from disgust, but from the sudden intrusion of mortality into the ordered world. He reached for the pen, his hand trembling. He needed a sign, a detail, something that would anchor the name Liam to this mass of dead tissue. He looked at the hands in the photo. They were curled tight, the fingers thick and distorted by the water. He turned the page. There was a list of possessions. A wallet. A silver lighter, dented. A keychain with a rusted washer and a piece of blue ribbon.

The lighter. Liam had that lighter. He had carried it for decades, a gift from their father, a Zippo that clicked with a satisfying snap, even though they rarely smoked anymore. The blue ribbon was from the fishing tournament in '98, where Liam had pulled in a cod that weighed more than he did. Elias felt the air leave his lungs. The details were anchors, pulling him down.

"You need to see him," the woman said, her tone softening slightly, professional pity bleeding through. "To be sure. Or do the photos suffice?"

"No," Elias whispered. "I need to see him."

She led him down a corridor of linoleum and white walls, passing doors that were closed against the secrets within. The walk was a march toward a trial. Elias's mind raced, trying to reconstruct Liam from the fragments of the past. The scar on the eyebrow from the time Elias had pushed him into the ice? The limp in the left leg from the boat crash? The way Liam held his head slightly to the right? But the body in the photos was a ruin. How could a face be unrecognizable? How could the strait erase a man so thoroughly?

They entered a small room with a steel table in the center. A sheet lay over a shape on the table. The air was frigid. Elias approached, his heart hammering against his ribs like a trapped bird. He looked at the woman. She stepped back, giving him space, her hands clasped behind her back.

Elias reached out. His fingers hovered over the sheet. He thought of the kitchen table. He thought of the locked door. He thought of the twenty years of silence, a wall he had built stone by stone, only to find that the wall was also a cage. He pulled back the sheet.

The face was a landscape of destruction. The skin was sloughed in patches, revealing the dark muscle beneath. The jaw was unhinged, the teeth visible in a rictus that might have been a smile or a grimace. The water had taken the features, smoothed the lines of expression, erased the laugh lines around the eyes, the furrow between the brows. There was nothing there that screamed *Liam*. There was only the universal truth of decay.

Elias stared, a panic rising in his throat. It wasn't Liam. It couldn't be. The doubt, which had been a hook, turned into a whirlwind. He searched the body for the anchor. The hands. He grabbed the right hand, pulling it forward. The fingers were swollen, the nails blue-black. He turned the hand over. The palm was mapped with the old calluses, the ridge of the burn from the welder's torch when he'd worked in the yard. But the water had swollen everything, made the hand a club. He turned it back. On the ring finger, there was no ring. Liam had worn a band for ten years, then took it off after she died. Elias remembered that. He remembered the photo of Liam holding the band, his face pale.

And then Elias saw it. On the inner wrist, just below the thumb, the faint, silvery line of a scar. The appendectomy when they were children. Liam had laughed about it, saying he had a hole in him where his guts used to be. Elias had pushed him to prove the point. The scar was there, pale and thin, visible even through the bloating.

The certainty hit Elias with the force of a physical blow. It was Liam. The body was Liam. The ruin was Liam. The name attached itself to the flesh, inevitable and terrible.

Elias made a sound, a low groan that started in his chest and tore its way out. He sank to his knees beside the table, his forehead resting on the cold steel. The smell of the room filled his nose, the antiseptic, the decay, the cold metal. He wept, not with loud sobbing, but with the silent, shaking racking of breath, tears dripping onto the grate of the table, pooling in the grooves. He wept for the brother who had stayed, who had fought the wind and the currents and the years, who had died alone in the dark, perhaps waiting for a boat that never came, or simply succumbing to the cold that seeped into the bones. He wept for the door that had been locked. He wept for the twenty years of nothing, the messages unsent, the visits unmade, the slow erosion of a bond by the salt air of indifference.

The woman placed a hand on his shoulder. He flinched, then leaned into the touch, the contact grounding him in the reality of the room. *It's over, Elias,* her presence said. *He is here. You are here. The work is done.*

Elias sat up. His eyes burned. He looked at Liam's face one last time. He tried to find the brother in the ruin. He tried to summon the voice, the laugh, the hand on his shoulder. But the face was a mask of death, and the memory of Liam felt like a dream dissolving upon waking. Was this what he had lost? Or was he mourning the idea of Liam, the brother he needed to forgive himself for leaving? The distinction blurred in the cold air. There was only the body, and the guilt that had crossed the strait with him, heavier than any cargo.

He signed the papers. The pen scratched loud in the silence, the sound of the end. He signed his name, the letters shaky, the ink dark. He took the lighter from the evidence bag, the metal cold and dented. He held it in his palm, the weight of it familiar. He could hear the click of the thumb wheel in his memory. He put it in his pocket, close to his heart.

"Take him to the home chapel?" the woman asked softly. "We can arrange transport. Or you can sign for direct disposition."

Elias stood up. His legs felt weak, the blood rushing back into them. "I'll sign for direct disposition," he said. His voice was rough, broken. "I don't... I can't be alone with him. Not yet."

The woman nodded. She processed the forms, stamped the papers, the sound rhythmic and final. She handed him a copy. The paper felt light, insubstantial. It was just a record. It didn't bring the brother back. It didn't erase the door.

Elias walked back through the corridors, the footsteps echoing. The port was dark now, the floodlights humming. The ferry was gone, vanished into the night, leaving the strait to its own devices. Elias walked to the edge of the quay, looking out over the water. The strait lay before him, black and vast, the current pulling away from the land, toward the open sea. Liam had gone into that darkness. Elias had fled it. Now they were both on opposite sides of the same water, connected by the body that lay in the room behind him, and by the guilt that bound them tighter than any flesh.

He took the lighter out of his pocket. He flicked the wheel. The spark caught, the flame flared, small and golden against the dark. He held it up, watching the light dance, and for a moment, he saw the flame reflected in Liam's eyes, the spark that had once been there, bright and fierce, before the fire had gone out. He let the flame die, the smoke curling up and vanishing.

Elias put the lighter back in his pocket. He turned his back on the strait, on the water that had taken his brother and the brother who had taken him away. He walked toward the street, toward the taxi that waited, toward the road that led to the city, to the life he had built in the absence of the north. He would never go back. Or perhaps he would, someday, when the guilt had aged enough to become a monument rather than a wound. For now, he was just a man with a lighter in his pocket, walking away from the sea, carrying the weight of a name across the threshold of his life, the name of the brother he had left to the dark, and who had, in the end, waited for him.
