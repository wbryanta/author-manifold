The sun stood white and heavy over the dry swale and the heat lay upon the orchard like a woolen shroud. Dust rose in slow eddies from the cracked clay and settled upon the leaves of the peach trees that had borne fruit for forty years and now stood half bare and brittle as old bones. Elias worked the spade into the hardpan where the irrigation line had burst and the soil came up dark and crumbling and dry. His brother Caleb laid the new section of galvanized pipe upon the trench and braced it against his shoulder and drove the wedges with a maul. The blows rang hollow and sharp in the stillness and the sound carried across the failing rows of orchard and down toward the arroyo where the creek had run dry for three summers now. You have to think it over he said. Not today. Not while the sun is up. Elias did not look up. He struck the earth again and the spade bit deep and turned a clod that shattered into powder. I have thought it over. I have thought it over every day since the bank man came. Since the letter came. Since your father laid down and the dust took him. Caleb wiped his brow with the back of a gauntleted hand and left a streak of gray across his forehead. He dropped the maul into the dirt and walked to the edge of the trench and looked out over the rows. The trees stood in their ranks like veterans with their arms at their sides and their coats torn by wind and sun and time. You think selling it changes anything he said. It changes what we can do about it. It changes the math. The orchard is dying he said. Not slowly. All at once. The soil is salt. The water table is down past the bedrock. The trees are taking their time to remember how to live. Caleb knelt and pressed his fingers into the dirt at the base of a young sapling that had put out a few pale leaves and then dropped them. He let the soil sift through his fingers. The roots are gone he said. The soil is tired. It has given everything. It is time to let it rest. You do not get to decide what it needs. You get to decide what you can bear. Elias stood and brushed the dust from his trousers and looked at his brother. His face was carved by sun and wind and years of working the land and now the lines around his mouth deepened. You want to be the one who buries it. I want to be the one who saves it. There is no saving. Not like this. The buyers are circling. They know the county is rezoning. They know the water rights are half gone. They know what this place was and what it is now. Caleb rose and walked back to the trench and picked up the maul. He did not strike the pipe. He held it loosely and let its weight rest against his thigh. What is it to you that it is gone he said quietly. It is what was given. It is what he died for. He died watching this land shrink. He died listening to the wind in the dead branches. He died knowing he could not stop it. And you would carry on. You would dig yourself into the ground and call it duty. Elias turned his face away. The heat pressed against his neck and the sweat ran in cold tracks down his spine. He bent again and pulled at the rusted coupling that held the old line together. The metal groaned and cracked and gave way. A puff of air hissed from the breach and then nothing. Only the dry earth and the silent trees. The line is dead he said. It has been dead for years. We are just digging the grave. Caleb set the maul down and crossed his arms. He looked at the eastern ridge where the mountains rose in layers of blue and violet and white rock. The light was beginning to lean. Shadows lengthened across the orchard and pooled in the furrows between the rows. You talk like a man who has already packed his bags. I am talking like a man who has to choose between a coffin and a grave. Elias stood and kicked at the dirt with the toe of his boot and sent a shower of grit into the trench. The buyers will double what we get for it. They always do. By the time we decide it is half what we need. It is not about the money. It is about letting go. Some things are not meant to be held. They are meant to be witnessed. And then they are meant to be left. Caleb closed his eyes and opened them slowly. The dust hung in the air between them and the heat made it shimmer. You think I do not know what it costs he said. I know what it costs to watch a tree turn brown and not be able to stop it. I know what it costs to stand in a field and hear the silence where the birds used to be. I know what it costs to listen to the bank man speak in his soft voice and his clean shoes and his hands that have never held a tool. But I also know what it costs to sell it. To watch them come with their survey stakes and their asphalt plans and their glass buildings and to see this place become a footnote in some ledger. Elias reached into his pack and pulled out a canteen and drank. The water was warm and tasted of metal and dust. He handed it to Caleb who drank and passed it back without a word. They worked in silence for a time. The spade moved. The pipe was set. The wedges were driven. The coupling was aligned and bolted. The earth yielded reluctantly and then accepted the work as it accepts everything. It accepts the dead and the living and the promises men make and break. It accepts the drought and the rain and the turning of the seasons that do not care what men say. You remember the year the frost came he said finally. Elias did not look up. He tightened the last bolt with a wrench that slipped in his sweaty hand. I remember. You said we would lose it all. We lost half. But the other half held. The roots went deep. They found water in the dark. Caleb set the wrench down and sat upon the edge of the trench. His legs dangled and his boots hung over the dry clay. He looked at the ground and his face was calm now and empty of the heat that had driven it before. What will you do with the money he asked. Buy another place. Start over. You would do that. You would leave this. I would take what is left and build something that can survive the weather. Caleb laughed softly and it sounded like a stone dropping into a well. You will buy a patch of dirt and plant something else and wait for the drought and the bank and the frost and the fire and you will do it again until you are dust and there is nothing left to plant. Elias turned back to the trench. He lifted the new pipe and aligned it with the old line. The metal was cool against his palms and then warmed to the sun. You think I do not see it he said. You think I do not know what it costs to keep trying. I know exactly what it costs. That is why I am trying. Caleb rose and walked to the far edge of the trench and knelt again. He pressed his fingers into the dirt and let it flow from his grip. He watched it fall and settle and disappear. You are fighting a war against the sky he said. And the sky does not fight back. It just watches. It takes what it wants and leaves what is dry. Elias bolted the coupling and tightened the nuts until his hands ached. He stood and wiped his face and looked at the sky. It was the color of faded denim and the clouds were thin and high and indifferent. He thought of his father standing in this same orchard with a cane and a coat too heavy for June and pointing to a tree and saying it will outlast us all and the voice had been so full of certainty that Elias had believed it and now he did not know what to believe. He struck the pipe with the wrench and it rang clear and true and the sound traveled down the line and into the earth and was swallowed. Caleb walked back and stood beside him and looked at the repair. It was a small thing. A few feet of steel and bolts and dirt. It would hold for a week maybe. Maybe longer. Maybe not. It would not save the orchard. But it would give the trees one more day of water. One more day of being alive. That is all he said. Elias nodded. He did not look at him. He knew his brother was right and he knew his brother was wrong and the truth was neither of them mattered to the land. The land was already gone. They were just standing in the ghost of it. They packed their tools and walked to the edge of the orchard where the dust road ran and the truck stood waiting with its tires sunk in the dirt and its paint faded by sun and time. Elias opened the cab and took out two canteens and a tin box of biscuits and a jar of pickled peppers. He laid them on the tailgate and they sat on the running board and ate and drank and watched the light change over the mountains. The shadows moved across the orchard like slow water and the trees stood darker and closer and the air grew cooler and the smell of dry sage and hot earth rose and fell with the breeze. You will call the bank man he said. I will call him. You will tell him the price. I will tell him what it is worth. It is not worth anything. It is worth what we remember. You remember it wrong. You remember what he said. He said different things. He said it would break us. He said we should run. He said stay until we could not stand. He said he was sorry. He said he loved us. He said he loved this place. He said he loved it enough to die in it. Elias closed the tin box and set it aside. He looked at the orchard and the trees and the rows and the dry ground and the sky and the mountains and the long light and he felt nothing except the weight of it and the certainty that it would continue without him. Caleb reached into his pocket and pulled out a cigarette and lit it with a match and held it between his fingers and let the ash fall. You will miss the silence he said. I am in it now. They sat in the quiet and the sun sank lower and the sky turned the color of blood and then of ash and the stars came out one by one and then in clusters and then in great sweeping rivers across the dark and the air grew cold and the wind carried the smell of dust and sage and distant rain that would not fall. Elias stood and stretched and his bones cracked and he walked to the edge of the orchard and looked out over the fields and the roads and the towns and the lights and he felt the old grief rise in his chest and settle there like stone. Caleb joined him and stood beside him and did not speak. They stood until the dark was complete and the orchard was a shapeless mass and the trees were gone and only the ground remained and it was flat and endless and indifferent and the brothers stood upon it and were small upon it and the night took them and the land kept its silence and the stars moved across the sky and no one was left to count them.

The morning came pale and thin and the frost lay upon the grass like ground glass and the brothers moved back to the trench with the same quiet purpose as the day before and the spade struck the earth and the pipe waited and the wind moved through the dry branches and the sky was the color of a bruised plum and the work went on as it goes on in all places where men try to hold back the turning of the world and the truth is that they cannot and the land does not care and the brothers knew it and did not say it and the day wore on and the heat returned and the dust settled in the lines of their faces and the silence between them grew deeper and older than any argument they had ever had and the trees stood in their rows and the soil was hard and the water was deep and the pipe was cold and the sky was high and the sun was a white coin in a vault of air and the brothers worked and the earth yielded and the day ended and the shadows stretched and the air cooled and the stars came out and the brothers sat and did not speak and the land kept its silence and the wind moved across the orchard and the leaves turned and the branches creaked and the roots slept in the dark and the brothers watched and did not weep and the world turned and the years passed and the orchard fell and the brothers grew old and the land remained and the wind moved across the empty rows and the dust settled and the sky was empty and the brothers were gone and the land kept its silence and the stars moved across the dark and no one was left to count them.
