The wind did not howl so much as it pressed, a solid wall of white noise against the tin roof of the lambing shed, vibrating through the timbers until the very straw on the floor seemed to tremble. April should have been forgiving by now, or at least familiar, but the sky had fallen down in sheets of ice and cloud, turning the pasture into a churning sea of slush and wind-scoured grass. Mara stood at the far stall, her back to the door, her hands deep in the wet warmth of a ewe in the midst of a hard labor. The air in the shed was thick with the scent of lanolin, damp wool, crushed hay, and the sharp, metallic tang of life breaking open. She worked with the slow, deliberate grace of someone who has done this work for forty years, who knows by the tension in a muscle, by the rhythm of a breath, by the exact angle of resistance, what must be done. Behind her, Leo moved from stall to stall, his headlamp cutting a stark cone through the dimness, calling out softly to the animals, adjusting straw, wiping blood from flanks, checking pulses with the quiet efficiency of a boy who is no longer learning but who is still becoming.

The generator coughed twice, then died. The fluorescent strips overhead flickered and went black, leaving only the gray daylight bleeding through the high windows and the scattered beams of their headlamps. For a moment, the shed held its breath. The ewes shifted. A lamblet gave a thin, reedy bleat. Mara did not look up. She knew the sound of a dead generator, knew the way it left the air suddenly heavy and still, knew that the cold would begin its slow work through the cracks in the walls and under the doors. She finished her work, guided the last lamb clear, cut the cord with her thumb knife, and rubbed the newborn vigorously until it gasped and began to kick. Only then did she rise, her knees popping, her shoulders aching with the deep, familiar fire of muscle pushed past its afternoon limit. She pulled off her gloves, wiped her hands on her apron, and turned toward the door.

Leo was already at the switchboard, flipping breakers that led nowhere. He was seventeen, tall and lean, all long limbs and sharp angles, his face still softening into the man he would be, but his hands already mapped with callus and scar. He looked over his shoulder, his jaw tight. Power’s out. Probably downed a line or the transformer blew.

Mara nodded, already moving toward the closet where they kept the emergency supplies. Kerosene lamps. Extra batteries. Wool blankets. A bag of grain. The weight of it in her arms was ordinary, but the absence around it was not. She set the lamp on the worktable and struck a match. The flame caught, bloomed yellow and steady, and threw long shadows against the corrugated metal walls. Leo followed with a second lamp, his movements careful, almost reverent, as if handling something fragile. He lit it, adjusted the wick, and set it down beside Mara’s. The light pooled warm and golden, chasing back the gray.

It’s the first April without him, Leo said, not as a question, but as a fact laid bare on the table between them.

I know, Mara said. She didn’t look at him. She was already walking toward stall four, where a ewe was breathing hard, her head low, her body trembling with the effort of pushing. There is no time for April. There is only the now.

Leo followed, but he did not speak. They worked in the language they had always shared, a dialect of touch and glance and shorthand. He cleared the stall, spread fresh straw, prepared the iodine dip, the towels, the rubber gloves. Mara knelt beside the ewe, her hands finding the familiar geography of birth. She worked slowly, feeling the progress, listening to the animal’s breath, reading the subtle shifts in muscle and pressure. Outside, the wind drove snow against the walls in furious bursts. The temperature was dropping. She could feel it in the draft that crept along the floor, in the way her fingers stiffened, in the condensation freezing on the edges of the windows. She wrapped her scarf tighter around her neck, leaned forward, and pushed when she felt the signal, guided when she felt the resistance, pulled with her whole body when it was time. The lamb slid free with a wet, slick sound, collapsing onto the straw. Mara caught it, turned it, cleared its mouth with her thumb, rubbed its sides until it coughed and drew air. The ewe licked it immediately, furiously, her tongue rough and warm, her body still convulsing with the aftershocks of labor. Leo stepped forward, already cutting the cord, already checking the lungs, already slipping the lamb under the mother to nurse. His hands moved with a confidence that belied his age, with a steadiness that made Mara’s throat tighten. She remembered those hands when they were smaller, when they had fumbled with the gloves, when they had asked too many questions, when he had looked to her for answers that sometimes she did not have. Now they were sure. Now he was part of the work.

The ewes kept coming. They always did. They came in twos and threes, in singles and in frantic pairs, as if the storm outside was a summons they could not ignore, as if the earth itself was demanding what it owed. Mara moved from stall to stall, her body aching, her mind running on tracks worn deep by decades of this exact rhythm. She delivered two straightforward births without incident, her hands moving almost without thought, her attention half on the animals, half on the house of memories that the shed always seemed to hold. The hay rack in the corner was still dented where Daniel had thrown it in frustration after a difficult spring ten years ago. The hook on the wall where he used to hang his favorite pair of boots was empty, the nail left behind, a small silver scar in the wood. The worktable still had the gouge from the time he’d dropped a heavy wrench on it, laughing, swearing, picking up the pieces with her. She did not touch the table. She did not look at the hook. She looked at the ewes. She looked at the lambs. She looked at her son.

By midday, the light had failed entirely, swallowed by the storm, and the shed existed only in the pools of lamplight and the scattered glow of headlamps. The cold had seeped into the bones. Mara’s fingers were numb, even through her gloves. She stripped them off, worked her hands under her armpits, pressed them together, felt the blood return in painful, prickling waves. She lit another kerosene lamp, its flame sputtering slightly in the draft that had found its way under the door. Leo had rigged a towel at the threshold, but the wind still drove ice crystals across the floor. He was breathing hard, his face flushed, his hair plastered to his forehead. He had taken to wearing his father’s old wool cap, pulled low, the brim frayed, the earflaps tied under his chin. It looked like him. It looked like Daniel. Mara turned away, busying herself with a towel, wiping a stall, arranging a nest of straw. Her hands shook. She clamped them still against her knees and waited for the tremor to pass.

They are coming too fast, Leo said, his voice tight. The ground is saturated. The ewes are stressed. I can feel it in their lungs.

You can feel it in your own, Mara said quietly. Breathe. Work.

I know what I’m doing.

Do you? She didn’t mean to be sharp. The words came out worn, edged with exhaustion and something else she refused to name. The ewes don’t care what you know, Leo. They care what you can do. They care what you can give.

He stiffened. I’m doing the work.

I didn’t say you weren’t. I said breathe. Your hands are shaking.

He looked down at his hands. They were trembling, just slightly, the way they always did when he was tired, or afraid, or carrying too much. He curled them into fists, then opened them again, deliberately. You’re shaking too.

I’ve been shaking since Tuesday, she said. It’s just Tuesday.

He didn’t answer. He moved to stall seven, where a ewe was lying on her side, her legs kicking weakly, her breath shallow and rapid. Leo knelt, placed a hand on her shoulder, spoke to her in the low, steady tone he had learned from Daniel, from Mara, from years of listening to men and women who knew that panic was a luxury these animals could not afford. He checked the cervix, felt the position, assessed the angle. He called back over his shoulder, steady, clear. Breech. Left hind first. I need you on the counter. Towels. Iodine. I’m going to rotate.

Mara was already moving. She set the kerosene lamp on the counter, grabbed the supplies, handed them to him without looking. She knew what he needed. She had helped him with this before, in a different spring, under different light, with different grief. She had watched him hesitate, had watched him find his footing, had watched him do what needed to be done when the animals did not care about his uncertainty. Now he did not hesitate. Now he trusted himself. Now he trusted her. She set the iodine within reach, rolled up her sleeves, knelt beside him, and placed her hands on the stall door to steady herself.

The storm outside rose to a fever pitch. It battered the shed like a living thing trying to get in, to claim it, to freeze it solid, to erase it from the map. The wind screamed through the eaves, rattled the gutters, drove snow against the walls in sheets so thick the windows were nothing but white. But inside the stall, the world narrowed to breath and muscle and pressure. Mara felt the tension in the ewe’s body, the stubbornness of the hind leg, the precise moment when rotation was possible. She guided Leo’s hands, corrected his angle with a touch, showed him how to use his body, not just his arms, how to lean, how to wait, how to push when the animal pushed back. He listened. He adjusted. He worked. Sweat dripped from his brow, froze on his upper lip, melted again as the kerosene lamp threw its heat forward. Mara’s own breath came in short, controlled pulls. She thought of Daniel’s hands on hers, showing her how to find the rhythm, how to trust the process, how to let go of the fear that always accompanied the threshold of giving. She thought of the empty side of the bed, the cold pillow, the way the house had sounded different after the first week, when the silence stopped feeling like absence and started feeling like presence. She thought of the bills, the debt, the way the bank had called, the way the neighbors had looked at her when they thought she wasn’t watching, the way she had smiled and said everything was fine and meant it, or meant to mean it, or would mean it eventually.

The lamb came with a surge, slick and dark and perfect, slipping into Leo’s waiting hands. He caught it, turned it, cleared its mouth, rubbed it vigorously, feeling for breath, for pulse, for life. The lamb gasped, coughed, kicked, drew air. The ewe groaned, then began to lick, her tongue rough, her body still trembling. Leo slumped back on his heels, his hands shaking now, his breath coming in ragged pulls. Mara reached out, covered his hand with hers, felt the tremor, felt the heat, felt the reality of what had just happened. The lamb was alive. The ewe was recovering. The storm raged. The lamp burned.

You did it, she said.

I didn’t do it, he said. It did it. I just helped.

Good. That’s what we do. We help. We don’t take credit. We don’t take blame. We help.

He nodded, wiped his face with his sleeve, stood, and walked to the next stall. Mara remained where she was, her hand still resting on the stall door, her body full of ache, her mind full of quiet. She lit another lamp. She checked the ewes. She cleared the straw. She counted the lambs, named them in her head, watched them suckle, watched their mothers watch them, watched the way the storm outside seemed to hold its breath, as if even the wind knew not to interfere with what was happening here.

Afternoon bled into evening. The temperature dropped. The kerosene lamps grew heavier in their glass, the flames shorter, the smoke thicker. Mara had to lean forward to keep them lit, to adjust the wicks, to keep the light from dying. Leo moved with a new rhythm, one that was less frantic, more sure, his body learning what his mind already knew. He brought her a mug of tea, steeped too long, bitter and hot, set it on the counter beside her. She drank it without comment, felt the warmth spread through her chest, felt the stiffness in her shoulders ease just a fraction. He sat on the upturned crate, pulled his knees to his chest, and watched the stalls. He did not speak for a long time. The only sounds were the wind, the breath of the animals, the crackle of the lamps, the occasional bleat of a lamb, the soft rustle of straw being shifted.

When was the last time you slept through the night? he asked, his voice quiet, almost swallowed by the storm.

She looked at him. She did not look at him right away. She looked at the ewe in stall three, at the way her eyes were half-closed, her head resting on her flanks, her breathing slow and steady. She looked at the lambs, huddled together, their ribs rising and falling, their fur drying, their strength returning. She looked at the hook on the wall. She looked at the worktable. She looked at her hands.

Before him, maybe, she said. After him, no. Before him, because I was tired. After him, because I was alone.

He didn’t say anything. He just nodded, as if he understood, as if he had understood for a long time and was only now allowing himself to acknowledge it.

I don’t know how to do this, he said.

Do what?

Keep it going. The farm. The ewes. The bank. The way people look at you when you walk into town. The way I have to be careful not to cry when I’m alone in the truck. The way I have to pretend I’m fine when I’m not. The way I have to learn everything myself when he’s not here to show me. I don’t know how to do this.

Mara stood. She walked to him, knelt in front of him, took his face in her hands, forced him to look at her. Her thumbs brushed his cheeks, felt the stubble, felt the warmth, felt the tears he was fighting back. She did not wipe them away. She let them be. You think he knew how? she asked. You think I knew how? You think anyone knows how? This isn’t about knowing. It’s about showing up. It’s about doing the next thing. It’s about not letting the cold win. It’s about lighting the lamp when the power goes out. It’s about rubbing the lamb until it breathes. It’s about standing in the stall when your knees are shaking and your heart is breaking and your hands are numb and you do it anyway. You don’t have to know how. You just have to do it. Again. And again. And again.

He closed his eyes. His shoulders shook. He did not cry. He breathed. He nodded. He pulled her into his arms, buried his face in her shoulder, held on like a boy who was learning how to be a man, like a man who was remembering how to be a boy. She held him. She did not speak. She let the storm rage. She let the lamps burn. She let the ewes breathe. She let the lambs nurse. She let the silence hold them, heavy and warm and real.

When he pulled away, his face was wet, but his eyes were clear. He stood, wiped his face, adjusted his cap, and walked to stall nine. A ewe was struggling. He knelt. He checked. He called for supplies. He worked. Mara returned to her own stall, lit another lamp, adjusted her scarf, knelt, and pushed. The lamb came easy. She caught it. She rubbed it. It breathed. She placed it under the ewe. She stood. She walked to the next stall. The work continued.

Night fell like a weight. The wind had shifted, driving the snow in fierce, diagonal sheets, burying the fence posts, freezing the mud, turning the fields into white dunes. The temperature plummeted. The kerosene ran low. Mara lit the last of the lamps, set them in a line along the worktable, watched the flames flicker, watched the shadows dance, watched the way the light caught the dust in the air, caught the steam from the animals’ breath, caught the wet on Leo’s face as he worked. He was covered in sweat and blood and straw, his clothes soaked through, his hair plastered to his forehead, his hands raw and red. He moved with a exhaustion that was not weakness, but depth, with a knowledge that was not certainty, but trust. He did not ask for permission. He did not ask for help. He asked for what was needed, received it, used it, moved on. Mara watched him. She saw Daniel in the set of his shoulders, in the curve of his back, in the way he paused before entering a stall, in the way he spoke to the animals, in the way he wiped his brow with the back of his wrist, in the way he looked up when he heard her approach, his eyes clear, his face calm, his hands steady. She saw him. She let herself see him. She did not look away.

By the time the first lambs of the night were born, the storm had reached its peak. It did not rage anymore. It waited. It watched. It held the shed in its white hands, pressing against the walls, testing the roof, searching for weakness, finding none. The lambs slept. The ewes rested. The lamps burned low. Mara sat on the crate beside Leo, her back against the worktable, her eyes closed, her breath slow, her body heavy with fatigue that felt like peace. He handed her a cup of tea, still hot, still bitter. She drank it. She set it down. She opened her eyes.

How many? she asked.

Twelve confirmed. Two in progress. One that may not make it. The rest are nursing. The ground is frozen. The wind is killing the heat. We need wood. We need grain. We need to check the south fence before the snow buries it. We need to call the vet when it breaks. We need to rest. We need to keep going.

She nodded. She closed her eyes. She listened to the storm. She listened to the animals. She listened to her son breathe. She listened to the silence between them, no longer empty, but full.

When she opened her eyes again, the lamps were dying. The flames were small, the glass clouded, the smoke thick. She stood, stretched, felt the stiffness in her legs, the ache in her back, the warmth in her chest. She walked to the closet, pulled out the last kerosene can, filled the lamps, lit them, adjusted the wicks, set them down. The light returned, steady, warm, golden. She turned to Leo. He was watching the stalls. His face was calm. His hands were steady. His eyes were clear.

You did good, she said.

He nodded. We did good.

We will do good, she said. Again. And again. And again.

He smiled. It was small, but it was real. It was enough.

The storm continued. It always did. It did not care about grief, or debt, or fear, or hope, or love, or loss, or life, or death. It cared only about pressure, and wind, and cold, and snow, and the endless, indifferent rotation of the sky. But inside the shed, the ewes pushed. The lambs breathed. The lamps burned. The work continued. And Mara and her son stood in it, shoulder to shoulder, hand to hand, breath to breath, doing what had always been done, doing what had to be done, doing what would be done, until the storm broke, until the light returned, until the ground thawed, until the ewes rested, until the lambs grew, until they could stand, until they could walk, until they could run, until they could live. And for now, that was enough.
