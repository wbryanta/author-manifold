The strait does not forgive, it only holds. The ferry moves through it like a needle pulling thread, a slow stitch of diesel and cold water against the hull. I stand at the forward window, breath fogging the glass, watching the dark water break into pale teeth at the bow. The engine hums a single low note, the same note my father used to hum when he worked the radio in the basement. Three frequencies only. One for weather. One for the shore. One for silence. I press my palm to the glass. The vibration travels up my wrist, into the radius, into the ulna, into the elbow joint where the bone meets bone. Everything is connected. Everything conducts.

The call came at four in the afternoon. A number I do not know. A voice that does not tremble but does not soften either. They found a body. Drifted into the channel near the old lighthouse. Male. Early thirties. No identification. The water has been kind, they say, or unkind. It takes its time. They ask me to come. They say the medical examiner’s tent is pitched on the south dock. They say bring a coat. They say nothing else. I pack a bag. Wool sweater. Boots. A notebook I do not open. I leave the door unlocked. The house feels too large for one man, too quiet for the absence of another. I do not think of him as I lock the gate. I think of the weight of keys. I think of the way a drawer slides on old runners. I think of the strait.

Dusk falls like a shutter. The lights of the departure pier are pale circles in the fog. People move through the terminal with the heavy, deliberate gait of those who know they are crossing something. They carry bags. They carry phones. They carry the invisible weight of destinations they will soon reach and forget. I buy a ticket. I show my passport. I do not speak. The woman behind the glass stamps the paper. The ink is blue. Blue as the water outside. Blue as the bruise that would form along a collarbone if struck just so. I take the stairs up to the main deck. The air is cold and smells of salt and exhaust and damp wool. I find a seat near the railing. I watch the shore recede. The trees become a dark line. The line becomes a shadow. The shadow becomes nothing.

The strait is wide here. It has no name that matters to anyone who has not lived in its shadow. It is called the narrows on charts. It is called the gap on weather maps. It is called the place where the water remembers what the land has forgotten. I have crossed it twelve times. Seven of them were to leave. Five were to return. The count matters less than the direction of the wind. Tonight the wind is from the north. It pushes the waves into short, choppy ridges. They break against the hull with a sound like gravel being poured onto metal. I close my eyes. I count the breaks. I count the heartbeats beneath my ribs. I count the years since the last time I spoke his name aloud.

He would have hated the ferry. He would have stood at the bow, arms crossed, jaw set, watching the water as if it were a problem to be solved. He was always solving problems. He fixed radios. He rebuilt carburetors. He mapped the constellations onto the ceiling of his bedroom with glow-in-the-dark stars. I watched him once, at age seven, lie on the carpet and trace the path of Orion with a flashlight. The beam trembled in his hand. He said, If you get lost, look up. The light was small. The ceiling was high. I did not look up. I looked at the dust motes in the beam. They moved like tiny fish. I will never know if he was speaking to me or to himself. I will never know if he meant the stars or the water.

The cabin lights flicker. A woman walks past in slippers. She carries a cup of tea. Steam rises from the surface in a thin, wavering line. She sits. She does not look at me. I look at her hands. The knuckles are swollen. The nails are short. She wraps her fingers around the cup as if it were a stone. I think of my brother’s hands. The knuckles were smooth. The nails were always bitten to the quick. He would tap his fingers on the table when he was thinking. Three taps. A pause. Two taps. He said it helped him find the rhythm. The rhythm of what? He never said. I assumed the rhythm of words. I did not know it was the rhythm of something trying to leave his body.

The ferry rolls. The rail groans. I open my coat. The inside lining is frayed. I pull out the photograph. It is creased down the middle. The edges are white with age. Two boys on a dock. The wood is rotting. The water is dark. The taller boy has his arm around the shorter one. The shorter one is looking down at a rock in his hand. The taller boy is looking at the camera. Night settles like a heavy coat. The water is no longer visible. It is only heard. The sound changes when the strait widens. It becomes deeper. More resonant. The ferry pitches forward. The wake throws up a spray that catches the deck lights. It falls like glass. I think of the body. I think of what they will have done to it. They will have wrapped it in plastic. They will have called it a person. They will have used both words in the same sentence. Words are slippery. They do not hold shape. They only point.

I remember the last time I saw him. The kitchen. The window open. The screen torn. The light from the stove casting long shadows on the cabinets. He was washing a pan. The water was hot. The steam rose and fogged the glass above the sink. I stood in the doorway. I wanted to say something. The words were there. They sat in my throat like stones. I swallowed them. I watched his back. The scapulae moved under his shirt. They were sharp. They looked like wings folded tight. He turned. He looked at me. His eyes were dark. They held no welcome. They held no hostility. They held only recognition. Then he turned back to the pan. The water clattered against the metal. The sound was final. I left. The door clicked shut. The house exhaled. I walked to my car. I did not look back. I have not looked back since.

The ferry’s PA system crackles. A voice announces the next town. It is a calm voice. It is a professional voice. It is a voice that has announced departures and arrivals a thousand times. It will announce this one too. It will do it without hesitation. It will do it without grief. I listen to it. I let it wash over me. It is a reminder that the world continues.
