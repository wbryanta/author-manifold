The strait held the evening like a closed hand. From the forward rail, the water was a sheet of hammered lead, catching only the ferry’s running lights and the distant, slow pulse of the lighthouse on the far shoulder. He stood with his coat collar turned up against the wind, hands buried in his pockets around a small, flattened envelope. Inside was a photograph, creased along the diagonal, and a letter that had arrived three days prior, typed on cheap stock, stating only that a man had been found near the old coal docks, that the coroner required a next of kin, and that they hoped it was him. He did not know if it was him. He had not spoken to his brother in twelve years, not since the winter the house had filled with the sound of boxes and the sharp, mechanical click of locks. Estrangement, he had learned, is not a single rupture but a slow settling of silt, layer upon layer, until the bedrock is invisible.

The ship’s engines thrummed through the soles of his shoes, a steady, low frequency that seemed to travel up through the deck plates and into the bones. He watched the wake fracture behind them, white foam curling and dissolving into the dark. A gull moved along the railing, its wings beating once, twice, then lifting on a thermal. The air smelled of diesel and damp wool and the faint, mineral scent of the cold water itself. He closed his eyes and listened to the hull scrape against the churning current, the way the ship leaned into the swell, righted itself, leaned again. There was a rhythm to it, a breathing. He opened his eyes and looked at the horizon line, where the sky and water met in a blur of grey. Somewhere out there, in the silt and the cold, a body waited. Or would wait. The uncertainty was a kind of hollow in his chest, not painful exactly, but spacious, echoing.

On the third deck, the dining room was empty except for a waitress folding napkins into triangles and a man in a damp pea coat staring at a cold plate of toast. The waitress looked up as he passed. The light here was amber, flickering slightly along the length of the galley corridor. She paused with the napkin halfway to her pocket.

You’re crossing for the identification, she said. It wasn’t a question.

He nodded. His throat felt tight, as if the cold air had thickened there.

They take their time, she said. The dead don’t hurry. Or they do, and the living catch up too late.

He watched her fold the napkin. The fabric made a soft, papery sound as she pressed the crease. She held it up, examined it, then placed it on the counter with the others. A neat row of white triangles.

I’ll be down by the time you finish, he said.

She smiled, or perhaps it was just the light catching the corners of her mouth. People get it wrong sometimes, she said. They want to see who they need to see. The face changes, even after. But you’ll know. Or you won’t. Either way, you’ll have to leave it there.

He walked back to the rail. The wind had shifted, bringing a sharper edge from the north. He leaned against the cold iron, felt the vibration of the propellers through his palms. He thought of his brother’s hands, how they were always stained with something: ink, soil, turpentine, motor oil. Hands that moved quickly, that gestured while speaking, that paused to light a cigarette with a flick of the wrist, that later, in the silence of the house, had packed a bag without looking at him. There had been words, of course. Heavy ones, thrown like stones, sinking into the floorboards. Then there had been nothing. The kind of nothing that accumulates dust. He had told himself it was necessary, that distance was its own form of preservation, that some relationships are meant to be left in the dark so they do not sour in the light. But the envelope in his pocket felt heavy now, as if it contained more paper than ink.

The lower decks were dim, lit by motion-sensor strips that pulsed on as footsteps passed and faded a minute later. He walked past sleeping cabins, their portholes fogged with condensation, past a laundry cart stacked with blue towels, past a crewman reading a paperback with the cover turned down. The air grew warmer, heavier, smelling of linoleum and coffee. He climbed the stairs to the main lounge, where a television played silently above a bar. A woman in a red scarf sat alone at a corner table, tracing the rim of a glass with her fingertip. Her eyes were closed. She did not look up as he sat in the chair opposite.

Do you cross often? she asked, still with her eyes closed.

Not lately, he said.

I cross to meet someone. Or to leave. Usually it’s the same thing.

He watched the ice shift in his water glass. The ship banked slightly, and the liquid sloshed against the glass, catching the light. He thought of his brother’s laugh, how it had always started low and then broke into something sharp, unexpected. He had not heard it in years. He wondered if the body would recognize him, or if recognition belonged only to the living. The dead are just flesh, he reminded himself. Just meat and bone and the slow retreat of water. Yet here he was, standing on a ferry through the night, carrying a photograph like a talisman, hoping the face in it would match the face in the morgue. The paradox was almost comical, if it did not sit so heavily.

The waitress from earlier walked past with a tray of empty cups. She glanced at him, then at the woman in red. She did not stop. The television above the bar switched to a weather map, the anchor’s voice muted by the bar’s ambient jazz. He closed his eyes and listened to the ship. The engines, the waves, the creak of metal, the distant clatter of galley pans, the sigh of wind against the superstructure. It was a symphony of transit, of being neither here nor there, suspended in the dark between departure and arrival. He felt his shoulders drop, just slightly, as if the water itself were bearing some of the weight.

At midnight, the wind dropped. The strait smoothed into a glassy plane, reflecting the sky like a dark mirror. He stood on the bow, alone now, the rail cold through his gloves. The stars were sparse, broken by clouds that moved slowly over the horizon. He thought of the house they had shared as boys, the way the afternoon light fell through the high windows, how his brother would sit on the floor with a notebook, drawing maps of imaginary coastlines, labeling bays in careful script. He had kept one of those maps, folded in a drawer, the paper yellowed, the ink faded to ghost-lines. He wondered if his brother had kept any maps of him. Or if he had been erased, carefully, without resentment, just the quiet subtraction of a name from a ledger.

A ship passed in the distance, its lights steady, moving perpendicular to them. It made no sound until it was nearly alongside, then a low horn echoed across the water, long and resonant, vibrating in his ribs. He closed his eyes. The sound lingered, then faded into the dark. He opened his eyes and looked at the wake again. It was gone. The water had sealed itself shut.

He walked down to the lower deck, where the air was still and warm. The corridor ended in a small observation nook, glassed in, looking out over the water. He sat on the wooden bench, pulled the envelope from his pocket, and unfolded the photograph. His brother was standing on the dock at the old marina, wind catching his coat, eyes half-lidded, a cigarette between his fingers. He looked tired, but alive. The light was late afternoon, golden, casting long shadows. He had taken it himself, or his brother had taken it for him. He could not remember. He studied the face, the curve of the jaw, the way the left eyebrow sat slightly higher than the right, the faint scar through the temple from a fall off the roof when they were twelve. He traced the edge of the photo with his thumb, feeling the worn paper, the slight curl at the corner. He slipped it back into the envelope, folded it, placed it in his inner pocket, against his chest. The paper was warm now.

Dawn came slowly, not as a burst of light but as a gradual brightening of the grey. The water shifted from black to silver, then to a pale, bruised blue. The ferry turned east, and the coastline emerged from the mist, first as a dark line, then as hills, then as buildings, then as streets. He watched the shore approach, the way it resolved from abstraction into detail: a crane, a warehouse, a row of houses with sagging roofs, a bridge spanning the inlet. The air changed, carrying the smell of fish, of damp concrete, of woodsmoke. He stood at the rail, feeling the shift in the ship’s motion as it slowed, the engines backing down, the propellers turning backward in short bursts.

The gangway was extended before they fully stopped, a metal bridge folding out like an arm. He stepped off, the sound of his boots on the grated steel sharp in the morning air. The dock was quiet, only a few figures waiting near the entrance to the morgue annex, their faces pale, their coats damp. A woman in a grey trench coat stood apart from them, looking out at the water. He did not approach her. He followed the signs, past the intake desk, past a narrow hallway lit by fluorescent tubes that hummed faintly, into a room with stainless steel tables and a sink that smelled of bleach and cold water.

A man in a white coat stood at the far end, rolling up the sheet on a table. He turned as the man entered. His face was neutral, his eyes tired but steady.

You’re the one, he said. Not a question. He nodded toward the table.

The man walked forward. The air in the room was still, cold, carrying the faint, metallic scent of the place. He stopped at the edge of the table. The sheet was pulled back to the shoulders. He looked down. The face was pale, the skin waxy, the eyes closed, the jaw slack. There was water in the lungs, he knew, or cold, or time. The features were blurred by swelling, by the slow work of dissolution. He leaned closer. The nose was broken, the lip split, the hair matted. He looked at the hands, resting at the sides, the fingers curled slightly, the knuckles swollen. He looked at the chest, the rise and fall long gone. He closed his eyes. He remembered the laugh, the scar, the way his brother’s left shoulder always sat lower than the right, the habit of tapping his thumb against his thumb when he was thinking. He opened his eyes. He looked at the face again. He looked at the hands. He looked at the chest.

It is possible, the man in the coat said quietly, that it is him. The dental records match the partial set we had on file. But the face… the face is not clear. You will have to decide.

The man in the trench coat did not speak. He stood at the edge of the table, his breath shallow, his hands clenched at his sides. He thought of the photograph, creased and warm against his chest. He thought of the house, the boxes, the silence. He thought of the ferry, the strait, the long night of crossing. He thought of how some questions do not have answers, only acknowledgments. He reached out, slowly, and touched the back of the man’s hand. The skin was cool, slightly waxy, but real. He did not pull away. He stood there for a long time, listening to the hum of the lights, the drip of the sink, the distant sound of the dock, the ferry’s engine idling in the bay.

He straightened. He took the envelope from his pocket, placed it on the edge of the table, face down. He did not open it. He did not need to.

I know him, he said. His voice was steady, low, barely above a whisper. He looked at the man in the coat. I know him.

The man in the coat nodded. He did not ask how. He did not ask why. He simply stepped back, giving him space, turning toward the door.

The man in the trench coat stood alone at the table. He looked at the face one last time. He saw the scar, or the line where it had been. He saw the mouth, closed now, but once open with laughter, with argument, with silence. He saw the eyes, shut, but once open with surprise, with fatigue, with the quiet recognition of a brother across a room. He reached out again, gently, and pulled the sheet back up, covering the face, covering the hands, covering the cold. He did not flinch. He did not cry. He simply folded his hands in front of him, feeling the weight of them, the warmth, the pulse in his wrists.

He walked out of the room, down the hallway, past the intake desk, out into the morning air. The sky was bright now, the water silver, the dock busy with crates and men in high-visibility vests. He walked to the edge of the pier, where the metal grated gave way to weathered planks. He looked out at the strait. It was calm, reflecting the sky, the boats, the distant hills. He felt the wind on his face, the dampness in his coat, the hollow in his chest that had not filled but had settled, had become part of him. He thought of his brother’s maps, the imaginary coastlines, the bays labeled in careful script. He thought of how all borders are temporary, how all crossings are repetitions, how the water always returns to itself.

A ferry horn sounded in the distance, long and clear. He did not turn. He stood at the edge of the pier, watching the water, waiting for the tide to shift, for the light to change, for the next crossing to begin. He knew now that he would not write the letter. He knew now that he would not call the house. He knew now that some silences are not absences but presences, that some losses are not endings but continuations, that some brothers are not found in flesh but in the space between, in the strait, in the water, in the long, slow crossing that never truly ends. He turned and walked back toward the city, his steps steady, his hands in his pockets, the envelope resting against his chest, the photograph untouched, the face at rest. The ferry pulled away from the dock, its wake widening behind it, merging with the current, disappearing into the blue.
