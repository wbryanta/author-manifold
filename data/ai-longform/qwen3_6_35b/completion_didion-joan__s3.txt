And you stand there in your press kit, holding a notebook you’ll never write in, because the questions are already answered by the engineers who’ve been counting microseconds since long before the generals got their uniforms shined. You don’t ask how many pounds of fallout settled on the coral because the water’ll swallow it anyway. You just watch the flash. You always watch the flash. Even when you know you shouldn’t. Especially when you know you shouldn’t.

I remember the first time I saw a full yield out at Enewetak. Shot Redwing Cherokee. Or maybe it was Castle Bravo. I keep mixing them up now, the way memory does when you’re trying to separate the light from the heat. The point is, the tower was this lattice of steel and canvas, rigged with cameras and Geiger counters and those little Geiger-Müller tubes that clicked like a thousand insects trying to escape a jar. The technical guys had been down there six weeks straight, sleeping in hammocks strung between the Quonset tins, drinking coffee that tasted like boiled tin and ration sugar. They didn’t talk much before the shot. Just calibrated. Just checked. Just recalibrated because the humidity warps everything, even the calibrations. You get down there, the salt gets in your gear, in your clothes, in your lungs. It’s not just salt. It’s iodine. It’s cesium. It’s strontium. But you don’t say that out loud. You just wipe your goggles with a rag that’s already gray.

Inez used to ask me what it felt like. Not the bomb, the waiting. She’d be sitting on the bed in her little room behind the mess hall, her hair still in those gardenia pins, though they’d wilt by noon anyway, and she’d say, Jack, what does it feel like? And I’d tell her it feels like holding your breath underwater. Not the holding part, the before part. The stillness. The way the water goes quiet around your ears. The way you know something’s coming but you can’t tell if it’s a wave or a wave that’ll take your head off. That’s the waiting. The technical guys call it standby. The pilots call it scramble. The congressmen call it oversight. We just call it Tuesday.

Tuesday turned into Wednesday, Wednesday into Thursday, until the weather broke and the transport choppers came in low, the rotor wash kicking up sand that got in your teeth and your boots and the creases of your maps. You climb in. The pilot doesn’t look at you. He’s counting fuel. He’s counting wind. He’s counting the seconds before the ground crew on the barge yells go. You drop down. You see the barge. You see the tower. You see the little guys in their white suits running cables, checking gauges, swearing in a language that’s half English and half Morse code. And then the light. Not the flash. The before-flash. The way the air turns thick, like the humidity just gave up and decided to stay. The way the pink fades into this pale, washed-out yellow, the color of old teeth. That’s when you know. That’s when your stomach drops. That’s when you remember why you came.

I remember Inez looking at me once, right after one of the Nevada tests, when we were back at the motel off Highway 95, the curtains drawn, the fan spinning overhead like it was trying to blow away the silence. She was pouring water into a glass, her hands steady, her back to me, and she said, You don’t talk about them. And I said, Talk about what? And she said, The ones that didn’t come back. The ones that didn’t make it home. The ones who just became numbers in a report. And I told her, Inez, we’re all numbers. Some of us just wear better uniforms.

She didn’t argue. She never argued. She’d just set the glass down, sit on the edge of the bed, and stare at the wall like it held the answer to a question she’d already forgotten how to ask. That’s what I liked about her. Not that she was quiet. That she let the quiet do the work. You spend enough time around men who talk to fill the space, you forget how much the space wants to talk back.

The Pacific tests, though. Those were different. The water held it. The sand held it. The coral held it, for a little while, anyway. You could go down there and walk the beaches after the shot, if the radiation counters hadn’t gone red. You could find chunks of glass on the shore, black and green and sometimes blue, smooth as river stones, warm to the touch. The pilots called it Shiva glass. The physicists called it trinitite analog. The cooks called it breakfast. You pick up a piece, you hold it to the sun, and it catches the light like a diamond. You know it’s poison. You know it’ll make you sick in ten years or fifty. But you put it in your pocket anyway. What’s the point of remembering if you can’t carry it?

I had a pocketful of them. Drove them out to Clark in Manila, wrapped in a handkerchief, gave them to that reconnaissance pilot who’d flown in from Johnston. He laughed. He said, Lovett, you’re hoarding fallout like it’s gold. I said, Maybe it is. Maybe gold’s just another kind of poison that pretends to be pretty. He didn’t answer. He just took the handkerchief, tucked it in his flight jacket, and walked to the chopper. I never saw him again. I don’t know if he died on a mission or retired in Oregon. I don’t care to find out. Some things are better left in the pocket.

Back in Nevada, the desert doesn’t hold anything. It just takes it in and lets it burn through. The ground cracks. The air shimmers. The heat doesn’t leave even at night. You sleep on a cot with a thin mattress and wake up sweating, even though the AC’s been running since noon. The engineers talk about thermal transfer. The observers talk about time. The civilians talk about the economy. Nobody talks about the way the sky looks after a shot. It’s never pink again. It’s this flat, bruised gray, like a ceiling you can’t see the edge of. You stare at it and wonder if it’s watching you back.

Inez used to hate the gray. She’d say it looks like a tomb. I’d say it looks like a clean sheet. We’d argue about nothing. We always argued about nothing. That’s how you know it’s real. You don’t fight over big things. You fight over curtains. Over coffee. Over who forgets to lock the door. Over whether a man should carry glass in his pocket or leave it on the ground where it belongs.

I remember one shot, maybe Grommet, maybe Plumbbob. I don’t remember the name anymore. I remember the morning of. I remember the sky turning that pale yellow again. I remember the technical guys standing in a line, their faces white under the sun hats, their eyes fixed on the tower. I remember Inez watching me from the jeep, her hands on her knees, her knuckles white. I remember getting in the jeep. I remember the pilot starting the engine. I remember the ground shaking before the flash, just a little, like the earth was coughing. I remember the light. I remember looking away. I remember looking back. I remember the tower being gone. I remember the silence. I remember thinking, That’s it. That’s all it is. Light. Heat. Silence. And we built a whole science around it.

She got out of the jeep when I did. Walked up to me. Didn’t say a word. Just took my hand. Her fingers were cold. Mine were shaking. She didn’t let go until the chopper came in for us. Until the pilot yelled climb. Until the ground was below us and the cloud was above us and the pink was coming back, slow as a tide.

We didn’t talk about it for days. Not really. We talked about the coconuts. About the mosquitoes. About the gin. About the way the paper wouldn’t hold the pen. About the way the technicians joked about their wives back home, how they’d send postcards with little pictures of beaches that didn’t exist and letters that said everything was fine when nothing was fine. We talked about the way the engineers measured everything but nothing. The way the congressmen came out and stood on the observation bunkers, sipping water from plastic cups, asking questions they already knew the answers to, taking photos they’d never look at again. We talked about the way the sky changes when you’re watching it. How it gets heavier. How it gets quieter. How it gets full of something you can’t name.

Inez died in ’78. Not from the glass. Not from the tests. From a cough that turned into something else, something the doctors couldn’t name, something that just took her slow. I was there. I held her hand. I didn’t tell her about the glass in my pocket. I didn’t tell her about the flash. I didn’t tell her about the way the sky looks before it breaks. I just sat there. I let the quiet do the work.

You ask me why I’m telling you this now. You ask me why I’m sitting in this diner with the cracked vinyl seats and the coffee that tastes like boiled tin, talking to you like you’re her, like you’re the woman I loved, like you’re the woman I failed. I don’t know. Maybe it’s because you listen. Maybe it’s because you don’t ask how many pounds of fallout settled on the coral. Maybe it’s because you just watch the flash.

I keep going back to Johnston. Not because it was special. Because it was small. Because it was just sand and sky and waiting. Because the weather could go off for weeks and you’d just sit there, cracking coconuts, catching lobsters, playing gin, slapping mosquitoes, waiting for the sky to turn pink. I used to think that was the point. The waiting. The being still. The knowing something’s coming but having no control over when. I don’t think that anymore. I think the point is the light. The pink. The wet air. The gardenias. The way the sand holds the heat. The way the water swallows the glass. The way you keep it in your pocket anyway.

You want to know what I’d tell myself if I could go back. I’d tell myself to watch the sky. Not the flash. The sky. The pink. The wet. The flowers. The way the air smells like something alive before it turns into something dead. I’d tell myself to remember the coconuts. The lobsters. The gin. The way the technicians laughed when the Geiger counters clicked too fast. The way the pilots joked about the weather. The way the congressmen asked questions they didn’t care about the answers to. I’d tell myself to carry the glass. To let it burn a hole in your pocket. To watch the sky turn gray. To let it stay gray. To watch it turn pink again.

You’re pouring your coffee. You’re looking at me. You’re waiting. I know that look. I’ve seen it in mirrors. I’ve seen it in Inez’s eyes. I’ve seen it in the glass on the shore. I’ve seen it in the sky before the flash. It’s the look of something that knows it’s coming. It’s the look of something that’s been waiting. It’s the look of something that’s ready to watch.

There was a shot at Kwajalein I never wrote down. Never told anyone. The wind blew the wrong way that morning, so the cloud didn’t climb like it should. It just spread out, like oil on water, like a bruise spreading across the blue. The technical guys cursed in three languages. The pilots circled too long, burned too much fuel, came back with empty tanks and full lungs. I stood on the observation pier, watching the cloud settle over the lagoon, watching it drift toward the atolls where the fish had stopped biting, where the water had turned a color no fisherman would recognize. I didn’t feel anything. Not fear. Not pride. Just this hollow, heavy stillness, like the world had been turned down a notch and I was the only one who noticed the volume had dropped.

Inez came out to find me. She wore a white dress, the kind that gets heavy with salt, the kind that clings to your legs and reminds you of the water you’re standing next to. She didn’t say anything. She just stood beside me, her shoulder brushing mine, her eyes on the cloud. After a long time, she said, It looks like a wound. I said, Yes. She said, Will it heal? I said, Not on this side. She didn’t argue. She never argued. She just reached into her purse, pulled out a pack of cigarettes, handed me one, and lit it for me. The smoke went straight up into the cloud. I watched it disappear. I thought about how we spend our lives trying to measure the unmeasurable, trying to name the unnamed, trying to capture the uncapturable. And then we forget to look at what’s right in front of us. We forget to look at the pink. We forget to look at the wet. We forget to look at the flowers. We forget to look at each other.
