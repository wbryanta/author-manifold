The strait does not care about names. It has its own geography, measured in fathoms and the slow drag of kelp, in the salt that crystallizes on the railing and the way the grey water refuses to hold a reflection. The ferry moves through it like a blade through heavy fabric, the hull singing a low, continuous note that travels up through the soles of his boots and into the bones of his knees. He stands at the glass, breath fogging the cold pane, watching the wake unspool behind them, a white scar that closes as quickly as it opens. Tomorrow they will be on the other side. Tomorrow he will walk down a corridor of linoleum and fluorescent light and stand before a stainless steel table and lift a canvas sheet and see a face that may or may not belong to a man he has not spoken to in eleven years. The letter had been terse, typed on a single sheet, postmarked from a coastal town three hours north. The coroner’s office needed a next of kin. There was only him. The envelope contained a ticket, a key card, and a photograph of a wrist, bare, with a thin silver chain and a small scar shaped like a crescent moon. His breath clears a circle on the glass. He presses his palm against it. The cold leaches through the skin.

He has carried the photograph in his pocket for two days. It sits against his thigh like a small stone. In the image, the skin is pale, almost translucent, mapped with blue veins that branch like river deltas. The chain catches the light. The scar is unmistakable. It is from a bicycle fall at seven, from the edge of the driveway, from the way he had landed on his outstretched hand and laughed through the blood, insisting he was fine, that pain was just the body learning its limits. He remembers the exact shade of green his mother’s kitchen wall was then, the sound of ice cracking in the windows, the way his brother’s hair fell into his eyes, dark and damp. He remembers the words he had not said. He remembers the phone calls he had let ring until they disconnected. The distance between them was not a single rupture but a slow sedimentation, layer upon layer of unsaid things, of missed birthdays, of holidays spent in parallel houses, of the quiet understanding that some bridges are built to hold traffic but not footsteps.

The ferry’s engine shifts pitch. A low groan, then a steady hum. He turns from the window. The interior of the vessel is a study in utilitarian grace. Corridors of brushed steel, carpets the colour of dried moss, windows that frame the water in shifting rectangles. He walks toward the dining area. The chairs are stacked. The tables are bare except for a few crumbs and a napkin folded into a clumsy triangle. He sits anyway. The vibration of the hull travels through the table legs, through the wood, into his forearms. He closes his eyes. In the dark behind his eyelids, he sees the kitchen again. His brother at the stove, stirring something that smells of thyme and browned butter, humming a melody he cannot quite place. It is a jazz standard, perhaps, or something older, something that belongs to the floorboards. His brother’s hands are long-fingered, restless, always moving, always shaping. He remembers the way they would argue over the radio, over the placement of the salt shaker, over the meaning of silence, and how the silence would eventually win, settling between them like dust. He remembers the last time he saw him. It was autumn. The leaves were the colour of old coins. They stood on the porch, coats buttoned to the throat, hands in pockets. The words had been careful, measured, each one a stone placed deliberately in a stream. He had meant to turn around. He had meant to say something that would bend the moment. He had not. The door had closed. The sound of it had been final, or nearly so.

He opens his eyes. The ferry has entered a channel where the water darkens to the colour of steeped tea. The lights on the far shore are scattered, irregular, like embers falling from a fire. He stands, walks to the upper deck. The wind is sharp, carrying the smell of diesel and wet stone and something organic, something that belongs to the deep. He pulls his coat tighter. The railing is cold enough to burn. He leans against it. Below, the water moves in slow, deliberate waves, each one a continuation of the last, each one forgetting the one before. He thinks of the body. He thinks of the scar. He thinks of the impossibility of recognition. How do you confirm a life through a face that has stopped breathing? How do you weigh a presence against an absence? He has spent years constructing a self without him, layer by layer, room by room, and now he must walk back through the walls he has built, touch the mortar, see if it holds.

The dining area is open. He descends the stairs. The carpet muffles his steps. He passes a vending machine, its glass face reflecting the corridor in fragmented slices. He presses a button. A can of bitter coffee drops into the tray. He carries it to a corner table. The light here is softer, yellowed by decades of use. He sits. He does not drink. He watches the reflection of the corridor in the machine’s glass. It bends, distorts, multiplies. He thinks of his brother’s study, of the way he had arranged objects on his desk: a compass that no longer pointed north, a magnifying glass with a cracked lens, a stack of notebooks bound in cloth, their pages filled with sketches of birds, of hands, of windows. He had called it a museum of fragments. He had said that every piece held a truth, even if the truth was only that something had been here, once, and is gone now. He had laughed then. He had not understood. He understands now, or begins to. The ferry turns. The horizon tilts. The water rushes past the windows, a blur of grey and white. He stands. He takes the stairs two at a time, up to the passenger deck, then down a service corridor marked with faded signage. The air grows cooler. The sound of the engine becomes a presence, a vibration in the teeth. He finds the door. It is heavy, steel, with a keycard reader and a small window. He inserts the card. The light turns green. The lock disengages with a soft clack. He pushes.

The room is cold. Not the cold of winter, but the cold of preservation, of suspended time. It smells of antiseptic and ozone and something faintly metallic, like the taste of a coin on the tongue. Fluorescent lights hum overhead, casting a flat, shadowless glow. In the center of the room stands a table, stainless steel, gleaming. On it, a body. Covered by a canvas sheet that drapes with the weight of something final. He approaches slowly. His boots echo on the linoleum. He stops at the edge of the table. He places his hand on the sheet. It is cool, slightly damp. He lifts one corner. The fabric is heavy, woven tight. He pulls it back, inch by inch, until the face is visible. The skin is pale, waxy, the colour of old parchment. The features are softened by death, the edges blurred, the mouth slightly open, the eyes closed. He looks for the scar. He looks for the chain. He looks for the hands. The hands are folded across the chest, fingers slightly curled, nails short, cut clean. The skin is pale. He lifts the sleeve. The wrist is bare. No chain. He looks closer. The scar is there, or a scar. A crescent moon, faint, faded, the colour of ash. He breathes. He does not know if it is him. He does not know if it matters. He stands there, watching the rise and fall of nothing, the stillness that has replaced the restless hands, the humming, the arguing, the silence. The room is cold. The lights hum. He reaches out, touches the shoulder. The fabric is cold. The body is cold. He withdraws his hand. He pulls the sheet back down. He turns away. He walks to the door. He swipes the card. He steps into the corridor. The door closes behind him with a sigh.

He walks back to the passenger deck. The wind has shifted. The water is dark, almost black. The sky is lighter, the colour of wet slate. He sits on a bench near a window. He opens his pocket. He takes out the photograph. He holds it up to the light. The scar is clear. The chain catches the light. He closes his fist around it. He presses it to his chest. He breathes. The ferry moves. The strait moves. The world moves. He sits. He watches the water. He thinks of his brother’s hands. He thinks of the kitchen wall. He thinks of the porch. He thinks of the door. He thinks of the weight of a life, how it settles in the bones, how it leaves a shape, how the shape remains even after the weight is gone. He does not know if the body is his brother’s. He knows that he has come. He knows that he has looked. He knows that he has lifted the sheet and let it fall. He knows that some questions do not need answers. They need only a witness. He closes his eyes. The engine hums. The water moves. The strait does not care. He opens his eyes. The horizon is grey. The light is grey. The day is grey. He stands. He walks to the window. He presses his palm against the glass. The cold leaches through the skin. He watches the wake. It closes. It opens. It closes. He breathes. The ferry moves. The strait moves. He moves with them. He does not know. He does not need to.

He remembers the summer they were twelve, the way the heat would pool in the valley like water, the way the cicadas would rise in the late afternoon, a sound that was less noise than pressure, pressing against the eardrums until silence became a physical thing. They had built a raft from scavenged planks and rope, something that sagged in the middle, that listed when they stood too far forward. They had paddled to the middle of the lake, where the water was so still it mirrored the sky exactly, and for a time, the world was only wood and water and the slow dip of the oars. His brother had sat at the back, humming again, that same tune, the one that belonged to the floorboards, the one he could never place. He had said, You’ll find it in a minor key. You’ll hear it in a train station, or in a room you’ve never visited, and you’ll know it because it will feel like coming home to a house that no longer exists. He had laughed then, thinking of architecture, of blueprints, of the way people built walls to keep the wind out and the silence in. He had not understood. He understood now, or was beginning to, the way a child understands gravity only after falling, the way a musician understands rest only after playing the long note. The raft had taken on water. They had swum back to shore, laughing, coughing, shivering in the sun. He had climbed out first, dripping, barefoot on the hot grass. He had turned to help his brother up the embankment, but his brother had shaken his head, waving him ahead, saying, After you. He had walked away. He had not looked back. He had not realized then that some things do not need to be saved. They need to be witnessed, and released.

The ferry’s lights flicker, a momentary dip in the current, as if the vessel itself is breathing. He watches the corridor through the window, the way the overhead lamps cast long, thin shadows that stretch and contract with the motion of the ship. Architecture is just time made visible, his brother had once told him, tracing the lines of a ceiling beam with a charcoal pencil. You can trace the weight of it, see where it has settled, where it has cracked. You can learn to read a room the way you read a score. He had nodded, politely, not knowing what to do with the metaphor, how to hold it without breaking it. He had left the room, walked down the hall, closed the door. He had learned to live in rooms with no cracks. He had built a life of straight lines, of right angles, of doors that opened and closed with quiet certainty. He had not considered that a straight line is also a distance, that certainty is just another word for fear, that doors are only meant to be opened.

He stands. He walks to the galley. The air is warmer here, thick with the smell of damp wool and stale coffee. He presses his palm against the counter. The laminate is smooth, cool. He thinks of his brother’s kitchen, the way he had always left the dishes in the sink, the way the water would pool around the bases of the glasses, the way the light would fall through the window at four in the afternoon, painting the floor in long, golden rectangles. He had hated it. He had called it negligence, a lack of respect for the order of things. He had not understood then that order is a kind of grief, that you arrange the world because you cannot arrange time. He opens his pocket. He takes out the photograph again. The edges are softened by friction, the corners slightly bent. He runs his thumb over the image of the wrist, tracing the vein that branches like a river delta, the scar that is barely there, the colour of ash, the chain that is not. He does not know if it is him. He does not know if it matters. He places the photograph back in his pocket. He closes the door to the galley. He walks back to the window. The water is darker now. The sky is lighter. The strait is wide. He presses his palm against the glass. The cold leaches through. He breathes. The ferry moves. The strait moves. He moves with them.

He remembers the winter they were seventeen, the way the cold would seep through the floorboards, the way the pipes would sing in the walls, a low, continuous note that traveled up through the soles of his boots and into the bones of his knees. They had shared a room, two beds against the far wall, a window that opened onto the street, a radiator that clicked and hissed and never quite warmed the space. He had read. His brother had drawn. He had watched him, from the corner of his eye, the way his hand moved across the paper, fast, sure, leaving lines that were never quite straight, never quite what he intended, but somehow always true. He had asked him once, What are you making? His brother had not looked up. Something that will outlast the paper. He had laughed. He had not understood. He understands now, or is beginning to, the way a stone understands the river, the way a house understands the wind, the way a body understands the passage of time, not as a line, but as a layer, a sediment, a slow accumulation of moments that settle in the bones and become the weight that keeps you standing. He had left the room, walked down the hall, closed the door. He had learned to live in rooms with no drafts, no leaks, no sounds. He had built a life of stillness, of quiet, of doors that opened and closed with muffled precision. He had not considered that stillness is also a kind of distance, that quiet is just another word for absence, that doors are only meant to be left ajar.

The ferry’s engine shifts again, a deeper tone, a change in the current. He watches the water, the way it moves in slow, deliberate waves, each one a continuation of the last, each one forgetting the one before. He thinks of the body, the scar, the chain, the silence, the weight, the stillness, the distance. He does not know if it is him. He does not know if it matters. He presses his palm against the glass.
