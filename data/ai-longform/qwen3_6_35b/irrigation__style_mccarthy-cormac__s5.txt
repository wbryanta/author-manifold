The sun came down on the orchard like a forge and the brothers worked in the white glare without speaking. The irrigation line ran between the rows like a wounded thing, its seams weeping slow beads of moisture into the cracked clay. Dust hung in the air and settled on their shoulders and the backs of their necks and the rusted iron of the pipe. The older man knelt in the furrow, his knees sinking into the powdered earth, a band of sweat carving a path through the grime on his forehead. The younger stood with the pipe wrench in his hands, the steel gleaming hot to the touch, watching the water escape in a steady hissing thread that vanished into the dust before it could find the roots of the nearest tree. The trees stood in ragged ranks, their canopies thinning, the peaches on the lower branches small and hard and covered in a fine down that felt like chalk. Summer had arrived without ceremony and the sky above was a polished slab of pale blue, devoid of clouds, devoid of birds, the kind of sky that seemed to press down rather than lift up. The older man ran a hand along the cracked sleeve of his shirt, wiped his face with the back of his wrist, and reached for the coupling. The younger shifted his weight, the wrench resting against his thigh, and looked out toward the ridge where the pines stood dark and unmoving in the heat.

He will not hold the torch. The older man said it without turning his head. The gas ran out last week and we used the last of it on the water heater. The younger man nodded slowly. The older man leaned forward, the joints of his spine clicking like dry wood, and fitted the new coupling to the end of the broken section. His fingers were thick and scarred, the nails blackened with grease and soil, the knuckles swollen from years of turning stiff bolts and pulling frozen lines in the winter. He did not speak again. The younger man took the wrench and braced his foot against the furrow wall, his shoulders tightening as he turned the fitting against the thread. The metal ground against metal, a sound like stone on stone, and then the coupling seated with a dull click. The older man reached for the torch, struck the flint, and the blue flame hissed to life. He held it to the seam and the solder melted and ran like liquid silver down the curve of the pipe. The heat rose in waves and the air shimmered above the furrow and the older man held his breath and kept the flame steady until the solder set and the flame died. He blew on the seam and it darkened and the hissing stopped. For a moment there was only the sound of their breathing and the distant murmur of the creek far beyond the fence line and the slow drift of dust in the light.

The water will run again today. The younger man said it like a fact and not a promise.
