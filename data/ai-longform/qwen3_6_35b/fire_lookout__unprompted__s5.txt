The radio was a tombstone of black plastic and brass dials sitting on the scarred pine desk, its antenna coiled like a sleeping snake, its silence absolute. Mara had pressed the transmit button until her knuckle turned white, her thumb working the rubber switch through a rhythm of hope and denial, but the airwaves were empty. The storm had chewed through the line earlier in the afternoon, she realized, the memory surfacing with the suddenness of a trap snapping. Or the wind had twisted the whip. Or the capacitors had finally surrendered to the forty years of oxidation that lived in the walls. It didn't matter which. The result was a hermetic seal around the cupola, a glass bell jar enclosing Mara and the smell of hot varnish and old wool.

She stood by the four corners of glass, the heat of the day pressing against the panes, turning the world beyond into a shimmering dream of green and gray. The mountain was a living thing beneath her boots, a vast, slow respiration of pine and granite and shadow, but her eyes had been drawn by the unnatural geometry of the sky. There, three ridges over, where the topography collapsed into the jagged throat of the Cedar Draw, a column of smoke was rising. It was not the wispy, gray betrayal of a distant lightning strike. It was a thick, bruised violet finger, pulsing slightly in the heat waves, climbing with the arrogance of a living creature. It had the density of intent.

Mara lowered her binoculars, the leather straps digging into her temples. The lenses clicked as she focused. The smoke was coming from the basin where the drainage cut a scar through the timber, feeding the creek that vanished into limestone sinks. And in that basin, like a tooth hidden in the gums of the earth, sat the cabin. The metal roof, once red, was now the color of dried blood, peeling in long ribbons. She could see the rusted chimney, the overgrown porch sagging toward the ground, the way the pines had begun to reclaim the clearing, their needles brushing the eaves. Juno's sanctuary. Juno's mistake.

The thought of the name was a physical blow, a dull ache behind the sternum that had been familiar for twelve years. Juno. Her sister. The woman who had laughed with her mouth wide open, who drank cheap wine from mason jars, who could make a fire dance with a match and a whisper. The woman who had walked away from the funeral with the wind in her hair and not once looked back.

Mara turned from the glass and walked to the map board. The topographic lines swirled like frozen water, the contour of the Cedar Draw circled in red ink that had long since faded to the color of a weak pulse. Eight miles. The red line of the access road snaked downhill, a thin scratch of gravel that wound through switchbacks and gullies, descending through two thousand feet of elevation before connecting to the county highway. Eight miles of graded dirt, rock, and gravity. Eight miles that Mara could traverse in her jeep, if the engine would turn over, if the battery would hold, if the tires would find purchase in the heat-haze.

She walked to the shelf where the emergency kit lay. Flares. A compass. A first-aid box with missing bandages. She opened the radio again, popped the back panel with a trembling knife, and examined the wiring. The main lead had sheared off at the junction, a clean break as if a bolt of light had severed it. There was nothing to be done. The tower, the decommissioned skeleton of the Forest Service's obsolete network, was truly silent. She was a lookout with no voice, a sentinel with a broken whistle.

Mara closed the panel and sat on the stool. The wood groaned, the same complaint it had made every summer since she was twenty-four, when she'd first taken the job as a penance for a life spent running. Now, at forty-four, she was in her final summer. The notice had been posted in town, printed on letterhead that smelled of bureaucracy. The network was gone. The drones would do the watching. The satellites would do the mapping. The towers would be sold for scrap or turned into boutique rentals for people who wanted to sleep in the clouds without the draft. Her contract ended in three weeks. This was the last rotation. The last time she would count the hours. The last time she would drink tea that tasted of iron and watch the world through the glass.

The smoke column had shifted. A cross-wind, high up, was tilting it west, pushing it toward the ridge that separated the draw from the valley floor. Toward the cabin.

Mara stood up, her knees popping, the sound loud in the small room. She felt the old urge, the reflex carved into her spine by decades of patrol. *Call it in. Coordinate. Evacuate.* But there was no one to call. There was no one to coordinate with. The road was eight miles away, and the cell tower at the ranger station was likely fried along with the radio. She was the only soul for twenty miles, a hermit in a metal hat, watching her sister's life burn.

*Let it burn,* a voice whispered in the back of her mind. It sounded like her mother's voice, sharp and practical. *Let the fire take what Juno clings to. Let the house burn, let the land burn, let the fire burn the stubbornness out of her bones.*

Mara gripped the railing of the cupola door. The metal was hot enough to blister skin. She thought of the last time she had seen Juno. The kitchen table in their childhood home, now just a memory of water damage and mold. Juno had been packing a bag, her hands moving fast, her eyes bright with a manic sheen that had become permanent. *You stay up here, Mara. You stay in the tower. You watch everything and do nothing. You're a statue. I'm going to live.* Juno had taken the silver picture frames and the good knives and a bottle of whiskey that had belonged to their father. She hadn't taken the sister who stood frozen by the doorway, paralyzed by the fear of movement, the terror of the world beyond the perimeter of the safe and the known.

Mara left the cupola. She descended the iron stairs, the clang of her boots echoing down the hollow center of the tower, a sound that used to soothe her, a sound that meant work. Now it was just noise. She reached the ground floor, the kitchenette, the sleeping alcove where the cot lay with its canvas sagging. She grabbed the heavy canvas jacket, the fire boots, the pack. She checked the weight of the water bladder, the flare gun, the multi-tool. She looked at the jeep keys on the hook. They were there, brass teeth gleaming.

She didn't need the jeep. The jeep was for the last week, for the pack-up, for the slow retreat from this life. If she took the jeep, she would spend hours fighting the heat, the mud, the mechanical temper. She would burn the day. She would arrive tired, angry, and too late. The fire moved faster than metal. If Juno was down there, if the cabin was involved, time was the enemy.

Mara stepped out onto the catwalk. The heat hit her like a physical wall, the air shimmering off the rusted grating. Below, the forest stretched out, a sea of green needles catching the sun, but her eyes tracked the violet finger. She could see now that the smoke had an orange core. The fire was active. It was eating.

She began the descent.

The trail down was a relic of the service days, barely a path now, more a suggestion of footing between fern and root. Mara moved with the economy of habit. Her body knew the terrain even as her mind raced. Step on the rock, not the moss. Watch the loose shale. Keep the center of gravity low. The altitude dropped rapidly, the air thickening, smelling of resin and the distant, acrid tang that was already beginning to drift up from the draw.

As she walked, the years unspooled. The tower was a place of time, but the descent was a place of memory. The first summer, when she'd come to this mountain to forget the man who had left her, the one whose shadow still stretched across the floor of her mind. The summer Juno had shown up, uninvited, bringing a casserole and a laugh that shook the dust off the windows. *You look like you're waiting for a message from God,* Juno had said. *I'm waiting for a fire,* Mara had replied. *Well, I'm bringing the spark,* Juno had answered, and that was the problem. Juno was always the spark.

They had grown apart not with a bang, but with the slow drift of currents. Mara chose the vertical life, the high clear view where everything was distant and manageable. Juno chose the horizontal chaos, the messy intimacy of the earth where things rotted and grew in tangled knots. Mara's estrangement was not a sudden rejection; it was the culmination of a thousand small choices. The letters that went unanswered because the pen felt heavy. The phone calls that went to voicemail because the silence of the tower was addictive. The birthday that was spent staring at a horizon line. Juno had stopped trying eventually, or maybe Juno had just stopped being heard, swallowed by the vastness of Mara's discipline.

The smoke grew thicker. The light shifted from bright white to a diffuse, milky yellow. The birds were gone. The forest had fallen silent, holding its breath. Mara's lungs burned. The boots clomped on the hard-packed earth, the vibration traveling up her shins, a reminder of her own flesh and blood, fragile and failing. She was forty-four, but the mountain made her feel ancient. The trees were elders; she was a sapling swaying in the wind.

Halfway down, the terrain steepened. The path became a scramble. Mara gripped the roots of a lodgepole pine, her muscles screaming. The smell of burning was now overpowering, coating her tongue, her throat. It was the smell of pine sap boiling, of sugar caramelizing, of destruction. It was the smell of Juno's cabin. Juno, who smoked tobacco that reeked of burning leaves. Juno, who never cleaned the gutters. Juno, who probably drank herself to sleep while the sparks climbed the roof.

A wave of anger rose in Mara, hot and clean. *Idiot,* she thought. *Fool. Reckless creature.* But beneath the anger was a cold terror, sharp as a knife. Juno was alone. Juno was sick. Juno was always alone in the end, even when she was surrounded by things.

Mara reached the ridge above the draw. The heat here was intense, the air dry and crackling. Through the haze, she could see the drainage. It was a gash in the earth, filled with scrub oak and fallen timber. The fire was in the oak. A wall of flame was moving up the slope, hungry and fast, leaping from branch to branch in a cascade of orange light. The cabin was at the bottom, nestled in a bend of the creek. The creek was dry, a bed of gray stones, but the fire was climbing the bank. The smoke was thick now, roiling in columns, blocking the sun.

Mara crouched behind a rock, breathing through her mouth. The fire was moving downhill, chasing her down. It was smart. It was using the wind, the draft, the fuel. It was a living thing, and it was closing in. She watched, her heart hammering against her ribs, waiting for the smoke to clear, waiting to see the roof. If the roof held, Juno might be safe. If the roof was gone, the fire might have reached the bedroom.

Then, a shape emerged from the smoke near the cabin. A movement. A dark figure staggering toward the dry creek bed. Mara squinted. It was a person. The figure fell, scrambled up, fell again.

Mara didn't think. She launched herself down the slope, her boots sliding on loose scree, her knees hitting rocks, her hands scraping bark. She slid, she ran, she crawled. The heat intensified, the air growing thin and toxic. She coughed, her vision swimming with black spots. The fire was close now, the roar a constant roar in her ears, like the ocean. Branches popped and hissed. Sparks flew like angry fireflies, landing on the dry grass around her.

She reached the creek bed just as the figure tried to stand. The figure collapsed again. Mara scrambled down the bank, sliding through the ash and the dry sand.

"Juno!" she screamed, her voice raw.

The figure turned. Juno's face was blackened with soot, her hair a wild tangle of smoke and wind, her clothes torn. One leg was bent at an impossible angle. Her eyes were wide, glassy with shock, but clear enough to recognize Mara.

"Mara?" Juno's voice was a rasp, a whisper torn from her throat. "You're... you're in the sky."

"I'm here," Mara said, grabbing Juno's shoulders. The skin was burning hot. "You're in the creek. You're safe."

"The cabin..." Juno coughed, a wet, ragged sound. "The whiskey. I dropped the lantern. The rug caught. I couldn't... I couldn't get up."

Mara looked up. The fire was cresting the ridge, pouring down like a waterfall of liquid fire. The heat was blinding. The smoke was a blanket, thick and dark. The roar was deafening.

"We have to move," Mara said. "The creek bed. We go down to the limestone sink. The water might be down, but the rock is wet. We can wait it out."

Juno tried to stand, whimpering as her weight shifted. Mara wrapped an arm around Juno's waist, hoisting her up. Juno was lighter than she should be, all sharp bones and dry muscle, her frame wasted by years of living on the edge. Mara took the weight, her own boots finding purchase in the ash.

"Walk," Mara commanded. "Left foot. Right foot. I've got you."

They moved down the creek bed, Mara half-carrying Juno, dragging her legs over rocks and fallen timber. The heat was unbearable. The air was full of embers that stuck to their skin, burning little red sores. Mara's lungs felt like they were filled with glass. She could feel the fire closing in behind them, the crackle turning to a deep growl. The cabin was behind them now, a silhouette of light and smoke. She could hear the timber popping as it burned.

They reached the bend where the limestone emerged. The rock was dark, slick with algae. Mara pushed Juno against the wall. "Sit. Stay still."

She scanned the terrain. Ahead, the creek bed narrowed, flanked by steep walls of gray stone. Further on, there was a cavern, a shallow cave that her father had shown her once when they'd come hunting. It was a good hide. It would hold.

"Can you crawl?" Mara asked.

Juno nodded, tears cutting tracks through the soot on her cheeks. "Yes."

Mara grabbed Juno's good arm and pulled her forward. They crawled through the ash, the fire snapping inches behind them. Mara could feel the heat on her back, the hair on her arms curling. She kicked at a burning branch that had fallen across the path. The smoke was thickest here, the world reduced to gray and orange.

They reached the cave. It was narrow, barely wide enough for two, but deep. Mara dragged Juno inside, then collapsed after her, pulling her legs in. The cave opened into a small pocket of still air. The roar of the fire was muffled here, the heat intense but bearable. They huddled against the far wall, breathing the thick, hot air.

Mara pulled the flare gun from her belt and lay it between them. It was useless against the fire, but it felt like a talisman. She turned to Juno. Her sister was shivering, her teeth chattering. Mara tore off her own fire jacket and draped it over Juno. Her hands were shaking so badly she could barely unbutton her coat.

"Look at me," Mara said.

Juno's eyes focused. They were blue, the same blue as the sky before the smoke, the same blue as the eyes that had laughed in the tower. "Mara. I'm sorry. I'm sorry for the tower. I'm sorry for the letters. I didn't want you to burn. I just didn't want to watch anymore."

Mara touched Juno's face, the soot coming away on her fingertips. "I was watching," she whispered. "I was always watching. I was afraid."

"Fear is the only thing that burns," Juno rasped. "Fire just takes. It doesn't judge."

They sat in the dark of the cave, the fire roaring outside, a beast pacing its cage. Mara held Juno's hand, her thumb rubbing over the knuckles, the scars, the calluses. She felt the pulse, fast and fluttering. Alive.

"I'm leaving the tower," Mara said. The words came out flat, factual.

Juno squeezed her hand. "I know. The paper was in town. I heard."

"I'm staying," Mara said. "After this. I'm staying here. Not in the tower. Here. With you."

Juno let out a sound that was half-laugh, half-sob. "You? Staying in the drainage? You'll hate the bugs. You'll hate the damp."

"I'll learn to live in the dirt," Mara said. "I'm done with the sky. The sky is just air. The earth is where the roots are."

The fire raged for hours. Mara counted the beats of Juno's heart to the rhythm of the crackling. She told Juno stories, not the grand tales of the fire seasons, but the small things. The time Juno had stolen the neighbor's goat. The time Mara had tried to bake a cake and set the kitchen curtain on fire. The time their father had taught them to tie a knot. The stories were anchors, holding them in the dark.

Slowly, the roar diminished. The air began to cool. The heat faded. Mara ventured to the entrance of the cave. The smoke was thinning, drifting up in lazy tendrils. The world beyond was changed. The drainage was a skeleton of blackened trunks. The cabin was gone, reduced to a pile of smoldering ruins and twisted metal. The sky was a bruised purple, the stars beginning to prick through the haze.

Mara turned back to Juno. "It's over."

Juno was asleep, her breathing shallow but steady. Mara pulled Juno close, tucking the jacket tight. They stayed in the cave until dawn. The light crept in, pale and gray, revealing the devastation. But it was also revealing the recovery. The green shoots of the fern were already showing through the ash. The creek was flowing again, clear and cold.

Mara carried Juno out of the cave to the bank of the stream. She filled the water bladder and helped Juno drink. Juno's eyes were open, looking at the sky, at the towers of steam rising from the earth, at the stars fading in the morning light.

"You saw it," Juno whispered. "You came down."

"I saw it," Mara said. "The smoke spoke to me. It wasn't just smoke. It was you."

Juno looked at her, a weak smile touching her lips. "You're a witch, Mara. A witch with binoculars."

"I'm just your sister," Mara said. "Come on. We have to get to the road. I'll get help. We'll fix this."

Juno shook her head, her hand gripping Mara's wrist with surprising strength. "No road, Mara. The fire jumped the creek downstream. The road is gone."

Mara looked downstream. A wall of blackened timber stretched across the path. The access road was sealed. They were trapped in the basin.

"Then we wait," Mara said. "We wait for the wind to change. We wait for the fire to cool. We wait."

Juno leaned back against the rock, her eyes closing. "You always know what to wait for. I never did."

Mara sat beside her sister, the cool stone against her back, the scent of ash and wet earth filling her lungs. She looked out over the burned landscape, the devastation that was also a blank slate. She thought of the tower, the glass, the distance. She thought of the eight miles of road, the radio, the silence. And she realized that the estrangement had been the real fire, the one that had been burning inside them for years, consuming the connection, leaving only ash.

She had come down for the smoke, for the threat. She had stayed for the truth. The cabin was gone. The tower would be gone. But the sister was here. The sister was alive. The sister was holding her hand.

Mara opened her pack and took out the last of the chocolate, the emergency ration. She broke it in half and handed it to Juno. "Eat. You'll need your strength. We have a lot of rebuilding to do."

Juno took the chocolate, her fingers brushing Mara's. "You'll have to show me how. I've forgotten everything."

"You haven't forgotten," Mara said. "You just learned the wrong things. The right things are the ones that hold when the roof falls in."

Juno ate the chocolate, the sweetness a small shock in the bitter air. "The tower," she murmured. "It's quiet up there."

"It is now," Mara said. "I'm quiet here. Listen to the creek. Listen to the ground. It's loud enough."

The sun rose fully, burning off the remaining smoke, turning the blackened world to gray and silver. The heat of the fire was replaced by the warmth of the morning. Mara watched the horizon, not with the eye of a lookout, but with the heart of a woman. There was no smoke to report. There was no fire to fight. There was only the sister beside her, and the long road back, and the summer that was finally, truly, ending.

She leaned her head on Juno's shoulder, feeling the pulse of her life, the rhythm of her breath. The tower would fall. The road would heal. The world would move on. But here, in the wreckage, Mara had found what she had been watching for all along. Not the danger on the horizon, but the hand in the dark.

"Stay close," Mara whispered.

"I'm not going anywhere," Juno replied, her voice steady for the first time in years. "I'm right here."

And for the first time in twelve years, Mara believed it. She closed her eyes and listened to the earth, no longer waiting for a signal, no longer watching the sky, but rooted in the present, in the ash, in the sister, in the survival. The excerpt of her isolation ended there, in the cave, in the smoke, in the hand that held on. The new story had begun, written not in the logbook of the tower, but in the scars of the land and the silent vow between two women who had burned down their distance and found each other in the ruins.
