The strait does not announce itself. It arrives as a darkening, a thickening of the air until the trees on either shore cease to be boundaries and become merely a darker shade of night. The ferry pushes through it with a low, continuous hum that settles in the teeth, a frequency older than the timber of the deck. Salt gathers on the windows in thin, crystalline veins. Inside, the lounge smells of damp wool, instant coffee, and the faint metallic tang of recycled air. He sits near the rear window, knees drawn up, a paper bag from the dining cart resting on his thighs. The coffee inside has gone cold. He does not drink it. He watches the water move past the glass, black and glassy, rippling only where the bow cuts it, leaving a pale, frothing wake that dissolves before it reaches the stern.

Three hundred miles of cold water separate him from the medical examiner’s office, from the fluorescent hum of a refrigerated hold, from a body wrapped in a yellowed sheet and tagged with a barcode. The letter arrived on a Tuesday, folded neatly, the envelope heavy with the weight of institutional procedure. *We have recovered a body from the channel off Point Grey.* He read it twice. Then he drove north. Then he boarded the ferry at midnight, where the air is thin and the engine vibration travels up through the soles of his boots, into his calves, into the small of his back, until it feels less like a machine and more like a pulse. He carries a second paper in his pocket, folded into a square, the edges soft from friction. It is not a letter. It is a photograph, though he cannot remember taking it. His brother’s face, turned away, caught in the periphery of a summer afternoon, sunlight catching the nape of the neck, a patch of sun-bleached hair, a shoulder pulled into a jacket that no longer exists. He does not know if the face will match the memory. He does not know if the face will matter.

The ferry is a floating architecture of suspended lives. Across the aisle, a woman sleeps with her mouth slightly open, a newspaper fanned over her stomach. A man in a navy blazer types on a laptop, the keyboard clicks sharp and rhythmic, a counterpoint to the engine’s drone. Near the door, a teenager watches a screen, light pale on his face, earbuds sealing him into a private frequency. None of them are carrying grief. They are carrying groceries, or homework, or the quiet ambition of a commute. He watches their hands. Fingers drumming. A wedding band catching the overhead light. A knuckle scarred from some long-ago impact. He thinks of the other’s hands. How they used to rest on the steering wheel of a rusted pickup, knuckles swollen from cold, how they would tap a rhythm against the glass when stuck in traffic, how they once held a cup of tea so tightly the fingers went white, how they now lie either in the earth or in a metal drawer, waiting for a stranger to decide their name.

He has not spoken his brother’s name aloud in seven years. Not since the phone call. Not since the silence that followed, thicker than the strait itself. Estrangement does not arrive as a rupture but as a slow leaching. Years of unanswered letters, of birthdays marked only by the turning of the calendar, of phone numbers that disconnected, of visits to the same town that never intersected. They moved through parallel orbits, bound by gravity, never touching. He told himself it was mutual. He told himself it was necessary. He told himself many things, as one does when the truth is too heavy to carry. The truth is simpler: they grew quiet, and the quiet grew legs and walked away. Now he is crossing water to see what remains of the quiet.

The dining car is empty. The cook has gone home. The stainless steel counters reflect the ceiling lights in long, distorted strips. He runs his finger along the edge of a tray. The metal is cold. He thinks of a kitchen in a house they no longer own. The linoleum cracked in a jagged line. The radio playing a station that broadcast through static. His brother standing at the sink, shoulders rigid, washing a single plate. The sound of water running. The way the light from the window fell across his back, highlighting the dip between the shoulder blades, the tension in the neck, the unspoken words hanging in the steam. He wanted to say something then. He wanted to ask why they had stopped speaking entirely. He wanted to sit across from him and drink coffee until the cups were empty and the silence felt like a bridge instead of a wall. He said nothing. The plate dried. The radio faded. The silence became permanent.

A crew member walks past the lounge, boots heavy on the deck, a clipboard tucked under his arm. He does not look up. The body will be released at 0400. The man must sign. The man must confirm. The man must bear the weight of the signature. He has done this before, once, years ago, for a grandfather he barely knew. He remembers the paper, the pen, the way his hand shook, the way the examiner’s face was pleasant and empty, the way the word *next of kin* felt like a sentence. He remembers thinking that death is not dramatic. It is administrative. It is ink on a form, a timestamp, a cold room, a decision to let go. He had expected grief to hit him like a wave. Instead, he felt nothing but a strange relief that someone else would handle the logistics. Now he knows better. Grief does not arrive in waves. It arrives in increments. In the smell of antiseptic. In the texture of a sheet. In the silence after a name is spoken to an empty room.

He stands. The deck is open, or rather, the outer corridor is open to the air. The wind is sharp, carrying the smell of kelp and diesel and the cold, mineral scent of deep water. He steps out. The rain has stopped, but the air is wet, clinging to his coat, his hair, his face. The strait is wider here, the shores blurred into a single dark line. The ferry’s lights cut a path through the night, but beyond them, there is only water, endless and indifferent. He leans against the railing. The metal is cold through his gloves. He closes his eyes. He remembers the sea as a child, how it sounded like breathing, how it smelled of salt and rot and life, how his brother used to wade into it until the water reached his chest, laughing, turning his face to the sun, shouting something he cannot quite hear over the years. He remembers the way the brother’s voice used to fill rooms, how it could shift from laughter to quiet to anger in the span of a breath, how it had always been a sound he wanted to live inside. Now the voice is gone. Only the memory of it remains, faded like a photograph left in the sun.

He opens his eyes. The deck is empty. The strait is dark. The engine hums. He goes inside.

The lower deck is a labyrinth of corridors, service elevators, storage compartments, and a heavy steel door marked *HOLD / AUTHORIZED PERSONNEL ONLY*. A guard sits in a booth, reading a paperback, a radio crackling with static. The man shows his papers. The guard looks at them, then at him, then at the door. He does not ask questions. He does not offer sympathy. He presses a button. The door slides open with a heavy, hydraulic sigh. Cold air rushes out, dry and sharp, carrying the scent of formaldehyde and chilled air and something beneath it, something organic and suspended. The man steps through.

The corridor narrows. Fluorescent lights flicker, then steady. The floor is concrete, slightly damp. At the end, a small office with a desk, a chair, a computer, and a counter. Behind the counter stands a woman in a blue uniform, her hair pulled back, her face pale and composed. She nods. He nods. She gestures to a table. He sits. She places a form before him. A pen. A stamp. A folder. He signs. The pen scratches loudly in the quiet. He waits.

The door opens. A man in a grey uniform pushes a cart. On it lies a body. It is covered. The sheet is thin, yellowed at the edges, stiff with cold. The cart wheels are quiet on the concrete. The man pushes it to the counter. He pulls the sheet back. Just enough. Just enough for the face to be visible.

He does not look immediately. He looks at the hands first. They are folded over the chest, fingers slightly curled, nails short, skin pale, the knuckles swollen, the tendons standing out against the cold. He knows this anatomy. He has held these hands. He has watched them work, wave, point, tremble, rest on a steering wheel, tap against glass, hold a cup, let go. The skin is lighter than he remembers, the color of old paper, but the shape is the same. The slight bow of the index finger from a long-ago break. The callus on the middle finger from years of holding a pen. The scar on the thumb, a pale line from a kitchen knife. He closes his eyes. He breathes. He opens them.

He looks at the face. The hair is darker now, or perhaps the light is crueler, or perhaps the years have caught up. The jaw is sharper. The cheekbones more pronounced. The mouth slightly parted, the lips pale, the corners drooping. The eyes are closed. The skin is waxy, the pores visible in the harsh light. He does not recognize it. Not at first. He looks for the angle of the brow, the set of the nose, the way the left eyelid used to droop slightly when tired. He searches the memory for a match. The face is familiar in its architecture but alien in its expression. He looks at the ears. One is notched. He remembers the other’s ears. The shape, the lobe, the way the hair grew around them. He looks at the neck. The Adam’s apple. The scar on the collarbone. He remembers the other’s collarbone, how it protruded when he was sick, how the skin stretched tight over it, how he used to trace it with his finger in the dark, a silent geography of a person he no longer knows.

He stands. He walks closer. The air in the room is colder. The light hums. He reaches out. He does not touch. He hovers his hand over the shoulder, feeling the cold radiating from the body, the stillness, the finality of it. He thinks of the years between them. The letters he wrote and did not send. The phone calls he dialed and canceled. The times he stood outside the brother’s door, listening to the muffled sounds of a life continuing without him. The way he convinced himself that distance was respect, that silence was peace, that forgetting was healing. He was wrong. Distance is just another kind of waiting. Silence is just another kind of noise. Forgetting is a lie we tell ourselves to keep from drowning.

He looks back at the face. He tries to see the brother. He tries to see the boy who laughed at the edge of the sea, the man who built a deck with his own hands, the stranger who grew quiet, the body that lies on a cart in a cold room. They are all true. They are all him. The distinction collapses. The years dissolve. The strait between them was never water. It was time. Time is the only true distance. Time is the only true strait. And he has crossed it, not by sailing, but by standing here, in this room, with this face, and admitting that it is his brother. That it is him. That it is both.

He does not weep. He does not speak. He nods. He signs the second form. The woman behind the counter takes it. She stamps it. She places a copy in his hand. She says, *He will be taken to the county morgue at 0600. Your family may visit after release.* He says, *Thank you.* His voice is quiet, rough from disuse, from the cold, from the years. He turns. He walks back through the corridor. The guard looks up. Nods. The door closes behind him. The hum of the ferry returns.

He steps onto the deck. The rain has begun again, light, persistent, cold. The strait is lighter now, not with dawn, but with the possibility of it. The water is still dark, but the edges of the waves catch the sky’s pale underbelly. He leans against the railing. The rain beads on his coat, rolls down his sleeves, disappears into the cuffs. He thinks of the brother’s hands. He thinks of the way they used to rest on the steering wheel. He thinks of the way they will rest now, in the earth, or in the air, or in the space between breaths. He thinks of the silence they left behind, and how it was never empty. It was full of everything they did not say. Everything they did not ask. Everything they did not reach for. He thinks that love does not end. It changes shape. It becomes the strait. It becomes the ferry. It becomes the cold room. It becomes the signature on a form. It becomes the rain on a deck. It becomes the hum of an engine crossing water.

The deck lights reflect on the wet metal. The water moves past the hull, dark and steady. The sky deepens, not to light, but to a different kind of dark, the kind that holds the last stars before they fade. He does not know if he will return to the morgue. He does not know if he will visit the grave. He does not know if he will ever speak the name again. He knows only that he has crossed. He knows only that he has seen. He knows only that the body is his brother, and his brother is gone, and the strait remains, and he is still here, breathing, cold, alive, and the ferry pushes forward, cutting the water, leaving a wake that will dissolve, leaving a hum that will continue, leaving a silence that is no longer empty, but full of the weight of what has been recognized, what has been released, what has been carried across.

He turns from the railing. He walks back inside. The lounge is empty now. The papers are gone. The coffee cups are stacked. The lights are dimmed. He finds a seat near the window. He places the stamped copy on his lap. He watches the water move past. The rain slows. The sky lightens, not to dawn, but to the pale grey of early morning, the kind that does not promise warmth, only clarity. He closes his eyes. He breathes. The engine hums. The strait waits. The ferry moves. He does not speak. He does not need to. The silence between them is no longer a wall. It is a bridge. It is water. It is crossing. It is enough.
