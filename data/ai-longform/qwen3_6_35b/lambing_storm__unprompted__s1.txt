The wind came down the hollow like a dragged chain, rattling the corrugated iron roof until the whole barn shuddered in its ribs. Inside the lambing shed, the air was thick with the sour sweet of birth, lanolin, and damp straw. Clara stood over the tenth ewe of the night, her gloved hands buried to the wrist in wet fleece, feeling for the angle of a head that refused to follow the body’s lead. Beside her, Eli held a kerosene lamp steady while the bulb above them flickered once, twice, then died. The shed exhaled into sudden, absolute dark.

“Generator’s out,” Clara said, her voice low against the wind. “Fuses blew when the line went. Check the breaker box by the feed room. And bring the spare lamp.”

Eli didn’t answer right away. He was ten-six in December and had been stretching through the spring like a sapling pulled toward a gap in the trees. He moved now with the careful economy of someone learning to occupy his own height in unfamiliar space, padding past the stacked crates of iodine, the coiled hoses, the shelves of gloves that smelled of talc and old sweat. He returned with the lamp, its glass warm against his palm, and handed it to Clara without being asked. She took it, nodded once, and let it hang from the hook on the door frame. The yellow light pooled over the straw, catching the dust motes that swirled in the draft from the cracked ventilation pane.

The ewe grunted. Clara shifted her weight, planted her boots in the damp sawdust, and leaned in again. “Come on, girl,” she murmured. “You know the way. Just turn your head.”

Eli watched her shoulders. They’d always been broad, but now the bones showed through the wool of her sweater, sharp as knife handles. He remembered the last time Thomas had stood in this same spot, his hands larger, the sleeves of his flannel rolled past the elbows, his voice a low rumble as he guided a first-time mother through the final stretch. *Don’t fight her. Let her push. You’re just the midwife, not the master.* That was what he’d said, the spring before, when the frost came early and the rams had been restless. Clara hadn’t corrected him. She’d just nodded and taken the towel.

Now the shed belonged to the wind and the weight of what she carried.

The lamb slid into the straw with a soft, wet gasp. Clara worked quickly, tearing the sac, clearing the nostrils with her thumb, rubbing the forelock with the rough edge of the blanket until the creature wheezed, then coughed, then cried. The sound was thin at first, then stronger, a high bell ringing in the cold. Eli moved to the side pen where the mother stood shivering, her eyes black and glossy. He hooked a clean chain around her flank and guided her toward the newborn. The ewe lowered her head, sniffed, then nudged. The lamb stumbled forward, legs folding like wet paper, and latched on.

“Good girl,” Clara said, though she wasn’t sure who she was speaking to. She peeled off her gloves, her fingers raw and wrinkled from the cold and the antiseptic. She wiped her face with the back of her sleeve, leaving a smear of grease and salt across her cheekbone. “That’s the third twin pair. Put him in the heated box. The other’s still coming.”

Eli nodded, lifting the lamb with both hands. It was heavier than it looked, all dense muscle and damp wool, still slick with the fluid of a world it had just entered. He carried it to the wooden box lined with straw and wired with a heating cord that glowed faintly orange. He placed the lamb inside, tucked a blanket around its sides, and closed the door. When he turned back, Clara was already washing her hands in the metal sink, the water running slow and gray from the well pump. The generator hadn’t kicked in. The house down the hill was dark. The phone lines were dead.

“You should eat,” she said, not looking at him.

“I ate yesterday.”

“You ate a piece of cheese and a handful of apples. Sit down. I’ll make stew.”

“There’s no gas. The lines are frozen.”

Clara turned off the tap. She dried her hands on a rag that had seen better decades, then picked up her penlight. “Then we’ll eat what’s left in the pantry. And you’ll sleep in the truck cab tonight. The floorboards in the bedroom still draft like sieves, and you’re too old to complain about it.”

Eli didn’t complain. He never did. He just stood there, watching the water drip from the sink into the bucket, listening to the wind climb the slope behind them. He thought of his father’s truck, parked under the lean-to, its hood covered in a tarp that had flapped loose in the storm. He thought of the empty stall at the end of the row, where the harness still hung, where the halter lay coiled on the ground like a sleeping snake. He thought of the way Clara had stopped leaving his side of the table set at night, the way she’d started leaving Thomas’s coat on the back of his chair until it caught the damp and smelled of mildew.

“Another one’s ready,” Clara said, already moving toward the next pen. “And the black-faced mare’s in early contraction. I’ll need you to prep the table.”

He followed her. The work was a liturgy he was still learning. He laid out the towels, opened the iodine, arranged the gloves in a neat row, checked the heat lamp’s cord for frays. He knew the rhythms better than he’d admit, better than he’d tell her. He’d watched Thomas do it a thousand times, his hands sure, his voice calm, his body moving with the quiet confidence of a man who knew exactly how much force a body could take before it broke. Eli tried to mimic that steadiness now, but his hands sometimes trembled, just at the edges, like leaves before a storm.

The next ewe delivered a pair without complication. Clara worked faster now, her movements efficient, almost mechanical. Eli watched the way she breathed—short, shallow breaths held during the critical moments, released only when the lamb took its first independent cry. He thought of the first lamb of his own childhood, born in this same shed when he was ten. His father had carried him out in his arms, wrapped in a towel, and set him on his shoulders. “He’s got your nose,” Thomas had said, laughing. “And your stubbornness.” Clara had smiled then, really smiled, the kind that reached her eyes and softened the lines around her mouth. It had been six months before the accident. Six months of light.

Now the light was just the lamp, and it flickered as the wick burned low. Clara trimmed it, her movements precise, and hung it back. “You’re doing fine,” she said, suddenly. It wasn’t praise. It was acknowledgment. “You’re learning the angle. That’s the hard part. The rest is just patience.”

“It feels like I’m waiting for something to give way,” Eli said. He didn’t look at her. He was watching the ewe’s flanks contract, watching the straw shift under her weight.

“It will,” Clara said. “Everything gives way eventually. Then you build something else in the space.”

He nodded. He didn’t argue. He knew what she meant, even if he couldn’t quite name it. The space his father had left wasn’t empty. It was full of the things they’d stopped doing—Saturday mornings at the diner, the way Thomas would tune the radio to the farm report, the sound of his boots on the porch steps. Those things were gone. But the work remained. The work was a kind of keeping.

The storm deepened. The wind found new ways into the cracks, whistling through the eaves, rattling the windows. The temperature dropped. Clara added another layer to her sweater, then pulled on a second pair of socks. Eli lit the kerosene heater in the corner, its blue flame steady against the draft. They worked in shifts, sometimes together, sometimes alone, the pen light their only constant companion. The ewes came without pause, as if the storm had awakened some ancient clock in their bodies, ticking toward a deadline only they knew. Twins, triplets, a stillborn that Clara wrapped in tissue paper and placed in the cooler. A difficult birth where the lamb’s shoulder jammed and Eli had to hold the mother’s leg while Clara reached in, her arm to the shoulder, her breathing ragged, her voice low and steady. “Pull on three,” she said. “On three. One. Two. Three.” The lamb came free, gasping, and for a moment Clara leaned against the pen wall, her forehead pressed to the cold wood, her shoulders shaking. Eli didn’t speak. He just handed her a towel.

“I miss him,” she said, finally. Her voice was rough. “Not in the way I thought I would. I miss the way he’d hum when he was working. Off-key. Always off-key. I miss the way he’d check the locks on the back door twice, then a third time just to be sure. I miss the weight of his hand on my back when I’d walk past him in the kitchen. The absence is… heavier than I expected. It’s in the floorboards. In the tools. In the way I keep waiting for the engine to start.”

Eli listened. He’d heard her say fragments of this before, in the quiet hours, in the space between breaths. But tonight, in the lamplight, with the storm holding the world outside at bay, the words landed differently. They weren’t complaints. They were inventory. A reckoning.

“He’d want us to keep going,” Eli said.

“He wouldn’t,” Clara replied. “He’d want us to be careful. To not push too hard. To remember that the land doesn’t care about our grief. It only cares if we show up.”

“Do you think he’d be proud?”

Clara turned to him then, really looked at him. Her eyes were tired, but clear. “Proud isn’t the word. I think he’d be relieved. That you’re standing there. That you’re learning the angles. That you’re not running from the work because it’s hard. That’s what would make him sigh with relief. Not pride. Relief. Because he knew what it costs to stay.”

She wiped her face again, harder this time, as if scrubbing away the memory of the stillborn, the weight of the night, the sheer fatigue that settled in her bones like silt. “Get the last one out of the truck. The white-faced one. She’s been pacing since dusk. I think she’s ready.”

He went. The truck cab was dark, cold, the seat covered in a blanket that smelled of wool and diesel. He lifted the ewe, her weight surprising, her breath hot against his neck. He carried her to the clean pen, laid her down, and stepped back. She looked at him, then at Clara, then closed her eyes. The labor came quickly, almost violently, as if she’d been holding it in. Clara worked with renewed urgency, her hands moving with the certainty of years, her voice a low counterpoint to the storm. The lamb came out headfirst, slick and bright-eyed, and immediately tried to stand. Clara didn’t let her. She held her steady, let her rest, let her breathe. Then she nodded. The ewe pushed. The lamb slid free, and this time, when it cried, it was loud, clear, a sound that cut through the wind like a blade.

They cleaned the pen. They placed the lamb in the heated box. They turned on the heat lamp, its orange glow warming the straw. They ate cold beans from a can, sitting on overturned buckets, the kerosene lamp casting long shadows on the walls. The storm showed no sign of breaking. If anything, it was settling in, claiming the shed, the fields, the road, the world beyond.

“I used to think the storm was an enemy,” Eli said, chewing slowly. “Now I think it’s just… busy.”

Clara smiled. It was faint, but real. “It’s not busy. It’s indifferent. That’s what people get wrong. It doesn’t hate us. It doesn’t love us. It just moves. And we have to move with it, or get buried.”

“Dad said that once. On the ridge. When the snow came early.”

“He did.”

“I don’t remember him saying it. I remember the way he looked at the sky. Like he was reading a letter.”

Clara’s throat worked. She nodded. “He was. He always did that. Read the weather like it was speaking.”

They ate in silence after that. The lamb in the box stirred, let out a soft bleat, then settled. The ewe lay down beside it, her flank rising and falling, her eyes half-closed. The lamp burned lower. Clara trimmed it again, her movements slower now, more deliberate. She thought of Thomas’s hands. Of the way they’d fit over hers on the steering wheel, on the fence post, on the back of her neck. She thought of the phone call she’d never got. Of the voicemail from the state trooper that played in her head on loop: *We found your husband’s truck off Route 9. The bridge was iced over. We’re so sorry.* She’d stopped crying by then. Grief, she was learning, wasn’t a flood. It was a slow seepage. It got into the foundations. It made the floors uneven. You learned to walk around the dips.

“Another one’s coming,” Eli said, standing. “I can hear her in the far pen. She’s been vocal for an hour.”

“Go check. And bring the towels. The thick ones.”

He went. She washed her face, poured a cup of cold tea, drank it in three slow sips. She looked at the empty stall. At the harness. At the halter. She thought of the first spring after he died. How she’d wanted to sell the flock, move to town, let the fields go to seed. How Eli had come to her, at fourteen, with his father’s jaw set the same way, and said, *I’ll learn the book. You just keep the sheep alive.* She’d kissed his forehead. She’d stayed.

Eli returned with the towels, his breath pluming in the cold air. “She’s in transition. I’d say another hour. Maybe two. The water’s breaking.”

“Good. Go sleep. I’ll manage the last few. You’re no use to me if you’re collapsing.”

“I’m fine.”

“You’re sixteen. You’re not fine. You’re tired. Go. I’ll yell if I need you.”

He hesitated. Then he nodded. He climbed the stairs to the loft, where a cot waited beneath a window that looked out over the frozen pasture. He lay down, pulled the quilt over his shoulders, listened to the storm, listened to the distant cry of a lamb, listened to his mother’s voice, low and steady, speaking to another ewe, another birth, another night that refused to end. He didn’t sleep. He drifted in and out, caught in the current of labor and wind, until the light began to change.

It wasn’t dawn yet. The sky was still the color of wet slate, but the wind had dropped. The chains were gone from the roof. The barn was quiet except for the crackle of the kerosene lamp, the soft breathing of the ewes, the occasional bleat from the box. Clara stood by the door, her hand on the frame, looking out. The snow was still falling, but it was lighter now, falling in sheets instead of slashes. The ground was white, unbroken, perfect. She thought of Thomas walking through it, his boots leaving the same tracks he’d always made, his breath puffing in the cold, his scarf pulled up around his nose. She thought of the spring ahead. Of the lambs nursing. Of the ewes returning to the fields. Of the work that would continue, indifferent and necessary.

“Mom,” Eli said from the doorway. His voice was rough with sleep. “Power’s back. The generator kicked in. The lights are on.”

She turned. The overhead bulb glowed steadily, harsh and white, stripping the shed of its shadows. It felt wrong. Too bright. Too clean. She walked to the switch, flipped it off. The shed returned to the lamp’s gold. “Keep it off,” she said. “We don’t need the glare. Not yet.”

He nodded. He came to stand beside her, his tall frame hunched slightly, as if still adjusting to the light. He looked out over the pasture. “Do you think it’ll clear by morning?”

“Maybe. Maybe not. The clouds are thinning. That’s something.”

“I checked the radio. The county says the roads will be open by noon. The power lines are down two miles out. They’re working on them.”

She nodded. She reached out, touched his shoulder. His wool sweater was warm under her palm. “You did good tonight,” she said. “All of it.”

“I didn’t do much.”

“You stayed. You learned the angles. You didn’t run. That’s the work. Everything else is just detail.”

He looked at her then, really looked. His eyes were his father’s. The set of his jaw, the quiet intensity, the way he held his face when he was trying not to show too much. She felt something shift inside her, not a cracking, but a settling. Like a foundation finding its level.

“He’d be proud of you,” she said again, softer this time.

“I told you, he wouldn’t,” she corrected herself, a faint smile touching her lips. “He’d be relieved. But yeah. He’d be proud too. In his own quiet way.”

She turned back to the shed. The ewes were stirring. The lambs were restless. The work was not done. It would never be done. But tonight, in the lamplight, with the storm breaking and the boy beside her, she felt the weight of the absence lighten, just slightly, just enough to breathe.

“Come on,” she said. “Another one’s coming. And I think you’re ready to try the angle yourself.”

He straightened. He rolled his shoulders. He nodded. They walked back into the shed, past the pens, past the tools, past the empty stall, into the yellow light, into the work, into the quiet certainty that the lambs would come, the storm would pass, and they would keep going, not because they could bear it, but because they had to. And sometimes, in the space between the cries and the wind, in the space between the loss and the labor, there was a kind of grace. Not forgiveness. Not healing. Just presence. Just the next breath. Just the next lamb. Just the next morning.
