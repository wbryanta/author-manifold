It was the hour after noon when the heat begins to draw the breath out of the land and the dust rises in slow columns from the fallow rows. The irrigation line ran like a black snake through the cracked earth from the ditch down to the valve house at the orchard’s edge. Elias stood over the joint where the pipe had split again his wrench a heavy iron thing in his hand. His brother Thomas watched from the shade of the old oak that still held a few green leaves. Their trunks were pale as bone in the glare. The branches were bare in places and heavy with dead fruit in others. The ground beneath them was hard as kiln-fired clay. Cracks webbed outward from every root collar. Somewhere far off a hawk cried once and was answered by silence.

Elias put the wrench to the bolt and leaned his weight upon it. The metal groaned and then held. He wiped his forehead with the back of his sleeve and tasted salt and iron. He did not look up. His hands were black with grease and old dirt. The knuckles were split and crusted. He worked in the manner of one who knows that the world offers no quarter and takes none in return. The pipe was steel and cold despite the heat. It had been laid by his father’s hands and his father’s father’s hands and the hands of men who are now dust and stone and forgotten names. The earth remembers what the hands leave behind. The earth forgets what the hands leave in it.

You are fighting a ghost, Thomas said. He did not move from the shade. His shirt was white and stiff with dried sweat. His hat lay on the ground beside him. His face was lean and dark and lined like a plowed field. He watched his brother with the quiet attention of one who has spent a lifetime measuring distance and time.

The ghost has a thread count and a pressure rating, Elias said. And it will hold if you torque it right. He turned the wrench. The bolt resisted and then gave. He pulled back and examined the seam. It was still leaking. A thin ribbon of water escaped and vanished into the dust. He set the wrench down and stood upright. His back ached in the familiar way. The ache was old and familiar and belonged to him.

You are wasting water on a dead thing, Thomas said. He kicked at a stone. It skittered across the dry earth and disappeared into a furrow. You can keep the valve open till the river runs dry and what will it matter. The soil will not hold it. The roots will not take it. It will sink and vanish and leave nothing but salt behind.

Thomas walked forward into the glare. He stopped a few paces away and looked at the line of pipes that ran like a spine through the dead orchard. They were old and pitted and patched with rubber and hose clamps and hope. They had run once. They had carried water that made fruit heavy and sweet. They had carried the hands of men who believed in seasons and promises. Now they carried only memory and rust and the slow arithmetic of drought.

I spoke to the broker again, Thomas said. His voice was flat and even. He sent the papers. They are at the house on the desk. The offer stands till Friday.

Elias tightened the bolt. The wrench slipped and cut his knuckle. Blood welled and ran down his wrist and dripped onto the pipe. He did not wipe it away. Let it stand, he said. He pulled the wrench free and set it on the ground. He looked at Thomas. His eyes were gray and hard and tired. He looked at the orchard. The trees stood like sentinels in a forgotten war. The ground was dry and cracked and hard. The sky was white and empty.

The water is gone, Thomas said at last. Not all of it. But what is left will not come back. The wells are dry. The aquifer is a myth. The river is a memory. The land is a debt that outlives the men who signed it. You know this. You have always known it. You are just too proud to look at the ledger.
