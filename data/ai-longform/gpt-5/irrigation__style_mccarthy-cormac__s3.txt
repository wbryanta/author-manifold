The day came up without haste and the light took the planes of the land like a slow hand passing and smoothing and all things risen in it seemed to pause and hold still as if listening for the word that would send them on. Heat already lived at the edges where the shadows lay and the sky was a chalky blue and bereft of birds and the orchard stood rank and furred with a gray skiff of dust upon the leaves and a gray dust upon the gray trunks as if they had been drawn with the same blunt piece of lead. Far off a truck went on the section line and the sound of it came late and small and the dust of it rose and did not fall again. The earth was cracked and in the cracks you could see yet a darker seam like a thing knitted wrong. The trees in their rows went on into the brightness and stopped at the fence and the fields beyond threw back the light in a dull glare of stubble and rock and the ragged stand of tamarisk out by the ditch took the wind and shook its long feathery arms as if in some slow dance that bore no joy in it.

The brothers came from the house in the first of the sun with their hats and their gloves and the old galvanized toolbox whose hinges squealed and the box gave off a dry iron smell and a faint sweet of glue and primer and the nameless funk of dirt that had been wet and dried again a thousand times and kept some memory of water in it. Jesse carried the tools and Ben the coils of blue rope and the black roll of tape and the sticks of couplers and the purple can with its stiff wand in the lid and a bundle of new elbows and tees wrapped in coarse paper. Their boots took the dust and left oblong marks and the marks filled with the siftings of the day so it seemed there was some thing behind them closing up what they had made.

You could hear the insects waking in the weeds, that small electric business. Awain near the fence a pair of magpies went stepping like dark-postured old men and they tugged at something in the grass and gave it up and one of them looked about like a clerk in an empty office. The orchard was mostly apricots and some peaches and there had been apples once but the apples had gone off in the blights that come in a light year and return the next and the trees themselves gone dready and hollow and you could break the branches with your hands. A wind had come in winter and felled a dozen and they lay in the rows with the thin limbs split and the heartwood white as bone. There had not been money to pull the stumps and the ditch company had cut the allotment again and the water rights turned out to be papers only when there was no water and the outlying men had sworn that that was lawful and it was.

The pump-house lay a quarter mile down by the river ditch and the line ran back from it in a low arc half sought and half found by the grader years ago and it lay shallow as a snake's coil in the gravel and broke and was mended and broke again at the same places in the heat of summer and again in the first freezes and the couplers grew like the bony nodes of a man too long broken and mended and there was an odor always of glue and hot plastic and cooked mud and mouse droppings. Their father had set the line with a transit once upon a time and pitched it like setting a thin spell upon the earth and there were marks painted on the trunks yet where he had sighted and called to one or to the other holding the rod and telling them to raise it this much or let it fall this much and the mouth by which he said it now lay dry and caved in a little as if it could never utter again even the meanest syllable of a man's complaint.

They loaded the bed of the truck and Jesse shut the tailgate with a flat smack of metal. He squinted toward the line of tamarisk where the ditch ran and he could see the shimmer of it beyond the trees. He said, We got the window at ten to take water. You talk to Tomás.

Ben swatted at a mosquito on his neck and tossed the blue rope into the cab and climbed in. He said, I talked to him. We're after the Holts. They said they'd be done by ten. If they are sober enough to shut their gate.

They ain't sober but they'll be done. Benny. You bring the strap wrench.

In the box.

Jesse lit a cigarette cupped in the fist of his hand and got in the driver's side and started the truck. The starter ground as if chewing a stone and the engine took and idled and then shook out and there was a sound like a metal thing taken up by a dog and worrying it. Jesse lifted the hood latch with his left hand and tapped the carburetor with the knuckles of that same hand and then shut the hood again like closing a book on something that would not be read. He drove down the lane between the trees and the leaves brushed the sides of the truck and the truck wore the dust like a coat long kept on its peg. The air came through the windows and threw the hair from the brothers' temples and the smell of the trees was a smell of old green dying and a faint gum where a cut had bled and set.

They got down at the low place where the line had gone soft and Jesse set the box in the shade of a dead tree and took a spade and began to cut the dirt in long flat chips and Ben did the same and they did not speak for a time because in the morning a man will not break the quiet of it if he can help it. The hole came by inches and the sound of the spade when it found pipe went hollow and then you could see the white of the PVC and the purple ring scabbed about one of the couplers and an old repair where the primer had run like tears. Jesse wiped his forearm with the back of his wrist and set the spade aside and hunkered and scraped with his hand until the pipe came rid of the dirt and lay in the trough they had made like the rib of some pale and monstrous thing old in the ground. He tapped it with his fingernail and the sound was thin as if the pipe bore not the least wish to bear water again.

He said, Here.

Ben got the tape and the rag and he wiped out the dirt from the break and you could see how the pipe had blowed out in an egg and split on the backside where you could not see it if you had not put hand upon it and felt that place go in. He could feel the heat of it in his palm.

We ought to run it deeper, he said. Or iron. It's not going to quit unless we put iron.

Jesse spat in the angle of his wrist and rubbed the pipe and looked down the line toward the pump house as if by some means he could see underground there and take its pulse. He said, Iron'd be three thousand if it's a dime. We ain't got three hundred.

Could sell.

Jesse rose and went to the truck and took up the hacksaw and the blade whined thinly and the whining went around the break and then the pipe came in two and the smell was of hot glue and hot blade and the smell of something wounded. He handed the saw back and said, Rope me that fitting.

Ben stood and unwound the rope and took the sack of couplers and set two on the pipe and they cleaned them with the purple stuff and the purple bled into his skin and would not wash out that day or the day after and the cap clinked against the can when he set it down. His hands worked without talk as his mind put up an answer like a man who raises a lantern in a dark used to see what will come to him without benefit of calling. He said, Other folks are getting out. You seen the sign up by Bender's place. You know who's putting them up.

Jesse fitted the coupler to the pipe and he turned it slow and his hands were big and scarred and had the color of walnut shells and the knuckles were sprung and he had cut the right forefinger at the last joint and that joint would not bend right no more and when he set the coupler on it made a small sound like a cork in a bottle and he held it and the glue ran out fine and bright as blood and made a shining bead and his face bent over that work and never lifted. He said, I seen.

They're paying. They're buying up paper. They're taking water. They'll take the trees down themselves. You ain't got to be the one. You could sell and they would do it.

And take the bulldozer to our names. No. They can eat the ditch with their money but they can do it after I'm laid down.

Ben looked past him at the trees. He said, They'll do it anyway. You think they'd stop for us.

I think I can set a coupler and glue it and go on.

Ben crouched and passed Jesse the next fitting and he said, You can't set a coupler on a bad river. You know it.

Jesse looked up from the trench and his eyes were the hard gray of a river stone. He said, Boy. I been eating at that same table as you and I ain't starved none yet. We got fruit to set. We got one more year in us and maybe two. You think I got hankerin to die under the roof with holes like a saint with a bowl. But I ain't selling to them. They ain't got no respect of land. They come from where they stand in fluorescent light and listen to a fan hum and they don't know the names of the birds and don't care.

Ben set in the dampness at the lip of the trench and rested his forearms on his knees and he looked off to where the river went in its ditch and the tamarisks stood and beyond that the line of low hills lay like the palate of some great skull lifted just out of the ground. He said, What they got they don't put in dirt. They put it in pictures of the money itself. They got stores of pictures of money in houses taller than the courthouse. They keep count of the pictures too and they don't sweat at it. You want to be the only fool in the country with mud on his knees because you love a dead man's stubborn.

Jesse stood and his hat brim threw his face in a long cut of shade. He said, You ain't got to stay if it stings you so. I told you that. I told you to take yourself to Phoenix with them boys and fetch in people's air and fix little leaks in them freon lines and watch your belly get soft. Nobody held your rope.

Ben watched the flies hover and settle in the sweat at the base of his thumb and he brushed them away and he stood and went to the truck and got the box of sandpaper flitches that had been his way of smoothing the pipe ends since he was a boy. He thought that his brother's talk of Phoenix was like a small stone used to check the wheels of a thing that had long ago come to rest. He said, Mama can't stay here forever. You know that.

She ain't here now, Jesse said.

She is and she ain't. She can't walk up to the mailbox without I help her. She can't find the sugar in her own kitchen and it never moved from under the second counter. She calls me by my father's name and looks at me from a long way like I was a thing she'd been told of.

Jesse did not speak. A lizard ran across the shallow cut they had made and paused and lifted itself up on stilts and cocked his head and went on. The light came harder in the rows and you could see the air like a curtain wavered by a hand and beyond it the mountains lay cut out of paper and flat. The men finished the coupler and the next and then they fell to shoveling dirt in again and tamping with the heel of their boots and they set a stone from the pile Jesse had made there years ago when he had set the line first time and he took the hammer and drove the stone down so the pipe could not lift when it took water. Ben watched this and the care his brother put to it as if the unseen under the dirt had some small holiness for him and he felt a jump of anger and something mean wanted to say that there were saints' bones in the ground too and you could not eat those either.

They drove next to the bend by the tall cottonwood where the last winter had split the bank and sent the ditch to wandering through the orchard like a thing come loose and the ditch company had come with a backhoe and set it back and left a sweet lip of mud that dried to pans and cracked in plates you could pick up and fling and the glossy red of crawdad holes in it like small mouths. There a section of pipe had come to light and it lay like a limb exhumed and the sun had cooked it egg-blue and it had burst so that you could see through. Jesse set his hat down and drew off his shirt and his chest was the color of tallow and the old scars gleamed on it and he had the painter's mark of a sun on his throat where the shirt collar had ended and he set to cutting the ruin out. Ben took his own shirt off and you could see in him a youth not yet scotched out and a certain lightness of joint Jesse never had even young and he bent and his spine made a line like a beam sprung just so. Their breath came loud in the heat and homely and the glue smell lay like a sweet over the sour of old mud and old roots.

You remember our daddy set this run after the flood? Jesse said.

Ben did not answer. He did remember. He remembered the flood with a clarity like the world would come with that same silver sheen should the clouds all pour themselves at once upon the earth again. He had stood upon the porch in the hip of his daddy's leg and seen the water come slow and then with a thought and then with nothing of thought in it but its desire only and he had seen the trees like men bearing a weight beyond them and the fence posts go like candles in the current and he had seen the pig shed just hitch and go with a sound he could hear yet and his father had stepped down into the flood and he had gone to the pump and he had tilted his head to look at where the river had no right to be and he had turned and said, Get that girl's baby in out of this weather and his face had been both anger and fear and the baby was their cousin and his sister had stood with her hair wet and the baby at her breast and the sky was the color of a bruise.

Jesse said, He set it with no help from these men now. He set it when there was leather to a man's hands. He done it with a length of string and a beer level and a shovel and by looking and it run five summers without me touching it. Don't tell me about iron. He wasn't no iron.

Ben set the primer and then the glue and he twisted the new pipe in with a sound like something swallowing and he said, And he set in the chair drunk and cried some nights at the end. He said he could hear water yet in his head and it wouldn't let him sleep and he said he seen men on the road that wasn't there. He said if he closed his eyes he was setting the same piece and he had to get up and look at it. He died bent into his own hands. He ain't your saint.

Jesse's jaw jumped and he held the pipe and did not move. He said, He ain't my saint.

Ben looked at him and he saw in the pinched white about his brother's mouth the flint that had not shivered in him since he was a boy. He said in a different voice, He ain't mine neither, Jess. He was my daddy. He was sick. He was small inside hisself. What was wide about him was the land and he died in a room not wide at all.

Jesse nodded like a man who has accepted a small portion of some truth not of his own making. He said, He didn't quit on it.

He ain't had to watch the trees go. That might have taught him. You can't beat a bad season with a shovel.

They finished the bend and Ben got in the truck and took a drink from the plastic jug and the water in it was warm and tasted of dust and of the plastic itself and he wrenched his head back and the water ran into his beard and down his chest and his brother laughed a small laugh that was not free of derision but was not much of derision either. Ben pitched the jug to him and Jesse drank delicate out of it like a calf would out of the bucket and the sky held not a cloud and the grass at the margins went to a gray like ashes that have been wetted and re-dried and he said, I seen a coyote last night.

Ben wiped his mouth on the heel of his hand and said nothing and Jesse went on. It was down by the pumps. He was standing in the cotton and his head was on a level with the wire. He wasn't no rash boy. He looked at me and he knowed me and he put his head down like a dog being whipped and he slowly went on out of my light.

You put a gun on him.

Jesse shook his head. He got to bear what we do and we got to bear what he do. There ain't a creature under the heavens think he got to do what he's told except us. We take that on.

Ben thought of the buyer he had talked to on the telephone. A woman's voice and clipped and distant as if she were speaking from inside the steel belly of some instrument that measured out things and was incapable of hearing back. She had said the price and in his chest there had been a small quiet he could not remember having known and afterward he had felt the guilt slam upon it as a man might put his palm upon a frog in a boy's jar. He had not told Jesse. He did now.

He said it while he was reaching for the tee and for the string of notes he had made on a card. He said, I called them. People you talk of. I called them and I told them about the place. They offered.

Jesse's eyes flicked not to him but down past his hands in the hole. Said, What.

They said they'd take the line of trees on this side. They'd pay the note and then some. They'd take it this month. He swallowed. It ain't a little money, Jess. It's a money where you don't walk to town. You ride. You buy what you want at the feedstore and you don't stand there rocking on your heels and count which way you like it to go. You put braces in Mama's teeth. You take her to a doctor in Santa Fe ain't got cowshit on his boots.

Jesse reached under the truckseat and took out the shade of a cap and set it back to front and there was a white V at his brow where the hat had been and his hair was close and peppered and lay like a short map of the skull. He looked at his brother, not smiling. He said, You done that.

I done that.

And when will they come.

They'll come when we say if we say. It ain't a thing went past saying yet.

He stood in the road with his hands out from his sides an inch and he looked off and his face flickered like a thin paper held to a heat and whatever he saw he saw no solution in it but only a measure of time and the things men will do in it. He leaned down into the hole and he hunted a rag and wiped his hands and he said, They got no interest in this.

They got interest in the river and the aquifer and what's in the page where your name is got wrote. They will buy. They will sell the water off and the ground with it will be a brown plate like a horse's hoof. You can't keep them from it.

Jesse hung the rag over a limb and the fly came to it and he waved it off. He said, They send you a paper to sign with a line on it they want a man's name upon and you send it back and they cut your old man's ghost off at the knees and put him in a can. He ain't even got no dirt to cover his face with. He raised us here to be poor and we was poor. You want to be rich, go be rich. You want to sell this land, you'll fight me.

Ben said, I don't want to fight you.

Jesse said, You will.

Ben looked at him a long time and then he turned and worked without speaking. There is an anger comes of feeling that what you would do to preserve is taken for betrayal. He placed the tee and the boot and his hands shook a little and the primer sloshed dark on his wrist and burned there like an acid and he did not pick a rag to wipe it. Jesse moved into the shade and drank again and spat and the spit fell and raised no dust in the damp they had made and he set the cap back on his head rightwise and watched his brother and could have said he was sorry and did not. They worked until the sun like a hard coin stood over them and then past.

At noon they went to sit under the one green cottonwood with the life in it and they ate the bread and the rind cheese and slices of an onion and a ham cut thin and laid on the bread and Jesse had a small tin of jalapeños his woman had put up and he fished the peppers out with two fingers and ate them and woke to himself that at that instant there was something like the pleasure he had as a boy in the creek besides the lower field, when the fish were small and quick and the light in the water went in ropes. They drank the rest of the jug and Jesse lit a cigarette and the smoke went up blue in that white air. He said, You remember when we set the ladder in the big tree out by the lane and the bees had took it.

Ben had set with his palms on the dirt with the fingers curled and his hat tipped over his eyes and the sweat had made a light shape beneath each arm the shape of wings and he said, I remember. You went up like ain't no sting worried you and they come out to you like you was a thing in their way and you fell back and your shirt was alive with them and you was wild to get it off and you threw it and run into the ditch and it held not a drop. You laid in the mud with bees on you and you said don't tell Daddy and I ain't.

Jesse laughed. He turned on his side and propped his head and he looked at the trees. He said, That ladder still lays behind that barn. Everything we've had stays. It don't want to go no place.

Ben did not say that things go of their own and don't ask if you want it. He watched a hawk far off making tight turns over what must be a vole or a bird the smaller birds were covering with crescent wingblows as if they could shame the hawk out of its nature and he thought the hawk took no shame. He lay back with the brim over his eyes and he thought of the woman he had known years ago in town, a girl with hair like wheat—yellow not gold because there is no gold in wheat though men talk as if there were—and he knew where she was now and that was not here and he was here. He slept and Jesse slept and their sleep lay as flat and unguarded as a boy's and a wind came and moved nothing and a fly went at their mouths and at their eyes and they did not lift a hand.

When they woke the sun had the long white edge upon it again and Ben's face had been slept upon by the ants and there were three bites in a close run. He rubbed them and sat and Jesse was already up and had his gloves in his hand and his mouth was set. They went to the pump.

The pump-house door hung by one hinge and on that hinge it squealed when moved and inside it smelled of mouse and of scorched dust and of the pump oil that he had slopped once young and it had soaked so into the wood that it rose by heat again each summer. The pump itself sat on a poured pad and the iron about the crank was red and flaked and the old belt lay like a black snake with no head. A red squirrel ran out between Jesse's boots and went into the cotton with his tail flat upon his back and he gave no call at all and Ben smiled. They set the tools upon the pump and Jesse took the wrench and he put his tongue in the corner of his mouth and his forearms and his neck corded up and he leaned into the nut and he spoke to it like a thing deaf and recalcitrant and the nut walked. They unwound the cover and lifted it and the gasket stuck and then gave with a fart of sound and inside lay the switch and the small world of the starting coil and when you touched it your knuckles came away with a gray smear as if of some old lampblack and Ben fetched the sand and the brush and they prayed the thing would know what they meant to have of it and would not break in that knowing.

We ain't primed it since last, Jesse said.

We ain't had enough water to prime it.

Jesse went out and dipped the pail in the ditch and the water came brown and it came full of grasses and of the drab bodies of things with legs and he poured it in the throat of the pump and the pump took it down and he poured again and they stood like men feeding a fire that had gone sulky. He set the lid and bolted it and cranked the valve and felt the valve back against his palm as if alive. He looked to Ben. You ready. He nodded toward the breaker.

Ben took a breath he did not need to take. He reached and flipped, and the pump took the current and shook and gave a cry like an animal and then fell into a steady clatter and rattle and began to draw. The throat of it moaned and the pipe ran to it trembled and the ditch-water found its way in and the line down the orchard went to singing in a light thin way you felt in your feet through the ground before you heard it. Jesse grinned despite all. Hey now, he said, and his voice had the boy again in it and he put his hand on the pump-house wall and a green snake that had been clung there slid away and into a crack and was gone like smoke.

They went up the line walking and the shade had grown the smallest ounce and the light of afternoon had gone white and made a hum all its own and the line seeped at a seam and then stopped as the glue took and then the line purred and the first of the water came at the far end slow and put out its tongue and set down into the ruts like a tired thing. Jesse took the shovel and cut little checks and raised small dams and sent it into the basins. It went into the bowls about the apricot trunks and sat there black and glossy and the earth drank and the bowls went down into their own emptiness like a trick and he sent them more. The leaves shook as if telling one of the others that this thing had not come in so long a time that they had forgot how it felt to hold.

They walked that row and then the next and puddled and cut and sent and Ben found with his boot a place where the line had blown again and he said, Here. They had not the coupler left to set there and Jesse said, Tear me one of those elbows and we'll run it into a crooked that will serve. They had done that many times and the line had known that indignity and still took water after. He worked and Ben stood and looked at the place where his father's hand had once set a post-line. The post leaned now and on the barrel of the post was written a word you could not read anymore for the sun and the rain had washed it and it was a thing spoken that had taken the air and gone. He thought of their mother asleep in the front room in the day as if she were another day early in her own life and she did not wake with the sound of them leaving and he wondered if she would ever wake in her life where he would not be there to tell her what there was to eat and where the knife lay and what the sun looked like. He thought of the woman on the telephone again and the voice devoid of shape and he was overcome for a moment by a meanness of heart he had no use for.

The elbow went on and Jesse wiped his hands on his pants and looked down the row. He said, If we get this run we'll get two more and then when the Holts shut their gate we got five hours. He made his tally as a man will tally the daylight itself with his thumb and forefinger to judge it as if it were some thing he could measure in that way and you could see in that the foolishness and the pride.

They went on. In the next row a porcupine had set upon a low limb some night and left a dark crust of scat there and the quills had fallen and lay in the dust pale and translucent as if made of sugar. In another the nest of a robin hung empty and it had been misbuilt and the bottom sagged and there was the half-shell of a pale blue egg on the ground and Jesse turned it delicate with his toe and set it back in the grass. He said, That ain't nobody's blame.

Not nobody's. Ben's voice was without tone.

By four the water came where it would with no more argument and Jesse had taken off his hat and put it back on and the sweat ran black with the glue and the dust. A belt of gray cloud banked up over the western ridges like a congregation of cattle but it had no rain in it as at this time of year it never did and it would go by and leave only a slight drop in the measure of heat and then more. They stood at the line and the line sang and the orchard took what it could take and Jesse put his hand to a trunk and felt the heat in it and thought of his father's hand on his own neck when he was no taller than that and of the dirt under the nails in that hand and a piece of white string and the way his father had laid the string across his son's hand like laying a thing between two boys disputing and saying in the one small word string and in the long in-drawn breath we will see.

Ben said, Jess. He said it quiet and Jesse turned.

He held out the small card he had kept in the pocket of his shirt. It was creased and the ink had bled a little from the sweat of his chest and on it the name of the company and the number and the amount they had said to him there in his ear as if the number meant anything at all like the taste of bread or the sound of a night train does. Jesse did not take it.

He said, Burn it.

It ain't a paper why they will come. They will come because they got motors on their cars better than ours and they got the law at their elbow. Burning it ain't stopping it.

Jesse turned away. He spoke not over his shoulder but to the procession of trees as if they could answer and had in them the old tongues of men. He said, What I got is I can set water to this ground and it will hold me up so long as I can take breath to throw a shovel of it. That is my law. He looked hard at his brother then. That is what makes the right of a man, not what they hold in a room where there is no earth on the floor and no window for a man to know the weather by. It's a lie they tell each other and they look so long at their own lying they swear to God, yes, that this is the truth and the truth is right here under my boots and going into these trees and I can smell it and I can taste it and when they come they can turn themselves around and go back.

Ben looked at him as at a man in a light he stood apart from and he did not know whether to pull him out of that light or go and stand in it with him and be burned the same. He said, If you put my name to that, this light you are in will go out for me.

Your name is a thing some clerk wrote to help himself remember the money. The thing you have to put your hands on is this. You want that to go then go with them and I'll stay and we'll see who walks the road at night and who don't.

Ben felt then that there are times words are like some food a man has mistaken for his hunger and he stopped himself from taking more. He let the card fall and the wind lifted it and it slid along the dirt and fetched in a goathead and hung there and in that small fact there was absurdity and in the absurdity the truth that all this talk couldn't shift the wind a degree. He went and got the card and put it back in his pocket and Jesse turned and started back down the row with his shovel over his shoulder. Ben followed.

At the far end by the fence a calf had thrust its head through the wires to nose at what small green there was and in hauling it back it had flayed the hide in long parallel furrows and the pink of it glistened and flies hung on in a blue cluster and their bodies wove and pulsed like a single thing. Jesse clicked with his tongue and the calf backed and bawled small and helpless and his mother across the way raised her head from chewing and blew and came and stood dumb with insensate love and rage as if she could in that way change what had already been done and the calf put its head to her belly not seeing that what it wanted had not yet returned. Jesse lifted the wire with his boot and Ben pulled the calf back and it stumbled and lurched and got to the udder and settled. The mother rolled her eyes stupid as a child's toy.

They walked the fence a way and saw no other thing. When they came back to the pump-house the pump had taken a hitch in its sound and Ben tilted his head. You hear it.

I hear.

It's pulling hard.

It'll pull and it'll hold.

We can pull any living thing apart if we ask it to do work beyond it.

Jesse went in and laid his palm upon the pump and the heat of it told him things he could not have been told by any syllable and he lifted the lid and looked at the glass and the water in the glass juddered and he thought if he could have got down under that machine and spoke to it the way a man would speak to a horse who had carried him home bleeding he would have. He shut the lid and backed and stretched his arm along the top and his hand hung from the far side and you could see on the fingers the purple stigmata of primer and the blue of rope and the dirt had put in the lines a calligraphy that no man had wrote. He said, Hold.

The pump said what all machines say when they are asked what no man should ask. It spoke in that single low plea before it goes or fails and then it either keeps and then men brag on it afterward as if it had not been chance but merit or it lets go and they say they knew it would, that any damn fool could tell. This one held. It found its howl and set itself and the ditchwater ran in.

Evening lay upon the place with the light near yellow and the lows parted a little and you could see under them to where the line of the land went and the shadows of the trees ran long and ragged east and made a net upon the dusty ground and you could lay your palm in it and it was cool. A little brown bird came and bathed in one of the checks and shook itself and water went in a light unto the shape of a fan and it cocked its head and drank and flew. The apricots above looked like a thing grown as if by made hands, round and perfect and carrying a sheen, but when you took one you found the flesh mealy and bitter and the stone come away stained and smelling of almond and some few had worm holes and the hole would be sealed with a varnish like sap and you could smell in that a sweetness that spoke not to you but to all small thieves. Jesse did not take any, nor Ben.

They stood a long time without talk and they could hear the pump far as a dog howls when you are down in the evening with your own mind and the trees held their black detail and the river ditch said what a shallow river will say not that it was speaking to men. The tamarisk flicked. Across in the pasture a mule deer came and nosed at the wire and the crows ran a low conversation in the cottonwoods and one of them flew and set upon the top wire of the fence and the wire sang a little in a faint metal way.

Jesse said without looking at him, Fella came last week. He didn't know where your truck was. He didn't know which one of us was you. He said he'd come if you called him.

Ben did not say, I know. He said, I ain't called him.

He nodded. They will call you again.

Yes.

What say you you will answer them.

I say I will give them no answer today.

He glanced at his brother. That meant only for today and they both knew it and there was no promise in it at all and if there had been it would have been a poor thing to hang the fate of those trees upon. Jesse turned and went to the valve and shut it a little and the water changed its sound. He stood with his hand upon the iron and you could see from the line of his arm the tendon stand out as if made of metal itself and Ben thought that his brother would work himself into the ground because he could and would leave no complaint. He saw in him then not their father but some other figure neither one of them had the measure of and he was both moved and a little frightened. He said, What if it's no use.

Jesse said, It ain't no use. He said it flat and there was in it no sorrow. He said, The whole world is coming to be the way you say you like it. I cannot put it back the way it was. I can keep this running until it won't run. That's all any man got. He said, You ain't wrong. I ain't wrong. There ain't no right.

Ben did not speak. The pump went on and the light on the rim of the world held fast and then slid. They set the gates one by one and the water went whither it was told and for once it obeyed. They went back to the house where their mother lay upon the short couch and her mouth had fallen open and the whites of her eyes would show in the smallest crescent and she breathed like a small bird asleep with the head turned and the feathers risen. Ben tucked a blanket without waking her and Jesse stood a moment and then went into the kitchen and washed his hands under the tap and the water ran black and then sand ran and then it ran clear and when he dried his hands the towel came brown anyway. He set the towel on the edge of the sink folded and square in his way and he went back out with his hat in his hand.

The night fell sudden as it does in that country, a lid set down, and the temperature dropped a finger width and the smell of creosote from somewhere came and the smell of river. They sat on the tailgate and there was no light in the yard against the moths and only a near star at the corner of the roof and the scar of a run who had once turned about the eave and gone and now was filled with caulk and dark. The field crickets started their looms. The pump's song came low and faithful.

Ben said, I can go put the call off a week. I can tell them we want time to pick. I can tell them we got fruit to pull and fruit to dry. He spoke as if he were practicing a prayer.

Jesse said, You tell them any damn thing you like.

You don't care then.

I care so much I can't think. That's why I set a shovel to dirt. It keeps me from looking at the wall and putting my head on it and beating it until it breaks. It don't do me no good to talk. He took his cigarette from his lip and hung it by two fingers and looked at the ash long and thin and holding and then being carried off whole in a sudden slip to nothing.

They sat. Down in the trees there was a sound like breath. A coyote spoke and another answered and then the second did not answer again. A freight rumbled east ten miles off and it seemed a thing that existed in a different order of time. The two men were two small things in a world made for large things and they were content in that measure of their smallness and discontent both and you could not have squeezed them together and said why.

At some time Jesse slid off the tailgate and set his hat on and said, I'm going to the pump. You can stay.

Ben did not. He rose and they went together and their shadows went with them like two brothers who had been born to them and who would not ever let them alone. They came to the house of the pump and it clicked gently like the teeth of some sleepwalker and Jesse reached and set his palm again upon its shoulder as a man will lay his hand on a horse to let it know he's there and it's all right. The heat had gone out and there was a faint change in the sound that only he could have heard and he shut the valve plain and the pipe shuddered and the orchard gave a long drawn breath as if it were a thing drawing a blanket up and it grew quiet.

They stood in the door and the darkness folded around like cloth. There was no good in the next day and the day after that but there was the day and its small portion to be had. They turned and walked up the path they had worn among the trees until their boots made no sound in the dust and nothing else in the world made any sound and the stars stood over them in their old uncounted host unimproved by all the ways men counted on earth. After a while Ben said, We'll rise early. Jesse said, We'll rise early. They went on. The orchard held them and let them go and held them still.
