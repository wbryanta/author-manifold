They walked the frosted pier before dawn, boots clunking hollow on old planks swollen with a century of tides. The cannery at the head of the harbor exhaled steam that hung in rags between the lights and the water. The last winter, and the boilers were still working, and the whistle still dragged a note through the cold for the morning shift, and the gulls still came, though fewer now, perched hunched as men on the roofline, their white heads tucked and yellow beaks buried to their throats. Harlan touched the edge of the piling with his gloved hand, a habit from so many years of gauging slime, ice, and rot with his palm. He was a man of these habitual touches. The granddaughter, Lena, walked half behind him, her mitten caught in the strap of the old duffel he carried and swinging with each step in small arcs that made a ticking sound like the bell buoy farther out. He slowed so their steps could find a rhythm.

The harbor water was black with a blue at the edges where the first light tried the surface. A couple of aluminum skiffs lay moored close, their gunnels wearing thin cuffs of rime. “You see it,” he said, his breath making a little fog of the words. He didn't say what, not anymore. She was ten and tired of being shown and told. Better to point to see whether anything had taken root.

“I see the ice,” she said, and pointed to a filigree tufting the rope between pier and cleat.

“What else.”

She peered over, knelt and set her chin on the top plank so her eye was low, so the surface was all. He’d taught her that: drop your foolish upright human vantage and get down with the horizontal. The harbor read from down there. Soot-grey jelly of some midnight bloom lay stranded in the eelgrass near the ladder; their bell bodies caught the first of the sun and flashed then went dull. The surface itself was alive with wrinkles that went one way and then another. Then she saw it, a line as straight as a taught string drawn across the water thirty feet out, a thin seam where on one side the tiny chop was full of light and on the other it was glossier, slightly darker, oily-looking.

“There,” she said. “That line—like the stitching on your jacket.”

“Good,” he said. “What do you think it is.”

“The current changing.”

“Changing what.”

“The... what's under it. The water from out there coming in,” she ventured, trying to place it among the words he’d used—flood and ebb, set and drift, the language of the tide like the grammar of a foreign tongue.

Harlan set the duffel down and squatted and set his own chin. He didn't need to. He could lie flat and name things blind. He made himself do it, for her. The cold from the wood went through the knees of his trousers, through his bones and into the ache he carried there now that had lengthened and turned into something constant. He let it be. “That’s right,” he said. “That’s the flood. Slack is past. See how it presses against the pilings? You can see it like a river in the sea. And that line there is the front of it, pushing the rest along. See the little bits of wood, the kelp? On this side they’re riding north, and on that they just swirl.”

He reached and pinched a splinter off the piling and dropped it. The splinter struck the surface and paused and then slid, nose pointed by the tiniest of forces, arrowing toward the cannery’s shadow. Lena's eyes followed it as if he had let a captive bird go.

“They say all you did was climb ladders,” she said, like someone repeating what they'd overheard.

“That’s true enough,” he said, and felt how the duty of accuracy and the duty of myth tugged at him. “I climbed a lot of ladders.”

“Mom says. She says you climbed ladders and everyone cheered.” The tone was neutral, just testing the shape of a statement that might carry weight.

He nodded. “Sometimes they curse too,” he said. “When I make ‘em slow down.”

He eased back from the edge and picked up the duffel and they walked on. Worklight leaked through gaps in the cannery's siding like the last of a fire through slats. The place had always sweated. The old sign on the windward wall said North Coast Packing in a script so old it made Lena think of chalk on the school blackboard, and its letters had leached salt so thick you could taste it if you ran your tongue across it, which she had once just to see if what he said was true. Years of fog had laid a pale crust on every surface. Two forklifts moved in the lot with a languid caution, lights rotating slow, the beep beep sounding apologetic in the muffled morning.

They were headed for the bait room. That’s what Harlan told anyone who asked, and it was how he justified the ritual to himself. Inside the duffel were the pails for heads and spines, the dull knife for trimming, the old gloves. Every crab pot along the docks needed fatening before being sent over. He told himself they came for heads and not for something he could not name: to be near what was left of the town’s heart while it beat; and because the whistle blew and the lights came on and the women and men came with their lunch pails and hair nets still. But also he wanted to be seen once in a while by people who’d known him when he was the one stepping from a climbing rope onto a moving deck with his tote under his arm and everyone on the bridge looking to him as if he had invented the channel himself.

They turned the corner by the smokehouse and there it was, the bait room door ajar and a man in an orange apron hosing down the concrete, steam rising from it. He nodded at them, not surprised, just working his hose. “Harlan,” he said. “Cold one.”

“Always,” Harlan said.

Lena had learned not to make a face at the smell. The first time she had felt it as a hand on the back of her tongue, ammonia and iron and something like pennies left in a cup of brine. Now it was almost sweet, the way you can come to love someone’s old sweater. She liked the hose and the way the water pulled skins along, dragging a pelt of scales into the little trough like wind driving snow.

There were three plastic barrels with BYCATCH written in pen so black and uncertain you could see the trembling of the writer’s hands. Harlan set the duffel down and they took out pails. The man in the apron stepped aside and let them pick. Heads, some of them still glassy-eyed, lids half open, the mouth a gape that made Lena think too much about the fish as a thing with thoughts. She preferred tails, the brown disk of the cut end with its scalloped skin like a drape. She chose pieces like choosing shells. When a head had a milky film across the eye she did not pick it. Harlan watched her and said nothing and took what she did not, knowing that what repelled you was often what you needed to take; but he deferred to the private superstitions of children.

“What do you think,” the man hosing said to Harlan. He had to point his chin to lift his voice over the hose, and even then he sounded like a man yelling across a river. “Think we’re done after this season.”

Harlan paused with his hand inside the barrel and the cold of it went through the glove. He withdrew his hand with a string of spine that looked like the pale ladder of a ribcage. He dropped it in the pail and the light metallic clatter it made seemed louder than it should have. “Looks like,” he said. “They’ll say it’s temporary. They always do.” He made the remark with the loosened calm of someone who had stopped believing in certain assurances long before he was told to stop.

The man nodded and looked at him as if he’d expected more. Harlan added, “We’ve said goodbye so many times already we ain’t practiced for the last one.”

“I got twenty-four years in,” the man said. He turned the hose to a web of guts stuttering across the concrete. “They say they’ll transfer me to Coos Bay if I want. I don’t know Coos Bay. My boy’s in school. It’s always some goddamn place else is the solution.”

Harlan grunted. Lena looked down to avoid being noticed. She did not understand everything and did not want to be expected to take a side. She knew her mother washed her hands for five minutes after a shift and sometimes cried in the bathroom with the fan on, and that was as much as she wanted to make into words right now.

They hauled the pails back through the lot. Their breath came white and quick. Harlan carried the heavier one. Lena tried to carry them both at first but he had taken one with a little humor at her pride. He did it like a thief, a habit of stealing weight from those made to carry too much.

Back at the dock they set the pails in the skiff and then they did not go out right away. He liked to use the pier for lessons like a blackboard. He crouched and dipped his gloved hand and flicked water into the air and watched where it came down. He had a stick of chalk in his coat for writing on the dock, but today the frost worked for him. He knelt and traced a plan of the bar and the jetty rocks and the dogleg in the channel with his finger. Lena watched his knuckle pull white lines through white frost and felt that the picture itself was cold.

“Wind is northeast,” he said. “You can tell by your skin. Tide is flood. That means the standing waves at the bar will be...?”

He waited.

“Small?” she guessed.

“Smaller,” he corrected himself, always reluctant to let any generalization stand without revision. “Wind against the tide, they’ll build. But we’re inside. What matters is the layers. See out there where the river water rides on top of the salt? That darker glassier patch. The density’s different. You can see the seam. You can smell it sometimes. Taste the brown of the river on your tongue.”

He had always been a man who tasted the air. On ships he had startled engineers with his sudden pronouncements about oil on the water before the oily sheen had even made it to the bulbous bow. He had called fog coming hours before the horn made any sense. He had smelled rain and said, Not yet, because it was rain from far inland and would die before it reached the coast; but he had also tasted a copper lick that made him say, Lightning’s in this, which scared people because on the water you want to pretend you are not in god’s pocket.

They launched just as the cannery whistle sounded for the start of the shift. The moan unspooled across the basin like a line cast into the dawn. It was a menacing and consoling sound both. It was the sound she had always known. It meant the town working. It meant her mother going and then coming home, bags of cheap cereal and the smell of fish threaded into her hair. Lena thought sometimes that the sound made the water itself go. That without it the tide would not come in.

Harlan pulled at the starter cord and the little outboard coughed awake. They both sat with their faces turned into the cold. The skiff slid between fingers of floats, past a gillnetter with a name painted in a careful hand that made the letters look admonishing—PATIENCE. He idled along the inside and pointed with his chin at a set of bubbles that came up in a row and popped in a straight line across the surface. “Air from the old pilings,” he said. “You see where the underwater ones run. You can read what’s below by what breaks the skin.” He turned then to the gap and pushed the throttle the smallest bit. The bow lifted just enough so they felt that lean that told you the boat knew the channel better than you did.

Later, farther out where the harbor’s clip tightened and then let go on the ocean’s big waist, Harlan throttled back and let the skiff drift sideways. “Now say it out loud,” he told her. “Like a prayer.”

“The dark is deep,” she began, because he had made her a catechism of it and because the words calmed her.

“The dark’s the drop-off,” he said. “Depth makes a color, but so does water from a different place. You have to let your brain hold two things that seem like one thing and not let them marry before they’re ready.”

“The boil is a rock,” she said, pointing where a round bulge on the surface pulsed.

“Or a spring. Or the back of a fish school.”

“How do you know which.”

“You don’t always. That’s the work.” He paused and let her feel that the port transom was lifting just so. “See this. What’s happening to us. Don’t look at the water next to the boat. Look at what’s far.”

They watched the point on shore. The cedar that rose up like a witch’s hat. The pilings that used to hold up a long-gone loading dock. The light was that delicate grey moment before real day trespasses. She felt the boat sliding sideways, pulled toward the northern flats.

“Drift,” she said. “Set is the tide’s direction. Drift is our speed sideways because of it.”

“That’s right,” he said, and thrilled at how easy it was to make it sound simple. “Now if you don’t like what the tide is going to make of you, what do you do.”

“We point the nose somewhere else,” she said. “And let the water carry us into where we want.”

He smiled. She always said we, never you. He had tried without saying it to cure her of a grammar that made the world all singular. Out here there was only we.

He told her then a story he told like a shell he took out of a drawer. He did not tell it to everyone. He did not even tell it the same way to her each time. He told it to her this time because the air had that tautness that meant he could almost see the little hairs on the back of her neck lift and could almost see her patience. “We were bringing in the Yamamori Queen,” he said, and the way he said the name had some of the awe in it that people use when they talk about a star they saw once or a famous dancer. “She sat low. Full to the eyebrows in bulk grain. Freeboard the width of a hand. A winter’s night like this only deeper into it. No moon, fog to the knees of a man. I could have put my palm on the water and weighed the difference between the river and the sea. Outside the bar the swell was running eight, maybe ten, but tight. Short-cresting. And the river was at full ebb so the outflow and the seas were talking, not agreeing.”

He paused for himself more than for her. Telling pulled the physical memory back and put it in his chest. Sometimes he had to swallow to make room for it.

“I went up the ladder and onto the Queen in my socks,” he said. “And the master, he was a small man but had shoulders like a jar. If he smiled you didn’t see it, his mouth had so much of itself. We shook and he pointed at the radar like it was a political opponent. I told him we would come in slow and he said we would never make it through if we came in slow. We looked at each other and then we both shrugged because we both knew the truth, which was that slow was the only language the water knows when it gets to talking like that. If you go quick you die quick.”

Lena had heard this before and loved the place where he said, And then in the very breaking he saw a white he never knew, and I told him Lean with it and he did, and the ship bent and swung and came in like a tired horse and you could have lit a cigarette off the bridgewing with the heat we made off the air. She loved it because it always sounded as if every single thing had almost gone the other way. As if her grandfather had done the tiniest thing and that was the hinge on which an entire city’s winter hung. She looked at him now and saw he wanted to say more and so she asked what she had meant to ask, which was not in the story: “Were you scared.”

“Worked up,” he said. “Focus burns off fear. Or changes its smell. Fear that’s useful smells like copper. Fear that ain’t smells like old milk. If it smells like old milk, you set the throttles and you leave and you make your living another day.”

This was a kind of thing he would say—besides the maps and rules and terms he would hand her these little binaries, little ways to think of choices, and she would put them with her pencils and here and there bring them out and weigh them.

A seal’s head showed then, black marble above the surface. It looked at them as if it owned what it stood in and laughed at their poor boat. Lena waved by drawing a circle in the air. The seal sank without hurry. The light came up the way it always did, not enough and then enough, with a suddenness like the flipping of a switch in a room where you have been sitting for an hour. The cannery’s windows put off their own yellow. The gulls left the roof in a rolling. You could hear their talk then, the little phrases of it, and not just the big pronouncements that always made it sound like they were demanding something from the sky.

They set the crab pots—the day would bring them and take them. They were not fishing themselves. But those things could be done with her, small because he was retired and because you had to be careful about what you were still allowed to be seen doing when you were retired. They were baiting pots for a neighbor down the row who gave them half the take in barter. Harlan never could resist a ledger he could keep in his head with pebbles. They cut the heads with the dull knife and packed the trays with flesh and then let the pots drop and the lines sing through his palm. He kept her away from the coiled rope and she kept her fingers clear and he nodded and did not say he was proud because that was not said in the moment. It was said later, or never, or with a sideways glance at her carefulness.

The day opened. The cannery’s noise knit into something like a long, patient weeping. It was the sound of work, and Lena had learned to love it. She loved the belts’ hum and the short shrill of a forklift and the laughter that was not even laughter, the sound women made that was bravado and exhaustion in the same place. Charley from the harbor office came by and pretended he had errands, but all he did was lean and talk. He was shorter than Harlan and had the hearing of a gull, which is to say he never came closer than yelling. He had a red hat. He had had a red hat for decades. “You hear next week is their last week,” he yelled.

“Next week ain’t the last week till they say it,” Harlan said.

“They said it,” Charley said, with a kind of satisfaction that came from the unity of rumor and truth.

Harlan stood half in the skiff and half out as if his body could be in two places at the same time. “They’ll turn the lights off and then they’ll see they can’t stand the quiet,” he said. “And they’ll turn them back on.”

“You’re a romantic,” Charley said, and snorted with the pleasure of calling someone a thing they weren’t.

That night Lena slept on the couch at Harlan’s because her mother worked over. He made her a bed with the old cruise blanket that had whales printed on it, and the whales’ faces were kind and dumb and she loved them. He made eggs for himself and for her and she sat at the small kitchen table with her book and the smell of the sea and grease braided in the curtains. He had a way of being quiet with her that did not demand you fill it. He read the obits and then folded the paper and then used it to start the stove. The stove had three sounds: a soft exhale and then a whoomph and then a steady tick. He poked the fire with a little iron he had made in eighth grade before anybody thought he was going to do anything at all with his life, and he still had the iron and used it and that was a thing she counted, that you could keep something from when you were a nobody and use it to tend the good heat years later.

He poured her a cup of cocoa. She had learned to accept cocoa as the shape of how he loved her. She did not like it much, it was always a little thin, but she held it in both hands and blew in it and watched the tender wild shapes the steam made. “Tell me about the range lights,” she said, because the lights, the idea that you lined two lights up and then you could go between, that was a miracle to her as much as anything else, that you could make two things into one and that meant safety.

He got up and went to the cupboard and took down two old chipped saucers and stood them one behind the other on the counter. He tipped one left and tipped one right. This was his other way of loving: to show.

“You see one,” he said. “And the other is behind. When they’re in line and one is atop the other they sew you to the channel. It don’t mean the channel is good, it just means you’re in it. I have to say that part because people think a thing being correct means it’s wise. Correct just means someone made a plan once.”

He sat and she reached and took his hand under the table quickly and then took it back as if she had not done it. He stared into his cocoa and then drank it.

In the morning the radio said a gale was warning off. The NOAA voice talked a little through its nose, a nasal recitation from someone in Portland whose shoes had not touched bait. The woman’s tone had an intimacy Lena had always found strange. Coastal waters from Cape Meares to Cape Foulweather, gale warning in effect through tonight. Seas building to twelve feet with occasional seventeens. Harlan smiled. “We could use that,” he said. He liked to see the water worked up, as if in its big temper you could read things clearer. Calm he mistrusted. In calm he felt like a fraud.

They went down, and the dock woke with a new sound. The ropes had a different word to say as they creaked. The wooden hull of a troller spoke in a vernacular of strain. The gulls lifted and hung like scraps. The water itself bargained with the pilings and the gaps between things filled with a small white that went away and came back.

He made her stand with him and face the wind and smell it. It had a taste like a nail held in a mouth and old wool. “You see the cat’s paws,” he asked, and she did: the little dark patches that ran ahead like small animals across the surface, the signature of small gusts. And beyond them the big dark threw itself across the line of the river like a shoulder. “The wind has handwriting,” he said. “The gale blows without much grammar. But you can find sentences in it.”

They did not go out. Today was for shore reading. They walked the breakwater, the big dolosse piled there a permanent puzzle of concrete X’s that even when she was small had looked like a game for giants. They watched a freighter outside, running light. She wore a rust apron on her bow and you could see sky through her anchor hawseholes. A slant of spray came over her forefoot and broke and made small ghosts in the air that the wind erased. “Pilot’s on her,” he said, with that little taste of his own old pride. He had stopped going out to the ladder the previous year. He told people his knees were the reason and sometimes he believed it. He kept the pilot’s phone in the kitchen drawer because it had no other use that he would let himself have.

He pointed out to her the place in the bar where the line of white rose and fell out of sync with the rest. “That’s a shallow bar on the bar,” he said. He laughed at the phrase and then grew serious. “See how the wave stands and holds its lip a breath before it throws. That breath is the consonant in the word shallow.”

“You talk like you’re reading,” she said. “Like you’re sounding things.”

“Same thing,” he said. He had always conflated the two. When he taught men to make up their lines he used grammar. When he read a good sentence in a book he looked up and away and remembered the way the fog up by the headland could swallow a mast and leave the foot of the sail showing as if the boat wore a white skirt.

They stood long. He did not push her. He had learned something about teaching: that you could pour a pitcher of water on a stone and watch it run off, you could do that for a year, and then the next year you could do it again and at the end of that year the stone would be a little darker where you had poured. And that was what there was. And also that sometimes a little green took root on the stone’s side.

When the freighter began her approach he got quiet. His body changed. Lena saw the way his shoulder blades drew together. The freighter altered to line up and then slipped into the bar’s mouth and the swell grabbed her by the ankles the way a child makes a doll walk. “You see that,” he said, and did not have to point. The ship had a little lift to her that was not from the pilot. It was from a bar he knew about and that about-to-break line that relaxed when you wanted it to work and worked when you wanted it to relax. The pilot inside her wheelhouse would be measuring everything in handspans in his mind, Harlan knew that, one of his kind could always feel the other. His fingers scratched at a ghost rope.

When she was inside, when her prop wash punched a paleness into the dirty flood, a flatbed truck came around the cannery with a sign TENTATIVE CLOSURE MEETING. The letters were so neat it made you distrust them. People came outside and then back in. The day went grey and then more grey and then gave up its will and went full afternoon.

A week went like that and then it was the day everyone had been talking at and around. The cannery would shut after the last of the boxes were packed. There were not enough fish to justify tomorrow. Harlan and Lena went down one more time because doing what you did with the hours was a way to tie yourself to something that did not go away. The lot was full in a way it rarely was. People came and stood with their hands in their pockets. A boy sold coffee from a cooler. There was a pastor and a town counselor and three men holding a banner that had once been used for a Fourth of July parade and was now turned around and written on the back in a pen that bled. The back of the cannery was open because the stackers were throwing boxes into the truck, and you could see inside to the belts and the women at the slime line with their knives and aprons, their gloves dark as if they were work animals in a dream. They were not crying, or not all of them were. The crying came in small private moments and not for a camera. Someone had a radio and it played a song that had been popular when Lena's mother was a girl, and the music sounded as if it came out of a jar.

Harlan led Lena down to the last ladder where the pilings were black with more than age. He did not want to be part of the crowd. He did not want to be apart from it either. He stood where you could make an excuse that you had to watch the water if someone asked you where you had been when it happened.

“What are those,” she asked then. She was pointing at a tide line that had gathered against the inside of the breakwater. It was not the usual scum of eelgrass and dugout wood. It was silver. The first pieces made him think of foil. Then he looked harder and saw they were bodies, and not dead either. They were a shoal, a run of herring jammed up against the stone by some invisible instruction. The water worked there like breath under a sheet. You could see the shapes come and go like phrases in a language you might learn if you lived long enough.

“Look,” he told her, and his voice came out weird because he had placed it low in his chest the way you do when you’re trying not to spook a thing. “Look, girl.”

They watched the silver flow wheel on itself and go still and then lift and pour, dimpling the surface with stabs and hollows as if something were writing on the sea. The gulls noticed in the same instant and fell like torn paper. Some men ran, some women too, somebody grabbed a throw-net and flung it and other hands grabbed the line and hauled and the net came in a sack of light and life. The bodies in the net were slick and hard, those silver darts of the world. They made a sound as if they had invented rain.

Harlan forgot the cannery then and forgot himself and his years and his carefulness and stepped down onto the next rung of the ladder and the next and then into the skiff and pointed at Lena just by moving his chin. She dropped down like she was born to ladders, and he allowed himself the thimble pride. He cast them off and the little motor woke in the most obedient way it ever had and they went toward the run.

“What do you see,” he said, as if he had nothing to do with what she would say.

“They’re packed,” she said. “They’re jammed into the angle. Something’s pushing them. Something’s behind them.” She did not say predator; the word carried fairy-tale freight and she was older than that and younger.

He cut the motor and they drifted in. Their hull made a hollow the fish filled and then unfilled. They pressed themselves into a lobe of the run and it moved with them like something living made of ten thousand lives. He dipped the old net, a long pole with a hoop, and Lena held the pail and he brought up five and six a time. The fish rained into the pail like coins, like the town blessing them, which is how a mind goes to simile when it wants to change truth into a story you can stand. He worked and then rested and watched.

“What are you seeing now,” he asked again. He did not stop although he knew he was asking too much and that there was only the silver, only the wild joy of harvest in a place where things had been taken from. She humored him. She knelt at the bow and put her face near the surface. He had taught her to do that and she did it now without thinking, her hair falling half in. Her breath made commas. The run moved, and she saw then what he wanted, not the fish but the way the water itself changed, the slicks their bodies made, the shadow they threw, the softness where their bellies brushed as if they made velvet on the sea. She saw a line moving where no breeze moved, a pull. She saw how the tide hands had the run and wanted it along the inside and the fish wanted out and the two wills made a jacquard on the water you could read. She saw a pause and a swell and that breath he called a consonant. She touched her lip and tasted the salt and the river dirt and the thin stainless taste of a school of fish. She saw him watching her and did not look away. She was learning his pleasure. That felt good too, like making a difficult shape in math clean without scratch paper. She said, “They’re writing,” and felt the foolishness of it and did not take it back. He nodded.

“They can’t read their own writing though,” he said. “That’s why they die. That’s why we do. Don’t let a pretty thing make you think it’s a wisdom.”

She had a line ready he had given her once. “We read to see where we are,” she said. “Not to tell who we are.” He smiled, a quick slackening of his face that made him look like the youngest version of him in the old photos on the mantle, a boy almost.

They dipped and raised and dipped until their wrists ached and their breath made steam clouds like the cannery’s. People on the breakwater called down to them to throw some and they did, flinging with both hands up into wind and watching the silver arc and hearing the startling sound of bodies hitting jackets. A woman caught one against her coat like a baby and cradled it and then laughed at herself and held the fish up by the tail and it was beautiful, it was the complicated dark on its back and the light on its belly like foil for the dead’s eyes.

The pastor came and blessed nothing, he only stood with the faces, which was a blessing. The town counselor said we are not done, which was not an untrue thing, no town is done outright, they always go on a little even after their heart stops, but he said it like a poster, and it meant as much as the pen on the banner. The forklift beeped, and then stopped beeping, and did not start again.

Late, when the run had finished or had only moved to a place they could not see, when they had too much and gave half away and kept the other half for the smokehouse and to trade for flour, when their arms had the good ache in them of having touched their luck, they sat in the skiff and let the movement under them become the only movement, and the sound of the belts inside the cannery lessened and lessened, and then someone must have thrown the brakes on a machine and it sighed and spooled down, and then they heard the quiet begin to enter. It came in small. It did not sweep. It talked in an undertone and wanted you to listen. People coughed in it and the cough sounded like a new thing. A gull's wing sounded like paper. The radio in someone’s coat sang a line and then somebody shut it off because it felt wrong; or because it hurt.

Harlan found, to his own shock, that the quiet nudged at the very center of who he knew himself to be. He had wanted the last whistle, yes. He had thought that would be the thing, the long pulling, the joint roar and moan and meant-to-mean-something of it. He had forgotten the after. He sat in it now and it felt like the inside of a seashell you put to your ear when you are small and they tell you you will hear the sea. He heard it. It was not generous.

“She'll come again,” he said to the boat or the girl or the town, he did not know. He said it like the thing you hang in a window to scare off birds.

Lena looked at the darkening water outside the harbor. Outside, the bar’s line glowed for a second where a wave toppled and held up its white; then the white fell and became part of everything else. She thought of the two saucers on their shelf and how when one stood above the other you could go home and when they did not the other thing happened. She put her hand into the cold and felt it for just a second hurt and then nothing. She took it out and put it under her own coat and then under his arm. He pulled her closer without moving his face. The seaward buoys moaned. The houses on the hillside, the ones that had light on in their kitchens, looked like toy rooms to her from there, and the ones that were dark looked like the eyes of fish too long dead.

“Tell me what you see,” he said again, so gentle it hurt her to have to answer. Not because she did not want to but because it was like being asked to announce a funeral right as it came.

She looked and made her eyes into the eyes he wanted her to have and then she made them into her own. She saw the flood still going and the last of the day leaned against it. She saw the line where the river’s brown lifted and fell like the hide of something breathing and how outside that the big blue-green of the sea gathered a little as if ready to shoulder in, and that would be the later tide, the longer one, the one that does not lift and let go but leans and leans until you forget there was ever any other weight. She saw, too, the tiny thing right by the bow that did not matter at all—two bubbles that had come up together and stayed together and slid along the paint and then were gone. She thought she could say a poetry if she were foolish, and then she thought of him and the long not of poetry in him and revised.

“I see the flood and I see the wind writ on it. I see a rip where the rock makes a change.” She pointed and he nodded like a man counting his own coins. “I see the seam. I see our drift if we were out there, and I would head us there—” she pointed again “—to meet it so that it would bring us where we want slower.”

He breathed once in and once out like a thaw. “Good,” he said.

“And,” she said, almost so low that the water took it and not him, “I see the quiet. I see what’s missing. I can read that too.” She wanted to say: I will watch the quiet when it comes and I will not be fooled by it like a pretty day in a dangerous month. She wanted to promise. She could not put the shape of it exactly into words and did not try to lie them into perfect.

He put his hand on her head lightly like laying a chart down and not wanting to crease it. “Aye,” he said, which was his yes when the yes was meant to be a heavy thing with its own ballast. He did not say more. He watched the mouth of the harbor as if he might see his younger self come through it, climb the ladder, put his feet carefully on the black-painted steps to the bridge, shrug and take the con, tell a small man to trust something he could not see.

They tied up. He made her lay the lines as he always did, each turn in its place so that if a thing were needed in a hurry it would not be chaos. They carried their pails and gave some to the woman on the steps with the crushed hairnet still on. They gave some to Charley who claimed he didn’t need fish and then suddenly did. They gave some to the pastor who did not even pretend he was going to give it to a parishioner first.

Back at the house he set water to boil and the steam in the kitchen lifted the lace curtain like breath. He showed her again the small moves: slide the blade under the gills and pull; knife not a saw but a shove. Save the roe. Set the bellies to one side for the smoker. He moved slower than he once had and when he forgot a thing and stopped, she gave it back to him with only the smallest smile. They worked side by side and it was quiet except for the small complicated sound a knife makes when it talks to a board.

After, when the fish lay in their rows and glowed in their simple fish ways, he sat and she did too and he did not turn on the radio. The quiet had come in. It lived now in the house with them as if it had bought a chair. She did not hate it entirely. In the quiet he told her some small true things he had not told before because he could hear the words. He told her that night on the Queen he had been more scared than he had let on because fear had put his tongue between his teeth and the shark of the bar had wanted it. He told her that when her grandmother, his Maggie, had died he had lain on the floor and smelled the carpet because it was the part of the house that seemed, inexplicably, to hold her smell best. He told her that when he was a boy his father had made him stand knee-deep in the winter water and hold a boat by a line for an hour until he cried, and that was the first time he had ever learned that a person can stand a thing he does not think he can stand if he looks only at the next ten breaths and not further.

She did not cry. She put her shoulders against his shoulder in the way of mammals in a den. He smelled like old oil and lemon and fish and the faded not-sweetness of a coat that has not been washed because you do not wash it; you let the weather do what it will to it. He put the iron on the stove and got up and poked the fire even though it did not need to be poked. He counted his day’s moments. The way the cat’s paws had looked on the flood. The white breath of the wave that had stood. The turning off of something like a lung. The reading she had made of the shape of the fish and then the shape of what had not been there anymore.

When he slept that night he dreamed of the ladder and the rope, and the swell picked him and set him down, and in the dream he was as quick as he had ever been, but when he put his palm on the water to weigh the river, his hand came up dry and he knew he was on a table in a kitchen and not on a bridge, and he laughed in his sleep. In the morning he woke before light and sat and waited for it and listened to the new kind of quiet, one without the belt’s steady sermon, and he tasted the air and could not say what was in it, and then the first gull called and he tasted that and it was something like a word he had always known.

They went down all the same. You would. The dock had a glaze but it was walkable. Two skiffs lay with their covers half on and filling with the last of last night’s rain. Somebody had put a note on PATIENCE that said she was for sale and the price was just barely not a joke. The harbor itself lay like a shoulder under a sheet. The surface spoke in small tones. It was an unremarkable morning if you did not count. He put his elbow on the top of a piling. She put her chin on the plank and then on her mitten because it was cold. They were exactly in the posture they had been in the week before at first light. He did not ask. She did not wait. “It’s ebb,” she said. “Just by how the kelp points and how the shadow of the pier stretches. The line out there is a wind line and not a seam, you can tell because the bits on both sides move together even where they look different.”

He grunted approval.

“And,” she said, and she smiled now without meaning to and without trying to stop herself, “you can taste the river if you’re foolish.” He laughed and reached for the water and his glove got wet and he did not care and he put the wet glove on her cheek and she flinched and smiled and did not knock his hand away.

They stood as the light came up and put a small shawl of gold on the tops of the pilings and then the shawl slid and clothed other things and they watched without saying what the names of the things were because naming was important but not obligatory. The canneries of the world would go on somewhere else. Boats they had never stepped on would be made and old boats would rot. A net of fish a thousand miles away would make a river in the sea. People would come with banners and plans, fine and not fine. Harlan would find some mornings that his knees would not hold and Lena would go down without him and put her face to water alone and say his catechism to the surface and then to herself, like a prayer and like a set of rules.

A small seal rose and looked and blinked and went. A boy on a bicycle rode past with both hands off the bar. The last of the last steam blew off the cannery roof like the breath of a horse in frost and then did not come again. The gulls came down and walked the roofline with their familiar indignation. Out beyond everything a line of white rose and held and fell. The harbor took a long breath and let it out. He felt in his bones the rhythm of it and let that be enough. She leaned where the board was warmest from the light and felt it through her coat and let that be enough too.

“Alright,” he said, when enough had passed and you had to let the enough be enough and not hold it for too long to bruise it. “Alright.”

“Alright,” she said. They picked up the duffel between them and turned, and as they went he asked her, just for the comfort of the shape of it, “What do you see,” and she said, as if any answer could be all of it, “I see water getting ready,” and he nodded and did not say, For what, because he did not need to. He had taught her what he could. The rest would out. The harbor sang a little and then less and then the bell buoy’s slow note came up the channel as patient as a heart, and the two of them walked into it like people in a picture that means something to those in the picture and is just a picture to those who are not.
