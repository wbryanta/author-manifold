They called me just after midday, when the day was all the same gray as the tea I hadn’t finished and the sky beyond the office windows. The voice on the line was careful and plain, a man who had done this before. Is this Daniel? he said. I said yes. We have a man here we believe may be your brother. We found him this morning on the rocks east of the harbor. It’s the tide line there. He’d been in the water a while. We found a number written in his wallet; it matches yours. We’d ask if you could come over. Tonight if you can. Better you see than we guess. His carefulness made me imagine the drill: the script on a laminated card above the desk, his thumb pressing each thing passed in training, the chair he chose to sit in so his back would be to the window and mine to the door. I told him I would come. I don’t think I said thank you, but I said I would come and that I would bring what I could remember. He asked did your brother have any tattoos or scars? He asked it like he was asking the color of a coat. In the pause that followed I saw only a boy leaping from a pier with a rope in his hand, a man stepping through the spilled light of a bar door. He had a scar on his thumb, I said. He cut it on a mackerel treble when we were kids. It made a little white comma. He chipped his front tooth when he was fourteen. He might have had a swallow tattooed on his shoulder but I can’t swear it; it could have been ink from a night that never was. The man on the line said all right, all right, we’ll see you then and would I ask at the terminal for a Mr. Muir when I arrived? I said yes and wrote Mr. Muir on a pad already fogged with phone numbers I no longer called. When I hung up, the office smelled of the goose-fat candles some junior had lit for “atmosphere,” and in the sink was a coffee cup with a dried smile of milk. When I stood up the chair rolled back and struck the sill.

By dusk I was in line under the sodium lamps with the rest of us going over—men with hard hats looped to packs, women in red parkas holding a child’s wrist, an old woman in a fur collar the color of a hare in winter. A man had a cardboard pet carrier with something alive and unnerved breathing inside. Steam rose from the exhausts and made clouds of its own weather beneath the ferry’s side. Above us, the ferry had her name painted in an indigo I couldn’t read at that angle, the hull all scuffed paint and teethmarks of fenders; her windows were already lit up as if a city were inside. The harbor was the color of coal and the gulls hung like scraps of paper snatched then dropped by wind. The sky at that hour in November undid itself rapidly. Men took the last drag on cigarettes and pinched them out between finger and thumb. The tide smacked iron.

We walked up the gangway in a tired procession that might have been the same in any of a thousand ports and would be in a thousand more after this one rusted away. Inside there was heat and, immediately, the smell of chips. The carpet was a severe blue speckled with beige, the way they try to distract you into believing it is cleaner than it is. I felt as if I had been in that ferry my whole life. The order of things came back to my feet as if they had been stored there: foot passenger lounge, café, the sign for reclining seats that tilt back a miserly angle, the racks of leaflets exalting things to be done and seen when you reached the other side. I stood for a moment and felt the old panic of forgetting something essential—passport? I didn’t need one. Ticket? Folded in my wallet behind my bus pass with its photograph of me looking up by mistake. I carried a small bag with underwear and a shirt and a book I would not open. I could have gone without all of that.

The loudspeaker scratched and a woman’s voice asked us to read the safety notices, to observe that the decks would be wet underfoot, to report suspicious packages. I went and bought a coffee and stood with it at the high rail by the window and watched the last of the cars go on. Their roofs shone. The straps of the tie-downs lay in orange coils on the deck like sleeping snakes. Beyond the ramps and the gantries the town laid itself out modestly, as if apologizing for its own exertion of being. Roofs at bad angles, a church spire, the supermarket where the automatic doors had insisted on closing too soon all summer and a boy had put his palm out and laughed as they banged it. Lights came on in kitchens and people inside reached up to untie knots of washing, to draw curtains across their lives. In one window at the edge of the town, someone had strung fairy lights in the shape of an antlered head and I thought absurdly of trophies, of something killed and fixed in tinselled lights.

My brother had liked ferries. He had liked them because they seemed to exist in a piece of time apart from everything else; he liked that if you were on a ferry you neither belonged to the place you had left nor the place you were bound for. He liked that the rules inside were strange and brief: the deck slippery, the bar open at hours daylight wouldn’t allow, the way men leaned close to talk in permission given by engines that pounded out talk. He liked the bar, too, but it would be unkind to make that sound all of it. He liked the space between. He liked watching the wake cut the sea in two and show him a line only a god could walk. Once, he took me to the observation deck and pointed out gulls riding that wake, their wings scarcely stirring, and he told me the word for it, which I’ve forgotten, but it meant a place of ease stolen from a great violence. He had a way of making a phrase and tucking it into my life like a talisman. I had a pocketful of them, worn smooth with the rubbing of my thumb.

When we were boys and still glad of each other we had crossed to an island with our father and he, our father, had told us to count whales. Whales, he had said as if he were telling us of uncles. When a wave hump looked darker you gasped and cried there, there, but sometimes it was just a natural trick, the eye making beasts out of water. Even so, I thought I saw a pilot whale turn on its side to look at us, and when I said so, my brother laughed and said, as if to the whale, mind you don’t learn too much. That was how he was: tender and ferocious at once, humble in the presence of something bigger and yet ready to mock me for still thinking the world might love me back.

The ferry’s horn sounded once and then twice, a long, exact groan pitched to enter a room you couldn’t enter yourself, and in the window I watched the ropes slacken and then the big hymn of the bollard, the twist and release, and inside me something answered that it was time. I drank the coffee, which was too hot and left a blister under my tongue. The ship moved. The quay moved away, which is to say we moved, which is to say the world behind me rotated slowly like a carousel in which I had left things, and would not climb back on for a time. The ferry threaded the channel between the markers with their green eyes and their red eyes, and then there was the trembling step out into the more open water, where the swell had its own opinion about our going.

I have imagined many times the things my brother did with the years I was not inside. I have seen him in boiler rooms and on quays whose names are lists, I have seen him sleeping in a berth with pipes over his head that popped and ticked with the small language of heat. I have seen him working as a deckhand on a trawler off the banks in seas that turned men to cups in a drawer, and I have also seen him in a parish hall putting up chairs for a meeting about whether to allow a wind farm past the bird cliffs. He wrote to me sometimes from places I suspected he had not been. A torn page from a tourist brochure saying the nights were purple there and the women fixed your sore back with their hands. A postcard with a photograph of a dog tied outside a shop, steeped in rain, and on the back in his same careful school hand, Sorry. No address, sometimes not even my name. Once he called to tell me he had seen the Northern Lights and they had been green ropes climbing and it had made him feel he could pick them like beans. I had said I read they were solar wind caught in the earth’s magnet tail and we had fallen into the same old joke that I was the little professor telling him about things he could see and he was the thing itself. If he had a tattoo it would have been a swallow inked by a man with hands like food. If he wore a swallow it would have been for travel and not for luck. The last time we spoke was about our mother, four years ago. She was not dead yet but she was tired of not being dead. He had come to the hospital and sat with her all through the bad hours and the nurses said he’d sung to her, old songs she’d sung to us when we were small, the one about the man coming o’er the sea, and then I came and he left the room and we did not see each other there. Outside the hospital we stood as if we were strangers waiting for different buses, and then one came and he stepped off the curb as if he would catch it and then didn’t. When we at last spoke, on the damp verge by the old brick wall with the moss that had wintered, he told me I had become hard. I told him he had become a boy in a man’s clothes. That was what our fights were like; they were not about ovens or houses or wills but about whether the other was human enough. That was the last day he touched me, pushing me just once in the chest with the heel of his hand and then both of us shocked at the sound of the wind because it was sudden after the talk. He left then and wrote me twice more, neither letter having my name in it. The second had inside it a small thing—one of those tiny plastic toys from a Christmas cracker, a little blue car. I put it in a drawer and it was there with old keys and a coin from a country I had never been to and the card with my blood type from the time I gave blood and felt faint.

The ferry’s bar had an arrangement of fake plants and two men at the counter already had pints, which seemed both wrong and exactly correct. The pint sat in the air like a small church. It was the way my brother would have done it: buy the drink that anchored you, as if the table might go sliding or the sea might tilt you out of yourself. I would have liked to sit with one of those pints and look around and tell myself a story in which I was not the man I was, going where I was going, but that ship was not built for me. I took my coffee to a table by the window and sat.

At the next table a woman took fruit pastilles from a bag and set two orange and two purple in order with a decision that made me think of altars. The child with her, a boy about seven, asked if he could go and look at the machines. I remembered asking like that. The woman said no now, not yet, when we’ve gone past the red thing, and the boy took that as exact law and sat and ate an orange pastille once the red thing—some buoy or post to my eye—had slipped by. The boy looked at me as if he could see the thing in my pocket. He had the look of a boy whose father is not in the frame, that careful watchfulness. My own father had been huge when we were small, his hand able to reach two plates from a high shelf at once and lift them without them clattering; he had been a man who could put his forearm under a dresser and lever it and say there you are, boys, get your toes out the way. He died in the way of some men of the old kind: the heart stopped speaking to him in a place where nobody knew him and the nurse closed his eyes. He never saw us grown, neither of us became the person he had in mind and yet we were both somehow that person in ways we could not forgive each other for.

The boy and I watched as the land took its time to turn into only a line of black. The deckhands walked past with their shoulders hunched the way men hold themselves when it is colder than anyone has said. One of them looked familiar, but that is the way with faces from ports; they belong to every port. The woman reached in her handbag for something and a string of beads came up with the tissues and looped itself over the lip. I saw the beads and saw a different string of beads in my mother’s hand, the rosary she pretended was a souvenir when my father teased her. Is that a worry bead or are you really going to waste your trouble on it, he’d say, and she’d smile and float it once across her palm, swift as a fish.

When the ferry went past the last light and the swell said, now, the hull bowed and rose in a way I knew in my knees. I put my hand on the table because the table was the one thing the sea had agreed not to have for now, and in that way steadied myself. The boy made a face and the woman said if you’re going to be sick then put your head down, hand on him, the old instincts. Somewhere aft somebody laughed too high. A man in an oilskin came in from the deck with his hairlaid by the weather and took off his coat and the room got smaller to make him fit. The loudspeaker told us we would arrive at five in the morning, that services would be limited overnight.

Between then and five there were always within me another ferry, ten other ferries, and because the mind requires a place to stand it gave them to me—overnights to islands where the people seemed to have been designed by crows, pale and keen; daytrips to small places where the seal’s head lifting in the brown water was as important as a church. I had crossed water to bury a father, to ask a girl to love me, to run from a town. I had crossed to ask my brother to lay down his stubbornness with mine and he had not come to the harbor. I walked then and again now. I walked the length of the lounge and looked at the photographs on the walls: a whale tail like a black hand saying goodbye; a line of turf cut like soldiers; a girl in a sweater laughing too much for a camera, her cheeks blown by the wind. I went out on deck without a coat because my mind said air even as the body said sleep. The deck was salted with a rime of salt. The wind had that honest cruelty of something that wants you gone. A cigarette paper whipped by me with such force that I felt it on my cheek. Out there in the dark there were small lights at distances I could not measure—fishing boats, or the low throb of a platform, a lit mast that might have been a planet set at the edge of the world that someone had labeled and then forgotten. I put my hand on the cold rail and thought after a time that if my brother was dead then he was here in the scud of the foam, in the blow of the air. That was the size of him: not large, no, but native to weather.

You left and I stayed, I said without moving my mouth. That is the sentence we both wore like a comb against the scalp. You left and I stayed and because of that both of us were accused. In the old stories, one brother goes and the other watches the fire; one brother becomes mud on the boots of a girl in a different village and the other becomes moss on a north wall. Neither is wrong. We made wrong of it. We were good at making wrong of necessary things. We had practiced on the small panics described as virile—he broke curfews, I did homework—until we had made for ourselves a craft we could sail anywhere and we sailed it into the wrong waters because the wrong waters are often prettier. He didn’t come to see our mother one whole spring because the herring were in somewhere else. I did not go to the small pub where he wrote to say he would meet me because I said to myself I would not be moved. We played at being iron till the magnet got quitted of us.

When the cold had finished whatever it was doing to me I went back inside and found a reclining seat and sat. Sleep came like a man in an overcoat sidling along the aisle pretending not to look for you. Around me people arranged blankets and made of themselves those asesorized nests people can make anywhere if they have to: a scarf in place of a pillow, a rolled-up coat under the small of the back. The boy had fallen asleep with his mouth making the same fish shape we all make to get breath when we are not noticing. A man coughed and you could see, even with the lights turned low, the way his wife’s hand landed on his knee in that way that says please live long enough for me to die first. Ceiling panels hummed. I slept, then woke sharply afraid that time had gone on without me and the ferry had docked and I had been shepherded in my sleep across two bridges I did not remember and delivered into a new life.

I had not expected a dream but there it was, the kind that comes after the hours when the mind is too tired to guard itself. It showed me the body laid on a rock shelf where we used to fish and the gulls taking over him—no, the thought of gulls is cheaper than the real thing, the real thing was steadier: it was my brother standing on the far end of the pier, a cigarette behind his ear, a snake of rope in his palm, and looking to where I held the other end, and I asked him what are we securing exactly and he shrugged with famously beautiful shoulders as if to say if the rope stretches between us maybe that is enough. When I woke my mouth was raw with an old taste: the copper of winters when you run too fast and your lungs scald.

At some point past three a girl with one side of her hair shaved came and cleaned around us with a vacuum that took crumbs into it with a serious tone. She wore a badge that said Anna. I thought of the girl in the photograph earlier and decided, selfishly and possibly unjustly, that she was the same girl before she had been told that laughter was essential to a portrait. The world makes us more legible to it. I have never known whether that is kindness.

At five the loudspeakers woke us with themselves without saying much. We gathered our gear with the gratefulness of prisoners and made a line again. The woman with the child braced the boy upright and breathed on his face to help him put his arms into his coat. The men with the pints had found bunks late and came out with their faces creased into maps of the last ten years. On the deck the wind had freshened and there was that particular smell of kelp and fish and something like iron that tells you you are closer to it all. In the dark ahead there was a thicker darkness that was the island with light points making a pattern that only the born know. I had been on the island twice. Once with my father when we looked at a boat his friend wanted to sell and decided not to because she had a twist in her that said she would not keep a course no matter what you promised her. And once with a girl whose idea it had been. We stayed in a room over a pub and we held each other with the exhaustion of young bodies in old rooms. It was winter then too. The morning had smelled of the pub’s old beer turned into some other thing by heat. She had looked at me when we lay there and said this is the kind of place we could be alive and I repeated a phrase my brother had once told me: that places like this were good for people at the ends of lives and beginnings but not for the running part. It was unkind. She did not forgive me for saying it, even when she pretended to. She left by spring and took her lemon-yellow scarf. In the cupboard I found it one year later underneath the bedclothes as if someone had folded it between winter and summer for us.

We docked with that tremor that is more caution than wobble and the ramp’s downclank was a sensation in the chest as much as a sound. When we stepped out the cold cut inward as if it knew my cheeks. The ferry had hung us over the island and now allowed us to fall into it. I made my way down the yellow lines with the rest and went into the terminal. It was too early for the shop’s coffee to be anything but bitter. There was a board with bus times and three of them had already been cancelled. A man with hair the rough brown of bog gathered our tickets as we crossed the threshold like the priest who takes in alms and tells you nothing. At the end of the room three men stood together in a small knot of competence—the kind of stance that tells you these are men who know where to put their hands when something goes wrong. One of them was broad and wore a blue jacket with a crest. On the phone in my pocket Mr. Muir’s name had grown heavier, like a coin that has passed from one pocket to another too much. I went to those three men and said my name.

The broad one—Muir—took his hands out of his pockets the way a diver takes a step too deep, and shook mine and there it was, the first moment of something already decided; his hand was warm and had a soft in it I had not expected. He said Daniel, is it? And I said yes. He said I’m sorry to meet you for this. He said we can go across now if you’re ready. I nodded and thought there was no point in not being. He had brought a car with the word POLICE down the side but it was scratched and in that way the fascinating vehicles of childhood are, it was naturalized by a hundred small uses in the salt air. He drove with his hands a precise ten and two. In the passenger seat I put my bag on the floor by my feet, as if I should not be seen to be carrying anything.

We drove out of the town with quietude. There wasn’t anybody else much abroad at that hour. The island had the shape of all islands in the dimming of the morning, not yet committed to being land; the fences went diagonally where the sheep had said they would go. The sea sat on all sides with the smugness of something that cannot be entirely contained in a glance. In the back of the car there was a blue blanket with a rough rib that wanted to catch on things. A radio muttered intermittently in the voice of men who have known each other’s wives since the wives were girls. The station played some old song with a drum machine and my brother’s voice came into my head, telling me that no one dies all at once, that you can die a little bit each time a song finds you at the wrong moment.

Muir had one of those soft Northern voices that always sound as if they are apologizing for interrupting even when they come to your help. He did not ask me anything I could not stand. He said you’ve come from the mainland, as if it were a different country, and I said yes. He said do you know the island and I said I had been once or twice. He said he was born here and would die here and he said this almost gladly. A small hard rain began and freckled the windscreen, made freckles of the world.

We turned down a lane with grass centre and went past a church with square shoulders and then through gates to a low building with a tin roof shot through with self-tappings. The yard had a skip and a gull on the skip had the eye for order that an old man in a cap can have. The building had a sign that said Clinic and underneath it in smaller letters, Mortuary. The front door had a scuff where many boots had learned to find it. In the small room inside it was warm to an unjust degree, as if the heat could persuade the liminal world to be more kindly. A woman came out from a side door. She had her hair pulled back and had eyes like tide pools, meaning that you could see straight through them to something that did not move.

I know that the proper thing is to drag it out, to do that writing trick of trailing you through all the senses until you are de-sensed, but it did not happen that way. There was a chair and Muir gestured to it. He said do you want someone with you. I said no. He said the woman’s name, though I have since filed it under the labels we give to people whose faces we never forget: Nurse. She went to the door of another room and stood aside like a very old-fashioned hostess. There was a white bit of paper stuck to a clipboard. It had a number. The number made me think of my birthday and then of numbers in queuing systems and then of the number my brother had written in his wallet that turned out to be mine. It was too warm. It had the sweet tang of antiseptic over a sour of skin. They had not tried to make anything pretty. I walked in.

On the table lay a man with a sheet over him. That was the extent of it; there is no better way to write that down. I had the thought, like a white burst, that it might not matter whether it was him or not because from this point on he would drag this sheet over him in my mind forever. The woman went to the far end of the table and her hands worked with a gentleness you would with an animal you fear. The face she had to reveal was not mine to ease. She folded back the sheet to the neck and I leaned on my own two legs against the impossible business of recognizing. The man had hair cut short and the sort of hairline that tries to tell you his age but lies. He had the saltiness of the sea on him still although they must have washed him. The sea puts a film on things that no cloth takes away, not for a while. I did not draw in breath and I did not let one out. I have prepared for this moment half my life; I have shown photographs of him to men at doors who said they had seen a man like that at a dance in Thurso or a pier in Mallaig. I have dreamed it. He had a crooked incisor that leaned inwards a little, the way a boy’s tooth makes a decision and a man has to live with it. This man’s incisor did not lean like that. His mouth, arranged by death into agreement, had a fullness my brother did not have. I looked at the hands because I had not thought of doing that until it occurred to me that men’s hands are more honest than faces. This man’s left thumb had a white comma on it.

There are things one cannot say because the language shies off like a horse. I leaned in and considered the scar as if I could press it and make it tell me a story. Scars can be had by many men. A treble makes many commas. When my brother did his he shook his hand hard enough to spray blood on the shed wall and our father shouted to mind the hooks and I laughed because fear turns to laughter if you want it to. This man’s comma had a tail at an angle that did not suit my memory. That’s the truth and the lie of it. Memory is not fit to call down a man from a ship in fog and yet it has that authority. I looked at the left shoulder and there was no ink. His hairline at the nape had a V like a boy’s haircut in summer. His earlobe had a nick. My brother’s earlobe had no nick. I could feel my anger arrive at the door like a taxi and wait to see if I would take it.

The woman—Nurse—asked is it him, in a voice that had practiced being many things and tried none of them now. I said it isn’t. My voice was not my voice; it sounded as if I had swallowed lake water. Muir nodded once, as if he had known all along that it might go this way and was not surprised that fate had left him without a scene to place on the desk of his day. He said are you sure, which is the inside-out of knowing. I said yes and no at once. I said I am as sure as I can be. I said he might have a chipped tooth that I can’t see there and he might have had a toenail badly torn off when he was thirty-one. The woman said would you like to see his belongings. I said yes because that felt like a promise I could make to somebody even if the somebody was unknown to me and dead.

In another room there was a box with a clear lid. It contained the offense against narrative’s clean lines: a grey sock with the top turned over; a key ring with two keys worn to fangs; a battered wallet with its leather soft as a well-used glove; a slip of paper with a number on it, my number, written in his way. I picked up the wallet and opened it and inside there was that old grief of men called themselves by names that did not hold them. The card said James Downie. The photograph was not of my brother. The number had been written lately with a fierce hand in a blue ballpoint like the bitter doctor’s favorite. He had written it wrong—one digit was the nine that should have been a four—and then corrected it into a four that looked like a nine wearing a hat. That was the way my brother corrected digits. It reached under my ribs and took something that was mine and folded it. I could not tell if this was simply the way of men with bad pens or if my animal wanted to be delivered of a signal.

Muir said we found him at the Kirksay steps, name like a wave under the land. He said some lads went there early to look for bait and saw a jacket and then there he was. He was—it did not need the word, but he said it—gone. He had money in his wallet, he had a packet of rolling papers. He had that number. We have had three calls in the last two weeks about a man matching—he did not say my brother—matching a description given us by a man in the bar, and that man is not the best witness. I said people like my brother use other people’s numbers without asking and he said yes. He said my own son dumped his bank card for a month because his pal said he would borrow it and it went round the island like a hare.

The woman took the box away again as if she were taking a loaf from the oven and wanted us not to step where it would burn us. We walked out into the low light. Muir breathed into his hands and then held them to the air as if the air might accept them. He said there will be formality. We will have to release him to people who are not you. He said I’m sorry that you came for it to be this and it wasn’t and now it is another thing. He did not look at me when he said that like a good man does not look at you when you are unmade in a way your clothes have no language for. I said nothing even though in me a noise was happening the way sometimes a feral thing is happening in a wall and you tell yourself it is simply the wind.

He drove me back to the harbor town but not the harbor and stopped at a corner where a shop was opening and the woman with the keys looked at us and then at the sky because that is the practice here: you let men have their talk if their faces are toward each other. He said there is a man at the east quay who might have seen your brother. He said this as if it were a favor he was asking of me for him, not a thing he was giving me, and I liked him very much for that. He said his name is Ronnie but people call him Row. He’s the kind that tells the truth too much or not at all and you have to listen to how tired his wife’s voice is from the kitchen to tell which. He said you will want to go and see Kirksay no doubt and I said yes I do not know why. He said mind your feet; the kelp is like cow tongues there. He put out his hand again and I took it. It was the right thing to do. I must have been white as the walls and with less depth. He turned the car and drove away.

I went to the east quay because I did not know how to stand still. The air over that side of the place had the smell of diesel that comes from boats yelling in their ropes. A man in a blue watchcap stood with one foot on an upturned bucket and smoked. He did not have to be Row; they were all Row. I stood near him but not too close and looked out at the view, which was the way men sign to each other that they are willing to share a horizon. After a time he said you off the ferry, and I said yes. He said where you for, and I told him the clinic and I must have said I for because his face changed a little, only a draw of the corners, and he said ah. Then he said I saw a man two nights since and he was not right built for these winds and I asked him his name and he said sorry instead and I thought well that will do in a pinch, but not for long. He said the man had a jacket with a broken zip and he kept on pulling at it as if brute force ever did anything for a zip. He said he had eyes that had been in a lot of houses and not lived in any. I said that sounds like him and he looked at me and said he had a way of standing as if listening for the next instruction. I did not say anything to that. He said he went toward Kirksay and I said how soon after and he said as if it were then, which was no time at all. He said there were lads down to the steps with lamps and the wind was in. He said sometimes the sea asks you a question and some nights men answer that they can or they cannot. He flicked ash and the gulls made small judgments, their feet shuffling to make maps.

I left him then because to stay was to begin to be part of the quay and I do not get to be that. I walked out along the road to Kirksay which was a not surprising road in the way of roads to places that make men think of edges. It was fenced with wire and sometimes stone, the gates tied up in that casual knot science of farmers, the spray of the sea reached even as far as the ditch. The air had got manners but kept its edge. I met only one person—a woman moving her dog from pavement to grass and murmuring to him, about us all going where we wish and yet not. At the top of the steps the island fell away to the hollow in that smart way of old land making secret places and gulls uncountable sat the way jury benches are full. There was kelp in a skein and it had that beige of thing that was black when wet and now is not. The sea moved with vigor and had the look of a body who has been woken early. I stood and thought I would see him, that he would come up the steps with the now-immortal silly expression of men who have succeeded badly in a minor task; and that is one kind of hallucination. Another is to see him float on his back like a saint down below in the surge, his eyes open to the sky as if still instructing you to learn the difference between fulmar and gull. Neither thing was true. What was true was nail heads in wood and lichen silvering to nothing and a lip of foam shaped for a second like writing.

The anger that had come in the mortuary got in at me finally and set to work as if it had a hammer. I was angry that it was not him because it required that I go on exactly as I had, adding only another layer of knowledge to the cake. It required that I step back from the clear shape of grief and carry instead a bundle of maybes. The dead man had my number in his pocket and I was angry at him too, which was none of his fault. He had written the digit as my brother wrote it and I wanted to demand of someone the explanation for that. I wanted to go into the pub and find him there warm in his wrong jacket telling a story to the two men nobody should tell stories to. I wanted to call out to the gulls the way my brother could until the smaller ones came and hung in the air on the trust of his hand. I had no way to make use of any of these wishes. I coughed and pulled the collar of my coat up. The sea slapped and then pulled like the saying of a hard truth.

If you have not been to a place much then your mind lets you think you can borrow it anyway and make a room there for your ghosts. Kirksay would not take me. It made me stand where visitors stand. I put my hand on the rail as a boy came up out of the hollow carrying a bucket of worms and he looked at me as if he saw the shade at my sternum and chose not to call me on it. He said you can’t stand there if the boats are coming in and I said are they and he said not now but soon as if time were his like line on a reel. He went past and the top of his ear was red with cold. He wore a hat with a pompom he would have objected to if older. The wind made a small absolute in his cheeks. I wondered what he would make of all this when he was a man, whether he would become the kind that stands without his own head for a while and waits for it to catch up.

I walked back to the town because you cannot remain where you have been given no work to do. The town was doing the business of morning now, bread in arms, milk delivered in crates that jangled, the postman with his bunched letters like fish in a handful. I went into the café that had the thinnest steam on the windows and asked for a breakfast that would test a man and ate it as if that were a task that could be completed in a day. The girl who brought it looked past me at the ferry and said are you taking her back and I said the noon one maybe and she said if the weather doesn’t and left it at that. I phoned no one. There is a thing to be said for being the only witness for a while.

At the noon ferry there was less ceremony. The boat had learned some of our names. The same men, the same rope. I found Muir on the quay as if I had been told where he would be. He was talking to another man who was not a man from here and had the look of a man sent by a wife to carry flowers had he been in a different place. He nodded to me and I said I am going back and he said I thought you might. He said we’ll let you know if someone comes to claim him or if he finds a name of his own. He did not say your brother is or is not anywhere. I shook his hand and tried to leave part of myself with him like a parcel without address.

On the ferry I did not seek the deck. I sat with the others in the warm box. The woman and her boy were not there anymore; perhaps they were home now with a dog who circled the rug three times and lay, perhaps the boy told his grandmother that the red thing had gone by exactly when his mother had said it would and she had said so it does. The man who looked familiar earlier looked familiar still but had a bandaid now on his finger. I watched the sea through the window and sometimes it came close and sometimes it did not. A man across from me had a newspaper folded to a small that would have pleased my father—he had this gift of folding things into themselves until they had an honesty of purpose.

Halfway over a band of sunlight climbed suddenly out of a rent in the cloud and fell like a silk dropped from a high balcony on the rough water. The boat passed into it and for a second the room went very bright. It made of us a company of people lit for portraiture and then the silk went and we were left like rabbits in a hedge. I thought of my brother on some deck somewhere and the sunlight coming to him in that exact way and him putting his face into it like a man offering his cheek to a warm hand. I thought of the man whose name was James, who I would not know if he had walked right up to me in a shop and asked me to pass him the butter, and how he had come into my life only to leave it and how that might be the shape of things. In my head I said his name as if names could make men more present. I said James and I said Tom—I said my brother’s name though I have not written it down here and will not—and the two names were the same weight for once instead of one of them being a weight I could not carry anymore.

When the ferry docked at the other side and the world returned me to a place where I had left a plant unwatered and a bed unmade and a job unasked for leave from, I stepped onto the gangway and felt the island recede behind me not as lack but as an exact thing drawing its breath again in its own body. On the quay the bus driver had the bus door open with an authority that suggested I would be grateful, and he collected the fares in a small plastic box as if he were taking confessions. I sat and the bus made that noise that is the history of buses leaving and the ferry’s horn said something behind me in farewell and in warning and in habit.

On the ride home through the low land with its grids of winter fields narrowed to into this bus’s window, I took out my phone and found my brother’s number, which I had not called in so long that it had been buried under a dozen Martas and a man I once hired to fix a leak. I pressed call and it did what it had done last time: it rang twice and then a recorded voice told me that this number was not in use. I let it say this three times, as if the doing of a thing might make it different. Then I left a message in the place of no one, as if the air might store it and deliver it as some air does: I was on the island today, I said into the device, which was all my voice was to it, and it wasn’t you. There was a man and it wasn’t you. They found my number on him and I couldn’t say. I’m going to water the plant when I get in. If you ever want to see the Northern Lights again they’re saying it’s a good year. I’m sorry for telling you once that I wished you would stop sending me jokes that did not land. I am going to buy a better coat. He didn’t have your tooth. He had the scar but not the tooth. You were always breaking things other than yourself. I’m coming home now. I didn’t mention mother or father. I said his name at the end and the bus went round a bend and in that bend a woman put her shopping down in her lap and took up her knitting again.

At home the plant had found its own way through the night and stood slightly paler but upright. The light had that mid-afternoon color you get in such latitudes, which is the color of a bell. I put water into the plant’s tray and drank some myself and stood at the window and looked at nothing I could answer for. On the sill there was the small blue car from the cracker. I picked it up and rolled it under my palm across the sill and then into my pocket and then back to the sill, the way you do with talismans that do not know their own work. The phone did not ring. I did not imagine that it would then or now. It was enough, in that moment that cannot stretch because of its own nature, to have crossed over and seen a man and known him and not known him, to have come back with two kinds of absence. The ferry’s name came back to me then, without my having looked it up, and it was of something floral, ridiculous for such a blunt vessel, and yet exactly right; a thing large and ungainly made to do the same journey over and again until it learns the bottom well enough to be gentle with its screws. I pictured it making the night crossing once more, the people inside it like lanterns in the rooms of a house, and above them the gulls moving in the long wake, and beyond the gulls the line in the water that could hold a man if a man were a god. I imagined my brother somewhere to the north or the south of that line, because there isn’t a man alive who holds the centre all the days of his life, and I let him drift for a little, and in that was a kindness I had not managed in the years where he lived in a house I did not visit. In the morning I would phone Muir and tell him something I had forgotten to tell him about my brother’s hands, about the way one knuckle was larger from a foolish fight when he had been nineteen. In the next week I would go to the drawer where the car was kept and take it out again and declare it free to be lost. In the next year I would recognize, in a different harbor entirely, a posture that would set me walking toward a man before I realized it was not him; I would not apologize to myself for the going. The plant on the sill would remember when to yellow and when to green. I stood there until the shadows completed whatever duty they felt they had to the furniture and then sat in the chair that rolled, letting it hit the sill the second time on purpose, because the knock it made was a sound I could name.
