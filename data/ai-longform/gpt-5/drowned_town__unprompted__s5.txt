At dawn the silt is hard enough to take my weight. It has crusted overnight into plates the color of old coins, a skin on something soft that still wants to move. With each step there is a crackle and then a give, the suck at my boots, a breath from the ground that smells like the time between rain and rot. The reservoir has pulled its waterline back through the valley like a hand sweeping a table, and what it has not carried away it has left on display: the ribs of a street buckling out of the muck, rusted signposts coming up like the antlers of some animal that died drinking, the chalked outline of a foundation long ago leveled in the name of something larger than a family, larger than our town ever was.

When I was small my mother would stand in the doorway in August and look up the empty road, squinting through the heat, and say it had been a river first. She had a way of saying it that made a river sound like a creature with a spine and breath and intention—it would go where it wanted if you let it. Later, when the trucks came and the hard hats, when the men with the clipboards walked into the kitchen and drew lines with their pencils that cut the air above our table, she said that too, teeth half-bared as if the words might be bitten in two: It was a river first.

Now, to get down to what used to be Main Street, I pick a line in my head and use the tripod over my shoulder like a staff to reach the higher ground. The lake is at its lowest since they closed the gates, the papers report. Sixty years. Lower than in the late droughts, lower even than the pictures in the county archive where you can see, at a great distance and in a kind of grayscale light, the edge of the old church steps, the curve of the retaining wall of the feed store, a child with a stick held out as if to measure the air.

I have the older map in my pack, the one a state draftsman lettered with painstaking care in blue ink in 1959, with all the lot numbers and the little squares of buildings and the names in a tidy print that wants to make everything permanent: McCrae & Sons Hardware, V. Hollis and Wife, Gideon Town Hall. He must have worked from plats and tax rolls and the charcoaled record of streets kept in the vault at the courthouse. There are differences between what he drew and the way we remember where we lived; memory will move fences if it needs to. On my copy I have traced a north arrow in red that is appropriate to the grid we use now. I have put a conversion between NAD27 and NAD83 for my own satisfaction as if my belief in the world’s shape had anything to do with epochs or ellipsoids. I am not here to repair history. I am here to throw points at the ground and net back what can be netted, to feed the coordinates into a machine and hear it announce the angle of a life in degrees, minutes, seconds of arc.

Above me, a line of golfers in their carts has gathered at the lip of what the lake made into a cliff at the one eastward arm. This new precipice took half the fairway and their eighteenth hole. It reveals a cross-section of turf and imported sand and native dirt that smells like the inside of a root cellar. Men and women in polo shirts with logos turn their heads until their necks ache. Every one of us has that inch of something in the chest, that tilt toward spectacle and toward homecoming. If a whale beached itself in Merrow County, we would all come to touch it too.

“Keep to the right of the sycamore,” a voice calls from far above, though the tree it names has been a ghost for decades under the dinge. I look up, lift the orange hard case higher on my shoulder in thanks, but I am not susceptible today to rescue. I have work to do.

The base unit is set up on the flat of what must have been the courthouse lawn. The bronze disk I remember with my father’s fingertip on it is sunk into the one chunk of granite they could not move, and the moss that grew around it in my lifetime underwater has fallen away like old felt. United States Coast & Geodetic Survey Bench Mark 1948, it says, and in the center a cross. I turn the tripod feet to bite the little ridges into the rock. The instrument head locks to the base with that familiar twist that is too easy to overtighten in gloves and which I always, even now, overcompensate for like a tall person ducking under doorways in old houses. I press the flat of my hand to the bubble, coax the world into a level. The radio chirps, and in the screen the satellite constellation arranges itself with a precision that made my professor in college talk about wonder for the first and only time.

Between me and the waterline, the exposed belly of the valley smokes. What looks firm at a distance is honey close up. You put your weight down and you learn which layer was just a rumor and which will hold. Ruts from the last patrol truck that came through, before the county said stop driving the reservoir bed, filled overnight with a silvery water that shivers at the slightest movement, rings around midges that come in clots and insist your ears belong to them. Every so often there is the small sound of a fish learning the limits of its new smaller world.

I take the prism pole in my hand and start toward what the map tells me is the corner of my block—the one where our house stood with its buttressed porch that bounced under my feet. In 1962 the county gave the men a week to knock down their own homes to salvage their own lumber; if you did not, a different set of men would come with a dozer and a plan. My father took apart our place with the care of someone packing a machine too heavy to be shipped. He numbered the windows with grease pencil. He laid aside the kitchen bench he had built with his own square hands and the door with the rhomboid scar in it that was my fault from swinging it open with a pot on my hip and catching the knob on the jamb. We moved the things a wagon could carry to the top of the ridge where an aunt had a place shoved among the cedars. The rest was supposed to have been leveled, and was, if you stood far off enough with a map that wanted all the lines to be straight.

I was nine. On the last night in the house we lay on mattresses in the room that had been my parents’ and listened to our own voices bounce off the walls. Every neighbor within a mile shot off their shotguns into the hum and ringing night the way a farmer will light a leaf pile to drive the ticks out of a fence. Kids down in the pasture lit paper lanterns and let them go, a thing we learned from the fair before they moved the fair to the far side of the dam. The lanterns floated up and then down at the river, and the kids who’d set them loose shouted the way you shout when something you loved drops and you are not sure whether you ought to cheer or grieve.

The weeks before, they had come with the backhoe and the crane and dug the graves up. They did not wear masks. There was a Sabbath about it that made even the atheist men stand back and take their hats off. I watched from the fence and from a distance, the way my mother watched things she knew would matter to me. Later there was a new cemetery above the ridge that looked down on the lake instead of up, and a newer plaque that tried to hold all the names that had had moss in their letters for three generations already. In the new place my father wore his hat low and hatched lines on a sketch he kept in his pocket and cried so little you could have mistaken it for the effect of wind in his eyes, except there was no wind that July that did not feel like someone exhaling against the back of your neck.

Down here, in the wide bed that was a town that was a river, the sun shoves its thumbs into me. I build a latticework in my head of what I need to shoot before it climbs too high, what will be too hung with glare to make my instrument happy. I have the county’s list of features to capture and a list of my own. Streets are still streets even without traffic. They hold their line in the earth with more stubbornness than the walls did; asphalt remembers itself even if it has been silted and salted and chewed by carp. There is the place where the backing trucks used to put down their tires to turn at the feed store, the arcs stamped in the roadbed under the shallow silt like the whorled print of some large fingertip.

We have called it Harper Lake in my lifetime, though in the beginning it was named for Gideon, the man on the Board of Supervisors who offered the least resistance to the state boys and so earned a naming for himself. It is always some kind of trade, and later you might not know even the terms. Our deputy director, a woman close to retirement with a habit of setting down her coffee and then forgetting where she put it, told me on my first day that all maps are apology.

The radio has paired with the rover thrice and unpaired twice, sulking. I hold the pole up by the bolt of my shoulder and cross the arena of what used to be the square. The sound of my boot pulling free is private and obscene. I plant the point of the pole, make the mark in the book, shoot it, log it, shoot it again in case some glitch between my curiosity and the satellite’s indulgence has opened and closed while I blinked. On the screen the numbers settle like silt—eastings, northings, the Z cool as a headstone. There: curb start, curb end, curb start again where the road does its jaw to go around the sapling oak that was tweaked by their county men even then, with their little white flags that said PLANT DO NOT MOW, and which now is a bleached pole with the cross-hatched bark sloughed off in long whispering veils.

At the edge of the flattening that must have been the hotel’s footprint, a man kneels and makes the patient, circular motions of the metal-detecting faithful. He has a bucket and a thermos and the expression of someone at a threshing he does not trust to his neighbor’s timing. We trade nods. There is a common decency out here between us that we do not extend to other places—we are all trespassers in this new dryness and so we forgive each other with a fullness that would be expensive on the pier at the marina or at the desk at the assessor’s office.

“You the surveyor?” he calls without stopping the metronome of his sweep. His accent drags its toe in the syllables and kicks up dust.

“County,” I say, because there is a politics to claiming jurisdiction more than there is to claiming gender or name. “You have any luck?”

“No hell of a lot. Nails. Ring pulls. Piece of a rifle, maybe. Saw a boy get his toe into a Mason jar. Don’t know what he was thinking. Well. Don’t know what he was thinking.”

He looks at me full on then and his eyes brighten the way eyes do when they have a face to fix to a story. “You from around here?” he asks.

And because I am tired of the half-explanations my life has felt like for thirty years, because the sky is a hard white and because I do not mind a certain small danger in the ears of a stranger, I tell him yes, and I tell him I was born in the town whose name the reservoir took as if the taking could be softened.

“Lord,” he says. “You got hometown under you.”

“I do not know if that is the word for it,” I say.

“Think they’ll put it back?” he asks, with a grin that shows he is not simple. He knows they will do no such thing. He smiles so that he does not appear to ask with anger.

“No,” I say. “There isn’t a back. They’re having me shoot the streets so they can redraw the—” I stop myself with a private laugh. “—for emergency access, and a contingency plan, and for some sense of completeness that will feel like work.”

He nods. He has found a corner of brown glass and has that look of a child banging two stones together for the fire that is in them.

“Found my wedding ring under our own porch when it did one of its lows fifteen years ago,” he says. “Got married after the water. We set the trailer up on the ridge above. I’d take my wife down here with me on Sundays after church and we’d walk up and down like we were going aisle to aisle in a store that sold nothing you could put in a truck. There was tension there. You could feel it. How it slid along the ground. I picked up a bent spoon and she lost her heart for it and cried until her hair stuck to her cheek.”

“This town didn’t care what you wanted of it when it was dry,” I say, surprising myself, my mother’s tone in my throat. “It will not care what you want of it now.”

He laughs softly and goes back to his sweep. “My name’s Bud,” he says after a bit, the way a man will the second time you pass each other on the same narrow path. It ought to be odd, to be naming oneself in a valley where names have been exhumed and reinterred and combined and cast through tape, but it is not. We say the names of the living like charms.

I cross the invisible line that was the shadow of the hardware store and step onto the slab that was the floor of my father’s shop. He had a habit of tapping each thing twice after he put it where it had to go, and now there is a subtle dimpling in concrete where hammerheads kissed it and broom handles leaned and the heel of his hand made its own confirmation. In the dirt at the edge of the slab a coil of blue string sleeps the way a snake would if it trusted the earth. The string is the same color as the chalk lines we snap to show carpenters the exact grid of the world.

A car tire rests in an odd way in the lee of a chunk of curb. Someone carried it to where it is and then set it down and sat down beside it and thought for a while and then left without it. There is an objectness to things in a drought that they do not have under water. Under water they all have the one shared purpose of taking the weight of the lake; in drought they reassert their differences. A board is a board. A bottle is a bottle. An old rifle barrel is a promise or a threat, depending on whether the eyes that a person uses to look at it are his father’s or a showman’s.

Toward what was the school the ground gives a little more. I have to walk upright, with my arms held away, to keep my balance when my boot sinks. A boy on the far side drops down from the ledge of a culvert with a rope of his arm that will take him straight into manhood. He yodels without thinking about the way sound behaves here and leans into the mud and levers a board up. His father calls something and he straightens.

“You all be careful!” I call out as if they were my cousins. “Do not step where it looks wet.”

“Lady,” the boy calls back, “it all looks wet.”

The school was a square of brick with a bell in a cupola when I left it. Now it is a shallow square hole with a line of bent rebar like the skeleton of a horse this town never had the money to ride. I touch the rebar with my wrist and it leaves a smear. The steps are intact. The number of steps is the same. I count them out as if I am late. My breath is a hot wire through my teeth. Near the top there is a depression where, as a girl, I pressed a penny into the bulging gum of mortar to see whether time would work the way grown-ups said it did. The penny is gone. The depression is still there. Across its hollow, a sparrow has left the imprint of its foot.

When they were building the dam there was a moment for me—the flat kind of moment that is not a point but a platform, a thing you stand on and from which everything after is measured—when I understood that men with tape can draw lines and declare their lives on one side and ours on the other, and that this can be considered work. I watched one of those men pound a stake into the bank above the sycamore. Then I watched another man twenty feet away sight down a scope and say, Yes. That was enough to set me on the path I took. I am not surprised by processes like anger or the passage of a plume of silt. What men are capable of, when they put their minds to it and give themselves titles and letters they can line up after their names, does not startle me either, but it fascinates me the way a diagram fascinates: the components named, the directions clear, the little notations, the way the parts all nest and move without squeak.

On my first job out of school, the crew chief took me to the hot black backside of a stadium where we were to shoot the tie-ins for a fence line. He told me to set the head at point six and to remember to pick up my plug when I was done. He had the ease of someone who could have been born with the vocabulary in his mouth and the forearms to do this work in the same way that some families are born on the water and nothing that happens to them later will ever uproot the way their bones move when a boat shifts under their feet. I wore sunscreen like a secret. I still had my mother’s hands. I still had in my body a sense of the town that was somewhere at the bottom of a lake.

I resect now from the barn corner—I can feel beneath the scum of leaves and silt and hash the crisp square of that poured concrete, a corner that was set out true by a man whose first instinct in setting up the world was to make it square. Knowing a thing is true is not the same as knowing that a thing is square. I still have to tell clients this sometimes on the phone when they like the way their eyes make a fence look when they stand at the edge of their patio late in the afternoon full of gin. They like their own vanishing point. They do not know that this is the oldest American argument.

From the top of the steps I shoot the first of the curb points for Elm Street. Elm, Maple, Oak—naming trees we had like naming saints we had declined. There had been one elm before the blight took hands through the county, and this one had split in a storm and taken out the power to the post office the week I was born. We named the funeral home after the men who had run it and gone to Chicago with their winnings, Perkins & Sons. We named the church for the Book that had made my mother hold her chin up through every implement the state sent to plow us under and the one that let her lower it when my father died.

On the far ridge above the old bridge abutment, a tent has sprung up overnight. The canvas is new. The cross they planted in the dirt in front of it is old—the wood gray as a bone and just as intent on keeping its shape. There is a hum from a generator. Later, the murmuring will start and then the singing. I will be in the middle of the old ballfield by then, the one they did not manage to fill to level because it was always sod below three inches, and I will hear Hallelujahs that will lift like birds with their own sense of when to stop rising. People will come in their old boots and in their new ones. They will cry in the particular way of those who believe they have been noticed by a god, a government, a daughter.

I am not a believer. I have felt that thing, though—the split down the center of my chest as if someone were sliding open a tray in a desk and exposing my papers to the air. It happened when I was in my twenties, kneeling on a ridge in winter trying to set a spike into a ground that did not want it, my hands shaking not from belief but from cold. There was sleet. It was a day like this one for the clarity of sound. I looked out over what was below me—the brown lake and the slick yard and the town the water held—and I thought I could hear my father say my name in a voice pitched for a moment when he could not find me. Later I told myself that it was something to do with the ductwork in my aunt’s house in the dry winter and the rattle that low humidity gives the clutch of your jaw when you are too far from yourself. There is a thin line between the ringing of memory and the call we attach to a god. A thin line and no survey mark to make it true.

At the northeast corner of our lot—if memory is allowed to keep its authority for an hour—there had been a pecan tree my father coaxed into giving. He watered it with the dishwater in the years before they told us to move. It made a small crop the last autumn we were there, and he cracked the shells by laying them on a plank and bringing down the heel of a bottle, each blow a soft gunshot, not the kind to scare deer. That same year I climbed the tree with my friend Ruthie and we looked down through the leaves at the boys cutting donuts in a Ford borrowed from someone’s uncle. I have not seen Ruthie since the day we stood with our toes at the lip of the pit where our house had been, and our mothers both said, Okay, ready, and we said yes in the voices of those who are lying politely.

Our house was at the top of a short slope. After the dozer the slope was still there, but it sloped down to nothing. In the hole where my bed had been, the man in the hard hat in blue with a nametag that said ADAMS had left his cigarette butt. I had not known adults in uniforms would do that kind of thing. My father picked it up between thumb and forefinger and gave it back to him and then we all watched the loop of it arc across to the back of a truck bed where it lay burning like anything else living or dead. No one stepped forward. No one flinched. In the afternoon, when the air felt like a spoon full of milk held in your mouth too long, we walked out to the river and pretended we were there the way you do when something enormous is taking shape beside you. Later the papers had a picture of schoolchildren standing on a newly poured walkway above the spillway, raising their hands in half-eaten waves. In that picture I can see the line of my jaw on a girl who is not me. The town became its best self the minute we were not living in it.

Now I step into what was the rectangle of that bedroom. The ricordos are here. The remains of a nailhead on the far sill. The stippled line in the concrete that shows where my father stopped the trowel and lifted for a stretch. I stand very still. Then I give the ground the weight of me one heel, then the ball of my foot, then the other. In the corner of the room there is what I came to find. It is there. It is not worse for the water. The concrete had been new that afternoon; I was four. While my father’s back was turned I took the rabbit my mother kept tied to the back of the chair with a ribbon and I pressed his paw into the wet—quick, firm, in a line with my father’s careful trowel work. I remember the feeling of being both in violation and safe, of making a mark that would either be buffed away or else remain as a private, forever thing. I had almost convinced myself, over the last thirty years of the lake laughing at our boundaries, that I had invented this. To keep the texture. And here it is: not my handprint or his or my name written childish and backwards, but the small, closed crescent of a rabbit’s foot in a place where no rabbit ever rested. The pad of the foot took more of the cement than the toes had. There is a grace to it I had not expected. It is the grace of an animal that believes its job is to remain quiet in heat and loud in snow.

I take the prism pole and steady it there. The instrument’s bell rings a message in the tone I taught it to use; I should bring hospitality to this part of the world even if I can’t summon it for myself. The numbers do what numbers do. They come, they wait, they leave imprints on paper. I hope my hand will not shake and spoil the significance. I breathe in through my nose and out onto my top lip the way a laboring woman will or a man lifting something larger than he has any business lifting. I press the flag into the seam of the padprint, write PT 317 on the plastic with a pen that remembers all the other things I have named and numbered. The field book has a neatness that my life has not always had, and so I give it her rabbit’s foot, my father’s broom-swished sweep, the judgment of a satellite.

By noon the past has had enough of me. Or perhaps by noon I have had enough of its permission to be here. The boy at the culvert has gone up for a sandwich. His father is sitting on the burl of a root that looks like a shoulder bone. Bud from the hotel slab holds up a string of washers he has threaded onto his belt. He calls it treasure in the voice that is meant to make me understand that he is not fooled even by his own humor. Above me in the tent the first kneeling has begun. Between the parting of the tent flaps I see the lines of a woman’s skirt and the way she clutched the fabric when she sat. I carry the base unit up the slope like a lamb I am required to raise but do not want anyone to see me pet. I lock it in the truck, get a drink from the jug that turns the road dust in my mouth to a cake of clay, and then I walk back down to the slab I will tell no one in the office about, the place where the small violence of my mark once mattered to me and matters now again.

On the way I find something I am not allowed to pick up. It is a marble, blue glass with a filament of white that twists through it like a river seen from too far above to be anything but its own idea of line. It is an eye that does not reflect me. I look around like a person in danger of being thought strange and very quickly I slide it into my pocket anyway. A hand in my department at the county puts things on shelves and is pleased by their dust. She will tell me when I am back that there is a protocol for artifacts that involves a form with boxes to check. What I have is not an artifact. The form is too neat to receive it. It is the exact weight of the inside of an apple in the palm of my hand. When I was six, a girl on Maple Street pulled the wool top from a hoop-and-stick yanked all that day in the dust and gave it to me because hers had broken two days before in a game out back and she had the sense to store her grief and make of it something like generosity while mine scattered and came to nothing. I put the marble in my pocket the way I am now and later my mother found it in the wash, a sound like the first hail on a tin roof. She held it up to the kitchen light. She said, Such a pretty thing for such a hard world. Then she set it on the sill and forgot it there for a time.

When I was eleven, with the water not yet to the toes of the sycamore but advancing, Ruthie and I came down at dawn before the men started their engines. We walked along the bank and tested the width of each puddle with the long rod of a broom handle. There was a way, then, of speaking to each other that we did not have the years to learn to regret. We said What if and Later we will and Or we could without making these utterances a test. I was stubborn. I liked to know where the bottom was. I remember thinking for the first time that a day like that was the right size for living. That we could keep the day the way a rabbit keeps heat under its skin and add to it as needed. Then the water came faster than we could add the new pieces to the shape of our living. The men set explosives in the old abutment and the sound came up from the hollow differently than any other sound; it was both a boom and a rule broken. My father’s face was the face of someone who had caught his thumb with a hammer and is holding his breath until he can release it in five equal parts.

In the afternoon, cloud rose over the ridge like smoke through a sieve and moved nowhere at all, just built itself at the top of the sky. On the other side of the new dam, water ran one way: consistent, controlled, explained. On this side I could not tell what it wanted. We yanked up cattails and smacked them against the water and listened to the thunk echo across the short tongue of the river there. A boy named Calvin had a little motorboat he ran back and forth in arcs that were either practice or warning. He liked me more than I liked him and I liked him more than my father thought any man who owned a motor was entitled to be liked by anyone with a Weldon conscience. The day of the first flood into the bottommost pasture he took his boat too close to the old brush pile and snagged his prop and so his father came down to cut him loose and said a string of things that made even Ruthie close her mouth around her teeth and look at the cattail head in her hand like she had forgotten it might be used for something other than hitting. She said, “It isn’t just water,” and I said, “I know,” and we did not say what else it was and then the men shouted for us and so we loped away across a field that later would have carp in it.

Now a dragonfly sets down on the top of my prism and balances there like a coin. Its body is the blue of the marble in my pocket. It is at home here in the cold of a hard sun at noon. I am at home too in the peculiar quiet that work gives me—the way the voice that narrates other parts of my day turns down as if someone were turning it on its side to bleed the last of its air and then cork it for later. I can hear the breath through my own nostrils. I can hear the far sound of a pickup’s tires on gravel as someone decides and then decides again that a road might be a good idea, might take them exactly where they intend to go.

The tent above empties in a slow-bursting stream. People fan themselves with the programs printed on the kind of paper that picks up the oil from your palm. They walk to their cars and then stand with their hands on the doors and look down at the valley as if it were a room newly revealed behind a wall they had spent their lives thinking was solid. There is, on some faces, hunger. There is, on others, calculation. On mine there is the blankness of someone for whom the word home has been made to do more than its share of the work.

Here is a small true thing: the silt in the bottommost corner of our lot is still different from the silt at the center of Main Street. It is glossier, gluttier somehow. The particles are the little look-suppose-of-clay that belongs to the runoff from the garden that sloped exactly enough to make a child roll if she lay down in the grass crosswise and relaxed. I lift a bit of it and rub it between thumb and forefinger. It leaves a smear on my skin that will have to be washed with a brush. It goes, for a moment, dry and then slick. My bee has no flower. But here I am.

When I climb out of the valley midafternoon, my shoulders ache, and my lower back has that singing pull that tells me what thirty years of this work will look like if I let it. I stand at the lid of the world and I look back. I have always been a one-for-the-last-look. When we moved, it took three men in a chain to pull me from the doorway of the only room I has thought of as mine. My father gently gave a fist to the headboard and said, There. You will remember it that way—shaking. Memory likes motion.

On the gravel beside the truck there is an old pair of boots. They are not mine. They are caked to their knees with a thickness that would crack like a creek in winter if I were to try to put them on and flex and then regret my vanity. Someone has left them at the edge of the little patch of shade that the bed of my truck throws for a wedge of the hour, a gesture like that of the poor box at the back of the church where I learn the size of the sins my neighbors privately are dealing in by the care they take to slide the folded money in noiselessly rather than slap it in like a dare to the deacons. I pick the boots up and set them in the bed of the truck next to my hard cases and my peace of false authority, the vest with the reflective tape. On the inside of the left boot someone has written LUCK in old pen. It is an ugly hand. There is no irony in it at all. I close the tailgate. A man I do not know in a hat that makes his head look like a walnut except not as handsome smiles at me out of the corner of his mouth and lifts his chin. Everything I do is seen and everything I do is private. This is the same rule that governs confession and hunting.

When I go home that night to the little square of a rented house I have kept as spare as I can so that I might forget each thing the way you try to forget the first syllable of a man’s name who hurt you, I empty my pockets on the table by the door. I set the blue marble on the plate where my keys lie as if it were a thing that holds the keys down when the air comes through the house too hard in spring. I wash the clay from under my nails with the brush and watch it spin in the sink across the white porcelain like smoke cut down to earth. I think of my mother, who did not live to see a lake too low to carry its name in comfort. I think of my father, who was not allowed to watch his rabbit set its foot in all the rooms he marked out for us. I pull the field book from the case. I set it on the table. I do not open it. I sit on the floor and lean my back against the lowest step and let the ache come in waves like the signal from a machine that has paired and unpaired all day and is only now telling me the strength with which it intends to keep me.

The next morning I carry my pack to the window and look at the corner of the street where somebody last month drew with chalk a hopscotch grid that did not have the integrity to sit either true or square. The numbers are gone to foot and first rain. There is the ghost of them. My neighbor, a woman with many bracelets and a predilection for moving her furniture late at night, smokes in her robe, and when she sees me see her she lifts two fingers and lets smoke come out of her nostrils the way it does from a horse in fall when the child who loves it wraps her arms around its neck in the first cold that wakes the lungs up. And because she looks softer inside that smoke, I lift my hand too, and we eye each other with a courtesy we have not troubled to learn.

Three days into my walking the ruins, my cousin Dale drives in from his farm and stops at the lip of the destroyed fairway and calls me up with his hand. We were small together. His mother died early and his father married the woman who taught me to play piano against the untidy sadness of a Baptist church where the red carpet was always trying to be not red. He looks at the lake as if he wanted to spit but remembered at the last second that the spit would not fall true.

“It’s all right,” he says like a man making a bargain with someone who cannot see that there is a bargain to be made. “Seeing it. It’s not all right. But it is.”

“I see it,” I say.

“They going to put up new houses?” he asks practically. He is not the sort of man who asks idle questions. He knows what the county can do. But he likes me and he wants to see whether I will make the county stand in its own suits.

“No,” I say. “They want to know the roads just in case. Just in case there’s a fire in the scrub and they have to bring the brush truck down to cut it off, or in case we get a body washing up in a way that doesn’t look like drowning and they want to be able to say how to get that out of there without making a mess of what isn’t their mess.”

He grimaces. “I dreamed of the yellow house the other night,” he says. “The one that had the swing set, that I wasn’t allowed to get on because Mr. Shroyer didn’t like the way I laughed. I dreamed the yellow had run out down the side in a syrup.”

“It always did,” I say.

He looks at my face and nods once. “You’re still in there,” he says, and he waves, and then he is gone in the way men like him are gone, leaving the air behind them chewed up and then flattened by the iron of the road.

On the fifth day I find the remains of a picnic bench on the point where the river’s oldest bend pushes up its elbow. I had forgotten this bench. It is one of those things small enough to be lost by any map, built by a man with a sense of the shape of simple joy and a plank. Parents set pies there and did not ask the flies to leave because they knew how the flies know when a thing is enough. I put the prism pole down and I lower myself and I sit. The bench groans. My weight is not a surprise. It has been taking the weight of water for sixty years. It will take one more thing. The breeze comes off the lake though there is no lake close enough to lift a cool the way a mother takes a sheet from the wash and shakes it before she sends it to the line. I taste salt that is not from the sea and not from tears; it is salt like the iron taste of a hand on a rail that has cooled itself down overnight. I close my eyes. Noise rises from the highway on the far shore like a low hymn. A crow sits on the crossbar of the yield sign that has made itself into a permanent cross and tells the world he is not afraid to be the only one here who says exactly what he means.

On what ends up being the last afternoon—because the water starts to come back, because releases downstream change and it rains once on the other side of the ridge like a mutter in sleep, because our superintendent needs me on a road that slid in the north of the county and because Bud packed up his thermos and quit coming and I believe in a kind of private weather and read it where I find it—I plant my last flag. I put it at the place where my father must have had the patient idea to put his chair in the evening when the light did that thing the air does in late summer when it has been made of grit for a week and then someone whispers through it and all at once everything is visible inside it without being harsh. In my mind my father is there. He has always been there. It is the only stillness I allow him. He looks toward the road. He is waiting for a car that is not ours and will never be ours. He holds a glass of tea. He has made a dent in his cheek with the inside of his tooth because he is thinking something he will not tell me. My mother is inside with a pencil in the place where she keeps her lists. On the list there is something like buy flour and something like remember father’s hat.

I push the flag down into the seam in the slab and I stand. I do not kneel, though I should. I do not take a photograph, though I am not above that kind of thing in other lives. I do not take any dirt. I do not say anything. The bats in the creases of the culvert come out and turn the air over as if burrowing. My radio beeps. It says I am out of range though of course I am, have been, will always be, out of range. I take the cap from the pole. I fold the tripod. I sling the case. I make the walk uphill with the feeling in my legs I had on that last night, when everyone emptied their guns into the invisible and the dogs hollered because the noise had nothing behind it.

At the truck, a woman I do not know but who knows me in the way that people in counties know where somebody came from asks whether I found what I was looking for. She is young enough to have the new phone that makes a person see their own face at all times like a lover and old enough to have made a child who comes out from behind her like an apology.

“I did,” I say.

“Was it in the ground?” she asks, in the sharp voice of those who have learned that the ground is not where their life will be decided.

“It was,” I say. “And it wasn’t.”

She laughs with her hand to her mouth. She has rings like lessons all the way up to the middle of her fingers. The child looks out from under her arm at me the way a rabbit does from a pucker in the brush.

“What are those?” the child asks, pointing at my flags in a cup in the back of the truck.

“Marks,” I say. “They tell me where something is when I cannot carry it.”

“Will the water eat them?” he asks.

“Yes,” I say, because it is good to tell the truth while the light is still kind. “But they will stay where they are in one way and they will move all over in another. There are many maps,” I say to the boy, though I am thinking this for myself. “Some of them are wet. Some of them are inside your chest. Some of them you can lose and then find.”

We watch as the first rivulets reach down between the plates of silt and return a sheen to what had gone to skin. It is not dramatic. It is not what movies have done to us, to expect sound and a forced running and foam. It is small and the kind of thing that will keep doing itself whether you clap for it or not. Across the valley a man in a white shirt carries a tin basin and he sets it on the old square of steps and he cups water into it with his hand and he looks at his hand as if it is something brand-new. Bud’s place by the hotel slab is empty. The cross above the tent is an X against the sky. The dragonfly cannot find my prism and so finds something else. The child reaches for a flag and the mother says his name in the narrow way that mothers learn when they are given children with hands.

I drive home. On the seat beside me the field book turns a corner and slides into the well between the seats. I let it. The road up is the same road we pushed our wagon up years ago when moving felt like a game you could make into anything if you set your mind to it, like a girl sitting a chair across the room and saying, That is my river and when I count to three you’ll all know which way it is going. Somewhere behind me, under a length of water no longer as flattering as it once was, a small prick of plastic with my handwriting on it stays in its place and intends to. That intention comforts me. It chides me too. I have not been square in all the departments of my life. I have pretended there are patterns when there are just rabbits. And when I get to my door and I lay down my keys and the marble stares up at me, blue with its twist of what is not sky and not vein and not anything more than the path a little air bubble wanted to take getting out, I take it in my hand with the seriousness of a woman testing a spoon in winter on the first soup of the season. I turn it until a line appears, and then I draw a map that has no boxes to check, no codes to classify, nothing that can be filed in triplicate or tax-coded or called into question by a man with a hat and an instrument or by my adult self who fell in love with those hats and those instruments and with the work of measuring the unpersuadable. I put the marble in the kitchen windowsill. The paper with my points on it I fold and refold until it has a wetness to it and a weak spot that will give the next time I take it in my hand. I set the folded paper under the rabbit that sits with one foot folded under him on the shelf as if he has only just stepped into wet cement and is waiting for me to say, That is enough now. Thank you. And when I sleep, I dream a dream without water, not because it has left me but because it is everywhere. In the dream I walk through a house I know. When I press my foot to the floor, the floor holds, and when I look at my hand it is stained with something that is the color of the day, which is to say the color of whatever has me in it. I wake and, for a long time after, cannot hear my radio at all.
