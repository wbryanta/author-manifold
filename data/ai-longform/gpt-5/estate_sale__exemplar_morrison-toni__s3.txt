I don't have to see the faces to know the hands. They come in wearing second-Saturday smiles and weekday shoes, palms already measuring what they have not yet touched, their fingers practicing the geometry of want. They step off Jubilee Street into my mother's narrow hall and dissolve into rooms whose windows have known seventy winters. They pause on the rug she braided herself, look up the staircase where she called us one by one, and then whatever sacrosanct feeling brushed their necks is traded in for a wet pencil and a paper list.

I stand by the front window because that is where the money box rests, and also because from there I can see where the lilac used to be. Vera May rooted that shrub the day my father left, said no sense leaving a spot bare because some man got it into his head he was sky. She called it the lilac of spite and got more bloom than any neighbor on the block until late May when the frost took its sweetness away. It never did die though. It would gray itself into a hard knot and then, stubborn and uncorrected, put up green again. She’s gone and that bush is a stump now, but the root under the dirt still holds, I can feel it. If I put my heel against the threshold and bend, I swear Jubilee Street tilts toward that root, like the whole town leans on what it can’t name.

“Excuse me, ma’am? How much for this set? The salt and peppers?” A woman thin as a pencil and sharp as the business end is holding my mother’s blue birds between her thumb and finger as if they ain’t glass but language. The words “not for sale” almost set up home in my mouth. Trouble is, everything is for sale today and has the tag to prove it. Vera insisted on tags even when she was selling pies at the fair. Tag it and it won’t tag you, she would say, a girl can hide behind a price.

“Ten for the pair,” I tell Pencil Woman. “Twelve if you want the matching tray.” I don’t say that the tray has a crack beginning at the corner because I am my mother’s child and I charge, then I confess.

She turns the birds over, tapping light with a fake nail, and says, “They’re cute. They’ll go with my spring platter.” She says cute like she’s pinning a tail on a donkey and missing, catching its flank. The man with her—her husband or brother or brother-in-law, these men with collars always look like someone’s relation you can’t stand—takes out a wallet fat with coupons and receipts and pulls a ten like he’s giving a tip in a church that ain't his. Money in the box sounds like a cough. I close the lid soft because the hinge is loose and because the bedroom down the hall still smells like wool and Vicks.

“Do you do Venmo, sweetheart?” a girl with a flower tattoo asks the air and then me. Her hair is white not from sorrow but from salon, and she fingers the handles of the aluminum pot like she wants to make soup right here and right now.

“Cash only today,” I say. “Or personal check if I know you.”

“You don’t know me,” she says without apology, smiling, “but my nana used to fry doughnuts in a pot just like this. Every Sunday after mass, we’d have two each and lick sugar off our wrists. She died in October. I only want the pot, not your memories.” She shrugs quick, like laughter is a rash she’s trying not to scratch, and counts off fives into my palm. I want to tell her to watch the pot’s belly, because it heats uneven. I want to say there is a dent where Vera May slammed the lid because my brother Tom came home smelling like someone else’s car. I want to say you cannot fry grief, but you can make it sizzle if you stand there long enough. I put the money in the box and nod. She hugs the pot to her chest like an infant and brushes through the door into the shock of white day.

They keep coming. The bell above the door was a wind chime once, but Vera took it down the year the Smith boy stole pumpkins and put them in our mailboxes. She said she was done letting cheap wind tell her what kind of day she was walking into. I put a bell back, then took it off because it was too cheerful, too Christmas. Now the door groans and then closes with a little exhausted whistle that is truer. The house is loud and careful like an old person crossing a floor he built himself. People murmur, shuffle, exclaim. They slide drawers out and put them back with a bang that means they forgot weight. They run fingers over the velvet chair arms, the plastic slipcovers long since taken off because my mother, in her last months, said she didn’t have to pretend to own nice things anymore when what she owned was the chair of her own body, and it was busted.

“Alma? Lord, girl.” Head cocked into the sun, Miss Collier leans on the newel post like it is a man who knows secrets. She lives three doors down and eats nothing that ever walked according to her own testimony, though I have seen her at Ryan's with a lamb chop right there on her plate. “You holding up, baby?” She says baby the way old women do when they want to give you something that you will refuse.

“I’m here,” I say. “I’ll be here till three.” I look at my watch though I don’t need to. The hand has moved from noon to one quick as forgiveness. Miss Collier nods like I spoke scripture. Her eyes slide past me into the living room and catch on the piano. You can feel when a person’s eyes do that; your body knows when someone has spotted a thing it labored to keep hidden or clean.

“How much for that upright?” She puts her palms together, not in prayer exactly. In measurement.

I think about the piano. In third grade I practiced scales on it in the afternoon when nobody yet knew I would have a daughter that sings like a bird that forgot its cage. I think of Vera slamming the cover shut not on my fingers but on an argument the year we could not afford lessons, and the teacher said he would take pie and ironing in payment and she looked at him until he stepped off her porch. I think of the way a man once sat at that piano during a party, a man named Earl who wore a hat in the house because his hair was a rumor. He played Sunday songs with Saturday music in them, and I was fifteen and tasted clove gum in the room without putting anything in my mouth. I think of the sticker in the back that says Made in Ohio and means nothing. I picture the moving of it: two men and a dolly and a ramp, and the way the house will hold its breath when it slides through the door that was measured wrong twice, the way the porch will buckle its tongue but not speak.

“It don’t play true,” I tell her.

“None of us do,” she says back, quick as she was in her fifties, and then she laughs at herself and looks ashamed of something I cannot name. “Well. My granddaughter… you know she picked up that tune, what’s it, the same five notes that get on your nerve and the radio tries to make you like it? She got it off YouTube with no teacher. I thought, maybe, you know. An old one might have more… more keep to it.”

“Four hundred,” I say, too high. If I price it dear, maybe it will want to stay of its own accord.

She flinches like a wing. “Lord, Alma. That piano ain’t worth four hundred tales, let alone dollars.” She puts a finger to the keys, just one, her nail pink and rubbed, and middle C says something without vowels. “Two-fifty?”

“Three,” I say, and face the kitchen so she can’t see my mouth argue with my numbers. I have been bargaining all my life against tides women cannot lower.

We will end at two seventy-five and both think we cheated the other.

A boy in a red hoodie is running his hand along the banister. He’s so light on his feet that the steps hardly acknowledge him. He looks back at me and then up, checking for what kind of adult I will be if he jumps the last three. I shake my head once and he shifts his weight in that way boys do that is both a promise and a dare. He slides down instead, the wood singing under his jeans, and lands like he was born to. He grins at me, is all teeth and new hope, and I remember a boy that used to visit—not mine but the church’s—and how he would come over after school when the gospel choir practiced because we were the only safe house on a block that had a man who liked to put his kindness in his pockets and take it out when you were tired. That boy sat on my porch and counted cars, making up names for them, a game that saved him till he left for some other trouble.

By the pantry door, there is a woman with a bottle of oil. She opens it and the room leans forward. Lemon. Kerosene lemon that rides foul air like a preacher on a mule. She holds it up like communion. “I thought they didn’t make this no more,” she says, to no one and everyone. “Haven’t smelled last week Tuesday since ’97.” She catches herself and sets the bottle down careful like it’s a baby with a fever. “How much?”

“A quarter,” I say before the price tag attached to its neck can shame me. Curled there in my mother’s handwriting, a 5 shows and an 0 blinks. Fifty cents. I swallow a correction, accept the dent the quarter makes against my thigh in my pocket. My mother’s letters are everywhere, still wagging their bottoms, flirty as Mayflies. She wrote like a woman without time, everything hitched together. From the inside of the kitchen cabinet a recipe for cider cake peels away from the wood like a decal with regret still in it. On the back of a picture frame it says, in pencil you cannot bleach, “Do not let Alma give this to Tom.” On the underside of a lamp: “dust me.” On my brother’s baseball trophy: “mine.” Possession was her grammar; it comforted her, that and closing doors.

I liked to open things. That was my sin or my praise, depends on which year we are narrating. At ten, I broke the skin on a can of peaches with my teeth because the opener slipped and mama’s temper slipped with it, and I wanted to finish something sweet. At twenty-one, I opened my legs for a man who had a smile like an apology you can’t drink, and out of it came Lark, and out of Lark came songs nobody pays for anymore. At thirty, I opened my hand and let go Tom, who was holding nothing anyway but claimed he had handfuls. At forty-eight, I opened the door to a debt collector and told him he had to come back because my mother needed the bed for dying. She heard me in the other room, and she laughed. Bent over with it. “Bed for dying,” she said. “What kind of bed you think you been sleeping in all your long life?”

“Alma,” someone says. “What about this?” A stack of bridal magazines nobody in our house ever should have had—Vera kept them to mock, flipping quick past the glossy pictures and reading aloud the prices in the voice she saved for death—sits next to the trash can. A cousin of a cousin is asking me if there is a price or a fire for them. She is red in the face from looking upstairs, where the room under the eave still has a slant that births caution. “They got this cake,” she goes on, flipping the top one open, “with sugared roses like they done froze springtime. I might like to try something like that for me and Darren. You know, once his knees act right.”

“You and Darren been engaged since Bill Clinton,” I say. She flips to the back, where advertisements give you the lie up front. She is family and so we trade bald truths like they are dimes. She laughs until she remembers she is supposed to be tender with me, and then she presses a hand to my arm and says, “If you need me, you call. Even if it’s the middle of the night.” She means she wants to be woke by a purpose. People like offers that show them they are not furniture.

Through this all, the house keeps its own conversation. The floorboards click in the hall where the same nail rose up every winter and threatened to take a toe. The stove breathes. The dining room table, minus its leaves that we could never store right and lost in the basement to mildew and reluctant spiders, pushes its legs into the carpet grooves like a woman dissatisfied with where she’s put. The big brown dresser, older than our blood in this place, watches the front door with dignity getting tired. When they finally carry it out at one-thirty, two men and a woman in cargo pants chasing their own shadows under their feet, it resists the turn at the corner, presses a shoulder to the doorway like a boy who decided to grow ahead of being allowed to. Then it just gives. Slides out like it knows what resignation can do to a body. On the top drawer we kids etched our small tallies, one for each inch we had over the year, our names wrong and proud. I run my thumb over the place my R used to look like a J because my hand didn’t trust me. My mother let us mark the dresser because she was a gardener of contained wildness. She said paint could cover sin but wood remembered.

“Wait,” I tell the moving woman with the cargo pants when they make it to the porch, my voice coming from somewhere not me. “Wait a second.” I take a breath at the threshold. You never know how high a door is until something you care for can’t fit through. The sun is white today because the air is cold, a liar’s sun that tells you summer is a rumor if your coat is thin. Birds make a noise not for us. Cars glide by Jubilee like sin is a different town. I put my palm on the dresser brash as a child. The walnut is warm. It is the only thing in my whole life that was always more than one person lifted, that insisted on being a we. “Be good,” I tell it like a fool, like a woman who still thinks she can coach wood. The woman with the pants looks at me like I’m a kind of charity she doesn’t have to donate to, like she’s decided to be tender and call it a tip.

“You want to keep the drawer liners, ma’am?” she asks, professional crime in her voice.

I shake my head. Those are for other noses now.

Behind me in the hall, a girl is humming. I know that hum. It is my mother’s cough turned into hope. A small thing wearing a T-shirt with a cartoon cat on it is sitting on the bottom step with my mother’s scarf wrapped around her shoulders like she is playing winter. The scarf is purple and full of somebody’s perfume, not my mother’s. My mother’s scent was boxwood and pepper and little sweet darkness from under her left ear. The scarf might have belonged to a church lady who left it once, and my mother put it in the closet and forgot it wasn’t hers. That was her other way. If it sat long enough in her house, she would bless it as property, then keep it with the righteousness of a baptized thief.

“Where is your person?” I ask the girl.

She points at a woman at the mantle counting money. The woman has a denim jacket with the words Blessed Mess sewn on the back. She is doing arithmetic like it owes her money.

“Can I have this?” the girl asks, pressing the scarf to her chin, bringing it up to inhale like a doctor with ether.

“It’s two dollars,” I tell her. Her forehead makes a little moon with ridges. She looks like she is trying to summon the number into the palm of her hand by rubbing it. I wait. She fishes a handful of coins from the pocket of her too-big jeans, pennies that still carry births and wars on their faces, a nickel that thinks it's more than it is, two dimes that shine with the relief of having been missed. I count with her. We are short by one dime.

“Baby,” I call to Blessed Mess. “Your girl needs a dime for the purple.”

The woman looks up without apology. “She already got one of those. Girl got a drawer of scarves, no job to buy them, just fools like me and men who like hats.”

I remember the drawer I had then. It had not scarves but folded-up letters and a Bible under them in case the letters got too smart. “She got a new closet to put it in,” I say. My voice picks up a little muscle that isn’t mine, the kind women get when they speak for somebody else in a precinct. The woman looks at me and decides which kind of woman I am. She reaches into her pocket and then pauses. Her eyes take me in, my coat in this cold, the thinness of my earrings, the soft shoe I am wearing because my back won’t argue with reason, not anymore. “Here,” she says at last to the girl, not to me, and puts not a dime but a dollar in the small hand. The child does not smile like she expected it. She pays me exact and then drapes the purple like she knows her neck is a promise.

“What you saving for?” I ask her because sometimes when you don’t ask, a child will spend the whole day begging inside their chest and nobody will know. She looks at me with that clear false frankness children put on when they practiced something in the bathroom mirror and it did not come out right in the open air.

“A rabbit,” she says.

“Get two,” I tell her. “They don’t know how to be alone.”

Down the hall I catch the light off the frame of my mother’s high school graduation photo. She is in cap and gown, all shining twenty, standing on the side lot of the school because colored honor wore their best outdoors where they didn’t trouble the trophies inside. Her mouth is cropped tight into something like honesty. Her eyes hold no one’s hand. The day she died, she was the same thing: a girl unafraid of bleeding in public. They said her heart gave out on a Tuesday like it was a gift she unwrapped too soon. She had given that heart to us too many times to ask for it back; the final refusal surprised nobody but her. “What am I supposed to do with all this air?” she said the last time she sat in her rocking chair, the one with the foot like a limp. “People so much more gone than I am already been took. Seems like a waste to breathe past them.” I told her that air is the only mercy we have no receipt for. She rolled her eyes and told me I talked like a book, and not even a book that paid its own rent.

They carry the sofa out at two, lifting from the bottom like a reputation, and the robe my mother bought at Penney’s slips from a pile in the corner to the floor so soft you would think it was doing us a favor. A boy in a hoodie bends to get it and then holds it at arm’s length because it has the imprint of a woman, something like religion and bread. The robe is pink, but not a weak pink. A survivor’s pink. It has a belt that went lost under the couch the year I moved out and then came back because my daughter came too and my mother said, “One more girl is not a victory, it is a war you got to keep fighting.” I wash my hand along the robe’s sleeve and find, deep in the cotton, a thread my mother worried whenever she was worried. I don’t claim it. Let it go into some life where money is different.

“Alma?” Pastor Lyons, who is a man you want to like less than you do, stands by the doorway to the small room we called the study though nobody in this house ever studied anything they couldn’t cook. He has the Bible under his palm, the big one with our births and our debts scribbled inside the flyleaf. It is not tagged. Tag it and then unlock it, my mother would say about the family book. If you don’t name it, thieves have to be tricked.

“You know that ain’t for sale,” I say, because some things you do not betray even when they done betrayed you.

He tilts his head like a dog taught a too-fancy word. “We thought,” he starts, and it is already too much. “The church,” he says instead, safer by habit, “would tend to it, make sure nobody… you know… just toss it or, God forbid, gives it to the Goodwill. Keep the record intact.” He slides his patty-soft hands across the leather like it’s a woman's thigh. I notice how far down the cover his fingers shine. He has done this before to other people's books.

“I’m going to keep the record intact,” I tell him, then because I am my mother’s daughter and she taught truth like a marinade, I say, “I haven’t yet decided what intact means. Maybe it means nobody else reading who my father isn’t.”

He pulls back from the leather like it burned him. Men in collars love a secret they did not have to earn. He adjusts his jacket like it is a lie he’ll get caught in. “Of course, of course,” he says, and steps back so a white man with a beard that has food and dinner’s nephew in it can carry my mother’s ironing board out the door. The man beelines to me with his empty face and asks if the irons come as a set, two with cords wrapped around their throats. I tell him the set is whatever he can lift without crying, and he laughs, relieved I am joking, relieved to think nothing gets stuck in you while you are being funny.

“Did your mama sew?” asks a thin girl with eyes like a cat who got into a bowl of cream and denies it. She is holding buttons. Big plastic ones. Buttons that would close a coat on a baby built like a wish.

“She pinned,” I say. “Pinned things to fit. Said sewing was for people who believed clothes could make sense.” That was my mother. Take it in at the waist for God and let the hem drag for men to trip on. She could fix a cuff with her hand shut.

The girl sighs in a way that tells me she plans on being tender to a needle in a life where nothing else is sharp. “I lost my nana last year,” she offers. This is how people do it; they leave the word loss unsupervised and expect you to leash it for them. “She had this jar of buttons. Every time something broke, she would pull that jar down and tell stories till the shirt was fixed. This one came off your great-uncle’s coat when he left for Detroit, and here’s the one your Aunt Poppy swallowed on a dare. She was a mess.” The girl laughs and then wipes it away like it was a bug that bit. “I ain’t got her jar. My cousin took it. But I got a feeling I can make my own out of your mama’s.”

“Take them,” I say, and when she counts out the pennies my mother tagged, I push them back. “They’re yours if you promise not to match them right.”

She smiles like a cat that found out it could purr without permission, and I don’t tell her that somebody’s always going to ask her what she plans to fix with those buttons and she better have an answer that don’t cost her dignity.

In the kitchen, a man stands in my mother’s light. He is holding the teakettle like it’s unfamiliar with water. He might be Tom with twenty years more on him and two teeth less. He might be a man who showed up to check the gutters and found out grief sat better on him than work. He stares at the stove burners with a voodoo seriousness. I ask if he is heating anything.

“No,” he says. He turns the kettle in his hands like he is looking for a switch that is not time. “Just remembering the sound of boil. Last time I heard my mother’s water sing was the day she forgot the pot on. Burned the back of it black as night. She scrubbed and scrubbed and couldn’t get it off. Sat down finally and said, maybe it’s a sign we got to stop drinking all these teas to fix what can't be fixed.” He chuckles and coughs in the same breath. “She was stubborn as a dish. An unbreakable one.”

“She died in Ohio?” I ask.

“She died here.” He puts the kettle down, too fast. “Seemed a foolish place to die. Nothing but sky. But she liked this street. People say hello. Dogs go on their way, minding they own business. One time I seen a hawk pick a squirrel right off that maple and all I could think was, not on my watch, but then I realized it was none of my watch. I was just borrowed eyes in a house that had held more than me.”

I want to tell him this house has watched some strange hawks. That what people carry in their mouths ain’t always food or song but something with teeth. I want to say that my mother raised us on things she had to steal back from a world that pretended not to have took them first. I say, “You want the kettle?”

He shrugs. “Don’t know.” He looks around as if waiting for a woman to tell him what to take and what to leave because that is how he’s always done it. “How much?”

“Three,” I say, because I like him. I think about giving it to him for nothing, then remember I have to pay the man who cut the grass so it would look like we cared for the picture the realtor will take soon as the last chair is out. We got to care past funerals or else the county gets in your bed.

At two-thirty, my cousin Glory shows. Her name is gospel and her voice is not. She does not step over thresholds like a person invited. The stair loses its nerve under her heel and says so. She walks in not like she owns the place but like she is rented to it, and the lease is almost up. She looks like every woman in our family looks when there is money to be counted. She frowns at the china and at the couch and at me like each of us has wronged some law she learned in high school and kept in her wallet.

“I thought you might decide to just give everything to Jesus,” she says, mean soft. Her hands perch on her hips like sparrows that don’t mind cats. “Find you far down at the church, handing the furniture out the back of a truck to any fool knows how to call you sister.”

“Hello, Glory,” I say.

She leans near so nobody but me can hear. “You better not be forgetting the ring.” She means my mother’s mother’s ring, the one with a stone we have fought about at every funeral since 1978. “Mama said it was supposed to go to the eldest girl born with a cry at midnight.”

“I was born in the morning,” I tell her. “Maybe you can have it.”

She opens her mouth, then closes it like the word got snagged on a tooth. “Don’t play stupid,” she says. “You know what I mean.” She nods toward the upstairs, where the small cedar chest sits under the dormer window like a child punished. “She put it in the box.”

“Then get it,” I say. “Open it. If it jumps to you, I won’t chase.” I am proud of the way my voice doesn’t break. It’s a parlor trick. Inside I am eggshell. Eggs everywhere, tipsy bright.

She sniffs and bends her body over the banister to see who is coming and who is going. Her hair is big and new and hers. She has the kind of beauty that gets brittle at forty and then, if it’s lucky, softens at fifty-five like bread when you wrap it in a damp towel and set it on the counter thinking you have performed a miracle for company. I don’t know if she is yet at the damp towel stage of herself. She kisses the air next to my cheek and whispers, “Tom called. He said to tell you he ain’t coming.” She looks pleased with the task of hitting me with his absence.

“Tom ain’t come even when he came,” I say.

This makes her laugh a little, mean as she is. She laughs and then bites it down into something like respect we can both hold two seconds.

At quarter till three, when everything has either a price or a memory on it and the crowd is now the kind of small family you didn’t invite but would feed anyway, I go upstairs to check on the chest. The stair knows me better than the ground in my mother’s garden knew her. It puts its hand under my foot. The light up here is a different religion. Yellow and patient. It shows the scuffs on the wall where we made height marks without a pencil because we were walls ourselves, because we thought bodies left a mark whether they wanted to or not. The chest sits under the window the way a good dog sits when nobody told it to. It is cedar but smells like powder because my mother put her virtue in everything and then put powder on top like a lid. The lock is a joke; any hairpin could pick it, but we never did. We were obedient, even when it cost us laughter that would have helped.

I lift the lid. The smell is the past, rehearsed and smug. Inside are envelopes tied with twine that has dried itself to hate. There is a lace handkerchief I have never seen her use—it would have been foolish in this house like a wedding gown at a baptism. A picture of my father’s left ear because he didn’t want to be documented and thought the Lord couldn’t judge a part of a man. A ring she never wore because the skin on her finger had more strength than a circle; a man gave it to her and then left and then came back and then died and then came back to her in the form of a bill we paid with pie. At the very bottom there is a cloth bag with a drawstring. My mother wrote on the outside and then crossed it out. She started to put “Vera’s—you touch and you die” and then crossed out everything but her name, which she underlined and then dotted with a heart like a liar.

Inside the bag is a bundle of hair. It is too much to be from a brush. It is the color of walnuts and December river. I lift it without touching it, because grief does not like oils and it will slip. I think at first it is a cutting from a baby because what else do women save when there has never been money or pearls? I sit on the bed and hold the bag like it knows cuss words. The note stuck to the string says “for the day I forget.” It is in that hurried public hand she wore at work when the bosses were standing over her pretending to speak nice.

For the day I forget. Did that day come? Was she saving some part of herself for the emptiness and then had no one to bring it to her? Did she think I would lay this hair on her cheek and she would say, “Oh yes, I was ten and had the flu and my mother cut my braid to bring my fever down”? Or would it be something else. Would it be mine. Would it be the hair I shaved off in anger the day Tom left and a man told me he could love me better bald because then I would be beautiful like a penitent? Did my mother collect my hair like a tax and keep it against me the way she kept my lies: tender and ready?

There is a letter under the bag. It is in a hand not hers. Sharp upstrokes like a man pricked himself with truth and it bled onto the page. I slide it out. The paper fought air; it crunches. It says:

V, you must let this go. Alma is not built for the tightness you keep putting her in. She is like me that way and I am sorry every day for how I did you same. You are good as bricks and that will keep a house up but it will also keep a girl out if you stack them wrong. I don’t know if I am leaving tomorrow or a year ago. I don’t know how to tell you right. No man taught me to build so I only ever learned how to run. You can keep this letter or you can keep the hair. Either way you can tell yourself a story that makes it not your fault when it was always mine. Don’t hold the girl like you hold your tongue. I loved you. I liked you. That is two different rivers that sometimes meet and sometimes flood. If I come back I will come as a sparrow and you will be mad you fed me.

My chest tightens and loosens like a hand doing dishes. I am both angry with and loyal to the dead. It is a difficult marriage. I put the letter back under the hair not because I agree with its idea of me but because I am tired of messages arriving too late. I close the bag; the drawstring resists and then obeys. I put it back in the chest and leave the ring out. I hold it to the light. It does not appraise me. It is too old for that. It says: you are the finger I should have sat on. It says: I know more rooms than you do. I put it in my pocket not for me and not for Glory and not for fairness either. For the middle of the night when something will ask me a question and there will be no language around.

When I come down the stairs, Pastor Lyons is talking to Miss Collier about tithes in the way that men talk to women when they know the women will give or not give regardless. The girl with the scarf is asleep on the step, face pressed into that purple like a bee that wanted just this one flower. Her mother is at the sideboard counting out fives and then subtracting them and then counting them again. She looks at her child and then at me, and I know right there she is both planning to leave and planning to stay and the plan she chooses will not be the one she tells later.

“Three o’clock,” I say to the room, my voice a bell I decided not to hang. The last regulars look at their watches like time used to write them checks. A man in a bicyclist’s hat holds a baseball glove in his hand and asks me how much and then says, “Never mind, keep it.” He sets it back down like a hurt dog.

They begin to trickle, which is the only way we ever leave anything. The air picks itself up and shakes out the bodies that have been wearing it. The rooms are different animals bare. The living room is a deer that knows it has been seen. The kitchen is a horse without its harness, surprised that it can. The hall is a question. The bathroom looks like it stayed too long at a party and realized nobody wanted it but the mirror. The house waits to see if the stage is over.

I sit in my mother’s chair, the one with the thread loosened by her finger, and I watch dust do what it always does when nobody is telling it otherwise: make a universe. There is a mosquito hawk bumping itself to sense in the corner.

“Alma,” says Glory, her voice not mean now. “Did you find it?” She is a child who wants to be told Santa is cruel and efficient.

“I found hair,” I tell her. “And a letter from a man you wouldn’t like.” I show her the ring in my palm and she takes it, weighing. She slides it on and it stops halfway up her finger, a memory of fat. She pulls it off and hands it back with a satisfaction that surprises even her. “Don’t you want it?” I ask.

“I want the story, not the thing,” she says. “Anyway, I never liked that color.” We both laugh because the stone is yellow and not much of a stone at all. She leans on the arm of my chair where the wood is smooth from all of us. “You all right?”

“I don’t know what that is,” I say. “People ask like it has a measurement. As if you can hold a stick to grief and say, well, you are off by three inches.”

“You talking like a book again,” she says, smiling her short smile. “Mama would say, you better put them words to work. All that pile of syllables and not a one of them knows how to stir a pot.”

“Mama also said words are cheap until you bury them,” I say.

We are quiet long enough to hear a neighbor’s laugh leak through the hedge. A truck upshifts at the corner. Somebody cries out not in pain but in effort, and I remember men lifting the piano. “Where’d that piano go?” I ask.

“Collier’s taking it,” Glory says. She sniffs. “She got no business with nothing with notes in it. That woman sings like God owed her a favor and didn’t pay.”

I picture Miss Collier’s granddaughter, fingers on the keys like finding a way out. You can want something for somebody you don’t like. You can want something for them so bad it shakes your bones and then go home and tell your own daughter none of it is her business.

Lark calls right then as if my thinking pulled her number through the wall. My phone rings and her name lights up, and Glory rolls her eyes in that way that says the next generation thinks technology can save them from funerals.

“Hey, baby,” I say, the word turning itself into something round.

“Hey, Ma,” she says in her singer’s soft, a voice that knows how to lay itself on somebody else’s heard and not hurt it. “How’s it going over there?”

“Everything’s gone,” I say, and it sounds like a joke I am not making.

She hums an understanding I don’t deserve. “You keep what you needed?”

“I kept the book,” I say. I don’t say which book. She knows. “And the measuring cup with the crack.”

“You don’t bake like Nana,” she teases me because she thinks it will lighten something. It does. For a breath. “You want me to come tonight?”

“If you come,” I say, “you’ll just be leaving.” I don’t want her to see me emptying, and I don’t want her to see the house in its slip. “Tomorrow,” I say, and I mean it. “Bring that man,” I say of the boy who is not a boy, the one with the hands that make sense out of at least three instruments and one grief. “We’ll eat on the floor. We’ll tell her stories like she’s upstairs in her bed pretending to sleep.”

“She loved you,” Lark says out of nowhere. She says it like a rehearsal, like she has practiced not rewording it into an apology. “She loved you so hard she couldn’t forgive you for parts of you you didn’t pick. That’s what I think.”

I hold the phone from my face a second because love is the only human affliction that doesn’t need air to spread. “You’re showing off with your wisdom,” I want to say. I say instead, “Sing a song to the house for me.”

She laughs. “On the phone?”

“In your throat. In the air,” I say. “Sing something that tells walls we noticed.”

She hums that same hum I heard earlier, my mother’s cough softened. Then she sings. Not words. A tune that used to live in the kitchen. It finds the corner and then the window and then the stair. It rubs its back against each room, leaving a purr. When she stops, I can hear my own swallow.

“Okay,” I tell her. “Okay.” We hang up like we’re closing a door not because we want to but because the street is loud.

Glory kisses my hair. It is an old kiss, thin as air. “You call me if the house starts talking,” she says, meaning if the night sets its jaw and asks for blood. She gathers her too-big purse and her complaints and leaves, closing the door in that way of hers that makes even wood feel corrected.

It is almost evening now because the light is trying new dresses. It slides low across the floor and shows you what you missed: a penny near the radiator, a bobby pin that remembers a different girl, a bit of glass that broke off something and did not ask pardon. Baseboards look sorry without a couch to stop them. I walk through each room and say thank you not because I believe in rooms but because I believe in thank you. In the study I run my hand along the desk and feel under it the old wad of gum I hid the day I spit in there instead of on the ground because Glory told me God counted on Saturdays. If I wanted to, I could tell you where every secret in this house has gone to lay. They do not know they are released. I might never tell them.

They took the curtains already. Lady with the flower hair said she wanted to make a dress out of a winter, and I nodded. The windows are unashamed. They do not do that thing windows do when they know you will judge them for their dirt. They are themselves finally: panes and promise. Outside, the lilac stump sits, patient. I go out to it. The air has that snapped-peas bite. I put my shoe toe under the dirt and feel the root’s insistence. It is not dead. It is exactly as alive as it needs to be to disappoint anyone with a chainsaw. I lean down and press my palm to the stub and feel the cold there more than the wood. “She watered you with her leftovers,” I tell it. “You know you owe us bloom even if you stop being pretty.” It does not care. It is what it is. The ground holds on to it like a habit.

The man who cut the grass in the morning has left a track of green on the hose. It looks like somebody laid a scarf down and then picked it up in a hurry when the owner rounded the corner. I roll it up, slow. Put it on the hook my father hammered into the foundation the day he got a new job and then left it like he left a thousand other starts: half measured, thoroughly believed in, woven poorly.

I go back inside because the house wants me to. There is a way a house speaks when it is empty. It isn’t a ghost exactly; it’s a mother checking windows before bed. I close and lock each one. In the bathroom I turn off the drip that has been our song since the seventies. The refrigerator is last. It hums like a mentor who won’t go away. I open it and stand in its forgiveness. Inside is a jar of pickles and half a lemon and a jar of something brown that used to be joy. I take out the lemon and breathe its rind. I put it back. I unplug the refrigerator. Its disappointment is immediate and long. Even dead things know how to miss habit. I prop its door open with a book so it won’t sweat into mold—an old housekeeping trick my mother taught me when she thought she would be gone longer than the summer afternoon she ran away and came home before dinner because she saw a girl cry on the bus and remembered she had three at home.

In the hall, I pick up the Bible—not because Pastor Lyons asked for it, not because it belonged to my grandmother or because it has the red letters in it where a man thought he ought to be loud when you can just as easy be kind. I pick it up because there are lists in the front and the lists have my name in the same space as my mother’s handwriting. You can pretend you are brand new in every town you move to; you can buy chairs and swear on them with somebody else’s money; you can put a bell on your door and take it off, and still handwriting is the law. The paper crackles when I pull the string loose. The green marker we used to fix the squeak is still there, making the ribbon a liar. I open to the front. It says births. The pages are full up of days that started out soft. I turn to deaths. There is my mother’s name in her own hand because she started writing it there the day she turned sixty-five, and then crossed it out, and then wrote it again at sixty-eight and crossed it again, and wrote finally at seventy-two in a hand that had no shame left. She put the date and a time and then she put a story after it. "Fell asleep like a baby I never had." She lied for me so much in life; it is new to see her lie about herself in death. It is a good lie. I will keep it.

I write my own name on no page. Not today. Not because I am afraid but because I want to see if one day I will understand where it belongs. I close the book and the leather kisses itself the way roofs do. I put it in my bag. The ring sits in the pocket making it look like I am carrying a stone somebody threw through glass and then apologized.

By the time the realtor comes, Sunny with her hair that behaves and a pen that clicks like an impatient child, the light has leaned all the way east. She stands in the doorway with her clipboard like a person come to read you your rights. “You ready?” she says, not waiting for no.

“No,” I answer anyway, because I have learned to say what is true even when it fights the room.

She winces—my honesty is something she knows how to stage out of a photo. “I’ll come early tomorrow,” she says. “‘Early early, before any lookers. You can be here or not. Better not, in my opinion. People say too much when the blood is still warm.”

She steps inside and then stops. The way an animal stops at the edge of water it knows is not its pond. She looks around, at the places on the wall where pictures little suns themselves once were, where the light is bright because the past shielded it so long. “It’s… quiet,” she says.

“Loud,” I say.

She looks like she wants to argue tastefully. Instead she hands me a set of keys I have already. Fresh ones. “Just in case,” she says, which is her trade. Just in case this. Just in case that. She tells me to sign something and then bites her lower lip when the pen doesn’t write because of the cold. She shakes it and then warms it in her fist like it owes her good ink.

“Do you need me to…” She spreads her hands like a mime, palm to the air, a person offering to help carry what she cannot see.

“I need you to go,” I tell her the way my mother used to tell the landlord we were not late, he was early. She smiles like I have reminded her she is not the center of this, even when she has the key.

“All right then,” she says, and steps backward, misjudging the step that is not there. She catches herself on the doorjamb and laughs like a woman does when a fall was almost visible. “See you when you want to be seen,” she says through that laugh.

I stand with the door open long enough to feel the way the house breathes when cold comes near. It does not sigh. It settles. My mother settled the same way. Not resigned. Ready. She would see a storm down the block and call in the laundry and her daughters and any boys had no place to go and she would put on a pot of soup like soup is armor. She made no speeches. She fastened no flags. She wiped the counter till the rag came away clean. “Be decent,” she would say like she was smacking the devil on his hand. “Be decent and hold the door when you ain’t got no business inside.”

I close and lock the front door and then open it again because that is how a person knows a door is real. I step out and turn the key in the lock and feel the tongue catch. I test the knob. Somebody walking past says hello without teeth. A boy on a bike glides by, hands not touching handle, feet not on pedals, daring the street to love him anyway. The sun puts one last lemon slice through the maple. I put the keys in my pocket where the ring already does its little gravity, and I set my hand flat against the wood of the door and say to it what I never said enough to my mother. You were good. You held. I thank you.

The afternoon is over. It walks down Jubilee with the rest of the people, steps in gum and curses it, turns a corner and becomes evening. I step off my mother’s porch into a town that doesn’t know it is smaller now. The lilac stump is neither more nor less than it was this morning, and that is the closest this day will get to mercy. I walk to the curb and then turn back to the house because I have never once left a place without checking to see if it was watching me go. The house looks like a woman sitting in her slip at the end of the day, hair combed into something honest. She does not wave. Neither do I. We do our part. We let each other leave.
