The sign was not level. It leaned hard to one side like it had lost its footing in the grass. ESTATE SALE, black paint on a banged-up plank set at the curb. There the afternoon sat, warm and waxy with June, lay heavy on the porch wood where her mother’s rocker had grooved out two half-moons, and setting square in the space between them was Eula with a pad, a ballpoint that clicked in her damp fingers, and a thin mouth trying to be thicker than it felt. She had on a skirt from the closet nobody had insisted she keep and a blouse her body stretched without drama. There were flies, slow ones, rehearsing their arcs into and out of the house as if they had paid too. There was the sound of someone from the end of the street calling a boy out the road and the boy sliding into his name like water back into the river.

The house itself was breathing. The windows exhaled the little they could and the floorboards hummed, pissed at footsteps that weren’t tender with knowing. Someone had parked a truck half up on the short, baked front lawn. He was the first. He was early. She could see the back of his neck, pink and new-looking, and the whites of his ankles where his socks did not reach. Beside him was a woman with a braid thick as a rope. They sat, looking, like they were in church trying to remember what you are supposed to do with your hands before the song begins. They were not from around here. Their car plates were a different county, not a different state, but enough.

“Starts at noon,” Eula said to herself, not to them. She looked at the square clock on the hall table that ticked too loudly for good manners. It was eleven and forty, then eleven and forty-one, and the open mouth of the door was already brown with shoes. At the top of the porch steps, the woman with the rope-braid took a long look at Eula and said, Morning. Not a question, not a wish, a statement.

“Morning,” Eula said, flattening the pad on her knee and drawing a straight line through the first blank page. She had straight lines and boxes drawn out from last night and amounts she could not imagine asking out loud, and the whole thing swelled and swelled until she threw her mother’s apron over it, the one with a rust-colored stain the shape of West Virginia on the hem.

In the neighborhood, they had known about the sale before Eula herself believed she would do it. They had felt it in the air, that thin prickling along the skin that means a house is about to let go of its breath and speak a different language. They had told each other at the A&P and in the dollar store, by the school fence, in the coin-op laundromat as if it were a storm coming with a name. They had said Hattie’s place, down on Madison by the blue house with the unpruned lilacs, is fixing to be a spread. Hattie who seamed everybody’s pants and kept a jar of buttons like marbles on the shelf. Hattie who fed the preacher and the stray same plate, who kept a hammer in the silverware and a Bible in a sugar tin. Hattie who had a way of looking you out of foolishness and into your right self. That Hattie was laid out proper in April and gone by May, and now June came with velvet heat and a sale.

“Go on,” Eula heard in her head. Not a whisper. Not even memory. That was the phrase her mother handed like a broom: in the way of direction that is also scolding, also prayer. Go on. It meant, stop standing in the door blocking air. It meant, don’t admire the rope of someone else’s hair when the front room is waiting to be turned inside out. So Eula went on. She stood and said it to the couple who had come early without asking, too early and too ready: “Starts at noon but you can come in. We ain’t fussy.” She did not tell them about the dream from last night when she was pressed under water in the kitchen sink while her mother rubbed salt into her scalp like the old days, scrubbing the lie of the city out of her head and the wild out of her scalp. She did not say that slow dreams made the morning sticky. She let them in and the air moved.

They took the room slowly at first, saw the brown couch with mildew behind one cushion where nobody decent ever left a thing, saw the jelly cupboard with the jar-bellied emptiness where all the sweet used to roam, saw the Singer with its arm raised like a swan and the iron bed sagging like a grandmother’s laugh she was mean about. The woman with the rope hair touched everything with two fingers like a spider or a piano teacher. The man did not touch. He circled and then stood straight, then circled again, then put his fist in his pocket deep enough to wrinkle the cloth. “How much you asking on that sewing machine?” the woman said, the rope sliding against her back.

“You sew?” Eula asked. The question came out meaner than she meant, so she made her face say she was only curious. You did not sell the Singer Hattie’s foot knew better than any step to a woman who wanted it for the look of the thing. She could let the jelly cupboard go to a woman who intended to fill it with candles and small terrariums. She could get fourteen dollars out of the good iron skillet, the one you could fry fish in and the fish stop smelling like the river just long enough to make folks happy. She could even watch a child take the old Jesus with his eyes that followed the room with such dignity you couldn’t turn your back sinless. But the Singer? It was a throat. It was a heartbeat. It was grief’s latch undone at the treadle.

“I mend,” the woman with the rope said. “I don’t promise gowns. I mend is all.” Her face did not blush with the lie. Eula nodded and rested one hand on the cool of the wooden table where measure-marks bit into the edge from a yardstick that had watched the children grow up, not by birthdays but by stitches.

“Sixty,” she said, slow, because in her mind her mother flicked at her fingers. Not too gentle. You ain’t running a charity. You ain’t running a pawn. What I worked for ain’t to be thrown after like popcorn. Sixty sounded like righteousness made shy. “And the oil and bobbins go with it.” Eula waited for the man to make a little laugh like the men made. He did not. He looked at the machine as if there were something under it making a sound in a key he could not hear. The woman nodded and said, Okay.

They wrote their names in Eula’s pad in soft round print. There were two children in their car who had stuck their arms out the open windows and were making their hands go wave over the wind, up, down, up, down, as if flying were a habit learned in a seat. The man came back with cash in a wrung-out roll and Eula put it into the box, the tin one that used to hold funnies cut from paper and a walnut that sounded hollow when you shook it, where her mother had once kept one good wedding ring that did not belong to anybody in the house. The man lifted the Singer by its wooden waist and the woman steadied it, and for a small second the old belt slipped in its groove and the wheel rolled with it, quiet, making something that wanted to be a hum, and Eula clapped her hand over her mouth like she had burped in a sanctuary.

People were coming now. They stepped onto the porch like the porch was the same as always but the porch knew different. It creaked under men swole up from roofing, under girls in sundresses with collarbones like spoons, under a boy whose mouth had not healed right at birth and now closed beautifully to fit his own lips, under old women with ankles like broom handles who did not care who saw them count. They came in from this side of town and that one, and from counties where the river’s other curve makes different fish, and they came with paper bags and plastic ones, with faces set like their faces always were, but their eyes, some were hungry, some were sorry, some were like a dog’s when it sees other dogs. They were polite. They were greedy. They were what they were every day except their hands worked more. They touched the sheet sets and they were gentle. They shook the mirror and the mirror showed them who did not want to see. They sniffed the canning jars because people learned different in these last hard years and if somebody’s mother knew how to make a thing keep you out of winter’s teeth, they honored that.

They called her Miss Eula because they had always called her Eula when they were ten and she was ten and they all ran summer with their shoes off until blood came and they put water from a hose on it and their cousins on the porch laughed. Or because they had never seen her before except at the bus stop one late sleet morning last January and they could tell the bus had taken her someplace and brought more back than she left with. Or because Hattie had been Miss Hattie and in a town certain ways of address trail a woman to the grave like ribbons on a casket. “Miss Eula,” they said, “what you want for the Bible with the cracked unh? The big one? That one.” The man who asked it had a scar in the shape of a worm on his cheek. He lifted the Book by its spine the way you lift a sleeping child under the armpits and his eyes did not make the joke about how heavy it was. He was not a minister and he was not planning to put the Book under a television. Eula could tell, the way you tell a stick that can be bent back and give and one that will break. She said, “What you feel about it?” The man’s eyes went around to each page like they had to. He said, “It got my name?” He meant a name like his. He meant a name that looked like him on the page in somebody else’s block hand. He meant the names her mother had inked in a tree inside that book and inked on a paper in the pantry and never said out loud after certain nights.

“It got a lot of names,” she said. “You want it?”

“Not for my sake,” he said, and she believed him. “For my girl. She learning from scraps. Who she come from. That sort of thing. She always asking me how we got here and I don’t always know how to tell the story without it sounding like somebody else’s.”

“How much you count for that?” Eula asked, staring at his worm-shaped scar like it might start crawling. He looked up, let go the edge of the Book, and the Book said a sound you don’t hear unless you lived under one your whole life. He said, “Say it, Miss Eula.”

“Say twenty,” she said and winced. It was a thing you do. You wince when you say a number to show the buyer there is hurt in it, that your heart’s skin is thin and you putting yourself to it brave. He nodded. He took the Book into his arms with a care she had not seen from him ever. He was the boy from Fourth Street who broke streetlights with rocks for a heaven, the man who went to Mansfield for a bit and came back straight as a cane in church, the neighbor who put his coat over his head to bring his little boy across rain just so his hair would keep its part for picture day. He paid and the Book walked out the door without making a promise where it was going, and Eula shut her eyes and her mother slid the apron off her mind for one breath.

That’s what it was like all day. That’s what the hours spooled into: a home being called to parts, a voice taking itself apart in the same order as the song. Every piece had a name and a use and a history and a thing it hid. The green chair that had tried on everybody’s sorrow and still kept its shape. The end table that was just a plank with legs and the legs were never the same length on Mondays. The dresser with a drawer that stuck when it rained and needed a whisper of a knife to coax it, and behind its drawers a square of wallpaper more blue than any other blue in the house where a little girl, not Eula, not Hattie, somebody else by name of Lula, had written the person she wanted to marry with a crayon to make the house, not God, keep the wish. The kitchen table carved in influenza winter by a father who had a fever for six days and milled that surface smoother than he'd ever touched his daughter’s face. The silver hairbrush that was not silver at all, that drew sound out of hair like it were playing. The pressure cooker that exploded one summer and put beans on the ceiling where they fossilized into tiny moons and Hattie stood in that hot kitchen laughing for a reason Eula never knew—maybe power, maybe relief, maybe the sheer right to have a mess she could clean up and put back to a new start. Cheating men had looked in that mirror and seen themselves beauty-true. A baby had drowned in a basin that rested on that crate. A sweet potato pie had gone down behind that stove six Thanksgivings ago and nobody said a word.

She took dollars; she took coins that had sat on a man’s bureau watching him shave; she took bills warm from a pocket; she took back her hand from palm to palm to palm who knew her and forgot her and never had a chance. She said no. No to the lady with a cold red mouth who wanted the iron bed for five dollars less than the price because the story she told was long and smooth and padded with pretty adjectives and no truth, and in the middle of it she said, “I’m sure Miss Hattie was a good woman,” and it was the Miss she stretched long like taffy that made Eula snap the tape off her own mouth and say, “That bed knows more than five dollars. You either want it or you want something to talk about over your dinner salad.” That one turned on a heel and said to the air, Some people, and the air pushed back with a hundred Sundays of chicken back and rice where there was love in a house that smelled like heat on a pan.

They moved through like a stream all afternoon, a strong, cool stream with rocks and minnows and private currents. There was the fat kind of boy who read every spine and took three comic books with cowboy on the cover and a science set with beakers nobody had poured into since 1979 and looked at Eula with accusation and said, “Ma’am, this ain’t got the red dye,” and she promised him some food coloring was under what used to be the sugar in the pantry and he found it and his spirit rose, and he gave her two crumpled dollars like he was paying a tax he should not have to and he went out whistling a tune she half-could name. There was the stoop-shouldered man from the other end of Madison who smelled like bolts and axle grease, who passed the room with the maternity clothes folded in a basket as if there were a woman asleep on them and you should never go too near, and when he came to the Christ picture he stood, he crossed himself as if he had been taught to and did not practice and then nodded to the face and took his hand off his head and walked on different, as if the picture had said, That is enough, go on. There was a girl new to breath, seventeen, with a tiny stud in her nose and hair that curled around itself like vines, who ran her fingers over the colored afghan Hattie crocheted out a thousand evenings she could not sit with her thoughts. The girl said, “It’s loud,” and Eula loved her in that second. Yes, she wanted to say, it is loud, it is shouting out how soft it will keep you. “Nine dollars,” Eula said. “Only if you promise to use it,” and the girl laughed almost from a tea kettle. “I freeze all the time,” she said. “I’ll wrap up like a present.”

There were neighbors who came to offer money and leave it, who bought one glass, who paid full price for a chipped saucer because the price was a way of touching the woman who had fed them twice and scolded them thrice and held their children five times by the wrist so they would not dash into some harm. There were cousins who came knowing they were not the kind of cousins who could make a claim but wanting to be seen in the house light once more, to get a faint tattoo of it on their arm. There were the men who’d loved Eula from a distance in middle school and stood awkward at the line of belts and ties, picking up her father’s old belt, the one that carried its own set of stories, some sweet as molasses, some bitter enough to trouble a heart, and drifted away like they had touched something electric. There was one who had meant to be her uncle, arms like countries, who placed his thumbpad on the edge of the old piano—they had not a piano, what they had was a box with keys that Hattie traded with a man for mending his shirts sewn into something he could throw over his shoulders—and he pressed one and the wrong note came out and the right note hid and that was when something in Eula’s shoulder unlocked and her first tear came.

She was not unsteady. The day had come to eat her and she was meat and she walked into it and did not run. She counted, she wrote names in her book, she drank water she had thought to put in the fridge and for that she blessed her morning self. She watched and listened and did not get much milk of comfort out of the things people said, though some tried to be sweet with their tongues and got clumsy. There are words that do not fit a woman’s mouth unless she is the mother of the dead person, and nobody else has the license or the skill. It was not that they were wrong. Death had put a trapeze between her and their talk and she was balancing. They were on the street, they were in the yard smoking, they were letting out neighbors’ business and holding in their own and she was tying up the inside of a life. There is privacy too big to be kept in your palm. It seeps.

She had been at this house, breathing its weather, since she was seven. Before that her mother had kept them in a duplex with a man whose name cannot be said in a mouth that still wants bread. Her mother had been plain with her about that. Some men are good to sleep with and bad to sleep near and worse to wake to. There were names that left shadows on the linoleum and there were names Eula and her brother Dare whisper-laughed because they tasted to say. By the time she was seven, the house on Madison—with its too-large maple in front that left a cumin of leaves in the gutter and its back room that had a crack like a river down one corner—was where Hattie’s mother had died swearing she had never had a day of rest, and where Hattie’s sister, Ruby Ann, had left in a blue dress to marry a man looked at like a door out, and where Hattie had made a way with two hands that would not forgive a dropped dish. From seven to twelve the house bruised Eula in corners and taught her to move her insides around the furniture. From twelve to twenty it watched her body do what it did without asking anybody’s permission. From twenty to thirty it added on a coatrack of hurts, small ones and one like a railroad spike hammered in from behind. From thirty on, the house was a partner that sometimes sulked. You could talk to it or not. It knew your business like a sister and it did not use your own stories against you unless you were trying to lie to yourself. Her mother had ruled it like a country that owed her everything and paid nothing late.

“Don’t you let nobody carry out my bed,” Hattie had told Eula the second week she took to the hospital bed in the front room. “Not with them hands on it. That bed is a talker.” Eula had laughed—too late—and her laugh had turned into a cough and Hattie had laughed without coughing, a small coin of a laugh she did not often spend. “You hear me?” she said, the lung machine raising and easing like a night pond with frogs. “You hear me, girl?”

“I hear you,” Eula had said, unbraiding the hospital sheet until it was a river. “Ain’t nobody going to put no hands on nothing you wouldn’t say yes to.”

“Everything gets hands on it, one time or another,” Hattie said. “What I mean is, don’t make a fool of the bed. Don’t make a fool of the thing that knew my dreaming.”

“You telling me to keep it?”

“I’m telling you, if it leaves, it has to have a rider. It has to know your skin was the last to bless it.”

That’s what she meant. Likely that’s what she meant. Or maybe Hattie, dying slow enough to hear her own death make a little scrape in the room, meant nothing like that and everything like it and more and less, and now folks came and lifted the bed-inch mattress, and the man with the worm scar sent his brother back for a dolly, and Eula stood by the doorway like a rag flag and could not make the word No into a shape her lips knew.

“Don’t you be no fool,” her mother said. She had not died twice to be ignored once. “You better do right by that bed. Ain’t no sense in holding on to a thing just cause it saw you toss and cry. That’s the worst loyalty.”

Eula put the pad down. She pressed her palm to the bedframe and the bedframe’s carved headboard grinned back. It had been grinning as long as she could remember, a hundred lilies, two doves round as biscuits, a vine that made a circle for a star. Her mother had faced those lilies and that star, her shoulder sung open and shut to the living room dark, for thirty-nine years. Men who were mistakes had faced those lilies and had their mistakes forgiven until they repeated them twice; the one good one, Mr. Tolliver with the teeth that looked like small stones, had once knelt right there and had his prayer answered in a yes that lasted six months. Bodies of Eula’s brothers and sisters—one brother was a return, another a leaving, the sisters not born to the bed but named in the mouth of it—had rolled in it like young bears and it had not leaned toward them or away. It had just held, breath by breath. You do not pay it back. You thank it.

She meant to press her face on that wood and say four stories in its ear and then lift it and walk out of the house with the men and the dolly and get it into the truck and pay them to drive and put it in her apartment across town where she slept too near the stairs and never could feel a quiet. She meant to hold it and not sell it. But the man at the dolly was earnest and the afternoon had moved to a place where the shade had shrunk and the flies knew where sweetness was and her hands shook like a woman on a ledge, and the small girl with the little nose stud was back, with her mother this time, and they were buying a poker set and a busted child’s chair and two aprons and a clothespin bag that said Sunbeam on it in red cursive. And a woman she did not like stood in her doorway saying, “Bed frames is heavy, Miss Eula. You going to break your own back trying to be strong,” and in that instant some tired and mean part of her wanted to sell the bed just to keep a stranger from thinking she had guessed the true thing about anything.

“All right,” she said to the worm-scar man and his brother with the dolly, “you can take it. But one of y’all give me your arm.” They did. She stepped up, foot by foot, and sat on the edge. She took the headboard in both her hands like a lover’s face and bent her forehead onto it until the wood grew warm and the lilies came in. She let four breaths loose into that headboard. She did not tell it four stories and later tonight she would scold herself for that until she turned into a girl again and sobbed quiet enough that nobody up the stairs could hear. She did what her mother had asked: she put her skin on the last part leaving.

“Watch the corner,” she said to the men, and they smiled, men like to be told a thing they already know if the voice that tells them has been careful elsewhere. They shuffled that bed through a door that was smaller than the bed’s memory, they took it past the Jesus who watched without blinking, and the bed went out into the afternoon, into its flat light and its bees and its little boys throwing a worn-out basketball against a rim with no net, and Eula could see the map of sweat on the men’s shirts, Ohio right there, or Africa, or a cloud.

Her brother had not come. Dare was two towns over and said he had a double shift and would try if he could and it was a lie after a fashion but not a hateful one. He did not have the engine for funerals, for house sales, for endings that look too much like the ones you rehearse at night. He would come tonight late and stand in the empty front room and blow the last dust of it with his brown hot breath, and he would fall asleep on the floor and wake with his hand under his own back like a stone he fit himself to. Eula did not fight him today. Her other brother was somebody else’s now. Onyx turned into John when he married that church woman who ironed her money. He was far down the highway and he sent forty dollars last month and love as if love could be sent in a hurry like that and land safe. Eula did not hate them. Hating them would take away the perfect little circle of hating she had been saving for herself in case she needed it.

They took the dresser and it moaned. They took the little stool Hattie had used to sit and peel like maybe every peel got the same start and if you kept the blade straight you would get mercy at the end of what the knife can’t slice. They took the pot with the dent she had thrown in a storm of January and red-haired fury when she and Hattie had spoken to each other ugly about the cousin who wanted to come stay after her man slammed her face, and Hattie had said No, I ain’t got no more room for company and Eula had said If you don’t, what you got all that space for, and the pot—the iron with fair sides—had caught a piece of that sentence and fell like brick and the dent was the letter S if you bent your head.

She knew where to stand. It turned out the place where a sale looks like it is going to murder you is the place you go to stay alive. She stood by the arch between front room and hall. From there you could see all the way to the kitchen window if the door were open, and it was, and you could see the window make its old habit of looking out like it was a face. She could hear porch and street and backyard and small talk and tears that would not get all the way down, and from there she could tell anyone the price of what they held with a guess sweet enough to make her mother proud and hard enough to make her mother approved. Her mother’s word for that was rightness. Her mother had sat in this arch and made rightness run through the house so steady there was water in the tap and light at night and a door anybody with decency could knock on and be let in, and a door not everybody would love to walk through because there would be no foolish welcome. Now Eula said the numbers and they were rightness if she made them so.

The little girl with the nose stud leaned to Eula and said, “I can’t believe how much stuff is in one house. We live in an apartment and I don’t know where half this would go.” The girl’s mother laughed, slow, and said, “It goes where you put it and then you make room.” Eula smiled at the mother and felt her knee talk. You lying, it said, and I have been with you long enough for the truth. Her foot hurt too and her shoulder still held the piano note from earlier like a memory of a man’s voice you don’t even like but that put itself into your bones without asking. It was not even four and her back said, Girl.

They took the blue plates with the dancers because they were clean to look at. They did not take the chipped brown ones because the chips looked like teeth and for some reason no one wanted their breakfast judged by that. A thin man bought a box of keys and clattered them onto his palm and said, “Don’t try to stop me. I collect the way folks collect rocks. I got a box for every house that’s emptied within five miles the last six years.” Eula looked where his eyes were when he said emptied. They were on his own shoes. He did not tell her what sort of emptiness he had at home. His fingers played the keys and the keys did not mind. Keys are hungry for hands.

In the kitchen a little woman lifted the rolling pin with a grunt like it was heavy and it was not. She held it pressed tight to her chest like a newborn. “This was your mother’s?” she asked, and the question had lace on it but also threads that scratched. “And yours too, sometimes,” Eula said, thinking of how many times she had floured that counter and rolled dough thin as paper for noodles. There were things, to this day, that she could not make thin enough. Her patience in that direction had never caught hold. Her mother had come up under her elbows and told her, Serious, baby, press out your trouble. It will take the shape of what you put it through. If it won’t go thin, that’s cause it wants to hold itself big and you either let it or you teach it, but you don’t just stand there frowning. Noodles listened; you threaten them with a blade and they go soft.

“Three dollars,” Eula said and the woman made a noise that said that is both too much and too little and she would talk herself into it in the car and blame the sun. The iron skillet walked out on the arm of a boy with a buzz cut who looked like a Sunday school picture of David but had a tattoo of a snake near his thumb emerging from a wash of green you could smell if you tried. He said, “My grandma had one like this. We don’t got her no more, but I can remember the way the kitchen sounded when she got mad. That sizzle.” Eula looked at him and saw there was something up under his eyelid like sand. He gave her a crisp twenty. He did not ask for change back.

There were the two old sisters from down Dove Street who came in a pair and always had, who smelled like powder and the store and the church basement at the same time, and they fingered the curtain sheers and the pillow slips and one took the set with little blue embroidery worn off in patterns Eula could still see like breath on a glass, and the other took nothing but stood leaning to Eula like a tree and said, “Your mama was not equal. She had a way. I swear that woman seen through me once and stuck me back together nice. She was not equal.” They said it the way they said the Lord is merciful, careful around every syllable. Eula nodded. There are some sayings you just accept. You do not try to do the math out loud.

At some point, the jelly cupboard came up and a small woman with a belly high with a baby and feet like bread said, “I have needed something like this my whole marriage. Now I’m going to get him to bolt it to the wall so it don’t tip when my boy gets nosy.” Her hair was parted in the middle and going gray in two precise rivers that met at the back where her neck made a proud column. Eula wanted to put her palm there. “Twenty-five,” she said. The woman’s eyes met hers, steady as rain, and she said, “I can give you eighteen.” “Twenty-two,” Eula said, and the woman considered into the middle distance where numbers live and said, “Okay,” and they reached their hands across air and shook. They shook hard. That cupboard would hold deserts and secrets and iodine and a name for a girl who would come in March when there was frost on the window from breath and time was late and you had to wait anyway. The woman came back by with a smile that was not to be shared. Eula wanted a photograph of that smile and then she remembered that was what memory was for.

Once, in the middle of the lower room where the rug had been rolled and tied and walked out of the house by two boys who looked like they should have wore back braces but did not, a man with a mustache that laughed before he did tried to barter hard for the oval mirror in a walnut frame with a little nick at the top that looked like a compass point. “It’s cloudy,” he said. “It ain’t good glass. I’ll give you ten.” Eula’s back was a hot nail. In that mirror she had seen her mother once see herself and like what she saw so quick and quiet it was a crime. Not that Hattie was short on pride. But self-regard: she stored it in things she had made, and her body was not one. But the day she put on the dress with the thin white piping and buttoned it up to the line of her trouble and looked and then did not look away, the mirror had turned clear for a second like a new window. “Twenty,” Eula said. The man clucked, rolled his head, made a theater out the length of his indifference, and Eula watched his woman get embarrassed and leave the room from the heat of it. He took the mirror at twenty and the mirror, Eula felt, deserved better than that business but would get another chance in a hallway over a tall arrangement of something green.

“Lord, look at all these pictures,” another woman said, holding the velvet frame with the little gold flake out the corner. Those pictures. That was something. She had not known what to do with the pictures, the ones of first days of school, of her brother before the shot piece caught his ear and moved him right, of Hattie at the river with her dress hiked up and her knees like cities nobody had maps to. She had spread them in a shoebox under the rocking chair and knew somebody would find them because people found what shone or looked like it might shine. A woman held one up: Eula at fourteen with hair parted down the middle in two braids and too much face. The woman looked and then looked up to look at this Eula standing here and said nothing which was a mercy. Another person held up a tiny black-and-white of a man Eula had never known. Her father. All the fathers. He was on a porch that might have been anywhere warm with a cigarette like he had something to say but liked smoke better. “You know him?” the person asked, and Eula said, “Everybody do,” and they put him back as if he burned.

Time was piling up in the corners, getting thicker. It got to be where you could hear the light leave a room, the small little inhale the place took back from what it had given to see. It got to be where the smell of old wood wore on you like a long good marry that had gone slightly vacated in the middle years and then came back. It got to be where Eula had to set her pad down and touch the lint trap on the dryer like a talisman or a friend she had not greeted yet. It got to be where her sandals felt like hard mouths on her feet and she wanted to put her bare skin on the plank floor and forgive it everything it had done to the soles she had been wearing since nineteen-year-old Eula put a foot out the door and said I will be somebody who wears shoes to bed if I want to and Hattie said Try it and you will be somebody who catches a fever that eats your good sense. They had been fine, then, those years. They had been something. Her body remembered all the beds and all the chairs and all the suns.

A honeybee came into the front room and swung its fat gold self round the picture hooks left in the wall like it was visiting old cousins. It went to the window and tested the glass with a patient head and left and tried again. Eula watched it and thought of an August when she was nine and she had found a nest in the gray cooling of the day, under the azalea by the porch steps, and put her ear to it and her mother had slapped her head so quick the sting came before the sound and it saved her a swelling because Hattie had kept bees in the back of her mind and in her bones and knew they do not love what is rude. Hattie had put her hand on the nest, flat, like blessing and said, These are ours. Eula had not trusted that. Bees are their own. But Hattie fed them sugar water and sat and sang to them when the plums fell light on the grass like accidents and grief held itself sweet and showed a little part of itself to anyone patient watching. People would come by and call Hattie witch and smile and ask for pounds of honey in jars and Hattie would say, Witch where, what you think my broom for, and sell them honey for cash and keep peace in the yard. The hive had died in a winter after the hard one. Hattie had not cried about it but she did not eat honey again as long as she lived. The bee in the house now knew nothing of that. It hummed until it moved on and nobody got stung.

Late afternoon made long shadows and gave corners each a small night. The rug was gone; the bed was gone; the dresser gone; the dining table went to a couple who said nothing at all to each other and that made Eula want to offer them a plate to break just to see if they would smile. The clock on the hall table, the too-loud one, kept on even after Eula put it out in the yard for its heavy tick to belong to the world and not her pockets. She put the money box under the chair because people were honest until they were not and if they were not you needed to give them a chance to be honest again. She had a small ambition to be kind today and a bigger one to get through. She reached for a glass and did not find it because the glassware was brung into the kitchen when she was not looking, priced with little squares of blue tape like postage stamps, and people had bought them like they were passing out awards. She turned to the sink and drank from the spigot like a young person or old or animal. Water base and cold enough to make the top of your mouth want to sing.

Three o’clock: the sun, tired a little, sat back. Heat leaned back too and put its arm on the couch of air. The porch had stains on it you could see now for what they were: a hundred shoes, a thousand. The light went off the pictures and off the Jesus and there he looked almost meek, and that was the time the white-haired man with pink ear tips came in smelling like mothballs and old typewriter, looking for a thing he would know when he saw it. Eula did not know him but she knew his world. He craved order like food. He had a list in his pocket of items and he put it in his head and browsed with a face like a store detective’s. At the back in the pantry he found a blue Ball jar with steel lid full of buttons and he took it and held it like you would hold a gourd, wondering does it hold more rain or more water. He shook it and the rattle made a sound he had been morning for. “How much,” he asked. “For these.”

“That jar been in this house since the house been itself,” Eula said, honest to God. “Fifteen.”

He nodded and put his finger to the glass as if to sign his name there. She did not ask, What you going to do with buttons. Some things have to make their own future without a story told in advance of them. He paid with quarters, some Buffaloes and some not, and he made a small joke about inflation that did not belong to anyone standing there and then his eyes filled wet without warning and he backed out of the room apologizing to nobody. The jar sat sturdy in his arm like a new animal he could love. He went down the steps walking carefully and got into his old sedan and put the jar on the passenger seat and buckled it in as if a person were riding along.

Someone came up with the plastic cover for the couch, folded in a square. It was damp. “What this?” he asked Eula. “Trash,” she said. The man looked at it and looked at her mouth and said, It ain’t right to call a thing trash just because it protected you. Hattie would have said the same. Hattie liked the plastic like she liked the Lord: as a sheet that kept a body out of some stains. Eula took the man’s measure and said, “You want it?” He shrugged. “I don’t know.” “Take it then,” she said. “We got memory enough without sweating through some.” He went off with it. The couch sat there like a big rock in the river. It must go too. Everything must go. Not the marks the bookshelf left in the wall. Not the smell cotton carries after so many washings in water that isn't quite sweet. Not the maple’s way of trying to root into the roof. Not your mother’s small footstep going to the sink in the dark and the splash of the first cup of the morning. Those did not go anywhere they could be put in boxes.

It was nearly five when the last big thing became two small ones. Inez from around the corner had held back on the pots because she was tired of cooking for a son who opened the stove as if it were a ghost he had to scare away, “but I’ll take the Dutch oven before this whole place goes to some flea slick and I’m left here standing with my apron on, looking like a fool,” she told Eula, and Eula said, “You ain’t never been a fool on this street and if they say so they lying to their pillow.” Inez laughed wide. Her gum flashed a crown of gold. She wrapped that Dutch oven in an old towel and set it in her Kroger bag like a baby then said, “Now I need me a spatula that don’t bend,” and Eula put one in her hand like putting a crown on a woman.

She was tired. The room was tired. The day had done its labor and was chewing the end of a grass stem and thinking, Not bad, not bad at all. There were just little items now: mason jars, the lint brush, a set of measuring spoons one was missing the smallest and a woman made a face like that mattered and Eula thought, You ain’t left this room yet, the smallest. Come back later. The iron stood there like a man with a belly, full of himself. The coat tree that had held every storm and all the wet in the town looked at her. You? Will you? She shook her head and put a tag on it; she could not take the coat tree because to put it in her apartment would attract rain. You had to fool the sky sometimes into letting you be. She took instead from the drawer under the table where she had hunted for twist ties a small thimble that had a bead of someone else’s blood, a tiny smear maroon like lipstick, and put it in the cup of her palm. It was her mother’s. It could live in any house. You could whisper into it and it would hold the whisper for you like a tiny jar. Eula put it in the pocket of her apron where the stain shaped like West Virginia lived and the apron touched it back polite, room given.

A boy came in at the end, door frame like a picture frame around his long body, and said, “Ma’am?” and his voice had break in it and gravel and milk. “You got any records left?” He meant 45s and 78s, he meant the big ones and the smalls, the ones with girls on the paper jackets with breasts like words you wanted to sound, the ones with men whose eyes were heavy like steak. “Back room by the window,” she said, and he went and came back with three: Billie, Mahalia, James. He did not know who Billie was and he held the name like it might sing his pocket into money. He did know James. He nodded like a relative and Eula said, “A quarter each,” and he looked like he had been asked to give up a tooth and said, “I got fifty-seven cents.” “I guess you got two then,” she said, and he took James and Mahalia and left Billie and Eula put Billie back on the table and thought about her mother putting that record on and letting her body lean to the wall and the voice coming out like water from a well. Somebody’s going to take you, baby. Somebody was. Probably a girl with a bite to her laughter.

When the last person had taken what they could carry and the light had turned to a shade you only find in houses that have been washed in sorrow and air, when the porch had quieted and the children had realized nobody was there to clap for them and had gone on to their swings, when the men with the dolly had done the last of the wrestle and clanged the gate behind them with a chime that had lived on Madison since before Eula had come into a name she liked, she sat on the floor with her back to the wall where the couch had been and the wall was cooler than she wanted. It was evening, thick. Crickets had rehearsed and now they sang. Across the street Mrs. McCrea was calling her cat like it was a child and the cat did not come. A siren far off held a line and cut it. The refrigerator in the kitchen said a low prayer and the fan over the stove turned and did not turn and that was its job. The house had changed its smell the way a person does after a bath and no perfume. It smelled like nothing with a little bit of thunder in it. It smelled like beginning and also like the end of something you don’t have to be brave about any longer.

When Hattie had been taking her slow plain for the last time through the front room, she said once, “It’s strange what you can’t carry.” Eula had thought then she meant the body, the weight of it. But now she put her palms flat on the floor and felt splinters that had been there since before she was born and she thought no, that ain’t all. You cannot carry the whole, and you cannot always carry the best part, and sometimes you can only carry a small sound or a small thing and the little thing is the one that fits in your pocket and lets you live. Tomorrow she would not be in this house. The key would not fit the same lock in her hand. The next morning would rise on somebody else’s breath here and the maples would lift their arms like they were asking for mercy again, and the people who moved in might be polite and might not be able to tell a room the truth and Eula would not be able to say to them the names of places where drafts wait to touch your ankle in winter like a hand that never learned to be nice. She would not have to keep the lamp filled or the salt jar even. She would be in her apartment with one window smart to the sun and the noise of the city’s next plan. She would hang nothing up for a while. She would put the thimble on her dresser in a shallow dish and watch it like a thing that could sprout legs and move. She would have a bed that did not know her. She would lay her grief on a plate and it would not be a big one.

There was a sound from the back door, a foot, a can knocked. Eula waited. “It’s me,” a voice said, and Dare slid in sideways because that was the way he knew to fit through a place. “You done?” he asked and his voice was a fist trying to open and not knowing if it should. He wore the shirt from the job smelling like gas and meat. “I am and I ain’t,” she said, and he nodded. He sat cross-legged on the floor where they used to sit and build little towns with blocks that Hattie made from two-by-fours cut short, each block with the smell of men in it and the mark of a saw tooth. He put down his hands and looked at them and made a shape like a bridge with his fingers. “You keep anything?” he asked.

“That.” She touched the pocket of the apron. “And this.” She had not told herself before then that she had also kept the snap herself out of telling anyone what she was going to do. She had kept her own self, a little bit. She had said yes to the less.

He looked around like people do when a story about a room comes to them all at once. “It looks like a mouth with a tooth pulled,” he said, and that was true. The space where the couch had been had a gum feel, raw and wanting to lick it over and over and you shouldn’t. “You hungry?” he asked. He took a roll from his pocket like a trick and it was bread. Hattie’s children were magicians: we could fill air. He tore it and gave her half and at the first bite the day settled lower in Eula’s bones and said Oh. “We ate in here,” he said. “We ate in the kitchen, too, and on the back step and once in the bedroom when mama had a fever, but we ate in the front room when we wanted to prove to each other we were fine. We ate pie on that little table and watched the TV and had to change the antenna and mama said we got to put these plates on this fancy cloth and act like we know something and when she dropped the fork we didn’t laugh because she would’ve whopped us for thinking she could be clumsy.” His voice went to a whisper that was not ashamed. “She was beautiful in here.” They each chewed and looked not at each other but at the wall where there was a shadow of a mirror, a geologic print of a thing gone, and Eula said, “She was better than here.”

The sky outside turned the color of a bruise you press until the pain is interesting and then let go because there is dinner to make. The porch creaked with nobody on it. The floor said a last little oh because everything wooden does when the day done. Eula thought of the woman with the rope hair threading the needle on the Singer now in her house, the first hum of it saying a syllable and the woman cussing because the thread got a mind and she knew the mayhem and the mercy both. She thought of a boy putting on a record and standing with his fingers on the edge of the counter, letting his mother sway slow, not to be looked at. She thought of the worm-scar man answering his girl questions she as she traced names she could not pronounce under the pad of her forefinger and slowing to tell the story right. She thought of the lady with the cold mouth putting the iron bed into a room where it would sit and be the cause of a fight and outlast the fight with all the patience in wood. She thought small: a jar of buttons clacking together in a drawer in a new place and the man who bought them turning to look for them when he had forgotten about them and feeling relieved and foolish both to be glad the jar was there. She thought of the jelly cupboard with the smell of jam three families and two recipes from now, and she smiled and her shoulders dropped. The word for that in her house growing up had been release. Out in the street a boy called out to another boy in a voice so full of play it made Eula’s heart twitch toward some other life for half a second in joy.

“You going to lock it?” Dare asked. His fingers had found a pebble and were doing that thing men do when they got a worry and a small object and a time. They pass the worry to the object and make the object into a talisman. The pebble had been in the house under the radiator for who knows how long. Eula took the key from the sill under the small plant that had been plastic longer than any of them had been living here and then had been thrown away because someone took the plant midafternoon as if it could grow. “I am,” she said and did not stand. She put the key to her tongue like people do, because metal tastes like a thing that wants to be trusted before it should. She stood and walked to the door and took Dare’s body heat with her for two steps and then she was just her. “It will be all right,” she said to him and did not know whether she was lying and did not care. The lie or the truth both would be a kind of handle.

She closed the door. It did not want to. It had the habit of staying open for anybody decent and there was decent here even in the people who did not belong sometimes, and it was not pride that made it swing back, it was the old hinges and the weight shifted without the bed and she put her hip to it and it said Okay, all right and latched. She turned the key. It sounded good. Even empty, the house was good at the sound of closing. A girl with a bike rolled past and looked, her eyes dark as lakewater. Eula smiled at her and the girl did not smile back. You do not teach your face softness in every light. That’s okay. She put the key in her bra, a place that had held every secret she had ever not told any man.

On the walk she had to stop and put her hand out. It wasn’t dizziness. It was a thickening. A humming. Her mother had said once, when Eula had asked about that hum you hear when you put a shell to your ear and your auntie says it’s the ocean and you know good and well you are in Ohio and the lake is not the same as that and you are not a fool, that’s not any water, that’s you. That’s your own river. You hear it when you got quiet and put your ear to any round thing with emptiness inside. “You can hear yourself if you like,” Hattie had said. “Some days I don’t like. Some days I do.” Eula put her palm on the porch post and the post sang a little. Her ear filled up with something like that hum and then it was gone and she went down the steps slow and at the bottom where the grass gave up and made room for sidewalk the little chip of concrete that had always caught her mother’s shoulder of a shoe when she came heavy out the door was there to catch her and she stepped over it and grinned. She could feel Hattie’s hand in the center of her back where it always was when the world was coming fast down Madison and the light was wrong, that small push a mother gives without anybody seeing it to keep her child even in the war.

They walked to the curb together, Eula and Dare. The maples made their black lace over the road. The house she had sold in a day and lived in as long as she could feel looked like a stranger and like her face in a photograph if she squinted. “We eat?” he asked and she said, “We eat,” and he put his hand on her shoulder knock-kneed like when he was five and he could not keep his whole weight from tipping off of his own earnestness. They began to walk. Behind them the sign leaned more. ESTATE SALE. The black paint kept its command with no one to read it and the evening made it a little more honest. They got to the corner. Eula did not look back again. Her mother’s voice was not gone with her breath; it was where it had set itself years ago in the space behind Eula’s ear. Go on, it said, and she did. The dirt under the dogwood in the yard would take a thousand footprints in the next year and not complain. The tiny navy star on the kitchen linoleum would keep shining under somebody’s shoe sole. The spot on the wall where the flower base had been put to cover a hairline crack even a patient man could not love would all the way out itself. Somebody’s baby would crawl and put its tongue on baseboard. Somebody would make a wrong in that room and make it right in the next. The thimble hit her thigh where she had it in her pocket with each step and the bump of it kept time and the time was theirs. It was June, in Ohio, and the light hung on like a last kindness. The people down the block laughed at something wrong and fumbled and found their answer. It was all present. She and her brother rounded into it and were held in its big open hand. They went toward food. They went toward night. The porch behind them got dark without their eyes. The bed they had blessed was headed for a corner bedroom in a house two over from a cornfield and a woman was going to stand there and look at it and say Hm. Everything that left had taken a address. Everything remaining had room. Eula put her chin up because that was just how she could breathe. It was a way. It was evening, the end of a sale, the first deep part of a day letting go of its heat without ceremony. She had her key riding warm just where some place under her heart had always kept a thing safe. Give me your arm, her mother’s voice had said to the bed and to the girl both. And so she took Dare’s and did not say the name of the street and did not have to. They were in it. They went. The door was the kind that would stick in rain. The sky had plans. The ground under their shoes had a memory you could go your whole life trying to curse and then the day sends itself like a letter and the postman is kind and the travel short enough and there you are, delivered back to yourself upright. There you were, there you are, humming. And though the room that had held you had a new echo now, the echo had your name on it in a hand you knew, your mother’s or your own, it didn’t matter. It would answer when called. It would rise. The night began its sentence and they walked into the grammar of it sure enough, with hunger and a little money in a box and a tiny silver thimble, and the air making them promises it made to everybody who kept going, and some of those promises would turn themselves into fact when they had slept and let the floor of the world hold them, some would not, but the walking itself was a kind of answer, the sale a chapter that had been read aloud very well and was over. They would eat and not talk some. They would let the story be quiet. They would let the house rest. House, you did your work, she might have said if she were somebody who talked to buildings in the street like they were dogs. House, you did your work, Hattie had. Go on. We gone. And the house said nothing at all the way a big dear thing says everything by standing there and letting wind pass through where words might go. Eula stepped and then stepped, the rhythm was the rhythm, and breath made a ladder she could climb. She climbed. She went on. The light held open the last crack of day. Then it closed slow and decent above her shoulders. Her mouth found bread. Her eye found evening. Her heart caught—hard, hard again, soft—and kept time with that little metal beat in her apron.
