The morning was barely up off the land and already the heat in it like a promise kept. The east was rosed and the sky beyond it pale and hard and without cloud. They came down the rutted track with a reel of black poly pipe rolled in a loop like a great serpent and a gray galvanized bucket with tools in it and two shovels clinking together and carried them with the caution of men who have been too long on making do. Their boots went in the dust and the dust lay in the weeds and in the ruts and rose a little round their ankles and the weeds were seedy with burr and made a dry sibilance against the denim of their legs.

The orchard went on before them a quarter mile and the trees in their rows leaned into that distance like mourners. Pear and apple and a few apricot and a contention of what had once been cherry gone to knurled black skeletons mummied with the wind. The grasses were yellow and fox-colored and the earth was a gray slate in flat plates where it would cleave under a heel. In the low ground the ditch lay with a film upon it that the light took in what scant measure it could and the scum shivered where a waterboatman made his minuscule voyages and the headgate of the lateral was ajar and the metal hunched itself with rust like a scab long held to the wound.

Harlan walked ahead. He was broad through the shoulder and had a hat with a darkened sweatband and a shirt pinned to his back by a burden of old labor. His hair was gone at the crown and the skin there pale and delicate as an infant's skin gone to a different use in this world. The other was Levi and he bore the pipe over one shoulder and his arm had a line to it like a bow and the cords in his wrist stood when he set the load down and rested. He turned and looked out over the trees.

You see the tops of them, he said. The dieback. All up there gone. You can cut and paint till your arm falls out of the socket and it wont come back. What dont sap dont live.

Harlan leaned the shovels against the fence and went down into the ditch. The water was not more than over his boots and it had the smell of whale-flesh gone, an old organic stink. In the shallows a crawdad scuttled backward with its claws lifted as if to ward off the century itself and Harlan reached and scooped it out and set it back in a little run that still winked over peagravel and turned the wormed wheels of the gate with both hands as if to coax and not to command. The water trickled and then surged and then subsided. Somewhere in the lines a burst had let by such that the lateral would not hold head, and the water master from the district would be up Monday wanting to know had they fixed it and had they made their usage or forfeited it.

It'll be down by the third block, Harlan said finally. You can hear it. Hear the hiss.

Levi cocked his head and stood like a dog in a field. I hear it where it breaks and where it runs, he said. And I hear it in you.

Harlan stared up at him from the ditch. In me? What in me you hear.

You want to hear nothing. You want the line to be true and the head to hold and the soil to take and it is nothing such. We are bailing out a boat shot sideways on the shoals. I'm telling you. We ought to sell it.

Harlan turned the gate until the knotted veins stood on the back of his hands and he stopped it there and let the water take the lateral. He climbed from the ditch and wiped his palms on his pants. Your boy come last night.

Levi lifted his face so that the brim of his hat showed the vein in his temple. He did?

He was at the gate. Maggie dropped him and sat and watched him stand there in the yard like he was looking what side of the fence he'd live on. I told him you were down the road.

Levi nodded and said nothing. He took up the bucket and slung it in his hand and followed Harlan through the orchard. The rows ran on and they walked the furrows and came to where the ground had a lowness to it and darkened and the weeds were flattened and in that place the hose ran with water and there under the popple leaves a flutter where a small bird sat with his beak hinged and his throat a bright pulse. When they kneeled and parted the cover there was the pipe parted and fanged where the sun had bit it and in the mire a skitter of worms and spiders who had set up kingdom in the shade of the leak and now were dispossessed.

Harlan set down the bucket and took out a hacksaw blade and held the pipe together and cut the ragged end square. He took the other end and sawed it straight and wiped the muddy slime with the tail of his shirt. He shook the can of cement and the rattle of the marble in it sounded like the deadman’s bones in the tale the old men used to tell in the winter. He smeared the purple primer and the clear glue and fitted the coupling and held it and his hands had the tremor of age in them no man admits till the knife wavers and he knows that it is. Levi held the pipe and set the clamp.

You see the ground, Levi said. Look at it. That white scum. Thats salt. The roots dont want it. It wont go back. We sell. We take what someone'll pay. There's men will give you money just for the water. Tear it all and leave it. The money is better than carrying that black line into hell with you.

Harlan kept his eyes on the coupling and he wiped the glue that extruded with a dirty rag and breathed and the sour of the solvent was in his mouth and in his eyes. He sat back on his ankles. You talk like a man in a tavern. You go to that bar down there and they all talk this way. They talk to you like the land is something you can hold for a minute. We opened these holes for the trees when we were boys. Daddy dug at their feet. He had a mule then. Hot as now and that mule would stop and breathe and the flies would be pearling the sweat on his flank and Daddy said get on and we got on and we were not big enough to hold that iron a day long but we held it. I will not see it dozed.

Daddy is dead, Levi said. He’s had his feed and laid down.

Harlan looked at him. The sound of the water running in the pipe was a sound out of a shell, a low and private sea. You'd sell him if you could, Harlan said.

Levi's mouth took a set and he looked off into the rows and into that shimmering distance where the orchard seemed to unmake itself. I already did, he said. I sold him when I left. He had one son here and he had none. He died and he was glad to.

Harlan said nothing and they watched the glue set. A small garter snake slid out from the grass and put its head to the flow and drank unless it was only tasting the world and then lifted and went under the leaves with that same inhuman patience that carries a snake from a long day into a longer night.

They turned the lateral down and the water slowed and stopped and the pipe sat quiet in the ground like something that was not there at all and Harlan rose. We go down a bit. There'll be another. Where there's one there's ten.

They hauled the pipe and the bucket and the shovels over their shoulders and walked on. The sun stood now a hand above the horizon and the flies were awake and white-tipped blackbirds working the weeds for grass seed and small crickets. A blue heron rose from the swamp place below and went up with that big slow beat of wing like some ancient instrument in a minor key.

The second break had the story in it already wrote. They squatted and Levi put his knuckles to the wet and rubbed and raised his fingers and the water shone in the lines and he said salt. You can see it come back up when the sun paints it down into the bones.

You read too much in the paper, Harlan said. You got some man wrote column that tells you what happened in the soil a thousand year ago and what will. He never had dirt in his groceries.

Levi smiled and there was in it a tired love that you might see in a man when he watches an old dog go down a porch step more careful than in other days. Reckon so. But when they come to put that highway through the bluff and then changed it to the other side and left this track and then left the ditch and then told us they would pay for the culvert and did not you said then what good is a promise? You said that. Not me. You said when a man from the county shakes your hand you better count your fingers.

Harlan squinted at him and his eyes had the pale blue of tobacco smoke washed out. I said that, he said. And I say also that if you sell the places in this county to men want to make them into them tin barns they put on places that aint looked at except from the highway you aint done nothing better. You only give them the right to piss on it for you.

Levi took up the blade again and sawed the pipe. On the crosstie of a tree a paper wasp had made her muddy pall and she went in and out with her bright waist and did not regard them. The glue burned in the sun and the air was a saw. They worked. Their hands went to their tasks as if they had some older life at it that had gone away and come back and seated itself in them without asking. The coupling took and the pipe quieted and they stood.

They had brought a jug of water and a mason jar and they took turns drinking. The water was from the tap and had in it the dead taste of the town plant and they'd left the jar in the sun and the metal lid was too hot to handle and they laughed a little without humor and Harlan wrapped it in his shirt and got it off and they drank and sat there among the weeds a moment. A grasshopper came and fell against Harlan's thigh like a little stone and clung dazed and then went off with a lopsided clatter. The sky above them was of that color that you cannot say and the far fields lay like plates and the mountains to the west were a flat and older blue as if something had been drug across them by God and left a smear.

You going to the meetin, Harlan said. On Tuesday.

No.

You ought to hear what the water folks are doing. They take rights you aint using next year. They said that. They said if you dont put to your allotment you'll have it took and reassigned. Theres boys will come up this valley with three inch suction and they’ll lean on the ditch like hogs. You wont get nothing back. I told them. I told them they were thieves. They tell you to shut up or else. I'll be there and I'll tell them again. Spell it for them if I have to.

Levi did not look at him. He was watching a hawk that hung far off the end of the orchard and held itself in the light like a scrap of paper forgot in the rafters. They will do what the river tells them to and nothing else, he said. The river is down. The dam is down. By August we will be kicking at dry stones to see are they gold. This is the way of it.

It's the way of it when men say it is and like it to be. The river was not down when you were a boy here.

Levi looked at the black and antbitten bark of the apple tree beneath which they sat. He touched it with the sole of his boot. How much you think, he said. All together. All the land. The creek rear. The orchard. The slope. What you think they'd pay if we took it to them and set it on the table like a pig.

Harlan laughed and it hurt something in him to do it and he stopped. You got a library in your head. You can read the page and have it told to you the names of the things. Pay. They give you paper. Then what.

You got no children.

No. You do.

Levi nodded. I got a boy dont like me. You know what I do with money? I buy his liking and he holds it for two days and then he sells it back to me dearer. It wont ever pencil. I dont have a plan. I got what is.

Then shut. We work and we see. Give the place the summer and see what it speaks. I wont turn a thing loose till it has told me what it wants and if you think that foolish you can call me fool to my face and I wont strike you less.

Levi had leaned back and he pulled a burr of cockleburrs from his sock and turned it in his fingers. The burr pricked him and he set it on his tongue and bit it and spit and wiped his lips with the back of his hand. I called you fool already, he said.

I heard you.

They set to again and went down the line and made their bends and their cuts and fitted joiners and in some places the roots had come up and laid their osseous fingers across the plastic and in other places the rodent things had gnawed and in still other places the sun had cracked it like old horn. A jay came and cursed them and they cursed it in return. Presently they stood at the place where the line turned and ran to the block that was oldest on the place and Harlan held the back of his wrist to his mouth because he had barked it and the blood oozed between his fingers black in that light and a fly came and lit and Harlan blew him off and he came again and Harlan smeared him and wiped his hand on his shirt and Levi watched him.

You going to look like a flag if you go into town, he said.

Harlan only looked at him. The block had trees that dated to their grandfather and those trees had in them a wisdom that was also a kind of stupidity in that they could not think to move. The limbs were wracked and the bark in great plates and the crotches with black rot where the rain had held. Beneath one a torn tin pail and beneath another the skeleton of a coyote fallen in the winter. He lay there with one leg up as if to rise and the ribbands of his cage torqued and the skull with its rictus. A deershed lay there phossed with age in the wet and the snails had little drilled him for the calcium and set their houses from him and gone.

Levi squatted beside the coyote and he took the skull in his hand. It was light and he held it up as if it were delicate. He put the eye in the sun and sighted through it the green gloom above and turned it and when he set it down he set it careful. I made a mask of a coyote once at school, he said. For a thing we put on. We painted it. The teacher said what is this and I said it's a coyote and she said a thing wears on a man is called a head. I said what if the man is not a man. She sent me out. I was eight. I remember that. Mama laughed and asked me what did I say to that woman and I told her and she said you ought to be careful Levi you cannot say what in you to people and I said why and she said because it scares them.

Harlan was on his knees working another coupling. You always had a circuit, he said. You still do. I never knew what you were going to say next. I dont know why you think I'm the fool.

Because I am myself a fool and I know my own and you are a different brand, Levi said. He picked up the hacksaw blade and offered it handlefirst to Harlan. Harlan took it and cut and the blade sung as it parted the pipe and he wiped his forehead with the back of his wrist again and squinted and spit.

They had gone when the sun stood overhead to the truck and ate out of the cooler the sandwiches that Maggie had made. White bread and thin ham and mustard. There was a Tupper vessel with peaches drowned in syrup from last summers canning and they ate those with their fingers and the syrup sticky at their wrists and bees came and orbited and Harlan waved them but softly as at a drunk uncle and the bees went and came and went and a shadow of a turkey vulture sailed across them and the air a different weight under it and went on. They had a sack of corn chips and an orange. Harlan split it with his pocketknife into halfmoons and they ate them and pitched the peels. Far off the sound of a tractor on another place with a mower behind it and the heat going up in rods you could see.

You never thought about working in town? Levi said. Inside. Where there is a fan and a coffee pot and you know what you'll make and how you'll make it.

Harlan chewed chips. I did that a year when I got back from the army. I run pipe in a plant. They shut the plant. I come out and I set a hand to the ranch fence with Daddy. Daddy said you either die a little put by daily or you die all the once. Either way you die. Best to set your own hand to the where and the when.

And what if you set it wrong, Levi said. What if you point at a thing and say there and that thing dissolves. What of the man that points after and has no hand left.

Harlan wiped his lips with the side of his hand. I guess he goes and finds what’s left in him and sets it again.

Levi nodded. He held the mason jar in both his hands and the clouded water in it made a lens of the orchard and turned the trunks in its circle into fish with white bellies and he watched them swim.

After a time Harlan said what I wanted to say to you last night and I didnt. It’s about your boy. He dont not like you like you think. He dont know what it is. He dont see anything that is not his. He’s like the old cat drinking. He doesnt know it was your saucer or mine. He knows his tongue and wet.

Levi smiled. That is a kindness to a man to tell him that. I havent had many kind lights thrown on me.

It's not a light. It is only that they are not us and what a man grows into is what he can see. Maybe later he sees that a man is his father and not his enemy. I seen it.

Not in me you aint, Levi said. He said it without heat. He put the jar down and stood. Let's bend our necks like them mules.

They went back into the rows. By two they had it held as far as the old block and Harlan rose under a limb and hit his hat brim and knocked it off and swore and Levi picked it and handed it to him.

Dont swear, he said.

What kind of talk.

Dont swear. It's the one thing you do that you wont get back payment on. Daddy said that.

Daddy cussed like a man building a gallows every day.

He only cussed at what he made. He didnt cuss the wind.

Harlan put on his hat and went on. They came to the head of the block and to the manifold where the lines were ganged and there the ground was black where the water had made a swamp for a week and the smell of it was like a mouth long closed and opened. They found the split at a tee and dug it out with their hands and Harlan took the coupler and the glue and looked for the lid and set his thumb on nothing and patted his pockets and turned and Levi laughed.

You had it under your knee a minute ago.

Harlan scowled and got up and looked down and he was kneeling on the purple bucket lid and he picked it and loosened it and the lid made that little soft break and he dipped the brush in it and the purple ran down like blood. He painted and glued and held. The world went on with its quiet. Ants in a column and a ladybug up a blade of fox-tail and the coyote skull with its remnant wisdom and the trees leaning toward nothing they could know. The sound of something small chewing somewhere in the vines. The sun printed the leaves upon the backs of their necks.

There come a time when a shadow was thrown and they turned thinking cloud and it was a truck. It came down the track lazy as if it had all time and the dog riding in it upright with his old paws upon the dash and the neighbor with the frost-white hair and the eyes so pale that you could not tell the iris from the stone. His name was Ames and his father had come to the valley a hundred year and they had kept goats and then they had run sheep and then they had a son who drank and put their place into hock and now they went on with five horses for board and a handful of mommas with their kids in summer for the morning ride. He leaned his elbow on the window.

Boys, he said. You still at it.

We are, Harlan said.

Ames nodded. He looked out at the orchard with no change in his face. They say they gone cut you two more days next week.

Who say.

The district. I was up there for to pay the thing and the fat one with the neck says it in the office like he's saying what's for dinner. They want to run a bigger head to Bretton down South and they run it when they can and that takes your off. He told me that and I stood and looked at him and I told him no and he looked back and he said he had it in ink. I said you can have a thing wrote and still have it wrong. He says suit yourself and I said I intend.

Harlan let go the pipe and it held. He rose and his knees cracked. He wiped his hands. I’ll be up and see them.

Ames looked at Levi. You aint got a mind to get out while the gettings got any juice? I sold my river right to them boys going to build the storage and it was like clapping my own cheek but I got the money in a feedbag and I pour it across the bed every once and again to remind myself what it looks like. They say when they build that thing up at the pass they got to buy all the little points and carry and it's good money and I am old and I didnt care to go with my head against no beaver dam. I tell you what. You dont owe nobody nothing.

Harlan looked at Ames and then at Levi. There was a quiet there like when a man dies and you listen for him because you cannot reconcile the empty with the breathing. Ames said I'd be careful of them boys wants a little parcel right here for some project. If it says project in it then there is a man somewhere with a pocket big enough to hide the law in.

Harlan nodded. You talked to Mabel come to clean Mama's house? Ames said.

Not since the funeral.

She says there's a man from over Mesa come by there and left a card. He says he’ll take ten acres up by the bluff. I say how the hell he going to get to it and she says he's got a way.

Levi said he got a way means he got a man with a gun or he got a friend with a seal. Thats what it means. He flicked the dirt from his fingers. He looked at Harlan. He didnt say anything else.

Ames took a breath. I dont want to badmouth nobody. They are all folks and a man has to do his. But the river is down. He looked away. It aint coming back. It is not. I am old. I sat with my granddaddy and he told me there was a time had fish in the ditch you could dip with a tin cup. I aint never seen that and you aint never seen it and your kids wont see it. Then he looked back at Harlan and he smiled then and his eyes went softer. But I tell you what. That old coyote lay there. He dont know any of this and he is twice as right as me. You want me I am down by the barn throwing the hay in circles for to please the mares. He touched the brim of his hat and ground the truck into gear and went on.

They watched him go. Dust boiled up behind the truck and hung in the path like the ghost of the truck itself. Levi said that was a sermon.

Harlan did not answer. He looked at the pipe and he looked at the trees and he took his hat off and poured water from the jar into it and set it upon his head like a crown of elemental wisdom and the water ran over and down and he stood and breathed and his eyes closed and he set back the hat. He reached and began to gather the cuttings from the day and coil the pipe tails and stack the empty glue can with the rest and tie the hacksaw blades together with a twist of wire that he pulled from the fence.

You go up, he said to Levi.

Up?

Go up and open the head a bit. Let her run and see what we've got. Keep your eyes I dont need a flood. Take the pole.

Levi took the gatehook and went off through the trees and then he was up out of the orchard and along the track and gone behind the willow. The light laid itself on the willow leaves and they seemed to tremble of their own sorrow. Harlan stood with his hands on his hips. He looked tired and he was tired. The years had made a weave in his flesh and the warp was work and the weft was the slow attrition of hope that seems only a thing you can put from you and you cannot.

He heard the water take and he followed it not by sight but by the sound. You could hear it say as if from some throat in the ground that it was going where you bid it and that it might drown or it might soak and that it did not care. The first furrow filled and bled and then the second and he watched the feet of the trees drink and he knelt and touched that wet black with his palm and held it to his face and smelled it and for a moment he had in him a small quickening and he stood and then saw down slope a plume.

Levi came back with the pole like a walker. He looked where Harlan looked. There it is, Levi said. The fox pulled the wire.

They went down and in that place the pipe had blown and the soil there had gone to a slurry and the circle already outwarding and through it ran a chittering and Harlan stood and the muscles in his jaw worked. He said all right. He said it twice. He set the bucket down and took up a shovel and set it into that mud and it made a flat plunk and he threw it and set again and Levi took the other and they worked as men work when there is nothing else to do but work. The mud was heavy and clung and they threw it and it slumped. Levi said watch the edge you dont want it to eat your boot and Harlan said keep your counsel and then shifted and the mud sucked and he leaned and his boot came free with a sound like something down there had let him go against its will.

There was a rattlesnake then. It lay curled on that tilting bank above them and Levi saw it first and put out his hand and Harlan stopped. The snake had his head propped upon his own body and he looked at them like any other citizen and his skin had a pattern upon it that no man would ever remember exactly and the sun took a scintillant shine upon his scales when he breathed. He had a slight abrasion across his back as if he had gone under the pipe and been scraped and under his chin the throat flicked and the tail made the small dry music that it makes.

Dont, Harlan said.

Levi had the shovel crosswise and his hands already shifting to put a blade to it. He held. The snake looked from one to the other and his tongue lapped with the immeasurable speed such animals have and then drew. He did not show his teeth. Harlan set down his shovel and picked up a piece of latigo they had cut from a hose and he laid it over the snake's forward half and the snake went over it and under it and in a moment he had his head out and the rattling stopped and the snake ran in his shame with his head down among the weeds. They watched him go.

He’s old, Levi said. I saw it in his rigs.

He’s as right as that coyote and Ames, Harlan said. He said it with a tone like a man who proposes to hang someone and expects assent.

They set back to it. The coupling there would not take and the pipe hissed at the seam and they tightened it and it hissed and at last they cut out a longer section and put in two couplers and tied it with wires to the tree and the hiss went to a whisper and then subsided and the water went on. Levi sat back in the wet and his ass fixed a dark print and Harlan leaned against a tree and his chest rose and fell. On the ground the reflection of the light in the water made a net of quicksilver upon the underside of the leaves and upon the bleached stick that lay there like bone.

If we are men we got to take a thing and see how it sits, Levi said. We havent done that. We been talking on both sides of our mouths since Daddy died. He hawked and spat. When Mama went I had half a mind to burn the house. I wanted to see it go in a night what it took fifty years to load. I wanted that bad. There is some call in a man to unmake a thing and it is strong and it is a kind of love too. I dont expect you to see it. You carry a ledger in your chest and I carry a tinderbox.

Harlan looked down at his boots and at the mud riding their sides and he looked up into the tree and into the black crotch where the rain had stood and killed and he looked out across the field and up to where the straw-colored hills lay and all that they had been had the feel of a thing already wrote and it made him sick to think it. He said when we were boys I hit you with a stick and you went down and the blood ran and I went to carry you and you bit me. You did. You did not want help. You bit me.

I did, Levi said. He smiled a little. I would do it now if you put your arm round me.

I know it. I know you would.

They stood and set to and by late afternoon the line held water all the way down to the tail where the seep went into the sandy cut and ran off to an old tile drain abandoned. The trees took what they could. The leaves upturned in a praise that no one had taught them and the sun dropped some and the wind had in it at last a little busy news from somewhere else. The heat broke as a fever will break and you feel the sweat on you go cold and you think yourself saved and you are not saved and you go about anyway with your head light.

They walked back up through the rows. Harlan counted in his head that he would go to town in the evening and put his name down to speak at the meeting and say what he had and they would listen or they would not but he could not do different. Levi counted in his head what the realtor had said on the phone and he had not told Harlan that he had taken such a call and the figure had been a thing with its own power and its own light and it had lain in his mind like a kind of snake and he could not look at a ditch or a pipe without seeing it there coiled.

At the head of the orchard he stopped. Harlan went a step and stopped also and they stood and looked at each other. The light was on their faces and on the stain of glue on Harlan's fingers and on the little dark hair curling on Levi's forearm. Levi said you will not stop me.

Stop you what.

You will not stop me going to see. To talk to the man.

Harlan set down the bucket very carefully and it touched the ground and made the smallest sound. He put his hands into his back pockets and he looked at the ground as if there was something there worth the looking at. You do what you do, he said.

Levi nodded. He turned to go. He stopped. He turned back. I will not do it without you. I will not sign with a stranger. I aint a viper. I come and I ask and if you say no I say come to the table and we see if the table holds. I cannot do it otherwise. I would go to a motel and put my hand to my beard and know myself a man without a name.

Harlan's eyes went bright and he stood a little more upright though he did not know he did so. He said we will go see him together. He said it flat. He said after the meeting. He said I will not sign. He said I wont. He said but I will hear him. I will look his eyes and see what creature lives there and what it eats. If it eats blood I will come outside and wash my hands.

Levi smiled. He took off his hat and ran his fingers through his hair. His hand went to the place behind his ear where long ago the scar lay from some childhood day and he touched it and he put the hat back on. Good, he said.

They went on up the track. The truck stood under the lone cottonwood and the cottonwood made a light rain of its seed that fell on the hood and on the windshield and on the shoulders of the men and in their hair and in the grass and on the water that went in the ditch and sailed and caught against the slimes and hung there like a small winter come in June. They put the shovels into the bed and the bucket and the coil of pipe and the roll of tape and Levi climbed in and Harlan walked to the headgate and stood there.

He stood and looked at the wheel and the plank and at the numbers burned by some dead man on the timber and he put his hand upon the iron and it was hot and he rested it there and he turned it not at all and then a little to tighten it and then back again as if he could feel in it the breath of the place and he did not want to choke it. He looked back. Levi was watching him out the cab with his arm crooked upon the window and with his head leaned like a boy's and the cottonwood seed all stuck to his shirt. Harlan nodded to him and Levi nodded and Harlan shaded the wheel with his hand and then he reached and drew it down a little and then he took his hand away and he came up and gotten in the truck.

They drove out. The track went up past the old barn with the cupola collapsed and the half a gate. The old scythe hung on a nail where their grandfather had left it and it had rusted under the weather and was not a thing anymore of the world and yet it was. The truck rocked. In the mirror Levi watched the orchard go small and the light on it and he put his fingers to the window glass and left the prints there and they were gone when the road turned.

They did not talk. A deer watched them from the shade of the locust with his head high and his nostrils paled and he stood not ten yards off the track and did not step. The river noise from beyond the cottonwood belt was a nothing now and whatever birds were there in that brake to go to roost had their say and the sky to the west had begun to take the color that comes a little before the gold, a color that has no name.

At the road Levi said you going to tell the water men that if they cut you you will die and go to their houses and haunt them.

Harlan kept his hands on the wheel. He did not answer.

Levi laughed softly. He said do not fall on them, Harlan. They are little men. They like the paper. Give them papers to eat. Put your rage away. You cannot spend it here. Save it for me when I come back with the man.

Harlan said you going to bring him over the hill like Jesus on the foal and all of them behind waving palm leaves.

Levi laughed outright then and his laughter was not a sound you had heard there in years and it went out over the ditch and over the field and the field did not answer and the ditch did not and the bird in the bush went on with her small business. He laughed and then he stopped. You mixing your talk with the book again.

I dont know the book enough to mix it, Harlan said. I got it from Mama sure where would I else.

They went into town. It was not a town big enough to be so-called and yet it was what there was and men there with their caps in their hands and their lives like two threads twisted tight enough to hold. The meeting at the district office.

The room was cool with its boxed air and the men from the old places sat in chairs with metal squeaks and leaned collectively as a herd might lean at a wall after travel. There were a handful of boys with checkered shirts new and their boots unworn as if some costume. There were women from the feed store in their ballcaps and a man from the bank always there to see what might be gleaned from the talk of water as a man might listen at the lattice in a confessional. The fat man with the neck indeed sat with his legal pad and his pen and a name badge pinned into his shirt so that you need not know him to know him.

Harlan sat and Levi sat and other men nodded at them and they nodded. The fat man shuffled a paper shuffle and scratched his name down and looked and his eyes went to the clock on the wall and to the phone in front of him and back again. He opened his mouth. The talk was what it was. The fat man said law and he said allocation and he said aquifer and he said the word drought as if to make it a thing separate from himself and he said we all have to take this in the chin together. An old man at the back said this is the third time you asked for my chin and I got but the one. A laughter like wind on dry corn moved through the room and the fat man smiled a workingman smile for the work of such smiles and went on. Harlan spoke his piece when he could and he cursed not and he told them that their ink had no right to stand over a man's field as if the ink were rain itself. The bank man wrote and the district men shook their heads in unison as if they had some harness upon them and did not know.

When they came out the light had gone down into the world and a smell of honeysuckle off the fence at the lot and Levi drew in his breath like he wanted to save it and put it away to take out when the stink of money was upon him. They stood a moment and then went across to the bar though Harlan despised it because he wanted at that moment a beer to cut through the dust of the day. They did not drink more than that and then they came back out and stood by the truck and Levi watched the door of the Chinese place where a man in a white hat looked out of the hot room as if he were trapped in a glass.

Tomorrow I bring him, Levi said. At ten. I called him when you stood at the window in there and watched them go to their cars. He is keen like a hound. Dont kill me.

Harlan looked up. The stars were not yet come very well but the blue out there was said to be infinite and the town lights held up a kind of dome that the night made its peace with anyway. I wont kill you, he said. Not yet.

Levi nodded. He put his hand on Harlan's shoulder. Harlan flinched and then suffered it. It had been years he could not say how many. Levi took his hand back. You know what I dream, he said.

No. I dont know what you dream.

I dream that this place is all under. Not of water. But a kind of sand that is water in the way it moves. Like the sea that is dry in that book. Men are walking on it with long legs and they do not sink and they wear red clothes like the kings of spades and the animals go down. We are standing in the yard and the wind is eating the porch rail from under our arms.

Harlan did not speak. He was looking at the hip of a woman as she went and the child at her hand with the little yellow light of a sucker in his mouth and the woman said something to the child and the child laughed and then cried and she stooped and the blue out there the more darker now and the first star there.

He said you always had another country behind your eyes. He said it without judgment, as a man will remark upon the color of a hill.

They went home.

In the morning the place smelled of cut weeds and of water and of the dry. The sun came and lifted itself and the cottonwoods along the ditch made a tremor in that early light and the orphan magpie with a foot without toes hopped and pecked and made his industry of it all. Harlan rose when the light came and took his coffee and stood at the kitchen door and looked out. The house was theirs though not both in the same measure and he had been in it his whole life same as the ghosts had and he saw the orchard and the line where they had worked and he saw a hen hawk drop off a limb and come across low and lift and he saw the water running and he thought of the fat man and his pen and he thought of the other man to come and his figure.

He set down the cup and he went out.

Levi came later in a shirt with pearl snaps and a vest and his boots had a lick to them and his hair he had combed and he had shaved and the place under his chin was red where he took it too close and he had on his face a look like a man about to speak in church for the first time. He walked out to Harlan by the head of the orchard and they stood not looking at one another.

What time you say.

Ten.

They stood and the light crawled, and a wind came then down the valley that had rowed late in the night over the basalt flats and over the river gravel and over the tin of a million roofs and it came and it set the cottonwood leaves in their speech and it moved the hairs at the backs of the men's necks and it made a small and fretful applause in the orchard and they did not move and Levi thought he would take the boy out into the trees and show him how to lift the limb and see what lives beneath and not be afraid of it and wondered if that could be done and Harlan thought of the gate and of the iron in his hand and the heat of it and of the rattle of the snake and of the words that he had yet to say to his brother that would cut and he did not know if he could.

A car came down the road and slowed and a cough in it like a laugh and it turned in at the cattle guard and crossed and the bars sang under it and the man came with his papers and his shirt that had his name above his heart and his hair. He smiled and said he was grateful to be out of the office and he put his hand out and Harlan took it and put it down and Levi took it and kept it too long and then let go and they stood and the man talked about water and he talked about a vision and he talked about moving old things to new and he laughed and you could see his gums and he walked with them into the orchard and pointed and said what could be and the words were like there had been a snow and the orchard under it and only the ghost of the trees like you could draw over them a different tree and have it grow by itself.

The two brothers walked there with him and they said not much. The man took papers out and he showed them something and Levi looked and nodded and Harlan looked and then looked away. The man said and the figure in him shone like a bulb and it lit up the weeds and you could not see the coyote any more and you could not see the insects in their minute hells. He smiled and said he was sorry about their father and about their mother and he said he knew it was hard. He put a hand on his chest as if to say that he had a heart and that it might be of use in this place.

Harlan reached and took the papers and rolled them and tapped them on his boot and unrolled them and rolled them again. He looked at the man and he looked at Levi and he said to the man you will leave us. You will go stand by your car and call your whoever and you will talk about how we looked like men who could be taken and we will not take. He said that now and he did not know that he would say that. He had meant to say something gentle as a hand on a shoulder.

The man blinked. His smile stayed though it changed its nature as a man in his sleep will change his dream and you can see it pass over his face. He said sure thing and he went and he stood and he looked at his phone and he made a little dance with his finger upon it as if to conjure something out of it and his mouth made the smile again by reflex like the knee of a dead man will jump if struck.

Levi looked at Harlan. You have to stop that, he said.

Stop what.

Talking like a prophet and a muleman both. It is too much.

Harlan looked back. He lifted the papers and he thumped them upon his palm and he said this. He said I aint going to do it if you say we aint, but I aint going to let you do it without this. He reached and tore those papers once and twice and three times and he let the pieces fall. They went out upon the ground like a small unanxious snow. Levi's face went white and then red and he came and he took Harlan by the shirt and Harlan's hat fell and rolled and in a moment they were boys there and they were men and the years all together and Levi's hand struck and Harlan's struck and they fell and got up and fell and the dust rose and might as well have been the plume of a bomb and the man at the car put his hands out and took them back and did not step.

Harlan got a hand at Levi's throat and he thought of nothing but the hand and he had that emptiness in him that is like a place where you could set anything and he did not and Levi's knee came up and they parted and lay there and looked at each other and their mouths bleeding as if they had had some red meal. They lay and they breathed.

After a time Levi rolled and got to his knees. He spat. He wiped his mouth on his sleeve. He laughed then. He laughed not as he had the night before but like a man who has just been told by the rope that the knot holds and that relief it gives you even if you are the one hung. He looked up at Harlan. He said I knew you would do that. I brought him for this. I brought him to see us like this and to know the kind of thing his paper is up against. He will go away. He will put a mark by our name. He will tell the other man he cannot do the same.

Harlan sat up and dust snowed from his shirt. He touched his mouth and his fingers came back red. He looked at the flakes of paper in the weeds and the ants already on them with no idea what to do and he laughed then as well and it was not a sound easily described. He said you son of a bitch. He said you son of a bitch. He crawled and he found his hat and he put it on and then took it off and looked at it as if some secret lay in its crown and he put it back.

They got up then and walked up to the man and Harlan put out his hand and the man took it and looked at both of them and he smiled his small and careful smile. He said well. He said I can come back. He said we can sit down at the table some other day. He said we are all men here.

We are, Harlan said. He looked at Levi. Levi was not looking at him. He was watching a bee go from flower to flower with that old monomania.

The man left. The car went up the road and the bars sang again and the car was gone and the dust hung and fell. The pieces of paper lay in the orchard and the wind took one and two and sent them against a weed and a grass blade and they stuck. The two brothers stood and neither looked at the other and after a time they walked down into the rows again and they came to the place where the coyote lay and to the place where the snake had been and to the place where the water had blown out and they stood and they were men with the sweat drying on them and with the little grasses bending in the slight breeze and they could hear in the far far the sound of the highway like the sea.

You will go to her, Harlan said finally.

Who.

Your boy's mother. You will go and you'll say be easier. You will tell her you did what in you to see to it that there was not so much to divide. You will say that on your knee and maybe she will hear it. Maybe not. I don't know nothing about that part of the world.

Levi did not answer at once. Then he said I will go. I will go because you told me to and I was going to go anyway and now you can have it as yours if you want it.

I dont. I dont want anything that you would do for me but I will take it because there is nothing else to take. It is like that.

Levi looked at the trees. The leaves had that thin underside showing and the light came through and you could see the veins in them, the maps that are their lifetimes and our lifetimes, and if you could have read it you might have seen that every journey ends where it begun and that the places you'd go are only circles of a different color. He drew in breath. He let it go.

They went to the headgate again and Harlan put his hand on the iron and it was not hot yet and he turned it down a little because it was enough now, that the roots would take what had been offered and that he did not want to drown them. He said take what you need and leave the rest. It was not a prayer and it was not an order. It was the smallest talk that men make to things that do not hear.

Levi said when we are dead what then.

Harlan said in the river there's always a big fish where the tailrace comes out and we go by him and he goes by us and thats the truth no man can sell even to himself. He looked at Levi. He put his hand out and put it on his shoulder again and this time he did not flinch and Levi stood and the two of them looked down the length of the orchard and at the last long row where the trees seemed to run into the light and go out. The wind came and spoke and they did not answer it and they did not not answer. They stood. The day held itself and then it went on. The water ran in the little black tubes under the weeds and out to the fruit trees and down along the furrows and into the earth. The earth took it. It took as if it had a mouth somewheres and was a breathing thing, which maybe it is. The men stood and did not know what all had gone into them and would not until it was called out. The cotton flurries of the cottonwood came down and clung briefly in their beards and in the wire and on the black pipe and then the wind lifted them and carried them off to nowhere in particular.
