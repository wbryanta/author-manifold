By the time they called foot passengers to board, the trucks were already reversing into the open mouth of the ferry, white plumes of diesel unfurling under sodium lights. The quay smelled of oil and old fish, tar and salt, the sort of smell that you think is a smell of the sea until you spend time on a beach that the sea still owns. He stood in line with students and tourists and three men in high-vis jackets with their lunchboxes. A woman in a red parka held a sleeping child against her shoulder and kept her head tucked into the child's hat as if that could make a second warmth. The tannoy clicked and a recorded voice told them that the crossing would be eight hours, that the bars would close at midnight, that the muster points were clearly indicated and there would be a safety demonstration before departure. The wind came in gusts along the quay and made the little flags on the terminal building slap flat.

The agent scanned his ticket and touched her index finger to the screen to show him his cabin number, though he had not booked a cabin. He had not thought about sleeping. He had booked a reclining seat in the lounge and told himself it would do, as if that scant sentence could hold the night. He moved with the tide of bodies up the gangway, felt it shift under him with each step. He could have said he was only there for the crossing. He could have said he was only there because he had been asked. He kept his mouth closed like a man carrying a note under his tongue.

On the vehicle deck he glanced at the straps, the chains, the way the men with beards and heavy boots leaned their weight into the ratchets to make a body of the machines and the ship. The bay was lit too brightly and painted with fire lines and chevrons and signs that said "No Smoking" where men smoked anyway, tucked deep just to the side of a pillar. A gull stood with one foot lifted and watched them from the shadow of a bulkhead, its eye pale and hard and older than theirs.

Up on the passenger level the air had that closed carpet smell he remembered from other boats and other times: spilt pepsi syrup gone tacky, the sweet plastic of children's play areas, the smear of a hundred kind of shoes and the breath of anxious people. The lounge had a mural of leaping dolphins with aluminium smiles. The television nearest the bar was showing a football match with the sound off. Near the windows, a father was teaching his boy the names of the ships at the next berths. "That's a bulk carrier," he said. "Say bulk carrier."

The seat he found had a footrest that squealed when he pulled it up. He set his small bag on the empty seat next to him and sank back and tried the posture of a man relaxing. On the screen of his phone the name from the earlier call sat at the top of the list like something pinned on a noticeboard: PC Innes. Unknown number. Had introduced himself as Police Scotland, had said that the coastguard had brought a man in that the weather had not been kind to. Had said: There was no ID. Had said: There is a ring, and a tattoo, and there are three people we think he could be. Your number is on the list. Could you come and see. Had said: We understand it is a lot to ask.

He had said he could come. He had not asked about the other two. He had not asked about the way the man had ended in the water. He had let the phrases rise and fall, the voice doing its work of making this sound like one more of the things in which people did what was required of them.

He should have called someone. Instead he went to the self-service counter and bought chips in a paper cone and a cup of coffee that tasted like something trying to be coffee. A girl in a blue polo shirt asked him if he wanted curry sauce with that and he looked at her hands, the way the knuckles had little bites taken out of them, and said no because he did not want to say yes to anything he did not mean. On the screen behind her head an animation ran, over and over, reminding them of how to put a lifejacket on, as if putting it on right could ever fix the wrong of needing it.

The ferry vibrated with the engines already at idle. Then the safety demonstration, with the men on the screen dragging the straps of the bright orange foam vests, pulling them tight across their chests, the children curious and loud because it was a game until it wasn't. The coffee too hot to drink sat on the table and the paper cup left a wet stain like a ring on the fake wood. He held a chip in his fingers and looked out at the darkness past the glass. The quay lights doubled themselves and floated shark-pale. The sea moved under the dock and made it breathe so that the ropes rose and fell. The body of the ferry shuddered and then tugged forward and they were off.

When the last lights of the town pulled back into a run of yellow that bent outward into a thin string and then into nothing, he took his chips back to the lounge, found his seat again. The woman with the sleeping child sat three seats over now and rocked the child's foot with two fingers, a habit of touch that did not wake them. Across the aisle two older men with big watches were taking turns telling each other boat stories. "Sea was like a table. You could've rolled a marble on it." "Aye, until it wasn't. Remember that time with Donal—" "Jesus, that man. Storm came on like somebody opened a door."

The name that had been said to him over the phone sat in him like a stone. Actually the phone voice had not said the name. The man had said your brother under his breath, the way you say it to bring someone closer so you don't have to say their name out in the big room where anybody could hear. He had said your brother as if testing him with it to see how it moved in him, if he would grip it, if he would hand it back. He had said: Might be your brother. Might not. Would you come.

He had said yes in the same way that sometimes men nod even when they have not heard the question. Then, later, had said to Sara, "There's been a call," and, "They think it might be Callum," and, "I have to go," and she had been quiet. He could tell from the quiet that she had not expected it ever to be anything else with Callum than a slow unspooling at the edges of other people's lives, gradually thinner, tending to nothing. "Do you want me to come?" she had asked, and he'd said no, and then she'd said, not unkindly, "I suppose you know what you're going there to do," and he had said he did and known he did not.

He stepped out onto the deck with his coat zipped to his throat and the hood up. The wind, when it blew, came as if flung from a height. He walked to the lee side and kept his shoulder against the metal. The paint was that municipal green all ships seemed to find, the screwheads under it domed like the backs of beetles. He breathed through his mouth and tasted the cold. The night was clean-edged, the stars squared up in their thousands, and the band of the cloudless dark thrown from one horizon to the other like a plank he was expected to cross. The bow worked like a chest in front of him: inhale and forward, breathless; exhale and a careful sag. Far aft, away from the half-lit patch of deck where he stood, he could see the yellow of the funnels and hear the ferry's voice less as a noise and more as the idea of a noise.

He could not remember if Callum had liked ferries. They had been taken on them as boys enough times that liking didn't enter into it. Ferries were what happened between mainland and island, how the day made a seam down its middle. He could remember, once, a time his father had taken them out on the fisherman's return trip and Callum had pulled himself hand over hand up the chain to stand right under the bow so that the spray would break over him, little boy sodden and high on it, and his father had put a hand on his belt to keep him from going farther and had pointed where you could see the line where the tide poured through like a river. "There. There you see what it's called. Strait," his father had said, and made them both say it out loud to own it: Strait.

He could remember, too, a winter storm when the ferries had stopped for days and everything ran thin on their street: milk, bread, patience. Then the boat had gone out at last in a weather window and come back into a squall. He could remember the sound it made when it drove into the berth: the long black moan of metal against rubber and the single short shriek when it finally caught. He could remember how his mother held a scarf over her hair with one hand, the other hand covering her mouth, even though she never covered her mouth for anything else. He could remember Callum huffing like a dog because the wind was so strong it stole his breath, and then the way he would tell that story to anybody who would listen, and then how he would tell other stories too, stories where he was always the one who went right to the edge and came back with whatever it was in his hand.

He could remember the two of them in a cheap pub in the city, years later, Callum nursing a pint longer than he had to, long enough that it made him seem careful. He could remember letting himself think that was a change. He could remember the story that time too, bigger now, testing other scales: a boat off Ullapool where the nets had snagged and something had given way and Callum had been the one up to his waist in it and quick enough to get out, and the man next to him hadn't. He had told that one once with a hand flat on the table as if feeling for the boat as he talked. He'd then stolen the fiver under that hand fifteen minutes later when he went to the toilet and never come back.

He went back in when his face hurt. In the lounge a boy had unzipped a carrier and let a little dog out onto his lap and a steward came and told him to put it away, not unkind, twice. In one corner, the two men with watches had moved on to telling each other about wins of a particular sort, the wins you got by being older than somebody else. He took out his phone and typed a message to Callum he was sure would not send.

On the move. Ferry overnight. Call me.

He almost typed: This is your last chance. He almost typed: I am going to go and look at a dead man and say it is you. He almost wrote: Don't do this to me. He erased it and pushed the phone back into his pocket and sat there with the knowledge of it as if he had still said it aloud and enough of the world had heard to make a difference.

The crossing had its little dramas. A child sick and the mother moving her hand in circles, murmuring there you go, there you go. A loud laugh from the bar from someone to whom nothing bad ever came as a surprise. An elderly couple with a picnic tin that had a dent the size of Africa in it. Past midnight he went down the hall to the reclining seat area with its lights dimmed and its snorers fallen into private groans. He found his number and laid the coat across him the way you do on trains. The ship rolled and lifted and the rhythm found a level in his body that could have been breath, could have been panic. Near dawn he woke because he dreamed he had fallen, because something in the angle of his body had changed and told the oldest part of him to wake, because in the space between wakefulness and sleep is the gap where you drop down through yourself forever.

He sat up. The air was stale and someone had taken their shoes off. His mouth was dry from the coffee. He took the phone out and looked at the time and then at the blank screen where there would have been a missed call. For a moment he wanted to speak to his mother, which surprised him the way you sometimes surprise yourself by reaching for the shoulder of somebody who's not there. Then he remembered in full, and not the kind of remembering you have when you retell a story, but the sort you do in the dark to yourself, with your hands on the thing you know. He rubbed at his eyes with thumb and forefinger as if that would press the shapes of the words back in through his skull so that he'd have some say over how they came back out.

He went up to the deck again because the motion was easier to take out there in the cold. He timed it so he reached the door when another man came the other way. The man held the door for him and nodded and he stepped out into a wind that had gotten into its stride. The sky to the east had a seam to it now, a place where it admitted that light might come. He walked to the rail and looked at the water and realised that the word on it today was not black exactly, not grey exactly, but some other thing that those colours agreed to be between themselves. In the nearest waves the lines ran like the grain in a piece of wood, knotted and then smoothed, something made by pressure over time.

"Flat as it gets," a voice said beside him.

He turned. The man had the look men who work outside get after the winter: grained themselves. Close-cropped hair gone silver, cheeks cross-hatched by an hour and another hour and another. He wore the company fleece under his oilskin and had the logo of the ferry stitched on it as if stitched onto him. He had a mug in his hand and steam from the mug climbed straight up and then broke.

"That's flat?" he said.

"For here." The man grinned a little. "You from here?"

"Yes," he said out of habit, and then, "No. Not really anymore. I used to be." It felt like a confession. As if a place could be disappointed in you for not staying the shape it made you.

"Aye," the man said, as if that explained all of it. "You going up or coming back?"

"Up."

"Work or family?"

He almost told him the truth just because the question was so ordinary and it would be allowed. "Family," he said.

"Good. Better than work most days. Worse on some."

"Are you from here?"

"Born over there," the man said and jerked his chin to the bit of horizon that he would know just because he'd wanted it to be light enough to see the line of hills he already had memorised.

There was a pause in which another ferry employee came out to smoke and glanced and then decided not to stand near talking men. The first man looked at him as if to say: Well?

He said, "They phoned this afternoon. The police. They have a body, they said. They think—" He didn't want to say it as a sentence. He said: "—it might be my brother."

The ferry man's face didn't change. He blew on his tea as if it'd be the tea that expected him to take sides. "Might," he said. "Loads of might between here and there," an expression old men used about a football passing eight feet to meet a foot: loads of might between your idea and the place you mean it to land. "You not seen him lately?"

"No."

"Hm."

The ferry man leaned his elbows on the rail and put his mug down and made the grip course for a fraction of a second so the mug shuddered, and moved it quick before the ferry did for good. He looked out. "You'll be the third I know this year," he said. "I don't mean your man will be the third. I mean the being asked. Folk get lost. Water keeps its own minutes."

"Have you—do you—" He did not know the question.

"Me? I've not pulled any myself," the man said. "But you see it. You see the family sometimes. You recognise the way a person looks when they try to see someone they know where there isn't a person anymore. The ones don't take it in their eyes, they take it right in here," and he touched the meat of his chest with two fingers, a quick gesture nobody would see if they weren't looking right at him. "Anyway. Best you ask them for proof, if you're not sure. They do their best. They like a name to put down. But it's your right to say wait. They should have prints if he was ever in the system. Teeth are good. Scars are better. People think they'll know a face, and maybe you will. But faces go away from you when things happen."

"He has a tooth," he said, feeling ridiculous as soon as he did. "One at the front, a chip. From when we were kids. A fight. I hit the glass of the door with his face." He remembered the sound, bright as ice. He remembered the bright thin cut of fear, too. He had been thirteen. He had thought he had killed someone. The drop of relief that Callum had only chipped his tooth was bigger than any religion.

"He'll have other things," the ferry man said, kindly now. "We all have other things. You keep a thing on you for twenty years, you'll have it. Rings. Burns. Any tatts?"

"An octopus," he said. "On his shoulder." He didn't want to tell the man about that. It was private and also gauche. It was something Callum had wanted when they were very young, a blue octopus like a cartoon on an American boxer on a poster he'd stolen from somewhere, and then when he actually got it at twenty-three it had been not blue at all but the sad purple of old blueberries, and not on his bicep but riding just off his shoulderblade like a hitchhiker. He used to show it to girls in the pub. Making his back ripple to make the arms move. "And a ring with a nick in it where he—" he made a gesture with his mouth to stand for all the small events of their lives, all the hesitations of knives and the places where metal and skin had touched wrong. "—caught it on a bollard once."

"Aye." The man nodded. "They'll have his things laid out if they know what they're doing. They'll be decent with you, I’d think."

"I should've seen him," he said, as if to the ferry's rail, as if to the water. "Before this."

"Aye," the man said. "Maybe. Maybe not. You can't go to a man like that every day to see him. None of us are perpetual." He picked up his mug and took a sip and hissed at the heat and made the shape with his mouth that meant burning, that meant: see, pain, it's only this much. "You go with them from the dock? Or you got someone waiting?"

"PC Innes," he said. "He said he'd be at the terminal with a car."

"Good man. Try their scones at the cafe, mind. Margaret's back from sick leave and she bakes when she's angry." He set his mug down again. "Alright. I'll leave you be. Good luck with it, pal."

"Thanks."

The man went back into the warm, and with him went something of the belief that the ferry had by itself that everything carried would make it. He stayed out as long as he could. The sky opened itself by degrees. The lower clouds were torn and running. Far ahead, not much more than a darker hunchback against the grey, the island came up where it had always been.

He dozed again sitting upright, woke at the announcement, woke at the different pitch the engine made when the captain throttled back to come in under the headland, woke at the backwards shaking that told him that the bow was turning, that the boat had made its last little slide to the black mouth where the chained tyres waited. The car alarms went off two levels below and made their sorry chorus. The woman with the sleeping child stood and hoisted the child to her other shoulder without the child waking and patted the child's back and looked at him as if he were an answer to something else she would ask later.

Ashore the air was a different cold, off fields and the harbour and the low houses with their curtained windows and the gulls already in committee on the roof of the fish plant. The terminal had a poster about the Junior Pipe Band's cake stall and a man in a fluorescent jacket sorting plastic crates into the colours that meant more to him than to anyone else. He saw the policeman straight away without trying to. Maybe it was the tidy beard, or the way he had his hands in front of him as if he had been told once to do that when he was waiting because it meant you were under control.

"You must be David," the man said, using the name as if he had seen it printed on something and had been checking the pronunciation against his own head for the last half hour. "I'm Alastair Innes."

They shook. The policeman's hand was dry and the squeeze barely there and then gone, like something to do to open a gate. "Thank you for coming," he said, and gestured with his head towards the door, as if to soften the fact that he was not going to put a hand on him.

"Do we..." He looked past the policeman to where there would be a mortuary van if there was going to be one and saw none. "Do we do it now?"

"In your own time. But best now, aye. The doc'll be at the cottage."

"The cottage?"

"Old croft. We keep the kit there now. The new place at the medical centre's not ready and we had to move things quick because of the flood." He must have heard the question moving in David's mouth. "The generator's on. It's all proper. You mind the doc's got a thing about shoes on the inside — he'll be in socks like a child, but it's only so's he doesn't have to clean the flagstones so long."

They walked to the police car and if it had not had POLICE on the side you would have thought it was any other decent family car, with some old wrappers under the passenger seat and a smell of air freshener that was trying to be an evergreen tree. As they pulled away from the harbour there were already three men standing at the rail looking at the boat as if looking at boats was what they did at that hour and the hour after that. The road took them right and then a sweep left, past dark fields and a house still with its fairy lights up in a single straight line like the teeth in the jaw of something patient. Sheep in pairs and threes motionless like white punctuation in the middle distance. A little wooden hut signed "Honesty Box for Eggs" with the hand-painted letters running up at the end as if the writer had been overcome by hope.

"How many do you have," David said, his mouth thick because of sleep, because of the thing they were going to do together, "like this? Bodies without—"

"Not so many," Innes said. "You'd be surprised. But it seems a lot when you hear about them. The water gives what it gives back when it chooses. Some we never get names for. There was a man three summers ago and we had him out of the water in a net. No face left on him. The North Mission gave him a plot, we gave it a number, the paper gave it two paragraphs on page five. It's a better way for him than a lot of the ways men go. The women you can't talk about at all without making it lurid. We had a lad on a motorbike in March and that did for me for a while. Didn't stop thinking about the mother. How she had to stand at the bottom of her stairs and think about the stairs upstairs."

They turned off down a lane, the tarmac giving up and the stones pecking under the tyres. There was a dry-stone wall black with lichen. The cottage stood with its back to the wind in a flattened space and there was a cow two fences over lifting her head slow and careful the way cows do when they think men have come to put them back where men think they belong. Innes killed the engine and there was a silence that did its best to pretend the car hadn't been there.

"Listen," Innes said, low, practical. "Whatever you say in there will be fine. If you think you recognise him, that is enough for me to start certain papers. But if you don't know, don't say you do. It's no sin to not know. There's things can be done. We can take a DNA scrape from your cheek and send it to the lab. We can look for his dentist. It's only we call you because sometimes it's as simple as the man having a tattoo of a swallow on his sternum with your ma's name under it. Sometimes it's only the shoes. People remember shoes."

David nodded. "His name is Callum," he said to make it real.

"Alright," Innes said, with a little tilt of his head to say he'd take that into the shape of what they were about to do.

They went inside. The flagstones of the floor were cold enough to speak up under foot. There was a table without ornaments and four straight-backed chairs and a kettle on the hob that looked new among the old else. The doctor was there, a small precise man in a sweater and socks, the blue elasticated cuffs of paper overshoes around the ankles making his legs look even thinner. He had a length of hair that fell across his forehead and made him look in that moment like a boy who had been told to stay inside and read.

"Mr Rae," he said. "I'm Harper. We're very sorry." He did not make the sentences longer. "Would you like to sit for a minute? I can tell you what we have."

"No," David said. "I—" He wanted to see it quick, for the seeing to be the thing that got done, so the rest could be done around it. He didn't — didn't want to admire the doctor's good manners. "Please."

"As you wish," Harper said. He glanced at the policeman and then at the far end of the room, where there were two large objects under sheets, as if the cottage had gone back to being a place where they had no desire to dust the furniture.

Harper went to the nearer of the covered shapes and put one hand on the leading edge of the sheet and looked at David and only when David made the smallest nod with his chin did he peel it back.

What there was under the sheet was a human being on a stainless steel gurney. What there was under the sheet was also, at the same time, something found. David had imagined rotting, had imagined the cartoonish bloat he had seen in films made by people happy to make a strange thing part of a plot. He had not imagined the ordinary of it. The man's skin was pale. There was a sound in the room of a small motor, something to do with the boil of the kettle or a pump. The man's hair was the length of hair you might have if you had cut it two months ago with a friend doing it with clippers in a garage. It had dried into the suggestion of spikes. The eyebrows were a surprise, darker and heavier than he expected. There were beginnings of freckles across the shoulders. The eye that he could see was closed; at the corner of it was a little smear of crust that at a distance could have been salt. The mouth was swelling a little, ridged at the edges where the skin was broken. The ear was bruised, fine little bursts under the skin like wine. He did not know the man. He could have said that straight away and made it a simpler thing.

"Take your time," Harper said.

There was a tattoo on the back of the left shoulder. To the left of the spine, sitting not on the arm but between shoulder and blade. It had the heavy-ink look of something two years old and cheap. It had been applied by someone who had looked once at an octopus in a book and then gone home and worked from a memory as if the skin were a page you could always rub out. It was not a good octopus. It had too few arms and the head was more like a squash. But there it was, ink, someone else's decision made into this man's idea of himself while he had been living. There were two healed cuts that traversed the ridges of the knuckles. One of the fingers had a nail with a half-moon of oil in it as if he had worked that week and it had been the sort of work where your hands leave cleaner only slowly.

"Could you—" he had to swallow to make a sound and hated that he had to. "—can you show me his left hand?"

Harper came round the side of the gurney and took the hand up with a steadiness that made it clear he had done this before, the way he held it under the thumb and let the wrist float so that the arm lay as if it wanted to be that way. The fingers were swollen. The index and the middle were longer than David would have put them, looking at them. The ring finger wore a ring. A plain band. When Harper turned the hand so that the inside showed there was the nick along the edge of the ring, a place where the light changed as on a blade.

He had a pressure up under his throat as if something was trying to get out without him. His body was stupid with it. He had wanted it to be a yes or a no like the push of a red button or a green. He was badly made for choosing in the middle.

"Do you need to see his face?" Innes said. "I can get you more photographs of your brother if you have them. We can talk to—"

"I want to see," he said, hoarse now and surprised at the roughness. "I should see."

"It'll not be—" Harper started, and then did not say the word he had thought he might say, which might have been pretty. "It will be the way a face is when it's not in use," he said instead, like a man making a repair by choosing the right sized washer for once.

Harper went round and took the sheet down further. The man's face had been knocked against something, or rolled along rock. It had that look to it: valleys and little purple hills, the broken geography of a land you would not live in. But under it, through it, hovering just behind the ruin, was a thing a person carried with them whether they consented or not: their look. The angle of the nose. The way the bottom lip curled when relaxed. The stubbornness of the brow. He waited for that to answer him.

In memory, things sometimes look at you from a position they never held. Callum's face, in his head, had always turned a little to the left because he had stood to the right of him when they were boys at the sink in the bathroom spitting toothpaste into the enamel basin with its chipped place near the plug, because the light had always come in past his left ear when they played cards at the kitchen table, because in the only photograph of the two of them together after they were men there was a girl to Callum's left that he looked at. He found himself sliding right, trying to get to the angle.

The man on the gurney had a small scar in the hairline above the right eye, a pale smooth worm under the hair. Callum had a scar at his hairline too, but on the left. From the time they'd been running down past the shops and there had been that boy from the next street—no, he stopped himself. He was not allowed to make the world tilt because he wanted. He was not allowed to slide the beads along the string to make the sum he wanted. He looked at the man's teeth. The front tooth was whole. The chip he had lived with in his brother's mouth for twenty-five years like a punctuation mark of their relationship was not there.

"No," he said. The room heard itself settle with the word. He said it again because it was also the other kind of spell, the one you had to cast over and over to make it do what you wanted: "No."

Harper nodded once and covered the man's face as if to make the word true.

"The ring—" Innes began.

"It's like my brother's ring," he said. "But rings aren't only one of a kind. It's not him. The tattoo is like his too but it's wrong. He had this—" He found himself moving his own shoulder as if to show them the orientation. He stopped. "I don't know who this is. But I know who he isn't." He needed them to believe him. He needed to be the instrument of this one right thing.

"It is fair to say no," Harper said. "I will record that you do not know him, that there are points of similarity, that you are unsure but you do not think it is your brother."

"I am sure it's not." He surprised himself with how fiercely that came. It was as if his body had stepped in to do the thing when the rest of him bogged down. "I'm sorry," he said then, because the thing you say to any room when a dead person is in it is some version of sorry, sorry, look at me, sorry that we did not do enough. "Who—"

"We'll continue," Innes said. He had not moved and his hands were still in front of him as if he had been told to pose for a photograph a very long time and had held that pose out of politeness ever since. "There's a man they know of on Mainland who went in last week. We had a report of a missing Lithuanian boy from a boat six months past. He had a woman in Kirkwall who still looks at the bus times. We'll run out the rope. Thank you."

"Can I—" he heard himself ask, "—can I sit for a moment?"

"Of course," Harper said, and in the movement of him offering the chair there was something like relief, because now he could busy his hands with a kettle.

David sat, and Innes pushed a glass of water towards him without looking like he had. The glass had been chipped at the rim and the good thing to do would have been to turn it to drink from the notch so that at least the chip would have done one useful thing.

"You said you'd wait," Innes said without question-mark.

"I'll phone my wife first," he said, and then found himself correcting: "She's my—Sara. I'll phone her. Then I'll get the cafe scone someone promised me." He tried to smile at the space where the ferry man would be, as if the ferry man could enter any room through any door and crack the good joke that would cut the tension for an hour. He put his hands on his knees and felt the wrongness in them that told him they were shaking and watched them until they obeyed him more.

"Can you tell me about your brother?" Harper asked, not gently exactly, but the way you ask a man to take off his jacket because it's too warm and you can see he'll be better for it. "In case there's something else we can use to make sure either way."

"He has a scar from a dog," David said. "On his calf. He used to tell it like he fought the dog, but really he had put his hand into the dog's bowl while the dog was eating. He never liked to wait. He has a name on his shoulder he won't talk about. He has a way he stands like he's planted there and would make you go round him. He—" He stopped. "Had."

Harper nodded as if those were the things you wrote down in a file and typed up and sent to a lab and they wrote back and said: we've found him; he's in aisle six behind the rows of tins. He said, "Alright. That's alright," and glanced at Innes in a way that said: leave the man his grace now.

On the walk back to the car the wind came in squalls and pushed at his thighs and made the roofs of the cottages into long thin things that felt like it would be easy to peel them off. He opened his mouth wide because that thing about yawning had come into it again, the thing where at the graveside the body wants to breathe big because it thinks it is being smothered. A crow set off from a fencepost across the lane when the car door thumped closed and went hard-tail off with a complaint.

"Do you need me to do anything," he asked Innes, "for—this man?"

"Only what you’ve done. If you want to leave a number in case we need to ask again. If you want to give your brother word we thought we might have had him, if that’s a word you ever give a man, you can." He put the car in gear and then put it back out. "How is he for making himself findable?"

"He doesn't," David said, and the emptiness that came with that made it feel more true than anything else he could have said. "He has a phone when he has a phone, he uses addresses until he can't. He uses other people's names until they won't give them again. He doesn't stand anywhere long."

"Then he may not know he was almost him," Innes said. Not as a consolation, but as something like it sideways.

He went with Innes back to the harbour because you go with policemen when they drive you, because it is a thing you are grateful to not have to think about. The ferry had left already, its stern away, white wash dropping into the holes the propellers dug. He stood on the harbour ramp when the car drove away and watched what the wake did to the surface of the sea. It cut a line that fanned and then thinned and then made no distinction from what the whole sea was already doing, all the long layered unrest under the face of it.

In his pocket his phone hummed once and he took it out as if it might be a thing you could catch. Sara's text asked: Are you there? Is it him? and he typed with the slow fact of it: Here. Not him. I don't think. And then he added: I'm sure. And he was.

He could see the cafe from the quay, could see the woman behind the counter picking up the big knife that cut the scones in half and the man's back in the kitchen bending a dough onto a table, the thin hose at the sink snaking up and down to fill the kettle like something being charmed. He did not go in yet. He went along the harbour and sat on the low wall, the stone with its green fur of algae on the water side a soothing thing under his palm. He watched two boys in waders lower a creel on a rope into a boat with a third boy in it and at the first boy's shout the second adjusted and the third reached, the triangle of them making a right shape for a moment, and he wanted to tell someone that he had watched the way the weight was transferred and how it was a thing any three people could do when they held each other in mind.

He phoned his brother because there was nothing else to do with the half hour and because the unfathomable tastelessness of eating before he had done that had loomed up as yet another stupid small crime. He didn't expect it to ring. It did not. It made the shape of trying and then cut and went to a message that had the tone of a man using a public facility. "It's Callum. Don't...," a ragged laugh at the back of it as if he had been about to say don't but then had remembered he liked calls, that he liked the jolt of being wanted even if he didn't know what to do with it. The message had been recorded in some other year.

"You daft bastard," David said when the beep came. He said it very softly so that only the daft bastard of the man he loved could ever hear him say it, so softly that if the wind had not held its breath just then the words would have walked away over the stone into the sea and been part of it instead. "Answer your phone. I went and looked at a dead man for you. It wasn't you. I'm going to have a scone. Then I'm going to get a ticket home. You should call me before I get on and tell me you didn't fall in somewhere. Or if you did, tell me where you are falling so I can put a hand out. That's all."

He ended the call and wiped his nose on the back of his hand like a child. The boys shouted at each other, the language mostly laughter. A gull dropped three times and took a fish on the fourth, cleanly, as if it had made the fish present by wanting it enough. The cafe door opened and the smell of yeast and hot sugar came out as if the morning had decided it could at least do this one thing right.

He took the ferry man's advice and ordered a scone and when Margaret behind the counter asked him if he wanted jam too he said yes and when she put it down in front of him with the generous scoops and the little cup with its ridiculous paper doily under it he thought that sometimes the world did, despite itself, remember it could be kind. He ate slowly. The glass front looked out at the harbour and he sat there with his mouth full of butter and found it in him to think that this was not the worst morning that could have been.

He booked a passage back and walked the length of the street twice to pass the time and bought a postcard at the shop with the black cat on the mat and wrote on it: "Going home. Not what we thought either way," and addressed it to his mother's old address as if she were there. He stood outside the postbox with the card in his hand a good five minutes and only at the last did he slide it home. He thought of it falling onto the other postcards and the bills and the small folded dramas. He stood there with his hands in his pockets and his head down as if looking at his shoes was a job anyone could have forever and then when the wind fell he walked back to the quay to wait.

On the boat back the air had changed. The light took the colour in it that made things shine as if wiped down with oil and the little shadows that ran behind the bollards on the upper deck were tender about it. He went up where he'd stood at dark and leaned with his weight on the one place where the rail had an old dip from men's hands. A girl came out with a camera and took three photographs and went back in. A man in a wool hat brought out his father, slow, each foot a reason. The father leaned and laughed out his phlegm and said something about geese and the son nodded and they stood and David wanted for a second to fold himself briefly into their picture, the way a man might step into the shelter of strangers' umbrellas in the worst of the rain.

His phone hummed as the mainland line made itself present again, finding the cell towers it knew. The same unknown number as earlier on the screen, and he had the reflex of guilt before he registered the thing itself. It was a message, written this time:

Mr Rae, it’s PC Innes. The man has been identified by his employer; not your brother. Thank you for your help. Should you think of further distinguishing marks of your brother in the event we need to try elsewhere, please send. Good crossing.

He let the words sit in their order. He read the phrase not your brother twice. The ferry went about its business under him. The wake made its V and the gulls that had learned this ferry's habits came to ride the low pressure behind them, barely moving their wings, all calculation made to look like grace.

He typed a thank you because that was what you did. He typed that his brother's scar was on the left side of his head near the hairline and that he had once broken his little finger playing football against a wire fence and the bone had not knit straight and sometimes in winter that finger hurt and would go white. He did not send it. He deleted it. He typed instead: He whistles through his teeth when he's thinking. It seemed important to make the world contain this fact of a living person.

He tried his brother's number one more time. There was no ring now. It made the shape of nothing and then failed and the screen returned to the names he had that were not answers either. He spent a while looking at a woman across the room who might have been nobody and anybody while she read a paperback and chewed at the skin at the base of her thumbnail and every now and then pressed her mouth closed the way you'd do if you were going somewhere you were making up as you went along.

He thought about the boy whose body lay in a cottage with the boiler humming. He thought about the ring on the boy's hand and the nick along it. He pictured hands touching that ring once while the boy had been living; he pictured someone smiling at the boy when he had shown them the wrong octopus. He pictured the employer who had called and put the man's name back onto him. He made the small human bargain with the world that if he thought strong enough about those other people then somewhere else in a morning he would not be present at later, another man would think strong enough about where his brother might be. He let himself remember a very particular thing: the time Callum had shown up at his door two years after that pub and had sat on his steps with a cut on his knuckle and had whistled through his teeth and had said he could do better and had not, not then.

At dusk the wind rose again and the men with the big watches told each other the same stories they'd told on the way up, as if the stories themselves needed crossing back too to be complete. He slept for an hour with his head tilted and woke with a streak of drool across his cheek that he wiped away with the hem of his shirt because he couldn't be troubled to find anything else. In the last of the light there were low lines of blue shadow laid like broken ribs across the water and the ferry pushed through them and the wake closed them together behind. In the reflection of the window he watched himself watching and saw that he looked like his brother around the eyes when he wasn't careful, and smiled, not for anything happy, but because sometimes resemblance is better than news.

By the time the ferry came in the lights of the town were the same string of yellow but reversed, and the quay made the same long black moan when the boat pressed home. Men went to their cars. Women shook children awake. He stood and put his coat on and took his bag from the seat and threw the empty chip cone into the bin. The girl behind the counter in the cafe was already cleaning the display case with the concentration of people doing a thing at the end of a day that had not broken them. He walked down the gangway and back onto the mainland with a relief that had nothing to do with place and everything to do with having done a thing and having it be over.

On the quay there was a fox again, or its cousin, moving like a shadow that had practised first and got good at it. The air smelled of sea and traffic and a bakery closing. He took a long breath of it. Somewhere, a phone would ring and a man would answer and he would be the brother. He walked toward the car park with small sure steps, as if it were icy and he did not want to slip, even where the tarmac was dry. He took his phone one more time and dictated the message to the things you cannot send a person who has not even told you if he wants to be found.

Callum, he said, voice catching at the beginning and then getting itself in hand, it's me. I'm here. I'm going to be here. He waited, and the floor of the ferry terminal vibrated with tides under concrete he would never see. He thought of the strait and of the word and of the way it holds two words inside it: straight, and state. He thought how there was loads of might between here and there. He raised his head to the night, and at that moment a line of geese went over so low he felt he could have reached and plucked a feather out of the fat of the air, and he said, again, softer, so an ear could pick it out if it wanted him: I'm here.
