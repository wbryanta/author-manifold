By the time the sun shook itself free of the low belt of gray that passed for June clouds and pressed its heat into the hills, Ben had already snapped the brittle PVC with his gloved hands and cursed the way it splintered rather than cut. The line ran from the old pump house toward the east block of apricots, and somewhere under the coarse ryegrass and sticker burrs a section had cracked the winter before, slow enough that you didn’t see the puddle but knew because of the way the ground went slick and sucked at your boots when you stepped. Now the earth showed its wound, the two white mouths of pipe packed with dirt and spiders, and beyond them the block sat waiting, a hundred and fifty trees with their leaves turned dull as coins from thirst.

“You could have used a cutter,” Jake said, his voice muffled where his face was tucked into the shade of his shoulder. He knelt on one knee with the spool of patch pipe across his thighs, fiddling with the loose end like a nervous boy with a cord.

“Cutter’s in the barn with half the other things that grow legs,” Ben said. He slid his hand into the pipe and pulled out a wad of roots, fine as hair and slick as string. “And that plastic piece of junk won’t go through a two-inch anyway.”

“There’s the metal one,” Jake said. “The big one with the jaw. The one Dad—”

“That one’s rust-siezed,” Ben said. He wiped his hand on the dry grass, left a smear of brown that would not come out, then pushed back his hat and squinted at the way the pipeline sloped. The line had settled bad here when the gophers went to town last fall. He’d meant to trench and rebury, then the first rain came and the second didn’t and he forgot there could be a second. Now the pipe lay exposed like a spine. “This is fine. I’ll square it with the handsaw.”

“The handsaw,” Jake said, as though the word itself were an accusation.

“You want to fix it or you want to complain about the way we fix it?”

Jake said nothing to that. He rubbed the heel of his palm over the knuckles of his right hand and watched the midges form and dissolve in the heat haze over the orchard. The apricots hung pale and dun where the leaves had shaded them; a few were gilded where a sunbeam caught, little coins of color like a promise the trees kept making and breaking. The old Royal Blenheims never advertised themselves like the supermarket fruit did. They hid their sugar under dust and a thin suede skin that bruised if you looked hard. You had to cup the fruit like a live thing and set it down in a shallow bin and hope a picker didn’t pour it instead of place it.

Ben took out the saw from the back pocket of his jeans and chewed off the ragged lip left by his hands. The teeth squealed on the plastic. His right shoulder twinged. The cut came out crooked and he corrected it, and then it was a neat mouth again, ready to accept a coupling if you knew where you’d set it. He wiped the PVC dust from his fingertips. He could taste the solvent already in a future that had not happened; could feel the tang of the purple primer in the back of his throat, the sting of glue on his thumbprints. It sat somewhere in the old blue caddy along with every other little can in their life, the same caddy his old man had carried until the handle rubbed against the tendon in his wrist and a numbness crept up the heel of his palm. When his father could no longer hold a wrench, he held that handle to say something about what it meant to keep a place like this. People thought a farm was land and trees and weather. Their father had believed it was a handle you didn’t let go of.

“Hold this,” he told Jake, and handed him the can of primer. “When I say, you smear the inside of the coupling and hand it back. Don’t get it on your shirt unless you want purple forever.”

Jake laughed a little, the old boy laugh, the animal laugh that had nothing cynical in it. He peeled the lid from the can and the brush came free and a drop fell to the dirt and left a bright obscene flower. Ben saw his brother’s hands then, clean the way a man’s hands could only be when they’d been working with ideas rather than with bolts. Jake caught him looking and drew the back of his wrist against his palm, like he could roughen what was soft.

“New?” Ben said.

“Not new,” Jake said. “New to you.”

“Desk job hands.”

“Call them graduate school hands,” Jake said, and let out a breath as if it were supposed to be a joke.

They worked in silence for a few minutes, the old habitual rhythm growing up between them again even though it shouldn’t have known how. Ben scraped an inch off the ends, de-burred the lips with a pocketknife that had belonged to their grandfather and whose edge showed his blood in a thin line darker than the rest of the steel. Jake handed the primer, and Ben painted the inside of one end with broad swift strokes, then gave it back. When the glue came he moved as if the seconds were counted, and they were, because if you didn’t seat it right the first time, you’d be ripping it apart and wasting a coupling. He slathered the outside of the pipe, the inside of the coupling, twisted the pieces as he slipped them together until the purple went lavender and the friction took hold, then held the connection as if it were a bad dog you had to teach to heel. You counted to thirty aloud or in your head. He counted in his head and somewhere around twenty-two he realized he was holding his breath and let it out slow.

He could feel Jake’s watchfulness on the skin of his neck the way you could feel a cloud move across your back even with your eyes closed. Jake was the child of that watchfulness. He had always watched like that at the edge of things, waiting to be called to; always the one who learned by seeing rather than doing. That was not contempt. There was a kind of respect to it. But respect didn’t pay the ag-chem guy and the fuel man and the man who came to service the well pump, whose invoices were always scribbled on torn-off triplicate slips like he was doing you a favor by not keeping track.

“It’ll go,” Jake said, when Ben let go of the joint and flexed his knuckles against his palm. “You make it sound like a death grip.”

“It is,” Ben said. He laid the coupling down and reached for the next. “If you don’t hold on it pulls apart. The pressure’s got a hunger.”

Jake smiled. It curved his mouth boyish and unguarded and for a flash Ben wanted to ruffle his hair. Instead he lifted the bottle of water he’d set by the valve and drank from it in full gulps and let the cool trickle down the front of his shirt. The sweat had already formed its map there. He poured a little on his wrists. The old men in town would have told him don’t waste it. But the old men who loved to say that had sold their houses to their children and their orchards to their enemies and somebody else watered their lawns.

“Turn off the pump,” he said to Jake. “If it kicks on while we’re still dry-fitting, it’ll blow your teeth out.”

“Which switch?” Jake said.

“The green one with the cracked toggle,” Ben said, and didn’t watch. He knew by the sound—the low hum throttled back to nothing, like a man finishing his own cough. He knew the way the metal building’s skin relaxed a note when the diesel fired off, like a woman loosening a belt.

When he didn’t hear anything, he called, “Good,” and then added, “Just for this last section.”

“Okay,” Jake said, then a beat later: “This pump is older than some species.”

“New impeller three years ago. New elbow seven. The motor is fine if you don’t mind the electricity company loving you like a wife.”

“In Seattle we pay by the month regardless,” Jake said. “It never goes over one twenty a month unless it’s winter. They say we have the curves right.”

“In Seattle you don’t irrigate an orchard where the only rain is your sweat,” Ben said. The second joint slipped together slick as a trout and he looked at his hands when it set. Purple on his knuckles and glove, the sharp smell in his head. It was same as when he was six and his father let him smear the inside of the coupling while the old man pretended he needed him. He could remember that the way he remembered his first baseball card: a sense of significance attached to paper and ink.

They walked the line to the intake valve that teed into the east block. It was a simple gate with a rusted handle and cobwebs. Ben scraped the webs away with a stick and spiders fell and ran. They were flush with the ground even here. No good. The earth had that slumped look to it, the way you could tell a bed had been occupied. It had been a bad winter. Dry and not even a hard cold to kill things. The trees had pushed late and now they held on with a kind of stale faith. He didn’t like the way the leaves looked. The wind had a way of rasping them.

“You sure?” Jake said. “You sure it’s just this one place that’s leaking?”

“No,” Ben said. “I’m sure nowhere.” Then he nodded at the pump house. “Throw it on. Let’s see what we bought ourselves.”

Jake went to the green switch inside the pump house and put his hand on it like a man who had never committed a sin and who needed to feel the thrill of breaking a rule. He pushed it and the motor coughed awake and found its note. The pipes shivered under Ben’s hand. He turned the gate a quarter turn and then another. He was ready to jump it back if he saw the joint go. He pictured the glue still fresh, asked his fingers to be confident. The line filled and took water. The coupling held. He closed his eyes. He could see the block in his head, the distribution of pressure, the way the far corners of the orchard would get their share only if you bled the first rows. He opened the valve a quarter more. He imagined the micro-sprinklers under each tree going from sag to spin, imagined their delicate plastic arms flickering in place like the hacked wing of a small bird. He could hear in the distance the first patter where the nearest heads took. It was a sound he had slept to for years, even when they hit the bed so late the world had already turned.

He remembered the night they’d had to stay up because the old elbow on the pump had blown off and the line sent a white spear of water into the dark for an hour before someone—their father—heard and threw on jeans and a shirt no one was supposed to see and ran barefoot, and the lights flicked on and then everybody was up, so that the crickets paused and the whole orchard listened as if something real had happened. Ben remembered his father on his knees in the wet grass in the torchlight, his jeans soaked to his thighs, and the way he grinned when the water finally came through right. “There she goes,” he’d said, as if he were talking about an animal he loved and had nursed back. He had wiped his face with the heel of his hand, left a mark of silt that settled into the creases like age.

Across from the pump house now, a small geyser popped in the weeds where they hadn’t expected, a shoulder of earth gulping and coughing itself wet. “There,” Jake called, pointing as if his finger could hold the water down. “There, Ben. See?”

“Yeah,” Ben said, and was already striding.

The break was old and small and everything a break should be to make you hate your life: a hairline crack on the top of the pipe feeding row twelve. It had blistered up under the pressure like an aneurysm, held and then decided not to. Ben crouched and clamped his hand over it hard. It spit around his palm and into the cuff of his shirt. “Throw it down,” he yelled toward the pump house, and heard the motor slow in obedience. Jake, at least, learned quick.

They patched again. This time the sun was high and the steel on the gate burned their fingers and the bugs had found the opening in the heat and came at the drops of water like a city takes to a fountain. By noon both men were splotched purple and smelled like some chemical fruit salad. The hatband of Ben’s straw left a wet crescent on his forehead. He could feel the headache coming—less from the solvent than from the fact he’d been up late with the numbers again. The envelope from the bank sat tucked into a drawer in the kitchen. He had left the drawer slightly ajar as he walked out this morning, because he wanted to see the edge of the paper when he came back in, to remind himself he was doing this not because he believed in some burly American idea of land but because he could only stave off calls for so long.

When they sat in the shade of a barrel that had once held something a nicer orchard sprayed on its leaves, Jake fished two apricots out of his pocket and folded one into his brother’s hand. It was warm the way fruit is warm when it is right. The skin bruised where Ben’s thumb massaged it without thinking. The flesh yielded. He bit and the sugar went up his spine with its strange watermelon-annoyed-with-lemon taste. He bit again and the juice ran down his palm and gathered in the crease of his wrist.

“You think if you go to Seattle they have apricots like this?” Ben asked, mouth full.

“They have farmers markets with things in crowded green stalls,” Jake said. He had a way of turning everything into language the way other people turned it to hand. “They have little signs that say Royal Blenheim and you pay four dollars for eight and you eat one as you walk back past the people with flowers and everyone thinks this is what the world should be.”

“We sell four dollars for eight,” Ben said. “We go home to our shiny important lives. We ignore that the people who grew them have hands that hurt when the weather changes.”

“I never ignored that,” Jake said, but there was no sting in the way he said it. Then, after and because of the brief stretch of quiet that followed: “I saw the envelope in the drawer.”

“I didn’t hide it.”

“I know you didn’t,” Jake said, and set the stone of the apricot in the dust with an attentiveness that made you worry for him. “I know you don’t hide things. That’s why I hate this.”

“Hate what?” Ben knew, but he wanted the word on the air anyway.

“I hate the way you’re going to tell me we have to sell like it’s obvious,” Jake said. “I hate that you’re going to talk to me about yields and water and—you know all those words you learned from sitting with men who’ve been doing this long enough to be tired. And I hate that you’re going to make me feel like a child because I love this. I just—”

“We’ve had this conversation,” Ben said, but he said it gently. “You had it with me on the phone last month, and I had it with myself last year, and the bank has been having it with me for three years.”

“It’s not the bank’s orchard.”

“It is when the fruit is payback for the money they floated us to cover frost fans and a new pump and spray.” He laced his fingers together and felt the crackle of dry skin on dry skin—his hands had lost their fat. “The packer told me last week he won’t pay what he paid last year. He told me they don’t want Blenheims anymore. He said, ‘I got guys with fifty acres of Bings lookin’ to sell a story. It’s easier to tell a story when the fruit is red and looks happy. These…’ He flicked one of our apricots with his nail like it offended him because it didn’t cost him anything, ‘These look like what a poem eats.’”

Jake laughed even though he didn’t want to. It came out like relief. “And because he said it, it’s true?”

“It’s true because there are ten of him and one of me,” Ben said. “It’s true because the man who comes for our bins is the man who comes for theirs too and he’ll pick theirs first if mine don’t fill the truck on a schedule.”

“Then sell to the city people,” Jake said. “Sell to the markets. Sell to the restaurant that has a name everyone pretends to pronounce right.”

“It’s three thousand miles to your restaurants,” Ben said. “And there’s one market in town and they don’t pay enough to even patch the lines we’re patching. You want to drive a flatbed into the Bay in the morning traffic and get yelled at because you look like what you are: a man with dirt under his nails?”

“I’d do it,” Jake said simply. “I’d come back. I’d live—”

“You’d live in the trailer in the north lot and you’d let the raccoons sound like men at your window at night and meanwhile you’d go watch your friends have their second child in houses with no dust,” Ben said, and then immediately he wished he had said that differently. He didn’t like the sour that came into Jake’s face.

“You think I left because I liked dustless houses better than this?” Jake asked. He still held the apricot stone, rolled it in the pocket of his fingers. “You think I left because I wanted to move to a city where the sound of rain on the window is people who hate you because they can’t park? I left because you told me to. You remember? You told me to go. You told me I wasn’t—” He stopped, pressed his mouth together hard, then tried again, less angry. “You said you didn’t need me here the way it looked like you would.”

Ben looked out at the block while they found their next breaths. The micro-sprinklers that had taken spun on low, anemic. The nearer trees showed their gratitude in a deeper green that wasn’t yet real. In the back of the block the leaves still hung limp as laundry.

“I told you to go because Dad wanted you to have what you were supposed to have,” Ben said. “And because if we were both here one of us was going to end up hating the other every morning and every night.”

“Why?” Jake asked, but not as a challenge. A soft why. A child asking the mechanics of weather.

“Because this place doesn’t have room for two men who both think they know what the one way is,” Ben said. “Because I knew I couldn’t stand watching you do it the way Dad did and the way I did and then watch you not make what you need to live. And because—I thought maybe if you weren’t here, you wouldn’t feel the way I do. Like I owe the idea of this place something that is shaped like a tax I pay in blood.”

Jake leaned back against the barrel. He dipped his head until the rim of the hat made him blind to his brother. His hair stuck out in damp crescents at his temple. He was thinner than last year and older, which is something you could say about most people these days, but meant something in particular here because their father had gone thin and then age had caught him with both hands and said, Now. And he had gone in the tree row with a harvest bag cinched across his chest and the strap had struck Jake’s name in a damp stripe on his shirt when Ben lifted him, and later the mortuary had cut the strap rather than slip it off because it was less complicated to do it that way and they did it that way for all the old men.

“What if it’s not a tax,” Jake said slowly. “What if it’s just—what if it’s the way you show up for what made you.”

Ben felt a surprising flare of anger then and he almost welcomed it because it was clean. “The way I show up,” he said. “You think I haven’t? You think this is me not showing up? You come and stand beside a man’s work for a day and now you get to tell him—” He heard his voice pitch and hauled it down by its throat. He was suddenly ashamed of a sound in it that he knew, that he had heard in his father, a kind of trapped avian beating. He rubbed his palms together—the glue squeaked—and then he said, “I walk the rows at night sometimes. When there’s frost I walk in a coat so big it makes me look like a bear. I put my arms around the trees and I ask them to just make it to morning. Right? Here I am, a man, talking to wood. That’s what I pay.”

Jake’s face was soft now, not because the anger had gone, but because he had made a decision to love his brother in the middle of his own feeling. He had always been good at that. It was why Ben had believed he could go. “I know,” he said. “I know that. And I know I didn’t. I didn’t show up. I left you here with him while he got himself into that—” and he faltered because he would not call their father’s stubbornness that word they sometimes used for other men. He said, “I wasn’t here when he started forgetting.

“You called me and you said he couldn’t tell you what day it was and he thought you were me because he kept asking, Does he have school? And you said he licked his finger and felt the wind and went out without his hat and then asked where he’d put his hat. And I said I was sorry and that I would come and then I didn’t come for three weeks because I had a thesis and a defense and then when I came he looked at me like he loved me with everything left and you looked at me like you hated me a little and I deserved it.”

“I didn’t hate you,” Ben said. He had, but it hadn’t lasted more than the length of a morning because his brother had made them all scrambled eggs even though the pan was ancient and the eggs stuck, and he had sung their mother’s song as he scraped, even though they both had to pretend that it was something else so they wouldn’t ruin it.

The day grew massive and slow. The heat pressed down like the weight behind a hand that turns your cheek. The bees increased. A pair of kestrels tilt-shivered over the tall cottonwood by the ditch and the ditch itself carried only a scum of green, slow as a man who was owed money keeping you waiting.

They went back to the line. They pulled weeds from around the outlets so the heads could spin fully. They unscrewed a handful of the little spinners and flicked their throats with a finger to clean them. Jake pinched his finger on one with a little barb and sucked at it, made a face at the taste of bitter plastic. He worked with a zeal that tried to atone for something. He had a way of wanting to fix more than was broken. At one tree he jacked up a head that had sagged and then his heel sank into a soft spot where a gopher had tunneled and he went half down and cursed, then laughed, then looked, and the surface of his laugh stilled because his face was open to the complexity of everything always.

“You remember the year of the rain we didn’t earn?” he said, because that was how he did it: he reached back to a story and pulled it forward like a rope to bind things. “The year the hail came one afternoon and Dad made us drag those tarps out and we all got soaked and then at the end we sat under the awning and he put that heater on his knees and said, Well damn, boys, if that wasn’t the stupidest brilliant thing I ever did?”

“We lost ten trees in the back corner from rot because we held the water too close,” Ben said. “And we saved thirty because we got lucky the hail didn’t last more than eight minutes.”

“And you tapped Mother’s watch and were like, Eight minutes, eight minutes, like you could make it have been that,” Jake said, and smiled. “I never told you this, but when I рan that eight-minute mark felt like something reliable in the world. Like time belonged to us.”

“Time belongs to the men who want to take your house if you don’t send the check,” Ben said. But he smiled too because he could see it. He could see his small brother with his hair plastered, the way his eyes had looked giant because of the rain, and his father with his heater on his knees and his mouth making that shape—how he would make a sound the way an engine makes a sound when it’s happy. He had loved that sound.

At midafternoon, when the heads at the far end still refused to go, Ben walked the row to the manifold and laid his ear where the plastic made its whisper. He shut off three rows so the fourth might drink. You gave up sometimes not because you chose to but because there was only so much you could make happen at once. Jake watched him do it and then he said, careful, like a man who knew he was carrying a fragile plate, “What if we just—what if we let a man come and tell us what it’s worth and we listen.”

“He already came,” Ben said. He hadn’t meant to say it until supper. He knew they would eat on the tailgate and watch the light go out of the row like breath and it would be a better time to have the shape of it. He said it now because he was tired. “Two weeks ago. He came and we walked the fence line and we stood by the corner where the cottonwoods are raccoons, and he put his hand on the post and he said, I can put warehouses here. See the road? See the road? And I wanted to take a shovel and put it through his belly. But then he named a number and the number was bigger than the one in the drawer, and I thought about how the man in the drawer had my name spelled right. And I thought—how many boys get to pay for their father’s mistakes twice?”

“They weren’t mistakes,” Jake said, fierce. “He did it the way men did it then.”

“And then the way men did it then ended twenty minutes later and no one told him where the new line was,” Ben said. He did it. He said the thing they weren’t supposed to say. But it didn’t feel like betrayal. It felt like finally letting a rope go slack that had pulled at the tender skin under your arm for years. “Do you want me to walk the rest of my life with somebody else’s catechism? This town is full of men like that. Oh, the good old days when the ditch company gave you water like it was church. When the bee guy brought hives and said a proverb and left with a fistful of walnuts. When the frost—” He had to laugh then because he could hear himself. He sounded like his neighbor across the street whose eyebrows were two white caterpillars and who had once told him he thought the country had died in the nineties because a man had to get a signature from the county to pull a permit to fix his own barn.

Jake put his hands on his hips and looked at the land. His face was a field too. You could see the weather move across it. He shaded his eyes. He looked as far as the ridge and then farther, past something that was actually there. When he spoke, his tone had a steadiness that made Ben pay attention. “If they put warehouses, there won’t be orchards for miles,” he said. “And then what? Then every morning a truck will back up and beep in reverse and the sun will rise behind fences made of chain link. And men who don’t know what time is will go to work in the dark.”

“You think I want that?” Ben said. “You think I want to go to the diner and sit where Dad sat and have people look at me like I walked in with a brand new suit and a sign on my back said Judas? You think I want them to say, There goes Ben. He gave up his father’s dirt. That’s what we do to men around here who sell. We treat them like they sold their children. We pretend we wouldn’t have. But we would have. Because the man with the number—that man doesn’t have to ask his wife whether he can use her mother’s maiden name to open a new line of credit.”

Jake smiled then, crooked. He looked at his brother with something like admiration even though they were talking about the ugliest part. “You told her?” he asked, half-teasing, because the other secret had been that Ben loved his wife truly and she loved him back in a way that made other people safer by proximity.

“She knows everything before I do because she’s the one who watches my face while I sleep,” Ben said. “She’s the one who knows I slide my hands under the pillow and curl them to fists when I think about the pump. She said to sell. She said, I like a thing that ends. I don’t like a story that has twelve middles. And I said, But that’s what stories are. And she said, Exactly. They are a mess and we get to choose when we step out. And I said, I don’t know how to step out of a mess. I was raised to sleep inside of it.”

“How much was the number?” Jake asked.

“For the parcels with the house and the rights,” Ben said, and named it, and Jake said it under his breath as if he were tasting a mineral. It was both huge and not. You could see ten years of health insurance in it. You could see nothing for a town that built solar farms on other men’s rooftops and sent its children to someone else’s football games because theirs had been packed away with their mascot’s head in a bag in a gym closet.

“We could keep ten acres,” Jake said suddenly, as if he had crested some inner hill. His eyes lit and made the boy show in him. “We could keep the east block. The good corner. The ground by the cottonwoods that’s sweet. We could put up a fence and say, This. We could do it.”

“With what water?” Ben asked, but not cruel. “With the well that runs to sand in August? With who’s labor? With what time? You would come back?”

“Yes,” Jake said. He didn’t even breathe between. “I would. I’ve been sleeping on a couch that isn’t mine since February and reading books to people who don’t hear me because I don’t believe their end of the argument anymore either. I would come and I would set my alarm for three and I would light fires in the cans under the lowest branches when we have to, and learn Spanish better than my high school teacher wanted me to, and I would—”

He stopped himself. Maybe he heard how easy it had gotten. He pressed his palm to his thigh. “I would try,” he said instead. “And I would ask you to let me try.”

The day shimmered in the way days do in stories about June, but this was not a story and the shimmer was not enchantment but air itself warping over heat. A pickup truck rattled past out on the county road and the driver lifted his left hand from the wheel the way men did to each other, easy acknowledgment. The first swirl of dust crossed the orchard floor and held itself like a small animal and then dissolved.

“How about this,” Ben said finally. He said it like a man tasting a word to see if it had teeth. “You’ll be here through the end of the month. You can help me convince myself that we are keeping something alive instead of digging a grave. We will fix these lines and we will pick and we will eat as much as we can stand and get sick and curse ourselves and then eat again. We will take a load in ourselves to town and we will watch a woman under fluorescent lights look at what we brought like it’s a religious object she forgot. We will try your market play and you will drive into the Bay and insert yourself into that much traffic and you will come back and decide if what you love is this place or a set of pictures you keep in your head. And then we’ll see.”

Jake nodded and then shook his head because there were two answers to that. He reached up and curled his palm around the bent neck of one of the micro-sprinklers and let the arc of its water finger his knuckle. “I don’t know how to do any of this,” he said softly.

“I didn’t either,” Ben said. “I kept not knowing and then I stood there anyway. That is all this is. You stand there anyway.”

They did for the rest of the afternoon. They pressed their eyes to micro sins. They taped a lateral that would not stop grieving at a joint. They unscrewed a broken head. They tossed the gopher mounds with their shovels and the soft brown of the mounds fell and turned paler as the sun had its way. They found a quail’s nest tucked against the trunk of a tree and bent over it for longer than men need to bend over such a thing, as if it could show them what any of this meant: seven eggs the size of the top knuckle on your thumb, tan with little mascara freckles. The hen’s scuttle-song slipped sideways and then settled back in the scrub. They left the nest untouched and between rows twelve and thirteen they found the heavier track of a deer and the light, outrageous scrawl of a coyote. Ben said, “He’s been here,” and Jake said, “He’s always been here,” and both were true.

When they cracked the last valve of the afternoon and the far corner came up just a whisper, Ben straightened and the sound across the block had changed. The micro-sprinklers had a way of making you hear belief. When they worked, they were like a human hand clapping for someone else. The lot breathed. Ben turned his face to the feel of it, his cheeks salted with dust he hadn’t noticed gather. For a moment his heart did a thing it had not done in a while, like a man arriving at the end of a sentence he did not know was a prayer.

“You hear that?” he said.

“It sounds like sleep,” Jake said. “Like when you used to—when you and Dad would argue and then stop and then argue again quieter and I would lie in my bed and listen for that and this.”

“Did we?” Ben asked, startled.

“Not argue,” Jake amended. “Discuss.”

“We did argue,” Ben said. It hurt like a bruise under a pride. “He’d say, I’m not having a—, and then he would, and at the end he’d tell me what to do and I would do it, and later he’d come in the night and stand in the door of the room where I was pretending to sleep and I could feel—like a dog feels—what his body felt like, and it would be sorry and proud all at once.”

Jake lifted his hands out as if to receive something. “I think about that all the time,” he said. “How a person can be sorry and proud at once. How often I am that. It feels like what being alive is: two good things that aren’t friends.”

When the angle of the light had turned from white to honey and the edges of the trees had softened out as though somebody had dragged a finger on a painting, they killed the pump and the orchard fell quiet in a way that was both relief and loss. They climbed into the bed of the pickup with their boots and their hats and sat with their heels on the bumper and looked out at their afternoon. Jake had that lazy grace he got when he was happy, a looseness in the wrists like a person who danced. Ben leaned his shoulder against the cab and ran his hand over his shin to knock the dirt from the hair there.

“Mom called,” Jake said, as if that were the same thing as the way the day looked. “She’s coming down next week.”

“She’ll stand and tell me the names of all the trees I can’t cut down before she goes and then she’ll go back and pretend she never stood here,” Ben said, without meanness.

Jake smiled. Their mother was a language in herself. She had taught them plants like animals, had given them words when their father gave them tools. Watching her stand at the sink, Ben had learned that a body could look out at a world and be punished by its beauty. When she had left, she had not said why. She had known that saying was sometimes worse. She had come back to sit in the front pew while they put dirt into the ground after their father, and then she had gone again because she had made a pact with herself about what she could survive and they did not own that pact.

“We could invite her to help pick,” Jake said, and his voice made it sound like that would fix something. Ben didn’t tell him she had said last time, I don’t pick because it makes me feel like I live here. He let his brother hold his pictures. He held his own.

As they sat there, a coyote loped the far fence line and turned his head in their direction and paused, and his body made a perfect leaning question mark in the air. He had that particular coyote arrogance, the way wild things look at you now and say, You made me bigger than I am; you made me your symbol; I am just myself. Then he was gone and the world knitted his movement shut.

“Okay,” Jake said presently, as if it were a word to end a chapter. He clapped his hands once, then rubbed them on his jeans. “Tomorrow we fix the head by the cottonwoods. And the filter—you said the sand in it’s old.”

“Three years,” Ben said. “It isn’t terrible yet.”

“We’ll change it anyway,” Jake said, and made that face again, eagerness converting to penance. “We’ll bake a pie. We’ll invite Ms. Alvarez down from two houses over and make her sit and eat it even if she says she doesn’t. We’ll—”

“Don’t make a list,” Ben said, gently. “The list is what kills you. You make a list and you think it’s a mountain and you think you have to summit.”

“What do I do then?” Jake asked, real.

“You look at what’s in front of you and you ask if the thing can be made better by standing here and trying,” Ben said. “If it can’t, you step to the next thing. Everything else you keep in the drawer in the kitchen where we put the things we fear. We’ll open it when we have to.”

“Okay,” Jake said again, and let out a breath he had been holding since January. He leaned his head back against the truck and closed his eyes and the eyelids were thin enough the light came through and made the veins in them a faint map. Ben watched his brother breathe like that and thought about the way a chest rises when a child sleeps and you go in at night to watch it because you no longer believe in the mercy that allows breath to keep happening without you asking it for you.

“What did it feel like,” Jake said after a while, not lifting his head, “the first time you turned the pump on after he died?”

“Like he would walk in and say I was doing it wrong,” Ben said, and smiled because that would have been nice. “Like the motor had no ear anymore. Like a dog that didn’t remember its name. And then the water came and I hated that something so big didn’t care.”

“I know that feeling,” Jake said. “I like it less. I want the world to—”

“To respond,” Ben said. “To be personal.”

“Yes,” Jake said. “To be like a man you can talk to who—who listens.”

Ben slid down from the bed of the truck, felt the ground receive him through his boots. He looked at the first tree in the row the way you look at a man you’ve known since you were born, whom you took for granted because he had always been and then one day you see he is stooped and his hair has thinned and you love him with a flare that feels like panic. He reached out and touched the warm roughness of the bark—on one side smooth where the sprinkler had hit it in a constant lover’s lick when they first put it in and they didn’t know better; scarred now in a ring where they’d corrected it and the trunk had healed the way living things do: ugly and perfect.

“Okay,” he said to the tree as much as to his brother. “We’ll do one more day.”

“One more day,” Jake said. He slid down too and came to stand next to him and they stood in the half light in that unthinking male way, shoulder to shoulder, looking outward, not at each other, each of them a little ashamed at how relieved they were to have postponed a sentence.

A wind came low across the orchard like a hand smoothed across a back to calm it. It lifted the hair at their temples. It moved the stickers in the grass enough to show that there were spaces between each barb and that a person had to be precise about where he put his feet. The heads out there clicked as they wound down. A pair of mourning doves traded their sad with each other like old coins.

The day held to its end with the particularity of a day you will remember because it was both ordinary and the one where you promised something you don’t know how to keep. Somewhere a truck started and then stopped and then started again, a cough of compression that had to decide about its own life. The twins over on the Hinojosa place yelled and then laughed. The neighbor’s dog barked because it had not learned yet that there was nothing to be barked at that would change.

“Tomorrow,” Jake said, a third time, a kind of oath now. He patted the tree’s shoulder the way their father had used to pat a boy’s back when he wanted the boy to be brave but wasn’t going to look him in the face to ask for it.

“Tomorrow,” Ben said, because sometimes the idea of tomorrow is generous.

They walked back toward the pump house to collect the caddy of glues and the small quart of misfit fittings and the torn gloves that had belonged to their father and that they still slipped on sometimes because it felt like a way to have him do the work with them. The door to the pump house was swollen and stuck at the bottom and Ben had to put his hip to it to make it go and when it went it went with a groan like an animal that had been on its side too long and had decided to get up. Inside, the smell of oil and the stale sugar of spilled diesel and the metal tang of water overtook them. The wall was still marked where their father, long before memory, had pried down a calendar of half-naked women and hung up instead a calendar of tractors because he wanted to be admirable without being a monk.

On the inside of the door, with a nail, someone—probably their father when he was younger than Ben was now—had scratched in a crooked hand a set of numbers that were now meaningless—rates for electricity in a year that no one who hadn’t lived it could imagine. Ben put his finger on the numbers and followed the line of them and felt the small burrs of the nail cuts and didn’t know whether to laugh or to put his mouth to them. He did nothing. He lifted the caddy. It was heavy with its small thingness. The glue sloshed lightly. He turned off the light because he was still a man who turned off lights, and stepped down the concrete steps into the wash of late heat.

“I can make labels,” Jake said, because his mind had a way of finding the thing he could do rather than the thing he feared he couldn’t. “If we sell to markets. I can make labels with our name. I can design a little square that will make them want to say the name. I can—”

“Do make them beautiful,” Ben said, and his own voice surprised him because he sounded like someone who wanted to be pleased by a detail. “If we’re going to be men who sell what they grow, I want to be as handsome as anything.”

“We can call it ‘Two Brothers’,” Jake said, and said it so lightly he could have meant it to pass. It hung in the air between them all the same, a small, hard truth.

“We can call it what we are,” Ben agreed, and let the smile happen and did not punish himself for it.

They walked to the house. The screen banged its old self behind them, and the kitchen smelled like all the kitchens of their kin had smelled—onion and coffee and floor soap and sun. He went to the drawer and opened it fully for the first time that day. The envelope was there, white as a piece of clean paper into which you could stick a tongue of fire. He put his hand on it as though he were comforting it or it him and then took his hand away, and closed the drawer, and let the wood slide on wood make that sound he remembered and had once believed he would hear for the rest of his life.

He turned toward his brother. Jake was holding a dish towel and had draped it over his shoulder like a chef, as if they would make supper and it would be simple. He looked hopeful and weary and so real Ben felt a pain that was almost joy in his chest.

“Wash,” he said, and gestured at Jake’s purple hands with a grin. “You look like a kid who got into the paint.”

“Do we have enough hot water for two?” Jake asked.

“No,” Ben said. “We never did.”

He went to the sink and turned the tap. The water came with its old delay and then ran clear and indifferent over their stained fingers until their hands stung. Outside, the last light leaned itself against the trunks of the apricot trees and then let go. The pump was quiet and the orchard, for the first time that day, did not ask them for anything. The day ended not with a decision but with two men knowing that they had gone as far as a day could be asked to carry. The rest would come with morning, as it always had and would until there were no more mornings in that kitchen, in that orchard; until what was theirs had become someone else’s sound to love or to ignore. For now, they stood with their shoulders almost touching, their hands under the same river, and the fact of the water on their skin was, for the space that such things hold, enough.
