I had not intended to come early but the train got in on time and the car I called idled under the palms on Cabrillo and there was not enough traffic yet to make us late so by four I was in the lobby, not yet changed, the garment bag not yet opened. There were people already at the bar drinking pale cocktails in ridged glasses; there was a dog in a carrier under a table; there were the white sofas low to the floor and the arrangement of monstera leaves on a travertine slab that suggested an idea of the tropics conveyed through air freight and event design. The air smelled faintly of eucalyptus even though none grew in the courtyard. A sign at the concierge desk, lettered in small gold, read Welcome Flynn–Kessler Wedding Weekend in a curlicue that would have been illegible to anyone who had not already been told what to expect. I stood there and registered the signage and the barware and the tilt of the afternoon light through the new glass wall and understood that I was meant to be pleased by this presentation, by the ways in which something old had been stripped and something new had been applied. A host in a soft blazer asked if he could help and I said I was here for the wedding and he smiled the same smile he would have smiled had I said I had just come in from Omaha on the Zephyr to kill time at a resort where the room rates were now north of seven hundred on the weekends. It was a professional smile disciplined by quarterly performance reviews and word-of-mouth.

The hotel had not called itself La Orilla when I worked here. It had been the Del Pacífico and its promise had been less explicit, a place of Colonial affect, arches and terra-cotta and a fountain that sputtered in the courtyard without quite disguising a certain damp. There had been mosaic floors slick under mop water and a brass stand beside the restaurant hostess that held the reservation book, a real book, pages turned with an index finger that had cuticles bitten to the quick. There had been a smell of Clorox that rose from the service hall and stayed, an overlay to the smell of frying oil, an overlay to the smell of what we called “guest”—hair product and luggage and perfume that cost more than my pay for a forty-five-minute turn. There had been an ownership group reachable only through a number in Miami and a general manager who was always out of breath and a second-shift engineer who drank a quart of milk from the linen closet to cut his heartburn. There had been me with the cart and the key ring, a borrowed safety pin stuck into my collar to catch the slack of my hair.

I had been twenty-three the first week I pushed a cart alone. It was January and the wind came hard over the ocean but the sun was strong and the skylight over the central stair bleached the carpet even then, even in winter. You learn certain habits in a place like that. You wipe the tops of picture frames with your fingertips first to check what the eye will not see. You never trust the “occupied” indicator, you knock twice with your knuckles, you say Housekeeping before you slot in the key and step your foot against the edge of the door so that no one can pull it open from the inside to shame you with their nakedness. You clear the ice bucket with two precise shakes, trash bag turned inside out over your hand, snap at the wrist, a way you watch and learn. You keep a hand towel on your shoulder because otherwise you will wipe your hands on your pants and no one forbids it but it looks bad. You never use the Coke left by the minibar to prop the door because if it spills the syrup binds the rubber gasket to the carpet and you will have to go down the stairs to get hot water and the houseman will always be on break. You do not use the service elevator between three and four because the wedding planner will insist that the wedding party photographs get the car when the light is exact on the mezzanine. You know the first three rooms on the western side of the third floor stay warmer by default because the line in the duct never really carried there after the earthquake retrofitting and you leave the AC a tick lower in those rooms even in winter. You get paid thirteen dollars an hour and you could make three more at the casino but the casino is inland and has no windows.

Certain dangers do not present as such in the beginning. The signs were there and I stepped past them every day. The scorch on the carpet inside the housekeeping closet on two and all of us calling it a cigarette even though none of us smoked inside the closet where the paper goods were kept. The hum the hot water heater made when the laundry ran a second shift and the night man loped through with his mop and a radio on his shoulder, a radio that gave the weather and the Dow and played the oldies at a volume that never wavered: Salt and Pepa, Tom Petty, Billie Holiday, Pet Shop Boys. The smell of toasted dust when the baseboard heaters kicked on in the older rooms. The needlepoint instructing someone, not us, that “This too shall pass,” framed above a loveseat upholstered in a pattern of ferns. The wedding parties who brought their own votives and lined them along the hallway outside ballroom doors because someone on a blog had called it romantic.

It seems wrong to say it like this but I did not expect to be the one alive. I had one performance review that September. My supervisor, a woman named Kattia who kept her receipts in a zip pouch and carried pictures of children who were not hers, told me I was “detail oriented” but she needed to see “more urgency.” I wrote it down in a notebook I kept in my locker with other phrases I thought would get me somewhere: competitive pay, medical-dental, requisition. We were all trying to arrive at some defined place and it was understood that the steps had to be memorized. You did not smoke in your uniform outside on Cabrillo because the guests saw. You did not speak Spanish in front of a guest even if the guest spoke Spanish. You did not take home the yogurt left in the minibar even if it had not been opened and was not yet past its date because then everyone would take the yogurt and no one would restock. You did not keep cash that you found unless it was in a drawer with a name attached. You did not ask why the smoke detectors nearest the ceiling in the service hall blinked red not green when no one else was around.

My name had been on the list to work that day and in the morning I had walked across the street to buy coffee because the coffee we had in the breakroom had been measured so thin it only promised coffee and never delivered. I did not know the specific hour of my survival until later, when I read it on a page someone else had composed. I was already thinking of the day as something else: the day of the nurse who left a note on a folded face towel thanking me for folding her towel into a fan because she treated patients and no one ever folded anything for her; the day of the bridal party that threw rose petals in the corridor and dragged the hem of the rug; the day I picked up a mirror that had been laid face down on the side table and saw that the mouth of my own face was set in a way I did not recognize, as if I had been concentrating for hours on an equation that would not resolve.

By the time I got to the desk that afternoon the concierge had an envelope for me, my room key inside, my name on the paper tag in a script it would be fair to call aspirational. Someone had drawn a heart over the i in my first name. I had the peculiar sensation, locating my name in someone else’s hand, of having been asked to act a part I had not studied for, a part that required certain props. I went to the restroom beside the ballroom and looked at myself in the long mirror with the whorled frame and pulled the garment bag from the plastic and smoothed my dress with the flat of my hand and thought about what was expected. It is not that I felt anything we might call dread. It was more an alertness, the way the body remembers an exit route the mind has agreed to forget.

The ballroom where the ceremony would be held had been reimagined as a lantern, this according to the copy on the website. There was a lighting rig suspended from the ceiling that was meant to make the room feel like deep water as the evening wore on; the wall panels were upholstered in a whiteness that suggested the absence of stain. Someone had set casual arrangements on the console tables in the corridor, garden roses the color of old ivory mixed with rosemary and a plant with small articulated leaves that will keep its composure even when deprived of water for a time. Tea lights in glass cups had been placed at intervals. There were hurricane vases as well, tall and smooth, the good kind, but I could not help the inventory. Vinyl dance floor, edge taped at the corners, no trip hazard, okay. DJ table skirted, wires coiled and bound, okay. Deco-style sconce wired into dimmer switch, face plates new, no visible cracking, okay. Candles. Soft drape in polyester voile running ceiling to floor over the glass wall. Candles and polyester are not friends. A young woman with an earpiece directed a boy in a white jacket to move the crates of stemware farther from the line of votives. It was a thing I might have said and did not. She did not look up from her clipboard.

I had seen the bride perhaps three times, in incandescent light and in fluorescent, sitting for signatures at the license office and later at a table in a restaurant where raw tuna came in rectangles and was described without reference to the fish. She was a person who applied herself to ideas. She had a calendar with tabs and a folder with invoices and could talk for twenty minutes about a product she had not used and would never use, her certainty making a kind of path through the conversation so that we would all finally be encouraging her down it. She was not a close friend but a friend of a friend, a network threaded through certain late-twentysomething years of dinners in apartments painted white because if you paint a rental any other color you have to paint it back. She had cried at a movie in a way that had caused two men at our table to look instantly at their phones with their thumbs already in motion, as if only a slender act of attention were required of them and they intended to donate that act cleanly and completely. She had said something to me about how she wished she were the kind of person who made her own granola. She had brought toothbrushes for us when we once got drunk on a beach and had no plan to get home except walking. She had never heard me talk about the Del Pacífico except once, in a way that made it sound like a summer fling who had never called when he said he would.

Of course I had read the coverage. The paper used the words “tragedy” and “unfathomable” and “tight-knit.” The television showed the flaming roofline against a sky bright with the sodium vapor lamps at Cabrillo and leaded in a drone shot that made the building look like a toy. There was one image that ran everywhere for a time, the arc of the fire through the arched windows of the old ballroom like an opened mouth. They said the bride in that ballroom had been barefoot and had carried her shoes when she went down the stairs and had cut her feet on something under the carpet where the seam had lifted. They said the DJ had kept playing until he did not. They said someone had locked a door and someone else had not locked a door. They said an electrical short and later a candle and later a foreign national accustomed to different regulations and later none of this but something else, a spark from the kitchen, a towel in a warming pan, a God that needed reminding. Among ourselves we had other explanations and none. Every loss gets cataloged one way or another and you start to see how the ledger can be made to read differently depending on where you start adding. There was a houseman we called Miguel who had a wife we knew as a photograph on his phone and she turned up on the local news trembling and repeating that Miguel was still inside and then not trembling when the anchor asked his question again because perhaps in her mind it was not useful to tremble on television. There was a bartender named Rachel who had already found another job when the paper printed her picture and later I heard she was living in Portland and would no longer go anywhere that smelled like citrus peel and sugar. Two of the chefs from the banquet kitchen turned out to have been fucking for a year and no one had known this until they both survived and told everyone at the memorial. The general manager spoke without verbs. The electrician from the firm that had the maintenance contract wore a blazer for his statement and had recently whitened his teeth.

The thing about any fire is that memory refuses to stage it the same way twice. In one iteration I come out of the service stairs onto three and the hallway is empty and I walk down it carrying a stack of towels and the light is strange, pulsing, a phenomenon of the emergency generator, and I think I hear a voice. In another I am on two, in the housekeeping closet letting the door rest against my back so that no one else will come in because the laundry bins are already in the corridor, and my elbow is damp from the condensation on the blue dispenser that holds the concentrated cleaner, the kind you are not meant to breathe. In another I am in the stairwell and the lights are steady and the painted arrow marked EXIT points down and left and the handrail is cold to the touch, a good sign, my key ring awkward in my fist. I am telling a woman to go down and stay on the landing and count the doors and she keeps saying her wallet is upstairs and it contains everything and I tell her that is not true. In all the versions there is noise like a swarm of unseen insects and in all of the versions the smoke comes in low and then drops like a curtain, because that is what smoke does; the movies lie about this. In some versions a man comes down the hall with his arm through the strap of a dress he is carrying and in some versions there is a child under a bed and in some versions there is no under to the bed because the platform legs are short and the carpet is new and thick. In one version I am looking down through the atrium and the air has already blackened and the palm in the pot looks for an instant like something white is in its fronds but that is only ash buoyed and then dropped, a slow snow that will settle and cool.

In this life the ceremony began at six. The groom’s grandmother wore a dress the color of a pressed leaf and the bride’s brother read an email that had been printed on heavy paper and folded in thirds. The officiant declined to wear robes and instead wore a navy suit and spoke in a voice that could be heard without amplification. The photographer moved like water, to the side, to the center, her hair in a knot at the nape of her neck, the shoes she had chosen silent and practical, a person who has thought ahead about loadout and the equivalence of lenses in low light. I sat with two women I knew from other years and one of them removed her heels and placed them under her chair and the other looked into her clutch for the Advil and measured out two into a hand and then one back again. Behind the drape the glass wall reflected the room to us and in the reflection, unfixed, we were all already leaving. The sound system sent out music that had been chosen in a way meant to be above what had gone out over the radio for the last fifteen years but not so far above as to confuse anyone older than fifty. The bride’s veil caught inside her bracelet without snagging and there was a minor interlude, only seconds, and then there was the thing we think of as the pivot and we all let our breath go because the form wants its points and we offered them silently and then with our hands. “Do you remember when you told me,” the bride said, but of course we none of us had been present for that and in any case memory is not a witness and in any case if we remember when someone else told someone else something we are not remembering the thing, we are fitting ourselves to the cadence of story. I stood when we were meant to stand and I watched the people at the back of the room and I was careful to note where the exit had been placed now and what kind of hardware secured the push bar and whether someone had slid a wedge beneath the door to keep it from closing on the current of this evening.

I did not drink at first. There was champagne in a bucket on the bar and I could have taken a glass but it seemed to me that I needed certain other assurances before I let myself off the leash of my own vigilance. The servers were dressed in black and were all young and beautiful in the way that youth finds beauty in itself and it was, I thought, a profession as valid as any other, this carrying of good things to strangers in exchange for a living. A boy handed me a canapé and I did not eat it and another handed me a small spoonful of something foamed. There was a station where a woman in a toque improvised small plates that took longer than you would think to come to completion given that they consisted mainly of leaves. The crowd coalesced and opened, coalesced and opened. The light went into that honeyed angle that makes everything seem a decision you made and I allowed myself to think briefly of the view those floors on two now had, how long it had taken to restore the casements, whether Casement & Co. was still the vendor we called or whether there had been an RFP and a new name on the work order. The tide below the bluff moved without our intervention.

At odd moments, by the dessert bar, at the threshold of the ladies’ room, at the turn in the hall where the new corridor met the stub of an old one, I heard a piece of a story I had not known I wanted to hear. People who remember the Del Pacífico say it had a kind of doomed glamour and this is not a thing one should say about a place where people died and yet people say such things because we make domes out of what we cannot reconcile, we fresco the inside and leave the structure to the engineers. One man said he had been at a bachelor party there where someone had jumped into the fountain fully clothed. A woman said her aunt had been married there in 1998 and the flowers had been hydrangeas in the dark color that never looks right in pictures, it always shows as blue. A person I did not know well leaned in and said she had heard everyone had been drunk and that was why they did not get out. Someone else, hearing that, said no, the door had stuck. She asked me did I remember ever hearing about it and I said I thought I had read something, yes, which was true.

I did not go upstairs to my room. I remained in public space, a choice I had not fully made until I noticed the way my throat closed over when I thought of sliding the key card into the slot and hearing the latch release and part of my brain, a sorted place, making a calculation about the length of time it took to reengage and the angle required to lever it with a credit card if need be. I walked through to the back patio where the heater trees stood with their silent mushrooms of heat red as stove coils and a woman in a dress like water laughed and put her head back and a man touched her elbow. The moon lifted unimportant over the parking lot. A boy had put his jacket on his date and his date tried to put the jacket back on the chair where it belonged and the boy ignored her. The ocean was farther away than when I had worked here and I knew this was a trick of the light.

At the first song of the dancing the people who had wanted to dance were the first ones to dance. They forced the rest of us up with them. One of my friends pulled me and our drinks remained on a table and neither of us would drink from those glasses again though I could not have told you why. There was a thin contrail of fog from the machine and it lay among us like a low cloud we were ignoring. I put my hand on the shoulder of a woman I had met once at a farmer’s market after too much wine and we smiled the smiles of women who had once danced before with strangers as if there were no consequence. I closed my eyes immediately and then opened them immediately because this too could constitute an error. I saw two vases along the edge of the room shake with a beat and resettle themselves. I saw the planner make a mark on her clipboard and I believed in that instant she was inventorying the planner and not the event, adding to a tally of things she did or did not accomplish, the part of her mind that would sleep only after the truck was loaded and the crew had been paid.

There was a woman I had worked with named Inés who taught me to tuck the bottom corner of a sheet under the mattress with my wrist and not my hand because if you used your hand you would crush your ring finger eventually and it would not bend correctly and your grip on your child would be weak and you would learn too late what you had given away. She had come north with a sister who cried in her sleep and a man who did not. She liked to put a little lemon oil on the wooden hangers because she thought it made the closets smell like clean money. She had a way of saving her body in small increments during the day: the squat instead of the bend, the way she leaned into the vacuum as if it were a friend who needed comforting, a dance she did with it that made sense only if you were watching for a long time. She had a son we knew because he visited once on a Saturday and sat at the breakroom table with the top of his head down over a form he was filling out as if the speed of his pen were essential to pleasing the jury of older women he believed to be his judges. She did not die that night. I say this because I know you will be thinking of her when I tell you other things and it will distress you to think of her that way. She did not die. She had been on seven, the wing that was already closed because of the lead in the old pipes, and because it was closed she had gone down to two earlier than she normally would and she had already clocked out when the alarm failed to sound and then did. She left anyway. She stood for a long time on the sidewalk and would not look at the building. She was difficult after, in the way that people who have been spared sometimes are.

What we remember changes because what we need from it changes. The man who started to sue and then did not was named Ethan. He wore a good chambray shirt and had a dog with a halter that fit like a harness and he came to what had been the site under a temporary tarp and he left Peet's cups on the curb. He talked to a lawyer who wore his tie a particular way, wide Windsor, and I watched them and made a note about the knot. He did not sue finally because I suppose that not everything inside him wanted him to; he married someone ten months later on a beach in Del Mar and their pictures were all light and happiness and there was never so much as a swirl of smoke in any of them, even on the grill. I rehearsed this fact sometimes when I woke up at three and felt my breath shorten and I believed this rehearsal could call a shape to what I had made; I was making, in other words, a case for the possibility that there had been one. A case for my name on a key tag inside a small envelope in someone else’s hand.

At the end of the salads the father of the bride stood to speak and his hand shook and stopped shaking and shook again. He used the word “commitment” five times in three minutes. He said he admired the way the couple approached “challenges.” He said the time had come to look forward. This is what we say when we do not know how to say what we mean. It is a way to pass the glass down the table and keep the evening from breaking. The kitchen doors opened and closed on their quiet hinges and the servers moved with their plates and the sizzle of kitchen labor slid out and closed back in as if it were always meant to be contained and we had only misheard it. Two busboys leaned together in the shadow of the service alcove and one of them had his hand inside his pocket curled around his phone and he was reading. It was a small square of ordinary life, this half second, and it felt to me like a revelation, as if I had been shown that at any given second someone was reading in a shadow and therefore the entire thing could go on. I cannot explain this except to say that I have not ever believed in macroeconomics or the steady hand of markets or that human beings will learn to do better by one another in the aggregate. I have believed in particular men and women with their sleeves rolled up; I have believed in the busboy who is reading.

You will want to know whether I went looking. I did. I walked the service corridor on the pretext of finding the restroom because the public restroom had too many women waiting and because the private one would not. I said I was looking for the restroom and a man in a chef’s coat pointed to a door with an occupancy light and I did not go in there. I walked to the end of the corridor and handled the latch of the door that had once gone to the shorter west stairs. The door had been painted a color I recognized from the cover of a magazine I used to buy at the grocery store and there was a square window in it with chicken wire sandwiched inside the glass and the lights beyond were on timers. My heart did the thing it does when I step into an old dream and find the furniture rearranged. The closet where we kept the vacuum had become a prep sink. The bulletin board had its own key and was locked. A sign said This Door to Remain Unlocked During Business Hours and I knew the man who had posted the sign had done so with a small rush of certainty that this small thing could hold the building together. I put my forehead to the painted wall long enough to make a faint imprint where the shine dulled. I held the metal bar of the door that gave to the stairs. It did not move beneath my hand.

The groom’s sister came upon me there and said she loved this place for the light and I said yes. She had a drink in her hand and a second poured for someone who had left their place and she said would I. I took the second. We stood in the service corridor and sipped our drinks and she told me how the ring had gone missing at the rehearsal dinner and had been discovered later by her fiancé under the table where he had kicked it accidentally and the ring had rolled and returned itself to the place it was meant to be, a small piece of good luck she had not told the bride because she believed in controlling the number of narratives at play on any given day. I thought I understood this. I told her a story about a hotel ice machine clogging because a lemon slice had been dropped into it and frozen into place and how the engineer said it was always the lemon slice that took down the ice machine, it was never the large thing, it was always the lemon slice.

At a certain point in the evening I was no longer where I had been when it began and the night had shifted and I could drink and I did. I danced with a man I had not met and he said he worked for the city and he was saving all his vacation time for a trip to Vietnam he would not take. He did not say he would not take it and I am not sure he even knew that and yet the sentence was present between us as if a projector had thrown the words onto the glass wall and we had read it and gone on. The bride took off her shoes. The groom’s tie got pulled loose and lay like something living down his chest. The doors were propped and the night moved through us and I stood at the edge of the room and felt the wash of the air and then for a moment I did not. I lifted my face and tried to hear if the HVAC had cut and when the systems had been set to cycle and there was a boy I had not seen move to wedge the door again because it had drifted closed and he used his foot and his shoulder together as if he were bearing a body forward and for an instant the room looked like the room I had known stretched across another and I felt the back of my neck go cold and then warm again and I did not breathe and then I did.

We think in signs until the signs stop serving us and then we think in events because we are busy. The word for that is survival. I stood with my hand on a cocktail table and counted four slow counts in and four out and because no one had taught me that trick it belonged to me, a thing I had that had not been given me by any of them. The band leader announced a last call and then another last call and then another after that and the men with the truck began to move their crates just off the patio as if we could not see and this is how it works, I reminded myself, the breakdown comes as softly as it can, the men stack and strap and carry and the night slides into another and this is how we are returned to our rooms. The brake lights of the truck made a loosened heart on the ground and then stopped because of a loading rule.

I did not go to my room then either. I stayed until the things had thinned and the grandfather in the pressed-leaf dress shook the hands of the band members and the young women with their shoes in their hands moved their toes experimentally against the cool tile and one of them cried without much noise and a friend rubbed her shoulder and then stopped rubbing her shoulder and ordered a burger from room service because she had remembered this as a possibility. I made my goodbye to the bride in a way I hope did not feel like the completion of a vow only I had made. She was shining with sweat and a quality of happiness that lay in her eyes and at the roots of her hair and I saw she had not brought her own toothbrush for us tonight and she laughed and said she had it on a list in her head and I said sleep and this felt like a threat; I did not mean it as one. I went down the hallway past the place where the tea lights had been and some were blown and some still flickered and the glass cups were warm to the touch and it felt like nothing because candles feel like nothing until they do. In the ladies’ room a woman had left a purse at the sink and when I picked it up to put it on the counter where it would be safer the bottom half of it brushed a damp patch and I imagined the arc of that damp and how long it would take for the dark to lighten and I placed it so that the dark would not show if she came soon.

Outside, the night was ordinary and I took off my shoes and carried them along Cabrillo the way you do when you are a girl coming home from a party even though I was not that girl and there was no home on this street for me to return to. The lights of police cars swam at the far corner and this was an argument in a car and not anything else. A woman carried a balloon loosely knotted with ribbon and the balloon bumped her hair in the wind. The palm fronds made their noise like paper tearing and the shape of the hotel behind me was a different shape now, glass where there had been stucco, light where there had been shadow, a certainty where there had been the promise of something else, and I felt inside myself a necessary detachment engage, the mode by which one leaves a place without turning into salt.

In my room the door latched with the good mechanical click and the lights turned on low and one lamp rose with a weightless halo and I walked from the bed to the window and back and then to the door and then to the bathroom and turned the faucet on and off twice, not because I believed if the faucet worked everything else did but because there is a point in certain nights when the evening reduces to a series of acts, and if you do not consecrate them you will not make it to the hour that is meant to deliver you to sleep. I put my dress over the back of the chair and I laid the shoes side by side on the carpet and I hung my bag off the closet pull and the bag turned once and then stilled. I lay down in my slip and it was a nice slip, something I had bought last year for myself because I wanted to see who I would be in it, and I am sorry to tell you I was the same person I had been before I put it on. The pillowcase was the good cotton that refuses to please you immediately. I lay my hand open where a person might hold it and I waited for my heart to slow.

There was a hum I would not have heard ten years ago and I heard it now and it took me a minute to locate and understand. It was the air handlers on the roof cycling down and the last of the kitchen dampers closing and a vacuum of a kind forming in the ducts but not immediately, not so that you could see napkins lift or the hem of the drape stir, a movement at the border of the seeing. I closed my eyes and tried to bring up the ocean and could not bring up the ocean. I saw instead a scrap of a receipt Inés had kept in her zip pouch for years because it had the name of a store where someone had let her use the phone and the name had felt to her then like an amulet and she could not get rid of it in case the name was the thing that had worked and not the person who had answered or the line the phone rode on through the air. I saw the photograph of the Del Pacífico printed in the paper the next day, framed by a border of quotes from people who would never again agree among themselves about what it had been like that night. I saw the tea light in the glass cup on the table in the corridor and the circle of wax it made, and I believed in that moment in that wax because it was proof of a time a flame stayed in place. I believe we are always looking for proofs that the flame will stay in place. We consider this naive only until we need it.

In the morning the light would be honest and we would drink coffee poured by someone with a spotless cuff and we would talk about the eggs and we would say who had cried when and we would give the hangnails of gossip to one another because this is what we have after a big night, our faith in small degradations, our tenderness for them. I would leave a tip on the pillow because there are customs which do not end even when you cannot know who will lift that pillow when you are gone. I would walk out into the day, the air already too bright, and the hotel would stand behind me with its glass and its commitments and someone would be vacuuming the corridor where the dancing had happened and someone would be wheeling a cart and someone would be picking up the cigarette butts outside the valet and putting them into a plastic bag and knotting the plastic bag with the peculiar precision of the person who believes knotting a bag is the one thing they can finish today.

There is a story people tell about this place and there will be a story they tell about last night and neither will be the story I have told you because the telling takes place beyond anyone’s control. The night was what it was; the flame stayed where we asked it to. I lay in the bed that did not belong to me in a hotel that did not belong to me and thought, for a long time, about the key card in its sleeve on the nightstand, how it could be kept in a wallet next to a picture of a child or slipped into a pocket next to a receipt that seemed important. I thought about how easy the cards are to demagnetize. I thought about the way you hold a piece of plastic between your fingers and move it toward a slot until a green light shows itself and tells you to go in now, that you may go in. I thought for a long time about the way the door releases. I listened for it. I waited for it to settle. I waited for the second click that comes when the weight shifts and the bolt accepts its housing, the quiet sound that means the alignment is true and will remain so at least for the night. And when at last the bolt received the strike and the parts met in a way that promised the proportion of safety the human body can be convinced to accept, I slept.
