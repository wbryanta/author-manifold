They loosed the last hawser and the ferry slid away from the concrete like a great animal tired of its tether. I was on the windward side, hunched in my jacket, with the taste of diesel and cold salt slicking my tongue. The gulls skittered low, white scraps against the gray evening, then turned, one by one, toward the town lights retreating behind the stern. The strait lay ahead of us dark and ironed flat in the harbor's shelter, but outside the breakwater the sea shouldered and heaved. People around me murmured and buttoned their coats higher. A boy with a knitted hat pressed his face between two rails and lifted his arms like a cormorant.

They always made a show of it on the quay. Vans backed up with late parcels, someone from the catering crew smoked one last cigarette, talking into his phone with a hand clamped at his ear. The fluorescent jackets of the deckhands moved against each other like fish in a school. When the announcer’s voice came over the tannoy—good evening ladies and gentlemen, welcome aboard—I did not hear the rest. My phone was in my pocket, a weight like a stone. The call had come at four that afternoon, while I stood in line at the supermarket trying to decide between pork chops and pasta. "Mr. Harrow?" a man's voice had said, careful with the name, each syllable by itself. "You're listed as the next of kin for a Lewis Harrow."

I had said yes to that without thinking. The words made a noise in my head like air leaving a tyre.

"There's been..." The man paused. His accent softened the t's. "There's been a discovery. A body recovered from down by the Black Head. The description matches. We'd like you to come over, if you can, to help us confirm."

It was a Tuesday and the supermarket muzak was playing the instrumental of a love song that had been popular when I was a teenager. A woman in front of me had three cartons of milk and a bottle of cheap prosecco. I left the pork chops on a shelf among the biscuits and went outside into the alley that smelled of mown grass and rotting fruit. I put my head against the cooled bricks and looked at my hands. There was a small split in the skin where the middle finger met the palm, a nick from some piece of office equipment. It had bled in a tidy way, not enough to need a plaster, just enough to notice every time I tucked the finger in, every time I balled my fist.

The officer had given me a name—Sergeant Hunter—and told me what time the ferry left and when it would dock. He said he would meet me at the terminal. He did not tell me to bring anything. He did not ask me to recall the clothing my brother might have been wearing, or whether he had any marks or scars or tattoos. His voice, even on the phone, had that harbor sound, a rumble under it, like a boat on a bollard. The line broke twice and both times he called back, apologizing, and both times he said the same thing about the Black Head, as if repetition could make sense of it.

Now on the deck in the last light I blew on my hands and watched my breath make a cloud around my fingers. The funnel gathered itself and exhaled, smoke moving away and then collapsing as the wind pushed it down. A man with a dog went past me and the dog scuttered on the nonslip paint. Down on the quay an elderly couple stood holding on to the fence as if they too were being towed out with us, the woman’s scarf a bright oblong in the gray. The town shrank, flattening into a smear of sodium and block shapes, and beyond that the black coastline ran low and mean, a cut made by the tides that winter had not healed.

Inside, the ferry did what all ferries do, became a town and a memory of a town. There were arcades blinking at nobody and a lounge with carpeting the color of rainbows trampled by cattle. There were chairs too close together with little plastic tables that tipped into your lap if you breathed wrong. There was a smell of frying and wet wool. Men in high-vis jackets drank from paper cups and checked their phones, the thumbs moving fast as crabs.

I paid for coffee and chips and sat with the tray and could not eat. The coffee shivered in its cup with every vibration of the engines. Out the wide lounge windows the sea went from flanks the color of pewter to white lines of breaking waves then to a blue so dark it did not look like water at all. I watched one woman peel a plaster from her heel and another lift a sleeping baby into the crook of her elbow without its head slumping. I watched a teenage girl take an elastic off her wrist and twist her hair into a loop, the bronze blades of the hair shining like eelgrass. The chips went cold and the steam from the cup thinned and the sugar stuck to the spoon in a paste.

They had turned the televisions on, without sound, and on one someone scored a goal in an empty stadium, the camera rushing for the face that made a little explosion of delight, and on another there was a news ticker with words I tried not to see. I could imagine the sergeant on the other side of his supper or maybe he was still at the path down to the Black Head, the one that ran under the gorse and through the hole in the fence and then down to the outcrop where as kids we had dared each other to look.

We were not from that island. We were from up the coast on the mainland, from a village with a breakwater that did more for the gulls than for the boats. We looked at the island on clear days and said its name as if it were a word that might move our mouths into a different shape. But my brother went there anyway. He took a laboring job, he said. Digging, humping cement. It was two winters ago, after the thing with our mother. He had always been good with weather and with machines that did not want to start. He had hands that learned how to hold.

When we were small he dragged a pallet down to the burn and we stole a coil of washed-up rope and we made a raft that more or less held us. We poled along with the broom that had no bottom. He said we were explorers, and because he was eleven and I was nine that settled it. He had a burst capillary in the white of his left eye from a fist of our father's during that season when our father came home only to sleep and eat and shout. The capillary made a little red star in the corner like someone had marker-penned him. It lasted a week and then faded. Not long after that he went to live with our grandmother for a while. He came back with new trainers and a new way of holding himself that made him look older.

If I tried to fix the reason we stopped speaking to each other it would stick on something that happened the week after our mother’s funeral. There had been nine weeks of a hospice bed in the front room and the way it made visitors lean and lower their voices, and the way he tried to lighten it, the way he flipped the cushions and fluffed them, fussing like an old woman, and the way he brought an air freshener that smelled like mandarins and it made her cough. On the day of the funeral he went to the pub in the afternoon and I went with the women to the church hall and we cut sausage rolls with pink fingers and lined them on paper plates, making nice for men who would plant themselves at the end of tables and tell each other where they had been when this or that wave hit.

He did not show in time to carry the coffin. He came in halfway through the hymns with his tie over his shoulder and his shirt untucked and he put his hand on the back pew and stood in the aisle and said our mother’s name as if he had something to say to her. The way people moved to make space in the row but he did not sit. The way the words got away from me in my mouth and I could not remember the prayer I was supposed to say. After it, back at the house, he said he had wanted to say sorry to her and I said she could not hear him and he said I was a cold bastard. We fought in the kitchen, between the oven and the door to the yard, bumping the bin with our ankles. There were sandwiches on a tray on the counter and one went backwards with my elbow onto the floor and my auntie stepped on it and slipped and laughed and then cried and then laughed again, and he was grinning, shaking his head, and I hit him, the stupid open-hand slap of one boy to another, and he laughed in my face and then I pushed him against the fridge so hard that all the magnets bounced. One of them was a picture of us on a pier. From that day he stopped answering my calls. And I, dignified fool, let the gap harden to bone.

On the ferry, the chips grew a skin. I took them to the bin and tipped them in and the woman at the next table gave me a look like she thought me a wasteful man. I wanted to stop at her table and say: my brother may be dead. I wanted to say: I could choke to get him back. I wanted to say: I am a coward and this is the most I have done for him in years. Instead I put the tray on the shelf and dried my hands on my jeans and went out again to the deck.

By then the coast was mostly behind. We passed the lighthouse that looked as if some child had drawn it and then folded it three times and unfolded it. The ferry rolled and then simmered down to its line. Out here the wind cleared your mind by taking parts of it away. The deck steel was wet and the light that still hung in the sky by the west was no help to anything. The loudspeaker crackled and a woman's voice said that for safety reasons the outer decks would be closed from ten thirty and to please use the handrails when moving about. A man laughed as if this was a very good joke about drunks falling.

I had a cabin I could go to—tiny, serviceable, a rectangle of soaps in paper and a bunk I would not sleep in—but I did not want to be closed in with myself yet. I wanted the cold and the noise, wanted something outside to force the inside into a different shape. There were five other people on the starboard viewing deck. A couple in their seventies who had that glazed look of folk who could not believe where their bodies had brought them, and a young man with a bicycle helmet in his hand and calf muscles like piano hammers, and a woman wearing earphones, her mouth moving, maybe to a song or a prayer, and a man in a long coat smoking, his cigarette a red point that guttered in the wind. He cupped the cigarette and the ash went sideways, a gray banner.

The boy with the bicycle helmet saw my look and said, "Bit lively out there," cheerfully, as if it were a ride and he had the right tyres for it. His accent put a question mark after everything.

"It'll settle," the smoking man said, and moved his lips and let the smoke out through his nose.

The young woman with the headphones looked at me and then at my hands. She said softly, "You okay?"

It was a question people ask each other on ferries and on hospital corridors and in the hours before dawn. It is a thing to do with solidarity and with the way public grief makes strangers into cousins for a minute. I said, "Yes," and then thought of my mother, who wanted us to tell the truth. I said, "No," and then left it there.

The way over was four hours if the weather held. The deckhand who came to close the gate nodded to us and put a chain across the opening, the kind of chain that a dog would not be fooled by. He wore a knitted cap pulled down so far it sat like a helmet. "All right?" he said to no one and everyone.

I went in. In the stairwell people moved as if they were underwater, swaying without taking any more steps than they had to. The carpet was patterned with compasses and ropes, like they were afraid we'd forget where we were. The bunk in the cabin was as narrow as a memory. I put my bag on the little desk and turned the key in the door and half-laughed at myself, what did I think, that thieves roamed the hallways of a ferry looking for the razors and clean socks of men like me. But the act of locking had something in it that said: you choose. You choose one thing to put a line around and call it yours.

I lay down and the pillow smelled of heat and bleach. Above me the top bunk hung like a lid. The little picture on the wall was a print of boats in a harbor, the way they all are, boats that had surely never gone in any weather like this. I put the heel of my hand in each eye and pressed until light bloomed behind them. The engine noise became another kind of noise, inside the head more than outside, and my skin began to understand it as background. Sleep is not generous at times like this. It comes like a bus that stops at every street, and the pauses are longer than the moves, and you think maybe the driver has gone off to take a piss in a hedge.

Somewhere in the second hour I got up, because staying still made the muscles in my neck ache. In the corridor a child was crying with that long, abandoned, breathless cry that leaves purple around the eyes. The mother was patting desperately, her hand making that repetitive little drum on the child's back, as if she could knock on her own door and be let back in. I went past them, not wanting to be a man a woman would have to notice at two in the morning. In the foyer by the gift shop two men in yellow waterproofs with reflective strips had their hands in each other's shirt collars without anger as if they were steadying each other. One of them looked up at me and smiled and said, "You know the one about the bishop and the skip?" but then I was by, and he did not have to tell it, and we were both spared.

Back on the deck again the ferriescape had emptied, and the wind was knifey, and the stars were so bright that they looked printed. No aurora. The sea had settled down to a slow side-to-side breath. In the dark, distances change. You think something is a mile and then it is right there under you. The wake stretched behind us like a rope you could pull yourself along to get back home. In another life I would have counted in that way to keep myself together. In another life I might have been a deckhand myself, not good enough for school, better with my hands and with mornings that felt like punishments others could not face. But I had escaped, hadn't I. I had got to a small office with a glass door and a lady at a desk out front who buzzed visitors up. I had got to a heating system that worked every time. I had got to a set of dishes that matched. I had left the cold and left the broken men and left the way our mother had hugged herself to keep warm in August. And I had also left Lewis. I had perfected leaving Lewis. I was a trained leaver of Lewis.

"You're gonna catch your death," a voice said beside me in that gentle teasing way meant to open rather than close, and I turned to see the deckhand who had chained the gate earlier. His cap was gone. He had a scar in the paler scalp above the ear like a ripple made when you draw your nail down paper too hard. His jacket had official patches. He had the long face of a horseman.

"Too much of a cheapskate to pay for the heating," I said, because that is how men talk to each other so they do not have to tell the truth.

He nodded toward the dark and said, "Sometimes bit of cold keeps it honest."

He stood with his hands on the rail not looking at me and for a while we listened to the slap of spray down on the car deck. He said, after a time, "You going over or coming back?"

"Over."

He nodded, as if I had told him what meat was on my sandwich. He said, "Storm last week. Bit of loose stuff floating. Keep an eye if you're out. Folk wander more when they can't settle inside. Stub a toe, over you go."

"The chains are meant to keep me in."

"Chains keep honest folk honest." He smiled and then put his glove between his teeth to bite it. The glove squeaked. He hitched it up and said, "You look like a man in a story."

"How's that?"

"The ones who go to knock on doors nobody wants to open."

The words put a strange tremor in my belly. "You see that much from here?"

"More than you'd think." He put his chin toward the murk behind us. "People come and go. They stand and they cry and they don't. We take them where they're going and then we come back for some other lot who are going somewhere else. I do this and sometimes I feel like a man who waters plants in a graveyard." He shook his head as if to get himself to shut up. "Ignore me. Long night."

"You ever been asked to... identify someone?" The word identify was one I had only ever used with keys and with birds.

"Aye," he said, and did not offer the story deeper. "Once. Not family though. Friend. It is worse than you'd think because you can't decide which would be worse, to be wrong or to be right. And also because of the hands."

"The hands?"

He lifted his own in the air, as if the idea was perched on them. "You think you'll know them by the face. But the sea, she gives you the face last. Swells different. Takes bits. Gives back other bits. It's the hands tell you. It's the way they hold. Even still. The way a right hand looks after twenty years of turning a wrench. The nails. The way the fingers sit. Folk have a way. They keep it even when everything else is... you know." He flapped one hand to describe damage without naming it.

I closed my hands into fists and then opened them again. We stood like that a while and the wind made a sail of my coat. The deckhand said, "Sorry. I shouldn't be saying shite like that. It's not helpful."

"It's okay," I said, and meant it. "My brother had a little scar on his thumb. From a fish hook. Right there." I drew a line on my own skin with my nail, though he could not see it in the dark. "And a tattoo on the shoulder, but if it's winter..." I didn't know whether I wanted it seen or covered.

"Hands'll tell," he said again, as if by repetition he could leave me something that would work. He hitched his gloves again and then turned to go. After a step he turned back and said, "If it's not him, you'll still have to do the door-knock in your own head. If it is him, you'll have to do it for real. Either way. The bit of shite ahead is very similar to the bit of shite behind. Just different weather." He shrugged once and left with his slow rolling sailor’s gait.

He was right about the weather. I stood until the cold began to go toward pain, until my ears throbbed, and then went to the cabin and closed the door on a room that had been waiting for someone more deserving. I lay down, not bothering with my shoes, and took the phone from my pocket. It had no signal. The screen was a scape of those unloved squares you never open. Somewhere in there, down the list, was a voicemail from last spring. I had not deleted it. We had had a thunderstorm and when it went the sky was the color of cooked aubergine and everything smelled scrubbed. I had stood at the window and watched the rain dribble from the gutter joint and the phone had buzzed and his name had come up. Two rings in it had kicked to voicemail. He had said, Hey. It's me. Long pause. He had laughed, his breath making that little fish sound in the receiver, as if he had put his mouth too close. He had said, It's wet as a dog's back here. Another pause. He had said my name, first time in months, as if tasting it. He had said, I had this dream. We were in that red Ford Dad had for a bit. We were going up the cliff road and the brakes were away. I couldn't remember if it was the handbrake you told me to pull or if you were shouting something else. Silly. Never mind. Then he had said, Anyhow. Do you remember the time we put pennies on the railway line and they came back thin as mirrors? I still have one. You? And then he had said, Right, I'll go. Love you. Then a little clatter, the sound of him putting the phone somewhere that became the sound of other things to do.

I had tried calling back half an hour later and a man had answered and shouted over noise that sounded like a bar and the call had dropped. I had never pursued it. On the days when I tell the truth to myself I know I had been waiting for him to show he was worthy of me showing up. I had needed him to come correct, as our cousin used to say. I had not wanted to risk being the one who apologizes and is laughed at again. Pride is a word that does not have in it any of the pus or the stink or the yellow bruise that brings it.

I listened to the message again now. The pitch of it made something in my chest buckle. He had loved me with the easy love of an elder brother who does not know how frightening he is to the younger one. He had loved me like you love something you found before anyone else did. He had been loud and often an arse and he had shared the last chip and he had put the fishhook through his thumb and not cried until I had cried and then laughed at us both.

When the sun comes up at sea it does it with reluctance. First there is more black and then a gray that is so careful you do not notice it until it has been there a long time. The ferry made a deep contented sound as if it were a dog curling into sleep. I sat up with a crack in my neck. My mouth tasted like old pennies. The tannoy bleeped and the voice said, We will shortly be arriving into Story Harbour. If you have a vehicle on the car deck, please return to it at this time, ensuring all passengers are within your vehicle. If you are traveling on foot, please make your way to the forward lounge, where you will be directed to the gangway. We hope you have enjoyed your crossing this evening, and remind you to take all your belongings with you when you disembark. Mind how you go. Her voice had a warmth in it that felt human and real as if she had said those last three words to someone she loved.

I stood and brushed my trousers with my palms, as if dust would care at this point, then zipped my bag and checked the pockets like a man who gets on and off planes for a living. The corridor was suddenly all motion. The crying child had discovered a second wind, or second sorrow, and was making it count. The men in waterproofs were gone. In the lounge a group of boys with striped scarves opened crisp packets and battered each other in the shoulder very gently. A priest sat with a magazine about tractors and a red case by his feet that looked like it might contain something serious. Men in baseball caps with Gaelic names on them looked like men anywhere. A woman in an orange jacket had a radio that staticked at her as if it were telling secrets. People stood, then sat, then stood again, then shuffled, the way you do when you are about to be allowed somewhere you have been told for hours is not yet.

In the forward lounge the air was sour and warm. The condensation had made little driplines down the windows. One of the television screens showed the harbor we were going into, filmed from the bow, so we could see ourselves arriving into the place where we were. The harbor wall had a gap like a missing tooth and inside it the water was dark and not moving. Shore lights shone and moved their shining on the water. In one of the skylines was a crane that looked like a religious object. It had been a long time since I had been here and I had never arrived by sea. The island sat with its shoulders hunched, low hills like animals sleeping. A slice of the morning was peach-colored out under a bank of low cloud. You see too much when you approach slowly. People in the lounge craned their necks and peered with that hunger that means they are not thinking about any dead person. I watched with my hand on the zipper pull of my bag.

When you go down to the car deck as a foot passenger you pass a last checkpoint where a woman with a smile in one eye will tear your stub and put it in a box. Then you go down the stairs where the air goes from cooked to raw. The smell of rubber and old oil is everything. The ceiling is low and the noise of the lifting ramp goes deep into your teeth. Men with keys in their mouths climb into cabs taller than your house. The trucks start up and let their brakes off with sighs. There are handprints smeared on the bulkhead at the level of knees and faces. You think absurdly that if everyone pressed hard enough the metal would buckle and let daylight in.

At the top of the stairs to the gangway a woman in a cap with the ferry company's logo said, "All right, love?" like a sister would, and I said, "Aye," like a man at a gate in a field, and we each did our job. The gangway had the wobble of a baby's breath. It led down and then along and the plexiglass creaked but did not crack. Out there, through the clouded walls, the water made oily dark shapes as if something large was turning over. The gangway touched the shore with a rubber lip. For a second we were both, floating and fixed.

I could see him at the bottom then, by the terminal doors. He had the uniform of police without the aggression of it. His face was broad in a way that meant his mother had been easy with him. He had a notebook in his hand and he saw me and he knew it was me. He put the notebook in his pocket and stepped forward and put his hand out.

"Mr. Harrow? I'm Sergeant Hunter."

He had my name, my brother's name, the island's name, the morning. He had all the right words for the wrong day. I put my hand in his and felt how men do this everywhere, pressing their lives into the narrow skin between thumb and forefinger to see who they are. His hand was warm and real and did not tell me anything about the man he would have to be for the next hour. Behind him, on the glass of the terminal, was a poster for a ceilidh that had the picture of a fiddle in tinted gold. There was a vending machine with a crooked Pepsi sticker. Out beyond the car park the first birds of the day lifted off the grass like something pulled out of a sleeve by a magician.

"Thank you for coming over," he said. His voice was as it had been on the phone, careful. He glanced at my bag. "I can take that for you, if you like."

"No," I said. "I'm okay." I was and I wasn't. The floor had stopped moving and suddenly everything in my body registered that.

"We can go straight up, if you're ready," he said. "Or you can have a minute. There's a cafe, just opening. Toast and that." He looked toward the double doors where a woman in an apron was sliding a shutter up with her shoulder and wiping her hands on a cloth. A smell of bread came through, stubborn and cozy.

"Straight up," I heard myself say, as if there had been a choice and I had made it bravely. He nodded once and then turned half away and waited while I took a breath and then we set off, the rubber soles of my boots squeaking once on the polished tile. He held the door and it sighed as if it was sick of all this. The morning came in with us and stayed in the corridor like a quieter person, not taking up any room, waiting for its name to be called.
