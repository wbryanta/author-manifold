By the time the piano goes, the light has shifted across the living room and turned the bare boards into something like a pond — long shallow gleams with tiny ripples of dust in them. Two men in matching barbershop bowling shirts tilt the old upright off its brass casters and shimmy it out with a rhythm that reminds me of a thing you hum to yourself on a day you don’t want to be where you are. I am at the card table with the cash box and a roll of blue painter’s tape because the neon price stickers we bought at Dollar General never stuck to anything for more than a breath. I write PIANO $100 in block letters, put it on the varnished side panel, watch it slip and drift to the floor, and end up just telling them the number.

You’re sure? the one with the darker mustache asks, and I am not sure of anything. The piano was a gift from my father to my mother on their seventh wedding anniversary, the year before he took his suitcase and a carton of Lucky Strikes and got in the neighbor’s Chevy and followed the sign for Route 42 south until he wasn’t anybody’s father anymore. My mother played the piano in the afternoons, the same three wistful songs over and over, until she switched to running her thumb along the edge and letting the shine lift up questions she never asked anyone. This is what I think of when the men hoist it — how the low notes sounded like the floorboards groaning, how the high ones made the dog put his paw over his eyes. But it is Saturday, and it is after noon, and the piano is only as much wood as two men and a dolly can wheel into a waiting truck.

We’re having a sale, I tell myself, the way you tell a nervous child the rules of a game they didn’t agree to. The sign is at the end of the driveway, hand-lettered in my crooked print: ESTATE SALE 9–4, NO EARLY BIRDS. At eight twenty a man in a windbreaker knocked on the front door anyway and asked if there were guns or coins. When I said no, he looked over my shoulder as if my no were the sort of thing that could be lying to him.

You’d be surprised what people have lying around, he said, and I didn’t answer him because we all had had a year of lying around and there are things a person keeps that no one else would ever put a price on, and anyway there are plenty of surprises piled on every horizontal surface in this house and none of them are coins.

Getting the piano out means the carpet runner can be rolled and carried, the coffee table can be trekked to the lawn and surrounded by three women who speak softly to each other in another language while they lift a teacup to the light and read the little black stamp on the bottom, someone can begin to measure the width of the front door with a tape measure that recoils into itself with a little song. I sit at the card table and drink lukewarm coffee from my mother’s chipped mug, and the world goes out and then comes back, as if I am the stubborn center and everything else is a patient orbit.

You okay? Sue asks. Sue walked over from two houses down just before the nine o’clock people arrived and set out a plastic tub of grocery sacks, a roll of quarters for change, and a stack of paper towels as if we might suddenly need to mop the grief up off the floor. Her hair is the shortest I have ever seen it, her husband’s name embroidered above her breast in red script as if she is wearing his presence over her heart. She was my mother’s friend from church, the one with a key to the house for when the furnace made a new noise or the mail didn’t get pulled in. She has been tidying and then un-tidying as customers pull items down, replacing a plate with a neat circle of pale wood on the wall, straightening the frame around the graduation photo with the white cap and gown, sliding the bowl of taped-together spoons and forks closer to the cash box as if silverware is what thieves most love to nab.

Define okay, I say.

She puts her hand on my shoulder and leaves it there, hardly skimming the shirt. I didn’t think it would be this fast, she says.

We priced it to move, I say, and that is true. The realtor told me people don’t buy houses with things in them, it spooks them. What if there are ghosts, he said and smiled like he was kidding, but I have never known a person from this town who will not carve a pie and leave a neat wedge on the plate in case a loved one shows up late. The realtor’s name is Todd and he is a young man with plumped out assurances and a mother on the school board and a way of holding my elbow when he speaks to me that makes me want to shake him off and walk out the front door to the sidewalk to nothing.

This is how we say goodbye here: we let strangers come in and touch what has seen us in our pajamas.

The couch goes to a woman with three children who hop on its cushions and then hop off at their mother’s soundless look. She counts twenties into my palm while telling me about the new apartment—two bedrooms, a balcony, a view of something green, even if it is just the Kroger’s lawn. The loveseat is gone already, I say, as if I owe her a warning. She shrugs, breathes through her mouth a moment, hands me more money than I have asked for, and refuses the change. She does not tell me her name but she tells me she will put the couch in the kids’ room so they can watch the little TV she got from her sister, so they can play Minecraft in a corner and not under her feet while she cooks. I say cool like I am someone who says cool, and she smiles and looks away, and I want to say, there is a stain on the underside of the third cushion in the shape of Ohio.

Across the street, Mr. Haley sits on his porch with his oxygen tank like a date. He’s got an Ohio State cap pulled low on his forehead and a radio with a cracked antenna set to the sports station. The game starts in an hour and a half and everything before that is talk about whether the new quarterback is as steady as the old one and whether the left guard has anything left in his knees. If this were a different Saturday in a different year my mother would be inside on the couch with a glass of iced tea watching men collide on a field and she would be either dozing or yelling at the TV with the sound down, because she said you don’t need the sound to see whether a play is good.

Now the whole parade is in here: church people, antique people, people who saw the sign and were surprised to recognize the house my mother has lived in for forty-eight years. A man with a leather visor asks me if the marbles are handblown, a woman with a deep red lipstick that makes me love and fear her at once studies the pattern of the Pyrex bowls and murmurs the names my mother used to call them — Butterfly Gold, Woodland, Autumn Harvest — as if they are characters who could be invited to dinner. Someone asks if we have picture frames without the pictures, and Sue laughs and says if she takes the picture out, what’s the story? and the woman looks like she has learned something and like she will not buy the frame after all. There is a kind of theater to this, and I understand why some people do it for profit, staging and de-staging the scenes of other people’s lives so that a sale can be a kind of curtain call.

The man who buys my father’s radio is younger than me and has a baby in a carrier on his stomach and a tattoo of a cloud on his wrist. He pays with wrinkled fives and stands with his palm flat on the wooden top like it is a barely-tamed horse. It is the sort of radio that has dials like knuckles and glows like a jar with a firefly in it when you turn it on. He says he’ll fix it up for his garage so he has something old to listen to games on and I do not tell him that this radio has held all the games that broke my mother’s heart and all the ones that made her clap alone in a room after sunset.

Ex-husbands and ex-wives have a way of inhabiting things, I say to Sue under my breath, and she nods without looking at me and swats a fat brown fly into someone else’s afternoon.

The light slides. People stop saying good morning and start saying enjoy your day as if there were someday to enjoy other than this one. I have not left this house for so long without taking it with me that the air outside tastes like the rind of a lemon. A man in a hunter’s orange sweatshirt lingers over the toolbox in the dining room, his fingers in the grooves of my mother’s ex-boyfriend’s socket set. He smells like the sort of gum you chew because you can’t spit. He is a stranger, and I do not know his name, and it does not matter whether he is a kind man or a brutal one, he will take the sockets and the rusted nails and all the flathead screwdrivers that have been used as paint openers and chisels with equal seriousness and he will put them in his own basement where they will hold a roof in place when he needs them to, and that is as good an end for them as any.

My mother never bought a thing she did not think had another thing inside it. The cupboard in the basement is full of pickle jars with no lids and kitchen twine and egg cartons with one brown-shelled egg still in the corner from the spring when Mrs. Kelly’s chickens laid more than she had neighbors. The hall closet holds sixteen blankets for a house that never had more than three beds because at some point she knew if there were a blizzard and we were stranded she could keep us warm for a season. She could not throw anything away because everything might someday be necessary; this was the lesson she learned from being a girl with a father who rolled cigarettes with papers he dried on the radiator all winter long. I have tried to not inherit that lesson and then find myself with three reams of paper towels folded into a drawer I will leave behind for someone to tug open and feel vaguely comforted by.

Do you have any jewelry? a woman in a denim dress that barely skims her knees asks. She is the fifth person to ask since lunch.

Just her costume stuff, I say, and she flutters her eyes like I am accusing her.

For my daughter, she says. She just likes to play dress-up.

I take her to the bedroom that still smells like powder and the peach lotion my mother used in the summer because she said men like women who smell like dessert and then would grin and say, if I ever sense a man in the room, which made me feel both disloyal and loyal at once. The bedspread is downsized to cover the bare mattress because someone bought the wool throw two hours ago. A braided rug hunches like a cat in front of the hope chest, and the perfume bottles on the dresser make a reflecting pool of cheap glass. The jewelry is in a tin that used to hold toffee and now holds necklaces that turn my fingers green if I hold them too long. The woman tilts open the lid to the song of a hinge that sounds like a door left out in the rain and sifts through the years.

This was my Easter necklace, I say, not intending to. It is a fake pearl with a yellow glint to it, like the inside of an egg yolk, and my mother made me wear it with a dress that had a white girdle-stiff sash. You wore gloves to church on Easter in this town even if you were only six and your hair was a stubborn thatch that had to be coaxed into ringlets around socks.

Do you have any boys’ stuff? she says, and I blink clear.

No boys, I say. Just me.

My mother had never been the sort of woman who thought a man’s presence could sanctify or destroy a room, and so we had a house arranged for two women to navigate each other, which meant mugs with chips turned to the back, pictures of me that she had arranged chronologically as if an average afternoon needed an arc. The top drawer of the dresser still holds the little accordion-pleated paper fans I made in second grade for a class Japan Day where Mrs. Archer wore her hair like a bun you could put a pencil through and let us eat rice out of plastic bowls, and I take one of the fans and slip it into the pocket of my jeans where something used to be and is not anymore.

At the cash box, I refuse the ten-dollar offer for the dining room table because the woman making it is looking me in the eye like she wants to see me try to hold on. The dining table is scarred in a way that reads like a story if you have the key, gouges and rings and a place where the varnish wore down from my mother’s elbows resting there through every phone call that drained her. I have no illusions about the necessity of tables. I know some houses where they are simply platforms for mail. Still, this table was where I did a puzzle with my mother every winter, edges first, then sky, then faces and windowsills and the inexplicable cat. It was where my mother ate spaghetti and wiped her plate with bread and ended every meal by sitting a minute longer and saying, now wasn’t that something, as if she had also had a conversation and not just noodles.

I can do eighty, the woman with the ten-dollar offer says after I have laughed in the kindest way I know how. She places her palm on the table and indexes it, like she is counting its ribs. She talks about her son who has outgrown the folding card table in his apartment. Then she says, or seventy-five? in a way that feels like she thinks money is an accusation and she cannot bear to be accused.

Eighty, I say, because I have learned that this is what mercy means when things have to move.

Outside, the wind carries the smell of cut grass and faint chlorine. The pool at the community center is still open because the old men who play water volleyball scream when they close it, and the closed season moves later every year anyway, into a September that swelters and then turns on us overnight, makes us pull quilts out and smell the cedar in them. I can feel the turning before it turns, the house rousing to remember itself as a box.

Do you want to eat? Sue says, and I realize it is two-thirty and I have had a half a doughnut and three cups of coffee, one of which had grounds stuck in my teeth and every word after tasted like a field. She presses a brown bag into my hand with a turkey sandwich in it, mayo in the proper amount, crusts on because I am fifty-two and can handle a crust now. I eat standing up with the bag still crinkled around my wrist like a cuff, and each bite makes the ground hold my feet a little more to itself. It has been a year of feeling unmoored, of stepping out of the house and then back in because the weather at the door met you like a stranger and would not make friendly.

She would have hated this, I say with my mouth full.

She would have stood at that table and told everyone stories in a way that kept them here for hours, Sue says, and then she would have been mean at me for letting them dent the couch.

She would have stood here and called me every thirty seconds when I left the room, I say. Where did you go? Who bought the pickle jar? Did you sell my good scissors for a song?

That woman has my bread tins, Sue says and points at a woman with hair grown out to a heavy halo and a headband dividing it into fields. That’s my bread recipe she’s taking with her, without paying a dime.

You can keep the recipe, I say.

She hands me a napkin she has folded into a spidery thing. Your mother would want you to sit and enjoy the people, she says.

If by enjoy she means tolerate, I say.

She squints at me with love in it. That, she says.

Three high school boys come in a pack, all shoulder and elbow and shoes with an idea of themselves in them. They whisper in corners and take pictures of themselves holding a silver teapot like a grenade. One of them buys the stack of vinyl LPs for twenty bucks and arms them like a baby. I don’t tell him that there’s a hair in the sleeve of the Fleetwood Mac album because I feel like every home they’ve ever known has been sanitized into a hotel lobby by mothers and stepmothers and I want him to take a thing with a remnant of someone else’s afternoons clinging to it. Another boy asks if any of the lamps are haunted and then looks embarrassed by himself, like he meant to be thoroughly ironic but stepped into a sincere sentence by mistake. The third boy discovers the shoebox with the foreign coins in it and holds a Canadian quarter to the light as if its moose is a god.

We travel to Canada once a decade, I tell him. We put the coins in a box and never cash them in because we like the reminder that you once felt lost and spent something and were made whole again.

I buy in, he says, and I want to tell him he doesn’t have to lower himself to old-woman talk. Instead, I charge him three dollars for the box and he looks like I have given him his grandmother’s hand knotted afghan without asking for thanks.

People offer condolences as though the bereavement is an object they can put down on the table and leave behind, and I accept them like glass I place carefully on a high shelf. When someone says, she was a good one, I say yes, she was a good one, but I do not say that the goodness of a person is not something you can tally like receipts in a little black book. The good of my mother was sometimes a casserole delivered to a front step, sometimes a spectacular meanness that sat in her lap like a second cat. I could have told anyone who asked about the time she dragged a folding table into the kitchen and set up a command center and called thirty people to make a food chain for a family whose daughter had driven herself into a bridge because winter had eaten her imagination. I could have also told them about the time she laughed as she watched a man who had cheated on his wife try to lift a too-heavy box into the back of his pickup and smacked my wrist when I moved to help him.

She contained a weather that you had to learn to predict and a front that never quite blew over, and I am trying to remember her as something other than a series of small storms. Holding a price gun in your hand makes the world look like it belongs to other people, and maybe that is a relief. Maybe there is relief to be had in being a person at a folding table who says three dollars to a sweater and does not have to account for how her mother cried into washcloths sometimes so that no one would see her eyes go red.

A woman with a toddler on her hip lifts up the group of 45s and puts them down without looking at the songs. The toddler presses her face into her mother’s shoulder and refuses the cracker she is offered. When she reaches for the plate with the painted violets, I say careful and she looks at me like the room is suddenly gray. It isn’t until much later that I wish I had said go ahead, pick it up sweetheart, because objects want the pure hands of the new upon them and I have put more caution in them than is necessary.

A man I went to high school with stands in the doorway of the kitchen and tilts his head the way you tilt a map. It takes me a moment to pull up his name from the cold storage of my memory and then I feel the ridiculous heat of accomplishment when it comes. Matt. He was the boy everyone said had a future when all he had was a speed that carried him into a tree a winter later. He did not die, but he reorganized, and I remember coming home from college and seeing him bagging groceries at Kroger with a tenderness that broke me all the way down and how he had said, paper or plastic, and I had said, paper please if you have it, and how he had lined up the boxes just so.

Your mom, he says, and there is a hanging wound in the sentence and we both look into it.

She was a force, I say.

My mother liked him because he kept his hat off at the table and yes ma’amed her. He buys the cast iron skillet and a wooden mixing spoon from the 1970s that has the stains of three generations of sauces and pays in singles folded to half their size. He says, I make breakfast, and for a moment we are both lifted out of the way we know each other and into a new town where two middle-aged people have breakfast at the same time, separately.

When the old upright is finally gone, there is a mark on the floor where it has been sitting since 1974. It looks like a tide line. I want to write my name in it like a child at the beach would write their name in wet sand and then wait to see if any wave will take the letters. My mother would have gotten the vinegar and a rag and knelt right there, and if she came across a story anyone told about a woman who knelt at an altar, she would have thought they meant the kitchen. The barbershop-shirted men tilt the dolly backward and inch down the front steps and I feel my heart in both my ears and my mouth because this is how you measure being alive: a thing you love is held above the possibility of breaking. When they load it into the truck, one of the men faces it and puts his hands on it while the other ratchets the strap over and around, and I am suddenly pulled like a tooth by the way a person can be tender to anything.

I want to be done; I want it to last. This is what I understand today. Some people stand in the doorway to the back porch and think about what it would be to sit there with the light coming through the maple and the smell of some thousand-egg quiche or the world's most reliable banana bread. They say, this is a nice yard, and I say, we never did much with it, and they nod like a yard is the sort of thing that will take offense if you say it forces nothing to grow.

At some point, I go upstairs with a woman who is looking for curtains and when I stand in the hallway there is a hush I haven’t known in decades. The hall bathroom has the mustard-yellow tub and the wallpaper with flowers that burst like fireworks, darkened above the heat register so that the flowers look like they have been smoking cigarettes. The tiny linen closet is open, towels rolled into themselves, a smell of Ivory soap that makes something under my ribs unclench. My room still has the paint I chose in seventh grade, a thin cheerful green that didn’t look like growth to me then but does now, and the track of the bed is pressed into the carpet in a rectangle that still holds the outline of something I cannot bring myself to name. In the little half-roof side attic where the Christmas decorations were kept, there is the box with the feather angel who has a look on her face like she is looking down on an error with both pity and delight. The woman takes the lace curtains and holds them up to the light like the ghosts of birds, and when I say fifteen for the pair she digs in her purse for cash and scents the air with the cologne her mother must have worn. She tells me her son is moving to Columbus and needs something to soften his windows. I think it is a strange word, soften, and also exactly right.

My mother once stood at the top of these stairs and shouted my full name into the house like it had wronged her. I was in the backyard with a boy named Scott who had a faded green ball cap on backward and I was changing the film in my camera and he was telling me what his father had said when he dropped out of college after one day. I can smell the heat of that afternoon and the sharpness of the plastic camera body and hear my mother’s voice split the sky. I think of Scott sometimes when I pass the diner he manages now, see him in a white paper hat behind the counter making pancakes for men who get there at six, and I wonder if he would let me crawl across the counter and eat a pancake there because I would feel safer with a surface between my belly and the world.

Back downstairs, there is a lull. The front door stays open and admits the sun and a child without a hand holding it who wanders half into the kitchen and then twice as far into the living room and then stops in front of the empty space where the couch was as if to say, the room is a hole. His father retrieves him with two fingers on his collar and looks at me like parenthood is hours and hours of discovering there are ten more ways for a kid to crack his head than anyone ever warned you about. Sue moves a stack of cookbooks from one counter to a card table by the door and suddenly they are something people will sell you their last smile for. Betty Crocker has become an icon in a way that makes me laugh because I can picture my mother cussing the woman’s name when the apple pie recipe leaked half its butter into the oven and made a smoke that set off the detector ten minutes before the neighbors were supposed to arrive.

People bring me coins with quarters in them that glint and names of streets, this one’s a state, this one’s a bird, this one is the year you were born, as if to tether me to a year might give them a discount. I break bills like a machine whose drawer sticks. I tape SOLD signs to a dresser and feel like there is relief in the word; it says that the transference of care has been agreed to by the universe and we may relax in its wake.

There is also the man who wants a deal on the suitcase full of old photographs, and his impulse is so alien to me that I do not trust my face to look like a face. You’ll never look at them again, he says with a kind of persuasive brightness you save for sensible shoes. Your kids won’t want them, I’ll go through and find the few that mean something and you won’t have to.

But they all mean something, I say, and he laughs like I’m joking. Sue materializes in the way she does when a person standing near me is about to sprout horns I cannot see.

We’re keeping the photos, she says. Church-lady firm; honey over steel.

I take the suitcase into the hall and slide it under the narrow table that used to hold the glass bowl with the pears in it that always made the house look like people lived here on purpose. I tell myself I will go through the photographs one a day like vitamins once I am back in my house in the city and that what floats to the top will be the ones I keep. There’s my mother with a swimsuit bow tied like a joke, my mother standing next to a brown shag tree she pretended for a season was stylish because she needed us to think she did not taste the dust on her tongue. There’s me with a dog I loved more fiercely than any person for three years straight, and there is my mother’s third car and her fifth kitchen and the winter we painted every room the color of something fruit and the trim stayed stubborn and yellow as teeth.

Later I will find the photograph of my mother as a girl standing in front of a car whose name she remembered until the night she did not, her legs like sticks that might in a cruel mood have snapped. She is fourteen and looking at her own life as if she could see down into it a half mile and knows she will have to forage and never arrive, and her mouth is set as if she will not call me when she falls out of bed on a cold January night. I do not know where I will be when I pull that photo up into the light, but I know I will run my thumb along the edge of the glossy and leave a grease there that one day a man in a windbreaker who trades in the residue of other people’s lives will tell someone lowers the value a bit.

Midafternoon, the sun has its sideways part of the day, good for photographs and for seeing the parts of the house you thought were clean. I realize the baseboards in the hallway have held onto a lace of dust and I crouch with a rag and then stop myself, because what would I be cleaning for? I am shocked, each time a new blank stripe embarks on the wall where a picture used to hang, by the lighter paint underneath, as if the house has been storing sunlight away under glass.

A man with ashy hair and a slight limp introduces himself as Ray and tells me he bought this house when it was first built, lived here with his wife for three years before his job at the paper mill moved him downriver. He says it’s gratifying to see someone kept the banister as clean as his wife did, and I make a noise and nod and do not say that the banister holds both of my mother’s hand oils and mine; it holds the record of every time I went upstairs in a rage and came back down ashamed.

You know, he says, put a skylight in this kitchen and you’ll wake every morning like you did something right. He walks into the dining room and stands exactly where the table was and takes an imaginary cup of coffee to his mouth, and in that instant I can see his wife as a woman with hair parted down the middle and a blouse patterned with neat dots, and I want to follow him around and ask him what else he knows about me. He hands me a twenty for a floor lamp, shakes my hand like a gentleman, and tells me he’ll leave me to my remembering. After he goes, the kitchen lights look like promises I am not sure I want to keep.

It did not rain at the cemetery, and I told everyone I hoped it would because it makes for a good story. It did not rain when she lay down to sleep and did not get up, and I thought it would because it made room for a little drama. The world withholds theatrics when you hope for them and spills them when you cannot take them. Three weeks after the funeral there was a derecho and the maple out front dropped a limb so big across the sidewalk you would have sworn a giant lay down and folded his hands. The power was out for three days and I stayed in a friend’s house across town and lit candles and taught myself to not reach for switches with my whole body, and when the power came back and the house made the crashing sigh of electricity finding its way, I felt cheated, as if the darkness had been the only thing honest in weeks.

Around three-thirty, the football game starts. I know because the street changes its sound; doors close with a kind of longing to them. A man holding a Pyrex casserole dish like a trophy calls into the living room to his wife, we gotta get home, and she yells, two minutes, and then holds up a crocheted afghan that looks like it could either murder you or save your life. Mr. Haley across the street turns his radio up and I can hear the pregame show through a gravel of interference. It is ritual more than anything else, I think, the way my mother stirred the same chili recipe in October and wore the same soft gray sweater on the first morning she could see her breath and the way she made calls on Sunday afternoons and we said the same phrases into the phone in roughly the same order no matter how the week had gone. I think some part of her believed that if you draped your life with enough repetition, loss would not be able to get purchase on it.

I do not watch the game. I check pockets of my mother’s coats for tissues, and when I find one folded into a neat square I press it back into the soft pocket like a small tide touching a shore. I hold the handle of her hatchet-handed teapot and I do not put a price on it. I look into the oven as if it might give me a last polaroid of my mother and see only the racks my father bought at the hardware store in 1972 to replace the bent ones that came with the house. There is no spell you can pronounce over a day like this that will turn it into something else. There is only money you can count and blue tape you can peel, and a list I made the night before on the back of a grocery receipt — ice maker, check; get more bags, check; stop the mail, oh God stop the mail.

Some of the people are kind in a way that makes you want to stay on your feet. They tell me a story about my mother that I have not heard, the doctor’s receptionist who said your mom always remembered my kid’s name, he’s thirty now and I swear I went a year without anyone saying it out loud until she did, the woman from the diner who said your mom came in every Thursday like it was church, and when she didn’t for two months after her hip she called me from the hospital and said, save my seat. Some of the people are the sort who will open a drawer I have not opened and pull out the ugly thing and hold it up and say, you want how much for this? and I want to smack their hand and say, I want you to go away. In the middle there are women my age with peachy lipstick who say this is lovely and mean it and who understand a price is a kind of fiction we agree to tell each other so that something can move on with dignity.

Around four, the light begins to go grainy and lovely. You can see the dust we have been stirring up all day like you can suddenly see the bacteria on your skin under a certain lamp. I count the bills in the money box and the amount is both more than I think love is worth and less than an afternoon. I tuck it into a Ziploc bag because my mother would, and also because I do not trust any box labeled cash to close its mouth when I ask it to. Sue plugs a vacuum in and then pulls the plug because somehow the sound would be an affront, and the little bits of paper and leaves and glittery confetti that came from who knows what stay where they are, a map to a place we were for a day. A man arrives late on a motorcycle, helmet under his arm like a moon, and asks if the books are still here. He has eyes like pockets and moves through the house with the care of someone who has only ever lived in places where if you break a thing there is hell to pay, and I want him to stay until the house empties around us and then sit down on the floor in the unrolled silence and tell me someone once kept him alive when he did not deserve to be.

He buys a stack of books that fills a grocery sack so full it could be a toddler. He asks if we are from around here and I say we are the most from around here you can be, and he smiles and says, my mom’s from down toward Chillicothe, and we look at each other then like we could both explain the fear of a boarded-up factory to someone if we had to. I put the money back in the bag and he ties the books to the back of his bike with a bungee cord in a way that says the books will arrive upright in another house owned by a person with exactly one chair and a refrigerator that hums like a hymnal.

When the house has drained to a trickle of people and the afternoon has slid itself into the bank, I take one more walk through the rooms. I don’t need to — I have been walking through rooms for hours, I have been telling people where to find the cookie sheets and the weird frosted jelly glasses with cartoon cows on them, I have been telling people yes there is a box of bolts, no we do not have an attic full of anything other than the heat — but I take the walk anyway because the house is all edges now and I want to feel my side on its frame.

In the bedroom, the divot on the mattress where her body was soft invites my hand. The hallway sings back a little when I step on the third stair. In the kitchen, the counters we tiled ourselves one winter catch the light like a lake does when you lean over it with your hair falling into itself and your mouth open to the water. The calendar still reads April because she died in May and I could not the week after bring myself to fill in the squares with anything that might make the days look like a board game I could win.

There is a thing that happens sometimes when someone dies where the light shifts toward the past in a way that makes the present look like a rumor. You feel like a person walking up the aisle of a darkened movie theater looking for your seat. It is embarrassing; you do not want to annoy anyone by standing in front of them too long. You do not want to bother the people who are watching, who are always in their places wherever you go. I am, now, in the aisle. I am in the place between sitting and walking. I am waiting for someone to say, excuse me, can I help you find your row, and when they do not I sit down on the end of the empty couchless room as if I am at a bus stop.

I suspect I will not come back here again. The realtor would prefer that, and my spine is tired of this posture. My mother will not come back and sit on the couch and put her ankles up on the table and say, put a movie on, any movie. She will not ask me to go get her a frosty root beer from the Izzy’s machines at the gas station and say on my return, you are a good one, and then not look at me for the duration of the can. She will not leave me two messages that start with my full name and deteriorate from there into the business of the day, which is whatever is in the paper and whatever has creaked.

Sue stands next to me with a single grocery bag in her hand — she has taken a plate and the butter dish that she always admired and never bought and I am grateful for the appropriation, the way a friend hands you your coat when you have lost the use of your hands. She says, you should keep the afghan with the squares. I think about it and say, my grandmother made that and my mother couldn’t look at it without crying for reasons she never explained from the middle of her, and so yes, I will.

You did good, she says, and I think of my mother filling out my progress report with her own handwriting because she refused to believe that a report was only supposed to tell you whether you had learned your multiplication tables and not who you were. Does anyone get exactly as much love as they need? We all go hungry and it is never the same hunger. I take the afghan and lay it over my arm like a shawl and it makes me look like a person who lives somewhere.

We pull the front door shut and it makes the sound I have heard all my life when the door shut — that final ssshhh the weatherstrip gives as it seals. The men in the truck with the piano honk in a way that is not at me and yet is, and a woman in a minivan waves, and someone has leaned a stool up against the telephone pole at the end of the yard like they are saving the spot for a better thought. The grass is bent down in ovals where the scouts of the day stood and chewed gum and eyed the grill. The sun is low enough to hurt you a little if you look it in the face and the maples across the street have that edge-of-beginning-burn color, and the little flag on the mailbox is up because I put it up myself by force of habit and there is nothing in there but maybe one last flyer offering to clean gutters or prune back a wild prayer. If this were a proper end to a story, it would rain or it would not rain; someone would come up the walk with something in their hand and say, you forgot this, or I would drop the keys and they would slip through the cracks of the porch and I would climb under and find a toad who would lick its eye.

Instead, Sue kisses my cheek and leaves a lip print that does not dry for a long time. I stand there with my hand on the doorknob like it has to be warmed to the temperature of my palm before I can turn it. Marbles are somewhere in a pocket; the paper fan is in another. I have the afghan.

Across the street, Mr. Haley who will outlast us all because he is oxygen and stubbornness presses his radio to his ear and shouts yes in a hoarse little joy I can feel shake the air over my head. It seems fair to let a game stand in for the good news we need. I lock the house, then unlock it and walk back in just to pick up one last thing — the jar of buttons shaped like planets that my mother kept in the sewing basket like a constellation. It is only weight, the weight of small decisions stored in a jar, but it will, in my house, click into its spot, and I will open it sometimes and smell the soft plastic and the old thread and think of the world gathered small enough to fit in the palm of my hand, which is as close as we get to anything.

On my way to the car, a woman I do not know with a cat carrier in her passenger seat asks me if there will be a second day. I say, no, we got it done. She smiles in relief like she can stop moving then, like a cloud that has decided where it will rain. I get in the driver’s seat and set the keys in the little tray that holds receipts and melted gum and something that will turn into a story later if I let it, and I hold the jar of buttons between my knees and turn one over with my fingers until it warms. The house behind me is a square that holds no one. The town around me is a circle that will hold me awhile longer. I start the car and it sputters once and then catches, and I put my hand on the rearview mirror and tilt it so that for a moment all it shows me is the blue of my shirt and a triangle of evening and the top right corner of the house that will belong to a person whose name I will not know when I drive through next spring, and then I aim it back at the road and drive away.
