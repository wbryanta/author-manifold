The mud gave under my boots with the sigh of something remembering how to be earth. It had been water so long that everything about it was reluctant—the way it took the weight of me and the tripod, the way it broke crust by crust into damp slabs that smelt like old pennies and algae and the shadow of fish. I had expected a field of cracked lizard-skin like the photographs in lean years past, random tessellations drawn by sun and thirst. But this drought had the quality of having taken so much for so long that even desiccation had become cautious. There were places tight and armor-hard, the outer skin resisting, shiny as varnish, the imprint of a receding ripple held like a freeze-frame. Beneath, the mud was stubborn and cool as the inside of a cheek. Every step made a wet sound, then a dry flake broke with the brittleness of something overdone, and the white dust it made was faintly greasy on my tongue when the wind lifted it.

I walked down the line of the old boat ramp because there were still the embedded grooves for tired summer tires to follow, and because the geometry of man-made things had always been the easiest way for me to slide from one time into another. The ramp ended abruptly at a shelf of empty air that had once been covered by twenty feet of lake, then forty. Now the last water had pulled back into a long wavering sheen, a skirt gathered and hitched, exposing a mangled arrangement of roots and posts and a stop sign leaned at an angle like someone’s face held underwater too long. The stop sign had graffiti in fish scales, a transluminescent banding. A gull used it as a perching place. When it lifted off, the wind tipped it and there was a creak from the hidden pivot of mud.

“You’re early,” the man from the county had said on the phone that morning, not meaning anything by it. “Sun won’t do you any favors today. The sheriff’s blocked off the public access up top. There’s trespassing we don’t want to deal with.” By early he meant before the heat quivered up and the dragonflies started doing their clockwork loops. He meant before the people who had not been born when the valley flooded showed up with their metal detectors and picnic baskets, to point at what had been our town and call it a ghost, as if it hadn’t been carried whole and panting into the belly of the lake. As if our houses had learned to breathe through fish gills and wait.

I kept walking, the one-wheeled cart banging against my thigh. The tripod legs were cinched with Velcro and the total station was tucked into its foam cradle in the yellow case. I felt the pull of the strap across my chest like a hand resisting. Behind me, the reservoir surface winked flat and indifferent. Up on the ridge, the white chalk line that marked the high-water ring gleamed around the dark bowl like the tide mark in a bathtub, an artifact of plenty left after a rinse. There were tire tracks in the drying bed, thin and wobbly, which meant someone had already come. I had a permit clipped to my breast pocket with the county seal turned outward. I had laminated my own name, though nobody would be down here to read it.

It had been almost fifty years since the lake took Confluence. I was five when the last moving truck rolled up our street. I was six when the ribbon was cut on the spillway, the band playing, my mother tucked back in the shade with my little sister asleep in her lap, the heat turning her face the color of a ripened plum. I was seven when I learned the word “reservoir” and tried to say it out loud, my mother correcting me—ver, not war, like a song you learn and sing wrong for years. I left at nine when my father finally stopped standing in the doorway each night and pretending that if he stared hard enough the ridge across the valley would roll back like a wave and let the houses pass. And yet my whole life has been a returning to lines, the way they bracket and hold, because what else can you do when you have lost the place where the lines of your days intersected.

The first place we had to set the base was the courthouse steps. I could see their tops like teeth above the mud, a familiar bitten shape. The courthouse had been brick and columned and the color of rust in rain. They had dynamited the top of it before the water came. The pictures in the paper had been dramatic—columns falling, dust rising, men putting out their arms to keep the curious back—because someone had decided that leaving the courthouse intact would make for dangerous currents, a trap. So what was left was the lowest stair and the shallow landing before the glass doors had been. The bronze plaque must still be there. I could see the circle of it, mud-tarnished, sun-warmed. It was a benchmark. It had been the father of all my measurements even when I had only been a child laying pennies there for good luck.

I sunk the tripod legs and twisted the clamps that bite the aluminum, feeling the residual warmth in the metal from the ride out. I pressed each leg into the bed until it found something more than just the thin hard skin—some shelf of older debris, a beam, a layer of compacted silt. My right glove made a squeak on the clamp that ran through me like a horse nearly bolting. It was hard not to personify. In the heat, everything had veins. I set the total station, balancing, bubble bringing the world to a center, my eye to the tiny circular vial’s bull. The black circle of the bubble moved like a thought. When it steadied into perfect green ring, I breathed. I found true and false horizontals. I breathed the smell of hot metal and wet earth. I came back to myself.

My control was the base for everything else. The sat-link would back me up, the corrections off the CORS network pinging in from the far ridge tower to my receiver. But I needed this, the touch of this disc that had seen my father in his Sunday shirt the day he was too restless after church to go home and had come down to the square to stand and look at the world for a long time. He had set his boot heel on that very top step, back when it was swept, back when Miss Annie from the clerk’s office had put out petunias in the two planters and watered them through August and when we kids used to race down the steps on our bellies even though the stone skinned us raw. The disc had been bright then, you could read the words stamped into it like an oath: NAD 83 COUNTY OF GREENE DO NOT DISTURB BENCH MARK 307.19 FT. It had a triangle in the center and a line shred to show direction. I brushed it with my gloved hand now and the mud came off in sticky curls and there it was, the old little god of measure, crusted, pocked, but legible. I wiped it with the hem of my shirt. I heard my grandmother in my head—girl, don’t you clean a thing like that with your clothes—but there was nobody to scold me anymore. I put the unit over it and pressed in the rod point and tied off my string to the same iron ring the city had used for their snowman competition that one winter when it sleeted and we’d had no idea what we were building, only that it was cold and rare.

“Home base,” I said out loud, because sometimes saying a thing locks it in the air the way writing a point locks it in the math. I set the frequency, waited for the beep and the satellite lock. The receiver’s display flickered. Fixed. Coordinates danced at the bottom of the screen, tiny green numbers, indifferent, a new world overlaying old words. I had brought the stitched-together scan of the old plat map too, a black and white thing saved from my father’s file cabinet, annotated in his tight engineer’s print, coffee cup stain like a rising moon over the west end of Main Street. I loaded it as an underlay into the handheld so I could watch the two towns talk to each other as I walked. Beneath the pixels, the names rose again—Boone Street, Sycamore, Market; the little alleys between houses where we used to ride our bicycles and pretend we were not being watched.

The courthouse itself had been cleared of its upper storeys. They had brought motorcycles down here in winter during those first low years and tried to jump the landing, videoing themselves for the Internet, whooping and sliding and occasionally breaking an arm. I read about that in the paper with a weird wash of possessiveness. You did not bring engines where I had been born. You did not fly your machine over my father’s bare footprint.

I clicked into the job and set my first point. P.0001: Courthouse base. There were dragonflies. The twining hum of them was as constant as the corrections beeping through. When I turned, I found that the low angle of morning made of every exposed thing a calling card. There were the church stairs, stepped like a trapezoid, and a bird’s nest in the dry arch that showed above the lakebed like a cutout. There was a bicycle wheel half-submerged, a deer skeleton pitched on its side like a wheelbarrow having given up, ribs open to the sky. There was the line of the old railroad, which had been lifted of its rails and sleepers, not lifted cleanly, the machines having yanked the spikes in a hurry. It made a gash through the silt like a zipper. Near it was the stump of an oak with its heart open as a mouth.

Getting down into the old street grid was like entering a language I had not spoken aloud in years but had dreamed in the night more times than I could count. I found Ivy Street by the way it dipped and then curved around the back of where the feed store had been. Its curbing still had the rounded lips we used to sit on in the summer when the tar bubbled and made bubbles we could pop. I put my boot on the curb and it held. I ran the wheel of the measuring meter along it, because there is joy in redundancies. The tape clicked and whispered, capturing nothing of feeling and everything that could not be argued with. A vulture overhead made an ellipse. I thought of the ones that will come, who count the flares of methane in their mouths like coins.

My house had been on the corner of Pecan and Ivy. There had been actual pecan trees on that street, and actually, also, a boy named Pecan who got into fistfights because of it and would not answer to anything else. We had visited our house once after they emptied it and boarded the windows. I had been the kind of child who liked to count the doorknobs and the window latches and the number of tiles in the bathroom. I had liked to take my finger along the line where the baseboard met the painted wall and feel the bump where the painter’s hand had hesitated. All of that lived in my fingertips as if I could still peel back the paper printed with asters and find the pencil marks under it where my mother measured us against the doorframe. When my mother and father took us there that last time, it had been to make my father stop pacing. A man in a hat I did not like stood at the gate with a clipboard and a polite smile that was the shape of a badge. He wrote my father’s name as we came in as if adding a feature, and my father looked at the handwriting and shook his head once. We were inside five minutes. My mother cried like something broke and kept spilling. I had drunk from the bathroom tap because I thought if I took some water with me it would hold the house to me. It tasted like old pipes. My father had opened the kitchen cabinet and found the fished-out spare key we always kept under a rubber band around the third jar. He took the jar and turned it upside down and nothing came out because the key was already in his pocket.

The foundation now was tiles and grid. The floors in the 1950s had been black-and-white in that checkerboard every magazine seemed to claim meant the future. After the boards and joists and beams were pulled, what remained were those squares, stained a brownish color from years of being kissed by the lake and then dried and kissed and dried—a marriage of geometry and breath. The pattern showed through in places, the black squares a pale grey like the marks a pigeon leaves on a statue. I stood on what would have been the kitchen and looked out at the line where the rest of the town used to be and then to the curve of the cove. I waited for the little pang that would tell me where the window would have been. I waited and it did not come. Memory comes like that sometimes, as a stain that broadens until everything is the same hue, or as a shape that never throws a shadow at all.

“Lina!” a voice called behind me. My name came down into the old street as if all the old names called along with it. I turned with a start. The voice came from up on the slope. It was just the gate guard. He was a sheriff’s deputy I’d known since high school by the set of his shoulders and the way he cleared his throat to cover awkwardness.

“Stay up by the cones!” he shouted. “County said it’d just be you!”

“It is,” I called back, though the ghost of everyone who had lived here strode at my shoulder and along beside me like men walking fence. My voice came out drier than I meant. He lifted a hand and walked away. The rules said no one else was supposed to be down in the basin: not the kids who came to dare, not the antiquers looking for bottles, not the man who would come tugging his little granddaughter’s hand and showing her what used to be his life. The sheriff would send them back. This was my official worksite. I set the receiver rod where our dining table had been. I logged a point.

In that house, my mother had done something with leftover fabric pieces she said was quilting, but half the time she ended up ironing them flat and laying them over the table as if she wanted to see all of them at once and not stitch any of them together. She ironed with a determination that looked like anger and probably wasn’t. My father came home smelling like transmission fluid and dust. He washed his hands at the sink and the water ran black and his hands emerged nicked and pale and always his nails were rimmed with dirt so deep it seemed a color in their own right. He smoothed those nails along my hair without being able to muss anything truly. He looked like someone always already fending off something invisible that approached him from the side.

Set P.005: Kitchen northeast corner. Set P.006: Dining room. Set P.007: Hall. The receiver beeped obediently, the screen lights easy and regular. The sun climbed and heated the back of my neck. The smell came up from the mud in a rush with that heat, not just moss and fish scales and the trace of rot, but something else, something like the way a closed old book might smell when it is first opened after a very long time. Pages that had not been turned had a weight to them you did not know was there until you turned them and found your hands slowed. My feet sank now and then where there had been cellar-holes. I stepped out because I knew where they were without looking at the underlay. The illustration on the screen glowed like a memory. I touched the place where the pencil lines once risen had now collapsed.

I ate a lunch of two hard-boiled eggs and an apple in the square of shade thrown by the courthouse step. Nobody who has never had a town drowned knows the mix of wanting to be there and never wanting to go back. I did not tell the county man I had asked for this job. I wanted it in my hands. I could have sent a junior, one of the girls who came in wearing latex-pink lipstick who collected their hair in bright elastics on top of their heads and whose fingernails would have come back clean. They would have come here with cheap sunglasses and made a joke at the ghost stories they had heard. They would have done a perfectly acceptable job. They would have reached for their phones and texted pictures with the filter on that bleached the world to a blue so sharp it made you think of running water.

When I was done with the base and the first line of points on Ivy, I went to the church. It had been a little white church. They had slid it on greased logs around the back of town to a small rise the lake would not reach when those who built the dam realized how many of us had begged for the church to be left. There was money in someone’s grandmother’s hands that said yes. There were men with caps who unscrewed the pews and carried them with a kind of soft profanity. They had to cut the steeple off anyway because everything down here had to be lower, swallowed, lesson given. It was gone now, over the hill behind a new peach orchard somebody had planted over the old cemetery which had itself been moved. The stairs were what remained. I ran my hand up their paint and it came away with chalk-white residue that had been made by water’s going and leaving, going and leaving. I set a point at the middle of the landing where couples took their wedding photographs. I had myself been afraid of that church when I was little because the preacher had a nervous cough and a voice that filled the wooden room with a kind of fear that did not feel like the fear of God but like the fear of men being told their wages were staying the same. My mother kept her hat pinned to her head with hatpins and would hand me one if I was sleepy to play with, to stick through paper with that precise puncture that could never be undone.

“Looking for something?” a voice said behind me. I turned and found a boy who had not violated the rule by more than which road he had come in. He was maybe thirteen, which is the age when you are either too curious to be careful or so careful you pretend you are not curious at all. He wore a T-shirt with a band name and jeans with one knee blown out, his hair long in front, because he had not had a haircut since his mother took him for the school picture. He had a metal detector and the sheepishness of someone who has to ask for forgiveness often.

“Just walking until the lake makes me stop,” I said.

“They say there’s bottles come up,” he said, “and sometimes you can find—” He stopped, because what he wanted to say was skulls, or wedding rings, or hair clumps, and he had the country grace not to say those things in front of someone who might still be talking to a minister about them.

“They say a lot of things,” I said. “You shouldn’t be here.”

“I came down on the north. Sheriff’s up at the big gate,” he said. “You used to live here?”

I looked down at my own boots, at the black silty outline they left, the delicate collapse of shapes where I had put my weight. “Yes,” I said.

“Which one?” he asked, because besides there being hundreds of them, the others in this county knew that there had been classes and neighborhoods even in a town that small, and where you had been put told the story about who had believed what kind of hope about you.

“Pecan and Ivy,” I said, but he did not even blink at those names. They were already the kind of words that sounded like somebody reading a genealogy off a stone in a place you never go.

“Cool,” he said, a word so flat it meant nothing. “You got to stay off the low places. It sucks you,” he said, demonstrating with his hand how the mud pulls. He showed me a spoon he had found. It was crusted with black until it had the reluctant shine of soap. “It’s for stirring,” he said, as if I had never stirred in my life.

“You keep it,” I said. “Make sure you don’t lose your shoes.” He grinned and ran, but he did not go toward the foundations. He went to the long stripe where the depot had stood and began making concentric ovals, the detector hymn across the ground. It made a thin, irritated whine that I tried not to let become a mosquito between my ears. I went back to my points because the day was short and the sun thinking. When the county asks you to map a drowned town they do not give you more days than the job description requires. They wanted simple numbers: Where were the streets; how far did the foundations reach into the basin; how often were the stairs of the public buildings exposed. They wanted to plan the next drought, as if already, while this one was still draining us, we must set up to meet the next with spreadsheets and claims. They wanted to find work for the men who would manage the water when it returned. They wanted a report to show to the paper that would say look, look, we send the one who was born there, we are not hard people.

I kept my hat on and pulled down the sides so hard the skin between my eyebrows hurt. The glare in that bowl was something living. The dragonflies stopped once to make romance above my head and then resumed their usual policing. There was a clay jar near the courthouse steps, belly-up like a pig. I turned it with my boot. It had a hairline down its belly as long and fine as a woman’s part. I wanted to take it and wash it and set it on my mantle and put in it the feathers I have collected since I got too fearful to collect stones. But if I begin taking things, where do I stop.

The call from my sister buzzed my pocket. She never calls at noon. She has two boys, one of whom is always upside down or fast with his hand over the cookie jar, and her husband is on the volunteer department and there are always small urgent things like a dog with heatstroke or a man in his shed with a saw. I let it buzz, then put the unit down on its side and looked at the number again, as if it might mean something different the second time. I answered and I could hear traffic through her car windows, thin and tinny.

“You all-at down there?” she asked, when I said hello.

“Yeah,” I said.

“God,” she said, and I could hear her hand smacking the steering wheel like a woman whose horse had decided to sit down in the middle of the road. “How is it.”

“It’s…there,” I said. “It’s our town, except someone took the lids off all the boxes and then left them.”

“You found the house?” she said.

“I think so,” I said. “It’s hard to,” I said. The apple against my teeth felt sudden and sweet. “I found the cornerstone, and the steps for the courthouse. I found my mind playing tricks.”

“I had a dream last night,” she said, and her voice did not make me brace, as it did most days. She is my older sister and for as long as the lake has been here, she has been the quartermaster of memory, deciding which we keep and which we send to the burn barrel. “I dreamt the water was clear and we were fish and we swam down to the house and everything was the same but slow. We touched the bead curtain in the kitchen and it didn’t move because the water held it. Mommy was there, but she had air bubbles in her eyelashes.”

“It’s not like that,” I said.

“I know,” she said, and her laugh was as brittle as the dry crust around me. “I just needed to tell someone. You take anything, you put it back. Don’t be like those kids in ninety-two going home with the pie safes in the back of their dads’ trucks.”

“You think I’d walk away with a pie safe?” I said.

“You know we had one,” she said, laughter almost for real now, and the simplest of things was to answer yes, we had one, it was painted a weird medicinal green and had tin inserts with holes punched in, and on the day the men came to list our possessions, I had sat on the floor of the pantry and traced those holes and made a prayer out of their pattern for the men to hear and go softer on my father that day.

“You remember Mr. Ames?” she said abruptly. “His boy died last week. Heat stroke. Worked on the pipeline up near Osborn. Fell asleep in the cab and they say the cab just cooked him.” She told me because I should know, because I would be the one to go if the heat got thick enough to keep them from church and she had to stay with the teenagers to keep them from starting a fire with the curling iron. I kept my breath in my mouth and did not say what I was thinking, which was that if they built the reservoir to keep men like Mr. Ames in work, they have built this drought right alongside it. Work is a wind that raises all roofs and tears them off with the same gesture.

When she hung up, I stood and felt the low ache in my back that blood brings when it thinks you’ve been careless. The bench mark was a comfort. It gleamed. The boy had gone to a part of the basin where the reeds cracked and whispered. I saw his bent back against the line of the world for a second before it winked out behind a lip of silt. I could draw a line of his path with my receiver, if I wanted to include a boy’s walking in this map.

It is easy to blaze and flog your body into doing. The trick is not to let the mind ride and slap. I walked and tagged and walked. I went down Market to where the gazebo used to be when the band played on July afternoons and men took off their hats to fan themselves and light their pipes. On the iron fence that had ringed it were the remains of vines like wire, not dead so much as transformed into something the temple of oxygen makes into a new god when the old god leaves. The gazebo itself had been dragged, like the church, to the safe ridge. Now it hosted four church suppers a year and a harvest festival where small jars of jam sat in rows like primary colors and women in their fifties walked past and did not touch them because they could not afford to buy even one. We keep the shape of things like a dog keeps the habit of sleeping at your feet even after you leave. The shape of a thing without the thing itself will keep you company if you let it, but it will never do the work.

On Third Street, I set a point where the school steps had been and expected to see phantoms. There had been two long flights and a landing between, and on the bottom step I had fallen and peeled the skin off both my shins and bled so much that Mr. Parker scooped me up and poured his cola over the wound because he had been told by somebody that sugar sterilizes and I had howled because I did not like being sticky and because I could see the bones like fish under my skin though they were not really visible. Now the steps were a shallow shelf, and the pebbles embedded in their surface made them into a small map of a place even smaller than this one, made for ants to travel. I set the rod and recorded and turned to see a man standing with his hands on his hips, a baseball cap shading his face so that all I could make out was the white thread letters: AMES SEED CO.

“You’re blocking off my drive up on the ridge,” he said, not moving, not hostile, simply stating the fact the way a man who has seen a town moved might give you his order at a restaurant—brief, clearly pronounced, the words the only power he has left. He was my age and he had one of those dog’s faces, sharpened around the muzzle, eyes too dark in a face too pale or too sun-worried to hold any more color. He took off his cap and put it back on and in that gesture I recognized somebody. Abe Renteria had done that all the time when I was a kid, only he used his hat to wipe his forehead with, and he had had a laugh that burst like seeds in late summer when the dry pods popped.

“I’m sorry,” I said. “We can move the cones. We set them because the cove over there pulls under and someone’s truck got mired, and then they had to call a second truck, and then the second truck got—”

“You lived here.” He said it as a fact, the way he had said I was blocking his drive. He did not ask.

“Yes,” I said.

“I used to sweep out the store my dad ran right there,” he pointed vaguely toward the feed store spot where there was a rectangle of something darker because the building had been bigger. “We had licorice twists in a jar by the register and I broke that jar when I was eight. My dad got so mad he slapped me and then kissed the top of my head so hard I thought he’d ironed my scalp,” he said. Then he shut his mouth like a man suddenly aware of his tongue. “You work for them now,” he said.

“Which them,” I said, though I could have said it for him. County. Reservoir authority. People who liked to have maps and people who laid asphalt. I had drawn lines of rights-of-way for men so loud the sound of their laughter made my shoulders go up two days in advance.

He looked toward the lake, light bleaching him out. “When they first started building the dam, we watched them pour cement as if watching would make it run slower. We watched the girders being swung in and I thought I was going to be an engineer then. Turns out I like goats.” He gestured toward the ridge where a handful of animals stood like accents. “Something that eats anything,” he said, and gave a little hitch of his mouth that was a grin if you put it under a generous light. “Would you like a jar of pickled okra.”

“I would not say no,” I said, and wondered what ledger he kept in himself where giving a woman who worked for the county a jar of your labor might balance the equation of a life. He shifted his weight and the mud did not release his boots, which meant he had been standing there awhile. “I’m going to measure the east-west,” I said, gesturing to my receiver and to the little knight’s joust the prism target made when the sun hit it. He nodded as if I had said something about the weather. He looked at the courthouse step. He looked down at the tiles. He looked at the rim of lake where it shivered like a promise if you saw it one way and like a threat if you saw it another. He raised his hand in a slow salute and walked away.

By late afternoon, my list of points was a hallway with doors off it both sides. I had left plumb bobs of orange flagging at intervals because you cannot trust a line in air. A dragonfly perched on the end of the stick as if it had been put there expressly as an insect raft. My water beads were warm in the cooler. The water inside the plastic tasted like plastic and sunshine. I tipped my hat back and the line across my forehead felt like the faint scar from a long-lost bruise. If I stood still, cicadas rose in a wave and eclipsed all thought. If I moved my right hand away from my body, the tendons in the back of my wrist showed and I felt, as I always feel when I am watched by heat, that I am only an instrument, like the rod, like the station, like the disc in the stone, and what I am measuring is looking through me to where the truth might be.

On the south edge of town there had been a peculiarity so fierce the paper had run a story on it. When the graveyard at the old church was moved, not all the graves went with it. Families came with lists of names and marked the stones with a X, and the men in their trucks with small winches and the boys with new muscles came and cradled the bodies where they would forgive the taking, brought them to light, put them into the waiting. But there were those markers whose family left or didn’t believe or had no cousins to come with biscuits and iced tea, those stones left leaning. And there were the unmarked ones we knew about and the unmarked ones nobody did. The dam builders had sent a crew to dig those up too. They had missed some. When the first drought came, Mrs. Hollis on the hill behind looked out her window and fainted at what the lake had coughed up. All that summer she would not look south. There were men who liked to tell young women about the day they found a rib, bleached, a white weather-streak like driftwood bone, sticking up. The men made the story bigger every time they told it. The rib moved to a skull and the skull to a family and then to a church bell and then to a wagon axle until finally the story had everything but the thing at its center. I did not want to go to that edge. But my map needed the ring roads. My job was not to favor the middle.

On the way, a heron rose, slow and obscene as a dream of a garment you have always wanted to wear but will never dare. Its legs dangled. It went up and then angled and I could see through its feathers a little, a quality of light that never shows up in photographs. I reached the old fence line. There were bits of chain-link where the men had begun and then given up or the lake had set a weight on their work and been done with it. Around a corner made by two half-hearted posts, there was a low shelf of concrete. On it was the name I had expected to see because I had traced that name with my finger until the stone had grown warm under my touch. R.L. MONTJOY 1902-1949. He was nobody to us except that he had been at the edge of my walking route when I was small and his name was a song better than my catechism. Montjoy. Joy like a word that was pretend in a concrete so literal you could scrape your skin on it. The stone remained and the grave was elsewhere now. We had poured R. L. into a new hole up by the new church and said new words over him in a starched afternoon and everybody had eaten cake.

I put my hand on the name the way I had as a child, as if rubbing a lamp. The heat of the day lived there like a resident. Somebody had left a button like a coin. I thought of taking it and then I didn’t. Around the base of the stone were four marbles, scabbed with silica, their colors held in tight like breath held too long. I took one into my palm, and then another, and then put them all back, my hands unwilling to be greedy in that place even in pretend. The boy came back around the corner with a spoon he had found. I thought of Mrs. Hollis’s faint and of the tongues of women in town when they say a name that means both gossip and salvation, and I could not make my mouth say anything except a hello that felt as pale as chalk dust.

By the time I had a loop on Sevier, the light had fallen to an angle I recognize from the last hour of any job when your hands and your sense of the world get both sharper and weaker. The ground hardens under that hour. The mud you would have worn as a film on your boots all day decides to flake itself off in big leaves so that you walk leaving pieces of yourself behind. I went back to the courthouse because I could not end a day, the first day, anywhere else. The bench mark still looked like a wound that refuses to heal. On the shallow landing, something caught the light just right and winked at me with a familiarity so rude and intimate I felt heat climb my throat. The initials we had pressed into the concrete had caught the sun. L C scratched with a nail and a heart around it so crude it looked like a potato. I must have been eight when I did it. It had been early morning while my father went into the clerk’s office and my sister had been told to hold my hand. I had pried my fingers loose because nothing holds you when you’ve decided to let go. I had squatted and hunched and looked down the length of my nose to keep my head from shaking and I had scratched and scratched and my breath had come shallow because the grey of the wet concrete called to you and you had to erase your own mark as you made it to keep from getting hollered at by a grown man who could not tolerate imperfection in public things. My fingernails had filled with cement paste so deeply I had to chew it out with my teeth. Now my little sin still lay there. The lake had lapped it, and when it baked it had held a filigree of silt and the loops of snails, and the edge of the heart had been blurred where something had abraded it, but it was mine. I put my fingers in it and the fit of them was terrible and exhilarating in the way that the same pair of jeans worn twenty years later would never, ever feel like the same pair of jeans.

The boy had stopped far enough away that he pretended not to be watching me. I took the receiver and logged one last point. P.0347: Initials heart. I did it without irony, because someday a girl will come with a lot of names stored under her tongue and she will want to be able to stand in the middle of a bowl of lost things and say very clearly: here.

The light went gold and then that weird equivocating color that makes everything a different species of itself. The heat poured itself back into the ground as if trying to hide what it had done. I gathered the total station and shouldered the tripod and walked back up the boat ramp over the old ridges. My boots left the steps reluctantly as if the stairs had grown toes since I had last used them and were trying to curl around the edges of me. Up on the ridge, the cones blinked stupidly reddish in the lowering. The deputy at the gate had a folding chair and a cooler and he lifted two fingers when I passed him, the way men do who mean what they say and also mean to say nothing. The sound of evening came in: the cars on the highway turning their treads, the wind lifting the cottonwood leaves and setting them to clack, the rasp of a grasshopper that had got hung up on a bit of wire. A truck came up slow with a shallow bed. Abe sat behind the wheel. In the back were two goats, both standing, both chewing, both looking straight ahead like soldiers on a parade float. He handed me a Mason jar with a rubber ring that had split and been fixed with twine.

“Don’t thank me,” he said, which is how men here ask for thanks. “Don’t be like them kids,” he added, glancing down toward the bowl, which was turning pink like a wound healing in the air.

“I won’t,” I said. “We did not raise me like that,” I wanted to say, but he had already put the truck in gear and the goats swayed and he made his way to the deeper ruts where a man who knows the land drives. The jar sweated in my palm its own little weather system. The okra inside, suspended, looked like toes.

I set the jar in the passenger footwell and buckled myself in. My hands on the wheel felt newer than they usually do. I let myself sit there a minute longer, engine off. The smell of silt had gotten into everything. It hung even in my hair. I tipped my head back and thought of my mother with her iron plugged in, of the way she would twist the cord into perfect loops when she was done, as if the iron might get ideas if she did not. In this heat, nothing wants to be straight. Lines want to bow. You measure and measure and in the time it takes you to set your points, the ground under you has adjusted a little according to its own sense of pride.

On the drive home I passed the arm of the reservoir where the fishermen were still, where they had always been, positioned Like the punctuation points at the end of a sentence I could not yet read. Their rods were set in holders, their bodies in lawn chairs. They had their beers tucked into foam pockets. They had their hats on with the brims that have already seen a hundred suns. They came out because this is what you do when you live in a county with water in it even when the water is down. You come and you bait and you sit and now and then the line sings and you stand and then sit again harder. The water was low enough that the rocks where we used to sit to clean fish were out again. It was low enough that the contours my body had known before I had a name for contour were visible again. I slowed and then accelerated because if you keep slowing for what you cannot fix you will never get home.

In the kitchen that is now mine and has never truly been anyone else’s, I set the okra on the counter and twisted the lid and it popped like the correct answer. I fished the spears out with my fingers and ate them leaning against the sink, the way my father would have eaten something he had been told to savor and which he negated by eating it too fast. The taste was perfect in that way things can be when you are too tired to be fussy, the vinegar a bright line and the salt a memory in every cell of my mouth.

I washed my hands and went out to the little porch and sat and looked at my truck. The lake stink would have to be hosed off. The orange flags would ride behind my head in a little bucket like a carnival. I would go back tomorrow and the day after. I would finish one sweep and then another. The county would get their polygon and I would get something I could not put on paper. What rose in me then was not grief and not relief but something like the steadiness in my chest when I look through the sight and see the small prism perfectly centered and know, with a clarity that belongs entirely to the human habit of fooling ourselves, that I am making a straight line between two complicated things.

In bed that night, the lake smell was still in my nose. My husband turned and said, “You are in over your head,” and I laughed and he did too and that lifted me high enough to sleep. In the last thing I dreamed, I was at the courthouse and my father walked up the steps ahead of me and turned and held out his hand and I did not take it, even in the dream. I reached for the disc in the stone and I pressed my palm flat to it and it warmed. His face went softer and then it went away the way lamp light goes when you flip the switch and a bulb finally decides it is done. When I woke in the early dark, the world had cooled and even the palmetto bugs had decided not to scurry. The grass outside the window hissed with the minute trickles of things moving. The first train of the morning moved on the ridge. When I closed my eyes, I could see the lines of my points as clearly as if I had drawn them in the air where they made a city only I could live in. I lay there and tried to imagine water rising and falling, the distance between my eye and the measure of what I had done, and the impossible narrowness of the place where they would meet.
