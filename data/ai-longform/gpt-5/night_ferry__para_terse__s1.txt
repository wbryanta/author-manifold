The night boat leaves at seven, and I have been in the terminal since three, counting glass panes and bird strikes while the light leans out of the day. The terminal is all beige and hoardings with smiling families, a plasticked optimism that seems at odds with the gulls bickering over stale buns outside, the black river of diesel snaking under me. My ticket is a thin sheet in a plastic wallet, the kind you get for trade shows, and beside it is the letter from the police with my name block-printed above my address, the address with the wrong postcode because they sent it to where I lived before. We are writing following the recovery of an adult male from the coastline, we believe an individual for whom you are noted as potential next of kin, and beneath that a signature not even the fluid loops of a person but a practiced slash. They asked could I travel as soon as I could, and even though there are flights, I came to the harbour because it seemed to me that a proper thing between brothers should be done with time and a horizon, not an airport seat counter, not that we’d ever agreed such things, not that we ever agreed on much.

At four, the first truck backs onto the ramp and the trailer flexes like a spine. There are seats bolted to the windows, occupants with damp coats and the smell of sheep somewhere, not sheep themselves but the ghost of them in lanolin and hay. We are the sorts of people you only notice when there are delays, when we stack in corners like old furniture. I sit where the heating pant leg under the sill is stuck open and stares up with a cataract eye. The wind shakes the panes. A boy with a red scarf keeps making a run at the sliding doors, pretending he is a magician and they are obeying his will; on the fourth go his mother hisses his name and he skids to a stop, pouting, and his breath leaves a soft oval of mist on the glass, perfectly centered as if the building were breathing too.

We file onto the car deck just before five. I stand on the steel ridges that snag at your soles and watch a motorhome with Dutch plates gulp its way in. There are yellow lines, red cones, a man waving an illuminated bat. He looks cold to the core, his lips grey. The ferry is white with a blue stripe at her waist and in the late light she looks like something from a drawing, not a real thing that will drag us into the weather. She is bigger up close; everyone always forgets the bulk of these ships, the way they are cities disguised as intentions. There is the smell of old oil, salt, rope that has known centuries. When I climb the stairs up through the throat of the ship, there is a mural in the first passageway, whales and northern lights, and a laminated sign asking us to keep voices low after nine for the sake of the sleepers. There is a bar with brass rails and the faces of regulars leaning into pint glasses as if trying to hear the amber more clearly. I find my cabin and open the door to see that my bunkmate is already there: a lorry driver with a thick neck, reading a paperback that could be a thriller or a tour of duty depending on your worldview. He looks up, nods, says, "Evening."

"Evening," I say, and stow my bag under the lower bunk because he has taken the top, generous without realizing it. My name is Cal, which is a name you can be shouted at across water. He doesn’t ask. He puts a thumb in his book to mark his place and says they’ve just closed a mile of north road for re-surfacing, he’ll have to go the long way round tomorrow, and there’s a calculation in his head now, a crooked ledger that I can feel tugging at his jaw. "Bad luck," I say, and he snorts as if the phrase has been wrung of meaning, as if luck has nothing to do with anything.

I go looking for the rail. I have always liked to watch the backs of things as they vanish from me. On deck four there is a viewing platform and the wind hits like a hand right between the eyes, the sort of slap your father gave you to get you to spit out the sweet you were choking on. Men with cigarettes hunch inwards with that peculiar politeness smokers have now, apologetic and stubborn both. The harbour light has come on; in the basin, the fenders glow green and the tide sucks at them. The gulls sit out beyond the breakwater in a dense white rash, rising as one when something invisible to me decides, then lying down again like they have rehearsed it.

When the last cars snarl onto the deck and the clamshell door closes with a sigh that comes up through the deck plates to the bones in my feet, we begin. Departure is always slower than expected, softer. It is like moving a sofa through a too-narrow door, the careful angles, the way you have to lift and twist to make the thing forget the room it was in. The river lets go just before the end, and then we are in the greater dark, where the lights are fewer and their meanings more ambiguous. Back behind the estates, the rugby posts make two ghost Hs against the gloaming, like failed algebra. I watch the land step itself away from me until it is one line, like the underline a teacher draws when you have done something well. The boy with the red scarf is back at the window above us, behind glass, smudging a heart with his fingertip into the condensation and closing one eye to see if it looks like a heart from far away.

"It’s supposed to blow up later," says the smoker to my left without turning his head; it is the tone of someone who has been blown upon all his life and considers it a conversational equalizer.

"Probably," I say, and think, as one does, of what I have in my bag: a clean shirt, a toothbrush, a knitted hat done by someone else’s mother and left at my place by someone else entirely, because it is a hat for a smaller head, the crown of it too tight, the rim digging a faint red crescent into my forehead if I wear it too long. The letter is still in my pocket, the edge of it beginning to go furry where it rubs against the fabric.

When I was nine, our father took us out in his dinghy, and he let my brother take the tiller for all of five minutes and then snatched it back because Rory had a set to his mouth that meant he would not turn when told. We went along the pier, past the place where the old ferry used to come in, before they built the new harbour and shifted everything further down. In my memory it is always grey, the water the same colour as the sky; in truth, there were days when the sunlight on the sea moved like mercury, days when even the dogs ran mad drunk on the beach and licked each other’s mouths to taste the salt. But that day is grey, and father’s life jacket is one plastic buckle away from unfastened because he never believed in strapping anything fully. "You’re steering an elephant," he told Rory, and my brother laughed then and stamped his foot on the ribs of the dinghy like they were the elephant’s spine. He wanted a motor you could feel in your arms, none of this one lung cough that puttered us along. "We’re not going north of Scar Head," father said, and we said no, because there were places that were names rather than places, and you knew not to go past them, not because anything so ridiculous as rules forbade it, but because your throat tightened when you looked that way and you saw, even on still days, the nibble and growl of the tide that wanted you.

I have not seen my father in eight years and he did not pick up when I called him this morning. There is not much to say now between us that is not already brittle with its own echo. He taught us to look out for each other, he said it again and again, he said it when we were boxing in the back yard and when Rory split his knuckles by punching the brick wall instead of the bag and when I hid the bottle tops I had pinched from the shop under the stair carpet. "You look out for your brother," he said, and then he went away for three weeks on a boat with a hull that whistled when it tacked hard, and when he came back he was old. He looked at us and we were taller; he looked at my mother and her eyes were somewhere else. He would wind a line with such care, standing on the slippery lip of the slipway, as if he could do with rope what he could not do with people.

The ferry finds her rhythm and the swell, which is nothing inside the harbour, thickens under our feet and begins its slow swing. When I go back into the lounge the singer is setting up at the microphone for the entertainment, a woman with copper hair that flickers in the light like something warming up. There are flyers for bingo. The man at the next table has a tray with six small whiskies, all for him, equidistant as if he has put them out like traps. He does not look like he will get drunk. He looks like a house with all the windows open, because that is how you get the smell out. I drink tea that tastes of the hot water tap, and watch the sea through the salt-blind panes. It is as black as anything I have ever seen and as soon as it is black, I want to ascribe a moral quality to that blackness, and then I stop myself like a fool because the ocean does not care. It does not hold for us the lessons we think we can wring out of it. It is indifferent and still it makes our chests ache.

My phone has signal long enough for a message to tip itself into the tray. It is from a number I do not know. Hi Cal, it is PC Ley at the station here. Just wanted to confirm you caught the boat okay. Any problems, call. See you at the pier in the morning. I type back Yes. No. Thank you. The one from my father remains delivered-not-read, a blue line that ends in nothing. When I called him, it rang and then went to his old answerphone, the one that says his name in my mother’s voice, not because she is still there to field his calls, but because he never learned how to change it after she left. Answer, I wanted to whisper, even if you have nothing. The blanker the better. Say the sentence that makes the person you taught to look out for his brother feel like that instruction was not a burden you knew you were putting down as you said it.

A group of students have colonized the carpet near the plug points and are sitting on holdalls eating crisps. One of them has an accordion in a black case as long as her leg. Another strums a guitar quietly. They are going to festivals, or they have been; they are the kids who are always moving, always just back or about to go, eyes full of places. They make me think of Rory, who had the opposite, a fixity, a way of remaining too long in any one position until the person across from him could do nothing except punch him. I do not romanticize him into a person who went with the wind. He did not go with any wind; he grew against it. He had a stubborn plant’s will to push open the pavement. And when he pushed, he took other things with him, the way roots crack stone, not meaning to. The last time I saw him I gave him fifty pound notes I could not spare and he said "You’re the only one who gets me," and I pretended that was a compliment and not a con, and then he put them into his shoe like a child.

There is a house we lived in that smells of mildew if I think of it hard enough. There is a kitchen with a lino floor that never lay flat. There is a tin of Celebrations on the top of a cupboard with four Bounties in it and nothing else. There is Rory’s laugh when he was ten, which was a crack of bull kelp you could hear all the way down the beach. Then there is the same laugh when he is thirty and it is a weapon he wields to shame other people into finding him funny. There is his son, who is also not mine to speak of. A boy with a scuffed head and thick ankles like a footballer and a mother who moved inland the second she could. I sent the boy postcards in the shape of whales for six months and then I ran out of whales and he ran out of patience anyway because pictures you pin to your door are not hands that feed you. He is the reason I put my number down at the station when Rory went off with a net and a man we did not know and promised he would phone in two weeks. They were going up to the islands because the islands meant work that moved in eight-hour shifts on a rhythm your body could understand, not the kills of the building site and then the sack and then the girlfriend’s keys thrown at his face and then the "you don’t deserve better." I did not congratulate him on the plan. I had long learned not to tell him when he had a good idea, because praise could turn too fast into something we both hated.

At nine he would be finished his shift, if there was a shift, if there were some other world in which he still lived. He would be walking back along the quay with a bag of chips the colour of the boat deck, eating with his fingers while the sauce ran along his knuckles, sucking it up. At the end of the harbour the wind hits harder near the lifeboat shed and he would feel it blow the heat out of the bag and he would walk faster, the way he always did when something rushed at him, answering rush with rush. He would pass the black wall and run his hand along the wet slip where the weed grow like old hair. He would pause at the corner where he could see back into the bars, and then he would tell himself not to, and he would turn into the street lit by an orange that flatters no one. These are all things I imagine not because I want to torment myself, but because imagination was always my way of being with him when I could not be with him, even if the things I invent are stunted, monochrome, lit without grace.

Eleven brings the deeper dark, the kind where you must make a choice to see or not; stare long enough and there is a light smudged on the edge of the world that no one else with you will admit they saw. I find a place out of the wind on the lee side just above the lifeboats where the big orange canister says in black letters not designed to comfort that it will take 150 souls. I put my gloved hand on the cold metal of the davit winch and I feel the frost that has already begun to assemble. The wake pulls away from us with ragged edges, unraveling in quarter moons. There is a smell of fish on the wind from a trawler that does not appear in the portholes until it is five hundred metres off our port, piled with crates and a single white light fore and aft. Someone shouts aftwards and the shout is snatched into the swell like a shirt leeched forward and out by the wash. My eyes are gritty. The adrenaline that got me here is burning down to its little wick.

"Can’t sleep either?" says a man, coming up beside me like a feature of the ship only now revealed by torch.

"No," I say. He has a long forehead and a beard that is trim in the darkness as a hedgerow in February. He is a person who projects his own cold onto you so that you can feel it too.

"They say somebody went in last week," he says. "At Mid Howe. You know it?"

"I know of it," I say, though my knowing is a postcard in a guidebook in a shop, a black and white photo of stone people under the earth. The name is sharp in the mouth, Howe like a holler.

"They call them brochs but it’s not a broch, not there," he says, it is a correction he uses to make you underestimate him. "Lads come to look. Waves look flat and then they aren’t. Always takes the ones who stand too straight."

He smokes, and the orange coal of the cigarette near your face in the dark is firstly a lizard eye, then a life raft, then a mistake. I say, to say something, "I’m going to identify someone," and I feel the words leave me like warmth leaves breath on a night like this, a small cloud, becoming nothing, but having been something for me to name.

"Ah," he says, and nods. "I had to do that once. Differently, like. My old man worked the rigs. When I was wee. Diver. They brought one up with a suit burst and the pressure—what it does—" He stops, and even though he has brought us to the edge of a thing, he refuses himself the drama of going over. "My old lady says, ‘Don’t bring the boy,’ and still my dad says, ‘He has to see the sea as it is.’ After, I didn’t go in the bath for a month. You’ll be all right," he says, kindly. "It’s never your one," he says, and then, because a man has to leave you room for superstition, he adds, "Till it is."

We stand together a while longer. He speaks of birds. They come at night to the light of the ship and hit in their hundreds some nights, and in the morning the deck is a sweep of feathers and their faces no heavier than thumbs. He has a bag, he says, for lifting them over the side to the sea because you are not to throw them off in the harbour, the feral cats get them there, he says, as if he has been deputized into some order that spares him from wildness. He says he can tell you which of the stars above us are satellites if you want to know. I do not want to know. "It’ll blow up later," he says again when the wind grumbles a little fit in the eaves.

The lorry driver in the cabin snores like a hoard of geese. Every now and then his snore goes silent and it is unbearable because I anticipate the restart, like one of those pieces of modern music where the musician stops playing and still it is called a piece. I lie on my bunk in my clothes, the thin blanket over my knees, and in the gap between the curtain and the metal this much light comes in, and my body thinks it is dawn. In that thinness, the old ache takes the opportunity to climb up and sit on my chest: the night when I was fifteen and Rory had a black eye because he had tripped down the steps between the old cinema and the alley and hit the lintel with his face and I had to bring him home and stand him in the hallway and tell my mother he had tripped and then sit with him till he fell asleep and know it was a lie. The time he took my watch, the one my grandfather left me, not because he needed the money — not only because — but because he wanted to see if I would call him on it, whether I would break our stand-off and turn him away, tell him there was a line and he had crossed it. I don’t know if I would have. He brought it back in a carrier bag under tins of beans, as if it were something he was returning to the supermarket because it had gone off in the can.

The ferry lurches, a big, helpless shudder, and in the instant the ship is between gestures, I think of the stone-deep quiet of a morgue and the way they will turn on those lights in the morning and I must be there to be the one who says "Yes" or "No" or a third answer I do not yet know the name of. When I sleep I am ankle-deep on a shale beach and the stones suck against my heels as I walk, a gently malicious sucking like the way some people draw their teeth across the pad of your thumb before they bite. My brother is in the water up to his chest and he does not know he is too far out; a wave picks him up by the sides like a mother with a toddler and flops him over; he comes up laughing and says "Did you see that?" and I want to say "Come back" and I want to say "Again" and I want neither. He turns and runs his fingers through his hair and it lies back down obediently and it is the year he is seventeen and there is nothing in the world he cannot yet get away with. The sound of his name in my mouth is a toll.

At five the tannoy comes on with a stomach-ish burble and a voice says we’ll be making land shortly. It is not anything like shore you see when you are on a ferry. You look out and there is no form at first, the world is a pencil rubbing of itself. Then the thinner line becomes a dark hunchback and the sliver of yellow at the base becomes house windows. The small places always look like they are staged when you first arrive. The lights seem too few for lives. You see one man walking his dog and you think, that is the whole cast. The ship shivers as they reverse the thrusters and we begin to drift sideways into the slot in slow motion. A woman in a hi-vis jacket appears to be in charge of everything with a radio and one raised eyebrow. I stand with my bag by the door and the lorry driver beside me gives a final suff of nose into a hankie and tucks it into his pocket like a magic trick. "Safe travels," he says, and we both understand we won't see each other again and that basic, generous sentence is more adequate than the ones in the letter in my pocket by a distance that makes me want to weep.

PC Ley is younger than I expected, which says more about my expectations than about him. He has the manner of a man who is too tall to be as conspicuous as he is and tries to fold himself into smaller postures in case he alarms someone. We stand looking at each other while the gangway makes itself behave like a floor. He extends a hand and then retracts it because his other hand is in a glove and he wants to present the gloved one because the other is cold, and he does a whole switcheroo of palms in front of me that would be comic any other morning. "Cal," he says, and I say "Yes," and then the present ugliness is upon us in the air like breath when the window is cold. He has a car with the heater on, which is a kindness that I notice too much.

"I’m sorry you had to come at this time," he says. He drives with care around a man with a wheelbarrow on the harbour road, who tilts the tray as if the car’s wind could blow it over. The harbour cranes look like someone has placed four linked house keys on the horizon. The sea shows its under-muscle now that the light is up and I am shocked again at the aliveness of it. It is the wrong word, but the only one.

"Have you had many…?" I begin, and then I cannot say the word, as if my mouth has decided to turn childish at this juncture, and he rescues me.

"This month? Not so many," he says. "But it’s that time. You get a stretch of fine and then one bad day and everyone thinks they know the way the islands will behave and the islands don’t read the memos." He glances at me, and it is true he is younger if age is the sum of years, but he also has age on him like salt on the hem of a coat, the way those who deal in afters get it, the silt under the fingernails. "You were brother?"

"Am," I say, though I do not mean to be pedantic. He nods as if I offered a fact he recognizes as sensible.

The hospital is small, a single storey made of good intentions and grant money, with a waiting room shellacked with leaflets about lactation consultants and the signs of a stroke. There is a pot plant that sensibly leans towards the only window. A nurse has the walk of a person who knows her own corridor. She says my name, and the way she says it is kind without pity. The overhead lights in the small tiled room buzz like flies. You think you are ready for it because you have seen so many films, so many shows, so many sheets turned back with such expression. But in the door, the part of my brain that is embarrassed for itself says, do not make a sound, because it would put them out. The table is stainless steel, the sheet is grey-white, the air is a cured meat. The man who stands there with us, who is introduced as the doctor, not because I need to know his name but because it is a way to let him be in here, places one hand on the corner of the sheet as if it were the wing of a bird, and my stupid heart wants to ask him to put it back, and then he lifts it and it is almost nothing, the smallest of movements, like removing a bookmark from a book you are not finished with.

The face is swollen in such a way that the face is not exactly a surface you can read, it is a terrain that suggests other terrain. The eyebrows have floated. The lips are twice as wide, the colour of ash near heat. The hair is wrong; it could be anyone's hair. The ears are fine, I think absurdly; they are still intact and I have always loved ears for being the same from babyhood to old age. He is what you would call of middle height if he were to stand. He has a blue-black newness to him that means water, not air, did most of its work. A line, as thin as piano wire, marks a scrape down the left cheek just below the eye. I did not know until this second that I was looking for that scrape. A memory floods the way briny water floods a mouth: a day when we were children and he threw gravel at me and I ducked and one stone came back on the bounce and caught him armpit-high and then the little cut under his eye bloomed like a bud and my mother put her face too close to his and said "Fool child," which is a thing she said to all of us when she fought herself down off the top of the hill called Love. If I believed that the body is a ledger, you could read it like that: here the line from a broken bottle, here the pucker from the nail in the shed door, here the three pale wolf's teeth on the wrist where the dog got him because he had pushed his face into the dog’s too many times. But I am inside time. I look and I cannot tell whether I am reading what is present or the minute book of my mind.

"Does he—" I begin, and then stop, because I have forgotten the word for "have" or I do not know if it is the right thing to ask, to say, "Does he have…" and I let the words die. The doctor, or the man standing by his own name, says, "We can take fingerprints. We can take DNA. But they asked us if there was a family who could—" he does not say "spare us the cost," the way the clerk at the boarding house used to say "spare me the change." "It can help, with timing," he says, gently but with a calendar behind his eyes. All these men and women who do their work inside human calendars, as linguists inside tongues they did not choose.

I look at the knuckles. Even dead, they are themselves, a little burst, a little permanent readiness. Rory had a scar across his right first knuckle where he had split the skin to the bone in a fight behind the club, and I could smell his blood in the taxi even after I had wiped it away with my sleeve. The left canine is chipped, a notch so small you have to know where to look to see it. He did that on a tablestand in the kitchen when he fell out of a chair telling a long story about a man in a petrol station and his dog; he was laughing so hard he forgot to breathe, and then his head went backwards and the tooth hit the table like a mark in the wood. The dead man in front of me has a tooth that is—could be—chipped like that, with a shape that jogs the same notch in my mind as if it is a mine in a field that I have to step around every time I pass. A ring, I think. He had my uncle’s ring for a while, a dull gilt thing with a telurian scratch across it where he dragged it over a bit of wire. I do not see a ring. He could have pawned that. He did pawn that, I remember. The voice in me that is cold says, You know he pawned it because you bought it back, you fucking idiot, and you put it in the drawer and you have not told anyone where it is because you know.

The policeman waits with his eyes on my shoulder as if something might spring from it. He is doing a new thing in his job today, being with me. I find the place in me where the answer sits, and at first I think, "It is him," because what else would I have come for if not to be able to say something in one syllable? Then I think, "No," because it seems too easy to acquiesce and be released into grief where a script awaits even if you hate what it says. Then I think a third thought that is not a thought but a sensation, and in that sensation I understand that this is what it is to have a brother: to be tethered to someone by a long red rope that you cannot see until it is pulled taut. The rope tugs beyond the table. It does not connect to the man on the slab. It connects into the fog outside, to something that is not in front of me. "I cannot," I say, and my voice, low already, drops as if it has found a trapdoor. "I cannot say—" and I almost say "for sure," that trifling phrase that seeks to slide us over danger with a silly polite heel. "I cannot tell you if it is," I say, and the relief in me at using the honest syllables is so strong that for a blink I lean against the metal of the table with my hip.

"We can take samples," the doctor says, spinning his chair a sliver. "We can do this properly." He does not make me look again. He puts the sheet back as if he were closing the eyelid of a horse. The nurse says my name again out in the corridor and it is like someone calling me to get my coat on because we are leaving Aunty’s. "There were items in his pocket," the policeman says, as if it matters, as if it is evidence, and he opens an evidence bag that sings when he peels it apart. Inside there is a key with teeth like a baby’s, the sort that lives in cheap locks; there is a receipt from a shop that has no name and the date washed to a blur; there is a the top of a biro with teeth marks packed so close together that the plastic looks like it was woven. I cannot tell a person by those things. All the men I have loved chewed pens. Once I thought I would know him anywhere because of the way he walked, his slight crabby shuffle that he hid under bravado; but death flattens the mannerisms that matter to those who have loved us and leaves the ones that can be measured.

"I’m sorry," I tell the young policeman, because in not telling him I feel I have unrefined his day into something difficult, and he says "Don’t be," which is a kindness that I do not deserve and wholly require. We go to a small room where the vending machine claims it can give me a Mars for a pound but the coin tray is a cave that already holds fifty pences, stacked together like cousins. PC Ley turns a paper cup under the machine and brings me something like tea and I lift it and my hands ring with the heat; the drink is vile and astonishing and I love it because it is hot and real and not a thought, as everything else is this morning. We make a plan that is not a plan but a small chronology. They will call me, he says, when they have more, which is both soon and not. I could stay, he says, but there are no rooms to be had unless I do not mind a form room in the station or one of the aunties in his second cousin’s line who keeps a house for the festivals. He says it as if it is a joke and it is not a joke; there are these houses, and they are dried with heat when the big week comes, and in between they sit with folded towels that smell of cupboard. I say I will walk a little and he begins to say something like "It will rain" and then we both hear it, all of us inside, the silence that comes before the weather makes its first clear choice.

I walk to the sea because it is there and I am afraid to go anywhere else. Beyond the harbour, the land opens into fields with no hedges, only posts with two muscled wires in between. The sheep make a low fog with their minds. A dog stares at me and does not decide I am okay. I walk on the road because there is no pavement and when a car comes I put one foot into the grass and I welcome the wet up that shin because it is proof. The grass is saturated. A plover does his sad piping thing as if in warning that cannot be heeded. The sea is now—what it always is: difficult, ordinary. It has the colour of all your shirts put together in the wash. It is not the place that took my brother because I did not give it permission. It is a place that exists on its own terms, and he—and maybe he, and maybe not—did a thing near it, and the two things are now forever conflated in my mind like an argument you had in a doorway and the doorway had nothing to do with it and still, ten years later, when you see that paint flake you feel your throat go stiff.

At Mid Howe there are indeed stones; not the high tower you see on postcards but the low-walled cairn whose grass roof is slick, the lines a ribcage laid bare with the turf skinned off. A man at the information board is explaining to his wife how the Norse built here and then he says "I am not sure I know what I’m talking about," and she says "You’re fine," and the love between them is more moving to me at this instant than the stones. I go to the edge. The edge is a straight black lip and the drop is a multitude. Men from here count their dead by edges. A sheep shit is squidged flat by rain near my boot. There is a white piece of plastic tie from a bundle of net, two inches long, too bright to be here, and I lift it and put it in my coat pocket the same way a mother picks up a sweet wrapper from the park though no child of hers dropped it, because it is easier to pick up one thing than the whole century.

I phone my father while the rain comes sideways, fine as spray. This time the phone rings to the end of the ring and then the robotic voice says "The person you are calling—" and it does not know how to finish because it has not been told whether to say "is unavailable" or "is no longer," and I hang up because I cannot teach a machine new phrases today. I put one foot down the sheep track and then pull it back because in the slipper of that shale I feel what it is to go, and I am a man who must live with a modicum of caution if he is to live at all. The rain stings. I am in a place where my brother went and looked at a thing because it was old and because, even though he would not have said it, the oldness of it made the present thinner and more negotiable, and he tried to negotiate with it and it did not yield and he went instead over the top into the glass-green muzzle of the water, or he did not, or another did and he is on a slab in a small room in a small hospital and I have laid my hand on the side of that slab as if that made it less cold.

On my way back the man with the beard who told me about the birds is waiting at the bakery door. He holds a white paper bag with grease like a watermark. He raises it and says, "Hot cakes," as if that is a greeting. "How’d you get on?" he asks, and then he adds, "If you don’t mind me—" and I shake my head because it is too late for that courtesy; he has already asked and I have already wanted to answer.

"I don’t know," I say. "I don’t know if I know."

"Aye," he says. He has crumbs in his beard and it humanizes him, drags him out of my myth. "My old man used to say there’s a thing in the water that is none of our business," he says, and I want to say "He was a diver, what did he know of staying out of other things’ business," and then I remember it is not his father’s truth that must fit me, it is only his, and he has brought it to me like a biscuit.

When I get to the pier the ferry is due in under an hour because this is an island and the geography organizes your hours for you. The harbour master stands with a clipboard under his coat as if he were private and the clipboard were family. He says it will blow up later, and then he says "You heard that, did you," as if we have said something conspiratorial. I take a seat in the little ferry office where the radiator is high on the wall and the chairs are wood that does not take your heat. I think of not going, of staying. There is a hotel with five rooms and the woman who runs it would see my face and judge my worthiness for a hot pie that has been waiting since breakfast. I could look for him. I am accepted enough here to do that because I have already met the policeman and the man with the beard and the baker can say, "Ah, that one." But what would I look for? The rope tugs, but I cannot find the direction.

The boat is white in this light with a blue stripe and she reverses with dignity that is inherently comic, a giraffe in a kitchen. The toolboxy clangs of the car deck begin and I stand up because a man who has not decided what to do next should move when there is movement. In the queue with me are the students with the accordion, laughing quieter now, the kind of quiet you have when you have not slept. There is a woman with a baby zipped into a pouch on her front like a kangaroo and the baby is asleep with his mouth open and he would sleep through anything and then in the night at three he will scream and she will not sleep through that and the world is reasonable if you think of it that way. The man with the whiskies is there, and he does not look ill, which is a relief for him and for me, though the smell of him is old tins. The lorry driver is loading again for his long way round. "You heading back?" he says, obviously, and I say "Yes," and he says, "If you’ve nothing to do, do it in your own bed," which is a type of philosophy I could print and tape to my cupboard.

When the lines are off I think of where the signal will die and where it will pick up again, and I send a message to the phone that is not a person’s but a glance. Hi. It is Cal. I am on the boat. It is not him. Or it may be. I cannot tell. I will be back tonight. He does not reply. I do not know if he knows how to, how to compose a sentence to his surviving son about the other one who is inside maybe dead or inside maybe not, and if you have lived long enough with indecision you are in fact an expert at it; you can go years in a house with a paint strip half-finished at the skirting. I send a different message, a different number, to a boy, and the boy is a man now, and his number is one I have in a notebook with grease on the cover, the sort of grease that used to live on pots in our first place. It has been years. He will not have this number. He may have put my name under something else. "It’s me," I write to my nephew, the only nephew I have. "If you want to talk, I will be on the boat until tonight." It sends. I do not watch it go through its tiny dance of delivery. It is either a thing now or it is not, and my watching will not alter either.

On the way south the sea picks up, as everyone said it would. The ship lifts, and then there is a long pause, and then the ship lands in itself. The sound rivals thought. People become quiet because speech becomes something you must take decision over. The students do not play music. The baby cries for the first time all morning and then gives up and sleeps harder as if to show his displeasure via the firmness of his eyelids. I go out into it for a while and I am the only one who does, which is always the way on a ship like this; there is always one idiot who wants to prove to himself that he can stand in weather. The spray stings. It is as if the sea has grown millions of very small teeth. I lean into it and I pretend that I am being baptized by a priest who has the sense to shove me under. I think of the dead face that might be my brother’s and then I think of my brother’s not-dead face, at fifteen, at seventeen, at thirty; and then I am overtaken by a thought that raises me like a wave: I should go back, not to the island, not to the slab, but to the boy’s house, to the place inland where he lives with his mother and the skirting boards are always clean because that is how she keeps the sorrow at bay; I should stand at the door and I should say something that makes the face inside not harder but not softer either, something like "We will wait together," which is untrue perhaps and wholly necessary. I think of the rope and I see it sag as if it were less taut now. I have not cut it; it is only that it is long enough that I can walk a while and not be pulled onto my back.

Back in the lounge, the lorry driver waves me over to a seat as if I were now part of his mild territory. "Did you manage?" he says, and I say "They’ll test," and he says "Aye," and we do not disappoint each other by being too open. He tells me about a pub on the road to Wick where the pool table slopes and you must play it like a hill, and he says it like a conjurer showing me he will not make the coin reappear behind my ear because he respects me too much to lie in that way. I thank him for the bunk I barely used to sleep in and he says "Anytime," which is a cheerful thing to say in a world where times are few, and then his phone rings and he says "Aye, Davie," and he is already living inside the next necessary co-ordinate.

The singer, the copper-haired woman, is back in the evening with a different dress, green this time. She sings a song about the sea that is not about the sea at all but about the body of a man and how it held love like a hull and when he sprang a plank it went everywhere and the people in the bar listen as if she has the news and she is reading it to them with the essential warmth taken out. I stand at the rail again as dusk comes down with the flat solidity of a hand pulling a shade, and I watch the track of the ferry like a scar you can see heal while you make it. The land grows. It is ugly, in places, attached as it is to all the lives I fled and all the lives I failed through in the years when I could have made different turns and did not. It is beautiful, in places, with the streetlights on in daylight when the weather decides to bruise and you can see, all the way along, everyone being as faithful as they are able. I put my fingers in my pocket and find the dead man’s plastic tie and the police letter and the hat, and I put the hat on and it digs into me and I deserve that. I think of the doctor putting the eyelid of his work back into place. I think of the boy with the red scarf and the heart he drew that, by the time he came back to it, had clouded back to glass, no trace of him left in it, except the warmth in the pane for the duration you pressed your palm flat to it and felt, back, your own heat.

By the time we make the harbour it is black again and the harbour mouth burns with an amber that makes every bald head a planet. I go down into the car deck before the steward asks me to because I am in the mood to be good. The air is sweet with petrol. The deckhand with the bat waves the first truck and it goes like a whale moving its jaw to let fish out. At the bottom of the ramp, the man who checked my ticket twenty-four hours ago stands in the same place with the same wool cap at the same angle and I wonder, like a child, if he has been here the whole time, if he is a figure in a clock. "Good night," he says, and by saying it he decides it. The rain begins in truth then. Not spray, not weather with a narrative, but the kind that is nothing personal, the kind that just is; and beneath my feet, walking over the slick tines of the deck, I feel a vessel still, in my legs, and I think, I am already in between, I can go in either direction now and be telling the truth.
