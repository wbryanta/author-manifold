They closed the steel gate behind us with a thud that made my shoulder blades come together, as if the sound itself were a hand placed between them. Diesel trembled in the air, a taste of pennies at the back of the tongue. Gulls stitched the light with their white wings and mockery. When I looked up past the dockside lamps to the sky, it had that exhausted depth of a winter night that refuses to be complete, as if there were still some substance left to scrape away to get to the pure black. There were shapes in coats and hoods shifting forward in the foot-passenger queue, the mass of us giving ourselves defensively to the ferry’s belly.

I had a small bag with me, big enough for one night and a change of shirt, all I could think to bring when the voice on my phone said, Is this Peter McAdams? and I said yes. The caller had been matter-of-fact. This is Constable Renfrew, sir. We have an unidentified male recovered on the north shore this afternoon. There’s reason to believe he may be your brother, Callum. Would you be able to come to identify? It's better if it's family.

Better was curious. As if there could be a better version of what they were asking me to do. I’d been at my kitchen table with a spoon in my hand, considering whether the yogurt in its plastic pot had begun to sour, when the phone rang. The plant beside the sink had laid one of its leaves against the pane as if to watch some tiny weatherfront inside. The world had flicked from tepid domestic to hard northern in the time it takes to say sir, I'm sorry to disturb your evening.

I moved forward with the rest as the crewman in the fluorescent jacket, radio clipped to his collar, ran his eye over boarding passes and stamped with a click that was both mercifully human and vaguely bureaucratic. My shoes made a thin sound on the gangway’s diamond plate. A woman with a child in a padded red suit walked in front of me; the child kept twisting to look behind, his hat with a bobble like a planet at the end of a string. I wanted to kneel and tell him not to look back; I didn’t want to look back myself. The town behind us was a sprinkle of low orange sodium lamps and one purple sign over the fish and chip shop where the oil has clung for thirty years. The raven-coloured water glared up between the slits of the gangway with the indifference of a large animal.

Inside, it was too bright. The light made the laminate tables in the lounge seem new even though they were knicked and rippled with many tray edges. A television mounted high near the alcove where they sold coffee was showing a football match from three days ago, the picture flicking and shuddering whenever a forklift moved on a lower deck. There was the noise of people arranging themselves for a night, proprietary without owning anything: the rustle of coats shrugged off, the clack of Tupperware, the thumping of bags onto the seats that fold down if you press the right spot. That economical choreography of strangers who know the routine.

I took a seat beside the window, not because there was anything to see but because I wanted to be pressed up against the idea of looking. Outside that glass was the strait like a hallway without the light switch. I put my bag under my seat with my foot on it, the same helper gesture I've done in cinemas and waiting rooms my whole adult life. Things have to be underfoot, near, otherwise they walk away.

The ferry made the long throat-clearing noise that means we are about to leave. There is always a moment when the city, or whatever you call that handful of lights, becomes past tense. Past tense is supposed to be tidy. The ferry shuddered, the lines took their slack then were spat free by deckhands with hands like old fruit. We slid, we nudged away sideways as if we were an ungainly insect too proud to simply push backward.

The woman with the child slid in across the aisle from me. Up close the child was older than the shape in the queue had suggested, seven or eight, long bare lashes and a small white scar above his eyebrow like a napkin corner. She took off his hat and his hair diffused like smoke. He looked past me at the window and said, Is it all dark all the way? The woman smiled. He likes to measure things, she said to me, as if that explained him and everything else. How long the dark is, how long the ferry takes, how many steps to his bed. I nodded. I did not say, I liked to measure, too. I did not say, you can make a life out of it, a God out of control. Instead I said, It’ll be lighter at the other side. That seemed correct, even though the other side in my head was a room with a steel table and the persistent hum of refrigeration.

My brother and I used to go to the river when we were boys. Our mother told us not to. She said drowning is the stupid way boys die. She said the water in the river was not water, it was a kind of will, it had thoughts and habits, it remembered where to cling to you with cold fingers and pull. She had the sort of fears that seem ornate until they reveal themselves to be ordinary prophecies. We went anyway, because prohibition makes logic more interesting. My brother went because he believed he could bargain; he threw stones like dice at the water and laughed when the water ignored him. He’d go up to the bridge and hold the railing with both hands and lean out over the drop until I could see the tendons in his skinny forearms like guitar strings under taut skin. He would say, Does this scare you? and I would say, You’re an idiot, and in that exchange was the whole grammar of our love.

On the ferry there were men in high-vis with little specks of paint on their boots that had been there for ten years, a tourist couple holding hands with the practised care of two people who have refused to marry for forty years, a student with cracked knuckles sleeping with a philosophy book under his cheek as if hope could be osmosis. I stood and went to the counter to buy coffee because I needed the heat of it, not the caffeine, and because giving one coin for a thing was stability. The girl at the counter had hair the colour of old rope and her apron was clean only at the top where the ties looped around her neck.

Long night, she said, will you need a blanket? They were stacked in a metal bin behind her, grey squares with the ferry company’s crest stitched on, like flags of a lost country. She said, You can take one and bring it back in the morning. I said, Yes, thank you. Thank you was inadequate and overpolite. Everything we do is over or under if you’re thinking too hard about the measure.

I took the coffee and the blanket back to my seat and did not sit. I felt the ferry’s movement begin to make a new rule for my body. I went out onto the deck. If you push the bar and the heavy door resists slightly, if your shoulder has to lean, if the cold air that slices at the edges of your face replies, then you know where you are. The deck was wet. I don't know from what; the sea throws and the sky drools and the breath of so many people condenses. The night was a solid frontage turned by our bow into a field where things only existed because we made them, the tips of our wakes scribing white.

There was a cigarette naive to the wind burning itself senseless in a man's hand near the rail. He nodded at me as if we were sharing something clear. I nodded back, hands shoved into my coat. The raven water glowed with its own light—phosphorescence, I nearly said aloud, to no one. Once, when I was twelve, Callum took me out at night, past nine, later than our mother permitted, to the small weir near the mill. He had a thrift-store torch that held three D batteries and seemed to weigh more than a child. He said, We’ll see the river’s teeth, like we were going to break into a zoo. He flashed the torch beam down at the froth and I saw that the water had its little snarls and snarls in the snarl and things that did not care about being watched. He turned the torch off; we stood in the dark, and it was the first time in my life that I understood that my pupils were expanding for a reason. The sound the water makes is a truth you can hear: it’s going even when you close your eyes to it, and it doesn’t remember you when you leave.

A gull, or some other bird that lives by theft, soared near me and then out toward the lightless place. Its cry was comic in that it seemed to be doing the same thing again and again with gusto. I looked back toward land and almost at once could not locate where we’d left. It takes so little distance to become a new creature.

I went back in. The ferry’s carpet had that smell which is not quite smell; it’s a familiarity of dampness and old sugar, of being cleaned more times than one could count with a strawberry chemical. People had begun to arrange themselves for sleep. The girl at the counter was giving blankets to anyone who shamelessly asked.

The child across from me had taken off his coat and was in a sweatshirt with a dinosaur skeleton on it. He was asking his mother how many bones the fish had that they would eat at Granddad’s. I settled myself like a hesitant rook into my territory. I put the blanket over my legs. I forced my eyes to close to see if that would drag sleep up like something at the end of a rope.

But this mind, the one I’m obliged to inhabit, doesn’t close. It offers me clarities I don’t want at the wrong times, and it is a slug at the crucial hours. I saw my brother’s hand. That is meant in the literal sense; my memory produced his hand. The back of Callum’s hand had a patch of paler skin that made a kind of map of the Hebrides or some invented country, a paler rash he got from some resin when we tried to build a treehouse. He was fourteen and I was ten, and the treehouse was going to be finished by September; he had a plan for the doorway and the ladder and a rope to haul up our secrets. September came and went and there was a half-platform and a rope that bruised our palms and winter rain that made the planks turn black and slimy. He said, We can still—, but he couldn’t find the rest of the sentence. He couldn’t bear the idea that anything he wanted could rot; I was a child who could imagine nothing else.

There were times – more than once – when he slammed the bathroom door hard enough to loosen a picture in the hallway. There were nights when he didn’t come home and nights our mother cried over a dish and then went into his room and came out with a handful of his dirty T-shirts, smelling them as if they would tell her where to go to collect him. He was the person my mother loved most in the world and the person she was most afraid of having to keep. When he left at twenty and did not leave an address, the house lost the air of being a place where someone could forget himself. It became a place where someone went to rest his elbows after work and eat soup while reading the newspaper. He wrote a postcard from a hostel in a city with a cathedral that squats on the landscape like a patient animal, He wrote, I’m fine, don’t worry. We took that as mercy and malice.

I woke because the ferry altered. It does that, the same way you wake when your lover turns their body a different way on the mattress. There was a new rhythm under the one that had been there, and in the middle distance of my dreams the emergency lighting bloomed. I did not think we were in danger; it was too gentle for danger. People around me shifted like creatures in a tidepool, not alarmed enough to run, not happy enough to sleep. The man with the philosophy book had lost it to the floor; I bent to pick it up and put it back on the seat beside his face. When I straightened, the mother across from me was looking at me in that way you look at a person you want to be brave for while still acknowledging together you are not entirely sure of your bravery. There was a vibration. A crewman went past with the clipped walk and expression of someone going to fix a thing that has been fixed many times before.

I stood and went toward the bow lounge, because that is what you do if you can’t do anything and you have to do something. There were six or seven men up there, truckers, judging by the way they sat, legs pitched wide, their boots explaining the world to the floor. One said, A bit of a chop, and another said, Might snow later, and a third: They say down this way it’s always two weathers at once as a policy. The ferry began to grind slightly differently, and then it settled into a more demure hum, like a big head pressed into a pillow after a cough.

I slept a little, because my body forced me. It must have been the sleep you get in a dentist’s waiting room, made of scraps and edges; I woke feeling like someone had borrowed my muscles and returned them wrong end first. The window was a darker shade of grey instead of pure dark. Somewhere out there, land was becoming a fact. People around us were waking like it had been ordained that they do so at the same second; coats were hauled on, blankets relinquished, plastic bags crinkled, the overhead tannoy gave its apology for the rough patch and its encouragement about the cafeteria being open for breakfast. The smell of hot bacon fat crawled across the floor heavy and moral, a smell that says Keep your strength up for whatever awaits.

I could not eat. I tried a tea and took two sips then set it down. I took my small bag, as if it contained an instruction manual, and went back out onto the deck. The sky was being leavened; there were lines at the edges of the cloud; if you squinted at the horizon there was mean light, the kind that can cut rope. The island, ahead of us, was a low-built thing, something that could be uprooted and carried away by a giant winter without it protesting. Houses were huddled together like sheep that know to turn their asses to the weather. I could see a pier with three figures on it, early up to watch a ferry come in. Maybe they were waiting for a daughter who had travelled to the hospital on the mainland to have a baby. Maybe they were just insomniacs like the faithless doormen of a city who need to watch the gate.

When we made the final turn into the harbour there was that grace that boats have when they manage to seem smaller than themselves. The deckhands in their piecework choreography threw the lines, caught them, cinched them, called out to one another words that are only words on a boat. The gangway came down like a tongue. There were two policemen at the bottom of it. One of them had the broad good-natured face that on another day might be delivering an official talk to a primary school about not swallowing batteries. The other had a pinched look like someone who had a pain that only he knew about and which made the rest of the world’s nonsense intolerable.

They watched the stream file off. They were not looking for me yet. I walked among the foot-passengers with the sensation you get when passing through a detector gate, that if I kept my heart steady and my pockets empty I would be untouched by whatever god they were kneeling toward. The cold at the bottom of the gangway did that thing slow air does where it peels layers of you away like an onion. The policeman with the good nature said my name in question. Peter? I looked up. He held out his hand, cautious, as if the person I was might change its mind. Renfrew, he said. I nodded. I wanted to ask whether he’d slept, as if that would give me an advantage.

He walked me across the tarmac to the car. The snow lay in little sumps in the low places where vehicles had gone over and over. The port had that look of all ports when their storefront lights are off, as if the business of leaving is a vulgarity someone has forgotten to broom away. He said, Did you want to stop for anything? Coffee? A—. I shook my head before he finished. He nodded once like I had passed a test. He opened the passenger door, and I got in.

We drove. The island was as small as you expect islands to be unless you are running away on one. The road took a long careful curve past houses low to the ground as turtles. There was a dog in one yard that barked as we passed not because it hated us but because it had been ordained to announce significant approaches. Renfrew said, It’s mostly older people up here. A lot of the young ones leave. No jobs that don’t come with wet boots. He did not say this with self-pity or even as an explanation. It was a sentence like the sentences people use for their own bodies. My knees go when it’s damp, the way the grass lies down when the wind comes off the water.

I said, Had he—? and could not find a subject for the verb. He had what? He had been found? He had been seen? He had been anything? Renfrew said, He was on the rocks below Aird Dubh. Unless he came in from somewhere else, the current brings things round there. He shrugged like someone explaining the flaw in a suit he wore because he liked the cloth. He said, There was a tattoo, a bird on the left shoulder. He glanced at me as if to see whether that was a stone he had just put in my hand or a flower. He’d seemed about right age, he said. And he had brown hair. He held out brown hair like a thing that could be rare.

Callum got a swallow when he was seventeen. He traded his bicycle for it. He came home with that bruise of ink and the skin around it glossy and hauled up his T-shirt to show it to my mother with the grin he had when he wanted to dare you to love him. Swallows, he said clearly, mean return. He said it in that arch way that told you he’d learned that somewhere and it wasn’t his, but also he needed it to be true. You can go away and you come home. He said it like a note in an old bottle, in case he could read it to himself when the time came and believe he had always meant to keep the promise. My mother lifted her chin and looked at the wing with her mouth a line. She said, It looks like a crow with a bellyache. When he laughed then, it was the laugh he used in the years that followed for all of his bravest scenes, big and fat and covering up a small man.

Renfrew’s car heater stuttered to life and put warm air against my thighs. The globe of the island opened around us: a reluctance of trees, grass that had given up its colour in late October and was now the yellow of old manufacture, water on all sides beyond low fences made of stone so close to the earth they looked like someone had pulled a comb over the hills. I could see the ocean in horizontal segments through the geometry of crofts. I kept pinching the inside of my right middle finger, not because it hurt but because it felt present when I did so.

We passed a church the size of a sheep shed. People white-haired as winter were standing in its dooryard looking up as the white tail of our ferry lost itself south. There was a grocer with cardboard displays in the bottom of the window that looked like they had once been new. A boy in a dark school uniform was on a bicycle in the cold without gloves, his fingers red and purposeful on the bars. He glanced at the police car with that quick squint a child has when trying to figure out whether his current category would allow for being pulled over, then he looked into the middle distance again because he had learned where to put his eyes in an island that knows too much of itself.

Optimum path to the clinic involved a left turn onto a lane barely wide enough for two cars to pass while breathing in. The building crept up over the rise at the top, the sort of medical architecture that apologises for existing, white render and two windows with blinds pulled. There was a weary ambulance outside, the kind that doesn’t do grand rescues, just takes old bones to be pinned and sends women home with cough syrup. Renfrew parked to the left, edging close to the muddy verge like he was trying to hide the car. He said, I’ll come in, if that’s all right. He said it like an offer to carry your bag that is actually an offer to carry your body.

Inside, the heat was the sort that makes your ear canals aware of themselves. The nurse at the desk looked up and did that brisk head tilt people do when their minds have been whittled to crucial points. She said, Peter? I nodded. She stood and came around and touched my elbow. It is the smallest thing in the lexicon of kindnesses, and it undoes me more than any speech. She said, Dr. Fosse will be with you now, and the now made me pay careful attention to the clock: three minutes past eight.

The doctor was a tall man with narrow wrists and the coif you get when your hair is clean and your hands are too tired to persuade it. He said my name as if he were saying grace. I nodded. We shook hands. The ritual was insulting and complete. He said, We’ll do this as gently as we can. The word gently made me imagine lifting a baby’s head, which made me feel ill in the extremities.

He led us down a corridor with bumps in the paint where gurneys have misjudged themselves. There were two doors; he opened the second. The room smelled like when you open a freezer you haven’t opened in months. There was a humming snake nest of pipes up near the ceiling where silver insulation flaked like mica. In the middle of the room was the table. It was stainless, but the sort of stainless that has seen too much and no longer bothers. There were always sheets, in films and in reality, because the living cannot not veil the dead. The sheet here was white in the way a December sky is white. I had the impression, when we stepped in, that there was someone praying on the floor. That is what my mind gave to this arrangement of matter.

Dr. Fosse said, We’ll uncover step by step, all right? His voice was the voice of a person who has become a single kind of voice in the world. He held the sheet near the chest and rolled it down to reveal the face.

The face was swollen. Water does that. It fills the body with a version of itself and makes the features go infantile, or as if the person had been their face for only a week and had not been taught what to do yet. The mouth was parted. The eyes were closed and blue-black beneath as if someone had tenderly painted them. There was a nick above the left brow and another in the hairline, grim little speech marks. The hair was combed back, not because he had worn it that way but because someone here had smoothed it for me. I listened hard – no, not my ears – there is a kind of feeling you do with some organ that is not named, when you stand upstream of the thing you think might tear you.

It could have been him. It could not. I tried to see the hand in my memory, not the face. The face is a poor historian. It changes when you’re not watching, it rehearses sorrow and joy until both are caricatures. Hands tell you what a person has done. But the doctor was lifting the sheet from the shoulders to ask, We thought you might know about a—. We both peered at the left upper arm as if querying a map for a road unnumbered. The skin was mottled, but the tattoo was visible. There was a bird. It was not a swallow. It was a badly done, small hawk perhaps, something predatory, the head wrong, the wing feathering more like a child's drawing of fire. It was healed old. The red injection of festival age you see in the new was not there. It was not the swallow my brother had walked into our kitchen with and announced his intention to keep. I felt an annoyance like when toy builders get the proportions of a real thing wrong. Renfrew made a small sound and let it get lost.

I said, It doesn’t look like—. My mouth had the texture of something left out in the weather. The doctor said softly, If you want to see the right arm, there are two small scars on the forearm. He lifted again. The skin there gave me its cartography. There were two white lines, narrow and old, healed almost flat, diagonal across each other like someone had thought to make an X and then was wrenched away. Callum and I had made paper boats once. My mother had wanted to read on a Sunday afternoon; she said, Make your own entertainment, like people did when they weren’t allowed to be bored, and so we folded newspapers into little hulls and went to the river, and he had tried to see what he could cut with our father’s old knife and it slipped and the blade kissed him twice. Our mother’s hands were sudden and hot, and she said then, And how am I supposed to sew you up? and he laughed like a man bleeding out who has just found a joke. That X had ridden with him for twenty years. I looked at those two white kisses on the stranger’s arm – the stranger, the not-known – and I wondered whether there was logic left in coincidence.

I have seen photos of accidents on the motorway, printed in newspapers that still bother to make a facsimile of ethics by placing a black square over a face. I have watched a man with his entire life in front of him get a text and step off a curb into the slow oafish front of a bus. Knowing and not knowing are not in opposition; they live next door to one another and share a washing line. I looked at the not-swallow again and then at the face, then at the surgeon’s mouth which had turned, at some point in his practice, into a sign that said: I can stand here while you go through this.

Dr. Fosse said, The current is particular on this coast. It will bring in from places you did not expect. He said it, I think, to make me feel more adequate to the moment if I chose to be indecisive.

I looked hard and then I closed my eyes and summoned one image. The one I picked was a day when we had been to the river in March when the trees look like pencils and the water says No. It had been so cold my knee bones ached with it, and he had stripped to his boxers and run into the water with his arms flung like a mannish Christ, hollering with a joy that was half pain, half proof. He had teeth that looked ungoverned when he laughed; they were the teeth he’d always have, unremarked upon by any orthodontist. I had stood on the bank with my arms tight around my chest and loved him for his mad spectacle and hated him for leaving me behind on the safe ground. He had come out and stood on the rock, water slicking down him like coatings, and shook himself in an arc, his muscles – such as they were then – an animal arrangement. He had turned his head as if he were testing his eyes in every direction, and shouted, Come on, Pete, you only do this once! It was a sentence that heard itself and knew what else it said.

I opened my eyes. In the room the hum was faithful. Renfrew put his hand up and lowered it again, not sure where to put it on his own self. The doctor did not blink. I said, I don’t think so. I said, I can’t say yes. It was not just cowardice. It was a refusal to read into the flesh of a different man what I needed. If it was my brother I would be making him into himself; if it was not I would be burying someone else’s mistakes. I was not entirely certain, and certainty in such a room is a kind of violence. I said, I’m sorry, as if the corpse had done me a favour I could not return.

Renfrew said, That’s all right. He said it briskly and as if he had that sentence ready for any weather. He said, We’ll have to do the other channels then. He meant the channels that do not involve a brother.

There were forms. There are always forms. The nurse brought them with a square of paper on a writing board and a cheap pen into which the scaled-down life of many rooms had already leaked. I printed my name. There was a box to tick: Is the deceased known to you? Yes. No. Unsure. The bright rightness of why that third box existed made me want to laugh and then sleep.

They offered me a seat, a tea, a room to sit in alone. I took the seat in the corridor where the heating pipe had warmed the wall a little and put my hands on my knees to keep them from reaching for my eyes. Renfrew sat on a chair across and beside me, angled not to be speaking to me unless asked. He said nothing. Silence is one of the muscles in the policeman's back; it can flex for minutes or hours.

After a while the doctor came out with his hair to one side and said, If we don’t find family, after a time, there’s the messaging to other police forces, to missing persons. He said, We can take DNA for the database. Then, in time, there is a burial. He said the last word as if he were teaching it to someone newly arrived to the language. I said, Can I see him again? and the doctor made an open-hand gesture like someone inviting you to the table for whatever there is left.

I went in with him. The body lay with the sheet up to the neck now. I was not going to change my mind; I had admitted that to myself. But I wanted to say something to the man. It is the height of arrogance to believe a dead stranger has any use for your words, but arrogance and grief are siblings. I stood by the table and I said quietly, Because it was the only prayer my mother ever taught me that I remembered, May you be known where you are going and may there be someone who calls you by the name you weren’t ashamed to tell. I don’t know if those were the words or if I had embellished. Words, like birds, return to the house altered by what they have seen.

We went out into the cold parking spot outside the clinic. The air contained a bright sugar of snowflakes that hadn’t decided whether to stick to anything. Renfrew unlocked the car and looked at me with a face that had boundaries to it but not a wall. He said, Will you be staying on the island? I said, I thought— I said, I booked the first ferry back. He nodded as if that were an equation he could solve. He said, If you wanted to go by the north shore where they found him—. He checked my face even as he said it. I ejected an air-clasping laugh without any humour in it. I said, I don’t think—. He said, Of course.

He drove me back through the island with the quiet bespoke commentary of a man who likes roads. He said, That man there is called Tavish and he is older than he looks because half of his body died in his fifties and the other half keeps going. He said, That shop closes on Wednesdays because her husband sits in the back to play the accordion, and if she’s in the shop her day is not hers. He did not say anything about my brother because my brother belonged to people who did not know him, including me.

He dropped me at the port and said he’d be on until four if I needed. I asked him how people on the island called this strip of water and he said, Some say the Sound, but on old maps it is called the Throat. It doesn’t look like much, he added. It can be. He gave a little nod, an islander’s nod, and drove off.

I walked beyond the terminal to the breakwater. There were four boys there with caps, they were throwing bread to gulls and then laughing when the gulls performed the robbery they had been asked to perform. A dog whose breed was island ran up to me with the dark hopefulness that some dogs bring to strangers. I put my hand out, and he sniffed and then did that small wag of gratitude as though he and I had made a particular treaty. A thin line of snot had frozen at the edge of his nostril. He licked it away and made his own decisions about what had occurred. Out beyond the end of the breakwater the water looked like hammered iron someone had beaten his name into.

I had three hours. I could have gone to the café. I could have walked up the lane and seen the stone with the hole in it that couples put ribbons through and then cut away if they changed their minds. I could have called my mother, but my mother is dead. She died slowly like a curtain being pulled a millimetre at a time. Her last week was August and smelled like peaches going soft. In the years before she died she asked about him less often. There is mercy in anatomy; the brain trims away the tendrils of longing until it can sit still and be stroked by the ordinary.

On the breakwater a figure walked up and took the place beside me with the ease of one who knows that place and does not need permission. It was a man of fifty maybe, wiry, with three days of beard that had decided to be white early. He nodded and said, Morning, and we watched the tide push in its agreed-on direction. After a time he said, You’ve come on the nine? I said, The all-night and back again. He said, Aye, your face looks like the deck slept on you. He wasn’t unkind. He had fisherman’s hands, cracked and not bleeding; he was an advertisement for grease. He said, There’s a boy lost? They had found a body. I did not correct that he was not a boy. He said, I was on the crew twenty years and you get a look for a while, when they drag the weir and the barnacle cutters bring what they bring; you walk about with your eyes saying, Put on your seatbelt and go to church for a week. Then it gets quiet again. He said it like how it is that the fleas of a dog go through cycles. He left after that, because he had said the one thing, and there was work.

I thought of Callum as a young man leaving, the rucksack on his back containing a pair of boots he had stolen from our father’s remaining wardrobe six months after we had last seen father; containing a tin with his dope money; containing the book that had changed his mind, which he never loaned, and which he would not name; containing three shirts of which one I had bought him for a birthday and which he wore for a long time not from gratitude but because it was blue and blue promised something. We’d had our last conversation in the hallway, shouting, both of us wanting it to become the line with which we could sign the painting of our distinction. He said to me, I’m not going to be what you are. He meant sensible, jobbed, unremarkable, trousered, later a man who buys bulbs in spring and digs them into loam and has a knife that only cuts apples and a woman who sighs in the kitchen not unhappily. I said to him, You’re not good enough to be anything else. He laughed then – it had real fun in it for him – and he went. We didn’t speak for a year after that. Then there were two messages, a postcard and a text. The text said, Pete, I was wrong. It did not say, About what. It came on a morning when I had my hands in a sink. I wiped them and read it and wiped them again as if my hands needed to be dry to engage my heart. I didn’t answer that day. By night, the number didn’t take my reply anymore. It is easy to believe that stories are things that obey rules because stories that do are the ones that get told.

Snow began in earnest, the kind that doesn’t come like sheets but like the river we were forbidden to go to – purposeful and innumerable. The ferry was coming back down the throat toward me, its lights looking temporary. People were hurrying slowly toward the terminal to line up for boarding, and a plastic bag ripped free of someone’s hand like a skittish animal. I thought absurdly of buying a pair of gloves in the shop, as if properly dressed I could have done better with the dead. A trawler went past at half stream, seagulls writing their names above its wake.

At the terminal the same crewman stamped our tickets with the same indifference. We queued. The smell of diesel wrapped us. I had the sensation of being allowed to stand on a thing that would become not-this by a mechanism outside my choosing. It’s the only kind of travel I can do.

On the ferry I took the same seat – not the same, of course, a different one in the same arrangement – and I put my bag down and this time I went straight to the window. The window now had something to show me. It had the island getting smaller, the houses ceding white to grey, the pier that had held my feet letting them go contentedly. The woman with the child was not there; a young girl in a green coat leaned her head against her companion’s shoulder like a bird learning to sleep standing up. Two men in business coats had their laptops open like they were altars. The tannoy spoke; we left.

On our way out of the mouth of the harbour, the ferry gave a small sway; a man in the aisle reached up and touched the luggage rack not because it was needed but because his body believed in the world’s grip. Beyond the breakwater, the strait made language of itself fast as if we had never been here. I thought of the man I had not claimed. I thought that he might have been called something like Stan or Robbie. I invented a sister for him who lived on a peninsula and grew herbs in winter under grow-lights. I imagined her looking up from the soft grey of the back of her husband's hand to answer her own phone and hear a constable say a name that beginners would always spell wrong. I imagined I would never know if she existed and that he might go into the ground as John Doe or Unknown Male 1 or whatever label the databases are hungry for.

The ferry’s hum became the one my bones now knew. I put my forehead to the cold of the glass and the glass felt like the cheap reverse of a jewel on a collar. I had not told anyone I was going. I had told my desk I would be away Thursday and Friday and there were four emails asking small subservient questions waiting for me to answer them, to draw lines under lines as if the test would be marked. I took my phone out and looked at the last message from my brother again. Pete, I was wrong. It did not light any of the rooms of my thinking with meaning this time either. I typed a message in the space under his name, a thought experiment. It said, Where did you go. Then I looked at it. Then I erased it. Then I put the phone in the pocket of my coat.

I went again out onto the deck because I had begun to crave the precise cheap clarity of frozen air the way a drinker can crave bad whiskey. There were four or five of us there, scholars of the in-between. One of the truckers stood smoking with his elbow set at that particular angle truckers have that suggests enormous distances even on small ships. He said, Wind’s dropped, in my direction and in the direction of God. I said, Aye, and I would have stood there with his smoke like a small cathedral around us if a feeling didn’t push into my chest the size of a rounded stone you put in a child’s palm.

The cold went to work on my ears. I watched the froth at the bow and the little sparks of plankton that confound the heart with their sudden intimacies. Nothing alive knows it isn’t seen. A single seal’s head popped up off to port and watched us with the round martyred saint look that makes you sorry for the world. He let us pass him the way a man lets his grief go by after a drink and a cigarette.

When we had been kids, there was a day in spring when our mother took us to the promontory and told us to shout our names. We were eight and twelve; we thought we could hurt the wind with us. Callum shouted first – he always shouted first because the world had made him hungry – and his name went out and the sea did not answer. I had shouted mine softer, and then we sat down on the heather and out of nowhere she told us that when she was pregnant with him she used to run her hand over her belly and call him by a name like not inviting future gods to interfere. Callum, she said, and later, out of some stocked well of family naming, me. Maybe it is not just bodies that are identified. Maybe all our lives we are asked to say yes, this is the thing I know and love; or to say no, this is not what I recognise, even if I want to make it be. I watched the line of the island behind us slump and let the day get on with itself.

By noon we were halfway and the light was something that only the north understands, an involved argument it will not let anyone win. I closed my eyes and slept sitting up. When I woke it was because someone’s child had dropped a spoon and it had bounded under my seat with that metallic sing that takes a long time to leave a room. I bent and fetched it out and handed it back and the child said Thank you with the grave courtesy of a small person who has been taught well.

I went back into my head and I saw what would happen if I told my brother nothing and he died regardless, somewhere else, in a room with a mattress on the floor and a note to a girl with a new baby, and his cell phone stopped ringing when his charge was done. I saw what would happen if he did not die at all but ended up on another island telling people he was a man without brothers so they would not ask him to sit at their kitchen table with their wives and their cups of tea and held breaths. I did not imagine the version in which he came to my flat, rang my bell, and stood on the mat with a kind of apology in his mouth like old bread. I could not house that in the current dimension. The ferry went on.

Land rushed toward us although we were moving, a neat trick. The town, if we called it that, where we would disembark was a harder sprawl than the island’s crouch. The cranes at the dock were painted lollipop colours as if someone believed children might see them from a hill and understand infrastructure. The gulls were louder. I stood up and put my bag over my shoulder and watched myself do these small things so as not to think about any large thing. The ferry nudged the rubber at the pier, squealed against the polished black with the squeal that even as a child made me put my nails in my palms. People flowed around me and could you tell from their faces what they were going to? The woman with the green coat had the look people have when they put themselves to use.

On the gangway a boy came down behind me, his hair slicked in a way young boys do now, hopeful and wet. He had a voice and at the last step he said to his mate, You know what my dad says, and his mate said, Aye what does your dad say, and he said, Keep your mouth shut when you’re ignorant and you won’t be found out. They laughed. It’s as good as any creed.

Back on the mainland, the salt smell was less honest. The road beyond the port was full of lorries grinding; you could taste the rubber and the water that had been made dirty by the rubber and the rain that kept the whole thing in business. I walked through the terminal with its posters for travel to places that had no names besides the ones given for tourists. I went out into the air and walked to the bus stop because I had thought enough of after to pack it into my day. The bus gullied over puddles to my street. When I got off and unlocked my door, my flat still had its bowl of fruit on the table and its plant with the leaf against the window. The sink had two cups in it, one I’d left when I went out and one my earlier life had left in anticipation of this life returning. I took off my coat. I went to the bathroom and ran hot water and watched the steam climb the mirror and then be undone by physics. I sat on the edge of my bed and put my head in my hands in a pose beloved of novelists and dull to live.

When my phone buzzed, I stood by the plug like a man answering an order. It was a number I did not know. I considered letting it die. I am a man who has considered letting many calls die. I answered. A woman’s voice said my name, careful as if it were what a moving company man has to write on a box. Hello, I said. This is Ruth, she said, from the hostel down by the quay. You left your hat here once. There was a pause where either she was thinking of a better introduction or remembering that my hat had been someone else’s story. Sorry to call you out of the blue, she said, it’s just that you came through a few years back. You were looking for your brother then. I didn’t recall telling anyone at the hostel my business, but I would have told someone my business because I was that sort of man then, with two beers in him and the weather making empathy seem possible. You left a number, she said. There was a man here last night, she said then, staying one night. He said his name was Cal. He asked did I know a Peter on the mainland. She said it lightly as if to let me get out if I needed. He said he’d be back through next week, she added, the way you say a thing that might be tested and cruel. I closed my eyes. He was tall, she said, with a bird on his arm. Not a swallow, I don’t think. He talked too much. He made the room seem smaller and larger at the same time. He said, Could you tell him I said I was wrong. I felt the tilt in my chest then like the ferry had eased over onto another way of cutting water.

Thank you, I said. I said it so soft she said, What? and I said, Thank you. I gave her my address as if that meant he would walk up to my door. When the call ended the room did not change shape. The sound of the traffic outside was the same. I sat again. I tried to arrange myself into a person who had been addressed. Snow began in the afternoon and sat on the roof opposite as if thinking about whether to commit.

In the evening, when the last of the day surrendered like a notion that had been tested and found unconvincing, I went back out to the river in the town, to the path where you can look down and watch the water insist. Boys were there, always boys, doing the math with stones. The lamplight made the surface look muscular. I stood on the bridge and leaned over, my hands white on the rail. A bicycle passed behind me and the ring of its bell made the hair on my arms think of childhood. I said my brother’s name to the water because that is what you do when you have names and a throat to push them through. The river twitched its shoulders like a man who’s been tapped and doesn’t want to be. You cross and you cross and you think that means you’re going somewhere. The ferry will go again tonight. Men will stamp tickets and women will give out blankets and the snow will tell the same story in another voice. There will be another room with a sheet when there must be and a man with a long face like a road that has done a lot of work will lift it and ask you and you will have to say yes or no. To something. I stood there until I could no longer pretend that the cold wasn’t mispronouncing me, and then I went home slowing, and the whole way I let the word wrong sit in my mouth like a lozenge until it dissolved.
