We brought the wrenches and the length of black poly pipe out to the north slope where the line had burst last week, and the orchard still had that early June smell—dry grass already bleached at the tips, dust with a sweetness under it like the ghost of last year's fallen fruit. The slope is gentle, a patient heave of the ground. Down by the road you can hear the trucks, but up here it's only the wind in the leaves and the latent rasp of insects that don't really get going until the heat sets their cymbals rattling. The apples are the size of marbles, light green and hard, and if you pinch one there’s the wet white where it breaks, sharp as green walnut. We don't pinch them anymore if we can help it. It just feels like theft. The old man used to come around this time and do his ruthless thinning by hand, patience like a priest all summer. He'd take the extras down to one or two a spur. "Otherwise the tree will throw them," he’d say, and there would be a week in June when you couldn't take two steps without small green orbs underfoot. I’d always hated the sound of it, that clean drop on the dirt, like marbles, steady as a clock.

Silas came up the row with the shovel slung over his shoulder, the ferrule knocking his spine as he walked. He's taller than me, looser in the jaw, more sun on his nose. His jeans were already grimy at the knees and we hadn't even started. He carried a roll of plumber's tape in his pocket like a faith.

"Pump shop says the pressure switch might be sticking," he said. "Might not. If it's the switch we can fix it. If it's the impeller we'll have to pull the whole damned unit."

"We get the ditch water at ten," I said. "I told the rider we wouldn't waste it."

"We're going to waste it." He stuck the shovel in the dry dirt and leaned on it. "Come on, you know we are. Not because you lied to some kid in a county hat. Because that's what this place does now."

"You could leave," I said.

"Don't start," he said. "I did leave, remember? I came back for this one idiot summer. That's what's funny. I thought one summer would be nothing."

We stood side by side, looking down the rock-studded furrow where the buried line ran. Even with it dry you can see the faint shading in the soil where water had run before, a darker seam like a vein through pale flesh. When you dig it up the smell is almost edible, like the good dampness under the surface of a loaf of bread.

"Hand me the paint," I said, and he passed me the can with the inverted spray head. The old man always painted a line over any buried run, even though he could have walked the length blindfolded and put his foot on every valve like a dowser. Paint fades. The grade rises, the sun hits some places hotter. The casual safety of it pleased him but it also struck me as rude then, the way he thought of the underground as his, shrugging the fact that the water didn't belong to us entirely but to the mountain, the ditch company, the bristled eel of the river curling through everyone. Now I keep the same can and walk the same paths, painting ghosts over ghosts, somehow feeling like I’m performing convincingly at adulthood in front of no one.

We found where the line had heaved up the last time from pressure and heat, an elbow joint thrown out of its socket and the black poly chewed ragged by a gopher or a very determined set of teeth. We cut away until we had clean edges, the black plastic glistening when you score it with the knife. He made that grunt he makes when he kneels, a small disgust at his own body, and set the shovel to open the trench again. The shovel made that nice flat bite and hiss you only get early in the day before the heat has made the dirt ring like clay. Dust rose warm and smelling faintly like rodent turds and the tannins of roots.

"You talked to Mom?" he said.

"She called last night. She was making pie. Blueberry. She said the Swensons have their cherries already, early, and she can smell the sugar."

"She still at Anna's?"

"She's still at Anna's," I said. Our sister's house on the edge of town with the hydrangea that always flowers in blue no matter how much iron filings her husband stirs into the dirt. Our mother made them pie, and washed their windows because she can't help acting like she’s working off a stay.

"Did you tell her about the offer?" he asked.

"No."

"Christ, Joel."

"Why would I tell her about an offer you think we should take and I think we shouldn't? You tell her. You take the heat."

He snorted and levered the shovel again. "Wilder said they'd give us eight-fifty an acre. That's market. It's more than market. You know what eight-fifty means? It means you keep your truck. It means I don't have to go crawl back to a commercial kitchen and sell brisket to assholes named Sage who want to tell me how to smoke meat even though they buy their fire out of a bag. It means Mom doesn't have to choose whether it's a tooth or the mortgage. Jesus, I'm not even dreaming big. I'm asking for not-awful."

"It's not market."

"What is it then?"

"It's a wound. If he wants it that badly then it's worth more than that to us."

"You think you can make a living out of this."

"I didn't say that," I said, but I realized I had. Somewhere in there, like a childish prayer. The thing about wanting is that you end up saying it with your hands long before you realize you've done it with your mouth. I'm the one out here most mornings when the sun is still level with the hedge and the quail explode like flung gravel and the dew makes your boots stick to the grass like somebody loves you. I'm the one who knows which valves still turn with a smoother feel, where the line hisses if you lean in just right. The old man would say that these are not good reasons to keep land. The old man kept the land because his name was on it, because his body had marked it with the years, because he had a particular dream he expected others to live in more or less with him.

"Did you tell him eight-fifty is not enough?" I said. "Did you tell him—"

"Of course I told him it's not enough. I'm not an idiot. He said maybe nine if we give up the right-of-way on the ditch and he can reroute his cul-de-sac through Mr. Ortega's lower field."

"Jesus. Nine. So the orchard goes and in its place: 'McIntosh at Parkview Estates' or some shit. Fifty-five and older, pickleball and dog runs and a pond you can't even go in. At least if you turned it back to pasture..." The words went earnest on me before I could keep them dry. At least if you turned it back to pasture you'd have the flicker and hum of cows. At least you would still be able to stand on the plank of the fence and understand your body in the place. Imagine. Stupid. That’s not how this works.

"Nine is money," Silas said. "Nine is the chance to take other chances."

"You're not living here," I said. "You've got a rental on North Eighth with a yard of dust and a busted chain-link and the neighbor eats Cheetos shirtless every afternoon. You fly the coop and come back once a week to pull me out of holes. You leave and you have no skin in what this looks like when you're forty."

"I come back once a week because you don't ask for help otherwise," he said. "If I wait any more it's up to your stubbornness and a crescent wrench to carry both your ruins, and the old man didn't raise me to be that particular breed of asshole."

He hates it that I call him out for living in town like I don’t sleep on the same sagging couch when there’s a frost in February and the house won't heat right. He doesn't say it, but the set of his lip says it: I'm not sleeping in the farmhouse now either. The farmhouse isn't fit. It has wallpaper with sailboats and the smell of mice in the pantry. The bathroom faucet shrieks. When it rains, and it doesn't rain between May and October except for a freak afternoon squall, the cistern gurgles like some old throat clearing forever.

I cut the length of pipe and shoved the barbed coupler into the end. The first inch is easy. The next half inch hurts so bad you could scream. You brace the heel of your hand on it and push, and the ribs of the barb catch and a tiny ribbon of black shaves off and you think: this is stupid. We should heat it. The old man used to carry a jar of dish soap in his crate and smear it on. It makes the joint go on like a dream. It also makes it come off later or blow under pressure, and he also believed in the friction of things, that a good fit should ache a little. I twisted it and my breath went hard and finally the lip came up over the last ring. I pushed the worm clamp up, turned the screw until the band bit down on the thin plastic, and the oval tightened into a secure-looking circle. He passed me the other coupling. Neither of us talked. You can feel an argument come together the way summer storms do. It sets up fifty miles away in the always-elsewhere, and there's sun and the weight of it on your bare shoulders and on your eyelids, and in an hour the canopy has rolled over and the light is mildew-green and everything gets sticky and then a noise like a train will fill the whole world. You hope it blows north. There’s always a ditch to your south that can take it, some other yard that can use the water when it drops. But it finds you.

"You know we're not going to get enough pressure to throw the spray all the way out at the corners if we don't close off that line under the pear block," he said after a minute. "You know we're going to have to let three acres just die if we want to keep the five."

"Don't say die," I said. "Say fallow." The synonyms matter. They lie in different ways.

"Fallow is planned," he said. "This is losing. This is triage."

"Don't say lose. Say—"

"Jesus, Joel."

"I know," I said. "I'm sorry. Hand me the T."

He found the T-fitting and I cut into the main. When the plastic blade bites you feel sudden gratitude for the human with a good idea to make a material you can work without fire or hammer, without solder and lead. Then later in the scraps is your contempt for all the crude molded stuff that warps on a hot day, that cracks with ultraviolet, that litters the littorals. Both facts can live. I wiped dust off the cut ends with the hem of my shirt and set the piece, one side into the live line, the other two stubs toward the rows. I could see the row of little micro-sprinkler risers, the black heads with their radial slots, the orange strainers like tiny netted bulbs. Each head throws a whirling halo the size of a car wheel if the pressure is right. If the pressure is wrong, it coughs a foot and drools and it's like a barn with no eaves—the edge gets parched and weeds take the drink that the good worker planted for.

"Ortega's got new heads," Silas said. "He said he can leave them out all winter, they don't crack like ours."

"He's not keeping them on a hundred-year-old line like we are."

"He's also not keeping a hundred-year-old anything, because he got out when the getting was good and he leases to that organic outfit out of Salem that has capital. The guy said they'd look at your fruit if you got on a spray program of theirs and if you dropped this block."

"I'm not dropping this block," I said. The north slope is not the best block. The old man grafted Jonagold back when the cooperative came by with charts and told everyone what the stores would be demanding in three years. He got it half right. The local store bought everything he brought in so long as the fruit was big and pretty and red. He liked to walk the rows in the fall with that little leather change-purse of brass tags, and he’d clip them onto the branches for the places to pick. "Everything else culls, or cider," he'd tell us, and then he'd heap the windfalls in a ring around the trunks and the deer would come and eat the sweet flesh in the nights like gentlemen, stepping around one another with their velvet dawn horns obsidian in the starlight.

We buried the pipe where we'd cut it. He tamped in with the butt of the handle and I'd use my boot sort of softly, the pretence of my weight doing anything against the fact that it's the rain, when it comes, that knits soil back around plastic. I followed the line to the valve and leaned on it with both fists and heard the small old sound of metal rubbing metal. That sound will be in my mouth when I die. Maybe in my son's mouth, if that ever comes to me. The idea knocks around me like a moth. Company and the sense of something outside my body, and then the powder of wing on lamp, furtive and unnecessary and not what I wanted at all.

By ten the sun had swung up so that the shade of the trees threw neat circles like hats on the dirt. The foreman from the ditch company texted; they don't come to your gate on a horse anymore, hat in hand, watching the clock and the feel of the river in his blood. They have tablets now that read the measuring weirs and assign an hour, initials next to each slot. My phone buzzed and I read on it that our water was ready, and I turned to walk down to the headgate, but Silas didn't move. He was looking at his own phone like it had finally told him something he knew it was going to say.

"She's two weeks early," he said.

"What?"

"Addie. She's having contractions."

"Jesus," I said. I felt weirdly happy, and then oddly offended that I did. A baby. In this heat. He put a hand to his forehead but didn't shade the light.

"Her sister's with her," he said. "I told her I would be there. What am I supposed to do, Joel? I thought we had the day. I can go start the pump. I can drive down and go start it and then head back and if you—"

"I'll manage," I said. "Christ, go. Why are you even asking me?"

"You'll need the couplings," he said. "And you hate the pump."

"I don't hate the pump. Turn your eyes on straight and go be where the little human is going to start breathing in heat for the first time. I'll text you if it explodes."

He laughed and didn't look at me. "Don't let it explode. No drama while I'm gone."

"Go," I said.

He went. I heard the gravel pop under his tires all the way down to the road, and then the sound of rubber on blacktop, then nothing but a hawk's single argument and the insect ratchet. Under the noise was the quiet of the orchard, which is not really quiet. You realize out here that your ear is hunting all the time for threaded water and for things that sound like animals and for the breath of your own age.

I walked the ditch. The headgate is old wood silvered to the color of winter mullein, with a big iron wheel that raises and lowers the plank. It sings in two ranges when it turns, low at the core, and around the rim a ribbon squeal. There's a weight to the water before you even get it. The ditch comes off the big canal a mile above and drops by grade. The right to it was the first paper this farm ever owned. The old man kept it rolled with a rubber band in the top of the desk and when tax men came to talk or neighbors came to argue, he'd unroll it slow like a conjurer and set his finger to the paragraph where his father's name showed with the year in ink and he'd look at their faces like now you see.

I opened the gate slowly, because you can blow out the hammer and turn the bend downstream into a cannon if you throw it all at once. The water, when it came, came fast anyway. There was no stopping the childlike speed of it. It took a breath around the lip of the plank and it slid in flat and frothed when it hit the first bend and my heart went up like a turtle’s nose.

At the pump shed the holler of the little two-cylinder made a good steady. It caught and ran. For a minute I stood with my hand on the frame like the way you steady a kid going down steps. It whump-whumped and then made a lighter tone as the pressure built. The old silver manifold purred and you could feel the vibration run up your wrist. The pressure gauge quivered to nine and then eleven and then thirteen, which is where these lines work and a man stops thinking of his luck as bad.

Back up the ladder of the rows I went and at the top seesaw I could see them: the little umbrellas of water, all in a row. When they run right there is the strict romanticism of them; all the circles overlap and the dirt takes the dark of it and you think: we are not tragic yet, we are not. You do the numbers in your head: four gallons per minute, twenty heads per hundred feet, pressure drop by run length, afternoon drift. I don't do the numbers right. I do them like the old man, with a hand and sense of the shoulder's own book.

This is where I wanted to stop. I wanted to sit under the tree where, when you tip your head back, the sky is very far but feels like a thing you could drink out of. I wanted that black coffee I had left in the crooked mug on the back steps. But the orchard is not a place where you sit in June. It wants you to walk. You walk until you find where sound is wrong. Here, at the last tree in the third row, there was no right hiss. You put your boot near the riser and the ground gave. I dug and the shovel went in easily and then it splashed and the cold of it found my shins, and I said to the water in a normal speaking voice: if you do this now, I'm going to be worse.

The leak was not a leak. It was a gash, the poly split endwise, a fat black mouth with a clean edge where it had been gnawed. We have field mice and we have voles, and neither has any respect for agriculture because agriculture is pretend to them, one season's idea of ownership atop their real maps. They find the water with the hunger their small bodies carry like a candle. Of course they chew the pipe. If you had a gnawing in your bronze little belly and no hands you would do exactly the same.

I went down on my knees and held my thumb over the pulse to let it slap me when it needed. I didn't mind the water; I minded the waste like it was my own blood. I stood and went back down the rows to the crate where we keep couplings and clamps, the screwdriver with a good bite left on the head, the rust-scattered hacksaw. There are days where your body will just not give you all your own things, and there are days where it will. Today it did. I carried the crate back like I meant something on this property more than my insistence.

It takes five minutes to cut and fit a line when the line is twenty-five years old and you don't come all the way around the worn places with your clamp because you can't fit your hand between the roots. It takes twenty when you're careful. I am careful. I am careful because I want not to be the sort of person who breaks things with hurry. I made myself wipe the ends, slide the clamps up, heat the cut with a lighter and then blow on it like a liar, seat the barb true.

There is a small private joy in a leak sealed: the sound changes instantly, harmony for a second, like a dog settling with a sigh. I let the hole in the trench fill and the black water go glossy and then I stood and wiped my hands on grass and said to the air: all right. Try me again. Show me the next thing.

It showed me a section nearer the pump where the water fanned from a pinhole in a bright jet and you could find the sun in it like glass. It showed me the valve I could not shift even when I put my whole weight on it and hung. It showed me how the head that had been plugged last year was still plugged, ringed with the pale salt we blow with our own ground, our winter alchemy leaving our hands. It showed me the patch I had made in April that held and the one we made in May that had begun to smear under heat. The orchard will show you what you are and are not good at until you either get better or leave. People talk about therapy like it's a couch, but if you want to hear yourself name your own wrong ideas go put a wrench on an old bolt in sun.

At noon I sat on the dented fender of the mower and ate from a Ziploc bag of almonds that I had to spit shells from because I forgot they weren't the kind you can eat whole. I drank from the fat cooler and let it dribble across my chest because I'm not nineteen anymore and I wanted the cool. A bee landed on the lip of the cooler and touched the wet and you feel like everything in the world is connected to your little stupid acts when that happens. The bee drank with its improbable straw and some spasm in me remembered the old man telling us at this very spot, his pant legs dusty while he cut a sandwich with that old case knife he kept grimy on purpose: a bee can smell water farther than a dog smells meat. That can't be true, but as with so many of his pronouncements, you choose the kin you want with facts. You are wrong alongside him as long as he lives, and when he is gone you take the facts in under your coat and try to warm them against the wind.

My phone rang while I chewed. It was a number from town.

"Joel?" a man's voice said. Level, practiced nice. "This is Kent from Wilder Development. Is this a good time?"

"It's not," I said. "We're in the middle of our water-turn."

"This won't take a second. I spoke with your brother last night and I just wanted to make sure you had the same information. We have the ability to be flexible with terms. If, for example, the family wants to lease a couple acres for the farmstead for a period, we can make that happen. It sounds like this place means a lot to you boys."

Everything means a lot when it's not yours yet and you want to make someone feel tended in the asking. I pictured him in a cool office with a jar of pens and a fake potted palm. I pictured his skin not on fire. I pictured him meaning some portion of what he said and not knowing what the other portion would do to his sleep.

"My brother talks for himself," I said. "I talk for me."

"Absolutely," he said. "We really want to respect what your father built. I hear he planted those north rows himself? Beautiful old-growth."

"Things planted in the eighties are not old-growth," I said.

"Of course," he said. "Bad term. Still, it's a legacy. People in this town love that kind of story. And the thing is, I don't know if Silas mentioned it, but we're willing to help with moving your pump house, making sure you can still pull from the ditch for whatever you keep. Our engineer worked up a sketch. You could have a beautiful little homestead with fifty-by-a hundred foot gardens—"

"I'm going to stop you," I said. "Is this you telling me nine is now nine-five? Because otherwise I need to go shut off a riser."

"Nine is where our board is comfortable today," he said, "but this market is dynamic. I just want to be sure you know we are good neighbors. And we're excited about what we can do together. My wife and I walk by that place in the morning and just imagine the families, you know? The kids on bikes. Safe, beautiful. We want to preserve a little of the feel. Maybe a little orchard along the entrance, you know? A nod to—"

"Goodbye, Kent," I said, and I hung up, and then stood with the phone against my thigh while the blood in my ears did the heavy tide.

It's not fair to him to make him into a single noise. He probably has kids who do turn their handlebars with their legs pumping right in front of him like creatures he could almost catch with his mouth. He probably sits on his deck and thinks of the big field where he's going to put the cul-de-sac and he sees afternoon light like slices of bread and all the people he will make happy fitting in boxes. The world is not tender with the tenderness we each think we own. The world spreads it out like manure and sometimes it grows a great thing and sometimes the weeds take it.

The heat climbed. You could call it heat if you wanted to sound tough about it. We call it sun here. The sun was a force. Skin flinched at it when you walked out from under the leaves. I dug under a junction where the manifold for this block connected. It had sagged on one end and the stress had made a hairline that weeped fine enough to look like soil damp when you didn't look dead-on. I loosened the band, slid the upper joint free. The black o-ring inside had nicked, maybe the last time someone impatient had yanked instead of eased. I eased it now, rolled the new o-ring in my fingers. The sweat on my hands made everything feel like a live fish.

I was thinking about the first time the old man left us with the water turn. We were thirteen, or I was, and he had to go to town to sign the paper that said the county could put the bike path along the railroad bed. We hated him for that, and hated the county, because a bike path meant people near our fence and people near our fence meant people seeing the failures we'd rather not show. He said it meant people in October could come and pick and ride back to their cars with a bag of what we grew, and maybe they'd like how it tasted and how on our road the light comes through the cottonwoods like you're walking through a hallway in a church. Maybe they'd come and buy more. We didn't know then that what people like is to walk through your life like it is a story with woven ends and then to go home and eat chicken nuggets over their sink. I am not immune. I like it, too, the story with woven ends. He left us with the sheet of slot times, the canoe he rigged to float the little bags of sand down the ditch to blunt the corners, his silver pipe wrench. He said "turn them on slow, don't starve the last guy, watch for holes" and when he left the pump stuttered and a belt squealed and we both looked at each other like we had been stood into adulthood and found to wobble. That day, nothing broke. The miracle of incompetence unpunished. We ate our sandwiches on the trailer and felt like we had survived something real. People will tell you it is the broken days that raise you. It's also the days when nothing breaks and you are not rewarded beyond being allowed to be tired and then go to bed.

I texted Silas when I had the manifold back together. Water steady at thirteen. Heads throwing good. He texted back with a picture of a hospital hall floor. Later a picture of his face with his mouth drawn tight, but a little smile because he can't not sew levity into even the stiff parts.

He called at two.

"She's here," he said. He wasn't crying. His voice was breathless. "Seven pounds. The exact color of our mother's knuckles."

"Addie?"

"She's fine," he said. "She's—women are—Joel, why do they even talk to us? We know nothing. We are useless bodies with your wrench on the wrong nut. She's sleeping now. They named her Sarah, I think. Did you fix the leak?"

"I fixed three leaks." I had the phone to my own shoulder while I tightened a clamp. The band cut a nick in my index finger and I stuck it in my mouth like a child while I kept turning. "The line by the pears has to go. It's just—it's split for ten feet like a balloon animal you popped with a fork. I'll cut it and cap it. We'll let it go dry."

"Say fallow," he said.

"Fallow," I said.

"You'll be there for the sprinkler check at four?" he said. "They're going to say we need a new system."

"Yes."

"Okay." His voice changed. He had loved something and the loving had made him gentle without removing from him any of his true edges. I could hear the beeping of some machine in time with a minor chord that had my heart in it. The sound of a place made for human distress and human triumph, as thin as a reed and as heavy as an anchor.

"Don't come back today," I said. "Seriously. Go sleep on that little sofa and eat their world of Jell-O and graham crackers. I'll be good. I will be so good you will give me your house plant to water."

"My house plant died," he said. "While we were talking."

"Go away," I said, and hung up smiling and then standing with the smile on me like a hat in a movie I would not wear in daylight.

The afternoon bent. The plums. The driveway. The neighbor boy who slid along the property line on his bike with a baseball card rattling in his spokes like he was both giving himself speed and making the sound of a thing that had already arrived. He lifted one hand in greeting without looking at me and his eyes were elsewhere, the way kids look when adults are speaking, like their duty is to pretend to be where their body is. A doe walked the far fence line with her neck high, her ears moving like hands indicating for traffic, and her fawn stepping behind exactly in the prints because evolution is not sentimental, it is instructive.

At three-twenty the sprinkler guy came. He parked his van under the walnut where the skinny shade was the wrong shape to make any difference, and came up the row like a recipe card for efficiency. He had a logo on his hat: a little arc of water that said somewhere somebody had paid someone to trace what water is. He had a handful of riser heads and a bucket of the little strainers and a set of nozzles with numbers. He said "Good afternoon" in a voice that had an indoor air to it even out here.

"You Joel?" His eyes took in the dry at the pears without moving. He had seen a hundred florid men with hope and a hundred wives with lists. He had heard the speech I'm about to give about this being land not real estate and line not product.

"That's me," I said.

He crouched by a head and snapped it off with that move that looks like impatience but is only knowledge. The water went straight up like a boy in a fountain and he put his finger over it and made it arc and he watched how it fell without looking like he was watching. "You want more radius," he said, "you need pressure and a bigger nozzle. You put a bigger nozzle on without pressure you get a dribble. Looks like dribble," he said, although every head I had gave me the sound I thought I had asked for.

"The pump's a hundred-horse with the worn blue paint and the door that won't latch," I said, as if personality of pump would change his mind about hydraulics.

"Yeah," he said.

"We get thirteen at the manifold."

"Yeah."

"Ortega's got new heads."

"Yeah."

He worked and the world of his work had nothing to do with ours except for the rectangle where it overlapped. He found six heads that were clogged, he banged them on his palm and the shards of snail shell came out and he held them in front of me like: do you see why. He replaced two orange strainers with yellow. He suggested we blow the lines in the winter. I suggested to him we did not have the money to blow the lines in the winter. He said frost is cheap until it's not. He said "it's a beautiful place you got here" and I believed him half and the other half smiled back like the top of a pie.

By four-thirty he was on the road again and I was back at the headgate in the combed light. I shut the ditch and the sound of the orchard changes you. The little umbrellas begin to diminish, then cough, then spit. The shiny earth goes dull. There's a minute when the frogs you did not know were in the ditch box say one long flat note of dismay. Then it is only the sound of the men you are or are not, the insects that don't shut up until they fail in their wish to be heard forever.

I walked the block and made notes in the old man’s book where I still write the day down and the heads that need haircuts. He kept the neatest numbers when he wanted you to see what you should do for him, and a handful of chicken scratches when he was telling himself he could leave it to tomorrow. I looked at his numbers from June four years ago. The yield he thought he had. The number of bins he ordered for September. He wrote a note to himself once that says "get new clamp by top manifold. Ask T about gopher." T is for Tom across the road, and Tom's advice for gophers is to kill them. You do not always need a neighbor to tell you that. But sometimes the telling is what makes the thing real enough to do.

I went into the house to cool off under the fan above the kitchen table that roars at one speed and it's always a storm. The table has a gouge where I cut a pork roast with too much anger. The light on the floor had dust in it that looked like Paul's letters to the Corinthians always seemed in my head when I read them in Sunday school, full of floating pollen that were the souls of cities. The old man's hat was still on the peg by the door and the shape of it is what hurts you. The hat remembers the exact skull bone. I looked away from it like it was rude of me to stare.

The house phone rang and for a second I expected to hear his voice in the suggestion of it, and then laughed at myself, and then let the laugh go because if you don't stop the small sentimentalities they'll have you and you won't have anything left.

"How'd it go," Silas said when I picked up. He sounded smaller on the old plastic receiver, like a bug trapped in the coiled cord.

"I shut it off five minutes ago. We got through the turn. Pears are—"

"I know," he said. He wasn't going to argue with me about what to call what I had done to them. He had a baby in his hands. He had had a mother delivered in the new form of her line.

"Did Mom come down," I asked.

"She's here," he said. "She's...You should see her, Joel. She is standing like I have seen deer stand, like the world is something she has lost the right name for. It's wonderful."

"I can come in the morning," I said. "After the seven-to-nine on the south block. Or maybe I can turn that over to the neighbor. It's their turn after ours if Ortega doesn't need it."

"You can," he said. "Or you can not, and instead you can go down there now and watch the last of the light do the two things it does here that you love, and maybe pick a green walnut and crush it in your hand like a murderer."

"I don't love destroying trees," I said.

"You love things with the kind of love that can't not press its thumb too hard," he said. And then he laughed because he didn't want it to have the taste that it could.

"When do they come home," I said.

"Tomorrow if everything looks good. Don't sell while I'm gone."

"As if you are the only one holding up the sign that says no," I said.

"I'm not," he said. He was quiet. In the quiet I could hear a woman somewhere talking calmly, and the shape of warmth around a new sleeping creature. "I'm not sure I am, anymore. I know what we would lose if we do it. I know it four levels down my chest. I also know what we won't get back if we don't."

"We won't get him back," I said. "That's not part of this equation any more. This is a different equation."

"I know," he said. "I think I always thought if we had enough water in the right places at the right time we could get him back anyway. I can't do math in heat."

"You'll be good at father math," I said. "Numbers where you lose count on purpose and call it right."

I hung up and the house breathed its hot breath around me and I went outside because even hot air is kinder if it isn't shaped into a room. The orchard in the lowering light is what it is. I walked a row and in the next-to-top tree I saw the black lash where fire blight had come for an arm, and I took my knife out and cut it back into clean wood and then cut another inch because you cannot be careful with blight. The pitch the tree makes at the cut is miasmic. I wiped my knife on my pant leg like it would help. If you treat the tree like a thing you can heal you are decent until you are a fool. If you treat it like a machine you are efficient until you are a bastard. You do something third that is neither kindness nor contempt and that's called work.

I knelt and picked three green apples the wind had done before me. I lined them on the fence rail. There is a thing in me that wants to name them. This one is called Look. This one is called Wait. This one is called Don't. The birds sat above me on their wires and threw their gossip down. A swallow kept turning figure-eights and always at the same angle catching his insect like a man who could only ever get the fish from the same rock at the bend of the river in the same week of the year and had built a myth around it because it had worked twice.

When it's fully evening you can hear the shift of the land as much as see it. Just before dark the trees give up a wetness to the air they had been holding for their own sake all day. With the water on their leaves and at their feet they have laughed without noise. With it off again and the sun leaving, they sigh and the orchard becomes engine you can't see. The driveway holds the day heat and then releases it over an hour like a hand coming off a shoulder.

I carried the toolbox back to the pump shed and set it on the shelf where the labels have melted to illegible. There is a tin framed picture in there of the old man in his gumboots straddling a line like he could stop it with his body, and me at six with a toy saw in my pocket. It's dirty and I don't clean it. Dirt is the currency here and I pay with it because it's what I have left cheap. The pump made ticking noises as it cooled. I put my palm on it. There are parts of machines that feel like organs if you have touched them long enough.

It is going to get harder. That’s not prophecy. That's weather. We will get one or two more turns where everything holds the way it should because we arranged it with attention. We will get one where the ditch rider is new and doesn't know the feel of a headgate and throws water wrong at us and we will get to carry buckets like monks with pails of flame through a village. We will get a surprise thunderstorm that smells of iron and wet hay and it will lay the blossoms down in the pears in some other year and we will say "we used to" even though we know memory is another job we take on and have to do without the right tools.

When you have a day you want to keep you can't. So instead you put in it as many touches as you think could ever be meaningful when the raw cord is cut and you are standing there with a baby or a sold deed or a half-open valve in your hand that will either shut to the turn of your wrist or just shear off. The sound the water makes in the ditch as it quiets is like someone trying to tell you something important in a language you almost remember.

I took the long way up the rows to the hill where you can see the town. The town is the same. It's not a picture, but it's the same everyday thing, all those roofs that are the right color to confuse the hawks. There is the high school, the warehouse, the water tower that somebody sneaks to paint dicks on because bodies will always insist on their shape. There's the column of trees that mark the creek and the slow bright snake of the river beyond, greener where it slows, shinier where it goes over rock. There are the fields in pieces, squares of something living at different health depending who fell in love with what this year. There is a gap that breathed like an invitation where a builder could see a good cul-de-sac to hold twenty-seven lives that would each throw their own tiny spirals of meaning in the light both ignorant of and related to the thing that had been here before.

I have wanted all the things, in turns. To take a hand and walk it through a child's head till they slept. To make an apple so crisp that a stranger remembers his ancestor's wooden teeth. To sell a thing and move and try to buy a lighter life. To keep a thing because separate from me it still is itself and deserves defenders in flesh. Beneath all that wanting, the dumb creature part of me that is good at nothing except continuing to try to do the sensible manipulations with my body that make water go where I need it when I don't have much to call.

On the way down, I stepped on a fallen green apple and it rolled and I almost went down on the back of my head like a fool and I laughed aloud at it even with nobody there to not mind. When I stopped under the tree you could hear the tiny sound of others hitting the earth. It's called June drop. The name is as simple as the act. The tree trims itself. The tree says: not you, not you. Not because you aren't good, but because the water only goes so far out the small black veins we have given and the light, even in abundance, isn't always a reliable economy. I wasn't sure I liked that. I also wasn't sure I disliked it in any meaningful way. Some part of me wanted to gather the little emerald fists and press them back onto the spurs and tell the tree that faith is rewarded eventually. Another part of me knew what a lie faith is when it is a command rather than a fact formed by a thousand inheritances in your blood.

Down at the valve I cracked it a quarter and listened to the small hiss bleed off a heat that had taken the top seam tight. It's a little trick to spare the o-ring. I had to learn it by blowing one, torching my knee, and sitting on the ground cursing until the ground lost patience with me and the sun told me I was embarrassing myself. I closed the box, brushed the dust off the lid with my hand, even though the wind will lay another thin scripture down by tomorrow.

When I got to the truck I put my forehead on the wheel like a penitent. The horn didn't go because I had pulled the fuse last year when it started crying in the night all on its own. That night was a kind of funny. I had rolled over in the truck bed and heard the bleat and thought the cows on Hill's place had got out, and then realized the only cow nearby was the F-150. I sat up and laughed at the sound for a minute in the rain we weren't supposed to have, and then I leaned over the headlight with a screwdriver and pulled the wear from the wrong. You do that. You stop the things that wake you up stupid. You don't get to stop the sweet things from startling you. My phone made the little chime for a text and it was a picture. There was Silas, his head bent, his mouth closed, his eyes uncharacteristically steady, holding a person the size of a bag of flour. The baby looked both arthritic and smarter than me. She was wearing one of those little hats that make you want to apologize for everything you have ever cheapened.

"Everything okay?" he wrote.

"You should sell," I texted back, because I am a horrible man, because I knew he would read it and know that I meant it as a test and a kindness and a question and a poke and a dare. He didn't text back for a minute. Then: "We should water our trees. Then we should talk. In that order. Come see her tomorrow."

I put the truck in gear. The gravel coughed, and the orchard lay back behind me like it does at the end of a June day, the dark coming from the ground up, the leaves gathering their gloss to the inside. The old man would be walking the last row with his hands behind his back if he were here. The fox would cross that lower trail at the place where the grass goes thin. At the end of the road the stop sign would glow like a church candle if you looked at it straight. We don't get to know what making the right choice looks like in the minute you make it. Mostly you get the temperature of the metal when you lean on it. Without thinking, because sometimes that is the only good instinct left, I turned into the ditch road instead of the highway and drove along the water until I reached the turnout that looks over the bend. There was a boy on a bike down there, not the neighbor but a different kid, standing with one foot down, looking at the smooth top of the canal like it could break for him if he said the right word. I wanted to tell him it doesn't. It goes where it goes. Then I wanted to tell him the other thing, the thing the old man would have said if he hadn't died and left me holding both sides of the word: sometimes it does, if you hold both your hands right and ask like you are willing to help it get to the place you're imagining. The hawk stood on the pole and did not move. The sound of town was a hum. The sprinkler guy's van had left a last dust on the walnut leaf that reflected the low sun in some precise private shade of gold.

I drove home slow. In the house the fan had spun all day without making any air, and on the table there was the list I had made in the morning, all the items checked with a pencil, each check a small fool's flag I could wave. Outside, three apples hit the ground and then a fourth, and then the quiet settled down again like a quilt, heavy and present, full of the squares of things your people made and the things you might yet.
