I can’t write in Michael Ondaatje’s exact voice, but I can craft an original piece that draws on similar high-level qualities—lyrical, elliptical, intimate with place and memory, braided timelines, tender attention to the physical world and the weathered edges of love and loss. Here is a continuous narrative in that spirit.

He took the night boat at eleven, the metal lip of the gangway cold through the soles of his shoes. Above him the ship was lit like a building left awake by accident, a series of white corridors and windows in which the same three shadows moved and reappeared as if they were the night crew of his own mind. On shore a crane arm swung against the last of the light and lifted a pallet of bundled newspapers, rain already hassling the pages. The strait at this hour looked not like water but like a road without edges. He carried a backpack with a sweater and two letters and the clothes he would need if it was his brother and he had to stay longer. He had not slept the night before. He was thin tired, which made the world both sharp and blurry, like a shoreline seen through the slow breath of fog.

Inside the ferry there were the usual maps with red dots where you might be if everything went as promised, there were vending machines, there was a coffee machine that groaned in its own language, there was a cluster of plastic chairs bolted like geese. He saw two boys with gear bags, their hair already salt-scraped, and an old woman with a caged plant on her lap as if she were being taken to winter. He saw a man asleep with his cheek pressed to a book that slid with the engine’s pulse. All of them caught between places. He avoided the television in the corner that offered the news without sound, mouths cutting the air.

He found a seat by a window smudged with something like greasy rain. There were smear marks on the glass where someone had pressed a palm to see better in the daylight, and now those marks held the reflections of faces that were not present. The loudspeaker lifted itself into their atmosphere and instructed them to heed the announcements, to make sure their cars were in neutral, to keep an eye on children who might wander off. Children who might wander off. He watched the harbor slip past, the pilings greased with weed, the ferry nosing out.

He thought of the last time he had been here. Years ago, daytime, with Simon—his brother—whose idea it had been to take the ferry and then hitchhike up the coast to look for work that never revealed itself. Simon with a blue scarf wound too tightly as if to hold in breath when it got away. There had been a girl on that trip, dark hair and a way of standing with her hands on her hips so that all her weight went into one foot. They had watched her for an hour without speaking, then argued afterward whose kind of girl she was, as if there were types and one of them had the right to claim her. They were too young then, and the argument had not been about her, not at its center. It had been about being attached to anything at all.

The engine worked its way toward steadiness, and they felt, all of them, the town loosening from their ankles. He put his backpack at his feet and leaned back. He closed his eyes as if that would prevent what was coming into view—cold tile, the rubber on the edge of a metal table, the particular weight that is left in a room when sound has gone out of it. He had received the call at 2 a.m. the morning before. A voice, formal and trying not to be cold, from the island hospital, the one with the yellow siding and the steeple-like stairwell. We have a male, early forties, pulled from the flats below Deadman Point; we found a wallet on the beach with the name Simon P., and your number folded into a pocket, and some other names, and we know we should be cautious saying this. Could you. If you’re able. We can tell you what we know but not who this is. He had put the receiver down after the first sentence to fetch his glasses and then come back and asked them to repeat it all, slow, so he could write in a hand that would look like no one’s once the morning came.

—Do you know any marks that would identify him if it is him? a second voice asked, a nurse, a different timbre.

He sat there in the small pool of the kitchen light, his hand on a shopping list, half-finished. Simon had a scar along the ankle where he put a spade through his foot on the farm, he said. He had a tear on the ear from running too close to the doorframe, all speed and no sense, he added before he could stop it, and the nurse was silent, kind enough to let the film of breath go over what he had just said.

—Sometimes those things help, sometimes they don’t, she said. We’ll be here. We’ll keep him cold. I’m sorry.

He had gone then to the drawer where they kept ropes and unused keys and found the old letter, the last one he received from Simon. There were two pages, dated four and a half years ago, postmarked in a town on the other side of the strait. He had read it the next morning, then again when the boat whistled low and turned its face to the open channel.

I am working nights, the letter said, a crow’s wing in a white envelope. They have me on the loading docks because I don’t argue. There is a man with a bruise like a continent on his shoulder—he tells me stories about how his mother ran a store out of her front room for fifty years and never learned the names of the men who bought rolled cigarettes on credit. I sleep in a rented room with the window painted shut. There is a woman across the hall. We pass at all hours and pretend we have not. The island is not an island if you are poor. It is a town with a fence in water. I sell my blood when I can. My ear whistles in the wind.

That last line. As if he'd wanted to sign with something that was not entirely tragic, so he gave music to the wound.

He had first met Simon at six weeks, of course, but he told the story of their formal introduction differently to whoever asked, as if the beginning of a brotherhood deserved a crafted beginning. He told it to women like a small apology for where he’d come from. He told it in bars so he could hold it between thumb and forefinger like a nail and people would watch his hands. The true introduction had been when their mother put Simon in the turned fruit crate beside the stove and told him—Eli—this is your second heart and if you hit him with anything bigger than your hand I will leave you both. He was four and Simon was a small noise. Their father, who had a way of coming into rooms as if he were walking along a cliff, half attentive to the drop, said Teach him what is sharp. Teach him what is hot. Teach him which cats will scratch the face off him. And then, quieter, Teach him not to be left alone on the water.

On the water. The house they grew up in had a dock that went forward into the bay and then a set of stairs that went down off the side into eel grass which was not grass and did not care who thought it was. There were rope burns on the dock posts where the boats had been tied and pulled and tied again. There was a heavy bench made from a cedar they did not remember seeing as a tree. There was a line of rusted fishhooks knotted like a rosary. When they were old enough their father set them on the skiff and let the engine die in a cove where a ridge of rocks sheltered the water and the drift of a cloud was enough to change what they thought the day would be. He spoke the names to them as if the names were what the places had instead of borders. Horsehair Narrows. The teeth. The slot. The water where herring came as if they owned the light, bringing with them, for a month, a city of birds into their otherwise plain sky.

He could feel those names at the back of his tongue as the ferry leaned into the night. The water here was deeper, dark in a way that made him think of clothes left in a basin, something of a home task that might never end. He set the letter away and closed his eyes and saw Simon waist-deep at the river’s mouth, thirteen, his chest like paper, the current solid enough to pull at the skin of his shins. Simon sang when he was cold, a foolish run of notes from the top of his head. He would not wade out of the green tuck where the eddy kept him but would insist on fishing there where nothing moved but sticks, feeling the good cold pain against his knees as if to prove to their father later that he was made as they had made him.

You made us out of what you had, Eli said once to their father. He had been drunk and generous with his hurt. The old man had laughed without a body in the laugh. Who didn’t? the father had said and turned his wrist toward the sun to see if there were any fish bones still there, visible under the scab.

The ferry creaked in a way that suggested it was growing and shrinking with the cold. People outside smoked. The smell came in through the door each time someone forgot to close it. In the corner a boy with small square glasses, fourteen maybe, tugged on the sleeve of the older man beside him, perhaps a father, to show him how the points of light moved on the black window and gathered into lines when he blinked hard. The older man put his hand on the boy’s head and made the hair lie flat. Eli wanted to reach for that hand and place it on his own skull. He wanted to be fifteen. Even as he was being forty-three with his brother’s name in his palm.

There was a ferry worker moving through the room, hands thrust into the orange vest as if she did not know they were attached to her. She had a long nose that made her face memorable. When she glanced toward him he thought he recognized something—the line of hunger that attaches strangers in a certain kind of passage. She came near enough that her breath, winter mint, came to him.

—You going far? she said, because this is what people on the night shift must say if they are kind, so that the hours are not entirely machines.

—North island, he said. He watched the effort it took her to not ask, in other words to only ask with her posture.

—I have a son there, she said anyway. He is learning to lay brick. There’s a good crew. The man keeps a dog that refuses meat. It eats carrots out of his hand. When he calls it it comes if he sings. You wouldn’t think.

She was almost talking to herself. She was telling him a story to keep herself here. He nodded, and she took that as what she needed to move on. She touched the edge of his seat and kept walking, and he had a sudden wish to give her the letter, to give someone the letter, to have it be out in the air where it could do its dying in public.

He went to the outer deck, where the wind was such that he quickly stuffed his hands into his jacket pockets in the idiot way of a person who has not been on boats as much as he thinks he has. It was a strange kind of relief, the dark of the channel and the thick ribbon of milky water rolled behind them. He could not see the shore on either side. He could not see the ferry’s own work other than the path it ruined for a moment.

When he went back in an hour later, the boy and the man were asleep, their heads tipped away from each other like a portrait of disagreement. The news without sound was now a view of a house on fire. A town, snowed in some years back, filled the next picture. He thought of how the world was always being entered and exited by the same camera. It made everything seem close to all other things, which sometimes made it easier to live.

He dozed in a position that made him not awake and not asleep, and moved from his seat to the carpet to the seat again, a small circle of the same square of light. Twice he dreamed he missed his stop. Once he woke and walked the length of the passenger deck to watch the sea fall past and not see it fall past and came back to find a fresh coffee cup in the place where he had left his hand. The ferry worker had set it down. She was three steps away, not turning. He toward her—Thank you—and she made a motion with her shoulders that might have been a shrug or a bow.

He felt the coffee and the first swallow fell through him. There had been weeks before when he had thought of Simon less than once a day and then other weeks when he brought his brother into his office with him and felt him sit on the edge of the desk like when they were boys and he had played schoolteacher without ever having been schooled. In those weeks he had to speak words like inventory, like distribution, around the shape of Simon’s mouth.

He had his phone, of course. He had a picture of the two of them taken with a camera that was not a phone. They stood in a field where the hay was already rolled into cylinders and their father had his arm over both of their shoulders so that the bones of his forearm crossed their chests. In the picture Simon is taller and leaning away; he is making a face as if all of this had been a dare he had not been consulted on. The photographer, a neighbor, had told them to stand still but the air had been thick with small gnats that were not biting but acted as if they owned his eyes. Eli could feel even now the stung soreness of an eyelid. The picture had been taken at a time just before something, just before whatever the thing was that happens when a person sees themselves in a mirror and insists on a key change.

He did not want to think about the last time he and Simon spoke. Which is of course why he started again on that loop. It had been a winter without much snow and the river had come low over rocks their father had named with names that would not mean anything to anyone else. They had come to the house to help him with the roof, and Simon had seemed older than he was, his face already tied to his skull as if it were a traveler and the skull was a trunk. They lasted three hours before a sentence broke into them like a night thing. Who had left when the father needed, who had sent money, who had written more than a page, who had been seen in town buying beer not from the store where their father bought kerosene, some idea that even their purchases were auditions for permission. The argument began with small technical words and moved into the waste of the body, where words touched the way the ones we love breathe and how long they look at us when we enter the room and if they look at us when they do that. It was one of those arguments that go on despite both participants being tired and ashamed. The strength of it becomes the story. Simon had left first, and only after he left had Eli found that he was pressing his thumb so hard against his own middle finger that he had drawn a small crescent of blood.

—You want me to be a picture you can love, Simon had said. —I want you to be anything at all, Eli had said back. It was the wrong remark, which even as it was coming up his throat he could not stop. Simon had looked at him as if the window behind him had opened and blizzard air had washed his chest.

On the ferry a child wailed, a quick bleating sound like a goat on one of the islands. The father put the child over his shoulder and walked small, endless circles. The crying stopped at once and came back in waves, no logic, which was its logic. Eli thought of the baby’s face, a trophy of this boat this night like any other, going to a place it would not remember.

He dozed again, and in that unclear hour before someone announces coffee and shores he dreamed of being a boy underwater beside the dock, the varnished world above him with a surfacing that could be the moment of birth or the moment of death. He held his nose as if he were still eight and looked out along the board’s barnacled edge. He saw Simon’s calves flex in the effort to hang on to the ladder and not be filled with water. He kicked away from him in the dream and came up further away than he had any right to be. When he put his hand on the rung then it was stone.

The ferry slowed mid-channel. No. It wasn’t slowing. The tone of the engine changed and he thought every person who had ever been on a boat believed themselves the first to hear that change. He rose and looked out and saw nothing new. Just the insistent non-color of the water, night but not. Perhaps they had cut their speed to allow a barge to pass them without fear. He could not tell. He went down to his car, to make sure the bag with the sweater would be where it would be when he needed it. Downstairs, the lower levels had their own climate. A reek of hot rubber and fishline, someone’s dog whining in a truck bed, the minor clang of a wrench that had been left on something and vibrated as if it were trying to talk. He put his hand for a second flat against the ferry’s wall. He thought that if he believed hard enough he would feel the vibration in his teeth. The cars beside him could have been totems. He remembered then how, as a boy, he had crawled from one end of his father’s truck to who knows where under tarpaulins and had been, for seven minutes, a creature all thunders.

When dawn made light out of black, it surprised him. He had not thought he would like the color of the sky at five. The island was a band of dark in front of the lighter blue. There were firs, the Atlantic kind, wrong as that was. There were two towers that meant radio or cell phone and also a line of roofs and one tall crane arm. The ferry made its own weather and then ignored it. He watched gulls take the air behind it as if the speed belonged to them. The idea that he had asked for this passage—he had dialed, he had spoken, he had put on shoes—seemed the farthest from the truth in that moment. This had been arranged before he knew any of them. His grandfather’s hand on his father’s neck when the old man had taught him to tie the knot that would not untie on water. Their mother rubbing their scalps one night each month with egg and oil for reasons that had to do with another country. His own younger girlfriend telling him, You are a small force, Eli, you are not the whole river. All these things organizing themselves to this morning.

The horn gave two sounds you could feel through your feet. They were in the harbor. On the dock were pickup trucks and a man with a bicycle and a woman in a fake fur hat beating her hands against her arms as if she was telling them to wake up. He went back down, waited where they made him wait, and then folded himself into the slow line of cars and drove off the boat and into streets that were wider than he expected, clean as if washed in a recent storm. The building where he needed to go was not far. He had the address written again on a torn piece of paper although he had typed it into the map thing on his phone. He did not trust the idea of being told by something without breath where to turn to go and see a body that might be his brother.

At the corner a raven took a candy wrapper into the air and it fluttered like a fish. He parked and sat with the engine running and then stopped it and sat again. What a word: identify. To say This is and then a name that has already been pinned to someone who may no longer be the pronoun he knows. The parking lot was small and seemed used to vans. A man in hospital blue came out to smoke and put his fingers up to Eli as if to say, Hold; he would be there soon. Eli nodded without thinking he had been asked anything.

Inside the hospital the smell of antiseptic had in it also a sweet thing like winter grapes. He held himself quiet. The receptionist did not look up. When she did she had her eyebrows raised as if she had been in the middle of a conversation which he had broken and would he please tell her what he wanted so she could go on telling the person next in her head. He gave her his name and the name of his brother and she made no expression. She passed a paper to him to sign and then stood to lead him, as if the fact of the paper had created a path that she could now see. They went down a corridor that had heat at the top and cold at the bottom. The lights above them were squares. In each one there was a moth that had gotten in when it had been a light’s idea to be open.

—The doctor is at a birth, she said. —We’re a small place. He’ll come. I’ll let Sasha know you’re here.

Sasha was the nurse from the phone. Her voice was the same only deeper. She came toward him with two hands in her pockets and came to a stop a polite distance from his shoulder. He put his hand out as if it were his face and she took it in both her hands as if she were the older one and he was the boy being told We will do it as gently as we know how. He thought he could not bear that gentleness. But there they were.

—I’m Eli, he said.

—I know, she said. —We met in the ether. Her face had the shape of someone who had paid attention to a long time of other people.

—We keep the men where the ice machines were, she said, with the small violence of truth and the making of what needed to be actual enough that even the lying parts of grief could enter the room and sit on the bench.

—It may not be him, she said. —You are allowed to say it may not be. You are allowed to say nothing. We have fingerprints sometimes. We don’t this time. We have a height. We have a timeline. We have a wallet found near. Bodies do not follow rules, Mr. P. The tide does what it does and then the barnacles get involved.

Her vocabulary was of this place. He nodded again. He would nod until they told him to stop. She opened a door and let him in a room where there was a light that was not good for reading. The table in the center had corners that had not been rounded off by use and that seemed careless. There was a metal shelf and a box of gloves and a sink into which someone had poured something blue. There was a hum. He thought it was the building talking to itself.

We use the sheet, she said, and he nodded, and then she pulled it. She pulled it with the small drama of her competence and the sheet fell back and then there was the face of someone.

He had not known where his hands were until then. He discovered that his hands had crossed themselves, his right holding the wrist of his left. He unclasped them. The face was not exactly a face. How easy it is to break that central truth. Or rather it was a face but it had had the skin spoken to by salt and other small animals in the manner of languages he did not speak. There was no color. The mouth was a mouth. There was hair that had washed away up high, as if it had been combed by a mother who had never loved combs. He had not known what he hoped for. He had not made pictures in advance, or he had and had been ignorant of doing so. He waited for the body to tell him who he was.

—We can look for marks. He had told her about the ankle. She came to the bottom of the table and lifted the sheet there too and he saw a foot that had belonged to a boy at some time. He did not see the line he remembered. He did see a place where a line might once have been had he been eight and a knife careless but the skin now was more like something grown over. There is no word for the surface of a person when their warmth has gone. You can call it skin but that seems to deny the rest of the room.

—We have time, she said. It was all he needed. He put his hand flat on the metal edge of the table as if that would keep him upright in this weather. He stared into what he had to stare into. The face did something. No. He did something. The muscles around his eyes magnified what they saw until he was seeing too much and too little at the same time. He could think of nothing small to anchor himself to.

He tried the mouth. Simon’s tooth had chipped when he was ten, the front left, falling on the dock where the assignment had been to untie and tie and untie and then he had talked too much and laughed and his jaw had kissed the edge. It had never been repaired. Instead a corner of his smile looked like a map. Eli asked himself while looking at the teeth in front of him if this was that. Teeth told the truth, he reminded himself, it was what crime shows wanted him to know. Teeth were a last indictment. He stared. He could not decide. There was a notch, small, but it could have been anything.

Something in his chest relaxed and something tightened to punish it for doing so. He did not cry. Or if there were any water near his eyes it was dispersed by the heat of his staring. He put the back of one finger to the hand that lay by the table’s edge. If this was his brother he could at least put a finger to him. If this was not, he would be kind to this man by acting as if he were someone.

Sasha moved around him, not touching him. She made a note on a clipboard. It felt like a violation to hear the scratch of the pen. He willed her to make less noise and knew how silly that was. He had the impulse to make a joke. His tongue knew what joke it wanted to make—to ask whether they dusted the dead men for fingerprints as they did doorknobs and if she ever got them wrong. He bit his lip like a teen.

Don’t, he told his mouth.

—Do you need more time? she said.

—I need less, he said and was ashamed of that truth. She did not flinch. She stepped away so that he would not have her as someone to join his eyes with.

When he finally spoke, he said the thing that would make his next day of a different shape from this day.

—It could be him, he said. —And it could not be him. The eyes—He did not want to say the word eyes. He felt it in his throat like a coin. —The teeth—I don’t know.

—You don’t have to decide this way, she said. —You can provide DNA. Or you can ask for time and we can ask the RCMP to look further for something that belongs.

—A wallet, he said.

—A wallet, she said, walking to a cupboard and taking out a plastic bag with a Ziploc top. Inside was a worn leather square. There were two folded twenties and a card with the face of a woman who had been seventeen fifteen years ago because she had not gotten old in that picture with the black bar down the side. There was a library card from a town on the mainland. And there was his own name folded on a small piece of lined paper as if someone had cut it out of a spiral notebook and kept it near the thumb, for luck or the opposite of luck.

—Where did this come from? he asked.

—It was found thirty yards up the shore from where the fishing guide called in the body. It was in the grass. Could have been dropped by anyone. Could have been dropped by him. Could have been dropped by the person who put that piece of paper inside it four years ago. Could have been stolen and tossed. There are many ways a thing touches another thing, Mr. P.

He put his fingers on the plastic. The note had some of his handwriting in it. He had written his number and the words Call if. The if was like a door between them that would never open. He could not remember writing it. Or he could. He had been in the store that sold rope and coffee. Simon had been a day behind. He had been looking for him and had given his name to the woman who said he had passed through the day before. He had written the number and said to the woman—He is trouble and he is almost yours and he does not know which of those sentences is the truest. She had not smiled at that. He had asked her to give it if she saw him. He had not known how to ask her to keep it. This was their genius, how in a room with an open door they chose the window.

He breathed. He touched again the back of the hand and then Sasha covered it with the sheet as if for the second to last time. He had not lied or told the truth. He had done something else, and he did not have a word for it.

We will hold him, she said. —We will take samples. —You can go and see the place he was found if you need to place it in yourself.

—You talk as if this is all made of kindness, he said.

She looked at him, not caught. —This is made of functions, she said. —The kindness is the shape we use to carry them out.

Outside he leaned against the brick and thought of his breath as something too visible. The parking lot had three crows in it, walking rather than flying. A boy on a bicycle with wheels that clicked went by and did not look at him. The island at eight o’clock had awakened into a light that had no origin. He took the car and drove, without entirely thinking about it, toward the point. The town soon fell away and the road lost its paint. There were ditches with thin brown water and grasses that had been flattened against themselves by wind. He passed a truck filled with lengths of rebar and then a stand where in July a woman would sell strawberries the size of fists, but now it was a wood table under an old poncho. At a curve there was the sign someone had hammered up in memory of someone else. White painted letters on a board. He almost stopped to read it and then did not, because he could not take on another set of names.

Deadman Point. The name had been earned in some years back when a capsized boat had not held enough air. Or much earlier by someone who had nothing to do with boats at all, who had stepped too close to the bluff to get a look at the next coast and had found the air had less ground in it than he had expected. He parked in a place that was more of an absence of trees. The wind was doing its work. The shape of the water was push and then pull, short-tempered. He put both hands into his jacket and then took one hand out and zipped it.

He walked down a path that two people had made by walking it many times. The grasses pulled at his pant legs and left nothing on them. In a small opening he could see the flat of sand and the place where stones took over. He could see a gull apply itself to a broken crab. This would be the place where the body had been spotted, floated up like a thought that someone had tried to forget. He did not try to see a body there. He tried to see the absence of a body. He succeeded for ten seconds and then failed and then succeeded again.

He knelt and put his hand on a rock that was not in the mood to be warm. He thought of the boys they had been with their pockets full of tiny woods—the fragments of bark and the shells of snails that had been empty before the taking. He thought of their mother gathering peeled apples into her palm and carrying them across the kitchen to the pot as if she were a juggler. How old would Simon be now. This question seemed to make no sense. Nothing he could do would change the mathematics. He looked north, where the headland made a shoulder into the water and the water, persistent, made of that shoulder the thing a man makes of a word when he says it too often—it loses its skin and becomes its bones and then you can see through it and there is no longer any point.

A man came down the path from behind him without stepping on the noise and then when he was beside him and Eli did not turn he coughed as if to teach the stones how to be moved.

—You looking for something? the man said.

—Not exactly, he said.

—Good, said the man, and there was a slow smile that was not anything like meanness, but it was not kindness either. He had a dog with a red collar and a scar across his knuckles with the exactness of a thread his mother had not wanted to cut and could not keep from cutting. —This is where we find everything, he said. —Tires. Shoes. Once in a while a love letter that only knows one side of the salt. Last week a swan that thought it was a piece of paper, and we did not know what to tell it when it refused to be the other.

They walked not together and then in a rhythm that made it seem like together. The dog occasionally stopped and put his face into the wind as if analyzing soup. The man had a blue hat with the name of a construction company on it. He said nothing more. Eli wanted to ask him if he had seen anything, if he had been the one who called, if he had a story of a body that had come up with coins in its eyes and the right name in its pockets. He did not speak. He understood something about the code of beaches. If you arrived early enough you were allowed to make a prayer with your hands in your pockets and if anyone joined you they were holy by accident for the time it took a wave to move from the one rock to the other.

Back in town, an hour later, he sat in a café that had a wooden patient polished by the elbows of a hundred fishermen who had come in before work and by the laptops of a hundred students who had sat there as if the world were a series of classes. A woman sat with a baby asleep into the hollow of her collarbone and drank her tea with the other hand. On the wall there were black-and-white photographs of the town in years with wider hair. He ordered toast he did not want. He ate half and kept the other half wrapped as if his hunger were something he was saving for later.

He called their father.

—Ah, said the father, as if Eli had told him the weather would be the weather. He was in the habit now of putting an Ah at the beginning of statements he wanted to turn into questions. —Ah, and what says the doctor. And is he sure. And are they sure. And are you sure, and how many levels of sure are required these days.

Eli told him what he could. He could hear the window open where his father stood, because his father could no longer stand inside for long periods without feeling the ceiling push down. He could hear in the mostly silent spaces the move of the river three fields away. The father did not ask for marks or for details. He asked what clothes the man had on. He asked what date the tide had run high and how the wind had been that week and whether there had been the kind of sky that could take the skin off a man whether or not the water agreed.

—Your brother was bad at leaving, the father said, without reference to what they had been saying the second before. —He would go and then wait a week before anyone knew he had gone. He would pack his toothbrush and then take it out. He would go to the bus station and hold his knees and ask for more money. He would not look at me when he listened. He would come back. He would always come back to the back step. He would not come in. He would sit like a man on a horse he hated. He would ask for the barn key. He would sleep near the rakes. He would tell you he would leave in the morning and that would be the middle of the night and he would not notice.

—I remember, Eli said.

—Do you, said the old man. It made the word more honest than it often was.

—Do you want me to come, the father asked. It was such a late question and yet it entered the room like a crate they had always needed to carry a thing and now they had it and it was almost beautiful.

—No, Eli said. —By the time you came it would be another day and an old man on a bus in a jacket that had forgotten his shoulders and a woman telling you the price of pears and then you pointing to a bird that is not here. I will do it. I am doing it. I did it. Or, I did the part that there is.

—There is no part that is, the father said. He clicked his tongue like a horse. —There is only the ache and the if. He breathed out. The sound of his lips separating from the cigarette he had in his mouth and had not lit and would not light. —Bring him home, if he is him, he said. —Bring him to the place by the old tree. Don’t take him to the one near your mother. He liked her too much for us to make that a geography he shares. And if it is not him, turn your coat up. That strait can change its mind in April and a man can find his car turned sideways on the road by a wind that has not come with orders.

He spent the day in the manner one does when the main act takes place in a different timeline. He went back to the hospital and left a swab that the doctor told him would solve for this equation, as if math were what they were doing. He sat in the parking lot for thirty minutes with the seat belt on. He walked to the used bookstore where blue books sat together, and maroon books, and a book with a hand-drawn map of the island in the first pages. He ate the other half of the toast and felt it become part of him in an ugly way. He listened to a girl on the sidewalk telling her mother that the number of dogs in the town had tripled overnight and the mother said, as mothers say, that sounds like a number worth writing to the mayor about. He considered buying a pair of gloves. He put them back.

At dusk he returned to the ferry terminal. He had considered staying, waiting for the call, the result, and then realized that the waiting here and the waiting there would be the same waiting and he had plants in the kitchen sink that would turn sour if he left them and it would make a smell that would tell him later of these hours. He bought a ticket and sat on a sloped metal bench that sloped just enough to tell him that nothing here had been made to serve him. A woman with a laundry basket came to a stop four bench-lengths away and looked at him as if he were the teacher of a class she had not signed up for. He showed her his ordinary face. It cost him.

On board again he went to the outer deck and watched the cranes move. He saw the lights of the town as if someone had shaken the box and they had moved into new places. He saw a man lift both arms above his head as if to adjust his jacket and could not help feeling it as a sign. He laughed. He laughed in a way that caused no one any pain. He laughed at the idea of being that boy again under the dock who thought his breath would last forever.

—You again, the orange-vest woman said, appearing beside him as if she had been made out of the same darkness as the sea.

—Me again, he said.

—Did the island give you what you came for? she asked. It was a terrible tourist question and a beautiful human one. He thought about it. He nodded and then shook his head and then shrugged, which is the body’s way of telling the truth without grammar.

—My son, she said. She was going to tell him about her son again. She did not. She walked past him to the rail and spit expertly into the wind so that it did not come back on her.

—When I was twenty-one, he said, because he wanted to give her something. —We used to swim the narrows in the river back home at night. We tied our clothes in a dry sack and swam with one arm. It was stupid and not stupid. We were trying to be seen by a god we did not believe in. My brother would sing to call the fish. They liked him more than me. He had a way with creatures that couldn’t sign a thing. We would be halfway across and the current would reach for our ankles like an old uncle and then we would decide whether to fight him or let him embarrass us. He never embarrassed my brother. He took me by the shoulder every time. And now I cannot decide whether this thing I am doing, this going and looking and saying it isn’t and is, is a fight or a consent.

—Maybe it’s the breath before you go under, she said. —Maybe it’s the breath after you come up.

He loved her then. For the length of that sentence.

Somewhere past midnight the intercom coughed. He fell asleep with the empty coffee cup in his hands, and in his sleep he placed it on the floor without spilling. He had small dreams. He saw his brother’s ear. He saw a blue scarf dangling from the tree at the back of their house and the shadow of it across the bench. He saw their mother’s hands lifting the lid of a pot and steam come up and make a temporary weather which all of them had to recognize before moving again. In one of the small dreams Simon turned and said, We were the ocean once, and Eli said, Don’t be impossible, and Simon said, I mean the small shallow kind, and Eli said, That is called the bay, and Simon said, You are called the bay, and put his fist under Eli’s ribs and took the air out of him as a gift.

The ferry banged itself into the dock with a subtlety that suggested whoever drove it had been this way enough times to know how far a thing needs to be pushed to know it is held. The cars made their soft thunder. The foot passengers were asked to line up. He got up and joined. He felt tall and then he felt like no one. He walked down the ramp and the wind met him and then forgave him and then demanded he name it. He did not. He went to his car and sat and put the key in the ignition and took it out again. He took the letter from Simon from his shirt pocket. He read the second page without seeing it. He put it back. He thought of the man on the table and of the hand under the sheet and of the ankle that he could not be sure of and of the corner of the tooth that had been love or had not.

At home he would turn the heat up and then forget to turn it down and wake to a dry mouth, and in that dry mouth would be the sound of Sasha’s voice and the sound of the water against rocks and the sound of his father telling him to set the boats higher this year because the storms would come earlier, as if winter had grown a tooth you could see from town. He would wait for the call. He would look at the place by the back door where sometimes a stray cat slept and pretend it was the shape of a boy. He would put his hand to his face in the mirror and find there the smallest taste of salt, which could be anything.

He stepped out into the muted night of the town and walked to his car without hurry. The sky was the gray of a fish belly. In the distance, past the cranes and the roofs and the small squared lights, the strait lay back down out of view as if it had been standing all night to do its job and was now allowed to rest. He thought: If this is not him, then somewhere my brother is walking the length of another town carrying a lemon in his pocket because someone told him lemons could alter a person’s luck, and he is tasting a word with his tongue before he says it, and when he puts the lemon down it rolls to the edge of the table and he catches it with his left hand, the one with the nick at the ear. And if it is him, then the river above the farm will go past the place we used to sit and tell secrets we had borrowed from other people’s lives and the grass there will lean in one direction because that week the wind has chosen a side, and I will go and stand beside our father and not say the words we both want to be true, and then, later, we will throw a rock into the quiet part of the water and watch the rings take their time and disappear, as if their work, too, is to cross a distance and then not be seen.
