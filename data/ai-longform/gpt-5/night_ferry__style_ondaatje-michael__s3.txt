The ferry waited in the basin like a low building, lights in rows as if each porthole were an eye reluctant to close. He walked out along the causeway toward its open mouth, the gangway ridged with slats to catch the soles of boots, and the big ropes like sleeping snakes along the concrete, smelling of salt and old kelp. On the far side, in the dark water beyond the breakwater, the strait showed itself only in movements and hiss, in that low unease that northern water has even on a quiet night. He had brought only a small bag and the brown envelope with the letter in it, his winter coat carried over his arm as if he didn’t trust its promise. In the letter there was a list in a flat, businesslike hand: approximate height, weight, hair a certain colour that could be many colours when wet and under that light. Found near the stanchions of a fish plant that had been closed for a decade. Wallet with no identification. A vagrant or a fisherman or someone in between. We believe it may be your brother, the woman from the coroner’s office had written. We would be grateful if you could come. She seemed to know to use the language of belief rather than knowledge.

It was almost full dark. The trucks, already aboard, sat idle with their cabins lit in small squares like little theatres, cups on dashes, photos clipped to the visors. A dog looked at him from one with calm eyes, suspended in its own moving room. He showed his ticket to a deckhand with hands too bare for the cold, and when the man smiled a tooth flashed gold and then went back to a quiet, well-tended colour. He liked this early, solitary part of the embarkation, the act of leaving the land foot by foot, the thought that the world on the other side belonged to a morning he had not yet earned. He had not ridden this ferry for years. Once, when they were boys, their father had taken them up on a similar vessel—smaller, older, dirty with rope and tar—so he could look for a light in a lighthouse, some repair he had promised to do for a friend who kept the light. That was a different strait. Every strait has its own grammar, his father had said. The tongues that enter it, and the tongues it takes.

The seating area smelled faintly of hot sugar—the residue from the candy-stick machine near the door—and of coffee burned and remade. He took a place by a window and pulled his coat on and settled his bag between his feet. In front of him, a laminated photograph of a shorebird was affixed to the wall with its Latin name and a note about migratory patterns, an arrow drawn from Siberia like a thread being drawn taut. The ferry had a habit of such notices. They were a kind of reassurance that all this water led somewhere. He read the card. The bird returned unerringly to a particular marsh in an inlet he had visited once, years ago, after a night of driving for no reason other than the fact that movement seemed, then, to justify itself. He had stood and watched the birds allocate themselves, each with a patch of sunlight on the mud as if it had purchased a ticket.

A man two rows away read a book by holding it low, on his thighs, his thumbs synchronous on the pages like the two sides of a shore. He wore a knitted cap pulled down and every few minutes lifted it to rub the back of his head with the frown of someone who did not feel the shape of his own skull accurately. Across the aisle a girl and her grandmother ate taffy, their fingers making unspoken agreements as they pulled the silver wrappers apart and let them fall into the grandmother’s reticule. Teenagers with jackets too light for this air moved back and forth in a current that seemed to be about showing they were not on a schedule. He had come aboard with the early travellers and would see all their small histories, he thought, and then they would go on their own roads, and he could not take any of them with him. He reached for his pocket and touched the photograph of his brother, taken fifteen years and some summers ago in a parking lot behind a bar, in which his brother stands with the shoulders open of a boxer between rounds and a smile that almost manages to be gentler than it wants to be. His name was Lionel, though most called him Lio, even when they were small. Their mother had liked how names could be softened with a vowel, their father had argued for names that would stand in a book and be read out loud with dignity. In the photograph there is a post at his brother’s back with a small sign warning about parking beyond the white line. He had looked at the sign every time, as if his brother’s rule-breaking existed inside the frame and he could enter the photograph and say one thing that would tilt the next ten years. He carried the photograph like a method, not a memory.

The first shudder of movement made the whole lower deck sound like the last notes of an organ concert. On the television overhead the news was showing a winter storm somewhere else—another country’s disaster—and a weatherman with too handsome a face to be sincere gestured at why it would spare them this time. He listened for the change in pitch, that parting-of-water sound that his body remembered from crossings he could not date. His father had been like that, listening for the walls of sound that meant work, lifting his face slightly into the wind before he took up a tool. After his father lost the lighthouse job—automation had come like a neutral god—and then lost, too, the small contract work at the cannery and then the job with the bridge crew whose pylons his father could have named in his sleep— Anchors, he would say, every part an anchor—he got quiet in a new way and took the boys fishing off the pier on mornings when they should have been at school. The boys learned the names of fish as if they were swallows one wished onto the tongue: flounder, dogfish, black cod, pie plate, skate. They could say diatom and copepod. They could tell the difference, in the dark, between the engine sound of a seiner and a tourist boat, the way one laboured and one lied. What school could teach this? Their aunt would scold and then show up with bread and a deck of cards in which a queen was missing and call the game anyway. Lio learned quickly to tie a knot a certain way, a way that could unfurl in a single tug, his fingers faster than their father’s despite that father’s history with ropes and cables, his patience and patience and patience.

When their mother left she did it in a silent, practised way, like the removal of a sail from its grommets, a quickness and absence of drama such that you could stand next to the mast looking at the horizon and only when you turned back feel your hand bat the bare air and understand what had been lifted. She left a box of shirts ironed on the kitchen table, and there was a note for the boys that simply asked them to look after their father, and a note for their father in another envelope, the contents of which became a kind of geography they learned only by inference. The town did not forgive her and Lio did, and their father never said he had and so those were the men around whom the boys grew their men. The nights after that were a little less strict. They ate stew late and slept odd hours. They went, on sleepless nights, to the warehouses at the water’s edge and listened to the cold asleep inside. Lio took to staying out. Their father took to looking for his screwdriver and then putting it down vertical as a candle, on the windowsill, as if to believe that all tools belonged to the same dignified family and were here to be admired as well as used. There was a year when the three of them went to a beach nearly every Sunday morning in May, just as the hills’ hair grew out again and the crows learned to hop sideways. They went to watch the logs that had drifted free from booms come in onto the sand like felled men, and they would pick through for wood to carry home. It was nothing like stealing, their father would insist. Once, a half-barrel came in, charred on one side, a long story of fire in its grain. While his father turned it and spoke about how to rejoin the staves, Lio waded out without shirt or sense and pushed a wet log as if he could make it turn by insisting, pointing his body like a prow, standing with his arms squared and looking back only to grin, as if the world were something into which he could be inserted with force. He was like that with everything for a time. He put his hands into the middle of scenes and made demands. He fell from a pier and opened his shin across corrugated oyster shells, and when the bleeding would not stop their father tore a white shirt and made a tourniquet and carried Lio to the car. The scar was like a bay, a long, shallow thing, with hinge scars where the stitches held. Sometimes to be a brother is to be a man with a map you do not fully own.

The ferry swung into the channel. Docking went away like a thought you had almost had then could not hold. There was a man in an orange vest standing on the windward side of the deck with a radio. The radio’s long antenna seemed to communicate with the wind as much as with any other radio. He thought to go outside and stand in that wind but something in him was tied to his seat, as if the ferry had ropes inside too and his had been tightened. He took out the letter and read it again. A county name he had never had cause to say out loud. Date and time of discovery. He pictured the body the way you should not, as if it had a script and was playing it out, as if his brother had met it at a rehearsal and decided to step in for the man playing the part. He had read once that a drowned man could look more like himself after a day or two, that the water and the body came to an agreement for a brief time before the other changes began and the man became someone else. He did not know if this were a cruelty of a story or a specific kindness. He put the letter away and looked up. There was a marine map mounted under glass on the wall with depths sounded by a machine long gone quiet. Names like sentences: The Teeth, Old Woman’s Ribs, Barrel Ledge, Deadman Bay, Bicycle Point. He moved his finger across the glass, feeling nothing but the thick shield. A boy came and put his hand next to his and said, This is where we are, and his mother told him to leave the man alone. He did not turn and say not to worry. He let the idea of his own silence hang for a second.

Around midnight he walked to the cafeteria for coffee and bought a sandwich that was more architecture than food, cut precisely in a diagonal as if to give deference to the idea of halves. The boy at the till took his money without looking up, the kindness of young people to the solitude of older men. Along the far window a man drew with a small blunt pencil on a napkin, speaking, it seemed, to the napkin, a whisper of route or weather. The truckers had gathered at the long table nearest the coffee pots and spoke of loads and the price of gas and the weather on the prairie as if they were continuing a conversation they had started in their twenties and would as long as their bodies held to chairs. The woman behind the counter wore a name tag in which her name had been erased and rewritten so many times the letters were a kind of grey fog. She leaned, without arrogance, on the counter and asked him where he was going. North, he said, as if this were a location only. She smiled. I figured that, she said. I mean, along this route are you heading up the coast or inland. The coast, he said. A small town, he named it. She considered this with a face he could not translate. My sister married a man from there, she said. He made boats out of wood. The kind that do what you ask, slowly. How are they? he asked. She shrugged a little. Some men run with boats because they can’t yet run with a woman. He couldn’t tell if there was a joke in that or a sadness. He said thank you as if she had passed him a map.

He took his cup to a place by another window and stared into the black that returned his own pale eyes like the eyes you see at the bottom of a pot. He listened for what boatmen had told him you could listen to: whales in the night, the sudden urging of heavy backs, the collapse of lungs and the exhalation like a god laughing hard. He had believed such things before and been given them. Under a not entirely unblack sky in August, he had once stood with a woman he loved and watched algae light as her hand went through the water, a neon trail off each finger, as if gravity and God were conspiring to make certain we are understood. He had held the small lamp while that woman stitched a cut in his palm using thread that had held his mother’s skirt straight once, and when he bled he did not worry. He was cruel then. He asked for the wrong things kindly, and when she left he only half blamed her for not wanting to write him notes on the kitchen table anymore. But there are departures that await further departure, older ripple requiring new ripple.

He thought of his brother, not the boy with the stitched shin but the teenager who would come over the fence like a cat and through the house like wind. They stole a canoe once and paddled it up the creek at three in the afternoon and felt like kings for five minutes, but someone had seen and called someone else and then called their father who drove down to the bridge with his face the colour of a ten-dollar bill, and stood in his work boots with his arms folded and let them come to him. In the car, the canoe tied to the roof now with borrowed straps, he said to them, slow, never steal from a man who knows what is missing. The boys did not laugh at the strange logic of it. Later, Lio found the man whose canoe it had been and worked two weekends hauling soaked gillnets from the bay, and came home with his fingers cut and two lobes of his ear infected, and asked to sleep on his stomach like a boy again. Years later he could still see that boy on a Sunday afternoon when the wind broke through the trees and reminded everyone where the water was. Lio at the end of the dock, standing and then kneeling and then lying down to see something under the surface. The men on longshore would shout at him, that he’d fall and wreck his face on a piling or a ladder rung slick with accident, and when he looked up his hair on his forehead was like the bright curve of a bird’s wing, a habitual invitation to risk. He had loved that and resented it and learned how to step aside to let it go by. Loving an older brother is a discipline in measured reach.

He put his forehead to the cold glass and let his exhalation flood a square into which he drew something thoughtless—a circle, then another. He watched them fade. He did not sleep. He tried to imagine walking along the street of that town on the other side, where the fish plant had once thrown ice onto the pavement and made it last three days longer than ice should have, where the gulls stood like executors on the roofline and looked for argument. He pictured the metal door of the coroner’s office. Hinge. The gurney would make its own comment on entering. He had come to think of such rooms as cleanly unhappy places. He had been in two. Once, for a boy he had barely known who fell on rocks drunk and unafraid. Once, for a friend from work whose name he felt, at first, he could not pronounce if he saw it printed under glass. Gowns, trolleys, a careful housekeeping of the dead. He would be asked to confirm without making it seem he could not confirm. He wondered if he would lie. He wondered which lie would be more honest.

He thought of their father on the day he lost his place at the lighthouse. The Service men arrived in a car that smelled like paper, and one of them shook his father’s hand and the other one did not, and they made conversation that was grateful without gratitude. At the end their father signed a paper and then went out alone and took down an old rope and burned it in a barrel by the shed. Why burn a rope? Lio had asked. Because I can’t put fire to the men, his father said. It was said without harm. It was the first time their father had admitted the existence of another impulse. Later, he made them hot chocolate twice as rich as their mother would have allowed and placed his hand, warm, on the back of each boy’s neck, as if to verify they were here. In the night, when the light fell across their curtained window not from their own tower but from the new one out in the bay, he lay awake and named the parts of boats. He named cleat and boom and bite, transom and topmast and boomkin, bobstay, crosstrees, parrels and parrel trucks, he named gudgeons and pintles and cap rails and he said their names low until the boys were asleep inside the language of their country.

Near one, he went down to the washroom and washed his face with cold water in a sink that had seen the hands of men from everywhere this line touched. He looked at himself in that curved mirror and saw the tired mouth of someone who had failed at insisting and then got used to the relief. The ferry rolled and the thin chain that kept the paper towel machine from walking itself off the wall clinked like a mindless animal under a dark thought. He thought of the day his brother had left. There had been no fight. There had been nothing as declarative as a slammed door. He had come upon an empty drawer and a space in the wardrobe. He had wanted to laugh at the symmetry of it, to stand in front of the spare place and examine it as a sculptor examines negative space, and then to be allowed to be sick into a sink. Their father had not moved. He had sat at the table with his hands on his thighs and said, when asked, a boy does this. He could not tell if he meant all boys or only this one. There was a postcard six weeks later from the north. There was a photograph of a bay like an unwashed pan and some buildings folded against a hill and a small black dog lying in sunlight in a way that meant it was not summer. There were three more notes in a man’s slanted script. There was a number with not enough digits that could be called in the late afternoon when a particular tide permitted some satellite to cross the right place. And then there was the matter of the sighting like a rumour: someone had seen him in a bar in Prince Rupert, he was thinner, he had a bottle in hand and a way of talking like someone who coughs through the end of a story. He got a message once with just a first name he did not expect: Lio’s voice on his machine saying, Asher? You there? Asher though no one called him that any longer except one teacher and the bank. He was not there. He saved it for a year. He knew people whose lives were built of such saved notes.

A man came into the washroom then and stood next to him and also washed his face and they did not look at one another. The man said, finally, the best sleep is on this thing when it’s almost empty. You can sleep with your mouth open on the carpet and no one will step on you. He might have been a good-grandfather to someone or someone’s first unpaid landlord. He said, they will sound the horn at two off the rocks, because they always are uncertain there. Then he produced a handkerchief in a military way and pressed it to his bald forehead and touched his temples as if calibrating an instrument finally used properly. He left, not expecting anything in return. This, too, is a version of kindness.

He went back past the rows. The girl and her grandmother now slept with their heads at angles against one another so that they made a shape like a mountain range. The man with the napkin was asleep with his hand open and the rough drawing of a something creased in his palm as if his palm had its own brain and had learned the line by line. He took his coat collar up when he went out onto the observation deck for five minutes. It was not a night for it, the wind asserting itself, but the dark needed to be seen close. The cold felt, the longer one stood, like someone with reason to complain but willing to wait. He stood near the starboard rail and looked out into something that did not want to be a picture. Somewhere a bell clanged three times and did not clang again. A man farther along smoked a cigarette that alternated between being a coal and a meteor. He wanted to speak to him but not about anything either of them could afford. He stayed until the part of his face that had been wounded years ago began to ache again with a weather memory, and when he went in and shut the fire door behind him, the return of warm air on that place was a small forgiveness.

He slept, at an angle, for an hour and woke with his mouth dry and the awareness that waking on ferries demanded a rehearsal of how you had gotten here. He got up and walked, and when he walked the ferry answered with small vertical arguments beneath his feet. Another map showed whales in blue silhouette and arrows showing how they migrated, a field of purposeful fish in a story of desire and large sacrifice. He had once worked in a library with floors whose boards tolerated the books in a particular way. On certain winter mornings when the heat delayed itself the reader in the far carrel would cough and it would come to him along the grain and then go through the stacks like someone rolling papers into a tube. He had run from work to the shore where he could climb over an old fence and stand where someone had, for a time, kept drying cod and wool hanging from the same beam. He had thought of following his brother north, to a cannery or to boats, the good honesty of hands that end the day a darker colour than they were in the morning. His father had said, there is more than one way to contribute to this line, and had waved his hand over his palm as if his palm itself might teach him, like a lens.

They had been men who spoke while doing something else. They did not sit at tables and speak straight at one another unless someone was dead. He knew men whose fathers had been priests or engineers or charlatans or gentle men with the patience to let their lives be misinterpreted. He had had a father who could make a door, who could shave a piece of oak with a plane until it assured you it belonged there, who could look at a pier from across the bay and know what part of it would go first when the weather came from the north. He wondered if his brother had known this and known that he could never grow into it. Lio had once swum from island to island, a distance of three hundred yards, to retrieve a piece of wood he had thrown for a laugh, and when he came in, cheeks like apples, he said, as if blessing himself, sometimes you have to make an example for an audience of one. In that same week he had been in a fight behind the school and had pulled someone’s hair and then been astonished by the blood on his own lip, as if his own body’s liquid were news. He had been, at certain times, someone who came to need to receive. Then he was gone and he wrote from the north: a job filleting Halibut for six weeks I’d sign on again if the room didn’t smell like we lived inside a fish. Then: there is a man named Eban whose whole left hand is made of cloth. Then nothing for a year. Then a picture of a boy on a bicycle with a coat too big for him, and a sentence on the back that could be read as I thought of you at this corner or as carry on.

He held the rail of the moving ship with a certain taxfulness. There were two women at a table farther along with needlework spread, mending something for someone who had insisted he did not need mending. They spoke that quiet language in which nothing too much is confessed. He thought of the letter in his envelope. He thought of opening a door in the morning that was made to be closed against things. He would walk in, someone would say his name, and he would have to say Yes as if this were not an act of imagining. He sat back down and closed his eyes and heard in his head the horn the old ferries used to sound when they rounded a particular spit in the river. He slept again.

He had been following a small, unhairy dog along a path through spruce and the path was lit as if the sky had fallen low but spared that narrow part and he could hear voices as one hears water behind rock, some older men arguing about their fathers’ shoes and there was a young voice saying do not go so far, come back you idiot, and he turned and in turning there was Lio, his heel pressed into his shin to get his attention, and then everything opened and he was in the water with his clothes on and he could taste the rope on his tongue and his father, calm, was in the boat in his work pants and they were laughing at the way the rope refused him, and then the dog was licking his lips and this felt like a generous mistake and it was his own mouth returning to dryness that ended the dream.

Dawn did not break at once but came on like grey believing itself into place. There were figures on deck who had learned to stand inside their coats. The low land showed itself like a thought of sobriety in someone who had not, until recently, practiced it. He stood with his bag in hand and his envelope under his arm and watched cruising birds step on columns of air. The ferry slowed. It had this habit of making you think all this had been practised a thousand times by the water and the metal and that what people performed was of less importance. He saw the spire of a church. A radio tower out of date. A cluster of old houses like men in heavy shirts waiting to be paid. He saw the long metal building with its paint scoured off one side by a faithless wind. He wondered how many people he could name on the other side. A woman who sold second-hand wool sweaters and remembered everything anyone had ever asked her for. The man who played the accordion outside the post office and would stop for anyone’s child. His brother, if he was. He made the last part of the journey standing. He did not go down to the car deck early and stand in his place like a man fearful of his own few feet of earth. He allowed his place to be replaced by the next person who wanted it. He held the handrail as if it had belonged to someone he had long known who had never refused him anything.

The bump and then the slow draw to stillness, that breathing out that a good docking requires. They made a noise that was not a noise but a dismissal of noise. The hinge-hum of sets of lights going off in sequence down the long corridor. Doors opened. The sharp attention of the dock crept aboard, a smell of soap and rope and old fish that had been cleaned well yesterday but still wanted to make its case. He let the first impatient half go down and out. He moved with the second.

The harbour road was wet not from rain but from fog landing and lifting and landing again. He walked up the slope to the big low building that was not a hospital and not a house. In front a man hosed off the deck boards the way men do, the stream of water making a bright path then being absorbed by the wood, the man’s gaze just off the place where he was aiming. Inside the door the air held itself. On the wall a framed picture of a chartered boat in a previous life, shining and painted and lying, it seemed, in its element like a thing someone made up. A woman at a desk raised her head and he put his name in front of both of them. She nodded and then stood and came around and said, in a voice that had contained other voices this morning, already, he is here and we will take our time. She led him past a room where men in shirts and ties drank tea with the solemnity of witnesses. She stopped by a door with a window with a blind pulled down and then raised a hand and opened the door. He followed her into a room designed to intimidate nothing.

It was colder without being cold. On one wall a chart of human teeth labelled with numbers that implied a system more precise than any he had encountered outside hymns. A black bag on a wheeled table. A silver gurney. The white sheet came up to the collarbone. He did not know he would be prepared for this, or that his breath would leave him then rejoin him with the professionalism of someone arriving at a station. The hair was too much. The hair told a different story than the letter. The mouth was partly open as if someone had interrupted. The skin an odd both-colour: someone alive under someone else’s film. The woman spoke in a way that made what she said footbridges. We found him in the nets. There was a tangle. There were two of them and this one we think came in earlier. She did not say the other was in a different room or had not yet been brought here. She kept her hand near his arm but not on it, a kindness learned by any number of men and women in this line of work who had been told not to touch and then had watched someone fall. He stepped closer. He had learned years ago what parts of a person anchored them most reliably to themselves. A scar that does not know it is a scar. A particular way a tooth concedes to crookedness. The way the ear, that strange tender shell, sits back a little from the head as if it had to keep listening to avoid being too close. His brother had a nick in his left ear where he had caught a stud in a jacket and torn it small, and though he said he would pick at it until it made a proper meaning out of him, he forgot, and then, after awhile, the body made the repair it could. He leaned and looked. He looked at the ear, left and then right. He thought he saw the nick and then did not. It is a small thing to undo a person if you do not move your eyes properly. The mouth. Lio had once had a small square scar under his bottom lip where his tooth had cut through when they were in a fight and he had fallen on the stove. He had touched that scar with his own thumb like a man pressing a button on a machine that was going under. He could not see it here. A scar on the shin, he thought suddenly, and then looked down at the still sheet and could not bear to ask the woman to—

But he knew he would have to. He heard himself say it out loud and the voice was not strange. There is a scar on his shin, he said. The left one. Oyster beds. The woman nodded. We can look, she said. She took the edge of the sheet at one corner as if it were a family photograph and folded it back in the right way, her hands remembering manners. There. Pale shin. The patch is a little like a country you do not know to be drawn to until you have been there. He leaned again, as if leaning could improve the time that had passed between now and then. He saw what could have been. The skin there had changed again perhaps with age. He had not seen his brother in skin in seven years. He could not trust his own eyes. He wished for three of him. One to believe hard, one to refuse, one to pull the other two to the table and make an agreement. The woman, not unkindly, said, take your time, and someone else, a man who had been ghosting the doorway with the back of his wrist against the frame, stepped away.

He thought of Lio laughing, a chair under him sliding, two fingers on the table keeping it all from tipping. He thought of the bruise that had once covered three-quarters of his brother’s back and looked like a map of a printing settlement. He thought of his brother silent in a hospital corner after their father died, crying without anyone else seeing because the crying had no face to it. He thought of their mother’s small slanted letters like men hurrying in a new city, and of their father’s heavy careful letters like footbridges over a creek you respected. He thought of a kitchen in the night with the stove’s light a small square on the floor and a wide-shouldered boy sitting cross-legged in that square as if he were waiting to be called on by no one. He thought of a man standing at a bar in a northern town with his back straight as a wall, unexplanatory. He thought of a thing he did not wish to think of: that some men, properly, are not identified. Not by the state, or by brothers, or by lovers. That they belong to the element that took them or let them go to someone else. He did not want to believe this. But belief is a complicated ship to steer in bad water.

The woman said, quietly, the family sometimes has some small thing, a mark. A tattoo. A ring that leaned. He had nothing to offer. The photograph in his pocket was the inside of an hour. His brother had never had a tattoo he would share with the house. He wore a ring for one winter, the winter after their father’s rope-burning, a ring someone had given him that he wore on his thumb until it bruised him, and then he wore it on his ring finger and then lost it, or said he had, and if someone had found it later at the bottom of a knapsack his story of the ring was not incorrect. He shook his head. The woman did not move. He became aware of the fact that the man on the table had a long, fragile hand. His brother had had hands like rope you did not save, fast and thinning if you didn’t keep them coiled. He looked again at the ear. He saw the notch now, or rather, he saw the place where one could say there had been one, and this was not evidence. He felt his own breath as a judge on the back of his neck. He thought of his father once saying, in his sleep it seemed, keep the line, keep some line, and he had not known what he meant and went back to sleep with a feeling like a finger sealing his lips into a boy’s mouth.

He could say Yes and it would be done. He could hold a funeral and men would put their hands in his hands and say things which, said properly, are true enough regardless of detail. Or he could say No and live with the particular after-echo of No which is unlike any other after-echo. He could say I don’t know, and then the man on the table would go to a shelf with a number and if someone needed him someday, in the records or in a loss that had not yet been named, he would be there like a light someone had forgotten to turn off. The woman said, we can take prints. He nodded as if asked if he wanted coffee. He stood then with his hands in his coat pockets like a man on a dock looking at something he would have to lift with his own shoulders. He heard, outside, a gull making a noise like a small engine that will not catch. He could not identify another man’s brother and say that in a town where there were not so many brothers one could afford to get their stories wrong. He could not be a story that fixed itself too well because it untied other things.

He thought of the first day of first grade when he had been given a tag with his name on it and remember having wanted to keep it forever, to approach the world with that single factual flourish. He thought of stepping down from the ferry at night on an island where there had been only one taxi, and the driver had looked at him as if he had gotten too old while waiting and had given him the exact right number of opinions about the wind. He thought of being kind. He thought of being accurate. The two were not, today, the same ship. He said, finally, in the small, even voice he had learned from men who lifted lumber and did not like being spoken at: I can’t say for certain. I can’t say it is him.

The woman nodded. It was not relief in her face and not disappointment. It was the governance of men and women who know that certainty is a strange thought at the best of times and a poor bed partner in the cold. We will take prints, she said, and we will see if he has any record. He nodded. He wanted to put his hand on the man’s forehead for a second the way he had done with his father in the room where his father had lain that last time in a room that smelled of a chemical common sense and then had changed into a room that smelled of rope and coffee because there were so many men from the docks in it. He did not put his hand down. The woman lifted the sheet and did what she had to do and then said, would you like to sit for a moment, and he said no, thank you, and then stood not moving for a minute longer. He did not want to leave the room in case leaving the room made this man abandon the last person who had looked down at his face. He turned, finally, and they walked back down the corridor to her desk where a calendar had a picture of mountains in another country and an appointment book had been written in for the next three weeks and then nothing. She asked for his address, and he gave it, writing it slow because his hands would not do it quick. He gave his number. He hesitated and then wrote the other number, the one with not enough digits, because writing it had a private logic, and then drew a line through it and apologized and she said nothing. He folded his coat over his arm in the way he had carried it onto the ferry, like fabric he did not trust yet.

He stepped into the daylight. It was a kind of grey that thins a man. The building that had been a fish plant was quiet among its old belts and hung hoses and benches. A man swept the concrete as if he were paid by the broom. There were three gulls on the roof who looked as if they had been arguing about what ships did wrong. He stood and watched a boy on a bicycle go past with his coat flapping, as if his body wanted to be taller. Across the way, a woman locked the door of the post office with two turns of a key and then looked up at the sky and then did not change her step at all. He found himself walking toward the shore and then stepping up onto a footing and standing where a man would stand if he wanted to see nothing else. The water was what it was—insistent and ignoring—and in it a few small whitecaps leaned like men on the second drink. He thought of the first time he had taken a ferry alone, a boy not wanting to look like a boy wanting to look like a man. He thought of his brother on the other side of a long summer, coming up the dock with a cardboard suitcase, walking hatless in the rain because his hair had been cut to a belief and he did not wish it wrecked. Lio would straighten his jacket then with the two-small motions of a man who never expected someone to do it for him, and his face would be bright like someone with information he was keeping for the right moment, and he would fool some people and not fool others and then win them all back with a laugh just a little too loud and a hand on someone’s shoulder. It was wrong to want to see that on this winter morning, and right. He remembered what he had said once to a woman beside a field we had no right to be walking through: I am trying to be the sort of man who does not make the bad versions of himself into his interesting stories. She had laughed and kissed the back of his hand. It was a country they had visited and then left.

He went back, slowly, toward where the taxis waited. There were three. The driver of the first car had his cap down over his eyes and did not move until he was at the door and then his hand went to the handle and the door came open somehow. He told the man the name of a place where there was a room with a window. On the ride the radio spoke softly of other men’s weather. They drove past the net pens and the half-painted ships lifted sidewise in their cradles and a yard with thirteen crab traps stacked so that they formed a city for small saints. He asked the driver if the ferry was running on time tonight, and the driver said, they will tell you yes and then tell you no. It depends on whether the men play cards on the other side and whether the captain had an argument at dinner. It depends on the tide, and sometimes the tide is as polite as a priest and you still can’t do a thing with it. He looked at the driver. The driver did not seem the sort of person to be romantic about water. The driver added, our dead are sometimes better travellers than we are.

He left his bag in a room with a bed and a thin spread that had outlived a number of small, untidy romances. He went out again to walk, because he could not trust himself to sit. He stood near the cannery and watched a small tug move a barge as if introducing a friend who did not wish to be introduced. He thought of the word strait as if it were a hung word, and thought of narrowings that lead to openings and of the north as a condition as much as a direction. A boy with a skateboard went by and hit a stone and fell and got up without even putting his hand to the blood on his knee. The boy’s small brother cheered him in a private voice and he knocked the cheer off with a gentle, joking hand. This, too, is a work of men that continues.

When he made his way back to the office, because he could not quite go away from it after all, another man sat in the chair under the calendar with that look men wear when waiting for a word about a person they have hired to be their father for a recent and nearly beautiful piece of their own history. He did not go in. He stood under the eave and watched the rain that now arrived without having been announced, a light rain that does not make a theatre of itself. The woman came out with her coat closed at the neck and said to him, in a voice quiet as the edge of a dress, we will call. He nodded. He thanked her as if she had handed him a small thing and he did not wish to be ungrateful about its smallness. He found himself at the dock at the time when the ferry sounds itself twice because nothing had gone wrong, and he stood among men with sacks and women with plastic bags and boxes in which were cakes and tennis shoes and a radio with its back open like an argument. He stood and thought, will I say anything of this to anyone. He thought of the men in the cafeteria on the ferry leaning on paper cups and nodding slowly when he said a thing that could be mistaken for confidence. He thought of his father’s rope burning again, the smell of old salt coming off the hemp like the breath of a man who had been careful for too long. He thought, if it is him and I do not say so, he will remain in the world in a particular way; and if it is not him and I say yes, I will kill something that had not yet died. He did not wish to be a man who performed that second act and then forgave himself. He wanted to be a man who carried his decisions as if they were good wood needed in winter. He wanted, for once, to say to himself, this is what the water allows, and to stand there.

When the ferry opened its mouth again, ghosts came out first, men with their mouths still smelling of sleep and coffee and whatever else they had said to women at a window before the dark, and he stepped into that mouth feeling, briefly, the line of the deck come up under his feet like the line of a name being written. He took his place by the window. He set his bag down and rested the envelope on his lap and let his palm find the seam of the paper. He thought then of a small thing that had happened when they were twelve and fourteen: their aunt teaching them to iron their own shirts. She had set the iron on the board with a ceremony and told them that they had inherited not love but the capacity for loving and this had to be cleaned every day in a small way, and she had told them that if you ironed a shirt with care then you could walk into any room in the world and begin necessary work. He wished to admit how much this had saved him. He wished to admit how little, at certain hours, anything in him wished to be saved. He wished to say his brother’s name out loud and then see what happened in the compartment of air where the body had been. He wished to give up, for once, on the grammar that had kept him safe.

The ferry shook itself like an animal made to be wet and unafraid, and the town lay behind him, a long light, a rearrangement of rooms. He watched the low houses turn minute, he watched the few men on the breakwater make small cautions with their hands, and then watched the water take the ship and the ship make its argument back and the wind do what it wished with both of them. He thought then, with a likely relief he did not deserve, of Lio walking along the gunwale of a boat somewhere twenty years ago, balancing like a small god, holding his arms slightly out and calling to no one or to everyone, and of how the sun was at his back and made a pale figure in front of him on the knee of the water, and of how there are some moments a brother belongs to you. And then the ferry took the deeper part and went out where names are sparse and the room around him filled gently with the wash of the others’ sleep and eyes and the unimaginable and continuous presence of the world, and he put his hand over the letter and learned briefly to let it not say anything.
