He watched the ferry push itself away from the quay as if shrugging off a theory. Ropes went slack and then were whipped aboard, black water troughed and rose. On the pier the few people seeing off hunched into their hoods. From a hatch below him came the slow cough of the engines gathering whatever part of the sea they could grip and pull against. The deck trembled and shivered. The terminal building retreated, lights becoming their own smeared versions in the stained glass of the harbor, and then the lights were just a row at his back and the night opened.

He had a scarf pulled up so high that he could taste the wool, some soap from the drawer it had been folded in. He kept his hands in the pockets of his jacket. A circle of older boys—though he was too far from them to hear—laughed at something, young men with their hair slicked under caps. Two truck drivers stood easy with their elbows on the rail, the brilliant orange of their high-vis waistcoats dyed in the sodium lights. A woman up the windline lit a cigarette and cupped it with both hands like an offering. He could smell the lift of diesel and old seaweed, cigarette and hot metal; all of it braided in and out of the breath that came past his scarf in the harsh wet cold.

He did not want to go inside yet. He wanted the fact of leaving to be larger than everything else for a while. The ship moved past the last stacks of lobster pots on the quayside, the neatness of a town settled behind it. Then the outer wall was abeam, a concrete arm raised against the ocean, and the ship slid through its narrow gap. Outside the wall the sea gathered, got height and body, flexed itself. The bow met it and unsaid some part of it into white churn and the rest rose to meet them anyway. In between the ferry and the first texture of the open, there was the short strip of water where the harbor’s kindness ran out. He had the animal certainty that if he watched the change over that strip he would understand something. And then it was simply black, stippled here and there in the beam of the deck lights, lifting with a muscle he felt through his feet and knees and in the bad knot of his stomach.

At an angle on the bulkhead a green sign showed a stick-figure running, the arrow pointing toward the foredeck muster station. Near it a laminated poster asked Are you feeling unwell? and offered the lie-flat places where you could be sick in comfort. He took his hand out of his pocket and closed it again on the cold rail. It had rained earlier or there had been a wash-down, and the water on the rail was almost a skin of its own. His fingertips started to go to glass and then to nothing. He kept them there until it hurt enough to make his eyes water. Then he went inside.

The blast of warmth was almost violent. It smelled like human effort. Carpet that had started life the color of blueberries and salt had aged into a brownish purple with bits of grit in its pile. Laminated announcements were pegged to a corkboard: children’s activities in the lounge at nineteen-hundred; an image of a trawler and the words Respect the Sea; a cartoon ship with the crew in blue boiler suits telling you about the joy of lifejackets. A wooden model of a nearly identical ferry was housed in a glass case, its name in gold on the bow. Below the case there was a photograph of the first skipper to hold the route, a man with a wide face and eyebrows like gulls.

He found the café and sat with a tray of food. The tea had the skin of brew that set too quickly and the fish was the kind that had known a freezer before it had known heat, but he ate it without taste. He thought of nothing while he ate. He tried to imagine himself being the kind of person who could register tastes under the circumstances and failed. A television in the corner rolled a channel his eyes kept catching. A weather pattern unrolled across a cartoon map: arrows slanting into a small archipelago off the northern coast. He did not hear the voice. A boy with a cat draped across his shoulder walked past with solemnity and the cat looked at everyone as if measuring.

There were too many faces. Every time a man with a beard turned to take off his jacket, every time a head went by with hair roughed at the back by a cap, some part of his gut lifted. He was surprised at how gullible his body was, how ready it was to assemble a new version of an old face out of anybody who went by. Then he remembered he had not seen the old face in seven years. He closed his eyes. Behind them, slick black shapes turned, long lines of swell with no landmark, and a grey jacket he thought he remembered hung on a nail by a door. When he opened his eyes again the channel was showing men using chains to strap down the trucks parked in the ferry’s belly, the steel of the chains bright where it had been polished by the same movement forever.

He had not told anyone else he was going until he was almost past the terminal turnstiles. He dialled and listened to the tone and the answer and said, I’m going up. He said it like you would if you were taking out the bins or running down to the shop for milk. There was no point in apologizing for not calling earlier. He had known as soon as the man at the other end of the earlier line had said, I’m sorry to ring you out of the blue, sir, that he would have to go himself. He knew the exact cadence of that old courtesy; he knew what came after it. He had held the phone against his head in the kitchen so hard that the phone got warm on one flat shape of his ear. The simple hum of the refrigerator seemed to find the phone’s own hum and harmonize with it in his skull. Are you a relation of Mr. Magnus J. Heddar? the man had asked, and he had said, Yes, I’m his brother. Even before the man went on to explain about the walking, about the island and the places where the cliffs fall clean away and each one is perfect unless you have to count them, he felt a reorganization begin in him, like the furniture of a room rearranging itself while he was still lying on the sofa. He had hated the question. Are you a relation. He had wanted to be asked instead, Are you the person he would have called. Were you the person he loved or insulted. The man on the other end went on to say a body had been recovered on the east of the island, that they could not be certain, that it would help if a family member would come.

It had sounded very procedural until the man had said, quietly, I am sorry, though, and then let the quiet run on, which told him there had been another room, off this first room, where things were said in lower voices.

He put his tray on the collecting station and turned along a corridor. On the wall was a framed photograph of the sea in summer, blue like a storybook, and another of people in bright clothes at the midsummer gala, a girl’s open mouth inside a laugh. He walked past them and into the low bunk cabin that was his. Two seats that could recline farther than a cinema seat but not as far as a bed, opposite each other and, between, a narrow table. There was a plastic cup on the table with the ferry company’s logo. Out the porthole a star shivered and was lost as they turned. The frame of the porthole had a chip in its paint like a bite taken from white chocolate.

He sat down and laced his fingers together and thought of how thoroughly he had not planned for this, and then he untwisted himself and took his small bag from under the seat and made his way without any reason back to the corridor and along. The bar at the stern had space along its long window and a handful of men sat with pints, their faces working through whatever they needed to work through in order to be quiet. He took the long window seat in its angle and ordered a beer and let his bag sit on the seat beside him as if he was waiting for somebody. Black swung against black outside. The horizon yielded fast and was a different place each time the ship rose.

A man in a blue sweater with flecks in it sat down a few places away and said to no one and everyone, We’ll be feeling the tide by midnight. Someone further along said, It’s a northerly now, and the man said, Not yet. It’s still west. It’s only borrowing the cold from the north to make a point. They laughed, and the laugh was the kind of laugh you make when you recognize a word-shape you like. Ewan—because that was his name, and he realized he had not thought it to himself since he got on the ferry; he had only thought brother, dead, up, island—set his glass down too hard and a little of the beer jumped down his fingers and onto the sill.

The man in the blue sweater looked past him and then past the window and then at Ewan and said, You look like you’re going to throw up or cry, I can’t tell which. It was not unkind. Ewan said, Both, probably. The man said, Aye. He tipped his head in a way that he might have used if he was going to give his name, but he didn’t. Ewan looked at his own hands and at the backs of them where the veins had started to be higher, ropes under sand, and at the pale half moons of his nails and at the short red graze he had made that morning getting the bag out of the loft. He could not think of any kind of honesty to deploy with this man. After a while the man said, You’ll be seeing someone? and Ewan said, If you could call it that. The man nodded and said nothing. They sat a long time together with that much.

When there was nothing left of his drink but foam ringed inside the tulip of glass, Ewan asked for water and drank it and went outside again. The cold this time was a possession. It found his cheeks and stayed. He went round to the forward deck where the wind took his breath entirely and the ship’s spray came back up as needles. The world was a hole torn in a paper sky and through it more black. It was ridiculous and true that he found himself looking for the lights of a cottage clinging impossibly to the cliff as if to say, See, it is possible after all. Of course there was nothing.

The bow hit a string of shorter waves, clapped them into white, and the metronome of that run-on run-back made him think of the steps up their mother’s old stair. There had been a cup at the top with buttons in it for a while; a house of flimsy timber they both hated and loved, because it had been their first, and they had been small there, and because it had seemed to wobble when you shut the bathroom door too hard. On wild nights the house made an approximation of the sea, the sound coming in under the roof tiles as if there were creatures scuttling there and as if those creatures knew your name. Magnus had said, once, lying on the floor on his back with his arms in a line over his head clasped by his hands as if he was already being dragged by someone, If I go first you have to say it was because I chose to run at it. No, Ewan had said, Why do I have to do your lying for you? Do your own lying. Fine, Magnus had said, I’ll do it. He had closed his eyes and put on his lying voice, I chose to run at it. See, I can do it. And Ewan had wanted to throw something heavy at him. He had hated the swagger then even as it sheltered him.

In his pocket the phone was a small gravity. He took it out and unlocked it and saw their call history. The last call seven years ago. He scrolled up, as if there could be more. There would be messages somewhere if he went looking for them, older ones in an email folder he had not had the courage to delete. His thumb hovered over the name. It would ring into the gap that had opened. He put the phone away. He stood another ten minutes with his breath making hard work of itself and then when he could not feel the middle of his chest any longer he went inside.

The ferry’s movement was stronger. Every time it lifted there was a lag and then his stomach caught up and then it was all fine. In between the fine, he remembered the afternoon in the left-hand field, the one with the good view and the bad drainage, when he and Magnus had carried the wind-felled spruce out in sections. It had been raining then too, and they had been as good as kind to each other, because there were only two of them, because their father had been in hospital where he could not be saved by anything they had to say to each other. They had sweated and sworn and coughed and, at the end of the day when they had a stack that made a kind of sense out of the tree’s violence, they had stood looking at it through the rain and not looked at each other and Ewan had said, We’ll need to borrow Archie’s splitter. And Magnus had said, No, we won’t. And Ewan had said, You and your fucking heroics. Magnus had shrugged, You want to make a job last as long as you have to, you borrow someone’s tool. You want to keep it for yourself and see what you’re made of, you use your own. Then he had smiled. Ewan had wanted to laugh at the idea that that ridiculousism would turn into a headline in his head that night. He had turned then and snapped, You know, you’re not my father. Magnus had said immediately, Kindly don’t remind me, and put the axe into the log as if he was trying to split his own sentence in half. They had both smiled and then because they had smiled they had been obliged, as if by the rules of their blood, not to for a while.

He tried to model the faces of other men onto the slight idea he had of the dead’s look. Somewhere on the island half-remembered he had a season’s worth of evenings of his brother who could not settle, who was bored in the wrong direction, who had decided that the best way to feel the floor under your feet was to jump. Observe me, he seemed to say even when he was not saying anything. The last conversation had been a bad kitchen. It had been about money and it had been disgust in both directions. It had ended with, Don’t call me, then. Fine, said the other, don’t get in a boat again if you can’t be sure you’ll get back. Fine, said the first. And then the phone had been put down like a small plate put down too quickly and chipped.

He did not know what those sentences had cost or earned either of them. He had thought the contempt he practiced afterward was a kind of truth. At first it had been hot. Then it had been his alibi. At the third or fourth year it had become a method for not tripping over the lack. When he pictured his brother he could only see him at the two or three ages he could retrieve quickly. He did not know the lines his brother’s face had put on since. The last time they had been in the same room had been at a January, in the church that smelled of stone and the flowers they could ill afford, their mother at the front, cold as any stone. Magnus had touched the leaf of one of the arrangements with a finger as if to see if the plant would disbelieve. Ewan had wanted to grab his hand. In the church after the line of people had stopped taking his hand in both of theirs and saying, You’ll miss her always, he had found Magnus’s shoulder at the side of the vestry and leaned his head against it and tried to pass as much of his weight into Magnus as Magnus could take. Magnus had not moved away. Afterward he had made a speech at the hotel that had been terrible and, somehow, sufficient.

It was only now on the black sea that he could feel something like longing. He had not given himself that word before. He had thought it was resentment or fury or simple contrariness. But it was longing arranged differently. He was too old now not to recognize its shape. It felt like the thing his body did when he was very cold and then saw a door he could go through. It felt like the opposite of bravery, the kind you do when you know it won’t be noticed and that it would be better if it were but you can’t manage to want it to be noticed. It felt like the need to press his face against what hurt and call it with its real name, as if that might change its habits.

The ferry developed a slow tilt to port and held it, took a long climb over some longer water, and then relaxed. He heard a girl vomit into a paper bag and then laugh in surprise at the sound her body had made, ashamed and charmed. He tried to imagine his father’s face if he had messaged now to say I am going up to look at Magnus’s body and failed to—his father’s face had been dead for a decade and it did not yet behave under recalling like other faces did. He could find the planes of it, the pad of the cheek, the pale of the eyes rimmed with that particular red his father had from whisky and weather. He could find the slackening at the end. He could not find, easily, that less grand thing, the ordinary way his father had of deciding a thing didn’t matter. His father had forgiven so much it had infuriated Ewan in other people and he had refused so many small generosities to himself that Ewan had wanted to tie him to a chair and make him take a day off.

By midnight he had the cabin almost to himself. He had thought he would sleep some, all the way through the churn into the strait that everyone named with the soft vowel as if that would make the water mill kinder. The berth beside his still empty. He set his bag down and lay back and listened to his own breathing writhe. He thought of the island in the morning, the truncated hills, the slash of sea between rock and town. He thought of the people who would know each other’s cars. He thought of the door he would go through. He closed his eyes and opened them and closed them and it was two o’clock and then three-thirty and the ideas he had of time had become unreliable. He got up and went and got another cup of tea, then something called a morning roll even though it was night, then went back. He did not try the phone again.

Sometime before five a child began to cry without confidence and then fell quiet, as if convinced by some adult sound that it hadn’t heard. He lost the line of being awake and found it again so many times that when the voice came over the tannoy telling drivers to return to their vehicles he could not believe anything had actually passed.

He was among the first out on the deck again. The air was a thinner kind of cold. The sky had a seam of green in the east, then pink above it with no commitment, then a paler place above that where the first grey would belong. The sea was still itself. The island ahead was a collection of lumps of a dreary green with stone teeth, then slowly those lumps made sense into the headland he had seen pictured, the odd low slungness of it as if the rock itself had decided it did not like altitude. The ferry turned, found the isthmus of breakwater and went along it and the town uncurled importantly out of its own dampness. He could see the plastic draped over pallets, the coils of old rope turned black with time and use, the ochre and white of houses and the cynical blue of a single painted door. Two gulls traced and retraced the bow, waiting for whatever the ship might promise.

They came in soft, pretended softness, and stood a moment near the quay without making contact as if both were thinking about whether it was worth it. His teeth jittered together with what he refused to call excitement. The gangways were set. People became purposeful and absurd, everyone in the same small space with their different kinds of hurry. He was shouldered slightly by a man with a bucket wrapped in a cardboard box and wanted to say, You are carrying your own grief too but there’s no reason to be rude about it. He took a deep breath of air that had sat on the island all night and felt it go all along him to the soles of his feet.

Across from the terminal building a minibus waited, a small shuttle to the center of town. He could have walked; everything here existed within the dimensions of a person’s tiring; but he got on it and sat near the front and—though there was no god of traffic lights and no need for them—the bus halted at a single held red lamp as an inspector lifted his arm at the incoming truck convoy. The driver looked out at the people standing at the harbor side in their coats and said, Every morning the same men, in the same coats, making themselves useful by looking. It was not unkind.

At the police station—a modest building with walls the color of pencil shavings—he was shown into a room with chairs in a line and a table with glossy magazines whose covers had been wiped to a high shine. A poster above the chair asked him if he knew where his child was and said also that the peak time for break-ins was in the hours after dusk. He sat with his hands clenched and then saw himself unclenching them as if for a camera. A woman in a cardigan and a lanyard came and said his name softly. Her hair had been pinned with attention to make it look like it had been unpinned. She asked if he'd like a tea. He put his hands close together and said he had already had three. She smiled at something not exactly the surface of that sentence. She asked him to wait. Then she said, We’ll go through when you’re ready. He understood he was being asked to decide something useless and powerful. He nodded. She said, It will be cold. She did not say, It will smell. Then she said the thing he had not known was coming, DNA will confirm. This is— She searched for a word and did not find one. We still need to do this, she said, and her eyes flickered as if she owed an apology for that to every person who ever came and sat on the chair he sat in now.

He had thought of this as an errand. The island was grateful for any story a person brought it; it was also full of tiring people in bright jackets who got in the way and whose hands knew ropes. He tried to file what he would say under one or the other and could not. He said, I can pay for— and saw at once that this insulted the idea of what would happen. He closed his mouth. He held it all there a moment and then moved his head. His face had a life of its own, and he could feel it agree to something that he did not have to.

She led him. The place where they went was not dramatic. It was under a lower ceiling than he expected. The lights were the persistent kind that were always waiting to be replaced but had not fallen far enough below their efficiency ratio to justify it yet. There was the clean cold of storage and the other cold trapped below it. He thought stupidly of fishmongers and then ferociously made himself not think of fishmongers. He looked at the series of silver doors in squares like someone’s misguided idea of modern design. So many squares. She opened one. There was a trolley. She placed both her hands near the edge of the sheet as if apologizing with them and then folded down just the top of it to show only a face. He had thought they would show it all. He had thought in some hard part of himself he was braced to be among the ones who are punished for needing to see everything. He was grateful to be wrong. The stretch of forehead, the lashes heavy with water that no longer existed, the collapsed lip, the fraction of movement the slack mouth made as the temperature of the room contested what the temperature of the sea had made of him. There were not enough words for what the sea did. The sea does, he thought, not past tense. The sea does.

This face, though, was not a face only. It was a set of comparisons assembled quickly and imperfectly by every part of him that knew a face’s grammar. The hair, wrong; it had been shaved with clippers for some reason, either by the man when he had been alive or by the sea after. The forehead—it was and was not his father’s. The left eyebrow had a kink, but which side had been the kink on? The right ear had a notch at the top. He had a memory of making this notch when they were seven and twelve and a branch had come back on him as they ran and Magnus had bled bright and narrow and had not cried. But his brain was too ready to make something of whatever line was available. The woman put her hand, palm down and flat, on the steel bar of the trolley. She was not looking away.

The man on the trolley had on his lip a thin pale scar, healed long enough to be pale, that began under the middle of the lip and went into the corner as if someone had pulled at one side of his mouth too hard when they were trying to be affectionate, the way lovers could. Ewan’s mouth twitched in an involuntary reflex around the same soundless vowel it would have needed to say the name. He could feel the way his own lip’s movement made a memory move. Magnus had a scar there. A bottle once. Not a fight; just tiredness and carelessness and a bottle’s base glancing off the lip and laughter at the blood and one old towel turned to a flag.

He tried to look for the thing in the eyes that would know him, but the eyes were clouded and did not. He thought about asking to see a hand. He said nothing. The woman said, Mr. Heddar, I know this is difficult— He flinched at the formality of his name. He had not heard his surname without the little undermining of the familiar, without the island cutting the d out of it until it was a soft sound, in months. He said, Could I— and she said, Yes, if you’d like to.

She lifted the edge of the sheet again and he thought he could not bear to be the kind of person who cared whether the nails were bitten. The hand was not the last thing the world had done to him. It was a hand. The palm looked like anyone’s palm. The back of it had the faint purple of having been colder for longer than it wanted. The second finger of the right hand, at its pad, had a small dead-lunar crater of puckered skin, and under the skin was a paleness that does not belong. He knew it because he had put it there, not with his own hand but with his neglect. When Magnus had been eleven he had wrapped his finger in electrician’s tape because he had thought it would be clever to see what would happen if you tightened something like that until the finger had to ask for help. It had left a mark long after, a circle of tissue that had never been right. Ewan had told him to stop. Magnus had said, Make me. Ewan had not. That unmade command had stayed a little fence in him. He found it now under the veil of the taut sheet. He looked up. He breathed. He reached.

He stopped his hand and held it stupidly in the air, the way you hold it if you are a child and you have made paint on your fingers and you are remembering how not to touch. He put it on the steel. He said, Yes.

The woman nodded. She did not reach for him. She folded the sheet again the way it had been. She said, I’m very sorry, Mr. Heddar, and he saw how tired she was. He saw the way the tiredness lived in her like a lodger who was careful with the china. He nodded back and found that he could not make a sort of sound that would serve. He made a different one, much uglier, and let it be there in the room. He made another. The second one had less argument in it.

She told him the rest of what had to be told. There would be formal identification by forensic means, of course. There would be questions, not—she shook her head, her hair shifting and settling—not now. He would want, perhaps, to contact someone in the family. She had the typed sheet with numbers he could call. He took it. He held it like a small plate. He realized he was shaking. He made his hands reasonable for him.

Outside, on the low pavement, the morning had developed the courage to be a day. The street had absorbed a wetness and worn it evenly. A man in a green fleece pushed a pram with both hands and when the baby inside it waved an arm a flock of gulls lifted as if paid. A van went past with the name of a butcher on the side in a font that had not been fashionable for twenty years. He walked to the corner and then did not turn it and then turned it after all and found the café that had no ambition other than to be there. He went in and sat down and the woman behind the counter placed a mug in front of him without being asked and then a plate with two slices of toast. He was touched by the breadth of the confidence with which the island did that. He put his hands around the mug and thought of the way his mother had done that and of the way Magnus had not, preferring to let heat be heat and then burn his mouth and then curse, delighted.

He got the phone out and put it face up on the table. He pressed the number that would call a woman who, seven years ago, was a problem and ten years ago was an oak. He felt the call go down into wires and up again and then across the air. He wanted to save her the stupid shape of the first syllables. He wanted to save himself it. He let it ring twice and then ended it. He put the phone flat like a card in a game and then lifted it and called someone else. A number he had not used in months because the person who carried it had a life held together by a pattern he had learned to know from a respectful distance.

His son answered. The sound of his son’s voice saying Hello put a seam in him he could not fix and did not want to. His son said, Dad? He said, I’m away up north. His son said, Have you? Is— He said, Yes. There was a silence and then his son said, Do you want me to come? and Ewan thought a long time about that wanting and said, No, love. Go to work. It’s all right. He said it as well and as slowly as he could, as if he was speaking to the room between them. His son said, Okay, but then asked, Are you sure you’re not just saying that? and Ewan was startled into something like a laugh. He said, Yes. He said, I have to sort some arrangements. It will be dull and exacting and you should keep your energy for the useful present. His son said, You sound— and did not finish. Ewan said, I know. They sat together breath apart over the line. His son said, I’m sorry. I know it was— and did not finish that sentence either, and Ewan loved him for not finishing it, because there were a thousand true things you could put there and none of them had the right weight.

When he had ended the call, when he had eaten half the toast and realized he had no interest whatever in the other half and then eaten it anyway because he did not want to waste the effort that had put it in front of him, he stood up. He went walking without the map he had folded into his pocket for confidence. He let the town tell him the way to the water. It was always that simple on islands. The air had a salt that had not been in it at the harbor in the south. A step of breeze moved the hair on his hand. He found the path that went around the back of the houses and out along the headland. He walked until the roofs of the town were out of sight and then he walked further. At a place where the path narrowed, a rock sloped into the sea with the politeness of a polished knee. He sat down very near the edge because it was like him to sit very near the edge if there was an edge to sit near. He leaned forward and looked down and the water there had the gray of hammered pewter and the white of its speaking and the green when you could see its depth. There was a fine rain of blown spray. It touched his face and his eyelashes and it was not unpleasant. He thought, in a sentence that came to him whole and refused to be argued with, I did not know how to love you properly because I did not know what you had done wrong belongs to you and what you had done right belonged to all of us.

He stayed there a long time and once got to his feet and thought he would go back and did not and sat again and the gulls stared at him as if promising nothing of any use. He had made the yes he came to make. The rest of it would be as procedural as he could make it be so that it did not have to be anything else. There would be papers and chapels and a wooden box in a room where he and perhaps another person, perhaps no one, would stand. He tried to picture the inside of the island’s church; he could not. His mind kept returning to the inside of their old kitchen with its ridiculous yellow door. He could not say exactly why he found this helpful, but he did.

He did not feel relief. Relief would have required a different story. He felt something else, a hard clean thing that grew its opinion in the middle of his chest and that did not allow the other opinions to survive. It was not forgiveness; it was not warming. He thought it might be accuracy. He had told a room, and in that room were the trolley and the woman whose hair had shifted and the persistent lights, and in that room he had said, Yes, this is him, and however much of him it had been it had been enough for yes. He would answer all their questions until the more clever parts of the world had agreed that his answer corresponded to what could be established by other means. If it turned out that his yes was inaccurate in the technical way then he would carry that in his pocket as well. He knew this even though he could not have explained it to anyone. He would believe his act had given a name to a thing that wanted one and that the name had done what names do, which is not magic but is also not nothing.

Waves ran in their long low sentences below him. In the far distance, under the grain of cloud, a white sail had the nerve to be decorative. He was cold and it did not matter. He got up when he began to have that particular tremor in his thighs that means the body is about to be angry with you for asking it for anything more. He walked back the way he had come. On the path, before the houses began again, before the smell of last night’s fish fried would rise and find him and the vision of the day would return and do its work, he met a man coming the other way, a grey-bearded person with a face that had primer in it under the paint. The man nodded and said, Fine day. Ewan nodded too. He had the sense to say, It will do.

He would go down to the harbor and book a place back across the northern strait, and when he crossed he would stand again on the forward deck and hold himself against the rail in the same hurt way and he would watch the space open between where he stood and the place he had named. He did not know yet whether he would go straight south or stop at any of the other small ports and hammer on any doors he had promised he would one day hammer on. He did know that when he got home he would take from under the bed the box that had their father’s last knife in it with the good handle, the knife he had told himself he was keeping because someday he might want to learn to carve. He would take the knife out and he would put it on the table in the light and he would have to decide whether he could hold it or whether it had belonged only to the father and he was foolish to have taken it from the house. He imagined his son standing in the doorway and watching.

He bought a card in a shop because he was tired of the phone. He bought one of the harbor at summer with unreal light. He wrote in it in a hand that surprised him by how much it looked like his father’s. He wrote, It was him. There was not a lot else to say. He added, I am all right. For once he did not feel the drag to qualify that sentence with details that would confuse its shape. He addressed it and licked it and the glue tasted like what it had always tasted like and then the card was in the red box. When he came back with it he found the postmistress raising the flap to push more down and she glanced and said, It’ll go. He nodded. He did not, thank God, thank her, as if she were a river and had been especially obliging about being a river that day.

By the time he went up the gangway again and found the place against the rail that he was almost on ordinary terms with now, the wind had swung the way the man in the speckled sweater had predicted. Northerly. It bit. It opened his lungs in two. He had the child’s thought that if he survived this cold he could survive anything. Out beyond the breakwater, in the place the swell decided to be its real size, a porpoise rose and went down again with no remark. He raised his head and said aloud, in case the unspectacular creature needed to hear him for some reason, We are not finished and we are not fair, and nobody above the decks and below them startled, and a minute later he was laughing at nothing much and then not laughing, and the ferry took its first long step away from what he had named and there was the strong song of water against steel and then the drift that followed it. He leaned along the rail and lifted his head and let the air take tears and give him breath back and, when the wake had rolled out and away and made a white that would very quickly not be white, he held on. He held on with both hands and with his mouth open and with the exact weight of his feet, and he would not have known how to let go even if anyone had asked him to.
