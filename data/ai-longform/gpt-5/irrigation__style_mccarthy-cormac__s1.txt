Outside the sound of the night rose a notch. Male crickets came on like a choir. A truck went by on the far road and threw light along the undersides of the leaves and the orchard threw them back and they were green and gold and black in that light as they had been in a thousand nights before this one and would be in some to come though maybe not so many as you would wish. He had known years when in an hour's time of this gone-to-night there had been a cool that run up the branches of a tree like a thought of ease and the animals were slow and sane and you would have said the world was forgiven. He did not feel that now. He could feel the one wherewithal inside him giving out and he did not want the boy to see. He rose at last and said I'll walk the line again. Jesse rose without saying and they went.

In the sixth row the joint they'd set late had a little weep to it and he pressed it and felt his thumb take the water and then the water was in him and not in the ground and he could not keep it and yet he pressed. It made a small shining like a star. He wiped it with his shirt and it came back. You could spend your life in these thumbings and likely he would. He took a scrap of rubber and wrapped it and taped it and it was not right and he did it and it would hold a time and then the time would be gone. Jesse held the light for him that he had took from the truck, a cheap battery thing with a lens scratched to hell, and in that light the patches of salt on the earth lit like frost, the little white alluvial of failure. Jesse moved the light and a nightmoth came and shouldered it and fell and got up and went and came again stupid as any man.

Will there be rain, Jesse said.

Not for us.

It had rained elsewhere and they had seen it standing on the horizon like a town and it had seemed not even to want to come to them. He could smell it sometimes in the wind and it was like a woman who had loved you once and now would walk past you at the store as if you were a post. They walked on and at the back fence they stopped and put their hands upon it and looked out into where the road fell away and into the polelight in the neighbor’s yard that hummed like a bug. The calf lay over there and something had come and worked at it and Levi went and put wire over its body. Jesse watched him and flinched when a gnat flew up his nose and he swore, a half chap swear that sat between them like something they'd better not turn to since there was no sense to be gained in it. They went back.

On the way they stepped off into a little draw the years had cut and never quite notched and where the grass had not yet been flensed from the earth by the new regime and there a toad sat who had lived on this place longer than he or any he knew. You could see in the toad's patient posture a thing of land that is exact and it is not pride but it is keep and it is set by God in the world's mind and it will be and be abandoned and accept both with no cry. The boy crouched and reached and the toad had not gone because they live by a belief that what is coming is not coming. He held for it and it hopped sudden and it hit his shirt and slid and then it was down the grade in two ardent strokes and gone into a pocket of earth so small a coin would not fit and Jesse laughed low. Levi felt that the laugh had inside it the only answer there is and he did not say that because he did not live in a world where people say such.

When they came back to the yard a man stood by the porch and he turned and he raised his hand and said their names. It was Roy and he had a paper in his hand and he was sober as always because he did not drink and he had never left a place of counting without something of it attached to him. He said evenin and they said it back. He looked at the orchard and he looked at the men's faces and he found in both what he expected to find. He held out the paper for them and Levi took it and looked at it and handed it without reading to Jesse. Jesse looked at it longer than he liked and then he gave it back.

What we have, Roy said, is three offers now. Halberd I told you. A man out of Santa Fe with his wife's money. And a man out of the east with no wife. The last is the figure there. He touched the paper’s shoulder like a doctor. They wont get better. Roy looked at the boy. He looked at Levi. He said if you are willing I will begin to move. He said I wont be here past tonight to talk about it because I have to go to my sister who has had a thing happen and if you would put in your mind that there is a last day for everything I would ask you to put it tonight. If you cant thats your right. He said these words as if he had set them in a field to dry and then brought them to market and they were what they were. There are not miracles for such.

Levi did not move. He could feel through the earth the water go round in its little dark loops and he could feel the weight of the day and he could feel the weight of every day like it and he thought then as he had not allowed himself that morning that there would not be enough. There just would not. Roy cleared his throat. He took off his hat. He was not a man you would think to do that and he did that and it was nothing and it was something. He said I'm sorry, boys. I aint done you good. I know that. He put his hat back and went to his truck half turned as if he still would speak and then did not. He started his truck and it coughed because all the old trucks do and then he pulled away and they watched his dust climb into the night and find its home among the older dust and then the dark took the road.

Levi drew in his cheeks and then let them back out. Jesse leaned on the post and went his head down and up as if he would nod for no reason. In the house the yellow light was not kind. The line made its small fragile noise among the roots of the trees and it was the only thing alive that had made such noise and not been told to hush. The headgate clicked once in its cooling and the dog thumped its tail against the boards as if it would admonish existence to have more going for it than it had.

We could sell the creekfield, Levi said again, and you could see that he had the words in his mouth and did not know how to swallow or spit and it sat and made him sick to hold.

We could, said Jesse.

We could keep the rest a year and see.

We could do that.

You'd stay. Levi said it as a question and as an order and as a prayer.

Yeah.

He nodded. He felt himself whistling in his chest and that was new. He did not mention it because that would serve to make it real. He said to the night what it is men say and that is nothing. He went and turned the valve a hair and he held his hand to the seam and there was no weep and he set the wheel back and he forgave it and walked away.

They set their boots one after the other across the yard and they climbed the porch and their heels made the old crack and roll and the wood answered them because it knew them. Inside the table was as it had been and the light as it had been and the chair as it had been and the fillings of the day like the guts of a deer hung on some tree. They stood there as if each waited for the other to say what came next and no one said and so nothing came and that was itself an order within the order. Jesse went to the sink and drank and the water rattled in the pipes and stopped. He wiped his mouth with his hand and wiped his hand on his shirt and put his head upon his arm on the sill and looked out at the dark.

What would he say, Jesse said.

Levi knew who he meant. He felt the ache in him like an old burning coal and he felt the easy answer rise to his mouth like a lie born already big. He said he would say keep. He looked at Jesse and saw a boy in him and a man and something else that did not have to be either. He said he would say keep and he would have kept. He leaned his hip against the table and he looked down and the line of his life ran then without the leaps and cuttings the day had put in it, he saw it go across the season and into a winter he did not name and then into a night he did not fear because he had bent to most of his fears and knew them gentled.

Jesse reached into his pocket and pulled out the paper Roy had left. He had taken it without thinking and he laid it on the table and smoothed it and looked at it as if it would change. The light made the letters thin and stringy and they did not hold together and the numbers were as black as an eye. He put his hand on it and slid it away as if it might stain. He said we will keep. We will keep for a time. His voice was strange to him then, like some other had come to speak through him, and he looked up to see if Levi had heard and Levi had.

For a time, Levi said. His voice had the edge of a blade that has been honed on rock, not stone. Yes.

Outside the night went on and the orchard took the water like a parable. The deer would come later with their small sound and stand under the trees and their eyes would walk back and forth like lamps. A train two counties away would call so soft it was like it spoke only to the horses tied in some dream pasture alongside it. The brothers sat in their own making. There was in the kitchen a smell of mud and glue and the faintest iron of blood and in that it was as if the oldest country had set a hand upon the house and said yes I know you and I will not name you out of my mouth yet. They dozed a little and the dog came and set its chin on Jesse's thigh and Jesse woke and put his hand on the dog’s ear and thought nothing further and slept. Levi watched the window until it held no image, and in the blank he saw a second field which was this one and was not and he could not for his life say what waited in the second. He slept where he sat.

Sometime in the night the pump kicked and ran for a long time and then it died and did not come back and not even in his sleep did that go unheard. The orchard lay under the starlight and in the half light the leaves were silver and you could not have said if this was limn for life or it was a cold that had come to them and would not go. The water in the line did what it could, went to the ends of its paths, held where it could hold, failed where it had to fail. Somewhere a coyote called and then another answered and a third did not. The sound was drawn tight like wire and hummed and was cut.

At daybreak the first hawk shouldered the sky and went above the orchard and looked at it all with a scorn that was only hunger. The brothers woke into what they had said they would do and there is no better or worse way to wake. You take the light as you find it. You take the day. They went out and the smell of the water in the rows was as if the old river had come secretly in the night and been ashamed and gone. They stood and listened and it was good. They stood and listened until what they had done made its own shape in them and they could not undo it and that was the end and the beginning both, and they went to work.
