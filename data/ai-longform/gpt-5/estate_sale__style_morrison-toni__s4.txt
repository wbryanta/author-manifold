The first thing they come for is the mirror. Not the bed she was born beside nor the cedar chest with its secret breath of moth and camphor and sweet cedar, but that long, narrow, forgiving mirror whose corners were nicked by belt buckles and hairpins, whose back had been papered with cheap roses that bled onto the plaster. The glass has been in that hallway since the summer of 1959 when Eula brought it home, sitting straight as a deacon in the back of Mr. Tanner’s truck, her hat on and her gloves off because the heat was that bold, and she had said to Viola, newly nine and too fast with her fingers, Don’t you sass this. This shows you. And it had. Year after year it threw back bodies growing and diminishing, braids and wigs and lopsided afros, wedding dresses held up under the chin to see if the lace might grace what a woman had been hoping was still graceable, funeral suits lifted in quiet counting to see if the black would match grief. The mirror has a memory. It remembers a mother sliding powder on a chest where milk had dwelt. It remembers a girl sneaking lipstick while thunder argued with the sun. It remembers the men who were invited and the men who thought they were.

By noon, the house has already lost its breviary of mornings. The coffee tin goes first, which is no matter. The money sits in its cigar box because Rollo from down the block brought two folding tables and some women from the church taped index cards to things like you would tag a calf. Fifty cents for a cracked saucer that once held Mr. Leary’s change and the faint thumbprint of jelly from when Baby Ruth came over with her little girl and they ate in the kitchen instead of in the front room as befits your elders—even then Eula had allowed bending, as if inside her something wide had been strung with sure thread and would not break for small tasks like lunch. A dollar fifty for the apron with the lilac embroidery that Eula never wore when company came because her mother had told her you don’t bring the kitchen to the table in front of folks who ain’t blood, and Eula had believed her mother until she didn’t. Three dollars for the set of blue jars that carried summers inside and gave winters to those who had not earned joy.

Viola stands by the door with her hand on the glass that had been sticky when she was small because the breath of children and fever, because the nose of a dog, because the summer of the frogs when they invaded the porch and sung and sang until Eula said Lord, it is enough. She is fifty-five, and her hands have changed their mind. Once quick, once dangerous, they now ask permission of the jar lids before they turn, of the money before they count it twice. She has put on one of her mother’s dresses that still lives on the shoulder seams in a way Viola’s dresses never did. A dress with tiny violets so faded you could think of snow. It has the smell of chest drawer and iron heat, a salt-and-flower smell that is not perfume and is not sweat and is the honest body of a diligent woman who never ate alone if there was a human in the house to share with. It falls over Viola differently, looser, as if it knows it cannot make her stand up the way it made Eula stand up, as if the spine behind it is its own and will not obey a stranger’s orders.

They bring their hands in, those strangers. Hands of women with the silent ring of loss on their fourth finger. Hands of men with thumbs that have met nails and hammers too often to retreat. Hands of children, flicking, unable not to touch the hawk feather stuck in the crack of the bookshelf, the little carved man with his mouth wide, the porcelain dog with one ear gone that a boy had set on the mantle in 1963 and Eula had refused to throw out because “everything in here is missing just one thing. You go on and throw him out if you got all your pieces.” There are hands with gold on them heavy and too bright that scrape against the wood when they pick up a bowl because you cannot carry memory without touching it with all your hungers. They set their eyes to mean fair, they say Mmm mmm in that way that denies and desires at the same time, they say How much you want for this like the asking itself is a blessing that will make it true.

“How much for the mirror?” the woman asks. She is soft in the face, her hair straight as the line you draw when you don’t mean to come back. The girl with her has a lip fuller on one side like someone adored it more. She runs her thumb on the edge of the glass as if she can make a friend with it first.

Viola looks at the little white paper with the number in blue marker that Shirley from the Missionary Society printed that morning in her careful print. Twenty-five. She licks a tooth, tastes nothing. “Thirty,” she says before she knows it. Her mouth has decided not to allow that piece to agree to leave easily. There is no rule, she told herself when she unlocked the screen and propped it. She can say anything to anybody and make it true.

The soft-faced woman searches her bag like something alive is inside it and does not want to be. She produces twenty-seven dollars, three tens and some ones and the backs of coins worn smooth. “It is a little chipped,” she starts, and Viola hears her mother say In a world like this, if you are not chipped, I don’t believe you.

“You pay what you can,” Viola answers, and the woman meets her eyes in a way that lets forgiveness move to stand by them both for a minute. The girl with her smiles into the glass, sees herself for a second at the size she will be, and flinches, poor child, when the light shifts. They carry the mirror out like someone who done wrong but not so wrong they can’t show their face on Eula’s porch for a goodbye.

Men come for the freezer that has three brown ice rings and the dream of squares of corn inside it like a quilt someone forgot to bind. The freezer is heavy with the memory of thirty years and you do not move thirty years out of a kitchen without men who have strong backs and a plan. They wear those belts like they are hugging themselves. They grunt. The freezer grunts right back. The kitchen counters are black with old linoleum that has taken what feet give it and said nothing. “You want the cake pans?” a woman in red says, her hand twitching toward the round ones, the loaf tin with the dent of holiday. Her child knocks over the salt and the little doll Eula kept on the windowsill to keep mischief busy. The doll does not break; it leans its cracked smile different and looks at all of them as if it has known worse and will not show you what.

“The pans go as a set,” Viola hears her own mouth saying, and she remembers that last Christmas when she burned the top of a lemon pie because she was telling on the phone and took the name of a dead relative into her voice and rode it all the way down to the burnt sugar that laced the kitchen with grief so sweet her grandson said Come on Nana, that’s good. The words that sat in her mouth now came from that sugar. You cannot separate what you cook in from what you put on the table. You cannot sell one loaf tin and think the others will not talk about you when the kitchen is empty. Eula had always told her that the bowls that know each other wash easier.

Outside, on the grass that shouldn’t have survived all it has survived—cars and children and a dog that made circles for two summers until the circle wore through and the ground came up to say What more do you want—the tables hold lace and lamp cord, a pile of comics held with twine, cane-bottomed chairs with their bottoms like nets slung low with weight that is not there. People come slow and then all at once; their sound is like a mouth getting ready to tell bad news and then choosing not to. There is a man who thinks his beard gives him certain permissions, and there is that girl who probably lives two towns over and has baptized herself in thrift for sport; there is Big Hank who once carried a piano up oaken stairs for Eula’s cousin and now stands with his hands in a way that says Don’t ask me to carry your mama’s.

It is a small town if you measure it by mouths and large if you measure it by the lists its people rehearse each morning in the same order they been rehearsing since 1974 when the factory siren took to faltering on Tuesdays. Maple, Third, Quarry. The river that means nothing to people who grew up without it can make you cross yourself here some springs because ice and fish and whatever people throw in it will pour out what it has swallowed all year and lay it on the banks as if to say Decide what you mean to keep. The hill behind the house is one of those Ohio humps that either insists on a sled or insists on none; some winters you hear children scolding ice; some winters only crows speak to it. The house sits on that little hem of road that does not get plowed first. Men know this and still bring their cars, brake wrong, and put their heads out to look at the sky as if it is in charge. Today is late summer, brittle with heat like a biscuit left on top of the stove. The only mercy is that the maple hoards its shade like a miser who has just learned what his coins can sing.

“She kept it good,” somebody says in a voice that tries not to, and somebody else answers in that generous-bitter way, “She didn’t know any other way.”

They come for the old television that never learned to show color right and that taught children new ways to say certain names. They take the stool that had been purple in 1971 and teal in 1983 and is now a gray that holds both those colors the way a widow holds an old story you are tired of hearing and tired of not hearing. They take the lamp with a base shaped like a horse, and a boy taps it to see if the horse will speak. His mother says Don’t be simple, and then her hand goes to the horse’s nose the same time. It is never about the horse.

Viola moves a bowl away because a boy’s hand is too much like her brother Neville’s hand. Long-fingered, good with spoons and cards. Neville was good with sense until he wasn’t. When Mama took to bed in that hot August every year with her blood thick and her breath thin, Neville would pound on the back steps like the devil wanted a door and only he knew how to make one. He left for a job in ’78 at a place that printed calendars on good paper. He came back in ‘80 with eyes that had rolled back to show you their whites and a woman who had five names, each one a different person. He cried like a child in that front bedroom when the parish woman came to lay hands and tell the devil she did not believe in him. He took Eula’s metal colander to sell for wine nobody wanted to drink and set it down on the yard when he saw a leaf almost like the hand of God and fell asleep on it like you do when you are ready to lie with shame until it turns into something like mercy. He died in a hospital where no one could get to him fast enough to say “Boy,” which is what his mama would have said because she believed there was power in that one word and would have loaded it with every beating and laugh she could remember, and it would have, might have, told him to come back. The boy in the yard stirs something in Viola. She moves the bowl to a higher table that is not truly safer and knows she can do nothing about random resemblance and that resemblance can spoon your innards with its bright flat shape any time it likes.

“Where the quilts?” asks a woman with eyes like flints. She has on a hat that would make Eula pinch her mouth fussing and then take it in her hands and fix it so it knew how to sit. Viola leads her to the bedroom that faces the alley. She has not been in here since the morning they brought Eula downstairs and the eight of them—church women so efficient they looked like the number eight turned sideways, infinite in their looping—put her on the dining table and washed her with hands that had not been hurried for decades. Viola has avoided this room because it smells not like Eula when she was herself but like sage and the skin of oranges they brought in an old bowl to sweeten the air. She gathers the stacked quilts with her chin and arms like a child holding sunflowers. Each one of those squares could tell a thing it knows. There is the one made of ties, all that slick sorrow somehow changed to a something you would put over a shivering boy. There is the one that repeats a pattern like an argument you have with someone you love until you are too tired and you lay your hand on their wrist and that is the end of it for the night. There is the one that Eula and Miss Pearl did together at the table with the radio eating the news and their mouths making sighs too small to name. Viola takes the top one, green and brown and little dots of mustard, and she wants to keep it. She wants to swallow it. She wants to throw it out the window just to see if grief can fly.

“How much,” the flint-eyed woman asks, and Viola says more than is on the card for that one. Her tone makes it clear that this price is not for the cotton and thread. The woman nods. She knows you cannot expect to get memory for free.

Rollo comes in with his breath. He always does, as if he must deliver it to you as separate from himself. He is a man who has carried a dozen living rooms up three flights for little pay and carried two caskets down for none. He pats Viola on the arm like you pat a wall after you nail it right. “You got to eat,” he says. It is unclear whether he means today or in general.

“I ate a peach,” she tells him. It was not good, but it made a sweet rip of a sound between her teeth that reminded her of laundry.

“You got to sell that piano while you got day in the window. You know that man gone come for it.”

“I know him,” she says, which makes Rollo roll his lip to say You always know the person who wants your worst thing.

“And the books,” he adds, sad because he is sure books mean sin to part with even when you never did read but liked to know you could.

“Not all of them,” she says, and they both stand in the hall like prayer and look at the books as if they are dogs at a pound. You can only take one, which does not feel like God’s plan. You want to take them all and go where they take you, which is anywhere but here.

The piano sits like story in the front room. It had not been tuned since men wore ties to the movies. The keys are yellowed the way fingernails get when you drink too much tea and believe that absolves you of not giving up coffee. Children pounded it. Teenagers who could play were not welcome in this front room because they would take the piano to places Eula did not want to go. She approved of hymns only when they were not let out by children who wanted to swing them. Still, one winter when storms pulled the lights out their roots and the radio was tired of lying, a girl named Cissy came home from college and found that this piano understood her complaint. She had set her mouth like a match to it and let the smoke travel. The house had widened for the sound like a woman who already birthed five and did not think she would again and then yes. Cissy cried like somebody told her she was pretty and she didn’t believe them. Viola had stood in the doorway then and knew there were doors that were really throats, and some you must not step through. Her mama had stood in the kitchen with a finger in a cup of white sugar, tasting for bitterness.

The man she knows will come for it is named Manley and is called, not by his wish, Manny. He sells. It is what he knows. He could sell a tin roof in a hailstorm and tell you that when the hail hits it, it will sound like God is juggling. He could sell a dress to a naked man on a day so hot the earth lifts its shoulder to get more air. He comes with a tape measure looped over his shoulder and a boy with him whose ears are young.

“Afternoon, Miss B,” he says. It isn’t accurate but it is solemn, and he makes it slow for respect. “I’m sorry about your mama.”

Viola gives him the thanks she has parceled for men like him all morning. It has weight in it and is not spendable elsewhere. “You going to take it?” she asks and he says I could, as if you do not just take a living thing in the afternoon and make it a stranger by nightfall.

“What you want to take it for?” Rollo asks him, and Manny says something about an aunt who got girls in her kitchen and teaches little hands to sit still, which could be true since everything is true if you carry it long enough for someone to turn their head and see it at the angle it blesses them.

“Mama always said pianos got to be earned,” Viola offers because she needs to say her mother at least once where this piano can hear.

Manny nods like he comes from people who earned every stick and is tired. “I got cash.” He always has cash. He pulls out a roll that looks indecent, and that annoys Viola enough to make her narrow her eyes at the piano, which sits big-boned and waiting, knowing that this day is the day any day can be when the air decides it is the season for leaving. The tape measure slaps his thigh. The boy’s ears tip toward a sound not yet made. Manny names a price that makes him fair to those who watch and a thief to those who have been paid in Sunday afternoons their whole life.

“Three hundred,” comes out of Viola’s mouth and it surprises her. It is at least a hundred more than what the card says. It is the price of corners of her own memory she does not want to hand to some child who will wipe their jelly on the wood and not know that that is a covenant.

Manny looks like he has been slapped by rain. Rollo acts like a cough. “Three,” Viola repeats but then says, “and you take them books with the yellow spines I never liked. They full of mean women who laugh at each other in Europe.”

Manny grins because this request is a gift to his mouth. “That is a sin, Miss V. Mean women are the best reason to read.” He makes a show of counting out the money slow like he is unwinding a snake. Viola does not look at the roll. She looks at the piano and does something with her eyes like a laying on of hands. She tells it something she has not ever said aloud: You did not save me and I did not ask you to. And there is no whimper, no crack. It is a piece of wood and sound. But then her left knee tells a story it learned holding a baby who would not be weaned. It bends, and she sits, and that is when she notices the hairline faint crack under the window where a tempered anger once went when her father left.

“Where he gone anyway?” someone asks out in the yard where men gather to argue for half a cigarette because if you smoke a whole one at once on a day like this, you will have to answer for your body. “He went North like a fool,” somebody else answers. There is no good answer to where a man went when he left a woman like Eula, like a pan left on a stove that burns now. “He dead, ain’t he?” the question says. Viola hears that word like water down a winter gutter that cannot carry it all.

“He is,” she says, and it goes through the wall as if walls are only suggestions. Her father learned to be sorry and then forgot. When he returned, a long while later, the man he had been was not with him. Eula never said he had been gone; she just asked him where he had been and then told him where he slept now. They learned each other again like bad songs that still you cannot stop singing because they they have found the corner of a hallway to sit in in your head, and from that corner they ask for things. He died three summers back and left her a watch that didn’t run and a photograph in which he was not looking at her. Viola put the photograph face down in a drawer with the war ration books and thought about setting fire to its secrets. She had not.

People bring their voices inside like it is church. Women who had not begged before beg. People who were taught not to cry in public reach for things and their hands cry. People stand in this house and tell things you shouldn’t tell in a kitchen but must in a kitchen because the chicken grease on the air and the cooling water in the mouths of glasses broken and re-glued make the truth stick right. That boy of mine, a man says, like he likes to start that way so the story has a walking stick. He is working nights, he says, and his mouth says proud and ashamed at the same time because nights. A woman says My aunt went to school with your mama and could run like good news. Younger ones can’t help but adjust their hair in the reflection of the dining table’s shine and remind their bodies they are still here even when the world tries not to notice. Somebody steals, maybe, a handful of spoons. Somebody puts five dollars extra in the box for the cake pan she doesn’t really want because she is leaving this town and wants to believe she is kinder than the place she gives the money to. Nobody asks for the big, cracked brown bowl that has been waiting since 1968 for the return of a man from a war. It is too honest. It is not for sale. Viola leaves the price column blank on its card and takes it off the table altogether when no one is looking. She will leave it in the cabinet like a stone in a mouth. Let the new children living wherever will be living in here find it. Let them ask, Whose was this? Let their mamas say Why you holding on to that old thing? Let that bowl answer in the night when only the house is awake: Mine.

The light under the maple tree shifts its shoulder and so the yard is a different person now. The little ones who had been puddles in the grass begin to be tears in clothes and tested planks in the porch steps. A woman stands in Eula’s kitchen and says This is where your mama slapped me once for leaving my baby on the ‘frigerator. Viola sees the baby then. He is a man with yellow boots in town now, but he is also that pink petal of a face, the lip like a rose blown wrong. He was left because the woman was holding too many sorrows and something had to sit down. Eula put her hand where it would make a next thing possible. There were lines a good woman drew and lines she knew were imaginary. She slapped you and then fed you and then called your mother and told her her business without using a word longer than six letters. That happened in this kitchen on a Tuesday when the river was pretending to be quiet and four months later acted like it had not promised anything. The house remembers. The walls are strong but they are not jealous: they let your voice leave and tell on you and then they don’t punish you for it. That had always been the thing that made Viola believe in houses the way people believe in plants. You water them, they manage a trick you cannot learn. At night sometimes she would lie in the front room on the good couch and think the house put its hand on her shin, light, and say There is something under there. Worms and new dirt and the endings of spoons. But also bones and rings and the one blue bead people lose when they think it does not matter.

“Baby,” she had said to herself at eleven that day the neighbor boy told her the sound she heard at night was a fox; he liked the fact that he could make his lips so soft for that word. “Baby,” she says now, reaching into the top of the refrigerator to get the check Shirley needs to take to the bank so she can get money to pay the man for hauling the freezer. The envelope is no longer where her fingers expect. She stands on her toes, her pulse doing a small drum because between the ice and the butter place she had put the bank address like a hope you put in cold so it will not rot. She checks the drawer that used to hold coupons that had never noticed expiration. The drawer gives up a button and a hair that is too long to be any she has had since ‘96. She is small as a child reaching for that hair in that moment. She wraps the hair around her finger like a ring. She thinks of what she will do if the check is gone and who she will blame and then knows that if it is gone, it will be because she dared not tie the grief up too soon, and grief made like water poured itself somewhere low in the house.

“You look like you fighting your blood,” Rollo says softly, and she nods. He points at the flour can that lives on the counter next to the stove, the one with blue chickens. She opens it, and there, between paper and cake flour that has seen no cake since before Thanksgiving, is the envelope. She laughs like someone let a goat into the church on purpose. “She always put things in there,” Viola says. “Mama never trusted the freezer. Said it killed more than it saved.” She can smell corn bread now plain as if it is in the oven. She can feel the slap of batter against her wrist. She feels foolish and glad that the foolish has been relieved. She moves the check to the pocket of her dress, which is where checks belong until they have done their work in this town.

Out in the front room, the piano moves at last. Manny and Rollo and a young one lift it onto a dolly like wrestlers who have made friends with math. The sound the piano makes when they shift it would break a child’s heart if a child had time to listen. It is a small moan like a woman who has given birth and is not going to talk about it. Viola wants to put both hands flat on its flank and tell it not how to leave but how to travel. Instead she sits back down and wraps string around a pile of dusty letters tied once by Eula and then untied and then retied by Viola when she found them again last winter and knew she would not be one of those who threw away words because there were too many. She has read some. She has refused others because she is old enough now to know that other people’s need for her curiosity does not outrank her own need for sleep. She printed on an old envelope in red pen: For when somebody wants to tell the truth at last. She placed these letters in a box with the scarf her mother wore when it was too windy for tears. She sets the box on top of the washing machine and hopes someone who will understand the way a stopped machine still holds moves will come for it.

“Who’s going to take the bed?” a teenage boy in a Southside Wildcats shirt asks, and the answer makes him blush because it is not about what he thinks it is. Beds in houses like these are heavy with everything they have done. They know your name damp. They know what you gave up and how often you thought that meant you were empty and were not. They know what small hips can do when they are asked to take on a world. They know what lonely weighs in February. Nobody wants to cost themselves that kind of knowledge for forty bucks, so beds have to learn to be lonely one last time. “We’ll leave the mattress,” Viola says. It seems kind. She does not know to whom the kindness speaks.

By two, the sun decides to take a walk, and the shade gives up a little ground. Sweat collects in the clavicles of women who are too proud to wipe it away. Men fold thin money into a heavy box. Flies choreograph. A young voice sings something you cannot with good conscience call a tune, and an older voice, never one to be outdone, sings something you must call a prayer. The smell of onion, always there like a whisper, tries today to move its seat at the table and cannot, so it tells jokes to distract them. They keep buying. Viola keeps naming prices like a woman practicing boundary. She puts numbers on scales and thinks of girls who have spent afternoons like this before her and will after her, not money but afternoons where something once held is registered on a little card and then you decide whether to be flattered by the attention or insulted by the measurement.

“I want the crochet like a table snow,” says a man with freckles that have conjoined and made a map. “The runner,” he clarifies to himself so that he can understand not to want too much. “Two dollars,” she says, and he is surprised that something can be worth so little and so much.

The church ladies, Shirley and Birdie and Mae, move like a wind that knows floors. They go around closing cabinets that no longer hide cousins. They stack plates in boxes like prayers in a line. Birdie clicks her tongue at the girl in the window who is putting a brass candlestick to her cheek as if it is a lover cool to the touch. Mae counts money with snicks and sighs and little glances up at God not for guidance, just to register that He is in the room and must behave accordingly. These women have been waiting for an afternoon like this for decades and dreading it and dressing for it, even in their sleep. They will not let this house go quick as a goose goes south. They will talk about today at least until it is time to remember something worse or better. They will be named when they are not in the room. They will be given cakes they did not want. They will be remembered at the next estate sale by a woman who will say, “There was that round one with the big blinks, and that one with the frown that never was about you.”

“I believe this is yours,” Birdie says to Viola, and she holds out a small tin box with a picture on it of a woman in a dress with sleeves the way dreams had sleeves in 1941. Inside is a thimble that has traveled from one finger to another and bears the shape of the first woman who taught it to sit. Eula’s mother. That woman who could make a dress out of drapes and then take it down on Tuesday and make curtains out of it again for the day when light was too bold. Viola places the thimble on her finger and feels like something sits exactly where it should at last this month. For a minute here she is relief. She imagines for one wild wet second wearing this thimble always, giving a speech wearing it, making love wearing it, ironing wearing it, and knows its gone weight would steer her hand to say no when no is a bleeding thing. She takes it off and puts it in her pocket because some powers must not be spent all at once.

She tries to keep back the Singer, too. She keeps its pedal under her foot as if she is a guard at the door of a club and the machine is a girl out too late. She tells the woman who comes for it that it doesn’t work unless you whisper to it. The woman says, “I know how to whisper,” and there is no keeping it then. Against the window, the street has gone ahead without them. A blue truck they have seen for years passes and has a new dent. A dog nobody knows runs as if somebody does know him and is calling his name. A couple on their first unkind afternoon together lean away. The sky is the color of a bruise that does not belong to this town and has learned to be polite. Down the hill a brown woman in a green dress looks up as if she is considering and decides against it. Birds manage between the maple leaves because that is what they do: translate air.

By three, the furniture has been gently less. There is a path through the center of the front room you would not have walked when morning was small. Viola can see the wall where the couch had hidden a child’s pencil drawing of a woman with her hair like a black flame. She moves to touch it. It is the shape of a woman you draw when your mother is beautiful and untouchable, and you are ten. It is the shape of a woman you draw when you want to make your hand recount what your eye cannot. It is the shape of a woman who will die and leave you the job you did not audition for. Viola presses her finger to the little stick hand and says Sorry, and the child in her accepts.

A gaunt woman with a baby at her hip, whose hand is sore from pulling a cart that once carried bleach at the grocery, pauses to ask about the clock over the mantle. The hands on the clock have stopped at a time that means nothing to anyone but the kitchen and the song whose last note fell there the day Eula put down a plate and sat. It is sometime after two. “That clock has been wrong as snow in August,” Shirley says, grabbing it down before it starts to act right just to show a woman it can. Viola writes a number on its card and silently flings, like a woman with salt, the last of her meanness at time. She is careful to let none of it hit the baby. The baby watches them all like a patient king and puts his wet hand to Viola’s face for no reason other than love of faces. That is a reason enough.

If there are ghosts here, they do not like to be named. They prefer their work to be unsupervised. As a boy lifts the brown lamp with its fringing which Eula cherished because it made light look like it was dressed for a ball, there is a cool in the room and a whisper like the back door telling the screen how to make a noise without being noticed. The boy’s question mark hair moves. He looks about to see which adult he belongs to and cannot find one quick. He puts the lamp back where it was not supposed to be again. The room obliges with its permission what it will only once more now before—what do you call it when a house wakes up to find it has been left alone? Not death. Houses do not die. They wait. They settle. They buckle their knees in a storm and then they stand back up when the world is done with its fit. They swallow mice. They listen to that one board that always talks too much. They learn the new names. They pretend they did not.

By late afternoon, the air in the house has changed. It has been used like a text and now is tired. It tastes of coin, wood, hair, lemon cleaner, and all the saying. They have taken most of the chairs and couches that knew worse and were kind about it. Someone has rolled up the rug, and there underneath is the color of what the room would be if it had not had to hold what it held. The shadow of furniture is a ghost’s footprint. There is a little boy drawing with his finger on a clean rectangle. It makes a squeak that raises the hairs on Viola’s arms. There is a girl in the corner reading a cookbook like it is a romance. There is somebody in the backyard turned away from the house to take something from a paper sack into his lungs because he would rather his mouth be busy than his hands. There is a woman leaning one hip against the sink and counting backwards from ninety in her head for reasons that will end in relief or regret. The church ladies, those Canaan pilgrims, have started washing the last of the something in the blue water and are telling stories to the plates they do not tell to their men.

And in the mind that has been dying to rest itself in one long line, there comes at last an open field. Viola steps into the front bedroom alone. The bed is stripped, and the mattress has made the small complain it could not not make. The nightstand that kept a glass of water and a Bible and a pin neither ever because you never know which one you will need sits stupid in its new honesty. The closet door is open. The smell is there. Not the oranges and not the sage but the other smell, the one that is not skin and is not powder but is the echo of a thing that large women carry in their pockets when they want to go out into the yard and see what the dawn will trade for their sleep. It is the smell of cotton, cold iron, and the part of your soul you cannot use up even if you are generous. She sits on the floor because the floor is all that is still there that can hold you. The dust has not had time to find its new home yet; it trembles like you. She considers lying down, face first. But the floor is honest and will tell on you. Instead she leans and rests her head against the side of the bed. Her hair needs water. Her lips are dry enough to crack. She feels for the pearl of the thimble in her pocket and does not take it out.

She tells the room something simple. “I am the last of her daughters,” she says, as if the room thinks she has more to produce and is waiting for the parade. It is a lie. She is not the last; there was and may be something else. People made in the dark that might still be telling their blood where she is. But in this house all the daughters Eula raised have either left or lay down and did not get up. Viola does not know what to do with the size of this. It sits on her as if she is a chair.

“What you doing in here?” Birdie half-sings from the doorway and then sees what she sees and shuts it. She stands with Viola in silence a moment because all they have left to take from each other is quiet. There is a noise somewhere of somebody failing to load a thing and a man telling him how to do it right in a way that is not helpful. There is a thud, then a memory. Viola looks at the window and for an instant sees her mother at it, just the shoulder and the deep, the deepness that was not sad but had in it the sadness of things that go on. She reaches a hand toward that bit of window, palm out. She lifts it in a gesture of plain for the leaving. She has more to do. Some things you do not have to do if you wait long enough, but this is not one.

By four, the house is a bowl with the broth poured out and a clean ring where it once was and a stray green onion, somebody’s thin good thought, left to be decided about. The money box is heavier in such a way that it hurts the women who must carry it to a car. The papers that will tell the bank that this is done are in a folder with a bent corner. Rollo, taking his leave and then returning two minutes later to carry one more thing because that is the way he says goodbye, tells her he will come back after six and do the last of the hauling for nothing because he is the kind of man who knows how to say nothing like a paycheck. Manny is gone with the piano and the mean women laughing in Europe. The quilts have found laps. The blue jars now stand in someone else’s sink under someone else’s good water. In the corner there is a small pile of what no one would take. A VHS tape labeled 1993 in blue pen. A chipped brown teacup. A tiny shoe with a brass buckle that never belonged to anyone in this family and yet lived in the back of the closet like a tune you didn’t know you could sing. A small, flat stone shaped like a wish. Two unmatched gloves. A jar of buttons like eyes in sleep. A map of Ohio with the truck routes thick and the counties named for the men they buried when they built them. Viola picks up the stone. She puts it in her pocket because it feels like a place you can rest your voice. She gathers the VHS tape. She does not own anything anymore that will play it. She puts it in her bag anyway. People keep things that cannot speak to them because it is good to be asked to learn another tongue.

Shirley brings her a glass of water and says drink. Viola drinks and realizes she is three-quarters tears and a quarter faucet. She catches sight of her reflection in the hall mirror as a woman passes it by carrying it out, the soft-faced woman from noon. For a moment, the glass holds both faces as if it is greedy in a way you can admire. Viola sees the lines in her own mouth and does not know if they have always been there. She tells herself she is doing something that can be called an act. She tells herself she is not the sort of woman who puts her cheek to the wall and says no. She tells herself many true things. Her mother had a saying: The truth can dress itself. It does not need your help to be good-looking. It only needs you to not whisper down its back like a woman jealous of her sister’s hour.

When the last person leaves, it is not a last in the way last pretends. They keep coming back for things they left, they keep sending children up to look under a pile, they keep asking directions to a town they were born in. But there is a time at last, sometime after four-thirty, when the house has her and the church ladies and one man asleep in his truck who was not asked to. The ladies fold up the last of the folding tables like a ribcage closing. They hand her plastic bags full of other people’s coins and say See to this. They say We go on then to the Fellowship Hall. There is kielbasa and onions, and if you don’t come I will be at your door tonight with soup. They say Call me if you feel that thing. They do not name the thing. They kiss her cheek in a way that makes their lipstick know her. They pass through the door and make their way over the little place in the yard where the grass does not grow because a boy with cars has lived there for three summers. They get into cars that behave for them because some cars choose drivers based on how they know how to be decided for. The truck that held the freezer grumbles, makes a choice, leaves. The porch tries to keep: it knows how to say Wait. It has been saying that, in one request form or another, its whole long life.

Viola stays in the doorway. The afternoon has turned. The shadow of the maple has been asked to show itself like a scar that healing forgot. The street is a frame now around a picture she cannot see anymore because the gloss is on it. She looks down at her shoes and sees dust. Dust in a town like this comes from somewhere and everywhere; it is the one thing they all share, the sugar and the dust. She takes the sweep broom from the kitchen corner—the last of the brooms because the other one was used to hit the back step when raccoons would not mind—and brings it to the doorway. She sweeps the threshold not because it is dirty but because a thing like this must be marked with an action that looks like a woman who has had an afternoon. She sweeps, and the dust makes that noise a broom makes that has hair from a person in it; she has always thought that. She says, softly at first and then to anyone who is listening and particularly to those who are not, Thank you for coming. She is talking, she discovers, to the furniture. She is telling them goodbye, each one as if it is a neighbor leaving on the bus for a war, as if it is a child she will see some Thursday but not this Thursday coming. She thanks the bowl that will not leave. She thanks the bed for refusing. She thanks the piano which is on a truck three streets away and is remembering the taste of the last key. She thanks the lamps for their steadfast lies. She thanks the table that has a dent in it the shape of a man’s palm because a man once thought he could press out of wood an apology.

She goes room by room turning out nothing, because everything is out. In each room, she puts her hand on one thing—the bit of wall where children once measured themselves in pencil and then eraser and then again in Sharpie when they were sure they were tall enough to survive; the blind that tries too hard; the back door knob worn gold in that one place where everyone preferred to catch it. She says her yes to them. She finds, under the paper that lined the drawer in the back bedroom, a note in Eula’s hand, not long: Put the spoons in the blue jar. Don’t trust water you don’t see boil. There is a phone number of a cousin who may be now under the soil or in a house five counties over that smells like this one. There is a word scribbled that looks like “on eagle’s wings” and might be “get eggs, wings.” There is her mother’s name written not in that stern script she wore like shoes but in the loose hand of a girl practicing. She folds that paper and puts it in the pocket with the thimble and the stone and the check, and she is as to be balanced as a woman with three children climbing her shins.

A cat appears in the yard, one nobody claims. It lifts its foot like a dancer afraid of splinters and looks at the steps like maybe you will not. She clucks her tongue. It pretends not to hear. It will come later, when the house is asleep, when it can put its head against the door and hear what wood says when it thinks nobody does. She hopes it will. Houses need things that do not human.

“Ready?” Shirley appears again and then again because she cannot help her body from returning once it has left; it is a kind of apology for all the other times she did not come back for friends when she should have. She is in the yard but she is also, in her way, in the front hall, reaching. She holds keys that are not to this house. “You coming?”

In a while, Viola tells her. In a minute. In a lifetime. In an afternoon. She does not know how to say any of that with a mouth that has been used their whole life to call small boys back from the ditch and say the money is on the counter, and have you been drinking, and God sees you, and which dress, and No. She makes the sound that is Yes and which is kin to the sound that is No in this town. She feels in her pocket for the big key that has lived in Eula’s purse since a policeman used it to let a lost child in and then wanted coffee, sugar no, and his partner said Girl, your mama don’t leave the house in the day, and Eula said Did I say she did? The key is heavy. It is older than she is. It is probably not true to anything now. The locks are new and will be changed again when whoever comes in here makes the house theirs. Still, she is its woman. She slides her hand around the key the way you slide a hand around the handle of a bucket of well water. She turns it in the old back door one last time, even though it does not catch anything. It makes the noise of old regrets and new weather. She does it again because she is allowed. She goes to the front. She pauses. She leans her hip against the framework of the open door and knows that to step is to be blind for a step. She tells the house the sort of truth you tell a creature you have loved and will not feed anymore: You were good to us. You were mean when you had to be. You learned all our names and did not tell on us to the wrong people. You were never too cold when somebody was left and had to sleep alone. If anyone says different, they do not know. She could go on. She is a woman. Of course she can go on. She does not. She closes the screen and hears the familiar pinch of it against its seat. She presses the big door until it catches its latch. The porch blinks at her. She steps down.

The air outside has changed from the beginning. It feels the way hands feel when they have held too much cold water and then you shake them dry. The yard is a new town, a field with all the crops pulled and the crows allowed to take their songs home. A neighbor grins and then looks away. A child announces a triumph with a stick and then looks for approval nobody owes him. The sky leans. The river, down the hill in its place where it believes it began things, goes as it goes. It minds its business until it doesn’t. Viola turns toward the street and walks where her steps decide to. She will eat kraut and sausage with Shirley and the round one and the one with the frown. They will press styrofoam onto wooden tables, and the sound will be like breath, and the laughter will begin somewhere and then come closer. Somebody will cry for reasons that will embarrass them later and that will become, by the telling two weeks hence, a story about napkins. A child will take a slice of bread that does not belong to him and be allowed to. She will put her thimble on the table and then put it back in her pocket because it does not like shining under those lights. She will drink iced tea that tastes like the porch in April. She will listen to a woman tell the shapes of her fear like a poem she hates. She will say Lord, and there will be an answer in the way a fork falls and the door opens. She will go home to her own house, a square one three streets over where the bed does not yet know she is leaving it. She will lie a while with her arm under her head and owl noises stacking their shelves in the dark. She will sleep, or she will not, and the morning will come anyway and ask little of her but to keep standing.

For now she stops at the corner, the part of the sidewalk where tree root made a bump when she was small that was good to jump. It is still good to jump. She does not try. She puts a hand to her lower back which has been complaining better than any Baptist all day and tells it It’s over. She does not believe herself. She hears behind her the house make not a sound. It would not. It is polite. It is proud. The wind makes the maple talk in its old language, the one you learn if you sit under it after dinner on a day when the sky can hold itself still. The neighbors in their kitchens lift their spoons and decide about more salt. Somewhere a boy kisses a mirror that left this house and does not know to be grateful. Somewhere a man unlocks a door with a key that used to live on her mother’s ring. Somewhere a crow says her name and means something else and says her name anyway. The porch, which has trapped more secrets than any minister, exhales. It is a sound so slight a stranger would miss it. Viola hears. She holds her hand in the air as if she is saying Wait to someone she loves. She brings it down and walks.
