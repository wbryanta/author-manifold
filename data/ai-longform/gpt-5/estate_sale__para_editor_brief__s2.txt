They were already lined up at the crabapple tree ten minutes before the hour, their shadows laid long over the dew-damp lawn like a flock of lean birds. Nora watched from the front window, two fingers hooked through the floral curtain she had not yet priced. There was a sign staked at the curb in her careful block letters—Estate Sale, No Early Birds—and beside it an arrow pointing up the driveway. That arrow, the bold insistence of it, seemed ridiculous now. The men and women waited anyway, some shifting from foot to foot, some peering over shoulders through the storm door, someone rattling a bag of coins like an infant with a toy. They’d parked crooked along the maple-lined street, big American cars nosing into the curb so their bumpers gleamed with water. Mapleton’s water tower rose blue over the roofs past the Methodist steeple, and you could taste the metal of the morning on your tongue if you stood still and breathed.

“Do you want me to open early?” Gloria asked behind her, already moving toward the front door as if the question were rhetorical. The smell of her perfume—powder and something sugary, lilac maybe—had been all through the kitchen as they sorted mugs with the chipped ones set aside and the “World’s Best Grandma” mug centered on the card table so clean you could tell it had been run through the dishwasher that very morning at Gloria’s, a kindness disguised as an errand. “You’ll get less haggling if you let them in before they get impatient.”

“No,” Nora said, surprising herself with the hardness of it. In her mother’s apron her hands looked like someone else’s, older hands, knuckles pink and swollen like a schoolgirl’s eraser. She had lined the pockets with masking tape ripped into little price stickers, tried to put numbers on what could be numbered. The walnut headboard: $75 firm. Depression-glass butter dish: $12. Her mother’s cardigans, beads still snagged on the cuffs: $3 each. She could not bring herself to label the piano, and so she had left it for last and shut the cover over the keys as if that would muffle the ache of it.

“Okay then,” Gloria said, and pressed her shoulder into her as if the two of them were the hinge the whole door swung on. “It’ll be fine.”

It had rained in the night. The day, by grace or coincidence, had broken clean and bright, and the weatherman would talk about a front moving through and unseasonably cool air, and Mapleton’s porches would ripple with conversations about whether you could leave the air off for a few days. Even now, the sun was warming the front steps so that the wet evaporated in ripples you could see if you squinted, and the concrete let up a smell like a sidewalk after Little League practice. A boy in an Ohio State shirt did a wheelie at the curb and let his bike fall to the grass, ran back to join the grown-ups already pressing toward her mother’s house.

She opened the door on the hour. She expected something ceremonious to happen—a bell to toll, a whistle to blow—but there was only the muffled squeak of her sandals and the shuffle of feet as the people filed in, their breath moving the air over her cheek like the draft when a door in a big building opens far away. A heavy man in a ball cap split the crowd, pointed past her shoulder and made for the dining room. A pair of teenage girls veered up the staircase in a spill of laughter like children let out of school. An older woman stopped two paces inside and closed her eyes and breathed deep like she was taking in the whole life of the place in one cruel, greedy sniff. Later Nora would think that must have been kindness, too, its own way.

“Morning,” a man said with a politeness that put a burr of pride in the word. He was thinner than the others, his mustache trimmed too carefully, his jeans bleached white at the knees in a way no honest work ever bleached them. “Heard you had a Lane chest.”

Nora put one hand on the back of a chair to steady herself. The hope chest had been against the big bedroom wall since the summer before she left for college, a gift her mother had scrimped for and made sound as though it had come down to her from mothers before mothers before mothers, although Nora had had the sense even then that her mother had bought it from a furniture store with a parking lot as big as a cornfield. She had seen June stand with her hands on either side of its lid as if praying into it, her head bowed, and when Nora had come home that afternoon and seen it there, she had thought what women must have thought forever upon receiving such a thing: so you think my whole life is linens.

“Back bedroom,” Nora said, and the man let the word lead him like a dog on a string.

Was it all this humiliating? That word surfaced in her like a fish and showed its silver side and dove again.

They had laid everything out with a fairness that felt almost pious, the way elementary school teachers used to set the prizes on Field Day tables: junk with dignity, knickknacks in rows, towels folded just so, a Mason jar of safety pins with the pointy ends all pointing up like a bouquet. The living room—the east room, June had called it, as if the word made it a place the pioneers pitched their first tent—was arranged like a museum exhibit of itself: one wingback chair turned to face the other like conversationalists posed in a magazine spread, the sofa angled toward the empty space where the television had been, its squarish impression still printed into the carpet. A tape line along the floor marked the piano where the bench had worn an arc, the carpet flattened by fifty years of one woman’s weight. Nora refused to put a sticker on the piano but had written on a 3x5 card PIANO - MAKE OFFER, and lying there, the card seemed like a note a person in extremis leaves for her family: I could not decide, do with me what you think best.

“Is this Pyrex?” a woman asked, already holding the mixing bowl in her hands. “Or imitation?”

Nora blinked. The bowl was yellow with black tulips marching around its belly; she could see her mother’s thumbprints faded into the gloss where innumerable egg yolks had been whisked, the slip of the spoon like a scar. Her mother had shifted sets constantly: Tupperware stained orange with spaghetti sauce; Pyrex that had come “free” with a subscription to Good Housekeeping; a metal bowl June said belonged to Nora’s great-grandmother although the story changed with the seasons. These things lived through fashions and then outlasted fashion itself.

“Pyrex,” Nora said, although she hadn’t the faintest and now that she had declared it so she felt guilty, as if she’d lied to a priest.

“Four dollars,” the woman said as if doing her a favor. The sticker said eight. Nora had imagined, when she was making those numbers, that they would be a kind of commandment: Ye shall not talk me down. Now the numbers softened in the heat.

“Six,” Nora said, and felt that she had saved something of herself. The woman made a little moue, a skeptical pucker of the lips, and then paid the six and stacked the yellow bowl in a cardboard box with a bed sheet inside it. The care of it was something like tenderness. Nora had to allow that.

By ten-thirty the house had become a sound. It hummed with the voices of people who belong to rooms neither in nor yet out of them—small sounds of drawers opening and respectfully shutting, the click of the plastic of hangers, low male talk you could follow to its shape but not to its content. Nobody shouted. That would have been indecency. People acted as if in church, thrifty church, measured. But here and there a stifled excitement rose up: a boy trilling a finger over the keys of the organ, a mother swatting the hand down without looking at him; a woman smothering a delighted squeal when she found three pink glass sherbet cups nested inside each other like girls huddled for gossip; two teenagers calling to each other from different rooms, their voices arcing in a private language. On the kitchen counter someone had set down a bottle of iced tea sweating down to a ring no coaster protected from it anymore. Nora wiped the ring with the heel of her palm and then, out of habit, fetched a dishrag and patted the place dry as if her mother would see.

From the back, in the dim of the hall, came the long thin sound of a drawer pulled for the first time ever from its slot and hitting the end stop with a clack. “Baby girl,” Gloria called, and then louder because the house absorbed the address, “Nora!”

“In here,” Nora said, and found her way past the card table piled with crochet doilies like fallen snow and the shelf of Reader’s Digests her mother had kept for the jokes, and the sergeant of the ball caps with unnamable stains.

“Money box is getting full,” Gloria said, and her voice was content. She tipped a fistful of bills into the cigar box and smoothed them with her palm like a woman petting a small, finicky dog. “That man with the cap took the chest.” She said it without question, a simple field report, as if somewhere in a ledger the chest’s leaving had been recorded long before anyone in this room was born. “He counted out every dime, too. Pride like that’s good even if it’s ugly.”

“How will he get it down the stairs?” Nora said. The chest was heavier than its size suggested, a piece with enemies. She remembered moving it in, the scrape it had made on the wall, the same scrape she had not spackled yet, an unfinished white scar at ankle height on the beige.

“Brought a son,” Gloria said. “She is not going to the dump, anyway, that’s the good of it.”

“She,” Nora repeated, and that was exactly right. The chest had been a her all along. She put her hand on the doorjamb and let her fingers find the gouge low where, when she had been twelve and had been in the delightful habit of slamming doors, her father had once put a screw in from the outside to catch it at the angle he liked. Once he had told her, calm as a recipe, that a house when it dries out in winter becomes smaller, air gone from it, and the doors won’t shut; and in summer it swells back and the doors are heavy like wet bread. He had died in March. March in Ohio swings like a gate.

“There’s a man looking at your daddy’s toolbox,” Gloria said quietly. “You want me to handle that one?”

Nora turned her face away so that Gloria would think she was a thing like a clock, checking the hour. She could not bring herself to go into the garage just yet. The cardboard rectangles of sunlight on the concrete floor, the oil shadows making geography there, the old newspaper with an end table leg set on it like a stone on a grave—it was all beyond her just now. Whatever fortitude she had woke up with that morning had pooled in her shoes. She shifted on her feet and tried to think of a reason not to let strangers carry the weight her father had gripped and slurred into tight bundlings for forty years.

“If he’ll love them,” she said. She hated the sentimentality of the sentence even as she said it. They were wrenches. They were sockets arranged like a dentist’s tray. They were a red Craftsman box with a dent on one side where her father had once been enraged at an engine that would not start and had not, because he was who he was, sworn at the car but had instead hit the box with a flat palm and then apologized to the box later by polishing it with a rag. Love had nothing to do with this now. She wanted them not to be junk to someone. That was all.

“Love is cheap at a sale like this,” Gloria said, and went to meet the buyer.

A girl came down the stairs wearing June’s straw hat, the one with the grosgrain ribbon around the crown, the ribbon frayed where a safety pin had held a silk gardenia for three Easters. The girl was seventeen, maybe eighteen, and the hat looked both ridiculous and inevitable on her head. Every woman in Mapleton had a hat like that once. The girl’s friend trailed her, carrying a cigarette case as if it were a jewel box. When they saw Nora watching they took the hat off and cradled it together, their faces open. “Is it too much?” the girl asked. “Three dollars?”

“It’s fine,” Nora said. It was her mother’s face she could not read. Was June somewhere in the room, muttering about young people and their stomachs out? Was she oddly pleased? If the hat left with laughter, would it be less of a leaving?

“Thank you,” the girl said gravely, as if Nora had granted permission for a wedding. She slid the hat on again. The brim tipped when she smiled. They paid and left with their little boxy shopping bags and that was how the hat left, part Lady Day, part cowgirl, something its maker had never seen coming.

For a time the day took on its rhythm. Every labor has its pattern. You open your hand and close it. You bend and straighten. You cup your fingers under the cool of ceramic and you pass it to a stranger. The light shifted through the big front windows from plain gold to veined, the cut shadows of the maple leaves making a lacework over the carpet like a grandma’s afghan. The next thing and then the next thing, and no thinking further than that. She and Gloria said a hundred versions of the same dozen sentences. Yes, it works. There’s a little nick there. No, there’s no remote. You can take that off the frame if you want the picture inside. Those words built a scaffold over the larger thing that had no administration in it at all.

Half eleven a man in a polo shirt came in and stood with his hands on his hips as if he meant to measure the east room with his breath. His hair was sheared so close you could see the shape of his head. He looked like one of the boys who had flicked acorn cups at her on the playground, grown forty years older and given a job that let him cut in line at bakeries. He turned in a slow circle and set his eyes on the piano.

“You want an absurd sum for that?” he said; there was no offense in the way he asked it, only a simple respect for the ritual.

“I don’t know what a sum would be,” Nora said, and the honesty made him look at her harder. The piano’s wooden belly was the color of honey; they had not stripped it in thirty years but it held its sheen like someone’s hair who had the luck of that kind of hair. Her father had found it in 1969 among the detritus of a church closing and had pinched his lip when he got it for fifty dollars and two jugs of homemade wine from a man named Slater who had later, it was said, set his own shed on fire for insurance and been exiled from the family to Dayton. It had been dinged when it arrived, the front left leg worn like a fingernail chewed short, and for a year the whole east room had smelled of Old English oil as June wiped the instrument as if she could feed it into repair. June had insisted that people with pianos were respectable, as if simply containing this freight of wood and wire conferred witness.

“Been tuned recently?” the man asked.

“Not since before,” Nora said, and the word stuck. “Not since last fall. My mother—” She stopped. The man nodded. He pushed the bench back with his knee and ran both hands flat over the keys and then lifted them, like a dealer flouring a table. He played a low chord and the floor replied. The house did. It was a perfectly good chord. He tried a higher arpeggio and faltered, smiling. “I took lessons,” he said. “Haven’t kept it up. Fingers remember even while they rot off.”

“I did too,” Nora said. Her teacher had been a widow named Mrs. Kellerman who smelled of powder and peppermints and had a mole on her chin that moved when she pronounced s’s. If Nora missed a week she would be scolded for disrespect, and if she missed two she would be made to bring Mrs. Kellerman a hyacinth bulb in a glass jar in spring as if atonement had horticultural rules. Nora had played “Claire de Lune” with more feeling than grace. The bench under her thighs had squeaked until June had oiled it, and then for a while the movement was perfectly noiseless and eerie, and then the squeak had returned.

The man was making a face of calculation—the throat jerk, the bitten lip—common to men about to say a number. “Four hundred,” he said, and he said it like a man at the end of his good will.

The number startled her. She had expected someone to offer seventy, eighty, a hundred if they were feeling flush. Four hundred would buy a plane ticket back to Atlanta. It would cover the water bill owed from the month her mother was in the hospital and had not been there to notice the leak in the upstairs bathroom. Four hundred would be a debt erased and a favor to herself she almost could not name. But the number hurt, too, because it was exactly what her father had mortgaged his late life to so they could always tell people they had a piano, and now that had its arithmetic and took up tidy space in a box.

“Five,” she said because she could not help herself and because June would have kicked her shin under the table if she didn’t at least try. “It’s worth more.”

The man stood straighter. He looked around the room as if the answer were in a corner, behind a plant or under the table. The air in the house was so used by then that it seemed a different species entirely. Everyone had breathed upon it and left their breath there to be breathed in turn. “Four fifty,” he said, and stuck out his hand flat, palm up, not as men put their hands out to shake but as you put out a plate.

“All right,” Nora said.

He smiled, only with his eyes. He took out his phone and in a minute two younger men were in the room, wide like refrigerators, both of them in shirts with logos embroidered neat above their chests. They had done this before. They raised the piano on its little casters as if asking a small horse to stand, and the movement of it made something in Nora’s throat dislodge. It was not as big as it had been all her life. It was a wooden object with a good back and a limp leg and it moved across the carpet with hardly any sound, as if it had decided to be agreeable to its removal. When they reached the door, someone—perhaps one of the refrigerator men, perhaps the neighbor boy who had wandered over to watch—stooped and slid an old dishtowel under the leg and the leg glided over the threshold without scraping. It was such a gesture of almost-free love she had to set her teeth to keep from crying at that and not at the piano itself.

The living room unmoored then. The space where the piano had been was not the space where a lack was. It was oddly clean, a block of paler carpet pressed flat under where the pedals had tucked, an area of wall a shade darker where the sun had not burned it the same way. Nora bent to pick up the 3x5 card and folded it and tucked it into her apron pocket as if it were some animal completed now, retired and ready to be put down for a nap.

A woman with a soft middle and shrewd eyes presented herself with two Pyrex pie plates and a half set of Corelle, the pattern called Old Town Blue that had been in so many kitchens around Mapleton that to see it now was to feel a certain civic coherence. “My sister had this pattern,” she said, and turned one of the plates in her hands as if to find a date faint in the glaze. “Hers got broke at Christmas. Husbands, you know.”

“Mine,” Nora said simply, and then said, “Ex,” as if it mattered, as if it explained something about durability. “Twenty for the lot?”

“Eighteen,” the woman said, doing the compact calculus women do when they know they will get their way and would like to let you think you have yours.

“Done,” Nora said. She liked this part: the money changed hands and she gave change and someone’s face turned slack with satisfaction. It reminded her of church bake sales when she was a girl, the jostle of the people in the fellowship hall, the tables with sheet cakes snowed in frosting, the quiet money passing down the line, the whisper of paper plates by the dozens. A person could feel purposeful there in a way she did not often feel in her life. Right now she was purposeful.

At noon Gloria insisted they eat the ham sandwiches she had packed and a container of potato salad the size of a small livestock waterer. She had set up the folding chairs on the back stoop. They sat with their knees knocked together and looked at the clothesline as if it were the ghost of some duty. The yard had been overtrimmed by a man she had hired by email from Atlanta; the hedges were squared in a haircut too tight for the house, and someone had edged the sidewalk with an efficiency that suggested a manual. Birdsong caroled from the hackberry, a full, rude noise. From the other side of the fence you could hear the thunk of a basketball and a little boy’s high voice narrating his own hoop skills in a stream of chittering talk that only once slid the word “Mom” into it like a triumphant note.

“You doing okay?” Gloria asked. Her lipstick left a tidy outline on the rim of the Solo cup. There was a softness in her when she turned from her hustle that made Nora want to lean her whole life up against her like an old animal up against a hitching post.

“I keep thinking I should be crying and I’m not,” Nora said, and said it almost cheerfully because if you could say it it was either not true or not something you needed to be ashamed of. “I cried yesterday at the vacuum lines, if you can believe it.”

“The vacuum lines are something to cry over,” Gloria said. “No one knows how to do them like our mothers. Why they’re a thing, I don’t know, but they are.”

“They make order,” Nora said. “Flatten a place that will not be flat. Mom used to do them and then make us walk around the edges of the room so we didn’t mess them up. It made my dad crazy. He would stomp across the carpet and point his shoes into every square like leaving footprints in snow. She would just stand and breathe through her nose and wait for him to be done with making a mess.”

“That sounds about right,” Gloria said. She tucked a curl behind her ear; sweat at the curve of her throat caught the noon light. “I found a recipe card in the drawer of that little desk. Said Corn Pudding II in your mother’s handwriting. Your mother, in her seventies and eighties, learned a great deal about the Roman numerals.”

Nora could see it, the neat loops of the script, the line at the top of the R that always went a little too far to the right, as if the letter wanted to become something else. She’d been carrying around the shoebox of recipes since Wednesday. She had not opened it. It sat on her hotel dresser like a pet you weren’t ready to look in the eye. There were things she could carry: letters, photographs, that shoebox. There were things she could not: a sofa, a handmade quilt too beautiful to risk spilling in her house where three cats lined up like apostles on the couch. She had left quilts from Aunt Lorene folded at the foot of the bed like flags in the funeral home and had told the first woman who asked “No” and then the second person who asked “No” and then the third had been a young mother holding a baby with its feet bare, and Nora had said that’s one-twenty and then taken a hundred because the woman’s husband had said he only had a hundred and she saw the way the woman wanted the quilt like she wanted a new life to wrap around herself, and wasn’t that what a quilt was for.

“The woman from over on Summit,” Gloria said, mouth full, hushed almost to scandal whispers, “she took the silver coffee service you never used. She said she would use it at Christmas. I wanted to make her prove it like you make kids do long division on the board. I wanted to holler use it on Tuesday like that would make Tuesday kinder.”

Nora smiled and swallowed the bite too big she had taken so she could have her hands free again to take the money box. Her throat hurt; she put a palm there, as if that could ease it. “I don’t even drink coffee,” she said, and they let the subject go gently into wherever humor goes.

After lunch it grew warmer. It was not, by the luck of the weatherman, a cruel August day that buckled mailboxes and melted the thought out of you, but the air thickened. You could feel each breath doing its specific work. The rooms warmed in small predictable bands—at the east window the sun banding down until it left and the shadow of the maple leaf lace cooling what it had heated. From the garage came the thud and soft curse words of men lifting things men lift, the sound of compromise: tilt it here, no, my end, your end, get under it more. The red toolbox went to a man with tar seamed into the lines of his palms and the beltline stain of a once-white shirt tucked into jeans with a boy’s belt. He opened each drawer and let his hand roam over the tools not as a thief but as a lover you had been faithful to. Nora told herself she would keep one thing, one thing that smelled of her father. She chose a flathead screwdriver with a clear handle and a nick near the tip where her father had, in a hurry, pried up a lid that did not call to be pried. She could tuck that much in her suitcase like a contraband apple.

In the hall between the two bedrooms, she paused and hooked her finger where a strip of wallpaper had peeled and never been smoothed back, a small scar, the curl poised and pretty as a girl’s bang. She thought of the way June used to tell the story of the tornado in ’74 as if the telling itself kept the roof on. The house had sucked in and out, she said, the walls breathing like a creature, the sound like a train coming through the kitchen, the light gone green in a way the world wasn’t designed to go. They had sat under the dining room table because June had decided it was the most godly of their pieces, a thing with legs that had held up all their Thanksgivings, and so it would hold up a roof if called upon. Later, when the sky had returned to blue and the street had been littered with leaves and someone’s laundry from half a mile over tangled in the maple, June had stood on the front step with her hands on her hips, and when the neighbors came around the corner alive and whole, she had wept without fuss, quietly as tea poured from a teapot.

“Thirty for the table,” a man said now. His voice did not come from the past but from the doorway where he had eased his hat back on his head and was looking at the tabletop with an appraiser’s thin mouth. June would have had his eyes out for sport.

“It’s oak,” Nora said, managing to sound surprised and patient both at once, though she had known it all the time, had known the weight of it and the way you had to put a pack of playing cards under one leg because the floor listed and the leg would list with it. “A hundred and fifty.”

“You’ll never get it,” the man said, as if she had said she would lasso the moon and drag it into the kitchen.

Someone else’s voice overlapped him—a woman’s bright and apologetic. “Excuse me,” the woman said. She had a neat face and the tired eagerness of late afternoon in young mothers, a way of smiling with her teeth as if that would stave off the ache in her cheeks. Two boys hung off her, one in each hand. The older one was at around the age of seven that makes a person into a machine for generating noises, the smaller had three goldfish crackers sleeping in the shelf made by his top lip. A baby kicked in a carrier on her chest. “Is this table taken?”

“It will be,” Nora said, and before she could say anything else, before the man at thirty could compress his contempt into a sigh, the father rushed in from the porch, out of breath from loading or from life, and said, “We saw it when we pulled up and—” and then he stopped, as if he had broken some law by saying he had seen the prize before he had been invited to see it. He was a close-cropped man like the piano buyer had been and had paint along his knuckles and a hairline the shape of a flight of stairs. He looked like a person who would do you a favor at eleven at night because you’d brought him tomatoes in August. “What’s the price?”

“A hundred and fifty,” the man at thirty said in a tone that mocked her gently and also admired her a little for putting the world at that rate.

The husband looked at his wife and the wife looked at the husband. “It’s too much,” she said in the voice wives use to say it’s too much, which means it is a small bit too much and not immoral in its too-muchness.

“We just bought a house,” the man said to Nora as if announcing an engagement. “We don’t have a table. We’ve been eating on a card table from my brother because we don’t want to eat off the couch. It does something to your day to eat off the couch.”

“Do you have a truck?” Nora asked, because sometimes you could love a story and make a bad decision because you loved it, and so she would make them do a little work for it. June’s voice in her ear said, Don’t you underprice that table to those starers. June had called everyone from Columbus starers, as if the closer you got to the university the more you were inclined to look too hard at things and not see them better.

“Borrowed one,” the wife said. “We…we had a smaller number in mind.”

“What was your number,” Nora said, and made it a question of tone and not interrogation.

“A hundred,” the husband said, and winced before he said it, and then smiled immediately so that the wince did not become the story.

“A hundred and twenty,” Nora said, before the other man could say thirty again. “And you come back and tell me at Christmas what you ate on it.”

The woman laughed. It was genuine and it tired her to laugh that way and she did it anyway. “We’ll send a picture,” she said, though they’d forget or they’d have a child with a fever on the night they meant to do it, or they’d remember at ten at night and decide that the morning would be better and then the morning would slide in a way mornings do. The husband held his hand out. His palm was cool. It had rained in the night. He smelled faintly of putty and soap.

Some man from the next street over appeared with a drill driver and loosened the bolts from the table legs and in a minute the table was in two big pieces and two little pieces and the family bent themselves around it in that way bodies do when they are trying to do something together and not to each other. The woman took a corner though the husband said, “Babe, don’t,” and she said, “Don’t babe me,” and the boys had the legs and pretended they weighed a thousand pounds, and the baby slept as if all this were the smoothest part of his day. Through the doorway and down the steps and then the truck with its welded tailgate, and the table gone. Nora thought of what had sat on it: a first birthday where her father posed the cake a moment for the camera and June smacked his hand for thinking of the photo before the baby; an autumn night when the newscaster had looked thirty years old and deeply tired and said a name for war; the way sunlight had always landed in the same square on it in December and warmed a place big enough for one plate. She pressed her palm flat to the air where it had been because the impulse was too strong to resist, and the warmth stayed on her skin for a surprising second as if you could touch what is not there.

As the afternoon loosened, as people moved slower, as someone opened the screen door and left it that way and the spring twanged its protest and Nora decided not to fight it, a man in a polo with a tiny horse on the chest held up a Ziploc of old buttons as if he were a pirate and these were emeralds. A boy came in who had mowed the yard last summer for June, his name Tomas, a boy so fine and serious with a manner that made the air around him feel respectful, and he asked if he could take the hammer and the string and the nails from the basement for his mother, who hung laundry on them in their own basement when the weather was wet. “Take them,” Nora said, and added, “Take the nails from the coffee can, too,” and she could hear her father from long ago grumble about waste even as she said it, and then could hear the softer grumble from a later long ago when he had become a man who said, Might as well take the whole thing, like a prayer.

Downstairs once to find something someone wanted—a cake plate, a particular extension cord with the big yellow head that would be safer in the rain—she paused on the lower step and let the cool creep up her arms like a draft. The basement smelled of earth and old laundry and whatever had made the cement in 1952, a smell particular and global all at once. The jars lined on the shelf had been empty so long the dust had a slope on them. The Folgers coffee can of screws had on its side someone’s handwriting in black marker that read SCREWS and then beneath it in her mother’s hand SCREWS, in case future archaeologists doubted. On the workbench someone had left a pair of pliers halfway open, a mouth frozen in deliberation. Taped above the bench was a picture torn from a calendar two Decembers ago: a barn lit like a fairy tale, snow heavy in blue light. A little bit of glitter clung to the barn that the printer had dusted there for effect. How long these trinkets hung on waiting for people to remember to take them down, and who then remembered.

On the stairs she found under her palm the rough grit of the mushroom-painted wall, a texture known to her hand since 1978, the year June had decided the basement needed cheer and had mixed an eggshell white with a cup of leftover red until the whole thing went the color of canned mushroom soup. “It’ll make laundry fun,” June had said, and Nora had laughed at everybody’s joke, which was that some things will never be fun no matter what you paint them.

At three, the sun fell into the yard in a soft way. She could hear a radio from somewhere—a ball game, muddled by static so that the voice of the announcer turned holy and distant. It was someone listing numbers into the air, numbers that made a hot afternoon into an event. The Methodist bells somewhere off downtown tolled seven longs for three’s hour. Mapleton had a way of marking time that made it easier and also heavier. Gloria brought root beer in bottles from her trunk and they stood in the doorway drinking, and people walked around them like the two of them were plinths, a part of the house, like columns. “I’m going to take your mother’s breadbox,” Gloria said abruptly. “I’ve had my eye on it for twenty years. I’ll bring you bread in it for a while, so you’ll feel like it’s not gone.”

“It’s yours,” Nora said, and in that instant pictured the box in Gloria’s warm kitchen, the lid lifted and fallen and lifted again like a breath, the dim yellow of her light, the steady hum of her refrigerator that you had to thump just there to keep it from rattling. She imagined the bread in it, a loaf glossy as a seal. “You’re a thief.”

“I am,” Gloria said cheerfully. “I stole you, too, when your mother died. I wheeled you out of that hospital in a chair and put you in my car like a person I had paid for at an auction. You were listing. You were no good to yourself that day.”

They laughed, the laughter a relief in her lower back, a sudden relaxing like unclasping a too-tight bra. She realized she had been standing for hours. Her heels hurt in a polite, insistent way like a child tugging her sleeve. She sat on the bottom stair and let her hands hang and looked into the living room from that angle, everything larger and less dignified from down there. A little boy ran through in his sock feet and slid, arms out, on the bare board of where the table had been, the rented runner displaced. Someone’s niece tried on a beaded necklace and then thought better of it and took it off and put it back exactly where it had been. A man patted a velvet chair purely because it was velvet. Softness commanded piety.

At four there were fewer people. The ones still there were the ones who did not mind the looking more than the having. A man stood at the bookshelf and read the spines of the books as if he had all afternoon to stand there in your life, and of course he did; you stand in other people’s homes every time you enter a grocery store. You read their lists into their carts and you know if they prefer cilantro or not. Nora felt that she could make a grand pronouncement about America in this moment and decided not to.

She picked up the small pink dish shaped like a shell that had always sat on the bathroom sink and had held hairpins and safety pins and a gold hoop earring that had been waiting for the other one to turn up from 1983 until last week. In the dish was a single bobby pin with a rust line. She put the pin in her hair the way she had put a thousand pins into hair while watching television and thinking unthinking thoughts, and she took the dish and set it in her box under the letters and the recipe cards. That, too, she would carry. It would fit just in the little curl of space to the left of the socks in her suitcase.

Her mother’s dresser gave way into being next. Two women—sisters, by the look of them, with similar ankles and different hair colors—stood with their arms crossed and debated midsize deleterious things: whether you could get lipstick off a satin drawer liner, whether the mirror’s foxing would make your face look blurred, whether the scratch on the top had a story or had just been a day-to-day scrape, an accident of buttons, not a memory. One of them, without thinking, pushed the drawer shut when it was open only an inch, and the sound it made was so exactly the sound from all the decades that Nora’s hands rose before her like someone playing piano for the first time. June used to shut the drawers with that exact impatience when everyone left their socks out or their belts half in and half out like tongues. “Don’t live like raccoons,” she would say, and the drawers would thwack and the air in front of the mirror would stir up a smell of talcum.

“How much?” the braver of the sisters asked.

“Eighty,” Nora said, and the bravest one nodded, surprised to have expected worse. It would take both of their men to get it to the truck. The sisters stroked the dresser’s top in a way that made it seem like it was their mother they touched and not the wood, any mother would do, the universal mother who told you to put a dishcloth under the lettuce so it did not go limp. Nora went to the next room to give them their privacy, to let them have the moment that happened when you left a room you would not come into again except in photos. People needed that.

What remained by late day was either too big to go in an afternoon, or too small to be worth taking. There were stacks of National Geographics that nobody wanted to lift and nobody wanted to throw away. There were baskets that had held nothing for fifteen years and could not be made to hold anything now. There was the lamp that had been June’s reading lamp the winter of 2002 when she had read nothing but thick novels about Ireland and burned through lightbulbs like biscuits. There was the brown recliner that had been the place where her body had rearranged around comfort after her hips gave her trouble and where she had watched the weather and then said the weather back to you like she had made it. A boy in a baseball uniform slumped in it and said to his friend, “Dude,” and laughed like he had said a hymn. Nora marked the recliner “free” and in an hour a man with an old pickup had come with his son and they had hauled it with grunting onto the bed of the truck and the man had slapped the boy’s cap down over his eyebrows in the way of fathers forever and had driven away slowly, the recliner peering like a sentinel over the edge of the truck bed.

When the last people had lingered and left, when the air had gone still as if an orchestra had paused, when even Gloria had, despite herself, looked at the time and remembered her cat and the way the sun comes through her own kitchen in the late afternoon and made noises about casseroles for tomorrow’s funeral lunch for someone else’s aunt, when the empty spaces were bigger than the full by a margin that frightened her a little, Nora sat on the east room floor and stretched her legs out and leaned her shoulders against the wall. The cool of the paint seeped through her shirt into the centers of her shoulder blades like hands. The house at this hour had always been a good house. The light from the big windows went down over the carpet in a long stripe. Outside, somewhere a bee insisted on a flower, the tiny motor of his work undeniable if ignorable. She could hear, far off, the long horn of a train single and then two together, and for a second she was eight again and then eighteen and then thirty and then now, all those her’s standing at the same window listening, all of them preoccupied with the bigness of June that pushed into any silence and now did not. Of course it did. It did, and it didn’t, and both were true and thinking of it that way was exhausting and so she let herself not think then.

Her phone, facedown on the empty bookshelf, wobbled and lit. Her brother’s name bloomed and then retreated like a fish. She put her hand over it for a second, an instinctive mothering of something that didn’t need to be mothered, not now, not this instant. She picked up. “Hey,” she said, and breathed. He said, “Hey,” and asked his smaller questions (“How’d it go,” “You eat,” “You need me to come tomorrow”) and she said the smaller answers (“Fine,” “Yes,” “No,”) and told him about the piano going and the man with the toolbox and the family with the table in a way that made those sentences into stones in a brook so he could pick his way across with her. Somewhere in there she said, “I kept a screwdriver,” and he laughed like a brother does, which sounds like childhood played backwards.

After, she walked the house once more like a person who has arrived at a hotel late and needs to stretch her legs after a car ride. The rooms were not empty, not yet. They had that quality of after-a-storm where you go outside and the birds come back and the sound is different because it’s the same but your ears have been wrung out. The path of the vacuum lines still showed up in the places no one had walked; the wet ring of a glass on a table would leave its lace for her to wipe; the beds, stripped and bare, showed the smudged outlines of bodies the way a paper does under crayon after you’ve rubbed a leaf. The kitchen sink had a pair of suds clinging to its basin like a dandelion head after the first good breath. On the hallway wall the family photos left their ghosts, rectangles a shade paler where faces had been, all that permanence peeled down and boxed. She pulled one more thumbtack from the plaster and put the tack in her apron pocket with six others, like a handful of teeth.

On the porch she sat on the top step and put her elbows on her knees. The air had cooled by a breath. Down the block, the little boy had graduated to his skateboard and went back and forth along the smooth part of the sidewalk in front of the Mays’ house, his shoulders hunched just so the way you have to hunch to balance. He wore brittle little kneepads like insects. Across the way, Ruth from the corner watered her geraniums and looked up, saw Nora, and did a funny little thing with her mouth like a bow and then lifted the hose as a salute. People in Mapleton had a handful of gestures each and they used all of them. A neighbor’s wind chime struck two notes and then a third, the way someone would play just enough piano to remind you it was still in the room. Somewhere a screen door thwacked, and a dog barked twice, the procession of sounds so everyday that they had, for this second, the glamour of fame. You could cry at such sounds. Or you could not. Nora sat without deciding, her bones chiming in their own small way. Down by her sandals a line of black ants traveled, single-minded, around the chalked rectangle the neighbor kids had made earlier in the week, HOPSCOTCH in pink letters, a square of numbers to jump through. The ants turned and turned around obstacles like water does, their work endless and simple. She did not envy them. She lifted her hand and pressed her palm flat on the cement step to feel the last of the day’s heat there, and left her hand there until it cooled under her skin and the streetlight at the corner blinked its little forehead on. Then she stood, went in, and pulled the front door gently to. The house took her quiet like it knew what to do with it. She stood for a second in the darkened east room, let her hand slide along the wall on the way to the kitchen, and then let the light bloom over the sink. Tomorrow she would sweep. Tomorrow she would box the books. Tonight she would sit at the bare counter and eat the last deviled egg in the Tupperware Gloria had left and suck the mustard and paprika off her finger the way a girl would. Tonight she would cup the pink shell dish in her palm like something living and put it in the shoebox and lay the shoebox by the door with her handbag and the screwdriver and the wrapped silver spoon she had taken to eat yogurt with in the hotel. The house exhaled with her and the day laid itself down inside it like a tired dog and that, for now, was enough.
