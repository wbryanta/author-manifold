They started coming down the walk before the shade had lifted off the grass, before the sugar ants finished their long commute from the back porch to the spidered syrup bottle Lorene had tossed in the trash last night. She had taped the bright signs the day before—Estate Sale, Everything Must Go—the letters fat and loud as if somebody else’s mouth had said them. Overnight a handful of flyers blew into the bare maple and fluttered there like a quarrel of yellow birds.

Lorene had not slept. She had folded towels in the room that used to be her mother’s room and then unfolded them again because they were not going to be anybody’s towels anymore. Somewhere near four a.m. she stood in the narrow kitchen and ran the cold water till her bones remembered winter. She listened to the pipes muttering to each other, those old men between the walls, and made coffee in the percolator because a drip machine would have seemed indecent in a kitchen with a linoleum floor cracked like a dry palm. In the minutes before dawn she could taste the melted pennies of the radiator and the ghost of starch in the pink curtains. When a mockingbird said his piece and quieted, she heard a house settle on its haunches, like it had once when a train used to pass, before the line was torn up and sold for scrap, before the factory turned out its last run of lawnmower parts and sneezed cold dust forever.

“You do it neat,” her mother had said years ago when the roof leaked and the roofer wanted cash. “You stack what you give away and you stack what you keep. You let the middle pile be the one you burn.” She said it about debts, she said it about grief, she said it the way some pray. That woman kept ledgers in her head the way other ladies hum hymns while stirring gravy. Now Lorene had stacks, all right. On the porch in vegetable crates Chipped/Cracked. On the dining room table Good China. On the braided rug: Toys & Books. In the little room off the kitchen where her mother plucked chickens at the counter, a box labeled Hardware that had in it no screws at all, but four skeleton keys and a hinge from the outhouse door.

They came with hats, with coolers in their cars, with folding money divined from elasticized waistbands, with babies on hips. A bearded boy, pale as piecrust, drove over alone with a trailer for furniture and backed it too far, kissed the maple trunk with his bumper, then pretended he had meant to do it. Women in church skirts came together and kept their purses zipped under elbows, the way you carry news. Two white-haired sisters wore matching rubber shoes and walked in a two-step that had not been choreographed but learned at birth. A tall man with a belly in a Pepsi shirt asked three times if there was any fishing equipment. A girl about nineteen with braids till her waist came with an old woman and stood by the vanity mirror turning her face left and right as though she was reading the wind for where it turned cruel.

“Lord, so neat,” said a woman with a brooch shaped like a leaf. “Some folk just pile it. Girl, you stacked this tidy.”

“It want to go tidy,” Lorene said, because the house demanded a kind of courtesy. “I put the measuring cups with the flour sifter. I put the quilts by the bed like they own to it.”

She had dreamed, or she had not dreamed exactly but rehearsed, what people would take. She had tried to make her heart do the same: this pile keep, this pile sell, this pile light with a match and see if there is a different way to watch smoke climb. But that organ, that rust-colored stubborn thing, had been keeping its own books longer than she could count. It had its own columns, not dollar and dime but want and refuse. It had no place on it to write down the taste of wasp in summer when you lick the edge of a soda bottle, the sound of shoes choosing their way down the hall in the nights when her mother’s ankles sang like nails in a jar. It had no place to write down the length of a porch where a girl could confess or deny depending on the way she sat.

She was fifty-four and said fifty-five if she wanted to be rude to herself. She wore her late mother’s apron because it held two deep pockets and because it seemed to her that an apron was a permission—busy and covered equals safe. She had a cigar box for change and a ledger with a pencil tied to it with blue thread. She had three checks already from people who said they would come back with a truck. She had an ear for who meant it and who was saying words to keep from admitting they did not know how to leave empty-handed.

“You take cash?” asked the Pepsi man.

“And checks,” Lorene said. “You put your name proper. Don’t put Junior if that’s not what the bank says.”

He blinked at that, scratched his nose. “You got that knitting needle set? My mama want some.”

“Knitting on the table under the window,” she said. “The case got butterflies.”

“I see them quilts,” said the beaded-brooch woman. “You selling the one with fans?”

“That’s for the church raffle,” said somebody who was not Lorene.

Miss Theola had come after all, wearing the hat she wore to bury folks and to welcome babies, the one with the netted veil like it had decided halfway it did not want to be a veil and fell open for rain. She had car keys and the church keys the deacons had given her last Easter, and she kept both on a chain that could anchor a boat.

“Raffle?” Lorene said. “You gone raffle my mother’s work?”

“Henrietta promised,” said Theola, which might have been true or a usage of the dead that could not be contradicted. “She said when she got to binding the fans she wanted folks to put their hands on it. She said some women wrap they sorrow in a quilt and some women cut it up like squares. Which one you?”

Lorene swallowed coffee gone cold. Her mother at the stove not looking around could say the words that folded you in with the flour, with the sugar, with the salt, could sift you till you was separate and you was not.

“You raze us up and you spending it all this afternoon?” asked the man with the trailer. He had a laugh stuck in his throat like a fishbone. He had the look of someone who owns a dog that would bite but only on orders. “I take that oak table off you. Two hundred.”

“It’s marked three.” Lorene had written the numbers last night after midnight, each nine and eight like a face with its own expression. She had put three hundred on the table because she remembered getting whipped for climbing under it, and punching holes in the oilcloth with a fork making cities, and because once Henrietta had pressed a hand there so hard in a telephone argument that the heel made a dent, and Lorene wanted to charge for that thumbprint. “And it comes with the leaves.”

“Leaves?” he said, his mouth adjusting to the idea of wood as plant.

“Two boards slide in,” said Lorene, making a motion that was not the motion, her fingers slow. “Make more room. You say grace longer.”

He turned to his friend who had shown up to help, a boy with hair like it remembered rain. “Leaves, man,” he said softly, and the boy nodded as though leaves were something can be bought and sold and got by weight.

Lorene watched them wrestle it, the table that had seen so many elbows, so many the dead and the hungry, out the door. It grieved her to see it cock its leg stupidly, like a washtub waiting to be kicked. She wanted to face the door and stand there like a witness for the crime of the sun on its surface, the way her mother had kept it shined till you could eat off it, till you could see your forehead frowns before company rang the bell. But she had to reach for the cigar box for another woman with a handful of spoons.

“Twenty for the lot,” said the spoon woman. “Don’t tell me the one with the flower different; that’s a sugar spoon from another marriage.”

“My mother had one marriage,” Lorene said. “And a handful of plans.”

“Mm,” said the woman, as though that meant all arrangements were temporary.

Lorene wanted to keep the radio. It had a big belly and two dials that made a click when they went past a sermon station to R&B. Henrietta had stood there ironing to the voices that promised another world if your money took you anywhere. On a certain Sunday, on a certain summer, a girl had danced to the radio in bare feet and cracked a glass saved from jelly because she forgot the floor was not a lawn. How that day her mother had not whipped her because grief had come and Henrietta’s hands were too full to lift a switch. Grief that day had come into the back yard smiling like corn and left crying like pneumonia.

“You selling that there?” asked a small boy whose mother was busy measuring drapes with her eye. He had a dollar folded until it would fight before it would open. He pointed at a wooden horse that had lived under a bed for decades.

“Quarter,” said Lorene without looking. “He got a missing ear.”

“So do I,” said the boy, lifting his cap to show where a dog had made a new shape out of him. “He race?”

“He stubborn,” said Lorene, which was answer enough for a horse and a boy.

A white woman with a tremor took two teacups and paid exactly, counting the quarters aloud as if to keep devils in check. Another woman, with hair pulled up high like promise, bought a box of canning rings that would never fit on jars again. “I like the sound they make,” she said. “You shake them and it’s like winter says hello back.”

“Miss Lorene,” said a voice from the doorway where the screen whined, “you got them jars still?” It was one of the Kendrick twins—nobody had never kept them straight growing up and nobody did now. He had a handkerchief that had seen all his years and looked like it had been pressed in the back of a Bible and used whenever the preacher got personal.

“In the pantry,” Lorene said. “Cases of them. We never broke for plastic here.” Her mother had called plastic “the kind of forever you can’t use twice,” and threw a spoon smudged with it out the kitchen window once like it had cussed.

He went in and stood a long while in the pantry with its high shelves and its papered walls where fruit had been drawn by a woman who liked the idea of pears more than pears. He did not touch anything with both hands at once. He said a sound that might have been a hymn but had no tune to it. “Your mama used to send me with strawberry jam when Bessie lost her boy,” he said when he came back. “She’d set two jars in a shoe box and tell me don’t you let that bounce. And I carried it like it was a baby to them. Her jam was the kind that told you the clouds would hold their water. How much for the lot?”

Lorene wanted to say for you nothing, for you take the lint and the spider under the shelf, for you take the way the pantry had kept summer for February and kept February honest when it said wait. But she named a price. He paid it and touched the door frame with his hand and did not look at her face.

They took the rug. They took the coat hooks in the hall with the key that made the screws let go. They took the butter mold that had a tulip on it. They took three handfuls of buttons and left the four that were the exact shade of smoke. They took the clock that had kept time without complaint when the rest of the house was late and took its own blue time when the rest was early. After a while the rooms felt like a mouth where a dentist had gone amok, gaps where there used to be a bite. Lorene’s jaw ached as if her teeth had been pulled instead.

She stood in the doorway to the back room while a couple looked at her mother’s bed. The wooden bed had high posts and the kind of headboard where you could hide a dime in the carving. It had held births and fevers, had held her father on the one night he came home muddy and soft, and held him the next morning when he said a certain sentence and put his hat on. It had held Henrietta through the three Aprils that took something from her and returned something else. Lorene had slept in it herself when her husband, that fifteen-year plan, left with his voice on a Saturday. In that bed she had tried the prayer you say with your thighs. Which did nothing but tell the rafters how old she had gotten.

“How firm is it?” asked the lady with the purse under her elbow.

“You test it,” said Lorene, her mouth finding sarcasm before it found sense. The lady blushed, and the man with her, who had red skin where a sun had loved him too much, pretended to tap the wood with a key like a baseball bat.

Lorene went and stood in the kitchen where the floor bore the mark of every step she had taken after her mother’s last Thursday, that long one where dinner would never come, that long one where Henrietta had sat in the rocker looking at the door like it was telling bad jokes. She had fought death as if he was a fool neighbor come for sugar. “You can have the white sugar,” she said to him, “but you leave me the brown. You have the Crisco; you leave me the lard.” Then she had lifted her chin and let him see the soft of it, said the come-get-me that belonged only to wedding nights and to last ones.

That morning in the kitchen there were circles cut in the air where the plates had been, there were pale squares on the wall where a calendar had prevented the sun from doing its year work. The place where the stove had mumbled was a shadow on linoleum that would not lift even under bleach. Lorene set her hands down on the counter and remembered the groove her mother’s wedding ring had carved into the wood: a river made of metal and soap. She had an impulse to set her own ring, the one she wore on string cause her finger swelled in July, into that groove like a seed.

“Baby,” said a voice, and she jumped like a wet match. Nobody there. Only the apron pocket with the tape and the pencil and the blue thread worn to its whisper.

She went out to the porch to shake herself like a dog shakes river water and saw that somebody had taken the garden hose and wound it in neat coils as if to say: I am a person who loves order, even in theft. Three teenage boys were carrying the sofa, which had been recovered twice and then once with a blanket when money got shy. They lifted it sideways and discovered the thing that lives under sofas everywhere: pennies with the queen’s head sideways, two hairpins, a piece of popcorn that had become bone, a half-finished bingo card, a photograph.

“Hey,” said the softest of the three. “Somebody left a lady.”

Lorene came and took the lady. It was from no year she knew, a niece of a day when collars were a species of cloud. The mouth had been cut out as if by a child making a coin purse. On the back somebody had written, in a tiny hand that thought it was a secret, a name Lorene did not recognize. She tucked it in the cigar box without meaning to. The boys heaved the sofa onto the truck and made male grunts to honor whoever invented torque.

“I think that bench is free,” said a small shy voice. It was the braid girl, her hands clean as laundry. She was standing near the low bench Henrietta had kept by the back door for worrying people’s feets. You came in with your sorrow dusting your shoes and tucked your heel there and she had her hands on you. She would say, Lift, and she would say, Wiggle, and whether you had been to a wedding or to a jail she could get the mud off the same. “Is it free?” the girl asked again.

“Nothing free,” said Lorene automatically, and the girl’s face folded.

Her hand had gone to her hair and come away with a small coil, preparing to chew it, when the older woman with her, who had been examining the egg beater as if it made history, came over and said, “Child, your eyes big as breakfast. You ask Miss Lorene polite.”

The girl did that, made the shape of careful with her mouth. “Ma’am, how much for the bench?”

Lorene looked at the bench and the bench looked back, the same old look it had given to all comers, an ennobling of the ankle if ever there was one. She did not want to set a price on the way her mother’s hands had moved over it. She did not want to sell the board where Henrietta had rested a heel and said to a woman with a torn dress, You gone leave him because you crouch like you waiting for the blow. She did not want to send that witness to a stranger’s hallway where nobody would know to whisper please to it.

“Give me ten,” she said, and the girl emptied her whole hand of coins and blew one that was sticky from being handled too much and said, like a vow, “I won’t put nothing heavy on it.” Her old lady smiled like bebe blueberry pie knows its own scent, and took the bench herself for fear the girl’s wrists would announce their frailty.

“Keep the bench in the shade,” Lorene said without planning to speak, “and it will never dry out and crack.” It was advice about wood and about whatever lived inside wood.

Out back a man with his own toolbox was taking down the birdhouse because somebody had given her two dollars for it, calling it Craftsman, as if birds had a union and had made their demands. He was frowning into the screws as if they were scriptures with hard words. Another man tried to lift the wringer washer and swore in a prayerful way when it told him how heavy water’s memory is.

In the front room two ladies wearing matching Bible covers raked through the photo albums, their mouths uh-uh-ing at people they did not know. “Look how sharp he dressed,” said one. “You’d think he had sense.”

“And look you here,” said the other, drawing a page up like laundry on a line, “Henrietta in a pants suit. You couldn’t get me in them pants to go to the toilet.”

“She could always wear a thing and it obey her,” said Lorene, and they looked up with love not for her but for the woman who had taught them to cover their heads when they went to court and to take off their hats when they went to bed.

“You keep any of these?” asked the first lady.

Lorene hesitated, fingers on the edge of a page where a small boy was hid behind a goat. The boy had once been her brother for two years because he had climbed into Henrietta’s lap and said he had not had a lap before and that he believed in hers. He was a child the county had given to them because the county had a problem called a mother with no house, and they kept him through two winters and a summer until the mother came with her hair fixed and her dress starched and a new man whose name was ringed on his finger and said she had come to get her boy. “We will send you jam,” Henrietta said, and then she had sat at the table and cut paper into shapes till her hands turned snow. The boy had left holding the goat’s photograph and would not look back. Lorene had not seen him since. He would be a man somewhere. He might be the man who had just taken the garden hose like it was his.

She said, quieter than she had meant to, “I’ll keep one album. You pick out five pictures you like and I’ll let you have them. Put them someplace where mothers have to pass, so they see dress and hat and try to do right by theirs.”

“Pastor’s wife got a cork board by her office,” said the lady with the trembling Bible cover. “She put dying notices and babies. We’ll lay Henrietta up there between.”

They went soft when they said Henrietta’s name. Her name had been made for a woman who wore aprons low and walked the road faster than the bus. Her name had been made for a woman who could make a spirit hush. She was dead three weeks, two days, and a part of Lorene still waited to hear the broom hit the baseboard and say get up now you’ve had enough sleep for a lifetime. Of course the broom would not speak. The broom had gone too—to the woman with the cleanest house in the county who said she needed it to touch the places under the beds where sorrow rolled like marbles. “This broom know how,” she had said. “It got memory already.”

In the space where the sofa had been, a small man in a too-large jacket squatted and smoked. He was thin as wind and had eyes the color of a bottle left in rain. “I fix things,” he said to nobody and then to Lorene. “I fix screen doors and bicycles. I fixed your mama’s toaster when it burned bread black as school shoes. She pay me with peach preserves.”

“I know you,” Lorene said, and they were both a little surprised at the relief in her voice. “You Jesus-with-a-wrench. She said that.”

He grinned like a girl. “I take that name only in fun. Though I did once get a wheel back under a baby carriage when a backroad stone knocked it out like a tooth.”

“You want something?” Lorene asked, cruel and kind in one word, and he shrank like the lit end of his cigarette.

“I was gone ask for the jar funnel,” he said. “The one like a green bell the way it sat on the jar mouth.”

“Take it,” she said. “Take the one with a chip. It a man has to earn his kitchen blood.”

He left five dollars anyway, creasing it like he was making a hat for a dwarf, and she wrote his name in the ledger, Smitty, and a note thereafter that meant nothing to anyone but her, a looped word that could be read as mercy, or measly, depending on the light.

A woman she knew from school days—never could find her first name again after they all got married and remade themselves—came to the door with a long face. “My sister want that cedar chest,” she said without greeting, and the words sounded like the chest had told them itself. “She waiting in the car. She can’t come in with her hip.”

Lorene pointed with the pencil. The cedar chest had been moved from the foot of the bed to the front room to save men from going down halls that had known more secrets than they should. It had been the house’s safety box in a town that auctioned off safety every Sunday with the pies. Henrietta had kept blankets in it and also letters from the women she had helped when they came to hide at her house for a week or a month, to get strong again, to feed babies till their eyes sat right, to heal from the man who thought his name designed the air around him. Henrietta had a gift for making strong people admit when they were weak and weak people sit up and earn their bread. The chest had held a little of this, a little of that. It had held Lorene’s hair in a cut-off braid because once she became a certain age she cut it herself with kitchen shears, standing in the mirror and telling the woman in it: the world will not carry what you will not.

“How much?” said the long-faced woman.

“It’s marked too high,” Lorene said truthfully. She had put an imaginary price on the cedar not to sell it but to keep people polite around it. “It’s worth the smell, and nobody puts money on smell.”

“We pay it,” said the woman quickly, then, seeing something in Lorene’s look, went slower. “Or we pay what is right. I can call my sister. She waiting in the car with her hip and her man. He say it look good at the foot of they bed.”

“If it’s for her,” said Lorene, “you tell her don’t put sorrow clothes in it. That cedar feed off loneliness. You lock up grief in there and it’ll grow red and oily and get into your shawl when you wear it. You tell her keep sweaters and baby sleepers and the letters that say we coming for Thanksgiving. That way the wood stays behaved.”

The woman nodded, then looked like she wanted to say something else and did not. She paid what Lorene had asked—a number both of them could believe in—and then went to the car to fetch two men. They lifted the chest like it had eggs sleeping inside.

“Henrietta let me sleep under her table when I had to go,” said a voice Lorene knew immediately because it had been the voice under her mother’s table saying thank you to the gum that had lost its spit. “She said to me, crawl on under and pray with your knees up and your mouth shut. That table held me like a pew.”

“Gladys,” said Lorene gently, because the woman who had crawled like a dog had been baptized as Gladys before she was ever made to go to a stranger’s home. She was dressed ordinary now, but the ordinary had been pressed into an armor with a iron that left a ghost seam down the middle of her blouse. “I sold the table. That make you mad?”

“I ain’t mad,” said Gladys. “I ain’t mad how you gone sell things I got no right to. She saved me for me. Ain’t no furniture in that.” She offered a twenty and took away the potholders she favored, the good ones that had never singed, and folded them as if they held bones.

By afternoon the house looked like a poem with a third of its nouns blacked out. Shade had moved up the walls and left its mark, a tide line of old-lady lavender. Someone had gone into the bathroom and come out with a bottle of aspirin the color of sugar skulls and said five dollars for the jar, and Lorene had said take the jar, throw away the lie. The kettle had gone. The best of the china had gone, the cups heavy as promises. The small things had been nickeled away, a doorknob, a hook, a bolt, like people thought strength lay in the things that confine opening and closing. The bed went finally in the cargo of the red-faced man, and Lorene watched it go and felt a restraint in herself she had learned from her mother: you can cry or you can count, not both at once if you expect to keep your numbers straight.

What had not gone was the rocker. Henrietta had worn its creak the way some women wear perfume. The rocker had its seat patched with a piece of blue jean where a minister’s boy had cut it with a knife he said was for whittling and that had whittled nothing but cloth in its life. The rocker had seen more repentances and more lies than a judge. Every confession made in that rocker had the same beginning—girl, and the same end—eat something. It sat by the front window and watched the traffic of the street as if it could keep drunks honest and boys on bikes from falling.

“You letting that go?” said Theola, who had returned from taking the quilt to the church and had come back with her mouth full of good silence. “That chair got a mind.”

“I can’t take it to my place.” Lorene had a two-room in an upstairs where the stairs apologized with each step. “It take up a whole corner.”

“You gone sit on what then,” Theola asked, “your pride?”

Lorene almost barked then, a sound she made when her mother did this to her, snatched the food off the plate and let her look at empty spoons. “I take the kitchen chair,” she said. “The one with green paint either steady as marriage where it held, and gone where it didn’t.” Her breath made a noise she could not identify. “You want the rocker?”

“I ain’t take Henrietta’s chair,” said Theola with the same shyness she had when a man came to her to ask if the deacons could keep him from drinking his funeral. “That chair belong to the kind of woman she was. I don’t rock like that. I sway. I hum. I put my hands on the table and lay down the truth. Won’t do to pretend me into her place. I learned. I learn slow but I learn. You better keep that rocker.” Then, seeing the ledger open to a clean page and how clean pages look like debt to people like them, she added, “Or give it to the child with the braids. She needs something to sit in when she think how heavy that bench is gone be.”

Lorene put her palm on the rocker. It was not warm. Wood at rest holds its temper. It remembers hands not like heat remembers iron but like a smell remembers a throat. “I keep it for a while,” she said, lying a little in a way that honored a future she did not see. “I keep it till Saturday.”

On the third hour, when the light dropped its shoulders on the mantel and made faces of things that were no longer there, a man came who had once worked at the factory with her mother. He had a thickened neck and a steady way of standing that made people get on with their business. He said, “Henrietta could out-grind men twice her size on that line. She keep tally for herself and for folks who lost count. You know what I mean by that.”

“I do,” Lorene said, because she remembered the way her mother came home with powder in her eyebrows, that gray on black, and a paycheck that had the boss’s thumb on it. She remembered Henrietta saying, not to the boss but to the money, you won’t tell me who I am. She remembered the day they struck and made signs in the backyard, and Henrietta with her mouth in a set it usually wore only to funerals, and how she had said to the neighbor boy, paint this big: You won’t get both my back and my soul. And how the management laughed into its plastic cups and then how it did not laugh for a week.

“I want to buy that hammer,” said the man. “The one with the tape on the handle where the wood split and grew back.”

“That’s my father’s,” Lorene said, hearing the way possession works in the mouth when the thing you say you own is the thing that left you.

The man nodded like he had expected as much as that. “I got a boy,” he said. “He like to fix. I figure it matters what kind of things you teach a boy his hands for. A new hammer tell you to hit. An old one tell you to place.”

Lorene went into the room where the box of Hardware had been a joke and came back with the hammer. It had the look of a man who had run before and remembered when to stop. She extended it handle first. The man took it like a communion cup. “How much,” he said.

“Whatever,” said Lorene, and then, catching herself, named a price that made the man lift his chin with gratitude rather than surprise. He left the money and the sound of his boots on the porch told the house that it had been properly addressed.

Near four o’clock a man Lorene did not know came with a posture of expecting to be agreed with. He had hair like corn silk and a mouth one might expect to sweet-talk or to spit. He went through the rooms as if he had been told to find something wrong. He tapped a wall and shook his head. He looked under a sink and made a face. He came to Lorene holding the small cast-iron skillet as though it had sworn at him.

“This pan of yours won’t sit flat,” he said. “I ain’t pay full for something that rock on my stove.”

Lorene felt her mouth go to her mother’s mouth before she could check it. “It don’t rock,” she said. “You rock. It sit the way it sit. That pan been known to discipline a bean. That pan seat-belted a biscuit.”

“I take it for one,” he said, and Lorene said, “It’s marked eight,” and he said, “It’s not worth that,” and Lorene said, “Then go somewhere where things have worth to you,” and the man squinted his eyes like a man in a bad photograph. He put the pan down with a click, then, seeing he could gain nothing in the mouth of a woman who had eaten from that pan and heard it forgive her twice, he turned and bought a picture frame for a quarter, a frame that once had sat on the back of a toilet tank, and left as if to character-reference somebody else.

“How you doing?” a voice came soft from the porch, and it was Alma, the woman with the hip, who had sent her sister to buy the cedar chest and now had sent her hip to carry her into the house like modesty on a crutch. Her man stood behind her holding his hat in his hand as if it might make a thunderclap. Alma had not come by in the nights since the news. “You holding?”

Lorene looked at her and had two voices run in her chest at once. The one her mother had taught her: Say fine. Say we make it. Say God good. Another that she had learned under a different roof later when she lived alone and had to tell the sink why she cried: Say it true or don’t say it. Henrietta had believed in slow spilling, in catching a tear with the web of a hand and smearing it like ointment. But also Henrietta had believed in stirring sorrow into the gravy and feeding it to people so they did not catch it by surprise at the table. Salt you use, she said, but you don’t keep the shaker in your pocket.

“I could be worse,” Lorene said. “I could be sleeping on cinder blocks in a field. I could be walking a road in bare feet that got glass claws. So I’m upright. That help?”

“It help me to know,” said Alma, and that was the speech she needed to make.

Behind them a little dispute had started over the price of a framed picture of Jesus knocking at a door, and the argument had a ridiculousness that might have made Henrietta laugh with her tongue. “I pay two,” said a boy. “I pay two,” said a girl. Smitty said, “He ain’t worth less than three,” and everybody, even Alma, smiled like they were all in papers, like every head was bylined. “Take it,” said Lorene, throwing the sentence like bread. “Pay me when you see me next.”

“You say you had three Aprils,” Alma said when it was quiet, and then had the look of a person who had stepped into a puddle wrong. “I mean, Henrietta said.”

“She had three Aprils that leave with hands empty,” Lorene said. “And a winter that gave her a handful. Sometimes it work like that. She never vacuumed in April after that. Said she vacuum one April and took something up she needed, and what’s lost is lost. So she left the dust to rest till May came and told it to move.” That was not answer to any question asked that day, but it was the truth Lorene owned. She paused and felt a weight sit down behind her eyes. “Come answer for your chest,” she said, because this woman had come on two legs that had carried more years than a body had and needed delivering from polite.

When Alma and her man had gone, when Theola had left the smell of her lotion on the porch rail and the twins had taken their cases of jars and lovingly cursed them for being heavy, when Smitty had held the door for a woman with a couch cushion because doors remembered good manners better than most folk, when the bearded boy with the trailer had leaned against his truck and smoked a cigarette so sorrowful it seemed holy, Lorene went and stood in Henrietta’s closet that was not a closet anymore but a rectangle of air that had known wool. She closed the door and turned without turning and saw shoe-scuffs on the baseboard, each a map of a hurry. Her mother had not been a woman to own multiples of anything. One dark skirt for court. One light dress for summer. One sweater that made your wrists look right. A coat you could tie and tie again until it became the name of your winter. The hangers rocked on their pole and sounded like someone on a porch swing far away.

“You are tired,” the closet said in the small-dry voice of cedar. “I am tired,” Lorene answered, and it felt sincere between them.

By five o’clock the bearers of bargains had dwindled to the curious and the mournful, like guests at a wake who were not invited and found themselves looking at the napkin edges. The air had turned that thin Ohio blue it got in late afternoon in a month whose name did not tell you what coat to wear. Lorene’s feet had decided they could be not-feet if they wanted. Her fingers shook when she touched coin. She let the last lady take the aluminum cake cover for a dollar though she had priced it heavier because how do you price the sound of a cake cover—a dull angel, lifting.

Only then did she think of the basement, the place under the low stairs where cold had done its knitting each winter. She had set nothing up for sale down there because nobody had ever found anything but screws and sprickets and last summer’s damp folded on that concrete like a sheet too big for the bed. But now, because it was almost evening, because there was a pause between one breath and the next where you either lay down or you pick up a broom, she went down holding the rail and let the cool on her cheeks tell her she had not done all she had to do.

The bulb blinked and then held. In its light the cement floor showed the parts where Henrietta had thrown water to keep the soot down; the coal bin still leaned in the corner though it had been empty as an insult for twenty years. There was a trunk, and Lorene did not remember a trunk. It was the old kind wrapped in canvas that had eaten color and kept taste. It had three straps like proofs. On it was written in white, in her mother’s block letters that did not so much communicate as command, KEEP LAST.

Lorene sat on the third step and let her mouth decide what breath to take. The thing with the afternoon is that it had been long and it had been a trickster—always more noon under the kitchen light, always time to look at a cup and remember every mouth. Now it went animal, now it went quiet as a fox. She got up and went to the trunk and touched the straps. They had a stillness that told her they had been waiting the way bread waits—patient, but with an edge. She unlatched the first and then the second and then her hands forgot the third because something in her rib roared.

“Not yet?” she said, and whether to herself or to the wood, she could not say. She sat back on the step and think of a time when Henrietta had set a pot to cool with a cloth over it and said, You let the heat leave before you taste, else your tongue will forget you swear. The trunk was that pot. The trunk was the last heat in the house.

“Miss,” said a voice from above, and Lorene turned like a crime, saw the small boy with the horse, cap in hand. “My mom say thanks,” he said. “The horse is stubborn,” he added proudly. “He bite my sister’s doll and won’t say sorry.”

“You take him outside when he like that,” Lorene advised. “Don’t you let him grow that way in a small room.”

He nodded and his eyes were gray as dishwater and clear as sky. “I find a marble,” he announced, opening his palm to show the world’s small round with its flame inside.

“You keep it,” she said. “You lose it, that’s on you.”

He went away, leaving the smell of child behind—a clean sweat, a call for supper. Lorene sat a long while listening to the house breathing through its pipes. She could hear the words her mother had not said fill the spaces where furniture had been. She could hear the way Henrietta chose not to lie. Not because of virtue—virtue was a lace doily Henrietta had no time to wash—but because lying is a kind of borrowing, and she paid cash. When Henrietta lied it was for someone who could not own the truth yet. “You hungry?” Henrietta had asked the girl hiding under her table, when the answer had been no and made out of shame. “No,” the girl had lied, and Henrietta had lied back: “Me neither,” and fixed two plates.

Lorene went to the trunk again. The third strap unbuckled with a sigh like a room tired of being closed. Inside on top lay a shawl, the cheap wool kind you got with coupons from the Green Stamp store and wore like prayer when the heat went out. Under that a box of recipe cards written in Henrietta’s hand, that square-chopped hand that cut its way through the meat of a word: Liver Loaf, Catfish Friday, Three-Bean Sometimes Four-Bean. Under that another box that rattled like a baby with a secret. And under that letters. So many letters, folded like little tents, soldiers from a war she did not know had been fought. Lorene touched the edges and felt paper bite her.

She lifted the top handful. Women she did not know who wrote of girls she might have known; men who wrote as if their teeth were bad, ashamed of noise. People who had worn the front room like a cloak and then walked out naked and clothed. Thank you, and We got to Cincinnati all right, and The baby eats and eats, and Tell your husband I fixed the porch step with that board I found and I didn’t take it from nowhere and don’t tell him I did // he know // because he a man and see what is wood. Henrietta had kept them not out of sentiment—sentiment is for picture frames—but because she never wasted a proof. If sorrow could be turned into a ledger, she had ledgered it. Here was her profit. Here was her balance.

Under the letters, at the very bottom, under something that might have been a folded dress and might have been a curtain that had decided to be a funeral shroud, lay a small thing wrapped in a white rag. Lorene held it the way she had held a bird once, not wanting to make it remember a hand. Inside was a lock of hair pinned with a paperclip and a note: For the child I kept until I could keep my own self.

She sat down on the floor as if a knee had left the bone. She did not cry. She had done her quota before the morning was done, in the bathroom where the mirror knew how to look away. What she did was bear, like you bear a bowl lightly so soup does not leap out. That hair had a kink just like her brother’s that time they cut it too close and it sprung up like prayer. That hair was serious. It had been in the dark for a very long time and said nothing, because hair is a historian and knows when to be silent.

Lorene placed it back where it lay. She was not sure if she was allowed to open the next letter. She wanted to feed those letters to Theola, to Alma, to the women with hands on their purses, so they would know that their own ledgers could be kept too, that numbers were not just numbers but names of days when you got up with nobody and made eggs anyway. But there are some things even a sale can’t sell. She closed the trunk and patted it the way Henrietta had patted a small girl hard enough to bruise comfort into her shoulder.

At last the afternoon let go of its top button. People came to carry away their lasts; boy with the trailer returned with an old man and they took the bureau and left a small envelope with the weight of apology in it. “My sister say this is from her to you,” he said, and Lorene opened later to find a picture of Henrietta thirty years back looking away from the camera as if the camera had asked something rude. The twins came back for one more case of jars and left a cantaloupe that had been warm in the car, left it on the counter like a season. Someone came to give back a key that would not fit their own life. The skillet man returned with his woman, she with hair rolled in fat sausages that could feed a whole county fair, and offered five—still poor—and Lorene, who had watched two flies make love on the windowsill without malice, took the money and handed the skillet over because nothing in this house had forgiven pride unless it was braided with hunger.

When the shadows in the rooms finally turned into pieces of the night and not a part of the day hiding, Lorene shut the screen and it complained the way it had every summer when teenagers came for slices of melon. The porch had a small tired in it she had never heard before. She counted out the money because counting gives you a thing to do with fingers when beating them on your thighs would be rude to empty chairs. She wrote names in the ledger—some of them real, some of them inventions because the man with the hammer had given her a word she wanted to put down: Place. Then she took off the apron and turned it inside out as if to undress shame and hung it over the back of the rocker.

The street grew dark one porch light at a time, called to duty. Somewhere a baby was being disciplined by a bathtub. Somewhere a woman in this neighborhood stood still and told the pot: simmer. Somewhere a man told a lie that would cost him, and somewhere a girl looked at the bench she had bought and sat on the floor instead, because she did not want to sit on something that had known too many re-shoed heels and thought yet that maybe it might teach her something about patience if she let it look at her awhile first.

Lorene went from room to room closing windows, hearing the screens whine like old ladies getting up. In each square where a thing had been there was a ghost of dust where the sun had tried to love the wall and been refused. She touched those tongues of negative and left a fingerprint. In the kitchen she turned the slow faucet off with both hands and thought of the first time she lifted a pot without being told, and how Henrietta had said, You grown now? and she had said no and the pan had said yes. In the front room she moved the rocker back two inches, because that was the spot where the creak would not startle a person into confessing too early. In the small room where changes had perched like sparrows, where girls had whispered on a phone made hot by ear, she found one hairpin that had defied all brooms and picked it up and slid it on her own hair behind her ear, the way you put on evidence when you want to see if it burns.

She opened the front door once more without thinking and stood on the step and the night walked with its fists open at last. Across the street the Popes’ grandson was fixing his bike with a light on his head and a look on his mouth like It’s me and this metal and we going to get somewhere. The smell of somebody’s grill whispered pork at her, and she thought of how her mother had rubbed spice between fingers like measuring for prayer. Down the block somebody put on a song too loud, a woman’s voice that had not been paid what she was worth to sing it, and she let it come into the yard and lay down in the clover.

“Ma’am,” said a voice. It was the braid girl, bench already in her house because she lived next door in the duplex that had been repainted on two sides. “You forgot this.” She held out the cigar box with change because Lorene had left it under the rocker as if money was a child to be told to stay put.

“Keep a quarter,” Lorene said, and the girl did, and then remembered and took the smallest smallest coin instead, not so much out of greed as out of the taste of learning. “You gone sit and think in that rocker?” the girl asked, not disguising the hope.

“I can’t think in that rocker,” Lorene said, and smiled at the girl’s hurt. “It think for you. Better you sit in a straight chair awhile.” The girl looked relieved to be permitted to be young. She left with a nod and the bench waiting inside to teach her ankles new mercy.

Lorene closed the door this time for real. In the empty house the sound of her breath made territories. She would sleep here one more night: That had been agreed with herself and nobody else. Tomorrow the locks would know other keys. Tonight she had a trunk in the basement breathing quietly below her and a rocker that remembered the same songs she did, and a kitchen chair with gum under it as if children had been there within the hour.

She undressed in the room that had no bed in it now and lay on the rug that had not been rolled because she had told the boy not to, and the smell of other people’s feet whispered. Above her the ceiling cracked in deliberate lines like a palm that had held a fortune it did not say out loud. She folded the apron for a pillow. She turned on her side and then her back and then on her other side and thought of the first night she had come here baby to a house too small for her cry, how Henrietta had sprung up and made room with the ferocity of a woman who had declared she would make space even if space had to be cut.

“I did this good?” Lorene asked the room, asked the chairs not yet taken, asked the sink that had learned the shape of her grief, asked the trunk that had allowed her to read the first lines and not forgiven her for not reading the last. “I did this correct?”

The house said what houses say, in the language of rafters, in the logic of late day, in the small notches a man’s hand had made in a doorjamb measuring child. It said nothing. It held. It let the night come in through two inch of window and lay down quiet, lay down like smoke lays down after a fire when the fire has not been mean. It put its floor under her, uncomplaining, and made the creaks that scare only thieves.

Lorene put her left hand under her cheek until her ring, tied to its string, pressed a message there. She closed her eyes and did not sleep. She went back in her head to those afternoons when Henrietta sat with a girl who said nothing and took her shoes off for her and said, Wiggle, and said, That’s your blood talking. She went back to the day the police came about a boy whose skin told them something that was barely true and whose heart told her mother the rest, and the broom had hit the baseboard with polite impatience. She went back to the first time the rocker had said its two notes: rough on the forward, smooth on the back. That was its truth—the push was harder than the return. She let that thought fan her, a church fan with a funeral home advertisement on it, and cities and names she would never go to. Then she let nothing fan her. Night did not land like a bird. Night seeped up from the basement, like cold does, like truth does, and wrapped her ankles first.

She did not open the trunk again that night. She would in the morning, perhaps. Or she would not. The letters there were the kind you carry in a pocket with a hole in it on purpose so they can fall through to the lining and stay. She did not count the money again. Counting would tell her how much she had lost to the day, and the numbers would not line up with what had been taken or left. She lay on the empty rug where her body made new impressions in the dust left from furniture feet and said in a voice even the house barely caught, Mama, I let them take the table, and waited for clucks and scolds. But no scold came, only a scuff from the porch where Theola had left her weight, a mouse sounding like a god in the wall, one cricket who had been fooled into existence too late in the season and sang like it preferred regret. The house did the thing it had always done. It did not answer questions it had already held the answers for.

Close to morning, a breeze came up the alley and found the front of the house and tested the screen, and the screen did its song, and Lorene smiled despite all the rooms needing feeding. She got up and opened the front door, let the dim gray of a new day rub her jaw with rough stone. The street looked small as if folded in half. Across the way, on their porch, a woman in a robe took in the milk that had not been delivered because no one delivered milk anymore, but she went through the motion because that muscle had memory. Somewhere beyond, a train that was not there anymore called out of time. Lorene lifted her hand and let the air fill it, then lifted the other, then put both down on the doorframe and leaned in as if to tell it something she had told it so many times as a child it knew it before she said it.

“Keep,” she told it, just once. “Keep what you can. Let go of the rest. Don’t keep it where it rot.”

She shut the door not angry and not soft. She left the key where it always hung, on a nail driven into a place no one had ever agreed was a place for a nail. She stretched out again on the rug and breathed air that had known the bodies of her and hers. Then, as light began to untake the darkness with the double-mindedness of a morning in Ohio, she slept, not holding anything, or everything, in the way you do when grief and thrift and love have made their piles and burned the middle.
