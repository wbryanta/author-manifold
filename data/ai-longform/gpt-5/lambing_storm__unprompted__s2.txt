By the time the wind turned from a long whistling to a flat slap against the tin roof, the first ewe was already on her side in the corner pen, neck stretched, tongue showing dark and heavy between clamped teeth. Ruth saw the pulse in the hollow of her throat and the hard hiss of breath and felt the old knowledge settle in: this one would not be easy. Her headlamp cut a yellow cone through the wool-dust and steam, making each floating particle a ghost. The radio was burbling something about a late-April anomaly, gusts up to fifty miles an hour, heavy wet bands through till morning, but the shed swallowed the sound, wool and wood and straw softening it until it was just a presence.

Jamie had wrapped his scarf around his lower face. He stood at the gate, skinny and straight, hands fisted in his pockets like he was holding warmth in there. The dog lay by his boots with her chin on the slats, ears forward, making small huffing sounds each time the ewe groaned. The smell of it was everywhere: iodine and molasses lick, wet wool, yesterday’s manure and last year’s hay, and something sharp and new every time a lamb slipped out and found its first cold air.

“I don’t like her eyes,” Jamie said.

“She’s tired,” Ruth said, though she didn’t like them either. There was a rolling to them, a glassy wideness. The ewe still had her bag intact, white and bulbous, hanging like a lantern from the vulva and rock-hard because the lamb inside was pressing against it with more body than it should have. Not one lamb, Ruth thought. Two, probably, big for this little Mule, too much good grass on that east lot with its early flush if you got down out of the wind.

Ruth pushed the pen gate and went to her knees in the straw, the damp seeping through the kneepads she’d strapped on two days ago and forgotten about until tonight had made everything inside her notice its own edges. “Come on sweetheart,” she murmured. “Let us help you, eh?” The ewe didn’t lift her head. Her flank moved under Ruth’s hand, hot and jerky, and when Ruth slipped her fingers into the tight ring of vulva, the ewe shuddered and gave a small moan that came from the back of her throat.

She had been a good one last year, nose-first with neat feet, two lambs, both up and sucking within twenty minutes, none of this violence, none of this effort, the kind of lambing that made you believe in somebody loving you.

“Gloves,” Ruth said, and Jamie was there with the long plastic sleeves, sliding one over each of Ruth’s hands, smooth it up over the elbow. He held the bottle of lube and squeezed a line onto her palm, and she slicked it with the other and then shaped her fingers to a beak and went hunting.

A lamb’s foot was right there but flat up against a nose, both on top of each other like a sloppy mess someone had kicked under a table. The little hoof was cool and smooth on her fingertip. She felt along the leg: yes, front leg, knee bending the right way, and another leg flopped somewhere, the angle wrong, the head not straight. “One leg back,” she said. “Head cocked. Can you see if we got power flickering?”

It was already dimmer in the shed, a change you could taste. The string of bulbs that had been put up along the lowest roof beam made triangles of light on the backs of the ewes in the main yard, and then it was as if somebody had put a heavy hat on the shed. The bulbs went to amber, then to a dull red, then nothing. The radio cut to a click. All at once the only sounds were breath and chains shifting in their rings and the wind trying to take the roof in its hands.

Jamie swore softly.

“All right,” Ruth said. “Headlamps it is.”

Midge sat up, as if light loss meant work. The dog looked at Ruth’s face and panted once, then lay down again, because she was twelve now and stiff in the hips and had been told enough times in the last year not to get up and try and run the pens. Tom would have clicked his tongue and jerked his chin at this dog, and she would have held the whole line with a single look, no chaos, no talking. Ruth let her fingers find the lamb’s mouth, felt the soft gummy thing of it and the velvet of the nostrils, and with her other hand she slid along a hard little jaw until she had it cupped. The ewe grunted and tried to push when Ruth could have used a breath to think, because this is how it goes: things happen in once-permitted slices and never when your hands are ready.

“Squeeze some more lube,” she said through her teeth, and Jamie did, the cold slick sliding down her wrist. She eased the head back into the wet body of the ewe, felt the muscles tighten like someone tightening the leather on a drum, and then she left it there and ran up the leg, cup-to-wrist along a cannon bone, until she found the knee. The hoof was folded to the shoulder. She worked blind, saw it in the map in her head, and hitch by hitch she brought that leg forward, feeling the place where it wanted to catch, speaking in the old low voice that had been in her since the first spring, not Tom’s voice but before that, her mother’s, her grandfather’s, the voice of the whole shed that winter you learned you earned what you kept alive. “That’s a girl,” she told the ewe, the old foolishness of praise to a creature who had no idea what any of it meant beyond the pain and the pressure and the hole she would pass it through.

When that second leg was up where it should be, she ran along the bridge of the lamb’s nose again and this time kept it straight. “Now,” she said, and Jamie had the rope ready, the bit of orange baling twine with the lariat slip in it, and she looped it under the lamb’s little mandible and around each forefetlock. “When she pushes, you pull.”

“How hard?” Jamie’s voice was thin. Boys’ faces weren’t made for swearing out here, they weren’t heavy yet and the words slipped off them. It made her want to cry and to laugh at once. His chin was rougher now than it had been a few months ago; it reminded her of frost on posts.

“Just enough,” she said. “You’ll feel it. Same direction she’s pushing. Don’t jerk.”

The ewe’s belly bunched and the world narrowed to movement. They were both in their bodies and the lamb was in its, and there was no room for anything else; there was a wind and a night and the things that had been lost, but there was also right now this.

Jamie pulled like he’d been taught. The little hooves showed, dark and plastic with their birth tips, then the chin, then the big head with the tongue still showing, eyes sealed in white film and then blinking. The shoulders, the sudden easy pour of a new body, all the slick of water and blood and the sharp stockyard metal smell of afterbirth. Ruth was already stripping the bag from its nose, thinking of air, air first and then anything else, and she rubbed the chest with straw. The lamb coughed and jerked its legs once and then drew a long awful sighing breath.

“There you are,” she said, like the baby had been hiding on purpose and she had found it.

The ewe looked at the lamb with a dawning interest. You could see the thought return to her in the old way: mine. She turned her head and bleated, weak and hopeful, and Ruth put the lamb up near her face and got fingers in its mouth to make it yawn again, make the lungs expand.

“Another,” Jamie said, and he was right; you learned to listen to women and dogs, and you learned to listen to ewes whose bellies still twitched. The ewe raised her leg to clean, her tongue rasping the lamb’s head, and Ruth went in again and found the next one cocked in the canal, only one leg down, the other out and elbowed back. Bigger. Of course bigger. Twins always took turns trying your patience. She did it again: wet, hot, slippery movements in the dark, feeling her way like she was reading words carved in old wood. When it was done, there were two lambs and one was bigger and slow and didn’t want to breathe. She stuck a bit of straw up its nostril and it shook its head and sneezed and she laughed, hard and relieved, and wiped her mouth with the back of her hand and left a smear of blood there and didn’t care.

“Spray,” she said, and Jamie was already following her in the rhythm of it: iodine on navels, tick-tick-tick of the yellow spray paint on flanks, a little six over the ewe’s back so they could match them if someone got wandering. He grinned, just for a moment, his headlamp making his eyes white. He looked like himself at six when he’d sat on the straw and let a newborn butt his shin and had whispered in its ear as if secrecy was necessary to life.

Wind went hard at the shed and tried to come through. Loose tin rattled on the south side. In the main yard the job pens were full of ewes who had lambed last night and the night before, heads stuck through the pallet slats, black noses twitching, breath steaming. Heat lamps hung where they could, red circles on the straw like little fires, but now they were cold metal bulbs hanging dark like fruit gone bad. A lamb in a corner made a thin desperate noise, and Ruth took one look across the crowd of bodies and said, “He’s lost his mother.” Jamie had learned, too. He was over the rail and wading through the small moving waves of backs, murmuring low, the way Tom had taught him, lowering his body to their level so they would not spook.

She could see him ducking between shoulders and counting. His shoulders were narrow but squared in some new way, his neck longer. He had on Tom’s oilskin coat, rolled at the sleeves, the collar turned up. All winter Ruth had caught herself from calling up the stairs at night, from asking for something in another room. Her husband’s name was a thing with weight; if she let it go it would hit the floor and break something.

Jamie found the lamb under the rack and lifted it and tucked it under his coat without thinking. The lamb vanished into that yellow slicker like it had gone to ground. “He’s cold,” he said, and came fast through the crowd to the pen’s gate. “He’s like a stone. We need the hair dryer.”

“Power’s out,” Ruth said softly, and watched the thought land in his chin.

“Oh,” he said. He looked toward the house, across the dark yard with the pattern of old tractors and the humped shape of the fuel tank and the ditches filling with the white stuff blowing horizontal. “What then? The warming box is— it’s just a box.”

“It warmed plenty of lambs before we got electricity out here,” she said.

“How?”

“Sheep farmer magic,” she said, and he rolled his eyes and she said, “Hot water bottles. The kettle. Blankets. Our breath.”

He nodded once, and then he flinched, because a ewe two pens down gave a sound that was not a sound Ruth liked at all. It was a long, flat, exhausted groan that ended in a cough. Another in trouble. And there were a half-dozen in the main shed yard who were pawing and sniffing and lowering their heads, contracting, beginnings all down the line.

“Go,” Ruth said. “Take him inside. Put him in the box with two bottles. Take a skin if we have to graft. You know how to do it.”

Jamie looked at her and did not look away. “I know how,” he said finally, and Ruth nodded, because he did. They had skinned one last week when a ewe’s lamb had been born dead and another ewe’s had been disowned and had stood there with her head held up and looked triumphant about the other one while this one shook on the floor. Jamie had watched as Ruth had cut two slits and made a little coat out of a dead thing and had slid it onto the live and the ewe had sniffed and wanted it all at once. He had not thrown up. He had cleaned the knife after. He had gone out after and looked at the wind and at nothing and Ruth had not called him back. He had learned a name for the knife’s shape: skinner. Like there were tools just for the bad parts, and that made it slightly better.

“Do you want me to fill the flasks?” he asked, lamb hidden under his coat, face sharpened by resolve.

“Please. And check the generator. Don’t start it; I’m not sure I want to burn gas for heat lamps all night. But see if it’s in a mood to run.”

Jamie nodded and went, his boots finding the thick straw places like he had done this every day of his life. Midge stood, got half the way to her feet, then lowered herself again when Ruth made a small motion with her hand. “Stay, old girl,” Ruth said. “We’re here.”

The next ewe was a heavy one with a belly like a barrel. She had a prolapse spoon in already, bright white plastic against the purpled tissue that wanted to decorate the world in front of it. Ruth had put the spoon in herself this afternoon between snow bands when the ewe had sneezed and tried to put all the important inside parts outside. Now the spoon had done its job and here they were, labor pressing on it from the other side.

“Let me look,” Ruth said. The ewe’s eyes were half closed, as if she were making something in her head that didn’t require sight. Ruth eased the spoon out and moved it aside, then waited until a contraction had gone by and went in with two fingers first, gentle, mapping. There was a foot, and another foot, oh good, and— tail. Of course tail. A breech. Backwards. Fine. She’d done hundreds. She set her jaw and eased the legs back, trying not to tear anything, not to let the air get in wrong, and then she had them out and tied with twine and worked the lamb backward, quick because the umbilical would pinch and this one would not breathe until it was out. “Come on,” she said into the ewe’s flank. “Come on.”

The lamb plopped into her hands like a net full of fish, big and heavy and too silent. She turned it and let gravity drain, rubbed hard with the straw, longer and harder than she’d want for a living thing, but this was the small violence people spoke about when they said such work was love. She pressed down along its ribs. It did not breathe. She put it on its back and compressed its chest with two fingers in little strong pulses. She put her mouth over its nose and blew gently, like a kiss. It lay there, slick and white-faced, eyes sealed, and then it coughed and spat and wriggled like a thing waking from a deep joke, all arms at once. Ruth felt something inside her unclench that had been there all winter. “All right, you little bastard,” she said. “You’re fine.”

“Jesus, Mom.”

She looked up. Jamie was back already, damp at the hairline with melted snow, cheeks flushed pink under the dirt. “Fast,” she said.

“I put more water on. The generator clicks when you turn it, but I didn’t pull it. The box is warm. I put him with the bottles and wrapped him in the towel and rubbed him. He blinked. He made a squeak. I think—”

“Good.” She smiled at him and he smiled back and the smile made him look like both the boy and the man he would be, and Ruth looked away because her chest hurt. “Milk that one,” she said, nodding at the ewe whose lambs were still damp. “She’s full. Strip some into a bottle in case the little red one doesn’t get up.”

“Can I stomach-tube if he won’t take the teat?”

“Two hours,” she said. “Give him a go. Tube after.”

He nodded, eyes on the ewe’s udder as he ducked to the side. He had learned to sit into a ewe’s hip and not get kicked; he had learned that the milk did not need dignity to be essential. He always had this way of falling into a thing until the sides of him were what they were meant to be.

Someone knocked, or something did, on the shed door. Not hands. Wind. The door puffed in with a deep whump and filled again. The trough heater out in the yard would be dead now and the pipes would be thinking about seizing, and all the things that had been held together would need replacing if you let them get too far in the night. Ruth put it in a line of thought and stood to check the lambing jugs. The redbulb sheen was gone. In one of the corner pens a lamb that had seemed fine two hours ago now hunched with its ears down and its mouth slack. The mother bleated at it and nosed it, then looked up at Ruth. “You,” Ruth said to the lamb. “Oh, you idiot. It’s too cold, isn’t it. Your engine stalled.”

She slid into the pen and scooped him, and he tucked his head under her chin because everything about it was too much. He was all corners. She unzipped her jacket and shoved him into her body heat and zipped it again, and she held him there like something stolen that you want to keep. He was light. She could feel him not doing enough.

“Jamie,” she said, and he looked up, his hands wet with milk. “We’re going to have to fetch more straw anyway. We’re going to need bales.”

“Now?”

“It’ll be worse later,” she said. “And if a ewe drops in the main yard in this, we’re going to be fishing lambs out of drifts.”

Jamie glanced at the door. Snow came through the crack between the slats in a fine line like sand. His nostrils were white with cold already. “Okay,” he said, and put the stripless bottle down and stood.

He had been scared of the dark when he was small. He wouldn’t cross the yard to the hopper without holding someone’s hand even at noon if the day had gone grey. They’d teased him, gently, and he’d allowed it and still not crossed until he could do it himself. Ruth went to the shelf and took Tom’s hat from the hook. She did not smell it. She put it on her own head and took her gloves and picked up the coil of rope. The lamb in her coat twitched like a thought.

They pushed the door and the wind took it and dug at their shoulders and there was the white of it, hard and flattening, like the world had been erased. Each post had a cloak. The yardlights that always made Ruth mad for their orange sting were out; the house windows were black. Somewhere in the distance a line snapped; a whip- crack and then the high whistling hum of a wire going wild. Ruth closed her eyes and took three breaths and then walked.

They had left the straw-stack tight and covered with a tarp big enough to sail a boat, ropes over in a crisscross that Tom had done like lace once because how else do you make the whole thing worth looking at. Now it was somehow more visible in the snow because it was a dark that had lines, the ropes proud under white. They went sideways like crabs along the fenceline. Jamie kept his hand against the boards to know the world was real and not just the stuff it had become. Ruth slipped into the lee of the stack and turned to let the door in her coat open to the lamb’s face. She could feel how cold her own chest had gone when she’d given him the warm place. Stupid heart, she thought; this is how you keep yourself beating, you give it away and hope it comes back.

Jamie hauled on the tarp and it stuck. He tugged again and this time it came like a sheet full of marbles, a drift sliding off and hitting him in the face. He ducked and swore into his scarf and laughed at himself in the same breath and was more himself than he had been in months. Ruth threw the rope over a bale and worked it under, half blind. Her gloves were stiff with old lanolin and now wet and would be cold soon. They got the first two bales into the sled and put one on top of the other and Jamie pulled, shoulders down, and she pushed, and they went to the lambing shed door like children getting a prize home in a storm.

Inside there was a kind of joy because here were ewes under your hand and straw under your feet and something to do with the dark. Ruth left the cold lamb in the warming box with one hot water bottle under and one to the side like a mother’s belly and flanks, covered him with the old wool blanket with the red stripe she’d had since she was a nineteen-year-old and thought this life was something you could set down one day and pick up another. His head poked out like a turtle’s. His ear twitched. If he had a name, it would be something you didn’t call him out loud, because you never name what you want to avoid loving.

She crossed back to the job pens and peered into each little world. A ewe nosing a lamb until it stood on matchstick legs. Another with two already trying to nurse, the ewe dancing in small circles like if she moved too fast, the need would outrun her. A lamb in the far pen, small and dark, lay with its legs stretched and its mouth a dark O. She put her fingers on the ribcage. It was working, but it was not working like it should. She carried it to its mother’s teat and put it on like you do a child, two fingers on the tin-soft jaw, two on the back of the head, nudge and hold and wait for the swallow. The ewe clamped. The lamb’s eyes went wide. “Drink, sweetheart,” Ruth said, turning the nipple and squirting the milk on the lamb’s tongue. It startled and then remembered somewhere deep in its gene-land why it was in this shed at all, what the years of their breeding had been for, how weed and wind and man and dog made them this way: eat and live. Its tongue flicked. “Atta baby,” she said, and the fat drop of triumph that a good latch is slid down into her and warmed a corner.

Jamie was back again and again with bales until there was a dry layer renewed in the main pen, and then the light changed a fraction because the storm thought of something new to do. Ruth looked at the clock that wasn’t lit and then had to look at her watch which was too dirty to read until she wiped it on her sleeve. It had been two hours, or maybe three, or ten. In a lambing shed the day is only when you look at the door and see a different color of cold. She wiped more and saw it was just past midnight. Dinnertime, some other world somewhere.

There had been no dinner for months that hadn’t tasted like its missing part. She made herself turn away and not call the missing man’s name into the straw. He had been dead since October on an afternoon of barley heads and blue sky. She had been lifting the net of wrapped bales with him standing on the trailer and he had laughed because the wind had whipped his hat off and the dog had taken it and run like a colt with a bucket. He had been the color he always was, then and then not. His body had made a sound on the trailer, a thick heavy sound as if a big bag of grain had been dropped and split. His hand had hit the low rail and hung there for a second. She had gone to him and his eyes had been looking at something else, and she had said his name twice and then had to say it in a voice she did not know she had before she put her face in his neck and learned that dying is hot. Later, when the doctor with the quiet voice had used words like catastrophic and sudden, Ruth had cried while the woman from next door had gripped her shoulders like she was a reluctant runner on a starting line and had said to hold on. Jamie had stood by the stove with one hand on Midge’s head and had not said a sound.

He had learned to use the tractor in November. He had grown six inches by January and the jeans could not follow. He had fallen asleep in his dirty clothes more nights than not since March. He had once said in the bitter way of being fifteen that farmwork was just homework for the body and she had told him that was the first good joke she had heard a kid make about land in twenty years. They had a way of not saying the thing and then doing the thing well enough to make the saying not necessary. Except in the quiet, when you heard your own bones click and your breath and the old dog and the storm, and then you said it to the roof.

Another ewe started and did it easily and Ruth got to stand and watch and not touch, as if you could make a child be born with only wanting it. She smiled and swore soft and kissed her teeth and said, “Clever girl,” to the ewe when both lambs stood and fell again and stood again and circled to find the udder. She thought of all the mornings she had walked out across frost and heard the high thin bleats and the answering low chuckle of mothers and had felt bigger than her one body, and of the nights when nothing lived and had felt smaller than a sheep tick.

“Mom.”

She turned. Jamie was at the door with his hood back and his hair plastered to his forehead and his eyes too bright. “What,” she said, brisk because he was thin glass at this minute and she would not bump him.

“In the west pen. I think her head’s back. I tried but I can’t— it wouldn’t—”

“Okay,” she said. “Show me.”

They went. The ewe in question had gone down and then up and then down again. The straw in front of her was a dark fan. Her sides heaved. Jamie had baling twine on his wrist like a bracelet and the gloves were already half on. She looked young for a ewe; maybe a first-timer. Your first is often what defines you. If it goes hard, you learn in your bones to fear the next.

“Tell me,” Ruth said, and dropped her knees to the straw.

“I felt a shoulder, I think. I felt a neck. There was a jaw, but it was— it was like tucked— like—”

“Head back,” Ruth said. She nodded. “Good call. That’s what it is.”

He exhaled and some of the air in the shed changed. Ruth slipped a glove on and got lubed. “When I go in, you push her when she’s not pushing, and you hold the outside open. Like you’re asking to borrow a whole of her and not just the part of her that hurts.”

It was a thing Tom had said once in that shed with a grin, and Jamie had heard it and had turned red and had rolled his eyes and then had said it himself two months ago with a half smile and the exact same voice Tom had used and Ruth had had to go and pretend to look for iodine so she could cry into an empty box. Now she thought of his father’s mouth saying it and pushed that thought away too, because now was her arm inside the ewe, hunting in a small space for a lamb-head turned wrong that had to be made right or the ewe would die and the lamb would, and then what.

She found the nose, pressed against the wall of the canal like a child hiding with both hands over his eyes. She found the lower jaw and tried to bring it, but it just slipped against everything. “He’s a slippery little bastard,” she said, teeth bared, and got her fingers behind the lamb’s poll, behind the knob of the head, and pushed it back a whisper so she could swing it under, hands full of bones and life, the ewe’s contracting ring of muscle going from a help to a hindrance and back to a help with each surge.

Jamie had his hands on the ewe’s vulva, easing the edges out of the way so Ruth’s knuckles would not take the skin and tear it. His hands were gentle the way his father’s had been: sure and calm and full of a sort of faith that had nothing to do with God. He looked at her face when he couldn’t see what was inside the ewe. His eyes asked everything and she tried to have every answer in her face.

“There,” she said into her chest and then, louder, “There.”

The head came forward— not all the way but enough. She slipped the twine around the jaw and the two fetlocks and then told Jamie to get ready. They were both so focused they did not notice that the snow had slowed its rattle on the roof to a softer hiss. The ewe pushed and Ruth pulled and the lamb came, shoulders scraping in the ring, like a boat through ice, and then he was out to his hips and then all of him. He did not move.

“Air,” Ruth said, and had straw and wiped, and Jamie had the bulb and squeezed the mucus out with the efficiency that made Ruth know he would live to someday not need her. He rubbed the chest and the lamb gave a cough and then a kind of wet sigh and then the breath was real, in and out, and he shook his head like a dog. “Oh,” Jamie said, like he was being given a thing no one had told him he was going to get.

They pushed the lamb to the ewe’s head and she licked, quick, even bitey, like she was angry she had been asked to work so hard. Jamie leaned his forehead to the pen rail. Ruth squeezed her shoulder and felt the line of his scapula like a ridge run under her palm. He had lost the softness long before this night. She wanted him to have it back for an hour. Tomorrow he would take his schoolbooks and catch up on things that did not feed him. Tonight he would be here until his eyes had sand in them.

The little cold lamb in the box made a sound then, a higher cleaner sound. Ruth jerked her head to the corner and saw an ear flick back and a tongue show and the small body try to stand and fail because it was wrapped and had a bottle for a mother. She laughed and the laugh surprised her, how it wound around the rafters and came back to her. “He’s up,” she said stupidly. “He’s up.”

Jamie’s grin came like a window thrown open. “You see?” he said to the world, not to her, daring it to disagree, as if all of this were another boy on a field who’d said something about him and now had to answer for it.

There were hours then that were like last year and like all the years, a string of small necessary tasks and small necessary decisions. They tubed two lambs who would not latch and swore when one bit down hard on the tube as if to test the length of Jamie’s patience. They looked at a ewe and knew she needed antibiotics and did not have a fever yet but would. They put ear tags in and snapped the metal and apologized for the sting. They made tea on the blue gas ring in the mudroom and drank it hot and sweet and burned their tongues and then went back because you could sit and not feel your back or you could stand and have your back hurt but see a hundred lives. The generator coughed and Ruth went out and let it be quiet; she didn’t want the lamps to persuade her she should keep a weak one alive when she shouldn’t, and she didn’t want to hear the steady hummed lie of comfort. She wanted the tea, the breath, the straw, the fact of it.

At some point in the night, the storm made a new sound and both of them looked up. A loud deep crack from the north where the old red oak by the house stood. Tom had wanted to cut it two winters in a row, had said each time, I’ll get to it next cold snap, and then when the cold snap came there had been ewes to feed or a neighbor’s bull gone through the fence, and then he had died. The tree must have held a hundred storms like this one and now it dropped something with a sound that was very like a door slamming in another room and very like a bone.

“Don’t,” Ruth said, out loud to only herself. “Don’t you put that on tonight.” The house was just a silhouette and very dark and not what she could afford to look at.

“Mom,” Jamie said into her shoulder. He had his face close enough to her coat that she could smell the topical iodine sharp on his skin. “I dreamed once,” he said as if he should explain something that could not be explained. “After. That he came and stood in the door and said, Well? And I said, What? And he said, Well? Like I was supposed to say something.”

“What did you say?” She held a lamb’s leg in her hand the way she might hold a word, delicately, like if she moved it too fast it would fracture.

“I said, We’re here. And he laughed and then he—” Jamie swallowed. In the half light, he looked nine and he looked thirty. “He went again.”

Ruth had no language for that. Once she had dreamed Tom looked at her over the top of the grain truck and said, You have to turn the auger on, it’s going to plug, and when she had woken, the phone had gone and the neighbor had said, Something’s wrong with my baby, in a voice that said the thing would not be put back right by speech. Once she had dreamed of nothing but his boots. She did not tell Jamie any of that. She said only, “We are here. That’s not nothing.”

He nodded, and then he made a face because a lamb had peed on his boot, and for a second she saw entirely the boy who had shouted at puddles, and it was so great an ache across her ribs that she had to close her eyes.

The wind laid down a fraction toward morning. The world was not silent; a storm is never silent, it is a thousand tiny hands on every surface. But the meaningful sounds came through better when the noise that meant no one was in charge dimmed: ewes calling, lambs answering, the creak of a pen gate, the squeal when a hinge stuck and needed a spray of WD-40 you didn’t have to warm with your hands because spring had come and just then left again. The breath of all those living things made a ceiling.

Ruth leaned on the fence with her mug between her palms and the headlamp still on her head though it had started to gray at the high edges of the shed door gap. She sipped and watched Jamie tie the twine on a lamb’s back leg and compare it to his mother with practiced eyes. He had her hands, she noticed now, broad knuckles, good nails. He had Tom’s walk and Tom’s way of hanging his head slightly when he thought so that you did not know if he had agreed or only listened and then later a thing you had said would show up done and you would not know whose idea it had been.

Midge slept on the old horse blanket by the feed bin, legs twitching as if she ran. She had run in her sleep more since October. Ruth went and put her hand on the dog’s ribs and felt them lift and drop in the steady old rhythm. The dog’s eyes cracked and she grumbled and then she went back to whatever dream she was having: probably sheep that never broke and men who whistled. Animals knew loss like an ache in warm weather. They did not traffic in the narrative. Lucky them. Or not. Ruth was less and less sure what lucky meant.

They lost one near dawn. A tiny one, the runt of a triplet, who had never warmed and had only lain and then only little breathed and then not. Ruth had known from the moment she put her hand on its chest that certain things are only gestures. She had rubbed and rubbed and then had stopped. She had carried it out to the frost shelf and laid it there and a while later, when the sky was blue as a bruise above the white and the wind had carved the drifts to fat loaves with knives, Jamie had gone and skinned it carefully as she had taught him, and they had slid its little pelt over the lamb whose mother had died in labor in the far pen, and the ewe with the dead one had snuffed and then nodded as if she had worked it out, that the thing that moved was hers because it smelled right. They had done this thing that felt like a trick and like a sacrament and not known which it was allowed to be, and the lamb had gone to her teat and made that little groan that means the first good meal of a life.

Jamie followed Ruth out to the ash heap with the little body that was now only meat, because the skin had gone to something, and he said, “Say something,” like a boy in a foreign service who wants the right charm in the right place to make a door open. Ruth put her gloved hand on the small warm bag and said, “Thank you,” and that was all. For being a lamb. For making the other one live. For not leaving nothing in its place.

“You’re weird,” Jamie said softly, but he stayed there a breath longer than he had to and pulled his glove off and touched the lamb’s face with one finger so lightly nothing moved.

In the gray of it, in the wider, the birds woke, or the birds that had stayed for the snow shook themselves out. A crow said something like a joke nobody got. The old rail fence had a cap of white on each post that made it look like a city. The smallest light came between things and turned the edges into knives and then into feathers.

They went back. Ruth checked the ewe she’d given antibiotics to and saw her blink slow and relieved. She sat a minute on the bag of feed and closed her eyes. She saw their kitchen in summer with light on the floor in rectangles and Tom standing in socks and his hair a mess and the kettle singing. She put her face in her hands and did not cry because she was made of mostly water this season and if she started she was not sure she would keep enough back to run her blood.

“Mom,” Jamie said, and she looked up. He had a lamb in his arms and the lamb’s mouth was pink as granite in sunset and he said, “Come look at this spot. It looks like a heart.”

He held the lamb like treasure. Ruth stood and went and put her hand on the lamb’s side. She could feel the gallop there, the language you are born with. She touched the white on its shoulder that had, indeed, the shape of the kind children draw when asked to draw what a heart looks like. She snorted, a small humored sound that she was surprised to have left. “Your father,” she said, “would have called that a marketing opportunity.”

Jamie laughed. It came like a released valve. “He would have. He would have called it Valentine and then pretended he hadn’t and said, Who named that?”

“He would have laid a bet with Joe across the road that it would make a better tup than it has any right to and then turned up late to church because the thing jumped the handling race and he had to fix the latch and he didn’t have a shirt that wasn’t hayseed.”

Jamie made the same scoffing sound Tom had made when teased. “And he would have eaten the last slice of that pie and told me I was growing like a weed and I should eat an apple like it was the same thing.”

“And he would have stood exactly there,” Ruth said, and pointed to the gap in the shed door where the light came now bright and clean enough to see the last of her breath go sharp, “and said, Well?” in that voice he had when he thought he was being funnier than anyone else, and you would have said, We’re here, and he would have said, Right, and gone to put his hands into a ewe like God had made hands for that specific reason.”

Jamie looked at her. It was like he did a small compromise with himself and then nodded. “He did say that,” he said. “Once. That his hands were made for this. I thought it was stupid. And then— I don’t know. Tonight I felt like my hands— I don’t know what I felt like.”

“You felt like your hands could make a thing true,” she said. “And sometimes they can.”

He swallowed and then he put his face in the lamb’s wool and took a long breath, and when he lifted it again there was straw on his mouth and he did not wipe it.

The power did not come on with a click. It was going to be a day of calls to the co-op and the line across the back pasture would need to be walked when the drifts settled and the light died inside the shed like a habit. But there was a pale gold coming up the east crack of the door that made the ewes’ backs like hills, and the lambs’ wet curls dried into little corkscrews, and the snow in the yard had that blue that means it will go where it must in the ditches by noon if the wind dies like it should. Such things matter only when you have counted hay and counted mouths.

They made rounds once more with the spray and the tagger. Ruth’s hands had dried in the straw and cracked; her knuckles ached like it had rained inside her. She flexed and made a fist and felt the pull. She leaned and checked the raw back wall where she’d moved the last panel and found no drafts. They had done it: a night, a thousand-inch night. Tom had always said you count a lambing in hours, not days, that twelve in a shed like this is a century anywhere else, and that spring turned on whether you could tell a ewe’s face by scent. Ruth breathed and thought, Here we are.

Jamie leaned his spine against the rail and made a hugely old man noise. He had straw tangled in the hair at the back of his neck like a nest. “Sleep,” he said, “is for the weak.”

“Sleep,” she said, “is for the competent, which we are when we have had it.” She pointed with her chin to the old camp bed leaned against the back wall. There had been a year when they had tried to take turns on it and found that neither of them slept when the other did not. Now it was a prop, a good idea. She had laid there once with Tom’s arm over her shoulder and listened to the pat and hiss of lambs nursing and thought of love as something with breath and teeth.

“I’ll just—” Jamie said, and sat on a bale and then lay along it with surprising grace and was asleep. He had a lamb at his boots that climbed and slid and went to lie in the fold of his coat like you might lie in the crease of a hill to get out of the weather. Ruth put another bale near him so that if he rolled he would not fall, because men who had grown men’s bones in a year could still fall like boys.

She stood for a second with her hands at her own ribs and her mouth closed and the breath going in and out until the ache softened a fraction. She went along the pens and leaned into each in turn, counting. She spoke low and told each ewe she was watching, like that mattered. She took the syringe with the shot for the ewe whose bag was hot and found the skin and did the quick sting and the ewe flicked an ear and went back to breathing her lamb. She picked up the little black one with the heart mark and put it to its mother again and when it got it she laughed aloud and did not apologize to the roof for being happy.

Outside the wind made a different run now, across the empty face of snow, not into it. The world’s edges were drawn again, fencelines and the road and the far hedge. A truck went by slow with its hazards on, the orange flash through the slats of the shed door making a low room light. Somewhere a rooster took it into his head that it was appropriate to speak. Midge woke and stretched and stood, looked stiff and then shook herself hard enough to make her tags clack, and came to put her head against Ruth’s thigh. Ruth put her own head down to meet the dog’s skull and they stood, both of them the same age when you took the years like this.

There would be more lambs in an hour, and more after that, and the day would stretch like something on a rack and then snap back at supper. There would be calls to make and a line to untangle and a pipe to thaw with the kiln heater moved on the wrong grade extension cord. There would be bread you took from the freezer and eggs you fried with a spoonful of the bacon grease from a jar with a lid that did not fit. There would be another night and another, and weeks yet of this. There would be the field green up and the ones that got sick and the ones you lost and the old ram that thought he could still jump a gate and would limp and recover and do it again. There would be a thousand details and one wide thing. There would be his jacket for a while and then someday there would be not his jacket but just a coat. There would be a boy taller than his father and he would put his hands in a ewe and he would make a thing true.

In the door gap, the light rose. The blue of the snow in the yard went to white and then to something close to silver. Ruth stood with her hands on the top of the gate of the nearest pen and put her forehead to the silvered wood and by accident she said, “Well?” like a test.

The lamb at her boot sneezed, and from the bale behind her Jamie made a muddy, altered noise that was a snore and a yes both, and the ewe in front of her turned and said a low thing to her lambs and then bent and went on with it. Ruth felt her mouth turn in her face like it remembered everything. She said to the shed, to the lambs, to her own body, to the man who was not here and to the boy who was, “We’re here,” and the shed held it the way the earth holds rain or snow, taking it in to feed something later, and this was enough for a breath and then another and then the next, and outside the storm went on where it had to and not where it shouldn’t, and they were all alive.
