The first thing I tell her is to stop looking at the waves and look at what the waves are doing to everything else. That’s what reading is, I say. Watch how the scum lines gather and pull apart, how the kelp bows its head, how the gulls hang and then shift. She giggles at gulls hanging—how can they hang, Grandpa?—and I point out the way the small ones—they aren’t gulls at all but terns—catch a rag of wind off the cannery roof and pull themselves into it the way a hand finds a pocket. “Nothing’s flying in nothing,” I tell her. “They’re flying in a river that you can’t see if you just look at the river.”

People don’t talk about winter as a season on water. They talk about storms and hunkering down and a leak in the roof you meant to fix in August. But the harbor has its winter ways that are as fine-grained as summer’s dog-days. The green is different. The sound a halyard makes when you brush it with a mitten is a different sound from the metal clack it makes when you slap it bare in July. The edges matter. The seams where harbor and river and ocean talk to one another are visible for anyone with the patience to wait in the cold. Nora has that patience. How I know is that she doesn’t reach for her phone when I say we will wait, and the cold doesn’t drive her back up the pier until her feet have earned it.

We stand at the end of the town pier next to the battered herring shack with its door half-off the hinges. Fish boxes are stacked like dilapidated houses against the wall of the shack and pressed under a frozen rope of rubber gasket that fell from somebody’s hatch and never made it back. The cannery across the basin—it still sends up its morning steam at six, even now when so much of the line sits idle. Its newer roof is an insult to the sagging town around it, some corrugated thing bright as a gull’s back when there’s a rare scrap of sun.

“You see there,” I say, and put my hand on Nora’s shoulder and turn her body the way you turn a sextant until the object of the thing slides into the sight. She does a small, earnest nod, as if she believes nods can steady a head better than a hand. “There’s a slick line. From the pump-out toward the bait freezers. It’s actually two lines, crossing like threads.”

“I see a puddle with a rainbow,” she says. “Oil.”

“Oil’s the shimmer. That’s one line. But the other doesn’t have shimmer. It’s darker. It runs heavy. That’s fresh water coming down past DiNome’s dock. You can smell it if you think about it. There’s a mineral taste in your nose.”

She tries. I see her little face pinch, all that fierce concentration that makes her seem older than thirteen until you see the shape of her new-molars, the softness to the cheek still. Her scarf is too big for her today because there’s a wind off the east that brings the ice smell from the marsh. “It smells like fish,” she says.

“Everything smells like fish, and it always will, or you can drive two towns over to that place with the grocery store with the two doors and a parking lot and decide that everything smells like oranges. There’s a fresh edge in that air. Fresh water’s lighter than salt. The black line is fresh riding over salt. That’s why you’ll always find a slick across the basin for a couple of hours after a hard rain. If you run your boat across that line you will feel it.”

Her eyes flick to the skiff bumping against the dock fingers. “Can we go?”

“Wait,” I say, and put my hand to the piling and feel the creosote give a little under my glove, as if the chemicals too are cold today. My thumb finds an old peg-hole from before there were ring-bolts. Everything you need to know is written down in the wood. It is not an easy way to teach a child these days, whose world writes to her in light and velocity. I have to make the water move slowly enough that it makes a letter she recognizes.

“Who taught you to do this?” she says after we have stood another ten minutes watching nothing happen.

“This? Nobody and everybody. A good man by the name of Hannigan when I was hired as a junior at the pilot station. A fella called Anderson who smelled of tobacco and mints and had a scar on his throat that sat like a wink you weren’t sure you ought to acknowledge. The water, mostly. I learned with a hand tiller in boats that had no batteries big enough to handle a gauge, had to listen to what you couldn’t see because what you could see was not a thing you should trust after dark. And then when it matters you find your own drill for it. Hannigan used to say good pilots have a line they walk inside their heads from dock to dock. But that’s not what I used. I use the skin on my forearms and noses of docks in memory. I use the way my spine hums when a standing wave lifts the stern. I use noise. I use smell.” I look at her and she is smiling. “You think I’m funny,” I say.

“No,” she says, and puts her knit cap higher on her head to hear me better through it. “I think your words are funny. You use your spine, Grandpa.”

“You think I’m talking about spirits or something. I’m not. I’m talking about bone. I’m talking about how your body is the instrument that reads the song. This is what your mother used to roll her eyes at, when she had time for a temper. I’d tell her I could smell the river ten minutes before the town and she’d say, That’s not your nose, Dad, that’s because we always come this way. She liked me to be ordinary. Ordinary’s not a way you get down past the bar in a norther when the swell’s coming up and there’s a ship pacing oval patterns outside the breakwater waiting on you.”

“Did you wreck a ship?” she says, which is the favorite question of any child in this town if they can get you by yourself on the dock. It’s the question of their own future more than it is the past—tell me there’s a story that breaks things and ends with you getting to go home.

“Once I bent one badly,” I say. "That was a morning like this, cold and bright and a little too much wind from the wrong way. A dredge had moved a bite of the channel and didn’t file the notice and I felt the bottom lift where it had never lifted before and there was no room to swing her and wait. I rubbed her on the sand and scratched hell out of the hull paint. My boss’s boss drove down with his hat clutched in his fist two hours later. He wanted to know why I didn’t wait. I told him I put her out there because of a crowd like this”—I make a circle with my hand at the harbor—“folks waiting to get in, boats waiting to get out, weather on the way. You make a call sometimes because of the net, not just the fish.”

“I like it when you pretend Mom didn’t say she hates that,” Nora says.

“I’m not pretending,” I say. “Your mother says a lot of things because she knows too much about what it means when a man makes a call and he’s wrong. She married a man who thought he knew everything.” That was Terry, and I don’t use his name with her because his name is a shape that brings a fight into the room even if the room is a wide-open pier, and I won’t bring fights out here. “She does not hate my hands or the lines I learned to read. She hates the ones I left, maybe. I left your grandmother too much. There is the truth. Here’s the other truth”—and I put my hand to the water again because it is always my fairness, my balance—“your mother can read a patient’s face through a half-hour of pain that covers the face the way fog covers this harbor. She says she is done saving hurt people, but that’s the story she tells to the other saved people who are now wearing ties. Watch the gull over the lobster skiff. He sees something.”

The gull drops. There are a dozen of them riding the taller masts like living weathervanes—the old men of the pier call them that, without any poetry, aligning themselves automatically to show where the real wind hides under the catspaws. One falls off the stillness like a kid off a rock and hits the surface at the seam line just where I have been making Nora stare. The fish it pulls is small and silver as a nail. Even from here I can see its life jump out of it when it breaks the plane of air, that astonishment that any one of us exhibits when the world loses us for a second and then puts us on a shelf someplace new.

“The slick is a language,” I say, mostly to myself. I am not so much a sentimental man as a man that words find after the fact. “Let’s go put the boat on it and I’ll show you how the tiller twitches.”

We climb down. This is the part I do not like, my weak ankle telling the truth about my age to the rungs cut in the side of the pier’s end. I was a man who could come down one-handed with a thermos under my arm, and here I pick my way like a woman with a cake. Nora is careful, as if she senses this embarrassment. She says nothing, she doesn’t look. She watches the skiff as it bumps and keeps one hand on the painter line that I draped up to the cleat, like she’s catching a puppy. “She wants to run around,” she says.

I stand into the skiff and I feel the water move itself under my boots, that little adjusting sink you learn to love. We are small things, and our best moments, in my opinion, are when being small allows you to say yes to forces that would kill you if you were big. The outboard coughs. It is an old Evinrude that my neighbor down on East drew breath into and then charged me sixty dollars out of embarrassment—he should’ve asked me for two hundred, I told him, but we’ve been poor in the same way for too long to let each other do right by the world. Nora takes the stern seat and wraps the painter around her fist without thinking, the kind of mistake grown men make. I touch her hand and she apologizes and then apologizes for apologizing, the way you learn in seventh grade to do. I toss the line up. I push with the boathook. The little engine finds the place in its throat where its whining hum lives.

We carve across toward the freezers. I go on a quarter-throttle because even now, with no one around but the handful of boats that are hanging dead on their piles for the winter, I work the inside of a harbor as if there’s always something smudged a foot below the surface waiting to take off a lower unit, and there often is. The scum line—call it that, but really it’s the division between two bodies of water that have been married for too long not to fight—the line sits in a fold of calm. The surface is freckled by a wind from one direction and before it gets across, it’s freckled in a different way. The difference is not easy to name at first. The light strikes little pocks on the western side and makes white dots; across the line, there is a finer texture to the filing, as if a craftsman put down a rasp for a file. You can hear a different sound on the skiff bow as it bites one or the other. That is the music that somebody put into my spine fifty years ago and that I am asking my granddaughter to hear without insisting she name.

“Okay,” I say. I hand her the tiller. She makes a face, a small Y of doubt between the eyes. “Hold it. Let it talk.”

“What if I tip us,” she says, which is a thing you hear out of land-people sometimes, as if boats tip without work. Boats roll, boats swamp, boats eat you a thousand ways. They tip mostly on the way to the dock after you have cleared the bar because you think you are safe and forget which leg your weight is planted on. Boats aren’t drunks—people are. She holds the handle as if it will burn. “Which way do I make it go?”

“Think about your hand like the tail on a stubborn thing. You want the nose to go right? You push the tail left. Don’t fight, guide. Always assume a longer conversation is being held in the water and that you are just putting in a word or two to make your point. Don’t be dramatic.”

Her eyebrows go up. People who are born now are born into drama. That is not a criticism; it is a fact. The world around them has been tuned to require a scream to be heard. It is perhaps why I love the harbor. A wave does not care about a scream. A wave wants a quiet man or a quiet woman. In a riot of foam, a man who is quiet can find the one dark patch underneath that will take him out. In a still afternoon a quiet man can hear the bell buoy speak in his chest because the air will carry it if he throws his body open to receive it. She pushes the tiller. The bow swings like a thought. We cross the seam. There is a little yaw, a little giving of the stern, a little coolness in the elbow that holds the line of the boat. It is what I would have felt if I were steering blind along the seawall in fog and I hit the dark instead of the lighter air that says, watch yourself, you’re in the lee of something solid. Her face changes. She feels it. I don’t say a word. We let her feel it again. We cross back. The bow nods agreement. The skiff is pleased by the correctness of the sensation. Machines like to do what they were built for.

“I felt that,” she says. “It was like the road had a little ridge on it.”

“Exactly,” I say, which is not my favorite word, because it stops the thought too soon. “It was like the snowplow came through this morning and you’re cresting over the line in the middle. You want to feel it in your teeth. When a ship is pushing herself down the channel under a ship-assist tug and the stern is being twisted by a tide that the pilot—me—didn’t calculate for because the dredgers got lazy and their pile of tailings made another seam like this where there wasn’t one last week, you want to be the first man in that crew of professionals to feel the change. And you want to feel it in your teeth, not your eyes. Your eyes are two seconds too slow. Your eyes are for later, so that you can say, Well I saw it. Feeling it is how you don’t make the papers.”

She laughs the way children laugh when they like the idea that there are papers to make and also that you did not make them. We cross back and forth until the laugh is out of her and the feeling is a feeling, not a performance. I look up toward the cannery’s tall door. They open it on warm days in summer and a smell comes out that is a cross of rich fat and rot and salt, a smell that is not pleasant and is not unpleasant either by the time you get to November and the fish are done and there is nothing to do with the smell but be the town’s definition. Today the door is mostly pulled and a forklift zips out like a kid sneaking a cookie and then pushes back in. I can see three men in their blue hats. There used to be forty at this time of day. There used to be trucks, idling, backed up to the road with their drivers sitting and reading lottery scratchers because there were too many fish and the plant had to slow-cook a day that was running too hot.

“It’s closing,” Nora says matter-of-fact, as if she were reciting the day of the week.

“Closing after Christmas,” I say. “And then opening briefly in January to run down the last of the cans from storage. Then a man from downstate will say some things in a room and then a different man from farther downstate will say sorry we cannot afford you and your particular arrangement of needs any longer and then there will be too much quiet in the morning where there was sound before. And that will be that and then nothing like that will come back.”

“Mrs. Alvarez says we could get a software place,” she says. “She says we have good bones. They like cheap buildings with big windows.”

I laugh and it gets in my throat a little, which is not a thing I let her see if I can help it. “Mrs. Alvarez is a lady with good intentions. Okay. Listen to me. We’re going to come left slow toward the wall and we’re going to stop without throwing a ricochet wave into somebody’s hull. That’s manners. Then we’re going to watch again. Then we’re going to go to the point and see the bar’s mouth.”

She does it. The muscle that runs along under her jaw pumps a little—it’s new, that strength, her body just starting to wire its power correctly. She backs the throttle with a carefulness that gets me a little, because she is trying to be gentle. The motor doesn’t reward gentle. It wants firm. She learns this half a second after it’s useful and then she gives it the right pressure at the right spot and the bow lifts its lip. We rock once. A small wave we made goes to whack the side of the Hanka Lee, which is a boat so old now that every whack is a friend calling to it from a distance. The leaning man on its deck is smoking with one hand and picking his fingernails with the other and does not look up, because we know each other’s boats like we know our own faces around here and our boats forgive things from the people we have loved.

We tie off at the sea wall’s rubber strips and sit and listen. There is a fog half a mile out past the headland today. I can see it rhyme with the horizon across the harbor; it will come in tonight if the wind shifts another degree. The bell buoy at the east end of the channel is working a little. It has two moods: giddy and drunk. Right now it is not giddy—there’s no sharp chop—and it’s not drunk—that’s when the swell is up and it’s rolling easy and ringing with a lazy slant—I’d describe it as an old man in a warm kitchen playing with the center of the bell with the end of a spoon. That is not a metaphor that will carry to her but someone used something like it for me and my mind still hears it that way and my hands still steer by the sound of it spoken in that tone.

“What’s it saying?” she says, because she is a good granddaughter and knows when I am writing more words than an answer needs.

“It’s saying the tide keeps pushing. It’s saying the channel’s got shoulders today. You could go now if you had to and you’d fight a little to keep the bow from wanting to jink left past the second green. It’s saying stay out of the eddy at the cannery’s mouth or you’ll flirt around the big tug’s stern and take longer than you meant. It’s saying the river is annoyed at something. It’s saying don’t go past the bar in a skiff this size without a proper jacket and a rope ready.”

“Who is it saying that to,” she says, “if nobody’s going out?”

“Me,” I say. "It always says it to me. It is unworried that I’m a man who hasn’t had the pilot call in three years. It still speaks to the same address in my head. Maybe that’s vanity. Maybe that’s what keeps men like me alive long enough to be your teacher.”

We go slow around the harbor in a figure that I use for teaching because it takes you by all the points that have their own voices. The ice float—the shallow pan by the boatyard where the fresh floats under the cold air—you can taste a sweetness in the breeze when you pass there that is all rotted leaf and tannin and a little iron. The town storm drain, where somebody always throws three cobs and a handful of greasy wrappers after a free lunch down at the hall, that line runs like a snake a yard off the dock and the rats take it like a sidewalk. The mouth of the marsh, even in winter, is busy under the water. You won’t see it, but you can feel it by the way the slight chop will pucker and unpucker faster than it does anywhere else. There is life there preparing. The old pilings at Grady’s, where the long dead clam house stood, pull a tide in circles like plucked strings. The phone company cable crosses the bottom between two painted boards on shore that have lost their paint and their boards and their memory of phone. The sandbar between the town mooring field and the point has a lip to it that dogs and children know in June, where you can go out in waist-deep a hundred yards and then one more step makes it to your collar bone and your mind writes your obituary quick enough to teach your feet how to go backward like a crab. All winter men who think they are not afraid of water because they wear bibs like statues ride up to the lip of that bar in their dories and poke over it and turn quick and grin and smoke. Nora does not smoke. That is one thing that I have done right, even if all I did was disapprovingly change the radio dial every time a song came on about making mistakes.

On the way out toward the mouth we pass under the length of the cannery’s wall that faces the main channel. A sign along there advertises an Advance Intake System with a drawing that looks like a spaceship. The local boys spray-painted a cock at the bottom of it and the manager made them repaint it ten times and every time one of them put back the dick at night with a flashlight while his girlfriend stood watch, and in the end the manager got tired and now the outer space intake has a proud extra spigot. “That drawing will stay there a decade,” I say. “After they leave, the building will hold itself like a drunk without his arm of friends. Somebody will say they’ll buy it to make a place for bands, and then a thing no one has a name for will take a piece of the roof, and the gulls will own it. And Nora will come back from wherever she has gone, with a child perhaps, and I will be dead, and she will say this is where once we went and learned to read a tide.”

“That’s morbid,” she says gently. “Do the bell buoy again.”

I tap the frame of the skiff with my knuckle and in my chest I hum, and she groans and laughs, because my sound is closer to a cow in a dream. But the bell buoy does call again, and its note is itself, and that comforts me more than I knew. There are places in the world where light changes. That is not a revelation; that is a fact. A pilot has to know which changes you can trust. A bell buoy day to day is not the same—that would be a lie—but its difference is within a known variability. A person can love that. When the men inside that wall stopped letting the whistle blow at lunch and five for the first time one summer when orders were slow and then did it every other day and then every third day and then did not blow a whistle any longer, I found myself mean and not know why. Then in the fall somebody drove a nail into the whistle with a sign that said We Regret Any Inconvenience. What child rides his bike home because of a nail and a regret? Who lets a nail know when it is five p.m.?

We idle past the point of the breakwater. My hand wants the other hand to go out and touch the stones the way you lay a hand on a shoulder when you pass your brother sleeping on a couch he is not meant to be sleeping on. There’s a swell today, modest and bossy, up from the low grounds way down the line where one part of the world mistakes itself for another. It comes in in lawns. You can see the line of it way out; it pulls itself darker as it gathers its thought and then as it gets to the lip of the bar, it forgets and breaks. This forgetting is anything but forgetful. It is the exquisite physics of a thing losing its depth and being forced to sing it instead. Stand here long enough and you can hear the period of the swell without your ears. Then there is the river out, meeting it, chuckling. That’s not a sound; that’s a motion your eyes write as sound because it’s too complex to take in at once. Then the wind, at a different layer, writing cats and commas on the top of it all. That’s an alphabet. If I gave up talking and lay on my back for a winter I could learn a better alphabet and a purer one. With a child here, I rob myself of some of that still attention. In exchange I get small hands freezing and a laugh and the way a girl looks at a thing once and then writes it permanently in the private book of her to carry when I am gone from the adult part of her life. “Okay,” I say again, and she knows that this is the word I use to mean we are going to do a thing harder than the last thing. “You are going to run us at an angle across the mouth where the ebb fights that swell. You’re going to let the bow find its own way into a wave instead of insisting it take it on your terms. You will pick a notch between lions and run it. I will kill the engine if I do not like something and start it if I do. We will not go outside. This is my blood promise to your mother. We will move the skiff through a complicated language and then get it back where it is easy to be.”

“I’m good,” she says. She says this with no bravado. She says it like someone accepting a teacher’s insufficient words and knowing that subsequent experience will make most of them pointless.

I let her go toward the first green can that stutters its light when the light is on. I point her to the right of it, not to its left, because a hundred people would say left because that looks so nice, that flat patch. That flat patch on the left is full of cross words today. The right is lumpy. Give me the truth lumpy over a false floor. The first wave knocks the bow and then the engine stammers because something small and old in it did not like that sudden bite of cold air slapping its throat, and in that one second I see more of my future than I want. The children of men like me have a way of pronouncing their own independence with courage, and ships and skiffs both have a way of making those pronouncements cost a body something. The engine picks up again and the bow finds the V where the two lifts will cross and wants to go into it because all things want to go into the middle of what is trying to shove them. She lets it. We rise a foot. We rise a foot and a half. She does not correct her course with her brain. Her hand slides and the stern is pressed and she sees a darker line in the break and she aims her teeth at it like a girl biting through fishing line and the next wave rolls us left fore and down. “Let it. Let it,” I say, as if a gallon of words will keep the next thing from happening. It lets. She breathes. I hear the children inside the breakwater playing on the rocks in my memory and the sound under my foot is the sound of the hull boards stroking old varnish that no longer exists. Then we are through the thin piece of the argument and we sit in the bent water where the channel runs true for a boat a hundred times bigger than ours.

She hisses a breath. She turns. That is the instinct of everyone—do a thing, turn, look to approve it. I put my hand on her hand. “Look forward always,” I say. “Nobody behind you is now here to help you. All the help is in front of you. The past is concrete only when you’re buying it. The present is only a step if you’re dancing. Which you are. See the green? Do not cleave to it like a boy at a dance. You are the boy. You lead.”

“Grandpa,” she says, smiling, “that was the weirdest speech.”

“Okay,” I say, too much my word now. The sea is meaner farther out. The fog lifts its sleeve and I see the thumb of land that holds the bell, and then it draws the sleeve down again. If we went farther we would crash into another water that is not for today. I can smell that fact as if there were a leaf fire under it. “Turn her back. Hit the seam again. Feel the difference. Remember it so your bones know it before your brain names it when it is dark and you are cold and someone is depending on you and you are right that someone should. That’s the whole lesson.”

We go back in and it is always easier to go in. A mother knows that truth about the door to the house when it is raining harder in the driveway. If you wait for just the right beat after a gust you can walk between drops. The channel is full of men who have never noticed that trick but consider themselves weather-literate because they can tell you what the barometer said last Thursday. She crosses the slick line again by the bait freezers and the tiller gives her goodbye and she smiles. It’s won. Maybe not forever—the next wind will write another letter—but this one will now always be legible to her. We tie up and we climb and I take the pain that is there beneath the bottom step and set it aside like a man sets a filled mug on a table near other men and leaves the room without a goodbye because he is not a man who says goodbye when things matter.

At the top of the pier her mother is waiting. Her hair is tucked into that knit cap that she will always wear when she is fifty and sixty because she wore it at twenty-four when her husband forgot to come home and once your life has a fixed point your fashion will find a way to sit beside it proudly. She looks at me without anger and I am grateful for that. Two months ago she would have hard-lined her mouth and there would have been a helmet of words across it. “He told me,” she says to the child, “no farther than the bell.”

“We didn’t go,” Nora says, and rolls her eyes at the crime of my exaggerations that she knows are already warming in my lips. “We went a little past the green.”

“And you heard the bell,” my daughter says. “We heard it at the trailer. It sounds sad today.”

“It’s a bell,” I say. “It doesn’t specialize.”

She smiles without humor. There is flour on her jeans. She works evenings in the bakery and nights and weekends at the care home when they need her and mornings she comes down and keeps lists for the cannery because they will keep her lists until there is no one she loves left asking for them. Women do that math without self-praise. If I had ten lives, I could not arrange them into one that paid her back the sums I owe. I am old enough to know that you can be grateful and that gratefulness will not change the world’s disinterest in your having learned to be grateful. Still, it is a good lesson to obey. It suits you the way reading suits you. She touches my collar as if my coat was inside-out and then, finding it is straight, shrugs at herself. “You smell like the cold,” she says. “I like that. Come by at two. The inspector’s up from the city. He wants to see your log from the year we had the barge hung up overnight because of the weather. He says there are no pictures.”

“There are no pictures,” I say. “There is my signature on a piece of paper and a note from a man who will have retired thirty years now if he hasn’t traded his heart for a bigger TV. Why does he want to see it?”

“They are looking for a story. He is hungry.”

“The last two months a man who lives three hours away picks over my shelf like a woman chooses a dress for a funeral. I will hold the paper for him and he will nod in the wrong places and write nothing down,” I say, and I do not add that I am a fool. I will go. I will let him take a piece and in so doing I will give myself the satisfaction of handing off a weight and watch him take it and then later know I bought nothing back with what I handed off. That is the shape of this winter. A taking and a nothing. “All right,” I say. “I will come.”

“And you”—she folds a hand into Nora’s collar and tucks a hair into her hood and the hand lingers longer than it does most days on most collars—“you smell like fish. We will have to wash that out before church, if you and your grandfather are to go to church. And you are going. Miss Lil says they have a food drive.”

“I’ll go,” Nora says.

We walk back toward town along the edge where the sidewalk crumbles and then lifts into good maintenance again because the tourist block starts halfway down. The cannery window that looks into the break room has a cartoon poster of a man lifting boxes the right way; somebody has drawn horns on him and written in Spanish a thing that made the manager mad and I know because I watched him erase it and then he wrote Please? in English under the thing. The parking lot has three cars instead of thirty and I could name each one. It is as if my mind has decided that without enough things to keep in it, it will grow more detailed about the few there are. The blue pickup with the dent that looks like the state of Florida in its box is Mr. Hori’s. He was a quiet man when his son worked here and he is quieter now. The white sedan that could be any white sedan is the manager’s and I hope his wife finds her way around to telling him he is kinder than he wants to be and does not have to pretend to be tougher. The little station wagon with the old ski rack is Marta’s. She sings in the line. She knows she sings and sings less than she wants because she has one of those superstitious understandings of attention that both entertainers and my dead mother had—if you take too much of it you will be asked to pay it back in a grief.

After lunch I go down to the office with the log under my arm because I am not a fool exactly in the way I said, I am only a man who does the right things to the wrong end. The inspector is not here. He has gone to see the outflow pipe at the bar and get a whiff of something he can call Excess. The girl at the desk is pretty and fierce and I watch her write the names of three men who will lose their jobs this week on a calendar that has roses on it and then she circles the payday after next and then draws a dot next to it as if the dot makes time friendlier. She looks up. “He’ll be back at four,” she says.

“I’ll leave it for him,” I say, and I put the book on the shelf where I used to put books. It feels good and wrong, as if it were a mass of earth in my arms and I could put it somewhere inside a wall to say that a person lived here who could carry and place responsibility. On my way out Nancy from the break room is making coffee on the small machine, and she drops a packet and says a prayer and then picks it up and laughs. “Heh,” she says to me, a person who does not go upstairs and make coffee now that the whistle is gone. “Heh,” I say back, and we are in love for a second because people always are when they share humiliation and have the decency not to show it.

The fog comes down by two like a law. It wraps the houses and drapes the flags at the VFW and the men on the stoop shake it off their boots and talk as if the fog makes talk more urgent. I have to push my own sense of urgency out of its way. I go back to the pier and stand with my hands in my pockets and taste the different damp. Fog is not just one thing. A fog off the salt is thick and chewy and it presses your eyelids and makes you wish for the old sleep a hand gives your head when you lay on a lap. A fog out of the river is papery and soft and touches each hair and is gone and touches again. This is a fog that has both. It is a wedding fog. The bell is strong now because sound is pulled like a coat in weather like this. The sea makes you feel alone, then it gives you back your company and you find you are hanging on to something with somebody, their hand in yours sticky and real, and it is a shock to have that proof of existence handed to you in the same minute as you can’t see the far end of your own twitch.

“She should not go back out in that,” my daughter says from behind me. She has the ability to appear in the place that my worst idea is about to have a chance to act.

“She won’t,” I say, though in truth the thought I had was not of going out but of standing here and making the fog say other things, longer sentences, that would write across her brain in a script that would be an extra hand for her later in a dark place. My daughter is right. Children fall in love with fogs. Fogs fall for children and keep them for neighbors and friends. Mothers do not fall in love with fogs. It is a fact of a place. If you want your daughter to come to the water in middle age when she needs a friend that makes no polite demands, you do not take her too far into it when her bones are new. “We’ll stay,” I say.

We stay. A small skiff—the Oleta—cuts like a careful knife along the wall and I put a hand up to the man at the tiller because I can’t help myself. He does not see me. His eyes are twelve inches, maybe fourteen, in front of the boat. That is the right place. He’s running in by sound and gut from the green to the red to the rubber at the cannery then straight across to the civic ladder. He has done it a hundred times but then today the dredge left a little wall and that wall is making a tiny river where there used to be a lake and he does not know that. He hits it and his stern yaws and his face comes open for a second and in that second he looks like boys look, which is to say, like a man you love who cannot see himself yet. He corrects. He raises a hand without looking to say to some ghost thanks. He is my student whether he knows it or not. I bless such men I do not create.

Later that week, we do what children have always had to do here, even when love and law fight. We go out in fog and we come home in near dark. It is the last week of December and the town has a small Christmas tree that somebody got from the forester on Salt Road because the ornaments look large and wrong on it, as if they were trying to be helpful but brought the wrong platter to a house with no plates. The cannery is open on half-days to finish the run and the forklift operator is still having affairs with the night-shift auditor and they think the town does not know but a town this size is like a belly—everything speaks through the middle. We leave the pier after noon and the bell walks us out and I take the first lines. Nora listens. She has her notebook because school is off and her teachers have learned that the only writing she does that she keeps is here. She writes what I say sometimes. More often she writes what I don’t, the sound a wave makes on the belly of a boat that rides up too slow, the way the fog grabbed the sawtooth rock before the point like a beard. And then after an hour of this I make her take us home.

She has not been in fog steering alone yet. I use a rope loop on the tiller and another on the throttle so that, with my elbows, I can cheat if she is getting us killed. We are not going to die today. I have two flares. I have a horn that is not a horn except it lets me shout louder when I have lost my voice. I have the image of the bell and the red and the green and the cannery’s mouth and the shoe of sand at the corner of the mooring field engraved more deeply in me than the lines on my palm that tell me I will live to see a thing I should not. I have the smell of the leather of the gloves I wore five summers in a row to run lines under ships where the steel smells like old pennies. In other words I have what a man calls his training if asked to boast and his luck if asked to tell the truth, and I have my granddaughter’s wrist in my right hand as I slide it off the tiller and then slide it back gently because it belongs there now.

“You will not look behind,” I tell her again. “You will hear and you will look only where you want to go. You will keep that bell in the corner of your eye if you can. But not if you can’t. You will feel the wake of what is not yours and you will love it by running alongside it until it spends itself. If I tell you to stop you will put us into reverse so soft that we won’t lose the stern. If I tell you harder you will put us harder. If I don’t tell you and you feel an edge you will take it away a little. This is not a test. It is a lesson I am giving your body. Your body is the thing that saves you. Your mind makes stories later so you can love what your body did.”

“You talk like a cult leader,” she says, and I laugh, because I am proud of her for knowing to say that to me.

“Go,” I say.

She goes. The bow lifts and it is only the lifting of a foot and the fog swallows the front of her hat and the back of my thought and the world is only four hands long and two of the hands are gloved and two are new to their work. Something calls in the traffic lane far out that none of us will ever see. The bell is a friend. The stern pulls. The tiller’s wooden bar writes a letter in her palm that her tendons read at school a month from now when a boy says a thing to her that people say on principle. She remembers the letter and guides past the boy with a humility the boy will think is his idea. We cut fart-slow by the cannery’s mouth and the outflow is faster there today because the scrubbers are on a run cycle they have not used in a month and the men who are left inside feel like the work left over in a roasting pan after a meal. The skiff wants to go there. She does not let it. We are seen and not seen by men in hats and breath. The bell and the green. Then a small sound. The sound is a child. It comes from starboard.

“Stop,” I say softly, and she does it so soft I nearly cry. It is the smooth stopping of somebody whose hands have decided to be a way between body and stone. I could hug every mechanic I have ever known for what they taught my child on a winter day.

We listen. The cry is there and gone. There is no river noise to cover it because the fog has asked the river to be quiet today. Nora is ten seconds ahead of me and already opening her mouth. “Hello,” she calls. “You okay? Hello?”

Not an adult’s Are you okay; a child’s Where are you.

“Here,” a voice says. It is a ghost voice. It comes from the ladder at DiNome’s where the water sits under the edge of a dock in fog like breath under a man’s shirt in a confession box. We idle. We bump. The bow kisses the rubber and a hand reaches out. It is a boy’s arm. The hand is small and the forearm is skinny and the sleeve is wet to the elbow. He is twelve. He is nobody I know in the center of my knowing but his face lives at the edge of a memory of a bench at a basketball game last winter. His hair is up and it has salt in it. The story comes in drips the way a leak does until the whole bilge is in your shoes. He climbed down for a dropped lure. He slipped. He grabbed the slime at the last rung and stayed there because his feet found a notch and fear has a notch everywhere if you’re just past small enough to fit in them all.

“We need the ladder,” Nora says. “Not the steps. Go a little left.”

“Left,” I say, and I am the echo now.

She bumps again at the bracket where the ladder bites. She is not graceful now but she is right. The boy reaches and his hand smears cold on her cold and he gets his knee in and he goes rubber and we hold the boat from going into his shin with our hands and then he is in. He thanks nobody with a howl that is his mother’s. I sit on the stern and do not say the obvious things that men say. I take off my hat and put it on his lap and then take it back and put it on his head because he cannot be trusted to know what warms him. Nora lights a flare because we need nothing of it except for its knowledge that it can be called. It spits its sudden blood and she looks it in the face and then looks away, which is how you take from something without being rude.

I put the boy at the wheel. “You steer us home,” I tell him. “You will get a ghost in your chest if you don’t. Your body will write this as fear. You want it to write it as I know. Like this.” I lay my hand on his and for a second all the hands are one and my daughter will never know this minute because I will not be able to say it to her when we bring her teenage neighbor whose mother is looking out at a wall of white and telling herself stories with nails and a board and her teeth.

We come in and the wall resolves into bricks. The shouts are written before we can hear them. He is not scolded. He is not hugged until the second breath because it seems a lie to hug a child who just made a mistake, but only for a second, and then there are arms, and the fog makes even the loudest man quiet for three beats. I sit in the skiff and pull the cord on the engine softly, as if it is a bell I can ring by myself. It coughs in my hand like a baby stuffed up. The fire in the flare dies the theatric death it always wanted to die. Nora watches it with the respect of a person who will never say she loves a thing that lords itself in a frightened person’s face.

That night after the boy is returned, after the cannery’s lights stay on a little later as if they are trying to teach themselves how to be lights without a world to light, after the fog goes ragged and gives up trying to be a person and becomes once again a set of conditions, I take the notebook where Nora writes and I read. I read her words as if they were another tide chart for a harbor I have not yet piloted. My eyes move and in the words I see a girl who sits in a boat and watches what moves because other things push it and says nothing because what she writes later is not the thing itself but the space behind it where it decided its move. I see the names of things she has made up to tack onto my names because my names are older and have trouble sitting in her mouth. She calls the scum line’s crossing X stitches and calls the bell’s valley tone the warm spoon. She writes that the wind had crumbs in it today at the pier. She writes, I felt the skiff’s letter as a kiss written on my palm in pencil because you can’t feel ink. She writes, He will not always be here. He will always say he is. He will always be here when I need what is in him and not him. I close the book. I open it again and commit to memory where that line sits so that I might steal a little of the comfort in it for a day when I am being told by my own blood that my company is not required.

On New Year’s morning the cannery’s whistle blows. Nobody expected it. No nail keeps a town from its nostalgia when a man on a night shift with keys and a moral code that answers to a different boss decides to lift a thing and see if the past is stored inside it or if like food, it spoiled. It blows and people laugh and people cry and three people grip the arms of their chairs and curse aloud alone because the sound makes the heart think it has to get up and go punch a clock and face a boss, which is something a heart learns to hate and then miss the way it misses air under a wave. Nora runs out of the trailer with half a boot on and no hat and then comes back and puts on a hat and the other boot and kisses her mother’s shoulder on the way out and her mother pretends not to notice but her eyes go shinier. We stand together at the pier and the sound runs around the basin and up the river and out to the fog and I am almost angry at the beauty of it because beauty in the last winter of a thing is like a mean child: it draws from you too much when you cannot say no.

They close the cannery two weeks later. Men and women stand in a room and a person uses the words footprint and sustainable and then a terrible phrase that I do not think an adult man ought to say in a town like ours, unspecified synergies. A woman says that corporate regrets. My daughter sits with a pen in her hand and writes the names of the last times by a column that is called Inventory on a sheet of paper that is made out of trees cut down three states away. Nora listens as if this were math class. She listens like the bell. She will use the words later not because she loves them but because she needs to have them somewhere to tell herself what was taken, the way a person writes down the sushi they ate the night before a fever, so that a year later they can warn a friend, though it was almost certainly not the fish.

After, when the men drift out and visit their wives who have come close to see if they can hold their hands in public without breaking them in their own hands, we go to the end of the pier one more time in a winter that will make no instruments for the next winter. I stand with her and put my elbow on the top of the piling where I sometimes used to lay my head when I was too tired to drive back around the harbor to home because there were no rides at three in the morning then. The bell speaks because it can. The outflow at the cannery’s pipe is slower, then it stops. The scum line the water used to take from a trickle of lube rinsing down the outside of the door is not there. The fresh riding over the salt has less to push. A stranger would say nothing had changed because there is water under the gulls and there was water under the gulls last week, but we see the missing letters as if somebody censored a sentence and left behind a black rectangle where a friend’s name used to be. I am moved to say grand things. I resist. Grand things are for plaques. They are for the kids who come back in ten years to read what the adults said about the day the thing died, and those kids will say I don’t remember it that way and then one of them will realize that this is why places lie on signs.

“Look,” I say to her, in my voice that is smaller and that I love better. “Do you see how the marsh mouth is hungrier? The tide has discovered it can come in there now. There will be a pool where now there is none this summer and little fish will learn to run in it. Point to where the edge of it will be.”

She points. She points wrong and then she finds it and points right. The knowledge is not a bird landing on a man’s hand. It is a bird that throws its shadow across a man’s shoulder and then returns to its own cloud. You do not get to keep it. You get to be brushed by it and remember the brush. Maybe that is why men like me get up on bad ankles and go down and climb into skiffs and put their granddaughters through a place where fear prowls like a polite dog waiting to be asked in. Because later, when a man who will love her or a woman who will hurt her or a neighbor who will give her what she needs stands before her and the words they say do not match the wind that lifts the hair over their ear or the way their car makes the gravel sound or the smell of their coat under rain, she will not believe the wrong part. She will put her hand on the tiller and feel the twitches before the picture shows her what to do. She will be safe, if safe is a thing the world decides to let us pretend exists. She will not need a whistle to tell her it is time. She will hear a bell and know if that day its tone is honest or the bell is asleep. I can’t ask for more. My asking days are mostly done. My reading days go until I do not lift my head to the window to see the way light writes a card of itself on the water and then I will be done and nothing will miss me in the way a person imagines themselves missed except perhaps a single gull that has the greed of familiarity and a grandchild who will stand at the rail with a child of her own and point to a fast patch and say there, where the road rises a little, feel it, there, and the child will feel it and not go where it wants to take her, and that will be my true obituary, written in a letter paper nobody sees, which is the best kind.

We watch the water a while longer, because the only good way to end a day on a harbor is not to end it. Then we go back to the trailer, which is warm because my daughter is generous to us in that simple way she does without speeches. The talk at the table is nothing to do with cannery or bell or tide. That is how it should be. We talk about a pie somebody made with too much nutmeg. We talk about the bus to the game in the next town and how its driver always smells like mints and old wool. We talk about a story from a book about a girl who was a detective and never cried and how that is a lie and a relief both. Nora goes to her room and I sit and listen to the surprising hymn the heater makes when it is at the edge of giving up and then does not. I think then not of the harbor I am retired from, or the men with hats in rooms with strips of carpet different from the places where we actually work, or the day they will tear down the wall and sell the lumber as reclaimed to a couple who want a table in a kitchen that once held no blood, or myself, or the times I will or will not be able to walk to the end of the dock. I think of a slick line and the way a bow crosses it and sighs quietly when it does and learns that in the world there are edges where one thing sits on another just barely and there is a way to move through that edge without making a storm. If I have made a gift to anyone, it is that. If anyone has given me anything I did not deserve and could not invent, it is that I have been allowed to stand here this long and watch the reading go on after me, uninterrupted, the water scratching its letters over and over as if practice were the point and not the performance. And I sleep well before midnight, the old man’s midnight, which is eight, and I dream of a ladder with a boy’s hands on it, and a girl’s hand on a tiller, and a bell that does not stop even when fog says my gifts are obscurity. I sleep and I am forgiven by the breathing thing in me that liked to be needed and is now learning to like to be present, which is better.
