The ferry had been idling in the harbor for an hour by the time I reached the terminal, throwing a soft diesel tremor into everything that touched it, from the glass balustrade of the stairs to the paper ticket I kept folding and unfolding in my hand. A wind came down off the warehouses and slipped under my coat, sharp as if it had points. The gulls were already taking position along the railings and rooftop parapets the way they do when they know a particular hand on board will crack open the bread bags at the first dark lull. I could taste salt on my lips though the tide in this harbor was brown and restrained, hardly the sea at all, only its thick rivered foretaste. And above the ferry, sodium lights threw planes of orange onto the white hull, onto rope, onto cleats, into the slack faces of men in wool hats waiting by their vans to be told to go forward into the ship’s mouth. Every so often a voice over the tannoy delivered information in a tone that pretended not to know anything besides numbers and safety procedures. It was early still, but the sky was already night, a northern winter deepening itself without respect for what the clock on my phone insisted.

When I reached the front of the line for foot passengers, the woman behind the counter smiled in the harried but correct way of someone who would be at that window for six more sailings. “Good evening,” she said. “Do you have your booking reference?”

I told her my name. She entered it, looked up at me, then back at the screen. I waited for her to say, I’m so sorry, in that tone that can belong to anything or nothing, but she only clicked, printed, slipped the ticket and a thin plastic keycard under the clear divider. “You’re in a single, deck seven, port side.” She slid across a pamphlet with a diagram of the muster stations, a little ship like an unfolded toy.

“Have you sailed with us before?” she added, making a ritual of her questions.

“Years ago,” I said. My voice felt unfamiliar in my mouth. I thanked her and moved on.

The staircase up to the gangway was a tunnel of cold metal. People went slowly, shouldering bags, making way for one another without saying it. There were two older women, sisters perhaps, with matching roller suitcases that had a tiny blue pattern of birds. There was a tall student with a guitar case and a backpack like an exhausted animal. There was a family with a toddler in a red snowsuit who kept tugging at the cuff of his mother’s glove and saying in a piping voice, View, view, view. I stood behind them and watched the boy’s fine hair lift in the draft from the door. I had no luggage except a small duffel, lighter than what I’d once carry to the gym, and the folder the policewoman had asked me to bring with my brother’s photograph and whatever documents I still had. I’d gone upstairs at home and opened drawers I hadn’t looked into for years, and found a yellowed envelope with Ewan’s passport photos from when he was sixteen, his hair cut crudely by himself, one eye on the edge of laughter and the other veiled as if by the knowledge of that laugh, a boy trying out masks. It was the last official likeness of him I had. After that, everything I had was happenstance—rail receipts, a letter that never arrived, three lines on a postcard with a lighthouse and a single huge gull. He had a way of not being in photographs that felt deliberate, as if he wanted to practice for the day he would no longer be glanced at or remembered except by the person whose washroom mirror he was in, and even then only fleetingly, because there was shaving cream and hurry.

On the gangway a crewman in a fluorescent jacket joked to a line of drivers inching their vans into the belly: “Not that one, Davy, you tried to go in the lounge last time and frighten all the bairns.” Everyone laughed in the obliging way you laugh to ease a man through a job like his. The wind took our breath. The gangway’s tunnel magnified the ferry’s constant hum, the whole ship an animal breathing under a sleeping blanket. At the door, another crew member, a woman with a small tattoo of a fish on the inside of her wrist, scanned my ticket and handed me a folded card that had the meal times, a map of the decks, a note reminding everyone the weather might make the crossing long.

I went to my cabin because it felt like the simplest way of being settled, to have a room with a door and a light and a bed you might or might not use. The corridor smelled of cleaning solution and stale heat. The keycard hesitated and then the lock blinked green. The cabin was small, as I’d known it would be. A narrow bed with a strap to keep you in it, a porthole with a cap on it that you could twist to see black already pressing close. A shower you’d have to commit yourself to like a promise. I set down the duffel. It was only after that that I looked at myself, by accident in the mirror. The ship’s hum made the air inside my mouth vibrate against my teeth. I took the folder out of the bag and laid it on the small desk. For a moment I stood and looked at it as if it were a hungry thing.

The phone call had come two days earlier. A woman’s voice, soft, well-practised in the choreography of bad news. “Is this Mr. Graeme Kerr?” she had said. “My name is Sergeant Lorna Caldwell, I’m calling from—” and then a town I had to ask her to spell. She said then a building that had no strong presence in my mind, only the words we give to rooms in which certain things happen and are filed. “We have reason to believe,” she said, and there a paper turning somewhere in her room, “we have an unidentified male recovered from the shoreline north of here. Found this morning by a walker. In his pocket was a folded receipt from a hostel with the name E. Kerr scrawled on it with a number. We rang the number and the man who answered told us Ewan sometimes used him as an emergency contact but hadn’t seen him in a year. That was a Mr. Lawson in Dundee. He gave us this number for you. Does the name Ewan Kerr mean anything to you? He might be your brother?”

I had held my breath, then let it out. “Yes,” I had said. “He’s my brother.” It was heavier than it should have been to put that handle on him. For years he had been nothing I dared give a handle to. For years, if someone asked me, as they do from time to time in this world to make small talk acres wide but shallow, Do you have any brothers or sisters? I said either no or one, depending on the day, and if I said one, I would shrug when they asked what he did and say he did whatever, which is a thing you have to say until whoever asked finds a rope to pull for a different bell.

“We cannot be sure,” the woman had said. “The man was in the water, we’ll need a formal identification.” Formal, as if I might otherwise make an airy pass of my hand and say no or yes into the weather. “Would you be able to come up?” she had gone on, and listed the sailings for me, as if I were thinking about theater times. I had said yes again. Then we were two yeses the woman wrote neatly on her form and I was a note above her desk for the day the ferry arrived. She asked me questions about scars or tattoos. I thought of the small triangular pale patch on Ewan’s left ribcage where he’d burned himself on a motorbike muffler when he was nineteen and briefly free with machines, grease under his nails like a sudden tattoo. I thought of the chipped front tooth he had after running full tilt into a low wall when he was nine, chasing the dog. I thought of his habit of cutting his own hair between bathroom mirrors with a beard trimmer and a sense that the wildness was part of the point. I told her these things in a voice that tried to be helpful. She thanked me warmly for each thing. When I put down the phone, I went into the kitchen and stood with my palm on the cold metal of the sink and the tap just open enough to make water appear, the first thing I had seen that day move without my telling it to.

I had not seen Ewan in eight years. The last time had been our father’s funeral. Our father had died badly on an afternoon no one in our town had anything to do with except watch. He was on a ladder against the gable end of the house and the ladder slipped on wet leaves he himself had told us to clear the day before but had not. In his hands he had a tin of paint and a brush that he had rinsed so many times the bristles lay like hair before a barber’s scissors. When I got home from work and found the small ambulance across our drive with its lights still rotating as if embarrassed to have been called and not be needed, I went in and there he was on the kitchen floor wrapped round the words he could find for himself. He had bitten the inside of his cheek; the blood on his mouth made him look like a man who had been out all night drinking and had come home to apologize. That night, leaning over the hospital bed, the nurse came and asked did I have any other next of kin to call. I said, a brother. I said the name in front of a nurse who wrote it on something without looking up at me. And after all those years of being called by no one, Ewan came. He came in a jacket that belonged to someone else, or if it had ever belonged to him, he had mistreated it until it wanted to leave. He arrived and was contrite without being sorry. He put his hand on the bed rail and did not touch our father and said, in his voice that as a boy had carried everything before it, “I came, didn’t I?” and then looked up at me to see if that would do. We sat on plastic chair seats that squeaked and we said the things people say when the person in the bed is all the weight of a man but none of the speech. After that there was the funeral home, and the minister, and the house, and the unsigned will my father had meant to sign and had not. The house sat above the town with its gable newly half-painted, a stripe of fresh white like an arctic fox racing out of a hedge. We stood in the kitchen with two mugs and no milk and we had the talk people have when there are the living things and the dead things all mixed.

“What do you want to do about it?” I said. The word it was ready for everything, both the kitchen and the space that had opened in front of us and asked to be made into a life for someone. Ewan picked up a dishcloth and then put it down, and that was him, always trying without a plan.

“What I want?” he said. “I want not to argue, that’s what I want. You keep it, since you kept it already. Since you live here and make it into something I don’t even recognize.” It was true I had replaced the old stove and sanded the table and filled the hall with books. It was true he had left the place like a dog leaves a garden, indifferent to landlord’s notices and the weather.

We argued anyway, because if you deny a current, it only goes stronger round the back of your knees and unsteadies you. He accused me of wanting to be the kind of man who looks like a man who kept things. I accused him of not being able to keep so much as a shirt from week to week. It was one of those arguments that strips any beautiful language off you and leaves you with grunts and the names you’ve called each other in your heads. It ended, as it always ended, with him turning once towards me, as if to ask forgiveness in a different language neither of us had learned, and then turning again towards the door. I heard him take the house key off his ring, the metal sound like him blowing air between his teeth when he concentrated. He set it on the hall table where our mother used to put apples for visitors. The next morning he was gone. And gone after that not in an the interesting way of beginning again but in the duller way of smoke when the wind changes and it disorients you and you cough in the street and then you find you’ve turned up a different alley and cannot say how you got there.

On the ferry, the lifts were already busy with children pressing buttons because their parents were tired. I took the stairs. Men in wheelhouse-blue held doors for old women. There were signs on the walls with pictures of lifeboats as if to reassure by reminding. past the lounge a television played a news anchor’s face with the sound turned down, his mouth opening and closing like a man shouting advice from a pier to boys in the surf. The cafeteria was on deck six. I went in and took a tray and then left it because my hands were not ready to decide what weight of food to add to my stomach. I poured coffee and held the paper cup close to my face and let my eyes prick in the steam and made a place by a window whose glass had the texture of a winter river. I thought of the first time Ewan and I had gone out across the narrow causeway to the island that lay like a sleeping seal just off our town’s little south beach. You could walk over at low tide on stones that grew what the old men called witches’ hair. Our father took us, his hand on a boy’s collar each side of him during the learning, then none at all when pride required. He told us the time the sea would change its mind. He said he would shout and we must come. He said it only once, as if by not repeating himself he made the instructions truer. Ewan went barefoot because he wanted to feel the slime under his feet and I went in my trainers because I wanted my feet not to be cut to ribbons. That sums it up, how we took everything: him with his feet on the world to see what it would do to him; me with something between me and it.

We found the island all rock and thrift and a few herring gulls who always look at young boys as if keen to advise them of useless things. Our father showed us the stone where his own father had shown him that there was always be a barnacle on the high side smoother than the low side. He showed us how to lift without cutting your fingers, to feel for edges with the pads and not push your fingers under like a man who has all the time in the world. He let us walk on alone a little way along the spine of the island while he stayed where a man stays who is looking without seeming to look. Then, when the light changed and you could taste more metal in the air, he called. Ewan, of course, did not want to come. He did not want the day to be shorter. He did not want to accept the sky as an authority. He squatted by a pool and watched little shrimp flick their tails and slant themselves into the weed. He said, “I’ll be there, Dad.” He said, “Hang on.” I remember the long moment I stood between my father’s voice and my brother’s back. There are people who say they know clearly the times their lives turned. I have never been entirely sure. But in me there is a tight little muscle that twitches when I think about how much longer I stood that day towards the boy who delayed than towards the man calling me in. My father did not shout again. He watched the pinch of grip creep over the causeway stone by stone. He waited until the last man could have waited. He went towards my brother and he did not take him by the scruff and he did not hit him, and all those things were heroic. He extended a hand and Ewan took it, laughing, and put a foot wrong and water went against his shin like a sharp breath, and then they came to the place you run and then the shallows and then it is only boots and cursing and the aliveness of it. Afterwards, at home, their feet went in the oven and their faces went pink and my father said to me, in a voice as if we were a football crowd he wanted to encourage, “You did the right thing, son,” and I felt ashamed of the relief he offered me. He went outside and smoked under the gable for the first time since he had promised my mother he would not.

In the cafeteria now a woman with a handspan of rings on each finger turned from the counter with two plates of fish pie and two bowls of peas, looking for a place. I moved my duffel and she smiled thanks and set down the plates and a man came with cutlery and vinegar. They were the kind of couple who do not look matched but are, the way the sea strips some rocks and not others and you only know why if you were there while the stripping happened. They ate without talking for a while, then the woman said, “I hope it’s not lumpy this time,” and the man said, “It’s always lumpy, you should hope for decent lumps,” and they laughed quietly. I wanted to lean over and ask them their names and whether they had children or were children never realised, but that is a tourist’s hunger. I drank the coffee and it was burnt and perfect. It made a place in me where heat could gather.

When the ship moved, it did so with surprising gentleness. The hum deepened a nightstep and in the windows there was the sense of something being pulled past rather than us moving. Down in the heel of the hull, trucks went forward and stopped and went forward. Ropes were made enormous around capstans, then let go. A man on the quay standing with a pole flicked it the way a dance teacher might and the great ship obliged. Then there was only the thrumming and the lights. The harbor walls slipped by, the gulls unbalanced themselves and corrected with rude cries. We went out past the semaphore angles of cranes and the little green light that is both faith and regulation and into what the charts call the approaches. I had meant to go out on deck as we sailed, but I found myself fixated by the window’s oval and its offering: stubs of light becoming thin threads between invisible poles, a red hazard buoy, a flash every few seconds that mapped itself if you were patient, the gaze of a lightship flattening me to a boy again.

There were not many children on board for the winter night, but the ones there had the special kind of permission night gives. The toddler in the red suit, who looked now like a tomato escaping its skin, pressed his face against the glass and blew a circle of breath onto it and made shapes with his finger in the steam until his mother turned his body gently and said, “Come away, Finn, you’re all wet,” and put a dry scarf around his neck and a kiss into the hair behind his ear. The boy looked at me and then looked past me to the place where this night was going. He had a way of holding your eye and giving nothing back that felt generous rather than cold.

I went up to the shop and moved between the books and the snacks. There was a display of glossy magazines on winter jackets with a lot of attention to zips. There was a rack of stuffed seals you could buy so you wouldn’t be alone while you slept. I did not buy anything. I went onto the outer deck from the lee door by the shop and the cold made me silly, it was so quick, so unafraid of me. The deck had that rough nonslip paint that stubs your shoe while you walk. The funnels gave off a smell that always makes me think of scorched toast and mended cars. I went to the stern rail and looked back at the city. It was already only points. It reassembled itself into a whole every time my mind permitted. Behind me a couple smoked, their faces lit in tramlines. At the very stern, a young man stood alone taking long exposures with a camera on a little tripod. I wondered if he was sending them to someone to say, Look, I am on my way to you, as if light leaving the lens were always only a message.

I stayed until my fingers ached and went back in, through the door that sucked itself shut with a cough. In the stairwell a man in overalls whose name tag said J. DALRYMPLE wheeled a trolley of bins that smelled of a thousand dinners. We smiled the way people do when one of them smells very strong and neither of them wishes to acknowledge it. The ship’s corridor was endless but at the right places it let you be where you wanted. I passed the lounge with its daylight-bright lights, the men in cable-knit, the woman with a paperback novel held in that professional reader’s way between finger and thumb as if it were weightless paper. I passed a noticeboard with a map of the route and the drawn-on times for the tide races and recommended places to be. Someone had tacked up a notice for a lost scarf. The scarf’s photograph was a piece of a life uprooted, coarse greens and a faint stain at one end.

In the cabin I lay down on top of the blanket, listening to the engines ride themselves like men on rough horses. The porthole cover was shut, and yet I could feel the black outside like cold skin pressing on the window. For a time I dozed and then I did not. In the room next to mine a child began to cry with that late-night despair that says no particular thing and so encompasses everything. The parent in the room spoke quietly for so long you could map their breath and know when it would enter the child again. The lull of the engine left a fringe of sounds for the night to decorate itself: cutlery knocked together, a ladder being moved, the squeak of a self-closing door with its oil long given up, the two-note whistle of a kettle with the spout too full. The bed belt lay like a climbing rope across my legs; when the ship rolled the rope twitched against me.

Sometime after midnight, when a particular heaviness in the boat’s movement told me we had exited the harbor’s spell and crossed without seeing the line into the long water, I got up and put my shoes on and went out again. A man in a fleece behind the information desk was dozing with a pen in his fingers like a conductor. The empty corridors had an intimacy I had not expected; the ship felt protective of its quiet. In the observation lounge—bless the silliness of observation in the middle of night—the rows of seats faced glass that was more mirror than window. My face superimposed itself over the black, my breath making circles for someone else to write a message in. A woman in a dark coat sat with her knitting on her lap, her hands paused in their speed. She looked up when I came in and then away the way women do when men enter rooms at night. I sat at the other side of the lounge and we watched the dark perform.

After a while she spoke, not to me, but to the whole space, a small offering to the night. “My mother used to ask me to knit through the rough bits,” she said. Her voice was almost frightened of itself. “She said knitting gets you through a storm if your hands are occupied.” She smiled to herself and then seemed to be embarrassed at the words.

“What are you making?” I asked, because the question would not frighten anyone.

“Booties,” she said. And then as if she needed to say more to make the booties true, “For my neighbour’s girl. She’s going to be twenty-four and pregnant if she doesn’t manage to have the child before midnight.” She laughed at herself. “I don’t know why I’m taking this with me now. They won’t fit any baby by the time I get back.”

“We believe in the old things to keep the new things from being unkind,” I said. It came out stranger than I meant it.

“That’s true,” she said gravely, which is how I knew she had been told this once by her own grandmother, over nettle soup or in the blackout.

I did not ask her why she was traveling tonight. I did not tell her why I was. We gave each other the quiet.

I must have slept sometime before two, because I woke with my neck angry at the wall and the taste of a strange dream in my mouth. The ship was rolling with more confidence, long lazy movements that made the monitors on the desk flash readiness for rougher waters. The northern sea is not a big sea in the way maps teach, but it is muscular and it does not like to be told what to do. When we reached the strait’s narrowness where the tidal streams meet under some long-buried channel like arguments in a family, we would feel it. Our father had shown us tide charts as if they were comic strips, but without the jokes. There is a place, he said pointing, where the water goes very hard. He did not mind what word he used for the water, because he had known it as everything from friend to bully. He had an expression for pointing with first two fingers and then three; it was vulgar and tender.

I tried to find a position where sleep and the ship’s rhythm agreed. I thought of Ewan adrift and then laughed at myself for that melodrama and then felt sick at the thought that even this might be kinder than what he had had. In the films a body drifts, because time needs to move forward while the hero solves things. In life a body will make decisions you don’t approve. It will snag under a pier for a week. It will lodge under a heave of kelp and comfort crabs. It will be found by a sheep and not a man. There was a moment, lying in the too-warm air, when I tried to picture Ewan’s face as I had last seen it—tired and wild and beautiful and everything I had not got—and then I tried to place it into a room with bright white tile and a stainless steel slab and fluorescent tubes. My mind refused. It did not know how to carry him from one to the other. I tried to remember things he had said that might tell me what to do when I stood where I was going. There is a letter he wrote me from Belfast, when he was twenty-two, that I have read more than I have read any bill. He wrote, “G—, listen: the sea is not a thing you own, you lend yourself to it like you lend your face to a mirror and then you take it back. It’s not forgiveness, it’s just time.” He wrote, “Don’t make every mistake I make by watching me not make it.” He wrote, “I love you like a wild dog loves the man who feeds it and cannot be chained.” I didn’t write back. It seemed then to be braver and more honest not to write back than to answer in a thin ink. It seemed then that if I did not answer the letter the versions of us we had as boys would be preserved somewhere without me realising it, that my not writing was a kind of glass case.

By three the lounge had emptied to the man with the guitar case asleep with his mouth open and a small thread of spit trembling with the motion of the ship. There was the woman with the knitting, asleep now with the needles crossed like swords. I went back to the cabin and lay down and pulled the blanket over me and then discovered why the strap exists. I wrapped it across my hips and buckled it and felt like a man in a child’s cot. Sleep came then without asking for a price or a promise. When I woke the light in the porthole had changed. Outside there was a gray that was not the ferry’s halogen but the sky deciding to be lit from within. The roll had gone. The air lightened. The tannoy coughed and then a voice young and masculine and too exact to be in command said, “Good morning, ladies and gentlemen, we would like to inform passengers we will be arriving at—” the name that I had heard only from strangers, and that I had to make my mind believe—“approximately one hour fifteen minutes from now. Breakfast will be served until—” times I forgot instantly. “If you have small children please take care when moving about the vessel as it is still a little bumpy in places. Please remember to take all your belongings with you when leaving the ship.” Please remember. Please remember, as if that request were adequate.

On deck the cold had moderated its cruelty to something merely indifferent. We were in a world of islands, a scatter of low hills whose colors were bleached and then, here and there, one orange patch like a hearth. Sheep made white lies on the slopes. There was a lighthouse on a tooth of rock, its paint flaking obediently. The sea’s surface was broken into fields by wind, light and dark shutting one another down and then opening again. The ship went forward with the authority that comes only from having done this too many times to get the words wrong. A pilot boat came out to meet us, bouncing like a child. The gulls who had come down with us gathered because their map included a line drawn straight from city to harbor that we can’t see. They were generous and ghostly and ruthless. I watched the men at the bow perform their knots with the indifference of men tying their shoes. The harbor itself was an idea that had been repeated where shelter made it possible. A line of oil, a line of chain, a line of men in hi-vis who looked reduced by the cold. Warehouses like hands in pockets.

I went back to the cabin and washed my face in cold water until I could bear the man who looked back. I took the folder and put it in the bag and the bag on my shoulder. I looked once more at the rucked bed and the belt and a place on the sheet where my breath must have left something different from the night before’s breath. There are men who make rituals of leaving rooms, aligning things to how they found them, setting a little order against a world that takes order and asks for more. I closed the door and slid the card into its slot to let it die. Then I went downstairs, past that section of carpet in the corridor with the throw that never lies right no matter how you set it. The lower decks had trucks hemming themselves impatiently; the foot passenger gangway had a man in a fluorescent jacket with a pinched face I recognized as the face of someone who had been up since four.

On the quay, the cold air was animal. It smelled softer than the departure, more like peat and old rope than salt. I put my hand to my cheek as if it were my mother’s. A van idled, its breath making a braid. People arranged themselves into lines for taxis and buses, even though there were not enough of either to make the lines useful. I held back because I knew there would be someone for me and it wouldn’t be kind to make him find me among a crowd. The woman sergeant did not come herself. A man my age or a little older, in a suit that had known the cost of it, came towards me with the walk of someone who would like to keep some dignity for everyone involved. He put out his hand and I put mine into it and thought as I did of how my father shook hands, with the softest part of his palm leading and his long fingers folding deliberately.

“Mr. Kerr?” the man said. “I’m Constable John Watt. I’m very sorry we’re meeting like this.”

“Graeme,” I said, because hearing my own surname then felt like hearing my father’s. “Thank you.”

“We’ll get you sorted as quickly and gently as we can,” he said. I could tell he’d said this to enough people that he believed it, which is almost as good as it being true. “Have you got anyone with you?” he asked, though he could see that I hadn’t.

“No,” I said. He nodded once, as if that confirmed some weight-of-word he had placed already.

“We’ll just go up to the—” he named the building as the sergeant had done. “We’re not far. They keep it… they keep it right up there by the old ropewalk. You’ll see.”

He offered me the front passenger seat without asking if I wanted it and I took it. The car was warm in the wrong way, dry and scented with something pine that made my tongue shrivel. We pulled away from the ferry and across a small roundabout where a sign with three place names could have been entirely invented: Scalloway, Bigton, Gulberwick. The constable drove the way men drive who have stopped taking risks and started doing things because someone must. He asked if I’d like to stop for tea. I said no, or rather, I made the noise that means no and he took it. He asked if the weather had been all right for the crossing. I said I had been asleep for the worst of it. He nodded as if that too told him something useful. We passed shops that were always the same in the north, their fronts a compromise between someone’s idea of tidy and the wind. A man with a dog whose coat was too long for this place crossed the road carefully; the dog trotted as if he were hoping to outrun his own hair.

“How did you find me?” I asked, because it had been pricking at me since the call.

He glanced at me as long as the road allowed. “There was a wallet in the jacket,” he said. “Not much in it. Tenner and some coins and a card for a hostel in the city, and…” He paused a fraction. “And a bit of paper folded with your name and number and—” he smiled not kindly and not unkindly. “—and what I suppose was your father’s landline, though it got one of those robot voices when Sergeant Caldwell tried it.”

“He wrote it down?” I asked. It was astonishing because it was nothing. If you have not been in the habit of being remembered, to be written is to be given something like breath.

“Some handwritten notes, aye,” he said. “You’d be surprised how often men carry their dead in their pockets.” He seemed to regret the sentence and changed to: “Mr. Lawson said he knew your brother from some work. He said he thought he was heading north for a bit of the fishing again. He thought he had a friend up here but he couldn’t recall the name.”

“Ewan has friends,” I said with a sudden fierce unwillingness to offer my brother to a pronoun.

The constable accepted this like a diagnosis.

We turned off the main road up into a lane that had been there long enough to have opinions. A long brick building lay along the left like a ship with its decks stripped, window after window like ribs. He nodded at it. “That’s the ropewalk. They used to lay the hemp here. Men used to make a life with their hands at arms’ length from one another. Now it’s a place for start-ups and these things.” He waved his hand as if the word itself required a fuzzy gesture. “The mortuary’s in the old laundry beside it. They keep it very proper.”

My stomach made its small noise, a preparation for something it could not do for me. The car pulled into a little forecourt with two spaces. The building looked like a house that had not quite stopped being military. He stopped the car and turned off the ignition so that the silence came down like snow in a photograph. He looked at me mildly. “We can go straight in,” he said. “Or we can take a moment.”

“Straight in,” I said, because anything else would be longer and I did not trust anything that took time now. I followed him up the three steps and into a hall too warm, with a radiator that hummed to keep itself from being noticed. There was a smell of something floral trapped from last spring in a bottle. A woman sat behind a wood-laminate desk and did not stand. She had the face of someone who was the right person for the room. She smiled once and then looked down again to give me the space to decide how human to be. John Watt spoke quietly to her and she made a call and then stood and came around the desk and when she put a hand on my arm the heat of her palm moved through my coat like kindness and I wanted her to keep it there long enough for something to transfer.

“We’ll be very brief,” she said. “You will see only what you need to see. If you want us to stop at any point you just say. If you want us to close the sheet at any time we will. There will be a sheet. I will be with you, as will Constable Watt.”

We went along a corridor with tile that took the sound of our steps and made it official, then through a door with a press-lever. The room beyond was small and white and bright. It had one window that did not open. The stainless steel table was exactly as my mind had finally managed to allow, and exactly different in its actualness. On it lay a man. The sheet was over him but fitted around his shoulders. It went around him like something you might have used as a joke when you were a child, playing ghosts in the sitting room. His feet were out of sight. There was a sound that might have been a fridge or might have been my body performing itself.

“We are going to fold back the sheet,” the woman said, and she looked at me steadily and with such self-belief that I made a small nod that did not come from any part of me I typically inhabit. She took the sheet in her hands and did what she had said. John Watt stood at my left with his hands folded in front of him in a way that made them look old.

When I saw the face it was at once like the face I had made and utterly unlike it. That this is always true of faces you see in the place where this one lay did not help. The skin had been the sea’s for a time. The eyes were closed and had not been his even while he was alive; the mouth was that slack absence I had seen on my father the first night. The nose, my god. The nose was my mother’s. The hair was cut by someone who had not minded much what it did but had known the way the hair lay in the first place. There was a small healed nick above the right eyebrow where Ewan had pulled a bottle crate down on his head when he was ten. There was not the tooth he chipped as a boy, because this man had his two incisors neat if a little stamped out. I tried to make the man’s jaw the jaw I remembered, the jaw that when it set you would have done well not to be around, and I could not—no, that’s not true, I could see it. It was there, under the slackness. His ears were the same as mine and not. It would have been a kindness to have a scar in the exact place I had offered Sergeant Caldwell, a neat triangular white on his left side at the ribs, so that I could say, Yes, yes, and then cry or not and be held or not and sign the paper and go with my grief and place it somewhere even if I could not lift it myself. It would have been good to have the odd crooked stitch of Ewan’s chin from the time he fought a boy in a playground and took a fence upright with his face. There were none of these, or they had not been asked to give up their secrets. There was a man’s face I could both recognize and argue with.

“Would you… would you like me to—?” the woman said quietly. Her hand hovered over the sheet, over the body, as if her hand were a sail searching for wind.

“There’s a scar on the side, left side,” I said, my voice low. “There might be. There was.”

She glanced at John Watt. He nodded. She went around the table and lifted the edge of the sheet a few inches. “It’s fine,” she said, to John as much as to me. She pulled the sheet down until the chest and ribs appeared white and not white. The nipple had darkened with the cold. Under the left was a shadow that could have been anything at this bright time of day. She made her hand into a cup and held it over the place to shade it. There was a faint wedge shape pale in the paler skin.

“Here?” she said. She did not touch the mark.

I closed my eyes. He is and he isn’t, my grandfather had said about a man in our town when we were boys, and I had not understood and had gone outside to kick a can until someone called me back in. He is and he isn’t. The world was a boat and it rocked. I put my hand on the edge of the steel and felt the cold climb my skin like water.

“Is this your brother, Mr. Kerr?” the constable said. His voice had changed not at all. It was not a voice for repeating things, and he did not.

I thought of the day on the causeway, how my father had called and I could not bring my body to go that way quickly enough to be the son that the sentence You did the right thing could love. I thought of the time Ewan had phoned me from a phone box in a town so small even the postcards were of somewhere else, and he had said, “I saw a woman walk into the sea today and walk out again, she looked cleaner coming out than she did going in,” and then the line had clicked and gone because the coins had been used and he had not had the caution to have more. I thought of my father in the kitchen before the hospital, how he had put down the paintbrush like a man laying a spoon on a table and had nodded to me though he had no idea who I was. I thought of the empty place at my table where a person could sit and eat and say things and be known. And I thought that if I said no now, even if the no were a scientific thing, a correct and upright no, I would be putting myself back on the island alone, without even the gulls to advise me.

“Yes,” I said. It was a small word and it filled the room like weather. “Yes. That’s my brother.” I did not know whether it was his blood or my blood that warmed the word as it went.

The woman nodded once and put the sheet back as if she were tucking in a child. She rested her hand for a second on the covered chest, and her fingers made small ghosts. The constable had a clipboard already and a pen and he did not ask me to sign immediately; instead he looked at me in a way that explained why he had been sent to meet me and not someone else. He said, “Take your time,” and meant it, and I nodded because I did not know what else to do with my head in that moment.

On the way out through the corridor with the tile, my hand lifted to touch the paint on the wall the way you do when you leave anywhere—your primary school, a stadium, a prison, a church—because it keeps you real if you feel something solid. There was the sound of a kettle somewhere and someone laughing in the room beyond and that too was a mercy, the world uninterested in my extraordinary day. Outside, the light had come properly. The wind had picked up a piece of paper and set it to chase itself in the forecourt as if it had been waiting for this exact day to do it. A gull flew low enough over my head that I heard its wings bite air. I wanted suddenly to go to the ropewalk and run my hands along its length and feel hemp and the ghost of men in their aprons, their breath sweet with tea. John Watt opened the passenger door and then closed it because I wasn’t coming yet. He stood with his hands in his jacket like a boy at the edge of a dance hall. He did not hurry me. I thought of the ferry sitting now in the harbor like a gentle patient thing. I thought of the sleeping cabins with their straps, one of them not yet cold from me. I placed my palm flat on the car roof and it was gritty with salt. The thought came to me that Ewan, if he were alive, would have laughed at the solemnity of my hand on a car, would have reached and smacked it and said, “Come on,” and started the engine without asking whose it was or what it would cost when we were seen and stopped. The thought came also to me that he would be quiet now and honest now in a way he had practiced sometimes when we were boys and he wanted to be on my team.

I got into the car. The constable cleared his throat and touched the wheel as if to check it was still there. He said, “We’ll get a cup of tea in my office if you like, and then we can talk about what happens next. I will do all the phoning and the men will do the taking and bringing. There will be forms. We can take time for you to—” he searched for a word that would not offend the dead— “for you to call whoever needs to be called.”

I nodded. He started the engine. We reversed carefully as if the building itself might step back for us but would not. As we pulled out, a woman walked past the gate with a bag of bread in her elbow crook and a smile at the gulls that was both invitation and threat. The car took the road and the road sorted under us into the lines it had been made to be. I looked out at the low hills and the sheep and the lighthouse and the little squares of polythene over the little crops and the man with the dog with too much hair and the shop sign that would always be a mouthful to a stranger and I wanted to say to Ewan what he would have said if our bodies could talk across rooms we had made forbidding: I’m here. Whether that meant in front of him now or only in front of whatever this day had placed in his name, I cannot say. But I felt his weight shift, wherever he was, and for a moment the sea lay down in me and was only water.
