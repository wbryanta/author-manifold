By the time I shoulder the tripod down the last incline, the shore line is a white ring on the canyon, a wedding band that does not fit the finger anymore. Every step crushes through a crust of mineral into silt that sighs and remembers weight. The water has slunk back to the serpentine of the original creek and the flats are a quilt of old streets, poured slabs, stone ribs of basements, the negatives of trees. The reservoir’s stink—algae and iron and something like a slaughterhouse left in sun—rides the cool of the morning. A gull I have never seen this far inland laughs from a light pole stump that used to be the corner of Willow and Third.

I set the tripod legs, twist each clamp with my gloved fingers until the prism head is level. The air is still enough that my breath trembles the bubble, so I hold it and wait. From up here you can see the dam, a gray, patient arc a mile away, its own mineral skin scarred by decades of water levels, like a doctor’s tongue depressor left too long in mouths. Two pickup trucks have parked up on the gravel of the new shoreline; men in hats are already on the flats with their dogs, calling to each other in a way that attempts casualness in the presence of a place they were told they would not stand on again.

I push the point of the leg into the ground and think of the way my father used to push fence posts, his shoulder to the long pry bar, the smell of the bent T-posts hot in the field, his method patience in his body even when his words leaked from him sharp as nails. He wasn’t a surveyor. He welded. He cut and mended metal until his hands took on the color. But he loved maps. He kept a roll of the old town plat in a tube we were not supposed to touch. He unrolled it once at the kitchen table when the papers came from the county and the blue lines of streets and lot numbers trumpeted their unassailable order. He tapped the block our house was inside and traced his finger down to the ditch and there to the creek, nodding to himself, as if the paper’s ink were both threat and salvation. When they condemned the town, he did not go to the big meetings. He was not a man for standing and shouting. He went instead to walk at night, his boots measuring the edges of things in the dark, his flashlight a brief and moving star.

Sixty years. The lowest level in sixty years, and I am out here with my county-issued tablet, a RTK receiver clipped to my belt, and a habit of making and believing in lines. I stand in the bowl of the reservoir as if in a lung exhaling, and the exhalation has shaped this earth in the same way breath hollows the chest. My childhood here is a time I have to step around carefully inside, not because I will fall into it, but because the thin crust of nostalgia will try to claim my boot, pull it right off.

The total station is back at the truck, locked up because last week somebody stole one off the east bank while a crew was eating lunch. Today I am only making a control network with the rover, stitching the flats with points so the company we hired for the drone can anchor their orthomosaic to the world. There is a jitter of anxiety in me about being seen, as if this is too naked an act: this putting down coordinates on a place that already has names.

The first point, a rebar with orange ribbon pounded into the mud near a confirmation of curb—still with the square notch for an old water box. I stand over it, rod at my navel, adjust to get plumb. The receiver hums, finds itself, deciduous, evergreen, satellite. A little icon blinks. Fixed. I press store and give it the name CONTROL-01 when what I want to name it is Willow. Or the corner where I fell and split my chin on a day the hardware store had a bin of loose nails reduced for flood, somebody’s job to sell the last of them. My mother poured hydrogen peroxide on my wound from a brown bottle and made the sound between a shush and a prayer.

Beyond this point the mud is patterned with small tracks. The dogs have printed a history of their own: last night, the night before. A heron has written a long, patient letter of toes from the east. And there are human prints, barefoot, the heel deeper than the toes: some kid out here feeling the planet with the parts of their body meant to be held on a mother’s lap and patted dry. The idea of naked skin on this mud makes my teeth ache.

The men from the trucks are calling. Hey there, miss! You surveying? You from the county? The first one has a voice like gravel in a jar. I keep my hat brim low and lift my hand to show both friendliness and boundary. The second dog, a wet Lab whose coat is already turned brown with this place, comes near and leans the shape of itself against my leg without looking at me, once, like old friends do.

“What are you mapping, ma’am?” the gravel voice says when he gets close enough to make my polite nod not sufficient. “You going to draw it all back like it was?”

“Streets,” I say. “We’re mapping the structures and where the streets used to be. It’s for documentation.”

“So you’re not going to rebuild it?” he says, and his laugh is something that wants to be sadness and can’t be bothered.

“We’re just—” I indicate the rod and the blinking receiver, “We’re just trying to capture it while it’s here.”

He nods, squinting. His hat says Armed Forces Roofing. His dog’s name is printed on her collar tag: Pearl. He points beyond me. “There’s the school, you see? And behind it, where the mailboxes used to be. My aunt lived over there. She cried herself mean when they came with the white paint to put Xs on the doors.”

Pearl hauls toward a silt rib that might have been an elm fence line and finds something to roll on, the complicated joy dogs have in rot. I imagine the white paint Xs, ghosted under the water for all these decades, now lifting like a scream.

“We lived down by the creek,” I tell him before I can stop the sentence. He looks at me then, really looks, at my mouth, my eyes, the way you assess somebody telling you you might have had the same first-grade teacher. The second man is younger, in a shirt with a company name too new to be real, the kind that is a man’s last name plus & Sons before he has any sons. He looks between us and says, “Dang,” as if finding commonness is a trick he doesn’t know how to do.

The older man kneels by where the school slab lies like the foundation of a camper lost to a fire and pats the concrete. He says, “You all moved when?” and I say, “In spring of ‘sixty-one,” and he says, “We were ‘sixty-two. Had one winter with no heat because my dad didn’t want to fix a thing they were going to drown. Froze peas on the inside of the windows. My mother hung laundry by the pellet stove and it smelled like pajamas and diesel.”

I want him to go away because I want it to be only me and this place and the childish part of me that thinks the bowl of the reservoir is a stage and this morning is the one-day only show. But I also want him to stay because he’s my category of human: somebody who believes in objects and thin line pens, somebody who has put a finger along a curb to measure a story. I have his dog pressed into my shin. The dog’s weight is all in, the kind of lean that doesn’t ask.

What do you say to somebody who has also held this street in their hands? He says it for me. “You came to do a thing and not get caught in the ghost of it. I get it. We’ll go. My nephew here wanted to fish catfish in the creek itself for a story to tell his kids, but that’s just more ghosts. You take care.” He points out into the sun as if we are on a porch and the sky is a road. “There,” he says, “Do you see that line of little trees? Cottonwoods, maybe? That’s the creek’s ghost.” He whistles to Pearl, and they go; the younger man is tugging with two hands to get the Labrador off her dream.

I work. I store points and name them with numbers and letters, and the tablet pings a little tone that sounds for all the world like a child’s toy, which I only hear because this is a morning full of small acoustic humiliations. CONTROL-02, CONTROL-03, CONTROL-04. The names of our four corners. My house was on that block, the one with the step-down porch, and if I go there now I will find nothing I have not found inside my mouth at night in the years since, running my tongue along the ridge behind my teeth where my childhood perches like a bird that refuses to be identified.

At the bottom of the reservoir, the ground flakes in curled scales where it has dried too fast. When I step, the flakes break and their white is all the bones of fish. When I was a kid, people said the carp grew large as barrels, gold and dumb under there, eating worms shaped like the words people said about them. We caught them from the bank when I was a teenager and they were not monsters; we set them back in the water in summer twilight with the sweetness of imperfection only teenagers can generate.

There is a line of stones that I know in the body of my knowledge even before I look up at the hill and orient: the Lowe’s retaining wall stones hauled in back when they were hauling anything, making places stay put. It is the edge of my grandparents’ garden. My grandmother tied twine between bent rebar rods and the beans found them and my sister and I made tunnels under the long green curtains and used them as the walls for a house we assigned rules to. Do not touch the dirt with the white stick, or you have to be the mother. Say shh when you hear my grandmother come near.

I find where the garage was by the hinge pins rusted off in the air. The slab is wrinkled at one corner like a tablecloth. A rectangle of darker, smoother concrete is the mark of the oil my father changed for thirty years, a kind of map unto itself, a coastline where his wrench fell, where his socket rolled. I squat and put my palm on that darkness, and it is warm from the sun, and that warmth crosses my palm into the ladder bones of my hand exactly like his blood when he took my hand in his grip when we went to look at the lake for the first time, him silent and more silent, a silence even the temptation to swear couldn’t break. The water was high then, touched the flood-swollen willows, the dam’s spillway singing. People on the shore had picnics. The county sent a band. It was a ribbon-cutting afternoon and the men in ties smiled wide and the photographers took their photographs of future. My mother had a white dress with orange flowers. She held my sister on her hip and the hem of the dress got a swampy stain and she said who cares, who cares, as if she could not be made to care again.

We left when he did not, and then we left when we all did, and there is no deep moral to it, only a different geological period of our family.

The receiver chimes and I straighten and move on and tell my bones that the past is not a set of coordinates I can store. It lives in a part of my head I cannot reach with a radio.

The old pharmacy is a rectangle defined by where the tile is still almost-blue. Scooped in the corner is a hole where a wood post had been, and in it, some animal has slept, or something like sleeping has happened there. I set a point at the corner of the slab and another at the midline of the street—no paint for the center stripe, just the difference between the smoother pour in the middle and the gravel at the edges. For years after the town was gone, the county used the bend in this road as the way to explain to people what eminent domain felt like. It is not reaching into your pocket for your wallet and finding it gone. It is reaching for the breath you take next and finding you have to pay for it.

My radio hisses. “Copy?” The voice is my boss’s voice filtered thin by handheld, too cheerful for this hollow. “You at Site 3 yet?”

“Almost,” I say. “Signal’s good. I’m going to tie into the old school weights and then move to the creek side.”

“News vans were asking if we were out there. I told them we were concentrating on deliverables. That’s your get-out-of-an-interview free card, okay?”

“Okay,” I say, and think of the vans up in the parking pullout near the dam, of the people with powder on their cheeks and their microphone cords snaking to equipment boxes. I imagine a woman reporter who grew up in a place that did not get measured and feels affronted by the existence of the bowl itself. I will not look up there when I cross so that the camera lens will not see that old flicker in me that is not anger and not forgiveness.

The slab of the school has the two lines of locker bases, the bolts sheared away, the smell of steel faint as perfume. Oh god, the June mornings. A teacher with a pin that said Fight Fluoride who smelled like oatmeal and wore sad jumpsuits. A boy who beatboxed in the back of the class until he did not and moved away overnight in a U-Haul that had his father’s life in it held with quick knots that undid themselves on the freeway. These were after we had moved, when the new school was up on the ridge, and the old was already under water, but the furniture and teachers were the same: ghosts moved house like everyone else.

Down near where the creek meets its bed again, there is a tangle of metal that from a distance looks like a submerged animal. It is in fact the skeleton of the old irrigation pump that kept the ditch high enough in July to save the late squashes. Its ribs are rebar. Its hip is a wheel. As I approach it, the mud changes, becomes more like clay, and when I step, I feel the suction and pull. It would be a silly place to die, even sillier to call for help. I skirt the low spot, placing my weight on the places that resist. Old knowledge rides up my shin, as if walking a creek bed were a muscle like any other and it remembers. Watch for where grass once grew. Watch for the small uprush of flies from rot.

We are to mark the streets first, then features like slabs, then isolated artifacts of value to the county historian—whatever that means—and then anything that seems to us to deserve a point. My sense of deserving is all out of alignment; I give points to a zinc Mason jar lid, a ring of it I pick up with my fingertips and hold like something alive, and to a set of concrete steps, only the upper three above the slurry, leading to nothing but sky.

By midmorning, people have peppered the flats, pockets of them in colors purchased recently for hiking, cameras slanted on straps. A child runs in zigzags. A man with a long gray ponytail walks upright as a judge but his bare feet are covered to the ankle in mud socks. At the edge of where the water still is, the brack-water of the far drop, men stand with extended rods and lines that draw electronic marks on their fish finder screens. Their eyes are those of men peering into a scrying glass that will not lie to them about where everything has gone; they are reading the earth as a kind of grocery list. My grandmother would have said there’s hunger written in those faces. She believed hunger was a thing you could see. She would point at a neighbor and say there, you see it? Like light on a windowpane.

At the end of the street that was our street, there is a square cutout where the sidewalk used to dip for the alley. There is the metal cap of the old well, bent but still a circle. There is also a ladder of brittle wood that looks like a section of fence and has something like electrical tape wound around one side. The wind does a small thing with the tape, unrolls it an inch, then lets it snap back. The sound is a handslap on water. I know with the instant, interior knowledge you have for three or four corners in the world that this was our alley. I go to my knees.

Under one wedge of old sidewalk is a confusion of glass. I lean down and lift a piece with two fingers. It is a bottle bottom with the maker’s mark. A child who was me had an egg carton of bottle bottoms dug from the alley—pale green and clear and an amber the color of iced tea. She would line them up on her windowsill, the light through them doing the work of church in saving her from certain things.

Something flashes. I think it is sunlight off glass and it is, but it is not the bright of a round bend of bottle. It is blue, darker than the silt, and then it is uncovered. A marble. The small weight I did not know I would be given. It is the blue of rivers in maps nobody believes. It is a blue cat’s eye with yellow inside with the exact loop of flame. It is a thing I lost once when I begged my mother to stop in the new town so I could buy a bag of marbles from the five-and-dime because there is nothing that measures childhood as the sound of heavy glass in a cloth sack and because I told myself objects could keep maps in them like medicinal powders. I lift it with my dirty fingers and hold it against the inside fleshy part of my wrist, and the cool of it finds my blood. When I drop it into my palm and close my hand, I do not know whether to put it in my pocket or the ash of my mouth as if to speak it back into the present would be to say something final to the past.

There is a rule against salvage. There are signs where we parked that say nothing can be removed from the site of historical interest. When I first read those words in the morning, their tone felt fussy and mean, as if the county—even our county, even my county—were a librarian full of scold. Now I bow my head in the direction of the dam and the office where the signs were printed and say aloud, quietly, just one thing: forgive me. I put the marble in the pocket inside my vest, the one with the Velcro to keep my phone from water damage. It is terrible, how the moral law changes with proximity.

The creek is the creek again, loud and small, pushing a brown line into the lipped ditch with a tantrum at finally being loosed in its own bed. Cottonwoods stipple the line of it as if somebody had salted the edge with seeds and hope. There are the animal bones I expected, sun-blackened and fragile, turning to lace. There are also pieces of the lives we had and the lives others had after we left. A stove door. A syringe. A piece of someone’s wedding filmy white. Somebody has propped up a headboard against the skeleton of an elm with a gesture that is both art and trouble and says this is a bedroom now because I say so. I make two points at the creek crossing, on the old culvert elbows that show where the street went once over it. I take a third point at the centerline of where the bridge would have been and name it what I should not on the tablet: BRIDGE. The word settles in the spreadsheet like a fish nesting in a depression.

A voice behind me says my name.

Not the married name I use at work. Not my mother’s maiden name from when we were here. The name I know to answer to when the caller knows me before the reservoir. I turn and Lew is there. He was in my class two years, maybe three. He was small then, tinnitus of energy, the kind of boy who made paper things and launched them, the kind who was born capable of summoning an argument on behalf of a wall he didn’t even want to stand behind. He is not small now. He is bony as if pressed, his shirt flapping on his chest as if it is not sure it knows how to sit on him.

“I wondered,” he says. He raises an eyebrow like the old days, as if we used to sit on the sleeping snake on the school cafeteria floor during tornado drills and tell each other our best lies. “I saw the county list and I saw the name and I said to myself, that’ll be her.”

“It is,” I say, lifting my hand. His face does a thing I could write about for ten minutes and not get right: the way relief wears sorrow like an earring.

“You were always good at the math,” he says. “I remember you with your pencil fisted up and the rows of numbers down the side of the ledger. You’d stare out the window and then you’d be done and the rest of us were still scraping our erasers into dust. As if you had the numbers running down a hall toward you.”

“You were always good at the paper cranes,” I say, because the past is a trap that requires flattery to open. “I saved one for years.”

“You put it in your book,” he says. “You salted it behind a postcard and forgot it until it fell out at just the wrong time and made you cry. Is that how it worked? That’s how it worked for me.” He says this as if he has been in the same kitchen I have in the same late hour, picking up something he kept meaning to throw away that is suddenly a live wire.

He looks out over the flats at the tooth-torn ribs of the town. His father ran a repair shop on Main Street; I can hear the toggled chandelier of wrenches and can feel the clean stick of my forearm to its Formica counter. His father drank and fixed things; I think he did both in the same room. I remember the back of Lew’s neck, an ordinariness of hair, the bump of a mole we said was the doorbell to his skull.

“Some lady with white shoes asked me if I felt sad,” he says, and I enjoy, for a small, petty second, that he believes in the shoe as a crucial detail as I do. “I said I did and did not and she made a face like, choose one.”

“They want a line,” I say. “They think they will be less frightened if a person like us, standing in a place like this, gives them one.”

“Are you frightened?” he asks, curious, not leaning any direction.

“I am,” I tell him. “I am frightened in the way you are when you pick up an engine part that looks like a lung.”

Lew looks down at my rod, the black rungs marching up it. He looks at the way the receiver sits on top like a radio, the way the bubble in its little window trembles when I breathe. “What are you calling this?” he says, his hand a fan to indicate it all.

“CONTROL,” I say, and we both laugh, the quiet, embarrassed laugh that says the world has made us into people who think in two languages and they do not always align.

He tells me he stayed. He did not leave the county like we did—like I did—on the fourth day after the for-sale sign and the third day after the auction and the hauling and the soft sound of my mother in my father’s arms in the kitchen made of a liquor store and the way she let herself rest the full weight of her head on his shirt. He tells me he worked the dying orchards, then the living ones, then the orchards in boxes that call themselves something else because they have no trees. He tells me his father did die and he did not drink. He says, too many images. Which is a sentence that makes clean so many messy drawers.

“What are you going to do with this?” he asks me. “After the drought?”

I know the answer I am supposed to say. We’re going to make a nice set of maps. We’re going to make a public portal with clickable points. We’re going to make a poster for the lobby of the county annex and hold a reception that invites donors to put their names on a wall. I say instead, “I am going to go home and make dinner for a person who is a stranger to me, in the best way. And I am going to put my hands under the tap and feel water run over them like a miracle and I am not going to use the word miracle in my head because that is how you split your own chin.”

Lew nods as if he too has the wooden ladder of guilt inside and it has steps he keeps wearing smooth with his bare stone feet.

We stand there until the news van appears at the edge of the flats, until the woman on the TV who is small from here and carries a bright-blue microphone steps down like a person arriving at a foreign airport. Her cameraman is enormous. He climbs down from the van with the heaviness of a good man with a lot of cables to carry. The woman walks toward us and then turns, because of the camera, because of the way you must decide a frame before you look through it. Her face shapes itself to what it believes it knows. Lew and I stand very still until she has made herself stop twenty yards away.

“You okay?” Lew says.

“I’m good,” I say. “I need to finish this set and then walk the creek line before the afternoon wind picks up.”

He reaches into the pocket of his shirt and pulls out a small thing and presses it to me, the press of a palm I remember from the monkey bars when he gave me a penny with a railroad on it. “You better carry a knife,” he says. “Just in case of coyotes or men.”

I lift my hand to say I have one in my pack, but he is already walking away, his shoulders down. He is not good at letting people see his leaving. He once left a birthday party in the middle of singing happy birthday, the cake still a flat undisturbed ocean. We thought he had gone to the bathroom. He’d walked home.

The afternoon begins to pass like afternoon does, slant. The sun steps away from its clear glass height and the hummock in the west turns itself into a mountain in my eye. I take points on my points and the tablet fills with names. The world becomes a trick of number and the unaugmented reality of it—the stink, the sandflies, the very faint sound of children laughing—becomes a humming under that soundtrack. I realize I am not hungry and that I also have an apple in my pack and these two truths can become a decision. I sit on the low rise of what would have been the lip of a manhole and take the apple out and eat it slow. The apple smells like itself. Its skin gives and then is crisp. I spit a seed into my palm and hold it as if I have stolen it. I think of seeds as an old woman’s courage: a thing you have to be patient enough to believe in, and I am not yet one of those.

Up toward the park, a group of teenagers has gathered and formed themselves into a triangle. One holds a skateboard and performs sorrow with his teeth. One dances. One watches like a saint. Their faces are built like all faces, but the portion of attention they keep for their own bodies is larger and denial does not touch them yet. The wind pauses. The sound of a car up on the dam floats down.

A boy steps where he should not and goes in to his knee. He swears and laughs. The girl goes to help him and her calf disappears and the boy with the board tries to pull her up and the two of them clutch each other and neither is as strong as the mud is. One of the men from the morning jog-walks toward them, the slow, careful hurry of a man who has a bad ankle but cannot leave a child to flounder. I watch, and the watching is a form of prayer. The girl swears and laughs harder and then all at once both of them find purchase, scramble like lizards, make three graceless moves that achieve a thing their bodies will not yet thank them for. The man says, “You okay?” and they are and he flags at them as if he does not care if he loses them as people, only that he has done his duty as a stranger.

The old swimming pool, which we were forbidden to play in after it was drained in ‘sixty, is a shape on the flats even under the silt. You can see the rectangle of it, the darker and the lighter, the energy of aquamarine gone like a verb tense you no longer possess. I take a point at its corner, the one where I sat in consciousness of my body in a way that surprised me when I turned twelve and had hands, it seemed, where I did not before, hands that patted me down for evidence. I see myself also in a shorter self, the one that held to the gutter until her fingers pruned and the lifeguard blew her whistle because she didn’t like the way my grip looked like a prayer. I did not know then that prayer and grip and hunger and map are all the same thing with different flags sticking on top of them. I told my grandmother that lifeguards were beings unlike us, part-human and part-water, marked by their whistles and their sacred red tubes, and she laughed and laughed until she coughed.

My mother would have stood on this slab and said then go on, mark it, as if she could assign me a portion of the past as homework. She died in a year with a number that seemed too tidy for a human leaving. When the nurse called and used my mother’s whole name it showed itself like something you take off the wall after years in the same place and find your wall was not white but pale where the frame was not. I think of her sun visor, the visor she wore when she drove us into our lives, and the way one hand held the top of it between finger and thumb as if she were pinching good fortune to keep it from flying off. I do not remember the pinched skin of her fingers as old. I do not remember my father’s hands clean.

At the culvert on the far side of the flats, there is a woman in rubber boots with a notebook the size of a math book. She is sketching. When she is done, she will have a likeness familiar to me: two lines too clean, shading in the wrong places, the wrong number of bricks. And yet the gesture will be what saves the thing from punishing tenderness. She turns and nods at me, and her eyes are the eyes of a person who learned to sit still when the sky wanted to kill her.

The chips in the tablet screen look like a constellation. I brush a fly off my damp lip with the back of my dirty hand and put my receiver in Standby mode and slide the rod down and clip it to the pack because there is the sound of a horn from the dam. The horn tells you to move to higher ground. It is a recorded kind of horn, like a holiday song you are supposed to trust. It is not the first horn I have heard in my life and not the last. I put the apple core in my vest pocket, careful of the marble, and I climb the last slope back up to the edge where the new shoreline has been drawn by an engineer who has met me and who laughs and who has let me cry in the unlit hallway of the county building next to the vending machine when I could not get it together in time to be the person who says new shoreline as if it is wallpaper.

As I climb, the mud cracks and flakes and tries to pull my boot and I say, not today. At the top, the world is crisp and too bright. The newswoman is turning to her cameraman and her hand is on the blue microphone and her voice is the voice one uses to tell the truth in a special way. Behind her, a man waves, the kind of wave meant to be seen. A white egret flaps itself heavy off a rib of concrete and aims for the willow line of the original creek, its desire for narrow waters as keen as any of us has for a bed. The trucks idle. The smell changes with the wind to sage from dead things. A woman fills a jug at the spigot by the restroom hut and tells her son to tighten the lid well. His hands do not yet know how to grip all the way around the cap.

I put my pack in the truck and turn the ignition. The air is a blast. The marble presses against my sternum where I put it in the small pocket I did not know I had, the one inside the vest above the company logo, a pocket made for keys or bail money or a list of things you do not want to say out loud. The control points in the tablet will knit together on my laptop tonight in a small house ten minutes from here with a rosemary bush that is too big for itself. They will form an image a person can click and enlarge and share in a newsletter. People will write to the county with corrections and tell me my line is five feet off and enclose their own memory maps in emails with subject lines like You Have It Wrong But That’s Okay. I will take their overlays and see that they are off in a different, shimmering way. I will not argue. That is not my job.

I sit there for a minute before I put the truck in gear, not to make of the place a visible elegy, but to finish something I did not know I had begun this morning. I reach between the seats and fish under a thermal blanket and a collapsed milk crate until I find the tin of chalk I keep for marking, the cheap, satisfying kind a teacher would use to make a cliff face into a film. I carry it down the first small incline again, and I do not go far, just as far as the first curb. With the side of the chalk, I draw a line across the old street. It is the old trick you use to count laps on a track, it is the farmer’s way of knowing which rows you have weeded. The chalk squeaks. It leaves its vanishing mark on this vanishing world. An older man, someone’s uncle, someone’s neighbor, someone’s father, pauses at this unexpected sound and turns his head.

“Why?” he calls, and maybe he means the chalk, and maybe he means everything else. I hold up the nub of chalk in answer, and maybe I mean I do not know and maybe I mean because a body has to.

I go back to the truck and this time I put it in drive. The horn on the dam blows again, this time with the long tone that says the lake has opened some mouth. The old creek rifles itself in the bed I would have loved that it found. Beyond, the flats are the kind of clean the eye confuses with innocence, which is a trick we all learn at six and relearn at sixty. I drive the perimeter road back to the highway, and up where the cattails are new again, where the earth’s hair has grown back over its scar, I stop and get out and stand for time I cannot say. In the road dust are the tracks of a coyote and next to them the smaller tracks of a child who walked after the animal, heel then toe, careful. The child’s companion had a dog, also. The dog’s steps are furious and everywhere. The coyote moved as if it knew all the important places would still be there if it took its time.

The wind picks up and does that thing the canyon makes it do, a kind of throat’s worth of sound. A folding lawn chair tumbles down the slope and stops on a rock that used to be under ten feet of water and now is a surface for aluminum. I imagine for a second the water rising, the flats going under again like a breath held too long and released. I imagine it and it is good work. But the weatherman has said this year will wring the sky like a rag and nothing will come of it. The lake will take again but not yet.

At home tonight, in a small house that has never known a county map, I will pour a glass of water and watch a frog of air ride up inside it and burst and my throat will want to sing at the sound the glass makes on the counter before my hand learns there is no such song and sets the glass down soft. I will pour another and carry it to the person on the couch who bought the basil plant at the grocery store as if it were a gift that could solve anything, and we will eat what we eat and sleep like people do who live on the edge of their own pictures.

And the marble will be in my pocket where it has heat now from the engine and the vest and the small furnace my sternum makes for it. I will take it out and put it in the small dish on the dresser that holds my keys and a stone from a river in a state far to the north and my grandmother’s safety pin. It will catch the light in the way only objects made for a game can do, an honesty you cannot extract from a plate or a fork. In the morning, before I go back to make the last line walk back to the water, I will hold it again and for once put nothing in its name. I will slip it in the pocket and leave it there while the day heats and then cools, while the flats throng and then empty. And if I am very careful, if I keep my feet under me as if I were a dog on a deck and the deck a ship, I will walk the foundation where I last saw above water the rectangle of my bed. I will place one foot and then the other where the headboard would have been, slowly, as if I were about to fall asleep, and when I feel the pressing shape of the marble at my heart, I will not reach for it. I will accept that objects do not go back where they belong. I will draw one last line in chalk where the kitchen table would have been. Then I will put the chalk back in the tin. Then I will stand with my face framed by the window that isn’t. Then I will step off into what has been waiting sixty years to be walked upon again, and I will let it press its dampness into the soles of my boots until the black in the seams says this is how it is done. We are measured by what we choose to feel and what we choose to leave in the ground. If I am very quiet, the lay of streets will sound under me like ribs. That sound will be mine long after the map, long after the water, long after the people with their cameras stop. I will carry that sound like a person carries the last note of a song she cannot help but hum when she lifts her tools. It is a small, elastic thing, this music. It is not a line one can store. It is what the wind does when it decides you are listening.

Up on the dam, there is a man in a yellow vest lifting his binoculars, writing something on a pad. He looks like me from here. He is writing about surface and depth, about inflow and outflow, about daily pool change. He is measuring the word lake against the word time. He has made himself a clean, careful list of what goes where and I would kiss him for it if I could, if he were here in the mud, because I have made the same list and we will both fail. But we will have tried with the loving ugliness of our clipboards. And in the morning, we will try again. The world, like that, will make a low, accommodating sound that you can hear if you are at the bottom of it, if your boots are stuck, if your hands are full of chalk and the small round truth you took. It will not let you go without first putting its hand on your shoulder like a father at the lake’s first summer when the band plays and every shutter clicks open and closed like a bird and he says nothing, and the nothing is a gift you do not unwrap because you do not have to.
