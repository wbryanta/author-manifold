The snow came early for the light on the calendar, and sullen, roiling, like pond water stirred by a hand. It stepped over the hill and swallowed the fenceposts. It took the land and made it new, and in the slant tin of the lambing shed the ewes turned their yellow eyes toward it and chewed and breathed and put their cloven feet in the same divots until all the straw was quilted down and shiny. The dog slept with his head on his paws and one ear lifting now and then at a bleat. The storm moved like a slow animal beyond the door and sometimes jolted, pressed its face right into the gap of the wood and the jamb and blew back their smell at them: lanolin, damp wool, iodine, hay that still had sun stored in it, blood iron.

When the lights flickered she didn't look up right away. She had a hand inside 118, fingers over the knuckles of a foreleg, working carefully. The lamb had come head first without its legs, its mouth like a small pink tulip, tongue swelling blue and back, and she had pushed the whole warm thing back into that dark slick place to find a foot. Her boy was kneeling with the towel, one arm slung across the ewe's rump to brace her. He was long now, the way boys go long suddenly, arms that didn't know what to do with themselves when they weren't needed, voice that got caught in the rafters of the shed sometimes and came back different.

"It'll be there, Ma," he said, not with certainty so much as with superstition. He didn't remove his hand from the ewe and he did not look at her face because she had a look when she worked like this, the look of listening with all parts of herself, and he knew if you interrupted that listening, if you asked for her just then, her hands would forget what they knew. He had watched his father stand the same way, one palm on a ewe's side as if that could send patience down into her.

She found the foot and slid her hand down it, the tiny heel and the hard angles of the little knee. The lamb kicked once inside her, an eyelash of movement, and the ewe made a low sound, the sound of someone who has been trying to lift something heavy and now believes she can. The shed lights went dark, a shrug of the whole building as the electric hum stopped. The thin heat lamps over the pens flickered once, shivered orange, and then were out. The storm pressed its weight behind the pause. The generator had been her husband's pride thirteen years ago and lately ran only when he sweet-talked it. It stood in the little shed behind the lambing barn and had a fuel filter that liked to clog, that liked to sulk in cold, and the boy could coax it as well as his father had, sometimes. Not always.

"Hold," she said, and he held while she looped the rope around the thin ankle and then the other, and with the next contraction she brought them forward. The lamb's head slid into the dark and emerged dripping and collapsed, a drowned thing, and she pulled gently and then with the swing of her body and it came. On the straw, a slick packet to be opened. The ewe turned, nose down, the automatic love of her species ignited by smell, and began to lick, to talk in that endless tremulous way that made the back of Dorrie's neck prickle with the urge to cry, every time. The lamb did not stir. It was too cold already in the long draft of the shed, with the doors opening and closing, with her own wet arm lifted into the air.

"Rub him," she said. Her own voice sounded like a thing leftover from the day before. She was alone in it in a way she hadn't been before this spring, like stepping onto the porch expecting someone to look up and grin and instead finding the shape of their staying written only in the still tools, the cup with the chipped lip, the worn edge of the stair.

The boy rubbed. He put the towel on the lamb the way he'd been shown, like polishing a boot. When she reached to part the lamb's mouth and clear it with her fingers he pulled the towel aside to watch, his face close. He had her husband's same small mole by the left eye and a way his mouth went thoughtful when he watched something being taught. He'd been in the lambing shed every spring since he was a baby in a carrier strapped to someone's chest. Last year he'd pulled three by himself before she knew to check, standing afterward as if he'd been dragged under a wave and spit back out, hair plastered to his head with sweat. Between them then, his father had gone through the far door with a dead lamb in a bucket and had not returned because he had remembered something about a chain he'd meant to fix at the back pasture that very minute, his brain like a series of windows all open, all with a breeze coming through.

"He's flat," the boy said, almost to himself. He didn't sound scared, just disappointed the way anyone is when a thing that should have worked simply hadn't yet.

She put her mouth over the lamb's and blew. There was the slat and succulence of it, the smell that was not quite anything. She pressed his chest with her hands. The ewe's tongue rasped the lamb's head like someone licking the sugar off a dropped piece of candy, matter-of-fact and attentive. The boy's hands were around the lamb's ears, and he rubbed the fur backwards, hard, his fingers red to the knuckles with birth slick and cold. Outside, the generator chose its moment to try. It coughed and then, as if ashamed of the impulse, went silent again. The dog lifted his head and then put it down.

"Come on," the boy said to the lamb in the same voice she had heard him use when he thought no one was listening, at the creek when he'd squatted there to convince a frog to sit on his hand. "Come on, buddy. This is a good place to be. It's not as bad as it looks."

The lamb had a white stripe down his face and his eyelids were bluish where the throat gave up. There was a moment in all of it when Dorrie felt the old thought rise like a mean word, the thought that the whole enterprise of birth was one she wished she had found another life away from, that she could be a woman who taught books to people who wanted them or folded towels warm and square, anything but this bargaining in the high wind between here and another place. In winter, in nights like this when the calves blew blue and the jack pine scratched its thin arms on the clapboard, she had held her husband's chest under her hand and thought of weddings and strange kitchens, of the exquisite selfishness of hotels. In the morning she had made coffee and gone out in her boots and pulled a lamb before breakfast.

"Atta boy," the boy said, and not to her, though she had thought it came from him to her for a second in the dark, the way the words fit into the way she stood. The lamb shuddered. It was a quiet little protest at first. Then it took a breath, and in taking it it struck its front legs out once and knocked the towel away and revealed himself to be already here. He started to bleat like a bent whistle. The ewe nosed his ear and lifted him with her nose the way a mother cat does a kitten by its neck, and when he folded both legs to get his hooves under him he swayed and the boy steadied him, steadying him too much, and then took his hands away as if burned by the idea of making a thing stand that should stand by itself.

"Get him to teat," Dorrie said, her arm shaking down the bones.

"Yeah." He crouched, one knee to the straw, and cajoled the lamb. The ewe stood, head high, staring at nothing, counting sounds in the storm. Her teats were swollen and slick; her belly had the map of the day scrawled across it, the muscles of effort quivering. The boy cupped the teats and stripped them once, as they'd practiced, the thick first milk sticky, and the lamb nosed and forgot and nosed again. It would happen because it had. The lamb would latch because it must. There was a whole language of should and must you could ruin yourself with out here.

When the lights didn't come back on, the storm was promoted inside. The edges of the shed went away; the pens existed in their own small sphere of light from their headlamps and from the thin windows strobing white. On another year as late as this in April it would have rained and the frogs would have started, and the night after the boy's father died it had smelled so strongly of thaw that she had woken because a robin was calling drunk on the telephone wire. This was a summer postponed, an ache held out. There were drifts as high as the four-wheeler's handles curling along the side yard right now, and in the black-out the only way to find the lantern was to remake the pattern of where she would have put such a thing if she were the woman she wanted to be. She remembered the exact weight of the lantern on her husband's hook. She remembered the smell of his coat, the wax on the canvas, his shoulders broader than his patience and the way he hooked the light without looking. She found the lantern by climbing past that memory like climbing a fence in the dark, feeling the top bar with your boot. She lit it. The small flame made fragile gold, and the lantern's light whistled and scolded. The boy had his headlamp on already, a square of the world pinned down where he put his eyes, and he had gone, while she fumbled with matches, to the door that led to the generator shed.

"No," she said. "Wait." The wind filled her words like a sail. He turned and grinned at her, reckless, and she saw in that grin the act of becoming something she did not know yet if she trusted.

"It's nothing, Ma," he said. "I'll get it going. You stay with 118."

"I don't need to stay with anyone. I need to—"

"You need light," he said, and the way he said it surprised both of them. He had grown, also, a thing in his chest, like a new room in a house you haven't toured yet, and he had walked into it without looking back.

"Fine," she said, and she didn't add I don't care about the lights. She didn't say, I care about you coming back. She had said both too often since August, and sometimes she heard in her own voice the noise a trap makes when it comes down. He was too much his father where engines were concerned, a stubborn intimacy with anything that secluded combustion. He had refused to walk away from the truck's guts even once last fall when it coughed and died at the turn to town, and he'd stood there with the hood up and felt along a rubber tube until he found the break in it as if it had been a wound on himself.

She checked the chalk board. There were dates scrawled in four colors, the marks of someone trying to impose order on the thrash of these weeks. Twenty-two had lambed, of the seventy-five that would. Their lamb count was decent, two sets of triplets so far, two singles out of old girls she'd have culled if he had been here to argue with. She had not culled them because he had said once, in last spring's lambing shed, while leaning his shoulder into a ewe's flank while the boy watched, that old ones had earned their place. He had said it without looking at her and that kindness, deployed without hope of profit, had made something in her lay down its head afterwards. He had died in a culvert under the lane with the jack holding a rusted hitch. He had died because he had done a thing he had done a hundred times, and because the metal that held the world up is bored sometimes by holding. She had not gone to him in the ditch; it was the neighbor Joe who found him because the dog had gone to the road and lay down and howled in a way that had made Joe pull his truck over just to stop the sound.

The storm turned holy for a bevel of time then, standing there with the chalk in her hand because it was the only hand free. It was holy in the way that a thing can be when it is trying to take you and other things are trying not to let it. The ewes were their own small lights, eyes shining. 207 bunted another off a flake with the same casual malice she had in all seasons. The boy put his shoulder against the generator's door and the door banged back. Dorrie felt her own stomach reach for him. She put the chalk down. 118's lamb made a suck of a sound of accomplishment and then went to it, legs braced, skinny tail flicking as if in imitation of a dog that's forgotten itself.

She went down the line of pens with the lantern as if she were visiting houses. In the third one down, 92 was doing the small stamping dance and cocking her head to look under her body to see what was there that wasn't there yet. 92 was always dramatic about the whole process and had twin daughters last year who stole lambs off other ewes so frequently that they had to be penned separately for a week just to break them of it. 92's vulva was stretched like a punching glove. The tip of the amniotic sac made a bubble the size of a plum and burst as she watched, so the straw got darker and the smell came up sweet and strong. The lanternlight made the fluids ruby and then brown and then absence, depending on where the flame was. At the far corner, 113 had her head sunk into her own wool, breathing hard. The dog got up and did his circular checking, nose to the bars of the pens, then came and leaned against her shin for a second without explanation. She put a hand down and let it rest on his head. He allowed himself exactly that and then merited away.

Her boy's headlamp flittered along the floor of the little shed as he crouched, as he swore abidingly, and then went still. The neatness of the generator's sound when it ran was a thing that had made her love her husband the first summer, when she was new. In that first season, he had shown her how you had to coax life, how you had to call it by its good names and not show it your disappointment. He had stood with the generator's choke in his hand and said to it, sweetheart. He had stood with a lamb and said, get here, you'd be so surprised.

The boy could not get it running and neither could she, stepping out into the wind for five minutes to participate in the not-getting. They pulled the cord and it came to cough and then sputtered and then die as if it had made up its mind already when it saw them. The hoop of the sky bowed. They stumbled back to the shed and let the door go quick behind them so the snow didn't syphon in.

"We'll be all right," she said. She had said that about bigger things and been made a liar, but contained here it was not lying. "We know what to do. We've done it without the lights."

"I know," he said. He had that same defiant softness he got when someone tried to pay him a compliment: a posture like an animal ready to flee but leaning toward the hand. "If we have to, we can warm the colostrum under our coats."

"If we have to," she said. "I've got the Thermos. Top shelf."

They worked. The work removed time. It laid out a new yardstick measured in the distance between pain and push, in the number of tries it took for a teat to find a mouth, in the way lambs that had been slick lumps twenty minutes ago were now standing wide-legged and stamping tiny feet in a not-quite dance. The storm took their power but also took their counting of minutes. 92 delivered a ram, all white, and then, slow, another. When she cleared him and he started to make that furious sound lambs make when they understand their own hunger, she laughed aloud and startled herself. Such an ordinary small animal and her joy at him disproportionate. The second of 113's twins was breech and came out like a secret, hind legs first, and she had to go in two hands. Her fingers learned again the blind biography of the inside of a ewe—the ringed, smooth, hot, then the hard angle of a pelvis that said to you with its own language that you had to stop, had to change the plan.

All night, they did small bright cruel things for the sake of life and would do worse tomorrow. They tubed colostrum into ones too tired to suck. They banded the navels and dipped them orange. They penned new families in small squares and marked their backs with apple green and sky blue and a pink that looked like a joke in the shed's dirt, numbers for their pairing so that no one would lose a baby to someone else's. They hauled warm water to the corner and let a few of the heavy girls drink and put their noses down deep to the steam. The boy's hands were always wet now and red with cold, and he rubbed them on his jeans as if that did something. He put a balled-up lamb inside his coat under his sweater against his stomach and walked with the ewe nosing his hip to the heat of their bodies and stood there making a sort of tent with his own torso while she found her child again. For a while they ate ham sandwiches like fuel and black coffee from a thermos that failed to make it good, just hot. The dog slept and woke and slept and woke as if rehearsing a sentence he couldn't say correctly.

At some point late, the edge of the world to the east took on a quality of blue that said it was a new day even if it was only the same one with more water in the air. The roof of the lambing shed had a place in it where the tin complained in the key of D, and it complained softer then, close to when the boy went out with the dog to check the far paddock for any animals that had somehow not come inside. It was a reckless errand and necessary. He took the rope halter and slung it over his shoulder the way his father had done and put his head down and went into the white. Dorrie watched him until the whiteness took him. It was a swallowing that had a sound to it. She wanted to call him back. She did not.

She sat on an upside-down bucket and took her boot off to rub a place on her heel where the skin had gone tender. The laces of the boot held ice. There was straw up her sleeves, the way there always was; it found the inside of clothes like burrs and traveled. On the nail by the door, his hat hung. It had hung there since last spring, a comb of dust forming along the brim. She had taken his work coats in when the cold came and gave some to a cousin and washed others and put one over the back of his chair at the table and let it dry there for days as if a body might arrive to fill it. But she had left his hat on the nail as if it owned the place. Sometimes she thought that if she took the hat down, the barn would fold in the familiar way the body of a man can in grief, like a barn in a documentary you watch too late at night that falls on purpose to show you how the joints release while you were looking for something else.

She was so tired. It hit her then with the efficient meanness of a machine when you have let your hand be in the wrong place. She put the boot back on because the taking of it off had been worse. She stood and checked 207 again and got gored in the thigh without ceremony, and the bruise would be round and terrible tomorrow and in three days would be green. She cuffed 207 back and felt alive to herself in a way she hadn't felt in the slow afternoons of winter. Winter had been a dark of a different kind. You could feel the way time rotted then. In the lambing shed, even storms were a kind of counting, a tally of what had been done and what was left, and sometimes the numbers were on your side.

The boy came back not with a ewe but with a lamb, a black one, plastered and hanging. He had it zipped inside his coat. The dog danced and was scolded. The boy's hair was cooked to his head with crystals and he looked like a figure on a card in a deck you kept on the counter: Bring Backer, the one who gets things you didn't know you'd lost.

"She was dead," he said, not with apology but defensively, prepared for her to say he had spent too long. He was learning that sometimes telling doesn't do the thing telling can do for a person, that there is no friction removed from the world when you explain.

Dorrie took the lamb. It was as cold and limp as anything she had ever held. The ears were stiff with it. The nose had ice. She shook it a little like a dishrag and then loathed herself and then did it again, rougher, because there was no time this side of the world to be tender except the tenderness of making yourself unlovely with a thing's future. She rubbed it with a towel until the towel was another plant. She blew into the lamb. She did everything. She handed him to the boy and he put the lamb inside his coat again and under his shirt and then they both stood there, their hands on their own knees, both of them holding the knowledge of when to quit inside themselves and not able to open that cupboard yet and take it out.

"There was a ewe by the hedgerow," the boy said. He was panting a little from the run back. The wind had marked his cheek red. "She's big with one and on her side. I couldn't see her tag. I couldn't get her up. The drift's up to the line. Do we—"

If they had his father now he would have gone with the sled and the two of them would have lifted that ewe onto it and tied her in and hauled her back while both of them said fuck with respect from the same mouth, and when the sled caught on the fencepost and spilled her off he'd have thrown his hat and then grinned and they'd have done it again and he would have told the story of it like it was a good joke later at the kitchen table. There was not his father now. There was her and the boy, and the distance to the hedgerow and the drift high, and the ewes here delivering and the generator that didn't and the lamp that shrunk everything to circles.

"We'll get her," she said. The way she said it decided, and in deciding made the going possible. "When 92's second gets a grip. We can't leave this many new ones."

He nodded once. It was agreement and also an indictment. He would have run when he found the ewe and gotten a rope on her and he would have broken his shoulder on it and not told anyone. He would have died in the ditch if permitted and then been mad at her after for not stopping him. She put her hand on his head for just a second and then let it go because he is a boy who needs what he needs not always where you think it is.

By nine there was no telling what time it was except that the light had a different flavor, milky through the slats of the storm, and that the coffee had ceased to do anything worth doing. The lamb inside the boy's coat, the black one, did not come back. Its head lolled to the side and once in a while its front feet did a thing like trying to remember the movement for walking and then it would be over. She took him and put him in the rough-sawn wooden box her husband had put together two springs ago and wrote LAMBS in pencil on and then crossed it out and wrote HOT BOX and then decided that was too much and crossed it out, and she put a towel over him she had warmed over the space heater before the power went. She touched his nose once in apology. Behind her the boy made a noise.

"Don't," she said, too quickly. "Don't name him."

"I didn't say—"

"You were going to say something that would make it worse."

He swallowed. He drew his mouth tight. He was sometimes a red mouth from anger and sometimes pale and tight around not-saying. He had not cried when they buried his father. He had cut wood with a private accuracy and come in when she told him and washed and gone to school and come back. He had yelled once at his friend by the shed so that the friend's mother called her and said, something's not right, and Dorrie had laughed in a way that made the friend’s mother look at her like a fickle bird and then apologize into the lunch meat. She had said no, you're right, things aren't right.

"There's no point in saving any of them if we don't do that," he said. He didn't look at the box and neither did she. The curtain of the storm made privacy of everything. She tied 92's second onto her with the harness because 92 made the old mistake and thought it belonged to some other story, and so 92 put her nose up in the air and cried for what she had not lost. The harness would make her wrong notice be the right notice. In three days she'd forget to mind and they could take it off and 92 would stand for the second lamb like she'd thought of it first.

"We can graft him onto 61 if he makes it," he said, unable to leave a door closed, and it was such a dear, foolish hope that she wanted to touch his cheek with the back of her hand in the way she did when he was six and ran a fever. 61 was in the back with a little one lost in the first hour, too cold, too small to hold, and had a bag of milk heavy as hope. You skin the dead one and put its coat on the living and the ewe thinks the smell is fate. You take the living's head and smear it in the dead lamb's blood. You tie her into a corner and let the hungry one find what it needs. You make a lie with your hands and then it becomes true because no one's keeping books but you.

"We will if he makes it," she said, and she heard that if fall in the straw and hide like a mouse.

When she and the boy went for the ewe by the hedgerow, there was a small breath in the weather, a lessening that wasn't an end. The drifts against the shed were like waves, and the lane was a place where the wind chose to shop all night, getting rid of what it didn't want. They took the sled the way you take a promise you intend to keep until you can't, one hand each on the rope. The dog went first into the white with his head down as if prayer was a physical place. The world was reduced to the three of them and the little red mouth of the sled and the rasp of their own breaths inside their buffed hats, and the whiteness that pushed back with its hand on their chests saying No and They said Yes anyway, and eventually that becomes the sort of thing the body agrees to.

The ewe was there, as the boy said, a susurration in the snowbank, breathing the way an old pump throbs in fits. She had made herself a nest and lain down and then the snow had packed in around her. She wasn't the old kind of stuck yet, she was still bright around the eyes, she still turned to look at them with dismay when they set the rope, not ready to have strangers in this intimate grief. The boy got behind her and pushed and Dorrie took her horns in her hands as she was able and the dog bit her heels and regretted it immediately and pretended to be sniffing a phantom thing in the hedge. The hedgerow, under its own white, remembered its hawthorns. The sled made a sound like a bird's wing in the snow. They got her up and they got her on and tied and then Dorrie looked up at the sky with the rope in her red hands and said, in a voice that surprised her, "Stop," and of course nothing did, the storm just went on being itself.

Back at the shed, 113 had stood and then relaid and was making that sustained moan that had a heartbeat in it, and 24 had climbed into the corner feeder and eaten without noticing that her daughter was in the pen behind her trying to be born. Dorrie got her hands into 24 and out with the uncomplicated grace that is sometimes given in payment for times when nothing yields, and the boy untied the hedgerow ewe in a pen he had bedded already without being told because he is someone who can see the way water will run and put a terraced curve to catch it.

They had to go into the hedgerow ewe, too, to turn the lamb. It was a back, a terrible wrong. Dorrie felt it and the ewe screamed and then was quiet, the old quiet of animals about to let you do what you will. The boy crouched so that his hands, if she needed them, would be there. "Use the loop," he said, and he handed it and didn't tell her which way she'd lasso because you can't tell a thing like that, it is in the wrist, in the place where the body knows angles. She caught one hock and then the other and made them feet and then the lamb slid out into the straw in a flood that turned a place of that straw to gold and showed all their footprints around it like a plan they'd drawn by accident. The lamb was a narrow one, and if it had been born without them it might have been alive; it might not. It sneezed ask-the-world and lifted its head.

While the ewe in the hedgerow leaned and muttered to her new child, Dorrie thought of the time she had sat at the far end of the table when he had gone to bed with a fever and the boy little, and she had turned out the lights and sat with her back against the stove and counted the regularity of his breath and tasted her fear like black coffee gone over bitter. She had thought she would be a woman who grew older beside someone who knew the names of all her noises and who could find the ditches of her mind without a light. She had become a woman who now knew where the generator's choke was, how the lantern might be coaxed, who could stick her hand inside an animal and find the hard question and then the answer. People in town had looked at her with a mix of awe and confusion since the funeral, as if she had been deputized to perform something unspeakable. "You are so strong," they said, and she had nodded and then gone home and screamed into a towel in the laundry room and then gone out and fed everybody, the goat with the bad tipped ear, the dumb old boar, the little sick chickens, and the ewes. Strength was a habit and then it was a story you told about your habits so you could live with them.

By noon the storm had become a fine steady thing instead of the burly man in the bar fight it had been. The power did not come back. The boy's cheeks wore two red flags and he had acquired straw in his hair that made him look not of the place but domesticated by it, and he leaned on the gate for a minute and watched 118's little white-faced ram hang onto the udder with all the ferocity of a saint at a vision. He smiled at that and then looked at her with some figure he'd been adding up all morning ready to be shared.

"We got three sets of twins and two singles and 61 to graft maybe," he said. "And the black one—"

"There's a law," she said too quick again and he winced like it pinched him, and she rephrased, a thing she had been learning like a foreign language. "I don't like it, but there is a way I have to be about the dead ones. Otherwise I—"

"You turn into someone else," he said. He nodded as if confirming an answer. "I know. I don't want that either. I just wanted to say his name would have been Boone." He blushed at the audacity of naming still. "Because he's black. Not for politics. For the sound."

She laughed, wrong to, and then she didn't apologize. She said, "That's a good sound."

He put his face into the crook of his arm on the top rail and hung there for a minute like a boy who has not remembered his weight. He had not cried when his father died. He had mowed the lawn instead and then stacked the wood without being told and then beat the barn cat's case to the vet open like a man making a path with a machete. She did not want him to cry now, not because it would be wrong but because the sound of it in the barn would make everything different and it needed to be the same for a while longer, just not out of superstition but so that lambs could make their first bleats and mothers could answer them and the dog could walk his circle and the storm could barter smallness for insistence and leave eventually as all storms do. She wanted the lambing shed to have the taste of its own life in its mouth even as their breaths made the lantern glass smudge.

In the back pen, 61 had her head up like a horse on the bit. She paced. The small one she had dropped in the hour before had been too small, not four pounds, and delicate like a hand you were trying to shake without breaking. She had licked it furiously and cried for it so that the dog had put his ears back as if he had done wrong. They had taken it away and she was still looking for the slot in the world where it went. When they brought the black lamb to her, naked to his skin now because they had taken his coat to try a fit that would have been grotesque and had failed because the sizes were wrong, Dorrie and the boy put their hands in the dead lamb's fluids and touched 61's nose and lips, marked the living with it and offered him the teat. They had to tie 61 because a mind at the beginning of a lie is not a mind you want loose. She fought the tether. She set her toes and tried to pull the barn down. The boy talked to her in the absent way he had, as if prayer was best said in the tone of giving directions to someone's house.

"Look," he said. "Listen. He's yours. That's your boy. Yeah, that's him. Jesus, it's cold. Yeah, that's him."

The black lamb did not have a name because they would not be that careless. But he had a hunger. He bore himself to the source and put his mouth there and remembered how to do a thing that is also discovered brand-new by each animal in the instant it happens. He sucked. He closed his eyes and opened them and his back legs trembled. Dorrie held the ewe's collar and the boy held the lamb's head in the way you hold a book open to make sure the reader can't walk away. For a second they made between them a tent and a world within it. Then the ewe's eyes went weird and wide and she blew air like a refuse of thought and stopped fighting. She looked sideways at them as if ashamed. She lowered her head. She made the long, sweet, stupid sound.

After, when the ewe was chewing off the rope with a patient malice and the lamb she'd accepted had wobbled in a drunk swagger to lie down and then gotten up again because lying down was now boring, the boy stood with his gloves in his pocket and his hands bare and blew on them. "We did it," he said in the tone of someone who means we did it for now and that is the only thing there is.

The day went in that gear. They pulled the ones that needed it. They let the others do their work unchaperoned and marveled at that so quietly they couldn't hear themselves marveling. The dog napped with his snout in his tail and woke to check on the welfare of anything that made a brand-new noise. The storm convinced itself to slack, and outside the heaps of snow leaned away from the fenceposts like old men in conversation. The lights stayed black, their bulbs holding nothing like mouths closed against foolishness. The lantern used up its fuel and she refilled it twice and the little sound of her hand pumping it felt like an ordinance being passed in the absence of a state.

Mid-afternoon, the boy's friend Jared showed up on foot with his hood iced, cheeks slapped red, the long-legged nonchalance of someone for whom getting to another place is its own story at school. He leaned on the doorway and wouldn't come in because his mother would throw a fit if he got near sheep in his nice jeans and because a boy like him was too quick to love the smell of animals if it got in his clothes. He said his father had a line on when the plows would make it up County M, said Mrs. Halvorson had lost two geese to the drift, which was not news. He offered to help and then didn't step forward when the boy made a joke about sticking your hand into a ewe. They laughed at him more than they would have liked to, too hard to be kind, and then Dorrie found herself offering them both an orange from the sack by the cooler and standing there while they turned them over in their hands and did not peel them.

There was a lull then in the births. In every season there's an hour where everything waits, an unforced pause. You live for it and do not trust it. She used the hour to clean, to pitch out the heavy straw nearest the pens where everything had pooled, to shake out fresh and make a place where a lamb could land that wasn't just the chronic bedding. The boy did not leave the shed. He sacked down in the corner on the bales and closed his eyes and was asleep so fast the dog came and looked at him hard to make sure it was ok. Dorrie sat on the bucket again and watched the rise and fall of her son's chest, the droop of his lips open at sleep, a habit he'd had since he was small in car seats, and tried to find inside herself the place where she could hold all this and not spill it. There are people who spread their living among friends like seed. There are those who keep it in silos. She was the second. She thought of calling Joe to say the power was out and that the generator wouldn't. She thought of texting her sister to say the boy had saved something today, a cast ewe down in snow, and that he had asked to be let to name it and she had said no and then yes, and that she had been wrong in either thing and right in the other. She did not call or text. The wind plucked at the tin. Her own eyelids fluttered and she felt the point of sleep like the point of a needle, and if she let it in, it would go all the way and she would wake to something she'd have to run to catch.

When the contractions started again along the line, it was as if a shuttle had been thrown at the loom, and they had to pick up the threads and catch what went across. In the dusk that isn't dusk but only afternoon turned wrong by weather, 79 started and then 12, the old ewe with the dip in her back from the car that had hit her six years ago and all her asks since then, all the season's worth of needing help and then not. They assisted and then didn't. The boy woke and ate half a sandwich without speaking and then started to talk as if mid-sentence, and she answered without remembering the first clause. The dog reknit his circle. When a lamb that she had assumed would not find its way found it, she felt a gratitude so absurd she wanted to thank the rafters. When 12 cried low and made a helicopter of her tail and nothing came, she put her hands inside and found a tangle and blessed her own hands for being hands with hands before them who had known this and taught it to her.

It was near evening and the light had a new permission in it, a gull's-throat blue unbecoming snow, when the power came back. There was a sound like a sigh through the walls, a ripple, and the heat lamps came back with their small orange jokes and the water tank sighed itself on. The generator in its small shed was a thing that didn't care about being shamed now; it would fail again tomorrow. The lights came back and made the straw vulgar like everything illuminated in a hall, too bright, and in that second Dorrie felt every nerve in her body wake like a leg that has fallen asleep and then you move it and the white heat begins, pins and needles and the reminder that aliveness is not always pleasure. She stood still in it until the wave passed.

The boy raised his face to the light and then put his head down again and kept dipping navels because it was what he had been doing. He lifted a lamb up under his arm and it blatted and he laughed and the dog laughed because a man's laugh is for everyone. He wiped his forehead with the back of his wrist and left a smear there. He said, not looking up, "It'll be all right," in a way that made it not a promise but a place he had decided to walk to with or without promises, and she nodded and then realized he had not said it to her. He had said it to the lamb or to himself or to the shed.

Through the new light the hat on the nail seemed both exactly what it was, a hat, and also the kind of thing that in a certain moment if you stared at it too long would crack your heart open with all the gushing that does not help anyone. She went and took it down. She did it slow. It left a clean place on the wood where it had hid the dust, a circle or halo the size of a prayer. She put the hat on her head. It didn't fit the way a love does not fit you sometimes when you try it back on after drying. It pressed down on her ears and the brim took her vision away on the left. She tipped it back the way he had. She wore it for three breaths and then took it off and put it on the nail again so that the dog would know where to look for the story.

He came over then with a lamb under his arm and a smile he was trying to hide inside his mouth like a tobacco pinch. "Look," he said. "She's got hers now without me. There. There." He was pointing at 113's second, a little white-capped one who had been a breech and was now standing at the nurse bar of the world as if she'd rented her place. He had that mixture in his face she had seen last May when he stood on the third-highest seat in the bleachers with his hands jammed in his pockets because he'd pissed his friend off and given up his own dessert as penance and she had not had the courtesy then to tell him he was her heart.

"We're going to keep them all," she said, mostly to make him roll his eyes, and he did, and it was so dear she could not stand it.

"No, we're not," he said. "That's how you end up with ones that steal other ones every year. Ask 92."

"Don't talk about her like she's not here," Dorrie said, and she felt giddy suddenly. It may have been the light coming back, which made a mind forget that it had been living like a star-nosed mole and doing fine.

By full evening, the storm had run down to its squeaks and muttering. The lights stayed, a mercy that felt a little like a taunt. Their muscles found the equilibrium of tired again, not in the way of collapse but in the way of a job that required a certain stolid joy. The boy fell asleep sitting up on a hay bale with his hand in a lamb's pen and the lamb came over to investigate and then, finding it was not food, put its feet on his boot and chewed his laces and the dog allowed this to go on as if he were a sheriff too wise to intervene in family matters. Dorrie put the last of the clean towels by the hot box and threw the last of the soiled ones in the bin. She wrote the last births on the board and underlined them with the chalk until the chalk broke and in that small valedictory fracture felt the satisfactions of a day you survived.

She stood with her hand on the gate of 61 and listened to the private noises of a barn with its business in progress: the chops, the clicks, the soft lamb-stamp; the throat-song of a ewe that has finally found the sound that will belong to her; the sigh of electricity; the dog chewing nothing. The lantern hissed even in its off-ness as if objecting to a demotion. There were so many small lives in here. Outside, the road would be drifted with the suggestion of beginning to passable tomorrow. There would be someone in town who would have an opinion about her gate latch. There would be calves due soon up at Danes. There would be school. There would still be August and the way the blackberries come in thick in that ditch that eats a man. But here, for now, there was the smell of blood and warm milk and the dog. There was her boy with a line of drool at his mouth, making him small and stupid to love, sprawled with his head cocked like his father used to when sleep came in the chair with the television's low mutter on the night of storms, television flickering crude lightning in the window's glass even when the sky stayed black.

She did not go wake the boy. She didn't cover him. She didn't pick the straw out of his hair like she would have that spring the year he was eight and tried to go to school with a cocklebur on his ear to make Annie Leander laugh. The straw would be part of how he would know himself when he woke. She touched his boot with hers to check that its sole was not against a slat where he might slide, and when it wasn't she left it. She took the hat back off the nail once more and put it on, and this time she didn't adjust it. She went to the door and cracked it and let in the smallest measure of the air. It came like a mouth blown on a burn. The sky beyond was a slab of gray with a promise cut somewhere into it that she could not read yet and did not have to. She shut the door. She walked the line of pens. She bent to dip another navel, to push a lamb's mouth toward what it needed, to pat a flank that had labored and had come home. She was an animal among animals. She was a woman who knew how to put her hands on what had to be done and do it. The ewes kept coming, because that is what ewes do, at all hours, indifferent to the storm and the calendar and the tender little tragedies of men and women. She kept the chalk in her pocket. She would take it back out and put names on the wall, numbers anyway, and in the morning when the road was a rumor of itself and the plow had scratched one side of it and gone away and the sun made the kind of appearance that made the snow look like the hide of a beast you could saddle, in that morning she and her boy would step out with their tire chains and their rope and walk down the path they had broken today with their rope on the red sled and their lungs and their old and their new hungers, and they would do it again.
