We found the wet spot by the shimmer of flies. It lay in the furrow like an old bruise, soil darker and clinging, and when Henry put his boot to it the heel sank and came away sucking. He crouched, his hat brim shadowing his eyes, and slid his hand under the skin of mud until his knuckles hit something solid.

“Here,” he said.

I turned my shovel and scuffed away the crust. The clay was gummy where June had baked it and the leak had made it slick again, and we took the layer off in slabs and heaped them to the side. The hawks cried lazy above us; the orchard hummed with the dull workday of bees.

Everything else was dry, even the weeds. The trees this year were holding their leaves tight as fists. The fruit—if you could call them that, green stones buried in fuzz—hung too light in the crotch of the limbs. They were peaches, Missouris that my father had grafted onto old plum stock two decades ago when he’d been convinced he could outguess markets and weather and luck. He used to stand at the end of this block with his hands on his hips and rock on his heels while he stared down the rows as if they were soldiers. “Stand up,” he’d say, joking at the trees as if they were his boys. “Stand up and drink.”

“My guess is a crack at the elbow,” Henry said.

He was right. From where the lateral came off the main and turned around the rootball of the old purple plum that should have been taken out five years ago, an elbow of white PVC peeked like a bone. I dug my fingers underneath and felt the wide seam where somebody—maybe me, fifteen years back with a hangover, more likely our father with a lit cigarette clamped in his back teeth—hadn’t pushed it home all the way when the glue was still soft. A hairline vent had opened, and for who knew how long it had been bleeding.

“Kill the water,” Henry said.

He stood and his knees cracked and the blue stain of primer on his hand looked like a bruise. He walked the line up to the valve at the top of the block. He moved slow, not from age but from that way he had of making work fill the day, parceling his strength. He’d been born with that, even when we were boys. I was ten the year we were set to our first irrigation, my hands too soft to earn blisters, and he was twelve and already had his own way around a shovel. The valve handle was rusted, and he leaned into it with his shoulder. The hiss died, a slither died under the ground, and the flies lifted and then settled back.

I cut away more earth and set it on the pile. He came back carrying the purple can and the blue, primer and glue, and the yellow handled hacksaw because we’d lost the ratcheting cutter somewhere in the rows.

He said, “I still can’t find that damn cutter.” He set the cans side by side in the dust. His thumbnail was ripped where he’d jammed it in the pickup door that morning. If I’d been there, I would have said, “Wait,” like you would to a cow trying to shoulder through the gate. But I had been late, and he had been up at five. The sun hadn’t even burned off the valley’s morning fug yet when he’d called and said he’d reached me three times already in his head. That was Henry’s way of making a joke, a dry fog on his own breath. Then I was there anyway, on a patch of brown outside the house where our mother’s rose bushes had given up, my city jeans rolled at the cuff and my shoes already with a cut in the toe from some other stair I’d caught myself on.

“You need the hacksaw?” he asked.

“Give me a second.” I stood on the elbow with my boot and wrenched. The joint squeaked. The white pipe, sun-chalked and brittle, cracked. The sound startled a dove up the row. Henry swore.

“Easy,” he said.

“I said give me a second,” I said, but I caught my tone and set the boot down and said, softer, “Okay. Sorry.”

We worked in the hole like surgeons. He took the saw and cut the bad section out and held the wet end upright so the last of the pressure bled, and I reached for the primer. I liked the smell of it, chemical and sharp, an old familiar sting. You could get it on your finger and it would bite and stain, and then the blue glue, with its opal sheen, would string when you pulled back the dabber. If you were stupid you wiped it on your jeans and your mother yelled about the marks for weeks; if you were bored and fifteen you’d paint your thumbnail blue and make faces at your brother until he laughed.

“You get old, time shortens,” Henry said, as if he were the one hearing the primer talk. “Spills out faster. We’re fixing things we already fixed once.”

“You ever think it’s just the wrong scale?” I said. I dipped the dabber and ran the circle inside the coupler, and then around the pipe. He held it still with his hands the way our father had—press and hold and give it a quarter turn and then another, counting not with numbers but with breath.

“The wrong scale?” he said.

“Like the whole system. Maybe this is too big for us, or it’s the end of its useful life. A smaller garden might be something.”

“A smaller garden?” He let out a breath that might have been a laugh and might have been the word itself dissolving. “I don’t plant zinnias, Liam.”

“Dad always said—”

“Don’t,” Henry said, and in his voice there was a cut I had put there. “Don’t start with what dad always said. He said a thousand different things.”

We worked around the elbow and pressed the stub into the coupler and the glue made that hot squeak, that plastic-on-plastic sound, and he held it and I put my hands over his and our fingers overlapped, mine soft from keys and screens, his knuckles lumpy, the low scars white. I felt the strength in his end-of-day tired. He closed his eyes and counted the breaths.

When we let go, the pipe held. He took off his hat and wiped his sleeve along his forehead and made a wet line of dust and then put the hat back. The sun licked us. The shirt stuck to my back. In the row, ants went about ants’ work, carrying crumbs of earth that meant something only to them.

“I told Ellen I’d be down at the bank this afternoon,” he said.

Ellen at the bank had watched us grow up from behind a pane of thick glass. She was my age and had gone blond in the last few years with a kind of fury; she was pretty the way a sorghum field at dusk is pretty, all gold and bristling. When my father died in October she’d worn a black dress and a glittering cross and had hugged me in a way that made me think of girls in junior high dances at the armory. She’d cried at the funeral like he was her own. All winter she’d been calling with a velvet thing in her voice, and Henry had been pretending not to hear.

“We don’t have to go,” I said.

“We do,” he said. “Payments fall noon Friday. This will help us sleep until then, maybe.”

“You could just call, you know.”

“You could just come home,” he said.

We stood on either side of the fresh joint and looked at it as if it could tell us something about our day. Up in the tree, a leaf shook like a small hand.

I said, “The developer called me again last night.”

He squinted at the glue that had oozed into a bead and hardened in the sun. “Floyd’s boy?”

“It’s not Floyd’s boy anymore. He sold. It’s a firm out of Visalia.”

“Firm,” he said, as if the word meant something that had to be spit out.

“They’re not all bad men in short-sleeve dress shirts, Henry.”

“They’re not men I know,” he said.

“They’re offering a good number.”

“What’d you tell him?”

“I told him I’d be out there today.” I squatted and scooped a crumble of dry dirt and sifted it, letting it sheet between my fingers. I expected the feel of water to come back, and it didn’t. “I told him we were talking.”

“Oh,” he said, and he stepped back like I’d swung the post hole digger in too close to his shin. He bent and rechecked the joint, though there was nothing to see. “And you think that’s where we are.”

“I think we have to talk about it.” I kept my tone steady, a plank I set with care. “We’ve been talking about it in circles for six months.”

“We have been hounded,” he said. “That’s not the same thing.”

I cocked my head and heard the sound we both wanted—the faint far hiss of water when the system went back under pressure—but it wasn’t there yet. We hadn’t reopened the valve.

“Turn it on,” I said. “Let’s see if we got this one done right.” He didn’t move. He had that thing he could do with his jaw where he set it and it made a shadow. His eyes were my mother’s hazel. The whites had yellowed a little, like the whites of eggs we’d left on a counter too long.

“I hated this orchard when I was a kid,” I said, because I did and because truth sometimes disarms. “I hated Saturdays and the way my friends could go to movies and we were here, moving pipe. I hated that dad called water ‘our other blood.’”

“He called it that once,” Henry said.

“He said it enough that I remember it.”

He stayed planted on the dry side of the trench we’d dug. Shade moved a fraction on the ground like a measure in a song. Heat clicked, the way it did under the hood of a car you’d just turned off.

“Turn it on,” I said again.

He walked up the row, one boot in the furrow and one on the mound, as our father had walked. I watched the small incline of his back. He threw the valve and the handle groaned and he let it fall over and when it reached the end of its arc it made that hollow clunk that meant a lot of water was on the move. There was a pause where the earth held its breath, and then something miles down the line woke. A hiss uncurled. Air spit and coughed from the risers. The line trembled, and at the elbow we’d fixed, a single transparent bead grew and quivered, laughed at its own surface tension, and then held. My heart did something like that, too, a lilt and a hold.

We went along the row and checked the heads. On some, the little white spinner rotated and made its fairy-shower of drops. On some, the emitters dribbled as if we’d starved them. A gopher had popped a run into a microline last week and flooded five trees’ worth of grass. The dogs had brought us a corpse that evening and looked proud. I stepped over a gopher mound that had hardened like adobe. The orchard floor under the trees was a map of work past—furrows here and there, compacted steps where our father had worn his way between high summer and late fall. The blue and green tape tied to branches, with faded dates, fluttered a little. We’d done that the year the codling moths had gotten into the apples—but the apples were gone now, yanked, this ground put over to peaches. Everything changes because everything either grows or it rots, dad had also said—or not, and it was something I had read and made into his.

“Listen,” Henry said, and we did. If water had a voice here it was always like whispering. You needed to be quiet to hear it. It spoke in syllables of soil and air. When we were boys, and before the switch to drips and micros, when it had been ditches and checks and canvas socks swallowing and swelling, water had said different things in daylight than it said at night. It had said go and go and go when the sun was up and the surface flashed, and in the night it was intimate and heavy and it said don’t let me get away. It lapped at the ridge and tried to hop it and it wanted always to go downhill where the county had made the land slope very slightly, a thing you couldn’t see on a map but that your body knew. The first time my father let me open the headgate I thought this was what command meant, to release what was always held under an existing vertical, to turn a wheel with the palm of my hand and feel a deep mechanical responsiveness.

Now the water said, Are you sure.

“We’ll have to clean the sand trap,” Henry said. “I should have done that last week.”

“Let’s do it,” I said.

We walked back up past the end of the block and past the ditch road that rose and ran along the ancient Modesto canal. The canal had the specific stink it had always had of algae and old wet earth and sunlight gone tired on water. There was a turtle on a half-submerged board that’d been there since we were teenagers. The sand separator was a gray upright drum with a belly lower than our knees. When the water came in, the heavier sand spun out to the bottom and collected. If you didn’t loosen it and let the grit out into the culvert now and then, it made its own island inside the drum, and then stones went downstream and hit the emitters like shrapnel.

“You get it,” he said, and I said, “Sure,” and tried to loosen the tiger-striped handle and made a face and he laughed because there is nothing so funny to an older brother as the second-born making a face over effort. He gave it a quarter turn with the pressure of his wrist alone and the valve sighed and he said, “Careful,” because when the sand came it came in a rush and it could take your foot and stick you in place like a stuck pig if you weren’t smart. It came in a smooth spill, the color of coffee with too much milk, and then a cough as an air pocket tripped, and then a stream, and then little chunks of grit hopped as if they had minds. We were both kids again, the way you get hooked into posture by tasks you’ve inherited.

We ate our sandwiches under a tree that still had its tag hanging—a loop of metal dad had labeled with a chalk pencil in his neat weatherman script that had once charted ETo on lined legal pads. I said, “Ellen called me, too.”

“Let me guess,” he said. “She said she had news. She said she could work something.”

“I think she wanted to talk.”

“She’s a talker.”

“You think she loved him?” I said, and we watched the dust motes in the shade air hold their ground. The bark on this trunk was rough as bread. My shoulders hurt the right amount, the kind that means you’ve done a thing that will end.

“Everybody loved him,” Henry said. “Loving a man didn’t make the ditch any fuller.”

He had that way of landing a sentence like a sledge, like something had been pounded all the way in. Our father would have nodded at that. He would have said, “Yup,” and the word would have had in it both heave and relief.

“He died at the right time,” I said, because I say the cruel things out loud sometimes to see what shape they take. “If he’d seen this year—”

Henry wiped his mouth with his wrist and looked at the orchard like it was a man you or I might carry—he knew where the weight would go all day. “He would have found a way to be busy about it.”

“And then had a cigarette on the end of the row and coughed into his shirt.”

“And said his clown lungs were putting on a show for the birds.” He smiled without showing his teeth. It sat on his face a second and then it was gone.

We heard the drone of a truck on County C. We heard a mechanical honk that could be an old horn or a wild goose trying to do a machine’s job. I stood and looked over the tops of the trees and saw the county water truck run the far road and spray down dust for a work crew. Beyond the line of cottonwoods that marked the outer boundary of our eighty, there were roofs where there had been none ten years ago, neat color-coded objects with solar panels like flat tongues. Beyond those roofs, a hard glitter where the highway lay, and beyond that the low smudge of the coast range wincing through haze. And behind us, the Sierra that sometimes shows itself in June but today didn’t, as if the mountains had abashed themselves behind a grill of light. The town had grown greedily and closer. At night from the house you could see the new traffic lights changing like a private weather.

“Tell me something,” I said. “When you say sell, what do you imagine happens? Where do you go, and what do you do?”

“I didn’t say sell,” he said.

“When you say stay,” I said, “what do you imagine happens? Where does the water come from? Zephyr started metering three years ago. Levels fall. Everybody’s pumping deeper. The district’s combining boards and I can’t pretend like I have a full grasp of SGMA, but I read what I can, and the long-term plan—”

He held up his hand. “Don’t office me,” he said. “I have different ways to say no than you do. Theirs aren’t on paper.”

“No as in not now,” I said, “or no as in not ever.”

“I haven’t had lunch without the taste of this dirt in my mouth since I was ten years old,” he said. “You ask me what I imagine? I imagine standing up one day with a sandwich and not seeing trees where I see them, not seeing the shade fall where it falls. I imagine a hole where the house is because they dig the basements deep for those new contraptions they call homes, and I imagine some guy whose grandfather sold cars, whose father juggled mortgages like bowling balls, I imagine him standing up out of his truck and looking down his nose at the pitch of this ground and saying it will do. I imagine him putting a sign on our road that says Harvest Pointe.”

“I never said they weren’t terrible at naming,” I said.

He shook his head. “You’re laughing, little brother, and that’s fair because half of life is crazy. But there’s a part I can’t laugh about. I can’t say our name to no one if there’s no place to go with it.”

We went quiet awhile. A jay did his gargle-growl, that crude throat sound. A dragonfly visited and left. The tree’s leaves whispered because a breeze decided to exist for a small time.

“Dad said once,” I said, and he almost spoke but didn’t, letting me get it out, “that his father’d come here because he wanted a place he couldn’t be pushed off. His father had been pushed off everywhere else. Dust and then debt and then liens and then what you get when you hook your wagon to a promissory note and the bank does what it has to. He said that, and he said the smart thing would be to sell this, too, as soon as we were both in high school, when the first big money came calling in 2007. He said the smart thing and then he didn’t do it.”

“He was drunk when he said the smart thing,” Henry said. “The days he said he would sell were only ever the days he could have thrown the match.”

“Don’t,” I said.

“I’m not telling lies about him,” he said. “He was a complicated man and I loved him.”

“We sound like people from a book when we say that,” I said. “We could say, He was a sonofabitch, too.”

“He was the means I had to learn how to swing the weight of a thing to my advantage,” Henry said, and smiled again, genuinely this time. “He was a sonofabitch like me.”

I couldn’t help it. I laughed. Something in me that had been standing at attention all morning sat down.

A truck came up the ditch road from the far end and slowed at the culvert. It was Richie, our neighbor three properties down. He was too young to be called old, but his shirt was always unbuttoned like an old man’s because he’d worked out of it. He leaned like dogs do. The years had pulled his shoulder down. He stuck his elbow out the window and looked up through his eyebrows.

“You boys losing water?” he said, by way of hello.

“Always,” Henry said.

“You see the pressure fall and the scum slick up on the canal?” Richie said. “They’re doing something down south. Froze the gate, maybe, or cleaning the trash rack. I shouldn’t be in your business.” He frowned. He looked toward our house and scooped the brim of his hat with two crooked fingers. “Your dad.” He always said that like it had just occurred to him again to say it. “Your dad was a man.”

“Yup,” Henry and I said, like a call and response. It had become the way we would not cry.

“When you boys work like him, you’ll kill yourselves,” Richie said, and this was his way of saying he loved us. “Sell this place and come sit on my porch and watch traffic.”

“You’re making his argument for him,” Henry said, nodding in my direction.

“The world’s an argument,” Richie said. “Figure who you’re willing to let win.” He put the truck in gear and it made that soft mechanical sound that old transmissions make when they’ve been pureed with decades of dust. He waggled his fingers and was gone.

We rinsed our mouths with warm water from the bottle in the shade of the sand trap. It tasted like hose. We ate the last of the bread and there was grit in it because it is ungovernable, the way dust finds you. We went back to the open hole and shoveled in the earth and pressed our heels into it and smoothed it with the back of the shovel. When I lifted my head I saw the tube on the tree nearest me, the long white wrap around the trunk with the holes in it for breath. When we were little, Henry and I had put those on the young ones. We had made sure the scapular of plastic fit under the first branch. We had felt like doctors stitching patients after famine.

“I dreamed about the ditch last night,” I said, because it had the weight-of-nap sensation dreams do, sticky on waking. “Not our ditch. The lateral at Grandpa’s in Oklahoma. In the dream it was clean, clean as glass, the way it never was.”

“You never saw that ditch,” Henry said.

“Only in stories,” I admitted. “He made it up.”

“Whose memory is it?” he said. He leaned on the shovel and stared at the trees and maybe behind them.

“Ours,” I said. “Not a good answer. The man from Visalia called me at ten. He said the number again. He said we could name the streets.”

“Ah, Jesus,” Henry said. It was not a prayer. “I know how they talk. He said we could keep—what’d he say—an easement to the house? We could keep ten acres at the back for a gentleman’s orchard?”

“It was that tone,” I said. “You know. You’re lucky, boys. You can see your way out.”

The phone in the pocket of my jeans hummed and I took it out. It was the man himself. His name was Mike or Matt, one of those workman’s names. I could feel Henry watching me like a hand at my elbow.

“You going to answer it,” he said.

I let it ring long enough to be rude. When I pressed, I didn’t use speaker.

“Liam,” the voice said. “Hey there. Tried you a couple times. You on the property?”

“We are,” I said.

“That’s what I like to hear. Working hard or hardly working,” he said, and I saw the white square of his teeth in my mind, the new shirt. “I got the latest from our lender. We’re in a place to move quick. If we had a handshake, I could put a binder into escrow by Friday.”

“We’re still talking,” I said. “Our lawyer said—”

“Of course, of course,” he said, breathy like you might calm a horse. “We want you to feel good about this. You boys have weathered some tough years. I can help you not have to white-knuckle anymore.”

He meant money. He meant a lot of it, squares on a lined piece of paper I’d draw for my wife and then, later, alone, add up again as if by rehearing the numbers my unbelief would make them multiply. On the kitchen counter at our house an unpaid gas bill leaned under a magnet shaped like a grape cluster from a tourist shop in Napa, something my wife had put there once because she thought it was cute. I’d stood with the developer on County C in March in a wind that shook the new green leaves like the tiny tongues of new cut dogs and he’d put a hand on my arm and said, “Look. This is right. This ground is good ground and it will be good ground for other purposes, too. Your dad did a hell of a job and now you can do yours.”

“What if we keep the back thirty,” I said now, because bargaining wheedles you even when you plan not to be wheedled, and because it had become a tic with me, and because sometimes you unspool a spool to see when you will feel the tug.

“We need contiguous,” he said, softly, like we were talking about the breaks in a prayer. “Economies of scale.”

I could almost feel Henry go hard beside me. He didn’t take a step. He didn’t interrupt. He had that way of holding himself that sometimes had been confused for patience and sometimes for common sense and sometimes for mule-stubbornness that carried a whiff of contempt in it. He did not have contempt for me. He had contempt for a world that folded itself into deals.

“Let me call you this evening,” I said.

“We’d love to make you an offer in writing before the weekend,” he said. “We’re not the only ones in the market, Liam.”

“You’ll get yours,” I said, sharper than I meant, and pressed the phone closed.

“What if,” Henry said, and he didn’t sound like he wanted an answer. “What if we didn’t live in a world made of phones.”

“We’ve always lived in a world made of messages,” I said. “Grandpa’s letters. Dad’s notes on yellow paper. Word’s word, Henry. There’s no outside and inside to it.”

“You make that sentence sound like a thing you learned,” he said, as if I had been set an essay and handed it in. His ragged thumbnail was bleeding again, a dot.

“You bleeding,” I said.

“Always do,” he said, and stuck it in his mouth and sucked, and when he took it out the dot had spread itself into a small stain.

We went to block seven, and then to eight. Everywhere, water had pulled back to its minimum self. Everywhere, our fixes caught up with the rout of time. We loosened couplings where roots had built themselves in. We replaced little white spinners with new ones from a plastic bag we had not bought ourselves. We breathed glue and the glue lifted us a tiny bit off the ground with its fumes and then set us down. We stopped talking for a while. Words on days like that are like water, too—you let them run where they will when they’re new, you ride herd on them when you might lose them.

In the afternoon the heat quit with a thump. The sky went higher. That was the feeling—it removed itself, as if the tent over our heads had been unlatched. It was up to us then. The line at block eight kicked, and we both froze, and then a geyser hit. It was a joint we hadn’t seen because a mouse had pulled the dead grass tall over it. The water blew spears of sun at us and Henry stepped in almost jubilant and pushed a palm to it and the stream knocked him and he smiled because what else do you do when the thing you love hits you. He slid in the mud down to his knee and his hat went and the water threw it into the road.

He was up as quick as he was down. I grabbed the handle and cranked. The hiss fell. He stood and water poured off his shirt and his hair was a wet rope and his face a boy’s, a shocked boy’s. He laughed, a hiccup of a laugh that he’d been making since he was four, a thing that sometimes seemed to have been given to him the way you hand a green apple to a kid on a porch.

“Son of a bitch,” he said, and then stood, really stood.

“You okay,” I said, and I meant more. His mouth answered, and then not-his-mouth did, too, the tight skin around his eyes, the draw at his jaw.

“Bring me the hacksaw,” he said.

“It’s hot,” I said.

“Bring it anyway,” he said.

I did. He cut. I primed and glued. We did the dance again. When we turned the water on, the geyser did not return. A mist came from somewhere else that we could fix another day.

He stood in the road and shook his hair like a dog. His shirt clung. You could see the rough pattern of his chest through it, like a field seen through rain.

“You can’t keep doing this,” I said. It came out before I could walk it in a circle to see if it had another shape. “This, these, this fixing and fixing. It is a body you carry and it doesn’t get lighter, Henry.”

“I keep saying that to myself,” he said. He had one hand on his hip and the other on the shovel handle. The world spoke with a lot of little voices then—the bees, the distant tires on the highway, the little metallic ring of the water through the standpipes in the shade a row over, a man at the next place over starting his old tractor that had had a honeybee hive in the cowl since 1999. “I keep saying that and then I do it anyway, all the things. Our father did that, that all the things. It is not a virtue. I know that. Some days I get the feeling like there is something in the ground calling up through my boots and some days I don’t hear anything. On the no-sound days, I still go.”

“Why.”

“Why,” he repeated, soft, not a challenge. “Why does a man go to a thing he hasn’t been paid by in the longest time?” He put the shovel down. He put his hands on his knees and then he straightened again and took a breath that seemed to have some other breath behind it.

“Because I feel some part of the ground when I sleep,” he said. “Because the trees don’t move when I talk to them and people move. Because you carry the rest of the world in you and when you are needed one place, the need wraps you like a snake.”

“And I carry a city in me,” I said. “You don’t.”

“I do,” he said, and for a moment I was not sure if he meant literally, as if on his back he had strapped the place of stoplights like a pack, or if he meant he carried it anyway simply because I carried it.

“You think I don’t think about what Anna needs,” he said, saying my wife’s name for the first time that day. “You think I don’t know the shape of her good face? The way she argues herself around to yes or no? You think Ellen has not told me if I sell I can buy a truck with air that doesn’t smell of mice?”

We laughed. We do that to survive the parts where we might otherwise have words that would make our mouths bitter later.

“I think you think with your hands,” I said.

“Yes,” he said. “And with my feet sometimes.”

We ran the line out to ten, and then to the edge. The far boundary was the spot that always made us squint, not because the sun was bright there but because it was where the world made an indecent proposal: give me your rear twenty and I will turn you around, give me your rear twenty and you can sit on your porch and breathe a little while. If I closed one eye and looked from the side, I could see the ghost of everything changed—the absence of the trees would be a thing you could feel on your teeth. I imagined, for a second, and it was a guilty second, new grass where there was only puncture vines now. I imagined a clever drainage system. I imagined money clicking from a machine. I imagined a boy, maybe my boy if we had one, throwing a football under an inhumanly blue sky, lungs clean of dust. My father’s voice told me to be careful around illusions. My father’s voice told me to be careful around myself.

We went to the wellhead and looked at the dial. It jiggled in that way it did when the pump drew air, not enough to panic us but enough to make a note. Henry squatted and put his ear to the riser. He remembered things with his body. I admired that in him and I resented it some because a body dies out of itself.

“Remember when the water came up brown in ‘14,” I said.

“Yes,” he said.

“Remember the lemon smell when we cleaned the tanks.”

“Yes,” he said, and he smiled a private smile, not an unkind one. “Remember when you got down in the settling basin because you said you were small enough to reach the plug and you got stuck and I had to pull you out and you put your foot in my face?”

“Yes,” I said. In my mind the water in that basin smelled like old pennies even though I know it didn’t. “Remember when dad ran off that man who said we were stealing water.”

“Yes,” he said. “Dad had a way of becoming the size of a barn when he wanted to. He grew to fill the shape of a problem.”

We were telling each other a story to get to the end of the day.

At five, the sun got low enough that the leaves began to revert to their other selves, not simply a color but a set of many colors you could name if you had that kind of mind. The shade lengthened to a particular slant that pulled some quiet up from the dirt. We stood at the top of the block and looked down the rows we’d fixed that day. The trees held their small green stones and they were, when I looked, the shape of next days.

The phone hummed. Not the developer. Not Ellen. Unknown: a number out of county. I let it go because I am not brave enough always for other people’s news.

“I would like,” Henry said, not flourish, not preface, simple, “to have one summer where I don’t wake to count debts. I would like, and I know liking isn’t a plan.”

I said, “I would like for you to not die here, alone, carrying a shovel.”

He looked at me, the face I knew and couldn’t plumb. “You’d like to say everything that isn’t a choice, too,” he said. “That way, when we choose it later, you can say you saw it coming.”

“That’s not fair,” I said.

“No,” he said. “It isn’t.”

We walked back toward the house. Between the second and third block, there was the willow that had seeded itself in ‘90 and had somehow not killed everything next to it. Our mother had liked to stand there, a cigarette she would not be seen smoking cupped under her hand, hiding from her own moral toy soldier. She’d been the one to tell my father to talk to me easy when I failed Algebra II and to talk to Henry hard when he sat too long on the edge of his bed with his socks like a flag in his hands. She had grown roses on canes and they had been the red of teeth. At the end, when the chemo had washed her clean like some tide, she had wanted water, just water, the sound of it, the sound of the hose. We had set a fountain in her window. It had gurgled like a fool in a waiting room. The night she died had been like late August when everything is spent and you are too. The orchard had been very still in the moon. I had told myself then I would leave and never come back.

We went up the porch steps. One of the boards had a split in it that had cut my toe when I was six. I know these boards in my feet. The screen door’s spring moaned its old quaver. In the kitchen, the clock whose second hand ran backwards, a gag gift from a cousin, ticked garments of time. We drank cold water like the children of kings.

“Do you remember,” I said, before he cut me off, “the day we went to the canal in June and swam in our shorts and dad came down and pretended he was mad and then he took off his shirt and got in and had his wallet in his back pocket and cussed like injured marble?”

“I remember,” he said. He was smiling up at the calendar of a cornfield the bank had sent us in January. “I remember he swore the canal would silt his lungs shut and he’d die there and then he didn’t die there.”

“He died on the dirt he wanted,” I said. I put my hand flat on the table as if some electric would move through me, a current from oak to living to me. “He died here.”

“You want me to sell," he said, and it wasn’t a question.

“I want you to live,” I said. “I don’t know if selling and living are the same things or not. You have that brown in the hard of your eye of late, the kind that looks like coffee that’s been scrimmed by a skin.”

“Oh, so I should be pretty for you,” he said, not unkind.

“I should not have said it like that,” I said. “I want—what do I want. I want to not feel my life is only ever an argument with my own father’s ghost.”

“Ghosts,” he said. “It’s a word lazy men like.”

“I’m not lazy,” I said. “I drive four hundred miles round trip with a child’s bed strapped to the roof. I fix. I hold my wife awake while she counts what she was not told and I count what I did not say.”

We were both tired. The day had done what honest days do—it had shaved off our pretense and left us with our old faces. It had also given us the small currency of what we had done—there was water in the lines. We had scabbed some leaks.

“Okay,” Henry said. He set his hands flat on the table, too. We looked at each other over the wood. “Okay. This is what is true today. You and I can agree to an agreement later if we can agree to something we can stand by now.”

“I’m listening,” I said.

“I meet with Ellen,” he said. “I don’t meet with Mike or Matt or whoever. I meet with the bank who holds our notes. I do it with you in the chair next to me. We lay out—in writing, for your moral comfort and because that’s how you move through the world—the three options we say are not lies. One: We keep this and we make this year happen the way men make years—that is, by going to bed and getting up and turning valves and writing checks to keep animals out of old equipment. Two: We sell all of it, save the headstone acre where they are, though I know you despise that thought because your mind has a taste for whole cloth. Three: We sell half, if they’ll have it, we keep the rest, and we stop trying to make it be what it was. We put something in where water is less god—goats, hell, goats with bells, some kind of petting zoo for the city people with their shining shoes who will come and forget the names they called us in fourth grade. We call it—no, we don’t call it anything. That isn’t real. But we imagine it. We give that a day. Then we don’t talk for three. We do work and we sleep. After that, we speak again. None of these are oaths. They are things we can put in our mouths and chew without choking.”

He was so like my father then and he was so not my father. There was a gentleness he had not had ten years ago. It came from something broken that had not gotten back up and also from something that had and had made two of him where there had been one.

“I can do that,” I said.

“And if,” he said, and stopped because the sound that came then was not the phone or the highway or a hawk—it was water. It was a specific trickle that was not the little inside-the-pipe shift. It was water outside, moving where we had not told it to move. In the crease of the yard a line we had run to the hose bib years ago had given up, and a small tongue of water was speaking to the grass where there had been none. It was such a kind sound that my shoulders, which had been high all afternoon, dropped.

“Fix it tomorrow,” Henry said. “Let it run a minute.”

We stood on the porch and watched it fall. The sun made its way into that glitter way it had, telling us it had decide to leave. Across the ditch road, a man on a bike with two plastic grocery sacks on his handlebars went by, standing because his seat was too low. He held his face set toward whatever home was. A car backfired and made the cat go. My father’s hat hung on the peg by the door like a man waiting who would never again put it on. My mother’s glass bowl held a finger of dusty water and a floating bit of spider. The orchard kept quiet where it had spoken, and toward the north the sky did its soft thing it does on June evenings like any country that does not know war. The water at the broken bib made a small pool around a patch of clover, and two bees found it and drank, their little tongues laboring like oars. The sound was modest. It would seep in, it would be drank by roots, it would meet the old dust and put its hands on it and say hello, I remember you.

“We are not men who get easy days,” Henry said. He wasn’t claiming nobility. He was describing a weather pattern. “We are men who do work you can’t always see unless you stand in a particular place.”

“You’re the one who is going to hate the HOA letters,” I said.

“‘Dear Sirs, it has come to our attention that the paint of your mailbox has done what paint does,’” he said in that prim lady making fun of herself tone he could do.

“‘Dear Sirs, a man filled our nostrils with an herb-smelling of his gods as he passed with his dog. Please fix,’” I said back, and we laughed, and our father’s ghost did not mind that because he had liked a laugh when it made a day go forward.

We would meet with Ellen. We would have words that could sit on paper and not fly away. We would let the new water shape its bowl. If we sold, and I don’t know if we will, the last day would be like this and not like a knotted up thing with gawkers and a backhoe. If we kept, the next day would be like this one and the next, in their way.

There was a sound from block six that meant a small head had popped. There was a sound from the willow. There was a sound from my brother’s chest that was a deep calm. I wanted to lie down on the porch boards and press my cheek to them. I wanted to call my wife and tell her we had done some little good. I wanted my mother to hand me a piece of melon she had cut with a knife that had worn a crescent in its edge.

“Okay,” I said, and I was not saying yes or no. I was saying that there is day and there is day, you stack them end to end and you call that a life, and somewhere in it you get enough water to make a tree say thank you. “Okay.”

We stood like that until the light made us see into the trees farther than we should have. Then he crossed his arms and I crossed mine, and when I turned my head I saw the glint of the little blue bead of glue at the elbow we had fixed that morning. It had hardened into something like a jewel. The bead was useless but pretty. I thought: I will remember that bead for a very long time. I thought: I will have to, if I am to tell this story later like a man who lived it. I listened to the little leak by the hose and pretended it was the ocean and I was ten. Henry reached out and put his hand on my shoulder and left it there, and we did not speak until the mosquitoes came and told us to move inside where we would again be men who lived in a house with bills and a table and a clock that ran the wrong way. But for a while we had been men who had held a pipe together and believed in its holding, and whether or not we sold what we stood on seemed then a question we could ask without tearing the linings of our mouths. The day had worked on us. It had its result. The orchard breathed. The water ran where we had told it and where we hadn’t, and there was a mercy in that, too, a small mercy under a big sky, in a June that had decided not to kill us yet.
