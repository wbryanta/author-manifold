The morning the column lifted from the drainage, it came up clean, a single white exhalation that might have been a dust devil at first if not for the way it kept its center, dense and sure, a knuckle rising through the blue. Nora saw it through the warped pane of the east window, sitting on the stool by the map table with her coffee, forehead still tender with sleep, the crease from the canvas cot printed in her cheek. She’d been tracing with a blunt pencil the routes she used to take her first summer up here—shorthands of desire laid down over a topography that never had interest in understanding what she wanted—and writing little notes in the margin: creek shadows, old blowdown, elk wallow if still wet. The pencil stopped mid-sentence when the smoke stood up clean. She forgot the pencil was balanced on her knee, and when she stepped to the window it clattered to the old plank floor and rolled in a half-circle, the graphite leaving a soft gray arc where it went.

The first thing drilled into you, back when there were people hired to drill such things, is don’t get declarative about smokes. Let them tell you what they mean. The wind does its own reading. So she made herself still. The little metal ring of the Osborne was cool under her palm, the brass sight sliding like a well-oiled habit. She brought her eye down to the peephole, the crosshair hovering on the map. Northing, easting, pivot. She gave the bearing the way she’d learned to give prayers, without expecting anyone to answer. Two-one-four degrees. She plucked the protractor off its hook, set the angle, let her finger slide out over names that had lived all her life in her mouth: Blackjack Ridge, Three Sisters Spring, Cottonwood Drain. There: the drainage that forked off the South Fork where the sycamores wore that silvery undershow of leaves when the wind came up. There, too, the little square she’d drawn on the topo herself years ago, an amateur cartography that meant no more than a mark in pencil means on real ground. Lark’s. The cabin was a thumb-sized smudge where the contour lines pinched together and let a shaded fan of seconds pour onto the flats along the creek. Two-one-four carried straight through it.

She stood upright so quickly the stool skittered over the floor. Her palm had left a clear print on the metal ring of the Osborne, something about that human mark looking indecent on a tool so old it might once have been her grandfather’s, and she rubbed at it with the hem of her shirt. Heat popped under the sheets of the roof like corn, all the fists of sun folded up in the steel and timber. The set screw for the window wanted two hands and a coaxing she didn’t take time for. She shoved the pane up and let heat and glare bring a swim into her eyes.

Wind out of the south, light now but you never trust morning. Air as dry as paper. She dragged the radio by its cord, a brick of a thing she’d been babying since June, turned the knob through its useless places, listened to the empty band hiss and the occluded burr of some distant storm maybe hitting metal somewhere eighty miles away. When she keyed the mic she imagined she felt it answer, some sympathetic depression in the skin of the world where her voice would fall. Nothing but her breath compressing and bouncing back from the speaker. The batteries had given her the stink of off-gassed heat two nights ago when the thunderheads spidered above and white fingers came down to crawl over the ridge like something alive. “You sulk if you must,” she’d said then, laying the radio flat on the shelf. Now it lay there too obedient, as dead as any other tool that only mattered because of the hands that held it.

Eight miles down to the road where an engine might pass once a day if the crew felt like they needed a junkyard dog’s circuit to show they owned it. Eight miles of switchback and talus where one wrong torque of the ankle would set you to lying awhile and watching the solemn march of ants. Or three steep ridges and the creek, if you went the way deer went and didn’t mind ruining your knees before whatever was out there had got tired of wanting to live. She was not twenty-five and good at falling anymore. The idea came with the hot clarity of fear anyway: go.

She took the battered pack from under the cot and dumped its contents on the nailed-down rug—a rug that had once been part of their mother’s kitchen, cheap and full of the history of dropped coffee and a circle burned by a pan that Lark had set down when she wasn’t supposed to, one hand to the baby sparrow they’d found, one to a phone that was not their business. Things you think you’ve put away still show up in other rooms. Nora sorted without looking at the piles. Canteens: two, fill to the lid. Bandanas: three, wet them and wrap one around your neck now. Food gone to chalk in the heat: scrape the best of the jerky, the two figs she’d been hoarding like a miser of sweetness, the little plastic bag of almonds she could pretend not to have found. First aid: triangular bandage, blister pads, duct tape that could serve for anything if imagination didn’t give out before the adhesive. Matches in their red case, lighter: yes. Headlamp: batteries scrofulous but swap from the radio’s spares. Kestrel to read winds if it mattered, if reading winds mattered more than having eyes. A coil of bank line, thin as prayer string. Knife. The old hat she’d had since high school that made her look like their father when she caught sight of herself in a mirror on the rare days there was a mirror; a thing that always made Lark shake her head and say, Ma kept that damned thing? The lightweight pulaski someone had left three summers back and that she’d claimed in the way people left things up here for others to claim. The weight of it steadied her the way a sentence that had a subject and verb does.

She scribbled on the torn corner of an old order form and tucked it under the paperweight of the bolt she’d found with the ridge’s old number stamped in it, the ghost of a name under her name under the names of other summers. Smoke column 214 degrees. Likely Cottonwood. Radio dead. Going. If you get here before I do, go down and find me or put your hand on the rail where mine was and tell me I wasn’t wrong. She didn’t write that, but the words uncoupled from their wagon and clattered hardly out of the corral anyway. She set the paper under the bolt. She stood too long with her palm flat on the map, not like to bless it but to press down a buckling she could feel under her skin.

No one had told her she could have this last summer. It had come in the way boons sometimes did, side-eyed and conditional, shuffled into her hands at the end of a meeting that was about something else entirely. Decommissioned meant the Forest Service had come up two years ago with their laptops and polite smiles, smiled at her notebooks and the careful lists of each one of her smokes and sent her a certificate with her name spelled wrong. She tacked it to the wall with a nail she had to hammer with the heel of her boot, because she didn’t have a hammer and it was funny to pretend to. The agency had replaced human eyes with satellite passes and lightning maps and a plane every three days if funding allowed. But someone up the chain still had a place in his mind that looked like this: a square room of glass, a woman or a man who watched. A minor line item they could justify because tradition made good press, and because when the maps stuttered and the storms stitched their ragged hem of electricity over everything, sometimes there was still a person who could stand up and say, It’s there. People like that were mostly too young to want to sit still all summer anymore. Nora, with the long quiet that had followed her around like smoke itself since the winter she and Lark had decided how to dismantle their mother’s life, had lifted a hand. She hadn’t expected to be given the keys. She hadn’t expected the old telephone insulator still to be stuck like a blue glass fruit on the dead stub of the line outside. I do not expect a lot anymore, she had written in her journal that June. Not made bitter by that truth. Only shaped.

Now she cinched the pack, wrenched the trapdoor up and then down behind her, and felt the steadiness of treads under her feet translate to something like grace in her knees and ankles. She had never been a runner. But she had learned a way of going quickly downhill that was a kind of dedicated shuffle, the poles of her legs swinging with a knowledge all their own. The first breath of open air hit like a thin wall inside her lungs. The world beyond the square house of her summer had been held for her like a breath held by a compassionate body waiting for a child to get her shoes on. The wild grasses along the trail were past their young green, pale and rattling, brittle when her shins brushed through. The mantles of lichen on the rocks wore the same living gray as before but a thousand ghost moths lifted from the edge of the bracken when she passed and their wings sounded like silk being thumbed.

She used the path that had been drawn with the habits of her boots and no one else's, slanting along the western face of the ridge where the slick rock was less treacherous when dew still laid a shoulder on the world. She paused once to look back. The tower looked improbable, as it always did when you left it, like a contradiction you squared while you were in it that became absurd upon stepping outside. Glass above trees. She tasted the metallic fatness of adrenaline, two fingers down into the little pocket of fear but not yet dredging anything out. She pulled the hat low and set her eyes on where the trail winked between two blocks of granite and then reappeared down canyon among the barberry.

A raven came up from somewhere below and made a tangle in the air in front of her, stringing a strand of sound between one wingtip and the other. She stepped to the far side of the trail to let it have whatever it thought it owned. Ravens never learned their size. The fire, if that’s what it was and not some ranger doing something stupid, would have not yet found a crown to ride. The column was clean, unshredded. But the day would pull it apart. The wind, you wanted to think of it as a mind you could outwit with cunning or flattery. It would blow upslope as the land heated, then lean back down as a tired father in the evening when the thermals slackened. The creek would be a line of life the fire would lick like salt. If Lark was home she would be watching the ridge herself, the field glass their father had hung by a nail in the cabin like a second clock. Maybe she had gone into town yesterday, or last week, which for Lark was the only measure you could be sure of. The cabin did not have cell reception even when the world worked. Nora had driven in last spring before the snow had quit its bad habit and found a note pinned to the porch by a length of deer bone. Town for supplies. Back soon. The bone had been an expression: even my thumbtacks come off the land.

She allowed herself to think her sister’s name like a mouthful you could keep, then shook it out. The thought made the going one step heavier and three more careful. You could turn any present situation into an argument about the past if you held it wrong. The house of the body, Nora had learned later and not from any doctor, keeps accounting in its floors and windows. There were places where you stepped and boards squeaked and you told yourself you’d fix them later. There were windows you never opened even when the air grew stale. She wiped her palm over the seam of her pants and kept on.

At the notch before the old rockslide she dropped off the trail and bushwhacked toward a game path she knew, then lost it and found it again by the freedom of scat in the middle of clay. Sage-scent opened, sweet as a drawer of dry clothes. She’d forgotten to lash the pulaski to the pack; it thumped her hipbones lightly and then hard when she took a lurching step. She cussed. “I’ve heard worse,” she could hear Lark say, that sideways note that always sounded like both forgiveness and a dare. The years had not eroded their exchanges, only smoothed the edges like water made the hem of a shirt new-torn at the creek less unruly.

The first time she had watched a fire come up a drainage with the earnestness of an animal at a fencepost was with their father. He had a way of peering that looked like he was being told a secret by the world and being careful not to move so that nothing would be scared off. Lark had been bored, at least to begin. She’d tugged the hem of her shirt and asked if they could go down and put their feet in the creek. “Look,” their father had said, not in a scolding way but as if that one word was asking them to stay long enough to have something revealed to them. The thin line of smoke he showed them then would come and go like a struck match when the wind breathed. “It looks small,” Nora had said, and he had laughed. “Everything looks small at a distance. You can use that as wisdom if you like,” he’d said. Later, when he left, taking Lark with him for a summer and then bringing her home in September with mosquito bites covering her legs and a bike seat tied with twine to a suitcase handle, he had left behind that phrase as if it was the uncle who would raise you.

The sight of the column again made Nora stop. She stood in the hard mottled light under the pines and watched the lid of the sky lift. Now the smoke had a flocculence to it, frilled at the edges, the starch gone from its middle. Someone—a logger maybe, or a ranch hand with a lazy impatience—could have set a pile ablaze and walked off thinking flame would behave itself like a well-bred hound and sit until he came for it. Or lightning had put a spark in a duff bench and it had smoldered quietly all dawn until enough oxygen had been sexed into it. It didn’t really matter which story you told yourself when you were putting one foot in front of the other. Human error or act of God; the animals didn’t parse those.

Her mouth tasted like a nickel. Down through a stand of needled shade where the ground held cold even at noon if you dug your fingers into it; then up over the slick red belly of a manzanita and into a tangle of chinquapin that grabbed at her shins with a perversity that reminded her of every time someone had told her that adulthood was when pettiness ceased. A lizard did pushups at her. She laughed once, a bark that surprised her. The hat felt like a pressure band around her forehead. She slid and sat hard and the right ankle, that old unfaithful, rolled and then righted itself like a boat that had shipped a little water. She stopped, wiggled it, spoke to it out loud as if it were a horse.

On the next saddle she could hear the creek, that old singing like spoons knocked in a drawer. The smoke was new now in the back of her mouth, resin-fat. A different smell than cooking or a house—green, full of the ghost of sap. It hit some childhood key in her and at the same time pressed on everything in her that was alert to danger. If it had been a bear in the brush she would have known what her body wanted to do: make noise, make herself taller, negotiate. With fire you wanted to bargain. It was larger than appetite and smaller than a god. It had rules that changed in different light. If Lark was home she’d be clearing a circle around the cabin now, moving the firewood stack away from the porch, dragging the ladder into the yard. Lark was good in crisis. She could not keep a calendar appointment to save her life but if the neighbor came pounding on the window at two in the morning with a woman in labor in the back of the Tacoma, Lark would be a surgeon with her hands and a singer who knew how to sing low to keep the body from flying off.

Nora dropped into the drainage with the tight-throated unease of going down toward something you’d rather imagine relieved. She found the creek by the foot-polished step on a boulder and the place where deer had cut a little launch through the sedge. The water was lower than it should have been for this time of year. She bent to dunk her bandana and brought it up shining, cowled herself with it, squeezed translucence from the tail ends. The smokes have been worse for fewer, she thought—the red flag days strung end to end like rosary beads this summer and the one before and the one before that. You could pretend not to count the changes, but your hand still remembered the count.

The cabin announced its proximity first with the windchimes on a porch that did not so much hold up the house as lean against it fraternally. Not wind chimes the way catalogues imagine them but a brace of bent spoons suspended from bits of wire, a trio of washers, some bone shapes no longer belonging to anyone. Then the smell of goats. Lark always took in what other people out here let go. A pair of Alpine crosses who had belonged to a family that went through a divorce took up at the cabin and ate the poison oak Lark could not. Sheds laid down like a darning bag. A hummingbird spun like something on a jeweler's lathe over the corner of the garden where foxgloves poked up, their tongues out. “Hush,” Nora said when it darted toward her hat, mistaking the sweat-dark crown for some new blood.

She said her sister’s name once when she could see the fieldstone chimney. It changed where in her mouth the air went. The call fell like a handful of dry rice against the cabin’s side. “Lark?” She waited, and silence performed its well-practiced trick of seeming like many different silences in succession. In the months after their mother died, Nora went to a meditation group in town because that’s what the woman who cleaned her office said had helped her when her brother died and she didn’t have anyone at home who would let her walk around the house without shoes. The teacher said a quiet mind contained multitudes the way distances contained rain. Nora sat and tried to be the quiet boat on the quiet lake for the dictated twenty-seven minutes, but when the bell rang she came to the same small house with the same holes. There was no ceremony that had a right fit for what she and Lark had done: sold the inheritance their mother had thought would hold them together with a circle of fence and a deed with two names. For months they’d conducted a negotiation the way children conduct a blanket they both want: pull, get mad when the other pulls too, accuse one another in language almost sing-song with its oldness. In the end they kept the cabin and let go the better land no one had loved. Neither sister forgave the other for what not selling had cost the mother those last six months when a better bed would have been under her and more time off work would have been in Nora’s hands. Forgiveness had less to do with what was right than what loss asked you to become. Nora hadn’t been able to become the person who could look Lark in the face and say, Never mind, or Yes, that’s true. Instead she had written long paragraphs in a notebook up in the tower about whether a lookout was a kind of penance or a compromise, and Lark had scrubbed the sink in the cabin every Monday night as if there weren’t a hundred other things needing doing.

Now the porch steps spoke under her and a dog that wasn’t a dog shouldered out from under the chair beside the door and looked at her with slotted eyes. “You beautiful bastard,” Nora said to the goat, whose horns scrolled like a thought you’d refused to entertain and then had to admit. It stared at her as if suspicion were the moral stance of the hundred percent betrayed. “Where is she?” she asked it. She set her hand to the doorknob and it turned. Inside was a humidity of heat that had to do with living bodies but also with being small and hung with things that stored air.

The field glass was on the table. A coffee cup had not been rinsed; a ring of brown at the bottom said that last night Lark had not wanted to waste water on a thing that served mostly ceremony. The shelves were stacked full, honey in quart jars lit up a shelf with their own electricity; bags of beans rubber-banded; a folded tarp; a little jar with screws she’d pick through and never find the right one. Boots inside the door, splayed. The room contained Lark the way a room contains a cologne even after the man has gone. On the pinboard by the bed were two slips of paper that said the same thing: Town Monday. Back whenever. In Lark’s handwriting, the whippy c’s that always looked like they were trying to be geese.

She stood where she was long enough to untie the narrow knot in the muscles of her chest. Then the recognition landed with the same insistence the smell of smoke had: if her sister was not here, her sister could not help do the work. So the work was hers. It was a relief, which she hated. She told Lark out loud that she was sorry about that.

Outside the light had gone from noon-white to stained glass. Embers were not falling yet. But a sound like a far train had started up, the uniform low hum that the long body of a fire makes when it has found its confidence on a slope. The edges of the smoke had torn into little banners. She put on gloves and took the dry lines of the clothesline down and coiled them and stuffed them under the porch, then set herself to the simple tasks that did not require thinking. Move the woodpile away from the house and spread it in a thin lazy sigh across bare earth thirty yards off, shift the ladder, drag the tall tank of propane a respectful distance. Wet down what she could. Lark’s water system was not an invention you’d trust in the city: a barrel on a raised platform catching the slope of the roof’s runoff, a lattice of rubber hose branched stupid across the yard but serviceable. Nora hauled the hose and let it ride her shoulder and the ammonia stink of the first water made her eyes prickle. The hose had the pressure of someone telling you a secret: reluctant at first, then eager to prove itself. She soaked the duff by the porch and made herself not waste water on the roof though it would have made her feel as if she were doing something for the part of the house that could not be reached. Bandana again, water again, hat again.

The goats followed her everywhere like accusations wearing bells. She wished idiotic, hotly, that there were children here to be told to do what they were told. Tell them, You get in the creek and stay with them because I am about to do something with fire. As if Lark would have permitted either goats or children to do anything but insist on their own ideas. The thought of children, specifically of the absence of them, rose with a speed and thrust that made her feel as if she had tripped on a root. She put a hand out to the porch railing as if she were a very old woman. The day she and Lark had said the worst things to each other had not been in the kitchen at the end of the long summer but on the shoulder of the highway in March, the snow not quite off the buckbrush, the two of them standing behind the idling car like teenagers taking a break from the school dance. Lark had said, If everything had gone the way you think things should go there’d be a boy of eleven now. As if she had plucked from her sleeve the exact needle with which to thread the bead. I know that, Nora had said, with a smallness she felt in the bottoms of her teeth. Lark had always had a capacity to turn the right word, the diamond-tipped one that made all the smaller cuts irrelevancies. But what you are is not a person who can imagine loving someone who reminds you so much of yourself, Lark had continued, and that had been unkind and accurate. They had not said anything for several minutes except what hazard lights tick. In the spring after their mother died, when the fetus that had felt like it would embarrass them all by daring to grow left quietly in the night, Nora had told no one. She had not told Lark. Maybe she would have kept it if she had. Maybe not. There are so many lives that never had a chance to be disappointed by you. Lark’s rupture of a sentence had not been cruelty. It had been Lark in her usual dogged relation to truth. You could hate the person who pointed out where your hands were dirty even if they’d used their own shirt to wipe yours off more than once.

Nora dragged the picnic table to the side and soaked the clearing around it. She told herself she did not have to know what to do beyond the next right thing. She made a little circle of bare ground between the sycamore and the rock where they had once lashed a hammock and fallen asleep in it like two lovers, their father reading in a chair beside them, the book on his chest rising and falling. She looked up, and the first cinder landed at that moment on her forearm and kissed her with its small mouth. It was so quick she might have thought she imagined it if not for the black dot it left, perfect and obscene. “All right,” she said to the air, the word sounding like a way to settle your body on a horse. She dragged the hose over the place she meant to be later. She put her hand on the sycamore and thought the cool of it back into to herself. It had lived through worse. It had rings for all the ways the air had remembered itself around it.

She took the rake and scuffed at the line she wanted between the world and this yard until it came clear and mean, without compassion in it. The motion bent her differently than she was used to. She felt the stir of little animals in the thatch trying to run and go nowhere. The roar in the trees upridged was steady now. A squirrel went belting up the sycamore as if velocity itself were safety. The light went to a weird bruise. The dimensionality of the world, the shading of near to far, collapsed and then came back like the tide.

“Lark!” She tried again, knowing it was the wrong time for waste. Her voice sounded like it would tear. It did not tear. It found a place in the air and sat down there and looked at her as if to say, You wanted me to do what?

She went inside and took the field glass because it felt like a way to share something with a person who wasn’t here. She slung it over her shoulder. The goats beat her to the creek and stood in it belly-deep and looked as pleased as men with cold beers. “Good,” she said to them. She drank from her canteen with a greed that would shame her later if Lark were here to see it and so shame didn’t arrive.

The fire showed itself on the far slope the way a predator does when it is certain you have seen it. Not a wall but a collection of individual ambitions, shrubs going up and then throwing their weight into a larger animal, a lone pine exhaling in a whoof. The heat came across like an emotion you were not ready to have. Everything gentle made sense one second and then was stupid the next. Nora dropped the pulaski and took the shovel—lighter, easier in the hand. She dashed at a place of leaf-litter and clawed it away like she was mad at it. The undertone in the fire’s voice changed when it took the draw, the way a song drops its key to go find the back row of the room. The wind came downcanyon against expectation and laid a hand on the fine hair at the back of her neck and made it lie down. She said full sentences under her breath without hearing their sense.

She did not want to burn anything. The thought of lighting fire in the face of something so much bigger now seemed like participating in a fight that had too many rules. But the duff wanted a line, a gap. She had seen a man in August build a line with nothing but a shovel and a will and save a winter cabin up in the headwaters of Bean Creek. He had been a prick, swigging whiskey from a pocket flask in the pockets between doing actual things, but he had done it and told no one. The virtuous were not the useful. The line here could be sloppy. She made it. She made the raggedy circle tighter. She kicked aside a coil of a green hose that meant to trick her.

There was a sound then that made her chest give. It came not from the fire but from the road, the old track down the drainage that used to be an access for timber but had not been logged in a decade since the mill closed and more houses grew than pines. A motor. The sound of a truck that had been tuned by someone who minded it. The whipcrack of a door. She walked into the open as if she couldn’t be surprised anymore and found out she could.

Lark came down the trail with the indecorum she reserved for emergencies, arms windmilling ahead of her bodily decisions, her hair a piece of night that had not read the sky correctly, the straps of the daypack digging deep pale lines into her shoulders. She had a shovel balanced on one of those shoulders and a bag of flour on the other. She wore shorts and boots and a shirt with someone else’s company logo. She was as beautiful and as alarming as any person you saw for the first time in a long while and remembered you had loved. “Of course you’d be here,” she said, not with any relief in it but with what felt like appreciation. She set the bag of flour down like a thing that could explode. “The radio?” she asked.

“Dead.”

“I tried the number.” She meant the county dispatch with the nice woman who used to call Lark sweetie. “No go.”

“It’s coming,” Nora said, and it sounded like they were both telling and not telling each other something harder.

“You moved the wood. Good,” Lark said, and went to it. There was a terrible congruence to the way they worked, born of doing the same things to the same yard in different years: what I do next is what you would do if you had eight more hands. Lark pulled the gutter clean with fingers and a stick while her sister raked the one fire the other did not have to fight yet. There was nothing to discuss and there was so much to say that it would take the rest of both of their days. They did not attempt even one syllable of that. The goats went slack-eyed with contentment in the creek and wrung their jaws like old men. The bag of flour sat useless in the shade a yard from the picnic table, a tender plan aborted. For what did you drag it, Nora wanted to ask—pies for when? Lark would be annoyed she’d asked while the fire was still figuring where it wanted to go.

“Roof,” Nora said, pointing with her chin. Lark spat onto her palm and took a step to the makeshift ladder and then hesitated for an instant the way an animal does when it remembers it has a body. “Don’t,” Nora said, or meant don’t be show-off, or meant be careful. Lark was already halfway up, the sawdust from the afterlife of boards in the shade glittering on her shins. She hucked the hose. The splatter of heat that came down through the leaves made the makeshift think of rain and then think better of it.

“Shit,” Lark said, “I didn’t think it would be today.” As if the fire had been a guest she had hoped would change its mind. “We’ll go to the creek if the crowns take. Or before.” She grinned a little, and the old diagonal scar on her chin—bicycle, age eight, a stone in the road—showed as if to say, I have always survived.

“You were in town yesterday?” Nora asked stupidly, as if time could be made smaller for the being said.

“Monday.” Lark threw a sodden rag down into the yard and the goats gaped at it like connoisseurs. “Adam needed me to get parts for the pump because he thinks none of us know how to read a gauge. He’s not wrong.”

The wind went another way. It took the line of smoke and bent it like a reed and Nora had to resist the bodily inclination to lean with it in a mistaken bid to push. The high branch of a cottonwood across the creek rattled with leaves that had not yet been told their day had changed. She wanted to take a seat on the ground and put her face in her hands and experience what it felt like to be safe and off duty. Instead she took the hose and let it run in a bead around the feet of the sycamore and made a spray over the table and let it rainbow for an unhelpfully long three seconds because she wanted the sight of it.

The change when it came was like a plan finally making itself known. The under-timber on the far slope took fire together as if a command had been given. She felt the heat at her face, the tiny raisins of sweat under her glasses. “Now,” Lark said, in the tone of a nurse who had to bark a direction without sacrificing her personhood to officiousness. Nora wanted to hug her and to shut her up and to desolate herself at once. “Now,” Lark said again, because the first now might have been for clearing a throat. “We go.”

They went. The creek was not deep but it was water. Nora waded until cold lapped the crease at the top of her boots or what would have been the crease if it had not all been bluntness down there for years. Lark stepped beside her and their elbows met and then did not move away. The dogs they did not have would have barrelled into them then, the absent boy eleven would have hooted because being knee-deep in a creek in August when adults were telling you to be serious would have been the most impossible set of commands. Instead the goats made noise like someone learning to play a horn. Sparks stitched a seam in the air above them. Nora bent and soaked her bandana and tied it over her nose and mouth. Lark did the same. The sound from the world went to a pure roar. It had nuances you could learn if you wanted to be useful: the slither of needles taking, the pop of resin pockets, the hot fat hurry of something catching that you hadn’t thought of. Lark put one of her hands over Nora’s where it sat awkwardly on Nora’s thigh. You could make anything sentimental here. The creek went on with its separate argument with gravity and stone. Smoke came hard into their faces and then slid away, a change in eddy that had nothing to do with mercy.

“You always wanted it neat,” Lark said into the rag, and the words landed as lovingly as they might have on some other day. Nora would never be able to explain to anyone not them two how not unkind that was.

“And you,” Nora said, pausing, because she wanted to get her own sentence bright and jewel-sharp and not break any more than was needed, “you wanted it to not be anything. You think if you don’t name it it’s free.”

A tree somewhere across the creek gave up, came in a slow bow you could hear in your ribs and then let go of itself and took what it wanted on the way down. The sound made the goats look up like tourists enthralled by the local customs. Lark’s finger ran down the ridge of bones on the back of Nora’s hand as if she were reading a book she hadn’t seen in years. “You felt like you needed to leave a lot,” she said, in a tone that would not have been allowed without the fire there to be louder. “I thought if I held anything that mattered I could keep you here by keeping it. I was wrong. I was so wrong that I would have given you to whatever wanted you if only you would decide it and stop making me decide it for you.”

Nora touched the scar on Lark’s chin with the side of one finger, as if it were still so tender that full pads would make it ache. The fire pushed again. Air got down on its belly and tried to find a way through the tangle of their hair and the fabric at their mouths and into their longing bits and do what air always did. She did not know if the cabin would stand. She did not know if the old barrel would burst and shoot a tongue of boiling toward them. She knew only that the hands they had were not smaller or larger than they had been yesterday and the day before that. She wanted to be next summer at the same time that she was not sorry to be in the last of this one. She wanted to tell Lark about the boy who did not become, to ask if naming him would give him a time he never had, to say there is no ceremony, I cannot keep anything. She wanted to tell Lark she had been wrong to hate her for loving the place more than the person and then had to check that thought like a horse near a cliff: neither of you has known what you meant.

The fire pressed across the creek as if it had been summoned. The top layer of smoke went a color she didn’t have a name for. It could have been the color of a bruise you forget you had, or the inside of a ripe fig. Embers came like flying seed, red and then no longer red, then red again when they kissed new material. The scooped bowl of their bodies in the water offered up their shirts to wet as if dressing for something that went on below your knees. Lark murmured to the goats and the goats murmured back. Nora held the cool she had like a loan she did not have to repay in full.

The door of the cabin—the old, mended door with its panels salvaged from someone else’s life—stood open, the way Lark always left it even when the news said coyotes had been brazen. The woodpile was far. The ladder lay on its side. The field glass lay on the picnic table. It still astonished Nora that when Lark had first bought them the house had needed nothing more than presence to make it a home. She had brought a mattress and a kettle and then, over the months, those jars of honey and the bones and the chimes and what looked like chaos became a pattern that remembered them. It occurred to her, stupidly as lightning and yet as plain as boards, that maybe she would die here—here or somewhere like here—with a body that knew what one creek did when another creek asked it to meet, with a hand that had contained the hand of her sister enough times that even silence had turned into an item in the cupboard you could bring out, dust, and use.

A leaf lit and went to confetti at their feet. The sycamore made a sound like a mouthful of cloth being torn. Heat made her think of their mother for no good reason beyond the idea of a constant presence that wrapped everything and then was gone. “After,” Lark said. “After we will walk to the road. Ai knows a guy with a pump who will come and be a penis about it and I will pet him and he will go away and we will go to town and I will feed you something with sugar in it and you will tell me about that—” she made the shape of a cradle in the air and set it down without finishing. It was the kind of respect only two people who had raised a long fight between them could achieve. “And we will see,” she finished. “Because saying any more than that is not something I can afford.”

“All right,” Nora said. It was a word like putting your bottom in a chair you have to sit in for a while and shifting until your bones find a less ungenerous place.

The fire did what it did, which was feed itself and then ease a little, take and then throw back heat onto what it had already made hollow. They stood long enough that their legs forgot distinction between warmth and something like humiliation. A plane made a sound across a portion of the sky where sky still had a say. It could have been scouting. It could have been nothing. She did not hold on to the idea any longer than it took to acknowledge she had heard it.

When the first long breath out came from the slope, a softening of the single note to something with more texture in it, Nora lifted her eyes over the leaves and the goat horns and the yard to the porch. The cabin still stood. The little jar of screws on the shelf through the window held its vague life as if nothing had been asked of it. The bag of flour was ridiculous. The windward side of the garden had gone a brown as sudden and complete as shock. A gopher moved from one hole to another like he had a schedule. The ladder lay with its legs in a modest splay as if embarrassed. The air had not cleared. It would not clear for days. But the rhythm in her shoulders had altered. She could make out the individual branches of the buck brush now where a quarter of an hour before there had been only a coherent wall of want.

“It will creep,” Lark said. “It will come around that draw if we ask for mercy.” Her hair was stuck to her head, a cap of wet black. It was impossible that anyone had ever thought it a good idea to speak the way they had spoken to each other by a roadside. “We need to watch. We need to go around the east fence and see what the neighbor’s done, if he’s done anything smart. We need to sit. We need to drink water. I need you to not look like you’re apologizing without saying for what.”

“I am apologizing,” Nora said. “But also for something I can’t say because the goat will overhear and tell all his friends.”

“His friends know everything anyway,” Lark said. She reached to pull the damp bandana down and laughed a laugh that was half cough. “You look like Ma when she’d had three margaritas and promised she could dance well again if we didn’t laugh.”

“You,” Nora said, and couldn’t think of a single thing not made of nettle or honey. She scuffed her boot in the water to give herself something that tied to the world and not to feeling.

They stepped out of the creek together like it was choreography set thirty years ago and they’d missed every chance to rehearse. The way the soles of their boots took the earth made a sound that was itself a history. They went to the east fence and to the neighbor’s property, to find, as in a parable, that he had made nothing but beer plans. They went back to the cabin. Nora picked up the field glass and set it to her face, sighting along the ridge to the corner where the light had gone from riot to tarnish. Lark stooped for the bag of flour as if there would be some comedy left in making biscuits for no one in such light. She straightened and looked at the tower’s notch on the far ridge through the milk of air. “I think,” she said, “when this is over and the creek has new ash in it and my hair smells like the worst thing it’s ever smelled like and you have found ten new things wrong with my wiring, I will come to your little square house and sweep. Because it will make me feel like a person who is good to another person.”

Nora dropped the field glass into her chest and let their harness strap slap. There would be days to come, or there would not. The road was eight miles of questions unasked and asked ineloquently. The radios would work again and then they’d be in a world where everyone had a thought about the wisdom of two women who had made the wrong right decision or the right wrong one. The tower would be taken apart by a crew who did not have, could not be expected to have, a sense of what it meant for four walls and a view to have kept a woman company for a summer that was the last of its kind. She took a drink and let her mouth get too full, spill some over her chin and down onto her shirt so that it would leave a shape like a country she had not yet been to.

She took Lark’s wrist in a way that did not demand anything except the center of gravity of one bone in the hand of another. “All right,” she said again, and the word was a pass, not an end. A bird flew between them and the ridge. It could have been a swift. It could have been a scrap of ash. The creek kept its sibilant like someone telling you to hush even when no one had spoken. They stayed there long enough that a second gust hit their ankles and decided not to say anything language would regret.
