We moved like animals being herded, in the blue dusk under the shout of gulls, a narrow rope of us as the ramp to the ferry scraped and shuddered. The smell of tar and diesel nested in our clothes, the low long building we came through had a floor polished by decades of salt-grained boots. Men from the trucks ahead of me were tying down loads, tarpaulins already slick with the evening mist, their voices calm, practical. There were those who crossed every week, with cards in their wallets that exempted them from everything except the ocean. I carried not more than a night’s bag. On the third finger of my left hand I had looped a length of copper wire, a habit, a circle my brother once twisted when we were children, saying here’s your ring, king of the ditch.

It was supposed to be simple. A policeman had telephoned, his voice polite, slightly surprised at its own accent. We are trying to locate the family of a man pulled from the north shore, he had said. We have a name. Your brother’s name. They had a photograph, not more than a smear into which a person was persuaded, the paper like damp bread between my fingers when I held it to the light. They asked can you come. It would be good to have a person.

They sold sweet buns and fatty sausages on the main deck. Neon from an arcade glowed against my coat; in one corner a boy steered a spaceship silently into rainbows of death. I smelt the breath of an enormous dog. There was a woman in the line with a red scarf too large for her neck, it pooled down her chest like the silk of a parachute. The sea beyond the windows was becoming part of itself, the edges dissolving, a field of iron filings dragged under a magnet. The announcement came in two languages that night and I preferred the one I did not understand because it made no demands.

I had not seen my brother in eight years. Not the rough of his cheek, not the slow patient way he bent to his shoes. We spoke three times on the telephone after our mother was gone. Once he was drunk and jolly and told me he had seen wolves cross an ice road and that it wasn’t what anybody said, it was still possible for wildness to choose its own muskeg. Once he called me from a gas station and he was crying and said nothing could be lifted from the way our hearts lied to each other. The last time he asked me do you know what’s coming, as if the two of us were men on the shoulder of a highway looking back the lonesome stretch of asphalt toward the storm of a convoy.

In the corridor to the sleeping cabins the carpet was patterned with compass roses, degrees marked out in gold thread. A boy crouched with a coin, rolling it on the thin rail that skirted the wall, saving it from falling as if he could practise rescue. I had a berth down a narrow hall that felt already submerged. The light was gentle and grey and the whiteness of the sheet was the only bright colour in the room. I sat on the edge of the bed and let my bag fall between my feet. On the white sheet the copper band leapt in its small way, glowed, then returned to itself.

There were objects a man keeps when he has no religion. A silver lighter that belonged to our father, even though we both stopped smoking. A map of the islands he never visited, brown as toffee at the folds. The coil of wire knotted on my ring finger. On the ship I took from the bag the maps and held them open, the light not enough to show anything but elevation where rivers used to be. In the picture printed in the atlas there were names for everything. The sound in the wall behind my bed was the low steady thrum of the engine, which I pretended was a heartbeat. If I could sleep I would sleep to that.

The first time we crossed a body of water in the dark was in a canoe we had borrowed and not returned. We were teenagers and my brother lit a match at the shore to look into the bow like a surgeon. We pushed out knees first and the water knocked against the hull with its careful knuckles. Above us the pale island we were headed for had a single spruce on its hill and our breath made the branches move. If we panned our heads slowly the northern sky swung overhead like a compass. He sat in the stern and I, smaller, took the paddle at the prow. He did not call me by my name that night; his voice was close like a scarf. He said you frighten me with your silence. I told him nothing because the black lake was the first thing we both believed.

When we were small, in the basement where our father kept his tools, my brother taught me to make paper birds that truly flew. We cut them with kitchen scissors from the Sunday supplements spread over the cement. The paper still smelled of perfumes in columns, a catalogue of gifts for women who had climbed once into the trees. With a ruler he marked a wing line where we’d fold. He made a flock in his mind and then he made it on the floor. The wire ring and the paper birds and the red breath of wolf-thin cigarettes his friends smoked in our doorway; without these, it is all just weather.

I stood in the cafeteria with a tray I did not need and watched the sailors move through their work. The ferry nosed out and the little city stepped backward. Harbour lights pushed beads of green and yellow out along the slip. Our wake was the white peel of an orange torn and tossed into blue-black oil. On the pier a man in a wool cap lifted his hand and all the hands of the boys on the video games lifted too. There is always someone raising a hand to something leaving. When our mother left our father, she put single objects from drawers into a canvas bag and the objects made no sound. I remember the thick glass ashtray with a chipped lip. A photograph of a man in army fatigues who wasn’t our father. The brass key to a locker no one would ever open.

She left a sealed envelope on the table with our names on it. We were at school. The house was so hot and cold it gave me fevers. When I returned that day the envelope was light as a moth in my hand. It contained no currency, nothing to weigh it down. Just a picture from a calendar of two men dancing at a harvest. Somebody had cut around their legs with a nail scissor. My brother tucked the envelope into his coat pocket and never surrendered it. Sometimes now when I dream I put my hand into that pocket and bring my fingers out dusty with moth wing.

Night on the ferry is not an empty hallway. It is a row of trucks each with a driver sleeping in its velvet belly, the straps like crossed arms. It is bunk beds in little cabins, and women curling in an extra sweater, the windows filmed with a low licking fog. It is the man with the cleaning cart pausing, leaning on the handle, watching the black eyes of the sea through the port, not wanting to move because the ocean is watching him and he knows. I put my book down face-flat. The sentence I was reading, I forgot not because it was not good but because the motors entered my head like a secret mouth and hummed out what nothing could say.

At eleven a small boy cried in the next cabin and then fell into a soft continuous sorrow as a dog might when its master snores. Above me was a boy’s shoe, a damp toy, something with wheels. I went to the little sink and ran cold water on my hand, pressing the thin skin at my wrist under the stream, counting to thirty. I imagined I was adjusting the temperature of a child. I did nothing like that in my life. I had slept with good women and said things I believed in the rooms where they slept but I never knew how to live close to anyone without turning my face a little. I thought if he was dead he would be pleased with me for coming and if he was alive he would curse me for taking so long. That was the scale I hung between.

There was a bar on the upper deck that had no personality until after midnight and then a woman sang without opening her mouth. Not a singer. A passenger. The song was old. The sort of song my mother hummed when she believed no one listened. The city lights dissolving from shore made me want to swim, but there was a railing between me and the wet and the wind not much stronger than the breath of a lover. We had once stolen a speedboat, our hands shaking with the game, and the motor had died in the deepest middle of the creek where bullrushes rose up like tall men to watch us. He had leaned forward and tapped with a key the tin heart of the engine cover, and it had barked once and turned like an old dog on a rug. Water tall as women broke around our thighs when we pushed the boat to the launch and laughing we dropped to a knee each time the keel hit a rock. We were the comedy of brothers to the men under their hats smoking by their trucks. A comedy that would season into quiet.

The woman with the red scarf sat by herself across the aisle from where I settled with a beer. She had a bag shaped like a doctor’s bag and its leather had crazed into a river delta. The steward had left two padlocks in a little tray near the door for people's lockers and had forgotten them and they lay like miniature animals, sleeping. She lifted an orange from her pocket and rolled it on the table as if she was changing its fate. We were two people swallowed by the same thing. I asked if she would like one of the padlocks and she laughed, her hand on the orange, as if I had asked her to dance.

Where are you going, I asked, because that was the question of the ferry.

To my father, she said, and touched the scarf with two fingers to square it. He is older than anyone I know. He has hands so wrecked by time his mistakes just fall through them. And you?

To write my name next to a body, I said.

She looked at me with a face that had learned squinting outdoors, that had learned breath on a cold day, to say this man is not dangerous but he will be if you enter him. She said, your hands are clean.

It is just cold, I said. But I had washed them as if something would be read under the nails later and I needed to be ready. She peeled the orange without taking off her gloves, working a thumbnail through the white underflesh, and when it broke in half the juice wet her cuff but she did not care. I thought of my brother’s ability to work with his fingers in water, how he could thread a leader with hands numbed by river, kick his boot against a rock, perform whatever tender gesture he had to do on the body of a fish to return it unharmed. He was better as a surgeon with animals than I would be with a child of my own.

When our father left, he left not by a door but by a mistake in our names. He had begun to call us one name, as if we were a single restless thing, and then he had taken the name and handed it back to his father, the soldier with the rifle, and had stepped through the space absence makes. We did not see his leaving except in the small steel dwindling of him on the street as he walked away after arranging for our rent and our winter fuel. A neighbour took him to the station, and my mother watched with the curtain lifted as a cat watches the mouth of a burrow. She never said my father’s name again. When I do, now, there is a taste like coin.

At night my brother and I lay side by side in the living room because the bedrooms smelt of the wrong breath. We had no curtains on the big street window so the lights made a thin river on the ceiling, and the cars passing kept the river alive. He would turn his face toward mine so that our noses almost touched and whisper, as if he were telling me a rumour from a far country, that there was in the world a bird that can fly forever and never land except to breed. I told him he was lying and he told me this was how birds became stars. I did not believe the words but I believed his breath. People who call each other liars are in love.

At the ferry’s stern the white water fell away from what contained it. I took my beer to the deck where one of the lifebuoys whacked gently against the wall, its rope dark and stiff with salt. It had the ship’s name painted on it and the letters dripped a little where a brush had paused. I thought I should remember these letters. The ship had the name of a woman perhaps, though some ships had the names of kings. I leant over the rail as close as I dared to the water and the girl with the orange came and leant beside me. For a while we were those people who lean and do not speak. Her scarf moved like a wing around her chin.

He may not be your brother, she said.

He may not, I said. Or he may be entirely and I have to walk the city for the rest of my life as the brother of a dead man.

She shook the tips of the scarf. I think I have already done that, she said, half laughing. He returned last winter to stay for three days and on the second night he died on the floor near the stove, and we continued to get letters addressed to the very place where he lay, as if the post was a kind of faith. We had to explain to his bank that he was a new kind of presence. I thought of breaking his fingers to release them from their rings and then I thought of marrying him to the ice. This is a long way of saying I recognised him completely because we were not in love.

I looked at her carefully then because I thought she had taken my story and walked with it farther than I could. In our family, too, there was this talent: to speak as if a person had already walked away and one would catch him in a few minutes, easily, argumentative, standing near a fence post, a stone in his hand. My brother talked to me through closed bathroom doors. He sat on floors with a back to the wall and told me why it would be best if I would be what I was not. When he drank he became tender and tired and imagined a war in which nobody got hurt. When sober he was a blade. Boys in our town knew his name and walked in circles around him like hitchhikers waiting for the same truck.

You will sleep, she said to me at last, as if it were a benediction. Your sleep will be good because you have made the decision to come. The hard part was your hand on your doorknob. I congratulate your fingers, she said, smiling into the dark where the sea wrote its cold calligraphy.

I said, if you see me at breakfast, say hello. She patted the railing with her flat gloved palm as if it contained a pet. The wind caught the long tail of her scarf and made the silk purr. She went away, her steps measured for the deck’s pitch. There is the way a person walks when they have arrived somewhere they do not want and the way they walk when they are going to a person in danger—her steps were the second kind.

I slept finally with the book open and the bed turned some degrees into a lake. I woke at three with my fingers clenched. In the upper berth the boy had turned and placed his small foot across the metal rung, as if holding himself to the ladder even in sleep. The humming in the wall had found a frequency like a trickle draining, a long pour of sand into a bottle. I wanted to put my hand through it and close a valve. In my dream the corridor carpet had been a real compass and I had to cross it without being torn by the needles. He waited at the other end of the hallway of course, and of course he was five or six years old, a boy with a wet cowlick and a shirt button missing a partner, and he held a ruler against his chest like a priest’s book. The person he looked like was myself.

There is a part of towns where the wind chooses always to commit. In ours it was the lane between the mill and the freight office. If you pushed a cart there your hat would leave your head and go halfway to the river. The men at the mill wore heavy rings with animal heads and these rings would occasionally catch on a burlap seam and tear it open and their hands would be dragged an inch along canvas in that way that burns. And they would blow on the burn and spit and tie down the commodity with wire and swear and laugh and call each other by single syllables. He loved those men more than he loved his own furniture. He admired their slow genius and the way their shirts became maps of their bodies when they sweated. There was a boy who lost his hand to a machine and learned with the other to write his name backwards. When my brother was fifteen he could lift a cask as if it contained a cloud. The first day he was paid in cash he handed it to our mother, put the money on the table with his palm on it and said, we will pretend the men on the street are just weather. We will forget the names, he said, of the ones who try to make us smaller.

At the morgue I had once identified not a person but a piece of wood that was the core of an antique violin. It was for a friend, a favour. The music school needed to know if the instrument could be saved. It had been dragged from a burned building and was warped and sprung and the name of the maker was erased by soot. I held it gently with both hands as one would carry a rectangular summer bird, and placed it onto a table and some young man wrote down what I thought. He had said to me then, I do not know why we need your name as witness, but we do. And I had written it, slowly. I thought of that now, how names rescue other things. I thought of bringing a piece of wood to a bright room and saying yes, yes, this was loved once.

We slid all night in the belly of the animal that was work. Sleep came and departed, came and departed, like somebody leaning into the frame of a doorway. When I woke for the last time the porter was already fitting mushroom lids to small dishes of butter in the galley. It was the hour before dawn that is not night and not day and the horizon had its black sugar there, and it was a good moment to shave. The mirror over the little sink angled my face into my chest and cut my neck soft as bread. I took my razor out of its case as if I had come to a town and opened a bank book; I put my hand to my chin and closed my eyes. My brother had a scar where sharp ice had caught him under the chin when we were children and he had shot his face forward as if to bite winter. He wore that flaw with cunning so that women asked him tenderly about it by the end of an evening. If the body I had to see had that mark I would know him. If not, I would float in a new lake that had no edges.

On the car deck, the trucks woke like animals. Men pulled down the tongues of the buckles as if they were bells and the flatbeds sang their iron songs. The dog made a soft single bark that was a greeting to the light. I stood with my bag and watched, holding myself still because forward is a kind of prayer. We were damp clothes pulled from a roof. The port arrived the way one wakes in a strange bed: knowing but not recalling. A silo, a red wall of a fish shed, a light rotating sleepily from a low concrete building that had its own humour. Once, a small boy near us had pointed at a tall structure that was obviously a crane and said to his father, look, a giraffe, and I wished then that cranes were called giraffes.

The cop waited at the foot of the gangplank with his hands in his pockets. I liked him at once because his hat did not fit. He stood not in a way arranged for the public but in a way arranged for weather. He had a widow’s peak that gave his forehead a small second roof. He said my name as if tasting it and put out his hand, and when I shook it he let my hand be the one to do the work. He would be embarrassed if I made this story about him, but he would also recognise that I loved him immediately because somebody had to. He said he would drive and he did, and we passed along a street that ended at a shore, and the shore had these small collapsing houses, all of them with woodpiles, and behind the woodpiles were three green chairs and the chairs had stains on the arms from the tea of the men who sat there in the evenings.

We went to the little building behind the health unit. It had two rooms and we were in the first, which had plastic over the couch. The walls were the pale brown of a reproductive system in a textbook. A nurse came with a clipboard and I could not tell her age because there was nothing that age could fix. She moved carefully to take care of the room. She pushed a strand of hair behind her ear and looked up and smiled as if this would make me better. She said it would not be very pleasant. She said the cold had made it better than it might be. She said, we have put him in the room beyond, and that there has been no interference with him. As if he were a crime scene or a shrine.

When we entered, the other room had that smell I can only call clean. It was the smell our mother wanted for our house and that we could never find. The stainless steel fit together with other pieces of stainless steel and the faces of the men who had worn the steel in their work were reflected faintly in it. He lay under a white sheet that had been ironed with a perfect crease and the crease ran down the center of him like a road through a night field. I did not cry immediately because I was not sure. His hair was different and his beard unpracticed and there were small cuts from rock on one cheek and the cold had done its mutinous stuff. The nurse lifted the sheet a little. She said we try not to show too much. The officer said I am very sorry sir.

The greatest problem was the distance a man would have to travel to find his own face. I had known his face in the second of our life when we opened our mouths and made out of a sound a plan. I had known his profile in the morning on a bike, I had known it under a blanket, in a mirror, in a puddle on a road under a gull’s cry and a pine. I had known it angered and sleeping; this thing, here, had become a place I could no longer swim to. I put my hand out and the copper ring had warmed already to my skin and I put my finger to the lower lip of the dead man and felt the texture there, and I cannot tell you whether what I wanted then was for it to be him or for it to not. The officer looked at his boots. The nurse did not hold her breath the way some people do, she did not leave me alone for the temple effect, she existed with me in the room with the machine’s clank and the sheet and the cold air that moved under a door where no door should leak.

There. On his chin. A pale bite of light. I cannot call it anything else. Because where the scar on my brother was, there was a lighter island of skin, and there, on this face, another kind of mark, not the same, not the old country of boyhood but something else, not a coincidence but not a story. My head floated away from me for a second so that the ceiling was my eye. I remembered a man in town who had been dragged on shale in a summer storm and how the scabs, when they came off, left new continents.

There was the hand, too. I asked to see it. It lay on top of the sheet lightly and I did not even recognise the knuckles. The nails were chewed down in a way no love would change. There was blue at the cuticles and the web to thumb had a small triangle cut out, old, healed rough. My brother had a perfect hand and he had done bad things with it and good. There was the smell of the green soap that is always everywhere in rooms like this and it monarchs over everything. When I was a boy I thought if I swallowed a cake of that I would be pure. I thought I would never want to steal again or make my mother cry for the small ferocities. But I became what this boy next to me had become, a man in a cold room touching something and trying to remember what that thing was like in rain.

The policeman cleared his throat not as a signal but because the cold was choosing his breath. He said, take your time, and I said I was. He said he understood. His eyes moved over the tiles and counted their cleanliness. The nurse put her two hands flat on the end of the bed as if steadying a canoe and then she removed them again and ran them down the front of her apron, her fingers making channels there, and then she looked at me and tried to pass some small thing through the air to me, some consolation that could happen without moving.

The brother I had was a story and the story had no end yet. I wanted to look into his ear. I do not know why. I wanted to put my eye near his and see what he saw out of the corner of it. He had a little fold at the top of the ear that made him look like a prince in cartoons. They would not let me turn his head. I bent and saw his mouth and the small scar at the left where a man had once thrown a bottle. I saw the mould of his cheek and the cup of it. I saw the beard that was both him and not. I had once shaved him drunk with a razor and he had sat on a chair with his mouth open, laughing, and I had wiped the lather from his chin with a towel we had no right to own. Later the towel went hard, it could stand by itself, like the wings of a gull.

If I say to you that I wrote his name, now, it would be a lie, but it would not be untrue. I wrote something. I took the pen they handed, a blue ballpoint chained to a clipboard, and my hand found a motion that was his. I wrote half his name and stopped. I said I needed to see what he had in his pockets. The policeman said there was a bag and he had it, and he went and came back with a clear plastic envelope and in it there was a photograph of a bridge I did not know and a scrap of paper with a number I did not recognise and a ticket stub and a small bottle cap flattened by a train. I saw a piece of paper that had a sentence in our mother’s rounded hand, only the last three words visible, and they said: come when ready.

I looked at this and held it in place with my finger in the bag as if I was pressing the last insect of summer.

Where did you find him, I asked.

On the beach near the point, the officer said. A woman with a dog called. The dog had found the sea’s business. He was tangled in the kelp like that. The tide had brought him up and put him down, without religion.

The body had been in the water some days, they said. It is a sentence like a knife with a point made of ice. Some days. Our mother used to say a thing like that too. We will wait some days and see if it returns to us. We will put a saucer of milk and he will come. The policeman did not look at me when he said it. He said, would you like to walk.

We walked out of the health unit and into the light that had taken its time. The air was raw and new-made, it hurt the cartilage of my nose. You move differently on a small town’s sidewalk because the cracks feel closer to your fingers. There was something about the light off the water that filled the backs of buildings and made their windows valuable. We walked to the shore. The wind came from the strait as if nothing would ever age. I bent at the tide line and picked up a pebble and then another and put them in my coat pocket like the coins of a country that had burned. The policeman waited for me again in that slow way that made him beautiful to me, because he did not come close to me and he did not move away.

I told him then some of the simple story. I said that my brother and I had not spoken in those years that could be called long or short, depending on love. That he was a man who lied, but not for profit. That he could forgive quickly and announce his forgiveness like a drink order. That sometimes he would call me by a family name we did not own, as if auditioning us for a different fate. That he left our town the first time walking, the second time on a flatbed, the third time with a girl who resembled a fawn. That I had expected something like this with the patience one has for certain kinds of weather. I did not say the other thing, the thing that would have given me away, that he was my father also, he would have been my son if it would have saved him, and that now when I stood in front of the ocean my face had become an example of what the weather can do to stone.

He said to me, I would like to show you something. Not there, he said, at the building, but here. He led me to the point where the willow logs pile like whales and he pointed with his chin to a place a few yards out where two rocks tilted toward each other to form a small doorway under the water. This is where he was jammed. He would not have left that without help. I said, did you bring the help. He said, we brought a rope and a hook and a boy with perfect lungs and there was a brief perfect silence in the way he said this.

The girl with the scarf was there on the shore walking her father who moved by the shoulder of a granddaughter who could have been her child. They nodded to me as if we had slept in a conversation and would resume it in the next town. They moved slowly, three ages of one person, toward the fish mart where they would buy something with bones. I watched them a long while with the intention of learning how to live like that, with a hand on a wool coat and a child’s head in the weather and a body in a cold room that obeyed order and the words we had not said.

There is a scrape on the sill of my little house from a night when, angry, my brother lifted his heel there and climbed through my window and into my kitchen because I would not come to the door. It is still there, the scratch in the paint. When I go home I will put my thumb into it and feel where the night and the leather and his will met. We were beautiful once when we were not observed. We were the two boys who found a deer in the snow and lifted it into a cart and took it to where a barn was and fed it whatever we had and did not tell anyone even when it stood. We lied to save it in our minds and then we did not save it. It died on a Saturday, on the edge of ordinary time.

I could have said to the policeman, it is him. He would have put his hand on my shoulder and it would have meant something. I could have said it is not, and he would have put his hand on my shoulder and it would have meant something too. He did not want to know yet which hand to use. He waited. The wind came steady as a system. I wanted to lie down in kelp and be held by it the way it holds stones in its gorgeous leather thongs. The gull made the sound gulls make in their throat when their beak is closed. The grey of the day was pleasant and the small shop opened and a bell chimed and someone laughed and turned the sign and someone set a pot of water to boil in a kitchen three houses back and it was an ordinary morning here and a miracle to me.

I put the stones I had picked up into his palm and he closed his fist and then opened it and handed them back and said, these are yours. I put them into my pocket and they were our father’s cufflinks and they were the eyes of our mother, they were the thimbles in which we kept all our rain. When I went back to the building I wrote my surname where it asked, and wrote a line through the space where there was the given name because I would not give him up, not to anything, not to truth and not to a body. The nurse took the board and looked at me and decided I would need someone to bring me a sandwich.

On the shore there was a little girl who handed me a yellow object and I took it because everything felt permitted. It was a toy, a plastic fish with its mouth open in a perfect circle, its body shaped too fat to be any fish. She said, this is mine. She smiled with her two front teeth missing and she looked up at me as if my head were a branch. I handed it back to her and said, I know. She said, I know too. She was loved so much by the weather that she could say anything.

The coroner was a small man with a squint. He came quietly into the room where we sat now, into the first room with the couch made of plastic. He said, we have ways. He said, there will be other identifications possible. He kept his hands at his sides like a stout dancer. We will do what we can to give you a certainty, he said, as if certainty were a jacket too large but we would tailor it. I could not say yes or no. I could not be the hand at the name. I told him, let me walk once more to the pier, and he said of course, and then he put his hand out so that my coat would come with me like a shadow.

At the pier the ferry had turned with the tide. A new long line of men and dogs stood in the rope’s corridor and there was a truck with apples and another with sand and a little van with the words on its side, Church Action. The cards in their wallets to prove they crossed all the time were at their fingertips like gun licences. The gull made a cry and tossed a shell and it broke and he ate the meat inside as if it were the first time and he had invented it. I remembered then the night we had not spoken. He had said to me in my kitchen, will you let me take her to the hospital, and I had said no because she was already gone. He had thrown his glass then at the floor and it had not broken and he had laughed as if I had given him a toy. He had gone out the window rather than the door. He had left his smell the way a man leaves a coat when he thinks he will return. He did not return to that kitchen in the way any of us mean, but he was there and is there still because I set things in the way my brother set things, because I become him in the way you take off your shirt and find some old smell still in it.

In my bag I had, under the map and the book and the lighter, a small folded piece of paper with my mother’s hand on it: Come when ready. I took it out now and made the fold smoother. I wrote on the white back in the pencil I had—Tom—and I wrote it as if my hand belonged to a boy learning to own a sound. The policeman read it with me, not touching it. Then he turned away and looked out at the thick water. He was a man who knew the difference between kindness and intrusion.

If this were a better story I would tell you he appeared walking around the corner of a building into the blue of the day, his mouth burnt with wind and his hair salted and his laugh like a seabird and his life still wanting. If this were a cleaner world I would say the dead are never our own. The small boy in the upper bunk on the ferry, the woman with the scarf, the nurse’s fingers, the cop’s hat—these are the town I have now and he is a house in it that nobody pays to heat. The body on the table had a scar where a man might catch himself on rock. It had a line of lighter skin that might have been the old country at the throat. It had a mark on the lip like our broken glass. It had the clean smell of pretending.

There are places on the island where in summer the light does not fully leave and at four in the morning a fox will sit and watch a woman bring milk to her mother and not move except to lift one paw and put it down again. In winter the snow makes the shore into a new shore. I had arrived at the place where I could say these things and they would not ask me to give proof. The girl and her father and the old man walked away with their parcel of fish. The church van made a soft turn and entered the road. The policeman stood and put his hands in his pockets but not because he was cold and looked at his boots because he loved them the way a man loves his first true shoes.

I went back and wrote nothing more. I stood and watched the light in the other room change as a cloud moved across something I could not see. The nurse came with a sandwich and I broke it in half and kept the smaller portion for myself, gave the larger to the policeman who said you shouldn’t but took it and ate it with care, the way a man makes peace with new bread. The coroner touched my arm once and it was like being named. At ten the sun was already a low coin again and the tide had turned as if it were a page and we read the other side, which was also our own.

This is how I left the room: I put my palm briefly to the white sheet at the height of his chest. I did not push or drum a rhythm or beat a love. On the white of linen my hand’s heat made a brief shadow that will not be in any record. I took the bag with our mother’s hand in it. I took the map that had turned to toffee in my fingers. The copper ring shone as if polished by the salt from my skin. Beyond the door the world made a small harmless noise with its throat, as if clearing it for a song we both disliked but knew by heart. The strait was there, the ferry was a large breathing thing that leaves and returns without failing and where men and women walk in small ropes of themselves, and someone on that ship right now is practising how to press a coin along a rail and not let it fall. I went with the officer toward the pier and the gull was there and the winter wind was a clean language and I understood nothing and I understood enough.
