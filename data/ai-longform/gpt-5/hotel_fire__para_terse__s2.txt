I arrived at the rebuilt hotel on a Thursday, under a sky so rinsed by wind it seemed newly laundered, and stood for a moment on the sidewalk to find, in the sweep of glass and pale limestone, any trace of what I had known of it from the inside out. A bellman in a neat charcoal coat opened the new revolving door with a gloved hand, and the first thing that came to me as I stepped into the lobby was not the memory of smoke, as I had rehearsed it might be, but the scent of something green diffused into the air by hidden ducts—basil or crushed mint—set against the inoffensive smell of carpet glue and orchids.

It was called the same thing, The Tremont, like a word someone had pinned back on after laundering. Where the old place had been a grand dowager with heavy chandeliers and brass that had tarnished in the joints, this one was all light and sheen. The floor under my shoes gave a crisp sound, and the ceiling—high, white, ribbed like a shell—reflected enough glow back at me that I could see my hair gathered in its unfamiliar twist and the faint, pale seam that ran from my wrist up under the cuff of my jacket. The name had been saved; everything else had been rewritten, line by line, until it was possible to pretend the draft had always been like this.

At the front desk a young woman with stiff-shouldered poise and lipstick the color of first cherries looked up and smiled. She said my cousin’s name before mine, and I felt the warmth of that old inversion—the bride first, as though she were a room and I a person only by proximity. A little white sign on the marble said Rodriguez-Mosley Wedding—Welcome! in a font that was curly without going silly. When I gave my own name and fished in my bag for my reservation email like a talisman, the clerk found me with the pads of her fingers touching a screen. “You’re with Ms. Rodriguez’s party,” she said, as if to contain me in a single word, and I nodded and lifted my bag closer to my side.

“We’ve placed the block on floors fourteen through seventeen,” she said, “and I have you on seventeen facing the river.”

I felt my fingers move before my mind caught up to them. I had been practicing this part, too, but my voice surprised me, calm at the edges and moving forward on its own. “Would it be possible to have something on a lower floor?” I could hear my own heart beating in my ears—not hard, just present. “I don’t mind the view.”

She looked at me with that practiced expression that is both listening and not listening until the listening is specifically called for. “We can see,” she said, and her hands went to work on the screen. “We’re very full with the wedding.” Out of sight under the desk, something clicked—old habit made me picture it as a printer taking a small breath. “Fourth floor?” she said in a minute. “Twelve, facing the atrium.”

“Yes,” I said with more gratitude than the change warranted. “Thank you.” I didn’t explain why. The words for what I had known had never arranged themselves into sentences a clerk behind a desk would need to hear, and in any case I could feel the subtle current of waiting behind me—an older couple with the map of the city folded like a napkin in the man’s jacket pocket, a woman in sandals with two kids who had turned the bell stand into a game of hide-and-seek already.

She slid me the narrow packet with the keys and a paper in branded gold with the Wi-Fi password typed in a font that mimicked handwriting. “If there’s anything else you need, Ms. Ochoa,” she said, and I tucked the keys into the inside pocket of my jacket the way you keep a thing that once saved you the trouble of fumbling in the dark.

The lobby had been laid out with the sort of discretion that hides itself—low couches in cool velvets that made your knees angle elegantly, lamps that put their light where eyes wanted to rest, staff gliding through it all with black coffee and polished steel tongs. In one corner near the elevators a glass case showed the history of the hotel in the way museums show wars, with a gentle voice and careful spacing: framed photographs of the original opening—women in gloves on the steps, hats tilted like flags—a newspaper broadsheet turned so an article that said BLAZE DOWNTOWN was visible as a handsome set of serifs; a render of the new lobby flanking a photograph of the old one from a similar angle. Under the glass was a list in small black letters. The light in the case was clean, no shadow, and when I leaned slightly to read the names my own reflection fell across the glass like a ghost of whoever might yet come to claim them. There they were—Bell Captain Luis Ramirez, Room Attendant Nadia Kovac, Guest Services Agent Patrice L.—the rest truncated by the angle of the mount. I had to shift my foot to see the lower line, the way one shifts weight to get a ribbon to lay against the back of a chair. Eleven names. I read them quietly to myself as if rehearsing for an oral exam. It was a list you would pass on your way to an elevator and never read again. People paused and said, “Isn’t that terrible?” and went on with their days. It was what it was meant to be: acknowledgement printed on museum glass, eliding the grubbier details of who had thrown a wet towel under a door and who had broken a window with a chair leg and who had died in a service corridor that turned out to be a dead end for those who had known it too well.

I stepped away before the urge to press my palm to the case made me ridiculous. I had not come to rub my face on the past like a cat. I had come because my cousin Daniela was getting married and this was the place she wanted and because she had called me three months ago, breathless with the vision of it—the high chandeliers and the river and the little string quartet she’d found that could play both Bach and Beyoncé—and had said into the phone, “You’ll be there, right?” as if I still needed to be asked in the way she had asked me to a sleepover when we were eleven.

The elevator was as smooth as a slipped glove, its walls panelled in a pale, dense wood that gave back ghostly versions of whoever faced me. There were people in it with me: two groomsmen smelling faintly of aftershave already wearing their suits with a slight awkwardness and a tired-looking woman with a baby in a fabric sling who stood with the fatigue of new duty and a small poppyseed of pride. The old number panel was gone. The new one was a mirror of brushed metal with flat dots that lit up white when pressed. The little screen above the door showed us moving floor by floor, a small elevator icon climbing with the implacable impatience of anything that knows where it is going.

I stood near the doors with my overnight bag snug against my leg and kept my eyes open—do not blink, do not let your head drop, do not let the breath climb into your collar and wire itself there; these were the small rules I had made for elevators in the intervening years. I watched the numbers notch upward—2, 3, 4—and stepped out when the doors sighed like a set of lungs exhaling. The carpet softened footfall; it had a pattern that looked like mangrove roots in a remembered tide. The hall was clean in a way that made the part of me that had pushed a cart and a vacuum and a spray bottle of vinegar and water feel both satisfied and cynical—it would be handed back to the morning with boot prints of weddings and strings of hair stuck to the base of nightstands and the small, mean ways people leave behind proof of their appetites. My room was down the hall on the right; I counted doors without meaning to, one, two, three, four, the habit nested so deep it moved in me like a tendon twitching. My key opened the magnetic lock on the second try after a small polite refusal.

The room faced the newly minted atrium, a plane of light and white structure that climbed like ribs to glass. My view was of other rooms and balconies across; somewhere above, light pooled itself like soup in a white bowl. The bed was made in the new hotel way, a clean slip of duvet on too many pillows, the corners tucked just so—yes, triple-sheeted, the top flat sheet folded back over the duvet then another sheet placing a layer between the guest and the cover, a ritual adusted to the expectation that guests prefer to sleep under sheets rather than duvets, and also to the reality that duvets are more difficult to launder as often as they should be. I ran a hand along the edge without thinking and felt the tuck for evenness. There are only a handful of ways to make up a bed to fool a stranger into feeling safe, and this was one of them.

I laid my bag on the luggage rack, which still had the satisfying spring of thick straps latched at precise intervals, and stood a minute taking my own measure. The spray of the bathroom was in sweet little bottles with promises printed in green; the towels were bright and thick; there was a card in the corner that said, for the earth, we offer you the choice to decline new linens each day. The sprinkler head looked like a tiny metal flower set into the ceiling, the heat-sensitive element a small glass bulb filled with a jewel-red liquid. You could fit that whole saving mechanism between two fingers. There was an evacuation map on the back of the door, fresh with printing and arrows. I went to it without embarrassment, counted the number of doors between me and the stairs (four to the left, six to the right), traced the geometry like a shape I would have to draw freehand later. I set the iron with its coiled black cord on the dresser the way you set something down that will not be used but which implies care, additional work laid upon someone who has learned to turn an appliance so that it coils neatly, the way the cheap little bag for the hairdryer never quite folded flat into the drawer again after the fourth or fifth guest.

I sat on the edge of the bed and then lay back with my shoes still on, not with the relief of arrival but with the ache of arrival's small lies. I could have told Daniela no. I had turned down other weddings in other cities, inventing reasons that were not entirely reasons. But when she called, I heard under her voice the sound of old sleepovers, of her rolling over to whisper some nonsense about who in the class had finally kissed whom and whether Mrs. Peale colored her hair and if we would always live within blocks of each other. She, too, had been there that winter, at the hospital, when I still smelled like the inside of an oven no one had cleaned in a decade and spilled broth on my own chest because I could not keep the tremor of my hands tamped down. She had sat on the linoleum with me and spooned me applesauce and told me in a voice she had borrowed from her mother that I would get through it and that everybody was bringing casseroles by the bagful. It seemed the smallest thing to show up in a dress for her when her voice on the phone had held the new shape a person takes when the future is about to rearrange them.

When I got up and went back down later, the lobby had put on a new coat of bustle. A cluster of people I recognized as relatives by the complicated tilt of their noses and the way they claimed space with their elbows had gathered at the bar. My aunt Meche, who had not approved of white wine at the rehearsal dinner but had been out-voted, was explaining the superiority of a well-made gin and tonic to a bartenders while the bartenders looked patient and deeply not interested; my mother had found a corner and was discussing with two of the groom's relatives whether salt or sugar on the rim of a margarita was a matter of morality or geography. Someone—my cousin Ana—saw me and launched herself into my orbit with the force of a small planet. “Marta,” she said, pulling me into the scent-cloud of hairspray and citrus that seemed to coat everyone in our family when we were happy. “We thought you weren’t going to come until tomorrow.” Her smile had that manic brightness of a person balancing five worries like plates, none of which you are ready to acknowledge by name.

“I wanted to check in today,” I said, which was the truth. It would have been too much, in the odd five o’clocks that weddings create, to be thrown into everything all at once. She touched my elbow briefly and then released me back into the undertow of family. Daniela, somewhere across the bar, lifted her champagne glass to me as a kind of semaphore, eye contact turning a moment private and trumpeting it all at once. She had always been beautiful in an uncooperative way, like a cat that insists on putting its best expression into a clumsy plastic bowl, and now that beauty had ripened into something steadier. Tomorrow she would sit in a white dress and play a role that required posture and grace; now she was wheeling through clusters of family like a juggler threading knives in the whetstone light.

I drifted to the edge of the bustle, where the carpet met polished stone and the staff moved in that proprietary quiet I remembered being asked to learn. A man in a black suit just a shade too shiny to be good stood with a tablet and a headset and spoke in that midnight voice into his microphone—“Copy, I need them at riser three by six, and service wants to know about the dairy. I'll confirm the rave light for nine.” The waiters at the kitchen doors had assembled like a small army in pressed whites; a woman with a pocketful of pens gave them last-minute instruction with the patience of a person who has learned to repeat the same clear direction until the world bends to it.

I edged near the hallway that led to the ballrooms and from there I could see the Staff Only door, the gray paint on it unremarkable as a napkin. It made me flinch the way people sometimes do when something slips into view that looks like an ordinary household object but is actually an old scar. If I had pushed it, I would have found a set of hallways that belonged to the hotel the way its veins belong to its body, flat and quiet and slightly too cold; there I would have found the hum of refrigeration and the peculiar tang of warm steel, the long mirrors at the ends of hallways that let you see around corners, the racks where we once hung our black cardigans on identical plastic hangers and wrapped our meals in plastic taken from the roll that hung beside the industrial microwave. The door looked like every other service door in every other hotel; it was what lay behind it that I would know with the soles of my feet and the bones in my ankle. I stood there and did not touch it, as if I stood on the shore and did not test the temperature of the water.

Someone brushed my arm and I started. It was a server moving past with a swallow of plates balanced on her forearm and the heel of her other hand. She was maybe twenty-two, her hair braided tight and pinned up, eyes focused ahead with all the intensity of a person keeping ten small things in view and six in reserve just in case. “Sorry,” she said, and I heard in the music of her apology the accent I had once brought with me to this city, softened only enough not to make your tongue ache at the end of the night. Without thinking I moved sideways to make her path cleaner; she gave me a little thanks that touched the corner of her mouth but not her eyes, so a co-worker emerging from a side hall would know that her gratitude had been expressed but that she was not available to waste a second of the coordination of these plates and this door and a floor slightly too soft under the thick carpet. She was paid the insult of invisibility in exchange for carrying other people’s celebrations on her forearm. I wanted to catch her sleeve and say thank you in a language she’d feel inside her bones, not because of what she did for me but because I understood what she had already told the joints of her second finger about balancing dinner knives and what she had told the nicked skin of her thumb about opening a thousand bottles of beer.

The rehearsal dinner gathered itself in a small ballroom nearer the river. The ceiling there was lower and the chairs—white Chivari chair backs lined in rows like bridesmaid spines—had been arranged carefully so that everyone would feel at once both seen and contained. There were toasts. Daniela's father stood at the end of the room and made the gestures he has made his whole life—opening his arms to his own metaphors, holding in the air the imaginary shape of what he wanted to tell her. He was not a public speaker by profession, but he had the authority of love and that made his voice larger than it might otherwise have been. I found a seat near the wall, my back to plaster, my face to whatever future the speeches would project. They spoke of journeys taken together and journeys begun, of love’s work and of the steadying work of family. Someone—cousin Arturo—used the word fire three times in a row to describe desire and I did not breathe for two of them. When the speeches moved to blessings, the aunts said their pieces and the children were coaxed to lift their plastic tumblers with earnestness. The hotel staff slid in and out between it all like fish through a coral reef, and the only detail that snagged was the chime of the service elevator at the far end of the hall, a sound that my body answered with a small, involuntary movement of my scalp, as if someone had said my name in a voice meant only for me.

When I got back to my room after the dishes had been cleared and my relatives had dispersed into small knots of plotting for tomorrow—hair, make-up, flowers, shoes—and my mother had kissed my cheek and said something in Spanish about the way being at the center of a day this large can change a woman's blood, I took off my shoes and lined them by the wall as if I were still sharing a room with another maid. My feet had swelled under the least of the day’s walking as if protesting gently in advance against tomorrow's invented poses. I took the pen from the desk—thick, heavy, black—and a small piece of hotel stationery and wrote two lines in a hand that came reluctantly, as if pulled out of a corner with a broom. Thank you for your work. From someone who knows the corners. I folded the little piece of paper and tucked it under the glass on the desk where a housekeeper would find it tomorrow, and slid thirty dollars under it to make the thanks feel like more than a prayer. I looked at the bed and at the sprinkler head and at the evacuation map again, and the memory that had politely waited at the edge of the day came in.

It was less a single picture than a smell. The air had been busy with it—in the room that had been 927 and in the hallway and in the service corridor that broke your sense of direction and wiped down every sign of human habit twice over. The smoke didn’t behave itself like in the pamphlets where it is a gray thing you can see and avoid. It had a thickness to it, like a pudding of burnt plastic and chemical and meat. I had been in 927 on my knees, tucking the final corner of the bottom sheet that would hold the mattress in a passable embrace against the onslaught of a new guest’s skin dragging across it, and I had thought: Someone is burning toast. It had been instinct to go to the thermostat to turn off the oppressive air so the smell wouldn’t settle in the curtains, which was why the alarm was only the second thing I noticed. The noise had started small, a polite note in the hallway, and then, like everything polite, it had become infuriating. In the bathroom the exhaust had rattled like a bug caught in a bottle. I had done what training and gut taught me: I went to the door, placed the back of my hand on the handle—it was warm, not hot, a temperature that, even at that moment and forever after, annoyed me with its ambiguity—and then I took the flat white top sheet off the bed and wet it in the bathtub until it gave that sudden heavy shirking sound that a cloth makes when it can no longer pretend to be dry. I had put the wet thing over my shoulders and wrapped it around my nose and mouth and opened the door.

The smoke was not a thing by then but a fact. The hallway’s lights had gone thick, people moving in it had become sudden disembodied parts—an elbow, a knee, hair. I had stepped through it and gone left. Why left? Because I had counted doors a hundred times and knew that left was closer to the stairs and that right would lead me to the ice machine and the water fountain, and because my keys were in the pocket of my cardigan and a person’s hand went there of its own accord when everything else on their body was panicking. I had knocked on 929 and 931 out of habit that had been born of a drill, calling housekeeping and then, louder, as if I were someone else’s emergency, fire. From 933 someone said, “What?” as if incredulous at the concept, and then their door opened and the smoke put itself inside their room like a person who simply belonged there. I yelled “stairs!” to them as if the word held a rope they could hold on to. I could not tell you what any of their faces looked like—my own heart was pounding in my ears harder than the alarm, more insistent, like a child tugging at your sleeve. Somewhere in that corridor a man said, lose the elevator, which at that moment sounded like so helpful a thing to say you wanted to punch him for it. Mr. Segal from 938, who kept his shoes by the side of the bed like the old soldier he had once been, had his hand on my elbow, and we moved toward something that was not on fire more than we moved away from something that was. The smoke alarm had turned from a shriek to a warble. Nadia was there, hand holding the sleeve of her cardigan to her mouth, eyes glossy as a seal’s, saying, “Here, come. Marta, you, I knock and you lead, yes?” Her voice had become that precise sleeve of language she wore when she was concentrating. I pointed her to the even numbers while I pounded on the odds. We went like that three doors, four, then the run became ragged.

I had stopped to go back for a pair of blue slippers that belonged to a woman I only knew as Mrs. D, who called me mija as if I were a loan from her own family. She had appeared in her doorway in a nightgown embroidered like an old blouse, eyes confused, and said, “But my slippers,” and because I was not as awake as I was trained to be and because I was that person by nature who cannot stand the thought of someone’s feet on a hallway’s carpet where God knows what had lived, I had said, “Yes,” and stooped, and in that stoop I could feel the heat pile itself above my head. “Leave them,” Nadia had said in a voice that could cut rope, but I stood and put the slippers into Mrs. D’s hands anyway, and by then the corridor had filled. We moved, the three of us, into a dampness of towels and breath and toward the red EXIT sign that, for once, meant what it said to my body. A man with only one shoe held out a hand; a woman with hair in pink rollers swore in Portuguese that I recognized from an earlier conversation about baked fish; Mr. Segal said, “Hold hands; it’ll be like grade school,” and I hated him for it and clung to his words at the same time. In the stairwell the air was clearer; the smoke seemed to be pouring up instead of down, a list of sensations that my body could not understand but had to move through. Nadia, who had gone back—had she?—for the next cluster of doors, was gone. It was not that she disappeared; it was that I turned around and she was not at my shoulder. If I had been writing this as a story in school and you had handed it back, you would have put a big circle around that soundless disappearance and written: Too convenient. But that is how people leave in fog and in grief and in fire. You say, wait, I forgot the— and the second sentence does not ever finish.

We crept down. At the third floor the door was blocked; at the second it was open; at the landing a firefighter in too much gear for any human weather said to us through his mask, “Here we go; you’re okay,” and I hated him for that, too, the way you hate someone for predicting your future when a second before you were sure you did not have one. By the time we got outside, someone had draped a blanket over my shoulders that smelled like other people’s emergencies and wrapped me in it so tight it made me want to thrash. There were other blankets and other cameras and the instant permission granted to us by calamity to speak in the vivid present tense. I thought, wildly, of the sheets upstairs that would be wrinkled now, that I would not be able to tuck back into chastity when we were allowed to go back and make it all sensible. I refused an oxygen mask, took it, then refused it again; the motions of those minutes were not in my power. Later, I would wash my hair four times and still smell acrid-bitter every time my head got warm. Nadia. They never found her in a place with narrative neatness. It was not even the smoke and fire that got her; it was a collapsed ceiling in the service passage next to the laundry room, an ordinary thing failing in a peculiar way. Those words—service passage—still sounded to me most like a translation gone wrong, when what it had been was the gut of a building I had learned as if I were a surgeon of the mundane. I had stayed home from the memorial service the hotel later held for employees and had sent flowers coded with meaning—rosemary for remembrance, blue cornflowers because they were as close as the florist could get to the eyes she’d had—but my absence had been read with a meanness I learned long after. I did not care. The one time I visited Nadia’s mother in the tiny apartment she’d moved to, where the kitchen and living room were the same slender thought divided by a cardboard bookcase, she had not cried. She had given me tea with lemon, which I did not drink because the burned taste that had never really left me made it taste like a metal coin.

Memory sat up in me and looked slowly around and then lay down like a cat in a new patch of sun. I let it have that and then went to the window that would not open more than a careful inch for safety reasons and breathed the hotel air that reached me softened by its own filters. Down in the atrium a woman in flats with a clipboard was pointing at a floral arrangement and adjusting it with two fingers as if she were a concert violinist tuning an instrument brought to her at the last minute. The hotel made its own oxygen with motion and with diligence. I lay down fully and then, like someone shaking out a blanket to rid it of crumbs, I gave sleep a chance.

It found me and then dropped me back at three in the morning like a toy it had lost interest in. The ceiling in the dark had the sleepy shape of ocean; somewhere a pump sighed. I watched the green LED on the thermostat make its small pulse and decided deliberately not to get up to check that the chain was caught. Morning came as a pale shoulder against my cheek, then in a drift of coffee smell long before the machine on the desk clicked itself alive. I got up and used the small packets in the room to make coffee that tasted exactly like every other coffee ever made in a hotel room—thin, bitter, satisfyingly predictable—drank half of it while sitting on the floor with my back against the dresser, then went downstairs to the lobby to let someone else hand me back a cup.

In the coffee shop tucked behind a tall column that hid the restrooms from the lounge chairs, a barista with a hairnet that gleamed like oil poured me something frothy enough to qualify as cloud. She had a scar along her jaw from some long-ago fight or fall, and when I caught myself staring she lifted her chin infinitesimally like a person who has never been offered pity without interest attached and will accept neither. Breakfast service for the wedding party fed an orchestra of women in curlers and one or two men who had slid from their usual selves into jackets more structured than they were used to wearing. In the glass of the case near the registers there were small lemon tarts and those square-cut brownies that look better than they taste; a child leaned sideways to press a hand onto the pastry case and his mother scolded him softly in a language I thought was Tagalog. I took my coffee and stood where the stream of people thinned, near a pylon of florist boxes. A housekeeper pushing a cart passed me and without thinking I put out my hand to steady her progress over the lip of the floor where stone met carpet. She looked up and smiled—a quick flash, the kind you keep because otherwise you will be taken for granted until you are dust.

“Thank you,” she said. Her accent sat in her mouth in a way I ached to name and then let go of, like a boat you want to tether and instead allow to rock og its tether because you do not own it.

“De nada,” I said automatically, though I heard in her voice that she would have said it a different way. I do not know why I wanted her to know me then, but I did. What would I have said? I used to be you? That is not how the sentence works. You cannot be someone the way you are a chair one day and later a branch. You can be alongside, inside, slightly askew. I lifted my coffee slightly in a cheers that was not a toast and she nodded and moved on, the little bell on her cart making that particular peal that says I am here and does not apologize for it.

I got dressed for the wedding in a dress I had bought after trying on five others under the eye of a salesgirl who had assured me, wrongly, that the third one did not show the delicate line that ran up from my wrist under its sleeve. This one was simple and the color of shallow ocean. When I pinned my hair the second time—the first twist having slid into nonsense while I was trying to coax a bobby pin into a patch of hair that resisted it—I saw my face in the mirror as if it belonged to a stranger I had been asked to comfort. I put my hand against the glass, palm to cold, then took it away because it left a print.

The ceremony was in the big ballroom that had been nothing to me once but twenty thousand square feet of vacuuming with the cord flung over the opposite shoulder so you didn’t track it underneath. Now it was white and mother-of-pearl and the florist’s idea of how a forest might look if it had been asked to dress for a gala. The string quartet flickered in its little circle of chairs near the altar; each young woman’s back had the exact posture I had been lectured into as a child and never quite achieved. The guests sat in their protocols of closeness and distance. I found my place not by a name card—this was not that kind of wedding—but by the logic of who will be comfortable next to whom, the calculus of aunts. Daniela came in on her father’s arm—the ribbon of her hair pinned into a low knot, a veil that had been my aunt’s and now was made her own by a loop of blue thread sewn into its seam. Her face did what it was supposed to: it lowered and lifted and smiled. The groom, whose name had never been the problem because he wore his kindness so like a jacket even his enemies would have had to admit he looked good in it, looked as if he were both very brave and very much about to cry.

The officiant—a woman with hands as compelling as a magician’s—began. She spoke of what it is to bind yourself to someone in the presence of your people, of how vows are less about prediction than about permission, that we let ourselves be known in the way that allows for tending. She did not say fire. The word she used was light. I breathed. The couple spoke and mewled out their rings when fingers swelled under public pressure. There was that moment at the end before the kiss when everyone leans forward, not to see the kiss but to be kissed by being near it, and then the privacy of them was lifted up and spread over us and we clapped because our hands needed occupation.

In the small spaces between the ceremony and whatever the hotel called what came next, my mind slid its fingers into the service corridor and felt around. The air back there, if I let myself think of it, had a plushness of its own, a hum of freezers and line cooks and rolling racks and the small clink of a bus tub set down on tile. I wandered that way without telling my feet we were going. If I had had to justify it to myself, I would have said I wanted to find a bit of quiet before the noise of the band rose; I would not have said: I wanted to set my body near the ghost of knowing.

The Staff Only door opened because a busser came through it with a tray of empty flutes; he held it with his hip and his knee and his left hand and so placed his right for a second on the upper bit of the door by mistake, leaving a palm print there in grease and glass. I went through the space he made as if he had been holding it for me. The hallway beyond was safer than any street—lined in white-painted cinderblock, the floor a tile you couldn’t grin on your belly over even if you wanted to because there was always a breeze from a fan or the refrigerator units and the air was edged with lemon and coffee and the hidden slap and slide of dishwashing. A woman stood at a computer and typed something slowly with two fingers, her hair in a neat bun, the pocket on her hip loaded with folded slips she could distribute like benevolences. The service elevator stood with its mouth closed. There were grease pencil marks on its frame—numbers and a letter, someone’s reminder of a delivery expected tomorrow at six a.m. My body remembered the sway of the service car, the way your knee took on a particular stance so that if the rails caught you would not pitch forward into someone’s bosom. I stood there and let the gooseflesh come and go along my neck. A line cook passed with a carton of milk and did not look at me; a busser whirled by with a tray above his head like a dancer with a shield and said “Door” so the person ahead of him would make space.

“You lost?” someone said, and I turned. He was a potwasher, a boy a man a person whose body had that watery unsettled look of late adolescence and exhaustion. His apron was marked with a half-moon of bleach, his hair flattened with steam. His smile did what mine had once done to strangers when I asked them if they needed anything—made a small coin drop in the jar without the sound of change.

“A little,” I said, truth dusted with joke. “I was looking for the bathroom and came the wrong way.”

“Guest elevators that way,” he said, pointing with his elbow in the careful way of someone who cannot or does not want to move the hand that holds weight. He smiled again, weary genial. We stood a second in the complicated kinship of people on opposite sides of a door.

“Thanks,” I said. “You have my gratitude,” I added softly, and he looked at me quickly as if I had said the name of someone he knew and then looked down because the cup of his hands was already full.

I went back through the door into the public face of the world. The cocktail hour had assembled and found its level—a burble of conversation, a small child making a game of scooting under the skirt of a table and popping out like a rabbit, a woman in blue with a laugh like broken glass who seemed to know everyone. In a corner an older man I didn’t recognize but who had the Rodriguez mouth ate a skewer of something as if he had been starving since breakfast. Wait staff pirouetted with balanced plates. I took a bite of something that was more architecture than food—cucumber shaved so thin you could see the light through it, goat cheese resting on it like a coin—and I accepted a glass of something that fizzed and sat cold on my tongue. I had never learned to drink with ease, but the cool slid across the irritations in my throat and made a map of them.

Dinner had speeches the way a field has birds that lift and circle; we ate and then we were asked to clink our glasses with knives to call down the old magic, and the hotel’s new springs answered. At our table we argued gently about the best weddings we had been to and whether money made them better or just gaudier. My mother said, “Yes,” in a way that made everyone laugh because it allowed us to be superior and forgiving at the same time. I slipped away once and found the emergency stairwell and pushed its door open enough to let in the smell of concrete. I went down two flights and sat on the landing with my back against a cinderblock wall that had been painted over so many times in hospital white that its bumps had smoothed. The light in there was not for people; it was for safety. Somewhere above me the band played a song that had been popular in my twenties that I had danced to with my arms up like orantes; below me the pipes of the building made their own small choir.

“I don’t know if you ever forgave me the slippers,” I said out loud into that hard space. It was a ridiculous sentence and I said it anyway. “I don’t know if it mattered,” I said, hand palm up on my knee as if I were asking for something you could pour there. “If you come, come just far enough to have some cake,” I said more quietly then and felt a small, mean sob wrestle the cords in my throat. It did not make it to air. I could smell lemon in the stairwell—not cake, but cleaner. The building’s bones held me. When I closed my eyes Nadia’s head scarf moved the way it used to move in the breeze off the fake courtyard when we leaned against the wall and were quiet for six minutes because we had decided together not to use the break room. “You would have loved that florist bossing everybody,” I said. “She had the right amount of audacity.”

Then I got up and went back into the evening because that is what evenings require. On my way back to the ballroom I passed the glass case again. A small group of guests had stopped to read it as if they were paying the bill. A woman with hair woven into a complicated crown of braids said, “So sad,” and the man with her said, “Yeah. Do you think it was like…was it negligence?” They did not say it loudly, but they did not whisper, and something in that normalcy, that appetite for stories that lay just under the human tongue like sugar, made me feel both tender and tired. The case did its job. It was a lid and a window both. I pressed my thumb to the cool glass near the bottom where the names ended and lifted it off before it could make a smudge someone would need to polish later.

The band came on in its second gear; the DJ found the exact volume at which old people can still talk if they are determined and young people can pretend no one else is there. I danced because my aunt made me and then because it was easier to move myself into the logic of drums than to stand still. An uncle with elbows marvelously unafraid of space twirled me and I allowed him to. My cousin Francisco, drunk on family and beer, kissed me on the cheek with the enormous innocence of a man who loved his entire clan as an idea. Daniela, freed into being a body not just a bride, danced with her hips the way they had always moved, a little faster than the song, as if trying to catch up to the future again. I lost my place in time the way your feet lose track of the floor when you jump. For a while I was precisely the height and speed of a song; my blood had edges that made sense. When the waiters brought the cake, tall and white and spun with sugar flowers I hoped were edible so the children would not be disappointed, I cried one true tear that moved so slowly down my cheek I could have plucked it up with my fingertip.

At the end, because this hotel, like all its kind, had subscribed to every ritual that involved a neat photo, there were sparklers. They were the kind that do not spit; they made a soft, controlled ember, a line of light that ate itself. A basket of them appeared and people lifted them with caution and then with glee. Someone handed one to me before I had a chance to excuse myself, and I took it because we are cows for custom. It flared, a clean line of heat. I held it at an angle so that anything ash-like would fall away from my dress and let it burn down to a hand’s distance. Above us the sprinkler heads glinted like watchful eyes; the head of security, a man with a starched white shirt that fit him like a reprimand, stationed someone with a red bucket just out of frame. The tiny points of fire were no bigger than the glass bulb in a sprinkler and no more disobedient. People passed beneath them—shrieking aunties, a child visor on his head, the best man with his arms up and his tie around his forehead like a prizefighter. Daniela and her husband went through last, her dress taking none of the light into its own self, her face carrying all of it, and we sent them into it as if we were pushing a small boat into the sea.

Back in my room the quiet lapped at my ankles, clean as a hospital corridor before the day begins. I turned on the bedside lamp and wrote another note for whomever would stand here tomorrow and fold away the evidence of our belonging. I lined up the pillows, not because I had faith in pillows but because my hands needed to talk to cloth. I took the little glass of water off the distressed-wood nightstand and set it on the coaster the way hotel advertising had taught everyone to do. I took off my dress and hung it on the back of the bathroom door, the way steam would pretend to help it. The iron lay where I had set it, its cord curled like a sleeping animal; I moved it to the closet shelf because I could not bear the thought of it startling me in the middle of the night with its potential. I shut the door to the hallway and set the latch. I did not put a towel under the door. I lay in the dark and let the faint hum of the building tell me it was working. My hand found the line of my arm where the skin had taken from that winter a small pearly seam. I touched it once, in a gesture I could not have named, and slept with more effort than it took to stay awake.

In the morning the city had arranged itself into a quotidian shape. The river was a line of light gilt with wind. My coffee was both ordinary and miraculous. I took the elevator with a housekeeping cart and then held the elevator door with my forearm for a houseman rushing in with a caddy of cleaning supplies, and he said thanks in a tired tone that yet held some humor. In the lobby the front desk had a different crew, fresh-faced and not yet fully armoured in their smiles. I stood a second in the curve of the marble and watched a concierge pretend his keyboard had locked up so he would have to look a woman in the eye while he told her that what she wanted to do in two hours would not be possible in three days. Daniela and her husband had left early to drive to the coast; my aunt and my mother had gone with them to throw a handful of rice they had hoarded despite the hotel’s instructions to use bubbles instead. The glass case with the list of names looked as it had yesterday. I took a photograph of it with my phone and sent it to my own email addressed to no one with no subject line, the way you make a note to your future self of a thing you will not want to forget but will not want to see often.

At checkout the clerk asked me a question that was easy to answer. “How was your stay?” he said without looking fully at me—his eye on the little blue square of information that told him I had been given a room on the fourth floor, that I was with a wedding block, that I had been comped a glass of bubbles. The script called for him to say, Thank you for staying with us, we hope your stay was exceptional, please come see us again soon. I could hear the phrases lining up behind his teeth.

“It was beautiful,” I said, and meant both the wedding and the small, hard thing in me that had uncurled enough to put two fingers on the hotel and not have to snatch them back.

He smiled and slid the folio across the marble. Behind me someone laughed in relief the way you laugh when an invoice is less than you feared. I took the paper and folded it and placed it in my bag though I would throw it away upstairs. On my way to the door, I saw a housekeeper standing at the corner where carpet meets stone, sunlight skimming the hair on her forearm. She was about my age now, which meant she would not be for long. I caught her eye and said, “Gracias,” in the voice you use to speak to a person you know will understand you as an equal. She smiled, a little line of humor appearing at one corner of her mouth.

“De nada,” she said.

The morning put its hand on my shoulder as I stepped outside. The revolving door made me feel like the ringmaster of my own little circus for three beats; then I was on the sidewalk and the current of the day picked me up. The facade of the hotel sent the face of the sky back into the street; in its glass I could see myself almost wholly and then gone again as people moved between me and that. Traffic flexed its muscles and then relaxed them. I looked up one last time at the windows that had nothing to do now with what I had once pressed my cheek against and then I turned into the small wind off the river, the city putting its complicated hands on my ribs in greeting, and I walked.
