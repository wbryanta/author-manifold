They came in the morning while the light was new and clean and the orchard lay in its own pale dust like a book long shut and opening. The air went up from the flats and hung and the far highway carried a low sound like the sea for there was no sea here and nothing that moved without reason in a place of reasons old as stone. They parked the truck by the concrete box of the headgate and cut the motor and sat. The birds had already gone quiet in the first heat and the leaves of the trees were tipped up like hands in a prayer no one heard. The ditch water lay a long tongue of brown glass within its clay banks, held behind the sluice of warped boards and rusted bar.

Eli got down and shouldered the shovel from the bed. He stood and looked along the rows as if counting some lost thing and Harlan let his arm hang out the window and turned his head and spit. He wore his hat too far back on his head and his hair beneath was gone gray and he squinted without much show of pain, like a man who had agreed long ago with the world about how much it could hurt him. In the bed of the truck was a roll of black poly pipe and a five gallon pail with primer and cement and two sticks of schedule forty PVC laid across and a hacksaw and the toolbag with the wrenches and the threaded fittings they’d hunted down at the co-op. A bundle of rags and four hose clamps and the old crescent wrapped in tape where the jaw had bit his hand a lifetime back and you could still see the crescent moons of scar faint under the dirt when the dirt was thinner.

We’ll see first if that head’s even ours, Harlan said.

It’s ours, Eli said. It’s still June. The turn’s Friday straight through Monday.

Friday to Monday’s gone if we dont take it, Harlan said. Water dont hold a grudge. It just leaves.

Eli stood with the shovel and looked at him. You think I dont know when we have the turn?

I dont know what you know, Harlan said. Except you come down here when they call you the noon of the day and tell you it’s like to be late and you get moved to the bottom and why a man would run his life at the sufferance of the next man in line I dont know.

Eli swung the gate bar loose and eased the warped board up and set it on the concrete lip. The headbox took the first water in a dull rush and the ditch roiled and the mud rose and tiles of dead algae peeled up like old paint and the water climbed in the box and shivered at its own lip and the iron bugs came up from wherever iron bugs are and began the same work because that is the work the Lord gives iron bugs. Harlan got the pipe wrench and they went along the supply line where it ran out of the headbox and down the trench and under the berm and into the first row.

The orchard was a quarter section and what trees stood did so with that soldier’s blank. There were bare spots where the wind had got them two winters running and the gophers worked a world of their own under the dead. The leaves of the living were blanched up out of disease and the cambium was sunburned where they’d cut back and painted and the paint had gone to chalk. There lay a scatter of hard black mummies among the furrows and last year’s grass in rope knots about the trunks and the mulch ring like a moat long gone dry. It had been their grandfather’s place and then their father’s and then their mother’s for a time on paper and now it was no one’s and everyone’s both and there was a question in the air about the last term the way there’s always a question in the air about the last of any old thing.

Eli put his thumb to the cut end of a lateral and it dipped and wept out a weak spittle and he went along on one knee and the other foot like a cripple feeling each one. Harlan stood with the wrench and scanned the length of the place not in hope of any vision but the way a man long learns to stand and take into himself the breadth and sum of what has borrowed from him his names and bones. At the far fence the white posts leaned toward the road as if in conversation and the road went trying to be gone and not be seen at it. Heat already began to come off the metal and in the wash to the east the sand lay bright as a rib of a dead fish.

You take the west run and I’ll take the east, Harlan said. We’ll dig where the ground’s wettest and that’s where it is.

We fix the blowout and the pressure will find the next weak spot, Eli said.

We fix each in its turn. There’s no world for which that aint the method.

He said this not in any comfort. They got back to the headbox and Harlan put the wrench to the riser and snugged the gate and the sound of the orchard changed. Water talking under ground is a more fearful sound than water when you can see it. It lays a hand to a man’s ear such that it seems he is being spoken to by something consenting to speak only as long as it is hid.

You think you can go to the bank like you go to the well, he said. You think it’s there because it was there when you went before. It’s not so.

I know that.

No you dont.

I been doing this work same as you.

Harlan looked down at him and then away. He walked off with the wrench hung in his hand and the sun made a glare at the line of his hat. Eli laid his shovel mouth in the low dirt and pressed down with his boot and the dirt came off in plates and crumbled. He stacked it in a flattish mound the way his father had taught him, respectful as if the earth were an understudy for a grave.

There’s a trick to reading the ground when there’s water under it. It has a softness not seen with the eye though the eye will learn to see it. The wild pigs had been in and rooted some, the noseholes they leave round as cups, and the coyotes come to the skin of the place at night and you saw the collected smallnesses at dawn—the paired dimples of the possum hands, the tiny ragged little manfeet of the skunk, the catpads like someone pressing three coins into mud and picking them back again, a story with a grammar as old as the world and more legible to those who will not read it than any printed scripture in a hotel drawer. Eli knelt and began to work his shovel along the pipe like a blind man reading. He had no gloves and the shovel handle left clean halfmoons in his palm where the old callus ran.

After a time he heard metal and turned and Harlan had found something under his foot and he took up his own shovel and they came and cleared the place and there was a hairline in the mainline that had become a mouth and the mouth had filled with sand and it looked like a creature’s wound in those few beats before the wound bleeds.

We’ll cut it, Eli said.

Hold on. Let’s get the pressure off and suck this out.

Water never forgets a weakness, Harlan said. You patch it and it waits and it dont have to wait long. Not in this world.

Eli stood and he had new dirt on him and old dirt and no man so clothed is poor.

A truck came down the county road like a boat coming down a dark and changeable river. It pulled even and slowed and a man leaned out with his hair already made and his mouth sweet with something meant to pass for care. He set his elbow out like a statement of permanence and they both knew him and neither raised a hand.

Mornin boys, the man sang. That your turn today?

It is.

She dont look like she’s much wanting it, that ditch. Water’s a mind to scald a man’s good that aint got no pipe to hold it.

We got pipe, Harlan said.

Mm, said the man. He looked out at the rows as one surveying what he owned. You hear they’re breaking ground where the Slater place was?

I heard it.

They got a dozer up there. Loud as a sermon. You can hear it halfway to the river. I said to my boy I said son listen. What’s that? He said it’s the sound of money and I said no. It’s the sound of something else.

What else, Harlan said.

He shrugged. He looked down along the arm of his shirt where his watch sat and he brought it out and turned it and he looked not at the time but at the face of the watch as if some message might be written there from the living world to the other and back. He was named Dodd and he had the knowing way of a man who has sold things and thinks he has sold God and will take a second bid for Him if offered.

You fellas think any about that offer, he said. Prices like they are I dont need to tell you—aint nobody goin to see money like that again. Not out here. Water aint goin to come back and the trees aint going to come back and the house that was worth a hundred thousand it’s worth three now and next year what. Five. Who knows. It’s a pretty miracle we’re in. I told Donny I said if I was you son I’d get married in this time right now. I said because you’ll step out into whatever the next world is and you’ll think it’s hard times and you’ll be right because it always is but you’ll know in your bones there was a mornin when your life was so cheap you could buy two of them for the one you had and give the spare to a beggar and you wouldnt feel it.

We got to fix this line, Eli said.

I see that, Dodd said. I see it and I dont. I look and I see you two boys. Not boys I know that. Men. Come into the world in the same clothes you’ll leave it in, I reckon, by the look of it. I see that and I see somethin else. That place built out here? Cheetgrass like hair on a corpse. Streets called this and that. All them nice names they give to things God dont recognize. Evening Shade or Turtle Dove or I dont know what all. And you wont live there and I wont live there. But someone will. Because the creatures of the world dont know enough to be ashamed to go where they aint been asked. You boys think on it. I can only bring it to you. I cant make you swallow. He laughed.

Harlan stared at him as if at a clock on the wall. Eli set his shovel tongue into the dirt again.

You talk to your mama? Dodd said.

What for, Harlan said.

She’s still on that paper, is what for.

She dont talk to me, Eli said.

Who she talk to then.

She talks to the dead. Heaven knows she’s got a big ear for it.

Dodd shook his head with a look of sympathy as deliberate as a man putting down a glass of milk so slow a child might think it was always there. I can do a thing or two about that, he said. Paperwise I mean. Make it easy for yall. Take that headache from you.

Eli got to his feet. He had to fix his hand to the shovel like he couldnt unclench. It was not hate. He could not hate a man so completely who was so transparently unable to hate himself.

You already said, Harlan said. You go on.

Dodd held up his hands in a peaceable way and rolled his wrist to show the watch’s face to the sky as if an obliging sun might sign something there. He rolled the window up. The engine idled. He put it in gear and pulled away slow and the tailpipe made a little sneeze at them and he was gone and a dust he’d left on the road as if he’d shed it and might put it back later if he came by naked.

Devil rides about in a clean shirt, Harlan said.

If the devil needed to sell you something there’s nobody he’d choose over that man.

Harlan spat again. Go shut the gate and I’ll make that cut.

They bled off the line and set the hacksaw teeth to the pipe and the plastic gave with its old dry creak and the saw teeth took shavings bright as gristle and the blade steamed a little where the friction warmed it. Eli sucked the muck out of the mouth with a short bit of hose and he opened it with two fingers and it had that rotten smell like dead dolls and they scraped and wiped and glued and set the coupling. Harlan looked at his wrist where his watch had been and was not. He was forever taking it off and laying it somewhere. He had done it since he was a boy and their father had walloped him for it and he had done it with beers and with sockets and with love and there was no bending it from him for a man cannot be turned from the terms of his birth even by a father’s hand.

Where’s your watch? Eli said.

He looked at his wrist and then at Eli as if Eli asked him to locate the new moon on a day of no visible sky. He smiled with half his teeth and he pointed toward the truck with his chin. I dont know.

You left it somewhere.

Mm, Harlan said.

In thirty minutes you’ll ask me where we are in the day and get mad I dont know.

Harlan knelt and held the coupling and looked along the pipe like a man sighting a thing he could not afford to lose.

You dream last night? he said.

Eli shook his head.

I woke up to a dream I cant remember and I was already too far gone from it but I had the knowing of a thing I never knew in waking, and there I lay with the one proof of a world you aint granted and then you open your mouth to speak it and the thing runs out of it like a bird out a barn.

We aint obliged to know everything we can know, Eli said.

We aint allowed to, Harlan said.

They set the clamp and cinched it. Eli’s hand brushed the shadow under the berm and it came alive and he flinched and withdrew and swore and they stood up. There lay a snake coiled in the cool and it was not a rattler but a bullsnake and even so Harlan set his boot to the ground near its head and the snake made to push itself away and in the same instant Harlan reached and gripped it behind the jaw and brought it up and its body wrapped his wrist and crawled up his forearm and he held it there with his mouth set and his eyes like he’d picked up a piece of the long machine that is the earth and it was running through him to see where the breaks were.

You’re goin to get bit, Eli said.

He’s bit me before, Harlan said. If that’s what he’s called to I dont know.

He unwound it and flung it into the weeds and the snake made out for a gopher hole like a rope being pulled under a door and vanished.

You dont have to manhandle every living thing, Eli said.

I know that. But it looks like I do.

I guess it does.

They turned the water back on and went to stand over the coupling and it held and they followed the sound of it on into the orchard and they found where the true break was and it was not the coupling and it was not the glue therefore but the glue and the coupling were good to have and to leave for the one later time when they would be needed. So they dug and the sun went up and the year leaned like a horse leaning into harness. Sweat ran and they drank from the plastic waterjug in the truckbed and it had the taste of old hose.

By nine the heat had got up to that point where the air felt like something you were moving through instead of around. Eli took his shirt off and tied it about his neck and the scar at his waist from a break of barbed wire when he was eight showed white as a string. Harlan left his on. He had a belly come to him he had not been asked to approve. He had a knife he wore on his belt and he never had cause to take it off.

They widened the trench with their feet and their work had become a kind of mute liturgy by then and they could not say when that had occurred for that is the way of all such things. A sound came from up toward the wash and Eli stood and shaded his eyes. A boy in a white shirt was walking the fence and he stopped and watched them and then turned and pelted away like a deer. He was no one they knew and in a place like that no one you didnt know was someone you wished to.

By ten they had the mainline cut back on both sides of a long split that ran along the top like a lip cut with a glass. The break had been leaking for longer than they’d allowed and the hole below was soft as pudding and it swallowed the shovel and Harlan worked down in it with his hands and the roots in the hole were pale and slick like eels and they curled and touched him and went away like shy things in a dream and he felt a stab at the back of his mind like something telling him how water is a coward and yet will outlive men who are braver than it and he did not want the thought yet it came.

They measured and cut and dry fit the pieces. The glue stung your head to open it and made the day seem like it was happening a little bit above you like someone else’s dream and there was a line of blue on Harlan’s thumb when he came to wipe his face and he left there a blue frown and Eli reached and took his hand and frowned at it and they both stood like that for a second in a private joke old as their bones. Then Eli set the couplings and they pushed the line home and twisted and the glue sucked and took and they held it and waited and they did not look at one another for the same reasons a man will not look at a woman he loves in the one moment when love is like a sparrow on a wire and any shift of the body will send it up and gone.

You think we can keep this, Eli said.

What is it I think, Harlan said.

You want to sell.

We need to sell.

Need aint a true word.

We got men wanting to be paid who aint been. We got a pump sits on a pallet and if I drag the pallet with the tractor one more time the pallet’s goin to give and then there goes the pump down that ditch and then we’ll sell it for scrap because that’ll be the only good we can get from it. Men come and they wave the paper and the seal is on it and they look at me and I dont know what they see. A boy. Their own father. I dont know. It dont scare me. It does not. But I see a number and that number is the measure the world will take from me to let me lie down in it at last and it is not a small number and I am not much of a man. I can tell you that.

You talk like men dont die anyway.

Men die anyway. This aint about men.

You talk like the land has no voice.

The land’s voice aint ours to hear. It talks to rocks and fishes and all them little things we see moving in the night that dont make no account of us. It talks to the dead where it lays them out. That’s its business. When the wind comes up and goes down that row and sets your shirt to flappin and it’s like a woman has put her hand on your back for the second she loves you and then she’s gone that aint the land. That’s wind.

They said little for a time. Harlan straightened and put his hand to the small of his back and the vertebra there popped like knuckles. He looked east and a hawk rode up the riverway and it was on its own errand.

At noon they went to the truck and sat on the tailgate and ate from a brown bag that had been used for that purpose so long the greaseprint was ancestral. Bologna slices in bleached bread and a ring of onion and mustard and a little cup of something that had been mayonnaise that a summer had made other. They ate and they watched the flat dazzle and the wind brought up the smell of a dead thing and set it by you and then took it back again the way men take infants from a woman’s arms because they know better and she lets them because she knows they know better and none of them know anything at all. Eli drank and handed the jug and on the hill a dust devil wrote its language in the air and then forgot its words.

That man Dodd, Harlan said. He’ll come back at three. He’ll sit in his truck and you’ll see him thinking. It’ll be as close a thing to prayer as he will ever know. He’ll straighten his collar and back that beast into place and he’ll put his mouth on and he’ll come smiling and he’ll hold his hand out like the handle of a well.

What will you do, Eli said.

I dont know. I think on it.

In the heat there are places where the old men disappear. They are not graves and they are not stations in a journey. They are chairs and porches and the long cool under the overpass where a man can smoke and watch the trucks go by and count his breaths and the world does not require anything. There had been such a cool place in the barn when they were boys and you sat on the sacks and put your back against the planks and the dust motes were like snow turned gold and what was there to ask for from any God at all. Their father had come in once dirty and bent and he had sat and they had watched him not wanting to interrupt the only silence in him they had. He had said boys and he had leaned his head back and his black hair was the last black thing they would see on him. He had said you got to learn that every hand you put to a thing is the world’s hand and the world will not ever help you do the thing you do not want to do. He’d died in a hospital that did not know his name right and they’d fetched him home and they’d painted his face with blush that made him look foolish and Harlan had stood and wanted to laugh and to punch a wall and to shoot someone and to look at the sky all at once and he had done none of these.

You remember that man that came about solar, Eli said.

I do, Harlan said.

He wanted to lay glass all over a corner of this place and give us three hundred dollars a month for the rest of the world or some such talk. Said we could grow shade grass under them panels. Said we could run sheep.

What would we have done with sheep, Harlan said.

I dont know. Not a thing.

I dont believe men want to fill this world with mirrors no more than they want to fill it with houses. They want to fill it with the picture of themselves and when they see that picture they are comforted and when the picture goes bad on them they kill it like an animal and they say it was never them.

Eli put his heel to the dirt and drew a halfcircle. He erased it.

You talk like mama, he said.

Let me alone.

She said a man’s life was a story and if nobody heard it it still was. She said she lay in bed in the winter and she could hear the old house talking and she knew who was there and who wasnt. I dont believe in that.

No.

I dont believe in nothin much, Eli said. I believe in this ditch because I opened it. I believe in them valves because I turned them and felt them turn. I believe in that bird because I could touch it if I had to and it would draw blood on my hand and all my belief would go out of me in one thin line and I’d look at you and you’d say did you learn and I’d say I did.

You talk like her, Harlan said.

Let me alone.

They sat smoking and heat rose off the hood of the truck and wavered the world like a cheap promise. A woman drove by in a car and she was nobody they knew and did not look. The car burned with her in it toward some place they wont remember when they arrive. Across the ditch a cottonwood’s leaves turned all at once and flashed their unders. A dragonfly went down the length of the headbox and it seemed to have a place it intended. A pair of blackbirds were in a quarrel with a jay and the jay was pleased with himself above all measure and men know a little of this too.

All right, Harlan said. Let’s get the rest of it.

They worked. They lay in the ditchwater like old men in a bath and they came out and their pants stuck to them and their boots were full of grit and they left little mud sculptures everywhere they went. They found the second leak and it was where the line came under the old manifold by the pump. They had set that manifold when they were still boys and their father had done the joints because he said you cant uncut a pipe and you boys aint learned to be careful with a thing that has only the one life to it. The manifold was cobwebbed and a scorpion walked along the mile of his own mulishness and Eli took the flat of a board and pressed and the scorpion came apart without a sound as if that was always his purpose. They dug and laid new pipe and where they cut they found the old bits of duct tape their father had used on a day he should of known better. They both came up with small smiles at the sight of it and the smiles were not so much for him as for the world which had given them him and would yet in time take their own foolishnesses and lay them out in a similar case for some hand to happen on and be made to know them too late.

A cloud formed up out of Mexico and stood like a black animal on the mile. It was noon after noon and then it was not. Shade came like an answer to a permission and the birds called up and the breeze came like a woman standing in a door and looking at men. Eli stood with his forearms along the shovel top and watched the light crawl over the orchard like a physical thing. He thought of a time he’d took a girl to the ditch and they had swam and the water had not been poisonous to them then as it was now and when they came out they had sat ashiver in the middle of June and she had laid her head on his shoulder like the world had cheated him of anything and was now returning it. He did not remember her face well. He remembered the braid of her hair dark as a hickory switch.

Harlan was under the truck with a wrench and the wrench knocked and rang and both of them thought of the old lying under a car the week before he died and the image of the blue sky in the sheen of the oil on his hand and he’d turned his head to look at them and he’d smiled with those young teeth and he was a boy under there and he was old at the same time and this is the way of that.

By three the sky had turned bad with the tendency to rain but it wasnt going to. Dodd came back as Harlan had said he would and he set his rig in the same track as earlier and he adjusted it back and forth like a man uneasy in some necessary posture and then he came. He wore the hat a man dont wear when he is by himself. He had his hand out and they could have pulled the tendons in his arm like the lines of a harp and played some simple sad tune on them.

I dont want to be a sticker in a dog’s paw, he said. I dont. But I cant leave it and not do my job. They wont let me. Who wont, you say. Well I think you know who wont. He smiled and the act of smiling touched on a deformity in the world like a wrong seam in a flag. It would have made you angry to study it too long.

We aint got an answer, Harlan said.

You aint got an answer, no. You think to speak and the voice that would say it aint climbed up you yet.

We aint saying yes.

You aint saying no neither.

We got our turn just now, Eli said. We got to run it. We got to see what she takes.

Dodd looked out at the place as if to consult with it privately. He had his hands folded before him like a midwife. Boys, he said. I got a buyer on the line. I aint supposed to say it. But I do. Man gets anxious. He’s got trucks on call and he cant just stand men up for nothing because then he’s got to go hire them again and they’ll say hell with you. I can hold him another day maybe two. You know what they’ll do. They’ll roll up them nice little strips of grass you got here and take it over to the ballpark and lay it out again and nobody will be the wiser. Men are clever. I told him I said these are good folk. They aint going to leave you to dangle. But you got to know the bus leaves when it leaves. You dont show at the depot, nobody blames the bus.

Harlan looked at him and did not blink. You tell your man if he comes on this place with his tape and his camera and all them stakes like a porcupine’s back I’ll take to him with a length of rebar and he wont like it.

Dodd kept the smile where it was. I will tell him just that, he said. Boys. Two hundred and eight an acre. Cash at close. You sign Friday.

Our mother, Harlan said.

He ticked his head. I understand. She’ll be managed. She’ll see her good. Old folks sometimes they dont want to look. Their regret is the last thing they got they think. You go easy with her. But she’ll be managed.

By who.

By me, Dodd said. He smiled. By the world.

Eli stepped past him and went to the box and opened the gate and water came full through and they stood and watched it. Dodd watched too and he had no comprehension of the thing he watched and he thought he did and that was worse.

We’ll call you, Harlan said.

You will, Dodd said. He touched his hat as if it were a brim to lift and he carried his hands like a man has just been convicted and is amused at it.

They went back and they set the valves and listened. The sound moved on through the place like an animal that had lost its mind. They walked and the sprinklers took and chuffed and slewed and they spun and the water was a veil thrown and reclaimed and the dust lay in it and went down and the leaves glittered and they looked up into the white bellies of the trees and there were still some small things to be saved that wanted to be saved because the way of the world is for things to want it even when they are not going to get it and what was wanted was not simply that they should be alive but that they should continue to be some specific expression of that, this white peach and not any white peach, this black limb and not any black limb, and what wants that in them is the same what that says a man is a man and not any man and then strikes his name from him when no other living soul cares to speak it.

When they came out to the end of the far run they found a small boy had made his way through the fence and was crouched where the dirt had turned to mud. He had his fingers in the mud and he was smearing it on his shins and he looked at them without fear and his mouth was open. He had a milk tooth come in crooked and the gum at its base was red. He said something in a language they did not have and Eli squatted and said something back to him and it was not the boy’s language and not Eli’s either and it did not convey anything except that they were both of them the children of someone and this place was where it had sent them for the hour. The boy grinned and tottered and fell on his rump and laughed. He got up and ran back to the fence and was gone through it like a rabbit and an old woman on the road called something and the boy answered and she took back from him whatever trouble she had let him borrow.

At four they shut the far valves and turned back down the slope and in the silence of the stopped sprinklers they could hear the hiss of those still running in the rows and it was like a tableful of men whispering and you will have heard that sound and known it meant nothing good and wanted only maybe to die.

On the way back they found an old coin in the trench. It was dull and slick and they washed it in the water and the king’s head there was not any king they knew. It was punched with a square nail. Harlan held it and then threw it up and caught it and he gave it to Eli who slid it into his pocket with a concentration that meant to make a matter of this thing wherein there might be none. If you live a long time in a parched world you get to where you hold water you cant drink in your mouth for the feel of it alone and then you spit it and lay hold to the next mouthful if there is one to lay hold to and there is no shame in that.

Back at the headgate they took their shirts and wrenched them and they hauled from the truck a coil of rusted wire and cut it and wired the boards in so they could not be tempted by their own nature to unmake what men had made and Eli stood with the hammer and struck and struck and the iron barked. The line of clouds had moved east some, its belly black and its hem a ragged gold, and there was wind now where there had been none and it smelled of a place not near. The light came stranger, down the wrong way between the leaves, and the sheds and the barn took on the unearned grace of the already departed.

You remember the crooked peach, Eli said.

I do, Harlan said. It grew there where the waist of the field is.

It grew that way because daddy planted it in the hole like a stick and never did right it and it never did want to take right but it did and then the fruit it bore was sweeter than any other fruit.

That’s foolishness, Harlan said.

It aint.

Sugar aint got to do with mistakes. Sugar’s in the water. They call it something else but it’s sugar.

It bore different, Eli said. You know it.

I know it because I bit it. You make your poetry you’re goin to make a man of you out of that? I dont know that any case has been proved about any man in that way.

Eli smiled faint. The wind lifted his hair and laid it and he put his hand to it as if it were a child.

They walked to the barn and stepped in under the dark eaves and the light in there was always the same because light forgets how old it is in barns and men look at it and think they know how old it is and they are wrong. The tools hung on the nails waiting on a hand. An old pair of harness lay over the beam and had lain there all their lives. Harlan ran his hand along it and the leather cracked under his palm like a thing that had kept a promise too long and wanted to know if now it might be let to make some evil and he would not and so it cracked.

They sat with their backs to a stack of pallet boards. The smell of hay ghosts and mice. A swallow batted from her mudcup and went in a slow turn round the rafters and then came back and hung a split second and went in and in that interval Harlan saw the blind babies as if he held his hand up and caught them like rain. He put his hand over his own eyes and there was war in his head.

I went to see her last week, he said.

Eli waited.

She said her mother is there. Sits in the hall. I said mama your mother is dead. She said I know. She said how many you think have to come and go before the wood of a house comes to know and say their names for itself.

And what did you say.

I did not say.

She’s sick, Eli said.

He nodded. He looked out toward the glaring world line across the door. When I was little I wanted her to be sick. Because it was and I thought it was in her and that way it did not have to be everywhere. That’s a child’s thought. But I had it and I carry it now and I will own it and you will not take it from me because I am ashamed in a right way.

What do you think if we do not sell and we watch this die. What do you think.

I dont know, Harlan said. I used to think I did know. That I could judge things. I cant judge them. There’s a boy in my head and he wont let me. He reaches and takes the scales from me like toys and he brings them to his friend and they weigh rocks and I ask for them back and he laughs.

You’re not good at that, Eli said.

No. I aint.

You’re good at standing in a door and not moving and a thing looks at you and it dies out of that.

What thing.

Whatever thing.

Harlan wiped at his face with the back of his wrist and left there a dark cleaner place. He smiled and it was true. It has been given me, he said. For my good or bad.

They went out and a thunder rumbled dry across the waste and they both looked up. Eli thought of a picture book someone had once given him when he was a boy, a story of a flood that never came, a place where the men built always and the waters hovered and a man in a hat not unlike Harlan’s had stood and stared and dared and the sky had been outstared. There is such a man in most places and he wears a hat. He dies and another one wears it.

They closed the headgate an inch and the tone of the orchard changed and the talk went lower and more hurried, the confidences exchanged among old things like sinners in a pew. The heat began to go. A pair of girls rode horses along the fence on the far side and they spoke softly to the beasts and their shirts were of some Indian color bright against the dull and they did not look at these men and the men did not look after them that long because they were old enough to let beauty go by and not chase it down and harrow it.

On the far hill a man in a yellow vest walked with a tripod and he made a careful artless revolution of the thing and he wrote and he moved on. They watched him and said nothing because there probably was nothing to say that would not make a small thing bigger nor a big thing small and that is not always wise.

Before dark they went again through the rows and listened and they set new gaskets where they must and they wedged one old valve with a stone and they shut another with a piece of two by and a rope and they did these beasts not to be clever but because they had to and the world commends such inventions when they are used only so. The trees drank. They bent and took the drink and in that act you could see them as creatures suffused with something beyond wood and leaf and whatever it was it is what men hold in their temples when they bow though they may not know it by any name.

You will have to tell her, Eli said.

He said this as one seating a thing gently in the world so that it might not startle.

I will, Harlan said.

Will you tell Dodd he can go to hell.

I will tell him he can go to hell. And he will. Go. Or come to it. He may not know it till he sees he cannot sell nothing there.

Eli nodded. He put his hand into the pocket of his pants and cupped the coin and brought it out and turned it in the falling light. The head on it was a head and the ring of letters that named him was not the letters they knew and then in the one side was punched the square and the corner of the hole was bent up and he laid his nail under it and lifted it and bent it and he looked sidelong at Harlan for the reproach that did not come and he bent it back. It was a small sin he made there and even that filthy little sin had in it the heart of man.

Something whickered at the fence and they both looked and there was a horse, a dapple soaked with that dust that lays itself down where it can be best seen. No rider on it. The animal lifted and dropped its head and blew and shook and the mane flared like it was on fire and settled. A piece of baler twine hung at the chin and went to the front feet and wrapped and the horse was bound not to run, its foreleg hobbled half to hell. It had got free and not got free. It watched them with the one eye and did not care to be known by them and did not care to know them and that is how a man would want it if he were a horse.

They did not go to it. It moved off and went along with its piece of string and its small slavery to some air or another and vanished down the line of the world.

Dark came with a suddenness as if the world had made up its mind. In the west the last red thing went out. They turned the gate down to a trickle to run the night and pulled the truck around and set the lights over the ditch while they put away the tools. Moths came out in their idiot piety to the lamps and set in sacrifice their small white garments and were burned and were not accounted of by anyone at all. The wound of the long day began to close in them. They sat and the engine ran and then did not run and they rolled out the windows and hung and the smell of hot oil and the smell of creosote and a skunk far off and the world enumerated for them the sum of the lives in it without their consent.

We can give it this season, Eli said.

We can give it this season, Harlan said.

And if it dont.

I dont know, Harlan said. We will then know what we did not know.

Dodd goes to hell then. Whether we take his money or not.

He goes there regardless. He has been going there his whole life and he wont get to meet nobody special when he arrives.

They both laughed at that low and the sound was not a bad one. Eli took his hat off and turned it and he did not think of his mother and then that thought accused him and he did think of her but lightly like one speaks a blessing over a loaf he will not eat. He put the hat back. The orchard was in that brief hour when all things out there are on the same side of some argument a man aint party to and he never will be, and it will go on without him to the end. He knew this and it did not make him sad. It made him kin.

We’ll go by and see her, Harlan said.

All right.

You’ll speak. I will stand there.

She thinks you are the man who was a boy once, Eli said.

So do I, Harlan said.

They pulled away and the ditch ran and the boards leaked and the night took congress with whatever walked there and men do not want to know and it kept it. On the road the lights went and caught the eyes of some beast and the eyes shone and then did not and the tires hummed and the dust rose and settled again where it began and in the darkness above the world the monsoon lay like a god turned down on his side and considering in a dull and ancient way the arithmetic of souls and of rocks and of all that water not yet given, and if it would come it would come not because men had asked or not asked but because that is the thing it does.
