By the time the first car nosed up the curb, Ellen had already taped the signs to the front door, propped the screen wide with a paint-stained brick, and put the cigar box on the card table like a small, practical altar. The cast-iron bell that had hung by the back porch for as long as she could remember had been unhooked and set beside the box, too heavy to trust to the thin card table legs, and people kept touching it as if palm-to-iron could prove it was real. A woman in a denim vest, hard hair sprayed into a lacquer, came up the walk with a wheeled cart, and then a man in a feed store cap who tipped it at Ellen in the way older men in the county did to show that politeness had not died along with penny candy and the harness shop. It was October and bright; the maples on Bradford Street were lit up as if the trees themselves glowed and the air had an apple-peel smell and the crickets had dropped their volume to an almost steady wire hum. The paper signs out front, black marker on a torn-down political poster stapled to a tomato stake—Estate Sale Today, 1–4, Everything Must Go—flapped faintly. The sight of them made Ellen want to laugh a little, then lie down on the grass.

Someone asked her if there was gold jewelry; someone else asked about records, primitives, cast iron. A woman with a teenage girl trailed them, eyes secret and bright. They were early; people were always early as far as she could tell. Estate sales made urgency bloom in people. The men conferred in quiet corners about what was inside and which pieces of furniture would fit into which trucks. Ellen recognized two of them as pickers who’d been at her neighbor’s sale in July—the balding one who’d bought a box of refrigerator magnets and flipped them for six dollars apiece on his Facebook page, and the blond who said nothing to anyone, moving like a heron through rooms, his gaze a precise instrument.

Each thing in the house had been labeled with blue painter’s tape in her own handwriting, which made it all worse somehow. There was her name in the two loops of an L in lamp $10, base only, as if she’d signed away her childhood with a price on it, as if she’d been the one to say yes to all those afternoons of cleaning, the eight garbage bags of expired coupons fished from the corners of drawers, the ancient refrigerator magnetized with funeral cards. She had—the yes had been hers—but the feeling folded over her in a new way as the denim vest woman drifted toward the hutch and reached for the milk-glass chicken that had sat there above the lace runner for forty years. Ellen had rubbed the bird’s head this morning with a drop of Pledge, and her mother, who had never liked figurines, would have said with a sniff, It’s a dust collector, and she would have dusted it anyway.

“Cash only?” the denim vest woman said, sharp.

“I can do Venmo too,” Ellen answered, and held up her phone like a shield. “Or check, if I know you.”

The woman pursed her lips. “It’s not worth ten without the lid.” There was an entire language to this, these sentences about the value of minor things that held within them the posture of the speaker, their history of having been disappointed by beds and men and wholesale lots of cracked saucers. “Seven.”

“Ten,” Ellen said. The numbers were not numbers so much as trial sentences, and she had promised herself she would not apologize. She had priced too low, she knew it. The men had been whispering out at the curb about that. She had stood in the kitchen the week before with the tape dispenser at her hip and a Sharpie warming in her fist and thought she could not do it, she could not look at the lace curtains, at the burnt sugar bubbles on the stove’s back burner, and choose amounts like weights to pin them to a table.

“There are bed frames?” one of the men interrupted. “Queen size?”

“Full,” Ellen said. “One twin.”

“Tsk,” he said, not to her, and kept moving, reading with his gaze what the rooms would give him.

Ellen stepped back toward the card table as a few more people streamed in. Their shoes made a furtive scraping on the old carpet despite the sign that said, Shoes On, Please, It’s Fine. The first man up the walk had the cap of Masters Feed tilted just enough that she could see the old scar that ran from ear to jaw, a white seam. He smelled faintly of horse. Someone buzzed by her with a long coat, a rustle like newspaper going in and out of a mailbox. She had put the photos away. She had left out the church cookbooks, the ones with translucent butter-stained pages and clip-art teapots. She had left out the jar of buttons because there had been so many buttons in the house all her life, each one a bright, hard coin, a possibility.

“Hi, Ellie,” said a voice, and she looked up to see Mrs. Howe from next door, in a rust cardigan and a scarf with pumpkins on it, still pretty at sixty-nine in the unapologetic way of women who didn’t pretend to be younger than they were. She had been raking when Ellen taped the stake into the yard and had stopped, leaned on the rake, and said, Are you sure you want to do it yourself? There are ladies in the county do these things, make it easier. Ellen had said, I’m sure. If someone else touched the skirts of her mother’s house, the house would bite.

“I wasn’t going to buy,” Mrs. Howe said now, lowering her voice to stay with her, “I got too much of my own. But I thought I’d keep you company.”

“I’m okay,” Ellen said, and that, she knew, was the chief lie she’d tell all afternoon, perhaps her whole life. She saw in Mrs. Howe’s face the quick glance past her shoulder at the throat of the house, the small space between the door frame and the hallway. Everyone always looked at that space, as if the house would inhale or cough up a memory. “Thank you for the casserole,” Ellen added. She had eaten it standing up at the sink with her mother’s fork, the tines slightly out of alignment in a way her finger remembered from childhood.

“I’m sorry,” Mrs. Howe said, the words like a card pressed into a palm. “She was a good neighbor. Kind of—”

“She was,” Ellen said, preempting the shared description of her mother’s meannesses, her thrift, the firm line of her mouth that could close a room. “She was.”

A boy darted into the room, skidding a little as he caught the edge of the rug. He couldn’t have been more than thirteen, maybe fourteen, and his hair, the exact shade of winter wheat, fell into his eyes. He went at once to the box in the corner marked Records, $2, and went through them with fingers so quick they made a hummingbird of him for a second. He pulled out Fleetwood Mac with a satisfied grunt, then a Conway Twitty album that had once been his mother’s, Ellen thought, from the years when Dad had let a little bit of country into the house after he came home from the plant and drank two beers and pretended to nod at whatever Mom said.

“You can look upstairs if you want,” Ellen called to the room in general, as much to fill the air as to say what was true. “Bedrooms, linens, sewing stuff.” That last word belled something in her chest—stuff, a way to make small what had been big—and she heard her mother’s voice, a light start of a sound, a click of tongue against the palate, the sound she used to puncture anything too soft.

“Is this Jell-O mold priced right?” called one of the women from the dining room, holding the bright aluminum ring with its scalloped edges like a crown.

“Five,” Ellen said. She didn’t remember if it was five; she didn’t know if this was the right answer; she didn’t think there was a right answer to anything today. She had put the Jell-O mold on the table and for a moment seen July through the kitchen window, the day her father had filled a kiddie pool and then folded himself into it despite her mother shouting, Harold, you’ll break it, and then cold sweet shivers of red across Ellen’s teeth that tasted like a holiday. The doorbell rang though the door was open. The sound made Ellen think of all the times she had sat here waiting on dates, polite boys fidgeting on the porch with haircuts like John Denver’s. Her mother had come to the door with a dishtowel still in her hand and a chin tilted not to be impressed.

“Your brother couldn’t come?” Mrs. Howe asked in a hush.

“He couldn’t,” Ellen said. He had sent her a gif of a cat patting at falling confetti with the words You got this! winked over it, and she had put her phone under a dish towel for an hour to suffocate the cheer. He lived in Tampa now, managed a call center, took his sons to Buccaneers games, posted photos of his wife by an infinity pool with sunsets that looked printed. He had meant to fly up but then a deal had come through and his wife’s mother had needed her back done and he had sent money, which was, she knew, a kind of love, the kind he had learned to be comfortable with. “He’ll come for the closing,” she said. “He said to keep the flag.”

“Oh, the flag,” Mrs. Howe said, and they both glanced toward the hallway where the framed triangle leaned against the hall table, the glass gone from the front for some reason since forever, and the fabric inside faded to a shy blue. Ellen had taken it down from the basement wall last night. She had left it there for years because the space around it felt wide and empty when it wasn’t there, as if a song had stopped in the middle and no one knew how to start it again. She would have kept it now except that Mark, who was six years younger and had not known Dad the way Ellen had known he was not entirely the flag he’d wrapped himself in later, had wanted it like a boy wants a throwing knife. She would wrap it tonight in a blanket and mail it to Tampa with a note that said, He’d like this with you, forgetting that like was a childish verb.

More people came in. The living room, small even in the time of small living rooms, filled up with bodies in coats unzipped and men with coffee cups. The sofa, its cushions dented as if two ghosts had been sitting there for a long time and just now got up to check on the lasagna, was claimed early by a couple in their thirties with a baby asleep against the woman’s chest, the baby like an ear pressed against the woman’s heart. The husband lowered himself into each seat cushion with the seriousness of testing a horse’s leg, then said, “We’ll take it,” with a solemn little nod. He was the kind of man you knew could fix the back of a drawer. His wife traced the arm of the sofa with two fingers as if it were a road.

“My mother hated this sofa,” Ellen heard herself tell them, because that was a thing she had never said aloud. The couple laughed, or made the polite sound that meant they didn’t know why she’d offered information like the butt end of a stick. “She thought it made the room look dingy. But she got it on sale and said it would just have to do.”

“That was my mother, too,” the woman with the baby said.

“What do you want for it?” the husband asked.

“Seventy-five,” Ellen lied, and sweat broke on her upper lip. She had put fifty on the tape, she knew it, and now the words had come up from somewhere far below her tongue, a place where money meant the doctor’s office, the cashier’s eyebrows when weeping women in Kmart wanted the blue layaway slip back because they had to get sugar and bread, the way her mother had tried to teach math by columns though numbers slipped in Ellen’s hands like slim minnows. The husband was already nodding and handing over twenties with the practiced speed of husbands relieved to be done with a transaction.

“Bless,” said the woman with the baby, but softly.

They left the sofa to the men with the dolly and their own practiced angles, and Ellen watched them while behind her, at the dinette table, an argument rose up about the price of a box of old Christmas. She stepped around a man kneeling to peer beneath the credenza. She said please and thank you until her mouth made the shapes without sense. She sent two girls upstairs to the bedroom she had shared with Mark, the room with the wallpaper that had little blue trellises and strawberries on it, and heard them ooh over the tins where her mother had kept hair ribbons and paper fans, things that had almost no purpose except to be pretty, which had always made them suspect in the house. She let the boy with the wheat hair take the Conway Twitty record for one dollar, and then he brought her the Fleetwood Mac and said, “Do you have a box I can put these in?” and it wasn’t lost on her that we were still all of us always asking women for boxes, women for paper towels, women for the small square kindnesses of oranges in Tupperware at halftime.

The jar of buttons sold to a woman who had no interest in them as buttons. “I’ll put them in a bowl,” she said, “just look at them. Old buttons are better than TV, I say,” and Ellen wanted to kiss her because that was exactly the thing her mother would have said and then resented herself for saying.

A man came up from the basement with the tackle box that had belonged to her dad, a cheap plastic thing fogged to an aspirin color and filled with spinners the faded cartoon of fish on the package called guaranteed. Ellen’s father had been the kind of man who believed in guarantees and then made of every disappointment a private Bible verse to study hard. He had gone to war and come back with a knuckle that never unbent and a way of hollering only when he thought no one was around to hear. He’d worked at the glass factory, then the tire plant, then the sort of job that, if you squinted, you could call We Do What We Can. He had liked a good tool, a good knot, a joke that didn’t make fun of anyone by name.

“How much?” the man said, lifting the box with two fingers as if it might break or explode.

“Eighteen,” Ellen said, and the number had been written in the fat black of the Sharpie but now it felt like something she had thought of on the spot, the way a child called from another room thinks of a lie.

“You got any guns?” the man asked, like a person asking, You got any milk? The backyard would be better for that question, and a truck with a flag on it, and a sense of hurry that made the air shovel down into the lungs.

“No,” Ellen lied. There was a .22 upstairs in the top of her mother’s closet in a bag inside another bag, her father’s from a time when her father had gone alone to a little salvage yard to shoot at tin cans in the field beyond. Mark had wanted that too, called it a family heirloom as if we were the sort of family who had inherited anything besides big hands. Ellen had thought about it last night, had taken the weapon out of the bag and turned it in her palm, the shining metal an insult to its ability to violate. She had no interest in it as a thing. She had not wanted it to be a story that ended with something in the paper, a woman alone in a house, a set of hands, a sudden decision. She had put it in the trunk of her car and in the grocery store lot after dark given it to a police officer she knew from school nights, a woman who was small and formal and said, Thank you, Ms. Wallich, I’ll be sure it’s properly disposed of. She had sat after in her car with the motor running and no sound and let herself cry one long hard cry that had made her lungs feel clean.

She sold the afghan in peacock blues her grandmother had made to an out-of-town picker who said, “We’ll take threes,” as if he were ordering ribs, and when she said, “It’s thirty,” he said, “Thirty? For acrylic?” and rolled his eyes gently in a way that said, You have no idea about the world. She turned away from him and sold it for thirty to a young woman with rough hands who said, “Oh, my God, these colors,” and who ran two fingers over the shell stitch the way she had done as a child while her mother watched Dragnet and shucked corn.

By two-thirty, the living room was thinner. A square remained where the mattress had been, compressed carpet that would never spring back, as if the house had slept there too and would now miss the weight. The dining room lost its table to an uncle-looking man in bifocals who’d come with two farm kids in matching hoodies, and he had told them to lift with their legs, to which they had rolled their eyes exactly the way teenagers in every house in the county had rolled their eyes on the day adults said such things. The kitchen lost a drawer of knives to a woman who planned church suppers, and she had asked Ellen, Handwashing only? and they had smiled at each other as if there were secrets to be had in such facts that would save lives.

The sun slanted in through the front window and caught the dust like confetti that rose and fell in place. Ellen stood for a moment when no one made a demand of her in the old doorway between the dining room and the living room. The jamb had a notch in it about the height of her hip, worry-worn by hands for fifty years, an animal’s gate. She put her hand there without thinking then snatched it back because it felt like putting her fingers in a pocket she had borrowed without asking.

“Are you sure you want to sell the clock?” a tiny woman in a windbreaker asked her, pointing to the travel alarm with the cracked face that had sat on her mother’s dresser ticking like an old lady with a knitting needle. “This old a one, it’ll always be five minutes wrong.”

“I’m sure,” Ellen said. Memory like the old Flex shampoo bottle bloomed in her head—her mother in her slip, white with little flowers, the way her body had been a body even when Ellen thought of her only as mother. The clock had woken her for years for the seven a.m. shift at the nursing home, then for the afternoon shift at the elementary school cafeteria, then for the morning of chemo where the nurse said brightly, We’re going to take good care of you, and then for nothing, for a while, and then for the morning her mother had died, when no one had wound the clock the night before and it had made its hollow little grind when Ellen picked it up and shook it. She took the woman’s five.

In the hallway, the flag leaned and the cuckoo clock above it was quiet. They had never wound the cuckoo when Dad was home. He had said, That bird would drive me to homicide, and then in the Christmas her mother had sent it off to be fixed after some part had broken and it came back and wouldn’t stay quiet, he had taken it down off the wall and wrapped it in a towel and put it on the top shelf in the back pantry with the ice cream maker, where now it could be had for twenty-five if anyone wanted to make themselves crazy. It had come out this morning when Ellen was alone to be part of the ordeal. In the hall, the light made a soft arc on the wooden floorboards that she could have drawn in her sleep. She could still hear the creak that happened when anyone walked past the closet like a sigh.

“I’m sorry,” said a man near her shoulder suddenly, and he turned and she saw it was Mr. Thompson from down another block, his left eye always a little red-rimmed as if it had been crying since 1975. He must have seen the syncopation of all old-life things playing like a radio in her eyes. “It’s the worst work,” he said.

“It is,” she said. She hadn’t known there were worse jobs than sitting with someone you love while they looked not at you but toward a different horizon and said things like, My mother will be mad—her mother long dead—and then drifted back and said, No, no, sorry, phone call, and you had no phone in your hand. She hadn’t known the work could also be this, a parade of strangers telling you how much you could get for the soup tureen and asking you to bring them a bag as if this were a grocery store and the pantry would always be restocked.

A girl went by in pig tails, clutching a pillow, one of the two with sunflowers on it that had been in the den for years, and the sight of the pillow under that child’s thin, hungry elbow—hungry for things, for a nest—made Ellen think about being eight and hating the smell of winter coats after recess, the wet wool and the drip of melted snow off boots left by the radiator, the way everything had felt filled up and she had been empty as a balloon with a slow leak. She wanted to kneel in the hall and put her face on her knees until someone took the money and locked the house and told her it had been enough.

She worked. She made change. She got a Sharpie mark somehow on her right wrist that looked like a bruise. She set aside a Tupperware for Mrs. Howe because Mrs. Howe had said she always wanted the good old pieces with the burp lids and couldn’t find them because everyone got wise and took them to vintage shops now and sold them to college girls wanting to live like their grandmothers until they had children of their own and wanted machines to wash their lives. She took a bag of Jesus lighters and put them with a box of romance paperbacks and thought about the woman who had sat for years in the corner chair in the bedroom and read those with her feet pulled up under her like a girl. Her mother had never smoked and would have killed anyone in the house who had, but those lighters had turned up in the junk drawer every Christmas like the same family of mice.

At some point, she went out to the back porch to breathe and then went all the way down the steps to the patch of yard where a muddy square showed where the kiddie pool had sat the year they had forgotten it outside and it had wintered into a slanted heap. She had raked last week and the leaves had already fallen again, small gold flags all over the grass. A boy—Mrs. Howe’s oldest grandkid, not the record boy—was there now with his finger up inside the rough pine birdhouse her father had made in 1973 in a stretch of Saturday afternoons when he’d believed in simple salvation. He had made six and given them away, but had kept the first, had not painted it, had said, I like things to tell what they’re made of, and now this boy was poking his finger up and trying to touch the ghost of a bird.

“You want it?” Ellen said, surprising herself. The boy looked up at her with his mouth open, shock as if a principal had come out and said, Would you like to go home forever. He looked over his shoulder at his grandmother and his grandmother shrugged in the uncommunicative way of a person who had stopped deciding what the world owed her. “You can have it,” Ellen said. “No one’s bought it.” She picked it up and a little soft black smear came away on her hand and she thought, Soot? Had her mother lit a candle near it? Had the world been burning touching their house when it hadn’t? She put it in the boy’s hands. The boy looked at it the way some of these buyers had looked at Pyrex, desirous and unsure. “It’s for the birds,” she said, the mothering in the phrase a soft thing she had denied herself. “It’ll get beat up again. That’s how you know it’s doing its job.”

“Thank you,” the boy said, as serious as Sunday.

Back inside, a man in his fifties with a preacher’s haircut stood near the hutch. He said, “Ma’am,” to Ellen and folded his arms like a thing made out of cardboard. He wanted to buy the set of green glass glasses, all eight, for less. He wanted to know if the price was flexible because his wife had always wanted a green glass set and they’d just had to put a new transmission in the truck and they had said they were going to stop spending on wants and now she was looking at him like she wanted him to be a good husband. He did not say all of that, but it was there in the way his chin jutted, in the way his voice had found a preacher’s cadence when he said, I know you’re firm on prices, and a little bit of firm came out like a prayer.

“Twenty-five,” Ellen said, though she’d written down thirty. “I hope she drinks something fancy out of them.” His face did something startled, a child’s pure I have been seen. He paid her with two tens and a five and she handed him a newspaper to wrap them in, the front page a breaking story about the county commissioners she hadn’t known till this minute and didn’t care about at all. He said, “I’m sorry for your loss,” as if he’d been waiting all afternoon to download the sentiment, get it off himself, and she said, “Thank you,” and for a second they were just two people in a room where an old mahogany hutch stood open like a mouth mid-speech.

Her mother’s absence did not come as a thunderclap but as a weak radio signal, a thing you had to tune your attention to or you’d miss because the house would distract you with the way the carpet edge had never been fastened right on the stairs and the way the closet latch still needed you to lift and press in at the same time. There had been a morning a month ago, in the hospice room where her mother’s bed had faced the window and the trees there had been confused, still green because of the heat, though the calendar had said fall, when her mother had opened her eyes and said, The potatoes, Ellen, will you, the potatoes, and Ellen had said, I’ll mash them, I’ll make them with milk the way you like, and her mother had paused as if to consider this act of love like a new menu item she might order or not, and then had said, No lumps, and then had, for a moment, smiled as if she could taste that smoothness in her mouth. Two hours later, her mother had been gone, and the nurse had unplugged a machine that wasn’t attached to anything because so much in hospitals had the air of ritual regardless of function.

“You throwing in this paring knife?” called a woman inside, and Ellen nodded and rubbed the place on her sternum where her chest hair would have been if she’d been a man. She wanted now to tell the woman with the green glass that her mother had preferred instant potatoes because they were easier and because you could add as much water as you wanted and make the hungry feel full. She wanted to tell the boy with the birdhouse that there had been a spring when swallows had nested between the porch beams and her mother had declared war on swallows, and there had been afternoons of arms flapping and brooms and a happiness in her mother’s meanness there that had made Ellen love and hate her the way a daughter can only love and hate an immediate god. She wanted to tell Mrs. Howe that she had hidden, at eight, the Avon perfumes her mother sold all the women in the church, had tucked them in the coal room because she had been afraid someone would say their house was cheap and that it smelled like oranges without oranges. She said none of that. She kept a ledger in pencil of cash in, cash out, and put each check into the cigar box like a paper door closing on a squirrel you meant to let out later.

Sometime around three, with the light suddenly weak on the front lawn like someone had turned the sun down, a young couple came in together in the way of new couples, a pairness that makes of the two a single small animal. The woman had hair that looked like dusk and the man had a tattoo of a willow wrapping his wrist. They met at the hall tree and stepped around the boxes with a flare of nostrils and elbows in an unpracticed dance that would, in bad years, make them hate each other for a small minute and then, in the sacred yearly math of marriages that last, would make them smile again. They went into the bedroom and stood in front of the cedar hope chest the way pilgrims stand in front of bones of saints, their faces serious as children’s.

“You selling this?” the woman whispered, though no one was sleeping.

“Yes,” Ellen said. She had written eighty-five along the front of the tag but turned it over after, had felt suddenly like punching a person on the street who had dropped a tissue. She had sat on it this morning the way she had as a girl, its top making that patient arthritic creak beneath her, and had almost changed her mind. Her mother had insisted on that chest when Ellen was twelve, the summer her breasts had come in like an apology, the summer she got her period while babysitting for the Johnsons and had taken toilet paper and made a long thick rope of it and pretended she was not bleeding until Mrs. Johnson came up the stairs after midnight and found her staring at the space where a living room had been. Her mother had said, We’ll save for it, and had circled chest like a dog, and then it had come one afternoon on the back of a truck whose bed dropped just above their lawn’s lip and two men had carried it in like a casket and her mother had said, There, and put her hand on it like a boy puts his hand on a hood of a car to make it his. They had filled it through the years with sheets purchased at a department store out at the mall that smelled like perfume that would bribe you into being a better woman than you were. They had filled it with towels you could pet and not feel hate for the softness of them. They had filled it with little phrases of a right future. In the end, Ellen had married Wayne in a room over the community center that had fairy lights strung by the basketball hoop and nuns U-turned over the punch bowl like unfamiliar fish. She had brought the chest to the duplex because her mother would have killed her if she hadn’t, and Wayne had said, That’s a real piece, El, and then the second year in, he had touched her shoulder with the tip of his finger and had left with a toothbrush and gym bag to be with the woman he’d been seeing at the auto parts store for nine months. Ellen had come home from her job at the insurance office and had sat on the cedar chest and had smelled the spice of it sobbing and had hated how the scent did not make her harmless at all. She had thought, How did I become a woman? Because I bought the right towels? Because I saved? Because my mother told me it was what decent women did, and if you were not decent you might as well open the front door and invite the devil in for iced tea?

The young woman was saying, “My grandmother had one like this,” and she touched the brass keyhole with the tip of her middle finger as if it were a forehead she were blessing.

“Eighty,” Ellen said, and then, without the courage of drama, without the attempt to make the world see her as a certain kind of film heroine, she said, “Sixty.” The couple glanced at each other like the weather had changed to their benefit and she thought, I am not the old woman whose love of her things makes her hate you for wanting them. The young man said, “Sixty? Are you sure?” and the question’s small deference was grace to her. She nodded. He looked as if he were tasting the word husband in his mouth and finding it not entirely terrible. He reached for his wallet as if he were reaching for a person’s hand.

They carried the chest out together, one at each end, bodies adjusting themselves, a choreography Ellen had learned with her father and her mother on the days when furniture had been moved across rooms in a bid to make the rooms new for nothing but the cost of a broken toe. The young man’s face was red with the weight as he backed down the front step. The woman, in front, laughed once, a real laugh, the kind that rises out of the diaphragm and makes the ribs look like a xylophone beneath the skin. Ellen followed them down and stood on the sidewalk as they slid the chest into the bed of a pickup whose paint had been replaced in stripes of primer, two colors testifying to a willingness to repair.

The street had the parade feel that comes on certain bright afternoons in small towns when everyone is outside at once, the man with the weedeater, the girl with the scooter, the teen with the latte, the grandmother with a bag of russet apples. Bradford Street had three houses for sale and one with a banner on the porch that read Jesus 2024, and the court down Kidd Lane was full of chalk drawings of a hopscotch board that went nowhere. The air was so clear you could see the little chips in it where it had been scratched. Ellen looked up the block and saw her father once in the skinny shape he had kept till he grew sick, walking toward the house with a paper bag with bread in it and the stems of parsley sticking out the top, and saw her mother coming out to meet him, and their bodies came together in an unshowy habit like two animals who are used to drinking from the same stock tank.

“Ellie,” someone called, and it was Betty from church, who had always fretted about Ellen in rough little out-loud ways, as if the words were hands that could pat a head into being all right. Ellen had stopped going to church when she’d moved to Cleveland and had then moved to Columbus and then had come back here when her mother had become a house, staying home at night because television had absolved her of walking to the car. Betty had a hand on the boy’s head; this was the record boy, whose face went a little shuttered at the name Ellie, which belonged to a person under twelve. “Honey, I want that Pyrex,” Betty said, and then, “Your mother—she was a pistol,” and laughed exactly in the way her mother would have hated, a loud downstroke, like a man chopping wood. “I never saw a woman get so riled about a bingo card. Lord, I miss her.”

“I do too,” Ellen said. It was close enough to a lie to count and close enough to the truth to make her voice make a small joke of itself. He’d brought her a soda after the funeral, this boy, placed it on the table with the ham and the potato salad and she had loved him for not trying to speak.

“What about the china?” Betty asked in the way people did, the thing you couldn’t possibly be selling always a question. “You keeping the china?”

“No,” Ellen said. “She never used it. We always had paper plates at Christmas after Dad died; she said, Life’s too short for dishes.” The china had daisies around the rim and a band of silver her mother had enjoyed holding on their laps to look at and think about a life where she would have had curiosity about sauces. “The girl with the baby took it,” she lied, and Betty made a small sound that was both approval and grief.

The afternoon tipped. Voices grew soft toward the end, people less hungry as the picking went spare. A tall woman with a streak of red through her hair took the orange mixing bowl that had mixed a thousand three-ingredient cakes and maybe could have mixed a few more, and Ellen in that moment could have given it away, could have stopped choking the money out of the day. She looked toward the kitchen and saw the place on the wall where the rack had been with the wooden pegs for cups and felt the shape of absence in her eyes like a migraine. She had left the rack; no one wanted it, the pegs were coming loose. She had unscrewed it and put it in the hall with a free sign and watched five people look at it with the face you make at the dog someone is trying to give away.

By four, the flow had slowed and then stopped. The light pooled low on the maple and then went varnished, the particular Ohio dusk that makes the air seem fur-lined. No one came down the walk except a jogger with two leashed pit bulls who looked at the open door and looked away as if there was nothing in this world that could be of use to all lives. A woman in a minivan pulled up and idled and then left. Ellen stood in the doorway and felt her jaw release. Her back ached in that way that felt ancestral, a memory in the muscles of all the mothers who had stood at all the edges of all the rooms saying no, yes, we’ll see.

Inside, the house had that sound emptying houses get, you never hear it until the couch has gone and then you know what couches mean to human noise. It was a mixed sound, not silence but the memory of voices dwindled to the bones of them. Ellen stepped into the living room and the far wall looked much too far away. The imprint of the sofa cushion remained on the floor as two pale rectangles. Dust had drawn a picture of where the piano had been and then not been forty years ago; it was the same place, all the way down. She went into the kitchen and stood by the sink with her hands on the rim. The porcelain had a notched crack in it her mother had insisted was not a crack, and over the years they had learned to work around it in the way of small truths. Ellen could see her mother warping in the reflection of the window above the sink like a ghost who had been microwaved. She reached for a dish towel and then laughed because she was done taking care of the day, no one would bleed on these counters now, no one would ask for popsicles, there would be no more burnt popcorn in this house unless someone new made it, and the idea that all kinds of small disasters would happen here with people Ellen did not know made her heart mischief around in her chest in a way that alarmed and comforted her.

The card table was a mess of one-dollar bills that looked like the leaves of plants, like any amount of money you could call success until the mail came Monday. The cigar box was fat and heavy. She had kept a running tally in her head and on a Post-it, had circled good numbers when they came, had surrendered bad ones. She had haggled exactly three times, twice with pleasure because the face of the other person had done that beaming of the newly lucky and once with a humiliation she would teach herself not to recall, with the woman whose lower lip lifted just a little after Ellen had said, Okay, yes, take it, eight dollars is fine.

She took the money to the bedroom. Up here it still smelled like Avon powder. The light in the window made a square on the floor the exact size of the old blue rug. On the dresser, which was one of the few things no one had wanted, a cheap piece, veneer peeled like a sunburn, stood the photograph Ellen had finally taken down this morning after hesitating too long last night. It was her mother in a polka-dot shirt in 1983, in front of the photo of Dad that the church had put out at his service with the lilies on either side as if her father had been a Catholic. Her mother’s face had settled that summer into the shape it would keep until the last weeks—the chin new-widowed women get, the mouth that won’t soften enough to cross the street for a kiss. The frame’s glass had been carefully Windexed each week for decades. Ellen wiped it now with the corner of her shirt. She put the frame in the box she had packed with her own name in marker—the few things she would keep: a pie pan with a dent in the rim like a signature, two recipe cards with her mother’s flat careful hand, a thimble with a nick in the rim, the little angel made of corn husks that had come every December and had made Ellen believe they lived in a storybook for one hour. The angel had black dots for eyes and the corn husk hair that made men’s sex blush and retreat into a little boy’s.

Downstairs, two women arrived as if we had called them to come later, as if there were a secret code for being last. One was maybe forty, the other sixty. A mother and a daughter, perhaps, though they could easily have been friends, the younger doing the older’s hair, the older lending the younger a book she thought she’d appreciate. They stepped in and looked around at the air the way you do in a church when all the church is done. “Are you closed?” the younger woman asked, and when Ellen said, “No, not closed,” the younger woman said, “Thank God,” and laughed, because the day had been too much for her too and she didn’t even know it. They walked around together, their arms brushing, and the older woman picked up the candlesticks that had never been lit, and the younger touched the back cover of a cookbook and the gesture was such that Ellen could have wept because it was gentle to a thing that had not been used gently. They paid for a few things, leaving a dollar on the card table that they thought belonged to the price and Ellen waved it back and said, Thank you for coming, and meant it as if they had brought bread.

After they left, there were only a handful. A man took the laundry basket and left the laundry soap under the sink. A girl with half her head shaved came and bought the thrift-store painting of a cow for five. Nobody took the magazines, the stack of Good Housekeeping from years when it had told Ellen how to remake dinners from two cans and a half-cup of fear. The house made the sound of a person exhaling after being held under too long. Ellen walked it. She went into the small second bedroom where she and Mark had slept, the window with its screen patched with a square of lesser screen, a tight-laced set of grays. She opened the closet and found on the shelf the shoebox she had written everything in on the top in her twelve-year-old rage, and everything had turned out to be the hard-mapped faces of boys she had never kissed, a lanyard, dried corsage. She took out the corsage and the petals folded themselves away as if a polite person was leaving a party, and she put it back and shut the box and this time, did not take it with her.

On the hall wall, under the clock, there were marks in pencil from two separate decades. One was their height marks, gone now under the paint; she had painted over them after her mother had fallen and had bled her head on the lintel and had said, Don’t look at me. The other was a set of phone numbers written there in her mother’s hand when her mother had worked a little side job sewing cushions for a guy in a shop out by the county line; the names had been men’s names, each with a thin phone number, and she had been twelve and had thought, This is a list of my mother’s lovers. She had called one once, in the heat of a summer when she’d wanted a change of air, when she’d wanted the movie of her life to begin with the way she could slam a receiver down and ring the house with a story finding its own ending. A man’s voice had answered and she had hung up and had run upstairs and had lay panting on her bed as if she had run a mile, and then she had gone downstairs to the sewing machine and watched the needle and her mother’s steady terrible hand. She touched the wall now and felt the bone under the paint, the two-by-four that took the nails in its mouth.

She thought of calling Mark. She thought of telling him that the hope chest had gone to two children who would hope openly, not from behind their teeth the way she had. She thought of telling him that she had given the birdhouse to a boy and that now the birds would make a mess of someone else’s porch and that would be a blessing, that there were worse things to scrub away. She thought of telling him that she had been good at this, at the choosing and the letting go, and that she had not taken the things his wife would have misnamed ugly on their porch anyway. She did not call him. She stood in the hall and listened to the muffled sound of her neighbor’s television through the wall, the evangelical preacher in a blue suit talking about the manifest gift. She thought of the blue suit in her father’s photograph. She thought of how things were the same even when they were not.

The light had drained. She went through and turned on the lamps one by one and it made of the rooms novels, soft circled portions of light like decisions. She counted the money in the cigar box and did not count it exactly right and then counted again and did, and then she put a rubber band around it as if the band could keep time itself from falling out. She took off her shoes and went out onto the front porch in her socks and stood while the air in her lungs felt both clean and wrong. Somewhere down the block somebody was splitting wood. The whap of it made a bird noise in the trees. Someone was frying onions; it made her stomach go tight and then release.

“You did it,” Mrs. Howe said from her short swords of mums as she stood up and wiped her hands on her jeans. Ellen nodded because nodding was better than any sentence. Mrs. Howe said, “I can take anything you’re done with to Goodwill, if you want. We got my niece’s truck.” She had the voice of a person a person could rely on. Ellen said, “Tomorrow,” and meant to go through what was left again tonight and to keep one more time not keeping things. Mrs. Howe nodded. “You need soup,” she said, and went toward the back door.

Alone again, Ellen went back in and shut the front door to the thin evening and the postcards of conversation that would go on without this house having anything to do with it. The silence came in, a good dog. She went to the kitchen and took out one glass from the cabinet that was supposed to go in the donations tomorrow and filled it with water and then, not liking the taste of the tap water, ran it again and stood drinking half of it at the sink. The glass was one of the cheap ones that came in a twelve-pack at the big box store. She thought she could feel at the rim the indentation of all the lips that had crossed it, a small geology of mouth. She washed it and put it back. She stood for a second longer in the kitchen and then reached up and took it back down again and put it in the box she was taking. The gesture made the smallest sound, a moving of an object across thin cardboard.

Outside, a car horn sounded in two shy toots like a goodbye at the end of a school day. Ellen stood with her hands on the edge of the counter and thought of her mother at the stove, the way she had not known how to mother beautifully and had surrounded that fact with acts of such competence that you could almost, on good days, forget you had wanted a softer thing. Her mother had had no use for a girl who cried and had been, on many days, a woman who cried in the car idling in the driveway with the radio up so no one knew. Ellen had gone years at a time angry with her and then had, at the last, lifted her mother’s arms and washed the sour places with a folded white cloth and had felt no anger at all the way no one can be angry with a newborn for needing to be fed.

She turned off the light in the kitchen and then, because light makes monsters in empty houses, turned it back on. She went into the living room and sat on the floor where the sofa had been and leaned against the wall and let her body go slack. The wall was cool and smelled faintly of dust and lemon oil and her mother’s last perfume, the one from the drugstore in the square bottle with the black bow, a cheap and decent imitation of a better thing. She put her palms on the floor and pressed down as if to find a pulse. She might have to sleep here one more night if the trash didn’t go out in time or if the Goodwill truck didn’t come tomorrow. She would lie on the bedroom carpet with her coat over her like a fox sleeping by a log. She had a bottle of Advil in her purse and a cellphone that would light her mother’s dark if she needed to look into a closet one more time and be certain. The realtor, a blond woman with a helpful voice that had a hard center, would come by in the morning and put a lockbox on the door, and Ellen would hand her a key, and when she did, she would say something like, Take care of it, as if the house would hear and could feel it.

She got up and went to the front door. She stood for a minute with her hand on the knob, a posture her mother had had when she was deciding if she would accept a thing. She opened the door and the air was so chilly it made her nipples hard in a slapstick, human way. She went down and pulled the tomato stake with its sign and then went to the yard and pulled the one there too, and it came with a little grunt that felt like a small birth. She leaned both stakes against the porch railing. A moth flung itself against the porch light three times, indecisive, then circled off toward the maple. Across the street, in the window of the Pinto’s house, someone had set up a Halloween village with three paper houses lit from within and a ghost on a stick. The ghost’s smile made her teeth ache.

Inside again, she turned off the lamps. The dimness was a mercy now. She could see the bones of the house when it was night. The furniture had hid they were old to her and now the house admitted itself to be an old body but not a bad one. She went to the bedroom and caught her reflection in the mirror briefly, the one they would leave because you couldn’t sell a mirror with corrosion around its edges and a place where the silver had leached away into nothing. She saw her mother’s mouth in her mouth and felt what people mean when they say inheritance. She stuck out her tongue at herself, causing the face to blur from grief into foolishness. She put the photograph of her mother into the box with the pie pan and the corn husk angel like a sandwich. She taped the box shut in a stupid, satisfying ritual. She brought the box to the front hall and put it on the mat and then picked it up again because you should never ever leave anything in front of a door, it was bad luck, it made a trip.

Before she left, she went to the hallway and put her palm on the wall again, right where her hand had fit since she was five. It was muscle memory and grace and simple adios. “I’m going now,” she said in a voice that starred past herself to someone who might not have been there. She did not wait for an answer. Her voice had been answered by this house for fifty-one years. She could not expect it to suddenly begin speaking English.

On the way out, she paused at the card table and put into her purse the Sharpie, the roll of tape, the small scissors that had fit in her hand like a thing meant. She picked up the bell by its iron hoop, the cast-iron bell her father had brought home from a flea market when she was nine and had hung for no reason beside the back door because the idea of a thing that could summon had pleased him. She turned it in her hands. It was too heavy for her to carry to her car right now and she did not, in truth, want it. But she did not want to leave it, either. It was the kind of thing a person would walk off with later and keep in a garage and then in twenty years sell for more money than had been exchanged in this entire afternoon. She put it back on the table. The bell made a small unearthly sound when she set it down, the metal singing itself when it met the cheap veneer of table. It sounded exactly like the last spoon in a drawer hitting up against another and then settling.

She turned off the porch light and then turned it back on because she would be out to the car and back, three trips, and she would not have to fumble for the step. She pulled the front door gently until it closed itself. The latch caught. The house held.

When she came back the second time for the last box, her arms already tired, her shoulder an engine that had stalled, she had to hook the door with her foot because she hadn’t set something in to wedge it, and when she did footage through the door opening like a person accustomed to lifting and carrying in small houses all her life, the box knocked against the jamb in the exact place where all the good coats had always brushed in winter, and an old, gray, worked-into shiver of paint fell, as fine and light as dust from a moth’s wings. She held very still to watch it, and it settled on the toe of her shoe like a blessing that did not know what kind it was. She breathed out. She stepped carefully, then, and went to the hall table to get the key. It was the old brass one, not the new spares with rubber heads the realtor had cut, and it left little gold flakes on her palm. You could tell a story with that. She didn’t need to. She put the key in the purse with the money and the Sharpie and the corn husk angel beneath the lid of the box and then turned off the lamp by pinching the switch with two fingers and felt for a moment like a person turning off a stranger’s bedside. She left the box by the door because she had to go lock the back and check the stove like a woman who would never not be a woman living in a house.

In the backyard, the grass smelled like a place you could sleep if the law allowed it. The neighbor boy had already somehow hung the birdhouse in his own small tree, and it was canted, and the sight filled her with such affection for error that her mouth went open without a sound. Something squeaked at the far fence. The sky was a gray bowl. She pulled the back door and turned the lock and checked the bolt as if it were her child’s face. She went through the laundry room, the place where all the worst arguments had always happened because people were always standing in doorways there carrying arms of work that looked like insult, and she paused because the washing machine still had some water in the rim of the gasket, a small lake she wanted suddenly with pure greed to dry off with the cuff of her sweater. She put the cuff against it and the water went into the wool and she had the mad thought that had she this whole time been drying the small water of her family off the rim of the day, she might have saved more of them. She wiped her hand on her jeans and laughed.

At the end she stood in the front door a last moment, the light around the porch bulb a warm, decent yellow, not holy, not accusing. She could see the marks of shoes on the floor, some circles and some haphazard V-charmed angles like geese on a lake. She could see the little piece of blue tape on the edge of the hutch door that she had missed when she’d pulled off the prices and she reached over and took it between thumb and forefinger and tugged and the adhesive fought her just enough to make it worthy. She rolled the tape into a tiny pelleted ball and for lack of anything better to do with it, put it in her mouth and bit it because it tasted like a medicine that would either save her or nothing at all. Then she took it out and stuck it on the inside of her wallet and closed the wallet and zipped it into the purse and picked up the last box. It was light. She had thought it would be heavier. She put her hip against the door in the exact old motion and pulled it to behind her with the same old care. The latch hit the striker plate with a sound she had known the shape of in the womb.

On the porch, she balanced the box on the rail while she dug for the key. She slid it into the lock and turned it and felt how, even in closing, this act was a giving. A car turned the corner at the top of the street, then kept going. Somewhere far off a train drew a curtain across the county. She lifted the box again, and as she did, the corn husk angel tipped and looked down at the porch boards like a Blessed Virgin in a tripped-up pageant trying to remember which direction to face. Ellen steadied it with her thumb and all at once remembered a morning at eight, frost silvering the grass, her mother coming in the back door with cheeks like apples and saying into the house, You can hear it, and Ellen had said, Hear what, and her mother had said, The earth hardening, and Ellen had laughed the way children laugh when they believe they are being lied to, and then the two of them had stood in the streaked kitchen near the clock and listened until the sound they maybe heard rose through them like a fact, or like its opposite, which is often the same comfort.

She went down the two porch steps with the box. She did not stumble. A leaf came down and caught in her hair and she reached up and it was stuck there, a small weight, and she let it be a minute, a little hand on the top of her head making a benediction that it didn’t know it made. She carried the box to the car and put it in the trunk beside the other box and the bag with the flag and the bag with hairbrush and the two spoons she had not meant to keep and had and the jar with the buttons. She let the trunk down slow and then pushed it the last inch to make it latch and the sound the latch made was the sound she had been waiting to hear all day and it was not tragic. It sounded like a machine working right. She stood for a second with her hands flat on the lid and then let them fall. She turned in the yellow light to face the house. Its windows made small squares of black like eyes shut tight. She lifted a hand as if one lifts a hand to a neighbor and did not wave because you did not wave at your own face in the morning mirror. Then she got in the car and started it and the radio came on with news and she turned it off and sat for a second, her hands light on the wheel, the way you do at the end of a story when you don’t know if you should clap. She waited a breath and then she put the car in drive and its tires made their cheerful, unpoetic sound on the street, and she pulled out and drove slowly past the house and did not look toward it overly much. Only once, at the very end of the block, when she had to check for an oncoming truck and there was none, she turned her head—one last reflexive human glance—and saw the porch light, still on, the square of yellow on the step, and for the briefest moment the shape of her mother sitting on the rail like a girl, looking down at her hands, then no one there at all, the railing only itself again, the street a simple street, paper fluttering half-loose on a stake in a small Ohio yard.
