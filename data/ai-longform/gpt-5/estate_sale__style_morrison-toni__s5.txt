By the time the first woman laid her two hard fingers on the porch rail and said Is this where—? the house had already decided. Windows swollen all summer, breath held, eaves sweating their rust, the house had made up its mind to leak what it had held and let the rest clamp down. Like a hand opening and a throat closing, it would release the things and keep the names. Vera Mae understood this as soon as she bent to tape the FOR SALE signs to cardboard and set the rows of blue glass jars out where the light could find them. She understood it in the way the kitchen door refused to catch and the smell of camphor from the cedar chest pushed itself into her blouse. She knew it when she felt the little scratch of grit under her sandal, that baby-sanded place where her brother had skidded when he was ten with a belly full of sugar water and scraped off half his elbow. Even the grit had a way of calling itself by its old name.

The woman on the porch had a red purse big enough to carry a ham. Her hair was a smudge of cotton candy and her bra strap showed. She looked past Vera into the living room that still wore the pressed dressing of Sunday: sofa spread smoothed, crocheted doilies pinned flat on armrests, a globe lamp with a crank that no longer turned, and the upright piano shy and hulking under a shawl. Is this where the estate—? the woman said again.

Yes, Vera said. Here.

She said it once softly, then again with her mother’s crispness, as though the woman might be hard of hearing or simply disrespectful. There was a part of her that wanted to say No, not here, not you. As if by answering such a woman she could wedge herself into a different day that would not be this one, the day the living came to take possession of the dead.

By the time the white man with the neck tattoo asked if somebody could help him lift the iron bed and slide it sideways through the hall, there were twenty of them and then forty. They were from Overton proper and from the strip of road they now called the Heights, from out by the river where men who worked nights at the sausage plant slept through the sun those years ago, from new-built places with smooth brick where lawns were shaved daily and women pushed baby prams that did not every now and then become grocery carts. They came with tape measures and phones and a hog’s patience and with faces that seemed to be choosing what kind of day it was going to be as they stood on the porch and peered in. They came with their loved ones and with their loneliness held out like a check, ready to be endorsed.

She had laid the things out not the way Mr. Eaton, who did this professionally, had told her—big things first, small things under a glass—but the way her hands had insisted. The frog ashtray went beside the picture of Jesus whose eyes followed you no matter where you ran, and the salt cellar like a dove with clear wings nested under the portrait of a woman not in their family at all, an aunt by mouth only, because to children any woman who knew when and how to cuss you counted. She set the sewing machine by the back door so it could smell the yard, the deep green of it, and put her mother’s Bible not on display but on the mantle with a price tag that said Not For Sale. The things that were hers, she had lifted last night into the trunk of her car and into the space behind the seat: the pronged fork with the handle like bone that her mother had used to pick the pecans clean; the jar of corn silk where the end of a braid lay tucked, spiky and gold even now; a strip of wallpaper her father had cut wrong and used to shim the kitchen table leg, a strip she had once licked to see if glue tasted like paper smelled. She should take the dressers, her friend Dot had said. She should take the bed where years lay arranges like spoons. But the forks, the shims, the braid—these were the weight she could carry. These were the things that did not look like anything to anybody who did not know.

Mr. Eaton had walked through with her the previous week clicking his pen like an undertaker. He’d said, You got to be ready for all sorts. Somebody’s going to ask for the pot with the scorch marks and somebody’s going to tell you the marble top to that table is not worth a dime anymore because something or other, and somebody’s going to ask who died. He’d paused by the mother of pearl-handled knife and frowned in a way Vera did not appreciate. That’s a family piece, he said approvingly, and she wanted to snatch it up like a baby, like a bird who thought the hole in the sky was reason to throw itself.

Her mother had died there in the house with the curtains drawn and ice on the back of her neck. It was May and the maple in front was gushing, the sift of seed helicopters spinning to the sidewalk. The neighbor girl had sat and plucked them apart and tried to stick them on her nose while the man from the hospital asked questions and the younger deacon from Mount Olive had held his hat in his lap like a reason. There wasn’t even time for the doctor to arrive. It was morning and then it was noon and then their Philadelphia cousin was calling the long way around, demanding what he could do from so far away, and Vera had said Come later. She had been the one there because the call had not rung for the others, or else it had rung but not landed. Her brother had said he’d be there after he got off shift but he was two counties over in a blue room listening to a machine. Her daughter had cried on a bus and told her to wait, as though a breath could be pinned to a corkboard and kept from flitting.

Now, June again and the grass making hay of their ankles, the house was open to strangers. A tall white boy with crushed ears came in and said Grandma had a clock like that except it was green and see-through, and a little girl with a ballerina bun touched the top of the sewing basket the way you would touch a sleeping dog's ear. People’s mouths were full of other houses. All the while, as they stopped before the jelly cupboard and the Jesus eyes, before the china with its hairline crack and the green rocker with its silent goose neck, she had to make change. She had to count money for this and that and hold a yellow pad where she wrote down names like wood: BED KING 150 CASH MAN IN RED HAT; PIANO INTERESTED KATHERINE; BLUE JARS 8 EA WOMAN PINK PURSE—. She had to say Yes and No and I can do fifty and We’re holding that for a pick-up later and The chair goes with the table, it’s a set. She had to say all that while her own mouth was a cold pot on a dead burner.

We remember when that table came, a voice seemed to say as she ran her finger along the oval. We remember the wagon, not the truck. The year the snow fell like cotton bales, like clean sheets refusing to come indoors. We remember your mother standing at the door in her slip and a sweater, hair plaited rope-thick and smelling of Dixie Peach, saying Lord have mercy let it not be cracked. That voice did not come from any particular place. It was not her mother’s or the house’s alone. It was the way that sometimes when a choir holds a note too long the chord that arrives is not anybody’s voice but feels like it belongs to your entire skin.

She looked up to see Dot across the yard shaking her head at a man in a camo cap, her finger wagging like a stump. Dot had come to save her, that was how she’d put it. I’ll come and keep the men from carrying your stove out for five dollars, she had said, and sure enough she was now wrangling the tea cart from a man who had already rolled it halfway to the driveway. How much for the cart, he said, and Vera, mouth slow, said Sixty, and Dot, without turning, said Eighty. Then Dot leaned in and said to the man in a tender soft voice reserved for the truly foolish and her own grandbaby That sticker there? That do not say Yours.

Through the open door, the afternoon was a shoulder that wouldn’t quit leaning. The bees had made their low, persistent line between the white clover and the busted plank in the fence where they stored something sticky. The husky next door chased his shadow and the boy from across the street was selling orange freezer pops from the little styrofoam chest his uncle had given him last winter filled with rabbits. The sun took the sharp corners off tombstones and trash barrels alike. The air had a taste that told you the lake was less than a breath away, even if you could not see its flat steel-blue from Beecham Avenue. A woman in an orange shirt tried to open the cellar door which was jammed by its own history and failed, then laughed and said She got you shut tight, honey. She? Vera said, though she knew. The woman said The one who cooked here, and Vera nodded, because even strangers sometimes knew when a kitchen had a reigning presence as a courtroom has a judge and a child has a scab.

She kept seeing her mother’s hands in everything. The thin ice-blue veins like leftover chalk lines in a school yard; the brown knuckles that had made even pity look useful. Her mother had not been what anybody would call gentle. She had sent them into the snow with duct-taped gloves and into the opinions of the world with only half of a wish. She made stew like an argument and pie like a contradiction and told lies only on behalf of others. When the old man up the street had died, more than one person had found themselves fluid with some necessary detail after Pearl had leaned into them with her hand on their shoulder and said Now what do you really need? When they had buried a boy who had been all football-laughter and that junk that turned the backs of your eyes into salt, it was Pearl who banged the spoons and got casseroles into foil and somehow found a blanket that the mother would accept. Her voice came now and then to Vera across the afternoon. Don’t let them people lowball you on my good stuff, she said. And later, to the house, she said You hold on to that window. I caught the wind there.

The younger man with the neck tattoo had a girl with him with hair like water. They wanted the iron bed. It had held so much: first a mother with her cramps like a dance somebody did only when their eyes were shut; then a father when the long winter cough that swore it was not TB finally lay down full length; then the daughter who wet it with milk bursts almost blue; then the son who came home bloody and slept upside down across it as though to confuse his shame. The iron had warmed and held and warmed again. Vera followed them with her hand on the post as they turned it sideways and thought that the hands could still be on it, a little oil of work and habit pressed into that black. You sure you ain’t got a truck, Dot was saying in the front yard, and the girl said We can strap it to the roof, which made Dot raise her face in four directions as though to ask what manner of fools the Lord had decided to send her today.

There was the man with the red hat and the beige socks pulled up too high who wanted the piano. He ran his small square fingers along the keys that had new color under their lids from years of slow light. Who played this? he said.

Me and my brother, Vera said. Which was almost true. Her brother had banged out the bass of despair and joy and she had picked at plump chords and then stopped altogether the winter the older boys followed them home and said they should come around back and hear what thunder really sounded like. After they had run, they had closed the piano and laid their faces against it and laughed and cried in the old way that makes no space between. Their mother played with two fingers when she was thinking of something. A sweet little clink and a hand rubbing the side of her dress in circles, then stopping, then picking out You Are My Sunshine and then stopping again. The man in the red hat said He could make it sing again. He said he knew somebody who knew somebody. He lifted the panel and pressed middle C, and the note that came out was a throat clearing itself of decades. Even now the hammers would strike if asked. But it was not the sound she would miss. It was the listening. It was the way the house sat still when Pearl slid her whispering hand over the keys and thought.

That’ll be two hundred, Dot called from the hall as though reading their very thoughts like mail she had steamed open. Vera said nothing. The man in the red hat said I was thinking one-fifty because the left pedal’s gone to Jesus, and Dot laughed without warmth. I don’t know nobody named Jesus who needs a pedal, she said. The man looked at Vera, who looked at the keys and then at his socks and then at the door where the day was having its own daylight.

She did not like the way the house had been looked at for years by men with makeshift professional clucks in their mouths. This one needs a roof, they’d say, or the foundation’s got a tilt you can see from church. As though the house were a man who had to prove he wasn’t drunk. You could not hear that kind of talk and keep yourself from thinking it too. You could start parroting doubt’s sharp little phrases even to a place that fed you. But the house had stood for seventy-one years. It had held water in basins and gossip in corners and laughter that brought people through doors they had only ever glanced at. And it had held grief not as a guest who had overstayed but as a tenant you had gradually realized could be a decent neighbor if you gave him the small things he asked for: a chair by a window, a clean cup to sit in the sink with at night.

Near noon, a group of young men parked a shining black SUV a little too close to a peony bush planted by an aunt who had driven five hundred miles with a biscuit tin between her knees the week Vera was born. She had never met that aunt, who had always been too busy with other people’s births and headaches to stay, but the bush had a way of reminding them there are certain things you can carry for a long time in a car if you keep the lid tight. The men wore the kind of T-shirts that hug a heart and drank from sweating green bottles and had the jumpy bright mood that comes from football you cannot yet play because the bodies you might slam into are now shoppers and their wives are right here.

They moved toward the tools. Every house in that part of Overton had a set of tools that showed a man’s best belief about himself: something rusting that could be coaxed into function when the right words were spoken; something fancy given at Christmas that never touched a screw; and one thing handmade that did better than anything bought. Hers included a hammer with a black handle that had pulled nails from three generations of mistakes and a level with a bubble that had made men put their heads on the side like dogs. Her brother—Horace, called Horse as a boy and called worse as a man by people who did not always mean it cruelly—had brought a box of things from his own garage the day after their mother died. He had set it down and said Nothing sharp, because their mother had scolded sharpness out of their hands all their lives. What you going to do with a box of nails when all that’s poor in you ain’t never been hammered right? she had once said. The young ones lifted the level, the hammer, a spool of wire that had a mind to leap off the nail and unwind its own shiny intestine. One of them, the tallest, turned to Vera and said Ma’am, he did not want to insult but also did not want to give more money than this afternoon required. He said, How much for the whole shebang.

Vera ran her tongue over her teeth as though a thin sugar, invisible, had been laid there. The word shebang landed soft between them and rolled under the table like a marble. She saw the afternoon gather itself at the edges and move away by a step. Ten, she said. The boys looked at one another, at the level, at the hammer that had been in the slow dance of her father’s hands. Twenty, she said, before they could move. Twenty and you get that there vice too. They peered into the dim of the cellar where the vice waited like a fat metal mouth and said Twenty, okay, and she said Thirty, and they laughed like boys and said Twenty-Five. She smiled without showing it and said Twenty-Five and bring it up yourself. When they bent to lift the vice, their good clothes burning with that iron smell, the one boy said My Papa used to have a vice like this, and another said He used to beat your Mama with it too, and the yard opened up with a small shock and then closed, and the boys pretended not to hear themselves. They hoisted their purchase up into the hum and heat. It was a long afternoon. It had room enough for every kind of truth and silence.

The man from down the block—the one everybody called LeRoy though that had never been his name—who kept his lawn proud as a sheriff and whose wife wore red lipstick to pull the gray down into her cheeks—came and wanted the record player. He said he had lost his in ’83 when the boys came in the second time and took the whole milk crate too. He looked at Vera in the way men look at you when they need to be let off for what was not yours to pardon. She pulled the record player’s plug from the extension and wound it around the belly, and she thought of her father the winter he decided to be a father again and came in cold as angry salt and said If anybody comes to our back door at night, you don’t open, you hear? You speak through the wood and you say What you want. The wood is a mouth too. She sold LeRoy the player for thirty and then put in the box three records she knew would not lie to anybody: Otis, Billie, and a gospel one with no title and a picture of a hand.

A small white dog peed on the lilacs. An aunt she did not claim saw her across the yard and lifted both hands in the air as though to let the Lord know she was there, then came to the porch and kissed the air by Vera’s cheek and said She is present and absent; we shall not all sleep but we shall all be changed—, and then asked if there was any coffee. It was the kind of day where the Bible lost its period and the pot that had once been full of coffee all day long had been put away as a tribute and as spite. Vera did have a jug of sweet tea sweating on the counter and a bag of cheep cookies sullen as oiled washers. She pushed the tea at whoever made an ask. People drank and said Mmm and Thank you, the same words used for births and wakes and painting Saturdays and pity.

There were children too. They wandered in and out underfoot, their chairs short and their ankles scabby the way knees must be to prove you still believed in air. A boy with an eye that turned in when he got excited found the Mason jar of buttons and said Truly? for a dollar I can have this? As though a dollar were otherworldly and the jar held something more than circles of color and a slip of bone. He took it and he shook it, the clattering sound as pretty as pennies in a melter, and said I will pay you on Friday because that is allowance day, and Vera experienced a love so light she hated it, it was that little, and wanted to shoo him away for his faith. That he returned after six in a mad gallop with two dimes and two nickels and forty pennies should not have mattered as much as it did. But his rush was a small clean thing in the day’s long-worn cloth.

Now and then, a buyer would take something and hold it in both hands as though it were a question. A salt shaker with a rooster crows only when the lid is turned, a framed print of a boy with a tear drawn onto his cheek with so much belief you had to forgive it, a green glass ashtray the weight of good news. They would hold the thing and look toward her and say Tell me about this. Sometimes she would. Sometimes she would say That sat on the window ledge and my mother used to watch the squirrels fuss while she peeled, and sometimes she would say My brother took a whip for that when he knocked it down, and sometimes she would say That has no story I know, make one yourself. There was a woman, thin as a new shoot, who picked up the black skillet and kissed it and said My mama had one of these and Lord the cornbread you could make could walk up the table by itself, which made Vera show her teeth. When the woman said she was from Yazoo City, Miss’sippi, something in the floorboards put its elbows on its knees and leaned in. The skillet left the house for five dollars and spoke with a new tongue down a side street.

Her daughter had promised to come for the afternoon, to help with the kinds of bargains that cut your fingers if you ran them wrong. The child—grown, mouth smart enough to tip you back at your own table went by the name she had chosen in middle school and had never let go. Sky. As if naming herself after upstairs she could forget the rug burns on her elbows and the smell of Vicks and the fact that she had not been born flying but had to be lifted like any girl. She texted at two and said Traffic. She texted again at three and said Almost. She arrived at four like weather in a short dress and good sandals and a scarf around her head tied arrowwise. She brought the beeping of a different city with her, her ears still on the flat to the middle distance. She said Mama with a soft open mouth, and they touched their cheeks something like a kiss and something like two years of refusal, and then the girl turned and began to pull her anger forward like a barrel.

What you think you’re doing with Mama’s blue dishes? she said, pointing to the table where Vera had placed the set in a hopeful circle. Those are going assigned.

Assigned to who.

Me. And if you say something slick I’m going to act like a thirteen-year-old in front of these people. The set of the girl’s mouth was her mother’s years ago and not her mother’s now. It had a citizen’s certainty and a child’s sloppy grace and the familiar shape that made a person wonder for the hundredth time how cartilage could carry memory the way paper did and houses did and crackers.

They will not fit in your place, Vera said. She could imagine her daughter’s little kitchen with its shrew bright utensils that had never seen gravy twice. The house her daughter lived in now had three locks and the good sense to ignore the men in the hallway. It was a house where you could call somebody if you smelled gas. That did not mean there was room in the cabinets for a service for eight whose pattern was called Blue Willing or Blue Willow or just Blue. Sky said I didn’t say nothing about fitting. I said them plates got me already.

They might as well then, Vera said. Take them. And please get up under that man’s elbow and keep him from taking the spice tins for fifty cents. They got cumin in still that ain’t never coming out of his food.

Sky—who could charm and bruise with equal speed—stepped into her doing like it was a dress she knew and had once loved and had unfairly outgrown. She stood by the tins and the man’s elbow and said in a voice syrupless as a city clerk Sir, you got something see me and I will see that you get it except for the things on this here short list and she patted her scarf for show. She did not have a list. She had only the same pulled tight sorrow her mother wore, but brighter. When she turned to Vera, she kept touching the doors with the backs of her fingers as though checking for heat. Mama, you still going to Uncle Horse’s after this? she said. He said he’d make riblets. Vera said she did not know. She did not want somebody else’s grill smoke in her hair on top of the you of this day. But you tended your brother even when he reminded you of a bicycle with no second gear that you lent and never got returned.

Mr. Eaton had told her a story once about a woman who had tried to run a sale herself and ended up crying into a piece of satin. She had beckoned him over and asked Was it wrong to sell the hat I wore when my boy came back, and he had told her something that had sounded like sense at the time but that even an hour later turned watery and cheap on the tongue: If you keep it, you are just keeping a piece of board that thought it saw a sky. Everything gets to pass through anyway. He had meant when the water comes. He had meant when the dirt claims. He had not meant today when your neighbor took home your mother’s kitchen table like a carton of ice cream.

You can stop anytime, a voice said sometime near five, and she knew it was not the house nor her mother nor her God but herself from a year ago saying that to herself on the worst of TV nights, when the man on the screen said something tender to somebody young and pretty and you couldn’t find the remote. You can stop anytime is a sentence that lies as pretty as it can. The afternoon had gathered into itself and lay belly-down across the living room floor. The buyers had thinned to a trickle and the ones remaining had the look of people trying to climb stairs that keep changing. They wanted something that would let them tell their wife or their aging mother that the day had not been for nothing. They wanted a quilt square to pin somewhere in themselves. They wanted to go home different.

One of them, a woman heavy in the generous way of boiling rice, wanted the Jesus eyes. How much, she asked, fingering the edge of the frame where the varnish had peeled up and had been pressed back by some mouth-pursed attempt. Five, Vera said, too quick, thinking of old Lena who had given her the print in the first place, who had said sashaying It ain’t right, Vera Mae, for your children to grow up without somebody watching them in that room, it’s why they so bad. The woman counted out four ones and a pile of change and then pressed Eva’s hand with gum-scented fingers. He watch you too, she said. Not just them. Vera lifted an eyebrow that the woman could not see and thought Oh. We will be watched in the pantry and in the slow hall and on the seat of the iron bed that was going to slide out the door any minute. She murmured something that might have been Amen and might have been Nothing.

The long piece of mirror in the hall that had seen children graduate into Sunday and lovers catch their own hunger bent and unbent—it had seen her own mother stand in a slip after her father ran off that third time and say to herself in the glass, No more, as though she really meant it, as though she could keep promises from evaporating—this mirror now watched a man lift it and ask How much for this. He was gentle with it. He was the kind who would cradle a song. He had a scar under one eye like a small comma. She wanted to say Give me your lunch. Give me your resting place on Sundays. Give me the sound of your wife laughing behind a door. But she said Fifty, and he winced ripe, and she shrugged as though all money belonged to the weather. Forty, she said. Then Thirty-Five. The man put down the mirror against the wall like a woman standing to fix her dress and said I can do thirty, and Dot lit a cigarette in the yard and turned away and said her unprintable prayer and the mirror left the house. In its place, underneath the air where it had been, the pencil marks remained. All up the now-naked plaster: Vera at six, Horse at nine, Vera at eleven when she had grown hard and straight like a blade some farmer lost and the grass found. Her mother’s hand had made those marks with a little poke of tongue at the corner of her mouth and a frown what did not mean worry. People love to write growth down, as though ladders needed numbers. When the mirror left, the marks were like ribs. She ran her knuckle over one. The dust that came away—she pressed it to her lip and it tasted like chalk, like the little lies of school.

It was a quarter to closing when the man appeared who had once been a boy whose mother worked the register at the discount and who had sold them pencils on school’s first day from a cardboard box with immaculate cheer. He had had the jumpy feet and the smooth mouth then; he had them still. He said Hey there, Vera. He said it soft like a secret and like a brag. She looked at him and saw at once the boy who had run laps to show the sweat what it had coming and the man who now wore out shoes driving to places where he was told his name was good and not as good. Don’t you remember, he said; we done catfished under that pier once till the mosquitoes made us look like we had the spots. He laughed. He looked away too because it is rude and dangerous to ask a middle-aged body to walk a sixteen-year-old mile. She nodded, could not help it, and for a little while she was sweet-watered and hollow and, like a gourd, ready to thrum. What you need, she asked him, and he said I don’t know. I don’t know what I came for.

It is a lucky man who can say that out loud. He wandered through the rooms and sometimes touched the doorframe with the back of his fingers, a gesture like Sky’s. He did not buy anything. He said My Mama gone too, and then he shook his head as though a fly persisted, and then said Well, all right, and went out to the yard where the sunlight made his shadow squat and arrogant. She watched him stand a minute at the peonies and then go, and she kept her eyes where he had been until the air closed its fingers once more.

Sky came and stood beside her with her wide sorrowful mouth tucking itself under. She said Mama, you want me to tell them people it’s closing? And Vera said, Not yet. People were drifting out anyway. The girl with the water hair and the neck-tattoo man had come back with a borrowed truck and four men to hoist the iron bed. They carried it out with a pant and a three-two-one glee. The girl came back and hugged Vera quick around the shoulders and said We make babies on this, her face a petal open and shameless, and Vera said Don’t tell me, and the girl laughed and said I won’t, and her man said much too loud We gon’ sleep in history, and Dot shook her head and said That is not history; that is just bed. But she looked after them with a small gladness.

Inside, the piano still sat with its shawl. The man with the red hat had left and then come back, three times, the way that men make their decisions like a scow in a shallow water. He stood now with his hand on its shoulder like you would touch your own dog and said He can come Sunday, which could mean anything: a mover, a ghost, a deacon. Sunday, Dot said, as though the Sabbath ought to have some respect put on it. Vera took his money. The feel of it made her body look for pockets she did not have. It sat in her palm like a cool stomachache. When he signed his name on the yellow pad, he wrote it with a sideways lean and punctuated it with a dot hard as a nail. She imagined that dot in the road, a little hole a fish could look out of.

It thinned. The air changed its sound. The neighbor’s husky lay down finally in the kitchen shade and gave his full sigh, more honest than a man’s. The frogs in the puddle two houses over unleashed themselves. People came to say They’d be back tomorrow and to ask about a set of glasses that had daisies squeezed onto their sides and to say Girl, your mother would have kicked us all out by now, and to squeeze her forearm and to put one hand on the wall, as though to bless or steal a blessing. The boy with the buttons returned with his payment and went home with his jar like a church. Mr. Eaton had said there would be a last one of the day who would make you cross. This turned out to be a woman in a green top with a chain of tiny hearts around her throat. She found the cedar chest and opened it and opened her mouth too, wide and round as a world, then found the price high for her purse and said so five different ways. Vera’s feet had started to whisper to one another. Her back had tied its own knots and hung them up for company. She was a too-small cup holding too-wide water. She wanted to lie down on the floor boards right there and pull the cedar chest over her like a heavy weather. Instead she said I can do eighty, and the woman bit her lower lip, a child’s move, and said Seventy, please, so plaintive that Vera had to look away from her because pity is a small mean dog that will get your pant cuff and hold on. Seventy-five, she said, the number round and good, and the woman nodded and began to pull the quilts out with both hands because she couldn’t leave them in anymore. The room filled with the soft smell of old soap, sun, and sweet patience. One was a churn of stars and nine-patches. Another had shapes like flying birds and boats. There were places where the women’s fingers who had made them had pulled too tight or left a little fudge to be fixed by the next hand. The woman folded them back down one by one, counting silently in some little moving of lip until she could close the lid and put her whole life in it again even though this time the lid would belong to her.

When the last of the peopled sunlight fell out of the yard like the old ball from the sycamore and rolled into the dip where water always pooled first, Dot ground out her cigarette and came along the porch with a tired slant. She was younger than Vera by a hinge and a season, but more practiced with other people’s weather. She had four living children and two to visit, or was it the other way round; she had loved a man with a quiet voice and then let him go for his violence and then, at her mother’s insistence, tried to love him again for the way he looked in that church pew, and then turned her head so hard on a Thursday one spring that all the neck bones said Praise, which was another way of leaving. She and Vera had learned their make-do together among the rough weeds of girls whose mothers believed in their hands more than in their laughs. Dot said I’m going home with that shoe stand, and Vera said Take it and get out my eye, and Dot kissed her on the side of the head and said I named my boy after your cousin because your cousin was the only one who came when the pipe burst, and then went to wrench the shoe stand from where it had been hiding being useless for years. This is how certain days end. With a friend telling you she has already done what would have been necessary to say and asking you about nothing that does not matter. Dot said You eat yet, and Vera said Shut up, and Dot said I knew it. I’m leaving two chicken thighs in your fridge and don’t cuss me and don’t pretend they not yours now. And she did. She left the chicken like a Bible—you can open if you like, or ignore and later complain.

Sky came and brought a trash bag and began to throw away the half-there things: a pen that would write only if held upside down and squinted at; an inhaler long ago emptied; a clump of paper clips shaped like a secret animal; a flyer from a time when men had been paid to repaint the porches of women who wanted to be looked at while their husbands were away. Don’t throw away that, Vera said seventeen different times to seventeen different pieces that would be junk tomorrow but were still story at this hour. Sky moved her head like a dancer each time, a small messy yes and no, until she stood, hip cocked, by the mantle where the Not For Sale Bible radiated its black solidity. Sky said You going to take that? and Vera said No. And Yes. And I don’t know whose breath is on that page. Sky reached and lifted it and felt its weight that could break a foot and said Mama, I can’t carry this with me and you can’t carry it in your head. Then she put it in a bag and carried it to the car anyway because that is what daughters do when faced with their mother’s don’ts. They say I can’t and then they do and then they mutter and then they have a strange pretty dream where the girl who had been them becomes their mother and squints at them and puts her finger to their forehead and blesses them with a wetness that is not spit.

The piano remained. The man in the red hat would not return until Sunday. Vera stood before it and lifted the shawl. The wood had that good old-world flake at the corner where you could stick a nail and make a wound on purpose. She remembered the day they had moved it in with six men and a cousin and a dolly whose wheel had a mind of its own. She remembered the face of the man second from the back whose lip trembled and who had sworn he could do it though his elbow quivered like long flour. She remembered how Pearl had watched from the door and said Do not stain my runner. And then later said Here, let’s see if it makes any sound worth keeping. It had been there all the winters and the hots. When people came to pray because sometimes your house gets turned into a hospital and a courtroom and a place to ask for the impossible, they had leaned their hand on this piano like an altar, as though the wood could deliver their hands to where a listener might be. She stroked the note C again and the note wobbled and then steadied and she felt herself do the same: wobble, steady, the way an old boot on an old nail holds while a woman lifts, still, the heavy end of her day.

She could have closed up then. She could have gone into the kitchen and put her head in the cold curve of the sink, the place where her mother had bent to cool her forehead and her babies’ fevers and watch the ghost of her own face in the silver belly. She could have said to Sky Go on, I will follow you, and to Dot Go home, and to the house Good night, an ordinary thing. She could have. She did not. She went to the pantry instead and put her hand on the shelf where once there had been rows of jars like a parade of knees. Pearl had known how to paper the inside of a jar with sugar and heat, to arrange summer in November and feed it to you with a spoon. Vera had tried and ruined three jars of plums and one of peaches with too much cinnamon, the fruit turning black with indignation. She had stopped trying. She put her hand on the shelf, then took it away black with old shame and quiet mold. She rubbed it off on her dress. The smell of the place lifted and said, You will go and you will eat the chicken and you will sleep and we will be here a while longer and then we will not.

Through the back door the yard had taken on that end-of-day shine that makes even plastic chairs look solemn. The peonies lay on the ground like girls who think they have fainted because it looks romantic and will get them called by name. The little boy with the cooler of freezer pops had gone home to eat something square and be told to wash his knees. The sun had gone behind the water, which meant the sky had stopped pretending it owned the light. They would have fish smell in the morning, and that was fine. The elm by the alley that had always shown its knees was whispering its private talk to the sparrows. Far off, a train said it was leaving. It said it again as though telling itself a thing twice would make it true.

Vera walked the rooms. She put her hand flat on walls the way a woman puts her palm to a baby’s back to show it how to breathe. Sky floated in and out, collecting what was left of a day: a cup of coins, three unmatched forks, a calendar with a hole where July’s smiling white girl had been. Dot banged a cabinet one last time to see if it could loosen a secret and made a sound like a drum that had long wanted to be played. Vera stopped in the hall where the mirror had been and put her back against the wall. She slid down until her knees were a little sideways and she was again ten and twelve and eighteen, just breathing there. She stayed like that until the air moved around her like the inside of a conch somebody had stolen off a beach you had not seen. When she stood up, the backs of her thighs had the print of the plaster on them. She would not see it, but it would escort her out and into the evening.

She turned off the lights that did not work. She closed doors that had not opened today to let them know they still mattered. She straightened the photograph of the unknown aunt because disrespect cannot be allowed to multiply, and because the photograph had always slid left like that and had always been corrected in a small daily ritual that had the plain holiness of teeth brushing and lint picking. She stood in the doorway between the living room and the dining room and was for a breath as many bodies as she had been in this place. She was the child who had once slapped the table because the milk was warm and had been slapped back. She was the girl who had once kissed in a shadow and then cried on the floor when a mother’s foot landed too hard in a different room. She was the mother herself who had watched her own daughter slam a door and had wanted to laugh and wanted to push and had done one and then the other. Now she was the woman whose mother lay now in that other land where food is both proof and promise.

She went to the door. The front. She had not touched it all day, not with her whole body. Men and women and children had pushed through it without its participation; it had been a shoulder, a lip. Now she put her palm on it and felt what it had been feeling. It was not lonely. Doors are used to being used. It was, instead, crowded. It held the through of the day, all the coming and going, the thank you and the Will you and the No I cannot and the What you need and the I’ll take and the Mmm that comes after somebody drinks cold sweet from a plastic cup. It held it all and had turned it into something it could stand. She turned the knob. It clicked its small sound. Sky stood behind her with the bag that had the Bible and something else heavy and said You want me to lock, and Vera said Not yet.

They stepped onto the porch where Dot had left her shoe stand and a cigarette butt in its little shining sleeve and a folded napkin with chicken grease. The yard had its evening faces on. The neighbors’ windows were TV blue. The street smelled like meat and lake and somebody’s anger. Down the block, old Mr. Marvin, who had a way of knowing when a thing had closed and when to step up to the edges of it, came out and tipped his cap and said Miss Pearl would be pleased, which was not necessarily true. Miss Pearl would have had much to say to his garden, and about his grandsons and his new wife who wore a pink robe to the mailbox. But he was a man offering his little striped hat of a blessing. It landed or did not. It was tossed and caught and set on the porch rail of fate. Vera lifted her hand because it was too much and not enough. She swallowed what there was to swallow, which had more salt than water.

She turned to Sky and said Take the money now before I decide it don’t belong to me. Sky, mouth ready to refuse, took it anyway. She stuffed it deep as though it were a hungry thing and said Mama, I love you, her eyes turned not away but softly toward a place that made her mouth braver. Vera picked at a splinter on the rail because love is as rough as it is silky and she had never learned to run her hand along it both ways. She said Pull your scarf down behind, it’s riding, and Sky said Don’t talk about my scarf like you know what it takes to wear it, and then they both snorted, because these things are always better than a cry until you are alone.

Do you want anything left inside before I go lock? Sky said. The light above the porch made a circle on the floorboard she had scrubbed four times as a teenager to get out a stain that had not been a stain but a shadow. I want—, Vera started. She wanted the iron bed, the sewing basket’s low coo of a lid, the Jesus who did not leave the room when men did woman things. She wanted the air of children’s after-bath, that round wet smell. She wanted the four squat chairs that furniture men called parlor rejects and that had held bodies she loved and bodies she did not love but still provided for. She wanted to take the house itself away folded in five and set it on her own shoulder like a shawl. She wanted to ask it to go with her and to live on her in a way other people could not see but could feel when they hugged her. She wanted to keep the house safe from the buyers, safe from a future that might rip up the lilacs and hang the wreath crooked and paint over the pencil marks and put up a rooster from a catalog and call it country. She wanted to tell the house There is a way to come where I am that feels like a sheet. She wanted to say to Pearl, Mama, it is easy and impossible. She wanted to say, and so you were right and wrong and gone and here.

Instead she reached down and picked up a spoon that had rolled under the porch table when somebody had set the tea and not seen. It was a cheap spoon. Picked once from a cardboard typed set that had clattered with the day. It was nothing; it was everything. It had been in the mouth of so many soups it had learned their languages. She held it as though it were a bird and did not put it in the bag with the Bible and the change because it had to roll like a thought under the seat of the car. She slid it into her pocket the way her mother had once slid the blessed oil there at funerals for suddenness and flash.

Sky went in and came back with the ring of keys on their old yellow string, the one that had always lived on the hook shaped like an ear by the back door. She dropped it into her mother’s hand and the weight of it, so familiar, made Vera’s fingers square themselves in reliability. She closed the front door then. She leaned into it the way you lean into a partner who can hold you and belong to you both and not. She turned the lock because you must. You do, even in a small town where boys will hop your fence if they want and sit on your upside-down tub to smoke and whisper stupidity. She turned it and the lock said it would not be here this time next week. That is how locks are. They are promises made to be remade.

As they went down the steps, each board spoke its name once, the way men do in jail. They reached the bottom and the grass lifted under them. Dot had left her car door open and the dome light was still making its yellow pool. The world had shifted a little. It had done that old solar tilt. It had done its one trick, which is to move and pretend to stay. Vera picked her way along the walk her father had poured from a bag when they had money once for that, his shoulders bare to the sun and his mouth humming along with the radio in his ear, and she saw him then in the small jitter of air—his laugh a choke he hated because it said he had felt something, his hands a little ashamed without their expected tool, his lashes a surprise of tenderness. He was there and not at the same time that her mother was there and not and the way that the woman she had been at twenty-seven was there and was not and the girl with the bonnet under the bed was dust and was not. She walked to her car and put her hand on the hood and it was warm in the way a thing is that has been part of your day and is loyal.

They stood and looked back. Sky put her arm through Vera’s and said I will come back tomorrow and we will finish, and Vera said Tomorrow the piano leaves, and Sky made a pretty low sound and said I hope that man can play, and Vera said It won’t matter if he can’t. It is going to listen. The house had a shadow now that gave it the look of a person who has chosen to keep a thing to themselves. The porch light clicked moths into being. In the alley, somebody laughed ugly and ran and a glass bottle made its end.

You can stop now, the voice said again, kinder this time. She had already. She had stopped there with her daughter and her friend and the last of the buyers and the boy with the buttons and the man with the memory of catfish and the salt smell and the chicken in the fridge. She had placed her mouth around the day as far as it would go and bit what she could and let the rest sit, soft, for later. She moved her hand as though closing a book. The sound the door made registered in her ribs like the small felted hammer of a piano key. She nodded at nothing and then she laughed, not because anything was funny but because a laugh can smooth the way between one room and the next. Then she got in her car. Then she put the spoon between her thighs and the keys into the burnished slot. Then she drove away down Beecham Avenue past the lamppost with the tilt that every boy had climbed up to kiss and she put on her blinker though there was nobody behind to mind her manners for and she turned toward the lake and then turned again and then again and she did not look back more than once because once is a lot when a house is watching you. She asked a low, out-loud question into the air that nobody had to answer. The air took it, made a pocket for it, and promised, with a smell like fish and June and old wood, to keep it a while.
