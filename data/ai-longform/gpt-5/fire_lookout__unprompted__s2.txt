The smoke shows itself the way a lie does, a faint binding of gray at the lip of the western notch that at first could be anything—a thin banded cloud, a breath of dust from a road I know isn't there, the morning's last thin venom unwinding off some shadowed snowbank—but there isn't snow in July and the light is wrong for dust and I have been watching this bowl of mountains long enough to know when something does not belong. I put my elbow on the old windowsill worn smooth by dead lookouts, the varnish flaking up in filigree like old stamps, and I pin the valley with my forefinger and line my nail up with the drainage like I would with a sighting. The Osborne is gone, sold to a collector by some bright idea down in the office after they decommissioned us, but I don't need it for this. I know the exact bite of the ridge where the creek breaks left, the V of alder that holds late wetness, the pancakes of granite at the first falls; I know the slant-browed slope with the exposed iron back of basalt and how the drainage drops and suddenly disappears. It is the place where the creek becomes the river we swam screaming in as girls, the place where June dragged those logs with a come-along and said she was making a bridge and Daddy laughed and called her his ox; it is the drainage where June keeps her crooked little cabin with the window toward the sound of the water and the porch sagging as if it bows to trees. The smoke climbs exactly from there, from that hidden seam of wet and shade, that place which in June is green when everything else is brittle. The notion almost comforts me—wet means slow, means chance—but then I watch the smoke thicken, not a vanishing thread but a tapering column, and I see how it mushrooms when it reaches a warmer layer and feathers out, and I see a darkening in the belly of it like meat turning.

I click the radio on, out of reflex more than belief, because for two summers it has been a tinny companion that catches things half a ridge too late: trail crews making a joke in Spanish, a scattered call for a ranger that never ends with a reply. The speaker coughs static and, blessedly, a brief ghost of someone's voice from the east, then it dies. I slap its side like a bad dog. The battery light flashes its little red warning and goes dim. I have a hand-crank charger somewhere under the bunk, in the plastic box where I keep candles that smell like lemon cookies, my one indulgence that I tell myself is practical. I haul the box out, my knees hitting the floorboards, and I turn the crank until my forearm burns. The radio comes on and gives me the weather in a woman voice calm as the woman who reads Psalms over the highway accidents on the local station, then the light flickers and it dies again. The hand-crank is a toy bought off a website at midnight in February when I was picturing myself noble and competent and the winter was such a weariness that competence felt like a kind of romance. Eight miles of road coil down from this tower and half of that is more rock than road and the last mile is swallowed by alder; the only place with cell service is the shoulder where the old miners used to park, if you stand with your phone held up and lean east like you're saluting. And the smoke is where it is, braiding its gray above June's trees as if it had been told to make itself obvious to me, as if it had been told to say her name.

The tower creaks in a way I only notice when I still. The sun is mean and clean. The walls rattle with the small ambitions of flies. I set the radio on the sill like a book I have finished and written in and know I'll never reread, just for the comfort of putting it in its place. I strip the bunk and fold the blanket and tie it into my pack with the rope I keep for hauling the grocery box because my hands need something to do and I am not allowed to let thinking put me into motion too slowly. I move the enamel basin from its nail and fill it and set my face in it for the heat; I check the can of bear spray though I have never used it; I find the extra headlamp batteries in their taped roll. I have done these things before at speed, when clouds were boiling over that little lightning-flat and I had to get down to look for a smoke gone out before it became a smoke that did not go out. But the part of me that knows June, the part that is my sister-sense and has not had a chance to exercise itself for seven years, says something that the fire part does not: that if I go down I will not be in time.

The last time I saw her, she was laughing with her mouth closed on the church steps, and our father's coffin was halfway into the ground and our mother had put her good ring on her index finger, the only one that would fit, and we were the strangers people comforted. June called me by my name as if it were a task to remember it. She had sawdust in her hair, which made me want to pluck it off for her the way I had done when we were ten and eight and I brushed her like a horse before school so the nuns wouldn't scold. She said she didn't know that working for the Forest Service made me holier than anybody else, and I said I didn't know that building a shack with your boyfriend made you an expert in grief. She had on the boots she'd been wearing since the rainstorm the night she left home. It has been seven birthdays each since then. I do not count the postcards that came back with yellow stickers and the one letter of hers I crumpled in a fit of cleaning and then pressed flat and smoothed with my palm, then lost under the refrigerator.

I wedge my feet into boots that have learned my ankles and tuck my laces in where they won't catch, and I take my hat off its nail and knock the old ash out of the brim I never knew was there. I take the spare key to the locker where the first aid kit lives and I do not open the locker but put the key into my pocket to remember I have it; I put the other key—the key to the trapdoor the kids from town sometimes break in through when it is not me up here, when they want to add their initials to the mess of them carved into the balustrade that says who loved whom and when—to where it goes, under the loose board at the base. I write in the log out of habit and because I know if this tower burns they'll come up before the ash is cold to see if I kept the faith. I write: 11:36. Smoke in drainage below Copper Spur, WNW, 18–20 miles? Origin uncertain. Column strengthening. Radio dead. Going down to report.

I step around the coffee can of nails they left when they decommissioned us, and I step over the gap in the floor that pinches like teeth when it cools after the afternoon and I wonder, as I do every time, how I got to be the age I am and still think a place loves me. Outside, the bones of the catwalk warm my thighs through my jeans. I could call it the final summer because that is true; they have not said I will not be allowed to come back, but they also have not said there will be a season, and last winter's budget made a vertical line down the page next to heritage programs that looked neat enough to be merciless. The new girl whose name I do not know, who looks as if she irons her pants and drinks water out of a glass instead of a canteen, stood up in a meeting and said, Well, we have cameras, and everyone clicked things and said, Yes, ha ha, cameras do not get lonely, and the old men said, Nobody cares about towers since the films stopped ruining them with romances. The forest shifts its shoulder whether anybody sees it or not. It moves under your feet even when you sleep; it gives you a dream of drowning and you wake with pine needles in your throat.

The stairs are a ladder and then another ladder, then nothing, then the ground coming up with a little bounce to meet your feet like a handshake you aren't sure you want. The lookout sleeps behind me in the way of shuttered rooms, closed on air and dust and that particular snug silence that old wood makes. I count six steps to shake off the tug as if I am the one being abandoned. Then I start down the road.

You do not say the word "run" when you cannot run anymore the way you did when you were twenty-eight. You say hustle, brisk, make time, beat feet. You do not think about how far eight miles is when your hip sings and your heel grinds. The first switchback drops you into shade and cools your neck, and the second one gives you a narrow view of the lower valley and the bowl of the meadow that from above looks as though someone ironed it and licked a hand to smooth a stray hair. I clock the smoke every time the trees open. It thickens the way cream thickens when you only half want it to; you have to notice yourself stirring. By the third switchback there is black in the column, ink made of earth, and the top off it clots. Far enough out it furls into a little blossom, pretty if you didn't know what it was. I am not pretending it is not what it is. I am not thinking, It is a burn pile, it is that old man with his cardboard boxes, it is a lightning snag cooking off from last week. There hasn't been lightning here in a month. I passed the cardboard man's driveway on my way in last Monday, and his truck was gone, and he had put rocks in the burn barrel to keep it from tipping even in his absence. I am thinking the word spot and I am thinking the words ladder fuel and crown and run. I think June, but it is a whisper, as if I am in church and the psalm is about floods.

A hawk skims my shoulder and my eyes water. Midway down I stop where there is a skinny bar or two of reception. It is a game anyway, a child's faith that maybe this time the phone will do what a radio won't. I have June's number dead in its list like a doorbell to a house that burned and was rebuilt by someone with no windows. I punch 9-1-1 and hold the phone out like a compass. The long electronic ring is the sound of one hand in a photograph, and then a woman says Where is your emergency in a voice that has seen more teenagers in ditches than grandmothers dying in bathtubs. I speak quick, stupidly polite, as if that will help—Yes, hello, there is a smoke coming from Copper Spur drainage, maybe a quarter up from its mouth, the kind of thing you would see from the old tower if it were anyone but me up there. She asks me again to tell her where, and I tell her like I am telling someone who does not care about me but does care about accuracy. She asks if anyone is with me, if there are any injuries. I say no and no. I try to say my sister's name; the first syllable refuses my mouth and then the second comes all at once, ugly, like the ending of a cough. The dispatcher says someone will call back. I look at the phone screen. There is no bar and no name and no way to know if I told the right mountain its own coordinates.

I go faster. The deer see me and raise their flags and then stand and look at me as if I am the thing that hollowed the logging road to bone. The memorial cross someone wired to the madrone at Mile Four is striped in new ribbons, neon pink that will fade to the color of tongues. The road gets rockier and then heals, as if it doesn't want me to twist anything right when I need it; the road is like a person at a party so good at asking questions you don't notice you have said every wrong thing. Down in the bottom I meet the creek, which is thin and hard and quick this time of year, hugging its cold into the green where it runs. It is the only thing in this forest that seems certain. I squelch through it and stir up a smell I could drink. The alder is a collapsed umbrella. I have to crouch and skitter. A thrush throws its slow coin of sound. A mosquito taps my lower lip. The smoke is felt here as a taste before I see it again, a resinous must like a church from my childhood where the candles had been burning all day for people who died years ago. I remember, not as if it matters, that June and I were not allowed to light the big candles with the long matches; that was for the sacristan, a woman with a face like parchment who would die one summer and leave all the casseroles in the world to our aunts to make and deliver.

I cross that ridiculous footbridge June built when she was with Ben, who worked for the county road department and had a peal of laughter he believed only men in pickup cabs could hear. The bridge sways under me the way a day sways when you have forgotten to eat. The boards are laid with their bowed sides up, so rain runs to the ends and then spills into the creek. One half-rot plank gives at the corner and I stumble and say the same word twice, a nothing word, a name that means no one.

After that there isn't a road; there is only a guess, and the guess is one I know from a hundred walks on thankgods and on slow Sundays when I came out not to see my sister but to see where she had been, to see if there were smoke, to see if the dog—a lanky blue healer mix with a knack for vanishing and the nervous habit of peeing on your bootlaces—was lying across the sun like a scarf. Last year I watched a woman from Portland march into these woods wide-eyed and clean-shoed like a pilgrim. She took photographs of her own hands on bark. I wanted to say It is not memory you are making, it is only proof that you were here; the woods do not take your proof. But I did not. I put her on the right trail to the waterfall, and she came back wet and ecstatic and said, I didn't know that noise would be so much, and I said, Neither did I, the first time, and we were friends for the length of a smile that did not know anything complicated.

I skirt a patch of blowdown, scramble up a bank where someone—June—has half-cut steps with the tip of a shovel. I smell a hot smell like bread left in the oven just a minute too long and then I smell, under it, the meaty stink of duff cooking. I stop. I listen. There is no helicopter. There is no fixed wing. There is no engine. There is the sound of a woodpecker doing his idiotic pounding, which is not a bad sign and not a good sign. The wind is from the south and small; it picks up the hair at my nape and sets it down again. I press my hand to the dirt not because I can tell anything that way but because I have been taught that there are some acts work as revelations even when they reveal only your own silliness. The ground is cool. I am not too late yet to be useless.

I find June's cabin by finding what is not meant to be here: the glint of a metal pail, the square in air that lines itself with other the squares we built into ourselves when we learned paper rules; the slow wink of a window. The smoke is ribbing the treetops behind it so that the leaves look like the hair of old women in fog. There is a scatter of kindling like a spill of tiny bones on the porch. A beer can squats on the top step with its tab pulled and curled. Beside it, a wedge of wood holds open the door.

Inside it is the same as it was the last time I came when June wasn't here. The photograph of Daddy taken on the first day he understood he was dying and tried to grin and half-succeeded. The iron skillet with its handle shaped like a dog tongue. A shirt of hers draped over the chair back that always wanted to fall backward because some idiot or some genius had cut the back legs shorter than the front. The same stubborn tangle of cotton twine on a nail. The room holds its breath in that way rooms do when they are waiting. I put my hand on the table and feel it shake the way you feel a truck before you hear the truck; I tell myself it is me making it do that. There is a note on a yellow pad on the counter. It says: Gone upriver to check the dam. Dog with me. Back by dark. The handwriting is drunk only by its character, not by liquor, messy but sure, the way she used to write permission slips for me that got me out of geometry or out of gym class and into the library where the librarian let me hide.

Upriver is a half mile and then another half, and then it isn't river anymore but a splay of channels and green slick behind a squat as if the earth grew fingers. The beavers have been building a citadel here the last two years, felling saplings like a half-drunk crew of woodsmen at a training. They are better at it than anyone I know. The towers they make are treacherous and kind beyond measure. I come round a bend to find the first face of the dam and see it is indeed high now, head-high to me on the tallest stones, that what used to be meadow is dark water singing small songs to the grass. I also see that the far bank beyond that faint pond is stippled with smuts of black and that above the second lip where the alder closes the fire has already walked. It burned as if it were thinking; it moved in leaps and then in slow cunning curves, tasting as it went: the cedar needles give themselves up easily and then the oak holds it off out of instinct, flaring and then dampening like a woman putting her hair back behind her ear. But it is not stopped. The smoke is lower here and moves in cats' ribs. There is a little hush under it, the hush of soft killing. The light is yellowing in a way that makes me feel rude.

"June!" I do not pretend I can say anything else. I do not yell as if she were small and I am across a playground. I shape the word with my mouth open to warn whatever else is listening that I am human and therefore both foolish and armed with a name. There is a pause in the bird-sound and then a stirring in the line of alder and the dog explodes out, blue and black and sooty as a coal, his nose cleaving the air like a tool. He circles me and sits and then stands and pees on my left boot as if blessed by old habits. "Hi, sir," I say stupidly, as if he were a veteran. Then a body follows him, a shape that has never been in danger since birth because it never thought it was, and I know my sister by the way her shoulders find their place under the weight of a shovel.

She is older in her face where faces go first, and she is younger in her picking up of the tool. She says, "Of course. Of course it's you," in the tone of voice for guests who refuse to be surprised. She has tied a scarf around her hair the way our mother tied rags around her curlers when she slept for the sake of the morning. The scarf is damp. Her neck is bare. She has a burn smack under her jaw where a coal must have leapt and she did not back away. It isn't bad yet; it will welt later. She is barefoot. My heart does something dramatic I wish it would not.

"Ninety percent of house fires start in the kitchen," I say, because I cannot say anything right yet and because she has a shovel and is near fire, which shakes me into a kind of joking my brain thinks is kin to prayer.

"Only ninety?" she says. "Amateurs."

We stand like that for a second, both of us cocked, both of us waiting for the shape of the other to shift into the new geometry this demands. Then she says, practical as any old woman halfway through making jam, "You remember that pump?"

"I remember trying to start it and you telling me I flood the carb like a sinner."

"Yeah, well, congratulations on finding Jesus. The line to the creek is probably dry; the last run choked it. You're going to have to clear the grate. The ladder's under the porch. Hose is snaked up—"

"I saw your note." I look at her feet. I look at the place on the far bank where the fire has crept down a log and made it into a candle.

"You saw my note? Good, then you know what we were up to." She slaps the shovel into the dirt near the dam face, breaks some little lick of fire, does not look at me. "You did your calling?"

"Radio's dead." The word empties itself. "I got through to a dispatch with a phone for a second. I don't know if they got coordinates. It's the wrong valley for fast."

"Always is." She turns and yanks with the shovel and lets water go in a clatter, then replaces the head against the mud like a seal. The dog noses the darkness and sneezes. I want to grip her bare ankle and say everything, and I want to slap her for not wearing shoes, and I want to apologize for trying to make her wear the notion of shoes when she never has.

"I can start the pump," I say. "Give me a minute."

"I can start the pump," she says. Then, softer: "You can clear the grate so my good hose isn't chewing gravel."

We do not say I am sorry. We do not say Where did you go on Christmases? or I wrote you a letter in October and I burned it in March. We do not say Remember when he took us to the ferry and we pretended we were going to an island where there was a man who would give us an orange for every cuss word we could think of. We do not say I waited, or I did not, or I married someone who did not like mountains because you were a mountain and there could only be one of you. We run.

At the cabin the pump is where it always was, a stubborn yellow thing rigid with grease and arrogance. June has it on a little platform she built crooked as everything she builds—so that the crookedness feels premeditated, like an act of mercy. I pull the ladder out and drop it to the creek like unrolling a magic trick. I wade in up to my knees. The water is bone; it cuts up into me like an idea. I curse without creativity. June pulls the starter rope and the engine stutters and sneezes. I dig my hands into the murk where the grate guards the line, and it bites with its wire teeth. I haul it up and find it is crowded with leaves as if they all wanted to go to the same party and couldn't get in. I peel them free. The silt shapes itself around my fingers like a glove. Something alive, small and frantic, beats against my palm and gets loose. The line shudders, the pump throws itself together in its ferocious rattle, and then a hose goes snake-stiff and spits and starts to throw a weak stream.

"You're a saint," June yells, and it is both sarcasm and an accolade. We move in our old efficiency as if we were reluctantly dancing. She takes the spray and sweeps it across the first line of wood at the edge of the yard, the part she calls the sacrifice zone. Steam lifts at once, and then the line wets, goes dark, drinks. The hose coughs and chokes and finds itself. The pump sputters, gulps air, throws a tantrum of noise that would make a biologist at a conference frown, then pushes water again as if it has a grudge. June wets the posts and the porch and the window frame, then points the stream up at the edge of the roof where stray needles collect like sailors. I go up the ladder after her, and it wobbles and I hold my breath and then decide holding my breath is stupid and breathe. She hands me the hose and walks a circuit of the metal roof as if she were walking a line on a map. She is fast and unafraid as always, and her bare toes crunch ash. "You're going to scream at me about shoes until we both die," she says without looking back, and I say nothing that can be heard over the spitting and the machine.

Out beyond the yard where she keeps the broken mowers and the piles of tin that will someday be a shed, the fire is undecided in the way a sure thing is early: it tastes the dried grasses, it hairpins on the duff and masks itself black. The little runs it makes go from stitch to seam. It finds a slash pile and burns it as if it had planned for this, and the smoke goes a meat red for a moment where the plastic in that old tarp in that pile gives itself up to air. I watch a junco dart and then vanish. I watch a mouse run in a circle and tell myself it is not my aunt's dream of hell and is only a mouse finding that what was solid this morning is not so now.

The pump’s second hose snakes back to the dam. She has a one-nozzle sprinkler on a spike she stole off somebody's lawn, and she drives it into the damp, and it makes a circle with a click, wetting what will be tinder at midnight if we live that long. The dog runs its established loop with the pointlessness of panic, and then June whistles with a two-note complexity that is the only elaborate thing I've ever heard her produce with her face; he turns and lies, panting, and watches the thing that could erase him.

"Where were you?" she says, as if she means yesterday, as if she means the time a week ago when there was a football-sized fluff of smoke down by the quarry and I called it in and then sat on my hands watching the wind blow it out. Then: "Where have you been?"

"Up there." I tilt my chin toward the tower as if it has its own weather. There is no movement up there then—no glint of glass, no shimmer that says another person lives. "Every summer that they'd keep me. You know that."

"I thought you'd quit," she says. "I heard you quit."

"I thought you'd died," I say. It isn't fair. It isn't kind. It is not anything. She flinches as if someone threw a pebble at her. Then the fire throws a little louder voice across the snail of the valley, a lowering in tone like a man clearing a throat, and the tone makes its own weather inside my ribs.

"You thought I'd died?"

"You don't write letters."

"You write them and them you don't send them, don't think I don't know your kind of neat," she says, and this is the moment, ridiculous as it is, when it becomes more obvious than the fire that we are who we are and could have been otherwise only at such a cost we would not have paid it. "I saw you up there sometimes," she adds, so lightly that it could be about a deer. "Your little light with the kerosene on fog nights when you couldn't stand the dark. I could see it if I went out into the clearing and picked a gap in the trees. I'd hold my breath, like a girl. How do you suppose your heart becomes a thing you must hush so you can watch a lamp."

"You could have waved," I say, and the childishness of it flushes me hot, the shame of the girl I was who waved at trains because the boys on the cabooses sometimes waved back.

"I could have," she says. "I was not wearing the right shoes." A grin, a slice, unfair, white in the gray. Then: "Shit. Watch that crown."

We both turn. A hemlock with its skirt of dead needles like a Victorian lady has a stitch of red at knee height and then yellow stitches on its body. The red climbs like a rumor. "Fuck off," June says to the tree with a fervency that would make a priest question his belief. I aim the hose and lay water to it. The water is thin and wounded by its own distance from the creek, a sad pressure, but it hammers enough, and I make a sheet of wet downwind and the crown blushes and sizzes and fails. I lower the stream and soak the ground, feeling something in my shoulder give or go cold.

"You just did that," June says to the tree, delighted and furious as if it were a dog that came when called after a year of chewing. "You just calmed down."

"You just did that," I say to her, and for an instant we grin at each other like thieves.

It is not the way it is in stories. There is no sudden wall of orange coming to devour us with biblical tongues. The fire is careful, the fire is humble, the fire is patient and there is its danger. It sends scouts of spark upwind on the weakest lull and they land in the weeds and think about it and then they make rags of orange the size of our open hands and then they pull their little bodies together and get vertical. It burns in the last place we are looking if we are not paying the right kind of attention. It learns the lay of the place the way a horse learns your hand. We string the remaining hose to the far side of the house and wet the brush there. We soak the logs she stacked last autumn when she should have been at the hospital with me, watching him breathe. The pump coughs. The sprinkler clicks. I ram my palms under the tap at her sink and drink water that has iron in it and now smoke. It makes my blood change its mind.

"Do you remember when we were little," I say, "and you got up on the woodpile and said you'd jump and I told you you were stupid, and you did it anyway and you knocked the wind out of yourself so that your face went this color and I thought you'd die, I thought you'd just decided to do it because I'd said something—"

"You thought the universe was something that listened to you," she says. "You thought you had done it because you thought you were big enough. That was awful of you. I loved you for it anyway." It is the closest she has come to kindness and it is entirely adequate. She wields the hose like a weapon and then like a rope.

A gasoline smell finds us and we both look to the pump. It is fine for now. The sky over the ridge goes a bruised pink. The wind takes a breath and makes up its mind to face south. That is when I know, not in any place I can name, but with the certainty of a woman who has counted the same number of wires in a bird's nest, that the wind is going to turn wrong in an hour, that everything that has been prelude will become event. "We should clear more," I say. "We should take the rake to that line under the apple. We should—"

"I started that yesterday," June says. "Then it was dinnertime and the dog looked at me as if I were a villain and I went to fry potatoes instead. Heroism is so boring for the first twenty minutes. I'll try harder next time," she adds, so lightly it might float.

There are not enough hours of childhood in a life to let you do this forever. You cannot stand on the roof with a hose and be little and think you can save your world because you love the person inside it. But that is exactly what we do. We mount and wet and walk the peak and water runs into my socks and turns my boots excruciating. I discover new places on my body where ash can settle like a thoughtful guest. June slips once and catches herself and grins without teeth and my heart stops with the primitive horror of it. "Don't be dramatic," she says. "There's a time for that." We do not name it.

Once there is a sound above us, a low engine; we both lift our heads in the same motion. We look for fixed wing, for a helicopter's shiver. It is someone's idiot car out on the pavement miles away, some boy with an exhaust he thinks makes him a myth. The sound falls away. We look at one another as if we are peculiar and somewhat cherished pets; I am not good at being angry at the wrong things.

We do everything twice because the first time cannot be believed. We wet and watch and wait for a gust that does not come, and we move a rakeful of leaves from under the propane tank, and we dribble water into the pump’s petcock when it hiccups, and we hold our breath each time the wind changes as if breath is a lever we can throw. Sometimes we stop and stare where we have known deer to stand and find nothing, as if the deer might reassure us by being their dumb, constant selves. Sometimes I think about my father, our father, sitting in the last chair he could sit in, asking me whether I was warm and then not hearing my answer and then asking me again whether I was warm, and I want to lay my head down on the patch she has just wetted and stay there like a little piece of paper.

She says once, out of nowhere, "Ma told me to leave. To go away. She said you'd make a good girl of yourself early if I got out of the way. She said you couldn't do it with me there because my hands were sticks in spokes. I thought she meant go to the fair. I came back with a corndog and she was crying."

I laugh and cry at the same time. It comes out a noise like a frog falling out of a bush. The fire finds a new note. The far pine, the one that shades the little bit of porch where she draws with a charcoal she makes by accident, hisses at me a secret I do not want to know. I lay the stream along its trunk and tell it to hold a while.

"I did not make a good girl of myself," I say. "I married a man who liked me quiet and then forgot to be liked. I took a job few people take so I did not have to ask anyone's permission to do it. I learned the names of places because I did not know the names of myself. I failed you because I thought you wanted to be failed. And I was very proud," I add, which is the thing I am ashamed of and also not ashamed of at all. "I loved you for never asking me to be small enough to fit in your pocket. You frighten me, June. You always have. That is not an excuse."

She wipes a black stripe of arm across her forehead and leaves herself whiter underneath for one telling second. She nods as if I have said we need more toilet paper. The dog licks my knee where ash has made mud. Then she says, with more heat than any coal: "I didn't build that place so you'd be frightened. I built it because I thought there should be a spot on this map where you come and nobody thinks you're on call. I built it because I was angry and then I made the angle wrong and then I was very pleased with my wrongness. It's ugly and I love it. Stay if it lives. Don't if it doesn't. Sometimes loving somebody is being very bored alongside them. We forgot to be bored. That was our trouble."

We grin at each other, nonsense grins, kids who got the answer wrong and made the teacher laugh and are therefore saved. It does not save us. The burn that has been licking along the far bank finds a fat of pitch some idiot with a birdhouse has been collecting for a winter that never came. It goes up like a letter being read to the wrong person. The air thumps; the heat gets ambition. A small draft runs up my underarms like a boy's hand I did not want there. "We should go," I say, all the words in me jealous. She reaches down and steadies the ladder with her foot while I climb off the roof, and I steady the ladder with my shoulders while she comes down, and her bare foot hits the rung and her toes are so dirty with ash that the wrinkles in them are clean. I think I will never love anything like the sight of this foot. Then she is beside me and we are more or less the same height against a world that has made a point of not caring.

We soak what we can soak, and we pull the propane tank farther from the house than any of its little rubber feet intended, and we close the stove damper and leave the front door unlatched because that is what you do when you suspect a place will need to breathe on its own. "If it crowns," I say, and she nods, and we are both good at not ending sentences we do not need, at not speaking stakes that are odd to put into words.

We take what you take. Each of us stuffs what we like into our pockets: a knife with the good edge, a photograph in a frame that you can drink a spill of water out of afterward, a jar of cherries she put up last year in a fit of domestic swooning and then couldn't open without a man so she took it down off the shelf in front of me and said, Here, do something useful, and I broke the seal with a sound like a kiss. The dog hesitates at the edge of the woods and then does what he always does, which is come if she calls. We do not pack the important papers because we do not know where they are; we do not carry the right things because there are too many right things and it is a test that gets us in other ways, has always got me in other ways. We head toward the creek and the bridge toward the slope that will take us away. The pump coughs like a smoker and then evens out as if it has realized it is alone and should be dignified. June turns and looks at the house, the crooked roofline against the newly putrid romance of the sky.

"It was an ugly little thing," she says, not quite to me. "I will build a worse one."

"Yes," I say. It is not patronizing; it is the pledge a recruit makes. "Yes, you will."

We shoulder through the alder where it clutches at arms, and I try to hold the branches back for her and she ignores my courtesy and simply pushes harder. The smoke has clothed even this. The sun is a coin you can look at. A squirrel has a fit I consider heartfelt and indecipherable. The world gives us its small pageants. There is a hummingbird on a twig near my face who has decided dying is not on her itinerary today, and her beak is a needle that writes down our names to be prayed for later by someone we will never know. At the footbridge June makes a face and then steps on the slat that is already half rotted, and it half gives, and she laughs and lifts her foot and says, "You really are going to hate this," and then steps in the creek and comes up wet and grinning. I follow and find the cold as intimate as a mouth. It feels like a thing you can die from and also like a story you could tell only with your hands. The dog shakes and paints our shins with his derision. On the far bank a new run of smut prints the grass, and at the tip of one a thing flickers that was not there when we went in. It is this kind of day: the world says Now, now, now, and you say Later, later, and both of you are partly right.

"You're staying, aren't you?" I say when we hit the slope and the air, for seven strides, is clearer and the pine-smell supercedes everything, a steadier church than my childhood conjured. "You're not going to go into town and argue with a girl at a desk about a form."

"The desk girl knows me," June says. "I forgot my guilt far back along the river and I am not wading back in for it. Maybe if it goes I will sleep at the gravel pit and cough black into my pillow and think about the dog chasing mice in his dreams. Maybe if it goes I'll take the money that man owes me and buy tin. Maybe," she says, considering, "I will stay with you for a while. In your tower. We'll clog up those little steps and I'll make you coffee and tell you where you are wrong, and you can do my burns with the kit you stole."

"You can't stay in the tower; it is not even properly habitable," I say automatically, and I am already arranging the room in my head, moving the enamel basin and taking down the notes with other people's handwritings so there is room for hers to be the sound I hear when I wake.

"Good," she says. "Better that way." She throws her chin up like a horse and makes a sound that is neither laugh nor cough nor sob. The heat lifts a hair at my temples with the familiarity of my first high school dance. She takes my wrist for a second, an old shortcut that stops us both from falling, and the pulse under her fingers runs like a rabbit, and I am for a silly second furious that the body tells such blunt tales to people we have not asked to listen. Then I am grateful. Then we are standing under a tree that has not known us until today and is impartial to our drama.

When we crest the little hogback halfway to the road the view opens and the column is now a presence like a city. It has structure, and its structure is harm and its beauty is obscene. It builds into its own weather like a lesson in making your own circumstances if your circumstances do not suit. I would like to be braver for her; I would like not to make the small involuntary sound that I make. She does not hear me or hears and chooses not to. The wind puts its hand on the back of our necks. People will ask later whether it roared. It does not roar. It proceeds. It informs. It has grammars we would be wise to learn but not curious enough to relish.

"I liked it when you sent me that photograph of the fox," she says, as she swats a branch out of her air with her shovel, the deplorable shovel she is still carrying because she always has to haul a bit of herself wherever she goes; there is always a job in it. "You had your boot in the corner of it. I liked the boot. I wanted to put my foot into it at the time. Not your boot. The boot in the picture. I wanted to put my foot into the boot in the picture and stand where you stood. That was unreasonable of me. When I'm unreasonable I prefer to be right."

"It was not a fox," I say. "It was a coyote. He had this gauntness in his face that makes you think of saints."

"Are saints all gaunt?" she says. "The ones I like are fat. They look like bakers." She spits in a ladylike way that our mother would have corrected into a napkin. "And you," she adds, with such blankness that I cannot read the intention behind it, "are staying, aren't you?"

"Here?" I say.

"In this goddamn life," she says. "In our family, which is the welt it always has been and the balm it sometimes manages, which is the aching and the answers. You're not going to marry again and be quiet into the future like a hermit is you? You're not going to flow into pity like mud. You're going to be a person. I need that of you, if I'm allowed to need something. I'm not good at need."

"You are very good at need," I say. "You have a gift for it." She barks a laugh that starts a bird, and the bird scolds. "Yes," I say, which is the answer to something I do not believe a question asked.

We walk. It is not eight miles back to the man with the billfold of forms or the girl with the screen and the sympathy script; it is not eight miles to where the engine can find us and spray our faces with relief or with contempt. It is another kind of distance measured in the length of a wet hose and the depth of a dam and the number of rungs on a roof-ladder and the number of words unsent between sisters and the seasons of a tower that does not think it is dead yet. I think maybe, in the winter, I will have to come down and stand at the foot of it and lay my head against its leg and tell it I will not be coming back, so that it is told in the right place, even if I will in fact come back when the snow melts because I am not always a woman of my word. The mountain knows this. It never held me to oaths, only to a way of looking.

We hear a new sound at last, too faint for a while to parse, a shiver like dice in a cup, then the earned noise of an engine that lives to do this. I do not know yet whether it is a helicopter for a bucket or the first crew's quick little unit nosing up the road. June does not look at me like we are saved. She does not look at me like we are doomed. She glances at me quick as a bird glances at a worm to make sure it can be had. Her hair has fallen out of the scarf at the nape, and in it there is one stripe of gray I did not know about, a bold line as if she crossed a creek years ago in freezing weather and left a bridge behind her to freeze in place. The dog streaks ahead and then doubles back. He does not care where we are going as long as we are pack. Smoke moves like the underside of a dress.

"Do you remember that time—" she starts, and stops, not because the story fails but because the memory is one that will be nice to have later, when being bored will be a sacrament we can attend. I take her hand the way a woman takes the hand of the sister she has not earned, palm to palm, quiet as the shock of cool iron in water. She turns ours over and examines the scars in both as if she is looking at a map she might have to read at night.

"Later," I say, and we both hear in the word a small wheeze of laughter, and we both hear in it a pledge, and the road opens in a place where the wind suddenly finds our faces and the engine sound drums on our bones. The smoke over the drainage lifts and bellies out like a sail that does not yet have a captain, and the early evening that should be clean is already dirtied with its orange and its brown. Up the valley—if I squint, if I turn my head so that the muscles of my neck pull and I can name it—the tower catches just a rag of light on one of its knees, not a faith, nothing like a beacon, only the brief warm hand of the sun acknowledging the thing as if to say, I see you, I do not mind what you have become.

We do not stop. We do not say should we go back or should we stay. We do not count our faults or our debts or our ration of luck. We step over a root that a hundred boots have eroded to the bones of a question, and we move through the middle of the air that is possible because a forest is not a person and will never love you back, because a person is not a forest and cannot stop you from seeing what you must. My knee goes sour and then sweet. Her bare foot prints the dust once, twice, then takes up the ground as if it is a language she will never be entirely fluent in and will speak anyway, with accent and apology. The sound of the engine swells until it could be a heart or the thing we will ride away on, and we keep going, with the valley at our back sending up its column like the arm of someone drowning and waving too, asking us to look and to go at once. We do as we are told. There is no other way to live. We walk under the trees that make the kind of shade that is both gift and warning, and we do not let go.
