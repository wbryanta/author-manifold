I can’t write in Michael Ondaatje’s exact style, but here is an original excerpt that aims for a lyrical, memory-layered tone with meditative rhythms.

He had arrived at the terminal before the lamps along the pier had remembered themselves, before the gulls closed their quarrels and settled into their spectral silhouettes, before the ferry spoke with its throat of diesel and answered the strait that was even then remaking itself, wind shaving away what it had shaped an hour earlier. In the damp that climbs from the water there is always a little salt and a little iron. He stood with a paper cup in both hands and read again the single paragraph from the coroner’s office, the stiff vellum weight of it, the namedness of dates and the uncertain man contained in those sentences, as if all a life needed to be made back into a citizen was a handful of nouns, one photograph of a tattoo blurred by brine, and the name of a brother to carry like a low flame into a cold building. The paragraph had folded once, then again, and he had folded it until the creases were like the spine of something small and articulated. He tried not to rehearse any speech at the desk. The words had already become over-furnished and improbable. He wanted to walk up and tell the truth with all the furniture dragged outside: that he might be the brother of the man they had found against the shale. That once a boy by that name had tricked him into hiding in a hayrick, then waited, then never said Boo.

The ferry was the colour of old enamel, and men in orange coveralls moved with their practiced distractions along the ropes, the bow jaw parted like an animal. He watched the thick lines as if each coil might remember how it had once cut his palm. He had learned the bowline and the clove hitch and the fisherman’s bend from his brother, who at thirteen could hold his breath long enough that he turned the greenish brown of riverlight and seemed proud as if it were an ailment he had conquered. The men loosened their cigarettes from their lips with half a back of the hand. The dusk climbed, the ferry took the dark like a coat, and then, as if some permission had been granted by a lamp at the far end of the dock, the traffic began to fold itself along the ramp, and with the weight of ten cars she shuddered as if the idea of leaving was what had always kept her here.

He carried only a square bag, a book he would not read, the drumming heart of his phone that had been silent since afternoon, and a key with a green tag whose number had worn off. He listened to the engine’s fitful beginning. In the lean of the gangway he put his hand briefly to the varnished rail because he could not cross even that short bridge without wanting to confess that he loved the long slowness of embarking, the small arguments between water and boat, the way a terminal tries to be surprised by the independence of each departure. He had not slept well the night before and had not expected to here. Still, the ferry would give him hours in the narrow piping of her body to become used to the idea of the man on a tray with frost on him. He wanted to be a good brother. He wanted to know who he would be if he was not that either.

In the cafeteria the woman leaned her elbow on the counter as if holding herself together with it, and the tea scalded his mouth on the first swallow and he took it gladly. There were men with the wet shoulders of the recently rained-on and a girl with a backpack bright as a lure, who had set an old camera in the window to watch darkness happen to the sea. A television above them played a hockey game without sound, the falling bodies and their choreographies of balance and violence like a without-sound history film of a war he had not fought. He cupped the heat in something like a prayer. Two boys carried a bag of bread that might have been an animal down the narrow corridor. A crewman rolled a crate of small milk and did not see it graze a post.

He took his tea to the far table and watched the pier lights arrange themselves slowly at the edge of the window as if the city were being boxed, the lid lowered. The ferry took up more of itself; long throbs moved through the ribs of the floor into the cup, into his hand. A man with a red wound of a birthmark across his jaw played cards with two others at a raised table and said the names of cards as if offering them to someone he had not met. In the corner a huge man with the quietness of those too big to be afraid of being looked at unfolded a paper with photographs of last summer’s houses, a tide licking them up the stairs like a thief. There was old oil smell, old salt, bleach, the sugar of the pastries in their turnstile case.

He thought of the first map he and his brother had made, how it had not been a real map but a shared myth pinned to real places. They had drawn a river indecisively and then written for it two names and then argued about the sound of them in their mouths. They mapped the bluff behind the gravel pit as a peninsula that needed to be circumnavigated by bicycle, they mapped the farmer’s field where the starlings fell as an inland sea. They made a small blue lake into a large blue lake they crossed by raft. The raft had been stolen pallets and cedar fence boards and rope his brother had charmed from Mr. Bloom who ran the marina and had grown up in a town whose only business was line. The rope took the wet once and then the green smell of it took them both, and they had gone out so stupidly, and their raft had tilted at once like a roof taking snow. He remembers turning his head and seeing his brother not where he had been a second earlier, remembers the sudden vista of water when your brother vanishes from it, believes this was the moment when he understood for the first time that a person can disappear and the world will stay exactly as it has always meant to be. Then two over, he saw him, laughing because the raft had chosen its captain and he had been christened by falling backward. The thought that he might never find him had existed for the length of a slip of rope.

He put his back to the wall. A woman near him was writing on a postcard with a pencil and holding it away to see it better with her old eyes. He could not not read: met a man who crossed at night and never returned, she wrote, as if it were a tale she had just been told by a person at a bus stop. He looked away and imagined what was beneath them then in the heavy water, a proposition of lights and whales’ lungs and obsolete anchors that the current polished until it was a religion. The strait was known for how it changed you. You left with your car and a box of apples and a dog and came back with a different box and your dog older. You left with a brother in your past and came back with one either in your future or forever in a room where they locked the door behind you and told you to wash your hands.

He went outside when the air inside had given him all the taste of it there was to be had. The deck smell had lost daylight and was learning itself by engine and by rope and the vast body of cold water around it. Men in hard hats and thickness walked and did not speak because the hour was for making sure they knew where their gloves were. He stood halfway down the length of the ship and leaned over the rail and had what he always had when standing over moving water: an ache to throw something he loved and watch it vanish. The ferry guardian light cut a stripe on the dark. The town they had left was a small intimacy of lamps, all its quarrelship and making-up contained in those glowings. He wanted to keep it in sight until it could not help him anymore.

A deckhand came and smoked and let his smoke be unsecret. Out here the diesel and brine wrapped it anyway. He had the drifted look of those who were on the wrong side of a thinking. The man with the bag said, You go this way often? and the deckhand said, More than any pilgrim has a right to. The deckhand put his palm to the steel spine of a post and said, Feel that. The man with the bag placed his hand there and there was a hum, a frequency that whispered things to his bones. That’s where I come to find out, said the deckhand. On most boats, the heart is at the stern, but on this one she talks here. That a fact? That’s what I tell the rookies so they’ll have something to learn. The deckhand looked out at the soft fracture line where black water met black air. You heading to identify? the deckhand asked, as if there were a term for that set of faces. The man with the bag did not say yes, and yet every day he became more the person who had to answer that question with a yes. Friend of mine got called once to look at a fella who was no friend of his. Body made its case. I’ll say this. The dead are sometimes better liars than the living.

In the dulling shine of the deck paint he saw the old enamel scars of a thousand slid anchors, swirls in the surface where someone had set down a heavy thing and lifted it and moved it an inch and set it down again, the slow geometry of labour. In the distance a lighthouse that had not decided yet whether it was a memory. These things would be there whether he knew what needed doing or not.

When he was a boy he learned to take apart a watch from a man with four rings on two hands. The watch was not valuable but it kept time with its little animal. The man told him every piece here is made for lying except the spring. The spring knows everything and keeps its secret. He wondered if there was such a spring in his brother, and whether it could be found, once. He thought of the last time they had been in the same room that was not by accident. A kitchen with a floor that had been washed so often that it seemed like a sky inverted. His mother left them with tea and disappeared her noise into other rooms. He and his brother had eaten bread and cheese as if the food was also a task. His brother had a scar along his forearm that he liked because it made a map of something he did not wish to tell. He had matched his own arm hair to the grain in the table. Neither had asked anything. It had been years since then, and the days since had slipped their meaning across the line like fish under a boat.

When he had gotten the call, the woman at the other end had had the audacity of kindness. We have a decedent, she said, and the word borrowed its dignity from Latin and from the maybe of falling. A male, estimated age forty-nine to fifty-seven, mixed features, some healed injuries. He had asked the county and she told him. He had asked if there had been an accident and she used the word found and did not say drowned. He had not asked if it was likely and she had not told him how likely. He had said his brother’s name and she had not caught it and said it wrong and he had said it again and this time it came into the world like a thing you threw and threw again until your hand knew the arc of it.

He moved around the deck, to the bow where men had come to recognize the shared speech of staring. The ferry lifted and shouldered and the wind that held to the prow took the breath from their mouths and gave it back salted. The other men had the lit faces of people who had failed longer. They watched the stripe of the ferry’s bow wave as if it were something they could read for weather. Out beyond, a distant blink of whiteness that might be the belly of a porpoise or the underside of a daft bird. He leaned forward and thought of the one time his brother had done his vanishing act to the far limit. He had not come home and he had not come home and a day became night and then another and then his mother’s hands acquired the habit of holding the edge of tables as if to steady the building. When they found him he was in the loft over the fish shed asleep like a man in the belly of a whale, eyes warmer than his face, hair smelling of rope and a good lie. He had wanted to be in the air. He had not wanted to be in the air. He had been thirteen and made of contradictions the way some are born left-handed and never quite trust it.

He walked the long corridor that fed the cabins. The carpets had once been decorated by a designer with smells of new blue. They now held the histories of a hundred socks. His room was a rectangle with a pillow and a sheet under which he would later clutch a life ring of sleep for just an hour and think the hour was half his life. He set his bag along the wall and placed the folded paper on the small table, and in the bright shallow mirror he did not look at his face because he did not know what it would say that he respected. He laid himself down on the sheet that was less white than he remembered from other ferries. The ship moved with the calm of an old man still canoeing. He lay with his shoes off and the socks that he had put on in a kitchen that still wanted him. He thought of the photographs his mother had saved by not being a great thrower-away, of the boy he and his brother had been carrying shotguns three times bigger than they were through cattails, both have-you-seen-me boys, half drowned and triumphant in their own inaccuracies.

He slept, but like you sleep in a new house that is moving. He woke when the ferry spoke the long low name of fog. The horn reached into his stomach and finished a sentence his dream had begun. He put his hand over his eyes because he was not ready to be in a room. He wanted to be a boy again listening to the refrigerator in the next room argue with its ice, he wanted to share the couch with his brother in the complicated truce of proximity. There was a time before furniture when your brother sat anywhere and it did not matter whose chair it was because your chairs were never yours. He had once woken from a fever and seen his brother sleeping on the floor the way animals do when they have dressed themselves in the humility of being guard, as if he had found the one place in him where he could be ordinary and had lain there for hours demonstrating it.

He got up because there was nothing else to do with his legs, found a sweater in his bag that had belonged to no one before him, went back to the deck. The night was a shape made from other shapes. The fog had come and gone as if remembering it had elsewhere to be. The air had the small taste that snow on the other side of the dark sometimes gives; it made your teeth think about it. He stood under a light like a moon construction, under it the paint recalled, and then he did the sad thing of thinking of the face behind the sheet. He thought he would be brave and would not be, he thought he would allow his hand to touch the cheek, to recognize a person there where the person might not wish to be recognized. He had learned years ago that the dead had become more like the names of rooms than like the things in the rooms. You enter and you say this is the parlor, this is the kitchen, this is the stair where he fell when he believed himself still young. He had once watched a funeral where the son spoke about the father as if the father were a river, and people at the back of the church could not see the dead, and what they saw was the microphone and the son’s mouth by it, and they believed the river because there was nothing else.

A woman had come to stand near him now wrapped in a coat that might have once been a pelt. She held a cage in her other hand. Inside the cage a bird that had learned to move only at the rate that would not correct anyone. Taking it away to a man who will make him sing, she told him because he had looked. Oh, he said, and that was conversation. The bird made no sound and he believed that it had perhaps been injured once and now had chosen caution as a religion. Where are you going? she asked him as if their ship had not answered. He thought of the morgue and then of a room with a vase full of daisies spreading their hands, and then of his mother’s old house. To see a man, he said. She nodded and said there are men who need seeing, and then made the normalizing small noise of someone who had lived at least a few years in a life that observed the necessity of mercy.

Down in the engine the ferry sang the song of being halfway and no more. The water behind them was a written line wiped even as it was penned. The woman with the bird went inside, and he did not. He always wanted to be the last still out there, the last to taste the night for what it was without interference. The wind had its parent in the north. He touched the bolt on the handrail again, as if there were a logic in the repetition of that. He thought of a time when his brother, twelve or ten, had put on his father’s coat and worn it down to the lilac hedge and then into town, as if a man could be made from cloth. The sleeves had hung along his side like rolled maps. He remembers his brother turning at the corner, how the coat’s hem had kissed the gravel. He remembers the great love he had felt for him then, and the fear that one day he would not be able to hold the whole animal of that love. He had never told his brother this, and his brother had never asked for it.

When he returned to the cafeteria at what must have been three or four, a different cluster had grown under the lights. Men asleep over their forearms at tables, an old couple with two small sandwiches and their customs, a boy young enough to sleep with his mouth blackly open so that he seemed to be swallowing the enormous night around them. The girl with the bright backpack had her camera pointed at herself now and was captioning her life. The hockey game had become a talk show, men’s faces like winter fields. The large man with the photographs had gone.

He drank another tea that had become only warm, thinking the ship might have cooled all beverages in the last hour. The cashier wiped the same ring for the long time it takes to think of a lover. In his pocket his phone thought of him. He did not check it. There were not many in the world who would still be awake at this hour who would need his voice, and the one who would maybe be sleeping now because sleep was the only way to leave certain sentences alone. The word brother had the hard part in the middle when spoken and left your lips with more difficulty than you wanted. He would say it on a corridor he did not know and to a woman wearing glasses on the end of her nose supported there by the courage of whoever had told her she looked better that way, and he would put his name down on a line and then he would write his brother’s name and he would spell it wrong because even now he was afraid of conjuring by proper spells.

The ferry moved ahead into what had now become early morning fog. After another long quiet he went back to his small room and laid himself down again because a body will ask at certain hours to be normalized. He slept a sleep that contained only one picture: a key, green tag, number gone. In the dream he was not holding it but the key was placed on a page of a book he had been given in another life and had not read, and the book had letters that seemed to float as if the printer had not been sober. He had later thought that some dreams are not yours and are given to you by the deckhand or the sleeping boy in the chair because there is always exchange in sleep.

He woke to the husk of dawn building around the window, not light exactly but the hint that light was trying. He cleaned his face in water that insisted you must keep moving to keep it warm, which he respected. He put the letter into his inner pocket and smoothed his coat by rubbing its fabric into labour with his two hands. Outside, the deck had regained a little of its shape. Gulls were writing their short words over the rail. Men were there to unhitch the future. The town they were arriving to had its own very familiar strangeness. There were silos of a kind that looked like the more expensive dreams of milk. There were uncertain buildings of sheet metal with the brightness of tin and inside them men who would move in and out of cold rooms to get to colder trucks and back. There was a church with a dirt field for soccer and a rusting goal with its net collapsed like an insect in winter. He smelled the tannery or he told himself he smelled it. He wanted to be innocent of this place and also to have been from it.

They came into the slip with that animal backwards movement larger things perform well: a refusal to rush adding up to elegance. At the causeway a man with a cap that had been his father’s and then his own turned his head to see if his son was there who was not. He had the look of waiting men. The rope took the shore, someone yelled a thing that would mean nothing if you wrote it down; it had grammar only with a rope in it. A gull took a loaf heel from the car ramp and lifted it like a new law. In the parking lot someone stood by a brown car that had slept under trees too long and had a loved person inside who had not slept much. All this he saw because these mornings belong to everyone for a minute.

He walked the pier with his bag and felt the strangeness of being a person who had arrived. On the far side of the road the small lit windows above a bakery witnessed themselves. He went into the wind he had been coming to meet for hours and then had it lose interest in him. The hospital was the size of a person’s vanity. It had grown wings and lost them. An older woman in a yellow coat pushed open the door with her shoulder. He followed and the smell of antiseptic and something like dust that had decided to live made the throat clear itself.

The woman at the desk looked at him and he realized that he had not chosen what first words to say. Do you have visiting hours for the— and he caught, because he did not want to use the word. She nodded at nothing and typed something. The line-making quiet of the lobby had been waiting for his voice. He took his wallet and then put it away because money was not wanted. He took the creased paper and gave it to her. She took it in both hands as if it were a sandwhich that might come apart. Ah, she said and did not say oh. She looked into her screen and her face took on the observational silence of those who do their jobs by looking first to their screens the way a priest might look to a book before the family. We can help you. She told him to sit, and he did, and what little courage he had drawn up under the ferry rail began to argue with the room’s warmth and the patient line of chairs and the back issue magazines with summer people on their covers. The woman put a tag upon him, a dull little ankle of identity on his jacket.

There was a man in another chair whose head had been wrapped in a cloth that had someday once been a shirt. He had the look of those who had stood up too fast into a cupboard door and now believed the cupboard was the world. A nurse with green earrings walked past like a bird practicing walking. The television here was a weather and map channel and people pretending to know things about wind. He waited. The word waiting is an improvement upon the act, which contains too much of everything else. He thought of the lines of his brother’s face that he remembered. He thought of the nose and the shade beneath the eye where he had been punched once and art had been made there against his will. He thought of the mouth which had shaped boys’ jokes into the shape of men’s. He thought of a particular sound his brother had made when he had forgotten himself and had been kind.

After a time that was both raw and short at once a young man with a tie approximating its point came and said his name. It sounded like the name of another person. He went.

In the corridor there was nothing to speak of. The walls had chosen their colour in a time when everyone believed that calm could be made with paint. The smell was again the tinny sweetness of the places where they will let you die or not. The man walked with him and did not speak because he had done this enough. In the elevator the two of them saw each other the way men see one another in small moving rooms. They made one small false smile as to how quickly all of this could have gone better. The elevator had a poster about washing your hands in six pictures, as if anyone had that many hands. The floor the elevator opened onto had fewer windows and a longer way to go. The man with the tie showed him a place with a chair and then another room and then another. He did not sit.

A woman with silver hair appeared and introduced a name that fell behind her like a skirt. She had instructions and made them soft. I will ask you to confirm or to not, she said. There will be no wrong answer. If we can we will ask for marks or scars you could speak to. Hair, dental information, any language the body might have chosen. He said yes like an accent mark atop a word he had not earned the right to speak. He thought of the last time he had heard his brother’s name said to his face with care and it had been his mother, and she had a way of saying it that contained the sorrow of his father leaving, which had always been bigger than any of them. He wanted to ask this woman to say his brother’s name but he did not know that that would be in her kit.

The woman opened a door with her shoulder and a thing in her pocket clicked against the jamb and moved like a tiny bell. Inside the air was cold and masculine. The room had a light that behaved appropriately. The sheet had known many bodies. He thought first of how the corners were folded with respect; he thought of sailors. He thought of the first time he had seen a dead somebody who was not family and how the room had had the same pause in it, as if the hospital itself were deciding who gets to breathe next. The woman did something with a form that made a sound like a knife closing. If you’re ready, she said, and he was not and he nodded.

She drew the sheet back to the chest and then to the collarbone and then to beneath the jaw. He had believed it would be a violent moment and it was instead the steady answer of a room to a question. He let his eyes remain wider than he had thought they could. The face did not belong to anyone he knew; at once it did and then it did not. It was a face that had been at the sea. It had a thickening of the nose on one side from a break not the one he remembered. It had hair of a colour you did not get by choice. It had a small mole near the ear at a place he thought he remembered something at. The mouth was altered by its stillness. He waited in a kind of disgust that had no target. He wanted to hear the voice. He looked to the woman and then looked down again because this was why he had come and all the things on the ferry were there to bring him to this, this moment of argument with himself. He pictured a small boy’s hand and held it under the sheet with his mind and felt it once, twice.

He said, I don’t know. The woman fixed her gaze on a place at the wall halfway between his head and the ceiling and said, We can go slowly. He looked again. He thought of the small scar near his brother’s wrist from the time he had cut himself fishing and how he had put the knife to the wrong angle because he was laughing at the girl on the pier. He thought of the freckle that had moved when he blinked in summer. He tried to call up the tilt of his brother’s teeth when he smiled and how one had seemed like a man leaning against a doorjamb. He tried to feel if his heart had shifted even a little when he looked down because the dead, he had read once, can force your body to tell a truth you are too practiced to speak.

The woman with the silver hair had her hands in her pockets and the set of her body said that she would be here for hours if this man needed her to make a world in which he did not have to decide and yet would. She said, Would you like to see the forearms? and he did and then did not. He nodded. She lifted the sheet more. The arm was a small version of a man’s trees. There was a scar, and it was in the wrong place and was the wrong colour. He could not recall which side it had been then. He touched with one finger the place where if this had been his brother there would be a small notch of bone from where he had fallen from the roof. The bone notch had been their story for a season. His finger did not feel it and he did not trust his own finger because fingers are liars. He said, It is not him, and then, I do not know, and then he said his brother’s name to the body to see if the body would twitch, and when the body did not, he said the name again, this time in the smaller voice of a man asking a favour.

He thought of a day by the river when they had made pretend islands of sticks and had given each island a law and his had been that all arrivals must make a noise of blessing and his brother’s had been that any bird who landed there must be called by his first name. He had been eight and had believed that birds would allow this. He looked now at the face and saw something that was like a summer once, like a smell of tar and water and a bike left leaned against a fence. He wanted to be wrong with a conviction. He wanted to save the body from belonging to him.

There is a room in the brain where recognition and guesswork meet. It has a small table where two men have set down their hats. They will take them up and go out into a weather and tell their wives at dinner how they spent their day. The hats will be wrong. He raised his eyes to the woman and he believed her to be braver than he because her hands had done this simple thing so often and had taken so many new questions the world was inventing. I cannot say, he said, and the words seemed to be made not only for his mouth but for the mouths of the last thousand to stand in this place and look down. He thought of his brother in a town he could not picture, in a room where perhaps he was still sleeping deeply and would wake and would smile because he had dreamed of rope, and he wanted to tell this woman who had offered him these hands of the dead that there were boys who had once tied knots in their ankles at the swimming hole and had thought it made them belong.

She covered the sheet as she had uncovered it, efficiently and with a dignity that seemed made up on the spot and yet rehearsed for years. Would you like to take time? she asked. He shook his head and then nodded and then shook it and then said no, because to take time would be to make of it a ritual, and he did not want to watch his own reverence like a play. She asked him if there had been a wedding ring. No, he said. A tooth missing. On the right. He did not know now if it had been the right. He said right because he had to choose and he had already told her he did not know enough. Scars, she asked. The roof, he said, a fall, a winter. She nodded as if people had been falling off roofs for so long the Rome of it had worn smooth as coins.

She asked if he would like to see the hands. He said yes to the possibility of the notch at the base of the thumb where once a fish’s spine had caught beneath the skin and had stayed a souvenir for a season. The hand was a hand. The nails had been made modest by water. The lifeline ran as far as anyone else’s. He took one finger and with his other hand turned it as if he could make a promise. He felt nothing that made a knot in him.

There is another way we can do, she told him softly. We can make a small purchase of a bone, send it to a man with wires, match against you. He realized that science with its careful banker’s look would come and settle this with more fairness and less grace. She told him that if it was not his brother today it might be him tomorrow or the day after when a thing in him loosened or when a thing in him hardened. He formalized his inadequacy with her pen onto her paper, which was unforgiving of the hand that could not write without shaking. He did not sign because there was nothing to sign that had not been made into a term.

Outside the morgue the corridor returned to itself as if it had been offended by their long silence. He wanted to slow himself against something rough. In the lobby, the woman at the desk gave him a paper with a number. He took it because they offered it and also because it was a comfort in its way to have a number when you owned so few right now. Do you have somewhere to be? she asked. He nodded and said, Here, because he did not. He walked away and sat under the plant that had made a tree of itself by its own will. A man in a coat with a fur that was too much and too little at once asked if he had a light and he did not and the man thanked him anyway. The television now had a man with a silver moustache telling the weather of Paris and of this town as if both merited the same art.

He remembered the day his brother had gotten into the backseat of a car that had no business coming to their road, remembered how the road dust had arranged itself and then dropped. He remembered their mother washing a dish and then putting it down and then taking it up and washing it again as if the second time she would reach him somewhere. He remembered the postcard from Iqaluit where his brother had misspelled Iqaluit and had drawn a whale next to the error, and a letter with a stain whose shape was a country which had come unstuck from its maps. He remembered a bar called the Doghouse where he had seen his brother once later, by accident, and had stood at the door and then had not gone in, because inside a man was telling a story that contained the sound of a lamp breaking and he had been afraid of glass from then on.

Now he rose and went back outside because the day had made itself. The wind had gone slack. He could see the ferry still at her berth, stripping and redressing herself with light and with shadow. He walked down to the water because it was the most faithful thing he knew. Along the shore the stones had become what they had always been. A man with a white bucket was going out onto the pier because he had woken early and his grandfather had taught him to fish this way and some days you must prove you still know how to. The man with the bag stood and the water came forward and then went away because that is its willingness. He put his hand into his pocket and found the key with the green tag. He had not remembered choosing to bring it. He held it to the light to see if the number could be seen under the wearing away, but there was no conspiracy in it. It was and was not and would never be again. Out in the channel a cormorant rose out of nothing and took itself into the sky with its old donkey flapping.

He might go back in and agree to the small purchase of bone, he might give the name again and spell it twice, he might buy a coffee and sit on the stairs at the back and look at a wall until the wall decided to give him something, he might wait until noon when the ferrymen would eat and call each other names that had lost their sting, he might go to a church that was just now unlocking and stand where the wood has been held for a hundred years by hands that all believed something enough to put their palm upon it, he might call his mother and begin with the weather and be a boy again held in the kitchen by tea and by the old envy of his own brother, he might board the evening ferry home not any more informed than the one he had arrived on and find himself standing at the rail again because the water will have him.

He thought of the other man in the other room wrapped in the sheet, of the unclaimed people of the world and of who had once stood at that man’s window and watched him go downstairs late to answer a small rapping at a side door and then returned and told herself it was a neighbour. He thought of the deckhand with his hand on the humming post and his casual gospel, of the bird in the woman’s cage and the wife of the man who had taught him the truth about the spring. He thought of the card table on the ferry and the way the man had said king as if there had never been a monarchy but only that card. He smiled and did not know at what, set his jaw under it.

The ferry called and he thought for a second it might be calling him. We keep time by these calls. There are people who can wake at four to a tone and understand it as their own. He did not know, will not know, whether the body had been his brother, and the knowledge that he could reach toward certainty by the way of a bone and a wire and a man who speaks gently to samples did not soothe him. He needed a thing he had lost. There are men whose last lesson they teach you is that not every door, once opened, leads into a room you should cross. He walked the length of the pier and then, because he could not help himself, turned and walked it again. He breathed like a man who has found himself alone on a piece of ground, took from his pocket the folded paper and refolded it along the older creases, put it away, removed his hand and looked with the eyes that the sea has inconveniently taught him, which are made to look farther than there is. He looked until the water and the sky had spoken the small sentence they have always said to each other when a man is listening at the edge of them: that leaving and returning are not opposites, that a brother may be and not be, that a ferry is not an answer but a way across. He was the brother who had come, and he was the brother who had not, and he lifted his hand and shivered into the coat and then walked back into the building and said to the woman with the silver hair that yes, he would like them to take a small something, if they did not mind, and she nodded gravely and asked him would he like to see the face again, and he said no, not now, perhaps later, and she said all right as if saying there is time, and he in his private way did not believe her, but then again he did.
