By the time the sun cleared the tallest pecan at the fence line the ditch had given up pretending to be anything but a cut of powder, and the mainline lay in it like a long, gray bone. My brother was already down on one knee in the furrow, thumb pressed against the cracked PVC, his face bent close as if the pipe might whisper how it broke. The air smelled of hot earth and the faint, chemical sweetness of last season’s spilled glue. We had pulled the caps off the ends at dawn and a little water had bled out and been gone in the time it took to squint into it, turned to a darker stripe and then crusted over. June had come on fast this year. The grove wore its leaves like it was tired of keeping them, and the peaches along the middle rows were a little smaller than my hand, the skins already blushed though they shouldn’t have been yet. Bees dithered at the clover that managed to stitch itself between rows, and gnats made a halo around Luke’s ear when he looked up at me.

“Found you,” he said, and tapped the line with his knuckle where the crack ran—hairline for a foot or so and then yawning at the coupler like something gnawed it. The split was clean and white as a bone marrow. “Right where the shade hits every afternoon. Like it softened itself from laziness.”

“That’s not how PVC works,” I said, but I was thinking of the afternoons I’d watched this very stretch when the whole world wobbled in the heat and the little micro-sprinklers stuttered and caught and spun. The shade slants where you expect mercy, but all it does is fool your eye about how much sun you already had.

He grinned, dirt on his front teeth. He always did that when he thought I was going to correct him—a kind of dare. “Heat on, heat off, day in and out. Even plastic gets tired.”

We had set the five-gallon bucket of primer and glue a few feet away, along with the hacksaw and a stack of slip couplers, and there was a little hill of fine plastic shavings at my feet already—from cutting back a cracked elbow near the pump shed. The pump itself sat under its corrugated hat and pretended to be sound. I’d rebuilt the impeller last fall with what I had left of Dad’s stash of gaskets, and for once, a fix had held through the cold months. June was the true test, though. June was when everything sweated. Me. The line. The money.

Luke brushed his hands against the seat of his jeans and stood, wincing the way he always had since he’d blown his knee out on a construction job near Fresno years back. “Textbook, really. Right at the fitting. Less give.”

“It’s textbook because you’re here,” I said. “Everything breaks when you show up, so you can get the pleasure of saying, ‘I told you so’ when we’re patching it.”

“You saying it wasn’t busted before?” He stretched, hand pressed to his back. “Because we both saw the puddle out past the fig yesterday. You sent me the picture.”

“I sent you the picture so you’d bring your toolbox and your good saw instead of showing up with your bare hands and a bad attitude,” I said, and he laughed, for real, and I was surprised at how good it felt to hear it bounce off the undersides of the leaves. The laughter had the same note it had had when we were boys and messed around at the tailwater ditch with our summer haircuts and scabbed knees. He’d always brought a certain joy to fixing what was broken, a cocky ease that had gotten him far enough away to come back and lord it over me in summers.

He crouched again and ran his finger along the crack the way you’d feel for a fish hook under skin. “You see the gopher tooth marks? Here, here.” He tapped the edges. “He chewed out the seam trying to get at the cool. They like to hear the water. They’re worse this year.”

“They’re worse every year,” I said. “We’re worse. We gave them the run of the place.”

The sun pushed down another notch. Heat lay across my shoulders, and I could feel my shirt turn damp. I stepped into the row and lifted a branch into my palm. The leaves felt like money these days. Everything did. The marmorated stink bugs had found us last season. The sugar levels had dropped and there were soft spots on a third of the crop. The packer had sent our pallets back with a little yellow note that said, “Noncompliant,” as if we’d brought the wrong child to school. Dad would have said that’s the risk when you depend on strangers to tell you your fruit is fruit. But Dad had died at the end of last summer, his fingernails purpled still from primer and his breath thick with orchard dust even in the hospital. I could not remember my last conversation with him cleanly; it was like a row seen down the length, wavering.

“We have to cut out two feet, at least,” Luke said. “I brought a new section. You can eyeball it or you can measure it with the tape like a civilized man.”

“A civilized man knows his own pipe,” I said. The way we could talk around each other was a muscle too, and we’d kept it strong. He passed me the hacksaw, the handle warm from his hand.

When the blade’s teeth bit and began to sing, the smell of old water came up from the pipe, a mineral ghost. My lungs tugged the air in faster. The white dust came off on my forearms like chalk, mixed with the pepper of ground dirt and the bright smack of the primer when I opened the can. I smeared purple on the inside of the coupler, turning it with my fingers, nails already a ruin. Luke had the new length at the ready, ends dry, and when I slid it in and twisted, glue smeared like snot. We held it a long breath and then another and then longer still, the kind of hold beyond what seemed necessary, the way you hang on to a word that has already been said because you cannot bear to let it go alone.

“You’re not going to get pressure if the sand trap is full,” Luke said, voice flat, as if he were talking to the pipe, not me. “Did you backflush it last week?”

“What do you think?” I said. I set my jaw against the coupler. “Every Sunday, like church.”

“Don’t talk to me about church,” he said. “You took Dad’s pew like you took his house. There’s people look at you like they’re smelling you.”

“They look at me because I got continuing obligation, and I show up,” I said. “They look at you on the days you show up like you’re a ghost. Don’t pretend you don’t like it.”

The glue got tacky. I could feel it set through my hands, the way you can feel a newborn breathing just by holding them. I let go. He let go a second later, in that old defiance that had nothing to do with glue and everything to do with the two of us.

He wiped his hands on a rag and tossed it toward the bucket, missing, and bent and missed picking it up on the first try too. “Sam,” he said, and then “Sam” again, like he was trying out how my name sat in his mouth. “Can we just be, today? Can we just fix this and not chase each other through the rows like raccoons at a corn crib? It’s too hot to be mad.”

“You went straight for church,” I said. “Somebody says ‘church’ and here they come,” I pointed, and a line of ants marched up the inside of the ditch.

He rolled his eyes. “Fine. Ants. Weather. The Mets. My knee. Anything but the thing.”

We’d been doing that dance for months since the Realtor’s flyer had found its way under the pump house door, since the man with the comb tracks in his hair had knocked and said words like “equity” and “untapped potential” and “escape hatch” and “options” and slid a glossy trifold that showed a two-story with faux shutters and a driveway wide as a road. Luke had emailed me Zillow links from his apartment in Bakersfield, joking that we could each have our own courtyard if we liked. I had deleted them without clicking, then sometimes, in the dark, had typed “nearby” into the search bar and scrolled until my thumb hurt, looking for something that looked like here and not finding it.

We walked down to the next riser, where the black spaghetti of micro tubing sprouted like nestlings. Most of the little heads were chalked white with lime. If I flicked them, sometimes they spat a thin thread of water and then died, like it was too much effort to keep on.

“You have to watch for pressure,” Luke said, unscrewing one and holding it up to the sun to see the grit in it. “If it drops, air sucks the sand. The whole orchard is a lung.”

“Tell that to the well,” I said. The water table had fallen the last decade, each year another inch or two like the ground was lowering its shoulder. We’d had a good rainy winter this year, and the creek had run a whole month longer than last, but it felt like memory now. The pump house smelled like hot metal and mouse piss and the throat-prickle of chlorine. The foot valve clanked sometimes at night and I dreamed of it, a bell in a drowned church.

Luke put the clogged micro head in his mouth and blew, his cheeks plumped, little boy for an instant. Grit left a gray crescent on his lower lip, and I flicked my finger at it without thinking, a brother thing, and he smacked my hand away—also a brother thing. He spun a new head on and hopped up to look at the next riser. His knee squealed something and he cursed, then grinned and did it again, as if to prove it could.

We went on like that for an hour, two, the sun doing its part and unspooling heat out across the block. The older trees hissed through their leaves when the wind came from the west, and the sound of it made me think of Dad’s snake belt, how he’d pulled it through the loops slow sometimes to make the sound and laugh at us for jumping. He’d learned the orchard from his father and the orchard had learned him too. He had the same habit of stepping over the row at the exact same place each time so that even when the ground shifted, the path held. When he died, people came to the house with casseroles that sweated in their tins, and they patted my shoulder like I was a heifer learning the gate and said, “He’d be proud of you, keeping it going.” I had wanted to ask them where keeping it going led, whether it was a place or a circle.

“The developer called me,” Luke said, too casually, as if the sentence had fallen into his mouth from a tree and he’d had to speak to avoid choking on it.

I jammed a little length of micro into a riser with more force than necessary and watched it bend wrong and stopped. “Of course he did. He’s called everyone from here to the canal. He’s bought that weed patch north of Turley Road. The old man with the black dog—he’s got a mailbox now with a number on it, and a slab poured. That’s whose phone you answer?”

He didn’t answer, just moved to the next riser, his shoulder hunching and rolling like it used to when he was pitching and the count ran full.

“And what’d you say to him?” The question came up as if on a wire. “What’d you say when he told you our dirt would make fine cul-de-sacs?”

“Sam…”

“What’d you say?”

He clipped a length of spaghetti with his teeth and spat the end. The spit hit the dust and made a dot that looked obscene in all that dryness. “I said we’d talk about it,” he said.

I set my jaw and unscrewed a head that broke half inside and cursed. This too was a liturgy. “We. Who’s we.”

He didn’t bite. The word developed its own coat of heat between us.

“Say it, Luke. You said you and I would talk about it?”

“I said,” he said, and then he set down the pliers and stood and faced me, and his face had that set to it that meant he’d decided to be honest. “I said I thought you’d come to your senses and realize that this. This”—he flung his arm wide and the orchard shimmered inside the gesture, leaves swaying and little strings of web glowing between them and the air itself a veil—“is killing you, Sammy. I said my brother is not some martyr to a row of trees whose time is done.”

I squatted and worked at the broken plastic inside the riser with the screw end of my knife. The plastic crumbled in a mean way, just enough to let me get a piece and then break it so I had to start again. I could feel his eyes on the top of my head, where a bald patch had begun and was a joke to everyone but me. “And what’d he say when you said that?” I asked, as if it were a funny story.

“He said it was good to be on the same page as the reasonable brother.” He said the last two words gently, and if he meant to soften it it failed. “It’s just a phrase, Sam. It isn’t a decree.”

“Reasonable,” I echoed. “Not the good one, you mean. Not the loyal one. Not the one who stayed and put his father in the ground and mowed the crabgrass off the grave when the mower blade threw sparks.”

He crouched so we were at the same height, his bad knee popping. He put his hand on my shoulder. It felt hot and foreign. “You are good,” he said. “You are loyal. You also are not a tree. You can move.”

I jerked my shoulder under his hand and threw the knife down so it stuck in the dirt a little and shivered and then fell over. “You moved,” I said, and stood and went to the pump shed without looking to see if he followed.

Inside the corrugated shade it was ten degrees less and smelled ten times worse. The pump motor had a scar across it from where Dad had slipped with a socket one winter and dropped it. There were spider eggs in the corners and a brittle hornet nest attached to the far wall. When I opened the valve to backflush the sand media filter, the water came out like a sigh, not a flood. A little spurt of brown went into the drain trench and then settled into a trickle. Luke’s shadow fell across the door behind me.

“You’ve already done it,” he said. There was no accusation in it. He leaned in and took the handle and closed it and then opened it again and watched the water like he was reading a book he’d read three times already.

“The well is tired,” I said. “Dad used to say the well gets tired but it’s us who feel it. This winter was good as we get. Next winter will be God’s business. The year after—who knows. But the way the county sniffed at the permit last time, and the way the neighbor’s pump threw its bearings last August…” I let the rest sit.

He stood up and ran his hand along the plywood shelf where Dad had kept his ball valves and pressure gauges. There were old receipts tacked to the wall, a calendar from the feed store five years old, a picture of a woman in a bikini holding a wrench. “Dad said a lot of things,” he said finally, something careful in the words that made me turn.

“What do you mean.”

He looked at me then, eyes suddenly the exact blue they were when we were boys and got caught sneaking peaches out before ripe and tried to look like angels. “He said things about land and men and work that weren’t necessarily scripture, Sam. He said never sell. Except he sold fifteen acres across the creek when Mom got sick to pay for the chemo. He said the water would always be there because there was always rain in the mountains until there wasn’t. He said we’d stick together and then he died and left me a note on a legal pad that said ‘Take care of your brother,’ but didn’t tell me how to make you take care of yourself.”

The pump ticked twice like a cooling engine. Dust motes pointed out the beams of sun that got in through the chinks. For a minute my chest did not know whether to lift or drop. Then it dropped and kept on dropping until it hurt at the bottom and then raised again in a kind of bruised miracle. “You got that note too,” I said.

He nodded, a laugh without sound. “He wrote it to both of us and left it on your pillow, I think. Maybe he was hoping the Spirit would carry it across the hall.”

“He left it on the kitchen table, under the stapler, because he knew if he put it on my pillow I’d put it by my bed and know it was for later.” I was not sure why my voice was doing what it was doing.

He said, quiet, “Can we talk about selling, Sam. For real, no jokes, no snakes, no church. Just us. Can we talk about what’s possible.”

I stepped out into the light and it hit me like a physical thing, like a person you weren’t ready to run into. We walked the length of the block to where the soil turned from the richer gray to the pale caliche near the road. The county grader had dropped some gravel there in the spring, and it had already sunk into the dust like cinnamon on the top of warm milk. The orchard on the other side—Vasquez’s—was greener. He’d pulled his old trees and put in new last year, low-vigor stock on dwarfing root. He’d had to bring in a dozer for the old roots. I’d watched him over the top of our fence, drinking coffee into the wind and telling myself I was not jealous. The new trees wore little white paint from the base to the knee, to keep sun from splitting them the first years. They looked like soldiers in a clean uniform.

“We owe thirty-eight still on the note,” Luke said. He said it while staring at Vasquez’s irrigation risers, which were green and marked with neat Sharpie, not like ours, which were labeled in my handwriting and had bled to unreadable. “The bank will give us trouble if we go into fall without a buyer for the fruit. You know that.”

“The packer won’t throw us out two years running,” I said. “I talked to Tom on Tuesday. He said if we keep the size and sugar, the market’s strong. People are buying again, apparently, even though the price at the stand for good peaches is six dollars more a flat than two summers ago. He said we got burned on the bugs last year, and so we know what it looks like to keep ahead of them this time. He said he liked Dad. He’ll try.”

“Tom likes money,” Luke said. “As he should. As we should.”

A blue jay heckled from the sycamore by the culvert, and two doves made their dumb coo and pretended they were exotic. Heat lifted off the road in a way that made it look wrong, as if air had scales it shouldn’t show us. There was the sound of a distant lawn mower from beyond the canal, one of the new tracts that had sneaked up while we weren’t looking. It was a small sound and it was already ordinary and it filled me with a fear that was not the old fear but its third cousin. When I closed my eyes for half a second I could see in my mind what the developer had shown us—trees cut down, stumped, the ground shaved, a yellow machine making neat piles of a hundred years. Did that hurt me because it was sacrilege, or because it was a kind of relief?

“You’re not wrong,” I said. The words tasted like when you bit a persimmon too early, that furry bitterness. “You’re not wrong about the money, or the work, or me.” I rubbed my palm along the fence post where, a long time ago, I’d carved my initials with a particularly wicked pocketknife Dad had confiscated and then forgotten to give back. The S had sprawled all over the post, the two curves like a river and a reversed river. The wood was angry splinters then. Now it was smooth.

He took a breath, exhaled it, tried again. He had always been the better breather, the better at letting something go without changing his face. “If we sell,” he said, “if we sell we could put you somewhere with a house where the roof doesn’t leak in the back room, with a job you clock in and out of that doesn’t follow you into your dreams, with time to do—” he made a vague wave in the air “—something. Anything. If we sell I could come out of Bakersfield and stop sanding cabinets for Pauly and pretending it’s the same as building something. You could be free of this constant emergency. We could be boys again for a minute, if we wanted. Go to a ballgame. Eat a hotdog. Dad never brought us to see a real game. He promised he would and then he had a pump to pull or a meeting at the Co-op or he wanted to show us how to split a cord in an hour. All right. It was good, a lot of it was good. But you and I aren’t obliged to be good, Sam. We can be something else.”

“The orchard will be houses,” I said. “There will be a cul-de-sac where I’ve pissed a hundred times. There will be kids on scooters under this sycamore and they’ll scream and then whisper and then drive somewhere else when they’re seventeen and look back once and not miss it. They’ll name a street after Dad and he’ll be a sign with a bolt through his R. Is that okay with you.”

He answered fast. “No,” he said. “No, it isn’t okay. But there are worse things than kids on scooters. There are men older than us dying on tractors in August because they wouldn’t get off them.” He let that sit and then picked it back up. “I’m not saying I want the houses. I’m saying it’s not just two things. We could sell to the conservancy, keep it in trees or grass. We could lease back a piece. We could partner. We could turn it into a U-pick and let city people take pictures here and pay for the experience of sweating once. We could do CSAs and jam.” He gave me a sideways smile. “You make good jam. Mom taught you.”

I laughed, one bark. “Me at a table selling jam because the world likes nostalgia with sugar. Me smiling while them with their clean hands ask me why their kid got a rash from the peach fuzz.”

“You can wear gloves,” he said.

“You can go to hell,” I said, but the bite had gone out of it. A dragonfly stitched itself up and down in the air in front of us and then landed on the fence post and pretended it was just another splinter. “Do you remember when we put the frogs in Dad’s boots?” I asked, knowing I’d changed the subject and doing it anyway. “Do you remember he put his foot in and then he didn’t yell, he just shook his foot and let the frogs fall out and then said, ‘Well, those boys like damp,’ and went on.”

“He was a cold bastard sometimes,” Luke said, and he said it like a blessing.

“He left us this,” I said, sweeping my arm wide. “And everything else. How do I tell him no from his own dirt.”

“You tell him no because he left you that too,” he said. “He left you the ability to tell him no and live.”

We went quiet in agreement with the heat for a while and then turned back and walked the rows, checking risers, flicking heads, cutting out swellings in the pipe where the sun had blistered it. A king snake slid from the edge of the berm where the clover thinned and lay across my boot for an indrawn second before it moved on, as if it had felt my heat and decided it was not worth the bother. When we reached the place where the line had split at the coupler we’d fixed that morning, the glue was set and clean and even beautiful in its way, the purple trench ringed like a bruise that would heal properly. We stared at it as if it were a small painting and then went on. We did not talk about selling for another hour.

By noon the heat was such that sweat gathered where my hat band sat and then let go and ran into my eye, and my tongue felt like it was wrapped in damp wool. We went up to the truck and sat on the tailgate and ate the sandwiches I’d packed in the morning before the kitchen had heated, ham and mustard and too much pickle. He had brought a Dr Pepper in a stubby glass bottle and two oranges. The Dr Pepper tasted like poor choices and summers when we thought poor choices were high adventure. We sat with our feet the opposite way and leaned shoulders into each other without thinking, the way men on a job do without it meaning anything but also meaning everything. The sky was white at the edges where heat made it forget what blue was.

“Mom would have laughed at us,” Luke said. “She used to stand with her hands in the suds and watch us argue about who got the front seat and shake her head like we were a show.”

“She would have put her finger in my chest and said, ‘Your father is what he is and your brother is what he is and you can get good at loving them both or you can go hungry,’” I said. “Then she’d have cut the hocks off the green beans with a dull knife and made them taste like they came from God’s own garden.”

He smiled with his whole face, new lines making parentheses where you’d print a joke. “I didn’t mean this to be a fight,” he said. “I don’t want to make your life bigger by making you small. I want to help us both stand up instead of leaning at these windstorms like we’re playing a game.”

“You’re better with people,” I said. “You always have been. You could say that to the developer and make him cry. He’d give us two percent more if you asked.”

“Two and a half,” he said. “Maybe three. If I wear my clean shirt.”

“You wore your clean shirt when we took Dad to the funeral home,” I said, and the sentence felt like an arrow I’d shot not knowing where it would land.

He went serious. “Yeah. I did. I kept it clean for two days. Then I came back here and helped you pick up the windfall and the shirt got blood on it from a cut and I didn’t care.”

We chewed. We watched the dust lift like gauze behind a truck going down the canal road. I thought about the times last summer I had turned on the pump and watched the needle on the gauge go to thirty, twenty-eight, twenty-six, and how I had willed it not to drop below twenty-five because below twenty-five the sprinklers only spit and didn’t spin and below twenty-five the leaves wore that particular gray that is like disappointment’s sweater. I thought about nights I had woken up to a noise and it was the wind and I had gone and checked the stretchers on the barn roof anyway, and while I was out under the moon I had stood and peed in the shadow of the fenceline and listened to the coyotes work it out and figured I was lucky to be a man pissing on his own land in the dark.

“Let’s walk the lower block,” I said. “That’s where the pressure drops first.”

“You know what to call it when you always know the worst place,” he said.

“Being alive,” I said.

We slid off the tailgate and the cab springs bounced and pinged. The tires had thrown up little chevrons of mud all winter and then hardened, so the mud had become a sculpture that would only break when the first true rain came next fall. Which is to say it would be there all summer, reminding me. We passed the fig, which had put on an exuberant green as if it did not know the rules of the property, and a lizard leapt from one leaf to another, showing its blue throat like a trick. Down in the lower block, the ground sloped almost imperceptibly and then enough that if you let a ball go it would keep going. The water from the pump up top had to be coaxed to stay humble, to not rush downhill and drown the first trees and leave the rest dry. The line down here always ran under stress. Dad had installed an air relief valve at the high end to keep from hammering the joints when we shut the pump down but he’d never trusted it, and at the end of his life when trusting mechanisms was getting harder he’d tightened the valve with a wrench until the rubber gasket cried like a tear.

We found three small leaks before we found the big one—a seam lifted by a root and then chewed by a gopher, a jagged mouth. The gopher mound sat three feet away in a smug little dome. Luke pointed at it and said, “Consultant,” and I laughed even though I didn’t want to. We cut the pipe clean. We dug with our hands instead of getting the shovel because sometimes hands know where not to stab better. The mud under the crack was the only shade-cool thing for ten acres and I felt like laying my whole face in it. The root that had pushed the seam up was no bigger than my thumb and that too felt like a sermon I didn’t want to hear. We slid the new length in and twisted and waited and the glue grabbed and we both exhaled and only then did I look up and see that Luke was looking at me like I was something not finished.

“What,” I said, too fast.

“Nothing,” he said. “Just you look like Dad when you hold your lips like that.”

“I never hold my lips like this,” I said, and my lips did something else to prove it and we both smiled in spite of everything.

By late afternoon we had made the circuit and I had permitted myself the thought that if the pump held and the pressure reached the last row and the heads spun and it turned the dust into that dark that means water, we could get through June and July and the first half of August and then the tempering of September would come and the days would slant and the nights would lose their iron and we would be ok, which is not the same as good but in this line of work sometimes it is better. Second cutting would come. The hawks would get bold again. The wood stork would come up from the river and stand at the end of the ditch like St. Peter at the gate. The peach skins would dimple, then smooth, then dimple again, and one morning I would walk the middle rows and run my thumb along one and it would give just exactly so and I would know the boys needed to come with ladders and bins and the wash tank would need a scrub and I would stand on the edge of the pad with a cup of coffee and watch a freight roll the back fence and I would feel a way I almost never permitted myself anymore. The pump would hold. The pump would hold. The pump—

“Fire it,” Luke said. We were back at the pump house and his shirt was the color of slate. He had a crease of dirt at the base of his neck like a necklace. The sun had rolled west and eaten the shadows so that even my ears felt lit. There was a taste of metal on my tongue that was not blood.

I crossed my fingers with dirt on them and put them in my back pocket where God couldn’t see. I put my hand on the switch and thought for a second about Dad’s hand on the same switch, the little arc the first time he’d wired it and how he’d cursed and then smiled. I threw it.

The pump coughed and spun and then found its hum and then, to my slow surprise, held it. The needle on the gauge shimmied up past twenty, past twenty-five, to thirty. It hung there a breath and then dropped back a hair and then climbed back like a fish that had been let go and then decided to bite again. The pipes sang, low. The orchard began to make a sound that does not exist on recordings because it is both water and leaf and dust, and people like to pretend that there is a silence in work when what there is is a music that’s afraid to be caught. Luke went to the first riser and whooped. “She’s spinning,” he hollered, as if the orchard had an ear for praise. “She’s spinning, she’s spinning,” and he moved down the row like a doctor checking on babies. I went the other direction, and it took me a minute to realize I was smiling. You don’t notice that you aren’t until you are. Heads clicked and spit and then turned clean, little bright discs at the ends of black lines, spinning and tossing an umbrella of cold onto the dirt. The ground under them darkened in a shape I knew too well, a circle that overlapped the next and the next, a simple geometry we had learned before we learned algebra.

We had to clear a dozen heads with a fingernail or a pocketknife. We had to twist a cracked one off and put a new one on. We had to press a leaking barb into a hole that had widened with age and feel the little betrayal of it when it refused to be tight. We had to reach into the water and our hands came out for a second clean. We had to do it again because the first time we were too eager and had done it sloppy. We had to admit to each other that we were relieved. We had to not say the other thing, because it was enough for a long minute to have water.

By the time we reached the lower block again the light had shifted from glare to something else. It stopped being punishing and became revealing, the way a thing looks when you take a white sheet off it. Dust hung in the shapes of people and those shapes broke and were nothing. A hawk moved in a slow, confident line above the neighbor’s alfalfa as if it were drawing its own future and was not willing to be distracted by ours.

Luke said, not looking at me, “Three offers in my inbox. One from BlueCrest, one from a man who uses his first name only, one from the county saying they’d like to talk about an easement to preserve ‘viewshed.’ They all want to make their money and we know the world runs that way. One of them says they grew up with a grandfather with an orchard and the smell of it is the base note of their childhood and they want to keep that note humming. They used words like steward and heritage and one of them put ‘tree’ where they meant ‘street.’ But they all have the same line at the bottom with the number. The biggest one would put you out of this debt and put something in your pocket that could be a life. The smallest one would keep some of it as it is and we’d bite the chain and live in its shade and make do. I can’t make this for you. I won’t. But I am asking you to let me sit at the table when you do it and not stand out on the porch listening.”

I put my hand on the trunk of the nearest tree. I felt the vibration of the water at its base as the micro threw its spray into the bowl and the ground drank and the roots pulled. My palm came away cool for a blink. The bark under my fingers had the alligator pattern of old peaches, but in what cracks there was green, and in the notches tiny spiders had built their bright right-angle webs. I had put my mouth to these trunks in Januarys and listened to them the way you listen to a shell, as if the sea of sap would tell me what to do, and the sound had been a rushing that was a heart or was not. I had cut suckers at the base. I had painted over scars where the sun had hurt them. I had run my hand across the rough place where Dad had nailed a tack-in hook long ago to hang a bucket and then taken it out and forgotten to pull the nail.

“I will sit with you,” I said. The sentence, coming out, felt like what it feels like to take off a boot after a long day, the ache and relief coming attached. “We will sit at the table. We will read all the words. We’ll call Carol Martine at the county because she isn’t an idiot and she knows what a viewshed is when it’s not on paper. We’ll ask Tom at the packer what he’d put in if it were his, not because he owns us but because he knows what he knows. We will ask Mr. Vasquez what he did about water when he put his new stock in because he’s noble and foolish and might tell us. We will look at numbers and we will look at the map and we will not let anyone pretend a line drawn with a ruler is the same as a row. And we will decide. We will do that.” Saying we made my tongue feel less like a foreign object in my mouth.

“And you’ll keep the option of not selling in even if the money is not the big number,” Luke said. “You won’t sign us into a corner because you owe a memory a clean floor.”

“I will try,” I said. “And you will keep the option of staying even if the money is the big number and the houses look like absolution.”

He laughed. “I will. I’ll keep my clean shirt in the truck for another use.”

We walked to the top of the block where the pump house stood and leaned against it and listened. The orchard made its wet music. The pump held its hum like a singer on a note. The sky let go of its white at the edges and gave it back some blue. Somewhere down near the creek a dog barked once the way a dog does when it is reminding the world that it is a dog and the world need not be afraid but should be aware. The backs of my knees itched. My hat brim was ragged where I’d set it on nails a hundred times and torn it. The silt that had been so soft in my hands when we dug the crack had dried back into flour in the sun and the cracked place in the ditch had already forgotten the shade of wet.

“Do you remember the night of the freeze when we were twelve,” I said, because the day had brought me to the edge of it and I had to say it across, “and Dad made us get up and light the smudge pots and he said we had to keep the orchard temperature up two degrees or we were going to lose half the crop and you cried because the fuel got in your eyes and he told you to cry in the other direction so it wouldn’t blow back, and I wanted to hit him, I wanted to hit him so hard, and then at dawn when the light came and the frost had burned on the outside of the loam but not on the leaves he kissed the top of both of our heads with his dirty mouth and he said we did it. We saved it. And for a long time I thought that’s what this was. That’s all it is. You stand in the smoke and cry in the other direction and then at dawn you take the kiss.”

“I do,” Luke said. He was looking out across the rows, his eyes a map. “And I remember three summers later when he put us in charge of the irrigation and we thought we were kings and we turned the wrong valve and flooded the low block and three trees drowned and he made us dig them out and learn the smell of drowned roots and then he planted three new and said, ‘You get to live after you kill a thing with good intentions.’”

“We’ve killed a thing or two since,” I said.

“Yeah,” he said.

We fell quiet. The sun dropped behind the roadside sycamore and the shade from it fell like a cloth thrown and touched our faces and we both flinched, then let it lay. I could feel the day pulling up its roots, preparing to walk away, reluctant. I could feel the far line of the mountains in my knee the way a man feels a storm in an old wound. I had not yet thought about dinner other than to know that if I was going to make something it would have to be something I could hold in one hand while I took a hot shower with the other. I looked at the pump and felt the old ache that had a name and did not. I looked at my brother and knew the name of him and the names for the parts of his face and the names of the fights we had and the names of the apologies and the names of the jokes and the name for the particular way he watched me when I was breaking and did not touch me until I asked.

“I am asking,” I said to him, though I didn’t know for what. “Just. This.”

He stepped close and put his palm on the back of my neck and left it there. We had not done that since the day they folded Mom’s blanket on the end of her bed and you could see the place where her body was not. He did not say a word. The leaves overhead hissed with their old song. A drip from the sand filter made its small punctuation in the trench. The pump put its shoulder into its work and held it.

“Tomorrow,” he said finally, voice somewhere between light and not, “we’ll call Carol. We’ll dig out the box with the latest tax bill and the letter from the bank and we’ll look at them in the daylight instead of at three in the morning with the kitchen light making the room look like a hospital. We’ll drink coffee that isn’t burned and we’ll write down questions and we’ll ask them. We’ll see.”

“Tomorrow,” I said. “Tonight we’ll walk and check the heads again.”

“Tonight we’ll drink two cold beers and sit on the tailgate and pretend we’re not thirty-nine and forty-two,” he said. “For a minute.”

“I could be thirty-nine again,” I said. “It was a better year.” We both smiled because it was a lie.

We turned and walked the first row again, shoulder to shoulder now, not caring which of us reached down first for a head that clicked wrong, not racing to be useful. The sprinklers spun and threw their arcs and the water glinted like pennies tossed in a fountain. The muddy rings under them grew, touched, overlapped; circles made a chain, and then a sheet. It looked for a little while like a promise fulfilled. The trees, which had asked nothing but heat all day, now asked for this and got it, and the way they breathed then, if you have stood in an orchard in June at dusk you know. We moved slow as the light stepped backward. At the far end of the block the ground sloped down to that little ditch that had been dust all day. Where the water from the backflush had trickled there was a dark line, even now, and along that dark line a run of little prints where a raccoon or a cat, something unlike us, had put its feet. I bent and touched one with the tip of my finger and it made a perfect half of itself on me, and then it was gone, absorbed into my skin, same as the water, same as the day.

From farther over came the shout of a man not happy or angry but finishing something. It might have been Vasquez. It might have been the man with the black dog whose slab would become a foundation. It might have been no one human. We finished our last row and came out at the top by the pump and stood. We did not speak, because the orchard was speaking for once in a voice even we couldn’t pretend to improve with our talk. The pump hummed its steadfast note, and the water broke itself into glitter again and again and kept doing it as if there were no end to it, and for this one hour, this single hour of a June day, I believed it.
