The steam from the cannery always hung lower in winter, flattening itself against the sky as if the cold pushed down with both hands. On the morning they began, the plume was yellowed at its edges and smelled of brine and burn and something sweet-sour that Cal associated with the year he first learned what the men called the cat piss corner—where the mackerel vats overflowed and fermented just so. He had told June once that the steam had its own calendar, that in June you could taste copper on your tongue and in December salt fought sugar, but June had been eight then, busy with a plastic bucket and the chase of snails along the rocks. Now she was twelve and taller and quieter, and he took her to the end of the breakwater where the pilings creaked and the sea made a script of itself he meant to teach her how to read.

He had a way of putting his hand at the back of her head as if it were a compass he could orient, palm to hair, turning her gently. “There,” he said, when she looked toward the ferry that hissed away from its slip like a dislodged kettle. “No, here,” when he wanted her to see the water itself, not the metal on it.

“What am I looking for?” she asked. Her voice fought the wind and didn’t quite win. She tucked her chin into her coat and sniffed her scarf, which was still redolent of the dog, Gus, who had slept on it until she left the house.

“Texture,” he said. “It’s all texture. The wire-brush of a squall. The fur smooth of a lee. See the darker strip, like someone dragged their hair over the top of the tide? That’s wind coming down in a line. The lighter mottles, cat’s paws. The slicks—those are where currents meet and oil and plankton gather. The sea leaves its fingerprints.”

June tipped her head. The water was a slab in her near-sight, blue-green where the sky was in it and gray where it was itself. But farther out, there were, if she let her focus soften, letters. “Those dark bits are deeper?”

“Not always deeper,” he said, pleased that she’d asked and that for once she wasn’t just pretending to understand him because she loved him. “Sometimes the light shifts. Sometimes the bloom is different—phytoplankton make the water look green-blue, and when they’re stirred the color goes. Learn to look for movement, not just color. The darker lines can be convergences where two bodies of water press together. See the bits of eelgrass gathered like the edges of a dress? That’s where they’ve met. The smell changes at those lines if the river’s running out. You get the tannic, tea taste where the fresh water rides over the salt.”

“Taste?” She blinked up at him, dubious. He stuck out his tongue and smiled with his teeth too big in his face, a sailor even now. “You’re not serious.”

“I’m very serious,” he said, and cupped a hand and dipped it into the harbor where the swell nudged stone and sloshed back. He brought the water to his lips and touched it and spat. “See? Tastes like iron today. River’s high. The rain last week’ll be running out for days yet. Your dad says the cannery’s still getting some chinook in with the crab, there’s your reason. The fish feel the fresh and go nose-first for it even if they’re late.”

She squinted again, this time toward the cannery buildings—long, low sheds that had once been painted the color of school buses and now had peeled down to sunburn and gray. The cannery had a whistle that blowed whenever a shift changed and when the boilers started up and stopped, and it had, once, a Christmas bell that the old-timers said you could hear clear across the bay. She had never heard it in the eight winters she had been in the world, three of which she remembered, two of which her mother had been alive for. “Dad says they’re not going to be open next winter,” she said. “He said they’re going to mothball the place.”

Cal steadied himself against the rail, his palm on cold iron. The paint here was so layered with hand after hand that you could see the years in the cracking. “A man can tell a lot from what he’s heard and a little from what he wants to be untrue,” he said. “He’s right, though. They’re done after the crab. They’ll do another month if the weather holds and the boys get through the bar with full holds. After that—some suits, the kind with smooth coats and shoes that don’t know our kind of grit, will come through and write numbers and say they can’t afford the steam. Those are currents, too, when you come to it. You can read the water, or you can get read by it.”

“It’s all so…” She spread her hands. “I never know where to put my eyes.”

“That’s what we’ll fix this winter.” He was talking to her and to himself, because forty years as a pilot had made his eyes busy and his brain not so much. He saw the world in transits and ranges and lines the same way his wife had once seen every meal as a budget and every kindness as interest on a debt. He had learned to slow that down after she died, the summer June turned five and ate watermelon until her hands were striped red and her face sticky with a joy that stuck him in the heart even then.

He took a knotted rope from his coat pocket. It was the longest of his small things, the way he measured his own life, what it was he could still do. It had knots in it from stern to bitter end, two feet apart, and a tin washer on the end he used for weight. “We’ll throw this in,” he said. “And we’ll count. Simple thing first.”

June took the rope and dropped it over. It swam in a curve and the tin clicked against the stone. He counted to ten and took it back. The tin was trailing north. “See that?” he asked. “Where does the river meet the tide, today?”

“Out there,” she said, with her chin toward the mid-bay where slicks made Willy Wonka rivers across the gray.

“And if you were a crab boat and you wanted to come in? What would you feel?”

“Scared,” she said without thinking, and then colored as if she had let him down.

“So you’re smart,” he said. “So you feel something. All the good captains let that sit on their shoulders like a bird. You don’t feed it too much and you don’t pretend it’s not there. You watch. You look at the buoys. See the number four? See how she leans? That’s set. Stronger on the outside of the turn. There’s a tide rip making up on the edge of the bar—little leaps of water. When I guided them in, we’d listen to the point whare, we’d watch the flags. We’d run the dogleg at marker six and square off because you respect the bar like you respect a priest with a temper. Don’t fuss the river’s work. Time it. Find the lull between sets. You ride the back of a big one. Then you walk her in. The harbor’s a live thing, and all it wants is for you to act like you know it.”

“You make it sound like the water cares what you do,” she said.

“It does,” he said. “It tells you and tells you, and then if you don’t listen, it ends the conversation.” He reached past her to the railing and traced the notch where a storm had pulled loose a section the size of a man’s hand ten years ago. He had stood there that day too, but alone except for March wind and the two gulls that had screamed until they gave in to the storm's noise. He had watched a longliner turn in the swell and go on her beam ends, then right herself, then run on. No one talked in the bar for a day after except to say the beer was cold.

The gulls were quieter today, sated on cannery leavings and the leftover bait that slopped out of buckets to the docks. June had a gull’s eyes, bright and curious and mean when she meant it. She tracked along the water until she saw what he wanted her to see: the harrier hawk that skated the stubble of grass beyond the shore, reading the land the way he read the sea. The hawk dipped and rose, its wings moving only at the edges.

“He’s looking for what moves wrong,” she said.

“That’s all we do,” Cal said. He coughed into his shoulder.

They went to the cannery for lunch because June liked the pies they set out for the men in the cafeteria, round things you ate with a fork and that had frosting like a church lady’s hand. June’s father was at a table against the wall with two of the boys who’d made it out after high school to try college and who had come back for the winter because crab money made all your numbers jump. Nate’s hair had a cowlick that would be stuck up even if he wore a hardhat. He had always been handsome and useless in equal measure until June was born; after that he worked like a sled dog to earn the right to leave his boots by Cal’s door, where he could walk into the sea and then into safety.

“You on break?” Nate asked, and put his hand on his daughter’s head with the same instinct as Cal. “You got school, bug.”

“It’s Saturday,” she said, and skewered a piece of pie with a plastic fork.

“You keep calling her that and she’ll go sting somebody,” Cal said. He sat with his coffee, which was only coffee if you believed the letters on the pot, and felt the cannery through the soles of his boots—machinery shudder and belt shake, the unsteady drum of water in pipes. He had brought men through the bar when the cannery lay asleep the way a sick man does, groaning into dark. He had brought men through when the steam struck noon and all the gulls lifted at once as if turned by one thought. He had not brought anyone in here when it was closing for good. He wondered what that whistle would sound like, the last one, who would be the last man to lean on its rope and pull, knowing everyone would listen and know, for years after, where they had been when the sound moved through them.

“They called a meeting,” Nate said. “Thursday. The big guy from Shaw will be here.”

“What’s he know?” said Tye, one of the college boys, who had the hands for music and the shoulders for a joke. “Never seen a crab but in a bucket maybe.”

“He knows numbers,” Nate said. “He knows what he’s come to tell us. He’ll talk about how the boats can run up the coast to Westport and truck the fish from there. He’ll say we can still work in the cold storage if we want, but that winter work will be gone.” He set his mouth and looked at his daughter’s face as if reading the numbers there too, the sum of what he could keep and what he could not. “Mrs. James will be ten grand underwater on her mortgage by spring,” he said. “That’s numbers.”

“The water stays whatever they do,” Cal said, because he was old enough now to say tautologies and make them sound like warnings.

“Tide don’t pay,” Tye said.

“Tide pays if you know how to ask,” Cal said, and June watched him from under her hair. With her he tried to be only one thing at a time, a teacher or a joke or the man who had made her favorite potato pancakes the week he thought bacon was going to go dear and had bought a side and cut it down himself on the table her grandmother had set flowers on every Sunday for forty years. But in there with the smell of fish and bleach and coffee and men—that was the smell of him as he remembered himself at twenty-five, at thirty-eight, at forty-seven, when he read men as if they were water as well, who came in hard and who came to listen.

On the way back to the breakwater, June stopped at the bait cut where a boy her age was using a knife his father had probably been born with. He was cutting mackerel, the silver and green shining when he split them open so that you could see all the ways a fish is put together. He had the bottle-thick fingers of someone who had never done anything careless with his hands. He looked at June with the too-wide eyes of someone who had already taken a boat out in fog and found his way back. “You want a bit?” he asked. “Cat likes it.”

“We don’t have a cat,” June said. The dog was old now and only liked her when he was tired. He had bitten a boy once who had swung her on the swing too high, or so she told it. She loved the dog more for that than for the way he slept warm at her back. She took the mackerel bit anyway and handed it to her grandfather, who put it in his pocket as if it were coins.

On the breakwater, he said, “You want to see the inside of the water?” He had a green glass jar with a rope handle that he had taken the month before from the top shelf of the garage where his wife had put it away the summer before she died. It was a poor man’s bathyscope, the glass thicker at the bottom and swirled with the imperfections of hand-work. He liked it because it made the world familiar again—blurred at the edges, clear at the middle, like looking at your life through a good day. He let June hold the rope and dip the jar below the surface. She looked through and saw the stones and the eelgrass ribboning under and the umbrella of a jelly like a slow thought.

“It looks like the bathroom window,” she said.

“It looks like water,” he said. “It is water. And through it you can see it. That’s a trick, but it’s all tricks, and there’s no shame in that. You learn what tricks tell the truth. You learn which tricks kill you.”

The first storm hit late and hard. December had been a gentleman that year—frost on the roofs and white breath and a week of calm water that had sent the boys out to pull pots and come in smiling—and then the second week of January the low rode in, flat and dirty like a woman who’d been up all night. The bar wove itself into a bad idea. The buoys laid down and didn’t dare get up. The wind blew so hard all the men said the nails squealed in their roofs until even the ones who weren’t religious slid a sentence up at the ceiling and waited to see if it came answered.

Cal took June down to the water anyway. He gave her his oldest coat and looped a length of rope twice around her waist and then around his, as if they were climbing mountains. It made her laugh, because the dock was slick and the gulls stood, all of them facing one way like at some bird church, and the laugh cut the noise more than yelling would have.

“Here,” he said, and led her to the window in the pilothouse of the pilot boat that had been pulled up on the cradle. He tapped the glass. “Look,” he said, and pointed not at the sea but at the boat’s own reflection. The window was wet. Rain came sideways and stung. You could see the streak it left. “That’s the angle. That’s where the wind’s from. Southwest, like I told you. That’ll pull everything here harder.” He walked with her along the breakwater and had her feel with her palm the stones that ate waves and spit them back. “Count,” he told her, and they counted the sets—one-two-three-four-five, a space, then one. “He’ll have to go on the back of a three,” he said, and he was not talking to his granddaughter now but to the sea itself, the way a man tries the same words on a woman to see which ones mean.

A crabber named Finch was coming in. You could hear him on the radio that Tye had brought down from the cannery floor when it was clear that everyone with a boat they loved would be watching for the crossers. The radio made Finch’s voice tinny and closer than you wanted. He had men on board—two deckhands, one who had a wife who taught the babies at the school and the other who had never told anyone whose bed he slept in. It mattered and it did not.

“Wait,” Cal said, the rope around his middle drawing tight when June leaned with him. “Wait.” He loved the men he had brought through and hated them too, because in their moments of fear they believed in him like children. There was no taking that belief on without it costing. Finch’s boat was there, then not, then there again, lifting as the set came, and out at number eight buoy there were white manes like horses running.

“Go now,” Cal said. “Now.” He said it so quietly he could have been saying it to himself and the sea could have taken it like a prayer. Finch must have seen what Cal saw or he must have been the luckiest fool south of Alaska, because he gunned and came, and he hit the back of the third wave and the stern came up and his bow was down and he might have gone end over end, and then he was in the green trough between and on, and on, and on. A gull screamed in his ear, or it might have been June. All the men on the breakwater were still, as if movement could unbalance the world.

“And like that,” Cal said softly, “is how a man wants to go and how a man comes home.”

“Did you help him?” June asked, her breath turned to steam that ran north.

“Maybe years ago,” Cal said. “With all the times I spoke to this water, once in a while it remembers my voice.”

She put her hand inside his. The rope around their waists bit and held.

That night the power went out and the town was a boat at anchor, moving but moored. Cal lit the kerosene lamp that he hated and loved. His wife had had opinions about lamps. She had once burned the hair off her eyebrow and pretended she had meant to. He had loved her with the steadiness of the tide, but his anger with her could come in like that too. It had never swallowed them. Sometimes, in the years after her death, he thought that meant they had done a thing most people didn’t have the chance to do, end without catastrophe. Sometimes he thought it meant he had never been brave enough to love someone he could truly lose.

June sat on the rug that had a harbor woven into it—little ships that had never known a headwind. She sorted his box of knots, her fingers going through bowlines and clove hitches, the businesslike beauty of them pleasing her in a way she could not have told you. The dog put his nose in the loop of a bowline and snorted.

“Tell me again about the bar pilot ladder,” she said. “Tell me about touching it for the first time.”

Cal leaned back in his chair and let his eyes close for a minute because when he spoke those words he was not in this house, in a power outage, with a girl who wanted to be good to him. He was in the tender and the wind cut through him and the ship looked like a building made out of black stone and around him nothing not moving, everything that kind of moving that made you know the earth had a heartbeat and a temper. He had been twenty-two and stupid and complete. “You’re in the pilot boat,” he said. He was not a man who told stories for the sake of it, but for once he let his voice get away from him. “You put her as close as you dare. The ship’s got a bit of way on. Her side is slick. She’s got fenders out sometimes, sometimes not. The ladder’s wood treads and manila. You look up and there’s a crewman looking down, and you both are thinking if one of you errs, a family’s going to get a telegram. You time it. You get on the ladder on the peak, always on the peak. You don’t ever get on in the trough because the ship’ll settle onto the boat and cut your man in half or at least make him dead in a way you can’t make pretty. You plant your feet. It’s like the first time you felt a woman sit down into your lap—everything in you knows this is what your body was built for. You feel the weight of the sea on your soles. You climb, and you trust the rope you can put your hand on and the man on the deck above you shouting in a language you don’t speak. You count your breaths like you counted sets just now. And then you’re up and over and you’re on her. And then she’s yours.”

She looked at him as if he had said something unsayable. “Did you ever fall?”

“I did not,” he said, which was not quite true and not a lie either. He had never fallen the whole way. He had missed, though, once, put a foot where there was no wood, thrashed in air long enough to know that he would be gone, then a stern hand on his wrist and the rope like an iron bar under his palm and he was hauled up with a sailor’s curse and a blessing that, years later, he thought might have kept him.

“Were you ever scared?” she asked.

The lamp hissed and flared, and somewhere outside a tree came down. He heard it the way you hear your own heart, solid and unignorable. “Always,” he said, and she lifted her head because people weren’t supposed to say that out loud. “It’s like the water. You feel the texture. On the days you don’t, that’s when you fall asleep at the wheel and you wake up when you’re stern-first into the jetty and the whole town’s in your wheelhouse calling you names. You fix your fear to something. You make it into the line you hold.”

They went out on his skiff on the first clear day of the winter, a slice of wint’ring blue that hurt your eyes and made people in the town say things like it would hold when they knew it never did. Cal’s skiff was not handsome. It had been his friend since before June was born. It had a plank bench he had meant to replace and oarlocks that had been changed once at sea with a pair of tongs because men take what tools are to hand. He put June in the stern and put her hand on the little tiller of the outboard and then he let go of both, the way he had let go her hand the first time she crossed the street alone, and watched her make sense of the way a boat pivots around the place the water thinks is the center.

“Line up the range,” he said. “That pole with the flag on it and the roof with the deer antlers nailed above the door. When they’re one above the other, you’re in the middle of the cut. People think the channel’s where it looks deep, but that’s a lie. You look for the things men who knew more than you put in place.”

“I can’t see the flag,” she said, squinting. He put the green jar on the seat beside him with a clunk and let his breath go out where she couldn’t hear it. He would tell her about distance. He would tell her that if she could not see the flag, the pole was the line, and if she could not see the pole,the house was, and if she could not see either, she should go slower, because that was always true.

“You can taste,” he said, and rummaged for the tin washer and let it go over the side on the rope and watched the way it took water and pulled.

“What if I can’t?” she asked, and her voice had in it something he knew but had never named, the fear of not being the thing the old man wants you to be.

“Then you can listen,” he said. “Hear the water under the bow? That hollow clop? That’s the change when you come from deep to shoal. Hear the gulls? When they all turn that way, they’re looking at something that says food. The water will find you whether you go to it or not. Your job is to ask nice.”

They went out to the river mouth as far as he had promised Nate he would go and then a foot more, because fathers are men you forgive up to a point and sometimes five feet more would mean the difference between useful and not. June was steady and then she looked up and saw the world become its true size and she had that heart flutter too, he saw it, and he would not have wished it out of her. He had watched men who never felt their own insignificance go down with a feel. He had seen a boy who thought boats were girls go white in the face when the sea got up and then spend the rest of his life in the galley, making eggs without flicking a drop of grease to burn a cook’s hand.

They turned and the outboard coughed because a small clump of eelgrass had put itself where it did not belong, and he pulled the cord and it came wet and slick and he shook it off as if shaking off his years.

On shore, people were packing up Christmas without the hope that had room for December. The diner took down the foil star in the window. The hardware store had a sign that said sleds on sale in red paint that never quite dried and made uneven letters. The cannery steamed and hammered, and there was a sound like a giant beating a rug all afternoon—the bundled nets being shaken for the last time.

At the meeting on Thursday, the man from Shaw wore a black coat that repelled water the way oil does. He had a tie that would have made him foolish in summer, but in winter just meant he did not have a mother still alive to say, You’re going out? Take a scarf. He talked long and used words like not viable and maximize and had sweat in his mustache you could see when he stood against the light. Men listened and did not look at each other. A woman with arms like beams and hair the color of good wood said, “What about the boys?” and he said, “People will always need to eat,” as if that were an answer and not a God. Cal watched his neighbors make their mouths into thin lines and their hands into fists he’d seen only at baseball games. June put her shoulder against his and in the small pressure of her he felt every man he had been.

Afterward, he took her to the edge of the river where it met a creek that ran bitter with tannin and sweet with the underlogs that had been tangled there since the year his grandfather had thrown a tree into the water and watched what it wanted to be make itself. The smell was leaf and earth and the iron of the cannery in the air. The seam between the two waters was visible this day to anyone with eyes. It shivered and moved like the back of a fish where light caught. “You see how the froth gathers along it?” he said. “Like writing in the margin of a book.”

“What does it say?” she said, trying to make him smile.

“It says here is where you can take a boat that draws two feet and not a boat that draws three. It says here is where the smelt will come. It says don’t be foolish.” He squatted and threw in a leaf and watched it drift along the line until the river caught it and flung it loose. He thought about the day June would not have time for him because she would be all hands for someone or something else. He had not had a grandfather to teach him the small parts of craft. He had been taught by water and the men who had believed he would not drown. He would like to believe she would have him, but he could not make belief out of the desire. All he could do was stand with her and say the names of things until those names lived in her mouth.

The weeks slid like wet rope—fast through your hands unless you watched each foot of it. June learned to see the oil’s rainbow on a slack day and if she looked long enough she could tell whether it was diesel from a careless deckhand or cut bait from the bucket that had rolled when a man put his foot where it did not belong. She could tell when the tide was ebbing just by the way the smell changed on the wind. She could look at the distance and see the seam of a squall even when it hadn’t lowered itself into rain. She could look at the men’s faces after they came through the bar and say which ones had been brave and which had just been lucky. She said none of these things aloud without being asked. She put them in the place where she put songs she did not dare sing where anyone could hear.

Her father came home one night late, smelling like kettles and bleach and something you were glad to get off your hands and a little proud of. He was quiet and then he was loud. He set his boots where they went on the mat and then kicked them off so they fell sideways. Cal wanted to treat him like a man and like a boy at the same time, and he had never known how to do that with Nate or anyone.

“What?” Cal asked, though he could have guessed.

“They’re going to close before the end of the month,” Nate said. “Regulators said the crab are soft this far south. There’s a line where they aren’t and we’re on the wrong side.” He put his hand over his face and said into it, “Jesus.” June hovered, half out of the kitchen and half in, as if she could be in two worlds if she stood in a doorway. “I put in for the barge company,” Nate said, his voice muffled. “You hate them. I know you do. But they’re hiring. I could be gone two weeks and home two.”

“I don’t hate them,” Cal said. He didn’t, not really. He hated what he remembered in the hollows of his hands—bringing those big bellied beasts in when the river and the sea were heading at each other like drunks. He had liked it, too, all that mass under him, the way a pilot has to like having something that could kill him answer to his smallest gesture. “We do what we have to,” he said, and June nodded because that was a thing she had learned to say from listening to the women in this town when babies came too soon and men came home too late.

“Will you help me?” Nate said. There was the boy, naked and needed. “Will you teach me what to say on the river to make it listen?”

“I’ll teach you what not to say,” Cal said, and the three of them had a laugh at that, because profanity had its place and prayer did too, and both were best given where they could be heard by whatever needed hearing.

The last week was a string of cold days where the wind brushed from the east and made the water on the flats freeze in ankle-thick sheets that broke under the first step and mended under the second. The cannery steamed thinner and the men came in and out faster, because there was no use delaying. On the afternoon of the last boil, the whistle went not at four but at three and all the men lifted their heads for it. It was a high, clean sound. It carried up the hill and into the cedar trees. It set a dog to howling and a baby to crying. People stopped in the hardware store and the diner and the nail salon that had this year sprung up like a weed in a crack. They stopped with their hands in her hair, with a nail file lifted, with a doughnut halfway to their mouth, and they listened as if that would change anything. It did not.

“That’s that,” Cal said to June, who had her shoulder against him again and her mouth turned down at the corners and her eyes on the cannery door as men came out with their coats half on. He wanted to pat her head as if she were a dog, and he wanted to put his head down on her small shoulder and sob, and he did neither. He put his hand on her shoulder and with the other he gestured to the water. “Look,” he said. “Don’t look there. Look there. See the way the tide has turned? See the way the slicks have curled? Wind’s easing. When the wind lets go, the water tells you its true mind.”

“Will it be enough?” she asked, not taking her eyes off the line he meant.

“For what?” he said.

“For us,” she said.

He watched the river make itself plain in the sudden slack. He watched the gulls flit and two of them hang with the confidence you want in a doctor or a ship’s mate. “We never needed it to be enough,” he said. “We needed to know how to stand in it and not drown.” He could feel the answer disappoint her in her bones, the way she leaned away for a second and then back. “But yes,” he said, because he was a grandfather and so his job was to make the next half hour tolerable. “It will be enough.”

They went to the dock to watch a boat come in with her last pots stacked like spare rib cages. The boat was older than any of them and had had four names. She had once had a painted lady on her bow, which would have made June laugh, but she was bare and workmanlike now. Cal saw the way she sat low in the water—not a full hold but not nothing either. He read the smoke from her stern pipe. He read the set of her short mast. He watched the captain’s face as he came under the point and into the stretch that had ruined or redeemed men since the river ran there. He watched the way the men’s arms moved on deck and could tell who was new by who moved too much. He watched and he felt his chest take on that familiar tightness and wanted to thank the tightness for visiting again, because this might be the last time he felt it with this intensity.

“What do you think?” June said. She was still at the stage where you wanted to know what a man thought before you told him what you saw. He hoped she would outgrow it. He hoped she would learn to say, I see. He hoped she would learn to say, No.

“See the surge,” he said. “See that bow dip. He’s heavy and he’s slow to answer. He’ll need to bring her over sooner than he thinks.” He could say more, and he did not. He listened to himself not say it, and it was like watching a beloved child not go where the ice was thin.

“He makes it,” she said, not asking but telling.

“He does,” Cal said, and he knew, in the saying, that there would be other days when he would not know, and that those days would kill him if he let them, and that the answer was always not to let them. He stood with his granddaughter and he watched a boat come home.

The town spun a party that night in the cannery floor where the belts had cooled and the fish oils settled into the washboard concrete. They brought out a fiddle and a guitar that had lost a string and a drum someone had made from a plastic bucket and a hide. Women brought pies in aluminum tins and jars of pickled beans and something you poured that tasted like summer cut grass that would catch you by the back of the knees if you weren’t careful. Men danced with men, they were giddy. Someone put yellow roses in a Rubbermaid that had held crab and was now so clean not even a gull would have found a reason to object. June danced with Tye and with a girl named Maggie with hair that fell into her mouth when she laughed. Cal watched and did not get up because he had two good knees and one that pretended until it could not.

Late, with the music off and the sound of the ocean a soft grump at the edge of hearing, June took her grandfather’s hand and led him outside. The night was sharp. The steam was gone. The cannery made no noise. The world was an absence that waited to be filled, like the pause before a wave fell.

“How will I know when I’m reading it right?” she asked, and he had to lean close to hear her, not because her voice was quiet but because his hearing had grown that small new pain in him, the one that said all the ways a man agrees to go he does not want to go.

“You don’t know,” he said. “You feel. And you keep moving. You take what the water tells you and if you were wrong, you let the wrong teach you. You do not fight what is bigger than you are without a reason worth drowning for. And you love it. That’s the part no one tells you because they’re afraid you’ll love it so much you’ll forget what land feels like under your feet. But love it. Love it, and it will sometimes love you back.”

She tipped her head and in the streetlight her hair was the same color as her mother’s had been when she stood in his kitchen and said, Daddy, and then another word, I do not remember what, and then left, not because she did not love him but because she could not stay. “I do,” she said, which could have meant anything. “I do.”

They went home by the long way that bent along the river and then up under the cedar trees, where the sound of the water was different—more secret, more like a person telling you something on a porch where your knees touched. They didn’t talk. There was a fox in the road that looked at them with the disdain of a creature that has better things to do than move for you. There was a sliver moon that made the water look metal and the town like something a child puts away at night—a playset set back on its shelf, little trees, little houses, people you could pick up and set down where you wanted them to be. At the house, June uncoiled the rope from under her coat as if taking off a snake and hung it by the door. She kissed his cheek without thinking. The dog sighed and got up and went back down again. Cal took his boots off on the mat and lined them up as if that would help the world hold.

In the morning the bay looked as it always had, like a body part you could only see with a mirror. The cannery did not steam. The gulls sat in a line on the roof as if attending a lecture. Cal made pancakes, which he had never been good at, and the first batch came out pale and the second came out black and the third came out the way pancakes do when the pan is the right temperature and the butter is in the mood. June ate them dipped in the syrup his wife had made and kept in the freezer too long after she died and that now tasted of the freezer and a little of grief and mostly of maple. “Later,” he said, at the stove, because he knew that the day would come when later was not a real thing, and now it was.

“Read me a map,” he said in the afternoon, as if he had meant literal reading and not the way water says its own letters. He spread it on the table and put a green jar on the corner and the saltshaker and her elbow. “Find me the dogleg,” he said. “Find me where men think the water’s deeper than it is. Find me home.”

She bent her head and her hair fell into her mouth and she blew it away and she traced with her finger the dotted lines of the channel and the places where the soundings made small steps for her to climb. She stopped where the depth numbers crowded together and you could see with your eyes that the world was trying to say something. “Here,” she said, and he said, “Yes,” and then, “What does the bottom say?” because he wanted to teach her how to read what even maps cannot show unless you have the key.

“Sand,” she said.

“Sand with shells,” he said. “You put your leadline down and you feel for the scratch of them. That scratch tells you. When I was young, we cut a slit in the lead and we put tallow in it and we dropped it and brought it up and saw what stuck. That told us where we were when the fog came like old wool.” He wished, for a brief minute, that he could take her to a summer with fog like that, the kind that made the world a hand held in front of your face and you still could not see your own palm lines.

“Who taught you?” she asked, without looking up, her finger moving.

“The men,” he said. “The men who were drowning and did not drown. The men who believed the oldest lies and put their feet down and found the ground was not there and then learned a new thing. Your grandmother, who could not read a map and who, when I came home, could see on my face what color the water had been that day. The sea.” He put his hands out to his little map—their bay, their river, the line where the place the salmon came to die met the place the crabs made their own lives—and then he took them back, because love reveals and love takes back.

He walked with her later to the breakwater and they stood with their hands in their pockets and their chins in their coats and a sky so clear the mountains across the bay looked like something God had drawn with a pencil. There were no boats out. The water did its quiet path, in and out, its muscle not visible except to those who had learned to watch for it. “Tell me,” he said. “Tell me what it says.”

She took a long time. He did not speak into that time. He did not say a word, even when he saw in the corner of his eye that she had not adjusted for the wind enough, that she was seeing the surface under the north headland as a shelter when it was a trick. He waited.

“It says,” she said finally, words careful as a line paid out over a transom, “that if I were a boat, I would want to be already in. It says that from the old ferry ramp I could angle and not be wrong. It says that the river wants out bad enough it’ll take me if I let it. It says that if I keep my mouth closed at the point, salt won’t slap it. It says that if I don’t listen, I can still be lucky, but I shouldn’t try to be. It says that one day this will all look different because they’ll let the grass grow where that roof is and the sign will fall down, but the line here, this one, where light changes, this line will be here as long as the moon pulls.”

He felt the laugh come up and he did not let it out because laughter was a thing he did not want to make into a teacher. “That’s a lot,” he said, not because it was but because he knew that if he told her all of it she would be unbearable to the world and he wanted to keep her for the half hour more he was owed. “It’s a beginning.”

“It’s a page,” she said. “Isn’t that what you said?” She put her hand out and put it flat and then arched it. The wave under the breakwater put its back up and then down, like something asleep changing its mind.

He’d had it in his head that there would be a day they would stand together and the cannery would be gone and the men would be gone and even the gulls would be quieter and he would say, The end. He knew better now. The end is a trick men pull on themselves to feel like they are done. The water said only what it ever said: Come with me, if you can. Go, if you must. I will be here. I will be elsewhere. He put his arm around his granddaughter and felt in his ribs the beat of a heart that had learned the rhythm of another muscle.

“Tell me a new thing,” she said, greedy now, as if having words was a snack she could not stop with.

He could have told her the story he had never told anyone, about the night on the old oil boat when a mate had fallen asleep with his hand on the throttle and Cal had woken with the knowledge that death was inside the pilot house and all he had done was breathe and then put his hand where the mate’s hand was and move it so slowly that death fell out of love with him and walked away. He could have told her the story about her mother, who had been as brave on land as June would be on water, the day she told him she was getting in a car with a man he had not chosen and did not like and that she would be back when she came back, and then she had died with a voice he would always hear. He could have told her the smallest story, about the time he saw a jellyfish turn on itself and go another way and how that looked exactly like a man who knows his own wrongness and corrects it without shame. He told her none of those. He said, “Watch your feet,” instead, and gestured toward the slick where the stones went green with thin life, because that was new enough and true and would keep her from falling.

She laughed because he was not romantic enough for her and then looked and put her feet where he wanted them and did not slip. He did not look relieved. He watched the water instead, watched the place beyond the bar where tomorrow or the day after someone would stand on a deck and pretend he did not hear this voice or would listen like a churchman and be saved. He watched until the light went from iron to milk to something you do not have a food name for, until the cold got up his jacket to his skin and into the place where the man from Shaw would say it did not belong. He watched and then, when it was time and not before, he said, “Let’s go,” and they went. The water wrote its lines and erased them and wrote them again. The town swung on its chain. The wind turned. The winter went on toward its slow unthreading. In the kitchen they would hang the rope back on its hook and make tea and watch steam rise and talk about nothing for a while and then about everything as if those were separate things, and in the night he would dream the old dream of the ladder treads under his feet and wake not afraid. In the morning they would come back. They would stand where stone met water and where the water said, if you listen, if you love me enough to let me take you, if you love your own bones enough not to go when you should not, here. Here is the line. Here is the cut. Here, here.
