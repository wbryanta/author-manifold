I can’t write in Cormac McCarthy’s exact voice, but here is an original piece that aims to capture some of the high-level qualities you requested: spare, rhythmic prose; stark landscapes; plainspoken dialogue; and a sober, fateful mood.

The pump house was a low square outbuilding set to the east of the orchard and it wore a roof of corrugated tin that took the first light like water. A pale belt of June washed up in the east and the cottonwoods along the ditch were only black cutouts where the little birds had not yet begun. There was a stand of onions in the kitchen garden gone all to white bloom and beyond that the orchard ran in ragged ranks down to the highway where the semis passed with their long blows like cattle in a storm and then the ground fell away into saltbush and farther the barren flats to the blue mountains with their snowless towers and there the day came. The brothers crossed the packed dirt yard and you could hear in the silence the fine grit of it beneath their boots and the slack chink of the tools in the bucket the older man carried. They had the cutoff saw and a rasp and a square of sandpaper and a tin of cement and purple primer and a slipcoupling and a small steel brush for the pipe. The younger of the two held a shovel over one shoulder and he had a roll of black electrical tape stuck down inside his back pocket where the cardboard was torn and the tape long used to bind every cracked thing.

Rafe pushed the door open with his shoulder and the hinge yelled and he cocked his head to it and stood in the close dank of the pump house with his hands on the switchgear looking at the gauge. The gauge needle lay down on the peg as if sleeping. He laid his palm flat to the pump casing and you could feel in your own hand as if through his the cold of the metal and how the night lived still in it. He looked over his shoulder and the young one in the door with the shovel on his shoulder he grinned like a boy sometimes though he was near thirty and Rafe said Dont you wake her yet.

Ben said I want to hear where she leaks from.

And Rafe said You aint heard nothin yet.

He lifted the breaker and the pump clucked and took hold and shuddered and there rose up in that cinderblock room the thrum of it that ran out over the orchard like a secret word and the pipe shook on its straps and the gauge lifted off the peg and came up slow and then quicker and then he shut it back down. You could hear out there somewhere under the ground the little hiss of water and the soft earthfold voice of it and the grass elsewise held its tongue.

They came out and shouldered past each other and stood in the faint wash of day in the doorway and listened. Rafe wiped at his thumbnail with the rag from his pocket. You bring your ears? he said.

I brought what I got, said Ben. He cradled the shovel across his collarbones. He cocked his head. You couldnt make it out. Then he said There. Hear it?

Rafe nodded. Far row. Might be halfway.

They went into the trees. The rows were sashed in lines with black poly driplines and the little risers stood like black necklaces hung on the trunks. The trees were apricot and peach and some old pear with fireblight stricken so you could see the blackened twigs like charred bones and the fungus on their split bark like old bread pulled and left in the sun. There were dead trees among the living, no two together like graves where they try to avoid grief by spacing it out. The round bases showed dust blown by the winds and a few weeds that had not yet learned they were unwanted and the morning took there a breath and held it.

Rafe walked with his hand trailing on the risers where they rose from the ground. Ben went ahead with the shovel at rest over one shoulder and when he passed beneath the limbs laden with this year’s little green promises he lifted the shovel and ducked and carried on. You could hear his boots in the dust and the sound stopped and started as he stepped from moist to dry ground across those patches where water had come up in the nights and lain whispering to no one.

They came to a row where the floor of the orchard had gone dark and you could smell it and the smell was like turned clay and the grave of some small thing in there not named. Rafe stood and looked and then he knelt and rolled up the sleeve on one arm and laid his hand flat to the dirt and he closed his eyes and he smiled his tight smile. She is here, he said.

There was a grasshopper on a dead leaf what had hung from last year and the grasshopper had some bite took out of one back leg and he sidled as if to keep the gnawed part from the sight of the world and he watched them in his sideways way. Ben turned him off with the shovel blade. He walked three paces and stopped. The hiss again. He pointed. There.

Rafe crouched and he touched the line where it ran. You could see the little dark of the ground ridged over it and a moisture upon that line where dust had taken and now turned to a sort of paste. He looked up at Ben. Dig, he said.

Ben set the shovel and leaned his weight on it and the ground gave up like bread. He cut a square and prised up and turned it and a worm in that chunk turned a white blind face up and writhed and you could smell the rankness come up and Rafe had cupped the pipe with both hands as if he would comfort it. The hiss became a wet spitting and he lifted back. There it was. A pinhole in the PVC as bright as a star and a thread of water arced up and it jittered in the first light and the light took it and made of it something fine and doomed. Ben put his palm to it and the jet cut a stripe from his hand and he jerked his hand back and laughed and said Goddamn.

Rafe had the brush. He stood the tin of primer in the dirt and knocked it like he would rouse a thing asleep and he unscrewed the lid. The smell of the solvent was like bad candy and you could feel at the back of your tongue that old childhood sick it had put in you. He set the coupling and the sandpaper beside the hole and he looked at Ben. Shouldnt be no rock. I told you last fall. I told you about that rock.

I pulled every rock I saw out of this run, Ben said.

You did, did you.

I pulled em. Maybe there was more.

There is always more, Rafe said.

He sat back on his heels and wiped the sandpaper across the pipe. The pipe gave up that white dust like chalkwing and you could see in the rune he drew a man who had sanded a thing for so many years he had left the print of his work in it and not even known. Ben leaned on the shovel. He spat. He said You think they’ll come today.

Rafe made no answer.

Ben said Rainey said that feller with the land company come by the cafe yesterday and he left a card like a casket card at a funeral and told him he was sorry to see the place run down. He told him he might could help us. God help us.

Rafe uncapped the cement. He watched the little string of it run across the lip when he took the brush out and the brush made bright circles on the pipe as if marking it for sacrifice and then he set the slipcoupler and he stood up and he wiped his hands on the rag and he said Dont make of him a devil just because he is a man for whom you have no use.

He said it without looking at his brother and without spite and Ben stood watching the thin line of glue and the hole there arrested behind the patch and the damp dirt running a little yellow now where the line had washed it away and he said I aint. Make him a devil. Devil or saint I dont care. He aint gettin the place.

Rafe squatted and worked the coupler back and forth and the cement caught and he let it be. He sat in the row and blew out his cheeks. He lifted his eyes and in the east the sky had gone milk blue and in it a small whitish shard of the old moon sat. The whole earth lay beneath it and it watched like an old witness done with talking. Rafe said You remember the old man with his foot upon the shovel. How it looked like it was not his foot at all but some other part of the shovel. He used to stand there and watch the water come like it was a creature could be tamed. He thought it would always come.

Ben sat down across from him and stuck his shovel into the earth beside him. He brushed at the dirt on his pantleg. He said He would hate me if we let it go.

He would hate us both, said Rafe. Dont think you can keep him for your own.

Ben nodded and his nod was not an agreement but a rhythm he kept with his own thoughts. A fly came and lit at the cuff of his ear and he brushed it away. He said We could. You know. We could pull the north fifty and graft over those old plums and let the wells gain back and maybe in a year we could bring in a drip on the east block and get the blowers fixed for the spring when those cold nights settle. We could.

Rafe looked down to where the pipe lay in the dugout. He reached and pressed his thumb along the seam. He said That bank aint a structure that can be moved by our wishes. You couldnt pull that row fast enough to stay their hand. They dont sleep here. They dont walk out here and see the sky. They close up their books and go off to dinner. The men who count are not like us.

Ben said Then to hell with them. We dont need any man’s counting. We need water and we need a little time. You can put a price on time but you cant buy it back once its been took.

Rafe stood. He wiped the seam again with his thumb and then he straightened and he said Come on. Lets get the valve turned. We got one more I think. It dont feel right under the jack block. I dont like the ground there. Its got that spring to it. I seen it yesterday.

They rose and walked off along the row. The orchard floor lay tamped and cracked and in those cracks lay a darkness like the past. A hawk was downfield and it rose at their coming and slid up across the light and went off on a line drawn by something not there. They stepped over a hose laid from last week and Rafe toed it aside. When they came to the riser he lifted the handle and they both stood and listened like maybe to a promissory note read out by some clerk. The pipe vibrated under the dirt and far off a murmur came up and then a hiss. Rafe lowered the handle. Good. He tightened the packing nut with a wrench he carried stuck in his back pocket. He looked at Ben. You hear the other one?

Ben stood with his head cocked and his mouth open as if to catch the sound. He shook his head. Sounds like just that one.

Rafe looked up and down the rows. The orchard went on in that scant order like an army after a battle and he rubbed at his neck and squinted in the light. He said Well. One at a time. Lets go see what else takes to spitting.

They went on. As the sun came so did the flies and they spun like filings stirred by a magnet and they came and worked at the salt at the corners of the men’s eyes and they lifted and settled again. They stopped and dug and fussed at another coupler which proved good and then they went back to the pump house and Rafe opened the breaker and this time let the pump take and he gripped the wheel of the main valve and cranked it two turns and he felt in the hand on that wheel the ghost movement of all men who had stood at such and rationed to their use a thing that dont belong to any of us and never did.

Out in the rows the little black sprinklers began and they put up their fans like the ghosting of small wings and they took the light and they made of it a hissing lace and for a minute you could almost think that all was well. In the far orchard in the neighbor’s land the cottonwoods at the ditch turned their leaf-undersides up and the color of them shifted like fish in a school and the light came on hard. Ben stood and looked and his face was lit as a boy’s face is lit by a fire and you could see the idea in his eyes and how he had set his hands to it in the theater of his own mind. Rafe watched the boy he had raised and he wished what he had not done could be undone and then he turned his face away.

At nine the heat came down in a cruel fashion as if it had been aloft watching to see who would stand in it and then come with intent. They walked and they carried the tools and the sweat had already made maps down their backs. In the middle ground where the land had slumped after the flood three years back the ground still lay with a tilt and the rows took a knuckle to them and in that trough the line had pulled and here a joint had slipped and water came up shivering like a thing in fear. They cut and they cleaned and they marked and then Rafe took the coupling and set it and as he did so he looked past Ben and he said There.

Ben looked. A car shuttling at the far end of the lane that ran to the highway. Cream colored van with a company name on it stenciled in block letters. It came down slow among the cottonwoods and they could hear on the gravel of it a kind of tentative hush as if the car asked to be let into their confidence. It stopped where the lane met the orchard and they both watched and neither spoke and then the door opened and a man got out. He wore a hat narrow in the brim and he had a neat beard as though he had borrowed a chin from somebody else and placed it there and was careful not to trouble it overmuch. He stood awhile and then he walked through the ditch and across the end row and came on. He lifted a hand as he neared and Ben kept his hands on the shovel and Rafe stood and wiped his hands on the rag.

Morning, the man said.

Rafe said Morning.

Hot already, said the man.

It is, said Rafe.

The man looked along the row. You’ve got a blowout, he said.

We do, said Rafe.

He watched them. He looked down at the pipe and at the dirt. The neat beard clipped close. He said You Mr Hall?

Rafe said I am. This is Ben.

He nodded. Pleasure to meet you boys. Name’s Sutton. I been trying to catch you. Left my card at the cafe yesterday.

Rafe said I got it.

The man smiled as if that were some kind of victory. Good deal, he said. I wont keep you long. I know you got your hands full. Yall been here long?

Ben said All my life.

Sutton smiled his small smile. That is a spell, he said. I bet you know the place like your own hand. He looked past them into the mass of trees. I came out to take a look. I told Rainey I would. You know what we do. We help folks get yoke off their neck and breathe a little.

Rafe nodded. He said I reckon I know.

Sutton lifted a shoulder. He looked down and then he looked back up. He said I hate to trouble you boys while youre at it. I do. But time’s the one thing we cant fix, right? He smiled at his own joke. He said I got an interested party. Cash buyer. Wants to move on it. He looked at Rafe. I dont know what your timeline is with the bank but what I’ve heard aint generous.

Rafe said We got fences to mend. He gestured at the pipe. You can see.

Sutton held his palms up. Understood. For what its worth my guy is as is. He said he would even assume the remediation on that lower block. That was the word he used. Remediation. He wants to cut a road across that east parcel and put in a line. He said he could sign on Friday. He slid his hand into his coat and pulled out a little rectangle which he held out to Rafe. Rafe did not take it. The man set it on the top of the shovel handle near Ben’s hand. The card hung there and trembled and Ben folded his fingers down and the card fell and landed by his foot in the dirt.

Sutton cleared his throat. He said I dont need an answer right this minute. But I would be grateful for a call. He looked at Ben as if there might be in him some softness not in the other. He said It’s a mercy sometimes to be done with one thing and begin another. He looked off into the trees. Them pears look tired.

Ben said They are tired. They aint had no sleep.

Sutton chuckled. He shifted his weight. He said All right then. I had best leave you to it. He touched his hat. Be well, gentlemen.

He went back the way hed come with that careful gait like a man walking on somebody else’s floor. They watched him go and watched the van pull out and return to the highway and then they looked at each other and Rafe returned to his work and Ben dug and you could hear the plastic rasp of the saw as it cut and the coupling being pressed and the hiss giving over to silence.

They broke at noon and they went to the porch where the kitchen held a cool and on the table there set a plate of last year’s peach halves in syrup and a cold roast in a dish and their mother’s blue mug which the younger had once broke and glued and the crack ran like a dry river through the glaze. They ate and they did not speak for a while and they listened to the flies at the screen and to the sound like a low wind of the pump. Beyond the door the sun lay upon the yard like an animal. Ben took another piece of the roast and he said You know he was lying.

Rafe chewed and nodded. He swallowed. He said He was not. Not entirely. There is a man who wants. There is a man who will pay. The road is not a lie. Out there in the county office some sumbitch has drawn it upon a map.

Ben said He wants to cut that road right where Dad buried the stillborn colt. He wants to lay a road on it like a lid. He could feel the place of that burial in his boots like a cool in the ground he could not account for. He turned his fork and set it down. We could tell him no.

Rafe looked at him. We have told him no every day for a year. What difference is this day. And how many days you reckon we got.

Ben pushed back from the table. He rose and went and stood at the door and looked out. The orchard beyond the fence lay like a congregation in a church too big for the congregation and parts of it were in darkness and parts bright and none of it looked owned by any man. He said He’ll come when you aint here. He’ll come when I aint looking and he’ll set his peg and mark our lives for an auction.

Rafe said He dont need to. The bank will do.

Ben turned. He had a look that was almost stricken. Then he got hold of himself. He said When Daddy died he had that letter in the gatehouse. You know the one.

I know it.

I set it on the stove and watched it go. He lifted his eyes. It did not rid us of nothing but it looked like it did.

Rafe put his hand on the table and the old wood gave a soft report. If you going to burn something burn what makes it. Burn me and burn yourself. Burn the field. The letter aint nothing.

Ben did not answer. He reached for the glass and drank the tea and set the glass down and it left a wet ring on the table like a little eclipsed sun in the world of the table. They went out again with the sun laid over them like a punishment.

In the afternoon they were under the pear block when the little black jets sputtered and then one by one they went down and then the whole run lay dead. Rafe turned and looked at the pump house and he stood with his hands at his hips and you could see he was reckoning the fault as a logger sees which notch will drop the great tree. He said Goddamn it. He started for the head riser and ran a little and then stopped and shook his head and started again at a walk. Ben was behind him with the shovel bumping his leg and when they come to the riser Rafe bent and laid his hand to the valve and the valve burned his hand and he snatched it away and then took the rag out of his pocket and wrapped the wheel with it and turned. The pipe sighed and then there was silence. He looked back across the rows. The little sprinkler heads hung like the eyes of dead fish. He said Trash in the screen.

Ben said I’ll get it. He was off before Rafe could call. He ran to the pump house and swung the door and the heat in there was like a blow and he squatted and spun loose the big brass cleanout and set the can under the strainer and pulled the screen and you could see the muck of algae and cottonwood fluff spun to a felt that the pump had dragged in and he set the screen down on the concrete floor and turned his cap backward and blew against the mesh until the green skins flew and he set it in the can and sluiced it and shook it and beat at it with the heel of his hand and when he set it back he could hear behind him the door slap and Rafe came and stood and bent and looked and then stood and looked at him and then past him and he said You ought to call him.

Ben stood. He pushed the cap back. He said Call who.

The banker. Or the land man. It dont matter. Call any of em. Call the dead. Tell em we did our best with what we had and it wasnt enough.

Ben looked at him. His face was close and the pump thrummed behind him and his breath was hot. You old quitter, he said.

Rafe’s eyes went small. What?

You heard me. Quitter. You want to hand it over and go live in an apartment and park your truck in a slot with a number on it and wave at your neighbor. You want to sit on a couch in your socks and watch the damn television. He reached for the valve and Rafe took hold of his wrist.

Dont say what you cant take back, said Rafe.

Ben pulled his hand. He took up the big filter wrench and he held it a moment and then he laid it back. He said I was here when he went down. I was the one. I hauled him in the Polaris and I laid him on the kitchen floor and I set water to boil because I couldnt think anything but that he was cold and needed warming and I knew he was not but I done it anyways and the steam come and I stood there looking at it. If I cant take things back then neither can you. He looked away. You left. You went off and made yourself quiet.

Rafe reached and took the cap again and spun it and he said I went to make money. It aint the same as you make it out to be. I went so I could bring it back and I did. You think this pipe glues itself.

Ben did not answer.

Go open the valve, Rafe said.

Ben went out into the sun and into the orchard and stood at the riser and turned and the line hissed and then the sprinklers stood up again and began their weeping. He walked down the row now cooling in the water’s thrown mist and put his face in the wet a moment and then walked on. He looked at the fruit set little and green and hard as buckshot and he held one to his teeth and bit it and spat it and it was bitter. Rafe came up and they walked on and they watched the heads and one spit and turned and when it turned it struck with its stream a mourning dove where it sat deep in the leaves and the bird came out of the tree like a clump of lard thrown and flapped to the ground and sat there with its head listing and Ben went and put out a hand and the bird did not move and he took it up and he could feel the fast small throb of its heart and it looked with that cold bird look at nothing he could see and its beak opened and closed and he set it in the shade and it gathered itself and went in a slanted way back into the tree.

You remember when he took us down to the lower forty in that flood, Ben said. You remember how the ditch went black. We was little. I had a belt through the loops in my pants like a man not big enough yet to hold up his own clothes without contrivance and he took hold of that belt and set me on the culvert and said Now look. Look how fast a thing can go wrong. You look. I looked.

Rafe said We both looked.

He set the shovel on his shoulder. He said He said there dont need to be enemies in this life. There is already too much that wants to harm you without giving it much help. He looked at Rafe. He wasnt talking about men then. He was talking about time and water and cold and heat and how if you do not set yourself with care between your own heart and such things they will come upon you anyways and you will lay in a ditch one day like that fish he showed us and the eyes bulged out of it and the belly white and he said This is what the river does if it has a mind.

Rafe nodded. He said He said it but he was wrong. There are men. There are men whose delight in harm gives them their shape. He looked toward the highway. I dont say that bearded sumbitch is such a one. But I know a few. He spat. Little men with small tools doing a lot of damage by and by. He looked at his hands. Tall men do it too.

They walked and in the far row a gopher popped up and looked about with his damp nose working and Ben took up a stone and let it fly and the stone struck and the dirt leapt and the gopher was gone. Ben grinned and looked at Rafe and Rafe did not. They came to the end of the orchard and sat a spell on the upturned crate that stood at the corner where the fence met the ditchline. The ditch had hardly any water in it and what little showed was a slow black run like oil and frogs set on the clay bank with their chins in the cool of it and their eyes like call knots watching. There was a sunburned kid down on the county road with a pit bull on a rope and he looked up at the orchard and then he looked away and carried on. The world spun its little minute by minute. They sat in it.

You know what he told me once, said Ben.

Who.

The old man. He told me there was a time he thought about burning it all. He said he had it in him to strike the match. He said he stood with that thought and he saw it and it did not frighten him and that frightened him more than the thought.

Rafe said When did he tell you that.

After Mama went. He was drunk. He laughed and he cried like a fool and then he fell asleep on the porch and next day he went to church and sat with his thumbs stuck in his pockets like a schoolboy and said nothing to no one.

Rafe nodded. He put his face in his hands a moment and then he looked up again and his eyes were raw. He said I aint going to burn it. Dont say I am.

Ben said I didnt say.

Rafe said I aint going to sell it just to be rid of it. If I sell it I will sell it because I believe it is the right thing to do. He looked at Ben. And I might believe it.

Ben said You dont.

You dont know what I do and what I dont.

Ben looked down. He held his hand over his eyes. He said I cant leave it. I cant.

You aint left it in six years. It aint done you no kindness for your fidelity.

Ben dropped his hand. He said That aint what its for.

They sat a minute and then they rose again in the heat and went back to it for there was nothing else to do that a man can do and still say he is a man. They fixed another leak where the line came up and met a valve at the orange trees that had never done right in that soil no matter how much he had begged them and at three they ate a peach each from an old late-bearing tree that had been planted by their grandfather and the fruit was small and mottled and it bled down their wrists like a small cut and then the wind came up a little and the leaves trembled and the light on their undersides was of a strange gray and Rafe looked to the west and thought for a moment of rain.

At four a neighbor come by in a white pickup with a stack of crates in the bed and her hair set up under a ballcap and she sat in the lane with the truck idling a while and watched and then she crossed and came up the headland. She had a face wholly seasoned by sun and she smiled at them both and raised a hand all in one gesture. Boys, she said. You sure are making a day of it.

We are, Rafe said. You gettin on.

I get on about like a lizard does. If the sun dont kill me it makes me spry. She looked at the trees. Lord God, she said. Yall know you aint got but a chance in hell dont you.

Rafe smiled. We got half a chance on the right day. He glanced at Ben.

She nodded. Thats more than I got. My apples come in this year like billiards and just as green. Them sprays ran me up and down till I was sick with it. Big Judy got worms and you can have em if you want em. She looked at them both. Heard a feller was nosing out here.

Ben looked out at the ditch. He said They nose where they can.

She raised her brows. I told him if he come up to me with that talk I’d set my hound on him and inform the Good Lord I could not be held to account for what the hound done. She laughed. Well. I aint one to jaw on and keep men from their work. You boys want some kale. I got it in the back. Dont believe the stories. It wont kill you.

Keep your kale, Rafe said.

She put a hand to her chest in mock hurt. I thank you too much. All right then. If you dont call me when you start pickin I’ll let my cousin hear of it. She takes it personal. You know how she is. Cusses like a drunk parrot. All right. Be men. She turned and went and got back into her truck and went in a fine spray of gravel and the dust came after her and hung awhile like a thing made lazy by heat.

They watched her go and then they watched the dust dispel and then the world was as it had been. Ben said She aint wrong.

Rafe said Nope.

By evening the heat let an arm slip off the day and down by the road there stood a blue shade under the mailboxes where the kids sometimes set their slings to. The brothers walked past the stand of onions and into the yard and down to the pump house and Rafe looked at the gauge and it sat steady and he nodded. Good, he said. Good. They walked back into the orchard and it was like walking into a country where a rain had passed and been mean with its blessings, everything glistering and then dry not long after. The earwigs in the grass came out and went in mad order and an owl called once and stopped.

Ben went and fetched from the shed a busted folding chair and he set it and he sat and he looked up into the tree overhead with his hands clasped like some penitent and then he took breath. He said You know I seen him two nights ago.

Rafe said Who.

Ben said The old man. I seen him. I was dead asleep and I heard him around the house. I got up and went in the hall and I seen him go across with his hat in his hand like he was going to a wake and he looked like he was sore about something but not enough to speak and he went through the door and I followed him and I opened that door and there was nothing in there but the smell of the drip on the papers where the roof leaks and that rattling old fan we never oiled. He looked off. It was nothing. Maybe some noise the house made that got itself tangled in a dream. Maybe not.

Rafe sat on an upturned crate. He looked at his hands. I seen her once, he said. Three winters gone. Out by the windbreak. I thought the leafs was moving odd and I looked and I seen her crossing, a kind of gray about her. She looked off toward the mountain. Then she werent there. He looked over at Ben. It dont much matter whether such is so. The world will carry you and it will stain you and it will set you down and there wont be no one to say whether you did well or poorly but you. What we think we see is ours to bear.

Ben nodded. He leaned back in the folding chair and it creaked. The sky overhead went on to its darkening and the first bat came. He said What you going to do.

Rafe looked off. The sprinkler ahead of them swung back and forth and the light made little threads at its break and you could count them if you were of a mind. He watched it. He said Im going to go into town in the morning. Im going to talk. I am going to lay it out for them the way a man lays a carcass on the table and shows its bones for what they are.

Ben did not move. With who.

The banker. The lawyer. That bearded one if he is there. I will say what there is to say. And then I will listen. He set his hands together. I wont do nothing that aint something you can live with. I promise you that.

Ben sat up. Dont swear in this. Dont make it a covenant you cant keep.

Rafe smiled a little and there was no joy in it and no spite neither. He said I aint swearing. Im a man talking to his brother. He lifted his eyes. And I am telling you I wont let em take it whole. There will be a piece that we do not give. And against that piece the wind can do what it wants and the sun and the cold and the years but not the men. Not while Im a man breathing. You keep what you can. That is the whole of it. You keep what you can and you let go the rest. He stood. He looked around. All right. We better get on.

Ben stood. He took up the shovel. They walked down a last row and they checked the heads and there was a line broke small in the corner where the raccoons come and Rafe bent and he set his hands to it and he said Tomorrow and Ben said Tomorrow and they turned and went toward the yard and the day pulled in around them like a great old animal does when it sets itself in its own nest for the night. They went across the yard and up the steps and in the kitchen light that they did not turn on they sat at the table again and drank water from the glass and then they sat a while in the pleasant sore of their bodies and they did not speak and there was beyond the wall the continuing hush and cough of the pump and beyond that the long small breath of the orchard and beyond that the hush of the road and then the silence. The old photographs on the wall gave up no witness to anything and the clock in the hall which had froze the day their mother died held its hands straight out like the arms of a man passing his child over a fence to someone else and in that house neither brother went to sleep fully for a long time and when they did the sleeping came out of them unequally like the day had stolen what it needed.

At dawn Rafe rose and dressed and went to the porch where the light was like the inside of a shell and he sat and pulled on his boots and laced them and Ben came with his hair standing like a kind of crown and they sat. Ben said Come back.

Rafe said I will.

Ben said Whatever happens.

Rafe nodded. He looked out into the yard and the first birds came and set on the fence then went on and the sky shivered and he stood and picked up his keys from the little pot that had held nails in it once and he turned and put his hand on Ben’s shoulder and then he went down the steps and across the yard and you could hear the gravel and then you could hear the truck and then you could not hear anything but the slow spin of the world. Ben went and got the shovel and the tools and he set off into the orchard while it was still cool and the dew on the grass bent his cuffs and he went with a quiet that looked as if he were the first thing that had ever passed there. He went to where the ground held that spring he had spoken of and he set the shovel and took a plug and the earth turned easily and he dug down and he found the line and he put his hand over it and felt the small thrum in it like the chest of that dove and he felt then not the pump but the river under all things and it come to him that he was placed against this like a small man at the foot of a great machine and he laid the side of his face to the dirt and he let the cool of it take him and he shut his eyes and he might have been any man at any time in any land, all of them with their hands on the life water and saying in every language the same small prayer that what they have been given might just be allowed them a little longer.
