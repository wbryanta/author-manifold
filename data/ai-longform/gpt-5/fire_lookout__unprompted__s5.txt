I see the smoke first as a thread the color of old wool, lifting from the wrinkle of earth where the creek tucks its body back under the slope. It comes up slow and sure, a column that makes a shape against the noon-rise air, and then it begins to bell and hook, the top smearing where the high wind takes it. I’ve watched summer thunder run its fingernail across that ridge a hundred times and leave only smell and a static twinge in the hair on my arms, the after-sound like sheet music ripped. I’ve called in far-off puffs that never amounted to more than two men with pulaskis swatting at duff, the smell reaching me only days later. I know smoke. I know the white of wet grass after a lightning strike, the gray from old pile burns rekindled by wind, the dark that only comes when heavy timber is eating itself raw and roofs are going. This smoke is not quite white, not quite gray. It has a bottom weight I can taste even here, up in the glass box the Forest Service stopped caring for but still lets me sleep in because no one has come to tell me no. Some part of me, the part that watched my father press his finger on the stove coils and say see, we learn by touching, already knows. The column is coming out of the drainage where June’s cabin sits like a knuckle that grew where it shouldn’t, the result of a hurt that calcified, the stubborn idea that a person can make the world smaller and so make it safe.

The Osborne Fire Finder waits, patient like an animal a little used, the ring dull with years of hands around it. I pivot the sights—north to the bald ridge, swing to the east past the scree gleam, south down to the cut that is the county road—and then on to the smoke. The crosshairs take it like a bead on a quail. One hundred forty-nine degrees. Distance my gut knows: four, maybe five miles as the crow would, eight by any road with switchbacks that respect a slope. The map under the glass is one of the older quadrangles; the creek is labeled with the name no one uses anymore. It used to be called Cotton Drainage. We called it just the drainage. In winter it’s only a burn in the ground a man could jump. In early snowmelt, it flattens and pretends to be a proper stream for a week before it remembers what it is. The cabin is drawn as a little unfilled square. June added that with a pen, not me. She set her claim to permanence in about as official a way as she could without a deed. She hadn’t asked if I wanted her ink on my map. She never asked; she did.

The radio sits where it always does, black, ornery, like a stick you can’t get your teeth around. It throws a small mechanical shadow on the counter. On mornings when the light is right, dust motes orbit it as if it were generating a field. This summer I’ve been the field. I’ve been the one the few who still patrol up here pull into with a wave and a half-smile and an obligatory joke: I thought they shut this place down, Silvie. I thought they turned you out. Who’s paying you? On whose authority are you still watching? My authority: the standing that comes from having nothing present to call mine and so taking old claims nobody else enforces. My retirement papers came through two summers ago when they cut this tower from the budget, and I took the papers and then took the stairs and hauled up water and my coffee and my battered cot and made a last summer, then another, and now the one I say is final. It is late July. The grass below the rock outcrops has the color of a lion’s pelt. The larch are showily green and sappy and throwing scents like promises.

I pick up the mic, click the talk switch, and my breath comes back at me, flat. “Base, Elk Bench Lookout. Got a start over Cotton Drainage. Copy?” My voice sticks in my own ears in the way it does when you close one of your hands around your throat, soft but firm. I turn the volume all the way up until status lights go to a blue that pretends at sea-deep calm. White noise thick as summer gnats. I step toward the shelf where the solar panel regulator reads out numbers through a green display that always feels like it belongs in a hospital. The battery is taking charge, supposedly. I unplug the Anderson connectors and plug them back. Flip the breaker. Try again. “Base, Elk Bench Lookout.” The call sign sounds childish now that it belongs to a thing nobody cares for. Except me, who has always liked to give names to structures meant to be empty around the edges of things. I look at the line of hills that hold the valley like a hand. The smoke continues; it has found its feed. A new tuft bulges at the side, a lobe the wind pushes into shape. A second head rising off the slope says ladder fuels, doghair, down low where the creek tongues, where we used to play.

Sometimes you sense that a thing has started and no man should try to unstart it. I don’t think that now. The road is eight miles downhill. The nearest active repeater sits high on Hyalite, brittle with winter sleet half the year. Sometimes the radio carries in air like the two are old men talking across a fence. Sometimes, like now, both lie down with their faces in the same damp dark and will not be moved. I still have a wrong taste at the back of my tongue. A tin, a coin. My heart is doing the small consistent hammer that tells me I have already agreed with something I have not yet said out loud.

June moved into that cabin six years ago with her duffel and three dogs and a fanning of paper—the only inheritance she took when father died, the survey we paid a man from down-valley to do back when we still thought the fence line mattered. She left the rest, including me with his debts and the worn-out truck and the feeling that some woods belong to us because we have always looked at them the same way. Estranged is the word they put in my file after she wrote the letter accusing me of stealing, accusing me of telling him things that were not mine to tell, accusing me of calling the sheriff when she tried to bottle her sadness into mason jars on our porch and sell it at the swap meet for money she would use to crawl out of the valley by any highway with an open on-ramp. And then she never left, not far, just over into the drainage. She told me once on a paper card thumbtacked to the corkboard in the diner: I’m not your problem. I’m the problem you don’t have anymore. The last we spoke actual words, she said you love everything that doesn’t ask any thing back from you, and the tower had been behind me then, and she had meant it to be mean, and she was not wrong.

The walkway of the tower groans when I put my weight on it, and the sound comes to me later because the air is so even. Years of sun have grieved every board to silver. Below, the ladder from the landing down to the rock makes a tinker-jangling in the small steady breeze. At my age you think often of the moments you decided not to do something and how those, more than the moments when you acted, were the ones that shaped the path you laid out for yourself to walk on. I pick my pack up from the hook by the door and lay it open on the table. A habit as old as my first season working smoke on a state crew: pull out all the things, put them in a pile, put them back in in the order you will need them. Water filter. Two liters in bottles I lied to myself I would fill yesterday but did not. A strip of jerky still in its paper. Tape. Headlamp whose batteries click when you shake it. Matches in film canister. Belt radio, the one smaller than a prayerbook, the one that sometimes will get through places the base box will not. A ten-dollar bill, folded to the size of a coffin nail. June’s keys. I keep them in the tin where the tea bags go because I never make tea. They have sat there five years. Once, when I had the flu two winters back, I held them cold in my palm and told myself I would go down and put them on her porch in an envelope and that would be the end of it, the ceremonial laying down of a grudge like a heavy thing that bruised your neck to carry. I did not go. The envelope is still on the sill with Welcome Home on the outside in a marker that summer dried out.

Last night I woke twice to dry lightning, horizon to horizon, and each time I lay listening for the follow-on crack of trunk and ground, the groan our mountain makes when something gives, the sound like a man labors to his feet. Then the small staccato of needles. Always after the spear comes sift. The smell of ozone and cat hair lit under a lamp. I keep a weather log even though there is no one to read it. At 3:12 a.m., a bolt struck just below the lip of the ridge across from me. No rain. At 4:15 a.m., a breeze began from the southwest, warm, steady. That wind has made a hallway in the sky and now the smoke is walking it.

I lift the belt radio and press the power. It gives me itself in a blink and then nothing. I key to the county channel. “Dispatch, Elk Bench Lookout portable.” A squall of whispers and then clear: “—ove you out of Lily. Any other resources close?” Men’s voices moving trucks on a fire that has nothing to do with mine. I drop the pack down by my boots and sit. The floor under me is cool through my pants. I try my father’s voice inside me—it is still there, a baritone that keeps its humor in reserve because it knows if it gives it all away at once there will be none for when it is burned or alone at the end of a long day—and he says what he always did when the truck stalled or the mower rope gave up in our hands: Try once more quiet. I turn the base box up. “Dispatch, Elk Bench Lookout.” Nothing. June used to say if there is a hole anywhere in a net, a fish will find it. June was the fish and the hole. And now, the thing I have tried not to say to myself since I saw the column, I say: If I cannot call, I am the call. If she is down there and it is only grass and shrubs, a quick rake, a small black, then I will be mad for months that I made this into more. And if it is more and I sit here watching because the tower made me useless at the time when I needed to move, then I will be mad at myself years until I am dead, and the dead cannot use anger for anything.

I swing the door of the tower shut but not latched—a superstition; a way of telling myself I will be back before night. I shoulder the pack, feel it settle along my scapulae like a careful animal. The first treads on the steel ladder down are always the body reminding itself that it has a weight and that the weight is separate from the mind’s airy plans. A long-ago hand has carved initials in the grain of the landing post. Shallow letters because early season wood is hard. I know who carved them. The boy who took his first assignment at this tower with me the summer after June took her cabin. He was fifteen and mouth-smart and died in a canal the next spring when the truck he drove into town to show girls skidded off the bridge coming around Mess Hall Bend. I ran into his mother in the grocery two days after the funeral and when I offered help she curled her hand at her chest like a squirrel. Keep your help, she said, and then later she hugged me so hard spots stood up before my eyes. People thought I would marry his father. People think many things about me because when you live on a ridge in a roomful of windows you are as much town as road.

The sun swings higher and the temperature on my wrist says eighty-one. The larks are braiding their morning abandon into the grass. You cannot will a bird that small to land on your finger; if it comes, it comes. I step off the last rung onto rock and feel the rock’s old heat, a holdover from yesterday. The trail is a rumor of crushed needles someone made ninety years ago with feet and a mule team. We said this year our final summer, but already I hear in me a lie. I am bad at leaving, worse at arriving. What I have been good at is enduring. Held longer than anyone else when the frame shakes. Compliment or a pity, I don’t know. When June and I were small and mother was not yet gone but practiced at leaving by doing it while still in the house—going with her face, going with the part of her that noticed—we would play at being Watchers. We would climb the cottonwood by the irrigation ditch and holler down like two buzzards and watch cattle. This hill holds a habit. The act of looking is a muscle that will keep pulling even when you are trying to use your hands for something else.

On the switchback where the trail takes you off the raw ridge and into the first shade, I stop and turn. The tower looks smaller already, like a child’s drawing of a tower, four lines and a box on top. I give it the same two-fingered salute the men give me when they pass on the county road. The smoke now has a base the size of a hay barn. The sun shapes it silver on one side, ash on the other. I can see how it is moving: the one head licking up the slope on what we would call the five percent, the other—the one that worries me more, the one a tired man will not notice until it takes his ankle hair—running along the jagged line of the creek bottoms, hitting willow and alder then backing into grasses then taking to the burned trunks that last winter’s cold popped like old thigh bones. We put in a project there to clear the downed and pile them into careful clean rows, but no one came to burn them once the snow was right because the paperwork took too long. June used to mimic my voice when I said fuel load, then add in an accent: we keep it in storage for just such a day as this.

She will have heard no thunder last night where she sleeps, drunk as she gets on the smallness of her life and how she controls it. Or she did hear, and she turned her pillow and slept to the other side of it, a farm trick I learned from a girl on the helitack crew one summer: fresh dreams on the flip. When we were teenagers, we ate a box of crayons for a dare because the boy we both had decided on said it would be funny, and funny is a drug you learn quick if it’s the only way you can take a day. She took the green ones and I took the blues and for three days our leavings were a party. That is the last time the two of us did something together that was the same stupid thing. Later, when mother left and then father started to flicker, June’s stupid and mine were not the same, and the boy didn’t come around anymore. I took a job on the line the summer after I graduated. I thought I would move on after I saved enough for a cheap house. June stayed and made trouble because there was oiled rope in her fingers and she knew no other tricks for rope than to swing on it.

I think of that canned summer like it held an affliction in it we each took from opposite ends. That is a thing about sisters: the same early story can sprout two plants that don’t even know each other by autumn.

Below the shade the trail crosses a smell that reminds me of cat tongues and sulfur. A chipmunk sits in the catbrier and makes his machine-gun cry at me as if I have wronged him by passing. Pine cones break underfoot with small clicks. I measure steps in twelves because I learned to do it that way one season from an old jumper who said you should always know, at any moment you choose to test yourself, how far it is to something that will fix a number in your head. Twelve, twelve, twelve. The secret not so secret. In the hours after dark last night I took my pillowcase and turned it to the cool. In those hours I also ran my fingers along the soft place along my jaw the doctor said was nothing but hormone and time. Menopause is a fire of another kind, and it takes what it likes and leaves a black behind that is probably good for the soil. I do not bring that fact to other women in town because some truths are best when they come to you through the thing itself and not a sign at the grocery. But here I can say it out loud to the grass: I feel the change and I do not hate it.

I come to the first creek crossing. In high snowmelt it is a hop. In August it is sometimes not there. Today it is a black seam of water no wider than my boot is long, and I step over it. Smoke smell has found its way up into this draw; it has the sweetness of things you save and cannot keep from rotting. I take a drink because I know enough to drink before I feel thirsty. The pack is a cut across my hips now, not hard, a friendly press. My father used to put his hand on our foreheads to measure fever. The mountain puts a hand now on mine and says nothing about fevers because it knows I know. I move to a walk I can keep the whole way. Elevation is a thing you borrow when going downhill. You give it back uphill your body’s coins, one by one, and if you have not squandered you get where you intended.

The trail here is not county, not even state. It is a wearing down in the duff where deer choose to cross and old men with their last load and their last breath go slow. A scrap of flagging flaps in a lodgepole like an afterthought. Someone was going to come back and finish a thing here. They did not. I sometimes think that is the story of my county, my state, my house: someone tied a loose end to a living branch and said this stands for now. They did not come back. The smoke edges into clean air. The creek to my left gossips because it is not big enough to speak proper. I will need to leave the road halfway and cut a straight line to the drainage to save time. There is a bald knob that acts like a watchman between my ridge and hers, and from there if I slither below the rock face and hold to the stringers of old deadfall like a hand on a rail I can drop into the mouth of the drainage where the willows start. That is how we used to walk to each other when we were still in the orbit of home and could make a call with our eyes from across a field and get an answer we recognized.

At the second crossing of the creek, the smell from the column stops being a smell and starts being air. That is the shift you track. It is no longer a strange thread woven in but the thing the whole piece is made of. I cough, and my old lungs, which have tasted so much of this season’s version of hell they would make a connoisseur of such things proud, complain. A jay throws itself from a spruce and flutters flat and then up like it is remembering how to fall and be caught. I think of June’s hair, always in a high knot, the ends sticking out like a brush, the time she cut it off clean at our kitchen sink with scissors that weren’t meant for hair and put the braid in a drawer. When she left for the cabin, she took that braid the way a woman weds herself to something heavy and rough. If someone had asked, I would have said June would be the one to leave without a forwarding address and then fill every wall of the new place with addresses to which no appropriate reply could be sent.

On the last bend before the grade steepens, there is a boulder that we always said looked like a sleeping cow. When the light is right, there is an eye. I lay my hand on it now, because that has been our way with certain stones. Press and then release. Promise and then take back the promise because the rock takes what it needs and then keeps the copy. I am talking to rock. This is the stage of fear before you admit it is fear. I eat a piece of jerky wider than my tongue and find a strand of meat that will not give, and I spit it and keep moving. A sound behind me like the lightest foot suggests a deer, but when I half-turn it is nothing, only the weight of the pack shuffling on my back, a small betrayal of age: to be fooled by your own rig.

The bald knob is sharper than I remembered. I crawl the last yard because the gravel is slope-loose and my boots cannot tell the difference between a lean and a fall. The wind runs here like it had training to do so. The sound gets under my sunglasses and lifts the edges of my eyes. I look down. The drainage opens its mouth. The column is now full of its own certainty. The color has gone to the dark I call stew. Somewhere, something more than grass is in it. There is a shine too bright for leaf—a tin or a barrel or siding. The top of June’s roof is a line you can only see from here at this angle. I find it in the sight chest high with the glass. It sits, small, still unbitten. A stand of young firs her ex-boyfriend cut from the backshore of the reservoir three seasons ago ring her clearing. They look decorative. It must give her joy to have taken the pretty trees, the ones the ranger asked her not to cut, and arrange them in a manner that made a clean picture from above. The wind now is taking the column and making a question mark at the top. The bottom of the question burns at the lip of the drainage half a mile up from the cabin. On her side. A hundred yards, maybe two, is all that separates her porch from the first people the fire will come to call on.

You cannot outrun in a straight line something that eats in a widening front. You can only go to its side and wait where its appetite will come. I stand and draw in everything I know from years of watching men a third my age go to a place like that and make moves I thought were foolish then understood later were only a different kind of smart. If the main run stays with the low willows, they will put a ring on it and shepherd it to the black from three years ago to the south. If it takes the bench and the wind chases it up, it will jump the two-track above the cabin, and then we will have nothing on it until it hits the cut where the logging road never did finish. That is the place where men still say a boy broke his back falling off the stump. I have found my mouth talking without voice, counting. One hundred forty-nine degrees. Four point eight miles. I look one more time for the flash that would tell me she is already outside, moving. Not because I think to wave. Because an animal likes to see another move when the smell is like this. It is proof a path still exists.

I start sideways instead of down. The straight drop into the willows would be bold, and today I have no bold. I have old. Old has sense that can get you killed because it makes you look longer at the thing and then longer still, and a fire can cross the spot you were looking at while you are counting what you know about it. The granite face to my left wears lichen the color of a bruise. I put my palm to it and it is cool. Down-climb with your eyes three moves ahead, father said. You want to find that third hold without thinking. He taught us on the barn loft ladder. June wanted to get to the ground fast and would jump, and he would let her until she sprained her ankle and then he said: pain is a teacher I wish you didn’t have to have. I see her coming down the beam with her arms out like a tightrope girl. I see her hair stuck to her face where the sweat ran. I see her in the creek behind the cabin splaying her fingers until the minnows teased them. Who am I really seeing? The sisters we were, the one I think I was to her, the one she maybe never thought of me as. Nostalgia is a fire too. It burns as good things as bad and leaves you with lots of stand-up sticks where you can snag a shirt walking through your place later, when you thought to remember only soft.

The larch needles lay in the small squares of my sleeves and I smell the last sap of them when I drag my arms on the way down. A warbler tells me I am in his space and then forgets because the smoke is telling his body different, a bigger thing. I want to be thirty and the land around me fixed and the tower still held in a budget someone fought for. I also want to be six and the fence not so mean a thing. All this wanting makes not even a stain anyone else could see on the rock. I keep lowering. Then I come to the ledge I remember and it is narrower. The rock has fallen away. I look at it and have the idea that the world is a throat that has swallowed hardest on the things I kept. I am not a poet. I tell myself: Step to the left, then right. I do. I make it. It is a long time until I stand on duff. Then in two steps I sink to my boot tops in it. A burn two winters ago took all the low, and the snow sat on that dead and made it sweet and then spring took it and left this rot. Good for mushrooms. Good for ankles to twist in as if a hand reached from the ground and squeezed for sport.

I am in the mouth. The willows stand. Their leaves hang tired. Somewhere past them a lick of wind coughs. The sound a sheet makes when you flap it for someone to pull it across a bed. I step slower because this is where snakes lie. The creek in the willows is a ribbon laid on a breathing thing. I push a stem aside and come to it and crouch and drop water across my neck. The feeling is immediate. My grandmother used to pour cold coffee in my hair when I was small and could not settle to school. It would make me lift my shoulders and bring me back to myself. June hated it. She would run and the coffee would leave her and make a stain and grandmother would say there, don’t you feel better, and she would say no I feel like a piece of bread someone spit on. I stand in the willows breathing through my mouth because the nose has started to think each breath is a punishment.

The first ash comes then. It does not fall so much as correct where it has been held up. It puts a point on my pack, my shoulders, the back of my hand. As if a thousand small old women have looked at me and dusted me for portents. I am too far from the base of the column for embers big enough to make a new experiment where I stand, but I will not be too far if I keep to this pace. This is the part where you choose: go faster and make a mistake that will end up as an apology with your own name on it, or go as you are and weather the weather. I slide through the last of the willows and into the first of the open bench. The sand here is powder-fine, the color of bread. Bootprints, not fresh, cross and rake it. One set is June’s. She always drags the one foot from the ankle down as if the tendon is a man she cannot forgive. The other set is probably that of her ex when he came to take the dog. He cried on my step one afternoon in March two years ago and asked me to tell her to call him. He said he could not find the way to the cabin on his own because he had only ever gone with her in the dark. I made him tea I did not drink and then lied and said I had no way to speak to her either. He sat and shook his foot and the dog’s hair stuck to my rug like a second small rug and when he left he took none of the hair with him.

The cabin comes into view at last as a horizontal line through trees. It always looks like a shadow someone forgot to put away. It sits on the little shelf the county says is far enough above the creek to respect setbacks. When we were girls, we called those shelf bits “balconies” because we thought we were city girls in a house we’d never see. You make vocabulary to hold feelings. You also make feelings to suit the vocabulary you inherited. Smoke runs in under my collar and sits with my sweat and the two have their talk and it is about me. The cabin holds itself still. The roof is good, newly tarred last autumn, thanks to a man from Canada who came for two weeks and changed a few things and left when his mother wrote him a note you could feel right through the envelope. I stop because the small clearing is now a theater. The edges are moving. The light is a molasses brown. Flies come to me and then leave, drunk on something better. Through the window I can see June’s table. Two mugs. A ketchup bottle a raccoon chewed. A postcard of a lake she taped to a wall pilfered from a gas station. I step into the clearing and the clearing breathes like a chest lifting. The breath is hot.

“June?” I say. Saying it makes me feel I have moved a weight. I say it once more because names are one of the few free things we have. “June?” My voice slides under the eaves and joins the spiders. Nothing. My feet make a sound on the porch she re-boarded with flattened scrap pallets from behind the tire store. The nails don’t match. Each board has its history and refuses to forget it. I take her keys out though I do not need to. The door is not locked. The lock has not known a turn that means what a lock is for once since she moved in because she does not believe anyone wants what is hers. Or because she believes everywhere is her living room now that nobody tells her to mind her feet. I push the door. The smell that hits is a man’s shirt left in a sunroom one summer and a mouse that died because it made the wrong choice about a hole.

She is not there. The eyes of the same raccoon are in the ceiling where it runs. There is a dent in the couch shaped like someone sleeping for a season in only one position. The kettle shines with an industry belied by the rest of the room. The note pinned to the calendar says: Back before supper. No date. A woman with no sense of where she stands would write a word like supper as if time cares to be that definite. A line of ants cross the counter into a bag of sugar. The ant line is the only one definitive thing here. Otherwise, this room gives away a person like a witness coached. I do not call again because my voice will tell me only my own fear. I go to the bedroom. Her unmade bed is a shore someone left at high tide. In the corner is a stack of blank legal paper as tall as my hand is wide. The paper says she had a plan. She always had a plan. The plans she had as a girl were all about leaving. The plans she has as a woman are all about staying.

I drop my pack on the floor by the bed and stand a minute because I have done the arriving and now have to choose a doing. The fire’s sound is thin at first, then full. A sound that cannot be mistaken for any other. It is the word we both learned before any letters: go. I think: She went up-canyon to look. I think: She went to do what I am doing now, only from where she is. It is a thing a person does when they live in a place the world tells them is behind events. You try to get your eyes on the event. Or she went down to the road to find her truck because she does have a truck, even if it is held together by paint that has hardened like cake icing. She is not stupid. She is stubborn. The two often put their hats on each other in a woman and then men tell her to smile because they think that will take the hats off. She might be at the cistern, the one we built together before the particular rupture that is now the only thing anyone who knows us mentions if pressed. We set it where the spring expresses regardless of season. The pipe gives even on the hottest afternoon. That is the hope here: a line of green that will slow the thing down.

I grab the shovel from by the door, and the shovel says what all shovels say in a fire: I am a plan and a prayer. June painted flowers on the metal with a paint that has already begun to flake. A daisy with its head bowed. I step back out. The clearing is a stage lit by one light on a low chain. I can see now where the fire will come over. It has run faster than my counting. Two deer bound across from the far side, their black tails high. They do not tip their heads in my direction because I matter less now than a shadow if it were under their feet. I hustle to the cistern. The lid lifts. The water is a taste like iron, a coin again. I fill a bucket. I throw it on a strip of duff by the porch and it drinks it and asks for more. The wind shifts a foot and curls a small white at my feet, and I hit it with the flat of the shovel and it dies. Not that way. This is not that kind of event. This requires a man with a pack full of hoses and no attachments, only the cut-off and the length, swaying across the slope and standing with a hip cocked, and another man calling: pressure. This requires a woman with a radio calling the air because the wind will go up-canyon at three and down at eight and your window is when the shadow crosses at five and no amount of calling will slow the fact the pine beetles left every hillside a page of book half-burned, ready to take color. I am wearing boots six seasons past their promise and pants patched at both knees. My radio is a man about to die shaking his head no when you ask him if he needs anything.

“June!” It comes out of me again because I cannot not. Then quieter because the word is mine to use how. “June.”

I take the straight trail up-canyon to the bend where the old mill used to make its smell. The mill burned the year before mother left. I could smell it from school and I thought first our house was the thing. I did not cry because I had made a deal with that feeling: it would be present in me as a weight I could drag and not as a sound. I do not have to do that now. I can say the bad thing and still be a woman with boots and a shovel and a burned tongue once because I tasted maple sap too close to the boil. The sound increases to a freight you cannot see. I turn the bend. The cut June made into the slope with her shovel when she put her dogs in the ground shows as a lint. I think of her older dog’s ears, the way they made the room quieter by making their own new sound. I pass them. The fire shows. It is a sheet tearing in a wind of its own making. The crouched fir holding the corner of the bend is already a candle. It sends sparks that might as well be words. Come on. Come on.

Up ahead, a figure. My breath stops. The figure is small, compact, a hat on. For a second I think it is her. Then I see the hips are wrong. The person is coming down in the way men run a thing they can still make part of their story. The person sees me and lifts a hand. The person is a boy with a face I do not know. Old enough to have hair on his chin he has not yet learned to trim without cutting. He carries a drip torch like a lunch pail. Behind him the slope is black. He is tying it in, saving the cabin side with a wet line of his own making. The Bureau never comes this far on a day this thin. This is a neighbor kid who likes to light ditches for the fun of it when the snow comes off. Some volunteer someone finally called. He reaches me. His teeth are very white when he smiles. He speaks my name like he knew he would find me here. “Ms. Sylvie. They said you’d show.”

“I tried the radio,” I say like it were a proof of something, honesty sworn.

“They got Lily Country of all things. You were under. We’re cutting it off up there.” He points to the line he is drawing. The torch throws out a smell like bad perfume. The fire takes it as a friend and as an enemy.

“June?” I say, because the word is still what holds me.

He shakes his head, no drama in it, just the movement of a man acknowledging his ignorance. “Didn’t see her. Check the spring?” He gestures with his chin to the line of green, and the words drop out of him and hit my boots and break.

“She leaves notes,” I say. “Convinced time will join her where she anchors it.”

He lights another small line. It takes. He is as calm as sleep. He is the first brave thing I have seen today. He is also too young to have the knowledge he says in the way he is kneeling where the heat is seeking. The wind catches a new thing and puts it in my hair. Hair catches heat a peculiar way. I am aware of the backs of my ears.

“Go look,” he says. “I’ll close this. You bring her to the cut if you find her. Or to me, if she can’t make that.” He keeps his eyes down to the thing in his hand because that is how to stay alive: ask the other to be the thing you do not have time to do for them and then make your world smaller until it is you and a line of fire and your feet. “There’s a guy up higher.” He nods. “Name of Duran. He’s got a radio that carries.”

The name is a river I know. The way men come in families. The way a name inherits posture. “I’ll go look,” I say. The boy’s name is probably Jonah or Cal. He will have a girlfriend who wants him to wear a different hat and a mother who stands behind a church folding chair putting out cookies for funerals with a silent efficiency that makes men think of sex without being able to make the line from A to B straight in their head. He will go home tonight if he goes, and a woman in his mother’s kitchen will hum. Or he will not, and the woman will hang a dry towel over the sink and touch it and think nothing. I turn and run, if you can call this what I do now, my boots scuffed at the toes like a nun’s.

The spring is a patch of green as brave as anything I have ever seen. It has stood through three years of the driest air anyone remembers. It hums with insect talk. The bottle hung under the lip of it is half full. A foot-deep trench in the grass shows where someone lay yesterday, maybe the day before. You can tell a head when the grass makes a shape. I step into the mud. My boot takes on the wet and thanks it. “June?” I say. It comes out of me like I have not yet said it once. A shape to the side of the main green is a coat, balled. I pull it open. Her old jacket, denim lined with the fur that remembers nothing. She wrote JUNE inside in a way befitting a girl on a bus. For a second I am swept with such a loneliness for the hands of my mother in my hair that I have to put my hand on my own head. In the coat pocket there is a coin, a lighter, a nail. No note. A path up to the ridge above the spring shows like a suggestion someone erased until the pencil left only shine. I take it.

From the ridge I can see back to the tower in a slit between two juttings. It stands where I left it, of course, a thing that looks kept whether or not. The column is fuller now. This close the sound has a new wall in it. If I say that wall is grief I will be the kind of woman who talks to bees as a matter of course. My shoes scuff on stone. A group of turkeys snake in the understory, their bodies a collective that has made one mind, their heads computing the change and turning in the same tick. If she came here she would have stood where I stand. There is the spot flat enough to make a foot be easy. I stand in that spot. In the dirt is a thing that could be a print. The little toes. I kneel and run my fingers in it and lift ash. She was here. Or I am lying to myself. Or all of us are lying to all of us all the time to make the air we have feel bigger.

The fire leaps a new line behind me and I turn into it the way you turn into news of your own body gone unright. I keep moving because stopping does nothing any longer but give the ashes a place to land. I go down toward the cut where the boy said. I think of the tower with its windows open to everything and the day I decided I would stay in it a while longer because I could not bear to be in a kitchen when a sun like this one was out. I think of a woman whose name I will not say even in my sleep who asked me once what would I do after the towers closed. I said something like I’d take up beekeeping and go to see the ocean. That was a lie and not a lie. Bees and ocean are ways of saying: I will put my body where the world shakes and hums and be part of it and not separate and no man will stop me with his need. June looked at me over her mug when I said that and rolled her eyes so hard I thought they might click in their cups. She said: You’re a watchtower you carry, Sylvie. You just have to find the hill.

I reach the cut. The boy is there, darker with soot now, his line of fire showing him where to step. Above him a man stands with a radio on his shoulder, the antenna a ridiculous flag. He looks like a Duran because he keeps his mouth shut and his chin up. He looks at me and then does something I expect and do not: he tips his head in the smallest acknowledgment and says my name and not miss or ma’am but the thing a person calls you when they remember a time nobody else panicked and you counted degrees and said hold and they did because you asked them to. He says, “Silvie. We’re taking it around.” He says it like we belong to the same sentence. “You see her?”

“I saw her not,” I say, and the not is the truth and also not because I have seen her in every stone and grass bit between here and the tower now.

“We sent a boy down to the old mill,” he says. “Her truck’s there.” He waits as if that were the line that closes a story. “We’ll pull this to the black and then it will run itself out. We got lucky with that last burn.” He points to the scar that the world left three summers ago, where the fire could come to a place and find only its own footstep and go stupid with the joy of it. “You can walk with me, if you like,” he says in the way a man says to a woman who belongs here as he does: I have a job and you can have it too because you always could.

I look up-canyon one more time because that is what I do. In the slice between the two rock skirts, a figure moves. It could be any moving thing. It could be an elk trunk. It could be a logging ghost. It could be June. It stops. It raises something. It might be an arm. It might be a stick. The sun cuts as though pulled by a chain from a cloud and lays itself there. The figure puts its hand out, then down. I do not wave. I cannot be sure. This is what life will do to you: it will put the maybe in a place where you have already decided. The voice in me that is my father says: Do the thing in front of you and the next thing will rise. The voice in me that is my mother says nothing because she is always on the way out the door. The voice in me that is June says: Don’t tell me what to do. The voice is strong and alive in me and not a memory.

“Okay,” I say. I put my hand on the shovel because it is a thing it understands. “Okay.” We step to the fire together. The heat pitches itself and my hair lifts in its wind. Above us the tower stands, looking. The road is eight miles. The radio is a mouthful of water you cannot swallow. The smoke carries the taste of metal and winter and the thick sweet of something you forgot. I take a breath with my nose open because my grandmother would have told me to taste the day, even this, and I think of every morning I have opened my eyes in a room of glass. I think of all the ways you can carry a watchtower inside you and never have to ask anyone permission to say which way the wind is from. I set my boots on the line. I say inside: June. I say it as the answer and as the call. Duran says: Hold. Someone answers on the radio: Holding. The boy laughs once, something small but real. I lift the shovel and make a cut that in another hour will be only another brown on a hillside already full of them, but for now will mean that something, someone, crossed where we decided. I step again, the ash shifting under like fine animal, and tell myself I will stay until the smoke is not a question mark anymore but a period and the day, or the me in it, will have come to an end it knows what to do with.
