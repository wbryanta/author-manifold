By the time the frost had given up its hold on the windshield he had the buckles snugged and the thermos swaddled in the woolen sleeve his wife had knit in a winter he still thinks of as recent, though he has outlived it by a decade. He clicks the garage door shut by hand, feeling the tug of the spring like an old tendon. In the trunk sits the long black case speckled with the road dust of three counties, lined with green felt gone bald at the seams: the hammer, the mutes, the fork, the slips of paper with penciled temperaments written in a hand that has become more deliberate, his fingertips laminated with callus that no work softens anymore. He sets the coffee on the passenger seat. The neighbor’s birches keep their last yellow and are made of light.

He pulls out onto County Nine and the car warms around his knees. The light is a poor joke this time of year, cheerful but thin, not built to forgive. In the rearview there is his own mouth, steady, and the curve of the hill beyond his barn where the plow scar from that big rain has grown into a new drainage. He takes the corner without thinking and meets the pothole where he has met it for twenty-seven years. The jolt goes up his spine. In the glove box is a no-bid notice on the old John Deere; he told himself he might get lucky but he knows better. His route today is a cloverleaf that will bring him home if he wants it, but he has drawn an arc instead: west where the land goes wheat-brown and wide, a turn north past the silos and the school with its boarded windows, a shot east along the ridge with the burned maples, and down into the hollow where the pines gather around St. Aloysius in their own litany of shade. He thinks he has told everyone it will be his last round, but then he has a way of saying things in his head and leaving his face blank.

The dog at the Miller place is dead; he still checks for her and feels the bright, ridiculous ache where habit keeps a thing alive. There is a new white mailbox on the Saltzman lane, with SALTZMAN spelled neat and blue: a granddaughter, probably, school-teacher neat. Crows lift from a stubbled cornfield and light on the caught electricity of the poles. The farm store’s plywood Jesus leans back from the wind, sainted by dust.

His ears are the honest tool he has left. The hiss that sings in them some nights is the pitch of his own tired blood and can be tuned around. He has trained himself to listen with the bones of his face, the hollows of his chest, the bruise in his thumb from when his father taught him to drive and the tractor bucked and the wheel and his stubbornness argued. He sets his hearing aids low when he tunes; he trusts the world direct as it is when he matters to it, string to pin, note to ear.

At Marlene Hobson’s he knocks, though he is expected the way certain people are expected in a life—feed truck, meter man, the man with a wrench for the hidden thing nobody else wants to touch. The screen door opens as if it has been waiting. She is smaller than the last time he saw her. There is a particular smallness that is not thinness but space turned back upon itself, as if the quilt has been laid over a low fence and the pattern gone: grief folded so long it keeps its crease. Her grandson’s bicycle leans against the porch and angles its handlebar toward him as if to shake.

“Arthur,” she says.

“Marlene,” he says. “You got the coffee on?”

“You know it.”

She hands him the key she needn’t have. The smell in the house is beans and dog and the cold smell of a bedroom nobody has slept in for a year. He leaves his boots at the mat and crosses to the piano in his socks. It is a console, a Baldwin that is somehow both narrow and heavy-looking, like a child meant to wear a father’s coat to church. On its top are five ceramic figs in brown cloaks looking at nothing and a photograph of a boy with teeth too big for his mouth and cheekbones like his mother’s. He lifts the lid and sets it against the hinge and breathes through the first wave of dust. He frees the fallboard with the care of a man disarming an old thing that has been waiting to be misused. The keytops are yellowed to tobacco and two of the black keys are chipped, but they go down with a good feel and come up willing.

He lays the case open and feels, even now, a correctedness in the sight of the tools: the fork that found him once and has found him since, unfolded from its felt sleeve; the slender wedges of rubber and the longer strip of red felt like somebody’s necktie as it drifted, forgotten, onto a piano that refused to wear it; the lever with its walnut handle smooth with his sweat and, he imagines, the sweat of the man who made it. He taps the fork and holds it to the plate near the front duplex on the A above middle C. He listens for what he expects to hear and receives it with relief—no warped trick of this room, this morning.

“Hot?” Marlene says from behind him.

“Same as always.” He takes the cup and sets it to his left by the case, out of reach. He does not drink when he tunes. He has learned to keep thirst as company without satisfying it. The sound of the first rubber mute sliding its wedge between the strings is private, a finger’s secret conversation, the felt strip coiling softly on the plate. He begins where he always begins, in the middle of the instrument where the octaves are cleanest in the wood and the steel is most ready to be told the truth. He will set his temperament there, the compromise that lets a piano live in the world with humans and their scales in twelve pieces. It is a compromise he still likes, honest and consistent in its dishonesty. If he had his way he would not have had others’ braveries for that kind of bending, but he has tuned to equal temperament his whole working life and learned to hear what a fifth that is duller than born wants to offer in return.

He isolates one string of A4, quieting its neighbors. He leans the lever on the pin, finding the feel of that pin in the block—how it bites, whether the wood has dried enough to turn it to powder, the slop and grittiness an old block confesses if you know its language. This one holds clean. He raises or lowers—today lowers, the whole instrument sunk by months of damp—until the beats slow and slide under his ribs and vanish. He holds his breath for the last quarter-turn and stops not at silence, but at the right argument. He feels the string move under the hammer’s bite with a give like the syllable between teeth when you say your wife's name in your sleep.

The dog comes to the threshold and rearranges himself with a snore. Marlene rattles in the sink, then stops, as if a memory has scolded her for making a noise. He leaves the A and finds the E, then the B, tempering fifths into the imperfect perfection that will carry across the rest of the board. He counts the beats with nerves along the sides of his neck; he carries them into the opposite interval and returns to be sure the family of relations agrees with him. He is half in the wood of the world and half under it. The cup of coffee cools by his left hand. He is wedded to this species of attention and always has been, the way some men are to sky or fire.

When he has the temperament set he works outward. He discovers the g above middle c is woolly and off enough he has to bring her out of the weeds cautious, the unisons moaning at him in a slow beat he thinks of as the sway of a boat. He has never been to the ocean. When his boy was small he asked to see it and Arthur told him simply that the ocean would not forgive them for wanting what it couldn't have them keep, and the boy, who believed in his father’s knowledge of sounds, allowed that the ocean might be truer for other people. He has not forgiven himself for the way those words built a wall and called it wisdom.

He leans back now and resets, shakes his hands until the blood tingles the pads of his fingers. He checks the dampers, the alignment of the hammers. The leftmost dampers hang just a hair at rest; they need two turns of their spoons and a patience for felt. He sets them while he waits for the old key slip to shrink to a shape he can coax off. He has learned to do what the instrument needs and not what his schedule wants; it used to bother him that his days would stretch like old rubber until they held a shape he'd not planned. Anymore he takes the shape the day gives. He tunes the treble until it sings thin as tinsel and sweet, the perforation up there honest. He works the bass, where the copper-wound strings open their bellies like the earth itself. The lowest notes are always the ones nobody bothers to name properly. He tunes them until the gurgle in their throats comes to order, then does what he can about the wobble in their unisons. Two of the coils are slipping on their pins and he knows they'll slink when he leaves. He tightens where he can without breaking faith and he writes a note on his pad to tell her what might break if someone lifts the lid in anger.

When he is done he plays a C chord and then lets his fingers run to the F like a suggestion that someone might, on a better day, play “Lean on Me” for a kitchen dance. He plays the opening of a minuet he taught his son at the table, the one from the book with the drawings of children with wooden shoes. It catches on a part of his hand he can’t convince of youth. He closes the fallboard softly and replaces the top and Marlene, who had tried to pretend to be in the other room, says, “Do you think he would have liked that?”

“He would have,” Arthur says. He writes on the invoice and then scratches out the number and writes Paid in his cramped letters and slides it toward her without looking.

“Don’t you dare,” she says, as if daring has never been so thin.

“It’s the last time,” he says, which leaves her nothing to say, because on a last round you can be the kind of fool who pays for his own history. She sets the money on the piano and he leaves it, which is a sort of honesty between them also, though he takes an egg on his way out and puts it in his jacket pocket as if carrying fragile would keep him whole.

The road is bright and flat. He eats half a sandwich crossing the bottoms and puts crumbs in the pocket with the egg as if he has not become his own humor. At the old grange hall he lets himself in with the key he was given when his hair was black. The hall smells of varnish and wrong dance shoes. There are still posters on the wall, curling at their corners, from a harvest dinner in a year when the river jumped its bank and licked the parking lot and nobody promised it would not return. He moves the chairs away from the small upright at the foot of the stage. It was pushed down there to be closer to the street door when they wanted it in a truck; it never went. The bench is scarred with the initials of girls who practiced waltz-time with the thud of heels and the point of a penknife. He sets his case on the bench and runs the cloth across the fallboard. The keys feel like hands he used to kiss.

Outside, a combine voices itself like a slow argument. A mouse stutters in the molding and is silent for a long time, then is not. Dust swims in the sunlight like a private weather. He opens the case and raises the fallboard and the old wood flexes under his fingers like a dog that will be walked if only you insist. The name on the plate, early-century American, was once made in a red building by a river whose runoff killed trout. There are coins on the plate, an old habit of players and a poor man’s poor-boy jar with no prayer of prize. He has never understood the coins. He leaves them, as he always has. It is not for him to strip a shrine.

He tunes. On this one the pins have a syrup to them, the slow argument forward and back until they sit, the set hard to find unless you come at it sideways and mean no harm. He loves this work. He climbs into it like a tree’s shade and lets the bark rough him up. He knows there are men now who tune with machines and fold themselves into predictability like a napkin on a lap. He is not against machines. The lever is a machine. What he doesn’t want is the quiet theft of a job done with a simple ear and a promise. He has held so many small pitchers of water for so long, he no longer knows how to set them down without spilling. He tunes the temperament with intervals he favors, then checks it with a different family to prove it not a story he is telling himself. When he corrects an F a hair because it wobbles against a C he has already set, he feels the world align for the duration of a slow breath. He knows he has been mostly alone because the moments that make him feel accompanied are moments when things no one sees meet and agree.

On the way out he sits on the bench because he is not yet ready to stand. He places his palm on the fallboard and thinks of the blackening of his own oil on the wood that is not his to claim. He remembers the night the wind took a limb down in this lot and rattled the roof with a child’s delight and he was alone inside with the instrument and he welcomed the shudder as one more noise to tune among. When he was younger he enjoyed weather. Now he wants days like a length of dowel, uniform. He tells himself the preference is about work and not about fear.

The school is unlocked. Nobody trusts the town and nobody wants to drag themselves down here if somebody does break in. The building is damp the way empty things are damp. The bulletin board near the office has papers for an art fair in a month that has passed; a trumpet on a poster is made of angles that would make it unplayable in a universe with ethics. He opens the library door and the smell of paper holds him by the throat. In the music room, earthworms lie curled and dried on the tile where the last rain found the sill. The piano against the far wall is a spinet, which he hates, because you have to unscrew their fronts and then unscrew their uns and you have to reach down into them at the angle of a shoulder you will regret in another season. It is a Wurlitzer that was delivered when Carter was president and a teacher with a braid and socks in sandals was relieved it would fit through the stairwell to the stage. He sets the front to leaning and rests its bottom lip on a towel because even this skeleton deserves to be laid properly. He peers into the action. The bridle straps, those rotten lengths of cloth with loops like hangman’s smiles, are crisp at the edges. He needs to pull a few to keep them from decaying into more of themselves. He puts the spinet hanger on a cord against the cheekblock and brings the action forward as if coaxing a shy dog into the light. He lubricates what he dares. He saves enough of himself for what he has to finish today.

This room has a rack of recorders like little grave-markers. He stands and looks at them as if he will count, but he does not. He hears the recorded cough of a thousand afternoons kept in this air. He hears his son practicing when there was not time. He hears his son laughing when he hit the right side of wrong and made something like new. He tunes the spinet to the degree that doing so is not a lie. He writes on a note a warning that the jack on D4 is wearing sharp and will shave the wood until there is no hinge, and he tapes the note to the fallboard because if he tells the board in a meeting they will nod and spend the money to replace the faucet in the bathroom that hasn't run since the birth of the town.

The custodian finds him on the way to the door. Tom used to carry weight over his belt until his wife left, at which point he carried no other thing anybody could see. He keeps a ring of keys and now and then he wakes up on the dark bench in the lobby with the arc of the keys printed on his forehead, a confession of having tried and failed to unlock himself. They stand in the doorway and do not say that sentence men always seem to need—how’s it going—as if the fact of it going at all is a good. Instead Tom says, “They’re talking about consolidating with Westfork,” and Arthur says, “They’ve been talking about that since there were roads,” and Tom says, “I know,” and Arthur nods like he has nodded his whole life for other men when they need someone to witness how much contempt a human can hold for change and still manage to keep his job.

On the way to his car Arthur feels heavy in the way where a man could sleep standing with his head forward like a horse. He thinks he will stop at Jansen’s for pie. He thinks he will not. He thinks instead of his son’s first run at reading dotted rhythms and how his small face oversaw it with a best seriousness that made Arthur laugh, and that was the wrong answer; the boy had become angry and then had been competent, and there are people who live their whole lives in that order. The boy grew older and didn't come to him for help with a lot after that. On the morning of the recital the boy had learned the piece, and Arthur had tuned the church piano until it was as bare and honest as a pelt turned inside out. He had put his hands on the lid and felt his work ready under his skin. The boy came in with hair he had wet but not dried. He was tiny in the clothes his mother had hung on him without telling him why collars existed. He stepped up and put his hands on the keys like tongs from the stove and looked out and saw all the faces, none of which were Arthur’s. He did fine. Then he did not. He lost himself in the measure where the left hand was meant to be a machine so the right hand could be a voice. He stopped. He laughed. He apologized to the world through his nose. He went on. The room clapped like weeds in a ditch when a truck goes past. After, they ate grocery store cookies in the church basement and Arthur said, not loud, You had it. You had it and you let it go. The boy, who was twelve then, said, I know, and that was the first time he said I know like that, with the knowing a brick. He did not play in front of people again.

There is a man in the next place who has saved up the most cautious amount of money for his old piano’s voicing, which is like telling a horse you will now fit him for better shoes. The man is not home but he has left the door open, as is the unwise way of a life that has not yet had a lesson in thieves. The Emerson grand sits in the corner of a room that was meant to impress the kind of guest they never have. The soundboard is clean as driftwood and a hairline crack stripes it under the treble bridge like a geography. The hammers are flat-topped, worn with the oval of a thousand soft collisions. He sandpapers the faces and resets their shoulders, one by one, feeling for the grooves and bringing them back, the way some men take a pocket knife to the thick at their finger and make the ridge new. When he is done he needles them carefully, three pricks in the shoulders of the louder few, like acupuncture for a patient who will tell nobody that it helped. He tunes the instrument and when he plays the chord that has been in his hand his whole working life it sounds like the chord you would play if all memories were forgiven.

Outside, the day has become that hollow toward afternoon when the sun will leave without the pleading it did at four o’clock in July. He opens the thermos and drinks the coffee cold and sweet. He takes the egg from his pocket and cracks it on the bumper and swallows it like a boy who has been dared. He sits for a long breath with his eyes on the ditch where a plastic bag has been lodged against a rock for, he would guess, a season and a half. It flutters with a meanness that is human. He had thought he would stop working last spring when the corn came up and lay his days down in a row. He tuned right through August and only now, with the air like iron, decided he had to tell himself a kind of truth he has practiced withholding. The hammer, in his hand, is an argument he has mastered but not won. He could have tuned until someone lifted his hands from a bench and propped them in his lap and said no, friend. He is doing this himself for nobody to praise, and that feels both right and useless, and that too feels familiar.

The church sits out of the wind because the pines like to keep what they can. St. Aloysius is painted white with a kind of insistence he admires; somebody keeps these boards from going gray. The steeple is wood and humble and keeps its clock at a guess near right. The side door is unlocked with the stubbornness of a century of people telling each other that to lock a church is blasphemy against either God or poverty or both. He enters and the coolness climbs him. The sanctuary smells as it has always smelled in every season—wax, hymnals, the citrus hint of a cleaner your grandmother swore by. The carpet is the color of sand at night. Sunlight is a geometry through the stained glass saints who gaze down like men who know a joke. He wants to laugh at their seriousness and does not. He wants to put his hand in the font and does. The water is as still as a question. He takes his cap off and sets it on the piano bench before he would sit and crush it.

The piano is a Kimball upright with a case that has been beaten daily by the feet of children for fifty years. There’s a scratch down one leg where somebody years ago must have hauled a Christmas tree in here and given up believing in forgiveness. He moves it away from the wall. The casters argue on the plank under the carpet and he makes the sound he makes when a weight insists on its own idea. He opens the top and the fallboard and peers in. The wool of the hammers is clean enough; somebody has dusted with care. The pins face him like the heads of a congregation. He says hello in his head to them. His fork is the one thing he will not leave to anyone; he will put it in the pocket of his suit on the day when someone puts him somewhere that will not be his house. He taps it, listens, begins.

It is late enough that the light through the colored glass makes a new confession of the walls. He works and the sound of his work feels more than ordinarily alone. He thinks, because he cannot help it here, of that afternoon with those people in those chairs watching his boy come to the end of the breaking place and meet it. He thinks of his hands after, gathering programs and cups with polite circles on their bottoms, his hands yearning for something to hold that would not make the noise in his head worse. He thinks of telling the boy he had had it and giving that sentence the edge it should not have carried. He could say he had thought he was telling the boy he could meet a thing. He was telling him instead that on the day your best is watched it will be judged a failure by the person you have brought all your failures to. It is not the content of the advice. It is the way it arrives on a morning when a boy has mixed sleep and water and starch together and called it hair.

He sets the octaves across the instrument one by one, taking confidence in the way the high notes bury their sparrows in the eaves of the ceiling and the low notes go down like a tide. He finds the beats and then knows he has gone a hair past and comes back and is glad for it: a lesson in any season. He hears a truck on the road and thinks of the river. He thinks of the boy at seventeen, not dead, not gone to any place a tide could name, but gone to a city to lift boxes then to a far job that was supposed to be one season and changed him into a man who learned where the sun rose over water that was salted in a different way than a farmhand’s sweat. There were postcards for a while, novelty images with whales or men in orange suits that zip to the chin. Then a single letter. Then a phone call the night Sarah’s breath went thin and he raised his hand to tell her to hold on because the boy was on the phone—he nearly did this, and hated himself as he lowered his hand. He said instead, “He’s on the line,” and she said, “I don’t want to talk,” and he could not blame her. The boy wanted to talk to the man who had trained him to listen, and that man was holding the hand of a woman with milk still on her knuckles from the day she had fed a hawk by accident from the quivering air of her husband’s name.

Arthur pulls the felt mute with the slow satisfaction of a man drawing a nail from an old board. He sets the unisons in a stretch of eight notes and then looks up into the light as if testing his work against something it cannot possibly answer to. He tunes the low E. He takes the lever out and puts it on the plate and waits to see if the string will slip backward. It holds. He taps each string with the tip of a mute because he has learned to check the quiet matters as carefully as the shouts. At the end he plays chords he knows you ought not to play in a church if you don’t want to make yourself ridiculous and he plays them anyway. He slides from a C to an A minor and back as if he is showing someone that a room can be a machine for grief and for its sweet apology without any moving parts but wood.

It is the piece he will not try that sits there anyway, humming, and makes him a young man again against his will. He puts his hands where a boy’s hands were once and could not be again. He plays the minuet in the right hand, because that is how the boy had practiced it, and he plays the left hand softer than the boy had, because that is how a father who has learned anything would do it. He strikes the sweet wrong note as his son did and does not let the laugh into his throat. He keeps on, and at the end he lets his hands float above the keys like the landing of something with its own air.

He closes the lid over the keys and he does not. He leaves it open and goes to the door and stands and looks at the pews as if they might stand up and apologize to him on behalf of the people they have held. They do not. He returns and takes a slow pencil from his case and writes on his pad the things he has never been required to write: to the next man, should there be another man. He writes in a long line the matters of this instrument—pinblock good, hammers fair, A’s at 440, tenor bridge hair noted but not cracked through, dampers slightly heavy in the bass; watch D3 coil. He writes, with the same hand and the same voice, Play this one sometimes, for it will forgive you for being poor. He folds the note and tucks it under the lower hinge where anyone looking with any intention will see what the paper has to say.

He takes the key from the window sill where it has always lived and goes outside and checks that it turns in the lock. It does not need to. He leaves it turning anyway, the small mechanics of an exit. It is colder. The pines do not shift. He thinks of going to Sarah’s grave first and then going home. He thinks of calling the number that did not work the last nine times and listening to the way hope as a sound is always static. He puts the case in the trunk and shuts it with a breath of his whole body. He stands beside the car and imagines his father’s hands, the way they would have gone around the steering wheel, military, certain. His own fingers are swollen at the joints in a way that makes him prematurely foolish in mittens. He rubs his hands together and the noise sounds like a bow across a string that is not long for this work.

He drives home not by the ridge road but by the creek because the water still speaks now and then when the rest of the world has decided to stop. He brakes for a possum that looks like an old man trying to recall whether he was on his way to the feed store or to confession. He passes the barn with the tractor somebody will buy and never bring alive. He pulls into his own yard just as the first star over the silo draws a spiderweb of frost in his eye. He clicks the garage by hand because he still trusts his arms more than a motor. He closes the trunk and lifts the case and feels its weight like this day collected into an object he can set where he chooses.

Inside the house is the sound of heat ticking like punctuation. He sets the case on the table where his wife used to roll out noodles with a bottle. He flicks the lamp. The room is the color of patience. He opens the case and takes out the fork and the lever and the mutes and lays them on a towel he has washed for years for this one job: making things cleaner with a cloth. He runs his thumb on the lever’s handle. It is wore to the shine of a saint’s toe in a church he has seen only in pictures. He puts the fork to his temple and taps it and hears A as if his bones could tune to it at last and make of him a thing that rings naturally and then is silent.

He leaves the case open and goes to the window where he can see the road. No truck. No young man he knows will not come. The neighbor’s birches shake like laughter in an empty theater. He goes back to the table and folds the towel and sets the lever on top of it as if setting a baby for a rest. He thinks of calling Tom to tell him he will not be doing the spring tune at the school and he counts three beats in the air and calls and leaves the message and says nothing that would suggest a grief. He sits down. He takes his pad and writes the names of the instruments from the day and crosses each with a line that does not quite get to the far right of the paper. He puts his glasses on his head and rubs his eyes as if that is where the day collects. He reaches for his pen and then stops.

He walks back out to the car. Night has its own frost in the air, the sort that is not visible until you breathe into it. He drives back toward St. Aloysius with one headlight doing the work of two, a hero of an evening nobody else will approve. He turns the car off at the edge of the lot and sits in the chill until the last of the engine’s conversations have concluded. He goes in, though it is not necessary and it is necessary. He goes to the piano and lifts the fallboard and puts his hands there again, as if touching the same wood twice in the same day can change the meaning of days. He plays the minuet once more, less like a memory and more like an offering. In the wrong note he finds the particular sweetness of knowing exactly where you are among your own regrets. He ends and places both hands flat on either side of the keys as if arresting a prisoner kindly. He lowers the fallboard at last as if lowering a rule on a page. He stands and takes the lever out of his jacket pocket where he had thought he would keep it until the last day. He lifts the bench lid and places the lever inside where the church keeps its extra hymnals and its pencils with their chewed ends. He leaves it there. He leaves the fork too; that is bravado, but there are gestures that answer you even if the world never does.

Outside he stands on the step and the sky is older than the two men he has been. The pines are black to their elbows and do not shudder. He takes the key from the sill and sets it back where it may or may not be needed. He walks to the car slowly, because he has used up this day and wants to see what it looks like to use it all up. On the seat the woolen sleeve lies folded like a cat. He starts the engine. The headlight lifts its circle. He backs and eases onto County Nine and is received by the road the way he always has been, without comment. He reaches to the radio and does not turn it on. He lets the tuning in his ears be the thing he hears. When he lifts his foot from the gas to meet the long hill down to his house the car does not complain. The road shines. He holds the wheel the way you hold a hand you should have held longer when it mattered and a little longer when it did not. He thinks of his boy on a boat in the kind of cold he cannot imagine, listening for the creak that says something under the deck is not settling so much as warning. He thinks of the first and only recital. He thinks of this last and only round. He finishes thinking. The ridge rolls by and in the ditch the plastic bag is gone. A trick of wind or someone’s cleaner instinct. It lifts him more than it should, that absence. He drives home. He takes the long breath that fits him now the way a shirt from your own youth will, surprisingly, for one night when all you plan to do in it is sit. He goes inside and turns out the lamp and leaves the case open in the dark, the white of the felt bright until it is not, and he is quiet long enough to hear the slow settling of a whole house, the sigh of wood giving itself back to the old shape he always loved, and the note that rings and finally fades into the ordinary.
