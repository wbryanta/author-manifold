When Evelyn came back to the American there were sea-glass bowls holding green apples on the registration desk and a framed photograph of the original façade, stucco and grillwork and the iron-scripted A, smoke dark around the windows. The caption under the photograph read Our Legacy in a serif that seemed to aspire to authority without inviting questions. The woman at the desk handed Evelyn a credit-card-shaped key and said We are honored to welcome you back, Mrs. Reyes, tone precise, smiling so that the muscles in her jaw quivered slightly and the lip gloss, a shade called Sugar Plum if Evelyn remembered the drugstore rack correctly, made a faint filigree of moisture on her lower lip. There was a composition on the credenza behind the desk of river reeds sprayed gold and three empty leather-bound volumes stacked purposefully, an arrangement suggesting books without the hazard of words.

She had not been Mrs. Reyes for six years, but people here had a way of calling her by what was on the reservation and then by what they had heard from Lucy O’Brien’s Evite, so that between the automatic and the social she answered to most things. She took the key and the directions—up the central bank of elevators past the water wall (water fell down plate glass, a faint static), left to the north wing, the Sacramento View side—and allowed herself to be guided into recognizing the floral pattern in the new carpet. The pattern was in shades of wheat and a diluted blue-gray, reeds layered on reeds, and when she looked directly at it she saw it moving the way fields did when the wind passed.

It had been called the American since Prohibition, or maybe after, old men would tell you both depending on how many drinks they had taken and how much they wanted you to feel benign about the past. Before the fire there had been brass banisters cold to the touch in winter, and an elevator bank that never arrived when needed. There had been the landings that nobody saw but her and the other women on day shift; the service stair where the light bulbs went out and were not replaced quickly enough; the laundry chute with a dent halfway down, making a predictable snag for sheets, so that the day-woman would have to go to the chute door on five or six or seven and tug at whatever was bunched there, a damp, resistant clutch in the iron. They had worked a circuit like prayer, rooms that were nothing but ashtrays and towel origami, rooms with suitcases open as mouths and underclothes like something lost and delicate (she had learned not to look, learned to see without registering, to move the glittering things in the bedside dish as if they had nothing to do with the people who would want them back); the quiet rooms sealed by security after a fight, sometimes blood on the pillowcase dried to a color like tea, and you pretended that the traces could be erased if the towel folded just so. In Spanish and Tagalog and English they had learned to say Housekeeping, they had learned to say is there anything I can get you, they had learned to say I’m sorry like a rosary.

The north wing had not existed when she worked there. You could feel the graft, the way a remodel looks inevitable after you learn to take it on its own terms. The gray light in the hall had the quality of the Delta, the river silt that gets into your teeth in August, the way your legs look in water when you step in and you think it is shallow and then you slip because the slime under the surface gives just that way.

Room 915 looked toward the bridge the way a person might choose to look past a person they had once loved. The bridge was a bronze color now and had a lift span, and when she was young she had come over it with Isabel on the bus from West Sacramento to downtown to look at prom dresses in Fife’s (she had not gone to the prom), and on the way they would sit above the wheel well and make a point of not looking at the hotel because back then the American had been where the wives of the men Isabel worked for went for lunch, white napkins, and it did not occur to them that one day a person could be in a uniform and a smock in the service corridor saying Escoba, con cuidado, there is broken glass in that trash. Isabel had left for a nail salon job in a strip mall near Florin Road, this was before the fire, and had, last Evelyn heard, married a man from Bakersfield who wanted children and wanted money in that order and had neither the year he left.

You could open the window in 915 two inches before it caught, a coded indulgence to an era when guests sued hotels for less. Water in the water bottle that had a paper seal cost nine dollars and there was a rate card for catering slipped into the leather folder on the desk, lists that calmed a certain kind of dread with their precision: crudités 7/guest; carving station, rosemary jus, thirty-two per person; beverage minimums applied, service charges taxable. The invitation had said Six-thirty, ballroom, formal attire. On the card the ballroom was now called the Delta Pavilion and the air in the room smelled faintly of new carpet waiting for shoes.

She was actually not the kind of person who would choose to come back to a place because a version of the place had been where she had almost died. There had been something in the click of the Evite that had suggested this event was happening without her, and also because of her. Lucia O’Brien (Lucy in high school, Lucy to her mother, Lucia when she got the job as assistant in the district attorney’s office, because Lucia had a ring to it, it called up a cruise itinerary and certain silver bowls) had typed: It would mean the world to me if you were there, and appended a heart and a palm tree, neither of which meant anything, unless you saw a heart as the pump that kept blood in its track and a palm tree as a way to pretend the river did not flood.

The O’Briens had lived south of Broadway in a house with a varnished oak built-in that had glass doors, behind which Lucy’s mother kept crystal bowls and one bowl filled with shells from a vacation they had taken once to Sanibel. The bowl, even then, had seemed the kind of object that said: I am a person of record. Patrick O’Brien had been a state fire marshal out of Vacaville in the years when there were not yet professional words for the thing that burned you down if it came across a wheat field with no break. He had had the walkie-talkie voice and the particular brand of fatigue that made him funny, until he wasn’t. It was never lost on anyone that Patrick, who had walked in smoke so often that he sometimes dreamed in a color like turned steel, had put his one daughter where there had been fire and not known, or known but wanted her to iron her own dress shirts out afterwards. He had retired the year before the American burned, and he had died the year after, and Lucy kept a photograph of him in her office with the peeling laminated Stop-Drop-and-Roll poster behind him and had learned how to say, even when she was not in a courtroom, the phrase With all due respect.

There had been a year, not long before the fire, when Evelyn and Lucy had sat on the same picnic table at the same apartment complex on a Sunday afternoon that smelled like barbecue sauce and chlorine because someone was making food at a grill near the pool and the pool had the chemical level turned up high so that you could smell its intention to cleanse. They had sat with their legs on the bench and their flip-flops under the table and talked about moving away. They had made it as far as Dixon that day and then gotten off, just to prove to themselves that they could go, even if it was only to a gas station where the girl at the cash register had three piercings in one ear and sold them an energy drink and a candy bar that neither of them wanted. It felt like a memory to someone else. Lucy had gone farther afterwards. Some people left town from a high arc and did not look back, not because they did not love the place but because they were shaped to behave as people who had always intended to leave. Others like Evelyn curved close to the ground and could not tell whether this meant that they were prudent or weak.

When she undressed to get into the shower the air struck the skin under her breasts cool and she felt the invisible seam around her left thigh. She had the scar from eight stitches there, when she had run into the drywall corner on the landing that night, not seeing the gray because the hall had gone from light to smoke to that particular twilight that comes when electricity resumes enough to throw a pale form behind the fog. She tilted her head to get the water down the back of her hair and thought about the sound of the sprinkler activation—the pop, then the hammering—like hearing the roof rain over your head when you are still in bed, that interval where the sound is pleasant and then when it changes from pleasant to indicative of a problem, once it has found a leak and you cannot move the bed fast enough.

It was not the fire you remembered, not in the way that people at court told her, not the orange image in the newspapers that marveled at flame. What you remembered was waiting for someone to tell you to leave and then discovering, with a disappointment almost like pain, that it did not matter whether someone told you or not, that you were in the corridor with the press of your breath already in your ears and the smoke an organism that could take your life without drama, that did not even want your life, that simply took it because nature does not pause to admire human obstacles. She had been on eleven that night, although she remembered it as nine or maybe five, because five had been the floor where they kept the dogs during one convention, and the dogs, cooped up in a room with the handler’s control on them, had peed in the corners and the urine, in combination with the chemical floor cleaner, made a new smell that she could still taste if she went at it deliberately. The elevator had dinged and then not come. The announcement voice had said something like Please stand by and the women from Housekeeping who were still there because they had been called back for turn-down walked toward the stair with carts too heavy to be taken easily and then let go of the carts, relucantly, one by one, as if each had money in it, and they did, they had the tips. The first person to die had been a man on twelve who had thought he had time to take his shoes from under the bed. The fire had traveled from a fault in a wall that the contractor had noticed when he opened the chase three months before and then, because the day was short and the schedule close, the man had closed the wall and said something to someone who said something to someone who said we will fix it on punch list and then forgot.

At the rehearsal cocktail hour (Asiago crisps with lavender honey, ahi tuna on a spoon shaped like a spoon out of a dream of spoons, a signature drink the planner had die-cut into a card labeled Delta Dawn), people spoke with their faces turned slightly upward as if certain of air. Evelyn stood with a plastic glass of white wine that had a crossing of light through it and listened to the mother of the groom, a woman who wore pearls in that way that made the pearls look heavy, like something in training. The mother of the groom said things that were at once frank and useless: There’s never a right time to do everything, and Still, we do what we can. The groom himself was five-eleven with the torso of a swimmer and that way of looking at his watch that meant he was not checking time so much as rehearsing being a person who needed to check it.

The bride was Lucy, who had made a decision to wear a dress that bared her shoulders and then, until she could take the first picture, raised her shoulders minutely so that the cut of the dress would remain clean. Lucy kissed Evelyn on the cheek with the concentration of a person touching a hot plate with the back of their hand. You came, Lucy said. I did, Evelyn said. She could not decide whether she felt gratitude or a preemptive surrender to what the evening would ask of her. There were paper lanterns under the eaves of the river terrace and beyond that the river itself moving toward Rio Vista, toward a place where the water widens and you are not quite sure whether it stops being itself. On the railing of the terrace there was a small plaque that had not been there when the hotel was a hotel and not a reimagining. It read In memory of those we lost in 2005. Eight names. Names without ages or roles, names that could be read as pronouncements, because a name without its context is a form of judgment. Someone had left a small bouquet of supermarket roses there, the kind that have the stems that never drink no matter how you cut them.

One of the names was Mrs. Hye. The older housekeeper. She had trained Evelyn her first week. If you made the bed wrong she would unmake it and then, fifty per cent of the time, she would let you remake it and, the other fifty, she would make it herself in those precise tucks that no hotel manager would notice and every person who came back to the room and sat would devote half a second to appreciating before they messed it up again. She had been in the stairwell on ten and had sat, they said, to rest. She had had a bad hip and the doctor had told her to elevate it at night. She had had a son who brought her to work and picked her up again at six. He had not been allowed to go in when the building was still a building. There were articles then about blame, there were press conferences. The union rep had said We will not stand down, the contractor had said We used approved materials, the hotel management had said This was an unforeseeable event. There was a photograph of Mrs. Hye from a family picnic in which she was holding a papaya in a white bowl. For a long time afterwards, for months, if Evelyn saw papaya she could not swallow.

It happened like this: She had been in 1121, which was a suite, and the people in it had been attending a gala because they had left a gold ribbon on the bedside table and the ribbon had the name of a nonprofit that later would become different in her mind. They had asked for extra towels and there had not been extra towels on the shelf in the supply room so she took them from 1123 because in her experience 1123 would not need the extra towels, the way 1123 did not need the Frette robe, the way 1123 did not need the feather pillow. She had taken the towels and smoothed them so that they would not look secondhand and when she came out into the corridor smoke was walking toward her, low at first the way you tell children to crawl, and then tall and active. She had had in her hand a rag—blue as the water in the bucket before you used it—and she put the rag over her mouth in a gesture that she had believed would work because she had seen it in movies and because there are gestures we have learned to take as talismans. She took the stairs. On the landing there was carpeting that had been new and it blackened coolly, it changed modes exactly at the moment when you put your hand down. Everything in those minutes felt like something she had known in advance and misplaced: the rail higher than you remembered, the fire door heavier, the voices on one side of a door that were not there when you opened it. When she cut her leg she did not feel the cut, she felt only the stick to her pants when she kept moving. She had the impression of moving toward ground when she was moving laterally through service corridors she had not gone through at that hour.

There had been a man in a uniform—security or a guest from the fundraiser with a jacket he had grabbed from a chair—that she could not pick out in memory. He had pushed the bar for her and said Go down and she had thought of the way in elementary school they told you to proceed to the field nearest the Maple Street side. A woman had held a door against the suction. The woman’s hair was in an updo with pins and she lost a pin and it fell like a bug. There were insects that came out of the wall for a while afterwards in her dreams and went into her mouth. She had seen then, because she had to, that there are moments when you know, without drama, that you might not make it. There was no noise in that understanding. Her own heart made less sound than the falling ash.

She had gone out the service entrance on the level where they kept banquet chairs. There was a roll-up door that, because it was Saturday, had been barred by one rusted padlock, the key to which was on a ring in a drawer at a desk in an office in the back of the kitchen. Later this would be part of the litigation. There was a way to get under the bottom slat of the roll-up door by kneeling. When her knee hit concrete she approved of how sure it felt.

She thought about telling that story the way you rehearse things you will never tell because the revision makes them smooth and therefore safe. At the table for the rehearsal dinner a man in a linen blazer with a plantation idea of himself asked her where she had come from and she said Here, I’m from here, and he said I mean tonight, where did you park, and she said Out back by the service entrance. He said Service entrance? and for a second she had the image, because words can give you anyone’s picture, of herself on her knees under the roll-up door holding her breath as if that could make the air not kill you.

She excused herself and went to the ladies’ room because that was the move she had practiced for years, had deployed to end scenes that did not need her. There was a sofa by the sinks and a basket with spare bobby pins and deodorant towelettes and a laminated card suggesting mindfulness. The mirror showed her a person with hair too smooth and eyes that told in some plain way what you did not want told. She wiped at the line under her right eye with a tissue until it reddened. In the stall closest to the wall she stood without doing anything, hands at her sides, and counted backwards from fifty. She thought of the buckets they had used to clean the tile in the old bathrooms, the smell of lemon oil and bleach, the vinegar they used when guests had insisted on scent-free. In the pocket of her dress she had a hotel key card because even dressed like this she did not like to leave a room without making certain of her return.

At breakfast Lisa from Housekeeping stopped speaking to her because Lisa thought she was a guest in a disguise, which was not untrue. Lisa had been around the first time (there are names you will hear only in one context and afterwards will belong there like a pin back to its board). There had been three women of approximately the same description then that management had evinced an inability to separate: Lisa, Liza, Liz. To keep it straight someone had written their full names in Sharpie on the small laminated cards that hung from their lanyards and in a week that someone had been told to forget it because guests had expressed discomfort at such direct display of labor. Lisa now wore her hair shorter and had a tattoo you could see climbing her wrist that had not been there before. She caught Evelyn in the periphery of the banquet foyer folding the napkins with an iron in a way that stops you because it is work and has to be done and is not noticed by most of the people who eat the meal with their napkins wrinkled or not. Lisa looked at Evelyn and kept looking as if memory had come upon her and was deciding whether to be trusted.

You’re the one, Lisa said, not a question.

Evelyn did not know how to answer in a way that did not conspire with a narrative she found suspicious. I was here, she said. She lifted her hand like a person who might be cross-examined, palm showing nothing.

Lisa said, I dream it sometimes and it’s in the wrong place, like it’s the grocery store steps or the school, like all the places with stairs are the same place. She set the iron down. You look good, she said then, a thing women tell women to let the air in. They got the plaque wrong, she added in the next breath, conversational, as if she did not want to stick the point. They didn’t even call it a fire. It’s like when you call a funeral a celebration of life. Who wants their life celebrated like that. I mean just say it. We lived through a fire. It was hot.

She laughed, and it was not quite a laugh. Evelyn had not decided whether she wanted to keep Lisa inside her afternoon. She said, Like a kitchen. It was the only thing she could think to say.

Like a kitchen, Lisa said, looking surprised that this would be what Evelyn had chosen in the range of possibilities for simile. Lisa returned to the napkins the way you return to the part of your day that is not waiting for someone else to tell you what your day means.

After the groom’s mother had cried about a slide of a child in a beard at a bar mitzvah that had not been his but had read as fatherhood practiced on daughters, and after Lucia had told the story of the pecans they had stolen from the neighbor’s tree when they were nine and had eaten until their hands stained and the neighbor had called and said they were showing poor character and she had not told on Evelyn and Lucy had gotten a look of no-nonsense about her because this memory was an exhibit and also because it was evidence of the person she required herself to be, after this, Evelyn went up in the elevator to the ninth floor and could feel her stomach pulling under her ribs that way it does when you have not eaten anything not passed around on a tray. In the hall she saw the door to the stair and its green-lettered exit sign and without thinking too much opened it.

The stairwell had the smell they had not covered up. You could make it smell like paint for a while and like concrete dust and, in sharp minutes where someone did not mop correctly, like urine, but there was a layer under that, the smell of the wall itself as it had burned, as if heat had taught the concrete something it then would not forget. On nine the light was good. She went down three flights and saw nobody. The railing was the height the code wanted; it felt both comforting and false, your hand on it and you did not believe it would not come off its mounting, and it did not. At six the numbers in the paint were a different font, done by a different contractor, or an impatient man who had stenciled quickly and to whom letters were less a gallery then than a matter of keeping to break.

She stopped at five. On five you could go out the service door and cut across to a part of the building that had not existed before. When she put her hand to the bar the alarm chirped and then stopped, the kind of alarm calibrated to startle but not require you to change. She found herself in a corridor with plates warming under cloches and a server who said Can I help you and she said Wrong door, not because it was wrong but because this was how you had to say it. She was aware of lip gloss on her front teeth. She was aware of a mote in the air that would not commit to being a real thing.

On the wall there was a framed front page of the Sacramento Bee from that week in 2005. It was a curated act, paper under glass, a reach into authenticity to ground the new with the old. The headline read HOTEL BLAZE 8 DEAD in type that would not be used now, not because there was anything wrong with the type but because the year had taught headlines to try harder to entertain. There was a photograph of the bridge double-exposed somehow so that it appeared to lift itself over the scene, the way a city lifts itself over its own acts, telling a story you would prefer. She put her finger to the glass briefly and then pulled it away because fingerprints were trouble. Through the service hall she could see the doors of the kitchen opening and closing, a choreography she could not help loving even though this was the organ that had failed her once, not because kitchens house fire (they housed it) but because in that minute the door had been heavier than expected and the bar had stuck, detail that her deposition would return to and return to, the lawyer staying soft, weren’t you strong enough, and her wanting to answer I was strong enough and also not, these can both be true.

In the morning Lucy sent a text with an adjective piling up like flowers. Crisis averted!! it read, and then: Could use a favor if you can come down for makeup and also Mom is stressed about place cards can you look them over? There had been a problem with the stepfather’s last name and where it was intended to sit, name cards taking on the weight of borders and wars. The stepfather had been in the picture long enough to expect something and yet had always suspended expectation. Evelyn went down to the room assigned to Beauty. In Beauty there were six women already and a makeup artist with a neck tattoo of a rosary and the move where he said Look at me sweetie so that the moment did not become a documentary about someone being made up against their will. The artist said I need more face under these eyes to make them pop, he meant highlighter, and Lucy said Do what you need. When the artist got to Evelyn he said You have great bones and she said As long as you don’t say structure and he said You’re funny. He was right about the thing with bones. Eyes reveal bones in a way that makes you see what you got from the people who made you, and what you didn’t. The artist had a soft brush in a glass cup with stones at the bottom to make the stems stand upright and she thought of the vase of shells in the O’Brien dining room, of choosing a life like choosing materials for an arrangement: two eggshells, a piece of sea glass, a rock in shapes that mean something you want someone else to guess.

There were speeches in the afternoon from people with titles before their names, because Lucy had gone to law school and then to a job with a view of the Capitol, and at work the person who had arranged flowers there put them in a certain way that said: No glitz. The kind of person who said No glitz would put something expensive in the arrangement, something you could not identify as expensive until a person told you with their hand open to show you its cost. Sewn in the hem of Lucy’s dress there was a small blue ribbon that had been cut from the sash of the dress she had worn as a flower girl and she would tell this to anyone who asked because she had been trained to answer anticipated questions as if the answers were hers to invent.

The ceremony itself happened under the river oaks, in air that read as clear but when you breathed had grains in it, or she imagined the grains, the way your tongue knows the difference between sugar and salt even when neither is there. The officiant said the things that officiants say: love is patient, love forgives, love bears all. He said you can go from here into a house you build together, and Lucy did not look at the man, she looked at a point halfway down the aisle where someone’s child was pressing a wadded tissue into the grille of a floor vent to see if the air would take it. The tissue did not move.

She had sat in the fourteenth row by luck or by Lucy’s intention. Behind her a woman in a silk dress whispered, as if the whisper would keep it from being real, that it had been daring to get married in a place like this, daring meaning brave meaning foolish. Her companion said I like a comeback story. The DJ played a song about home and Lucy’s stepfather stood up and swayed a little in a way he would not commit into dancing. Patrick O’Brien had liked Merle Haggard because Merle was from right over the line. When he died Lucy had made an iTunes playlist for the gathering at the house with the shells, but the speakers had made a sound like bees.

At the reception the ice sculpture—a swan, these are never swans, they are intentions to be swans—began to weep. The planner looked at it and judged the speed of the changes. A woman at the gift table ran a box cutter lightly, as if that would not leave a mark. Evelyn signed her name in a book with thick pages that would not take the ink right. On a easel near the dais there was an eight-by-ten of Lucy and her husband at Mount Lassen, wind at their jackets, faces not smiling the way people smile when they frame this. The last time Lucy had wanted to climb Lassen they had been in a rental Honda with a crack in the windshield like a river in a drought. They had stopped at a pullout and gotten out and looked at the valley and been paralyzed by how far down it went. Lucy had talked about a smaller mountain as if the mountain could hear them.

There were times during the dinner when you felt resentful of the service of the forks. The man to her left—tanned, watch large in the way of men who want their wrists to be an explanation—told her that he worked in insurance in a voice that did not request response. He said he was in catastrophic and paused for appreciation. He said We see these events, as if there was a way to see them that did not involve heat or water or loss. He said We lift people up. She said like a bridge, because it came to her. He looked at her as if she had punned and then, when she did not smile, as if she had not.

During the toasts, Lucy’s mother stood and gripped the back of the chair as if it might rock. She said We have come through a lot. She did not enumerate. The O’Briens had a way of keeping a ledger in pencil. The groom’s father, a man with a face like a gate unlocked by laughter and by alcohol, spoke about markets and about being lucky that the young would take over the houses and the jobs soon enough, and in this there was an impatience that did not suit the words. Someone brought out napkins with the initials in a monogram that spelled something else if you looked quickly, not love or their names, but an acronym with meaning to no one. Toward the end of the speeches a server dropped a tray on the hard floor and in the first second she thought gun. She put both hands flat on the white cloth and the cloth wavered and then settled. One knife shifted.

She had a thought then that did not make her panicky but precise. She needed to walk the service hallway. It was not compulsion so much as a practical measure of the mind, the way your fingers go automatically to the place where you know the pulse will be. Past the kitchen where the heat came out like science, past the racks of glasses on blue plastic crates, the dishwashers taking bowls from machine to machine, a conveyor belt to the future; past the woman who had cut her hand on Saturday and wore a bandage that made her look bandaged in a movie way, gallant and incompetent. She kept her eyes down. There is a way to move in work spaces that tells anyone who knows that you belong, shoulders as if you carry, elbow close when you turn a corner, and she had kept that way the way you keep handwriting you learned in third grade.

The service corridor at the far end had a dimmer switch at hand height and a fire extinguisher in a bracket. She put her hand near the extinguisher and did not touch it. She put her hand on the cinder block painted white and it felt cool. She thought: The walls do not remember. She thought: I am the one who wants to assign a memory to something that will never have one. She thought of the young man on twelve who had died with his shoes not on. The details you give yourself when there is nothing you can do will ruin you if you spend time trying to keep them perfect. She tried not to picture him tying a shoe. She tried to be adult, which is to say not to be superstitious.

In the greenroom, a name they gave to the smaller ballroom where the flower girls were sleeping in a pile on a blanket and a woman with bright hair had been told by the planner to keep the wine there where it would not leave red stains on the carpet, she sat for a minute on a reasonable couch upholstered in a fabric printed with so many small leaves it gave an illusion of pattern. There are rooms you would only enter when dressed. In those rooms the chairs do not expect to be used. She took off her shoes and rubbed one foot with the edge of the other and watched a woman adjust her bra strap through her dress with a expertise that made the move invisible. When she went back out she took the narrow stair used by the catering staff. It had one bulb not lit. She counted the steps in twelves.

Uncle Peter put his hand on her shoulder during the last song, an old cousin of Lucy’s mother, a man who had gotten religion in Texas but kept the rectangle of some other thing like a pattern on his tie, because he had always kept a pattern on his tie. You look like you see a ghost, he said, and he meant: I know what happened here and I wonder if you are dramatizing. She said No. She said It’s loud. He nodded. When the lights went up the planner’s assistant clapped her hands twice softly like a teacher and in that moment you saw the classroom in the ballroom and then it went away. She looked around for Lucy and then decided that Lucy was elsewhere in a room with candles and glass and another woman pulling at a stuck hook with her teeth.

She went upstairs. She undid the clasp of her bra and lay down on the duvet and watched the ceiling. You could feel through the soles of your feet in this place a mechanical and certain hum that did not vary. She had a postcard in her suitcase from a place in Utah where someone she had slept with once had gone and had not sent her the postcard, which is to say she had bought it for herself. The photograph on it showed a canyon in which the water was red. Water only looks red if you want it to or if the geology insists. There had been a time after the fire when she had wanted only to look at photographs of places where the organized detritus of natural disaster had been kept in place for people to learn from: the burned chaparral with the grid of black lines like veins, the inundated subdivisions with street signs poking out at rational angles. She had thought then that looking would protect her. It did nothing of the kind. There are acts you think will prepare you and they do nothing. There are acts you think do nothing and they prepare you only after you have forgotten you did them.

The light in the room changed once. She had not realized she had fallen asleep until the air intake shut and started again. She stood at the window for a minute and looked at the bridge and the slow reflection of the lights on the river. There was a houseboat or a barge moving without telling. She saw a man outside on the sidewalk in a black vest have a thing said to him by a woman in a dress that had decided to walk away and on the third step he chose to follow her. In the mirror above the desk a woman with hair coming down looked back at her with that expression she had when she had not yet chosen between fight and float. She pressed her finger into the scar on her thigh with enough pressure to register human presence.

I’ll stay for breakfast, she thought, as if breakfast were an act of control. She would not tell Lucy that she had gone into the stair. She would write a check later for whatever they had registered for that had not been fulfilled and would not include a note to make her act of support purely financial and therefore adored. She would go down to the plaque again and put a finger on the glass and then, because of fingerprints, would not. She would drive over the bridge and not look back at the building because she had looked at it enough. On the way home on West Capitol there would be a man holding a hand-lettered sign and the sign would say Hungry or it would say Not going to lie, need beer, and she would put a dollar out the window because dollars are a way of managing your evening.

In the morning hotel breakfast smelled like bacon in the hallway and there were three men at an end table speaking Croatian into their phones between bites. The coffee came in a carafe with a thumb press and an attitude. She ate a grapefruit with sugar and felt the sting on her tongue because the sugar did not have any reason to soften what the grapefruit wanted for you. There was a little pot of jam unopened. The waitress asked if she wanted more toast, and although she did not she said yes, because saying yes is sometimes the way you make it to the next hour. The waitress turned her order pad over not like a ticket but like a card trick and smiled in a practiced sympathy of the morning. The light went pale and then pale-blue and then came up into something more like white.

On her way out she stopped at the desk where the apples always renewed themselves. There was a young man there with skin that pulled tight over his jaw because he had not learned to hide a hangover. He asked how her stay had been and she said Lovely, meaning: I did not die. He said We hope to see you again, and she said That depends. She said it with a levity you use on a person who had not been born when you left a place on your knees. He said something about the view, and she said The view is good. She wanted to say: There was a time when you would have put me out the service door. She wanted to say: In my dream I’m in a stairwell that is a grocery store, that is an apartment building, that is a school, and the hand on the rail is my hand and not. She wanted to say: When I think about the wall that burned I think about the things we cover. She nodded instead. She took an apple out of the bowl and put it into her bag without deciding to want it, and for some reason this pleased her, the addition of weight, the shape like a heart held in your hand, the unthinking refusal to look at whether this was the kind of apple that bruised.

In the street the air smelled like the river and the bakery that baked for other bakeries. A bus went by with a placard that read Paradise Falls Luxury in the window, the kind of development name that suggested a story you could correct with a match. She put her hands on the steering wheel and waited for the light to change because to run it would be to change, in scatter, a life she had been practicing to keep entire. She thought of Lisa and of the napkins and of Lucy with her shoulder pressed up to the dress so the strap would not slip off, careful against gravity. She thought of the way the roll-up door had been open this time, her having only to walk through. She thought: It was a fire. She thought: I was there. She thought: I am not only there. The light changed, and the bridge was wide enough for three cars abreast, and there was a space in the line. She took it.
