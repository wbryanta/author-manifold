The wind had been pushing all afternoon, a steady shove from the northwest that turned the pasture into a white field shaped like a clenched hand. By dusk it found its voice and came howling over the ridge, blowing the flakes sideways so fine and thick they seemed to make their own smoke. We watched it from the kitchen window, the glass beaded with melt, and the ewes in the lower lot had gone clumpy and gray with rime, all their patience boiled away into motion. Winter had been lazy and then late, and here it was in the last week of April, coming at us with insult and a meanness I felt in my bones.

Owen shrugged into his coat by the door. The coat had been his father’s before it became his, the sleeves still a little long, the cuffs stiff with work. He was sixteen to the day last week, though I kept forgetting until the night fell quiet and I saw his mouth as it had been when he was small, all softness even when set. “Lights are still on, Mom,” he said. “Maybe it’ll blow past.”

He always tried to give the weather an out, as if not to hurt its feelings. The radio on the counter cracked, the storm warning read for the third time by a woman in Bismarck who could have been reading recipes for all the lilt in it. Blowing snow. Thirty-five miles an hour and gusting to fifty. Six inches to a foot. Higher amounts in the coulees. The words meant very little standing in a warm room where the heat came up through the floor and the sink breathed steam. They meant everything out where mouths came into the world and lost their heat just as quick.

“Put the second lantern in the sled,” I said, and filled the thermos to the top with the coffee I had left over from noon and poured hot water into a blue enamel pot to carry with us, lids clacking. I set the box of OB sleeves, lube, iodine, syringes, and the little stomach tube into the sled and then the muzzle, the chain, the bottle of powdered colostrum nobody had believed in until they needed it. The chains rattled louder when the house went quiet.

Owen looked at me when I pulled on Tom’s heavy mitts. They were dry on the inside from the radiator, the seals cracked in the palms. His look wasn’t anything sharp, just the usual way he had now of taking a measure in silence. He would have me in his reach if he could. He had fathered himself this year like a boy will do if the man goes and the work stays.

We shoved out the back door and the snow hit like a wall, then wrapped around me and flooded my throat with cold. The dog shot past, old Addie, dropping her nose immediately to the ground that wasn’t there anymore. She barked once at nothing and then stayed at my heel, one eye white in the lantern light. The drift had built up along the house to my hip and we were through it in a lunging step, pulling the old pony sled between us, and the wind took our hats and threw them back down again. There were inches caught in the collar of my coat by the time we reached the yard gate and I shook myself like a sheep at the walk-in.

The shed was warm by comparison, thick with the smell of wool and hay and iodine, and sweet-almost with the birth stink that coats a room when it’s been going all day. The ewes made a wall of patient bodies along the south side, the ones who had already lambed idling with their twins, horns clicking sometimes on the panel in slow conversation. I kept the first pen, what Tom always called a jug, nearest the door for the bad ones, where I wouldn’t have to look up and find my way. Those boards were mostly new, winter-improvised from pallets and the ribs of the old doors Tom had never gotten around to fixing.

He was everywhere and nowhere. The ledger with his notes leaned in among mine on the shelf, the dates he’d circled in pencil ghostly with rubbed graphite. His jacket hung from the nail by the light switch, the lime stains on the hem like map rivers I could trace now in the dark. There was hay snagged in the zipper. I told myself all the time I would take it down. Some days I put my hand on the shoulder and moved it an inch.

“We’ve got the spotted Suffolk in three,” Owen said, setting the lantern down where the glass would be out of the way of butting heads and swinging hooves. He had already been out here twice after lunch. “Bag’s out. Ten minutes, maybe more.” He was calm with it now. In September he could not say bag without the blush running up his throat, even after all his years with lamb buckets in spring.

The heat lamps were still orange in each corner, their light turned greasy by the air. It felt safe to see them lit. I heard the storm only as a throat noise under the roof, the tin banging in rhythm like a grandpa snoring. Out there it was the end of things; in here the lambs lunged at udders and slept with their mouths chalked up.

The Suffolk stood with her butt to the panel and her neck set like a woman in the grocery aisle who has decided she will not move for you. Her back legs made careful little cuts in the straw. She turned her head when I came in and her eye rolled white, then went brown and flat again. The bag came glossy between her lips, and there was a white foot wedged inside, the toenails yellow as corn. It was a small hoof, no hoof meant for dread yet, and that made me brave.

“Let’s get her here,” I said. “Tie her.” Owen moved around and took the nose with his gloved hand, a thing his father had done before him, his face just over the ewe’s face, talking to her like a pal until she blew and stood resigned. I looped the old lead around one of the boards and held her hips up against it as she dipped. The bag popped between her legs, a heavy spill of water and old blood and something lemon-sweet, and the hoof slid further and then went still. We had a head, a tongue out fat and sodden, one leg, and I could see the point of a knee on the wrong side, the second leg back.

“Lube,” I said. He squeezed the bottle into my hand, the gel cold and viscous, and I wanted my gloves and hated them for not letting me feel. You reach without thinking, and right away you know whether the bones are where God made them to be or if they are where you will have to live the rest of the night to get them to. I slid past the ring and up along the jaw, my hand following the line of one ear to the throat and then over the shoulder. The second leg was not fully back, not completely lost—stuck at the knee and bent like a jammed hinge.

“Hold her,” I said. Owen braced his knees against the panel and leaned his shoulder into her flank and took the nose tighter. I pushed, withdrew, adjusted the chain. We were clumsy, then we were not. I found the cannon bone and swept my fingers under it and pulled forward. The lamb came at the same time that the ewe who had been idling and chewing behind me decided she needed to be somewhere else fast and knocked my hip as she sprang. I swore, soft, and got the outside leg up and the head back into the curve and we were there. That first slide is always the mercy of gravity. The rest is will.

The lamb slipped out in a slick of red and lay very still on the straw, his neck too long, his head heavy and slack. “Come on, boy,” I said without meaning to say it out loud and wiped his nose and his mouth with the hem of my sleeve, rubbing the mucus away until I saw his nostrils. I pressed my fingers along his ribs and bent the chest and pumped, then rubbed again, rougher, his hair rolling under my palm in waves of wax. He was small, tighter than he should have been, and I could feel Owen holding his breath. The ewe pivoted and walked her back feet forward and bent down, all of it by script, and blew against his flank. Sometimes the wind outside and the wind in a ewe’s nose are the same, and this was one of those times. The lamb coughed, a small internal sound, and then began to breathe like a fish drinks water, and then he made a noise that wasn’t any animal’s, and I sat back on my heels and let my own air out. When he shuddered and tried to climb half a minute later I wanted to cry and I didn’t.

“Good lad,” Owen said, but he meant them both. He set the iodine cup to the belly and dipped the umbilical fat with his left hand, not spilling a drop, a habit he’d gotten from saving things the old way. If you keep the navels clean, you keep a lot from walking right into your barn.

“Twins?” he asked. He bent his head down close to the ewe’s hips to listen the way Tom always did, the old man theater of it. I slid my hand in again and found slicks of membrane and empty wetness and the soft scrapes of uterus, then a hard marble of nothing. “She’s done,” I said. “Well done.” I heard myself and heard him and, for a beat, felt the flinch of it.

The shed was a moving of lambs now. They were not pretty yet, not what they would be in a week when they’d stand in the doorway and jump sideways like skipped stones. They were gray and red and black in parts, limp-backed draggy when they slept, twitching with electricity stored up. Owen leaned his back against the panel and we watched the little Suffolk drop her back to make room for the rooting mouth, her head turned as far as she could place it to keep her eye on his back, adjusting a step forward, a step back. Some ewes will not even look. Some put one foot up like dancing.

“You okay?” Owen asked, soft now, because we were in the quiet between again. His cheeks were raw across the short shorn hair where his hat had rubbed, his lashes rimed. The sweat under his collar would turn against him when we went back out. I took the thermos from the sled and uncapped it and we drank the coffee that had turned brown and oily.

“I am,” I said. “You?”

He nodded. “You make it look easy.”

“Nothing easy about it,” I said. “You’re supposed to think it is when it goes right.”

“What about the Dorset in six?” he asked, and right then a bulb over our heads clicked. It was nothing, a tick of the filament, but the next second everything went and the orange lamps died like colored flies caught in a jar. The dim radio in the corner cut that one inch lower and then vanished, and what was left was the sound of the wind finding fewer obstacles between it and us. We sat in that new dark without sighing or swearing, because that was not going to help either.

“Lantern,” I said, and Owen had already moved toward it. He read the match in three strikes the way most people now swipe a phone. He stood with his hand around the glass to keep the first light from spilling into the eyes of the ewes, and we slid the lamp to the nail meant for Christmas tins and wrapped a wire around its neck to hold it there.

“We should try the generator,” he said. He said it the way he would say I should call Grandma tonight. It was a thing that must be done but known to possibly offend in the doing.

“We will,” I said. “Check six.” He went off and I stood for a second with my eyes closed and counted back heat lamps and corners and outlets and remembered which of the extension cords was chewed and which had been taped. I found the old wool blanket under the workbench and shook it out. I would rather a blanket with shit on it than one with holes.

Tom had built the animal box when he was twenty-three with wood he’d taken from the barn his father had pulled down the year before to make space for an Amish man’s hay. The box had a screen on one side and a slot for a hair dryer on the other and a method of slatting that made it possible for me to work with three little bodies in there and not kill any of them with heat in the first five minutes. We had used it for hunting dogs in later Octobers when nose and ears needed a pull of heat, and again a time I sat in there myself with my feet hanging out to get warm when I was ten days post-partum and stupid with fever. The dryer would be no good now. The lantern would warm it some and the blanket would hold it. The old ways nod to the older ways. I lined the box with wheat sacks and set the blanket in drifts like dunes and put a mason jar full of hot water we had brought from the kitchen into the corner like a small sun.

Owen was back, moving faster than usual. “She’s got feet,” he said. “Two. And they don’t match.”

“Show.” He took me, holding the lantern for me, and the ewe had her eye rolled to the ceiling like an old horse being gentle. Two feet showed, the left one small, the toes raw white. The right was big, and the hoof turned the other way, the dewclaws forward. One lamb coming forward, one coming with his hooves on the wrong side—breech, stubborn, or both.

“Not the same lamb,” I said, and slid my hand in to be sure. “We have one who thinks he’s on top.” I was speaking for him but also for Owen. If you say the thing out loud and in order it is as if you could master it. I could feel the skull of the forward lamb and the bucket of his cheeks, his mouth already yawning and closing against me, and then behind him the braced boards of the hips of the neighbor who thought he would swim upstream.

“I’m not going to like this,” I said, and smiled because that meant I might anyway.

“You want me to try?” Owen asked, and the way he said it made me want to sit down on the floor and just look at him and let it all tell itself. “You’ll be my other arm,” I said. “We work it slow.”

In stories this is where breath gets close and faces get fixed, but in a cold shed with an animal pushing and the air moving and your wrist going numb and your shoulder aching and your thigh burning from bracing against the board there is no big picture. There is only the one thing you need to do and the things you need to hold out of the way long enough, and also the things you need to say to the boy so his mind doesn’t fall down a well.

“We’re going to put the first one back a bit,” I said. “Just an inch. I swear to you, girl, it’s not personal.” The ewe snorted and then moaned, and you know when it is a pain sound and when it is a sound for company. “Push him back with your fist against his mouth—there,” I said to Owen, guiding his hand. “You feel that? He’ll take your knuckle like a teat. Don’t let him. Now hold that press. Don’t let him come. I have to get around this hip.”

He nodded once, the only moving he allowed his head. He had Tom’s hands, long and thin on the top and too broad in the palm to get certain wrenches started, and he had my wrists, right now smaller for going in. I slid along the curve of the breech lamb’s butt and found his hock, the little ball tight as if made new. You don’t pull a breech straight. You push it forward and bring a leg up, and then the other leg, and then you get him turned. You do it while a ewe is doing what she has been designed to do without your help and while her partner is doing his own effort to come into the world at the very minute you most wish he would wait. For a few heartbeats there were three of us with the same small wish, and we were all wrong to have it.

“Now,” I said to Owen, and he let the press go at the moment I had the first leg clear and the second half up, and the ewe did the rest with a spasm of muscle so old it might as well be stone moving. The breech lamb slid with such speed I didn’t catch him and he thumped to the straw, his back legs kicking once and then laying out. I caught the forward lamb under the jaw as he came at me and swung him in a low arc to get the fluid loose, and then the ewe turned her hips and set her feet and blew at both their faces in turn, and then laid her chin to the floor and let herself shake from one end to the other in relief. If you didn’t love the species before, you would then.

Owen took the small lamb to the animal box, his face in a fold of his shoulder like he could smell anything but iodine and a shuttered lantern. He rubbed him like a man starting a fire with his bare hands and when he saw me watching he raised his eyebrows as if to say, This is nothing to me, and I laughed without laughing. The other lamb was up and searching with the loose-legged urgency of a drunk. I reached under the ewe and checked her bag to be sure we wouldn’t end up killing one with kindness, and she knocked me with her knee and I took that on the chin as my due.

The power did not come back. The generator did not come at first try. It coughed, and the sputter I had feared was exactly the one that told me the carburetor had been left to its own varnishes last October and was now a small funeral. “I’ll pull the bowl,” Owen said, already unfastening the shroud with cold-numbed fingers, and I let him because his knowing would be the only fuel better than gasoline. I could hear Tom in the sibilance of his breath and I wanted to say—he would not care you left it—he would care you are fixing it—but saying anything was wrong in that weather. I went back to the mamas and their babies that had been easy and thought about what could be done for the ones that would not be.

The first little breech lamb in the box hiccoughed and then shook like a dog and sneezed out the longest string of slime I had seen, and Owen cheered once, with the incautious joy of childhood, and then looked to see if I had heard it and smiled not sorry at all to find that I had. He slid him out under the blanket to the ewe and the ewe licked his eyes like she was sweeping the barn with her tongue. He came up on his knuckles and then slid down and then pushed and pushed and held. I watched him think. When a lamb thinks, his entire body tries to become thought. He finally stuck his head under his brother and found the nipple with the tipped teeth and then lost it and then found it and clamped down and then made the sound of being suddenly full of liquid for the first time, which is a sound you can hear all the way into bad dreams later.

“That one’s going to be trouble,” Owen said, but he said it like a promise of fun.

The generator failed us again after good faith and WD-40 and a prayer. “Leave it,” I said finally. “It’s making you hate life. We’ll run the lamps off that panel if it comes on by itself.” I held his wrist for a second as he stood, feeling the cold in his bones there, and wanted a stove that could make a sound. Addie came back from wherever it is dogs go between chores and let me rub her head and blew a breath across my kneecap like an apology. The storm had not slackened; it simply had grown sure of itself.

We did not count the pens. We only counted what needed doing. The Dorset in nine had twins without our help while we were busy with the generator, both lambs born with faces like cartoons and legs too long. One was on her chest before we saw her and had already done a side step toward the udder, clever and wrong, and I turned him and set him under his mother’s belly and pressed the teat to the roof of his mouth and then tutted at him to make him pull. The other I took in my palm and rubbed and he peed a little and I wiped it off with the same palm because there are not enough clean surfaces in the world for lambs.

“Mark them,” I said to Owen, and he clicked the chalk and drew a line on the ewes’ rumps and on the lambs’ heads. He had his own order. He put a circle around the ones that had needed us, and a check on the ones who had not. He insisted on blue for singletons and orange for twins even though the colors ran like old paint.

“What number?” he asked. We had ear tags, and the spreadsheet, but we were not even that dear to our own record keeping any longer. “Later,” I said. “Count their legs.”

A ewe who’d lost a lamb two nights ago watched us from the far pen with a terrible kind of leisure. She’d been a poor mother to start and the lamb small, and although we had not been caught sleeping or caught proud or caught lazy, we had been caught. She was fine and the lamb not. I had not thrown a bucket and I had not cried and I had not broken my promise to myself not to say sorry aloud. There is no apology you can pin to the wall that matters. You only do better and then you know that better is loss waiting later with better knives.

Around eight the night settled, as if it had been sitting on the rail by our shed door and had only now slid down to the floor. My hands had gone through so many pairs of sleeves that the formica table by the windowsill looked like a wig if seen wrong. The old coffee cup I set the iodine in had a bite out of the rim from a winter when it had lived in the cab of the Ford and the wind had made a sound there like a man thinking. I had thought that winter was a hard one. The lamb count kept clicking. Owen ate half a peanut butter sandwich with one hand and tubed a lamb with the other, talk me through it, Mom, do I stop if I feel him cough? when do I stop? and I showed him the way the tube felt under the skin of the neck when it was in the right place, and how the lamb sighed when the milk hit his stomach like a first fire in a new house.

Sometime after nine the storm surged and all the cracks in the wall let in the fine sift that papers the world and turns it alien, and the top of my boot took on a little drift. We banked straw against the bottom of the pens as if to keep the water of the room in. A ewe with a half-black face hollered once and dropped two feet and then swore in sheep and then pushed so hard one of the panels rattled and a lamb came out on a froth like a beer poured too thick, the skin of the bubble so tight it held form, and I put my thumbnail to it and slit it and the lamb slid into the world. I had no words for that being beautiful and mean. He was born with a blue tongue. He pinked up while I watched but slowly and I slid him into the box and tucked the blanket up to his nose and kissed his head and did not tell myself who I was thinking when I did it. I could not carry two men tonight.

Around ten Owen took his hat off and wrung it out and then laughed at himself because you cannot wring a hat but he could. “Red hat,” he said to no one, and put it back on and went to fetch water. The pipes to the shed had not frozen because it was only spring, for all that this evening thought itself January, but the pressure was sulky and the spigot spit. He brought back the bucket and set it down by my boot and I looked at his wrists where his coat gave way and had a flash of hating everything this place had given us. The bucket moved like a small animal. He went back out and I saw his back go and come, go and come, carving a line through the drift so that by the tenth time he had a path you could have driven a calf.

Just before midnight we had the first stillborn. It was a triplet from a small ewe who had no business with thirds. The first two had come fine, breathing and gulping, and there was a still place under my palm when I reached back in for the third. The skin on his head felt loose. The ears were too small. He had been dead for longer than the weather had been weather. Owen stood with his hands in his jacket pockets and his face was the new face I had only seen twice, once on the hill in August and once at the funeral. He got down on his knees and bent, slowly, and put his hand to the lamb’s jaw and then slid it up and over his eye, closing him like a man in a movie. He had never done that. He had never seen me do it. It made me want to sit down again and put my face into the crook of the ewe’s leg and sleep.

“Skin him,” I said. “We’ll graft him onto the cull.” He looked at me and his mouth made a small movement I knew meant he would have words later, but he stood and got the knife and came back without asking why. You say the thing because it is time to say it and because the next thing that happens will be something else hard. We took the dead lamb to the little room at the back where we kept the shovels and the matted twine and the sacks, and I showed him how to peel a lamb in a line that made the least wrong of it, and he went at it careful and slow, and I made myself look at the ewe through the open spot in the wall. She stood and chewed cud. She was done with what she knew to be her business. I thought of a funeral director with a new apprentice and wanted to laugh and vomit at once.

Back at the pen with the skin on my arm like a wet shirt, I slid it over the foster lamb that had been banging his head against his mother’s knee for an hour to no avail. The fit is never so good as you want it to be, and the holes never line up perfect. Owen dipped the lamb’s hock in the leftover blood and crammed his nose under the slick and held him there, and the ewe lowered her head and took a breath and blew the way she had blown into the warm skin of her first two. His eyes rolled sideways with ecstasy at the smell of a mother who did not want him ten minutes before, and I felt a kind of hatred at how easy magic looks after you have done the blood work to make it. Owen saw me and did not know what he was seeing, and smiled that he had gotten to be the one who brought a miracle into a room with no electricity.

Tom had taught me to graft when I was twenty-three with hair past my waist and a body that had just learned how to bleed. He had said, Watch the mother’s mouth, and if she opens it when she licks, you’re halfway there. He had used a knife he had inherited from his father and his jokes had been older than his own father, and he had told me, If you can get a ewe to love a thing, you can get a cattleman to admit he was wrong, and that was his way of saying something could be done that had not been done before. I had thought him silly and then he had turned not to be. He had died in August on the hill where the pasture leans too far to look down from standing upright, and the baler had gone with him like a faithful thing. There had been no chance to say anything to anyone. Afterwards I had spent a week in the field of sunflowers at the north end and had my mouth open then too. It was the only body of his I had been able to be with later, the sun and the flowers making a kind of buzzing that ached at the edge of my hearing, and I had wanted to crawl into the stems and be under the heads and listen to the bees until the whole county was quiet.

Just before one in the morning the dog barked low and made a circle that looked like caution. The shape at the walk-in door was a man grown to a snowdrift, and the second I knew who he was he was inside and dropping the drift in a pile like we needed another one. “Brake froze on the feeder wagon and I thought I’d see,” he said, and then he saw the room with one eye open and the fact that we were working by lantern light. “It’s the goddamn end of the world and here you have a den,” he said, and laughed and then took his hat off and shook it and snow dust went all the way to the back.

“Dallas,” I said, and he put up his hands like a man who had just been caught stealing. He was our neighbor to the west, which in winter is a sentence. In summer he was a friend you forgot you had for weeks and then he would show up with a submersible pump in the back of his truck and a pie someone had baked without asking for blueberries you did not have. “Thought you might have called,” he said, but in a way that did not make the edge of it too sharp.

“The generator’s gone varnish,” Owen said, nearly proud, and Dallas made a low appreciative noise that said he had used that word too many times. “You get the wagon free?” I asked, and he shrugged. “I got myself into it, and then I got myself out of the driveway, and then I thought about sitting in the shop with the lights on and pretending the lambs don’t know the difference,” he said. “So I came to see someone else swear.”

“Get in,” I said, and he did what men like him do, which is tie his hands to someone else’s task with a rope of words. He moved among the pens the way a man moves in a house that is not his but has been a little, careful as if he was in church and did not want to bump a picture. He took a lamb in his palm and blew into its nose and rubbed its ears and his hands were already raw. He was more of a cow man, but he was one who had been made tender by things that did not care what you stamped on a tag.

“You see this?” he said after a while. He stood at the open spot by the workbench with the skim of snow coming in through the light cracks and held up the plug end of one of the long cords. “I had a cat chew this right off last winter,” he said, and the cord in his fist was nearly gnawed away. “Had to have been fall. That juice is out but you plug this then and there goes the cat. I suppose it’s a chance you take in worshiping things that hum.” He swallowed one of his own jokes and then laid the cord on the bench as if it were an animal he had found still breathing. “You want my heater?” he asked. “I can bring it if I can get her back started.”

“Don’t,” I said. “It’s a thing with bones that chews you back.” He nodded and rubbed the jaw of the Dorset that had wanted to kill him two years ago and had fallen in love with him after he’d pulled a dead lamb from her backside while I had stood on her neck. Love is a fluid. It dries and then the stain is there anyway.

He stayed an hour and I did not see the snow on him melt and then I did. He left when a lull in the wind made it possible to imagine a lane forming again in the world. “Put your knee into spring,” he said to Owen as a joke, meaning the generator, and my son smirked like a man who has a thing he will try against his mother’s advice.

After Dallas left a ewe opened like a yawning door and out slid a lamb so sharp and thin and long I thought he was an old lilac branch. He was dry before I got my hand to him and culled the air like a raptor. It is a thing you forget if you count only blessing on top of blessing that some creatures throw their legs straight before they know they have spines. He took his first feed like a soldier takes orders. I wanted to wrap him in Tom’s coat and call him something like Lieutenant and then didn’t. We had enough names in this shed without making one more name count a thing it could not carry.

Around two the power blinked so that it was the darkest moment of the night, which is when a thing you have no control of reminds you. We moved in the lantern ring like men in history and did not feel grand. My hands were cut across the knuckles by straw and wire and a tooth, and there was blood there and I licked it and tasted copper and iodine and sheep. I had not eaten since the peanut butter bite I had stolen off the corner of Owen’s sandwich and the taste of it was in my mouth like glue. We moved because the ewes kept coming. We moved because time was not leaving us alone.

At three a ewe threw her ear back and I saw the white of her eye hard as porcelain and I knew she was afraid of pain the way I know the dog is afraid of thunder. I put my palm flat against her side and felt the roundness of lamb under there and felt my own stomach seize. “Don’t,” I said to my own muscle. “This is not your story.” The lamb came backwards, or tried, a leg stuck and a hip lodged, and I could see the light through Owen’s fingers at the nails. He held the chain and I had my hand against the bag, and we sang to her in ugly crackers of voice. “Now,” I said, and we pulled and there was a give and then there was a drop and he was out, and he lay still, his tongue long, and I rubbed him as if I had something that would light inside him that was mine to give, and then I put my mouth around his nose and the whole shed fell away and the wind fell away and the dog went deaf and I blew into him like he was a chorus in a church I had never learned the words to. His chest moved and then did not. I set my hands and did compressions, thumb to finger on the place I had learned as a child, and his chest went in and out like he was a bellows with nothing to blow on, and then there was a small sound like a boy in his sleep who has had a dream that does not need to be told, and I knew he would live. I wiped my mouth on my sleeve and tasted his life, which is to say blood.

You can love a thing in a moment and then be required to love it for the rest of your life.

At four, the storm had the dignity to waver. The edge of the window showed not white but black behind the white, a shift that told you dawn would do what it always did and spread a blue in a stripe along the horizon like a bruise darkening. Owen had put his back to a panel and slid down without meaning to when he had finished tugging and I let him sit. I pulled my hat down over my eyebrows and tightened my scarf around my neck and stood looking at the row of heads and backs and hocks. The ones who had finished lay down and chewed and farted and looked at me like I was a person who had the nerve to imagine her hand in the thing. The little ones lay in the hollows of the straw like spoons with their own heat and then would sit up with a start and make one sound that said, I am here. I watched their sides and counted the rise. If the numbers got slow I kicked the panel with the edge of my boot just enough to make their mothers blink. You need love in quantity sometimes, and this shed had none to spare that wasn’t the ewes’ to give.

The radio clicked and the woman in Bismarck cleared her throat and I wanted her to be someone else. She read the weather with one degree of heartbreak less. Downgraded to advisory after ten. Roads impassable in rural areas. Plows will not run until daybreak. The old world was lifting her skirt to show us her knees and say, Look, I have bones, and I felt the old certainty lift in me. We could not leave. Nobody would come to us. The lambs would keep coming and then they would stop. Dawn made at least one thing human.

Owen had his head down on his shoulder but I could see his eyelids moving. He was in the stage that is sleep and not sleep, and his mouth was open a little and I remembered that his first nights home he would fall asleep with his mouth agape and cry a little and not know it. I had done then what I did now, which was put my hand on his chest without him knowing and count the rise. It is how you mark time if you do not have a clock. He woke without apology and stood with the set to his jaw that was his father’s improvement on his grandfather’s. “I can go again,” he said. “What do you need?”

“I need you to be,” I said, and he smirked at my having gone to college once and said something like that. “Start them on the bottle in twelve if they haven’t got it yet,” I said, more useful. “And the Merino has that look I don’t like of thinking about taking yours instead of hers.”

He went and I stood and avoided the old jacket on the nail for another ten minutes before I gave up. It was heavy and cold and smelled like summers I had wasted on the north road. I put it on and the sleeves came down over my gloves and I rolled them up. In the pocket there was a bit of bailing twine and a small stone, smooth as if it had been in a jar with others. It was a stone from the south pasture, from the place the creek galls in the heat, and I knew in a way that struck me by the sternum which boy had put it there and what year. He had held it in his palm and then dropped it in because pockets require filling. I wanted to swallow it and keep it in a place with fewer names.

Out beyond the small openings in the tin the snow had piled in drifts with white spines and in those drifts there were tiny avalanches. The fence lines had disappeared. The cottonwoods beyond the house the color of wolves’ backs. The cattle would be turned into their mangers, chewing their cigarettes of hay, and the horse would be stamping one hoof like a clock. The chickens would be pressed together in a heap on the roost like a sentence of punctuation marks. The world slept in its coats.

The last ewe before dawn came with a clouded bag and a trickle of blood that wasn’t like the others and I felt in my back a lift of worry. “Lazy ring?” I asked of no one. Some ewes do not open. We call it ringwomb like a joke, as if it is a jewelry decision. It’s a narrowness like a purse drawstring that refuses your hand and then the lamb’s head bats at it and makes it tighten. I slid my fingers in and could not get past. I cussed once, the one I do not say in front of any but the dogs, and breathed through my nose like a sheep in a cartoon. “She’s not giving me much,” I said. Owen came to my elbow and held my wrist in his palm for a second. “I can fetch Dallas,” he said, and we both laughed and then let it die.

“You be the ring,” I said, and he nodded. I had him press where I pointed and he held until his fingers shook and then held more. I oiled my hand and slid it and pressed with my knuckle and worked a slow miracle that was more biology than any religion you could name. The ewe panted and then made a cross noise and I stopped and counted to ten with my teeth on my tongue and began again. We did it all while the window behind us slowly turned a paler gray. When my arm was in to the elbow and my eyes had water in them entirely from the sting of iodine I felt the ring give a quarter inch and then half. “There,” I said to him, my voice winter small, and he said, “There,” like an echo, and then the lamb’s head came through and we were out of a bad story and into a better one, and when I put him down he breathed at once, a good breath like a man in a movie after he has been underwater.

“If we had a vet,” Owen said, after, in the tone that meant he was not going to say anything about the absence of further, and then he buttoned his mouth. “We were it,” I said. “If we had it, we used it.”

Dawn arrived broke as a man I had known once who had owned five dogs and only one good coat. It made a blue on the straw. It made the corners of the shed less like places you tell stories about. The ewes seemed to stand taller. The dog found a knot in the floor and worried it. The wind made one more bluff and then went somewhere to talk about how it had done. We moved more slowly because we could. The lambs had pale ears in the new light, thin as boiled onion, and they quivered like flags on their heads soon.

Owen took a lamb that had gone cold even under his mother and pushed him under his coat and held him against the soft wedge of himself and walked with him. The lamb’s eye rolled and then settled. The fleece on his head had dried into curls like a girl from a poster in a barber’s shop. “You name that one,” I said, and he shook his head. “No,” he said. “It’ll make me goofy.” He was right. We were goofy enough.

By seven the edges of the world were given back and the radio said that the county plows had decided to test their god. A man I had not known from school but had bought hay from once, whose wife had green eyes like leaves in a magazine and a braid the thickness of spring rope, came by on his snowmobile and waved, standing in the yard like a man in a postcard, and then kept going because that is what men on snowmobiles do. The world was again something you could go through, even if you would be brought back.

In the slack that followed we gathered the gloves and the used sleeves and the empty cup of iodine and the luer locks. I swept with the old broom, pushing the bands of wool up against the panels, the sound of it the sound of a hundred small things saved. Owen made piles with a fork where the straw had gone to heavy and sprinkled lime. He took a bottle and a lamb took the bottle, chuffing and blowing milk out his nose, and he laughed and said, “Pig,” and the lamb walked back into his own life and we watched him and let him go.

The lights did not come back but the daylight made them unnecessary. The lantern went out with a last giggle and the smoke of it rose like a loose hair. I poured what coffee was left into the old cup and we took turns drinking it like it was something you shared at a fire. I put my hand on the jacket I had on and this time instead of twitching away I held it and set it to my own heart and pressed. I would take it down someday and put it in a box and curse myself for waiting too long and not waiting long enough. Not this morning.

The ledger on the shelf looked like an old face. We were behind in the writing of it. We were not behind in the work. “I’ll put it down later,” I said out loud as if the pages cared, and then I did, just once, and wrote in my own hand on the top line: 4/28, Suffolk, single, both, good, and signed it the way Tom used to, with a T like a cross that made a word of itself. I would let my handwriting pretend that I had married not a man but a flat confidence.

Owen had straw across his back like hay seed and I picked it off the way a mother does. He stood without moving and let me, and closed his eyes as if he could be sleeping at the same time. “You go in and sleep,” I said. “Eat a real thing. Tell the stove to make soup.” He shook his head and then shook it again, stubborn and then giving. “You want me to,” he said, not asking. “I want you to keep,” I said. “You go and you rest and you come back and you are still here.”

He went, then, in that jerky half-run he had had since he was four and had scraped both knees on the ice and had had to explain to me with his whole body that he was going to be fine. He put his hand on the dog’s head and the dog closed one eye and let him do it and then he put his hand on the post by the door and shook the snow off his boots and looked back once and then did not look again. The wind took him and made him a figure and made him a boy and made him a man and set him down.

I stood and listened to the ewe sounds and the lamb sounds and my own heart and counted. There were bodies beyond the panels who had not asked to be given anything and had eaten without asking to be given and then slept where they fell and would get up later and find new heat. The world lifted her white and showed the brown she had been boring into all winter. We had got through the night when it wished us worse. There would be a time later when the day would want something and we would be tired and say no and then yes and then find that we could not say anything but the right thing. For now this was enough.

The little breech lamb, the stubborn one, the one we had half warmed in a box made thirty years ago, walked to the edge of the pen and put his nose through the wire and blew at my thigh. He had one blue eye and one brown, which meant nothing at all and everything, and I blew back gently and he jumped sideways like a fish. “You’re not ours,” I said to him, which was stupid and exactly wrong in its words and meaning, and then I reached and took his head and kissed the spot between his eyes, iodine and salt and the lanolin-smell like a hand me down, and did not let go right away. Outside the wind carried the last of the storm away with a flounce like a girl leaving town. In the house my son would be dropping his boots on the mat and the sound would be a whole new thing I had never heard before, because this morning he had done work with me that we had not done together, and that changes how all sounds in a house can be.

When the door banged, from the wind or a neighbor or a ghost or the stove, I did not jump. We had done so much and we had not done anything extraordinary at all. The ewe put her chin along the back of her lamb and it was as if the night had never happened and as if it had and it would be always in the bone of the wall where snow had sifted in and later would blow out in dust on a day in July when the light would be a different kind of pitiless. I picked up the old broom again and swept in slow lines, not because the shed needed sweeping but because my body needed to know what to do when it did not have a live thing in its hands. With each stroke I thought of Tom saying, You make it right in little ways and the big ways come along after, and of my own father who had said nothing and left when I was eight, and of my son walking into a kitchen in a coat with his father’s smell in it that had become his smell now, and of the lambs who had made mouths under my fingers and then had made their way down to the floor and up to their feet again because that is what they were built to do unequaled, and of the wind that had given up being cruel and gone back to being a motion, nameless again.

When the sun burned a hole in the cloud and the light came through and made a bright coin on the far wall I put my hand into it as if to test its quality, and then I laughed at myself because it was light and it was warm and it was what it had always been. When you have been in the dark a while you forgive what is ordinary very hard. Standing there, with my fingers just at the edge of the warm, I told it aloud, Not today. I will not be clever today. I will be only what is required and what is kind. The lamb closest to me sneezed and then looked offended and then forgot and went to sleep. The ewe closest to me put one black hoof up on the rail and stood like a ship with its plank down. Somewhere in the house a kettle hissed. The day opened.

There is a way to lift a lamb that does not startle his mother and does not slip the slime back onto his face. There is a way to touch a boy’s coat when he isn’t there that does not make him smaller in the world or make you imagine him in the places his father could stand. There is a way to push your arm up to your elbow into darkness and find not only a small skull where you thought there would be a wall but also your own hand, moving right where there is grief that is not, right where there is the second ring that gives when pressed. I do not care anymore about lessons. I do not care about the ribbon you get later for pushing the right way now. But this morning, after the April blizzard, after the night with lanterns and no warmed lamps and the generator that did not even lie well when it died, after the neighbor and his joke and the boys with their coats and the dog who is old and sleeps by my left boot and the old jacket that still has hay seeds caught in the zipper and the ledgers with their T’s like small crosses, after the ewe and the lamb and the other lamb and the other—this morning I cared only about this: that the smell was of it all, sweet and rank and so alive it felt like a second air—and that when the next ewe lay down and groaned and looked back and made her eyes hollow and the bag showed and the little white feet slid, there was enough warm in the room and enough heat in our means and enough of us, me and my boy, to make that work as if no man had left with a machine up the hill last August and the ravine had gulped him. There was enough.

Out beyond the open yard the sun shouldered up, as if tired of us and our small lamps, and I laughed at it a little, quiet, for coming late. I put my hand to my sternum like a woman who wears a medal and felt the beat in there that still sometimes surprised me with its insistence, and then I reached for the old coffee and found it nearly warm where the lantern had been, and took a sip, and made a face like Owen does and then shrugged and drank the rest. And then I got back to it. The ewes keep coming. They come whether the lights are on or not. We learn what to put our hands into and what to let lie. The day keeps bringing, and we keep being brought. That is the order. That is grace, or if it is not, it is the warm side of something in a place with so much cold. I bent to another horn, and the ewe put her head into my palm like she had done it every spring of my life. And though it was not true, though it could not be, though nothing was that easy, it felt true in that minute, and I let it be. I did not look up when I heard the footsteps at the door. I did not say the old name. I only said, in my best talking-to-ewe voice, almost without words, There you are; there, and look at you; look.
