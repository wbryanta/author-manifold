The smoke rose like a question I had been avoiding all summer, thin at first, then thickening into a column with shoulders. It did not boil like the hot script of a full torching, but it didn’t feather, either; it made a fist and kept it, lifting into the still noon as if every particle could not quite decide whether to fall back to its source or flee. I had the firefinder ringed to my map and my forehead pressed to the cool brass sighting bar before I understood where it sat—three degrees left of Granite Notch, over the saddle, between the black slash of last year’s burn and the green tangle of alder that let out onto a flat. The drainage where my sister kept her cabin made a blunt V that you could trace with your finger, knuckles pressed to the paper. I did not need to turn the map or read the grid numbers to know it—the long spoon of the creek that ran brown in August, the retaining wall of stacked rock our father taught us to build before he forgot how to instruct anyone in anything but his own bitterness. The columbine place. The mosquito hell. The road that scribed eight miles down and back up through cutbank and switchback, hairpins like the curl of an unplucked hair on a sink.

I called dispatch out of habit and out of hope. “Lookout six to base, smoke in the west quadrant, possible start near Alder Drainage, do you copy?” The old radio cleared its throat and died. It wasn’t even mine. It had belonged to a time when someone would have replaced the battery before it also remembered its own obsolescence and sat back, shoulders slumping, to watch whatever happened. The tower had been decommissioned two summers before for budget, for concatenated reasons couched in gentle language in emails that used my first name as if we were all on a softball team together. I had argued for one more season with my voice small in a room where voices were not supposed to be small. They let me have my last season the way fathers let their kids drive the truck around the field, hands always near the wheel. The solar panel had been taken. The stove door rattled when the wind came up. The leveling jacks had rusted into the pad. I pretended the old gray paint peeling from the legs of the tower took the shape of states we had never been to, as if the creases and the flake might instruct me how to leave.

I called again, on the other frequency, on the handheld with tape around the antenna. Silence is a presence in places like this; it sits with you like an old friend, feet on the rail, not needing to speak. That day it did not sit. It paced. It laid its hand on my shoulder and tried to turn me. I could feel it in my teeth.

Beyond the window the ridges breathed in their hot way. No wind up high. A small breeze might be skittering the leaves down there along the creek, a cross-draft in the notch bending the column to the right, then to the left. I watched. In training one old guy had told me it is important to let a fire declare itself: color, shape, rate. White smoke can be steam, green a sign of live fuel, black the breath of man-made things. This was gray that leaned to the dun and the brown, a beginning shrug of duff and fir needle and dry grass. No jets, no helos; even if there had been I would not have heard them over the thick of my heartbeat.

I could have told myself it was logging slash, or an idiot lighting a trash pile, or a lightning strike far from human construction. I could have let it be a thing without a name until someone else went and named it. But the drainage was no one else’s responsibility inside me. I traced again, the way a mother might trace the cheek of a child in a photograph, the cut of the creek from the notch through the glade, the small bench with its knotted stump, the bent aspen, the cabin tucked on the north lip where the sun could not bake it until days like this. Sylvie had put it there because the old place rotted out on the south face had broken our father’s back and his heart in equal measure. “North,” she had said, thirteen years old and angry, “everything lives longer on the north.”

The smoke column moved with the interplay of slope and convection, not fast, not quick, but steadily, the kind of pace a hard conversation assumes when you both have agreed to stop walking out of the room. I put both hands flat on the map table and the wood was warm as a living thing. The chair had a habit of catching me behind the knee on its whittled-down bolt heads; I did not notice. There were beetles killing the whitebark below us and the sap they pushed out against them winked in late light like pearls on some rich aunt. The idea that I should have seen the beetles sooner, that I could have gone out with a can of miracle and sprayed each tree by hand, sat in that part of my brain where grief goes to stretch and lash its tail.

She would have been at the cabin. She did not leave in summer. There was a running joke that she didn’t leave in winter, either, but that was only half true. She came down for salt and gas and the radio tubes she still thought could be replaced. The kind of person who would keep a cabin in a drainage where the road was sane only on maps and the culverts sighed in dry days because the weight of water had run from them. The kind of person who would not forgive a sister who had left, first to school and then to whatever you call the never quite returning.

Sylvie had when we were children a way of walking into fire that was half heedlessness and half knowledge. You could not distinguish the two until afterward, when she turned to you with her hair singed at the tips and said, casually, see? When we burned the slash pile up by the old orchard, she had jumped the hearts of flame to the safe ground without looking. When I got my first job up here, she said nothing other than to bring me a cheap lighter and set it on the shelf under the south window, as if what I might need from her was the means to begin a blaze on purpose. There were people in town whose lives she improved by hard quiet degrees—a fender hammered back into shape in someone’s yard, a cord of wood stacked for a widow who did not know who had left it, a stray cat tamed and docked of its burrs—but not mine. It was as if she had decided that the only person I should be to her was the sister who had failed.

What was the fight about? They always reduce, when you tell them, to money or men or who unfolded a certain sheet wrong. Ours was about the cabin, but also about the body that had died in a bed that looked like a cabin. Our mother had been brief and beautiful and then long and tired. Sylvie stayed. I sent money and then finally myself back when it could be done only with the rigor of someone who has not loved enough. She stood in the kitchen and did not look at me, and when the body was in its hospice clothes and the iris in the jam jar turned its face to the light, she said, “You want to sell the place.” I had said something that was honest and vile in one: “I want to not be ruined by it.” She opened the back door like she was letting smoke out, and I followed her onto the porch, and there was the creek making its small talk. “You left,” she said. “You liked it. You like leaving.” I said I had gone because the only way to make a thing in a place like this was to not have to ride your father’s name like a borrowed horse. She said, “If you can’t use a borrowed horse you’ll never go far,” and smiled a smile that made clear the smile was the knife. That winter she moved what black money and odd jobs would allow and put up the shell of a new cabin with her friend Luis from high school. She sent me a picture torn from a phone and mailed like a postcard, so that the distance would be required to do some of the speaking. It showed snow on a roofline that did not know age yet. Back off, the picture said.

The smoke column lifted another story. I saw birds make for the shoulder. A hawk hung like an abandoned handkerchief in the stillness. If I did nothing, the fire would make its declaration without me and I would become—what? The woman who sent a letter too late. The lookout who did not look out.

I couldn’t raise base. I could not will the wind to shift or the fuel to green up. I could go down. I could go down and be on that road in twenty minutes if I ran the stairs and did not meet a broken strike of wood on the wrong tread. Eight miles in heat, but not impossible heat. I was keeping an emergency cache in the locker under the bed: two liters of water, fire shelter, bandana, old Nomex that had been my lover’s before he left for a different kind of weather. There were triangular stone chips on the floor from where I knapped a half-baked attempt at learning the land’s ancient arts because an old man at the historical society had said, genially, you look like someone who might need to hit something with a rock. There was a list tacked to the post with my scavenged nails: sweep, check guy wires, count mosquitoes on the window screen for insane entertainment. There was a note from the kid who ran the dump that delivery of my mail would be “whenever,” and a second note that simply said, in someone’s careful block capitals, appreciate you. I had come here in June swearing to sit like a monk and make peace with my own uselessness. July broke records for heat we did not celebrate. August had been waiting, apparently, for this.

The door stuck because the jamb had twisted. When you unhitched the latch it made a sound like a throat clearing. The stairs down from the cab had a give in the fourth leg where a lightning strike had spoken to the tower in a winter thunderstorm. I’d sandbagged around the base in May in a lazy hour when the bugs were stupored by cold, and I wished briefly for those cold bugs and for the idiot luxury of boredom. The inside of my mouth had gone a little dry. Down on the catwalk the whole country was spread like a seam ripper had been through it. You could say I thought about calling someone else—Gray Butte, East Point—but their towers had fallen to welders and interstate lovers who bought their bolts for art. I knew who had them now: the man in town who collected angels and old farm grates would put a cabin on his barn made of six such towers, and you could sleep up there for a price while your dog ran in his lavender. The consolation was the light. It made the fiberglass insulators on the phone line wink like fish.

I went back for the bag. This is the decision point, people say later, if there is a later. I did not have children. I could go to a fire like someone goes to mass because it mentions nothing about them specifically. I did have a sister in a bowl of land that could gather light and then the absence of light until it concentrated in the wrong place. I slung the pack and stood in my socks for a moment, listening to the sound the tower made in heat—boards easing their small complaints, a nail head rising fractionally like a mole on skin. When I was a kid and scared to sleep I would go to my mother’s room and lie on the rug and count those noises in the house we built around us without knowing where we would go when it was gone. She would let me touch the hem of her tunic, the simplest mercy.

The eight miles were in my legs the way old routes live in you; you could wake me from a dead sleep and say, turn right at the stump that looks like a man, and I could do that. The road under me was powder. It got into my boots and mated with the sweat and made a paste that pulled skin from heel with a quiet logic. There was a waterbar where we always jumped the bikes, and a cutbank with its gravel face and desiccated lichens from the time the county sprayed and the spray washed down in an ugly rain. Two swallows had taped their nest to the eave of the pit toilet at the bend; gone now, or fledged, but their feathers had left the faintest ghost on the wood. In late June a mountain lion had left her print in the mud by the seep and I had placed my own palm in it and thought about the way we hold in our heads a schema for the people we love and then let it stand for them when they have put themselves elsewhere. She was not who I thought she was; I was not who I had sworn to be.

At the third switchback the road losing half its width to a slide forced me to step over light where ground had been. I like fear when it is useful; it hones. This was not useful fear but it sang near the useful kind and it made me want to keep a hand always on something that would hold me. I made the turn and a gust of wind came up from the trough as if the country had exhaled for the first time all day. My hat tried to fly; I fetched it. The smell reached me. It was not the pleasant campfire smell of childhood lies; it was the layered stink of a house you forgot to visit too long. Resin and mold burned like old grudges. It had the tang of paint, or perhaps of some solvent spilled on the dirt, or maybe that was memory putting its thumb into the page.

I thought of Sylvie with her hair in a crooked knot, not using the clips I sent because plastic tried the patience of her fingers, taking it down when it snagged a branch, letting it fall long and dark and shot through with the odd white thread she would pluck like a surprise burr and lay on the table, as if to count. She had refused in her plain-chinned way to take on the narrative of self-sacrifice offered to every rural woman like a coat pressed into her hands at the door—you’ll be cold out there, they said, you’ll want this one against your skin. She did not want to be a heroin, either, with the extra syllable of swagger that implies, as if the person who named you had stepped in something. She wanted to fix carburetors and lay in gardens and not be told. The men who came around her wanted to be improved by proximity without having to cooperate. Our father would have liked to make of her the son he did not feel he had; she declined. With me he tried to requisition my mind. I bucked out and took my mind with me and called this independence. Sylvie stayed and called that resistance and then refused to call it anything else again. I was the one who named and shaped and arranged, because names and shapes and arrangements had been how I left. The argument we had about the cabin was so only in the way that a last straw is only some grass.

Halfway down there is a spring, a pipe jammed into a seep with a trickle even in bad years. I stopped. The shade was a single, trembling plane. The water tasted of steel and stone, which is another way of saying it tasted like honesty. People tied goods they could not afford to lose under that pipe in fall to keep late season cold on them. The raccoons happily undid the knots. I poured a capful over my head and it ran into my left eye and made it sting. The cigarette burns on the wood from when boys courted girls here and girls feigned mild disdain but moved their knees together like someone who had already learned to ride a bicycle and gone off the road. There is a kind of passivity we celebrate in men who call themselves stoic where in women it is called a failing. Sylvie had rejected both. I could not decide whether I admired her for not playing or resented her because she would not play with me.

Above the spring the smoke twisted. It had found pockets of dry, the way pain finds its old tracks in a body and marches them like returning soldiers. I could hear now a sound like far water. In some ways we live our lives walking toward sounds we can name. The pop and shudder of sapwood being asked to do too much is a horrid small joy; it hurts and it delights you with its straightforwardness. I took off at a more deliberate pace. There is an incline two miles on where the cheatgrass made the whole slope a fawn pelt. Its seed would be in my socks by evening. The lodgepoles did their spindly act and the jackstraw of an old small blowdown offered chances to twist an ankle I did not want.

If it was in the draw I feared, the drainage would act like a flue. The breeze had a habit of rising up that bowl around midafternoon and pouring downhill just enough to lick an ember into more than the capacity of a boot to beat out. We had done that, as kids. When I was eleven and she nine we had grabbed frayed gunny sacks and wet them and run a line along the ditch with my father behind us stamping and swearing and laughing as if his mouth were three instruments at once. We had sung a nonsense song to keep time and the fire front had moved slow as dignity. He had called us his crew. That was the night he told us the story of his father paddling a canoe in runoff high enough to use the porch steps as a dock. It was also the night his friend came to the door and said: you owe. My father always had someone at the door.

The decision points in a day are not marked. You cannot retroactively put a flag on the moment you should have turned left and instead turned down. In the years I lived in places with elevators I would stand and watch the floors scroll by and feel a certain relief that no one expected me to scale anything vertical that did not come with a counterweight. Up here, everything had weight and counterweight and everything came with the penalty of gravity. At the third mile my calves fired off their little bright mouths and I did not answer. A bundle of wire lay where a pole had died and fallen. The insulator like a blue gumdrop glowed in the dirt. Up-slope someone had piled rocks to spell a name that I did not know and would not be allowed to learn because the forest would tip the syllables back toward soil as it always does.

People think about God on a road like this or they do not. I think about sisters. I had, when we were small, the mixed misery and happiness of being loved in the alternating current of her attention. She could be just and then capricious, careful and then feral. If we are talking honestly, I had not wanted to be bound to one person in quite that way. Marriage to me felt like consenting to be observed by a hawk even when the hawk was not there. When my one lover who loved me the simple way asked me to return east with him, I stayed on a fire my body had not needed to be assigned to, as if proximity to a thing that made sense to me in a visceral way could swap for proximity to a person who might make sense in every other. This is what you should not do with love: make of it an equivalency problem. He left a sweater here once that smelled like the petroleum ghost of chainsaw bar oil and I put it on in January and thought, I can live alone in any case.

At the place where the road leaps the creek on a culvert forced there by a drunk with a backhoe, there is a little bend where the world insists on lushness for the sake of being contrary. The alders huddle, the dogwoods put out little flags like yes still, the thimbleberries guard their rubied smallness with spider’s nets. I stepped there and smelled the boom of the smoke much stronger, as if the country had been whispering and then turned its head and sang. There is a bend beyond where you can make eye on the mouth of the rock chute; you can see, between trunks, the line of the cabin’s roof in winter and the flash of tinfoil around the trunks we had wrapped foolishly against deer. I had been to that porch once since she made it hers. I had stood on the top step and not lifted my hand to the door and then taken two steps back because to go in or not go in would be to declare something I could not say.

The first sight I had of the fire itself was like seeing a person in line you have not decided whether to greet. It burned low through the duff beneath the fir where needles had made their prayer shawls and fox scat registered the history of small lives. It had found punky wood and drunk. It had blacked a thumb of hillside like someone had put on too much mascara and then cried. It had stuttered at a ditch line and then found something it liked about jumping. Its leading edge was a creep, not a march. Up a log’s punk and down again, licking around the base of a stump like a lover you will try to forget. It had a catechism. It had a whispering. It had not yet crowned.

The cabin was not visible from where I stood because the angle hid it, the way the truth hides itself in your life by stepping left. But I knew it lay three hundred yards down and twenty degrees east, tucked to the base of a rock that gave you a place to sit in autumn and watch the light get honey in a jar. There were fruit trees a person with more money would have fenced from deer; there was a patch of dill that reseeded itself in its obstinate way, and blue enamel pots on the steps because she liked the weight of them. I moved downslope and the dust caved under my boots and let out that mushroom smell parched places make when you hurt them.

When I saw her it was the back of her and I knew her from my hairline to my ankles. She was walking the line of the garden with the shit-slow logic of someone making triage and hating the word triage. She had a Pulaski over one shoulder, its ax end bright from use and love. She had a five-gallon bucket in the other hand and a blue tarp rolled at her hip. She had her shirt knotted at the stomach and her throat was the brown of someone who had come to some kind of terms with her sun. She put the bucket down and I watched her tie the tarp to a stake and make a sort of skirt around the first apple tree as if any one kindness done in time would be better than all the large gestures we had ever made too late. She had a bandana around her wrist and no hat, which was very like her. She turned to reach for the second stake and saw me.

If you have siblings you know that the first expression is the true one. There was not joy or anger. There was recalibration, an instrument gauging pressure, and then something like a levity she did not let me hold on to. She looked back over her shoulder at the fire front and then down at me again, like choosing seats on a bus. She said, with studied calm, “Well. Hell.”

I stepped to her and the smoke put its thumb under my chin and tipped my head. “Radio didn’t answer,” I said, as if this explained everything. As if all uncommunicated days could be hung on that hook.

“I know,” she said. “Lines have been down two days down in the drainage. I sent Ricky out to call but you know Ricky.”

I did not know Ricky but I nodded as if I did. I realized in a foolish second that this is what I had been missing. To say the name of a person in her immediate life and make it sound to both of us like that life had anything to do with me. Her lips were dry. She had a crack at the corner of her mouth that bled when she smiled in winter.

“I saw the smoke,” I said.

She stepped past me, leaned, shouldered me in that old way that says, move. “You always saw the smoke,” she said. “You just didn’t always head toward it.” It was not quite a knife. It was something you use in the kitchen daily and stop noticing until someone puts it in your hand cold.

We set the tarp around the next tree. She untied knots with the eyes of the loop away from her because she had learned early and by force. I was always the steady fingers; she was the impatient teeth. When you meet in middle age you invent new work for your hands because the old jobs—braiding each other’s hair, taking mittens off and on for one another at the bus stop—are too tender to bear. We scuffled duff away from the base of each trunk, a black ring where dirt gave breath to wood. Sparks fell like punctuation. “Main line is at the bench,” she said. “If it takes the alder run you won’t be able to do much about it. But it might not. It might eat around and be satisfied with stupidness.”

We had both in our bones the knowledge of the way uphill and downhill work. I tamped a heel on a punky log and it exhaled embers in a slow blessing. “The wind is—” I began, but she’s been paying attention longer than any of us.

“Yeah,” she said. “It’s nothing and it’s changing. Yesterday it thought it was moving north, but today it can’t be bothered.” There was a humor there so dry it would splinter under its own weight, and we both knew it.

Behind us the cabin’s door stood open, an animal thing like a mouth awaiting food. On the steps her mug top half chipped made an O. There were potatoes in a colander in the kitchen sink and a shawl with moth holes on a chair from when the nights still thought to be cold. Her boots were by the door, a second pair at half-cock behind them like a dancer you clapped for with one hand because you were holding something in the other. A man’s jacket hung on a nail, unconsidered. I will not tell you that seeing that jacket felt like any of the things you imagine. It felt like a practical object that would flame if asked.

“Where is your shelter?” I asked.

She snorted. “Under the bed,” she said, “with the dust and the things that came before. Where’s yours?”

“Pack,” I said.

“You always were…” She trailed it there, because we had both agreed in that instant, without agreement, that the words would be small while our hands were busy.

We worked. There is a rhythm that repeats, no matter what you’re doing together. Her job sometimes was to pull and ask me to release. Mine was to hold and tell her when to let go. We rolled the hose out. The water pressure at the spigot was a vanity and then it did something, a fan that made our knees wet. We watered a line and watched the dirt turn the color of a lie you cannot tell anymore and left our footprints thick in it. There is a modest joy in these small. The fire, for its part, accepted our offerings the way a strong old aunt takes the plate you’ve made her for the sake of the day. It grew in the other places and crept in the notches of root in which we had no purchase. We could not love it into submission and neither could we let distance hold when the proximate thing was this.

“You could have called,” she said, almost neutrally, when we crouched to lever a stone from the base of the stairs because stones hold heat and this one held too much.

“You could have answered,” I said, no inflection, as if we were rehearsing a school play.

“Had my hands,” she said. “In a thing.”

“We all had our hands in a thing,” I said, and the words tasted like bread left too long out.

“Yes,” she said, prompted to a smile that was a wound. “But mine weren’t clean.”

This is the work: We chose some words and left most. We saved the apples and watched the west line flare up into fire altogether less polite. She darted into the cabin and came out with a wool blanket which she wetted and draped on the porch steps and said without saying, if it comes through here we’ll put that in our mouths and hit the ground like sisters.

At one point when our throats required, we went inside. The room held the opposite of smoke: all the kept times. There was a pot on the stove you fed with skill or else it sulked. There was the radio with its orange shell and a knob missing. There was a note tacked to the wall that said in her handwriting, Luis, stop leaving your goddam socks under the chair or I will burn them with great ceremony. And below it in a different, taller hand: you don’t want a ceremony you cannot stop. God is a sock under a chair in August. It told me more than she would have told me if asked.

She did not say, I thought you would pick Boston and then you did. I did not say, I thought you would pick me and then you didn’t. She handed me a glass with water like the sky in it and I held it against my forehead and then drank and in that simple exchange some part of a truce invisible to us in other hours signed itself affixed. Not a forgiveness; certainly not an absolution. Perhaps a knowledge that neither of us had been strong in the places we had longed to be, and that we did not owe those strengths to one another if it meant we would break something else.

Outside the afternoon had blued up, that thing that happens when smoke and light make a joint decision against normality. Ash began. It does not look like what we draw as children. It looks like feathers from a very small bird. It lands on skin and you blow it away as if it were a message that you can attend to later. “You remember the squirrel?” she asked suddenly. My breath was winded; I hate when you’re winded while you want to remember a thing. “Which,” I said, to buy that breath.

“The one you tried to save and Dad had to tell you you can’t save a squirrel that has already decided to die?” She turned her wrist in the air as if there were a smoke rope in it, coiling. “You made a nest of his old scarf. You were mad at me because I told you to bury it. You said I was mean and I said, I am busy.” She looked off at the west flank where a snag torched and gave a pillar of black to a sky that did not require it. “I was ten,” she said. “Maybe I was mean. But I could not do both, not that day.”

I said, because we were allowed some liens against the past so long as they did not accrue too much interest, “You could not be a mother and a kid.” She laughed in that helpless way. “And you could not be a sister and a woman at once,” she said. “It’s okay. The world is not designed for boths.”

The fire stepped down. It made a lunge at the ditch and then thought better of its own imbalance and sent a scouting party toward the alders. We went there and kicked back stone to mineral and felt in our shins the heat that says you will not want to be standing here in another minute. She touched my arm and I did not think it was only to move me aside. Far off a siren shaped itself and unspooled like a ribbon on a fair day; it might have been at the highway, it might have been at some other sadness. That a siren would try to make its case against wind and mountain felt both silly and like religion.

“I told myself I would not call you unless I had to,” she said, like a person confesses on a Tuesday afternoon to someone who cannot use it against her. “I told myself you would come up here when you wanted.”

“I never want to do anything before more than one part of me wants it,” I said. “It takes a while.”

“You always were a coalition,” she said.

“Whereas you are a—what—Republic of One.” It had the old fun in it, a muscle memory.

“That makes me sound like a pitied country with no currency,” she said, grinning despite it. “I prefer island dictatorship.”

We held the line around the trees with all the small stupid joys of work done properly within the pretense that human hands hold very much at all. She went at one point to unroll a length of fence from the side yard. The fire made a run like a dog across a square; it sat back and licked its paws. The our of us was however old we were that summer we strung tin can lids on twine across the beans because the deer learned to open the gate with their clever tongues. We found laughter the way you sometimes find a lost coin in a pants pocket, astonished at only a quarter. It paid for something necessary: the hour, perhaps. The sight of her beside me. The way the afternoon was bright and then not bright and then as if lit from the wrong direction.

At some point we had done enough that the house would not go without a fight. The bench above blackened. The alder leaves turned away their faces. Something behind the ridge made a new line that I prayed belonged to people with eyes in the air and hoses that outran our ideas. I considered walking to the bench to see their hats move among the trunks—if they were there at all, which was a fantasy I let myself have as a sugar. Instead I sat on the porch step with my elbows on my knees because the human body bends that way when worn, and I looked out at what you always think you can see but cannot until it behaves otherwise. Sylvie stood above me and then sat, too, shoulder touching mine and then not, both of us pretending we did not notice the almost. She took the chipped mug and wiped its rim and handed it to me. Always with the unheroic things.

“You’re...you’re working the last summer,” she said. She knew, of course. It was not as if my being up on the tower would be anything but a rumor she could test against a glance up in the clear days. The tower was a finger above the ridge, and you could know a finger belongs to the body by the way it points when it has no business pointing anymore.

I nodded. The air had the bitter undertaste of things money makes. Somewhere something plastic had cast its small surrender into the day. “They took the panel,” I said. “They’ll take the legs down next year. It will become someone’s wedding platform for a time, and then a pile of metal that names no direction.”

“You’ll miss it,” she said.

“I’ll miss the chance to be the idiot who thinks watching is useful.”

She set her chin in her hand and squinted the way our mother did in reflective light. “It is useful,” she said. “It just isn’t sufficient.”

“And your—” I glanced at the jacket. I am not proud of the metaphor couched like a scout. “—your not alone.”

She smiled with more than her mouth. “He’s good,” she said. “He is odd as a stone in a pocket and he works hard. But he is out. You came. It’s good.”

We sat and the fire talked down to something we could hear without flinching. It had found the green in the ditch and it was work to convince it elsewhere. It did not rage. It admonished, which is worse. At the very edge of sight the spare bodies of three men in yellows winked between trunks and then were gone. Somewhere a pump sound beat its patient drum. The morning that began with a column of smoke had, by some action of air and happenstance and us and everything else, come into afternoon that would become evening in which we would decide, perhaps, to put a pot on and eat and then slide our legs under the blue quilt and feel, not safe, but in a known boat on a known stretch of water.

We were not saved. I will not say that. There were days after when the little fire behind the hill that had been this one’s cousin or ancestor made a joke at the expense of fence lines and small property claims. The fall brought a wind that taught the alder leaves a grave lesson. The winter killed three of the apple grafts and we swore softly. She would leave me on the tower on a night, later, and I would see the new column from farther and its color would be a bronze I had not yet seen, and the radio would laugh in my hand and clear and yell names and I would run them into the crucible of the map. But the afternoon that we made it through without killing each other on a different plain than the physical, that day became in me a container I could open whose hinge did not rust. Each time I opened it a smell rose off of it that was not quite smoke and not quite the soap she favored and not quite water. A sister smell. It does not leave you even when it leaves you. It takes a seat in the row reserved for family and refuses to move.

That evening, when people in town who do not know us drove home under a sky the color of an old bruise and said to one another in the car, “You smell that?” and rolled down the window and did something like gratitude for living where it felt consequential to be alive, Sylvie and I did the dishes under water that ran brown at first and then cleared and laughed because we had to. She dried her hands on her pants and then on mine, a mischief. We took a walk to the creek and the frogs made their remarks and we sat on the rock our father had claimed for his and then refused us, and we put our hands, without burying the lede of it, into the water and left them until pain taught us the lesson we had asked to learn. “It’s cold,” she said. “It’s cussedly cold.” I wanted, but did not say, that I would have taken her wrist if she had offered it and set it on my knee and sat like that until the night resigned. I wanted, but did not say, that you can be humbled by a looking-out you thought was the last and find that it is the beginning of a different species of watching.

Up on the tower later, the black‑headed grosbeaks who made the forsythia in town shake when they fell through it would simply be silent while the world burned or did not. They do not lend their opinions. A nearby log would go to coal and glow as if illuminated by the scandal of its own existence. The bed would be warm only on the half where body had been. My radio would blink a petty light and pretend to be power while having none. In the south window a little moth would die in the cobweb and the spider would not come, not yet, not in the big heat.

I lay there and counted the boards again and realized for the first time since counting them had lost its power that there were fewer than I imagined. As if the house had grown smaller when no one was measuring. Someone would come with a wrench and a trailer next year and take the last bolts. They would stack the panels on the flatbed and tie it with orange strap and wave at the cows on the way out. Someone would dye the wood white and hang a wedding dress from it and take a photograph with her mouth in an O to name her astonishment. I could not hate that; I could hate so many other things. The morning had put in me the sight of the smoke and my feet had put in me the rest of it and the evening had put in me my sister’s voice like water over rock, the old sloganeering and the new. The slow of it. The insistence of ember like a word you cannot translate and so must hold in your cheek like a seed until spring.

When the flash of it came again on a later day I would go because I had gone. The deadradio would die and I would walk because I had walked, knees leaving two small moons of dirt on denim. The tower’s rails would be warm under my palm. Each breath would take what it was given and give what it had to. The path would be the path we know and do not. What I want to say is, I saw her there. That is the sentence at the root of all my going. I saw her there and she saw me and against every practice both of us had because practice makes not perfect but persistence, we let our throats open. When a smoke column rose and asked its question, neither of us refused to answer. We could not make it end. But we could—the way you do with the humane tasks, the small and decent—make a beginning that did not smell only of ash.
