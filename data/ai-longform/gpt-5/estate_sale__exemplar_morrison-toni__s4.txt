The first thing to walk out that house was its face. A beveled mirror, ribbed with dust like a pressed penny. Two men in work boots grunted it down the stairs, tilting so the hall caught itself multiplied: doorframe inside doorframe, the newel post’s ball upon ball, her own jaw small and squared the way her mother used to set it when something had to be borne. Odessa did not look in it. Not on purpose. The thing had been hung opposite the coat closet so anybody coming in saw themselves right away: throat, buttons, stray hair. Her mother believed in that. Ain’t no need to be catching yourself in storefront glass, she used to say. You are like company to yourself; greet you properly. The day the mirror came was the day the house quit hiding. Clear as a broom handle, her mother would put that mirror to work. Baby girl, lift your chin. Your neck is where the Lord keeps your angel breath. Don’t go around strangling it.

Now strangers were walking the chin and the angel breath down the steps. The narrowness of the stairwell clenched around them, as if the house had decided to hold on. Odessa stood at the foot with the cash box, the one the funeral home had given her, gold trim around fake cherry wood. She had put her own money in it first, a kind of pledge so the first buyer’s bills would not have to fall against the felt alone. Children ran past like spilled marbles, catching themselves only when their mamas hissed. A neighbor woman in a quilted vest petted a doily as though it would purr. A man in carwash blue held up the mirror by the hooks and asked, How much the glass?

Seventy-five, she said, knowing it was too little.

He clucked his tongue like a hen. Fifty will save you a little time.

Time? Odessa said. She squinted at his throat, the stayed-lower lip of a man used to filth and orders. Time was something nobody had ever saved for her, not even her mother, who scraped and hoarded everything else. The man looked over her head toward the porch. A truck engine answered him. Everybody had brought trucks. The town might have been a river and all of its men felt a flood coming on. If she dared she would have said: only my time gets saved today, sir. Everyone else’s runs on.

Fifty is fine, she heard herself say. He folded the bills proper, first lengthwise, then down, and set them not on the table but in her palm. It was small courtesy, or else old habit, to put payment into a woman’s warm hand. His fingers were stiff, not from courtesy but from the way a man guards himself in front of other men. Which is how he lifted the mirror too, arms braced and back tight, so as not to look like he was letting a woman haste him along. He shuffled to the door, and the mirror swallowed the room as he went, took it all: the narrow carpet, the box she had taped shoes into so the pair wouldn’t lose each other, the square of light at the end of the hallway, the list she had made last night of everything not to cry over.

Outside, children called the way swifts call, thin and slicing. A little boy hopped from one cement step to another and counted only the hops, not the steps. Somebody had dragged the green rug—the one that used to be called good—onto the porch, and it hung there like a lawn hung to dry. You sure? the woman in the quilted vest asked Odessa, patting the fringe. Odessa nodded without trusting her mouth. That rug had been rolled up every summer, unrolled every fall, beaten over the swing set with the cut-off broom, fussed over with vinegar water as though the square of floor under it could take sick from neglect. Mama believed in rugs the way some folk believed in watchmen. The right rug and the right oil and trouble would pass your house thinking you wasn’t home. You sure, baby? the woman said again because Odessa’s nod must have been the kind that keeps a thought stuck behind it. I’m sure, Odessa told the woman, and in the saying she felt the hinge in her chest ease or bark—she wasn’t sure which.

When she was ten, the mirror had cut her. She hadn’t even been doing wrong, only rushing past, fingers out, her own shadow hot on her heels. She caught the edge not the face of it. It sliced and bled her, a nick no bigger than a comma at the root of her thumb. Her mother cleaned it with her mouth first, looking over the shoulder all the while at the stove because the rice had to be turned down, the greens stirred. Ain’t nothing but a house trying to mark you, she said, pushing Odessa’s bleed under running water like she was showing it something. Her mother’s tongue had a tiny crack in it, a line that ran from tip to middle, and when she got excited or angry or prideful, the crack darkened. The girl would watch it every time. It opened only for prayer and sugar mints, never for foolishness. Mama would say the house kept what it paid for. And this mirror had paid. It paid. Meaning Odessa’s blood bent to its silver that day, became part of what it reflected, made her and the wall and the foyer and the door a single thing with a price.

At one o’clock a white-haired woman with an emerald pin picked up the brass canister that held kindling years ago and said, My mother had a beautiful one like this. She looked through Odessa as if Odessa’s forehead was the window she needed. Does this open? she asked. She could have been asking of Odessa’s chest. Odessa lifted the lid. It smelled like rope and the inside of piano keys. The woman’s hand hovered then pounced, lifting not the canister but something small and ugly tucked beside it: a matchbook with ivory women on the front, their legs crossed to occlude what the legs couldn’t. Odessa felt heat in her face, old and wasted and still plotting a comeback. She had hidden that thing when she was thirteen, took it from under her brother’s pillow because she was tired of his breath going thick and messed around with it. She had slipped it behind the canister, cried when her mother found the matches missing and took it out on Odessa’s shins. Girl, I see everything that come in my house and everything that go. You can get gone yourself if you think paper women smoke in my living room.

You selling the can? the white-haired woman said.

Everything not nailed, Odessa said. She tried to joke it—Everything not nailed and even some what is—but the woman had already turned to somebody behind her who wanted a set of little teacups painted like winter coats. Words moved around like moths. Everybody talked to everybody the same way they would if the town was the living room of their grandmother, and each was sneaking out with a fork while praising the potted fern. Odessa made her change slow. She liked the pressure of coins exchanging owners. She could hear her mother count in that voice she used only on numbers and Scripture. The voice had slices of honey in it, and the honey was in a hurry to pour.

Sis? a man said. He was standing at the top of the basement steps with a rolled-up rug on his shoulder the color of pond scum and money. You got more down here? He had a face that insisted it was not ashamed of anything it asked.

What you see is what I got, she said. He nodded toward the basement. The space behind him breathed out brown cold, and with it that smell every Ohio house learned after a winter with nothing moving under it: iron weep and forgotten lemon. Odessa thought of her mother’s jars down there in the dark, the ones she herself had told herself she would not open because what could be in them would not smell good now and would accuse the nose that wanted them. Peaches float their way apart when nobody is looking. Okra displeases the jar. Tomatoes can forgive but they hold grudges. Odessa had not opened the jars, had not let herself see the drunk light in them the day the power went out and she had to feel for matches. The man waiting on the step did not know about the jars. He wanted rugs because rugs lay down and let boots beat them. You can take that one, she said. Twenty. He hollered up to someone to bring him money. The little money ran down the steps as if it was ashamed to be cash. No one hid shame anymore, Odessa thought. That might have been the real proof that you were free: the dirty things stuck to you like a sticker and you walked surefoot with it, didn’t bother to peel nothing.

At two fifteen the rain came. It was not a real rain, not the kind that could scare grass. It was an accident of clouds. People turned their faces up, pursed their lips at the drop on them like a surprise kiss, then went on making their careful piles. Some of them had brought their own old blankets to wrap her mother’s things, as if they believed that strangers’ objects get cold crossing porches. In the corner by the stairs the quilt waited. Odessa had not put a tag on it. Her fingers found each pass of it in the night, each path that had been cut from a dress or a curtain or what used to be Mr. Castle’s shirt before he sat too hard on the church steps and cracked the board. Her mother’s quilts could tell a stranger the family’s names if the stranger had the ear. This one had formal bones. Cross-hatch that made eyes dizzy, a border of three impossible blues, a risen sun in each square like a small lie you’d swear was truth until, old as you are, your ear goes keening for honesty and you find it again. Every stitch was a sentence. Every sentence was the edge of something. When Odessa lifted it, the weight was less than it would be wetted with use, and the smell of lavender—or the smell that had agreed years ago to be called lavender—pored itself out.

You letting that go? Mr. Byrd asked from the doorway. He had only come, he said, to see how she was bearing. His hat was too clean for a day like this. He held it as if to say he understood the foolishness of hats. Mr. Byrd had been thirty and smooth when he first came to sell insurance and lay his eye on her mother. Insurance. Ain’t nothing sure but leaving, her mother had told him. We all of us pre-paid. Mr. Byrd did not sell Lona Ward a policy that day, but he did come by with sausage one New Year’s, visited when a windstorm peeled the porch roof back, brought bags of ice the week Odessa’s father forgot his where-abouts and beat the washing line until it cried. Mr. Byrd had a way of sorrow that made you mad to be seen crying. You letting that go? he asked again.

Not today, Odessa said. And even when she had said it, she felt the weight lift as if she had put it into someone’s arms. Holding on can trick you like that, she thought. The refusing can feel like giving.

You keeping any of Mama's? a girl said behind her. Odessa turned, and the small zip in her heart found and fixed on a little mouth she knew. Keema. Her own. Loping out of the mud in those shoes the girls in town wear like an apology for how pretty they are. Child had hair that argued with the day and won. Keema did not so much look at her as set her eyes to a question about her, let the eyes do the work so the mouth could pretend it was lazy. You even told me about this? the girl said. Which meant you didn’t tell me anything, coasting on the burn of Mama left us and now I got to do, I got to do, you grown and think you the only one feel.

I called you last night, Odessa said, and it was true. She had called and the phone had hummed and then turned dizzy in her ear and said, The number you have dialed is not willing.

I was at work, Keema said. And then, because youth gets information like lightning gets weather, she added, How much you getting for the good table?

A man had set his hand on it like a doctor, pressing for a heartbeat. Not for sale, Odessa said, matching her girl’s eyes instead of matching the man’s palm.

Please, Keema said, surprised by a prayer her mouth did not know it had. That table was the one place her knuckles had been taught to knock before touching. Anytime she had come home smelling like some boy’s hide or a store’s perfume she hadn’t bought, she had to sit at it. Lona put a pitcher of water on it with ice clinking like teeth and said, Talk. Each piece of the table had a lawsuit against somebody. A nick where the knife the boys had sent through a phone book chewed and bit wrong. A burn the iron could not deny, ever. Ink like blood at the seam where the planks met. That was before the wax took over and made a sedate old gentleman out of it—all polished shoes and two spoons in one pocket. That table had presided over whippings, repentance, feasts of string beans. Keema had learned the difference between a lie and a story at that table, and between a story and a miracle.

You better not, she said, and took a seat, as if claiming it now could burn a brand in its leg.

A man in a camo hoodie, a boy really, hovered around the record player. He lifted the needle with the care a boy gives to a thing called delicate only because he had not broke it yet. He lifted album after album and shouted the ones he knew: M-town, this real right here. Woo! James Brown? He read the name like it was the law.

That one is scratched, Odessa warned.

That means it already lived, the boy said. His teeth were too big for his mouth. He looked like some bird that had fallen out of a nest insisting the ground was where flying started. He laid the vinyl back and let the needle fall on it from two inches up just to hear the noise. Odessa flinched. Her ear remembered the old ritual: breath held, arm low, needle eased, the way you gave a thing its life without mussing it.

How much? he said, running his tongue along his gums like there was an answer there.

Twenty for the player, two and up for the albums, she said.

Two? the boy said, practicing grown man disappointment. He made his mouth to argue, then spied the cover with a woman on it who wore a dress of smoke and shut up. He burned his disappointment for fuel. When he carried the player out, the cord swung and tapped his leg like a braiding whip. The woman in the smoke stared back into the house from under his arm. His back said: I was here, and also: I am going.

Lona would have said, See. You think you can keep it. You think keeping is a kind of prayer. But what door can prayer lock against time? Even the Lord went around with no place to lay his head and still folks try to fold him up in cloth.

At three a woman with a baby on her hip asked about the rocking chair. How much for the sit even if it creak, she said. The baby was sleeping in that wide-jawed way babies have when they trust gravity and milk to keep them earth-side. Odessa set her hand on the chair’s two shoulders, felt again her mother’s weight in it from the nights when the tornado warnings ran like a radio play across the state, from the mornings when flour had to be pinched into biscuit while the kettle hummed. It had a stripe on the runner where the cat had made claim. How much could she sell a pact for? Each ring in the wood was notched with stars for years her mother had persisted, stood against her own body’s leave-takings. Price can’t be named, she thought, and, out loud, said: Ten? then heard Mr. Byrd cough in a way that meant a man keeps his throat from telling what he thinks is sinful.

Girl, you want all of her gone in one day? he murmured, and for a moment she thought he meant she, Odessa, was the one walking out of the house plank by plank and rug by rug. She set the price higher and the woman paid, kissing the baby’s forehead hard, as if to teach a forehead what it was for.

What had any of this been for? Dishes with chips at the lip. Lampshades that knew every fly in the county. A radio with a back like a turtle. She had gone to Goodwill last week and looked at other people’s leavings to learn how to price her own. Heavy things were cheap. Light things were dear. The finer the woman who once dusted it, the less it fetched. A man with a bale of arm tattoo paid five for a mason jar full of buttons and lifted it like a snow globe. His eyes said he was seeing some other winter. What buyer did she look like in their eyes? A seller? A child they pitied? A woman whose mother left her something she could not hold without both arms, both knees, two throats, and an extra back?

Lona’s voice moved through the hall like a breeze that didn’t want credit for being a breeze. We ain’t got to make pretty to be proud. Look at me, Odessa. Name me two things I done I never would. I never wore another woman’s dress and I never let my sadness see my floor. But Odessa could make a list longer than a prayer shawl of what her mother never did. She never said please before mercy, though she said it after. She never cried at the table. She kept her magazines in a brown paper bag. She kept her money not in the bank the bank kept but in a jar the jar kept, sunk in the flour like somebody dared to bake wealth. She kept her daughters in bodies that were not to be trod by strange shoes. She kept the boys harder than they came to her. She kept a secret in the back of her throat like a lozenge she would not let melt.

Around four a skinny man with tired hair came to the kitchen door and said he had been sent for the stove. Not the white one with a cracked face, he said. The black one. He wore no belt and held his trousers the way a man holds breath. Odessa looked at him and then at the black stove, glossy as a ministry. It was not for sale. It was not even thought about. The stove had parented boiled dinners and burnt offerings alike. It had stood in one place and still gone somewhere, its mouths open, its back hot, its surfaces telling the story of every spill. It had been the only steady flame in a winter where she and her sister stuffed newspapers between cracks and willed the storm to get bored and go. Odessa shook her head no, little slow, a child being taught to spell. The man shrugged, and then his shrug took on a shape, the kind of shape that meant: things happen at my house too. He stepped back out into the rain and left his shrug like an empty frame in her kitchen.

Odessa’s sister came at four-thirty in a jacket that said Columbus on it in a thread the size of frogs’ legs. Ruthie. Ruthie who can make a laugh in a kitchen the way some folks make soup. Ruthie who loses keys and men and finds hymns and lost socks and used to carry a blade in the bottom seam of her skirt for just instances because why not. Ruthie set a paper bag on the table and took out a jar of pickles and a roll of paper towels. I brought something briny, she said. For salt’s sake. She hugged Odessa with her narrow arms that could wring a wet cloth near dry. You doing? Ruthie asked, and in the asking both of their eyes took inventory: the wall that used to be called Sunday Yeller was now a Sunday shadow; the bowl with its crack like a highway; the wide-mouth jar that had caught six generations of change and now could not even buy cigarettes. Ruthie lifted the jar, tilted it, heard the coins talk like metal in a pocket. You emptied it? It sound sad, she said.

I hadn’t the heart, Odessa said. She looked for the word that would do. She found none. She would have liked to say, I have many hearts and none of them empty. Or, I have one and it is a boy and he is bad. Or, I have one and it is a bitter little old wife that scolds me for wanting what I want. Or—true and not true—Mama took my heart to the place her breath kept and the box at the funeral home was where their business got done with it, and I did not sign nothing but they put my name like I had.

Ruthie wiped a glass and wiped the wiping and stood with her shoulder against the door like a policeman resting. Mr. Byrd, you still around? she called, and he raised his face from a chair like a surprised grandfather to say, Where else.

Odessa had told them all the homegoing would be small, just Pastor and a plate of chicken and the three of them who had done the living with Lona. She had ordered carnations. The flower girl had given her two free sprays in case the cold hurt the money. She had dressed her mother’s body herself. Think about that, she thought now at the stove: hands that had been taught to dress a turkey, a baby, a wound, a lie—used now to dress the body that had smacked them, fed them, steadied them so they would not fall down drunk in church from love. She had slid the stockings up her mother’s legs, whispered at the knee so the skin would not get mad at being used. She had buttoned that white dress the women at the church had washed five times to make not only clean but invisible. She had put her mother into it like a story-translator slurring the rough places smooth. That morning the funeral man had said, Some keep something back. Jewelry. A scarf. A scrap. Some pull their own hair and tuck it in the pillow. We let them. He had said it like explaining how a train once took his only aunt away and brought her back only for Sunday dinner five years running. Odessa had tucked nothing, because her mother’s body had never kept anything she did not mean to.

It had been the way Lona could cut the world and season it that made her famous on their street. Called names. Not indecent names, not even pretty ones. Names that had weight to them like beans. Names like, There she go, the keep-she-keep-us woman. Or, There is Miss Lona with she eyes like the Mr. Christmas tree—they both so precious they shine. Lona had played with her own prettiness like a cat worries string. She had not disdained it when she was a girl. She had two church looks, one for the first Sunday and one for the day they took communion, both of which would bring women into the vestibule to talk about their unrepentant children and talk about whether or not they were wrong (and they were) to have a drink with meat. But she had put it down quick. Not even mid-thirties, and she had got done with men the way you get done with a stick you used to walk a dog with once you bought a leash. Odessa never knew if the broom of it was heartbreak or sense. It was not the kind of thing her mother would speak—the what of it, the where and when—but if you stood beside the saucepan while it spit its grease at you, Lona sometimes said, as if to the meat, not to you: I do not feed the kind of love that want to eat me.

Buyers thinned as the cloud closed the afternoon. Trucks backed over the curb and then pulled forward to correct themselves. Somebody’s car had that high whistle a belt made when it begged mercy. A boy with hair the shape of water asked if he could take the marbles. He opened the can and poured one into his hand without asking, rolled it against his knuckle. These blue ones all got a crack in them, he said, like someone wrote on the sky and the letters sank to the bottom.

They been fought for, Odessa said.

He grinned. Cool.

Keema took one too and set it in the notch at the base of her throat and said she was a rich lady at her mirror. A richer lady than your Nana, Odessa said before her teeth could approve the tongue. Keema let the glass fall into her palm. She was wronged quick, as quick as wind changes an alley. She had not been to the funeral. Her fire called her elsewhere that day. She had texted a heart and a cloud. Odessa had texted back a bull and a bell because she did not know what any of it stood for and was tired. They had stood like two animals with different rooms talking through the wall. Now, while Mr. Byrd told Ruthie about his grandson’s graduation and forgot this was not the time or place, Keema stuck the blue glass in the smallest pocket of her jeans and took up the heavy cutlery. What for? Odessa asked.

We need forks at the place, Keema said, simple as the truth, which it might be. Or she might be making a rope out of a string of alphabets she’d picked up and was now flinging around the room to see if it would catch. Odessa let her take them. Her mother’s forks had no ambition like silver did. They had laid themselves down in any hand, they had scooped egg and pinned chicken, they had felt the tongues scrape them like front teeth and did not remark. Only the spoon with the bite taken out of it kept anger. Lona’s second boy, Odell—gone since nineteen—had been chewing at it while telling a story of his own bravery. Lona had told him, You got to teach a tongue better jobs than to bite what feed it. Odell had laughed, that low-of-milk laugh boys get when they know their beauty has been given extra. The next day he stole a car he could not get out of town without the town recognizing it and had to stay with a man he did not owe who used him unpaid. Ruthie had told Odessa later the man kept Odell like people keep a china swan. Kept him on the dresser of his life for looking at. Self in a mirror of a boy. Odell had gone north after that and wrote twice. He was still somewhere being looked at, as far as Ruthie, who liked simple lies for their flavor, could tell.

When the sun found enough edge to set, its coin-faced light slapped the west side of the house, and the dining room got a fever in it. The light made old wax smell like apples that had laid too long. Odessa breathed through her mouth. A man in a stiff vest stood at the mantel and took down the clock like it was a baby’s head. He turned it in his hands. It wasn’t a splendid clock. It was a clock that did its business. Her mother had pretended to wind it only on Sundays, but Odessa had seen her touch it on Tuesdays, Wednesdays, the day a letter came with stamp and her father’s name on it written in a different hand—no curl, no promise, all sting. The letter had said his arm caught and the blade did not feel kindly to bone. The letter had said they wanted to say sorry. The plant sent one of their men with a suit bag. You can tell how much they love their men by how they pack them when they send them off, Lona said, looking at the crease in the knee too crisp, the zipper keeping its secret like a saint. We gon bury your father with no wishbone, she had told her children. Ain’t nothing ever going to catch in his throat now.

The man with the clock counted off the ticks like he was making tally of sin. He tried to haggle. She stared him down to full price. The clock stopped in that house for the last time. Ruthie rang a jar lid with a spoon and said, You women, listen, telling what story to tell who. Odessa could not look at Ruthie and not see the day she came home with a swollen lip and hair undone and refused to say which of her men had been taught that day that women bleed but do not die. Ruthie had sat on the kitchen floor and put an ice cube on each nipple until they cried cold like two little girls. Lona had swept a straight line to the door and said, If I could I would lay down my own lip in the street and let a truck tell it a new way to be. But I cannot. Fact is, I cannot. And in the saying lay her first apology Odessa had ever heard, though it had nothing to do with what was being apologized for.

The last bidder was a man who went through every drawer as if drawers kept secrets that must be bidden into telling. He found a letter in one, not old—Christmas stamp from last year—and opened it right in front of them all until Odessa told him with no voice, with only her hand on the lip of the drawer and her mother’s bent knee in her memory, that he could not. He blushed the way white folk blushed, way up to the edge of his hair. Keema snickered and caught herself and then snickered again, for her age was that kind of snicker, the kind that comes to the lip twice. He bought the dresser cheap as if the blush had to be converted to currency. As he left, it began to rain for real, and the yard grew slick like a mouth in distress.

When the trucks had sloped their way into the street, and the last neighbor had held Odessa for a second longer than a polite person would hold somebody—long enough to smell her lotion, long enough to decide about her—when even Mr. Byrd took a deep breath as if packing up his organs for a trip and said, Call me should you need, the house sat. It had lost itself heavier than it had kept itself. Odessa could hear the hollowness like a girl can hear when other girls have stopped skipping.

It was only the three of them then: Odessa, Ruthie, Keema. Their breaths made three clumps of the same air. No chairs but the table’s four legs standing like an old horse in a field nobody mows anymore.

You hungry? Ruthie asked, though the question was half hope. They ate the pickles with their fingers and Ruthie found three deviled eggs that had lasted in a tin unremembered. The eggs were sweet as gossip, their tops rubbed with paprika like a bruise. The pickles made their mouths be honest.

We keeping the table, Keema said again, though no one had offered again to take it. She stroked one place at the corner, a scratch that curved like cut water.

We are, Odessa said. You got to decide, she told herself. You cannot be the rope between every two of anything.

I know why Mama kept it, Ruthie said, her head cocked in a way that meant she had smeared tomorrow’s grease across right now. On this she received the world, you know it? She had babies to it, she broke the fever of a boy to it, she watched Daddy be bent at it. She signed for the mortgage at it, with her purse open big and church hands turned over, forefinger pointing to the line. She wrote the prayer list atop of it and kept the paper under the salt so the devil could get at it last and be too tired by then to raise much fuss. You see this line? You see where the varnish worn to bone? That’s where she did numbers. That’s where she kept us from the red. Where else she gon protect herself but on what hold?

Who protected you? Keema asked the air, as if it had ears, as if it was listening from the stair, as if she wanted not an answer but to put the question down finally. She set the blue marble in the little worn place and watched it try to roll. It did not. It stood there, bright in evening’s false sun.

Odessa’s bones had their own questions. She had a man once who said he liked to see his mouth on her skin afterward, the little bruises making script, said he wanted to read himself. He had a laugh that put his feet on the table when he sat, a laugh that thought of itself as a horse and ran. He left before he got boring. Before he could settle into the habit of her as if she was a chair nobody talked about. She had a girl with his stubbornness that made you proud if you had pride and made you ill if you had sense. She had a boy whose anger had no edges to it. He would run it along anything and nothing caught. He was not in the county now. He did not call. She kept a green sweater in a drawer because she could not not. Today when she opened that drawer, the sweater had not said anything to her. It had not cried, nor begged, nor tried to scratch her mouth. It lay down like uncut grass. So she had shut the drawer and put a price on the dresser.

You hear that? Ruthie said.

They stilled. The house made a little sound the way a creature makes a small breath to reassure itself it is. It wanted to be known. It might have been the rain telling the window it had learned its lesson, knew the frame intimately now. Could have been the drain in the sink swallowing what the pickles had to say. It could have been something had come unstuck—one of the places where old paint holds new love together. Odessa did not jump because she had been waiting on the house to speak all day. She had waited the way a woman waits for the last bus: upright and with her foot toward the curb.

You know what, Odessa said. Let me show you something, and even as she said it, she wasn’t sure what part of the house would know what to show. She went to the pantry and knocked at its back, a foolish childish act she hadn’t done since little—once when she saw a mouse go where it had no right and her mother whistled with her tongue between her teeth and the mouse reversed like a car ashamed. The panel in the back was not loose, but it lifted when Odessa insisted. She had thought for years there might be a hollow back there because the room behind the pantry never lined up right on the outside. Her father used to say the house was built by men who had wives that made them tired. Inside the cavity, at one side, was a small tin, like the kind tooth fairies use when they are done being fairies and take up hairpins. Odessa slid it free. Ruthie’s eyes went big as if she had been hung in a place where she could not see air for a week and then somebody cut her down and kissed her shoulder. Keema’s lip twitched the way it does when young blood is about to decide wrongly.

Childish, Ruthie breathed, sinful, be careful.

It was no magic, no gold. A packet of letters tied with string made soft by touching, a rag doll with no face, a rosary somebody had been ashamed of or not ashamed at all of but not willing to show to the women who would have taken it out of their hands and said, My, what a pretty piece of pretend. One letter had a stamp with a boat on it and a country greened around with water. Another had only two words on the outside: For later. Odessa looked at their mother’s hand in the addresses and saw it grow two ways here: careful and reckless. That was Lona, that was her. She could thread a needle with a roast if she wanted, and she could drop a word so hard you would look on the floor for where it fell.

Ruthie reached her fingertips and pulled the bow. The knot came easy. It was waiting not to be a knot. The letters did not birth secrets as quick as Ruthie wanted. They were dull and gossipy the way old letters are to children of the woman who wrote them: they spoke to them about money and pots and what Miss Janey wore to church as if money and pots and Miss Janey’s sin were never all that fit. But one—written in a hand not Lona’s—said, That boy of yours put his head in the wrong idea. He got shamed but still he pretty. He whiter than he ought to be for his safety and he going to meet the world with his looks and not his papers and not no Lona’s good sense is what you tell him to take. I give him two months before he come home to his eating. If he come home angry, you send him to me before you do something you think is mercy. You hear, Lo? Send him to me. Sign: W.

W? Ruthie said. Who was W? They sifted their memory. They held up the letter as if the air would write a name on the empty of it. Lona had kept a small set of letters in the hollow of her pantry, behind jars she had not opened, while telling her girls she had no secrets. But a woman’s lies are often the only fence she thinks she has. Odessa felt the fence now under her hands and hated that it scratched her.

Keema had the puppet in her palm. The rag had been dressed in a dress cut from a little sprigged shirt Odessa remembered from when she was six. Girl had pigtails so tight her eyes could have cried from the pull if Lona ever let them. The rag had no face because faces get you in trouble. She threaded a string through the doll’s waist and flicked it like a fish. The doll leapt and sat and leapt again in a foolish dance. Keema grinned and then stopped. She looked at Odessa and then at the letters and then at the pantry wall with its secret just now had and knew in that knowing that you can move into a house all your days and still not live at one of its walls. Anywhere can hold you. Nothing will be your own unless you make it. The realization put another small anger in her, like a pea that rolls around the bottom of a pot and burns a shadow into the steel.

Put that down, Ruthie said to her lightly. Baby, these is graves. We got to step round them nice. She set her hand on Keema’s wrist and the thin trellis of bone there said: don’t grow without me.

Take the letters, Odessa told the pantry, and for a second she almost did put them back, loop the soft string, tuck it in that darkness that made the paper pretty. Instead she set them on the table where the marble watched them not roll. Ruthie went and brought the Matches that the white-haired woman had not bought, the wicked women one, lit the flame, let it singe the talk off the air and then pinched it dead. Don’t set us alight, child, she told herself, not you.

Light had leaked out of the yard. Rain turned from song to complaint. Odessa closed the blinds one by ridiculous one, the way the world is taught to be shut with slats. In closing, the house made a new noise: a swallow in the throat. She thought of the day the men had come to read the meter and how her mother had fussed about dirt on their boots but let them do what they did anyway because you cannot stop a man who knows where your numbers live. She thought of nights when tornado had been the word whispered by the leaves, how she had slept under the coffee table with a pillow and the table had been friend and enemy both. Odessa wiped her hands on the front of her skirt. The skirt was one her mother had eyed for its navy not its cut.

Take what you want, she told Keema after a minute too long not talking.

What’s left? the girl asked, not hands on hips but words on hips.

What matters, Odessa said like it was something she had set on the counter, like a pie. Like a trick. She did not know what silence to leave after. She did not know if the thing you leave your children should stab or kiss.

Keema looked around and the looking could have been cruel but it wasn’t. Child had her mother’s anger and her grandmother’s weather. She went first to the corner where her calendar had hung when she was still in high school, one of those big ones with gospel words and women in red hats. She peeled the tape and photo-grease off the wall and made a face at the dust line. She touched the light switch as if it could tell her something. She plucked one curtain rod and let it vibrate. Finally she reached to the table and put her fingers, same as Odessa’s but more notches for more doors she had opened without asking, into the groove where the varnish ran sudden thin. You ain’t moving it tonight, she said, predictive as midwives. So I’m going to lay myself right here and show it who it is. She slid onto the tabletop like a grand sweet thing and lay on her back and stretched both arms out and smiled, obscene and holy. You getting splinters, Ruthie said and slapped her foot as if to flick the splinter away ahead of it coming.

Odessa laughed. She did not mean to. It broke up through her chest like a soup bubble, had the same heat, the same fishy truth in it. Lona would have frowned and then turned away to hide the smile that had the nerve to be living in her like sin.

They helped each other pull the rug from the porch and fold it, not to keep but to stop it from swallowing rain like drink. They set the rug upright against the wall the way language leans sometimes, slept on, not awake yet and still it stares. Ruthie stuck the pickle jar in the refrigerator which was humming in that loyal foolish way refrigerators have. Odessa wanted to pull the plug and give the thing a rest. We keep on and keep on, as if keeping could come unstuck from on. You work, cold box, and then you sleep with Jesus. But she did not. She taped a sign on it that said Refrigerator free to whoever will haul.

The house now knew its echo. Odessa smelled damp and Mr. Clean and onion in the wood. A woman will tell you what her mother used to scent her floor with same way she will tell you a recipe for whipping a lying child: long, precise, certain to fail now. Lona used to splash whatever was on sale into the murk bucket and then doctor it. A cap of Clorox if sickness had come and looked around like it had ideas. A drop of pine if company had come for condolences but not to help. Orange just before holidays, vinegar when anybody had done wrong inside the house and needed their steps to squeal truth at their heels. Odessa had not used anything today. It made her feel unmoored, like a girl who had taken her shoes off in a river and could not remember where on shore she had left them.

You going to sleep here tonight? Ruthie asked.

I couldn’t sleep if I tried, Odessa said truth. And then because Ruthie had a habit of walking out with tendernesses not meant for her—taking them like baskets to other porches and tantalizing women with how pretty they looked holding them—Odessa added, I ain’t twelve.

I ain’t, Ruthie said back, but her laugh had a twelve in it sliding around like a coin.

I will, Keema said, jaw clenched the way she did when she dared herself. Mama, I’ll stay. I got a boy who’ll run errands for me all night long if I... She stopped because she might have wanted to impress and then thought again. That was new. Odessa liked it the way a woman likes a second bite of something unexpected.

You ain’t got no bed, Odessa said. Keema threw an arm grandly toward the table. Ruthie shook her head and clicked her tongue. I can bring you the air mattress, she said. Or I can sit out there in my car like a policeman married a drunk and pat you with my attention every hour. Odessa smiled, felt the house lean toward the girl as if offering what softness it could. You will not, she said to Ruthie and meant both of them. She found a sheet and shook it out. It fell like a quarter of a sky. They folded it on top of the table and then hunted for pillows in the closet Lona kept for rugs rolled like maps. They found two and a stack of towels like pancakes.

Before the dark turned itself all the way down, Odessa went room to room with a trash bag for what she knew she could throw out now without anything in her stopping her hand. She took a stack of coupons tied with a rubber band like a torniquet. She took six elastic gloves from the bathroom that had waited in their box so long they cracked when she breathed on them. She took a sweater of her own she had left years ago and forgot: green, the green of grass if grass could steal. Its elbows were shiny. She shoved it in the bag and then took it out and put it where her hand could find it in the morning. She was not done with that argument yet.

In the back room, where the light chose not to live and where once the ironing board stood like a kneeling woman popping her hip to be less obedient about it, she paused. The window fogged with its own breath. The matchbook with the ivory girls sat on a shelf. Odessa picked it up and flicked one of them open. Four red-tipped matches stared like tongues waiting to be used. She put it down. She had no more fire to invite into this day.

By the time they returned to the table, Keema had fallen asleep without permission, her arm under her head defying the pillow’s point. Ruthie’s breathing aboard was a small boat bumping a dock. Odessa pulled the sheet like a mother who had no more babies but a habit. She tucked it around her girl’s hips so she could not be stolen by a draft. She looked at the marble, blue, still watching from its nest in varnish. She touched it and rolled it once for mischief. It rolled a hair and came back, faithful to the dent.

Odessa stood in the kitchen and went to the window that looked over the sink toward the alley and put her forehead against the cool. The backyard said the same thing all backyards in Ohio say at night: I am only a place. I will hold you or not depending on your choices. I do not bless.

She let her eyes close. Behind her lids, Lona came over the hill of her mind in that way dead mothers do: whole and confounding, bearing in their mouths a taste of the thing that killed them and yet insisting it did not. Odessa did not talk to her. She was not the kind to say Mama like a question. She opened her hand and let the air lick her palm. It wasn’t forgiveness. It was practice. People think forgiveness is something you do to the person wrong. It is a craft you learn with your own hand, your own mouth, your own winter. If you get good at it, folk think you soft. If you fail, folk think you mean. The trick is knowing your own inventory.

She went to the pantry and took the letters, the rosary, the faceless doll. She put them in a new tin and wrote on the lid with a marker in a hand that mimicked none of the dead: Not for sale. She set it on the highest shelf next to the jar of flour where no baker’s hand had been past three Easters. In the flour, under where a spoon had once gone to find something richer than starch and more dangerous, she pressed the blue marble.

In the morning, she would go open the window and call into the alley the way Lona used to call to see if Miss Janey needed to borrow what Janey would later report she already had. She would take the table apart if she had to and move it with help that did not come from men who wore their palms forward. She would see what girl Keema would be that day: the kind with hands on the easy wrong or the kind with hands on the hard right. She would take the rug to the curb and watch for the kind of person who would lift it and the kind to walk by and say, Shame about that, and not touch. She would go to the funeral home and sign one more paper for one more thing, smile politely at one more man who had a job to do that took a woman’s name and bent it until she recognized it again.

Tonight, though, Odessa let herself sit on the floor under the table where the legs made square corners like serious men. She leaned back against the wall and closed her eyes and let her mouth open, not to cry exactly. To breathe like someone who chooses to. Upstairs the old plaster ticked with rain. It sounded like someone walking and pausing, walking and pausing. She did not give the house that personhood, but she let the sound be company.

Ruthie rolled over and grunted and said Odessa’s name like a soup bubble too, and in the saying was the girl she had been when Lona burned her bang off trying to straighten hair into politeness. Keema smacked her lips and smiled the sleepy smile only a little child and an old woman can do as if their mouths were sisters, and Odessa, against her best judgment and her worst, blessed them without touching them. She did not say Lord. She did not say Mama. She said the one true thing she had in her that did not need a person attached. She said Stay. She said it to herself and to the table and to whatever thing in the world that was kind and able and wander-lustful. She said Stay and then, because no stay holds without its opposite being respected, she said Go. Go and stay made a home between her teeth, and she let their breath move across the room, through the pantry, into the wall, along the grain of the chair that now blew with rain on somebody else’s porch and into the breath of a clock, sleeping in the backseat of a car headed over the bridge out of town.

It might have been that this is what the house had wanted all day: not for her to weep across its threshold, not for her to count the monies and settle the shapes, but to say introducing, presenting, this is the act you have learned your whole life and never named: the letting go and the keeping, the two hands of the same woman, the two eyes in the same skull. And the house, being old and a liar like other old things, felt it and knew it and settled—emptier by most anyone’s count, richer by the only count that mattered to the bones of it. It fell to sleep then. Even its mice respected it.

Odessa swung her legs under her and let her shoulder rest a second more against the wall. The plaster made a chalk mark on her jacket she would not notice until she was home. The tin up high sat like a plain crown. The marble slept like a planet in the flour. Rain with nothing better to do sang at the window in no particular key. The girls at the table breathed their women's breaths. And Odessa, who had been called to be inventory and security guard both, who had taken a fan to the small of grief’s back and walked it outdoors to see a squirrel, finally drew air easy. She did not pocket nothing. She sat with what the day had made of her.

In the morning she would be asked: How'd it go. She would answer like a woman from Ohio answers most things: It went, and then, if the person asking deserved more, she would say, It left and it stayed. And if they were the kind who had standing to put their hand on her shoulder light and leave it there long enough to count, she might tell them about the mirror first to go and the last thing to be hidden, busy as you please, lovelier than you’d think. She might say there was a matchbook with naked women on it and she had kept it from burning. She might tell them a clock rode in a backseat like a grandbaby and that a table slept three women, and two of them had their mouths open. She might say that she had wanted to spit and couldn’t because the taste in her mouth was not useless. She might say that she had found a piece of sky in flour and pressed it into white where no spoon had been. Or else she would say nothing at all and walk to the bus with her spine in the place her mother put it, the shape of a woman walking next to her, proud and tired and gone and everywhere.
