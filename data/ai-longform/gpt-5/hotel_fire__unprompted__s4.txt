When the car turns the last corner and the hotel appears, narrowed by the windshield and the rain, I do not recognize it at first. The line of the roof is different; the old awning—green, ribbed like an insect’s wing and always a little mildewed—has been replaced by a slab of glass that juts out from the facade as if it has been shoved there with a single impatient hand. The sidewalk is brighter, new pavers laid in gray ribbons that pretend at river rock. There is a man in a navy coat at the door holding an umbrella out for someone who is not me. For a long time I watch the black fabric tilt and duck with bodies, watch the rain bead on its edge and leap into space, the beads hardly falling at all before they shatter on the ground. I tap the driver’s shoulder so he knows I’ve arrived where I’m meant to be, and I hear myself say the name of the place out loud, the way I used to when the switchboard rang for service and my hands were sudsy with cleaner or linen water. It sounds prim in my mouth, the syllables like a prayer I don’t yet feel brave enough to utter fully: Halcyon.

Of course they changed the name. They made a new sign in brass and underlit it so it floats at night like something sainted. There is a logo now, some interlocking H’s like chain mail. I step out and the rain is cold enough to make me flinch. It is not hard, exactly, more like a steady weight, the kind of rain that seeps everywhere without visible effort, that wants to be a part of you. I hold my own umbrella clumsily while the bellman approaches from the side with a smile I can see is not yet tired. He reaches for my suitcase with brown hands, his jacket making a little sound like a gasp when he bends.

“Checking in?” he asks, without too much of the set script.

“For a wedding,” I say. “Check-in’s at three but—” I think about making a joke I’ve made before, something about bribery and early keys, but it feels like a surface that would give way if I tested it. The boy nods and whispers it the way mischief is whispered between children.

“We’ll see what we can do for you,” he says, and I feel the prickle, small and electric, of being taken into confidence with the people who see the other side.

The lobby doors hiss open as if the building is folded ribcage and I am lifting it to find a heart. The old revolving door is gone, the one you had to commit to, step with the rhythm or risk being mangled. Now, air whooshes and there is the scent they pump into it deliberately, a white tea and something citrus, something I can’t name that I learned not to challenge by calling it fake. The carpet is new and pale, dangerous. People will be terrified of stepping there with their wet shoes, and yet here they are, leaving their prints, damp silhouettes that will evaporate into nothingness as if their passing were a rumor.

There is the reception desk, longer and sleeker than before, clad in a wood that looks expensive and wishes to be touched. Behind it, a woman with a precise bob and a lapel pin that mirrors the logo. She smiles and lights up like something charged. I stand in front of her and she asks for my name. I give it to her and the syllables settle between us like someone else’s child. She repeats them back as the screen’s pale brightness makes a rectangle on her face.

“We do have you,” she says. “Two nights for the Caraway-Ramos wedding.” I catch my cousin’s last name coupled with one I have only seen at the tail end of email invitations and feel a small push in the ribs at how official a thing becomes this way. When we were children, Nina and I drew pretend wedding invitations on the backs of placemats at my mother’s kitchen table, adding glitter like salt. The men we married were always unnamed because it was the party that mattered: balloons, cake, a parade of friends, the trick of changing.

The woman goes on. “Your room is not quite ready, but we can store your bag and call you. Would you like to wait in the lounge?” She gestures at a space to the left that is all curved seating and lamps that look like overripe fruit.

“I might take a look around,” I say, and she nods as if that too were something the Halcyon could provide, an attitude of curated wandering. She tells me there are water stations on every floor, as if the future might call for such provisions, as if the building itself might run a fever.

There are parts that are the same because they cannot be erased. The elevator bank was somewhere else once, but it’s in the same place again because the blueprint of the city below ground and all the damp, slanted truths of it demand it. The call button is a small thing that glows from within and I feel every muscle in my hand tighten as I reach for it. There is no sound when I press. We used to hear a little bell behind the panel and then the distant groan as the car rose. It would crawl at first, then race, and sometimes I stood at the edge of the elevator and felt for the wobble that meant the cable needed tending, the way people stand at the lip of the ocean feeling the tug and claim a knowledge of tide.

“The Phoenix Ballroom is to your right,” a voice says behind me, and I turn to find a woman looking at me as if I have asked for directions without moving my mouth. She is holding a tablet in one arm and a radio in the other. The radio gargles a little with languages. “You’re here for the wedding, right? We’re seating guests in an hour for the ceremony.”

“Here?” I ask, surprised at the word ceremony. The bride had mentioned a rooftop, had described the way the city would be a landscape spread for them alone if the weather held. Of course the weather has not held. The rain has its own opinion about vows made under the sky. The woman shakes her head, glances at the ceiling as if it might offer luck if she stares at it hard enough.

“Plan B,” she says. “The couple is so chill about it. It’s a dream. Some people cry.” She sees something flicker on my face and laughs at herself. “Vendors. We have vendor stories the way nurses have the body.”

I smile and am already a part of this story in the making. It is easy to slip into the old deference of staff, the small jokes you pass across the counter to one another to share the shape of what you’re doing together. Easy because I know the weight of a bathroom trash can when it has only been used for tissues. Easy because I have balanced a tray with ice and lemons in the palm of my hand and felt a kind of pride that had nothing to do with ambition, only with steadiness. I nod and the woman is on to the next person, her smile arranged and generous.

I tell myself I will ride up to see the hall first. I tell myself I will step into only the parts that will host the celebration, that I will not begin to test the back doors already labeling themselves in my mind. There is a door to the left that says Employees Only, but the font has changed and the door is not old green but a smooth charcoal, and when I stand near it, breath held to catch the whisper behind it, I hear only the low buzz of electricity. I do not touch it. It is not a rule that holds me back but superstition; I imagine, faint and foolish, that the door will test me as well, that if I put my palm on it now it will know.

There is a glass stair that curves around the lobby; beyond it a bar whose shelves are lit like theater from beneath the bottles. The hallways have art—abstract things in shades of indigo and oat—that will match any wedding color scheme and offend no one. I count steps the way I always have. I count because numbers stitch time together. The elevator arrives and there is the small thump of air pressure release and I step inside trying not to feel the cold steal up my calves as if the elevator is deep water.

There is no thirteenth floor. There never was and there never will be because some superstitions are baked into American confidence even when we laugh at them. When we cleaned the rooms we called the twelfth the thirteenth among ourselves just to say our mouths could be braver than the brass numbers. It was the twelfth that smoked first. On this board, a small square of backlit numbers, the twelfth is soft and anonymous. I let my finger hover and when I press, it is not a decision, it is a muscle memory like reaching for a list of names on a rooming list to see who needs extra hangers.

The doors seal. I am alone. I watch myself in the gold-polished interior, a figure that flickers and sinks as the elevator rides up. The small lights overhead are rehearsing their flicker, like minnows. I can feel the pull in my knees as the car meets each floor, that small prayer the machine makes to its owner: another, another, another.

The door opens and there is the corridor. New carpet. New sconces that throw their light in patient up-down. The corridor is wider than before, as if they wanted to prove something about largesse after what happened—see, see how free space can be. The wallpaper is pale woodgrain, all suggestion. I take three steps and everything in me goes so violently still I am not sure if I am breathing. The hallway has a smell—this is true. It smells like carpet when it has been steamed, like lemon where the rag has touched the door handles, like the ghost of soap. But there is another smell, which is only in my head, I know it, and yet I hear the hiss in my ears as if air itself is lighting: the smell of singed hair, the smell of plastic hot enough to turn to vapor, the smell of a body running because it must.

On the night, I heard the alarm first as a chatter, as a nervous little toy rattling its beads. We were on ten. Irma had shown me that week the way to fold a king sheet so it becomes a square, how you have to put your hands into the corners like a puppet and then lay it down and smile crooked at it the way you would at a child who almost understands. “The sheet doesn’t know it’s meant to be square,” she told me. “You have to teach it.” She was the first person to make me see the work as kindness. Irma had a child of her own then, left in the care of her sister when she came to work beyond the bus schedule’s mercy. She had a way with the vacuum, a music to it; she moved with the machine the way a dancer moves with the floor. Sometimes I would get to the point where I forgot it wouldn’t last, because women like us do not stay in hotels except to work, and then some conference would come and the men would leave breakfast on the nightstand like evidence of being loved.

We were in 1008. The bed had made me think of a small ship. It’s strange what comes back first. The calls down the hallway that we thought were someone’s television. The first taste in the throat like mint and then the way the air thinned in the room, how you could feel the pressure of the building shift. There was a moment when I thought I could be patient with it, that the alarm would stop and we would laugh about the woman on nine who burned her toast in the kitchenette, who calls emergency when the word emergency is handed to her with her room key. Then there was the voice through the gray: now we go.

When you make a bed you stand at one corner, you smooth, you tuck, you run your knuckles under the mattress to coax the cloth in and your arm aches and the breath you’ve been holding is released. You work not to think about what sheets look like when they’re pulled back, when a body has been there and left a shape in sweat and hair. You refuse to think of the stain that cannot be coaxed out, that has settled into the cloth as if it were always a part of it, woven in. But there was that sheet on the tenth floor when we staggered back through the smoke holding our breath like a bundle and it caught my ankle, how absurdly exactly the linen under my foot felt. I tripped and Irma pulled me up with a strength I have never matched, and we saw the fire is a way of wanting. It wanted up. It wanted the carpet. It wanted the pictures off the walls. It wanted us. Smoke is a kind of language the body comprehends better than the mouth; we do not have to tell our legs to run, we do not need to say the word escape. We bang on doors. We watch a man in his underwear open and stare at us as if we have intruded on something private. We do not have the time to apologize.

Eleven was worse. There was a wedge of orange under 1112 like a letter. There was someone pressing against the other side and the door was hot and Irma cried out for him not to open it and he did, of course he did, and then there were pieces of a curtain like birds. I am saying it bluntly because the part where I was afraid is irrelevant; fear makes a character of me and it was not like that. There was work to do. A man fell on me and I was suddenly wearing him like a shawl, his sobbing breath sticky in my hair. I have not loved many people and I have loved that man. That he was heavy and warm and alive and his scalp stubble pricked my cheek and I could not see because he was clinging to me so fiercely, squeezing me like a child in a wave. I have imagined that he wrote to someone later. I have imagined him and his wife at their breakfast table deciding to tell the story different ways in different rooms.

We did not go to twelve. The hall by the elevator was already a different color, a color that does not exist in paint chips. I had a thought of the laundry chute, of the compact files of sheets that would be in there, of the way the heat travels through habit. Irma, their eyes said, Irma—go. We know some of our names like hymns.

Now, on the empty twelfth as I stand in my dress shoes on a new carpet they ordered from a book so thick it took three men to lift it, I make myself walk to where the old linen closet would have been. There is a door here that says Service in silver letters. I can picture the interior without opening it: the shelves that flex under the weight of folded towels; the caddy of tiny shampoos lined in their plastic ranks; the smell of whatever all hotels agree is clean; the loved, bad broom in the corner with the bent handle; a bucket holding the heavy wrist of the mop; boxes of tissues; the hot-eyed vacuum with its rubber mouth. If I push the door it will give with the resistance of a hand you do not plan to hold for long. If I push the door some of my body will stay in the hallway and some will go in.

I do not push the door. A hundred doors I have opened in this place in another life and a hundred thousand handles I have pinched and turned, making people’s rooms newly theirs again. To not touch this one is an act of respect or superstition; I cannot tell. The elevator doors ping and the sound is clean. A couple in wedding clothes emerges, laughing, the woman thin in a backless dress that makes me worry for her on a night like this, even here under climate-control certainty. Manicured hair is a species of armor; it stays in storms. They look at me just enough to confirm I am not the person they are looking for and I give them a little smile that says I am both a person and a wall.

I do take the elevator down because this is part of the ritual. The doors open on the Phoenix Ballroom floor and I cannot help but put my hand out to the wall and steady myself on the word. My cousin had laughed on the phone when she told me. “It’s not bad taste if the hotel named it that,” she said, the sound of chopped onions behind her, the slap of her hand testing pasta for doneness. “I mean, I’m sorry I’m making you come to this place, but I need you to wear a good pair of shoes, okay?” We are not the kind of family that asked about any of it after it happened. We said: that was terrible. We said: you’re lucky. We said: what was it like? and then we never asked again, as if all answers had come pre-stapled to the question. My mother used to say, in a voice that hoped for blessing, “At least it paid good.”

The ballroom is gold without being gold, designed by people who understand that money is the absence of edges. There are high arrangements of white flowers on tall thin stands that make me think of birds again, and small candles in clear glass that are pretending to be safe. The staff is setting silverware and all the forks look like they have been taught to sit up straight. A woman is half under the head table, plugging in lights, and the man on the ladder is adjusting something that will bathe the room in a warmth that can be bought by the hour. They see me but do not. I am the bride’s cousin, which is to say, nothing in their calculus. That feels like relief. There are windows along the far side that look out onto the city where the rain salts everything, and a woman from the catering team presses her palm to the glass for a long moment as if she has run, as if the glass were her horse, and then she goes back to her tray of butter curls. The napkins are folded into a shape that looks like dignity, like a bird that has been promised a lasting home.

At the far side, near where in the old days the hallway narrowed and turned into a service corridor with a trash room that always smelled faintly of rot and lemons, there is a half-hidden door. You do not make the mistake when you build a new hotel of trying to erase the fact that a hotel is a machine. People like the illusion of domestic life but someone has to feed the ductwork and the pipes. The new door is quieter in its posture than doors like this used to be; it is a matte gray with no sign, but there is a sliver under it where I can see the bruised light of back-of-house fluorescents. I stand near it for a while and the drummer in the band—sound-checking in the tiered alcove—brings his sticks together, one-two, and gives the hi-hat a kiss. The shape of my body in this room is different from any I have had before. I am here as a guest and everything about that status tugs at me. I know exactly where the glassware would be stored, I know the mapping of the ice, I know the electrician who will have to come and adjust the house lights at some point not in the plan, and I am meant to sit later in a dress I borrowed from Maggie—that is a fact I have not admitted because the new manners tell you to be vaguely embarrassed by money, not by the lack of it—and cut my chicken with silver that has seen more wedding nights than I have.

The ceremony is in another room because the ballroom must be preserved for applause. In the tender-swift hours that follow I will sit next to my mother and the two of us will take turns touching one another’s arm in that particular slightly nervous, slightly proud way women do when someone gets up to speak and they do not know if the speaker will be good or a problem. Nina will look like someone I might not know at first because she has decided on a new self with the veil, because she is not the girl who honed a spiteful laugh on her tongue when we were twelve and broke it like cheap glass again and again on me. She will take vows with a man whose kindness has edges; all men’s kindnesses do. He will have a small red place on his jaw where he shaved too close, and he will cry well, which is to say not like a child and not like a man restraining himself either, as if he has rehearsed a way to be soft. People will say the word love into microphones that click at their s’s. It will be too warm in the room. It will be, for certain minutes, the longest hour of my adult life.

But first I leave the ballroom and wander back through the lobby. The bar is full of people who have decided to stay dry rather than try the wet city. There is a man at the end with a notebook who looks like he is writing a letter to someone he used to know. The bartender is a woman with hair coiled in a knot that has an iron opinion, and her hands are clean and fast. I sit and ask for water. I watch the way she holds the bottle, whether she puts ice in first or second, whether she throws away the garnish when I decline it or leaves it to slowly soften the air around the stemware. She puts the glass down and I see the small circle it makes on the wood as something almost innocent. I drink and my nervous system arranges itself into a mouth and a throat and a stomach. Liquids are their own gospel. We are mostly liquid; we are mostly promise.

Someone says my name and when I turn it is Nina. She has on a robe, white with a collar that makes me think of a ship again. Her hair is half done, pinned back loosely as if the stylists are letting it remember its former life for a minute before taming it completely. Her cheeks are pink with what the women at the salon called bridal oxygen. She is not supposed to be here, in the old way of things, where a bride is tethered to a bridal suite with water and grapes and adoration delivered on a schedule. But Nina was not reusable, ever, and I knew she would outpace the rules.

“I knew you’d come early,” she says. “It’s all going to be inside. We’re being so cool about it you have no idea. I said, ‘We’re not going to let the weather ruin our vibe,’ and the planner practically cried with gratitude. Everyone is being so nice, Mara. All the vendors are angels.”

She talks like that now, with words she got from somewhere else, and I love her because she is trash and angels both. I stand and we hug and her robe is damp on the sleeves and I know instantly she was out there in that rain a minute ago seeing something through that the planner would not. She smells like spray raspberries, like sprayed-on luck. She steps back and looks at me the exact way she used to look at her list of girls for the middle school slumber party.

“Thank you for coming,” she says, cleanly. “Thank you. I know this is a weird place. We didn’t—” she begins and then stops, because the story in my family has a different door Nina cannot have the key to. The door is every time my mother said the smoke made my hair smell sweet for a week and then we were quiet for the rest of the dinner.

“It’s not weird,” I say, because the kindness you can afford today doesn’t have any business waiting until tomorrow. “It’s just a place with bathrooms and a ballroom and some ceilings low enough to make tall people nervous. Also, they have adorable little salt bowls.”

That makes her laugh. She takes out her phone and takes a selfie of us where we look like someone’s future memory of us will look, and then she tucks it in her robe. “Come up and see the dress,” she says. “Come say hi to Mom. She’ll pretend she doesn’t think you’re too thin.” She grins. “Also she wants to tell you about the seating chart again.”

Irma used to say there are only three conversations that can break a woman: her rent, her child, and who sits where. I follow Nina to the elevator and she chatters about cousins and who they finally decided to brave and put at which table. She is joyous, and there is the scent of hair spray in the car that tightens something at the back of my eyes. We ride with the buckets of flowers and the women with little kits on belts, like soldiers. There are two little girls in the hallway outside her suite practicing their walk with their flower baskets, arms held tense with purpose, the way people hold umbrellas in wars. The suite is one of the corner rooms with views in two directions and a central room arranged to serve as a shrine to the bride. There is a table full of glasses with a light-dry wine in them for women who claim to not like wine but drink what is handed to them by a hotel staff member with teeth like punctuation.

“Do you remember when we came here and swam in the pool?” Nina’s mother says as soon as she sees me. She is a woman who has decided to make memory her kindness; it is her automatic move. The fact that she has decided this here, in this place, near a woman with a curling iron that hisses like an animal is its own gift. She tells the story of the pool, which is a thing I do not remember. She insists I was five and Nina seven and we ate chicken fingers in towels that grieved us with their generosity. In the mythology of our family, this hotel was always a place where good and ordinary things happened, where men did not raise their voices except in greeting. I let her tell it because she needs to, because the new story is easier to hold if it has a handle from the old one.

“Look,” Nina says, pulling me toward the bed. The dress hangs from a high hook like an argument. It is shimmering and not, a material that has memory in it; when someone touches it above the hips it wavers down in slow sympathy a minute later. I do not cry. I am not certain of the way to cry for something someone else wants with this much ferocity. But I put my hand on the cool satin at the bottom and I say “Oh” in a way that lets everyone in the room exhale. I take a little plate and let someone put on it two berries and a wedge of cheese and a pretzel and I eat them and the pretzel sticks to the roof of my mouth in exactly the way pretzels do.

I excuse myself eventually. There are parts of these rooms I have to see, and parts I must not.

There is a hallway that leads past a little room with an ice machine. I stand there a long time watching people come up to fill their buckets. It is fifteen minutes to the ceremony and people are not outside. There is a man with his tie draped around his neck like a lanyard, a child who pounds the lever with both hands until his mother stares him into stopping, two teenage girls who speak to each other in a language I do not know that makes me feel like the human species has a slang I will never get to learn and that this is fine.

Across from the ice machine is the door to the service elevator. At least perhaps I have described it that way in my mind because I rely on fixed points. Appearing next to it is a young woman with a cart. She is so young I would like to hold her head in my hands and tell her something her mother told her while she slept as a joke when she was little, that she is the breath someone else took before stepping into something difficult. She wears a name tag: Luisa. Her hair is in a tight, severe ponytail like a lighthouse rope. On her cart are things so familiar my fingers itch: amenity bags, rolls of T.P. that look to me like loaves of too-white bread, the tiny bottles in their soldier line, bags for laundry, a caddy of rags that are folded neat and kind.

“Can I, do you need, are you okay?” I hear myself ask, which is a ridiculous string of words for a stranger to hear in this exact place. Her face does not shift to suspicion; that will come later with time. She smiles and says sorry, as if she has blocked my way, as if her presence is trespass. It is the smile housekeeping people learn that offers all absolution and little, or none, to themselves.

“You’re okay,” I say, and the words rush me, bring tears to my mouth before my eyes. It is the phrase I want someone with authority to say to me even now and I am a woman with a job that has nothing to do with the way a fitted sheet is defeated.

“Do you need anything?” she asks, looking at my hands, which are empty. “Extra towels? A rollaway?” She already knows I am not this room. She already knows the shape of need when she sees it.

“Do you like it?” I ask, and am mortified immediately to be the sort of person who asks a worker to taste the taste of their work as if it were a garnish. But Luisa is young enough to still answer questions that she is not sure will help her.

“It’s okay,” she says, looking down at her cart like you would look down at your child, privately, lovingly, in equal parts indulgence and worry. “They are very strict about signatures.” She taps the clipboard on the top and I smile. The way housekeeping in a hotel is really a system of lists. The way you can go home at the end of the day and say there were twelve rooms, and ten were nice to us and two were not, and the rooms we turned over have been returned to the world that pays better than we do, and this is a kind of success.

“Watch the linen chute,” I tell her, so quiet I am not sure she hears. “They can over-fill. Don’t stand right under.” It is something I have always wanted to tell someone a long time after it was relevant. She cocks her head and then nods as if this is just courtesy, like saying watch your step to a person approaching a curb. She asks me what floor my room is on and I tell her and she nods as if that matters, as if this will be a part of her day in a way she can hold a number for.

When I turn away, I put my hand on the service elevator door, just for a beat, just to feel the cool the way you might put your cheek to a gravestone. It is not a thing I would explain to anyone. It is just the act of letting one metal thing recognize your animal heat before you go.

The ceremony is nothing like the ones in the magazines except for the flowers. The words the officiant says are a bricolage from someone else’s poem and someone else’s declarations and the part of the law that requires them to say something about the state. The rain runs down the window in the corner like someone thinking hard. The bride shakes a little in the line of her jaw as if she had just been told something barely funny. Her veil stirs when a vent kicks on. She says her I do like she is setting it down somewhere high on a shelf where it cannot be knocked. The groom says his with good breath. Their mothers look like someone turned their heads at the same time to listen for a train. We clap. The baby two rows behind squeals like a hinge. The staff makes the room different around us while we stand and uncomplainingly drink a thing called a French 75. I hold the glass and think of the way we polished brass in the old hotel, the ugly fixtures in the old bathrooms, the paste of it under the fingernails no matter how much you tried to wash.

The party is a machine. It is the kind of machine you would take apart if you wanted to have a happy life doing some other job; you would put all the china in one pile and the glassware in another, you would chart the movements of the guests like bird migrations. I sit and watch the first dances and feel no bitterness at the way the bride’s body grows to fill what is asked of it and shrinks to highlight someone else’s. I do not get drunk. It is a kindness I do myself these days, to not court the easy love a vodka cran offers. I watch the servers strim through the room like threads, tying us all to one another whether we know it or not. I see a woman drop a fork and blush. I see someone tilt a centerpiece with a careless strong hand and right it immediately as if there’s a universe where it didn't happen that he wants to live in.

In the old hotel, on the night, I found the back stairs by accident. The door had been propped open with something, something that had been placed there long before by someone like me to allow someone else like me to cheat. I went down a flight crooked because a man was holding a dog and the dog had decided an emergency was a chance to go hog-wild with its joy. I looked down the well of the stairs, over the banister, and saw the ribbon of orange pulling up like a child’s paper streamer in reverse. I thought, succinctly, I should not look at this. Irma had gone a different way and for some months after I was angry with her for it, the human error of thinking someone has chosen anything during the fire of their life. I found her later outside; she had taken a man by the back of his belt as if he were a child and he screamed that he was going back for his watch. His wife hit his shoulder with a flat hand because she thought that would wake him up. Irma’s sister called to tell me when Irma died years after in a different accident, and I held the phone to my ear in my kitchen while the dishes dried themselves on the rack and felt that such a call should be piped into you bodily, injected, ought be bigger than sound. In between, we had worked more shifts, folded more sheets, gone to more houses where new babies smelled like milk.

In the Phoenix Ballroom, someone toasts. His speech is short, merciful. There is a long one later, the best man reading something that is precisely the wrong length for its sense, like a face in a mirror that is warped so slightly you won’t immediately say so but you won’t smile at it either. Much later, the DJ plays a song that is new and one that is old, and the old one grabs the room by the throat; everyone sings as if their mouths were a bowl they are pouring from. Nina dances with her father. He’s had two drinks more than he meant to and the heat of him is visible like spirit photography where their bodies overlap. He whispers something in her ear and for a second her head tips just enough that I recognize her exactly the way you do when an old friend turns and offers you a private angle of their face, when the old age of a person arrives first in a gesture. I have a vision of the two of them in a backyard when she was small, of him letting her step on his shoes to dance, a story my mother told me twice. I have not seen my own father dance and do not expect to. I let the thought be a thought and not a fist.

I slip out between courses. I go down the hall I know. The door is not even latched properly because the back-of-house believes itself safe in its invisibility. It opens like a bequest. I am in the corridor that is not for me if I am what I am tonight. It is painted that very specific color that wants to be cleanliness and courage and ends up hospital and marine. Somebody has taped to the wall a schedule, and the names are there in block letters that have been ruined and saved by so many hands: DAN, KEL, AHMED, LUIS. There is the warehouse jerk of the big fans that keep the air from heating. There is a metal folding chair no one sits on but that has sweat on the seat like a prophecy. There is a mop sink with a gooseneck faucet that sighs. There is a cake rack rolling past with racks (empty, full) like an organ and from around the corner a voice says “Two more for 24, but for God’s sake don’t let them see you.”

I stand and breathe and my lungs do the dance with the air they know. For a second I have a thought, stupid in its grandeur, that if I stay here I’ll get to see the hotel’s ghost walk by. That a woman in a gray dress with a white apron will swish past and look at me and I will either recognize myself or her or both and we will nod and then get on with it because ghost or not there’s still a stain in 1512 that’s new and stubborn and the new ones are always the ones that stay. I see instead a man smoking next to a cracked window because the cracked window has been chosen as the place people do that and no sign will stop them. He smiles at me with only half his face and does not try to stop me being here because the back-of-house belongs to whoever finds it. I put my hand under the hand dryer and let it blast my palm. I am not washing my hands but I pretend I am. It is a small theater, a helpful fiction.

On the wall someone has pinned a photo of a Christmas party from years back; I see that the room number written on the back of it in the slant scrawl of a person who writes on boxes. There is the grin of a woman whose name I don’t know and want to know badly and I realize with a shock that a person can die and no one at the hotel knows, that the hotel goes on and is a hungry thing and that this is not evil exactly, that it is just the nature of bodies larger than your own. I pluck the photo unfelt from the tape, look at it, and then tape it back as gently as if it were the first skin of a fruit.

A man in a bow tie and shirtsleeves high on his arm brings a plate back on a tray; on the tray is the half-sliced wedding cake, and he flicks at a corner with his thumb, taking a scoop of frosting into his mouth in the greedless, practical way of people who work around sugar. When he sees me he swallows the instinct to pretend his mouth is empty, and we grin at each other like we’ve committed a crime together which makes it better.

“You one of theirs?” he asks, meaning the party.

“Yeah,” I say, and he waits like a pro to see if I have a real need or am just wandering. “I used to work here,” I add, uselessly and truthfully, the way you might tell a man at a gas station that you once learned how to siphon petrol as a child. He nods. We exchange nothing else that looks like words and I step back into the ballroom. The light snaps me like a towel across the back of my bare legs.

I dance when Nina pulls me. She puts her fingers on my hands the way that says we are doing this, not because we must but because it would be a bad story if we didn’t. Her lipstick has smeared a little at one corner in a way that makes me want to hold her face with my hand and fix it like mothers do; I do not. We turn and the band hits the bridge of the song. The floor under us is not the floor it once was. The wood has a spring in it even though the men loading it in swore they had flattened it within an inch of its ability to stand. Moving across it I am surprised to find every step I take is answered by a familiar one. I do not want to name it, because naming makes it cheaper somehow, a trick that could be sold. But the room holds me and cares for me, and the hotel does its job: collects people like me and staggers their tenderness so no one will leave empty.

By midnight I have held a handful of hands. I have let my aunt press a kiss onto my forehead and then pull it off with her thumb the way she has always done to me after a show of love, as if to say she knows the residue makes me itch. I have watched my mother fall asleep with her mouth slightly open into her napkin the way she did at movies when I was small. I have gone down to the ladies’ lounge, which is not a thing I ever had the right to enter here before, and stood in the alcove under a chandelier and thought about the cleaning rag we used to use for such pleats, carefully addressing each drop of glass with an attention that would have changed anyone who had had it paid to them. I have thanked with my eyes the woman standing outside the bathroom door asking if anyone needs anything, because she is the soldier I used to be. I have taken off my shoes and the carpet has been kind to me because the Halcyon is, after everything, proud of its flooring.

Around one, after we have sent the bride and groom upstairs to a room where there will be a tray with strawberries and two glasses of real wine, after I have taken one slice of cake to my mouth and then another to the inside of my wrist as if seeing if I might pretend to be tasting for poison, I slip out with my coat on over Maggie’s dress. In the lobby the night man is doing the softest part of his shift. There are flowers on the end of the front desk, head-heavy and a little drunk. The rain has stopped. I stand at the glass and look out into the city, which prefers to look back at itself. My phone is in my bag and I could call the number no one picks up at that hour because they have decided to sleep. I could send a message to someone I am not brave enough to be in love with. Instead, I reach into the pocket of my coat and find that at some point this evening I took from the service corridor something that was not mine, a roll of trash bags no bigger than my hand. I turn it over in my fingers as if it were a silk scarf.

It was Irma’s habit, when she was feeling tender toward me, to tear one bag off the roll and fold it small so she could fit it in her pocket. “Just in case,” she’d grin, as if the world could be ordered by preparedness, as if God would look down and say: Ah, she has a bag; I cannot do her harm today. I think of her hands. I think of her sister’s voice on the phone, miles and years of traffic behind her words. I think of the two men in their underwear on the hallway floor, hot with life.

I take out my room key, slick and anonymous, with its logo that is meant to endure any pocket lint, and ride up to the floor they have assigned me. I open the door and step into the cooled dark and switch on the lamp that does not flicker the way the old ones did. Everything smells like the smell that has decided to be halcyon, pale and uninsistent. I take off the dress and hang it on the single silk hanger that has been provided. I look at the bed with its six pillows arranged like soldiers and its runner that is only decorative, that exists to be folded back by someone and then put back in its place by someone else. I pull the runner off and put it on the chair. I pull the sheets down. I run my hands over them as if I have something to teach them that I know they already know.

I sleep with the roll of trash bags in my hand the way a child sleeps with a toy that is not the right one but is hers now. In the morning, the hotel will have a different face. It will have staff like me once was who do the slow cleanup of the aftermath of a party, which is its own rite. They will find confetti ground into the carpet like herbs; they will vacuum and the movement of the vacuum will be one of the only human motions whose sound is not grotesque at this hour. They will find a napkin folded like a secret under a chair and a hairpin stuck in a crack and a smear of lipstick on a water glass that no machine has learned to recognize as such quite yet. They will empty urns and fill new ones. The lobby will smell like coffee and the day manager will be two clicks more awake than he likes. They will knock on doors with a politeness that can be gospel when executed well. They will not know that I have been here again and they will not need to.

Before I sleep I take the little card from the desk and write a note on it. It’s the card that says Welcome and then something else marketing about your stay, and I turn it over to write on the clean side because I was raised by women who did not grow up with pages to spare. I write: Thank you. And then, because that is thin and I am ashamed of thin things, I write: We see you. Then I put money between the sheets of the card, the amount Irma used to smile at, the amount she would put her hand to her chest and say, Spasibo, as if it were God’s name and not the word for thanks.

In the morning, I will leave the money on the bathroom counter because there is no better place. I will fold the card to tent it into meaning. I will make the bed imperfectly because that is the new habit I have learned, to leave other women some work. I will step into the hall and for a second I will hear the elevator the way it used to sound and I will breathe once more through my mouth and feel the slick of the air like a hand on my face. I will ride down into the lobby where the light is generous. I will pause next to the front desk and tell the young man there that you need to oil the hinge on the door near the Phoenix entrance because it complains, and he will nod the way people do when a stranger has told them how to do their job. I will step out into the day and the city will be washing itself in slow strips of sunlight, the way we washed our hands after a shift, the way we always have: with patience, with care, knowing the work never ends and doing it anyway. I will look back once and see the new awning with its flat confidence and the sign with its tidy promise and I will not be afraid, which is the only gift I asked of the Halcyon when I put my palm against its skin. I will walk to the corner. I will lift my hand like a person whose life belongs to her and I will hail a car as if the act matters this morning more than most.
