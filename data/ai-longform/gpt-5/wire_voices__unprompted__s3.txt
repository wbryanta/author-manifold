Number, please.

That’s how my day began, with the lamps winking as if they had a secret I might be allowed into if I slid the cords true. The headband of my receiver left a dent over my hairline by the end of the shift and the bakelite was cool against my ear when I pressed it there, like a seashell pulled to hear the ocean. I worked the toll board we called Position Five, in a room where everything hummed gently to itself, a nursery of machines. The cords were an extra set of hands if I thought kindly of them; if I thought unkindly, they were snakes. But a snake’s smooth cunning is useful when you’re trying to pass a voice from a rainy little town to a man three states away as if it were nothing to teach distance manners.

“Long Distance,” I would say, and every time I tossed the words out to the person who wanted to cross.

I learned voices long before I had names. There was a girl with hiccups who called her cousin in Cumberland every Monday afternoon for a while—the hiccups became her stutter and I knew her by that. There was a man who said hello like a door sighing open after rust. There was a woman who laughed with the sappiest, smallest laughter but it sat on a thick body by my reckoning; I imagined her always in a peach dress, sequins sewed on by her own hand. I named them all because names stuck to a person better than a ticket number or a city code pecked through carbon.

And then, a few months after Martin died—that’s what we called it in the break room, simply, as if he’d been a second cousin and not a man we’d only ever seen on the television—after the coffee had tasted like ashes twice in a row and nobody stopped talking about the country arming itself like an argument, they came to me. Or rather, they didn’t come to me so much as brushed past my ear the way a shawl does in winter, and I caught hold of the fringe.

It was a Thursday after seven, which was when the rates went down and talk became cheap enough to be love. The winking lamp lit up on my board and I keyed in, watching my own hands with the fondness women who sew feel for the way a seam runs straight if you don’t hurry.

“Long Distance,” I said, and a woman’s voice came tight and clipped to the edge of politeness, the sharpened end of a pair of scissors.

“This is Miss Hart calling person-to-person to Miss June Renton, Spartanburg, South Carolina,” she said.

There were the particulars and then the ring, silver stride after silver stride over circuits I couldn’t see. When I got Spartanburg on an operator assist I repeated, the way we repeated everything—one more time so the listening felt official, like an oath.

“I have a person-to-person call for Miss June Renton from Miss Hart.”

“Just a minute,” the other operator said, and I pictured a hand cupped over a mouth, a door hanging open and a name lifted over a parlor.

“Junie?” the first voice cut in impatient as the hush it entered. “Are you on? It’s me.”

“Yes! Yes—oh, hush, Pauline, or she’ll charge me when I have not even said hello.”

“Oh, I beg your pardon,” I said into nothing, aware for the thousandth time that I was a ghost standing in everybody’s kitchen and nobody could decide if I was a thief.

The two of them—Pauline in what must have been a Northern city because of the slice in her speech, and June with a slower something that had to be Carolina—met there on a wire stretched tight as a song string and I was the bow.

We called them person-to-person because it cost more to guarantee the person you wanted was the one you got. I made a little hash mark on the ticket when the called party accepted the charges because the pencil liked the feel of the paper. I set the timer to see to their three paid minutes and, because I am not made of stone even when I pretend to be, I always watched the second hand slide grudgingly through its course as if giving seconds away was an operation it didn’t approve of.

“Three minutes,” I would cut in as nicely as I could, and they learned to say, “Just another minute, please—please,” as if they’d grown up with me at their elbow asking them to wash their hands before supper.

I learned their names quick because they were good with the calling and the courtesy of repeating, as some people were. Pauline and June. It made me think of two little girls in felt skirts getting scolded by a woman in a house dress, and the image stuck. But they were not little girls. Pauline had the scrape of city in her vowels and she insisted on exact things. June told news like it was a pie recipe—slow at first, then in a rush, then back again to stir. On that first Thursday of them, I listened as I was supposed to, to see the call through its lid.

“We got the curtains up finally,” June said. “The lilac ones I wrote you about, the ones with the white trim. They make a morning look like coffee with milk.”

“That room could use some cheer, Lord knows,” Pauline answered.

“And the magnolia’s going; you can see it from the hallway like a woman turned out to look at herself in a glass. It took forever for those buds to be brave.”

“Tell me about something that does not bloom,” Pauline said in a tone I recognized from all my sharper women callers—tell me something less pretty, say me a thing I can believe in.

“Well, Lenny’s ankle still looks like two ankles,” June obliged, with humor that tiptoed. “He won’t keep off it. And the right rear tire keeps losing air something terrible. I told him, change it, but he wants to see if he can outlast the leak.”

“Of course he does.”

Lenny slid into the conversation, and from that and a mention of a blue suit at one point I gathered a husband for June. Children followed like always—no voices but many consequences. A boy named Henry who was old enough to talk back and a girl named Eloise who sat down wherever she was and refused to come along. There were women who talk this way and women who do not. I preferred the ones who did. Their talk went around in a circle but when it arrived at its start again the whole thing had been moved a little, as if on a turntable. Pauline didn’t talk circles. Pauline’s words had ends on them.

The ticket timed out and because it was my job to be both the open door and the one who closed it with a chain latch, I cut in.

“That’s three minutes, ladies. Would you care to extend for another minute at—”

“Yes,” Pauline said. “Another minute.”

“Just one,” June begged Polly-like, and Pauline said, “Two,” so I marked two and let the second hand make its slow humming migration across the face.

It was my little game: each week when Thursday came and the clock on the break room wall hit a quarter past seven and stood there like a stubborn midnight, I found myself adjusting the headband where it slipped and willing them into my board. It often happens with calls, that I never learn if they are saved or squandered after they disappear back into whatever rooms I had linked. At least with these two I could count on continuity, which is a luxury for women who keep house for other people’s urgent matters.

At lunch that night, in the room off the switch hall where the fiberglass chairs goose-pricked the backs of your thighs, Mabel chewed her sandwich the way she did everything, as if if it wasn’t precisely in order it offended her. She’d been on Position One since before anyone had heard of dialing their own distance. Mabel wore her hair so close to her head it was a suggestion of hair, and she took her coffee black because it wasn’t right, she said, to trick it with sugar.

“Got your hots for somebody’s whispers?” she asked, seeing me smile at my thermos. Mabel delighted in suggesting people’s soft spots lay in inconvenient places.

“You know who,” I said. “The Spartanburg sisters.”

“Sisters?” She raised an eyebrow. “Might be, might not.”

“That’s not nice,” I said, feeling the heat climb into my face as if I had been the one presumed to be under somebody’s hand.

Mabel shrugged and blew on her coffee even though it had been cooling all night. “It ain’t nice or not nice. It’s a thing in the world like a filigree brooch in a box. You can admire it or you can leave it where it sits. They tend to have pretty talk, those sorts. Not the huffing and puffing, more remembering to say Good night when they’re supposed to.” She watched me watch nothing. “Don’t start counting on anyone, Evvie. It’s not the work. Don’t write your name on them. Better to write theirs on a card and shove it in a pocket you’ll forget about till laundry day.”

She was always metaphorical when she had to hold a thing away from me without making me flinch. I had a child who brought me dandelion heads on their last white breath; I’d learned flinching was a mother’s first and last sin. He was eight and had been born during a summer so hot you could taste the tar off the street when the wind went sharp. His father answered to Paul only when he was being praised. It had been a long time since anyone had used his name in the necessary way and meant it. We lived with my mother in the duplex next to the bakery, which meant I walked home smelling like icing most nights and Will fell asleep believing sugar was the air of our city.

“Their voices,” I said to Mabel, staring at my sandwich as if it might complete my thought should I raise it to my mouth, “I like the way they take turns going to the middle. Most folks pull at the middle like they think it’s a blanket and if they get it, they’ll get warm. These two, they sort of lay it on each other’s laps like it’s Sunday and they’re sitting in the same pew.”

“Good for them,” she said. “It makes no difference to the charges.”

It didn’t. But if charges were all there was, I would have found another kind of work.

That spring the radio hummed in the break room with wars we had no way of plugging into or cutting off. When Bobby was shot, Dot came out of the ladies with the hem of her slip in her fist and her face still wet, and for once nobody told her to fix her clothes. A boy called from San Diego with an ugly bruise in his voice and asked to be connected to his mother nurse’s line because he knew she’d be up. A girl called collect from Jackson and whispered into the telephone as if to keep her words from being seen by the man whose ring she wore. And every Thursday after seven Pauline called June, person-to-person because neither of them had dimes to spare that might be wasted on a second cousin who’d pick up the receiver and make them talk loud about the weather.

They always pushed it to five minutes, sometimes six. I let the last fifteen seconds slide now and again when the round vowel of June’s laugh made me think of the soft-of-something in a jar. Pauline would sigh, which was her version of thank you, and ask for one more minute. If the board was a riot and the lamps popped off and on like popcorn and men with frowns came and went between us like narrow boats in a harbor, I cut them when the cut was due. I’m not a saint. I am a woman who thinks certain thefts are the exact size of the world and the reason to live in it.

One week June told Pauline about her aunt who’d taken the streetcar wrong way so often that the driver began to collect her like a wayward prayer and deliver her to her own front step.

“He asked her mister to put a tag on her coat, but Mr. Leary is too proud and now never you mind, he says to God. The driver told me himself! He said, Miss Renton, you tell your aunt that I know the brim of her hat better than I know my own wife’s cheeks!”

One week Pauline described the undersides of elms where she walked home, the way the city threw shadows like someone flipping rugs. She was the one, though, who always folded humor into something like felt that could be packed if there was to be moving.

By July, I knew the sound of their breaths as if I slept between them—June’s a bit open-mouthed, teeth catching on her sighs in a way that meant not a lot of neatness. Pauline’s through her nose, the breath of someone who carries how she feels up there between her eyes where no one can kick at it. And like any eaves-dropper who lies to herself and calls it knowing, I put them in rooms. June lived with a yellow crock bowl on her counter and the towel hung over the oven handle; if you washed your hands at her kitchen sink you were instructed to wipe them on something made of cotton and last year, not the good towel. Pauline had books along a low shelf and a chair that fit her because women like Pauline get a chair and make it tell the truth. She also had smudge on a window and a reputation for noticing, and in that noticing the truer, meaner smallnesses of the world were not permitted to sleep in.

I have to bring the world in because it was in everything that year, not like a shadow but like a hand that was stronger than yours was. You couldn’t get to the end of a day with your ribs the same as when you woke. The papers on the stand by the bakery next door were always a little damp with something that could have been dew or could have been a baker’s carelessness, and sometimes the headlines bled. It sat in me anyway, all that bleeding. It made voices brighter going into the week’s carved-out places. If I am honest, sometimes they were the only parts that seemed as they should be, those bright even steps across a wire.

The week before everything cleaved open, our supervisor came along the aisle with his long arms folded behind him like a Sunday uncle and frowned when he saw me working the corner of my mouth with my teeth while I wrote. “No extra marks,” he said not to me precisely but to a place between my left shoulder and the fall of my hair.

“It is on my own time,” I answered out of the side he wasn’t looking at. I had been practicing writing out the words the way they said them because I wanted to see if the shape of a person’s talk looked like the person.

“Your time,” he said softly, amusement like a pit in his voice that invited tripping, “is sometimes our line.”

By August, the evenings were thick. The fans in the hall pushed the air around without changing it. Sometimes the end of a call made the inside of my ear slick. Sometimes I wished all the heat would settle into one place and make a thunderstorm, even if it meant the strike of something would knock out position two and I’d have to run lines from six with my left hand like an octopus under a hat.

The Thursday it shifted with them was hot that way. The heat had been inside since morning and I could feel the least sensation as a bother, the scratch of my blouse seam, the click of my own jaw. I set up for seven-thirty the way you settle your skirts under you when you sit and try to pretend you didn’t mean to look as if you were making ready to be courted by a particular thing. They were always around seven-fifteen or twenty. Something had changed and I felt it the way a room does when a man who drives hard has walked into it. The lamps winked and I set up a Rochester to Omaha and a Philadelphia to Ann Arbor, which required a rate check that sent me to Dot with her long nails tapping a little dance on the book.

Seven-fifty came and I told myself it wasn’t my business whether a person lost the thread they wound for themselves. I told myself sometimes you put your apron over your lap and no one brings you the little bowl of peas to shell and if you go looking for peas, you’ll get somebody else’s and there’s no point in that. I thought this hard. I thought it like an incantation against wanting.

At a little past eight, the lamp glowed for a person-to-person to Spartanburg, South Carolina, and there was the scrape of Pauline’s voice. She sounded as if she had taken the edge off herself on a stoop by rubbing her thumb on the cement.

“Long Distance,” I said. I nearly said, You are late. I am sometimes transparent, that way children are.

“I need Miss Renton, please,” she said.

“What is the number?” I asked for the sake of order, though it was already there under my pencil from a habit of convenience.

She gave it and I repeated it back. It felt significant to say it out loud for the first time in weeks after reading it silent. Spartanburg answered on the second ring because the heavens were watching every little errand that day, and I said my part. The operator at that end put me through to the household and the ringing began, the way water will drip in another room and keep you waiting for the sink to be turned off. Usually it made me think of the counting games we played as kids, the ones where the counting didn’t change anything but made you feel you had your hands on the moment. That night it was a new thing, it was measuring the length of a hallway I didn’t want to walk.

On the thirteenth or fourteenth ring a woman picked up and I asked for Miss June Renton as I was meant to.

“Not here,” the woman said. “Who’s inquiring?”

“This is Long Distance with a person-to-person call for Miss Renton from Miss Pauline Hart,” I replied, and the woman went quiet, but not that quiet that is empty. It was the quiet where something is bleeding and you put your hand on it and wonder if that will hold.

“There is no more Miss Renton at this number,” she said.

“I beg your—” I began, but then did what I am trained to. “Do you refuse the charges?”

“No, I ain’t refusing nothing from anybody,” she said and did not laugh or do anything near it. “It’s just—she’s not taking calls. She’s gone on. Junie’s gone on.”

She said it to me as if I were her neighbor who’d brought over a dish, as if she understood that the messenger is more than the message because the messenger has a mouth to put the message in and a mouth must be talked to like a person or it will close on you. Then she cried. Not a long time. Just enough to set me to rights. The sound of Pauline shifting against the map of silence on my side of the line made the back of my neck prickle.

“I’ll inform the calling party,” I said in the same voice I would have said The line is busy or There’s no answer at that number. The woman at the Spartanburg end hung up the way a person sets a dish down careful, not wanting to break the thing or the table.

I switched back to Pauline and put my ear to the receiver like I would a door.

“She’s not there,” I said.

“I am aware,” Pauline said quietly. Her breath was doing that nose thing, but it was broken like the cartilage was failing her. “We—she—we missed last week because the baby was down with something and Lenny… but I was going to call on Sunday and I thought, no, better keep to the Thursday so she’ll be there and say Oh now then to me, the way she always says Oh now then, like I was some fool out in the yard with nothing in my head.” Her voice did not move. It kept still while a person spoke behind it. “She’s dead, isn’t she,” she said after a moment like a drawer that sticks.

“All I can say is that she is not able to take calls,” I heard myself say, the words rote and wrong. “I’m sorry for the inconvenience,” I might have added if my heart had been any of the machines around me.

She went on without me, which is a mercy certain people give you when you have done your job and stumbled at the same time. “I read it yesterday in the paper because Ruth sent the clipping with a quarter taped to the top like I was going to call the paper and thank them for choosing an ugly picture for the notice. She sent no letter. Just the quarter. There was no mention.” The breath made a sound. “No mention of me.”

I had the pencil in my hand. I had the move that would end the call within reach, too. I held both like you hold a child’s coat, ready to slip over him but not yet put to use.

“We have a condolence policy,” I said because sometimes an operator is a girl with a tray of crackers when a house is full of grief, “where we can extend a courtesy—”

“Will you stay on a minute?” she asked, and the question came with a niceness that made my teeth hurt. “Just for—just a minute more.”

I slid my foot forward till it nudged the wooden panel at the base of the board and I pressed it, as if there were any part of me that didn’t know how to make an extra minute come alive.

“How did she die?” I asked before I could stop the place in me from letting its own voice out. It was wrong, and I have done worse wrong things for reasons I can still explain without stuttering. “Only if you want to—”

“Hurricane,” she said, and then laughed, one cough of disbelieving sound that had nothing to do with weather. “No. It wasn’t a hurricane. That was somebody else in the paper. It was—a car, of course. Of course. She had those slow children and that man who liked the wrong things best, and she had—” She stopped like you stop when you’ve seen a shard you are about to step on and you are deciding. “We used to comb each other’s hair,” she said. “We were eleven and she’d carry my satchel for me so Ma would tell her she was sweet and tell me to help with the potatoes because I had both hands free. Lenny made her brown sugar pie and he never makes brown sugar pie. He only makes lemon if there are guests, for show. He made the pie because she liked it. Or told me she liked it. For five Thursdays we talked about the stupid pie he made because it was sweet to think of him making something for her. Do you see? Maybe I made it up. She could have been too polite to say she put dark sugar in her tea and couldn’t look cake in its face after lunch. She was always—oh, quit crying, Pauline.” She chided herself the way you chide a child who you know will hear you but not heed. “And there is my name not in the paper and his is in there and he is We regret to inform with a black border at the top and mine is a horsefly that slipped into the church with the light.”

The work hall did that thing it does where a thousand thousand miles of voices make one hum and the hum sounds like it is thinking about you. The lamps all up and down blinked; a woman two positions down answered into the mouthpiece with her throat thick and her eyes set toward someplace you go when you want to answer kind and you’ve been knocked into the corner. I sat there with Pauline like a woman who has gone to deliver a plant to the house of a grieving family and found herself in the front room alone with the person who is not crying because she has done that already while everybody else was sleeping and now there’s this terrible neatness to the day, dishes drying in their racks like teeth.

“We can try again,” I said. It was both a job thing and a human thing. “We can—tonight or tomorrow. We can—” I was pitching words like you pitch coins at a fountain.

“It won’t take, will it,” she said. “I thought, I thought if I called the number the number would still be the number.”

I breathed in so carefully my ribs thew up little flags. “The number,” I began, trying to keep my voice in its lane, and then Mabel’s words came to stand beside me like a sister. Don’t write your name on them. I could no more help it than I could help reaching for my boy in the middle of the night if he said my name a certain way after a bad dream. I bent in. “Her voice,” I said, my throat doing something I didn’t know it could, “sounded like the beginning of rain. And yours sounded like the thing you carry from the house to the car when you know you’re going to need it. If that is of any comfort to hear.”

She breathed like something had knocked against the building. “You,” she said, and I felt her place me in a living room I had stepped into so carefully for months and months. “You.”

“I answer the board,” I said. “My name is Evelyn. You don’t have to—”

“Evelyn,” she said and made a sound like a name being put into a pocket under somebody’s blouse. “She did not like thunder. As a girl. She would get under the bed. I would pretend not to notice because Ma would have had her out of there and dressed for company if she knew. I lay on the rug with my book and talked loud at her ankle. I don’t know why I am telling you about her ankle. I don’t know what part of her I should be telling people now that there is no her, not that way, and I am greedy for it. I am greedy, do you see? For the sound of her on the line saying Oh now then like I was a smart dog she had trained to not jump on her dress.”

“Keep talking,” I said.

It was nothing, it was a thing a stranger can offer that costs different than anything else in the world. The charges would be on my head; I could tuck them under the minutes for three other calls if I had to, though that was a math I didn’t relish. Mr. Lansing came past and gave me a look because my mouth was moving. I kept my face set and my shoulder touched the board the way you incline toward a person in church who has said something and you mean to be sure you heard it.

“She bought a hat once that made her look like a drum majorette,” she said. “Ma said it made her look common and she wore it anyway to Piggly Wiggly to get her own change for once. She’d whistle if you sent her to fetch you, just little notes that meant I’m coming, hold your horses, don’t faint on me and make me pick you up. She was ridiculous and patient in the same drawer and the drawer always stuck till you thought to pull it sideways. She—” The little river of words snapped shut. “Do you know what color her eyes were?” she asked me suddenly. “This is a terrible question and you will think I am a fool. Do you know if she blinked a lot when she talked to you? Did she do that when she was looking for the word? Or am I making that up too?”

“She sounded like she blinked, yes,” I said. Perhaps it was a lie. In these matters what is a lie. “Not a lot. Just at the end of long words.”

There is such thing as building a house on nothing but making the space for a person to stand in anyway.

She took another thick breath, and someone in my periphery spilled pencils onto the floor and they rolled four different ways like decisions. I picked up my spare. “Thank you,” Pauline said. “Miss Evelyn.”

“Do you have someone there?” I asked, hearing in my own voice the narrowing a mother’s voice gets when she is about to place her mouth on a child’s hair uncurling from a bath. “Will you go to somebody?”

“I have a man and two children and a husband who will put his hand on my throat and tell me to drink water and nod at me as if he has made the world square again. I have Ruth who will bring me a photo of a field and tell me that sometimes you cannot tell plants apart until you pull them by the roots and then it is too late. I have a bed with sheets that do not need changing and a chair that will hold me whether I sit right or slouch and I will choose slouch and feel guilty for it.” Pauline made a dry little sound. “And I will have Thursdays and not know what to do with them.”

I turned the pencil in my fingers until the point found the shallow well in my thumb that it always found if I gave it long enough. “I can put a notation on your card,” I lied because there were no cards and there was no such thing, “that the operator will route your Thursdays to a quiet place for as long as you like.”

She laughed, which meant I had done something more like right than not. “You’ll wrap them in tissue and lay them flat where they will not crease.”

“Yes,” I said. “I will tuck them in the drawer with the scarves.”

She said my name in a way that parted my bones just there between the collar and the rib, and then she said, “Good-bye, Miss Evelyn,” which is worse than someone hanging up without courtesy. It is farewell from a woman who has decided not to touch the doorframe with her hand when she leaves because she knows she will try to come back if she does.

I let the line hang for one second after the click, as if the click were a child and the second a blanket. Then I pulled the cord, and the lamp winked itself out so politely I wanted to pinch it.

Work stacked itself again like a deck of cards and I cut and joined and asked and answered and a man told me to go to hell and I said, Not on your line, sir. I was smaller and bigger than my chair at the same time. That night I walked home through August so warm I could have set a bowl of butter on the stoop and it would have softened like a heart in a fairy tale. The bakery clanged; the cats along the alley wore the dark like black suits. Up in our rooms, Will had his cars in a row like benedictions. My mother had folded the towels, blue and then white and then blue again, her sense of pattern something she wrestled with like a stubborn cow and won every night at nine.

“Did you bring the tomatoes?” she asked from her place at the table, not looking up as she cut coupons clipped from a paper I had not yet read. They floated when she let them go, like pieces of a boat you keep thinking will fit back together.

“I thought you were getting them,” I said, and heard the pleading in my own voice from another place.

“What’s the matter with you?” she asked, eyes up now, quick. She had raised a child with a man who left before his teeth had learned to lie. Her eyebrows reached up into the lamplight ready to shelter an entire army if they were small and if they remembered to say please.

“A woman I knew died,” I said, and knew I could not make the words obey all the fences. “A woman whose voice sounded like when you turn the first page of a book and you think the paper is too new and it will never feel like a thing that will be yours.”

“The one with the Thursday laugh,” she said, and I was warmed by her memory of how I spoke before coffee and after and in all the small spaces like a woman with pockets full of marbles she rolled one at a time across a table to see the way they moved.

“Yes,” I said, and sat with my hand flat on the table like a flattened bird.

“I hope her people take her photograph down soon enough that the light does not fade out the edges more than a loving touch would,” my mother said. “Otherwise people will come over and sit and say they think they can see something in there by her ear and you will have to tell them about a ring or an earring that she never had, not even at her wedding.”

“She liked hats,” I said.

“Well. Then she must have been an honest woman,” my mother replied, and went back to her coupons that made her feel like pennies meant something in the face of a world that sometimes took dollars as if borrowing them, never to remember from whose purse they came.

In bed that night in the room I shared with my boy till his legs got long enough to push my patience, he woke and asked if I had brought home the cookie the girl sometimes slipped me with my coffee. “Not tonight,” I said. “Tomorrow.”

“I dreamed I was a station and the trains all stopped and nobody got on or off, they just said hello and then they left,” he yawned.

“What did they say?” I asked.

“They said how do you do; and grab your hat; and don’t forget your shoe; and see you when the frogs come; and I got to be the window,” he mumbled, full of a child’s way of taking the world into him like bread.

“Did you like that?”

“I liked your eyes,” he said so absurdly that I laughed and kissed his back where it made a warm indent on the pillow. “I liked you,” he corrected toward the dark. He was already turning into the pond a stone had just dropped into.

I didn’t lie awake. I am not that kind of woman. Instead I slept with the cord under my hand and woke with its mark across my palm.

The next week, I took my seat and put on my headband and lined up the pencils end to point from habit, like a pianist limbering up. The Spartanburg person-to-person lamp did not come up at any of the times it once had lived. The desk sergeant from the precinct called a number in Albany and a woman from Paulding County called to ask how many minutes her sister was permitted at the hospital phone. The boy with the bruise in his voice called again and said, “It’s a girl,” and some of the women on the line on which he said it put their faces in their hands, not because they were sad but because they needed a place for joy to land.

At eight-something, a lamp, and Pauline’s scrape.

“Long Distance,” I said, and didn’t write her name down. I had made a deal with myself about names. They were heavy. Some days you needed to carry only the sound itself.

“Miss Evelyn,” she said, and I shut my eyes a little just to hear it the way it was meant.

“Yes, Miss Hart,” I answered.

“I want to be connected to a number that isn’t,” she said. “You know the one.”

“Yes,” I said. My mouth remembered how to make the shape. “Shall I ring it anyway,” I asked like a very small suggestion, and waited to see whether we were playing Horse with sorrow or Hopscotch.

“I’ve got a pie cooling,” she said. “Nobody likes brown sugar pie except the girl you are not crying for.”

“I will ring it,” I said, and I did. It rolled out into a room that held its breath and then told us what we knew.

We stayed on the line after I got the woman again, the one with the house like a barrel catching rain. She didn’t give us any information we could fold. She said, “We miss her,” which is a sentence more like a dress you stand in and less like a thing you expect to be read out loud. I thought of June climbing somewhere to take down the curtains to wash them because it was August. I thought of Pauline looking at a piece of fruit and putting it back in the bowl and then taking it again because that is how grief eats, like a person who is not welcome at the table but chooses a morsel and dares you to say no. I put through three other calls while Pauline breathed in and out, we are trained for this, we are supposed to be able to mend the world on one side and cut it on the other. I did both. I watched the lamps. I let her stay until whatever minute made its own argument to go.

“I will not take more. It’s not fair to somebody else,” she said finally. Men like Lansing believe keeping a thing for the right time is a skill. Women like Pauline know it is sometimes a wound.

“I will be here,” I said.

“On Thursdays,” she said.

“On all days,” I said. It was not a vow. It was the shape of a life. It was me at a board that hummed like a beehive and flashed like a little carnival and held the far-away between its fingers and gave it back out and hoped the giving was a kind of making, not just a series of little unmakings.

After she hung up, the board asked me to do a thing and I did it. I only cried in the ladies when I got lipstick on the edge of the sink and had to wet a wad of bathroom paper and rub and rub to make the pink go. “Stop it,” I told my eyes. “Stop it,” I told my heart. “There are pennies to be counted,” I told my mother when I got home and the mail had brought the gas notice and a letter that said our neighbor’s boy had been given a number he must answer to.

On Sundays after supper, when my mother likes the way the light comes into the kitchen through the lace because it makes a pattern on the linoleum she says she has seen once in a church, I tell Will to call his grandmother and ask about her week. He ties the cord around his wrist like a lasso and says, “Hello there, fancy lady,” and my mother pretends to be a woman on a stage. Sometimes I put my ear to the door the way a child will when she knows the world is alive and she is not sure anybody else believes her. I learned this year that the world is not a stage at all, even when people dress like it is. It is a switchboard. It has hums and winks and wires that do not behave and operators who do their best to connect what they can while their own mouths shape other people’s names.

That ought to be a comfort. It is. It is not.

Some nights I lie down and I hear all the calls I could not place, and all the ones I did and cannot imagine anymore, and a thousand women’s laughter smooth itself across city blocks I have never walked and a boy saying Mama with a crack in it and a man explaining what tomatoes cost in a different place, and a grandmother in a flat far away telling the history of a freckle. And sometimes, in August, I hear a voice that has gone and the other calling it anyway. Some miracles are built like telephones. You pick up and say Hello into a line that may or may not be working and sometimes, even after the call has ended, you can hear someone breathing at the other end, as if breath could be delayed and arrive late, tapping the glass between angel and human and asking you to answer, please. And you do, even if it’s just to ask if they will take the charges.

On Thursdays, I press my hand to the warm wood of the board near Position Five before I sit down. I think of Pauline’s hat with the band she would never buy because she was careful and then put it on anyway when the day said Do it. I think of a towel with a stain that will not come out and you keep it anyway because it keeps. I think of June’s curtains and whether anybody dusts the top of the rod the way a person ought to, with a damp rag so the dust won’t fly. It’s the habit of women, we attach to the cloth and the glass and the plate and the way a voice says Oh now then and means—well, that is what it means. It means, in a world sharpening itself to a point, a place to sit down that isn’t the piece of ground you were standing on. It’s my job to pull that chair out. On time. With a steady hand. And sometimes, when the chair is empty, to keep a hand there on the back of it anyway, so a person can feel that they were expected. Even when the person who was expected is gone. Even when all you can do is say their name in your mouth like it’s a lozenge you might love for the way it slowly disappears.
