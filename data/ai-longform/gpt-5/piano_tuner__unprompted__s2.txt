He had learned to judge the season by the way the cold traveled into the hardware of his tuning hammer. In September it only cooled the knurled handle, a suggestion as gentle as a shawl offered by a friend; by November it set into the steel itself so that the square end that fit down over a pin felt like a small, concentrated planet of winter. That morning, he could feel the tilt toward it, the tool case breathing out its lanolin and wood, an animal warmth that plumed into the barnlike garage of his rented house and then faded into the draft. He lifted each piece as if counting them with his fingertips—the lever, the mutes like little socks, the strip mute with its rubber beads, the voicing needles in their polished bone handle, screwdrivers, pliers, a roll of sandpaper worn to onion skin, a half-used bottle of powdered graphite, a folded piece of 1000-grit paper for dressing a sticky hammer, a wedge of chalk, a half-crooked brush for dusting keys. The fork, stamped 440 and dull with the thin patina of decades of oils and a careless touch of solder from an experiment long ago, he lifted last. He tapped it once against his leather belt and brought it to his ear, its shoulder against the delicate grit of an old man's hair. The A like a thread.

Emmett closed the lid of the case and latched it. The wall clock's hands leaned at six but had never been exactly right; he had tuned pianos by it for years, more for the habit of glancing than for time itself. The ledger lay open on the workbench, and he traced with a fingernail the inked form of the day—a loop out toward the flats: Elsie Bell's farmhouse, the school-turned-community-center at Stray Branch, the Legion hall after noon, and the church in Spring Vale last. He had written Last Round in the margin and put a line under it the way a schoolboy would underline a spelling word he meant to remember. Then, as if embarrassed by the declarative force of his own hand, he had gone back and tried to soften the pencil stroke with a bit of spit on the corner of his flannel cuff, and only made a gray bloom around the word. He left it.

Outside, behind the garage, the soybean field held a rinse of fog that lay low enough to look like something overturned and heavy, a coverlet tucking the square, tilled earth. His truck, an old Chevy with a bench seat polished glassy where a thousand entries and exits had rubbed it, put a hipshot shoulder to the wind. He warmed it, drumming his fingers without a pattern on the steering wheel, the curled leather like the ridge of a spine. The radio read nothing. He had taped a scrap of cloth over the place where a heater vent blew too fiercely at his shin. On the floor the tuning case hulked like the body of a guardian dog.

He knew the way to Ruby's without looking. The roads in that part of the county didn't so much travel to places as pass through them. The ditch grass stood high and beaded where the dew had decided to hold itself until light. He drove slow, not because of age, he told himself, but for the simple reason of seeing—the fox slink through the ditch, the cranes in the shorn fields, a child’s bicycle knocked on its side under a rusting mailbox, a line of damp laundry hung under a tacked-up lean-to and left to the elements as if elements were what would bless it. Emmett had always taken the time, even when he had more jobs in the book than he could fit in a day, even when the county road commission hadn't filled potholes after a winter of rugged freeze and thaw. He didn't understand hurry the way other people did; maybe he lacked the part of himself that flared with urgency, or maybe in devoting his hearing to the slow crush and release of beats, his life had adopted the same.

Ruby had the habit of leaning on the pass-through with both arms and cradling her coffee cup the way another person might cradle a small pet. That she was always at the same station when he came in, even though there were three other waitresses and a man with a shaving scar who washed up plates and took money, made him think of a player who had learned how to stay in the warmest spot on the stage. She said his name loudly across the clink and ring and held it in two beats, "Em-mett," a caress that held its own echo. He'd always liked the timing of her.

"On your last big circuit," she said, sliding a mug across the counter, black, and a small glass of water because she said the coffee in that place could twist a man's viscera into a tourniquet, and he had once told her he thought the word viscera had both meat and music in it. "Anyone actually playing those museums, or you just practicing a private faith?"

He smiled and pushed the mug's handle to three o'clock, a habit. "Tune a thing," he said, "and someone will come along and put down a finger." He didn't add that sometimes that someone was only him, two chords, something like a greeting to the room.

"You're a romantic," she said with feigned disgust. "Romance isn't cost-effective."

"What is?"

"Certainty," she said, and then laughed as if she had confessed a joke to herself.

She had a son now away in Boise working something with computers, she told him. She had told him this about three times now, worrying around the same three facts: Boise, computers, a girl with a shaved side of her head. Emmett had a son too, and when she had first offered the parallel, he'd nodded and said yes, as if that closeness were a slow-moving lifeboat and one could be pulled aboard without moving a limb. It had been a way of avoiding details. His had left before computers were in houses, and came back in postcards at holidays from a damp city where music was apparently background to everything and nothing, and then not at all. The last thing his son had sent had been a photograph of a child on a swing, dervishing. The back had been blank. He had put the photograph in his ledger under M for a while as if it were a client's address. When he turned the page and it slid out after a month he put it in the drawer where the rubber bands lost their will.

"You going to say goodbye to your church piano?" Ruby asked as she set a plate near him with a cinnamon roll split and buttered into strata and refitted.

"I'll see it," he said. "I'll make it right."

She looked at him with the degree of disbelief she reserved for good men, that they might mistake their own stubbornness for grace. "All right," she said. Then, softer. "All right."

He ate half the roll and wrapped the other half in a napkin and slid it into his jacket pocket the way a boy might pocket a treasure. The coffee hissed slowly into him, unwinding, and he could feel his fingers again. He left a five and three singles even though the check was three twenty-five and waved two fingers to Ruby through the door glass. She stuck her chin out and a strand of hair slid to her lip.

The Bell place sat on a tire-chewed spur off the county road, the mailbox listing toward the ditch like a veteran. The house had been sided in aluminum sometime in the seventies and repainted with a color that had originated as a certain green and now was a tone one would only call end-of-mint. Elsie had tied a string from the porch rail to a nail by the door and hung paper cranes on it, some so sun-faded they were down to skeleton creases. When he stepped onto the floorboards the whole porch breathed, and he thought again, as he had every year, that to move into a house was to pick up another set of lungs.

She had the piano in the front parlor under a lace-draped window with a view of a maple that had grown from sapling to a decision. Pictures of babies in porcelain frames and her late husband's folded flag and postcards with sunsets and birds were arranged in that wide, useless strip of wood above the keys. It was an old upright with a once-cherry case that had been kissed by sunlight until it was honey. He had, over the course of two decades, replaced its damper felts, eased the backchecks that had stuck, reset a dip that had grown yawning, repaired a keytop that had chipped in a story Elsie wouldn’t tell. When he touched it—it was always a "her" between them—he felt the years through his skin the way he would have felt a river's slow undercurrent through a fingertip.

"You'll have a piece of you in everything in this house," Elsie said from the doorway, her voice soft with a morning's biscuits. "Furniture, too, if Will had ever learned to fix a drawer, which he did not, rest God."

"Will and I were a team," Emmett said. "He broke, I mended."

"Were you here when Nate played that… what was it? That song about the moon?"

He had to pull his hand back because the expectation of touching made him ache and he didn't want it seen. "Debussy," he said. "Claire de Lune."

She laughed like bells that might fall from a high place. "I remember he could not keep from chewing his lip like a piece of jerky on the slow notes. And then you tapped his shoulder when he missed something and he glared like a cat."

"I didn't tap his shoulder," he said, smiling at the unhelpfulness of time. "I knew better than to put my hand on him while he was playing."

"No, you did some version of that. A hush so loud he had to look at you, maybe. Mmm. He was something. You'd think I'd see him in town with his tallness and his hat, but I don't. Boys now are like birds."

"He's taller than I am," Emmett said, which was true whether the boy was around or not. He lifted the key cover, eased the bench, which complained, reached into the case and set aside the mementos as if undressing someone delicate. He pulled the fallboard forward until its last millimeter, listening to the joints. Sound was not just the sounding.

When he got the front board off and leaned it against the wall, he looked into the machine. He did every time: as if someone had changed something while he wasn't looking, as if a different instrument might be inside, a baby hidden in a telescope. The action like a neighborhood, hammers at home, dampers like flat hats resting. He could see where mice had tried to set up along the left block two winters ago; a few bits of stuffing still confetti under a register of low, unused notes. He blew a slow breath and the dust lifted like pollen and fell again. Elsie had laid out a dish towel as if that were going to catch what the world shook out; he had long ago stopped requiring these gestures, but he never refused them either.

He tapped the fork, listened, lodged the first mute against two strings, set the lever on the wrought iron and felt the pin through the walls of wood, almost like a pulse. The A. He could hear the beats before they started, the way a man who's walked a trail for fifty years shapes his foot to a root before it arrives. His work had always been a negotiation between the mathematics that existed like crystal in the air, and the natures of the instruments themselves, wood grown under one sky and not another, iron poured on a day the factory floor was humid or a day somebody had overslept. He had been taught, once, to tune strictly to the equal—slashes of temper evenly spaced like white pickets. He had learned after that to sweeten, to let thirds rest easier under a finger, to allow octaves to stretch a little into a truth that wagged like a dog's tail, inharmonicity not a word but a fact that lived in his body, what the long strings did and the short, their thick wires and their thin.

Elsie carried in a plate of cookies and set it within his reach, then took it away and moved it because the bench had cut there the last time she had been in the room and she had not dusted it for that. He was aware of her moving as he would be aware of a pendulum swinging in the next room. Around him, all morning, would be these small offsets, the way people made space for him, the way they stood farther from the piano the longer the work went on, as if watching a man talk to himself and not wanting to embarrass him into acknowledging them. He didn't mind. He adjusted his pads under the unisons, and each time he breathed he could hear the A answering back, then calming. The pin moved under his hand more easily this year; the wood had said yes to water in the summer, then gave it back. It would be a tune that would not hold as long. He did the work anyway. He did not skip steps. He always set the temperament. He always checked the octaves both directions. He always tuned those high high notes even if, for some pianos, the top key was a thing like a map of the ocean—evocation instead of fact.

"She sounds better," Elsie said from the doorway where she had made herself so still that even her sweater pilled itself slow.

"She sounds herself," he said.

"And yourself goes with her," she said. "Would you eat a cookie I made with too much cinnamon?"

"Yes, ma'am," he said, and when he was done with the octave that connected the middle of the piano to its gift for flight, he set down the lever and reached without looking to where the plate would be, and found it. The cookie's cinnamon had been overdone as a person might overdo a thing if she wanted to make sure that it was noticed. He sucked the grit from his molars and thought of when he had put too much pressure on a pin twenty years ago and the tenor string snapped with a gunshot. His heart had pounded like a boy's. Elsie had been the one to hold her hands over her own heart and laugh. "It's a living thing," she'd said then, meaning the instrument, meaning him.

He worked for the hour it took and then ten minutes beyond, adjusting a sharpness in the third octave, doing what he could for a short bass that had always held itself stubborn. He closed the piano the way a doctor might close a chest, careful with the cloth that slipped between the key ends and the fallboard. Elsie paid him the way she always did, with cash and four jars of things she had put up—a jam with seeds, a relish his son hadn't liked, a shade of pickles that sat dim in a winter light. He loaded them into his cab and they chimed in the way jars do against one another if you think not enough about their safety.

"How about your hands," she said as he stood with his key ring out, thinking of nothing but the weight of small, similar circles. "They look like…" She didn't finish; whatever she was going to say had sadness in it. She lifted her own and set them palm-up; the skin was crosshatched with fine lines like a map of streams.

"They do what I ask them most days," he said. "Which is already too much." There had been a morning—last winter—when he had laid the lever over a pin and his thumb had not closed, not right away. It had hung, a door that needed two swings to catch. He had waited, and the hand had complied as if under protest. He had not told anyone. The next day it had been fine, and he had learned the new pattern: how to ask, and then ask again.

She walked him to the porch and he watched the plum tree with its leaves gone to coins, his truck with its long delicate rust lowered along its bottom like lace. "You make a day a little cleaner," she said, as if accusing him.

"I have my share of dirt I bring," he said.

"Don't we both."

He let the truck find the road with a little fishtail on the gravels that had rolled from the shoulder down, and he let out a breath like the first note a player gives a clarinet, unlovely and necessary. The Stray Branch school had been one of those brick foursquares with a bell tower like a hat, and now the tower had a square of flush tin where the bell had been, the rope window shuttered. Someone had painted a mural years ago: ablaze color flowers and faces, a creek with fish hung like laundry because the painter didn't know how to make them swim. The inside still smelled like paste, though no teaching had been done there for two decades. It did duty for rummage and for non-denominational weddings the church didn't want to hold.

The piano there was a spinet—he'd hated them when he was younger for their compromised actions and the way you had to work with a stubby tool and a will to reach the pins in their weird angle front—bought in the sixties then shined at by a hand that had belonged to a custodian named Rollo until Rollo had died in a field buying through the weather with a rake. He had promised people who asked that a spinet could be made, if not pleasant, then not cruel. He had learned to say little and do more. He leaned down and removed its panel and then swore under his breath, and laughed at himself for doing so. A mouse had died somewhere unreachable inside. He did not cuss again, because the building had a way of amplifying everything unseemly. He fished with the hooked end of his voicing needle and brought out desiccation and hair and his own disgust caught like a burr. He gave the thing a little eulogy in his head while he shook the bits into the corner garbage, a thin metal can that still had the words Stray Branch Cougars on its side in blue. He was kinder talking to things that had not asked him to be.

No one came in to watch. The air itself made the only audience, moving under the window, rolling around the algebraic presence of fifteen tables on wheels. He tuned, sweating because the sun had rolled up to the window he faced, and when he'd finished he looked through the opening where he'd removed the fallboard and watched the corridor, and for a moment the depth of it turned inside out and he had to grab the edge of the piano with both hands. He knew the stoop of attention too well to mistake it for dying. When he thought of it that way he could smile at its own anxiety. He was not dizzy so much as aware of the world tipping and tilting within a range not available to other faculties, a sense that had adjudicated the tiny wars between wires for so many years that it had learned to war with distance and angles too.

By the time he stepped outside again, the day had warmed to where his breath no longer made a faint ghost. He sat on the step and unwrapped the half cinnamon roll from his jacket pocket and ate it with the slow relish of a man eating something both sweet and also entirely done with this world's requirement for sweetness, the way it calls and then calls again.

He did the Legion hall quick; the piano there had been subjected to nightly B5s hammered by drunks for a generation and then a ceremonious yearly rending by a man who knew "As Time Goes By" as if it were a synonym for his name. The bass notes had bloated like old bodies in water. He tuned it enough that it had its ancient dignity. He nodded when a picture above the bar caught the light and the face of a soldier had a flash in his eye and then it went away again, the phenomenon not repeated no matter how long he waited with the logic of piety to see it again. He had, over time, learned not to take such sights as instruction.

Between jobs, he let the truck's engine persuade itself up the hills, the land moving from cattle to corn to the long-held flatness given over to turbines, white arms feeling the idea of air. He could hear them turn before he saw them, an oiled hush. He had once thought that noise like the breath of a very patient, very exhausted instrument. The closer he drove the more he realized it had nothing to do with his ears at all; it was a noise for the ribs.

It occurred to him just after he passed the old feed store with its windows knocked for transparency and no plan, that he had in all his years never once been stopped by anyone for going too slow. He wondered if slowness itself, if achieved with conviction, bought its own invisibility. Or perhaps in this place where even stone fences took their time, he was simply no problem. It had taken him a lifetime to want that.

When he turned up the lane to Spring Vale Fellowship—there had once been a hyphenated name that married one faith to another and then some cooler man had thought of removing such hint of mishap for something like all-purpose neighborliness—he could feel the past recalibrating. It was a road his truck knew too, leaning a certain way around a particular dip, the place where rain always put a fist to the low culvert. The church's steeple had a tilt if you looked at it straight on, but if you came at it from the north, where the cottonwoods lined the ditch and overhead wires made a static sizzle when it was damp, the tilt looked like a nod. You learned what you wanted to see. He parked behind where the Pastor's space had been stenciled, because there was no pastor now, just a circuit man who came on the second and fourth Sunday and scrolled notes down. The side door, under a light that had had bugs six-deep all July, was unlocked, had always been unlocked until they learned better and then found they had not.

He let himself in and shut the door and listened. He could list, like a boy naming baseball teams, the noises of the building: the heat sifting differently through each register, the settling of old wood that had borne up under hundreds of decisions to kneel, the swallow's nest high by the stained glass on the north that the women had never knocked down because the bird had gotten grandmothered in, the way a restroom door shut itself with a gasp. He walked slow down the hall to the sanctuary and the dip in the green carpet tugged him to the left where James Macon had dragged one boot for years, and though James was six years dead the carpet still remembered.

The piano had been on the right side of the platform for most of Emmett's life, tucked into the angled crook of the choir loft behind a balustrade that had kept toddlers and their fingers from running scales during an offertory. He had tuned it that way once, his back to the pews, and then had spoken with Sam the old pastor, and they had dragged the thing a foot, then another, then three, until they had set it where its sound would go out to the left, under the loft, and up. There were tongues who did not like change, and then suddenly there were tongues who remarked that the piano had gone back to sounding fine again. "This place," Sam had said next to him, his breath barley-sweet. "It's a lung."

He stood over it now without touching it, his hands behind him, the fork in his pocket like a little cold heart. The bench was the same, the same gouge on its underside from where someone had hidden something there once and yanked it away quick when a girl had come around the corner. He slid his hand along the side of the lid and felt the mar in the wood, the one he'd made himself on a dry winter Sunday when he'd laid the lever down too close and it had slid. When Nate had sat on that bench he had been eleven, and his feet had not found the floor on their own—they had set a footstool, a home-made box with a rosette carved in its side by someone who understood relief enough to make it symbol and not a mouth. Each of his son's ankles had curled slightly because of the height, and Emmett had worried that the tension would go up and seize his knees, and then had corrected himself—it was not his job to look at posture in a boy whose fingers were about to outlaw their childhood.

There had been three other children that day who played and played as if the sun were not capable of setting in any useful hurry. One had been Amanda Self with a lace ribbon in her hair and the will to emphasize every last note in "Für Elise" as if emphasis itself were the measure of love. Emmett had sat three pews from the front on the end by the aisle. Ruth had sat in the middle and had put her hand on his wrist once, in a coded system they had used for years when the world was going to go one way and they wanted to remind each other of another. He had been thinking of the B two octaves below middle C and how he had not, perhaps, trimmed its unison quite enough; he had been thinking that Nate's second piece was a Bach prelude and that Nate had a way of placing his thumb that was clean and that he had, in their kitchen at their old table, said "clean" and Nate had flinched. He had been thinking of how, when a boy plays a piece of Bach, the world moves back into itself for a while. We ask small things to pull great weights, he had thought, and nodded and then not nodded. He had, in other words, been many places and too few.

When Nate had stood, the noise the bench made had been tiny but clear enough to part some woman's heart, and the boy had looked out at the faces as if they were ladybugs, countable. He had started. He had started so soft that Emmett had thought for a second that there was a noise in the building that had not been there before, a thin silver noise that came out of paper, and then he understood that his son had chosen his first chord to be an offering and not a flag. It had been perfect. The second had been perfect. Emmett had moved in his seat and made of his own small shift nothing. He had heard then, in the high D, a beat. A small one. A horse thinking of the fence. It would not have bothered a raccoon. It had not mattered. The child played on. The beat stopped. Then he heard—this time not just with his own apparatus but with a man's lifetime of instruments—a thing weeks back in the making, years in the making, maybe, a place where a string had set its mind to moving away from rightness. The third unison on the F above the staff jostled a hair. He should have taken this information and closed it in his rib cage as if it were a woman meaning to tell him of a crime, he should have made of it a little island where he could go and stand and look and not act. He should have done what the fathers do when they are wise. He had stood instead.

What he had intended—he had replayed this tilt of intent so many times that it had become a groove, a track his horse could go blind and still find—what he had intended was to walk, as if going to the restroom, to the side, to the little drawer where he kept the lever, to step to the piano the way one approaches a startled animal, to lean over the instrument's world and lay the tool down over the minded pin and touch it until the beat lay down as if it had chosen to do so. He had stood and his name had stood on him like a sign and all the people in the first three pews had looked at him and then the boy had looked up and in that look had been the entire book of all possible silent conversations between men who were related and had not yet made the truce they needed to make. "Sit down," Ruth had hissed at a volume that would never be counted a hiss and had put her hand on his belt. He had sat. If the world had stopped then, the only cost would have been that look. It did not. In his own head the beat rose up and became six beats and then ocean. He had twitched instead. It was nothing. It was a man not able, that day, to be small. Nate had looked up once more, this time at his hands, and played the rest of his piece with no errors his father would have permitted himself to note, and then he had stood and walked off the platform without acknowledging the applause except to blink. He had not, from that day, played again where anyone could hear. He had, in a summer when Emmett thought the disagreeable matter had been washed down the drain of unimportance, cut the pad of his ring finger with a utility knife doing a job for a neighbor as if blood could make his choice final. Ruth had taped it and clucked and then walked into the other room and leaned her shoulder for a long minute on the doorjamb where no one could see her.

"Hello?" a woman's voice said from somewhere in the building. "Hello? If that's you, Mr. Hargreaves, the spare cups are gone. The kids took them." He had not gone by Hargreaves in anyone's mouth in this county since his mechanic had drawn his name out like a song on a day in 1981, but he knew that voice. Ada had kept the keys for the place since the time when men had lived by saying women needed help. She was sixty, and then she was seventy, and now she was eighty-two and still wore tennis shoes with arch support and still scolded his back with how he lifted his case.

"I'm here," he said. "Leave me the cups you have left."

She put her head around the doorway and grinned like a schoolgirl—what age did to some people was not entirely unkind. "You look like a boy about to climb a fence," she said. "That hair of yours is mad today."

"It's always been straight with an argument at its end," he said, and she stepped into the sanctuary and put a hand on the pew as if to reassure it. "You tell me if you need heat up. This church takes heat as a suggestion. I'm going to take down the last of the bulletins and go home and feed my cat a thing it will not eat. There is a note—I put a magnet on the fridge. About the check."

"I'll read it," he said.

She stepped close and put two fingers to his shirtsleeve, to the little knob of his elbow like a person might check the doneness of a steak. "You are good to do this," she said. "No one will notice and it will matter."

"I am slow to stop what I do," he admitted.

"Well," she said. "Then we are a pair. Lock up. You know we depend on you to make everybody honest."

He nodded and when she had left he moved his kit up to the platform and set it down as quietly as he could, though quiet was relative in a place that had learned to magnify exhale. He lifted the lid, pushed the prop, slid the fallboard forward and took off the front panel. The instrument inside—an old Baldwin upright—rinsed with a light his old eyes would once have called churchlight and now would call late. The strings rusted a color some interior designer would call burnished and some other person would call old blood. He knew which pins had always been reluctant and which had tried to take him with them, sticking and then giving a sorry little heave. He set the fork once and slid his left hand against the fork's leg to hush it while he put the lever on A. The first time he stroked the string his right thumb forgot and did not close. He paused. He waited, and then it did, as if acquiescing to a compromise to which no one had been invited. He leaned his temple into the end of the case the way he always did, a man making not love, he thought with a small private amusement, but vantage. He breathed. He brought the note down into a place where the world slowed its quiver and made a stillness he recognized. He smiled and moved on.

By the time he had worked the temperament across the center, the light on the sanctuary's north glass had changed to a cooler shade, a long cloud had shouldered itself in. The swallow came, hovered once at her doorway and then went away because there were no idiots worshipping at her nest today. He tuned octaves for the sport of it, leading the top notes out of their cattiness. He worked the low notes into something that did not grunt but made of its slow vibrating a tone you could put against a voice and not flinch. He did the thing, at the last, that made him longer: he voiced. He set the needle to work in the grooves two men in Cincinnati had cut into these hammers sixty-four years ago, and he pricked them three at a time, a tiny triune vertigo giving to the felt the humility it had forgotten. He made the treble two shades less metallic and the tenor less like an old woman's shawl. He made the top note, the one that did not deserve, in any world, to be a note, into a whisper that would not suffer the mockery of a child. He did this as if someone would play Brahms there next Sunday. He did it for Ada's cat. He did it because when he was twelve he had opened his father's wristwatch on a bridge and watched the balance spring breathe and had thought, what a wonder that the world is made of such necessary defects.

At some point in the voicing he put the needles down and leaned his head in and pressed his ear, very gently, to the soundboard. He had done that when he was younger because there had been a simple joy in hearing a whole chord as resonance, in knowing the sound not as a tone but as the way a wall of wood thrummed. He did it now because he could not hear as he had, because the hair cells had given themselves up to the years the way hay gives itself up to a wagon. The chord he had struck had been a C major. Against the board it dismantled itself and became not a note so much as the building's breath again. He could feel, there, with his jaw and his sinuses, with his skull's little sea, how the church returned sound. He found himself thinking then of Nate holding his own jaw with one hand when he laughed as a boy, as if it would fly away. He thought of the boy being so careful, sometimes, with what escaped him—anger, wonder, relief—that he would hold his own face as a measure. He thought of a time in their kitchen when he had told the boy to listen to the beat of a perfect fifth and the boy had said that if it were perfect it would not have beating, would it? and Emmett had answered the thing fathers answer on their way to their own defeat: not all words mean what they sound like.

He took a breath and composed himself the way he always had at the end of an instrument, allowing himself the leisure to re-check a treble octave, to push the top hammer felts again, to breath-test the B in the center as if it were a candle just about to gutter. He closed the piano with the same slow caution, and then he did not move away. He sat on the bench and waited. His hands lay where the keys were not. He did not look for anyone. There was no one. He lifted his right hand and set it down in the middle and pressed without force a chord that had meant "we are done here" in his head for his whole life. F major, because he had once read somewhere that F was the tenderest key. The little chord swelled in the building and then, well-bred, stopped.

Only then did he try a few notes he would have avoided in the regular run of a working day. He played the opening to "Be Thou My Vision," slow, and then stopped because he could not pull the rest up and did not want to summon lyrics. He played a run of scales because they had, for as long as he could remember, insisted on being their own company, the way lines on a road hold a lane even when you swerve. His left hand joined, halting with an ache, and then something in the bones decided to collaborate and it smoothed. He was aware, as he had not let himself be in years, of the fact that he wanted to call his son's name into the room. But names had powers he did not trust. To say the name would be to risk the coil of regret, the crackle of old argument he had with himself in the truck when the weather turned and he had no radio to break his head's habit. He did not say it. He let the scales do what their predictabilities do: comfort you into somebody else.

When he unpropped the lid and put all the parts back into the order that made the instrument look untouched, he left, as he always did, the smallest of things on the key bed, a half piece of chalk, though he knew there was no one to come with him after the service to see what he had done, no choirboy to lift his eyebrows at that little gesture and think it was a code. He wrote a note on a bulletin from last month and taped it to the inside of the case with a bit of blue painter's tape. It said, Thank you for all your years. It said, There is more to fix than this. It said, I will not be here after this month. He signed it with the H he had always made like a gate, and something that might have been his name and might have been a child's drawing of a river.

At the door to the hall he paused. Ada's refrigerator magnet held a picture of a cat that was not her cat, a joke he had heard her tell more than once: The cat in the house is never the one in the brochure. He smiled and put his hand on the doorjamb the way Ruth had done the day their boy had turned down a scholarship with a shake of his head so gentle and decisive that anyone who loved him would have had to have learned, in that moment, to set themselves against their own will. Ruth had been gone five years now, the house too big and too quiet and then the garage had become the heart of it because inside was his work and his ledger and all the littleities he could arrange into sense. He did not know, this morning, what would fill its air in the same way. He suspected that emptiness itself had a climate and that he was tuned to it more than he had allowed in talk.

He locked the side door and stood with his hand on the cold metal for the extra second he always did, as if holding a pulse down. The sky had decided to dishrag itself, the light muddled. The wedges of field across the road had a hawk sitting on a telephone pole crossarm, his head moving incrementally as if language itself were riding past in invisible lines. The turbine beyond him whispered. Emmett put his case up on the truck seat as if it were a baby, and he moved behind the wheel and took one breath for himself and then he took another. When he started the engine the truck made the same reproachful cough it had made every day for twelve years, and then settled in.

He did not drive away immediately. He let the truck run and leaned forward until his chest touched the steering wheel and laid his forehead on its high rim. No prayer came. Or if it did it was horizontal and untranslatable to the talk of men. He waited and then lifted his head and looked out at the church's side, at the bricks like loaves, at the white trim his own hands had painted on a day when there had been a work crew and he had, after hours, slipped inside and put his palm to the piano's lid as if it had said his name. He pulled a jar of Elsie's relish onto his lap and held it like something that might break. He put it back. He eased the truck into reverse, then drive, and he took his foot off the brake as if he'd been given permission. The little stones under the tires crackled like old ice.

As he drove out the lane he looked into the rearview. It did not matter whether he looked or not—he knew the shape of the building in a way he'd known the shape of his son's scapula when the boy had edged sideways through a door to avoid brushing someone's elbow. He drove onto the county road and down into the low place where the culvert pulled water whether it rained or not. A tractor passed him going the other way, a young man in its cab with a face he recognized from no one and everyone, a look of someone who has yet to be told to hurry and has chosen on his own to do so. Emmett lifted two fingers from the wheel, and the boy lifted two fingers back, a bare acknowledgment, like a grace that had no prayer to couch it. When the truck took the long curve by the Miller's place and the church fell from the mirror, he was surprised to feel nothing dramatic happen to his chest or his eyes.

There would be a supper in town later, Ruby had said, a thing with cheap spaghetti and bread and the good butter or the near butter if someone brought the wrong block. He might go. Or he might not go and sit in his garage with the lights to low and an oil rag over his knee and go through the case tool by tool and decide, madly, as men do, what to throw away and what to keep. He would not throw away the fork. He might not pass it on to anyone either. He would keep it somewhere he could lay his hand on it in the first dark hour of the morning when winter finally came and the house's breathing was inarguable. He would lift it and strike it on the leather and lay it against his good ear and listen, and the note, tired and always itself, would walk into him, even as everything else in his life, obediently or not, walked out.
