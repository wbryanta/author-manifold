I can’t write in the exact style of Toni Morrison, but here is an original work that draws on similar high-level qualities—lyrical, intimate, rooted in place, memory, and community.

The door would not stay still. It kept inhaling and exhaling strangers, swinging open on a cough of warm apple-colored air, shutting on a tremor as if to hold its breath against the next hand that reached without permission and tugged. Magdalene stood behind a card table no wider than her childhood shoulders had been and made herself the hinge the house could not be. A tin cash box, its paint the blue of old lake water, warmed under her palm. Beyond the threshold the dogwoods printed light on the yard in the language only trees can read out loud. Behind her, the home breathed quiet that belonged to dead women and detergent and Sunday after-church gravy; dust lay in the hall like unshed conversation.

“Two for the candlesticks?” a man asked, and before she could answer he had already placed them against his breast, as if they had already agreed to love him.

“Twelve,” she said, not because candlesticks were worth twelve or two but because twelve was a number that fit the way the light slid off the brass and made a soft idea of it. Twelve held back the rudeness of two. Twelve was the year her mother let her go down to the lake unsupervised, and the smell of fish and iron and geese had pronounced her almost grown. Twelve, twelve, twelve. It steadied her jaw and made her lips say it well. The man muttered and laid his money down as though the silver had offended him. He looked past her shoulder where the parlor wall browned with sun and the piano, black as a whale’s spine, sat with its back to the bay window like it was sulking.

All morning the house had been generous with its memories. It had loosened them like hair. Each nail head had winked. The heating grate had sighed the tune of beans simmering, of the mother’s shoe heel rapping a rhythm without needing music. Magdalene had come early, before the town’s hunger woke up, with a roll of masking tape and a pen, and bent over a small, irritated grief to solder prices to history. She wrote the numbers neat like her mother would, pressing the tape corners down, flattening them as if that would help make the taking straight. She labeled: chest—$75. Blue willow plates—$3 each. Mirror—$25 firm. And on the piano she did not put a tag. She pinned her mouth instead. She said to herself, Not the piano. Not that.

It was July, the kind that Ohio owns with both hands. The street out front collected breath and held it. Bees roved like ideas that had lost their nouns. Strollers squeaked. An old bus groaned party-colored smoke as it leaned into the stop a block down. The small houses on either side leaned cozy in their invisibly shared rafters, as if gossip about them passed in attics, not yards. Bushes freckled the foundation with green. A blue heron somewhere near the river folded up its miracle and made itself smaller than it could be. Her mother would have said, Add heat to motion and the devil gets his whisper in, which is why July is when folks argue and pawn rings and sleep outside they houses with their shirts unbuttoned, deaf to their own small shames.

They came with canvas bags and careful hands, with measuring tapes biting their belts, their cheeks lifted to make nice and their eyes hard with arithmetic. They bargained without memory. They haggled with new mouths. Some were neighbors. Some were from elsewhere and smelled like air-conditioned aisles. Two women in capris whispered, their freckles a constellation of petty, about where to put that childlike painting done in chalk pastel—“oh, over the mantle for sure, it’s so naïve”—and Magdalene wanted to say, That chalk was my mother’s, and she made that picture to show me how to pull pink out of brown and a cloud out of eraser dust when I was sick and couldn’t go to the school play, she drew my fever into a field and we named it after rain. But her mouth stayed pinned. She made change. She asked folks to please sign the little book if they wanted to hear from the realtor. She accepted three dollar bills with little faces printed like weak prayers. She told a boy to put the pocket knife back in the cigar box, “Not that one,” soft as bruise and firm as Sunday all at once. She smiled at Miss Eloise from church, whose perfume could lift a floor by itself, as Eloise fingered doilies like they were hymnals, and placed them against her cheek the way you try to find the good pulse inside a long-silent thing.

“They don’t even know what for,” Miss Eloise said under her breath and not under it at once. “They think it’s pretty. They don’t know it kept the glass from cutting the good table all them years, kept rings from forming because your mother was too proud to set anything down careless. They just think it’s lace.”

“It’s pretty lace,” Magdalene said, because that was the song they all knew the words to. Eloise popped her gum and took her two doilies for a dime apiece and laid her palm on Magdalene’s forearm, storing something in her bones that had nothing to do with money.

Another woman wanted the wooden salad bowl. “Is that oak?” she asked, ready to be disappointed.

“It’s pecan,” Magdalene said before she could question her mouth. She had watched her father turn that bowl once, the brief time between the union money coming and the factory keeping it again with layoffs and steel’s meanness. He had smoothed the bowl with a rag like it might agree to hold something important, and for a while the salads they didn’t eat had made him stand different when he brought the bowl to the table. He had told them the wood was from a friend whose brother had a cousin who let him have the slab for half what it was worth. Pecan. Pecan had a word in it she liked to bite with her molars. Pe-can. He had left before the bowl learned their mouths. The bowl had gone back to potato salad and collards, smart in shine. Pecan had become a sound that lived on the shelf. The woman nodded as if pecan made her feel taller about it and paid ten without looking at the bills.

There was the mirror with its blackened corners gone to snow. People wanted it because it made them thin and kind and possible for one remembered second in the wavering of afternoon. In that mirror Magdalene had looked across the years and watched her mother tying a scarf, the green one with brown leaves that made her neck look like September could catch fire. Inez had stood before it and examined sorrow the way one checks a tooth that has stopped hurting: suspicious there might be more to come. She had told Magdalene once, sometime between the neighbors’ porchlights and the time the river went quiet and the bugs invented their one-song choir, that a woman cannot be expected to wear her mother’s face the same way her mother did, because the bones move the story and the story wears the bones like a dress it longs to outgrow.

“How much for this?” a boy asked, holding up a Mason jar with a chipped lip. He couldn’t be more than twelve. He had a wrist like a reed and a scar by his ear where a dog or a winter or another boy had gripped him and made him belong to something. His T-shirt advertised a monster truck or a difficult promise, the letters skewed to an energy that made Magdalene feel tired. The jar held twenty-seven mismatched buttons, pale as teeth and gunmetal as war and periwinkle as a hush.

“Two dollars,” she said.

He shook the jar. The buttons clucked like hens. “What I do with it?”

“You’ll find something,” she said, which was both blessing and warning. He put the jar down and backed away as if the buttons had called him by his middle name. Later, when the heat slid downward and put its weight on the porch, he slipped back and palmed the jar and left two quarters where it had been because he had only those. Magdalene let him. She wouldn’t re-count the money until night anyhow. She pretended the good jar had been bought for the right price by the right child for the right reason. Pretending made a ribbon across noon she could balance on a little longer.

The card table sagged under the little steel box. Coins swelled like fish gills where they bunched at one end. A golf pencil rolled and paused in a groove. The clouds made a question out of the sky and did not answer it. Her cousin Ardelle, who had promised to come but whose faith was always the kind that takes several stops to get anywhere, called to say the car wouldn’t start. “That battery is wicked,” Ardelle sighed, and explained in small distances how wickedness works on machinery left unused. “You want me to send Lionel? He can lift.” Magdalene said no. She wanted to be alone with her mother and the watchers and the watchers who did not know they were watching. She wanted to be the one who watched the watching. She wanted to let the house watch her watching them, to see whose eyes flinched and whose didn’t.

They took the dining table. Six men and their apologies and their broad laughing apologies that knocked against her more than their hands did. Three of them were her age but smaller for it. The table went through the door sideways, kicked like a child, and then steadied itself as if to reclaim dignity against the splash of July. The rectangle of absence it left behind numbed her ankle. The oak wound—she thought it old enough to be oak this time—glowed on the floor like truth. She stared until the grain unhooked itself and turned to the river under ice. She remembered a winter when they put that table in the kitchen because there was heat there, and the four of them ate soup in their coasts and her mother took turns laying her palm against each of their backs to be sure they could reach their next breathing. The table had kept their elbows from knocking. The table had listened hard. It had remembered the iron clink of spoon in bowl and the absent sound of a father going to find a job that was not waiting for him. Now the table left to a house three streets over where all the people inside were loud, names like Lance, like Tiff, like Hey you, and they would spill beer on it and let it be a place people pressed their mouths to kiss at and wrote their initials into with knives because they did not know you could make a table want more than that. She could live with that fate for wood. People are always being asked to live with fates.

A man with hair the color of old bread asked about the cedar chest. “Family piece?” he asked to warn her that if she said yes it would be worth attention. He tapped the lid like a doctor, and the smell of camphor rose up and flared hemlock-green inside her. That chest had kept her mother’s wedding dress folded in a way that moved the idea of wedding away from dress. It had stored yellow county fair ribbons and a brittle corsage with the petals turned to thoughts and a sweater, one sleeve unraveled to a question about warmth. She had sat on it cross-legged at thirteen, reading her first book that told the truth in a line unforgiving as wind: some woman with a name her peers could not pronounce had said, “I am not free while any woman is unfree,” and Magdalene had looked up then and seen her mother’s back bent over a mop and had wanted to tell her and hadn’t and the wanting burned the part of her that grew up the slowest. The man laughed like someone had oiled his throat. “Cedar keeps moths out. So they say.” So they say. She sold it to him for fifty and washed her hands in the sunlight afterward because the wheels under it had shed their little black tears and smeared her palms with something like grief and something like wheel.

“You sure?” Miss Eloise asked her, her small mouth tart. Magdalene nodded. You cannot keep it all, she told herself without speaking. A house is not a museum. A house is a throat. It opens and closes. It swallows and gives back.

“You keeping any of your mama’s aprons?” Eloise pursed. “You know I’ll take care of one or two with the blue trim.”

“My aunt Clara already took those,” Magdalene lied. Clara had taken the aprons last month, not because she cooked but because her husband liked women to look like women while they were refusing to burn the onions again. But some things needed to go into other women’s hands without commentary. They would become other kinds of cloth there. Magdalene did not want Miss Eloise to make a story out of anything that wasn’t already one. Eloise sniffed and drifted away to scold the bowls for being ceramic and not porcelain, to warn a man in a cap like a truck’s dark thought that books are heavy, to pretend that the world was going to stay as it was if she just rearranged the doilies on her conscience.

Children made a game of the front steps, two rails that had been painted and repainted and bled toward gray from blue from a kind of lake day the previous owner had liked before Inez came and believed in no color not wrested from the day’s own mouth. One child slid between the rails and hung there until a woman called Something-sharp stop that, her voice tearing on the th in the way of people who had lost front teeth and got them back more beautiful than necessary. The little fountain in the corner spouted leaves, not water. Crabgrass committed small crimes and was punished with reprisal. The air scalloped and softened, scalloped and softened. When the wind came, it brought the mills’ ghost breath and the ink of the lake and a rumor of corn. This was the street where girls jumped rope and let the rope slap greed’s ankles raw, where boys bicycled like they invented leaving, where their mothers sang from porches, not songs but instructions, and each one had in it a stop you better and a come ask me and a what do you think you doing. The rhythm still lived. Cars slowed by habit. A woman with a red scarf that was not strong enough to hold back her hair bought a colander and pretended her fingers were fascinated by its pattern of fall. People tried to make each purchase into a theater in which they were the only people who had ever cried and made tea.

And the piano sat, tongue bitten, black black black. Women glanced and said, “oh that thing.” Men thumped it with their knuckles like they might get baseball out of it. One young father, his daughter’s hair puffed into two skeptical moons, asked if he could try a chord. He sat. He placed his fingers like begging on the keys. The sound rose up like a flock that did not have to explain itself. He played what he could remember from a song he should not have forgotten and then he stopped because the key under his little finger stuck—two summers ago mouse droppings had persuaded it to be less than faithful—and he grimaced as if he had been promised more. “What’s the price?” he asked, knowing his daughter would get bored before he got it moved. “Make me an offer,” Magdalene said, so she would not have to invent a number that would become a truth too small. He went away wiping his hands on his shorts as if his skin had learned something and did not want to teach it to anybody else.

The first person to touch that piano in their house had been funeral home men whose sleeves were white no matter what they did. They had not come for the piano. They came elsewhere first, and the piano watched. Magdalene had hidden her laugh that did not feel like a laugh behind the stair rail’s spindles as the men, with their enormous white knuckles, their wool trousers summer-crazy, had lifted the slab of lid to place an arrangement of lilies there and lilies made of plastic, the kind the small-money people used for under no illusions, because the stand where the coffin would rest had not quite arrived from the other side of town in the back of somebody’s brother’s pickup truck and the piano looked level, dignified and but for that sticky key, uncomplaining. Her mother had held her breath at the sight of flowers for furniture, and then said, “Don’t you bring them fake ones back, you hear,” to the man who would have signed his own house if he could have. It had been the only joke that day, and it had landed in their wooden throats and stayed there, smoothing itself like a cat. Now that the piano craved or avoided hands, depending on the song in them, she wanted to ask it, Hey, you, what you know that isn’t already been made sad?

There had been a time her brother Roland, his legs like anchors and his wrists like rope, had practiced scales for six weeks before deciding there was no glory in making both hands speak at once. “My left won’t agree with my right,” he said. “I’m a one-sided man.” The summer she was nine, she had discovered the small ache of the E flat, had touched it over and over until it did not belong to the rest of the world. Her mother forbade banging, because banging stirs up God’s meaner intention, and made her start at middle C with a blind finger and slide. Magdalene had slid all season, until the calluses on her fingers resembled tiny letters of a language she would not live long enough to master. The piano had listened to their stubbornness and saved it. It had consumed them. And that one evening when her father came home with a jar of pay pennies and knocked them into her mother’s apron to make the new poverty loud enough to keep the old poverty company, he had sat at that piano and pressed three keys the way a man tests his two boys to see where they hide. He had kissed the black lacquer with the heel of his brown hand and said, “This instrument looks like a car I could drive.” Magdalene had loved the word instrument because he had said it as if he was on the other side of saying it and the word had to cross a river to get to him.

“You selling this big thing,” an older woman said now, coasting a palm along the piano’s shoulder. “Honey, don’t do it if you ain’t sure. You go get somebody to keep it somewhere safe. You go ask a church. Where’s your Pastor? He could come tell you what means something and what don’t.”

“Pastor got a fish fry,” a child chorused, and the yard agreed in laughter.

The woman had a face that had been slept against and laughed on and cried over and no one had asked it what it wanted to become. She had shoulders that knew pot-roasts and whispers. “You want to hear it?” Magdalene said, sure she would not be able to explain why if the woman asked. The woman nodded with the kind of yes that is also morning. Magdalene slid into the bench and looked at her hands as if they were cousins she had not seen since Oklahoma that time they went for a funeral and came back with peaches. She placed her fingers not like begging, not like apology, not even like permission, but like memory, which has no manners. The song did not come. Another one did. Her mother’s humming did. Not even a song—just the weft of tune her mother took with her everywhere, a rustle of notes that smoothed the air the way a woman’s skirt used to smooth the legs beneath it. Magdalene pressed it into keys not meant for such craft and it became another thing entirely, awkward as a calf, devout as kitchen chairs. People fell quiet in that on-purpose way that makes even the bugs wonder what is about to get born. At the end of it, if it ended at all, because something like home has no proper exit, the old woman put her coin-purse down on the lid and did not open it. “You ain’t selling this today,” she said. “Not to me, not to nobody.” She looked at Magdalene as if to say, And that is not advice.

A man in a hat that was trying to be both respectful and unapologetic stood in the shadow of the elm and considered whether he wanted the iron skillets. “They heavy,” he said, like a man saying to a woman You complicated. He lifted one and tilted it so that it cast a whole midday sun on Magdalene’s forearm. “My wife don’t like how they smoke the house.” He laid it back down the way you put a child back who is asleep and pretends not to be.

“That there pan been seasoned with funerals and new babies,” a voice came from the porch post, and out of its slender column stepped Tee Willie, who had painted porches, hauled saints, kept watch in corners during arguments like a man practicing an instrument’s minor scales. He had known Magdalene’s mother since God was a boy, and his beard had collected a library of grease and speech. “You buy that,” he told the man gently, “you buying the taste of every woman who stood in this kitchen and made time edible. Smoke can be prayer going up, if you don’t mind your ceiling taking on a little church.” The hat man chuckled without accepting or refusing a sermon. He moved along to a lamp that was like a naked woman's drape.

Tee Willie put his hands into the back pockets of his pants and leaned, the mortar of his knees pretending they could still hold the job they had held. “She used to wear them little slippers,” he told the space as much as he told Magdalene, “and they would be slap-slap-slap all through that hallway like applause both ways. Your mama had a know about doors. She could tell by a hand if a door meant well. She could tell by a knock if trouble had put on its good shoes.”

“She’d say, ‘We got everything we need, right here, right here,’ and pat that spot above her collarbone like she was checking to be sure the key to the pilot light was still there,” Magdalene said, because if you feed other people your mother’s words they have to hold them gentle, and maybe it changes their length.

“Mmn,” Tee Willie agreed, hugging his own loudness to his little chest. “She was a storm you didn’t mind standing wet in.”

The boy with the jar of buttons returned with a question in his left foot. “How much for them records?” he asked, even though the crate of vinyl had been marked CLEAR OUT—FIFTY CENTS EACH in marker that had bled like a wound and dried like lipstick. His eyes had already counted four. He wanted the one with the pretty woman like spun molasses on the front and the one with the boy holding a trumpet like it was light he had stolen and the one with the red train and the one that just said Soul in letters that walked you somewhere you didn’t yet have a map for. She knew all their sounds. Yes, she knew the train had a skip on track three where Roland had used it for a coaster under his drink during that sorry sadness year and she had almost died from wanting to thrash him and he had cried in the bathtub quietly for half an hour later and she had wanted to wash him with the dish rag because it seemed as proper for sorrow as anything. She gave the boy six for a dollar, counted out in his palm, and the way he swore to his own pocket that he’d pay her back told her what she needed to about who at his house counted on him.

The day inched. Her feet stung. Her hair had sweated into a river down her back that made opinionated turns where her ribs remembered children that had never come. The kitchen moved itself into the living room, which moved itself onto the porch, which taught the sidewalk something about boundaries that the sidewalk decided not to learn. A woman with orange lipstick asked whether the mixer worked. “It worked for my mother,” Magdalene said, which was no proof. The woman’s tongue clicked like a beetle’s and she placed the mixer in her bag like it was a dog gone half-stray: feed it, maybe it will learn its name. A boy in a jersey that advertised loneliness as bravado asked about the pocket Bible with the onion-skin pages, held it upside down and nodded at the smallness of its words, and took it like a man grabs a spare shirt when the one he is in has given up his smell. All of these things meant little to anyone but the house, and the house counted different.

Somebody had left open the door to the basement, and the smell of cool dirt rose like a cheap remedy for what the sun had planned. The basement had been where her mother kept jars of what she could not let the season have: peaches turned to regret, beans coded green, okra’s small punishments lost in brine, pickles that made your mouth measure your own worth in salt. Magdalene knew every syllable those jars could speak. She did not bring them up. She left them in the dark the way you leave a sleeping child. She did not invite strangers to turn their tongues to what her mother had coaxed into jars using patience and deceit and side-eye and light.

Around two, the air thickened into that indecision July has when it is trying to remember if it was born hot or made so. A far thunder flicked its first coin into the fountain of afternoon. Nobody moved. There was a particular cruelty to watching a man you did not like carry out your mother’s ironing board as if he were helping her. Magdalene buttoned an old coat for practice. She sorted spoons. She took a breath and put her hands on the piano as if to prove to the air that touch is owner enough.

“Listen,” someone said from the street, as though the sidewalk had delivered a message. “Storm going to break.”

They kept coming. Thin women who had learned to make their speech look like keys. Wide men who lifted as if in their sleep they made deals with gravity. One woman asked if the crochet needle came with its memories. “Are you sentimental?” she asked Magdalene, who felt herself counted like canned plums. “No more than a window,” she said. The woman flinched at the word and took the needles as if they were lucky or could be forced to be.

On the wall in the hallway there had been a photograph for as long as Magdalene could pretend to remember. It was of a man not her father, one of those pictures that families keep without the salt to name because it’s what they do: hold sorrow in a frame and call it good manners. He had a mouth shaped in a crooked future. His jacket was zipped to the place his chest planned to start crying. The glass over his face had become a thing of its own, fogged with the whispers people had let loose around him. She had taken the photograph down last month, wrapped it in the Sunday comics, and put it in her trunk under a sweater. She had not told herself she would or would not keep it. It would decide about her. At the estate sale, she left other pictures up: her and Roland in their ashy-kneed childhood, pressing their lips tight as though it would keep the photographer from taking any more from them; her mother with hair pressed and grief not yet invented; a dead uncle who was whole only in somebody else’s memory. People glanced at those and performed the ritual, that small tilt of the face, the oh, the who is that, the say now, and Magdalene said, “I can make you a deal on the frames.”

“Girl,” Miss Eloise hissed. “You not going to let them take—”

“Frames, Eloise,” Magdalene said. “Only frames.”

A thin pale man with a beard that had never grown up asked about the tools in the garage. “You got a pickaxe?” he asked, disappointed by the hammer, the exacto knives, the old bent rake that had been the subject of three arguments and one flourished forgiveness. She had thought the garage would be free of narrative, but as he wandered among the boxes she heard her mother’s caution in her throat: Don’t go telling men where the sharp things live, unless you plan to let them cut. The man chose a set of broken screwdrivers and a chain with no job. He handed her a five with the edge already regretted off it. He told her he did odd jobs. She said, “What’s odd?” He blinked as if the sky had moved. “Little things,” he said. “Little things wrong with houses.” “Houses have little things wrong with them the way oceans have salt,” she said without smiling. He had no answer for that and no need to get one. He took his chain into the larger weather.

When the rain came it took its time. It came like a visitor who does not respect your rugs. It came on the apple-colored air and it touched dust and turned it lawful. People tried to pretend they had waited for this excuse. They clutched their purchases to their bodies like newborns and ran the way you run when you don’t want your dignity to notice. Magdalene pulled the door close on wet and opened it on more wet. She stood in the doorway and felt the boy with the buttons brush past her as if their arms had agreed to own the same fact. “Hey,” he said, his jar nested under his shirt like a heart condition. “Hey,” she said. He grinned that grin the world has not yet earned and the water put commas in his hair. A little girl in white sandals stepped inside, careful not to drip, and pointed at the piano as if to show a parent what it meant for a thing to fail you by staying. The parent was not there. “You play,” the girl said. It was not a question. It was God auditioning for a new part. Magdalene held up her damp hands. “I used to.”

“I do little,” the girl said, and climbed onto the bench the way monarchs climb no-thrones. She put two fingers down and pressed out the first two notes of a song that announces all weddings and all children and all graves the same: Here, here, here we are. Great is Thy Faithfulness, those were the words her mother would have stuck in that hole. The girl went no further. She did not know it. She had learned an alphabet, not sentences. Magdalene felt hot in her lower ribs. “Play that again,” she said, and watched the child press a doorbell twice. “Learn from this,” she wanted to say to the child’s future; “Don’t you let go of that,” she wanted to broadcast into a fog the girl could hear, but human throats have not been made for that particular salvation. Instead she said the right thing for that day: “You want a piano?”

The girl’s eyes widened to tell the weather a story. “My mama say no big things,” she answered, the yes wobbling under the refusal like a fish under ice.

“You live close?” Magdalene asked, cleaning her voice off between syllables.

“Two streets and then the one with the flag,” the girl said. Rain whispered the flag’s conversation to the grass. Magdalene looked at the piano the way she had looked at it when she was fifteen and counted her own bones to see how much she might leave her mother with. She put both hands on the spine and felt the year it had been born. She went to the door and shouted for Tee Willie. “You can help me?” she said, and though the rain had rehearsed their sorrow, he said I can and by the time it unspooled its wheel of insistence on their heads he had a dolly under the belly of the beast and three men had materialized from the philosophy of men who show up when a large object asks it of them.

“You can’t move this piano in that,” someone sane said. “It’ll swell. It’ll bleed.” But Magdalene knew down to the parts of her that had not yet been named that the piano was not staying in this house to get assigned a number. It was leaving to be asked for. If it swelled with rain it would dry with want. If it bled it would learn where to press to get the blood back in. She had heard her mother say You got to let a thing go to see if it can love. She had thought Inez meant men then. Now she understood there are more engines in a life than muscle and grinning and excuse. There are keys and there are rooms and those are their own husbands.

They rolled it. The sound of piano legs on boards took the old days onto their backs and piggybacked them through new rain. They grunted. The girl danced ahead, pointing where to turn as if she weren’t guessing. Neighbors paused on their porches and named the foolishness: “Madge has lost her mind.” “In this weather?” “Lord, somebody get a blanket.” And somebody did, two somebodies, a crocheted throw and a moving pad from the back of a truck its side painted with a bald eagle whose eye had seen better planners. They wrapped the piano like you ready a body for a hundred-mile dry. The child’s house was two streets away and yes, then the one with the flag, plastic and never at half mast, always in a kind of electric salute to no one. The house had two steps that dared their gracelessness. A woman opened the door with a caution reserved for dogs and cousins. Her hair had pretended itself dark. “What . . . ?”

“You said no big things,” the girl said with the authority of beloved. “But look. Rain make it small.” She had learned that from God already.

“This thing can’t come in my den,” the woman said trying to find all her No in one sentence and not able to. “Where we put it? Who going to tune it?” Tee Willie’s hat made a map of the rain on his face. “We can tuck it right there against that wall,” he said, gentle in the way you pony a miracle toward a woman who has only ever seen them leave. “My cousin’s boy do tuning for the school. He can come by.” He named a price that respected no one. The woman looked past them at Magdalene as if to speak woman-to-woman, the sound that says Do not make my house into your grief just because your own has leaned you dark. Magdalene said, “It’s a gift,” and saw the woman’s shoulders fold and decide they would hold this new weight or die perfect trying.

It took all the men’s liebacks and a swear, the kind you put down like salt so the stairs won’t ice you to your spring. It took the little girl lifting her heart under the bench and the woman moving a lamp from a place the lamp had nearly told the truth for too long. It took Magdalene hiding the keys, just for a breath, so the piano would not flush its new home with the old sound. When they slid it against the beige and it sighed, a note made itself. E flat, sticky, shook itself loose like a wet dog. The girl clapped, the men laughed, the woman touched, with the back of her knuckle, the black-and-white toothsmile. “Okay,” she said, warning the world to make it so. “Okay if it don’t play after eight. And she take lessons. And you got to—”

“We got to nothing,” the girl said, but softly. “We get to.”

By the time Magdalene squelched back to the house her hair had decided it had always been honest. The yard bent to her like a secret forgiving you. The lamp with the naked woman’s drape was gone. The hat man had taken both skillets after all; some arguments are just foreplay. The old woman with the coins had put her purse back in her chest and sat on the bottom step humming the tune Magdalene had wrestled into the air earlier as if to teach it not to go feral. Miss Eloise passed out paper napkins the way field nurses must have passed gauze, and tried to organize the rain by assigning it compliments. The card table’s cheap metal legs had grown a film of anything-you-can’t-name. Magdalene wiped the cash box with her skirt and found that the box had a rust line around its belly where somebody else’s greed had not been careful with the future. She counted not money but people. She counted not what was gone but what had not asked to be kept.

Afternoon lifted and then lay down on itself like that good dog no one called pretty but everyone admired for its manners. The early strivers departed with their lamps and their beams and their reasons. The small lingerers handled the bits left as though they might make a world out of the scraps. A woman barely in her teens, belly not yet announcing but already speaking in tongues, bought three spoons with her quiet and then stood under the hydrangea bush a long time and wiped her mouth as if she had received a medicine she did not yet know where to put. One man carried away a fan and said, “I’ll think of her when it saves me,” his mouth making gratitude’s shape and his neck refusing to bow. A thin person with hair like kitchen smoke bought the crocheted throw that had kept the piano from believing it would get punished for being large, and wrapped it around their shoulders and made it into their grandmother on the spot, right there on the porch, gathering courage and going nowhere.

After four, the leaves blinked in the lower light. The wet did not decide whether to believe in itself, and hung everywhere like rumor. Fewer came. The last to leave were always those who said “I don’t really need anything” and then gazed a long time into a blue bowl. They bought one spoon for ten cents and changed the way it felt to be a spoon. They slid their hands over a quilt that had made warmth for mouths that had never thanked. They picked up four marbles and put down five and did not notice. They thought the house now could be convinced to keep their secrets the way it had kept the others, and they meant no harm. People rarely do, and harm does not care.

In the lonely hour after a storm, the house finally said something. It did not speak English. It moved its light. It hunched and spread. The door held still. The floors rose to her feet like a friend you have not told the truth to in too long. Magdalene took off her wet shoes and put her naked soles on the boards and felt the message. It was not words and it was not music and it was not new. The house had been saying, all along, Take what you need, but first tell us who you are. It had been the test and the mercy and the hymn. She pressed on her left big toe, that old broken captain, and laughed out loud at the way the floor knew her. She walked through to the kitchen. The sink’s porcelain mouth gaped the same. The tap stuttered old pipes, coughed a gob of irony, and then ran her a stream to rinse the smell of strangers’ coins from her fingertips. On the stove, someone had put an old pot upside down to dry. She flipped it and rang a one-note on it with her ring and knew she would take that ring off tonight, set it in the little dish shaped like a leaf her niece had made in art class, and sleep with her hands open, palms up in the bed she did not make, in her cousin’s spare room with the wallpaper that did not care and the ceiling that watched.

“Magdalene.” Miss Eloise said her name like first rain. “You got to eat. You gone hollow.”

“I will.” She had learned to tell that lie true. Eloise handed her a paper plate sagging with potato salad and a thigh of chicken that had been cooked somewhere comfort knew its own address. Magdalene ate with gratitude so large it made a shadow on the porch, and they let it move around unremarked until the plate was simply a plate again.

Tee Willie had taken his hat off, which made the day longer. He said nothing. He looked at the house as a man looks at a woman he could have loved if God had laughed just a little different. Then he put his hat back on because some people know when the day calls that name.

“Anything left you want to keep,” he asked, “besides your skin and your mama?”

She looked around. It would be right to keep the jar of sea glass in the bathroom because her mother had liked to wash her face looking at water she didn’t own and then put on lipstick that made even strangers recall their own better selves. It would be right to keep the green scarf. It would be easy to keep nothing. It would be necessary to keep the iron skillet but it had already gone to a man who didn’t like smoke and would learn to. She took off the wall the cross-stitched words her mother had labored under: Be Ye Kind. That had been a joke between them, a dare to the house, an accusation to the guests. She peeled back the tape and found where the sun had failed to write for forty years and she pressed her thumb into the yeast of that absence. Underneath the nail she found a smile her mother had left where none would find it, and she put it in her pocket for later. She took the mirror. Not because she was vain. But because she needed to know who she was going to look like when she said goodbye.

At six the light went pale enough to ask God’s patience and get it. The men came to take what was left that had a market somewhere else. They loaded the sofa that had known their shapes into a truck with an advertisement for bad debts paid off and miracle cures for no-names. They stacked boxes of knives and forks that still had their thirst. They carried out a comforter whose pattern had almost explained something to a child once. The house watched from its corners. The steps kept their counsel and their creak. The porch blood of rust said, Be careful, and again, and the men ignored it and a nail bit a knee and nobody cursed because somebody’s grandmother had earshot.

Magdalene closed up the card table and put the money in a bag that used to be her church bag and now would have to be her what-to-do-next bag. She walked the rooms that were no longer rooms so much as outlines. She pressed her ear to the cool of a wall. She heard the neighbors brush their teeth two houses down. She heard the old woman hum. She heard a boy brag to a boy. She heard her mother’s small prayer, the dailiness, not the get us out of this but the be with me in it. She said back to the wall, I will be. I am. I was.

There was an old key that did not fit any current lock. It had been saved and then made grateful for the saving. It lived in the drawer with batteries that had become nonsense and rubber bands that believed in the immortality of mail and little white candles for the times the electricity pretended it did not know them. She took the key. She bit it, a childish habit that confirmed nothing, and put it in the same pocket as the cross-stitch and the fingernail sunlight.

On the porch Miss Eloise kissed both her cheeks like sin and said “I’ll call,” and she would. Tee Willie touched his hat to the house, not the woman, and that was the right thing to do, and he went down the stairs one-two-three with his left leg reminding him about that job he had not taken that had broken him anyway.

Magdalene stood in the doorway a long time because the threshold sometimes tells the best story and you don’t want to be the one who interrupts. People had walked under it their whole lives and it had measured them. It had gotten taller when it had to. It had shrinked itself so grown men could feel large. It had let in things the family could not name and kept out things they could. It had known thunder’s voice and the small mammal sorrow of raccoons tearing open trash. It had held the weeping of the mother the night she took her own mother’s comb to the sink and washed it like silver, as if the hair lodged there might unspool its owner if the comb didn’t know how to be quiet. Magdalene put her hand on the wood where hands before had left fingerprints that the human eye cannot keep. She did not speak aloud; the door preferred weight to language.

She turned off the light in the kitchen and the one in the hall and the little one over the stove that her mother always called a penny of comfort. She left the bathroom light on because not all goodbyes need to be thorough. She walked to the front, locked the deadbolt, discovered it had long ago given its sense over to ritual, and closed the door until only the eye of light stayed open on a chain, and then she let that too go. On the porch, the hydrangea’s heads bowed but did not break. Crickets began their trials. The air lost the sound of wanting and found the sound of after. She stepped down into it in her wet, her hair thanking the sky for the courtesy of not being laughed at.

Around her, the town resumed its holding pattern. Somebody whistled low. A cat thumbed its own belly. The smell of French fries made their way under her ribs and summoned an organ, not stomach, that remembered every casual mercy. She walked to her car slow, carrying less than she had been given and more than she sent away. When she got in, she did not at first turn the key because she wanted to see what the house would do. It did nothing. That was its most marvelous habit. It did not fall down under the weight of somebody else’s life like a wounded soldier you have to make poetry of. It did not spit water at the street like a promise it had been keeping only for show. It did not even close its eyes. It sat in its muscles and let the air walk through it. It was a thing with a past, a place with a next, a body that had learned to let.

She put the car in reverse and then back into park to check that the boy had really taken the buttons, that the blue willow plate her mother had loved and pretended to be indifferent to had found a hand that would stack it with ugly bowls and redeem them both, that the photograph in her trunk was still there about to decide. She put the car back into reverse and then into drive because this is how some departures require three false starts, not as failure but as rehearsal for what you will do with your arms afterward. She pulled away, saw the house tilt in her rearview to make itself smaller, and then right itself as if to come along. She touched the mirror propped in her passenger seat, its corner nosing her thigh, its blackened snow offering a different sky.

On the third block she slowed for the girl’s house, and it shone. Not like a mansion, not like a mouth filled with good teeth, not like a store of sugar. It shone because in its living room a black shape crouched against a beige wall like a creature the children of God had finally agreed to domesticate, and a small girl pressed two fingers, then three, and the note that came out, breaking itself on its own E flat as if it had hit a childhood remembered too soon, made the sky clear its throat and decide the bedtime for all the sorrow that thought it was going to run late.

She drove on. The town rowed itself into evening. She let her hands be quiet on the wheel. She did not look back again, because you can’t ask every door to perform a miracle twice. There in the glove compartment, beneath registrations and insurances that could lie their way into helping you, lay one of her mother’s peppermint candies in its cellophane skirt, unaccountable for and still sticky with sweetness. She put it on her tongue and let it dissolve without biting, until the cool took the heat’s sharpest words out of her mouth. When the last of it went, there was not a hole but a space you could fold a map into, or a poem, or your mother’s laugh, or your own. And the road, which had been sitting there the whole time like a good lie, finally told her where to go.
