The taxi left me at the curb with the valet's gloved hand already reaching for the handle, and as the door swung open I had the disorienting sense of a mouth opening, not to swallow me but to recite something it wanted me to believe. The facade of the hotel was all glass now, a skin of reflected sky, its name in clean steel letters that made a coin sound in my head when I said them to myself. The Lark. They had kept the bird and thrown away the rest. Above the canopy, the windows were the exact same number across as they had been before, but set off by polished stone that wasn't stone, and there were tiny brass plaques just inside the entrance telling you about sustainability and filtration and the awards they’d won for having everything in place.

Simon took the garment bag and the small suitcase out of the trunk while I stood a beat too long at the curb, feeling myself wobble between being seen and being invisible. He had picked this tie in the mirror at home and asked, twice, if the knot looked okay. It did. The sky had that gray-white early summer haze, thin as gauze, and there were flutters of leaves from the plane trees that lined the street, trapped dust in their large hands. The doorman said Welcome, as if he were welcoming us to a set. Inside, the air was stirred with a precise scent—green tea, someone decided, without ever having to go to a grocery store to buy it—and music just loud enough that you didn’t notice it until a violin phrase ended and left a bare silence where you expected the next note to be.

There was a bowl of green apples on the concierge desk. New ones, variably bruiseless, shone like glass tulips. In the old lobby there had been a bowl of the same. The light then had been bluer, always too much winter, but the apples had glowed with their own little suns. I had wiped those apples more times than there were apples, turning them under the cloth, making sure not to polish so hard I bruised them. Once a woman with a small boy had watched me and the boy had said, with the supreme confidence of spared children, She’s cleaning the fruit, Mama, and laughed as if it were a joke I had invented for his amusement. The laughter comes to me sometimes like an ice cube against the scalp, startling then gone.

At the desk the woman in the tailored jacket and the man beside her who matched her, even down to the bite they had both taken out of their smiles, looked up in unison. I had expected, for some reason, for one of them to be her, for a moment presented to me like a trick, but of course the old faces had gone. The old ones had been pale in the blue light, a flock of ghosts with good posture. This lobby had warmth to it, pools of gold dropped onto floor and faces. The staff now had richer complexions, a wider range of hair textures. There were men who had grown beards and weren’t made to shave. Their jackets were navy, not black. The olives in the bowl on the bar were bathed in oil that caught light like sequins. On the shelves behind the bar were books that no one would ever read, spines too clean, lined up like soldiers in a parade.

“Nell?” Simon said. I could feel him turning to offer to the air an explanation for my pause.

“I’m all right,” I said. “It just smells—”

“Expensive?” he said, trying to make a joke.

“New,” I said, and didn’t say the other things it smelled like, which had not existed yet in the nose of this hotel.

He put a hand on the small of my back, that practiced gesture that was half earnest and half borrowed from a movie about how to show you know where the other person ends and begins. We stepped to the desk, we gave our names, we were told what we already knew about check-in times and ballrooms and the welcome drinks happening later on the second floor. There were place cards for the wedding guests, calligraphed like whispers, each line a long exhale with a flourish at the end if you had a pretty surname. Our card was there and I could see the flicker in my stomach when I saw my own name written as a guest’s. I’d fantasized that it would be faster, that I would walk into my skin of this place with one gesture and have it zip up around me, but it was slower than that, the way a coat dries on a chair near a radiator—some parts swift, some parts stubbornly damp.

The elevator doors were mirrored now, floor to ceiling. Riding up, you saw yourself multiplied, yourself and the other people in the cabin, strangers who would share with you that small slice of future that starts and ends with the bell. In the old days, the elevators had been brass-patinaed, an attempt at grandeur that always ended up looking like fingerprints. They had smelled faintly of old carpet and that tired, secret perfume of other people’s days. I could tell you who got on at which floors by their shoes, whether it was the salesman with the rollaboard who said room number to himself like a prayer or the woman with the stilettoes and the big straw bag whose shoulders were already burned where the dress didn't cover them. I could recognize the housekeeper coming in with a cart crowded with fresh towels by the particular impatience she wore across her face like an apron. I had even recognized myself in the mirrored doors then as a ghost with a rag in her hand, her hand moving without ever tiring, forever finding new smudges to lift.

A chime, and here we were, the carpet underfoot forgiving in that expensive way that means it cost more than a month of your rent and yet must be replaced sooner than tile. At our door, I lifted my hand at the last minute and let Simon swipe the card. The green light blinked and the door let itself be pushed aside as if it had been waiting for us behind it the whole time. Inside, there were rectangle pillows arranged like a diagram, the bed a stage no one ever slept in except by accident. There was a small sitting area near the windows with leather chairs designed to be uncomfortable enough to keep you from sitting longer than it took you to realize it. Outside, the view was the same city, red-brick facades and brittle cornices, a water tower perched on its spidery legs. Two floors below I saw the roof of the annex where the laundry used to be. The laundry now was somewhere else, contracted out to a company in a vast warehouse in the industrial district, where men and women in gloves fed sheets into the open mouths of machines and listened to the radio. You didn’t smell bodies pressed and released anymore, that wet, human steam that found its way into the hallways through the service corridor door and thinned into cold by the time it reached the lobby.

“What do you think?” Simon asked.

“It’s handsome,” I said. The word came out rounder than I intended and sounded like a compliment you give a man you don’t want to kiss.

He dropped the garment bag on the bed and sat next to it with his hands splayed on the duvet, not quite touching. “We could go downstairs, have a drink at the bar before the welcome thing if you want. Or we could rest a bit.”

“You don’t need to stay with me,” I said.

“This is going to be okay, isn’t it?” he said, and took hold of the neck of his tie with two fingers and immediately let it go.

He had known me before and after, but he had not known me during. He knew the story I told, in the telling of which you choose what to say for the other person’s sake as much as your own. He knew that my hands sometimes went cold in movie theaters and that the smell of rain on hot summer pavement could wake me from a dead sleep like an alarm. He knew that when we passed a building with scaffolding and green plywood, the kind that lists a company name and an architect’s name and an expected completion date, my heart would begin to work as if it had been asked to run down a long hall. He knew it in that good man way, as a series of facts arranged like a row of bottles, easy to look at and easy to forget to dust.

“It’s good to be the ones being given towels for a change,” I said, and kissed the top of his head through the fine hair and went to the window and put my fingers on the cold where there was no summer.

He stood and unzipped the garment bag and took out the suit he had chosen because it didn't show lint. He hung it on one of the hangers with those little felt grips they had cleverly included to keep your straps from slipping. I watched his carefulness in my reflection in the window. He glanced at me again and then went into the bathroom and I could hear him turning on the tap, rinsing his face. The bathroom had that smell of filtered water, the absence of chlorine a presence all its own. They had installed spray heads with several options, rainfall, cascade, massage, as if one could choose how to be forgiven. In the old hotel, the water used to go cold without warning. I had seen men come out to the desk in towels—big men with hair on their chests, their moles like coins—in a rage because the water had gone cold at the wrong point in their shave. Nothing had ever gone wrong at the right point for them.

On the desk there was a card with the wedding’s monogram printed in pale gold. H and E intertwined like twigs. Below, a schedule. Welcome cocktails this evening at seven. Ceremony tomorrow at four. Reception to follow. Sunday brunch. Underneath was a note from the couple thanking everyone for traveling and reminding us of the hashtag we were to use if we posted anything online. It was strange to see the outline of strangers' joy organized so neatly, a scaffolding erected in the air, a place where you were expected to climb and be photographed smiling.

I felt the unease in my body as a series of small movements, a succession of hands adjusting my hair. It was not a feeling of drowning, not what people think when they hear the word. It was more like being expected to sleep in a bed that someone else had made—no matter how crisp the sheets, you knew exactly where their hand had smoothed and where their hand had hesitated. Looking out at the city through the glass that had not melted, I allowed myself briefly to think of myself at nineteen, in the old back staircase on the fifth floor, the clatter of the maid’s cart behind me, the smell of cleaner in my nose as if the liquid itself had climbed up into my head. I could feel the particular weight of the spare pillow still inside its plastic, the air inside it making a noise like an apology. I could hear Sohana laughing two flights below, calling up to me, “Nell, you are too slow for a wedding,” then cackling at the look on my face, and the manager’s voice coming up like a cold front, “No racing on the stairs, ladies,” his humor an umbrella we were all required to stand under.

I turned from the window. “Let’s go down,” I said. “If we get there before everyone, we can find a corner.”

We left the room and the door sighed behind us. The carpet underfoot had a repeating pattern of interlocked birds, which irritated me and then, perversely, comforted me, the way a nursery rhyme does. At the elevator bank, a woman in a robe pressed the call button and looked at her reflection with the irritated concentration of someone trying to read a prescription. She had red hair piled up on her head with pins and the robe gaped at the neck in a way that made me want to reach over and tuck it closed, the way you do to redraw a runaway line. She had a drink in one hand she had clearly brought with her from somewhere else. “Wedding?” she said to us without looking.

“Yes,” Simon said. “You?”

“Spa,” she said, “and then wedding,” and took a sip, and I thought, This is how some people manage. They order sequence. First spa, then wedding. First wedding, then bed. First bed, then breakfast. A map that keeps you fed and rested through your grief, through your joy.

The bar had low stools upholstered in something that would age well and was designed to be unavailable for the stain of ketchup. Behind the bartender, neat shelves of bottles in shades of honey and smoke. There were already men in jackets at the end of the bar, one foot hooked on the lower rung because sitting in a complete chair was too much of a surrender. The bartender wore a vest and a tie that made him look like a version of someone who lived somewhere else. I ordered a club soda with lime. It came in a glass that looked like a test tube for cocktails, narrow and elegant and insulting to thirst. Simon ordered a beer and put the napkin under it the way a man would who had learned from his mother not to ruin nice furniture with his habits.

A man I half-recognized from the pictures on Hannah and Evan’s wedding website came over to us with his hands spread in a way that asked to be clapped by other hands. He was the sort of man who had learned all the rules and decided he was the one who now wrote them down for others. He introduced himself as Dylan and said he and Evan had been in the same a cappella group in college, which he said as if it implied military service. He snapped his fingers and hummed a chord to himself and laughed. He turned sideways so the light caught the ring on his right hand, the one that told you he had already been given something he was proud of. He asked us where we were from and when we said, He from the city, me from the city now, he chuckled as if the city were a thing that followed you around like a balloon on a string. “Evan and Hannah,” he said, shaking his head with fondness, and the fondness did not extend to the waiter who had to lean past him with a tray, the waiter whose hand steadied itself at the last second because if he spilled, it would have been as if he had spilled on Dylan’s story, not just Dylan’s shoes.

He told us the story of how the couple met again in the deliberately unglamorous way of people who believe in glamour but pretend not to. They had both been assigned the same ugly paperwork in their office that no one wanted to do, and while they did it they had discovered that they both said the word “February” in a funny way, and that their birthdays were two days apart, and that neither of them could eat eggplant without getting red, so they fell in love. Simon nodded and I nodded, because it was wiser to nod. We left the bar as the welcome drinks were beginning, ghosts in reverse, stepping into a room that got louder instead of quieter when you entered it.

The second-floor salon had curtains that reached all the way to the floor and then gave up just before they touched it, as if to say, We know when to stop. Waiters wove through with their trays, and the little things on the trays were as intricate as brooches. Cheese arranged in squares, fig jam smudged like lipstick in controlled smears. In the corner, the table where you find your name for dinner tomorrow night, the little fold-over tents arranged in ranks, the calligraphy leaning the way beloved calligraphy leans. The bride’s mother, I recognized her from photographs, was in a navy dress and her hair was the exact color of rich dirt. Her whole body was a teapot, steam rising from the part in her hair. I felt an immediate, unreasonable affection for her because she looked as if she would carry a glass of water across a room to someone without being asked.

Hannah came in with Evan, hands clasped, the room arranging itself around them. She was not a child, and not a fairy, she was a person, her chin strong, her upper lip trembling only a little when she saw one of her aunts, whom I didn’t know, and the aunt said something in her ear and they laughed the way a mother and father laugh when a child falls down gently on the rug. Hannah’s dress for the evening was a white silk thing cut on the bias with a row of buttons down the back that would have taken me an honest ten minutes to do with my own fingers. She had chosen earrings that looked like dew drops. Evan in a light gray suit had the sheen on him of a man who has been told his life will be wonderfully long and has made plans for it. They made their way, as they were supposed to, toward each group, and the groups opened like a mouth to receive them and closed around them like a cup.

We said hello when they reached us. Simon shook Evan’s hand, the shake that means, Thank you for inviting me to be part of your day even though you won’t notice if I’m here and I won’t be angry if you don't. I took Hannah’s hand in mine and the shock ran up my arm the way it does when you are handed a bird with a heartbeat. There are some people you love for no reason that has anything to do with you. “You look beautiful,” I said, and her lip trembled again and she pressed my hand hard against her little not-bone between thumb and wrist.

“So do you,” she said, reflexively, her reflexes good, and then she looked at me as if she were seeing the person holding onto the balloon string for a second, and she said, “I’m so glad you came.”

“I worked here once,” I blurted.

Her eyes widened slightly and then she made that face I wanted to kiss her for, the one where you let your surprise stand and then put your body around it like a cloak. “Before they rebuilt,” I said, and then I smiled so that she would know it was a thing I could say and still use my mouth later for other purposes.

“I don’t know what to say,” she said.

“You don’t have to say anything,” I said. “Say congratulations. Say, Let’s dance tomorrow.”

“Let’s,” she said, and there was the murmur from the room, the murmur that you could sink under and drown in, or the one you could learn to breathe through like air that has a little smoke in it.

The rest of the evening was small fiddlings with the angle of my head, the position of my shoulders against the chair back. I saw set after set of eyes rest on me and then skip off, the way you see swallows flit over the surface of a river and take what they need and leave. I saw the new staff circling, their faces composed, their mouths ready to smile with their whole face and not just the lower half. At the edges of the room where the waiters made their turns, you could feel the air they dislocated with their bodies, the breeze they made with their hands, the small storms necessary to keep a celebration going. Sometimes one of them bent down at a table and whispered a question you could answer with a nod, sometimes one of them stood very still next to a column as if he were trying to make himself smaller, so he wouldn't be seen by a man slapping another man hard on the back.

On the way back to our room, I didn’t say much and Simon didn’t either. In the elevator, the mirrored walls made it seem as if there were eight of us. I remembered all at once the first time I had ridden up to the twelfth floor alone, with the chime sounding once, twice, the door slipping open, the corridor beyond smelling of turpentine and new carpet, the painter whistling under his breath while he taped the baseboards, his tape measure curled in his pocket like a creature at rest. I had thought at nineteen that the world moved along in a series of corridors that connected if you were clever, that the closed doors were all waiting for you to press your ear to them and listen. I was not wrong, just young about the pain of finding the locksmith.

We lay side by side in the bed, the duvet puffed into shape like a cloud pretending. Simon turned out his light and within five breaths, that enviable man-thing, he slept. I flicked my lamp off and watched the slice of city that remained between curtain and tray table. The red light at the top of the radio tower blinked its warning into warm air. In the hallway, a hotel had its experiences without me. Somewhere a man asked a woman if she wanted her massage pressure lighter or heavier and the woman tried both out in her mind and then said softly, Heavier. Somewhere a dishwasher was running. Somewhere a family had pushed two double beds together to make a giant bed and lay across it diagonally, rejoicing in the casualness of holiday. Somewhere a housekeeper had stacked little shampoos in a perfect line and then put them back into the closet because a guest had asked her for six at once, one for each child at home, and the housekeeper had said Yes and then forgotten, on purpose, how she hated the hotel on Fridays when the tips were mean and the rooms were all turnover and the men watched you while you made the bed as if it were a show for them alone.

In my sleep, I walked the old corridors. The carpet had bursts woven into it that were meant to be flowers in bloom, but at two in the morning if you stared long enough they looked like sparklers burned down to their wires. The farthest service door on each floor had a way of getting stuck if you held it open with the stopper, that rubber cone that bruised your ankle bone if you weren’t careful. There was a smell in the corridor where the ice machine was on each floor—copper and cold—and the good rooms were on the corners, and the quiet men came and went but the loud men stayed because no one would ask them to leave. I had learned where to put a knee to leverage the fitted sheet’s last corner under the mattress. I had learned to pop pillowcases onto their pillows with a single well-placed breath. I had learned where to look and where not to, how to find without seeing. I had learned that the women who came with their lovers and asked for extra towels without saying Please gave the best tips the next morning, as if they had been ashamed all night, and that the married men with wedding rings in their keys pocket were the worst, tightfisted as pinchers on a crab.

I woke twice with my own name in my mouth and pressed the heels of my hands against my eyes until I saw purple lemon shapes floating behind them. The second time, when my breath had slowed, I slipped out of bed and went to the window. I opened it the two inches it was allowed and let the city blow into my face. There was a smell, not clean, but alive. A truck passed with the whine of something heavy behind it. I could hear the faint ping of the traffic light’s timer. I stood there until my body believed me that it was not burning. By the time I got back in bed, Simon had rolled onto his stomach, one arm flung up into the empty space where my head had been. I looked at his palm in the light from outside and I pictured it holding water.

The ceremony was the next afternoon. That morning we walked out to get coffee from a place three blocks away that frothed and foamed your milk while it punished you gently for not being young. The sun was bright in that way that makes dirty cars gleam. The street had been cleaned recently, the gum lifted from the cracks so their absence was an absence you saw. We came back through the side entrance past the loading dock, a place my feet knew better than the lobby. The door was open to the alley and from that far pocket of the building came the sound that swelled my chest the way a song does when you have to pretend not to sing along. The sound of dishes, of their stacked certainty, of water running, of men’s voices and women’s voices blending into one soup.

“Hang on,” I said to Simon, who was looking at his phone, his thumb scrolling as if he had been born to scroll and had to work hard at stopping.

“I’ll meet you upstairs,” he said, glancing up, deciding, then leaning to kiss my forehead.

I slipped in through the door as if I had been improperly invited, which I had. The corridor was painted a shade of beige invented to excuse itself. The floor was tile, that practical tile that shows the dirt so you have to clean it or else ignore it on purpose. A woman in a gray shirt and black pants pushed a cart past me with the calm velocity of someone who knows she cannot be spoken to because she is a storm with legs. She didn’t look at me, her eyes already trained on whatever was ahead. A man stood by the staff time clock punching in and his face was set in that squint some men wear to keep their thoughts inside their foreheads. I could hear the muffled hum of the ballroom above, the test of a microphone—is this on?—the nervous laughter as the person testing pretended to know how to joke about themselves lightly.

A woman came out of the janitor’s closet, closed the door with her foot, and set down her bucket. She was small and dark-skinned, with her hair under a bandana the way you tuck a pie crust around its filling. Her name tag said Rocío. She looked at me and smiled because she had classified me: guest, lost, harmless, kind if I was the kind of lost guest who smiled back. I smiled back. It was muscle memory, the way you hand over a pen at the front desk without asking for its return. Then I said, the way a child asks an old woman whether she knew the child’s grandmother, “I used to work here.”

She tilted her head, calculating what I might mean. “Here?” she said, and I realized the building had a pronoun.

“Before,” I said.

She looked down the hall and then shook her head. “Everyone different,” she said. “Only the building, no?” her accent giving to the word a softness as if you could put your face into it like a pillow.

“It wasn’t so different then,” I said, and then because this was not the time to build a museum around my pain, “Are you on housekeeping?”

“Housekeeping,” she said, tapping her chest, then wiggled her fingers as if her rib cage were a harp she kept in tune with them. “Wedding is messy,” she said, smiling, and I returned it with the wholehearted warmth of one who knows a mess is an honest thing.

“Do you smoke?” I asked.

She lifted her shoulders and lowered them in the universal language of secret indulgence. “My break in eight minutes,” she said, looking at the clock. “You know the door to outside?” She pointed toward the laundry, the hum loud enough through the closed door that you put your hand on the doorknob and the sound climbed up into your arm and hummed in your elbow.

“I do,” I said, and waited in that corridor with the smell of lemon cleanser and old water and the metallic tang of an elevator pulley. It was not the same as the old back-of-house, which had smelled older, and of the paints they had used thirty years ago that gave you a headache that bit instead of throbbing. But the feeling was the same, the family feeling of places where you were necessary and not honored, where the small efficiencies were the difference between a day that ended in sweat that could be washed and a day that ended in a long quiet you kept to yourself because no one else had the standing to look at it with you.

She came to find me after eight minutes, exactly, and we pushed the door at the end of the corridor that led to the loading dock and then to the small square outside where someone had placed a plastic chair that had lost some of its rungs. The sun fell in a right angle there and lit a rectangle against the cinderblock wall. It was quieter than the alley, away from the rolling bins and the men unloading crates with symphonic energy. The chair made a sound like a laugh underneath Rocío when she sat. She offered me a cigarette. I held it and didn’t light it. She scanned my face once and then performed the courtesy of not asking. She lit hers and leaned her head back and exhaled as if she had remembered how to be one thing at a time.

“It was a fire,” I said, because I could not pretend this corner of the building was only a corner and not an intersection. “Years ago, in the old hotel. I was working nights, turndown. There was smoke in the service corridor. The alarm didn’t go at first. When it did, it was only on one side of the building. The manager told us to check the rooms and stay calm. We stayed calm. I banged on a lot of doors. I yelled fire and then apologized for yelling. It was the middle of the night.” I laughed, the dry laugh of someone who has decided to be her own joke before someone else can be.

She looked at me with her smoke in her mouth, narrowing her eyes slightly at the word manager the way a woman will when she has lots of different managers inside her head and is trying to see which one she would bring up in a story. “You saved people,” she said gently, not as praise, but as a fact in the way that a wall is a fact and a window is a choice.

“I don’t know,” I said. “I woke them. I put towels in their hands. I showed them the stair at the end and the other stair—the one with the door that gets stuck, the one I had learned how to handle with my hip and my foot. I put my shoulder under the arm of a man who could not walk because he did not have his shoes and the ice, melted from the machine on that floor, had made the floor slick, and we slid and we laughed, and his breath was right there in the gray of it. I left one woman. I left her in her doorway because she would not move. I said, Ma’am, now, and she said, No, and pulled her robe tighter around herself with both hands. There were other people to wake and I thought I could come back and be different with her. I thought, When I have moved these two, I will find something to say to her that will make her more herself. By the time I came back, what was left of her was not enough to hold. I am telling you this because my mouth wanted to say it to someone who understands the way a floor feels under your feet when a hotel makes a plan and the plan does not include you.”

She listened with her mouth tight at one corner around the cigarette. “I understand,” she said. “I understand floors.”

“The men have always known to run,” I said, not bitter, a register like a hymn. “The women count. Towels, comb, jewelry, children, shoes. You put your hand on ten things and choose four. Even now, if an alarm goes, my fingers begin the counting. The smell is in the clean things now. I smell hotel water in my sleep. I smell vacuumed dust. I smell that lemon as if it’s a cousin.” I had not meant to talk this long to her, but the way she watched me was an old way, the way I knew from women peeling apples on a stool and watching the pot.

She laughed at the last word. “Always lemon,” she said, and flicked ash off to the side with a quickness that was not ashamed. “You are here for a good thing,” she said then, as a way back.

“I am,” I said. “A wedding.”

“I like weddings in hotels,” she said. “Always there is a auntie who comes to the door when we are cleaning and she puts five dollars in my hand and says, Thank you for doing this, and I want to ask her, Doing what, the invisible? but I put the money in my pocket and that money feels hot all day, like a secret. I like when the father makes a speech and he cries and he tries to stop it. I like when the little children get in the way on the dance floor and the men do not see because they are looking up at each other. I like the smells of the food. I do not like the smell of the wet dresses after, hung in the closets. They are cold fish then. You hear them drip. And the glitter. The glitter is in my clothes all week like I have been somewhere shiny.”

She had made me laugh. “Glitter,” I said, as if it were an uncle you tolerated.

We sat there with the sun sliding on the wall, and in the pause I saw again with a specificity that beat in my head the hall on three, the ice machine’s door left open, light throwing itself sideways across the floor. I saw the plastic vines that someone had wrapped around the banister last December because there had been a conference then, of librarians, and the old manager had decided that a wink would make everything all right. I saw Sohana’s scarf hanging on the back of a chair in the staff break room, the scarf I had given her when the buses had been late and she had missed her first class at the community college and cried angry tears into her coffee while Fariha rubbed her back and said, What did you expect, something else? as if something else were a promise she did not want my friend to believe. I saw the part of the ceiling that had given way in the west corridor, tiles slumping like tired shoulders, water doing its slow, surprising work because you had thought that fire would be the end of things when in truth water was what moved in afterward with its toothbrush and its good intentions and ruined the rest.

“Who was with you?” Rocío asked, not as if she had a right to know, but as if I might be relieved to know she wanted to.

“Some women I had made friends with because we were put in the same spaceship,” I said. “Sohana and Fariha and Annelise. There was a man whose name I would know if I could remember it. He had a mole on his cheek like a tear. He stayed behind to prop open the back stair door with his back because the rubber stopper was somewhere else. He held it with his whole body and told each person to look at him and breathe in their nose and out their mouth, to go down and turn right and then left, not to turn left first because that would take them into the corner and the corner would be the end. He said it so many times that I thought what if that is my job now, to say right then left to everyone for the rest of my life because he cannot do it anymore. He was the kind of man who would have asked a jar to open itself rather than admit he could not. In the stories later there was someone like him, and then he was not there. The stories have to choose because they cannot tell all the parts. I was a part they chose for a while, when the papers needed a girl who had good teeth and looked like she would make it. Then they did not choose me. I don’t say this for pity. It is the way the papers live. I took money from a silent envelope and I paid two months ahead on my rent and then I did not buy anything for a while that I did not absolutely need, which turned out to be different than I had thought.”

“You are here now,” Rocío said, and leaned to knock her ash into the metal can at her feet. “That is the thing. Some people would not be.”

“We’re going to dance,” I said, and surprised myself, and she grinned and stood, and the chair gave its little laugh again.

At the ceremony, the chairs were white and the flowers were white and the women’s dresses were any color that was not white, as if everyone had decided to be petals for the bride. We sat on the second row because we had misjudged how aggressively other guests would seat themselves. The aisle runner unrolled like a tongue. The officiant, whom I had expected to be a friend, was indeed a friend; he had that bland handsomeness of men who have never had to be disliked. He made a joke about vows being like recipes, which I could not forgive him for. Hannah’s father, when he walked her in, had that look I like, the one that is not burdened with symbolic knowledge but only says, I am walking my child to a place where she will be made taller, and I am in my own body with her right now, walking. Halfway down, he saw someone he knew and wanted to say something to them, and you saw it pass across his face and then he put it aside, and the putting aside was the statement, better than any toast.

They stood in front of us with their hands open to each other. Evan was pale in that way that makes the whites of the eyes into part of the story. Hannah’s chest rose and fell slowly. The music they had chosen was a string arrangement of a song that had once been about sorrow and was now about joy. The officiant read a poem. It was not a poem anyone would have burned a book over, but he read it as if you might try to. He talked about weathering storms together and about tending a flame, and I was almost on my feet with my head thrown back to say, No, no flames, find something else to tend, tend a plant, tend a neglected porch. Instead I sat and squeezed my own knees between my hands until the muscle complained. Simon’s thigh was warm next to mine and I put my palm on it flat the way a swimmer puts her hand on a tiled pool edge to push off.

When they said, I do, there was a wave from the room, a held breath let go, the applause that sounded like rain on a tent. The white runner had a dark smudge from where someone’s heel had told a small truth. The flower girl popped one of the blossoms from her crown off with her restless fingers and put it in her mouth and her mother took it out, exasperated, and put it back in the crown as if a circle had to be a circle. Above us, small lights wove across the ceiling like a net, and even the net was kind, letting all of us through; we were fat fish slipping past woven patience.

During the reception, after the soup and before the meat, they brought out small fountains at the corners of the dance floor. They were the new kind, the ones you can almost pretend aren’t really fire. The sparks were white, a clever, cold white, and they rose only as high as a man’s chest. It was supposed to be safe in a way that congratulated itself for being careful. When the tech pressed his little remote and the four white fountains burst into white, the sound they made was the exact pitch of a smoke alarm reconsidering its purpose.

I was already standing by then, so it looked as if I was merely eager to dance. I slid out along the edge of the room and through the door that I had, earlier, leaned my shoulder against for the satisfaction of resisting its closer. I kept walking until the walls of the hall did that thing they do for me where they become both narrower and farther away. I took the next right and the next left—the opposite of what I had been told to tell other people—and then took the stairs down one flight and found myself on a landing between floors where there was a window of frosted glass you could not see out of. There was a small table there with a plant on it in a beige pot. The plant was alive only at the tips. I put both my hands on the table and breathed, in my nose, out my mouth, the old man’s instructions finding me as if they had been hiding in my lungs all these years.

A door at the bottom of the stairs opened and a young man in a chef’s jacket came through loading a tray with the little ramekins for the crème brûlée. He saw me, checked me for a second the way someone who has been broken into checks their locks every night, and then smiled as instructed by his manager and went on. Above me, the band slid into a song that had been at every wedding I had ever cleaned up after and then, later, at the few I had attended. It has a chorus you can sing with your mouth half-closed. The words say love but really they say time, and no one minds the substitution.

I thought about the woman who had said No and pulled her robe around herself tighter. There will always be a person you cannot move along the hallway. There will always be someone who said, I will not be made to obey something frightening by a young girl with a basket of sample soaps. I wondered what it would have been like to be her, waking in that smoke dream and feeling some other dream pressing on it from above, your brain slow to choose. If I had stayed with her, would I have said something better? Would I have used the voice I reserve for frightened children and dogs? Would I have taken her face in my hands the way I do with my niece when she tries to make two choices at once and ends up crying into both? There are versions of the story in which I sat with her and died in that doorway, and even in those versions, a part of me is relieved I did not have time to make that mistake.

When I came back into the world, the white fountains had fizzled back into the metal mouths that had birthed them. The room smelled like cinnamon. People clapped as if they had just seen a bird be tamed. Hannah was dancing with Evan now, her face lifted like a flower held to warming light. Her father danced with her mother and then with her and then her mother danced with Evan and I watched the families attempt to include each other in their circles as if circles had doors with doormen who would wave you in if your name was on the list. The women’s shoes clicked on the wood of the floor like the sound of a dozen typewriters at once. I stayed at the edge and then, when a song came on that had made me and Sohana dance in a break room long ago, arms above our heads like our hair had been tied to wires and then the wires had been cut, I went forward into the crowd.

There is a feeling you get that cannot be faked, the feeling of your own movement contributing to a sound larger than yourself. I tasted the clean in my mouth and felt the sweat under my hair. I pressed my palms against other palms and turned and stepped and let myself be something that had nothing to do with doors or their habit of sticking. I was within this joy, not watching it through a window. Simon found me then and his hands on my hips were an amusement, not a rescue, and he leaned to my ear and said something and I did not catch it and I laughed and said, Yes, and that was enough. The white fountains did their white again once more when the cake was cut and this time the sound they made was the same and my bones recognized it and nodded without telling my mind.

At the end, when the lights had come up the smallest bit and the band was playing their contractually obligated encore and everyone had taken their hair down or wished to, I stepped outside to the terrace. The hotel’s new terrace looked down on the side street and there were the backs of heads making their way towards taxis, the women holding their shoes in their hands with defiance. Smoke rose from cigarettes and from the breath of those who wore less and were colder. Beyond the glass balustrade, the city did its thing, loud and tender, a place that could take you in its mouth and chew and chew and never quite decide if you were to be swallowed or simply savored and then spat.

I put my hand on the glass and felt its cool. The glass did not even admit that I existed. Below, a staff member rolled a bin to the service door and its wheels made a sound like someone dragging an old suitcase through a station. The scent of lemon reached me on a faint breeze, or else my brain put it there obligingly, like a mother who smells her child before the child reaches her arms. I thought, They built this building again because they wanted to say that it could be built again, because the city requires its résumés to be impressive. They built it because there was money and because money hates holes. They built it, but the old one remains, because my feet remember where to turn and how to avoid the corner. I can sit in a white chair and I can eat small tartlets and I can kiss the bride on the cheek under the net of lights, but the old stair is in my muscle. I am in both. That is the best and the worst.

When we went back to our room, we undressed without ceremony and with familiar jokes about feet and linings and the way clothes pretend to love you only until it's time to take them off. Simon lay with his hand across my stomach and said, “You danced like a person who dances,” and fell asleep before I could kiss his sound. I lay awake a little while longer listening to the slow drain of the last songs in my blood. I pictured Rocío, somewhere below me, doing what she did with her hands, lifting and smoothing, counting and deciding when to count and when to stop. I pictured a man turning off a gas line and checking it twice. I pictured the plant on the landing doing its best. I pictured the woman who had said No and felt, for an instant that did not want a name, the heat of having been both of us.

The window was still open its two inches and through it a thread of air came in that had been touched by the body of the city, and I took that small thread and wound it around my wrist like a bracelet. Then the noises below diminished, and the hotel had its private conversations with its plumbing. At last, the skin around my eyes softened and the floor stopped moving under me and the only fire left was the one in the poor white fountain wick that had never truly burned. I set my hand flat on the sheet at my side where I used to store hand towels for turndown and I slept as if I had somewhere to be in the morning, because I did.
