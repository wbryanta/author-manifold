The first time I saw the new lobby it was lit the way they light lobbies now, the light no longer golden or diffused or forgiven by cigarette smoke and bevelled mirrors but clear, intent, the kind of light in which you could pick a gnat out of a glass of club soda and hold it up for inspection. There were orchids where there used to be poinsettias, a stack of white vellum cards that said Welcome, in a hand meant to look informal, a looping hand that suggested a person had been involved. There were eucalyptus branches arranged in a low cement bowl, a photograph of the ocean in winter blown up and made into a mural, matte, the horizon shaved straight and the sky left the exact color of an eggshell. At the valet stand there was a small sign that said Event Parking on Us, as if the concept of “on us” would put you at ease. I did not think it would but I appreciated the effort. I appreciated the way they were doing what you were supposed to do about hotels now, which is to make the visible parts resemble nothing so much as a house you might pin on a board on your phone. The invisible parts were the same as they ever were.

I had not walked into this hotel in eight years and the carpet under my shoes felt both new and the exact memory of what it had been. They had selected a pattern intended to resist stains and reorder perception: a tone-on-tone leaf in a gray that was called fog but read more as tired silver, the repeat calculated to throw the eye. In the old lobby there had been a place where the pile dipped slightly because the housekeeping cart always rolled over it at a certain angle and the houseman had a habit of braking there, a minor rut in the exact route from the back door to the elevators. The new carpet did not dip. It rose and fell in the prescribed way, like the ocean in the mural, like the dress code at a particular wedding. I had been invited to this particular wedding because the groom's mother had been to high school with my aunt, which is the kind of sentence that would have made me laugh in a different time and did not make me laugh now. There are ways families reorganize around events. There are lists you are on because someone once made potato salad at your cousin’s funeral. There are hotel rooms set aside in your name because there is a sentence someone remembers about you having once worked here.

At the desk there was a young woman with hair the color of pale toast keyed into a bun by a stick that looked like ivory and was not. Her name tag said LAURIE in small capitals. Her smile was the required smile, the one that registers as relief to a guest and as muscle memory to an attendant. When she spoke there was the soft calico trace of Nebraska or South Dakota or a place where the word “route” can be a vowel both ways. She offered me a small burlap bag that said Welcome in that same looping hand and contained two glass bottles of water and a bag of sea-salt caramels with a screenplay font. “Please let me know if there's anything we can do to make your weekend exceptional,” she said. She said it exactly like that. There was a man checking in next to me who corrected her to say “weekend exceeds expectations” or “exceed expectations,” I could not tell which, and she absorbed the correction as if it had never been made. There is a way you learn to absorb such things in a hotel. You put them on a shelf and you do not get them down again.

Because the wedding was at five, because it was June, because the heat that had come through the canyon the whole week before had broken in a way that felt less like a reprieve than like a negotiated armistice, the lobby smelled vaguely of damp stone. There were children in tuxedo vests running their hands along the velvet rope at the bar. There were three women sitting on the bench by the elevator talking about the dress code on the invitation, where it had said Festive Attire. I had on a dress I did not own twelve days before. It had buttons down the front and a bias cut that was meant to flatter and did not or did in a way that seemed polite, the way a cashier calls everyone sweetheart. I had my hair in the way I used to put it when I did not want to think about my hair: pinned and out of the way, practical, bridal if you squinted and did not care about detail. I had lipstick that in the bathroom light looked the color of a plum and in the elevator light looked the color of my own mouth after I had bitten it, which I had.

“Welcome back,” Laurie said, and I did not ask her what she meant by that. People say “welcome back” because that is what they have been told to say to build loyalty. People say “welcome back” because they are working for a brand that is not a person. There were a few weeks in the old lobby when I would see someone who had come before, a convention guest with a belt buckle that said HOUSTON and a business card that said Southland Rail or a woman with pink nails and a tote bag that said Napa Makes Me Happy, and by the third morning with their towel on the same rung they were the ones saying “good to see you.” The language slips and accumulates as it can.

Upstairs the guest room was everything a person would want a guest room to be if the person had never seen a guest room at all. The bed was low and wide and covered in a white that had to be laundered in bleach and was therefore already giving up a little at the seams where the duvet folded. The bathroom had a sink set in a slab of honed limestone. There were three small amber bottles by the sink, the labels letterpressed so that the word cedar seemed as if you could feel it with your finger. There was a card that explained the reuse of towels with a tone that indicated we could all be in this together if we tried. There were two robes hung precisely, the belts fixed by that small trick of tucking them once so they will hang in a particular drape. I glanced at the ceiling without thinking, the way a person who has had a child will glance at the pool gate as if it had something to say. There were sprinklers recessed and painted to match the ceiling, each with a silver mouth. There was the appropriate number of smoke detectors, two, one nearer the bathroom because that is where steam will flirt and one nearer the door because that is where people leave trying to pretend it is not their cigarette. You notice these things when you have been inside a place when it burns. You notice the air.

I was told when I first started here that the rule is if you can smell smoke you are already inside it. A cigarette is smoke. A steak put down on a grill too hot is smoke. A wool jacket held too close to a candle at a Christmas party is smoke. I was not in the lobby the night the old lobby burned. I was on the seventh floor, where there is also a way things happen, housekeeping with the carts lined up against the wall and the doors propped and the vacuums waiting like docile animals tethered to invisible loops. It was a Thursday. People tend to set more on fire on a Saturday but a Thursday is when you will smell it first. In the laundry room there are rags soaked with solvents, the lint from the dryer clean enough to use as paper and so compact and so ready that it will flash like a magician. We had received training. We had watched a video of a room as it went from tidy to charred in three minutes, a video that had been meant to frighten us into efficiency or into compliance or into not putting oily rags in the wrong bin. I had believed in the video and also not.

There had been a man then I was trying not to think about, and the way to avoid thinking about a man is to count. This is something I knew before I knew the names of chemicals or the way to fold a blanket so that it falls in an even overhang on both sides. You can count the number of shampoo bottles there should be when a room has been turned, you can count the number of hangers, you can count the number of minutes a kettle should be left before you empty it and wipe the interior with a paper towel lest it leave a ring of calcium. You can count the steps from the closet to the bed, you can arrange the remote controls in a right angle, you can tug the corners in what we were taught to call hospital corners, although the hospitals, I learned, do not call them anything. Counting was what I was doing that afternoon in the seventh-floor corridor when the first sound that was not somebody’s son watching a video game through the door was the small pop. A pop is nothing. A pop is ice in a glass. A pop is a wheeled suitcase going over a seam. The smoke itself was visible before the alarm began its little strobe. When the alarm began its little strobe, the light felt private and intimate and not for me at all.

It was not a story I was telling then. It is not a story I tell now. There are events that exist in your hands and feet and in the way you sleep and wake and cannot be made otherwise. For a long time I did not come here or to any place with carpet running endless down a hall. There are people who imagine that the way to get control over a thing is to pick up a different thing adjacent to it and measure that instead. I did not do that. I did not go to the beach more often because I had not been drowning. I did not take a class in painting because I had not been in a museum when the sprinklers went off. I bought clear bins and put things in them and slid those bins under the bed where they could be looked at or not.

At four-ten in the afternoon on the day of the wedding the door between the elevator and the service hall swung open and back like a pulse and I could see for a moment the way they moved the carts now, the wheels a different kind of rubber, the shelves made of a softer plastic to deaden their contact with the doorframes. There was a girl on the cart, a girl with a braid the color of wheat straw, and her braid was caught into the collar of her uniform shirt and she did not notice or did and decided not to fix it. If you have a shirt that pulls at the nape of your neck in the wrong way you will be frantic by two in the afternoon. She had fixed it in a way that indicated a certain stoicism. Her name was not on her shirt. There is an argument about names. People feel both safer and less safe when they know the names of the people who enter their rooms when they are not there.

“I used to work here,” I said to her as she passed, and she looked up and then past me as if I had told her I used to be a teenage girl myself, which I had. “Huh,” she said. “That must have been wild.” The word wild has been returned to people like her from people like me, is now something offered without irony, to be applied to an October wind or a traffic situation. When she was out of my sight I could see exactly where she would go, which of the linen shelves she might favor, how she might have arranged the microfiber cloths folded into quadrants because if you do not fold them into quadrants the glass will streak. It was, for a moment longer, as if I were there for a shift and not to sign a card with a silver pen on a lucite table that said Please find your seat.

The wedding took place in a courtyard I did not know. There had not been a courtyard in the old plan. There had been a parking lot whose only beauty was that you could stand in the absolute center of it and look at the eucalyptus that rose on the ridge and understand that in certain months those leaves would rattle. There are winds that begin before they arrive. The courtyard was ringed by olive trees in planters sunk to look permanent. There were twinkle lights threaded into the branches and candles massed on a stair and a man in a navy suit had the job of setting a candle back to a place whenever one went black, and he performed his function with a devotion that might have made you think of a monk or might have made you think of a very good retail manager. The bride wore a dress with a skirt made of fabric that moved as if it were water and took its shape from the air. The groom cried before we started. There was an officiant who had written the ceremony and would say the words you would think you recognized in their general meaning and notice differed in the specifics: he would say community when you expected family, he would say the work when you thought he might say the vows.

I watched the bride’s mother’s hands. She had been a girl in my aunt's year, she had once worn pale lipstick and a scarf knotted in a specific way for football games, she had married a man from the next town with a jaw like a carved fruit and had been the kind of woman who took a pie to a person in trouble and did not feel magnanimous about it. She had a gold charm bracelet and on it there were the standard things, the little boy, the little girl, the tiny wedding bells, the hand-etched heart, but there was also a small gold dustpan and broom, the kind of hospitality charm someone might have bought her once as a joke. It hung awkwardly at the inside of her wrist and clinked against the program when she clapped. I wanted to know who had given it to her. I wanted to know if she would take it off before she went to bed or sleep with it and wake hurt by the hinge.

There was a discourse at my table about whether candles at tables are a hazard. Someone had read an article. Someone had met a person whose cousin was an underwriter. “They’re not allowed to have pillars,” a woman said, and the man next to her said, “Those are in glass, so it’s fine.” He had a tie with small fish on it that I recognized as the Brookline Road Race tie, though we were very far from Brookline. He had hair that would go if he had a son and his son grew old enough to trouble him. The woman said, “I still worry,” and I put my hand on her wrist and removed it before she knew it had been there. The automatic part of my brain, the tiny lobe where the rules sit, had already tilted the taper back from the eucalyptus garland because certain leaves flash more than others. The automatic part had measured in my eye the distance from the flame to the napkin's edge. You could not, I knew, make an argument to people at a table about an overhead sprinkler and have them continue to enjoy their night. I put my glass down in the way that means I will not drink this drink. I picked it up in the way that means I will.

On the list of questions asked me in the year after the fire were these: Was there a smell of gasoline. Did I see any unauthorized personnel. Was I or was I not an authorized personnel. Did I ever prop a door. Had I seen other employees prop a door. Had I retained any mementos.

I tell you this because of the word mementos. I told them no. I told them what I would have told anyone who called an object a memento, that I was not a person who took things. I had not, for example, taken the cufflink shaped like a ship’s wheel that had sat on the sink of 514 for three days the previous Thanksgiving while a man from Pomona or Pasadena or another place beginning with a P had waited for me to find it. “I’m looking for something,” was what he said when I answered the door because the Do Not Disturb had been reversed and we were meant to go in. “My life, probably,” and we both laughed although we both knew it was a joke of a certain category and had been told in another room just like this one. He had found the cufflink in the cuff of his coat. He had put it into the front pocket of his shirt where it sat like a small child asleep and then he had offered me a tip so elegant it could have been mistaken for generosity. I had said what you say, which is I can’t accept that, and then, which is what you say next, Thank you.

What I did take, once, was a ring. It was not during the fire. The ring was in a dish next to a hotel Bible in a drawer that had not been opened in years. It was made of silver and was not expensive. The dish itself was hotel property and the ring was not. The person who had left the ring was not a person of means or if he had been a person of means he was a person who did not spend money on rings or if he did he did not choose to spend it then. The silver was slightly soft and had been pressed a little out of round by a hand that had put itself into a pocket and back again in a season of patience. There are objects that say use me and there are objects that say I was used for a while and was not used again. I took the ring not because I wanted it, although I put it on and it fit my left ring finger in a neutral way, not intimate but not loose. I took the ring because the idea of it staying there in the silent dark where I was the only person who could see it felt intolerable in a way I cannot explain. I did not wear it to work but I wore it to the grocery store and to the dentist and to a day when I sat on a bench and tried to learn to say a thing to a person I would not end up saying. I wore it and then I put it in a clear bin with other clear bins. On the list of questions there had been one about whether I had ever seen a person leave something of value and the answer to that was that value is determined by a market that is not the one I subscribe to and that the large stone earrings I turned into Security were not worth as much to me as the tiny pencil sharpener I found in a desk drawer in 819 that worked in a way that other sharpeners do not.

The band played the songs they play now, the retro set followed by the ironic set followed by the sincere set in which the men who sell office furniture and have daughters try to get them to dance. The bride held her dress with her fingertips and leaned back when the groom pulled her, the way someone not from the ocean might lean back when a wave comes. The aunt who had called me to insist I come here, who had said it would be good, who had said it would be closure, sat for a long time and then got up again and said, “You should go say hello to the bride’s mother,” and I went and said hello and I told her that she looked beautiful and she told me that I looked rested and for a moment we were two women in a place people come to rest, and it might have been that simple. “I’m so glad you’re here,” she said, and when she said it there was a softness in her voice that made me think she knew something or had made up a story in which she did, that she had told herself that my presence meant a thing about perseverance or about forgiveness or about the continuity of events. I smiled and asked about the cake. It was a carrot cake with cream cheese icing piped in a way meant not to look piped. There were six layers. Cakes like this are made in hotels by people who see them all the time and know that they will not travel well out into the world. The slices were too generous. No one ate all of theirs.

At ten, after the sparklers, which no one really says yes to anymore although they do and then post the photograph, I went back to my room. The air in the corridor felt cooler than it should have. By then I had told three people how to get to the service elevator as if I worked there, not because they needed the service elevator, because they did not, but because they had been looking for the restroom and I had answered in the way I answer any question about a place like this: around the banquet room, left past the easel with the seating chart, right through the double doors. There are certain marks on my tongue that never leave. I had stood in the dark of the courtyard for a moment by the back gate where I could see the path that led to the dolly dock and the chalk on the asphalt where someone had markered PALLETS GO HERE and I had watched a houseman flick a cigarette out into the gutter and then go after it with his foot, not because he was worried about fire but because he had been told not to smoke and had been caught more than once. The small motions of work did not change. The wedding would end. The linens would be pulled. The eucalyptus would be thrown away because it would have absorbed the smell of wax. If I had wanted comfort I could have found it in that.

The mirror over the room's desk had been hung not quite straight. I could see it because the expansion joint in the wall that was meant to be hidden would not hide. I had not noticed this when I put on lipstick because no one sees what is crooked when it will help. I straightened the mirror and then straightened it again because when you straighten a thing once you will see more that could be done. There was a knock at the door and when I opened it there was a young man holding a plate on which there were four macarons in pale colors and a note that said, With Compliments. He said, “Late-night snack,” and I said, “For me?” which is something I do when a person is bringing me a thing I have not paid for. He pointed to the bottom of the paper where someone had handwritten my name in a pattern of circles, and I understood that the bride's mother had asked for this to be done and that I could be grateful or annoyed. I was grateful. Or I acted as if I were. The young man said, “Enjoy your evening,” and I tipped him more than would appear normal because if you have certain lines on your hands you will not forget how they etched there.

It is possible that on another night I would have tried to sleep and failed and put the television on. I have learned to leave the sound off and just watch the movement. People on television do not light things on fire in a way that will hurt you. They announce that they are doing it and then do it and then another person puts out their hand and says, “We will be right back.” I could not make another person do that the night the old lobby burned. I could not make the night manager answer his phone. I could not stop Mrs. Jensen in 704 from opening her door to tell me that there was something wrong with the alarm because this had happened once before and turned out to be a drill. I could ask her to come with me and I did and she did not and then did. I could not get the woman in 709 who had left her baby with her sister by the pool to stop looking in her handbag for the baby’s bottle, because people in hotels have only so much attention at once and it was being used.

What you do at such times is hold your list in your head and move down it. Evacuate, activate. Use the stairs. Do not use the elevators. You knock, you announce, you open the door with your key that has the weight of a thing people believe about you, which is that you are allowed inside their room and not to be afraid of that. The corridor that afternoon was a pale tunnel and then was gray and then had light. When I reached the end of the corridor there was another door you have to open with a key and I had the key and there was a man behind me who had wanted to retrieve his watch and his medication and his wife and I did not let him. The smoke did what smoke does. It went where there was air and took it. I could see, because in a time like that you see what you will see later, that the trim around the exit sign had a hairline where the paint sat, that the lock needed more oil than it had had. I saw that the stairs were not a place for anyone to be who was not ready to walk on their hands if the railing turned hot.

If there is a story there it is that we came down. We did not go up. We came down and turned and came down further and found a door that wanted a key and there were people on the other side who had air because they were in the hotel’s idea of outside, the corridor between the sleeping rooms and the kitchens. We went through and then there were more bodies in what you might call line if you wanted the movement to make sense. There were people under tables because the rules about earthquake and the rules about fire got confused in their heads, there were staff from banquets with towels wet in their hands, there was the pastry chef who had come from another hotel and knew how to say calm down in Spanish, French, and the language of people who do not understand language. It was both a long time and a very short time. There were men with radios who had known what to do three minutes before and could not remember two minutes after. There was one houseman named Luis who had brought a mother and child down in the service elevator and then gone back up even after he had been told a thing about the way elevators think when they are hot. There is not a day I do not think about him. There is not a day I do not hear the elevator car's counterweight moving like a thought above our heads.

In the rebuilt hotel there is a plaque. It is not in the lobby where the orchids are and the woman from South Dakota who are perhaps from Nebraska stands. It is on the wall in the corridor near the service entrance where the van brings the linens in and the carts of wine go out. It says something about those we lost and those who responded with bravery. It says the date and it says that we will not forget. It is possible to get there in your satin dress and it is possible no one will stop you. It is possible that the night you go there two banquet staff will be standing under the exit sign reading something on a phone between them and not see you at all. It is possible to put your hand on the brass screw that holds the plaque in and feel as if you understand the way the metal conducts the cold.

The question is always what you will do with your hands. I had put the ring in the pocket of the dress when I left for the party because the idea of it being at home and there and this place being here and not being the same felt like a false separation and I have grown too old for false separations. It had sat there, solid, warming with me, not me. I took it out. The plaque was screwed into plaster and behind it there was brick that had been whitewashed in the previous hotel's simple idea of purity and then cracked, and then the new hotel had come along and plastered over the crack and painted the plaster and put a plaque there to fix the story. I had envisioned a gesture. I do not live in a world where gestures hold. I imagined placing the ring at the base of the plaque in the crack between the wall and the wainscoting where it would sink. I imagined that later a houseman would vacuum and hear it and shake the bag out for the pleasure of the clatter or that it would stay there forever, the silver slowly turning the color of leaves that no one sees in their native form because we are at the edge of a country that subdues its metals. I did not do this.

Instead I walked with it to the back door, the one that opens to the dolly dock and the ramp and the place where a person might stand and smoke because if you have been made to change out of your jacket and into your T-shirt you are still a person who might need to do a thing with your mouth. There was a trash can with a lid that opened on a hinge and had inside it a clear liner because in the back of the house we do not need the illusions of thick black. I held the ring and the ring was cold and my hand was warm and I thought, As if I will make a decision and it will mean a thing I have not already decided. I thought of Luis. I thought of the way the trainee from last week had folded the corners with her whole body and then looked at me and said, “It’s so smooth,” with the kind of joy that takes you by surprise because you have not felt it that way in a while. I dropped the ring into the can and the sound it made on the lid was not pretty. I took it back out. I am not proud as I tell you this. I put it into the clear plastic bag in my purse where I keep a Band-Aid and a granola bar and a spool of dental floss, the way we are women now, prepared and practical. I did not decide that it would stay there or go anywhere else. I did not write a narrative.

Later at the bar, because the party continued to a room that had curtains the color of midafternoon rain, there was a man who used to be a person who would have mattered to me and he said he had been on the fourth floor of a hotel in Dallas when the fire alarm sounded and he had put a pillow over his head and gone back to sleep. He said it in a way meant to provoke. He said, “The odds are with you,” and I did not answer him. He said, “You have to be careful not to live in the exception,” and I could have told him what the exception is, which is you. I did not. I do not have a taste any longer for losing small arguments in public. I asked for a club soda with lemon and the bartender said they were out of lemon and I said, “Lime then,” and the bartender said, “We’re out of that too,” which was impossible but not untrue. When you barkeep for a wedding you run out of all kinds of things. The bartender said, “Orange?” and I said, “Sure,” because that is what you learn when you put towels in the right bin and not the wrong. There will always be someone who asks you to be happy with what you have not ordered.

At one the music stopped and the people who had been holding the edges of the dance floor as if anchoring a tarp let go and gathered their coats. There was a child sleeping on a banquette under a pile of linen napkins as if some conscientious goblin had tucked him in. There was confetti on the floor that had been made to look like petals and was not. There were two men who had been on the same football team in a town without a stoplight talking about hip replacements. There was the bride in slippers and she was radiant in the sense that she made her own light and the difference between the light and the light's source would not tell itself until Monday afternoon. I went upstairs.

When I sleep in hotel rooms now, I push the door again after it closes, feel for the suction of the lock. I lay out the key card and the shoes in the direction of the corridor. I put my phone on top of the room directory in the drawer where I will not see the Bible because I do not sleep when there is language in the room that is not mine. I wake twice and the second time is when there are wheels in the hall and men saying their names into the radios because the continental breakfast needs more plates. I woke and I lay very still. The thing that happened next is the thing that always happens: a small pool of heat in me went cool. I went back to sleep.

In the morning at the brunch there were chalkboards with the word Mimosa written in a wide hand and a smaller hand labeling strawberries, blueberries, granola. There was a carving station with a man who had put up a sign that said SANTA MARIA TRI TIP as if that settled anything. There were people doing what they do the next day, which is to tell a story about how their shoes hurt and how she cried when her father spoke and how the person at the table had been inappropriate about ivy. “I had to move the candle,” I said once to the woman from last night by way of a joke and she said, “Oh, thank you,” and I realized that the pieces of the evening that mattered would be these: that a person had moved a candle and had spoken to the bride's mother and had tipped the boy for the cookies and had danced during the second song. No one needed another thing. The bride's mother hugged me. She smelled of powder and cream and behind that of something that had not been made in the last twenty years, an aldehyde that has gone out of style except around women who do not believe in that.

“You'll stay?” she said, and I said, “No,” because it was easier than saying I could not stay in a hotel longer than necessary. “I have work in the morning.” This was true. I had a list. I had a set of calls to make and a form to sign and the person who does the scheduling is a person you please because she remembers. She had said I could have Monday if I needed it and I had said no because I wanted to have made that choice. There are people who find comfort in rest. I find comfort in starting the dishwasher and standing there until it finishes the first cycle. I find comfort in wiping the seal so mildew will not grow. I do not know if this is maturity or simply a twist of the heart that has turned itself one way and rusted there.

When I checked out there was no longer Laurie with the toasted hair, there was a man with a beard who intoned my name as if it were on a list he would be tested on later. He asked if everything had been to my satisfaction. I said yes because I am not a person who makes a scene. He asked, “Will you be joining us again soon?” and I said, “I don't know,” because I am not a person who lies unless the person and the lie are deserving of each other. I stood for a moment in the lobby where the light was no longer relentless but filtered by the late morning and the people who would come and go. There were young women wheeling suitcases. There were men returning keys to wives with a shorthand that indicated survival of a decade. There was a bellman who had a way of gesturing that suggested that you were being invited into a card trick and could figure it out if you wanted. There was a small boy sitting on a suitcase and singing, singing the same fragment over, a thing he had heard before, and his mother had the expression of someone who had once worked a late shift and knew that you do not always look at the clock.

Outside the air was warm enough to soften the eucalyptus but not enough to set it to motion. I stood in the shade because old habits instruct new reflexes. The valet brought up my car and I tipped him and he said “Thanks, boss” and I did not flinch, because there are things that would have once made me flinch that do not anymore. I sat in the car and pressed my hand to the wheel and waited for the part of the dashboard that thinks for itself to decide where it had been last turned. The navigation was on and the last address was a grocery store in my part of town. I did not program a new one. I drove out of the circle and down the road and past the place where the new stucco meets the old brick and past the old gas station where there are bougainvillea now instead of oil stains and when I came to the light I waited for it to change. I watched the opposite direction get its turn. I counted the cars. I do this, now. I do not pretend it changes anything and I do not pretend it does nothing.

I will tell you the thing that stayed with me. After the fire, in the late afternoons when the sun went low and found the gaps in whatever curtains were up in whatever place I was staying, I would think of the seventh-floor corridor and of the way the vacuums sat, their cords wrapped around their waists, patient. A vacuum is a thing that waits. It will stand there for you no matter how the day goes and take in what you ask it to. It will hum dutifully and make lines in the carpet and then it will go back to silence. I thought of the lines. I thought of the way I used to make them, not all in one direction like the promotions we would watch on television for steam-cleaner companies but in alternating paths like arrows or herringbone, the pattern meant to show that something had been done here, the way a wedding shows a thing was done there too, whether or not it endures. I thought of running the vacuum and of pausing and of knowing I had missed a cigarette butt in the corner near the drapery because a man had flicked it there while he looked down at the parking lot. I thought of catching it the second time. I thought of the quiet. I thought of the list. I still think of them. It is, if you want to call it this, how I go on. I did not tell the bride's mother that. I did not tell the officiant. I did not tell the young girl with her braid caught under the collar of her shirt. It is not something people need to hear at brunch. It is a thing that lives in the corners and comes out when you pull the bed away from the wall and see what is back there and decide whether to tell someone. It is the part that is mine and not yours. I drove home with the window open enough to make a sound. I kept my hand on the wheel. It was enough, for then, to count the breaths until the light changed, to make one after another, to move.
