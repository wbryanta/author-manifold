The first winter morning I went down to the south jetty with my grandfather the cannery steam was visible before we saw the building, a damp ghost rising out of the low roofs and rust. The whistle hadn’t yet sounded shift, and the gulls seemed put out by the quiet, hopping along the caprock, peering into our faces with that awful confidence of hunger. He walked ahead of me with his hands tucked into the sleeves of his coat, the cuffs patched with different shades of oilskin, his hat pulled low to where his eyebrows had been scoured pale by years of salt. His boots made a slow, sure rhythm on the wet concrete, as if he were listening to some other sea we couldn’t hear.

“Don’t look at the birds,” he said when I fell in step, breath fogging. “They’ll only try to sell you some drama. Look at the water.”

We stopped near the last bollard, where the green can buoy had a paint shine like fish scales in weak light and leaned always a little upriver. The bar beyond was a wide gray smudge between jetties, the breakers knitting and unknotting farther than I could throw my thoughts. I had always known the sea as background to the town—the smell of it in the school hallways, the wind that salted our dinners, the men and women at the cannery whose wages were suddenly dependent on whether air moved one way or another that day. But looking the way he wanted required a different shape of eyes.

“Wind’s out of the south,” he said softly. “See the cat’s-paws?”

I looked and only saw a whole palette of ocean, a gray that swallowed greens, a billion little snatches of the sky’s dead cloth. Then I noticed darker ripples under the dock, a clutch of tiny, fast-moving shadow tongues touching the surface, traveling, then vanishing.

He tilted his chin. “There. Those. They run ahead of the breeze. Cats’ paws. Little first touchings. You can tell where it comes from by where they run to, and how the lines lay.” He showed me with a hand, and the skin of his hand was as seamed and nicked as if water had written on him. “South wind on an ebbing tide. See how the river’s going out? Where the brown slides under the gray? Follow that seam.”

He pointed, and I worked at following. There really was a line, like something almost drawn in ink—not straight, but that same pull the edge of a page has. Bits of cedar bark and foam scummed there, riding a conveyor belt that disappeared out beyond the jetties where the waves got tall and difficult.

“Rips line up along that seam when current and wind argue,” he said. “And on a day like this they’re not fighting so bad, but you can still hear it. Listen.”

We both stood and listened. Close by all I could make out was slap and slosh and that hollow knocking of water under the jetty blocks. But some ways beyond, where the swell met the falling river, there was a different timbre to the breaking, like a bowl tipped and ringing after it lands on a table. It was a distance in a sound.

“Sometimes you watch as much with your ears as your eyes,” he said. “You’ll learn.”

I wasn’t sure it was something you could learn if your head did not begin in that place. He had begun in the place where you read by weather and wind. Before he got the pilot job, he grew up under a foreman father who measured time in tides and days in weather, and before that, there had been a father who’d gone away in a storm and never came back, and a grandfather who had come west from a port where the water was warmer and people still left their dead aboveground, wrapped in cloth. I had begun in this town where the cannery changed shifts with a whistle and my mother took fifteen minutes to drink her coffee at three-thirty in the morning and then buttoned on her apron and her hair in a net and stood under fluorescents all day until she could not taste anything but bleach. I had begun in the winter they put up the flyers that said there would not be another winter.

“Everything you need to know is in the way the water throws back the light,” he said, as if he had always believed anyone could learn it and had just been waiting for me to be old enough. “If you can’t see the underside, you look for what the top tells you. When I started at the bar I thought I knew enough. Then I learned how the bottom writes its own story on the surface. Ripple patterns off the shoal. Slicks over the deep holes. Color change off the river water. You don’t make it through if you think you are smarter than those scripts.”

He did not often let old stories slip into speech. They lived in him the way an anchor chain lives in its locker, coiled and ready and smelling of iron. But a few came out, and always it was because he had seen the shape of them again on the water.

“Come on,” he said, and we went farther out. The wind cut through my hat as if I had forgotten how to put it on. He raised a hand as if to shield his face from wind, but I knew it was so he could hear the high frequency hiss as chop shaved at the noses of waves. I wondered what else he heard that I did not.

After a while he knelt and put his hand on the jetty caprock, where the concrete ended and the old basalt boulders began their caged tumble into water. He picked up a piece of kelp, dark and strong-smelling, with those tight helium bulbs. He squeezed a bulb in his fist until it popped and the sea-smell rose and wrapped around us.

“Some mornings I swear I can smell which direction the swell came from before light finishes lifting,” he said. “South storms stink different than west sets. South’s got that alder leaf rot in it, and west’s got that long fetch and the powder of smashed barnacle. You’ll get it.”

I made a face at the popped kelp. “I don’t have your nose,” I said.

“You don’t need my nose. You need your own eyes.” He held the kelp bulb up. “See that line of accordion wrinkles on the side? That’s where it stretched in the tide and contracted when the water dropped. Something simple like a weed can tell you as much as a fancy instrument if you know what it says. Reading water’s like reading a book that’s writing itself while you try. It tells on the weather ahead of it, and it remembers yesterday too. If you look long enough you can watch thoughts come up from the bottom.”

When the cannery whistle finally blew the birds all got up off the caprock, impatient for tossed guts. The sound used to be the sound of the town to me, the same way the ferry horn five miles up bay was the sound of big world coming in from the long road. The whistle meant men and women and teenagers who shouldn’t be allowed to legally cut anything standing next to tables full of fish and pushing little bits of them into cans with quickness of fingers or trimming slime off the belts. But it wasn’t loud that morning. It sounded as if some of its voice had been turned to face inland, like a neighbor with one lung.

My grandfather lifted his eyes past the cannery to where the river bent and you could see, just for a breath, one of the sawmill stacks through a slot between roofs. The stack wasn’t smoking. That was older news. Men had been carrying their lunch pails home from the mill in a line for two summers now, flat in their eyes like all their inside air was saved up for something else. But the cannery had stayed, until the flyer, with its too-polite words and their uncarved date.

“Last winter,” the flyer said. “We will operate through February.” My mother folded it twice and three times until it looked like a card trick in a magician’s hand and then slid it under our salt shaker at home. The way she did this told me more than anybody’s voice had yet.

He had moved me, weeks earlier, into the small room at the back of his house where the sound of mast halyards rattling taps all night against aluminum masts like the dreams of slender people. Our apartment had a roof leak that all of a sudden dropped through in the big November blow, and my mother said the landlord wouldn’t fix it because who fixed anything in a town leaving while it stood. I took my school clothes in a black plastic bag and my backpack and my pillow with its flattened geese and went to the house with the yellow porch and the pelican weathervane and the wide knot of winch rope he’d left by the door as a mat. We did not say how long I would stay. We didn’t say whether it was for me or for him or for the both of us. We did not make all those lists people make in towns where buses come more than twice a day.

In the kitchen he had a radio that only stayed steady on AM, and a little flicker of post-it notes with times next to names of boats, even though he was done being called out to the bar. It was just that he had listened for so long to the voices on sixteen, to the bar report every morning, to the crackle and hiss as if it were a second kind of wind. When he walked past the radio he always smoothed the antenna with the same care he known when his fingers were younger and quicker and there was a pilot ladder to coil or a line to flake or a burr of rust to knock off a stanchion before a ship read your deck more honestly than you’d like.

After the whistle the light felt like a tiny move had been made inside morning. He gestured to the break in the bar. “See the sets,” he said. “They come like trains. It’s never just a single wave. You count: one, two, three... then a breath, and the next group. Then you learn what those sizes mean, and how far apart they come, and whether the tide adds teeth. Thirty-second period and you don’t even see it until it stands. Ten-second period and the bar’s teeth are fast and close and a good sandbar fisherman stays home.”

He pulled back the cuff of his glove with his teeth and held his wet hand to the air as if testing something unconcerned with us. He closed his eyes for a second. I saw the carved-out place in his temple where an old cut had etched its map. They had told me once that the sea took bits of you in out-of-the-way places the way a kitten takes the threads out of a sweater, a tooth here, the ball of your ankle there, a spread of hair.

“Count with me,” he said. “Don’t look down. Look where the swell stands up.” We counted. Nine before a big set, then a lull, then eight, then ten. I did not know yet how to make this arithmetic into sense, but I liked the way his voice made the numbers sound like a language at home in his throat. He laughed once when a gull stole a splintered piece of breakfast from another and took off screaming over our heads. The gull heard its own sound and believed it.

Years ago, when his beard was darker and he had all his knuckles, he guided the big ones in, I had heard: freighters on dark mornings with a deck load wrapped in blue tarps and snow blowing like a kind of dust. My mother told me how he would go out in the pilot boat, teeth bared to the spray, climb that rope ladder with the ship’s bow burying and rising past him, wait for the captain to hand him a cup of coffee in a mug that read something proud in a Chinese or Greek language, and look at the chart that meant less to him than what he could smell off the surface. He wouldn’t say much to them, my mother said. He would put his hand on the binnacle and keep some of his weight there, like he was a little bit holding the ship to earth, and he would breathe that measured way he breathes still when the tide is wrong. Three times the helm broke under his hands in gear and the ship did what it wanted, not having learned his name, and he still brought them in. Twice he waited outside the bar for hours until the sets stacked or fell apart according to his heart’s count, even as the captain paced and named cities like they were threats. And once—this was always the last part of the story—once he turned a ship back and told them no, and the captain looked at him with a face so white it had an animal’s clarity and the company on the radio said words my mother would not repeat within reach of a refrigerator door anyone had ever touched in their life.

In our town there were two people who had lasted longer than a winter in the same house and the same marriage and the same job and not been made smaller by it, and he was one. He had what a lot of wives called steadiness, and what some of the men privately translated as a stubbornness no tide could get under. You had to have that to read the water and do something about what you read.

Later that morning I went with him to the cannery to bring lunch to my mother—cheese sliced pale as fingers, and bread we could afford, and an apple that had traveled more of the world than any of us. The bright lights and the wet were there before the door. We went through the boot wash. The smell of brine and bleach choked my throat. He balanced the lunch bag in one hand while he signed a visitor’s clipboard with the other. The rubber apron on the man at the door had bits of dried scales stuck to it like currency pressed to a shrine. We stood at the edge of the floor and waited until she looked up.

She had that cannery posture, back half-bent to the belt, eyes moving somewhere two beats ahead of her hands. She moved her hands so fast you couldn’t see the trait of them that made them hers—the scar she got when she was younger because she cut a mackerel wrong and it flashed and leapt and she apologized to it for years; the way she pinched because she had never grown out of her youngness entirely. She did not blow kisses in the cannery. She did not break the rhythm of the line for air. Later, by the pallets out back that smelled of cedar damp, she ate and looked past us where the stacks and silos and the old ice house made a skyline and told us what the foreman had said. February. Then maybe a fish plant upriver would take some of them, if they had a car and no kids. She laughed without a sound.

“They’re moving the tally sheets to a computer,” she said. “As if that’s it. They’ll bring a laptop right into the slime. As if data will change what the fish have been saying.” She wiped the heel of her hand over her forehead and left a wet smear there. That was how I always thought of my mother, with a line of wet like a new baptism. “Somebody’s counting differently,” she said. “That’s all.”

He did not answer. He had already given her a winter by not saying the first things in his mind. He folded the lunch bag into smaller and smaller bits and slid the last bit in his back pocket while she reached up and touched the side of his face with two fingertips as lightly as if the skin there were a sea.

After she went back in we stood for a while by the back door and watched the gulls play catch with bits of belly skin in the updraft from the cannery roof. “They smell what they need,” he said. “They smell when something’s dying and they’ll be there before the family. When the cannery goes there will be one last time, and they’ll know it before any of us.”

“How?” I said.

He smiled. Lines stacked around his mouth like the tiers of the jetty. “Wind. They smell the change in the air when there’s no more stink in it. You think you’re not an animal until something goes missing and your nose tells you first.”

We walked home past the fishermen’s memorial, the names on their bronze plate polished by the hands of people from festivals and from long afternoons with nobody else to touch. He touched it because he always did. He didn’t run a hand all the way down like the men who’d lost brothers, like he didn’t want to finish the names. He touched two letters on one line with a knuckle, a habit I had known longer than my own breath.

At home I lay on my back under the window and watched the halyards knock their thin Sunday rhythm against the masts while he took off his jacket and hung it on the back of the kitchen chair that always belonged to a jacket. He made tea by heating water until it tried to get away from its pot and then he counted to twelve out loud and poured a stream that bent slightly because the spout was less than whole. He set the tea down on the table like something he had to refuse himself and reached for the radio.

It sounded bad that afternoon, all the voices with too much reverb, as if sound had to travel around more corners than in other weather. He turned the stick antenna and leaned close. I listened to half-words and numbers: seas running eight to ten, occasional to twelve. South fifteen to twenty-five, gusting aimless. Visibility variable. Somebody called somebody else by their first name and asked if the chill had got into their bones yet. Somebody else said they might cross at the top of the hour if the bar behaved like a neighbor. He looked at me as if I were either too young to have to carry some weight or too old not to, and then he turned down the radio.

“You know the mackerel sky?” he asked. “Those little fish-scales of cloud? Mare’s tails and mackerel scales make tall ships take in small sails. Old words. They were alive when I learned them. You tie your weather to poems, you don’t forget.” He smiled. “You think I just made that up to keep your attention.”

“You do,” I said. “Sometimes.”

“Here’s one you can keep: Green water makes your mothers pray. White water makes your deckhands laugh. The worst is green.” He was rolling back his shirt sleeve then and looking at the line of scar that ran from his wrist into the muscle of his forearm. It was white as fish, slick in the kitchen light. “She rolled on me in the green water once off the east jetty and I went down under the pilot boat and the world looked bright and wrong and I thought about not climbing back. Just for three seconds. Then I remembered the deckhand had been at my table two nights before eating my soup and cleaning the bowl like he owned it and I kicked like a mule.”

That night the wind went southeast and took the smell of everybody’s dinners and put it out where the seals stole it and slept with full dreams. I lay in bed in the little room and kept my nose above the line of my blanket the way you keep your head above any water. He passed the door twice and paused each time, a shadow as lined and known as the inside of my glove. The radio had men on it like crows on a wire.

In the morning I rose to his whistle and the smell of dry bread in the toaster and we put on our boots and went out again, this time not to the jetty first but to the town float where the boats touched one another and traded paint and gossip. Ice had rimmed the rope on the cleats and the dock had that glass look that made your legs take a different shape. He walked differently then, his shoulders coming forward as if he were going to take hold of the dock with his body. He ran his finger under a warp line that ran from trawler to shoreline and paused.

“Feel that,” he said. I did, and the line thrummed under my fingertip, a hard humming that made my hand bone feel like a tuning fork.

“Ebb,” he said. “She’s still going out hard. But look.” He flicked his eyes to the side of a pontoon. “Behind the pontoon, what do you see?”

A calm patch, an oval of slick under the downstream side. Then beside it, a cuff of little currents all at cross purposes, pinwheeling along the float.

“Eddy,” he said, pleased. “Little river behind big river. You always have a place like that. On a boat, if you watch a thing long enough you can find where it calms. Mind is not so different.”

“Are we allowed to go out?” I asked, my eyes already bent toward the skiff we had borrowed from the same person all my life, the person who had always left their key under a rusted winch handle that we returned with extra bread.

He looked between the floats. The ebb made a thin river between boats. He put his tongue to his upper lip the way he did when he was reading more than maps. “We can go as far as the first piles,” he said finally. “And you row as if my hands are your hands. I’m done stopping things that don’t want to stop.” When he said that you knew he was reading about more than boats.

We slid the skiff down by hand and tied the painter in a way that made nothing of a knot and yet would hold a man’s life. He stood with his boots braced and I stepped in like a cat onto a wall. I sat on the middle thwart with the oars out and he cast us off with a little prayer that was just a sound. The water took us, but it did not take us far. I rowed. At first the oars blew out of my hands and then I had the feel. When you move the oars through water you learn something about muscles you don’t have, and when you lift them you line your whole inside up like your bones could hum with a note right down from the sky.

He let me pull us into the lane where the float fingers ended and the little channel opened to the river and the ships and the jetty beyond. He reached into the water and lifted a hand, then put his fingers into his mouth. He spat. “Sweet,” he said. “She’s more river than sea right now.” He watched my face. “You look shocked that anybody would put anything like that in their mouth.”

“You don’t know what’s in that water,” I said, but he did, that was exactly what he knew. “Won’t it give you a cough? Worms?”

He grinned. He had three teeth missing in a place that made him kind. “Not today. You can taste the cedar and the mud and the fact that the snow hasn’t all come down. But you can’t have this water in your mouth when it’s raging—the tannin will make you sick and the cold will close your throat. And the sea’s different. Salt and iron and the body’s own taste, and if it’s bad weather there’s a sharpness like a piece of tin on your tongue. You learn your tastes. You put the world into yourself in small pieces until it can’t surprise you.”

We rowed to the piles where the sea lions lay sometimes and where the river poured itself around the legs like a woman trying to take off a tight dress. He told me to hold us there in the eddy behind a green-slime ladder while he pointed at a place on the far side where the dark water curved in toward the jetty and then winged away like a bird. “That’s a back-eddy,” he said. “See where the river tries to go out and the sea says not yet? The sea’s lip curls, and the river turns. There’s always something turning. Remember that. The trick with reading water is half the time you are looking for where it goes and the other half you are looking for where it comes back.”

I tried to keep my hands like his when I sculled, wrists loose, elbows low, his shoulders in my shoulders as if he had made them. It made my back ache in a kind place. He watched my face as much as he watched the water, and after a while he said, “Your jaw goes to the side when you think. Your mother does that.”

I pulled the oars into the skiff and let us drift for a minute while we both thought about what we were going to be when the cannery shut its fans off and the birds realized another kitchen had closed. There were towns like ours up and down the coast all with the same shapes: jetty arms, cannery roofs, tanks with faded paint, a church whose steeple pointed like the stern of a boat, bars with photographs of men in their fish boots taken years ago and never changed. The people running the bars were starting to sell the photographs back to the families for cash.

We stayed on the water until he glanced at the sky the way he did when there was a sentence there that needed a louder voice. “South lowering,” he said. “Cloud bottom falling out. Let’s go in.”

The following week the town smelted. The river came thick and fast one dawn, silver fish with mouths that made them look at you as if they were going to tell a joke. People stood on the banks and by the bridge under their coats and watched the water take light in herds. Someone had told somebody else and then everybody knew and came. By the time my mother finished her shift the fish were tired and had vanished under the bridge piers where nobody could snag them, as if they had decided surprise was their best defense. She laughed and said it was just like the town, to get a run you didn’t expect when you’d packed up your nets and your licenses.

We went down to the north jetty that night because he said the wind would tear us less if we stood with our backs to rock there. He had a pickle jar under his coat. He took it out and unscrewed its lid and said, “Hold this,” like he always said over all kinds of things you would not want to break.

“Why?”

“Just do it.” Sometimes the lesson only made sense when you had already taken its weight. He led me to a place where the jetty sloped down to spray, and he bent and dipped the jar into a seam of water that looked as if it were unarguable. He pulled it up half full.

“Watch,” he said. He tilted the jar slowly, letting the line between jar and air tilt too, point and hip and shoulder. Inside the jar was a tiny sea, slops and bubbles and lightenings. “See how the air slants? If the wind’s three feet above the water it can look like this all day, and you won’t see it unless you watch reflections. A harbor pilot I knew read the water from reflections on a bulkhead once when the fog made eyes into liars. He got the ship side onto the pier like he’d measured it with his hands. That’s reading water too. It ain’t just what moves—what doesn’t move will tell you its own story.”

He talked like that when he had a thing to say. Sometimes he said nothing for so many days in a row you could hear air moving between his molars when he slept.

There was a boy at school who said once that old men’s advice was always a long way of hearing their own voices, that none of us had to be taught men’s tricks anymore because the phones could tell you the tides and you could buy a fish from a freezer with a cartoon label. He said this with his hair spiked up the way you could only get it if your house had a water heater that worked and time. He moved away that winter to a place inland where his uncle had a garage. I can’t remember his name, only the full way he looked at me one day when I was trying to take my own anger out on a thing that would not break easily. He had said the thing about old men with a look that was kind and mean at the same time. My grandfather would have said he was a weather system in need of a barometer.

December’s last storm came late and went only grudgingly. In the middle of it the bar was closed to recreational boats, and a voice on eighteen said in a tired way the thing you hear every winter just before someone ignores it and then the Coast Guard has to leave their hot soup behind. He didn’t like to listen to that part. He turned the radio down so the wind could have a clean shot at him and he went and stood in the lee of the shed and looked at the cottonwoods bending past the school. The river swelled and threw its roots into the air and we all held on. “Sometimes the best reading is refusing to read,” he said. “You put the book down and you say: my eyes, not today.”

But the storm did its other work too. The roads in and out of town closed because a fir fell and took its neighbors with it. The ferry broke down because some part bought from a far country decided it was ready to die. The cannery kept its lights on even when the power flickered and the generator had to sing its deep song in the rain. All evening the men and women on the slime line pushed fish that had come to see a different shape of afterlife, and at midnight my mother’s face when it came out through the door had more tired on it than her bones should have had to hold.

There was a day in January when the air stilled so fast I was sure someone had put their hands around the town. The fog came as I was brushing my teeth and did not leave even at three when the afternoon should have admitted it had broken itself. He put out a hand and opened his front door and watched the fog roll under his knuckles.

“Fog don’t mean a thing if you’ve got your ears on,” he said, which was a thing he had said into so many fogs it had got hard with salt. We went down the front steps and the fog closed behind us so quiet it reminded me of a cat. At the float it was all muffled like somebody had stacked quilts around the world. He stopped and listened, and his face had the little tilt to it that people’s faces get when they don’t realize yet they’re listening with their mouth. “Hear that?” he said after a minute. “Whistle buoy, outer channel. She’s rough on the bar. Hear the interval? It sounds shorter than when it’s flat. Sound comes faster over simple water. Over rough water it scatters on the peaks. And the ferries—they’re heavy on their horns this morning. They’re nervous. People think fog is thick air. It’s not. It’s a lot of little waters hanging up. Your voice goes around those if you know how to send it.”

He stood and counted between bleats and bawls and whistles. “If you ever have to read fog, you slow down your heart. You breathe soft. You let the sound lay over you. It’s like that time when the doctor was listening to your lungs and told you to not help. Don’t help, and your body will tell on itself all day.”

Some afternoons he would take his logbook down from where it lived on the shelf under the framed certificate with its rope border and the old names. The logbook had been written in by hands that had not always been careful with lunch or gentle with tools. In it there were numbers and times, but also more besides. Wind phrases. Smells. What kind of birds were at the river mouth that day. In December of the year I was born he had written only: Green water over the bow and the taste of a coin. And once, a line: Bar closed at 0830 for a man whose luck ran out two days ago. He puts the final shape to our mistakes. I did not understand that then. I read the line now and picture his face, tilted and still.

After that fog day, I began seeing things that I would have walked over before. A streak of smoothness along a windy bank that meant oil or the stripe of a different current and a seam where two stories met and made a heavy clause. A spiral in the drift that seemed to collect itself and then spin apart every three heartbeats where a pilings’ shadow changed the wind. The way the color of the river changed when a cloud went between it and the sun was a different change than when salt water pushed into it, and you could tell which because when the change was the sky’s work, your skin told you the same. He was teaching me to listen and to look at the same time and to trust the parts of my body that knew more than my mind could.

He took me to the memorial on a Wednesday, which was the day we did not go because we liked to save ourselves the weight of all those names mid-week. He stood a while and then he smiled suddenly and put a hand into his coat pocket and pulled out something that caught light the way his words did on a good day. It was a ring, big and thick and plain in a way that made its plainness into a pride. A pilot’s ring. He had not worn it in a decade. He set it on the bronze edge under the names and took his hand away and let it sit in that winter light and then he put it back into his pocket, the way you might test if you could leave like people in stories did. He did not say what he would have said if we had been short of air. He let his hand press the ring through the coat where it could warm against the skin of his hip.

“You miss it?” I said, because there are questions you can ask in front of a certain noise and the noise will keep the pain out of the answer.

He made a soft sound that could have been a word or a bird moving in a tree. “I miss the feeling of being useful that nobody could argue against. I miss telling a thing to itself and it having to say yes. I don’t miss the nights when the water took something and I had to send men home to wives with my head already full of what I wasn’t going to tell the kid.” He slid his hands together and then apart. “I miss the way the bow rose when we got the time right.”

February came faster than time should know how to come when you are counting in winters. The meetings at the town hall had folding chairs that seemed made for the backs of bodies no one had. The guy who owned the property on which the cannery sat talked about tax advantages as if they were a kind of fish that could be gutted and sorted by hand. The woman who ran payroll spoke in a voice in which you could hear the years when she had been the cousin at weddings who danced until lights. People left early because the weather was going to go bad and because it is easier to leave a room than a town. They took their folding chair sounds with them and the room forgot to echo.

The last week the cannery ran a boat came in with a load that had no business out on the bar that day. It was one of the old cowards of a boats, the kind you can’t sink if you try, scarred like a man who laughs at the wrong jokes. Someone had told him he would get two cents more a pound if he brought it in on a day when nobody else could run, and someone else had told his deckhand not to go out if his wife still hadn’t had the baby. The deckhand had gone, because passing on money before the money lands is a prayer no one has taught yet.

We were on the jetty that morning because he had woken at three with his old shoulder a throb and said to the ceiling, “All right, then, I’ll go.” He did not say what we had already read in his shoulder. He did not say that he had known men who could keep their bodies out of other people’s weather but could not keep it out of their own pain, and how they suffered more than they should have because of it. We went to the jetty because there are places where you can put your eyes and your eyes will take a shape that will hold some of your heart without breaking.

The tide was on the last of the ebb, and the swell trains out of the west had that fat, patient look that means they will not be hurried. Far out past the whistle buoy, the white wings lifted and fell as if the horizon were breathing. Closer, between the jetties, the water did its splitting—green water standing into white lines, then collapsing in a shock that sent sound up fluey into the town. He counted with his lips and his knuckles, sets and spaces, and his breath was quiet in his scarf.

He had brought the radio. He had turned it down because he liked to hear the water with his throat, but when he lifted it you could hear the breath of a man on the boat out there as if his lungs were within our walls. He did not say his name. He said only, “We’ll make it when the next lull comes. The tide will turn in fifteen minutes. We can set her in the south channel and slide.”

My grandfather’s jaw went to the side. “South channel’s shoaled,” he said softly, to the jetty and to me and to the gulls. “He’ll get a roller under him that’ll turn him.”

An old friend of my grandfather’s came out onto the jetty then, a man named Jory whose beard was like a rope half unraveled and whose wife had died last year in the other kind of water, coughing out breath in a bed too small for all that air. He put his shoulders against the wind and said, “You hear him? He’s going to cut across the top of it.” He didn’t say the word fool. He didn’t say anything you wouldn’t want carried on a stray bit of frequency.

“He can’t,” my grandfather said. “Not if he wants that bow on top of something when it breaks.” He lifted the radio and he spoke, low. “Boat inbound, this is old history calling. You want to live? Wait. You wait until she gives you the color.”

There was a breath on the other end and then a laugh like a cough. “Who’s this?” the voice said.

“Man who has done this twenty more years than you have been alive,” my grandfather said, his voice quite friendly. “You wait. You count. You will see the darker water make you a tongue to speak on. When it shows, you go. But if you go before she shows—well. Your wife will have to tell that baby you died trying to bring him something I don’t see on your deck.”

There was another breath. Then no sound.

“You can tell him which one to pick?” I asked. But he wasn’t looking at the boat or at me. He was reading me the way a man reads a thing he has loved too long to give away.

“Where’s the tongue?” he said. “Show me.”

I narrowed my eyes and felt stupid. Everything was a shade of steel with milk poured into it. f you looked too hard you could not see—like those trick drawings where the picture agrees to be just a picture if you don’t try to make a thing out of it. I tried to stop my thinking. I tried his trick. I counted the sets and watched the slide of water along the jetty boulders. Three big ones and a trouble of little ones, then a long breath, then two with shoulders on them, then a flat as wide as if someone had laid a hand on every ripple and pressed. In the flat there was a darkness, not a color but a promise of it, stretching from a little left of the south jetty mouth across to where the river had a bent shoulder. It wasn’t simply darker. It had fewer teeth in it.

“There,” I said, and pointed, my finger cold as if it had been kept for a while in a drawer.

His eyes closed a line tighter and then he nodded, once, like a man to his own idea. “Good eyes,” he said, and something in me unlocked in a way I did not know I had been curved. He hit the radio. “Inbound, the break’s got a seam. Starboard of the south jetty. Line her up on the green can and watch the cat’s-paws on the surface. You’ll see where the wind falters. When she does, there’s a hole. Take it. Now. No—now.”

We all held our breath because we could not not. The boat beyond moved the way a dog moves when you call it—head cocked, one foot up. Then it went. It went fast and straight and then faster, and when the first wave built under its bow it rose over it and didn’t get its nose pushed. The second one had more on it, and it slapped the hull like an uncle, but the bow went on climbing. Then the third bent as if something had pressed a hand into its belly. It cracked green and my own belly went to ice and then the boat was over and its stern was down in the fold and the bow rose so high that the keel must have shown to the gulls and then all at once it was in. My jaw hurt, I had been clenching so hard.

Somebody on the radio made a sound like a man bending to his knees to put his hands on his thighs. My grandfather said something that might have been Thank you or might have been Good boy or might have been just Oh. Jory lifted his hand and held it in the air as if someone were going to clap it and for a heartbeat nobody did and then my grandfather did and they both laughed in a way that goes low and fast and has more in it than breath.

We watched the deckhand step off the boat like a man who has not yet learned to put his weight evenly and takes one more step than he needs, and we watched the skipper sling a tired arm over his wheel in a way you would not exhort your son to watch. The cannery men and women picked themselves up and went to the dock edge to meet the boat because it might be the last time they did. The cannery whistle did not sound because the man who pressed the button was at the dock too.

The rest of that day was a long after, the way days are after the thing has done the thing. My grandfather said very little. He let his voice get all the way quiet so the leftover thunder of waves could sit down in him. He came home and made tea and didn’t drink it. He put the ring on and took it off. At two in the afternoon he went into the little room where his winter sweaters lived and took the old pilot coat off a hanger and put it on and stood in the living room in that coat as if it were fourteen below and then took it off again.

“You did good,” he said to me that night, when the radio was off and my mother was still down at the cannery catching up a thing that did not want to be caught. “You saw the tongue.”

“I don’t know how,” I said. But I did. My eyes were at last beginning to understand what my skin had always felt and my ears had always known when they lay on the pillow at night as the winter water moved in the marina. I had stopped telling the water what to say and had begun letting it say what it was saying and in that somehow more of me had stood up.

He smiled. “You waited.”

He did not go to the cannery the day they shut it down. He sent my mother with a loaf of bread and, under it, the ring in a dish towel, which was how he gave her the things he did not want to make a parade of giving. She came home that night with the dish towel folded up and the ring under it still.

“I can’t wear that,” she said. “My hands are wrong for it.” She had a bite of something like there were small flies in her lip. She pushed the towel toward him and he pretended to see something under the table and the towel fell into his lap and the ring slid into his pocket and everybody stayed afloat on that.

The cannery doors closed and they hosed it down and the smell went out of the air so fast it made my back hair rise, like he had said the birds could smell a kitchen’s last dinner. The gulls went away before the men took the last table through the door. The forklifts stopped beeping. The boats pulled in their shore cords in a housekeeping way, the way of a person who intends to return, even if returning is done to an empty room. A poster for a salmon festival three summers ago went on peeling beside the front door at what had been the office until the paper was just two corners and a third that caught air on windy afternoons and made an argument of it.

After that we went to the jetty on days when it was possible for the ocean to be pretty. There were some of those days that winter, pale and flat and easy as eggshell, when even the men with scars sat on their buckets and drank from their thermoses as if their faces could, if they kept their eyes angled right, convince their hearts that other things had not been lost. He would stand with his foot up on the bollard and lean on his knee and point the things out that other people were missing: the thin sheen where an upwelling had turned the cold inside up, the way a color of green learned to be light only when a cloud thin-limned itself and passed, the slap off the end of the jetty that ran back against the running sea in stripes like a flag you could not take a picture of.

Sometimes he would put his hand to the caprock and I learned that this was when he was putting a thing down into the stone that he could not keep any more in himself. He always stood up slow after that and shook his hand out as if it had got too cold, and we would go home, his boots sound sure, my steps quicker because I was warmer and younger and because something had begun singing in me that had nothing to do with songs.

The last day before the first day that was not theirs, the cannery men and women went in and they made sounds like they had made every morning of their lives except that they were different in their heads. They took themselves away from their floor and they stood out on the dock with their arms folded across their own air and they looked at the river like it had let them down, then they went home and looked at the person who slept in their beds like they had let them down, and then they looked at the inside of their hands and forgave nothing. The gulls were somewhere else. The radio muttered about weather. The ferry horn sounded like a horn in another person’s mouth.

“Come on,” my grandfather said, and we went down the front steps and along the road with the blackberries off it and the spruce cones dropping like small thrown rocks under crows, and we came to the south jetty all over again. He stood at the end. He did not take the radio. He put his hands in his sleeves the way he had on the first morning of that winter, and he nodded at things only he had names for and then he took his hands out of his sleeves and placed them, flat, on the scene.

“What?” I said, because it wasn’t what men did who left rings on memorials and sent their daughters dish towels back.

He turned to me very directly and he smiled and his mouth’s edges made their old gentle map. “Now,” he said, “tell me what you see.”

I looked. The river was full and slow, and under the green can buoy a swirl turned on itself regular as breath. The wind came low out of the south toward the bar and put cats’ paws on the half between us and the mouth, and between those there were a handful of dark ovals like the eyes of a person lying on their back, lids closed. Out beyond, where the water tripped and stood and fell, the sets were fives and fours, longer than later in the day, the marbled flanks of them taking the scant sun in a way that turned their edges bright as trimmed nails. Along the north jetty a waterline brushed stone and slipped back and brushed again, steady as someone’s foot in sleep. There was a slick like oil to the south where a seam made a s of itself and gathered floating bark and a dead cormorant feather and a small plastic bit that would not go home. And the tide, when I went to the ladder and took hold of it and leaned my weight, hummed. You could not see that, but I knew it. Behind the ladder there was soft water and in the middle beyond a bow in the current where something down there persuaded the top to shiver for it.

“I see the river telling the sea a thing and the sea telling it to wait,” I said. I flushed at hearing my own voice say this. “And I see a tongue where the water is darker, to the south, like... like somebody is sticking their tongue out at you and daring you to step on it.”

“And would you?”

I smiled. “Not until she says I can.”

He laughed. “Good girl.” He put a hand to my shoulder. His hand was still seawater warm. “Now you’ll take me there someday,” he said, “when I need to put my hand in it one last time.”

I knew before I knew like that sentence you hear and think it means a thing and later you hear it again and realize it meant another. We stood there longer than the cold thought we should, and when we turned to go we both turned the same way without speaking so that our feet took the step together and the old jetty watched us go like a dog that knows the cover of its own people.

We walked home with our faces into the small south wind. The cannery was quiet in that way a thing is quiet that wants to be polite about it. He carried nothing and that made his hands both empty and full. I looked sideways at him and tried to take a measure of the number of days I would have him and failed the way a person fails to keep sand in cupped hands when the sand intended to run. He must have seen me see him like that because he added: “Even when the cannery’s gone, the water keeps its books. We read them together. That’s ours.”

We turned down the road with the big fir and he looked up at the great busted snag where the crows rioted every day at four, then down at the part of the road that turned, naturally as breath, to go to his door. He jiggled the ring in his pocket like a man worrying his luck, then took his hand out again and ran it through my hair in a way he’d never done since I’d been small enough to ride his shoulders and bang on his hat with an open hand. We stepped onto the porch and he said, almost to himself and almost to me, “See the way the water looks when it’s got no one coming to it?” He was looking not at the river anymore but at a thing beyond and under. “It ain’t empty,” he said. “There’s always a shadow running, even in the flat. You keep your head for it.”

In bed that night the halyards hit the masts in a wind that changed its mind every five minutes. It made a rhythm that never quite resolved and so taught you to sleep while waiting. I could see, with my eyes closed, the tongue he had asked me to point to, and I could smell more of the heaviness in the water than I had been told was in the dark. The town breathed, and its breath was shorter than it used to be, and still I thought if I opened the window and put my hand out into the night the river would come up and taste my fingers and know me the way a dog knows the people who live under its roof by the wrist and the heel and the salt of worn things. I slept and woke and listened to water lying down and standing again and the song of the empty cannery doors as they settled on their hinges into their new habit of shutting or of staying open, and I felt under it all the hum of tide changing, the slow turn from out to in.

In the morning he would take me out again. He would show me the parts I had expected would have been changed and the parts that never would. He would put his hand on the rail and steal a little heat from the winter wood and he would press his knuckles into the caprock and leave a record. He would let me show him, when the river wrote new letters for me, what it had put where. We would go on like that until we had learned all the language there was time for, and then I would have to stand one day on the jetty in a different winter and put my own hand down and give the story back. That winter, the last winter of the cannery, the water took our watching and made a shape that had room for us. And we filled ourselves with it, so that later, when the gulls forgot to come and the rings on men’s fingers lost their snugness and the wind smelled of the absence of fish, I could look and still see dark tongues and cat’s-paws and the breath between sets and read a thing that would not stop writing.
