I had not planned to come back here. That is one way to tell it. Another way is to say that I have always been coming back. The drive out felt familiar, the way the same sentence feels familiar even when it appears in a different paragraph. There was that long fall through the pass, the turbines staking their claims along the ridge, blades making the very air look provisional. By the time I turned off the interstate the light had gone blunt the way it does when the wind picks up, a light that flattens the mountains and cuts the palm fronds to paper. You already know the wind I mean. It was that wind. It enters your lungs and makes you want to speak sharply. It makes you believe that nothing is fixed, that everything is a matter of voltage and luck.

I had taken the exit for the resort without looking at the sign, which still had the same old-fashioned promise of “Oasis” in its name. I could have done this in my sleep. I had done it in my sleep, in the mornings I dreamed long after leaving, in which I was looking for a room that was not the one I was meant to clean, a room with someone in it. In the dream you can’t tell the difference between “in it” as in “occupied” and “in it” as in “trapped.” The exterior had been remediated, the marketing copy called it a reimagining. The stucco was now the color of pale sand you could sift through your fingers. There were perforated metal screens cut to look like Moorish lattice. There were fire-resistant plantings, that was the language. Fire-resistant is always language for something else. The valet parking manager had an earpiece and a tablet. He asked for my name like my name was the syllable that triggered the correct choreography.

I gave my name. He found it. I placed my overnight bag in his hand like this was not the place I had once punched a timecard. I stood there in the white-heat without shade for a minute longer than I needed to and did not know why. A bell cap, a boy with a neat knot to his tie, took the bag from the valet and gave me a bottle of water disguised to look like a luxury product. It was the same water. It had the same label no matter where you were. The flowers in the entry were lilies, lilies like the lilies my mother sent when my father died, lilies that smell like grief even when they are purchased for a wedding. There was a marble bowl with floating candles and orchids and the candles were battery-operated. It was midafternoon. A gushing sound came from a water feature designed to be seen but not registered. There was a card stack advertising “wellness,” an elision of body and intention that made the body feel suspicious, and in any event I had never trusted spas, not the way they show you your reflection and call it a service.

Inside the air was cool and uniform and smelled of a fragrance that had a name, if you pulled down the little brochure from the check-out gift shop, and that name would be “Desert Rain” or “White Cedar,” a phrase designed to make you forget what any of it actually was. At the front desk the clerks wore black suits and spoke in a tone I recognized, gentle enough to quiet the brash and brisk enough to repel confession. I did not recognize their faces. This made a kind of sense. Nobody who had been here that night would still be working the front desk. You could be reassigned after a trauma. You could be reassigned to your life and still not recognize it in daylight.

“Welcome,” the woman at the desk said. She wore a small badge that included a first name and an origin story. I had never liked that policy, that little country flag ironed onto a lapel. It proposed a intimacy that could be refused. Her flag was the United States and her story was three words, “Palm Desert, Local.” Not everything requires a story, but we keep attaching them.

“The vanities,” she told me, “have been updated.” She told me about the blackout shades and the Bluetooth mirror. “If you need additional towels,” she said, “just press zero.” These were sentences she said so she didn’t have to imagine what my asking for additional towels would mean. I did not tell her that I had once been the one who brought the towels, the one who made the bed so tight you could balance a book on it, an old trick managers liked to mention in orientation because it allowed them not to say we were paid what we were paid. I signed the paper slip that said I agreed to their policies. I agreed not to light a candle in my room. I agreed not to cook. I agreed not to smoke. I have never been a smoker.

I rode the elevator with a woman who had her hair up already, large pins like beaks holding it in place, and a white garment bag embroidered with “Bride.” She was not the bride I had come to see. You think there is one wedding and there are eight. There were women in matching silk pajamas carrying boxes of shoes. There was a man wearing a polo shirt the color of dehydrated grass carrying a garment bag with a tuxedo folded into it like a trick. I felt the bubble of them, their air, their voices raising and lowering together like the tide in a pool when the wind is up, a tone of attention to the new pecking order, who gets the comb when the stylist is held up on the freeway, who gets the hotel iron first. Every wedding is an economy like any other.

My room was on the third floor. The corridors felt narrower. They said they had expanded the outdoor plan. They had brought the outside in. The outside is a set of problematics in June. Anyone who tells you otherwise has a lobby to promote and an HVAC invoice to bury. I have a friend who calls the resort aesthetic “the tyranny of vases.” Vases taller than you. Vases you could shelter in. Vases suggesting safety through repetition. There is a map on the back of my door. The map did not move when they rebuilt. It still gave me the old sensation, light headed and absolute: the urge to find a stairwell, to hold that knowledge folded like a bill. If you were here, if you had been here on that night, then you would have held the stairwell in your head like a person you meant to keep.

Here is what I had not realized I would think the minute I stepped off the elevator: where were the carts. The metal utility of them. The galleys behind the guests’ doors. If you had asked me the first time I worked here about my ambition I would have given you a sentence someone might have said in a movie, something like “to be useful,” something like “to move up,” because I had believed in ladders then, actual and figurative, I had believed that you could climb a thing and end up somewhere else. I had been the girl in a smock pulling the cart. I had not worn gloves. They told you to. Your knuckles get chafed by the bleach. I did not wear them because they slowed me down, because I had bets with myself, because I liked the weakness of my skin better than I liked the idea that protection was free.

On my first day they took us through the fire exits and showed us the pull stations. We nodded. We followed the man with the key on his retractable ring. He was our manager and his name was Paul and he had a wife who made a certain casserole and brought it to potlucks, and he could recite the state law about occupancy as if he were telling a joke, he could make minimum evacuation times sound like a comedy. Paul died that night, which is only relevant if you think that knowledge makes you careful. Knowledge makes you careful until it does not. I am careful. I am careful and then I am not.

I set my bag down and drank the luxury water. I turned on the mirror with the Bluetooth function to see if it made me look better. I sat on the edge of the bed and felt the same resilience beneath my thigh; they had not changed the manufacturer of the mattresses and I knew this because the squeak had a reluctance to it. I opened the blackout shade and watched the sun hit the pool in a way that made me believe the water had a skin. There were children handwalking along the shallow steps and three men at the bar not yet truly drinking but easing their way into it with the ease that comes from permission, hotel bar drinking being its own release clause.

The invitation had been pink cardstock. The calligraphy had been done by a person with a social media account that documented clean brushes and pale roses. The bride was my cousin. Not my first cousin, the way this would have gone when people lived within a certain ten-mile radius their entire lives, but the child of the eldest daughter of my mother’s dearest friend, which made us cousins in the way that has dividends if you are a certain kind of person and nothing if you are not. They had met at Berkeley, the story went. He was in venture capital, the two words that can mean either missionary work or plunder depending on who is saying them. She taught something about design to undergraduates who wore their hair in topknots and told her she should be on TikTok. The photograph on the invitation had been taken on a beach where the sand had been curated by a retoucher to look cooler, as if proximity to blue were the point. Her dress was shell-colored. His shoes had been removed, his toes pointed toward you as if he were listening.

My own mother had called when the invitation came and said, Do you think you’ll be all right? as if the words on a card could burn. I told her yes and meant it in that moment the way I mean to go to bed at ten and drink water before I have coffee. People revisit scenes, my mother said. She meant that I was one of those people. She did not say: you will look for the housekeeping closet just to know where it is. She did not say: you will know by the smell if the stock is vinegar or Quaternary. She did not say: you will tremble when you see the sign that says Please Do Not Leave Towels On The Ground Because California Is A Desert As If A Sign Could Alter A Geography. She did not say: you are trying to measure whether a thing that happened can be lifted and set down somewhere else like furniture. She has a way of not saying what she knows. She is a kind of tact I have acquired and failed at all my life.

At four I went down to the lawn where the rehearsal photographs were being taken and stood on the periphery, the way I stand in big rooms, as if the floor around me might give. It was one hundred and six degrees according to my phone. The photographer was a thin man in black who made everybody smile with the brisk authority of someone who does not eat before five. The women held their elbows out like birds and asked if their sweat would show through silk. The men held their jackets over one shoulder like catalog models. Someone handed me a Champagne flute and I took it. I never loved Champagne. It seems like wrists and manners to me, but it does change the breath. I watched my cousin lean back against the man she was about to marry. I watched her mother correct her posture without a word, the same touch on the scapula every woman learns. I watched the wind move the hem of her dress. I thought of the smell that comes off new carpet when you first unroll it in empty corridors, or perhaps I did not think of it then but later and applied it to the moment, because memory is nothing if not a series of corrections.

If you had asked me to tell you about the fire I would ask you what you wanted to know exactly, because there was the question of when. You could start with the sound of the alarm not going the way you imagine it does, not the staccato from film but a wide note, a continuous admonition that seemed to thicken the air like a vapor. You could start with not seeing flame but seeing a cross draft of smoke that behaved like a person, a smoke that would not stay in its lane. You could start with slipping and thinking the water on the tile was from someone’s shower leaking under the threshold, because that is what water on tile means in a hotel, a leak, a complaint, a discount, not the thing it was. You could start with the housephones. We used to call them “red” even though they were not red. We used to call them “for emergencies” as a way of teaching ourselves that some calls were different. I did not think it was a different call until it was.

I do not tell you about the man who ran past me without his shoes because he is not my story to tell. I do not tell you about the woman who would not leave without her dog because she is not mine either. I tell you about the closet where we kept the vacuums because that was where I went. I tell you about wet towels pushed under a door because somebody somewhere on a safety video showed me that difference between gas and smoke, because there are these lines you believe you can draw between things when your body tells you there is only the single thing. I tell you about the metallic taste. I tell you about the way fluorescent lights behave under stress, how they lose their detachment and start to chatter. I tell you about the sound a door makes when you shoulder it open because it is swollen with heat and your hands are slippery. I tell you about the feeling when the air shifts and you think: if I were a person paying three hundred dollars a night this is not the air I would expect. You become a concierge of your own survival.

Back on the lawn nobody was thinking about survival. They were thinking about the fact that the florist was late. The flowers were being driven in from the west side because they were specific, peonies in June flown from a place that grows them when they are not supposed to grow, and the traffic had been bad in Beaumont. I watched my cousin’s face when she heard this information and I saw my aunt prepare herself to absorb it. I saw her make the transaction, the mother always making the transaction, the thing nobody else wants to do, paying attention to tiny disasters to hold off the larger one. The men ran their hands along their jawlines and asked where to get a shave. The girls in the silk pajamas ordered iced coffees with oat milk on their phones and sent a boy to pick them up at the café that was new, where every leaf had been placed under a bright light to look like a promise. It would take three months before the Eastern redbud planted just for picture shade attached to the ground the way it was expected to. There are always things we expect of the ground.

My cousin saw me then and she said my name and came toward me with that sudden attentiveness the bride grants to any extra body who might wish her well. She kissed me on the cheek and thanked me for coming as if it had been a journey, which it had. She did not know the terms of the journey. You could argue that neither did I. “Do you love the update?” she said. The thing about brides is that there is a right answer and a wrong answer and they do not need to be told apart. “It looks beautiful,” I said, and it did, the way magazines look beautiful, the way a set for a film looks beautiful when the key grip blows air and makes the nap on the sofa stand up. She told me where to go once the sun moved. She told me what the planners had decided and then dissolved back into the frame of her photographs. When people ask you if you love an update they are not asking you about what was taken away.

By six I had been introduced to the man she was going to marry three separate times by three different people and in every iteration his gesture was the same, that slight bend of the knees, the hand extended with an invincible optimism, the thing men of a certain era learned in sports or in rush week. “We feel so lucky,” he said. That is a sentence you can say only before you know what the word means. I said what you say when a person says “lucky.” I said “So happy for you” and he looked directly at me with kindness and said “Thanks for making the trip.” We had traveled the same number of miles except his were not going to fit in a sentence later. You measure your distances and then you learn they were approximations.

Back in the room I put ice in the hotel plastic and watched it turn to water and watched the water sweat and left a ring on the table as if a ring could mark anything anymore. I lay down on the bed and felt my chest go sharp. You think you will not be the person who gets a migraine on the afternoon of the rehearsal dinner but you might be. I have carried the small amber bottle in my bag as long as I have carried keys. I lined up what I had: my lipstick, my phone, the card with the embossed “A” for the hotel placed in a sleeve embossed with my name, the program with the schedule, the bottle with my name on it, the one I kept in an old sunglasses case because I still felt the need to keep certain medicines undercover. All these items in alignment felt less like readiness than like a ritual I could not finish. The air came up from the vent with a regularity that made me think of machines down the hall. When you work in a hotel you think of the machines. The machines do not stop. The machines outlive you.

I dreamed. I dreamed I was pushing the cart and the corridor widened and then narrowed as if it were breathing. I dreamed the maid’s closet held not linens but rows of blue dresses hung on plastic and that I had to keep them from falling. I dreamed my mother was ringing the housephone saying she could not reach me and I could hear her down the hall. I dreamed somebody from that night, her name was Griselda and she had a tattoo of a sun on her ankle and a daughter in preschool who liked dinosaurs, I dreamed she took my hand and said “No sirve” about the alarm and I told her we should still go. I woke with my heart like a drum dropped down a stairwell. I am telling you this because the logic of dreams seems pertinent when reconciling who you were and who you are pretending to be. You wake up and you smooth your hair and you put in your earrings. You tell yourself this is a party.

At the rehearsal dinner I sat at one of the satellite tables with a pair of uncles who were not anybody’s uncles and a woman from Chicago who said “I do comms” and understood that this needed no elaboration. The centerpieces were low bowls of roses the color of fingernail moons. The wine was poured by a man who, by the position of the little silver tongs in his apron, had once learned to do it differently. People stood up to talk about the bride and the groom and their virtues. There were stories about the way they looked at each other in a coffee line. There were stories about a certain hike and a certain orange that had seemed in the telling to be a part of the storyline of a relationship. There was a joke about spreadsheets and there was a joke about proposal fonts. I sat with my fork lifted and thought about the way metal conducts heat and wondered if anyone at my table had a specific noun for a certain kind of flame, the kind that refuses to be seen until it is everywhere at once. I have been the person at a table who brings up a news item because silence can be functional and because names of cities can be ballast. I did not do that here. I ate the salad and smiled when the comms woman told me that this bride’s brand was “classic fresh.”

After dinner I walked. I did not walk far. I walked until I was standing outside the unmarked door that houses what you would not ordinarily see. The basement is where hotels tell themselves the truth. This is where the laundry goes. This is where the folding tables are and the rolling racks with sheets and the tanks for solutions and the opaque plastic bags for biohazards. This is where the bulletin boards are with notices about benefits and schedules and English as a second language. This is where you return the radios. This is where I had stood pounding at the metal with my open hand because the handle ran hot. This is where a man with a mustache had once put his mouth to my ear and said, “Breathe through your shirt.” This is where the point came at which I thought: this is what it looks like when you leave it. This is where the air cools because the ducts are wide and low and smell of the work of breathing that wages were not allowed to show.

The door opened and a girl in a black blouse and black slacks came out with two trays and saw me and smiled the standard smile, the one that applies in all situations and offends nothing, and said, “Can I help you find something?” and I said “No” without explaining that waiting outside an unmarked door was a kind of catechism for me. She had the nerve I recognize. Her hair was in a bun framed by a hairnet that had been ordered by a manager who called it a “uniform requirement” and therefore did not need to explain that the hair in question belonged to a specific class of women and not to others. She had a little pencil in the pocket that would leave a graphite mark on her shirt. She balanced the weight of the trays with a grace that belonged to someone who had done ballet until her mother could no longer drive her to it. “Big wedding,” she said. I asked her how long she had been here. She said, “Two years, but I was at the Marriott before.” She spoke Marriott as if it were a husband. “Do you like it?” I asked. She lifted the corner of her upper lip in what could be called a smile and said, “It’s work,” and pushed herself back through the door with her hip.

Work is not supposed to be what defines you until it’s taken away. I could tell you who I was in the years after the fire as if that would make an outline. I left the hotel after the memorial. The memorial was in a ballroom not unlike the one where the wedding would be held. We held posies. We read names typed in a uniform font on folded paper tents. People without accents mispronounced names they had coaxed into the mouth of a shared grief. The general manager wore a black tie and told us what we already knew, that we were a family. We were a family in the way people become family when a plane goes down. We are a family and we are not at all. I took the bus to San Bernardino and lived with my friend June on her mother’s pullout couch. I worked at a call center and learned that time could be chopped into five-minute intervals I could sell to people for whom five minutes mattered. I walked in the afternoons past eucalyptus trees that shed their bark like a lesson. I did not talk to the people from the hotel. I did not talk about the hotel. I learned the map of a different set of streets. I learned to get headaches and to take pills and to tell myself it was the fluorescents. I learned to sit on a porch in the heat and not talk until my body cooled down because anything else would be a commitment.

You want to hear that I found God or alcohol. I did not. I found a man who sold insurance and who liked to wake at five. We had breakfast at a diner off Highland where the waitress called everyone “hun” and wrote long numbers and hash marks on the ticket that added up to not enough. We lived together for three years. I listened to him in the shower singing a song from years before as if it were on the radio. He had a mother who said exactly what she thought. He had a father who did not. He told me to go back to school. He told me that if I had stayed at the hotel I might have been sued and he meant this as reassurance. He told me we would have kids in a way that placed the future in a jar where it could be watched. We did not. We got married anyway. We got divorced. This is not a story about a marriage. This is a story about the way a place can be taken out of you only when you build it again.

You might find the fact that I was here now, in a dress I bought on sale and with new shoes I was trying to break in, perverse. You might think I am courting it. You might think I am this person who drives back to the scene not to understand but to hold the scene in my hand again, to make it small, to make it fit. You would not be completely wrong. You would be missing the fact that I liked my cousin and loved her mother and that they had stood in parking lots with me, years ago, and handed me folding cash and demanded no plan. You would be missing the fact that I wanted to see their faces when they were happy and to put that in the same file as the other things, the file where the paper gets too thin to hold the clips, the one drawer where familiar and dangerous both live. You think you will have more than one drawer. You have one.

On the morning of the wedding I went down to the pool and swam ten laps like a person who believes in the way her bones work. I counted while I swam, even though the tiles did it for me. A man in sunglasses the shape of shields watched a girl put sunscreen on herself with the kind of attention that is both maternal and medical. There were two women with their hair wrapped who spoke French and ate from plates delivered under a silver cloche before nine a.m. because their day called for provisions. I watched a boy with a scab on his knee discover that the steps got deeper as he went. I felt the water on my skin and thought about the scorch I could see in the crowns of the palm trees, where the wind had taken it upon itself to remove what was extraneous.

On another morning, a morning in a different year, the houseman had handed me a pile of rags bleached to a common denominator and said “We’re out of towels” and I had thought no we are not, what we are out of is the capacity to admit what we are out of. I told you I would tell you the truth and then I did not. I have not told you about the person who did not make it out of the conference room off the spa because the oxygen was pulled out of that room so fast she sat down. I have not told you because it sits on my chest and turns my words to metal. I have not told you because if I begin to tell you it is raining inside me even when it is not raining in the sky. I have not told you her name because it was Virginia and I had always meant to like a woman named Virginia but I had not known her at all until I saw a picture of her later, a picture where she was holding a dog and laughing at something to the left of the person with the camera. You learn these flashes later. You hold their smallness and their ferocity.

At noon there was a sound. It was the sound of testing. The hotel tests its alarms on Tuesdays, except it was Saturday. The sound was not an alarm. It was a short percussive thing the engineer uses to make sure the horns will not stutter. I was not the only person who flinched. A woman getting her makeup done raised her fingers to her temples and closed her eyes to preserve the eyeliner. A man refilled his cup from an urn labeled “Infused Cucumber Mint” and did not spill. The girl from the unmarked door came out and looked down the hall toward me. I looked back. We held the look for perhaps too long and then I made a face that meant “Well, okay,” and she made a face that meant “It’s always something,” and we resumed the day. This is the choreography of living where you once had to run.

The ceremony was held on the far lawn under a structure that had been hand built and draped with fabric in a way that pretended weightlessness. When the procession began the speakers played a song that made the older men nod along and the younger men check their posture. The bride came down the path with her father. He was crying in the precise ratio a father is permitted to cry during a ceremony in which photographs will be taken. The officiant was a woman who had a way of reading from paper as if none of the words had ever been said and would not be said again. She used the word “intention” and she used the word “community” and she used the words “lean in” not in the sense of career but in the sense of weathering. The wind picked up and the fabric above them pumped like a sky. The men reached to hold down the programs. The women reached to hold down their hair. A child stood up and said “Look” to no one. The lighting people took the wind into account. They adjusted the amber gel and made the bride look like what a bride looks like in magazines. If you think I am making fun of this you are not listening to me. The thing about weddings is that they make the logic of the world explicit. The structure is this: we will all sit in rows and watch two people tell us a story. They will cry slightly. We will cry slightly or not at all. We will bear witness to a thing we have agreed is meaning.

After, the sky did what it does in the desert at five in June. It went coral and brief and then the light fell out of it. The air cooled by the uncertainty of it. There were drinks under a tent where the men in black with the tongs poured. There was a table with little cards written in the calligraphy with our names on it and a line that pointed us toward the places where our places would be. There was a gravel path and a man in a vest telling us “Watch your step” as if the step were the only thing between us and falling. We watched our step. The band did a sound check and then the band only existed as sound. There were speeches again. There was a story about how the groom had a fear of heights and then there was a joke about altitudes of feeling. There was an aunt who tried to keep the sound of her voice on the level she used in living rooms and then realized she needed to pitch it to the lawn. There was cake with buttercream. There was the first dance and the father-daughter dance and the mother-son dance. There was the song where everybody stands up and the one where the single people are indicated and the one where the old people amaze everyone briefly with dexterity.

I stepped away. I took my dessert fork with me. I moved to the periphery and then further so that I was standing under one of the olive trees the planner had rented. It had been wheeled in that afternoon on a dolly. It had a root ball like a secret. Its leaves caught the light of the bistro strings and turned them into something more believable than an idea. The hotel rose behind the tent, its white skin reflective and without history. I tried to imagine flames against this wall. I could not. This was not the wall. This was the replacement wall. The pumps that fed the water feature were humming. Behind the unmarked door that would never quite be in the same place again the washers with their metal mouths were agitating. The girl with pencil marks on her pocket was probably in a stand-up meeting in the corridor that feeds to the banquet kitchen, somebody saying “keep clear of the service route, we’re going to break down the dessert station quickly and swing to the after-party set-up by ten thirty.” There is a language there. It is a relief. There are verbs like “strike” and “drop” and “swing” and “load-in.” It is a dance the body knows when it has lived with institutional time.

Somebody in the tent knocked a candle. The candle fell into a dish and everyone laughed and the woman whose hair was combed like a waterfall put it right. The smell of a certain kind of smoke entered the air. It was nothing. It was not even a candle. The candles were battery-operated. I understand that. The smell was the idea of a candle. We write our own fictions in every room. And then, very softly, not in the insistent voice of the mother of emergencies but in the calculating voice of a system, a sound. People looked up. The way they looked up was reptilian. It is in you, the looking up. The sound stopped before it had fully started, a feedback loop aborted. People pretended it had not occurred because that is the weather of a party.

It occurred to me that I had been waiting for a sound all day. It occurred to me that I had set a trap for time and had hoped it would spring. I watched my cousin’s hair come loose while she danced. I watched the boy with the scab run along the lawn until he tripped and then he lay on his back and looked at the strings of lights above him like a map. I watched the bride and the groom step into a little anteroom beyond the tent where the planner had made a space with chairs and water bottles and two napkins folded into triangles, where the bride’s mother would go in a minute and lean in and say a thing that would steady her. I watched the way the servers carried plates high like the old seductions. I watched the girl with the hairnet tuck her pencil behind her thumb to lift a tray above a man’s shoulder. I watched her cough and then stop. I watched the face she made when she had to navigate two men in conversation who had not learned that not everything expands to make way.

The band switched to a song that had a sotto voce part and everybody leaned forward to catch it. The air cooled further the way it does just before midnight in a desert with watered grass. People felt safe. They were. That is the other truth. You can rebuild and close the circuits that were once left open. You can triple the sprinklers. You can put doors on magnet holds. You can put carpet that will smoke less. You can insist on flame-retardant drape and nonflammable foam. You can insist on signage and audits. You can. And you also cannot unlive a thing. You can only live it with new furniture.

I do not mind telling you that at the end I went not to my room but to the basement. I went to the door and this time it was not open and I stood there the way I have stood at other doors, at doctors offices where I was going to tell them everything, at gates to flights I believed would take me elsewhere, at the doors of people I loved. The door was metal painted the color of putty. If you put your hand on it you would feel the hum of what the building considers its own body. I put my hand on it. This is the thing about thresholds. They do not mean much until you have to convince yourself to cross them. A boy came walking by with a stack of trays and he pulled open the door with his foot and said, “You need back here?” the way a person will ask a question to see if there is anything unusual about the answer. “No,” I said. “I used to work here,” I said, and the way I said it was a contraction that included the years and the receipts and the smell of bleach in my hands and the day I brought a banana with a bruise and the way we laughed when Andrea found the pair of underwear in the hallway and the morning the ice machine was off on 2 and we found the broken line. I said it the way you say a name you were given and then set aside. He said, “A long time ago?” not as a challenge but as a way of calibrating. “Long enough,” I said, and he lost interest and shouldered the door wide, and the smell came up then, not smoke, not fear, not the air of that night, but the cool soapy smell of linen. He moved onward, and the door closed, and I stood with my hand to the metal and felt the thrum pass into me.

Upstairs the bartender was counting cash. A woman in a dress with a seam like a question mark was asking the front desk to call a car. My shoes had succeeded in breaking me in. My cousin took off one earring and then the other and set them on a saucer of a coffee cup because that is where there was space. People were hugging with the sleepy largesse of the last hour. Outside the wind had dropped. The turbines above the pass had slowed and brightened and taken on the aspect of punctuation. In my room the blackout shade fell with a finality that was also a small mercy. I stood and brushed out my hair and found that my hair had acquired the scent of the place, a thing that would linger on my pillow for days. I undressed. I lay down on the bed and placed my hand on my chest as if my heart needed to be contained. The air came on and then stuttered and then came on again.

I made a small inventory. I had the lipstick, the two aspirins, the card key, the program now folded in half around the part of the night you forget, the receipt from the bar, the matchbook where they still put the name of the hotel as if anybody is in the habit of taking fire to their own pockets, and the ribbon from my cousin’s bouquet which she had handed to me without reason and without comment, a length of silk the color of that moment after sunset when the world cannot decide. I placed these on the table. I turned off the bed lamp. I did not turn off the bathroom nightlight. I lay there and listened to the quiet and then the not-quiet and then the quiet again. I thought of Griselda and Paul and Virginia and the man who ran without his shoes and the way the fluorescent lights chattered down the hall. I thought of the girl with the pencil in her pocket. I thought of myself in the mirror with the Bluetooth that made my face into a screen. I thought of my cousin sitting in the anteroom with two triangular napkins and a bottle of water and the way her mother bent at exactly the right angle to speak. I thought of the door to the basement that I had touched and the way the metal had felt like a weather. I thought of the map on the back of the door with its green arrow indicating You Are Here. I do not know what they mean by here. I closed my eyes. I knew what I meant.
