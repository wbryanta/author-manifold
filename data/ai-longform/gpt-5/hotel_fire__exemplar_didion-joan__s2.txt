They had rebuilt the hotel with a facade of pale brick and a tendency to call itself by a name that had not meant anything before the fire. The new brochures spoke about legacy, about a careful nod to the region’s “agrarian past,” about how the glass panels of the lobby reflected the “luminous sky-flooded light of the Sacramento Valley.” All this had been written by someone who had never at five in the morning snapped a ragged bedspread into the air and watched the dust float in the only shaft of light between the blackout drapes. I had the brochure in the side pocket of my overnight bag because a girl with sprayed hair and a sash that said “Guest Experience” had handed it to me at check-in, together with two thin bottles of water with silver caps and a paper that said if I needed anything I could text TESS-EVAN-LOVE to a number and someone would bring me whatever that was.

When I had worked here it was not called the Elysian on the River. It was called the Pioneer. The marquee had been an old neon sign in the shape of a paddle wheel and, in summer, June bugs had collected around the bulbs. The old lobby had been heavy on brass. There had been a planter with a birds-of-paradise that the night clerks had watered too often and which had gone limp and fungal at the roots. You could smell it when you came on at six to strip the family suites, a smell like cut green stems left too long in a glass.

“It doesn’t look the same,” my Aunt Rena said, shading her eyes with three fingers as if the glass front and the river too had the sun on them and not just the parking circle. She had driven me in on a Thursday afternoon because I had told her I did not want to drive my own car over the causeway. The traffic made me sweat and pick at the skin around my thumbs. She had a new nail color, a gel with tiny gold flecks that were supposed to look like champagne. “You said it was dark inside. You said it had that old-hotel dark.”

“It did,” I said.

“And now it isn’t.”

“They took anything that held heat out of the brochure,” I said.

She lit a cigarette and then put it out because a man in a light blazer said in a friendly way that there was no smoking on the property. She nodded and held the cigarette between her fingers for another minute, as if it might go on working by itself.

We were here because Livia, who was my cousin and the daughter of Rena, was marrying a man whose family had a vineyard up by Winters. The wedding planner had suggested the Elysian as a venue because it was back in operation and because there was a new terrace with glass balustrades that looked over the bend of the river and took the light at six o’clock. There were drone videos of the terrace. In the drone videos a child in a smocked dress ran across the pavers and you could see a little glint where her hairpins were. In the drone videos a woman lifted a glass of prosecco against the blue and no one had sweat on their lip.

“I can’t tell if I’m supposed to be in awe or if it’s all a trick,” Rena said. “Where’d they put the coffee shop? You said there was a coffee shop.”

“There was,” I said. “It was called the Rudder Room. It sold cigarettes and postcards with the Capitol on them.”

We stood there because I felt a thing I could not quite describe. It was the feeling you get when you go into a hospital you were once in for a very specific reason and now you are only there to see someone else hold out a baby. The air was cold in the lobby and it smelled like a clean that had money to it. There were no smudges on the revolving door. The fish in the aquarium were new fish. No aquarium had existed here before.

A man we were supposed to know came toward us with his hand out. “Now the two of you,” he said, as if we had been expected for a show. “We were wondering if you’d get lost and end up at the other hotel. There’s another hotel somewhere, someone said.”

“This is it,” Rena said. “We got parking like we belonged.”

He laughed, and then he was already looking for someone else, the way men here looked past you not because you were nothing to them but because their attention was a social budget they had learned not to exhaust on any one person. He had a name that began with a K and a wife with shining hair and a new chipped bracelet from New Mexico. Rena watched his back and then made the face she made at her own mirror, a look that pulled down one side of her mouth and that she did not think was visible to other people.

“I thought you might want a minute,” she said to me, low.

“I already took it,” I said.

She took the room keys from the girl with the sash. She had the heart-shaped welcome cookies, the itinerary in charcoal ink with the words in a typeface that had little hooks on the capitals. She had a little bag with a jar of something that tasted like jalapeño chutney and a card from Livia and Evan thanking guests for traveling far for their day.

“They didn’t mention you in the history,” Rena said later, when we were in the room and she was opening the chutney, not to eat it but to smell it. She made the face again. “I mean they didn’t mention the staff at all. It’s like it happened to the building.”

“I only made beds,” I said.

She placed the jar on the desk and turned the itinerary so I could read the times. There were many times. There was a welcome drink on the terrace at five, then a dinner for “bridal party and immediate family” at seven, which did not include us, then an after-dinner drink for “all out-of-town guests” at nine. There was something called “sound bath” at ten the next morning for any guests who wanted to “center.” There was an instruction not to bring gifts to the wedding.

“The mother of the groom texted me,” Rena said. “She asked could I do without perfume on Saturday because someone’s aunt has a scent sensitivity. I said I don’t do perfume but I’ve got body lotion I suppose counts.”

“That’ll be fine,” I said.

“Don’t wander off,” she said.

I took a shower in the new room because I had been thinking about the water pressure since we parked. The shower was glassed in and the water ran over my hands with the force of a downpour. The tile was arranged in a pattern that suggested herringbone and looked expensive without being stone. On the back of the door a card said WHERE TO FIND YOUR NEAREST EXIT and showed a plan of the floor in a way that made the hallways look like a simple loop and not like the little dead ends and closet alcoves I remembered.

Once at six in the morning the sprinkler had gone off in a third-floor room because a girl from housekeeping had hung a dress on the head of a sprinkler and then gone to do the sink. That had been before the fire, when we were all still doing shifts in shoes that had not yet been greyed at the toe. The water had run and run into the bedspread and over the carpet to the hall. You could not get it to stop by turning anything with your hand, not even if you pushed the little tongue where the water came out. You had to call Maintenance and Maintenance had to call someone who had to come from home.

When I think about the day of the fire I think first about what time it was. It was a little after four because the news on the Channel 13 morning show was showing a story that had been on the night before about a ballot measure. The sound on the television was low because Inez, who was my partner then, liked to watch the screen with her own words running. The channel logo said 13 in a red square and a woman with white teeth was moving her mouth about tax repeals.

“Those are not white enough,” Inez said, adjusting the dust ruffle under the bed with the flexible wand of the vacuum. “She pays four hundred dollars for each tooth and that’s what it buys her. Four hundred dollars a tooth,” Inez said, then said it again because that made it truer. “What would you do with four hundred dollars, let me ask you.”

I had said something about plates. I had said something about buying six white plates because mine had the chips on the rim where your lip catches.

“There,” she said. “There’s your fire plan.”

“Their fire plan is a cardboard on the door,” I had said, because I had just read it. You had to read the fire plan in each room because that was a rule the supervisor had once found in a manual. It did not say you had to memorize it. I had not paid attention. In my life I had not imagined a hallway full of smoke.

When the first scream came Inez turned the vacuum off and we both heard our own breath. I took my hand and put it flat on the TV to hear the noise that lived inside it, the little sizzle you could hear even when the sound was at six. Then other sounds. Metal on metal. Someone saying “God” and then “oh God oh God.” Inez shut the door to the hall on a force that pushed the door back at us. When we opened it again it was already thicker. I am trying to tell it the way it was. We had learned that you kept low. We had learned that you took the stairs. The words ran through my head like something I had read and told myself I understood.

I do not know why I tell you all that now except that I still in lobbies look for the closet where the extra vacuum bags are kept, and the door to the stair, and whether the front desk is staffed by someone who looks like they would risk their breath to call your name.

We went down to the welcome drinks because Rena had changed into a dress that looked like something you would wear to a shower at the country club. We do not belong to a country club. The dress had dropped shoulders because that was the thing in the store and she had bought it at a place that put tissue paper between each sleeve. She held her shoulder with one hand as we went out on the terrace, as if the dress might move without her.

Livia came across the terrace in a dress the color of the dust on a lily pad. She put her hands on my wrists when she hugged me, like they had taught her somewhere, like you were not meant to hold anyone’s hips or shoulders anymore because that could be misunderstood. “You came,” she said. “I knew you would but I was afraid you wouldn’t.”

“I was afraid too,” I said.

She made her mouth wide, not certain if that was the kind of answer you were supposed to laugh at.

“We’ve got so many things,” she said. “I can’t even keep track of them. There’s something involving wish paper, you’ll see, and these Christmas lights like a ceiling. I know we should have done it sooner or later but Evan’s mother had an opinion and you cannot argue her out of an opinion, you must just live as if it had been yours all along.”

“She thinks we’re all allergic to perfume,” Rena said to Livia, admiring the pine needles stuck in the center of the hydrangeas on the bar.

“She sent me a list,” Livia said. “The list says ‘avoid’ and then it lists out the world. Anyway. Thank you for coming. I meant that,” she said, her voice low then high then low, as if she were adjusting it to sound sincere because she was. “You look good,” she said to me. “I mean you do.”

“I put on lipstick,” I said.

“She was worried,” Rena said. “She thought you would forget.”

“I thought I wouldn’t get here,” I said.

“Same thing,” Rena said, and shook one of the fake pine needles back into the hydrangeas.

A man with a microphone and a well-rehearsed casualness introduced the evening as if there were cameras. He said that the Elysian wanted to welcome us back to a property that had been “part of our river city’s story for going on a hundred years.” He did not say what had happened. The river was running slow, brown and one surface. You could not see the current from the terrace, only the way it held the reflection of a cottonwood as if the cottonwood were on its way under. The wedding planner had pinned paper lanterns in the trees. The lanterns were not yet lit.

I saw the new manager. He was younger than the last one and he had a way of standing with his hands behind his back as if it had served him on cruise ships or in uniform. I had known the old manager. He had a son who had cut class and come by to sleep on one of the lobby couches. He had taken a plastic spoon from the coffee stand and clicked it against his teeth until it was sharp enough to scratch his name into the varnish on the side table. The old manager had not known what to do with his son at two in the afternoon in a lobby. He had stood with his hands on his hips as if he were listening for a pump to turn itself off.

The new manager saw me looking at him and gave me the hotel smile. The hotel smile is neutral and bright and not about you, not about the place either, only about the fact of being here with you in a continuous transaction. He came over and asked if we were “with the party.” When I said we were he asked me my last name and when I told him he glanced to the right as if that could trigger recall. He held out his hand and could not decide if he was meant to shake Rena’s, mine, or no one’s.

“I remember when it was the Pioneer,” I said.

He lit then. He said words I have heard all my life. “Oh, the old girl.” As if the old building were a woman and had moods and we had all been there together when she had them. “I’ve only been here since the relaunch,” he said. He said “relaunch” the way people say “concert” about a wedding band and “programming” about a television. “I hear the stories,” he said. “Love a good story.”

“I worked here when—” I began and felt suddenly that I had told this too many times to the wrong people.

“When it was a different time,” he said, giving me the hotel way out of telling any particular thing.

“I worked here,” I said.

“My heart,” he said, hand to left breastbone. “We stand on the shoulders.” He said it like that. He moved away. I could not tell if he had meant it.

The bar had a jar with cucumber slices floating and a picture of a dog in a necktie to say tips were appreciated but not required. A woman with heavy earrings offered me something called a Paloma. I took water. I watched the man with the microphone direct two teenagers in the matter of the cords for the lanterns. I watched a busboy carry a bucket that had a rubbery top to keep if from sounding like what it was.

“That him,” Rena said then, and nodded almost imperceptibly at a man who had come out onto the terrace with a girl on his arm who was not his wife.

“Who,” I said, because I had not wanted to talk about any of the men who had any need to walk across any of the terraces in this town.

“The one who used to take you home after the late shift,” Rena said. “The one with the eyes that were too blue to be real.”

“That wasn’t his name,” I said. “And anyway he didn’t take me home. He took me to Round Table for a slice.”

“That one,” she said.

He had dyed his hair in a way that made the years show more. He was wearing a shirt with a pattern of small flowers. There is a thing with men when they get near sixty and discover a shirt they never would have worn at thirty. The girl was talking with her hands to someone else. She looked like a babysitter you would not trust with your child because it might be your child who needed saving.

I did not look at him too long. There are people who bring with them the exact weight of a time and if you look too long you will feel your sleeve catch. I went to the rail and looked instead at the band of gooseweed at the edge of the water and the three pigeons bathing in it, right at the part where the river made a second brown on itself.

“Come,” Rena said. “We don’t have to know anyone. That’s the freedom.”

We did not stay long. It was not even dark when we went upstairs. The elevators had a way now of giving you a shine in the metal walls that made you look as if you were deeper into your own face than you had wanted to be. Rena took off her shoes in the elevator and put her hand on my elbow for the last foot of the hall. She had a toe that bent under when she wore heels. In the room I sat on the bed and made the television show me a list of channels. They did not have Channel 13.

“Don’t go look,” Rena said when she saw that I had my key in my hand again. “Whatever it is you’re going to look for, it isn’t there.”

“It’s still there,” I said. “You can’t get rid of an angle in space by giving it a brochure.”

I went out. The hall had the square black numbers at the top of each door and the light of the sconces made the carpet a long blur toward the exit. The old place had smelled like carpet deodorizer and bleach. This smelled like cedar oil and ice. The stair door made no sound when I opened it. In the old building there had been a clang that sat in your teeth. The new stair was upholstered in this thin rubber they put on stairs so a mother with a stroller is meant to be able to do it in a dream. The light had that hard white that wants to be a day. Between the second and the third floor the wall had a little framed thing with a photograph of the hotel in 1936 when there had been a dock here. Men in hats stood with their backs to the direction of wherever they were going.

I put my hand on the rail. It was cold and rough, a metal that had not been glossed. Down one flight. Down two.

I stopped at the landing of the floor we had not taken, the fifth, the one where I had been when. I could see the landing door, which was not the door I had opened that morning. I could see the number 5 on its round plate. I opened it and went in because I had told myself what was I afraid of. It was not the same hallway. It was shorter, cleaner, more logical. Where the long stretch had been with the wide interruption of the laundry chute, now there was a straight shot to the end and a frosted door that said “Service.” The ice machine had no sound. The wall art showed a canoe.

Then someone said, “Can I help you,” and there was the girl in the navy dress with a key badge and the shoes you can buy only if you expect to stand a lot and not care. Her name was Letty, it said.

“No,” I said. “I used to work here. I just wanted to see.”

She had a neutral look that I could not read. It was not the hotel smile. It was the look of a girl from Yuba City or West Sacramento or Galt trying to gauge if this interaction was the one that would change her life for the worse.

“You were housekeeping,” she said. She said it like a question but also not a question because I was looking at the place where the laundry cart would go and because some of us carry the posture of our first jobs.

“Yes,” I said.

“I’m in laundry,” she said. “My aunt was here when they had the—you know.” She tilted her head. It is so that we tilt our heads at some things, as if by changing the way our ears take in the world we can make it sound different. “She said it was the smoke that felt like it had a hand.”

“What’s your aunt’s name,” I said.

“Estela,” she said, and looked to see if that meant something to me.

“I had an Estela,” I said. “This was a long time.”

“I wasn’t born,” she said. “Do you want to see the back? Sometimes people want to see. The photographer this afternoon wanted to see. He said he’s into liminal spaces,” she added, without making fun exactly.

“They’re all liminal,” I said. “Backs of houses.”

She opened the Service door with her key. The hinge did not scringe up like anything in my memory. The service hall smelled like laundry powder and dampness. There is always a dampness that lives in any place where sheets go into machines and come out to be folded. She led me past the Housekeeping Office, which had a whiteboard with the names of the girls in columns and long checkmark lines next to each name. There were posters about preventing slips and the angle at which you should lift. There was a shelf with the gray bins where the paper product is kept and a box of something called “eco guest amenity.” Someone had pinned a smiling baby on the bulletin board. Someone had pinned a name tag that said DOLORES and a ribbon that said HAPPY 10 YEARS.

“I just want to see the stairs,” I said, and then when I saw the stairs at the end I did not go into them at once. One thing I had not remembered until the door was in front of me was the laundry chute door we used to throw the bags down. Inez would say “Make it look like it was a trick shot on TV,” and then swing the blue bag so it fell just so. Once a houseman had been there with his arm inside for some thing he had dropped and we had laughed because we were not paying attention. He had pulled his arm out with a strip of red and he had not made a sound. He had shown us the cut like it was a prize, proud that he had not dropped enough blood to make more work.

“Do you want some water,” Letty said, suddenly maternal.

“I’m okay,” I said. “Do you have—” and then it was as if to ask anything more would be to make her part of anything I had come here to do.

“I’ve got to go get the steamer,” she said. “There’s a dress.”

“That’s always true,” I said.

She looked over my shoulder at the long corridor and I could tell that she saw something I could not, some overlay of the living with the map of the old. Then she went back around the corner and I stood still in the service hall in a place where once there had been a mop in a bucket and a feeling I had then and did not have now that I would be here long enough to be known.

In the stair I sat down. When you sit in a stair in a hotel you are not anyone to anyone except the manager who will ask you to move because of a policy. These stairs were clean. There were no scuffs from the luggage. There was a mark on the second step where someone had dropped a bottle of something oily and then cleaned it with the wrong product so that it showed as a slightly different sheen.

In the old stair there had been noise like an animal’s breathing. The people who were on their way down had been making that high breath that comes when a laugh is stuck. I had thought of calling my mother and then remembered that I did not know what my mother would have been able to do for me between here and where she had stood at her sink that morning in the kitchen whose window faces the fence. There are things you know that you cannot articulate during an emergency. That knowledge was one of them. I knew that if I was meant to bend down and crawl I would do that because someone had told me that once in the sixth grade. I knew that if I knocked on the door and a voice came that I would put my hand and then my shoulder against the door and then the plastic of my name tag would melt. I knew these things and I knew how to strip a king bed in nine motions and I knew which floor the man with the yellow teeth would be on based on his cigarette and his hair.

I heard the hum of the building. I looked at the red glass of the alarm that did not yet and did not then. Someone opened the door two flights below and shut it again, one push, one sigh, the seal. I thought about my friend Alba, who had been on the eighth with me and who had not come back to work after. She had gone to stay with her sister in Turlock and then I had lost track of her. I have lost track of many people. There are any number of lives I could call to ask if they remember the girl whose mouth used to move along with the soap operas while she scrubbed a tub. There are any number of voices I have asked for in the morning and at night, and then not found.

I went back out because there would be a dinner I was not meant to attend and Rena would be in the room with the air so cold her bits of hair would stand. I found the elevator and pressed LOBBY and then stared at the red of the letters as if they mattered. I pressed 4 instead. When the door opened on 4 I was faced with two tripods and a man with a tape on his wrist measuring the height of a peony in a cylindrical vase. He saw me and raised his eyebrows.

“I’m not,” I said.

“Us either,” he said, not moving.

There were many wives with daughters in the lobby. They were all talking about the seating chart. There was a rectangle with names on Post-its and a method I did not understand. Livia’s future mother-in-law made a face like a person looks at a chessboard and moved a Post-it to a table that had a little printed card that said WASHINGTON. The girl who was a bridesmaid and who had taken on the role of enforcer said that Washington had been full and that Washington had meant “the older cousins.” The mother-in-law said that the older cousins could be Maine for all she cared and the girl said that Maine had been Evan’s college friends.

“What do the states mean,” I asked Rena later, because she had come down with a sweater on and her hair wet, and had seen too much.

“They don’t mean,” she said. “They structure.”

People took pictures in front of a wall with pink flowers stuck to it. A neon sign said BETTER TOGETHER. A woman with a clipboard told a man with sleeves rolled up that he could not please everyone and he said or so they say. In a corner a small child had discovered the strip that plugs into more plugs and flicked its switch to on with a judge’s satisfaction.

“Go get us ice,” Rena said, as if my connection to ice had continued past everything else.

In the ice room there was a smell like cold and a little machine making the sound of falling, like gravel. I touched the front with my palm as I always do because a lifetime of knowing whether the machine is working is not undone by a brochure. I filled the yellow room bucket with one hand. A man came in behind me and asked if I thought it would rain. I looked at the little picture of a cloud with a dot on my phone and said I did not think so.

We sat in the room later and ate the cookies they had given us in the heart bag and Rena cried and did not admit it and I said I had been in the stair and she said that had been a bad idea and I said I would not go again and then I did because there is a way you test things so that they become less of themselves.

The morning was blue and flat. The sound bath was in the Camellia Ballroom and I could not imagine what that was, and did not go because the idea of lying on a mat in that room where there had been once a fundraiser where men with cufflinks had shaken hands until their palms were a shine felt to me like a risk. I saw Livia, hair in a loose thing, bare face, touching the corner of a napkin to suck the lipstick off later. I saw Evan in a suit I had never seen him in before and which made his waist a different waist. He looked very young. I was older than him by only a little but what is a little when you have seen people breathe the way they breathe when the lights have gone to their night shape at four in the morning.

The ceremony was at six. They had set up the chairs on the terrace to face the river and the man in the microphone was back but now with a smaller voice that tried to pretend he was not the hotel. He had a string quartet behind him. They played songs we all knew reimagined as here we are. Someone had thought to give everyone a tote that said L+E RIVER LOVE and inside had been bug spray, because they had been told that the river at six would bring a lace of gnats. The gnats did not come. The river was still. The hotel stood in its glass. The one gardener who always pulls into these spaces when everyone is watching to wheel a can of compost through the arch had been told to do that at seven.

“Don’t cry,” Rena said, and then cried the most.

I did not cry. I watched the candle that had been lit to stand for the grandparents who could not be here because they lived in other cities. I watched it tremble and then recover and then I watched the boy, the ring bearer, pick his nose with his ring finger and look at the sky as if he were the first to see something there.

When people stand up and talk at weddings I tend to watch the person in the second row who had been asked to make sure the bride’s train does not fold on itself. That person does not know about the life they are watching begin because they never have and never will. They know the shape of the satin. They know the way the pins must be pulled out like fish bones.

A voice spoke and we listened and a breeze came up and the paper lanterns overhead moved a little and made a sound that was like a very quiet applause. The man in the microphone said we were all thinking of those who could not be with us today and there was a sound that was almost a prayer. People held their breath and I heard someone say under theirs that this was just. I do not know what they were going to say next.

The candle went out. I saw it before anyone else did because I was watching it. It went like a thing that has made a plan to leave in the middle of a conversation, a thing making itself small in case anyone tried to stop it. Someone reached toward it and then did not touch it because that had been decided too. Livia saw me seeing it. She had a look for a second as if the whole thing had been a test. She took a breath and smiled and said the words they had taught her to say and then said some of her own. A fly landed on his lip and he did not move it and she did not laugh.

After there were drinks again. After there were little pieces of fish with a hot sauce on them and there were waiters with trays and there were people you had seen your whole life who now made you feel they could see past you but would not, not because of anything you had done but because there was too much to hold at once. The girl with the flower earrings told me her grandmother had been on the tenth when. She asked me what it had been like.

“Loud,” I said.

“Like loud loud,” she said, as if clarifying would make it something I could hand to her.

“Like loud,” I said, and put my hand on the rail of the glass balustrade because I was not falling and had no reason to hold on and still did.

The speeches began and people laughed when the men said they had known the groom when the boy had done some boy thing in a field. People used certain words. “Aristocrat” was one. “Class” was another. I have never liked the way people here talk about class as if it were a thing you could dress in or out of. A woman in a dress with a pattern like a tile you would see in a bathroom in Spain laughed her laugh that took over the air and said they were blessed, all of us blessed, and I went inside because I could not think of a way to be polite to that.

It was only a step into the lobby. The light had changed. It was the hour when the glass shows you yourself and also the lights behind you in layers. The aquarium in this new place did not bubble. A man in a suit that had too many buttons was asking the front desk for a place to go after the last dance. The girl at the desk said there was a bar on J Street. She said there was a late-night food truck lot on R. She did not say what you could tell from her that she would be going home to a little house on a street where the porch light was not turned on unless she reached up and did it. She did not say that her feet hurt in a way that would alter the bones.

I went to the little museum of itself the hotel had made of a corridor. It had photographs and captions that spoke about “our past” and “a proud legacy on the river.” There was the shot of the old neon sign with the P and the missing O. There was the mayor of 1972 shaking hands with someone whose name was not given. There was a paragraph about the year the hotel had “fallen victim to an incident” and a nod to “the brave actions taken by first responders and staff alike.” It was the word victim that held me there. I touched the glass so the light would show me what was behind it but of course what was behind was only the picture stuck to the backboard.

Letty came by with a little cart with napkins. She had put her hair up differently for the evening. She saw me standing there and looked away and then looked back because it would be strange to ignore someone you had shown the back stair to earlier.

“Do you want to see something,” I said to her in a voice that I did not expect to use.

She did not say yes but she set her cart to the side against the wall and followed me to the service door and into the hall and then into the stair. The band was playing something that was meant to be from Motown but which had none of the blood in it. In the stair it was only our breathing again. I sat on the step and she did not, and then she did, the way we all eventually accommodate ourselves to a thing that has been said to be happening.

“When it was happening,” I said, and then stopped because there are rules and one of them is that you do not hand the children a story you have not spit-polished into a lesson.

“Estela said she grabbed the phone,” she said. “The one on the housekeeping desk. She said she tried to call her sister because her sister had told her if you just call someone they have to answer because that is human, to answer. She said the phone had gone dead like it had never been there.”

“I don’t remember the heat first,” I said. “You would think you would. I remember the change in the air.”

She nodded as if that made perfect sense to her. It is possible it did. People are born into rooms where the air changes. People are made to stand there while the men in their shirts too tight for summer talk about class. People are told to hold the satin and not let it fall.

We sat. The door opened and someone in a tuxedo came in with his phone to his ear and went up one and then two and did not see us. Someone else opened it and said in a whisper “Are you crying in here,” and the voice that answered said “Not yet,” and the door shut.

If there is a thing you want me to say about the fire I cannot. Not because I would not but because there is a silence that has grown around it, a useful one. I will say that Inez pushed me by the shoulders toward the stair. I will say that I did not know until later that she had gone back to knock on a door that had a sound behind it. People want you to say the door did not open. People want you to say that you learned something from the way the handle felt. I do not know what I learned except the thing I learn over and over which is that you can look at a person and see them not see you. The thing I learned is that there are alarms that sound as if you have heard them all your life and yet when you are asked in depositions to imitate them you cannot.

There was a hotel across the river we could see then and cannot now because of the trees. Some girls had been standing on their balcony watching the smoke come out and one of them had held her phone up the way you do to get a better signal even when there is no such thing as a better signal for the thing that is not happening to you. It felt for a second as if they were watching us the way you watch a parade, a thing planned and announced in the paper.

“I still make beds like that,” I said to Letty, suddenly wanting to give her something she could use. “You stand on one side and pull to your shoulder and then the other side, and when you tuck, don’t go under too deep or the foot will kick it out.”

She nodded as if to say that this rang true and then looked over my head at the sign with the man running toward the door and the arrow that always points the way you would have chosen without it.

“Do you have children,” she said.

“No,” I said.

She waited for me to say more and then did not wait anymore. Someone upstairs shouted and someone else shushed them and then the band did their first slow song and somebody’s uncle gave a small whoop because he had been waiting for that since six.

When I came back to the terrace Livia was dancing with her father. He had a face like a man who had just finished mowing and was not supposed to tell anyone he had liked it. She had her eyes closed. Evan was talking to the photographer and holding a koozie around a beer and nodding as if he had been told about taxes. Mother-of-the-groom was talking softly into the ear of a woman with a velvet jacket. The candle for the grandparents had been re-lit. A breeze moved the lanterns and their cords, and Configurations fluttered their white ends against the empty chairs.

“Come,” Rena said. “Dance with us.”

“I don’t,” I said.

“Nobody does,” she said. “Everybody is doing.”

I thought of the service stair. I thought of the lawn at the side where the smokers went now because they could not be on the property and still their shoes made the noise on the gravel and still the way they stood said what it always had, I am waiting for the thing that takes me to a different place, but I will not be the one to go find it. I thought of the glass on the wall in the lobby that told me the picture behind it mattered as if it mattered to me.

I took Rena’s hand and danced this way and that to the song about a river that is not this river. I watched the edges because I always have. I watched the manager notice a shadow. I watched the girl who would take the bouquet stand alone and examine her phone so carefully you could tell she was holding the air in her other hand. I watched the sky change color and then change back, the way it can sometimes in this town in August when the heat comes again at nine in the evening the way a dog gets up and moves from one place on the lawn to another and does not leave you.

Livia came to me when all the things that were meant to happen had happened and people were trying to fold the night into quarters so they could put it in their handbags and drive home with it. Her hair had moved and then not moved back. Her eyes were wet. She held my hands. “Thank you,” she said. “Are you all right here. I know this is—”

“I am all right,” I said.

“You are right,” she said, and then laughed at herself because that was not what she had meant to say.

“Do you remember when you were a little girl,” I said. “You came by with your mother and I put you in the laundry cart and pushed you to the end of the hall and you laughed so much a man came out and said was there a roller coaster in this place.”

“I don’t remember,” she said, then saw that she wished that she did.

“I do,” I said.

“You look beautiful,” she said again, and went back to get her picture taken with someone else.

Rena put her chin on her wrist and watched Livia. She had a face like a woman who has survived a county fair with all her children. She wiped her nose with the corner of a bar napkin. The napkins had L+E in little gold dots. The dots were flaking off on her chin.

“We’re going to go home tomorrow morning and sleep till one,” she said. “We’re going to pretend it didn’t take a year to get to this day and cost what it cost.”

“You are,” I said.

“You’re coming with me,” she said. “Don’t wander.”

“I won’t,” I said, and thought about the hall with the picture of the men in hats and the girl Letty and the smell of damp sheets that has been with me a number of years that is more than I can say out loud. I thought about the day I left and the cash in the envelope and the signatures on a paper that I did not understand. I thought about the drive across the causeway with the sun in my eyes and the crows on the wires like beads.

At a certain point the lights in the lobby went not off but to a lower tone. The night clerk came on and took his place behind the desk with his shoulders that were meant to say we are here when you need us. The river ran as it always runs, holding every light that fell on it as if that light were not even there. In my room I took off my dress and put my hand flat on the back of the door to feel the air from the hall, as if it would tell me the story I had asked it to tell. The card that said WHERE TO FIND YOUR NEAREST EXIT was clear about it. I lay down and watched the red of the alarm that was not and thought about the people who had said it was a miracle and the people who had said it was negligent and the people who had said nothing at all because they were not in the mood. I thought about the girl whose mouth moved with the Channel 13 news and her four hundred-dollar teeth and Inez and the houseman with the spoon-sharpened scratch on the side table and a smell like green stems in water too long in a sunless room.

In the morning I woke at five out of a habit I had not meant to keep. The river wore a gray then. The terrace chairs had been stacked into rows. A single thing of ribbon came loose and moved in the breeze. I dressed and went to the stair again to see if it would be different and it was not. I sat. Somebody in a chef jacket walked down with a bucket and a rag and touched his pocket to check he had what he had. He did not see me or he did and did not show it. The hotel made its low continuous sound. The signs pointed one way. The thing I have learned to do in the mornings, what I did then, was to wait until the light was the light I could accept and then go where I was meant to go. I went back up. I knocked on the connecting door to Rena’s room. She said “Come in,” as if she had been waiting all night.

She had a cup of coffee made in the machine with the pod. We drank it. We packed the cookies we had not eaten. We took the chutney although we would not. We left the itinerary on the desk with the shaded name where the name had been. We took the elevator down. The girl at the front desk asked if we had enjoyed our stay and I said yes because there is no other kind of answer to a question like that that helps anyone. A man in golf clothes said he had parked in the wrong zone and would it matter and she said well as of now it had not. We went through the revolving door and the air of the river morning hit us like a thing from a cooler, a lift that does not last.

“Breakfast in West Sac,” Rena said. “That place with the waffles that are really pancakes but you like them anyway.”

“Yes,” I said.

She put on her sunglasses. She did the thing with her tongue where she checks her tooth for lipstick. “You’ll tell it sometime,” she said then, unexpected.

“I tell it,” I said.

“You’ll tell it once and it will be over with,” she said, and opened the car and slid in and held her dress with the same hand.

“It won’t,” I said to the windshield and the bridge and the light on the bridge and knew that this was not the kind of thing you argued with, because it was not an argument. We drove across the parking circle and the man in the blazer waved us out like a plane. Rena made the turn too wide and then corrected. The radio was on low to a talker about traffic at the split, as if that were the event. Here is what I have told you. Here is what it was. Here is what I think of when I think about it: a stair with the light that wants to be day, a girl folding a sheet to the corner, a candle making its own small weather toward or away. We drove out. We went to waffles that are not waffles. We did not say perfume. We did not need to. The hotel was still behind us. The river was in its bed. The chairs were stacked. The lanterns were out. The story was what it was and what it was not. I put my hand on my leg to feel the heat through the fabric and it was there. It was all there. It has never not been.
