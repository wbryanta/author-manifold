By noon the yard is a map of shadows from the maple out front, the old one that used to spit maple seeds like little helicopters I stuck to my nose when I was ten. They fall on knees now, on shoe tops and the backs of bare hands as I count out change from the green plastic cash box that used to hold band-aids in my mother's bathroom. There is a ring of early birds in the driveway though the ad said no early birds; a tight cluster of women in sunglasses and men in baseball caps who angle as if at a buffet, glancing not at the house itself but at its seams—what kind of person lived here, what brand of toaster she had, whether the garage door is original. Truth, she lived here fifty-seven years. Truth, my mother kept the toaster from Woolworth's that burned one side and left the other side pale because she said if you flip it and run it twice you have a perfect piece. I know this and also that nobody looking for a bargain has time for the long version.

I tape the sign that says estate sale today to the mailbox with blue painter's tape that will come off without leaving sticky residue. I write all sales final underneath in my mother's small neat block letters because when I traced over the word savings on the ledger in the top drawer of her desk last night, I felt her caution in my fingertips and wanted to borrow it. The ad is on Facebook and Craigslist thanks to my junior associate at work, who at the last moment taught me how to make a post into an event and how to fend off "you up?" messages from men who thought that was somehow connected. There was one phone call at six this morning from a woman named Patsy who said she'd been to the house ten years ago to buy an afghan and was the red Pyrex still here could I hold it. I told her no holds. I told her noon.

By noon the neighbor boys—no longer boys but men with tight haircuts and beer guts, the Hendersons who used to play kickball in the cul-de-sac—have pulled their pickup into the driveway to save the spot for their mother. They hug me hard, and the younger one, Kyle, says, "Miss R, I am so sorry," as if I were still a second-grade room mom. He smells like laundry detergent and sweet tobacco. He lifts the garage door for me and the grease in the track makes the same frayed hiss it always has. My mother never got around to oiling it, because she said things deserved to keep their familiar sounds. "You know," she told me once when I was trying to talk her into a new washing machine, "a new noise is always work."

It's ninety and the air tastes like wet pennies. A pair of college-age girls in sundresses stand under the maple conferring over a milk glass vase with bumps like fish eggs that my mother loved because she said it made the stems look like they were wearing pearls. A man in a camouflage hat and wraparound sunglasses lifts the lid of her cedar chest and inhales. "Cedar," he says to no one, pleased. A younger couple circles the dining room table with the long shallow scratches I can tell you exactly how they happened: my brother tried to slide a science fair volcano across it the year he made it from plaster and red food coloring. The man with the hat taps the cedar. "You taking offers on this?"

"I have a price," I say, and he laughs like we're in a game. "Everything's a negotiation," he says. He looks at my face and recalibrates. "Okay, ma'am. What's your price?"

"A hundred," I say, though in my head I had listed it at two. All morning the numbers have been sliding in and out of focus, attaching to things, then loosening. I am surprised at how quickly it feels like I am betraying my younger self who hid comic books in the bottom of that chest and my older self who stacked my daughter's outgrown clothes in it for when my sister-in-law's baby needed them. But the hundred comes out. The man counts bills into my palm and whistles for his son, who appears at once, lanky and silent, and they carry the chest out together, breath catching, awkward around the front door's narrow angle, and for an instant the chest is paused there with the bevel catching the light and everything in the house is visible in its sheen—the hall mirror with the crack through the corner that my mother called "our lightning," the dust on the piano keys, the wedge of kitchen with the window over the sink where the bird feeder hangs. Then it's gone, down the steps, into the truck bed lined with a blue tarp.

I stand in the doorway because part of my job is to guard, to watch the edges and be traffic control, and because I cannot bear or don't know how to step into the rooms yet. The air inside is cooler, thick with polish and lemon oil that I applied last night because I thought shining a thing would make it easier to sell, as if it would read to bargain-hunters as love. On the stoop I can see the hydrangea my mother cut back in March against my advice and that has come in fierce and blue anyway because my mother had always been right about plants and wrong about other humans in a million ways I could list. The grass is patchy where my father used to set the sprinkler, moving it by time instead of distance so the corner by the mailbox was always wettest. When I was a child the asphalt in August went so soft we could push a stick into it and make our initials, which I did, with my brother, with a bark chip, one hot day when my mother slept on the couch with a washcloth on her forehead because she thought lying down was lazy unless she called it headache. The initials are still there, R and T, at the bottom of the drive, fossil in tar. I am the one of those letters still here.

Someone nudges my arm and when I look down there's a girl with dark hair to her waist, maybe twelve, holding up a set of Nancy Drew books like she is offering me bricks. "How much for the lot?" she says, serious. They are the yellow-spined ones that seemed grown-up when I was eight, the ones I took in the car to Lake Erie in July, stomach rolling on Route 6 as Nancy got locked in attics and pantries and seldom got food. The girl eyes me as though I might be insulted by her lowballing. "Five?" she says. I want to say I cannot sell them, I want to say I must, I want to say take them and be very kind to them, give them water and watch where they go. "Eight," I say, because my mother would have liked eight and because I need practice asking for anything.

She counts carefully. Her fingernails are bitten to quick and one has a Band-Aid around it with cartoon dogs. "My grandma had these," she says. "She made me read them. Like homework." She says this with fondness. "She live around here?" I say, and the girl shrugs toward the other side of town, toward the ranch houses and the Dollar General and the field that pretends it's not going to be condos next spring. "She died," she says, matter-of-fact. "And my mom says maybe we can get a cat, now that we don't have to go anywhere every day after school and can't leave it alone."

"That's good," I say, and she looks at me like I have misunderstood. "The cat part," I add, and she smiles a little, relieved to be understood in some small, correct way. She tucks the books under her arm and goes to find a mother to tell.

In the dining room a woman in a T-shirt that says Sunshine State in looped script picks up my mother's rooster cookie jar and sets it down again and says to her friend, "My aunt had this exact one," and her voice warms like blacktop in sun. I can say with certainty it's not exact—my mother prided herself on telling the difference between Grandma Supreme and knockoffs—but I am not going to intervene in how people fold their own past into mine. The rooster goes, to a man with knuckles like walnuts who tells me it's for his wife's collection and then confesses, as if he never tells anyone anything true, that he's buying it because she cried when they sold her mother's house two months ago and he didn't know what to do and thinks a surprise might ease a thing. It might. It might not. I make change. He disappears out the door, shouldering aside a pair of teenagers in Empire Records T-shirts who are holding a lava lamp and a framed photograph of the Hoover Dam that is not ours. "Does the lamp work?" one says, and I say, "It has always worked. It is a miraculous lamp," and I believe that even if it has lost its glow that is not the fault of the lamp. They overpay in crumpled bills and don't want change; they say keep it, Miss, like I am handing out pizza at a high school car wash.

I am surprised for a moment by how quickly the house becomes marketplace, how the air hums with talk like the cicadas that line up in the maple after dark. In this town we practiced church basements since birth: sheet cakes on folding tables with pastel sugar roses, aunties and their elbows, men in navy union jackets and their congregations of grandchildren. People know how to move as a swarm and apart. They cluster around the box of Mason jars like sparrows around a feed dish. They stand in line for the electric weed whacker, they stroke the old chenille bedspread like a dog. They say sentences that compliment the dead. "She kept her things so nice," an older man in suspenders tells me, nodding toward the bureau my father bought her when he got the manager's job at the lumberyard and thought he could make himself bigger with cherry wood. "Kept a neat house," the man says, and I want to tell him how she kept more than neatness—kept ledgers of every check written in pencils sharpened as weapons, kept the grocery list tacked to the inside of the cabinet door because she didn't want people to see Spam on it, kept my brother safe until that winter she didn't.

The piano is what makes me sweat. It sits in the front room with its lid shut like it is holding a secret under its tongue. The polish has dulled to a surface like touched stone. People slow down by it, as if they can hear our arguments about scales still vibrating a little. "Make a joyful noise," my mother would say, voice like syrup and steel, and I wanted to make a noise but not that one. The piano is not a great one, the keys a little yellowed, the A key in the lower middle with a chip my mother covered with white nail polish that shone like a false tooth. I have not decided whether to keep it. My daughter texted me yesterday from Oregon asking if we could somehow get it to her. "Is teleportation a thing yet?" she wrote, clowning. It would cost too much and she lives in a third floor walk-up and even if the miracle occurred to make it possible the piano would still be the same reluctant instrument that presided over my childhood with such authority it might as well have been a magistrate. And yet. And yet.

"Any wiggle room on the piano?" asks a man with a salt-and-pepper beard who looks like he belongs in a commercial for trucks that climb mountains. I have put no price on the piano because bravery sometimes comes to me in the form of not committing. "Make me an offer," I say, and he tilts his head like he is measuring its secrets by sound. "Two hundred," he says. I laugh before I can stop it. "I'm sorry," he says quickly, "I didn't mean—I'm not here to lowball you, ma'am." He calls me ma'am in a way that is not insulting, mostly because he looks like exactly the kind of man who laments the loss of manners in society. "I own the school of music in town," he says, and my throat tightens because he is handing me a soft landing. "We got a couple kids on scholarship who could use a practice instrument. I can get it hauled."

"Three hundred and you take the metronome and the sheet music, too," I say. "Chopin that is mostly tears, and a schottische." My mother brought home that word from a Grange hall dance and I have loved it since because it sounds like something you could eat and instead it's something you could do with your feet.

He grins. "Done," he says. He counts cash that is crisp like it came from a bank, like goodness, and his assistants, who look like strong patient baby giraffes, edge the piano away from the wall gently, then more, then more. They tilt it onto a dolly like it's a client they've been trained to move—lower back safe, head supported, the body a puzzle of weight distributed. The house moans a little when they take it, the floor complaining, or I imagine it, the way you imagine a child is crying from two rooms away. When the music stand slips unexpectedly I flinch so hard I knock over a glass paperweight of a tulip that I am not selling. It rolls into the corner and hides. One of the giraffes calls, "Watch the doorway," and I do. I watch as they make their slow shuttered ballet out through the rectangle that was the limits of the world for so long. Outside there are three steps and for a second I think we are all going to be part of a story people tell about a woman who couldn't let go of her mother's piano without it killing a passerby. But the men are skilled at this, veterans of narrow angles and front stoops, and they glide down and into the truck. When the piano is strapped and the truck pulls away I stand under the lintel and hold the metronome in my fist like a small, held heart.

People stream in and stream out. They hold two things they know and one thing they don't. They crouch in corners and look under things as if more value will be found in a shadow. A woman asks me if the old glass doorknobs are original and I say yes, rubbing my fingertips over one because it feels like a knee. A man points to the bed in my old room and asks if the mattress is included and I say I will not sell you something I woke up on as a teenager with my first heartbreak still tucked in the seam. He laughs and says okay and buys instead a box of mismatched forks and spoons, which he calls "well-loved." A woman with a soft face buys the iron in its felt sleeve and tells me her mother taught her to press a man's shirts when she was eight, and then she catches herself and laughs and says, "At least I can iron a straight crease," and no one argues that a skill does not retire you from your own worth.

The ladies from my mother's church come in a clump. They bring me coffee in a takeout cup with the curve of lip life-saving and a folded napkin tucked under the lid. They all have white hair in different arrangements and I know each of them by their particular bun. "I'm bringing a cake to the house for after the service next week," says Jean, who taught me in Sunday School that Jesus was also a carpenter which is why he cared about my father's heart, which she meant literally and I do not. "Yellow," she says, "with chocolate frosting like your mother liked," and then reflexively, as if I'm twelve: "Don't you pick at the edges before we cut it."

"I will ruin it entire," I promise, and they laugh. In the doorway behind them is Luanne, my mother's best friend from the plant, wearing her tan purse and grief like they both go with everything. She is the one who sat with my mother through the chemo the first time and through the decision not to have chemo the second. She hugs me once hard then holds me out by the elbows to look me over, like I'm the corn she grew from seed. "You look like trouble," she says, as though it is a compliment. "I look fifty-three," I say. She peels bills from a tiny blue coin purse for a set of Pyrex mixing bowls that I should keep and don't. "I will use the hell out of these," she says. "I'll send you pictures of ambrosia in them, and of chocolate pudding skin."

"Keep them," I say, thrusting the money back at her, and she slaps my hand without malice. "You better stop that," she says. "You'll make me feel poor." Her voice softens. "I don't let you buy me dinner and I don't let you give me bowls. Take my five dollars and cry about it later."

The day moves and stretches. The shade shifts until the maple is falling across the neighbor's driveway, touching their basketball hoop with one finger. The high school down the street lets out a marching band thump that comes up like a heartbeat from the two o'clock practice, brass burring something that is almost but not quite the school song I sang, some new arrangement for a rivalry I no longer follow. A delivery truck rumbles, loading, unloading, the ordinary economy of objects everywhere in motion making even this seem like just another exchange of goods. The town's one deaf cat slinks along the hedge like a small white cloud. I can tell from the way she moves she plans to sleep on something and make me feel obligated to keep it but I am ruthless where cats are concerned although my daughter would say I am a liar.

A tall boy with acne and kind eyes asks me if the metal sign that says No Smoking is for sale and I tell him it is from my father's garage and it is a lie because he lit Winstons in that small concrete room for twenty-seven years and when he quit his hands didn't know what to do except make fists. That small concrete room is in the back, and I am pretending this afternoon that it isn't, the jar of screws and Sanka cans of nails and the bent saw blades. I could sell the vise he clamped bicycle frames in for me, could sell the coffee can full of sponges, but I am steering strangers away from that rectangle by the tactic of moving my body across it whenever anyone drifts close. On the top shelf in there was my brother's baseball, the foul that cracked a windshield we couldn't afford to fix until winter. There was a milk crate of clay pigeons he never learned to shoot. There was the amber bottle of turpentine that my mother labeled with three layers of Scotch tape the year the neighbor boy drank half a mouthful and we all learned how quick you could find the right number on the yellow rotary phone if you needed to and how fast twelve-year-old legs could carry a mistake to the Hendersons' car.

The Henderson mother brings me a little plastic baggie of grapes and a roll that is too soft. "Eat," she commands as if she is a nurse. "You are pale like paper. You need sugar." She leans into me and her perfume smells like carnations the way funeral homes smell like something else pretending to be something else. She picks up the quilt my grandmother quilted the summer my father worked nights at the plant and my mother needed her hands busy at midnight with the only woman who never asked her to explain herself. "You selling this?" she says, astonished, the way people are when you make a practical choice that offends their version of sentiment.

"I can't," I say, and she exhales like I'd done the right thing. Then a woman behind her says gently, "If you decide you can, I will buy it," and I think for not the first time that people who show up at noon with open hands deserve more than the people who call at six asking for special dispensation. "My husband is allergic to quilts," the woman says, and she laughs so her joke doesn't land on me like a curse. "If he wasn't, I'd have a hundred."

My mother used to point at quilts in strangers' houses the way other people point at flag etiquette. "Never use a vintage quilt," she said, as if the word vintage had been birthed only to keep this one instruction company. "They are meant to lie politely on a guest bed and accept compliments during daylight hours." Of course, she used ours, flung it onto the grass for picnics and wrapped it around me when my fever spiked and let the dog sleep on it when his hip went out. "Objects should live as long as they are alive," she said, and later this contradicted her conviction that things needed to be kept in their original boxes with their paper and the receipt folded on top. She was a river and I wanted her to be a fence. She tied gift bows like architecture and said please like a prayer. When my father died on a Thursday and the funeral was on a Monday she ironed every dress she owned and then ironed every dress she did not.

I do eat the grapes, and the roll, and the handful of peanut M&M's someone presses into my palm like communion, and it keeps my head level for another forty-five minutes until the low cloud I have been watching from the corner of my eye becomes thunder. The light tilts, and we all feel it at the same time, and you can tell the resellers from the neighbors by what they do. The resellers move faster, calculating which heavy item they can wrap in a tarp and get to the truck before the downpour; the neighbors say, "Well, we'll just step inside then," as if the house has always been their house too and they know where the towels are. The sky mutters, and the old gutter on the north side drips once and then more. Luanne moves a stack of sheet music out of the way of a potential drip with so much practiced bossiness that I get to pretend I thought of it myself. "Check the back bedroom window," she tells me, and one of the Henderson boys nods that he will.

Rain smells like pennies only larger. It musters itself with patience then decides on a train. It comes in sheets. People cluster in the front room as if we are at a wake and the deceased is our sense of being dry. We stand among strangers with other people's lamps making double the usual light. An old man in a veteran's cap sits on the upholstered bench I had intended to keep and pats his knee with two fingers because rhythm is the last thing that leaves some people. A mother with a toddler on her hip apologizes for the wet footprints and I say, "Who do you think taught me how to mop?" and she smiles like I saved her life. The man with the truck made out of truck—he must go by "Troy" or "Jim"—hands me an umbrella and helps me hold it over the yard sale tables that have become rain tables in record time and we both laugh even as water slides up my sleeves. It occurs to me that this will be a story: the rain came and everyone just brought in more folding chairs, the rain came and we all learned what floorboards do when you get them wet and the answer is not scream. The rain drum-drum-drums for ten minutes, fifteen, and then the clouds are spent and the world is raw and cleaned, and an hour later you can hardly tell except by the smell, the sucked-up scent of wood and asphalt newly named.

In the cleared, rinsed air, sales pick up again. A sheepish man in a suit I can tell is his good one by the way he treats it asks for a price on the suit rack itself because his son likes to play store, and I want to say that his son is going to grow up and live somewhere else and blame him for it, because that's what sons do, but he is kind and his wallet is thin so I say five. A black-haired woman buys my mother's winter coats, the long camel hair and the fake leopard my mother wore to the church bazaar because she thought it was festive. "I'll think of her when it's cold," she says, and I think I always have. A man who moves like concrete is poured into him buys the metal snow shovel. "We don't get real winters anymore," he says, and we both glance at the maple as if it might argue.

On the kitchen counter is my mother's recipe box, turquoise with a hinged lid and index cards not in alphabetical order because she believed in systems based on memory more than those based on logic. I have not listed it for sale because I cannot. People open it as they pass as if it's a book in a library you lift because something about it makes your hand want to see if it will fit. On the top card in my mother's precise print is pickle roll-ups; beneath that is fudge; then Aunt Lucy's Funeral Potatoes; then two cards for upside-down cake because once my mother forgot to invert it and we ate it out of the pan and called it right-side-up cake and felt rebellious. On the last white card in the back, in a child's uneven capitals, is EGG. The ingredients: egg. The recipe: crack and cook. In parentheses, after cook, my mother wrote, until done. That was me. I turn the box in my hands and for a second I feel like I am holding not this turquoise object but the balance of a whole world I could carry somewhere else. I should keep only this, I think. I should keep the recipe box and the brass cicada from the mantel, which my father bought at a yard sale himself because he liked that it was heavy and nobody could tell why, and the wool throw from the couch that my mother rejected as "too scratchy" and then used every night the winter she dyed her hair brown to feel young and then owned the gray the following spring. But I have already told a woman she can have the throw, and the cicada is packed, and I put the recipe box in the old tote I have by the register for things that are not for sale like the death certificates and the paperwork from the bank and the picture of my mother as a teenager wearing a dark lipstick she would later mock with deep devotion.

A heavyset man in a faded Ohio State hoodie hefts the iron skillet that has held exactly one thousand five hundred and twenty-nine breakfast-fried eggs because I counted once in junior high when my mother was at work and I was home with strep and numbers soothed me. "You selling this?" he asks, and I have this whole prepared speech about seasoning and commitment and the way you must never wash cast iron with soap, never, lest you offend the ancestors of grease. But he looks at it like you look at an instrument you have not played for years but could, if asked, and I say, "Yes," and he pays me honestly with exact change, which is the currency in this town, and he says, "We make cornbread in ours and I swear it tastes like something you grew," and I want to bless him with oil. He walks out of my childhood with the thing that taught me that heat and time and fat and work can make a crust that holds.

A white-haired woman buys the hand mirror with the monogram from my grandmother and calls it elegant. A boy not yet old enough for driver's ed buys the wooden crate that says BALL JAR with its slats and announces he will put records in it and I say red wine and he looks at me like I accidentally said his full name in public. A teacher I recognize from a conference last year buys the posters that say Parts of Speech because she cannot stand to see them die in cardboard. Two men in matching pressed shorts haggle enthusiastically with me over the price of the dining table as if we are old adversaries who like each other and I say the price and they say less and I say a little less and we are all satisfied in the way that a game completed is satisfying regardless of who won. They stroke the depleted scratches with their thumbs. "She loved on this," one says, and the care in his voice makes me agree. She did. She ironed on this and cut out cookies with the metal cutter that left the imprint of Christmas and wrote out the checks that once made me ashamed because we didn't have enough and I thought that meant something about me. She put down the plates with the tiny rosebud pattern from the A&P and then one day I came home from college and the plates had no flowers and I could not name the loss and was angry for three days for reasons that had nothing to do with dishes and everything to do with what she could not keep, which was me.

A small dog tied to a signpost under the maple trembles with the lightness of a thing that has learned to quiver for praise. A boy in a baseball uniform who should be at practice touches the dog with his knuckles and says, "You are brave," and I feel a sharp affection for everything with a body. It's past three, and the sunlight has gone to honey. Pieces of the house are leaving like people. The mirror with the gilt frame one of my aunts gave my mother goes under a woman's arm and reflects the corner of my mouth in its passing. The lamp with the crooked shade that my mother never fixed on principle because "things remember what they are supposed to be" goes out the door like a friend leaving a party early. The hum of talk calibrates, shifts; people are less to be convinced now and more to be reminded. "That is the price," I say more often with less apology. Someone remarks that I am "making a killing," and I look at the pile of tens, the twenties that arrive soft and used, and think that you only truly make a killing when something is dead.

A man with a gray beard asks me about the stump grinder I have no idea how to price. We walk around to the back and he touches it the way you would touch a sleeping dog. "Pulls to the left," I tell him as if I have used it, and he grins. The garden is medium dry, the tomatoes still heavy as if not getting the news is a form of protest. I watered them this morning like a person leaving notes for the next tenant. The row of zinnias my mother planted in April in their military precision is a riot now, like anything sorted by a body overtaken by wildlife. She always said the secret to a garden was to cut things down before you loved them too much. "Everything is more beautiful if you believe it will be reduced to stem by September," she once said, and then punished me by pinching her basil too hard, making it bruise. It was a kind of faith. She loved things at their quarter-dark, their September, where effort shows. I have never loved that moment and am trying to.

Back inside, I sit for the first time all day on the bottom stair. Through the banister I can see the shadow like a question mark where the piano stood. I am dizzy from walking back and forth over thresholds and I remember suddenly the summer I got my period and could not move for hours for fear that someone would read me, that my body had become like these price tags and anyone could come with money and take it. My mother was not unkind about it; she was brisk and unsentimental and brought me a hot water bottle in a cover with ducks. "You'll be fine," she said, and if she also said "Women do what they have to," that was implicit and I was not yet ready for it. On the same stair ten years later I called my daughter from the front hall because my cell phone did not get reception in the kitchen and told her my marriage was over and we both cried like babies into our separate little plastic rectangles. That gatefold of sorrow, opening and opening, feels like it is printed on the bannister this afternoon. I can feel the identifying notches.

A pair of siblings come in, grown up and home for who knows what weekend, and they orbit one another in a way I recognize in my bones. They are completely different and could never bear to admit how similar they are. They pick at a photograph, fight affectionately about whether that is Lake Hope or Lake Loramie. "You can have it," he says, and she says, "Only if you're sure," and he says, "Take it," and you can tell they measure their presence in the world by the weight of what they give away. "Do you have siblings?" the sister asks me at the register, and I almost tell her my brother is dead. It sits like a smooth stone in my mouth and I could make of it a skipping, drop it into her afternoon to ripple her day. I say, "I did," and she is attentive in the way of people who know the chasm under that sentence. "I'm sorry," she says, and I say thank you without falling into the hole. It is a relief. I think if I begin to tell every stranger where he is, I will have to set up a booth with a sign that says confession.

At four, the last necessary thing goes: the dining chairs, because people can always make room for more chairs. A woman my age with gray threaded into black hair buys six and does not blink when I say the price that surprises even me. She says, "My daughter is getting married in the backyard in October," and I congratulate her and love for a moment that sentence where an American backyard can be a chapel. She tells me how they will string lights between the maple and the garage, and though in my head I say but you have to be careful with that north gutter, it will rip if you put too much on, out loud I say, "Use command hooks," and pointed to my own eaves I don't own anymore. She says, "My mother died," and in this town that's a way of saying hello, and we do the small quick credits you do in a store built for such exchanges: what hospice, what casserole, what month. There is an economy to it that is not cold. We are declaring ourselves able to put our hands on the hot pain and not scream.

At four-thirty, a man with a face so open he could be an usher at a good theater asks about the grandfather clock by the stairs. "It doesn't work," I say, and he looks at its face with a patience I find myself wanting to be near. "It can be made to," he says, and I wonder what it would be like to marry him because of that response. "How much," he says, and something audible in the house tightens, the way your jaw does when you prepare to imprint your teeth into a memory. I name a number. He flinches, then I do, then we both laugh. "Split the difference?" he proposes, and I nod because I don't need a clock to tell me what time it is when he is in my front hall and the shadow on the floor has stretched from the kitchen doorway to my foot. He hands me cash that smells like someone's wallet from before hers was emptied for co-pays. He and his friend tip the clock into their careful, practiced hands, and I go watch the doorframe again because now I do not know how else to say goodbye. They angle, they lean, there is the rapturous sliding scrape of wood against air, and then the clock is in their truck, straps down, looks straight ahead. I am empty of the thing in a way that is not absence but a place the size of it, the shape of it, where I can stand later and remember where it was.

Between customers, I wipe the kitchen counter with my mother's dishrag because to do nothing with my hands feels like drowning. The dishrag is a square of old towel she sewed a bias tape border onto in 1972 because she loathed waste in an operatic way. I run the rag over the laminates my father installed himself and that, if you put your lips against, smell like glue and lemon and the day my brother shaved his head after his diagnosis and we didn't say anything at dinner and then my mother said, "Well if you are going to be bald, now is the season," and we all laughed because anything less than laughing would have broken the windows. I move my hand over the burn from 1984 when my cousin Irene set a hot pot down, and over the place where I carved my initials under the lip with a needle and my mother pretended not to know and then one day when I was twenty-nine and my daughter cut her own hair into a mullet at preschool my mother said, "You know, honey, you are reaping what you sowed with that needle," and we were both so delighted to be correct about small things.

Five is nap time for the old and the small, and even the resellers soften. One of them carries out the old green armchair grudgingly with forced affection, like it's a toddler who must be buckled in, and as he navigates the step he calls "heads up" and then "Thank you, ma'am" to me just because I am there. People who have hung around since noon are doing that thing where they circle one last time as if a treasure might appear that had been invisible at one, at two, at three. They buy small things for themselves they earlier felt guilty wanting: the odd candlestick, the gravy boat with the hairline crack. I look at the gravy boat and have an argument with joy. My mother loved gravy so much she made it a season. Thanksgiving had the whole family and an argument and sugar pie; Easter had ham and my father in a tie; and Gravy had any Tuesday in March when she had a bad day and needed the body her grandmother's recipe promised, floured fat, salted heart. She would pour it into this boat as if it were a precious chemical, made it pool in our plates like a man-made lake. I put my hand on it now and then take my hand off because the young woman waiting is in earnest, and all this is not mine after all except in the way gravity is.

By six, the floor is bare where a rug was for forty-five years. It makes the boards look wider than I knew. The air smells like old house: a mixture of damp mint, scorched bread, sadness only in the way cedar smells like pencil. People have written checks on the dining table I no longer have to deposit. People have hugged me in my own doorway and said things meant to keep me upright. A van driven by two teenage boys who look like cousins backs into the driveway and out unsubtle as a swallow, and they carry the couch with its indentations of heads and thighs like a prom date between them. "Careful," one says to the other though the other needs no such instruction; they both want to be good. The couch was what we lay on, stomach down, to watch Alex Trebek nightly absorb our ignorance and ask for more. When I vacuumed under it as a girl I found the pennies and hair ties of our lives and once a dried cherry from a Manhattan my mother drank one New Year's Eve before she began refusing all holidays on principle. When it's gone, the line of sunlight on the wall is wrong, a line from a different day.

Late in the afternoon, a young man who looks too clean for this town asks for the bookshelves my father made. They are just planks on blocks but he says he likes the look and I realize I have to explain that these are not decor; they are what happens when someone makes you a thing with their hands because they think buying it would be wasteful. I am in a mood to grant meanings where perhaps there are none, and I decide in my head to give them away and then ask him for twenty dollars like a normal person. He is gracious. He touches the wood with uncallused fingers and says, "These will do me through grad school," and I wish on the spot for him ten thousand careful late nights with a spine open and a pencil behind his ear and no disaster.

I choose what I have been choosing all day: what to release and what to keep. It does not get easier. I hold a spice jar full of buttons like an infant so I can run them in my palm and hear their small polite clatter. I put them on the Not For Sale tote that is my sky even as my ex-husband's voice in my head says, funny for a woman who hates to sew. I keep the tie with the rabbits we gave my father for Easter where he neither laughed nor scolded but wore it and poured gravy onto it by accident and dared anyone to name the stain. I keep three photographs that did not have their doubles in albums because my mother didn't trust me with the good ones: one of my brother on his Schwinn, one of me in a makeshift witch hat with a crooked broom, one of my parents in matching blue windbreakers smiling their first midwestern spring after they moved from the city because it was easier to afford and love didn't mind. I put into the maybe pile the painting of the big lake we never got to because you can say Lake Erie as a category of hope, but there are ten thousand pictures of a place you have never stood and they all feel like a lie.

The sky deepens, becomes not purple or pink but a midwestern deep blue that is defiant more than pretty. The maple shakes itself once and then goes still. The teenage boys come back for a dresser they promised their mother, and underneath the dresser when they lift it is the scatter of what was never claimed: a grocery club card from a store that no longer exists in this town, a dime, a cough drop in melted cellophane, a graphite stub of pencil. I become unbearably attached to the pencil in the way you do when a thing is about to be thrown away. It feels illicit to pocket a stub from my own floor. I do it anyway. When we get the dresser to their truck the tallest boy pats its side and says, "She'll go in the far room," with such ownership and relief that I nearly weep for somebody else's far room.

As the hour slides past seven, the street grows quiet. The sign on the mailbox looks tired. A horn beeps as someone who bought a rug comes back for it because it wouldn't fit and now it does. The house cracks its knuckles the way it does at dusk when the heat of the day is leaving its bones. The sounds that were swallowed earlier—ice shifting in cups, a throat clearing—return. I turn off one lamp, then another, and the corners pull in close. I have stickers left in yellow and green with small numbers written on them that now mean nothing, and I am seized by the urge to put them on myself, price my shoulder, price my knee. The room where my mother's bed once stood is square and bare. The carpet has a rapture of pink squares where the bed's legs sat, and I touch one with my toe as though it is alarmed. On the closet shelf sits the hat she wore to my daughter's graduation, a ridiculous round hat with netting, and I put it on my head and look ridiculous and then lovely and then ridiculous again and then take it off and put it gently back to be ridiculous and lovely for someone else.

There are a few diehards who hunker down at the end of a sale like gamblers who cannot believe the casino is closing. A woman in scrubs with a name tag says, "Would you take a quarter for this?" holding up a tarnished spoon with a curlicue handle, and she flinches, ready for my disapproval when she should not be apologizing for anything, so I say, "I will take a quarter," and we both grin like bandits. A man with an eye patch buys the four dining room napkins that match the one we never found because my brother wrapped it around the cat's neck one Halloween and the cat made its escape over the fence with it like a cape. When the man pulls out his wallet I glimpse the crisp picture of two kids, a rainbow magnet, and I add the two extras that were in the laundry basket because I say I cannot handle houses where things are odd-numbered and he looks at me as if I just rescued him from some platonic imperfection. "Thank you," he says, sincerely, and I say you are welcome, and we mean a thousand other things.

At the very end, when the sun is gone and the porch light is the only faithful thing in the face of a town that sleeps early, a woman I half-recognize, thin and sharp as a reed, comes back. She has been here three times today, orbiting the same thing, and now she stands holding the quilt again. "Are you sure," she says, "you are sure you're not selling this," and I almost say no again from habit and mercy but something in me that has been taut all day relaxes suddenly and I hear my own breathing like someone else's. I think of my mother using the quilt to move a sick dog from couch to floor in one wintry night, not caring if the dog vomited on the old orange dots; I think of us lying under it in August in air so ironed you could cut your tongue on it; and I think of the long way I have to go, the house I am going back to where there are only two plants with dependable lives and a neighbor who plays bass at ten p.m. and a bed that I made, in more than one sense; I think of my daughter who wants me to visit in October when the leaves are a rumor and then a reality on her faraway streets and who says, "Bring me something that smells like her," and I know that quilts smell like laundry and not people; and because I am not ready to be Orpheus to anyone's Eurydice today, I say, "Two hundred," naming a price that is not my mother and not a month's rent but something true.

The woman looks at me, startled, and then she nods, once, like assent in church. She reaches into her bag and pays me not with a wad of small bills like most people but with a little stack of twenties, folded, worn at the edges, that suggests they were set aside for a thing hoped for. She holds the quilt to her chest and looks at me with a shy ferocity. "My mom never had anything handmade," she says, and this is not my fault and also it is everyone's. She says, "I'll put this on the bed and tell my little boy that a woman made it with her hands," and I think about how we are all the little boy and also the woman, on different days, sometimes at once. She asks me my mother's name, and I tell her, and she says it out loud, reverently, like a spell, and for a moment my mother is in the room like flour dust you can see in the light if you stand in the right place.

Now it is dusk. I have kept the recipe box and the brass cicada and the pencil stub and the rabbit tie. I have kept a pair of salt shakers shaped like ears of corn just because they make me laugh. I have kept the photograph of my mother at twenty in a swimsuit on the bank of a river I cannot name, her hair slicked dark and her gaze direct. The house feels lighter, as if it floated an inch above its foundation while I was busy, as if it could be tipped and emptied like a box of cornflakes. Luanne kisses me on the cheek and calls me matchstick and tells me to text her when I get home and not to drive into deer because they gather in the median like church ladies. The Hendersons take down the cardboard sign and roll the painter's tape into a little ball which the youngest for some reason pockets as if it is harvest. I am left inside with the radiator's faint hiss and the refrigerator humming its old reliable hum in a room that has no glasses left.

I put my hand on the wall by the front door with the pencil marks of our heights, me and my brother, the lines wobbly and then straight and then smug at thirteen. My mother wrote the dates in letters the color of bread crust. I have no plan for this; I cannot unpeel it to take it with me. I put my palm flat and measure my heart against it. The door still sticks if you don't pull it exactly right, and I want to shout to the next person: pull at the end, not at the edge, and use your hip. I want to leave index cards of instruction in the drawers: this window lifts if you press here, the washing machine sings if you coddle it, the maple will drop all at once and if you don't rake the second it reaches damp you'll think it is forever. I turn and look at the empty living room and it looks like a photograph taken after a storm. I sit down on the floor where the rug used to be and lean my back against the wall and let my head fall forward and finally, in the square of absence where the coffee table was, I cry the way I have been refusing to all day, which is wet and ugly and inconvenient and as necessary as heat. Nobody is here to be embarrassed for me; even the cat has deserted me. I put one hand around my throat as if to keep sound in and the other flat on the floor to feel the house.

It holds. It is a good house. It held us. It is no longer required to know me, but it does. I pull myself up and go to the kitchen and take the last of the trash bags tied under the sink and move around the rooms with the strange intimacy of picking up rubber bands and loose screws from the backs of drawers one last time. I find a dried piece of toast in the back of the breadbox as comical and holy as any relic. I find in the hall closet the box of the Christmas tree stand and the star we used when I was five and then again the year my daughter said no angels. I put the star in the tote. I should not take so much, and I am taking very little, and it is never enough.

Before I go, I open the freezer and there are three Ziploc bags labeled with the same rushed italic script on masking tape: chili, chili, chili. My mother survived so many winters this way. I take one home as if I were superstitious. I shut off the lights in an order my mother taught me that makes no practical sense and feels like a tic of religion: hall, kitchen over the sink, then living room, then front room. In the fading light, the stairs look steeper than they are. At the door, I pick up the metronome I have kept there like a good luck charm and put it in my tote among the cards, the rabbit tie, the corn shakers. I put my hand on the doorknob and do not open it yet because the world on the other side is ordinary. I lean my forehead against the door hard enough to feel the grain. Through the wood I can hear what is always outside in a town like this at eight: the whine of a motorcycle three streets over, the scraping of a plastic chair, the talk on a porch you cannot make out the words of.

Finally, I open the door and stand on the porch. The maple is still. The street has that soft lit look as if someone shaved its whole head. The mailbox leans slightly from an angle my father never got around to correcting. Somewhere, someone laughs at a frequency that makes me think of basketballs thumping. I turn and look back into the house from the threshold the way I used to when I was small and went out without permission, making sure I remember the dark. The bare rooms hold their shapes with an animal patience that I had not noticed when they were full. I think of my mother, who wanted a new sound but also wanted the old one, who lived an entire life under this roof and thought the house would outlast her, and it has, and it will, for a little while longer.

I lock the door and jangle the keys and then, because habit is a kind of prayer, I check the lock again. I step down onto the concrete with its hairline cracks that look the same as they did when I was eight and decided I could walk only on the lines that formed triangles. I let the screen door sigh behind me in that familiar old-man way. I go down the steps into the street and tuck the tote into the back seat of my car amidst the packing materials and the last box of knickknacks that I still have to decide about tomorrow. I sit in the driver's seat with my hands on the wheel and my eyes on the house and I breathe until my breath and the house's breath match, until what is leaving and what remains are not in argument.

Then I start the car, back slowly, and as I roll past the maple and the Hendersons' yard and the dogwood my mother pruned into obedience even when it insisted, a truck drives by with a lamp in the back that I know, its shade crooked but settled, shining under its tarp like a lit room, and it is going with its new people to their place. The porch light behind me makes a warm round on the door, the only light left, and beyond that the rooms hold what they hold, and outside the street breathes and the air is thick with late-summer sweetness, and I turn at the end of the block with my hands at ten and two like a girl, and aim the car toward the part of town where, in an apartment that smells like laundry and cumin and new paint, there will be space for a turquoise recipe box on a shelf, and I am suddenly ferocious in my desire to keep going.
