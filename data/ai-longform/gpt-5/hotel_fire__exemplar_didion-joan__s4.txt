I can’t write in that author’s exact voice, but here’s an original piece with cool, restrained, observational prose—elliptical, image-driven, focused on memory, social surfaces, and the charged pause.

I had not been out on the levee road in years, not since they put in the bypass and planted the new vines where the pears used to be, and even then it had only ever been to drive past the levee club and count the lit windows when I could not sleep. On this day the sun was out but the heat had not set in, the kind of blue air that looks chemical, and I had the window cracked because I preferred the high grass smell that came up from the slough to the air-conditioner’s tepid draft. The river was low in its concrete bed and the egrets were whiter than I remembered them. We get these details mixed up, time after time. We say the egrets are the thing but maybe they were cranes. The hotel had a new name and a new logo that looked like a stylized reed, a rush, some graphic designer’s memory of a river. I tried not to look for what I remembered. I told myself it was a different place.

When you come up to it from the levee you see the water first and the shade off the new structure. There had been a skirt of lawn at the old hotel, a few cypress trees, the long pale gravel of the drive. People used to sit on white iron chairs in the evening and drink gin fizzes as if they were in a different century. The new hotel has poured a plaza across the front and stamped it to look like slate. There are uplights in the squares where the trees were and music in the hedges and the hard hum of the air handlers if you stand in the right place. It is supposed to look like solidity. It reads best in photographs.

The valet said ma’am and I let him take the keys because I could feel it starting at the base of my skull and they have that line in their script that says we are so glad you are here with us and I did not want to hear it said in the wrong tone. I had not known until I saw that it would bother me that the doorman’s jacket was a color between bronze and brown apricot. I stood under the portico and looked through the glass and the embossed words on the doors told me in white letters what I was allowed to do. We inform you. We prohibit. We welcome. There were four separate entrances now where there had been one.

Inside, the air was twenty degrees cooler. I stood a moment unused to the glass, the light off the water through the windows, the smell of limes and something floral pushed by a diffuser and the more particular sweet smell I knew to be steam turned out of towels, but faint, a back note. I recognized no one and no one recognized me and this was as it should be. I gave my name at the desk and the girl at the desk put it into the machine and said just one moment while we get you settled. She was thin and look-at-her pretty in a way that the light flattered even more. I wondered if she had ever made a bed with one thumb raw from pushing pins into a sheet that did not fit. I wondered if she had ever pulled lint out of a filter and watched the lint roll out like felt, looking as if it could be peeled and reused for something else. I wondered if she had ever looked down into a stairwell and thought about jumping.

She had a script. She had the words curated experience in the script and I saw the word curated make its way across her lips and then maybe, as if it had bumped up against meaning somewhere at the edges of what she could remember, she looked directly at me. I saw recognition where there should have been none. Not personal recognition. Institutional. The hotel has a file on the surviving staff. There was a steering committee after the fire. The phrase you are part of our story came out even as she was placing the key folder into my palm and folding my fingers over it like we were closing a deal that would help us both.

“I used to work here,” I did say then, because it was simpler than letting her work for it. “Before.”

Her face did not change. The word before is one of those that will do the work for you if you let it.

“Well,” she said. “Welcome back.”

I had the dress in a garment bag and a new pair of shoes in a box with tissue that I would later put into the trash in the room because I have always had this habit of rewrapping the tissue around the shoes as if someone else is going to open them next. I have these habits. I do not trust the written promises of elevators even when they say Inspected on and give you a day and a name. I do not put the do-not-disturb card on a room door. I do not leave a bag packed when I sleep.

They had given me a room that faced the water and the carpet had a blue-gray pattern like waves or deli paper gone upscale. The ice bucket was black and there was a wrapped glass with the word sanitized in blue. I left it wrapped. I went to the window and closed the sheer and then opened it again because the seam in the voile tracked the view like a crack in glass and made it worse to look through. I put the dress in the closet and ran the tap. It took two minutes for the hot water to make its way. I set my keys and the phone and the room folder with the phone extensions on the desk. I read the emergency map on the back of the door and then I read it again. The exit nearest you may be behind you. I tried not to think in italics.

There have been other hotels. There have been other rooms. You sleep and you try not to hear the voices of those who came in late. You try not to hear the cart’s squeal at seven a.m. You try not to hear the ice machine’s clunk. You try not to put your ear to the door and listen to people in the hall. You try not to parse what is not your business. It seems a simple discipline when you begin it and then you realize that listening at the door is a way to believe in neighbors, and sometimes you find you want to know someone is there on the other side of the hinge. You tell yourself you do not want that and then you do.

The card for the wedding had come in cream paper with the embossed initials and a line about the honor of your presence and an RSVP line that assumed I might not come. I had been surprised at the extent to which the assumption helped me come. Tessa was the groom’s sister or cousin. It occurred to me as I read the names again in the room that there are a number of women in my head whose ages and parentage I keep getting wrong. Tessa’s mother was called Celia and I had seen Celia carrying the big white bag they give you in hospital when you are discharged, the night of the fire, at about four in the morning. I remembered that exactly. I had watched her move through the lobby barefoot and the bag hitting her leg with every second step. She had not seen me. By four in the morning I had placed a dozen plastic trays of coffee on the hall table for the men from the fire di—call it whatever you like. It had been cold in the lobby. The smoke was in the ductwork. If you want to know what smoke is you have to stay after the fire.

I lay on the bed and closed my eyes and tried to put the ceiling in the right place. I had the card on the bedside table and the program from the rehearsal dinner I had not attended because I had told myself I did not need to be at everything. It takes practice to not be at everything. It takes practice to not sit in every room where a thing might happen. I had not been to a wedding in a long time.

At four I went down to the lobby bar because there was a line on the card about mingling prior to the ceremony and if you do not go where you are supposed to go you will never finish the day. The bar had a name that did not mean much, a single word in capital letters that could have been a brand of bottled water, and a line of bottles up-lit against a frosted panel. There were reeds in a vase and a bowl with limes. There was a black-and-white photograph of the hotel before the renovation, which made a point of the balustrade and the car with whitewalls, and no point at all of what happened after. There are agreed forms for this kind of remembering. There were men in jackets and the peculiar quiet of people who do not know whom to speak to first. There was a woman in a dress the color of cloud who could only have been the bride and another with a paper program folded into a fan who looked like someone’s aunt and had a way of deflecting voices with the back of her hand, a way that made her indispensable.

“Is it her?” someone said behind me, and this was flat and low as if it were a joke being controlled. I did not turn around. I did not want to be introduced to myself.

I saw Celia when she came in with the bride. She was not wearing shoes that day either, not at first. She had them in her hand and then she sat on a chair and slipped them on with the kind of concentration that makes you think she puts the world together by looking at parts and not all at once. She saw me then and there was a check in her face and for an instant she looked as if she were going to turn. Then she did not. She smiled and this was a relief to us both.

“You made it,” she said when she got near me.

“They rebuilt,” I said.

“They rebuilt,” she said. “How is your mother.”

“I don’t know,” I said.

We were not the kind of friends who could carry the same conversation we had put down. We had not been talking then. We had not stayed in contact. She had moved with her husband to a place where the sky is bigger and I had not. She would have said they were growing. He was in property. In those days people used that word as if it meant something strong and simple. He had a way of leaving rooms and taking a certain air with him when he did. He had worn a signet ring and the impression its shape left on his finger was a thing I remember from the old days, he would twist it and the little oval would leave a red coin in the skin. I had not seen him in the lobby. I was not sure I wanted to.

“You look—” she said and then stopped. She touched the edge of my sleeve with two fingers and this was one of the times I remember why we were friends.

“Older,” I said.

She laughed. “We all do. Come on,” she said. “Tessa wants to say hello.”

The bride smiled and asked the right questions and put her hand on my arm the way brides are taught. I am told they have people now who teach courteous gestures. There is a photograph of my mother on the day she married, she is squinting and holding on to the bouquet as if it will get away from her. I do not know if someone taught her how to stand. Tessa had pearls and a blush and a manner that said she had learned certain things. She did not need me to say much. We all agreed to see each other again after. This is what you say.

Someone said he would like to make a toast and we all paid attention. The man with the signet ring had appeared and was adjusting his cuff and he had a voice that he used for rooms. “To those who cannot be with us,” he said, raising the glass. He did not say what that meant. I had always thought that toasts like this were about gratitude displaced, and I have less patience now than I used to do for displacement. People murmured. A younger man with a clipped beard and a white knit tie that did not belong to this part of the state repeated the last phrase as if to own it. The bartender looked down and pinned a lime to the edge of a glass with a pick as if it were delicate work. The bartender had a tattoo of a compass and I tried without success to make a point of it.

In the elevator there was a woman with an updo that made her look like a version of herself. She held the back of the dress with two fingers to keep it from catching. “You used to work here,” she said, and turned to the man beside her as if explaining. She may have been repeating something she had just learned, as people do when they do not know what to do with a piece of information. She smiled. “I bet you know all the secrets.”

“Housekeeping does,” I said. It was as much as I intended to give that day.

The ceremony was on the lawn by the river and the grass was sod that had been unrolled and tamped not long before, you could still see the seams at the edges if you wanted to find them. We sat in white chairs and watched the water. Someone had decided on pale cloth for the runner. There were ropes and poles because there had been an accident last month where a groomsman had fallen into the river and it had required a rescue. The stories collect. There was a breeze off the water and the microphones cut in and out exactly twice and someone had tape for the cords and applied it as if the aisle were a set. There were lilies.

If you have tried to tell this part as if it mattered you have been lying. We all say the vows are the thing and then we cannot remember any single word beyond the familiar ones. The older people sit with their mouths not quite closed and the younger ones take pictures of themselves pretending not to take pictures of themselves and the light hits the bride and you think you would like to be the person for whom that light is. You think you would like to be the one who knows what to do with it.

During the reading the wind picked up and I saw one bridesmaid’s dress lift and settle as if she had been waiting to be told when to breathe. There is a rhythm even then. The mother of the bride cried at the appointed moment and the man with the signet ring put a hand on her back. This meant something simple the day he learned to do it and something else now. I watched his hand for longer than I should have and did not look away when he looked at me. He held my gaze and then not, as if the middle distance were more hospitable. People want to be seen for what they have done and then they do not.

Across the river there was a stand of reeds and one dead tree with no leaves. I thought about the ridge where the fire had started as if it were visible from here. I have thought for years that there was a ridge, that there was a place I could drive to where the first spark occurred. In fact there was a dryer drum. In fact there was lint packed in where it should have been cleared. In fact there was a fuse that had been blown and replaced with a piece of copper wire that should not have been there at all. You think you will remember the heat and you do not. You remember the clean edges on the towels even though people call them fluffy. You remember the phone in your hand and the way your thumb lost the number. You remember not being able to say the room numbers in the right order to the operator. You remember how clear the water looked even while it boiled.

After they kissed we all clapped and I told myself I had done my part. I remained in the chair after the people in my row had moved. I am not always good at walking right away. I do not always leave first. Celia came and sat beside me and smoothed the cloth over her knees as if the dress might crush itself without help.

“Come have something,” she said.

“In a minute,” I said.

She looked at the river, at the dead tree. “We should have gone somewhere else. Everyone told me that. But it seemed best to—face it, do it, have it all here. Is it unbearable for you.”

“It is not,” I said. “It is not, actually.” Then I did not say anything else because it is best when people say that particular word in a sentence to leave it alone.

At the reception the lights were strung on wires and there was an arrangement to make them look like stars. We ate small food off round plates and people moved chairs. There was a band at a sensible volume and the kind of cake that says someone decided to be restrained and the way you knew it was not a mistake was that the frosting had no pearls. I saw the girl from the desk working the seating chart. I saw the bartender from earlier and he pointed slightly with his chin and the woman with the updo took that as her cue to approach and tell a story about the city where she grew up. The story was supposed to be funny. When the story ended no one at our table knew if they should laugh or not. Then the man with the signet ring told a story and when he finished they all laughed because that is how evening light works in a family.

Between the salad and the thing with the steak the hotel’s safety officer came to stand near our table. He had that look of the back-of-house: an ID on a lanyard, the shirt tucked hard into the pants, the watch that is big and black. He introduced himself as the director of risk. He did not meet my eyes for longer than was needed.

“We’ve met,” he said then, as if it were a statement about ourselves and not a statement about the file. He said this was not the time, but would I like to see how we did it now. He said that some people liked to see the pumps. He said that the hotel wanted to make this part of the story. He said that if I did not want to then never mind.

We walked through a corridor where the carpet stopped and the cement began and the light turned from soft to harsh and there was that shift in air that happens when you leave the idea of a place and enter the machinery of it. He wanted to show me the door seals and the stairwells. He had a flashlight and he liked turning it on to circle the place I should pay attention to. He pointed out the intumescent strips around the door frames and the red-lighted signs that said press to exit that he made me press and I did not want to. I stood on each landing and tried to locate a smell. He had a key for every door and you could see his stance change each time he fitted one into a lock, there was a relaxation in his jaw that came when the key turned, as if the turning itself assured him of one thing at least.

“We do a burn every quarter in the training yard,” he said. “You can come sometime if you like.”

“I would not like,” I said, and he said of course, of course, as if I had said I would consider it.

The linens room was new. It was in a different place. I had counted the halls and I knew this. The new place had light like a grocery store. The racks rolled on tracks. The shelves were steel and had been measured. He wanted to talk about the moisture sensors. He wanted to show me the printout from the dryer and how the numbers trended. He wanted me to understand the faith we place in numbers even after. It is a problem in this country that we think numbers do not lie. The numbers have other matters to attend. He told me about the staff training and the posters and the way the posters were printed in Spanish and English and a third language that he did not know the name of. He said it might be Mixtec. He said the forms acknowledged a thing called safety culture. He said the word culture as if we were discussing a gallery.

I took a folded sheet in my hand and I could feel that it had been pressed with too little attention and I did not release this thought. I stood with my hand on the folded edge and I did not let go. He watched me do this and did not tell me to stop. It might have been one of the things they trained him to do, to let the survivor do what she needed to do. It might also have been that he was tired.

“You know,” he said when we stood near the freight elevator and watched the numbers rise and fall, “when they decided to tear the old place down I tried to get one of those old keys with the long blanks, brass, like a souvenir. The contractors took everything. Anything that wasn’t bolted.”

“I had one,” I said. “From a room I liked because the door stuck and nobody made me fix it.”

“You still have it.”

“I left it somewhere,” I said. “I left quite a few things.”

He nodded as if this were a thing he might someday need to tell a jury.

The cake had been cut by the time I came back in and there was a piece of it waiting at my place, white on white. I sat and ate two bites without tasting them and an aunt leaned over with a ribbon between two fingers and asked me if I minded trading places so she could sit near the air. She was the kind of woman who apologizes while she moves you out of her way and makes you feel you have given her something she can use. I say aunt but she may have been something else. There is a class of women we call aunts in public even if they were born into other names.

The father stood up again and tapped his glass with the handle of his knife so that it made a strained sound and we all looked up as if there were an emergency. He told a story about the groom that put the groom into a particular light and then he made a face to tell us he was joking. We take what we can from these details. It is not that we do not know how to read them. Later I saw him by the river in the glow of the uplights and I thought he was crying but he was laughing. What I thought were tears was the straight run of sweat on his face. He stood with a man I did not know and they spoke in low tones with their shoulders arranged to tell you they did not need a wider world.

Celia did not dance. She watched as if it were necessary. She waved when I looked and I watched her look down at her hands and then at the floor as if she had dropped something and could not quite think what to do about it. I went to find a place where I would not be seen because I find it easier to talk when I am not in a place where people expect it, and I came to a hallway off the restrooms where there was a glass case with china from the old hotel. A tureen and a platter. The card said 1929 and 1933, a catastrophic year for floods. It is one way to frame it. There was a little plaque that bore the names of the people who had given money to the memorial fund that placed the case in the hall. I stood there with my hands on the strap of my bag and felt the shift in the floor under the weight of someone moving behind me.

“Do you remember me,” a voice said, and I did not turn because I knew. I knew it in that way memory will present itself like a photograph you did not mean to keep, undeleted, at the bottom of a roll.

“I was in 1127,” he said. “You knocked.”

I turned then because not turning is a form of rudeness I reserve for people I will not see again, and I understood that I would see this man in the hallways of my head for as long as I had hallways. He was shorter than I thought he would have been and he was wearing a tie with a dot and if you have made beds you know about ties and dots and what stains show. He touched the center of his chest with his fingertips as if drawing a shape. He said his name and when he did I felt that odd lift you get when you remember a room number before a person’s name and then a second lift, the humiliation of it.

“I did not open the door,” he said. “You should know that I did not. A lot of the stories—they make it sound like a person goes with instinct and if the instinct is good the act is good. I did not open. I went into the bathroom and I wet a towel and I sat on the tile. I waited for the smoke to come, and it came.”

People who have not been in a hotel at three in the morning imagine panic as a scream but it is a still thing. It is a weight you place on your own chest. He was telling me about his weight. He was asking for a thing I do not know how to give.

“There was another door to that bathroom,” I said finally. “An old door. It had been sealed with paint but not well. Some of the old rooms have those. Is that how you—”

He held up his hand. “I don’t think so. I was pulled out in the hallway later. I have the report. The report says you knocked. A lot of doors. On my door you wrote a mark. I saw it later. You drew a triangle.”

“They taught us to mark what we had done,” I said.

“The mark helped,” he said. He looked down at his shoes. “It meant you had been there. It meant I might be there too.”

We stood there a few seconds longer. The light in the case hit the edge of the tureen and the reflected arc of it landed on the wall and I could not stop watching that arc. He put his hand out and we both looked at it as if it were someone else’s hand, and then we did not shake. He did not know there are certain gestures I cannot complete in rooms with no windows. Neither of us moved away until someone came out of the ladies’ room and saw that we were stationing ourselves there and understood it for what it was and moved on without pausing. The skill of understanding a scene and stepping out of it without leaving a ripple is not taught and I appreciated it more than I can say.

“Do you live here,” he said then, to fill the space between us.

“No,” I said. I gave the name of a town inland where the summer air wants to keep you still. “You.”

“Back and forth,” he said, which could have meant anything. “My wife could not come. I think she still blames me for staying in the bathroom. We do not talk about it as such.”

You do not have to call a thing a thing for it to be the thing. We were finished. He nodded the way you do when a thing is over and he did not need to say more. There are those who will tell you that closure is useful. I have not much found it so.

On my way up to the room later a housekeeper stepped into the elevator with a cart and told me with her eyes where to stand. We have a common language. She had her hand on the rails and her shoulder against the cart because that is the only way a cart goes straight on carpet, and she said good evening without looking at me and she asked me what floor as if she intended not to remember it. She had lotion on her hands because you can smell it and the smell is a practical thing if you have ever cracked the skin between your fingers and bled onto the linen. She leaned toward the panel with her left hand and reached the button with her knuckle so she did not touch it with her palm. It is possible to tell yourself you have left a life in the past and then recognize the way someone moves and learn that you have not. It is possible to decide that you will not make an on-the-job joke to someone who will not laugh. It is possible to let the machine close and put you twenty feet at a time higher into the air and it is possible to say nothing at all.

At two o’clock I woke and did not know the shape of the room. This is not unusual in a hotel and you do not need a trauma to have it happen. I felt for the seam in the sheet to know which way was up. I had placed the phone on the floor on my side of the bed and brushed my hand against its cord to know where I was. For a few minutes I let the dark be a neutral thing. I let the way the air turned in the vent tell me that nobody had opened the window because the new windows do not open. I let the absence of ice machine clunks tell me this was not my floor. I let the line of light under the door tell me there was no one in the hall. I have tricks.

I must have slept because the next thing was the alarm on my phone and the square red numbers on the hotel clock if I opened my eyes and then closed them because the numbers were not the right red, they were thinner and looked weak. I got up and went to the window and opened the sheer. The river had that gray the river gets when the air has not yet decided what to do. A boat moved out from under the bridge and made a V you could draw a protractor on. There was a piece of caution tape on the far bank that I had not seen before and I told myself it had been there the day before and I had not noticed it. I showered. I stood in the water longer than necessary and watched my breath make a fog on the glass and let it diffused back in. I do not keep a mirror by a shower for that reason. I do not want to see what water does to a face at seven in the morning. I do not want to see what water has done to a body.

At breakfast there was the buffet you get in places like this and the way people will approach it as if they are on the edge of a scarcity. We think about plates differently when we can fill one ourselves. The scrambled eggs had the look of something formed by extrusion and the bacon had the look of something formerly alive. I took a piece of melon and a coffee. I saw Celia sitting with the bride and the man with the ring and a woman with hair the color of polished wood who could only have been a sister because of the angles in her face. I did not think to go touch anyone on the shoulder. I did not think to say goodbye. I took the coffee to the porch and leaned against the rail and watched a flight attendant with the hotel-lounge bun take a picture of herself with the water behind her and I let the choice of the photo moment belong to her. It is not nothing, the way we choose to show ourselves.

When I left I did not let the valet bring the car. I took the ticket to the slot and waited because I wanted to feel the hot air of the garage come up into my face. I wanted to smell the oil in the cement. I like the dim spaces in parking structures. There have been days when I have not and this was not one of those. The car came around with the morning’s dust already on it, a film that shows up on silver paint like newsprint on a white towel. The valet said ma’am again and then he saw something in my face and changed it to have a good rest of your day. I drove out under the arm and into a day that had decided to be hot after all. I did not take the bypass. I took the levee road because I wanted to see the pear trees that are new now, not the old orchard I think I remember. There are stories people like to hear about fear and what it teaches you. I do not have the one you want. I have a story about a woman who learned to carry matches for a long time after and did not strike them because she liked their weight in her hand. I have a story about a woman who learned to leave a door unlatched. I have a story about a woman who cannot decide if she went back because there was a wedding and a dress and a woman she once loved who needed to see that nothing would happen again, or because there are places where we would like our names to fade and you cannot fade a name you have not said aloud.

At the last turn before the bridge there is a place where the river runs against the bank and the carp flick their backs out of it like something from an old story. I pulled off and rolled down the window. There was a smell of tar where they had patched the road, and fennel, and the brown border smell of hot water. I told myself I might do this every Sunday now. I told myself I might not. I told myself I could stop considering whether this was closure. I told myself that if I needed a story I had the tureen in the glass case and the information on the card and the list of names on the plaque. I told myself that was more than I would have had if the old building had just fallen down under the weight of its own water, the way buildings do in flood year, the way the men down at the levee club will tell you they do if you wait and buy them a drink. I told myself that sometimes numbers work and sometimes they do not and that you cannot learn to live with either fact, not in a way that holds. I told myself that the young woman at the desk will end up with a story of her own, involving a dress and an uninvited guest and the kind of afternoon you do not ever get back, and that her story will not be mine and this is for the best. I told myself to drive. I drove. The road went narrow between the willows and the reeds and when the road goes narrow you pay attention. It is a way to love a road again. I drove into the air that sits low over the fields and learned again that it does not matter, the model of the car, the kind of music, whether you left the radio on or off, who you were in that building and who you will be next. There is not a way to fix it. There is only a way to sit with it until it is not as loud, and even then it can start up again with a smell, with the way the lines on a plate crack under a glaze when it gets old. I did not look back. I do not look back as a rule. I know what is there. I know who is not. I know now what a river looks like when it is made to behave. I know that from certain angles it still looks as if it has a will. There are days when I think that was the thing I wanted to go see, that stubborn line, the will that remains. There are days when I believe it. There are days when I do not.
