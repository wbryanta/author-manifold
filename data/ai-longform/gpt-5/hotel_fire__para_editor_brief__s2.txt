The new hotel had a smell I could not place, something like cedar shavings and lemon rind threaded through chilled air. It was the smell of money made pure. I stood for a beat under the automatic doors while they inhaled me, my rolling carry-on waiting behind my ankle like a well-trained dog. The glass shone without a single streak. Somewhere in the back of my mind a rag lifted, a wrist turned in the precise flick that banished smears and left corners crisp.

“Welcome,” a young man in an excellent suit said from the podium where people once smoked between cars. His mouth formed the word as if it always tasted good. The façade was all vertical lines, a skin of light pleats and whitened stone. They had left the marquee up as a kind of concession to memory, but the old sign with the peeling gold letters was gone. This place had been built on the bones of the other place the way a new tooth grows under a chipped one, pushing, patient.

“Checking in for the Lowell-Levitt wedding,” I said. The young man’s smile furled another inch. The spiral of the earpiece behind his ear made a ripple in his hair.

“Elevators are just past the bar,” he said, gesturing with an open palm. “Signage for the welcome reception is up. Cocktail hour starts in twenty.”

All renewed. The old lobby had been tight and warm, carpet swallowing every sound and the receptionist behind a half wall. Now the space poured around columns like silk, hummed and bubbled. The bar was a bone of marble. Stools in enormous velvet throats gathered people, already leaning on their elbows and laughing. I had an urge to find the seam where things joined. It was more urgent than thirst. The seam would tell me where the building had been cut and sutured.

At the desk, a woman in a lipstick matched to the hotel’s palette asked my name. I told her. The same old syllables, though on paper, once, there had been another last name. Her hands danced on a keyboard, clacking politely.

“Two nights, queen room, wedding rate,” she said. “Will that be all?”

Her nails were like small mirrors. I watched the infinitesimal movement of my face reflected in each one as she slid my key card across. The smell up close was more obvious now: not cedar at all, but a engineered green note, water and stone and something like cucumber distillate. The corridor off the lobby had a forest mural along one wall. In another life I might have dusted those leaves, kept the wooden slats along the baseboard free of lint. In this life I adjusted the strap of my dress, smoothed the hem, and nodded like a woman who did this all the time.

On the elevators someone had etched the words “ascend” and “restore” into the mirror panels. I could have mocked it, but I let the rhetoric pass. My face looked better than this morning, when I’d sat in the car at a gas station and considered turning around. I had slept bad last night and worse the night before. I’d told Cal I wanted to come alone and then when he asked if I was sure I’d said, too quickly, yes.

Room Twelve Twenty-Seven opened with a muffled bolt. Light fell in as if poured, a small indentation of the bed, the neat parade of pillows. For a second, before I set the bag down, instinct took my hands. I pressed my palms flat to the duvet and smoothed, dragging invisible creases toward the corners, flapping once to make the fabric settle. The motion I’d been taught made a tiny pigeon’s sound in the throat of the cotton. Habit lives longer than flesh.

I set my carry-on against the wall and went to the window. I had asked for a high floor without asking. The river ran below, a slick black belt, and beyond it the old warehouses simmered in post-rain heat. The sky was a thin, pretty blue, not like the viscous blue of night when it carries heat so heavy you can taste it. A long time ago, on a night like this, I stood ringing a white towel over a housekeeping cart and watched smoke climb out of a hallway door like a hand.

Nora’s email had been bright and fierce with exclamation marks. Can you believe it? At the rebuilt hotel! They gave us a rate! Everyone’s staying there. You have to come early and promise me you’ll help during hell-hour. You’re so calm you keep everyone else calm, she’d said, which made me laugh out loud in my kitchen. I had been the one to get grass stains out of the knees of our jeans, to put our mother to bed when her migraines bloomed, the one who knew how to fold anything flat. Calm is not a personality. It’s work.

The room had a square white chair with an intentional angle that punished the lower back. The desk sat empty except for a leather folder shaped like an envelope and a brand-new menu with an obscene club sandwich photographed to look like a person you could learn to love. The pen was heavy. The paper had a watermark like rain, fractional, impossible to trace. I ran the heavy pen over my thumbnail and pictured Nora in white, Nora frowning up at me while I pinned tulle or took a safety pin from the emergency sewing kit I always packed as if disasters were just loose hems.

I showered for too long. The body remembers the feel of water in a certain kind of air. Hotel showers always make me feel borrowed, like a person you put on for a night. The glass was so new it squeaked at the drag of my fingers. I left an oval of steam with the heel of my hand and watched it evaporate. Years ago the towels were a rougher weave, and two of them never felt like enough. I had known to grab a third from an empty room and fold it in so it did not look stolen. This towel was soft and large enough to be a flag. I came back into the cold room with it like a cloud around me and checked the clock. If I went downstairs now, I’d have fifteen full minutes to stand at the edge of a glossy crowd and remind my face of the shapes it could make.

At my suitcase I hesitated. Under the top layer of dresses and shoes there was a zippered inner pocket where I kept an old bobby pin bent into the shape of a question mark. I had found it long before the fire down behind a nightstand. A woman’s forgotten bit, copper catching the dust. I used to hold it in my mouth while I rolled pillowcases, to not lose it. After the fire I found it in the pocket of my khakis, fused a little at the bend from heat. It could have been garbage. I marched it into my new life as if it were an amulet. I slipped it now into the small clutch I’d brought for the evening, its weight familiar as a coin.

The elevator was cold enough to raise a film on my arms. Two teenagers in suits duelled at whisper volume about something large and abstract. Their hair was so glossy they reflected me. I smiled and they looked startled and smiled back like they’d been caught out in kindness. The carpet on this floor had angular leaves in a gray on gray pattern. The old carpet was navy, and you could trace where it went thin in the main corridors, places where hotel staff walked, as if a river had worn away at stone.

The welcome reception had already become the welcome anything. On a raised platform someone had arranged towers of raw oysters on broken ice, a lynx of lemon wedges curling around them. A woman with a small bell tapped it twice. “Make sure you see the couple,” she said. “Make sure you sign the book.” The book had glossy pages with pictures of Nora and Max at various places a couple can afford to go when the couple has early success and two sets of generous parents. In college Nora had called me from a pay phone outside a bar to ask if I had any ramen at my place and would I mind heating the water now, she was on her way. We had eaten kneeling on my thrift sofa in a sublet I shared with three other girls, our knees touching, and when the spaghetti water overflowed her eyes had gone wide and happy at the mess.

Now she rose toward me in white, small and fast, wrists already jangling with gifts she could not name. “Lena,” she said into my collarbone. “I thought you’d come later.”

“I missed you,” I said. It was true. It was not the true thing.

She pressed her cheek to mine. Her makeup smelled like a cake. “Can you clip me?” she said, lifting the strap of her dress to show me a tiny silver hook. “Max is hopeless. Did you meet my cousins? Don’t let Aunt Vera talk at you.” Her eyes were a neon sign that said help in friendly cursive. I looped the hook, fastened it, straightened the tiny white wing of lace as if it were a thing I understood, and she exhaled and was gone, drawn toward a cluster of people who mattered more in this minute than I did.

I found water with a lemon wedge that made my mouth twinge and drifted to the wall where the windows looked out at the river. I watched my reflection stand inside their reflection of the city. The edge of the bar curved like a shoulder. To my left a woman in a teal dress took an oyster down as if it were medicine. The room was loud in the way of rooms that want you to feel loudness is a consensus.

“Lena.”

It was said as if the shape of me had just occurred. I turned. The man in front of me had a head I almost knew. The years had changed the hairline and put his shoulders inside something stiff and expensive. His face, now, was a face I had seen a hundred times a day in profile wheeling the luggage cart, looking ahead as if the floor had no chance of buckling. I had known him when his tie was frayed.

“Luis,” I said, and his teeth showed in that harness-breaking grin he had used when we ran the housekeeping cart racing down the service corridor at 2 a.m., both of us knowing the night auditor would hear and lean out to yell and still not stop.

“God,” he said. His eyes pinned the air over my shoulder for a second, as if he had to locate a version of me there to be sure. “I thought it was you. And then I thought—no.”

We stood there a moment as if the room had slid back from us, Norwegian wood receding. It would have been the sort of conversation we had to take elsewhere, if we were in a movie. In a hotel, everything is elsewhere.

“You work here,” I said, though his suit had already answered that question. There was a small gleam on his lapel. Whatever title it signified I did not need to read.

“They brought some of us back,” he said. His face did a small quirk. “New owners. Old pipelines. Some of us follow the pipes.”

“How long?” I asked.

“Two years,” he said. “They finished right before the first wave hit and then nobody came. Empty for months. The ice machine complained to itself. We practiced carrying trays to imaginary people. Now.” He lifted a hand to indicate the good problem of a crowded room. “You never—”

“No,” I said. “I never came back.”

He glanced down at my arms. I had not thought to cover anything with makeup. The scars are not what they used to be; the skin there looks like watered silk. He looked and looked away in the way people do with courtesy and without apology. “I heard you got out,” he said. His voice made a small cup around it to hold the heat. He did not say whose name followed.

There was always a name to follow mine when I told this story, if I told it. The person I didn’t become. The person I left or the person who was already gone when I looked back. Which name was the worst to say changed, depending on the day, the drink, the weather.

“I did,” I said. “You?”

“Barely.” He tilted his left wrist, showing me the shiny circle none of us used to have. I could see the ghost of the bracelet the paramedics had cut off that night. “I was in the vestibule with two suitcases, arguing with a man who thought he deserved to go back for his watch. You remember? Mr.—”

I did. Men like that had a way of bricking themselves into your memory. He had become the shape of something you could hate if hating were useful. The fire came like a quick decision and then like a long refusal. By the time you realized this building would keep insisting on itself, the geometry of out mattered more than anything about why or where it began or anybody’s individual watch.

“And after,” he said, “I went to the other property for a year. They needed bodies. You know how it is. I kept thinking I’d walk by and the old entry would say hello. That strange porch the winter shelter guys slept on.”

I knew. The old entry had been shallow and a little mean. The lobby’s carpet was threadbare at the place where people lined up for coffee. There was a dent at the wall where a suitcase had hit it, the wallpaper patched with a worse pattern.

“I don’t know why I thought you’d never set foot in this place again,” he said, and smiled apologetically. “But Nora’s wedding—your Nora? We ran towels together for a week once. She gave me a twenty for finding her earring.”

“My Nora,” I said. “Who met Max at a rooftop dinner somebody else paid for and kept the menu after.”

He reached for a passing tray and snatched a stuffed mushroom and put it on a napkin and handed it to me with the flourish of a man who knew what good men looked like. “There’s a staff hallway on twelve that doesn’t go anywhere,” he said, dropping his voice minutely for the fun of it. “They built it to match the old design and forgot that no one would need it now. If you open the gray door next to the storage for banquet chairs, you can stand in that hallway and look at the new wall where the old wall wasn’t. I thought—you’d want to see it.”

“Maybe later,” I said. I didn’t know whether I meant it.

“Come find me,” he said. “I’ll be around pretending this is all simple.”

I watched his back move through the room like a known rhythm. It made my throat tighten to see that gait, the way he looked left, looked right, carried three things in six fingers. I ate the mushroom. It was worse than it looked and better than it could have been.

Someone made a speech full of jokes about how the couple had never been on time to anything, but especially not to their own lives. People laughed the way a room directed them to laugh. My gaze kept slipping to the fire exit signs like small red eyes. It wasn’t even the flame that worried me, anymore. It was anything with a choke. Ceremony candles, kitchen grease, a sleeve too near a range—yes, but also a crowd pressing, the old physics of not enough air in a stairwell. I could not hear the toasts for the noise of the HVAC ducts somewhere, a rush like a river behind the walls, the hotel breathing on our behalf.

I went to find a bathroom and found a mirror instead. The vanity was backlit like a mercy. The soap containers were fixed to the wall. I liked something in my chest was moved into a different pocket when I pressed the foaming pump and it obeyed. A girl no older than I was then came in wearing a black dress with the hem safety-pinned to new perfectness. When her eyes found mine in the mirror, she smiled small and fast and then busy’d herself with a tissue and a lipstick the exact color of cherries. The staff had a different uniform now, sleeker. Their name tags were triangular. The one on her chest said ARACELY. I could fold a fitted sheet with this girl.

“Do you know where they took the smoking deck?” I said, and then laughed at my own question. She smiled reflexively and shook her head as if smoke were a concept with cute ears.

When I came out, Nora stood in a corner with her fists pressed to her hips as if trying to push them into better alignment. “We can’t have open flame,” she said to me as if we were mid-conversation. “The manager says it’s a hazard. For once in my life I feel litigious.”

“Think of our mother with a glass of wine near tulle,” I said, and she allowed that there was a point. “Use the electric ones. No one will notice once the lights go down.”

Her breath made small weather in front of her face. She linked her arms through mine without asking. “Do you remember when you told me that trick with the tie?” she said. “About the seam side in?”

“It wasn’t a trick,” I said. “It was a rule.”

“You always liked rules because rules obeyed you,” she said, and we both laughed, because that is not how rules work and because on our best days we told the same story about our years. On bad ones we insisted on two separate books. We drifted to the guest book table. Nora told me each person’s name as she waved. She said, “That’s Aunt Vera in the zigzags,” and “Max’s cousin Jonah, the one with the podcast,” and “The florist we found on Instagram who turned out to be older than—I don’t know. Than God.” The florist, who was a woman so small she looked portable, lifted two white roses and touched them to each other like animals greeting in a wood.

“I’ll see you in the morning,” I said after another beckoning called her. My mouth wanted to press to her hair. Instead I tapped her knuckles twice the way we had as children when we meant, secretly, that we meant more. “Try to sleep.”

“You try to sleep,” she said. “Write me if you can’t. We’ll tell each other lists of men we hate from high school and that will put us out immediately.”

I looked for Luis and did not look for Luis. The elevator up to twelve made the lights in the mirror tighten, as if the room were swallowing. On the twelfth floor the corridor had a hush that made me wish for the vacuum’s snore. I found the gray door. I did not open it. The new wall where the old wall wasn’t could wait one more hour for my hand.

In the room I kicked off my shoes and sat on the edge of the bed without letting my full weight fall. Once you make a bed on twelve rooms in a row you know what bodies do to mattresses. A small paper had been slid under my door. Tomorrow’s schedule of events. A cartoon of two champagne flutes meeting. The line that said “midnight—send off—sparklers outside.” When I read that my chest went flat. The tiny white sticks fizzing in twenty pairs of hands looked pretty in somebody’s mind. In my mind there were twenty points of ignition and a stiff breeze.

I texted Cal. Arrived. It’s very clean. He sent back a picture of the cat lying on its back as if asking the arc to throw it. He said, Call if you want a voice. I held the phone and let his sentence move over my skin like the tide just covers a toe.

Sleep came in the stop-starts it always did in a bed that wasn’t mine. At three I woke and lay still, listening. I am not a religious person, but some noises you hear as names. The old hotel had the blanket noise of the washer turning in the basement. The new one had a different hymn. For a minute I could not understand my body’s insistence that something was wrong. My mouth dried. The bobby pin in the clutch under my bag felt like a key. The air sat, holding. Then the fan unit kicked in and the room exhaled. I let out a sound that embarrassed me and then made me laugh. I laughed again so I would not begin to cry. I counted to the number I knew to get you through a stairwell if the light’s off.

At seven I showered again, just to perform the possibility of morning. My scar puckered in the steam. I touched the places on my side where heat had convinced me it would enter through the skin and make me its house. I had been working the late shift. Mr. Funicello had called down to say the iron in his room was sticky; could we please send someone? Not a man. The lobby was a dim aquarium. I had knocked on the door and said housekeeping in a particular tone that makes people think about their habits. He had paced while I scrubbed the iron’s face with a magic eraser until a chalk of black came up. “You girls must learn this from your mothers,” he had said. “Nobody taught my wife a thing.” The joke of this should not have made me want to press the iron down on the nearest thing to hand. After I left his room I rolled the cart to the service sink and wrung a towel and watched the gray pour out of it, and when I lifted my head the hallway was smoke.

The fire was later in the official report than it was in my body. The sprinklers in some places did not turn on. The stairwell filled. People pressed to go down past an obstacle we could not see. A woman screamed and the scream had the clink of something breaking in it. Alma, next to me, had her hands out in front like someone feeling love in the dark. “Left,” she shouted, and then, “no, no, no—” and then I could not hear her. Smoke nameable, acrid as money. The heat pushed. I had thought heat was a matter of degrees. Heat was a person with its hands on your throat. My breath learned to be thin. I learned how a towel over the head is not a trick but a dialogue. Somewhere someone pounded on a door as if it had debts. When I came out finally into the alley with somebody’s child under my arm who did not belong to me and the night air hitting me like all the glass on the building had broken at once, I could not stop the green water from coming out of me. It went on and on as if my lungs had downloaded a reservoir while I wasn’t looking and now wanted it back.

In daylight you learn to carry your ghosts like umbrellas, folded, polite inside your bag. I put on the dress I had brought for morning coffee and friend stuff, the one you can sit cross-legged in without worrying about your knees. I went downstairs to the café that wasn’t really a café but a place for pastries to be stacked in glass domes like museum objects. The barista was twenty and kind to the point of reverent. I said, “Coffee,” and he asked, “Hot or iced?” like it changed everything in our life.

Nora texted me a string of emojis and then, Are my mother and your mother both breathing. I answered, Yours is, mine is at the nail salon arguing about tips and what a tip means if service is slow and she had to wait. I saw your aunt a minute ago explaining to the manager why the lighting in the ballroom is a personal attack.

Nora typed back, God give me strength.

At ten there was a rehearsal I was not meant to attend, but I went and sat on the far edge of the balcony like a person who enjoys architecture. I watched them practice the walking: the orchestrated, the feinted. The woman officiating did not wear a collar, just authority smugness. She told them when to lift their heads. Men tested giddiness like shoes with slick soles. After, Nora found me on the stoop of the old door-that-wasn’t.

“You look very sad,” she said.

“I’m thinking about a laundry chute,” I said. It was a stupid answer. She kissed my cheek anyway.

“Don’t leave,” she said into my ear. “I mean later. At the party. You have to stay. If I don’t see your face on the dance floor I will divorce him within the hour.”

“I will stay,” I said, and made myself mean it.

At noon my phone chimed with a blocked number. I didn’t answer. Later there was a knock on my door, too early for turn-down. When I opened it, there stood the girl from the bathroom with a stack of linen like a cake. She smiled in apology, saw the numbers wrong, admitted to the wrong floor. I said, “Wait,” and then, “if you have a second.”

She stepped in on her toes like someone who didn’t want to leave any dust. “Do you need extra towels?” she said.

“I want to show you something,” I said, and it was not generous, exactly, to want to give her time. It’s why managers hate former staff. We infect the timetable. Still, she stood holding the linens like enormous thick doves and watched me lift the fitted sheet off the mattress with the slow swift roll and the four corners popped free and I showed her my way of tucking and how to corner without bulk, and she nodded as if I were not from a century ago. “It’s just a way,” I said, and heard my younger self saying the same words to a woman older than my mother who had been patient with me and said, It’s all a way, mijita. Put the sheet on the bed.

After she left I stood there with all the fresh air the open door had brought in and laughed into it softly for no reason except the small relief of usefulness. I put on my long dress for the ceremony. In the elevator I caught my reflection and we both surprised ourselves. I looked like I had been arranged.

The ballroom had been made to look like someone else’s meadow. There were trees, real but artful, in pots disguised as stones. Little white lights vanished if you looked directly at them. People cried. People made jokes about always crying. My seat card was written in calligraphy that looked like a hand someone had learned just for today. When I sat, a woman I’d never seen asked if I was from Nora’s work or Max’s. I said, “I’m from before,” and she nodded solemnly as if that were a place on a map.

The vows were the kind you could tell would be made iron by love and then softened by time into something nicer to hold. The unity candle lived in a hurricane glass the height of a wine bottle. When the mothers lit their small tapers and then bent over it, a ripple passed through my arms. I breathed. There were three beats where my breath and the room’s breath couldn’t agree. Then the wick caught. The tiny noise flame makes when it begins, like someone inhaling to say a word, was all over my skin. I held the metal chair with my whole hand and placed my mouth in that neutral line that says: happy. And it was beautiful, the night and the promise and the smile that involved even the smallest muscles in Nora’s face. The woman next to me wiped her eyes with a napkin and we agreed silently to be embarrassed about it on the way out.

Sparklers, too, are tiny promises. I considered leaving before the send-off and thought of Nora at thirteen making me swear to be at the first day of high school outside the gym because she wanted to walk onto the polished orange floor with someone who loved her looking. I could not make another promise and break it in the same hour. Between ceremony and party I went to the balcony to look at the river. The wind had stiffened, unhelpful for little flames. “Are they making you do it?” Luis said, appearing at my shoulder as if he had been arranged, too.

“I am making me do it,” I said. He gave me a long look, a ledger of care.

“If you need to not,” he said. “There’s the gray hallway. There’s also the bottom of the laundry chute.”

“Which is where I come from,” I said, and he smiled in that tired human way. “I’m all right.”

“All right isn’t a place to live,” he said. “It’s a place to pass through.”

“I have a room,” I said. It came out as a defense and then, hearing it, less so. He nodded.

“Okay,” he said. “Okay then.”

The band had violins because money likes strings. The singer had a voice like a bright penny. By the time they called us onto the lawn out front for the exit, the sky had gone that black that eats the edges of buildings. Someone handed me a sparkler and a lighter with the hotel’s name on it, printed sleekly. The manager made a speech about standing still and holding the lit side away from hair. The cluster of girls in chiffon made a noise like a flock taking off. I could smell the metal of the unlit stick, which made me remember the voice of the fire alarm on the old second floor, which sounded like a child you might not run to comfort.

This is what I did: I stepped two feet back. I took a breath that had my name in it. When the people on either side of me touched metal to metal with someone’s lighter, I held mine out, too. The first fizz of it leapt up my arm. It was joy and it was terror. It was like telling the truth. Sssssst. The blue-white at the hub, the tiny spitting stars at the fringe. The crowd made one sound like a long oh. They were beautiful. They were tiny teeth. They were scratches of lightning you could lay between your fingers for a minute. Nora and Max ran through the lane of light like runners between honeysuckle hedges. Her dress lifted like a small, tame gull. He looked so young there would be days when he’d be angry at himself for it. They ducked into the car someone had garlanded with flowers, whose driver blinked stoically as if this were either well compensated or a test, probably both. The sparklers died in handfuls on the other side of us, the smell like rocks, a clean char. My hand shook and then did not. I looked at the blackend end like a wand that knew the trick was over. I waited to feel ruined, and instead I felt the exact size of my body and the air around it. I let the breath go slow.

“Now eat cake,” Nora said in my ear an hour later, her makeup smudged in a way that made her lovely and human. “And then take your shoes off.”

There is not much to report about dancing except that it can be a short freedom. I danced until my chest loosened and my calves hummed and sweat sat like a proof along my spine. I danced with a woman and then with a man and then with nobody but the counted beat. The waiters moved like a game of jacks around our feet, reaching down and retrieving glittering empties and my eyes followed their hands as if they were a story. They were young. One of them laughed at something a guest said, the laughter bright, and then turned away and let his face be his again.

After, I walked the long corridor of floor twelve in bare feet. The carpet had warmed. The gray door off the banquet storeroom was exactly itself. It opened with a thumb-soft pop. Inside, the unneeded hallway held its breath. There was the hum of nothing particular. The lights were on sensors, and so for a second it was dark and the walls were there only because I knew walls existed. Then they came to life one by one, squares of light waking. The wall in front of me was where the old service door had poured us out, rinsed us down into the laundry. I could see our bodies slide there as sure as if I were watching a tiny film.

A maid back here once had stored a photograph in the tiny crevice between molding and wall because it was a place her wife would not look. I had helped her slide it in on a Friday with hands hard with soap. Another time Alma had left a note to herself in a red-checked notebook inside a broken vacuum, a list of what to buy with the overtime check if it came, if the gods of OOS and guest complaint smiled. Once I had seen, in the corner by the service panel, a single button from a wedding dress, dense as candy.

I lay my hand on the new paint where the old door wasn’t. Cold came back. A little gray held on the heel of my palm. They had made this place without ghosts. We still carried ours in. I thought of the girl—ARACELY—of all the girls whose names I kept like a rosary. I thought of holding a clean towel to a mouth that had forgotten air. I thought of Luis, and the line of his neck in an inexpensive black tie, and how we had taken backstairs together laughing, how we had been younger than anyone had a right to be in responsibility’s balm. I thought of Nora, whose ten-year-old self used to stand on my shoes while I danced so it would feel like she was tall. I am a woman in a hallway in a hotel with a history and a hand on a wall that did not exist when I almost didn’t. In the soft far murmur of ice in buckets, of faucets, of a stranger two doors down sobbing once, hic, then quiet, I could hear a thing you might call mercy or you might call the continued presence of ordinary life.

When I came out, the air from the ballroom down the way had the conclusive smell of sawdust and beer. The staff had begun their late ritual of stacking chairs, pulling linen, making square the chaos we had made. I stood and watched a minute like a person at the fence of a field, admiring other bodies’ work. Luis saw me and lifted his hand in a skeptical salute. I lifted mine back. Then I went to my room and washed my feet in the sink like a pilgrim or a thief. I left the towel on the floor in the precise way that signals a fresh one and then on second thought I hung it where it could be reused, because if you have ever done a cart count you know there is no such thing as a towel without its own story. Lying down, I put the bobby pin on the nightstand and it gleamed like a small fish in the lamplight, aquatic, difficult to hold. I took out my phone and wrote Nora: I stayed. She sent back, Good. Good girl. Good everything.

I clicked the light off. The dark in the room settled like a thing that allowed me. Far below, a bus made a comment to itself. I let my breath find the duct’s breath. We would all of us go home soon and say what you say after weddings: the flowers were beautiful, the food, they looked so happy, it meant something to be there. There was a time I thought I would never say meant again. I say it now. Meant, in its mouth-shape. The word held a room for me to sleep in. I slept.
