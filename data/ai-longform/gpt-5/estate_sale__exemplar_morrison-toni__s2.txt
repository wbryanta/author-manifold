The cash tin was the color of a winter lake, dented on one side where Pearl had once dropped it trying to swat a fly that lived just to taunt her. Cold even in June, colder now though the day was in heat-soaked chorus—cicadas tuning their legs, a boy's bicycle bell like a foolish bird. Inez set it on the card table that had been Christmas and Monopoly and the place men leaned one hip on to say Is Pearl in? The table made that ragged scrape on the porch plank and already there were feet on the walk, the pebbled concrete scolding down to the street they still called by a man who had died mean but fixed the potholes himself when the city would not. She had taped fluorescent price tags on the things that could be tagged, stretched ribbon around the banister to make a boundary like a sheriff would, and wrote on a piece of cardboard with the fat black marker DAVIES ESTATE, 9–5, CASH. She corrected it twice: Pearl never married a Davies she kept, and Inez had long ago removed the second name from her own mailbox though the Postmaster said it would tangle the route.

Folks had circled slow from ten, some of them known enough to have crawled her mother’s steps in socks; some of them new—the kind that show up because they cannot keep their own things long, or because they have too much and need to fill or test their plenty, or because they simply like to see what the living leave. There was a woman with an ice-froth laugh standing deliberate on the porcelain rooster umbrella stand, testing it with her heel. There was a man who came with a short measuring tape clipped to his belt. He pulled at it and let it snap, pulled and snapped, pulled and snapped, as if to tell the couch it would have to measure up. A blond boy whose face was all eyelashes and bravado sucked on a red stick of sugar and reached without seeing for the glass bowl that used to hold the holiday ribbon candy nobody liked, only to have his mother switch his wrist in a move so casual it had love in it. The bowl rang like a bell but did not crack. That bowl had been there fifteen Christmases and had outlived Pearl by two months.

“Ma’am,” said the man with the tape, already in her living room, already lowering himself to the dimpled cushion of the camelback sofa. “Ma’am, this is—now what would you call this era? Federal? Real pretty. We’d have to reuphol—”

“Seventy-five,” said Inez. Her voice came easy and dull, like she had said it in a dream last night forty times. “Two men to lift, two to get it out the door. Don’t rattle the springs.”

He made a noise with his mouth and looked past her, behind her, trying to call on a male principle that would make his eyebrows meaningful. “Sixty,” he said, like they were already intimate enough to haggle. “It’s got some fade here.”

“That’s where lives happen,” she said. “Seventy-five. Cash.”

He blanched, she thought. Or bristled. Or just did math on his own thinness. He nodded, not smiling, and stood slow, and she knew: he would come back when the day was long with wanting and she would have to decide if the couch belonged best to him, to his woman who would never roll on it the way two children had, or to nobody at all.

Inside, the house smelled like Pearl—bleach and starch and something sweet she never ate. Even emptied of half its furniture the scent held; it had swum into the wood, the door frames, the hairline spaces between floorboards where grit, God, and paperclips went. Inez had not slept in this house since the funeral, when she had lain on her mother’s bed sideways and kept her coat on because she could not imagine rest in that room without something that kept the world off. She had for two weeks before the sale come here each afternoon and make lists and unmake them. She had wrapped cups in newspaper and found Pearl’s handwriting on the backs of old church bulletins, notes for stew and notes for scrubbing and little prayers so bald—Lord, if You let me see her do right I swear I won’t ask again—that Inez would have laughed if her throat were not a sore rope.

“Miss Inez?” Mrs. Battles, from across the alley, raised a paper bag like an offering. “I brought you them price dots I told you. We got a roll a while back when our Ella moved to Texas—Lawd them people took near everything that girl owned with they trucks. She left the whole house shining. Had men on them straps like mules. You want a lemonade? I put a pitcher in your icebox.”

“I’ll get it,” Inez said. “Thank you.” She squeezed Mrs. Battles’ hand even though the woman had been the one in February to tell Pearl her tulips would not take if she planted them in that ragged line against the porch—too damp there, too much shade. It had taken Pearl three days to give up on that shade and even then she had gone out with a spoon and moved the bulblets like eggs, whispering at them.

A boy came out the door with a lamp shade on his head and a yodel on his tongue and a woman behind him in a shirt that said I HAVE PLANS but her hair said she had not kept them yet. “How much for the…for the…My mama had one just like that. With the little horse on the side.”

He was pointing to the radio—solid as summer, with its green eye and its row of brass buttons that stuck. Inez’s father’s late-weather voice lived in that radio; it could not be played without spitting out his laughter somewhere. There had been a year he slept in the basement and it had felt like the radio upstairs had borrowed him for warmth. He had called in to the station one night, the way people call in to declare or to dance, and the man on the air had told him bring a woman to get in free and he had said he did not have one who brought him in free of anything. His laugh on that call had been all teeth; Inez had ached at the way it did not sound like a man who lived in the bottom of the house with his cussing and his rig smell. She did not know if the man on the radio had laughed back or if the air had betrayed him, hung him up there like a scrim for anyone to tear through.

“Twenty,” she said. The boy’s mother’s eyebrows rose in theatrics. “Twenty? They’re charging ten at Goodwill for whole radios that talk.”

“This one don’t talk,” Inez said. “It says. You see right here? Says.”

He read with his mouth moving. Not Working. The boy looked at her like he could fix not working. “Fifteen,” she said, because the boy had done nothing but be alive and want. The mother nodded, and fifteen dollars lay oily in the tin.

“Miss Pearl never did nothing by half,” Mrs. Battles said under her breath, touching the doilies, the small pitcher with the crack that made lemon water taste like well is deep. “She got them curtain rods straight out a magazine, recall? Put them up by her own self. My Willie said, I don’t see no sense in drilling that close to plaster—he talking man, you know—and she say to him, You don’t see because you don’t look. That Pearl.”

Her mother had been a woman whose wrists were narrow but could turn a screwdriver faster than any man in church. She had scrubbed the kitchen white by force of will, not Clorox. She kept a drawer for twine and a shoe box for hair, little coils of what she had combed from her head every Thursday evening sitting with the bowl on her lap, the Good Times crops behind her eyes. Pearl had had ways. She rinsed her clothes in vinegar, wore her house key on a ribbon that lived under her blouse, and would not allow a glass to dry upside down because she believed water had to remember its direction or it would come to harm. When she laughed, which she did with care, she made a sound like the first click of a stove lighting.

“I’m sorry for your loss,” someone said who did not mean it, but they said it in the tone of those who mean to be known as the kind who say sorry.

“Thank you,” Inez said, who did not mean she accepted, but meant she would be the kind who let them leave here with a good feeling about themselves.

People had body-checked their way into the small rooms, shoulders skimming doorframes that had held up men and dust for seventy years. The sugar canister went early, though Inez had wanted to keep it; it had a measure-scoop that fitted only that canister, useless in any other. A woman from out of nowhere, hair like Sunday meringue, took all the magazines and all the scissors and laughed when Inez said, “You cut words out of papers with these till they got no words left?” The woman said, “I make collages. You can say anything if you glue it to something pretty.”

“What you asking for that blade?” a man with salt in his beard asked, his eye on the cast iron like it owed him.

“That blade?” She did not even look. She said “That skillet belonged to God before it belonged to us. Forty.”

“Now see.” He splayed his hand on the counter where Pearl had cut biscuits without a cutter. “You know it ain’t but fifteen at Wal-Mart for cast.”

She leaned in then, because she felt it coming—the heat prick on her chest, the wish to slap. She smiled, regretted it. “This one got the flavor of every Sunday morning for thirty years. Got cornbread ghosts in it. Got pork fat you pray over. Wal-Mart can’t sell no saved grease.”

“Thirty.”

“Forty.”

He laughed then, truly. “I love to hear you talk, lady. But I ain’t about to get to heaven through a pan.”

“Then get out my kitchen.”

He clicked his tongue like men disappointed in life but not in self. He reached for his wallet, laid two twenties down like he was covering a woman naked on a plank. “Bless you,” he said, and the word sounded like an insult but softer. When he left she stroked the skillet’s lip with her thumb before handing it off—as if it had a trembling mouth.

It was past noon when the cedar chest got noticed. She had pushed it against the hallway wall—between the bedroom door and the bathroom that had a medicine cabinet lipped full with cotton and the round kind of aspirin in a yellow tin because Tylenol made Pearl’s knees itch. The chest was truck-long, low, with a lid that stuck in summer and would slam so quick in winter you could catch a skirt in it and take your hem off without meaning to. It had been Pearl’s first proud purchase after working for a year for Miss Leland, that white woman who breathed like she was holding a coin in her mouth. Pearl had come home the day the chest delivered and wiped it with a cloth that had not dusted anything else, then refused to let anybody put blankets in it for a month. “It need to learn our air,” she’d said, and Inez had laughed because she was twelve and hated the way the chest smelled like rot perfume and hated even more that her mother had spent money on something that did not cook or shine.

A girl with hair shaved close and little gold hoops clicked her nails along the chest. “Nice. Old. It open?” She tried to lift the lid. It moved, but held. “You got the key?”

Inez bent and felt with her knuckles under the center lip the way her mother had taught her and there it was: the turn of iron shaped like a little fish. She put it in the hole, turned. It made that shop-grit sound, grudging but willing. Inside, the blankets smelled like Pearl’s grandmother’s house rusting, an autumn smell that did not like the sun and made it sit anyway. Laid in the blankets, under the black coat with rabbit collar that Pearl wore twice a year even when it choked her throat with heat, there were the letters, strapped with a belt that had once held up a man’s trousers, a man who no longer had them.

“Baby, you didn’t tell me this got secrets,” the girl said, but her eyes went wide with something like respect. “How much?”

“For the chest?” Inez’s mouth was full; her teeth felt tinned. “Eighty.”

“Seventy,” the girl said, quick, as if she had practiced. “And I’ll come get it in thirty minutes with my cousin, that boy right there with the calves. We go to Youngstown, they give you two hundred for this, punking you with that ‘we got to refinish’ lie. I like to keep it in my house.”

“Eighty,” Inez said, because she had put the belt back and the letters thudded something against the floor of the chest that could not be explained to anyone with calves like that. The girl shrugged, and the shrug moved her shoulders like small fish in a clear bag. She nodded like a man, spit in her palm and made a show of not touching Inez with it, and went to get the cousin.

She scooted herself into the hallway like a crab then, closed the chest with a care she had not used on her own knees in years. The letters were heavy though they were paper. Not heavy like knowledge, heavy like time. She had seen them before, as a child forbidden their place. “What is it?” she had asked. “Not for your eyes,” Pearl had said. “There’s a man in there?” “No.” “A woman?” Pearl had not answered. She had what Inez called her shut-face, where the corners of her mouth went east and west and the rest went north. Inez had put her fingers on the lid like she could soak in what lay beneath. She had imagined the chest full of worms that ate unscrupulously or of hair that had grown without the owner knowing. She had dreamed, once, that she had opened the chest and there were both: hair snaked into worms, worms knotted into a plait that had all her own baby ribbons woven in.

Now, with the girl with the calves pumping heat in the lily-drenched hallway, Inez pulled the belt from the letters and sat, the tin on her knees turning her skin to still. She did not open the one on top. She opened the third. It fell into her hand like a warm bird. The date at the corner had not learned how to be old: 1969. In the same upright hand her mother wore like a blouse when she wrote numbers in her checkbook, there was a name at the top and nothing like Dear or honey, no flourish to put a net under what would fall.

Evelyn.

Her mother had never been an Evelyn to anybody Inez knew—Pearl to friends, Mother to her, Miss Dee to white children who could not get their tongues around anything but what they were told. Evelyn was a mouth she had not seen on that face. She read.

I made the bread the way you taught, though this kitchen does not have the right corners for rising, too much draft and not enough wall to hold the warmth. The little oven hammers and sometimes I think there is a man in there with a stick teaching the flame how to behave. I have to sit in the doorway to catch the breeze because the windows do not lift but hang, and when I am in the doorway the women across the hall see me and we say as much as we can with our chins. They have names that do not stick to me. I lied when I told you I would not kiss her again but there was the rain and that red ribbon of a thing she puts on her lip and I am not stone. The window over the sink is good for light but a bad look-out. I saw him pass today, the man that used to live with me and call himself husband. He saw me but he did not see me and I thought, There go my years, walking, carrying more years.

I find I cannot speak here without my shoulders. Nobody knows my talk. I did as you said with the dress—let the hem down and wear a jacket. They think I am less poor than I am, which is a kind of lie but also a runway. Thank you for the five you sent. I will put it in the coffee can under the heart pine board near the stove leg, left side, because that board lifts clean. If I have an anger it is a whole color. I have been trying to make it a ribbon.

E.

The paper was thin but did not tear. Inez did not care that there were people stepping around her feet, cooing at the afghan—“My grandma made one like this, look at the colors, they knew color back then”—or that a small girl in a yellow dress was sliding down the banister on her stomach and her mother could not say “Don’t do that, baby” without smiling first. The letters were a chest under the chest. She read the first this time and the last. They were not love except in how they talked. They were a long bright hold between two women who had pulled each other across something like a river. There were names she did not know—Miss Leland, yes, and a Miss Feldspar whose mouth spat when she spoke and who kept oranges on a plate and would not let Pearl taste because she said citrus gave colored girls bumps. There was mention of a baby unnamed and sent to a place up on a lake for the summer and then not returned. There was money, small and frequent, though Pearl had told Inez she had never took a dime from anybody that could use it. There was a picture pressed in one that bled through—the corner of a hat, not smiling.

She rolled the belt in her hand like a snake she could domesticate and put it across the letters again. She slid the stack under her blouse, against her stomach. Her stomach made a fist. She thought, Not yours. Yours never. But the skin there said, You carried somebody once whether you brought them out or not.

“In thirty,” the girl said again, and the cousin’s calves indeed were ripe fruit under shorts that had been white before. They squatted and lifted; the cedar chest moaned with the song of his mother in the mornings washing jars. And because Inez had that belt hot to her, because she had pulled it out like a thief and did not feel forgiven, she said, “Eighty-five,” though the number had been said and seconded. The girl pulled her mouth to the left and then laughed, tiny. “You cold,” she said. “All right. Eighty-five. I like to give more for the pretty smell anyway.” She paid, and they were gone, the chest trailing two inches of a third life down the sidewalk to a truck with bungee cords like blue snakes.

The house emptied slow and then all at once like a bowl under a sink. There was a man who wanted all of the screws in the coffee can, and came back with change not because he had overpaid but because he had underfelt. There was a young thing with a tattoo between the bones of her hand who asked straight out, “Did your mama suffer?” and when Inez said, “She did not,” the girl stroked the banister with her palm like she would a dog's head and said, “She ain’t here mad then. That’s good.” There were two sisters in matching T-shirts from a trip they had taken to Myrtle Beach six summers ago, who argued over a lamp like it was a man you could keep if you insisted. In the back room, behind a door that had been hung just a little off plumb since the house was built and would swing toward you like a scold if you did not latch it, a mirror met the floor. It met with that eager, many-voiced sound, glint spun in a fan across the linoleum. The boy who had bolted with the red sugar came out into the hall with his guilt in his hands, cupping the idea of a sorry like it could be blown on. “I didn’t,” he said, though his shoe had the glitter to prove. His mother blew her breath down through her nose and said to Inez, “I’ll pay for it,” and Inez said, “Can’t pay for what you already owe,” and bent to pick the glass up like candy off a table. A triangle dart of it caught her finger; she put her mouth to the meat and tasted iron coins.

In the front room, the walls showed where the couch had protected them from the mercy of light; the paint there was the color of a teacup’s inside and the rest was dishwater. The little shelf where Pearl had nailed up every wedding invitation and birth announcement that had the family name in it, even when the babies were white or light like paper, was bare. The nail holes were honest; she put her finger in one and felt how far in it went. The Reverend from St. Luke’s slid his hat off as he came and put it back on as he left and between those actions he took a chair that had creaked under eighteen of his sermons as he ate sweet potato pie and told Pearl he would pray for her health and he had and yet. The woman from the hairdressing place on Elm looked at the combs like they were fossils she could learn to be from. A white couple new to town—young with a serious recycling bin in the back of their station wagon—asked about the wood floors and what species of tree the banister would count itself. “The kind that don’t burn right,” Inez said, and they squinted as if she had told them a riddle and they would leave with the answer if they only put their heads together the right way.

She ate a piece of baloney she had folded into itself so the ends met, over the sink while the faucet dripped three measures of a tune. The kitchen was her favorite room only because it could be kept. The place above the stove where Pearl had once blackened a circle into the wall no one could paint over without it showing again if you looked long enough. The spot on the floor where gravy had claimed its square. The window—short, stubborn—that let exactly as much day in as would do. She wanted to keep the blue-handled knife whose handle had cracked into a star and the pot with a lip chipped like a tooth and the ceramic salt shaker shaped like a girl with a basket and the label maker that had punched D O N ' T O P E N on coffee cans that onion lived in. She wanted to keep the curtain that was not her curtain because it had, on every Saturday morning for year upon year, been brushed aside by a hand with bleach half moons under its nails. She wanted to keep the corner by the trash can where, when she was five and her father had knocked the back door in because somebody at the plant had sent him home for telling his supervisor what he could do and where, Pearl had grabbed Inez by her little Tshirt and held her between her knees waiting for the clatter to end. She wanted to keep the ugly brown linoleum with its pattern of small pebbles and green things like algae because it had been the raft they lived on when the water rose.

“What you taking?” she’d asked her brother on the phone last Friday. “I know it ain’t much you need, you with your taste.”

“Do Inez,” he’d said, a laugh in it that did not fit. “I’ll come for the records. You don’t have to mess with them. Just make a box.”

“I ain’t got to do anything but die.”

“Don’t start,” he’d said, and his voice went west, the way it did when she used the word start because it gave her more time than him to unstart. He had not come for the records. The crate of them—coated by dust and the sound of a man singing to your ankles that he’d bother with money tomorrow if you let him in tonight—sat in the back hall under the calendar that had two of June and three of December because Pearl liked the pictures and would buy five if the man on the truck was sweet.

She carried on the sale like a woman with two lives held up in her arms, refusing to let one fall to save the other. She took fives and gave back ones, made change for a man who had change for a woman who had none, held a baby while its mother reached for a vase and learned it was glued to the table by grief and furniture polish. She showed where the step sank and where the screen tore and when a girl asked if the attic had a ghost Inez said, “You think a house like this can only hold one? They up there lining the eaves, baby, cross-legged and catching crickets in their hands,” and the girl said “Ooo” and winked at her friend and left with a photograph of women in hats that had cost two dollars and a piece of her own luck for free.

At two, the air changed. The light that had been full as an old woman's arm got thin. The sky did not darken yet but it freed a smell off the river like pennies. People who knew weather made that sound between their teeth and put bills away in safer places. The man with the tape measured again. The woman with the ice-froth laugh bought the umbrella stand and called it camp and her husband, who had a throat like a rolled sock, said “Whatever you want, Lilly.” The boy who had knocked the mirror shot out the door and down the walk, his mother in a long step behind, neither of them looking back because for some folks leaving is a sickness: you look back and you’ll come down with it forever. A teenage boy with his hair in little tails counted out nickels for three books he had no business with and Inez threw in a fourth that might save him or harm him or both.

She went into the bedroom she had slept in as a girl and found it smaller than her skin. The bed frame had already gone to a woman with a king suite and a full mouth who said she’d use the frame for a daybed in the den—when there was no den in her house, just a corner a little farther from the television than the other corners. The window had been cleaned so often the panes were gossamer, and broke light into little loud squares. The wall above where her bed had been had a mark like the mile measured in a straw. Inez went to the closet where Pearl had kept her dresses and then her coats and then nothing but her smell. She put her wrong hand on the latch and felt the wood remember the right one. Inside, on the top shelf, there was the hat box with no hat in it. The hat had been buried with Pearl, perched on her hair like memory. In the box, there were receipts, folded into triangles, neatly. A pair of gloves with two fingers stained like someone had licked them. A note card with her name on it written in a hand not Pearl’s: Inez. She opened it. It said, in that hand that wrote as if the wrist had to be taught grace:

If I kept anything from you that belonged to you, forgive me. If I kept you, forgive me. If I named what you did not want named, forgive me. If I died too slow, forgive me. If I died too fast, forgive me. Let them take what they want. Keep the knives. Pearl.

She smiled with her mouth shut. She put the gloves in her pocket like she would be a woman who wore gloves. She carried the note in her palm back into the kitchen where the cast iron had left a wet kiss on the counter and her blood had marked a round where she had sucked it slow.

“You selling this?” a woman said, her voice the kind that had grown up ordering chiffon at the five-and-dime until everybody learned that only one woman in town got what she wanted on time. The woman was pointing at the Bible on the mantle. It had a spine thick as a man’s neck and a cover so dry the gold letters had flaked to little light. If there was a thing Inez had assumed she would leave by the door with the keys, it was the Bible. It had been opened to Psalms more than any other because grief forced you there and because the songs in that book were how folks learned to make noise pretty when the day asked. Pearl’s thumb had worn the 23rd down like a path between corn.

“That ain’t for sale,” Inez said. The woman shrugged as if she had only asked to sharpen her tongue.

“You got a sewing machine?” A boy, this one big enough to mean man but not old enough to know that his own question would sound like a test to someone’s womanhood. He had a face full of wanting to fix. He had a smell of heat on him that was not unpleasant. “My girl make things,” he said. “She got a machine we borrowed from her aunt but the pedal—”

“In the den,” Inez said, before she could decide whether it was called any of the things people called that room. “You lift it by the base, don’t take the arm. The cabinet got drawers that always stick first two times but then behave. It’ll bite if you let it. Thirty.”

He smiled with all his gums and gave her thirty-one. “Lucky,” he said.

“Watch your luck,” she said. “It’ll slide right out your mouth if you open it too wide.”

He laughed like he’d been smacked sweet. He moved the machine like a dance partner, careful of its feet.

By three the heat broke. It did not drop so much as relent—released its teeth one by one until the porch boards sighed like grandmothers rung out. Rain rolled in with the smell of sweet rot and metal. It hit the porch hard and the roof harder. People hesitated in the door as though they were at the lip of a place that would change them. “We’ll come back,” they said, but some of them did and some of them found another house on the list has a sale, and some of them went home to mop around their mother’s tub. Inez took the quilts off the table—the ones she had not sent already to Cousin Myra who ran her hands like a fortune teller over old squares and said, “They talk, Lovie, they talk. You sleep under purple and you dream in verbs”—and folded them into the tin basket that had held feed when the only thing that ate garden was rabbits.

“You ought not leave that trunk open like that,” she heard from the doorway, and when she turned there was Pearl, or at least there were the dense eyes of her, the corner of her mouth that would rise a fraction for a joke and then refuse to follow through. “Rain on cedar.”

“It ain’t mine,” she said aloud before she caught that she had said it.

“Nothing in this house ever been,” said the sweat that ran down into the valley of her back.

“I got your letters,” Inez whispered, to nobody and to the wall and to the space where a woman would stand if she were made of steam.

Pearl did not come in. She would never traipse water onto her own floor, not even in the imaginations of her child. “They don’t belong to you,” the voice said that was all Inez and all not. “I kept them to hold what they held. It stayed.”

“You could have told me.”

“Girl. You always think telling is relief. Sometimes telling is a whole other room you got to clean.” The rain pinched then into needles the size of rice. “My mother had a girl before me she didn’t keep long. I had a girl after you I didn’t keep long. There some things if you say them you set them free to make their own noise. I kept my noises inside. Didn’t trust the neighborhood.”

“You shoulda—”

“Don’t ‘should’ me, child. You fifty years old and still believe in the right way like it’s a recipe. The right way that didn’t keep your knees from ash, the right way that didn’t stop you with that boy with the key and the gold tooth and the bicycle who could talk you into spoiling even your Sunday skirt. I did how I did so you could do how you do. Now hush. Don’t let that woman walk out with my good can opener. She gon’ puncture her own heart with that thing.”

Inez laughed then, ungentle. There was no woman with a can opener in her hand; there had not been one all day. Still, she went to the drawer: the can opener sat like permission in the shadow behind rubber bands. She pulled it to the light and set it on the counter on top of the note that said Keep the knives. She heard, unmistakable though the house was emptying of ninety years of talk, the click of Pearl’s tongue she used on babies and old men.

When the rain let go, the light was new—clean as if somebody had mopped the air. People returned in smaller numbers and with less money; hands were cold and not wanting to be parted from bills that might buy supper if this weather got bad and the garden did not forgive. Inez reduced what she did not care about and stiffened what she did. “Twenty-five for the table? Girl, you going to bring folks into your house to feed they feet? This table got names carved into the undersides of it like a church pew. It remember who slammed a fist and who pretended they cared about somebody else’s knife scraping.” “All right, twenty-two, you poisonous woman.” “Fifteen for all the forks?” “You planning on losing your friends?” “Can I use your bathroom?” A little girl’s knee pressed together. “You want me to follow you? Have you wash your hands? Ain’t nothing in there to admire but the tub that’s chipped and the mirror that ain’t there.” “We just moved here.” “From where?” “From Cincinnati.” “Bring that city tongue back here if you think you that.” “Our landlord said Miss Pearl was a firecracker.” “She was a fuse.” “I mean.”

By four the rooms were nearly hollow. She had been a child of this house and now she was the parent of its leaving. The only things that remained were the light shapes on the walls and the floor where furniture had protected paint and boards from the adventure of sun. Those shapes made ghosts of objects. There was also, in the center of the room where the piano had once been before it had to be taken out the window because angels had been small when they put it in and too lazy to help later, a square of floorboard that had never matched the others. Her father had replaced it in '77 when he’d cut a hole to check on what he called the belly of the beast. He had found mice droppings and the bone of a chicken somebody had got lazy about carrying out. He had also found, down there between the joists, a spoon Pearl swore had belonged to her mother and that had been awaiting rescue since 1952. “Things you think lost just biding their time,” she had said, and for three days had been easy as pie to love.

A boy came to the door with rain in the band of his baseball cap. He looked twelve but had the shameless air of ten. He held in his palm the thin spoon, bone-handled, with a notch near the bowl where somebody’s tooth had bit and held. “This fell out from under,” he said. “I was looking. I didn’t take nothing I didn’t find. You can keep it.”

She looked at him, and his face did not flinch. He was not sorry and he was not proud. He was only having a day and this was a piece of it. “You can keep it,” she said. “Better you eat with something got a bite than with something slick.”

He grinned like he knew a riddle’s answer and ran down the steps like running was the thing saving his life over and over.

There was the Bible. There were two folding chairs. There were the curtain rods. There was the photograph still turned to the wall long no one knew without looking that it was a man in a suit with a crease so right it cut, and his shoes had been so shined they had caught the picture of the clouds the day it was taken. There were dust dancers in every corner because the rain had risen them. There was the sound of the old floor complaining that nobody had stepped on it in fifteen minutes. There was Mrs. Battles, her breath like onions and mercy. “You look skinny,” she said, eyes the kind that could lay a quilt.

“I ain’t,” Inez said. “I’m only emptied. That ain’t the same thing.”

“You got your mother’s coin-purse mouth,” Mrs. Battles said, touching it with her fingertip. “You got her ankles, poor thing. You gon’ fall if you lean the wrong way. I got that Tupperware set I—can I?” She did not say she had coveted it for years, had thought of it in her kitchen when she looked around for something to say you sorry with and found nothing. She had not stolen it on a day Pearl was at work and the back screen latch was half closed because that would have required lying and the truth was better for this woman because then she could walk back and forth on it like a plank. “Two dollars,” Inez said. “For the one with the lid don’t fit.”

“Dollar fifty.” She laid the money down in a way that was a handshake and not.

When the last truck coughed and moved on with a dresser with drawers that tended to drift left if you let them, when the two sisters had at last agreed on who would keep the lamp with the ceramic greyhound and had taken it away with them like the one thing that might keep them sisters, when Mr. Jenkins from the block over had come not to buy but to sit and say, “She kept me when my mother got that fever and I peed the bed and she put me outside with a blanket and said, the night air like a cure, boy, and I ain’t took sick none after,” and had left with a candy dish and the last of Pearl’s peppermints in his pocket because they were fused and would not fall out, when all that was over and there was a hush in the halls that had never happened when she had been a girl because somebody had always been slapping a rug or shouting or singing or coughing up sorrow, then Inez shut her eyes and listened. She heard the house swallow and try not to. She heard the ghost of the couch give up its springs. She heard, a long way off, a train practicing grief. She heard Pearl say, “Close the windows against the night,” and she went and did.

There remained the letters against her stomach. She took them out now and put them on the counter where flour had been and good thyme. She did not cut the belt back on. The leather had stuck to her a little and she had three red marks there that would purple by morning. She took the one from 1971 and read of a baby boy with eyes like a man who had not stayed and how to name him—if to name him after a father or after a word. She read of Cleveland winters and of a laundromat where a woman folded other people’s shirts and made small prayers for the men whose arms would go through. She read of the riot that had not made it to the white street and what fire did when it was full and what it did when it was hungry and how sorrow could be used like a map. She read of a girl who had a body for ruin and a head for rescue and how the older woman wanted both for her and would choose neither. She read, finally, a last note with a date that made her think the paper had been written yesterday in a day not yet come:

I knew you would not remember me the way I wanted. You were built with a spine I did not recognize because mine had been made a different day. I wanted you to come to my room when I did not come to yours because I believed myself the moon. I was not. I was a stain on a pillowcase that no one got out. Do not make me a woman I was not. Do not make yourself the child I was not built to love. Use what I sent and not me. E.

She held the belt then and had to sit down or fall down, it was not clear which. She sat on the floor where the piano had set its long feet. She put her fingers in the places dust did not recognize yet. She thought of her own boy—the almost who had not made it to breath; the clench and the loosen; the small sound her body had made in a room with tile; the way the nurse had said The tissue, and Inez had said His name is not tissue and the nurse had looked at her like you look at a child reaching for a hot handle. She thought of the man with the gold tooth and the bike and how his jacket had smelled like rain and oil and how he had made her feel like a house you could run through from front to back with all your clothes off; how he had been, for a day, the kind of human that insists on using today up and leaves tomorrow to hang itself. She thought of all the knives and how Pearl had told her to keep them.

She went to the stove. The pilot light was out; she had not lit it since. She turned the knob and clicked and clicked until the blue holiday came up and licked the air. She brought a metal bowl and put the letters in it like bread. She did not have any holy thing to say. “Go on,” she said, as if to a mule. And then, “I’m sorry,” because that was for her and for them. The paper caught as if glad—or reluctant in a way that felt like a last flirt. It burned ugly, with smoke that made the kitchen into a forest people had forgotten. She held the bowl with a towel, because the heat had no manners. She watched the words go to shape and then no shape. She flinched once when a corner leapt like a grasshopper and went out and left a black lace against the side of the bowl that was beautiful if you did not know what letter it had been.

“What you doing, Nessa?” That was Pearl again, and she did not have the strength to tell herself it was not.

“I don’t know,” she said. “Putting them where they been.”

“You always made up rituals like a gypsy,” Pearl said. “You born that way, I swear. Don’t burn my house. Don’t burn my curtains. You better not ruin that pot.”

“I ain’t burning down nothing but what already on fire.”

That seemed to suit or not. The burning finished sooner than she had thought; the ash was light, like satisfaction. She scraped it into a jar that had once held jam that had been sweeter than sense. She put the lid on. She did not know yet whether she would throw it in the river or keep it on her dresser in Cincinnati or sprinkle it in the yard in a line as if she were saving a door.

She moved through the rooms with a towel under her arm the way Pearl had taught her to move—never let your hands come naked to work. She wiped the last rings from the table because if she were going to let it go she would let it go with a clean face. She straightened the picture turned to the wall and saw again the man and his crease and the sky he had stolen and she turned it back because he had always made her feel watched. She went to the bathroom and washed her hands. The mirror was a mouth now with a chipped tooth. She saw her face broken into pieces around the place where the glass had gone. In each separate bit she did not look new. She looked like herself in the ways she had not chosen. She reached up and took down the little bottle of Oil of Olay and rubbed a sign of water into her cheeks, the way she had seen Pearl do when she thought nobody looked.

When the key came out of her pocket finally and she put it into the lock, the house breathed. When she turned it, it balked and then gave. She felt the tumblers decide. She withdrew and the door was just a door. She stepped down the two front steps, remembering all the ways they had been used—platform, judgment seat, runway, rest. She crossed the yard that had been a patch once upon a time when her father thought they would make cucumbers and make money and had ended up with cucumbers and a little boy from next door who would not go home unless they gave him some. She went to the maple whose roots pushed up the sidewalk near where the mail carrier tripped every Christmas and cursed. She set the jar under the lip of the root. She did not bury it. She did not leave it out. She hooked it with the edge of her shoe until it found a place that did not look like a place. She stood and the afternoon had turned into the kind of evening that makes women set peas snap-snap on their knees and think thoughts they would not tell their daughters even if asked sweet.

“You going to be all right?” Mrs. Battles had come to her own porch, a glass like hope in her hand.

“I ain’t ever,” Inez said, and then, “Yes.”

“You need anything, you call me. I’ll come carry you to the store if your car act ugly. I got a poundcake. I ain’t like your mama but I got a poundcake.”

“I know,” Inez said. “I’ll come by and taste both.”

The street was itself—menina with a baby on her hip; a porch radio whispering baseball; two little boys playing war with sticks and one of them refused to die properly and the other said, You dead and the first one said, You can’t kill me. A dog too little to matter barked his body until he was dizzy and then sat and looked content as salvation. Somewhere somebody had opened a jar of pickles and the smell had traveled like news. The clouds over the town were comforters you did not have to earn. A train blew long and then twice and then once, which meant something to somebody. Inez took her purse. She went to her car, put the cash tin under the seat and then lifted it, finally, to the front where a woman reached for it without quite asking. She put the keys to the house next to her wallet and felt her pants pockets empty and full.

She got in. The seat had remembered her from earlier and warmed itself. She put her hands on the wheel and then off, like testing a person’s forehead. She looked up at the house like you look at a child you have to leave with a man you love even when you should not. The porch gave no sign. The curtains did not part. The door did not open and call out, You forgot your—. It sat there the way a thing that has been so much must sit to not become a mouth always open.

She started the car. The radio played a man who sounded like he was singing to his mama and to a woman at the same time and did not mind mixing them up. She turned it down because she wanted to hear the engine, and it told her Yes and Not yet and I can if you will. She pulled away slow, as if the earth had softened and she might leave ruts that would be used by deer. She drove to the end of the street and then turned because you had to. At the corner, a little girl in a red T-shirt looked at her hard and then opened both hands as if to show her there was nothing in them to take and nothing to give.

On the way out of the neighborhood there was a pile of things by the curb that had not been there in the morning—somebody else had made a decision and set it in the yard to argue with. A chair whose seat had been sat down to strings. A broken vacuum, cord tied neat as a moral. A shoebox full of wire. Inez swallowed and did not cry because it would only make her nose thick and the car had no Kleenex. She put her left turn signal on and nobody cared. She understood that was a kind of mercy.

She would go to the motel that smelled like other people’s deals and cry if she wanted to cry and then sleep hard and wake up with her hair like a crown she did not deserve and drive her car through the morning to her own place where the plant on the sill would have turned itself toward the reading chair because it was vain and believed in speeches.

The house behind her made no complaint loud enough for the radio to hear. It had let itself be thinned and was both lighter and lonelier. It had learned something about breath, about air moving in new corridors, about how a wall is not a person and a floor cannot keep secrets if there is nobody standing on it. It was not all right but it was done. The people who had walked through it, who had told it about themselves without meaning to, had left their particular air which would go stale by midnight and vanish by noon. The room where the piano had been would feel like a lost tooth with a tongue always wanting. The socket of the mirror would hold only a bit of her face or none at all, depending on the sun. On the mantle the Bible would sit and think of storms that had knocked it to the floor and of hands that had opened it to the middle because they did not know where else to begin.

The jar under the maple would not be found by a child eating dirt or by a raccoon with a scientist's mind. It would sweat when the heat bore down and cool when the ground remembered winter. Its lid would rust but not at once. If anyone could hear through glass, they would hear less and less. The belt marks would fade on her skin. The skillet would be put on a new stove and, the first Sunday there, would smoke the way skill does when you forget it is not just in your hands.

“What I got to do now,” she heard herself say at the light, to nobody, to the man next to her in the truck with the bungee cord snakes, to the God who had not been invited into the sale but had come anyway and bought nothing, “What I got to do now is go home.”

And then, more quiet, under the radio, under the June with its hard fruit:

“What I got to do now is keep.”
