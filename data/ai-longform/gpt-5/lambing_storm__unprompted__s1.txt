By half past five the shed had a vapor that turned the light into soft, fogged moons, and the smell of lanolin and iodine reached even into the corner where the bales were stacked. The wind heaved at the tin walls in long, shouldering blows. Snow made a tight rasping under the door, and every so often a handful of ice rattled down the roof sheets and slid off in a rush. The ewes were restless, shifting and huffing, the low conversational mutter of their rumen rolling in them like the creek under snow.

Ruth ran her hand along the spine of a ewe that had sequestered herself against the panel at the far end. The ewe's wool was damp at the flanks, tacky from her own attempts at licking the lamb not yet born. The bag had long since dropped and her backside bloomed red and wet in the light. Every time the wind shouldered the walls, the ewe ceased and set her jaw, holding the pain quiet. Then she would go back to her small insistent circles, choosing and unchoosing the same square foot of straw.

Eli came in with a skein of snow down his shoulders, his cap pulled low and already rimed at the edge. He had Sam’s old jacket on, the elbows polished by years and going to ravel at the cuffs. On him it hung looser than it had even at Christmas, and she had to check the thought that wished him smaller, something that would fit that jacket yet. He shook off, careful of the pens, and brought the steam of the outside with him. In his mouth was a hang of twine he’d been using to mend the broken board by the loading chute.

"It’s starting for real now," he said, without turning his head. He was working something with his thumbnail, the way Sam had when he came in from the cold. "Visibility’s nothing. The radio says ten inches by nightfall and more overnight."

Ruth nodded like the weather report had any say in their shed. "We'll keep it to the straw," she said. "We prepared."

He looked toward the outlets along the post where they’d wired the heat lamps. The red discs glowed like moons at noon. He raised his hands and warmed them over the one above the jug with twins who’d come at dawn. Those two were neat and bright-eyed now, their navels stained orange and their sides puffed out where milk had settled. They took turns knocking at their mother’s udder, and every time they butted, the ewes in the neighboring jugs turned their heads as if to mark the sound.

"Generator?" Ruth asked. She said it lightly, but the word had gone heavier these weeks, put on the same weight as all the others that had once belonged to Sam and now had become hers by the state of his absence. Grease trap. Blower. Jumper cables. "You checked everything again?"

Eli tapped the heel of one glove against the post. "Changed the oil last week. It ran. I know where you put the plug wrench this time. Fuel's full. If the line ices we can—"

The lights hiccuped then, a blip like a blink in a migraine, and a ripple went through the shed as the ewes readied themselves, suddenly from animals in a room lit by certainty to animals in a cave. They froze, nostrils widening. The light steadied. Ruth found she was breathing shallow.

"Or," Eli finished, like a joke deferred, "maybe we don’t even need it."

The ewe beside her stopped circling and took the kind of breath that at once went to her spine and her shoulders. She stretched her neck and made a sound low and wet and private. Ruth squatted. She kept her hair in a braid that shifted forward and fell against her chest like a rope. It had been half gray for years in the way that hadn’t particularly meant anything. This year it was more than half and had started to feel like a story people might notice.

She grabbed the small bowl of iodine and set it next to her in the straw, got the towel ready, the snare. She’d told herself over and over since the first ewe had started pawing straw three days ago that the motions would be there even if the voice wasn’t. She could do the work even if nobody said run the snare under the chin, babe, not over, in a soft voice that made the ewes' ears flick forward like they could understand the English. Bodies knew. Fingers had their own kinds of recall.

The ewe’s water bag showed as a pale grape, then went to riddled wet cellophane as she pushed and it slid out and broke. Steam rose. Ruth reached, gloved, and felt gently between pulses. She always, always waited for the next contraction, the next voluntary, before she asked more of them. Sam had been particular about that. "Don’t make work of what the body can do itself," he’d said once, a dozen Aprils ago, when she’d been eager and reached too soon. "Your job’s to be there for the places the body can’t remember." He’d laughed when he said it like he’d said something bigger than ewes.

There it was—two small hooves, no bigger than two of her fingers, and the smooth velvet of a nose riding on them. Normal. The mouth opened and closed when she rubbed the face through the sac. She broke the sac and squared the lamb’s shoulders. At the next contraction she pulled a little, down on the arc of the pelvis, then out, and the lamb came slick into her palms, the world’s softest creature, already mostly trying to be in the world.

"Hey, baby," Ruth said, like she was greeting a neighbor across a fence, and set it on the straw. She stripped the membranes from its head, wiped quickly once, twice, took the suction bulb and drew the fluid out of the nostrils and mouth. The ewe, mad with a focus that blotted out wind and light and pain, turned and began to lick as if scoring wood, those rough, insistent licks that put a lamb to rights, that told some part of the lamb’s cells the world had arrived and it had to turn itself on.

Eli crouched on the other side, his glove hovering, his mouth open, the way it always was at the first one of a long night, as if surprise could survive years of expectation. "You going to have another, Mama?" he asked the ewe, almost under his breath, already reaching for the marker spray, blue, number nine on the ewe’s flank and nine on the lamb’s hip. He did not say Mama like some people did at the sale barn. He said it like they were admitting something not untrue: that this one was allowed a title. He scribbled the nine and it smeared. His hands had always been too rough for neat numbers. He blew on his fingers. The air bit even in the shed. "She thick?"

Ruth lifted the ewe’s tail again, quick glance, saw the second hoof tucked where it should be. The ewe was already carrying the afterpains like small weather in her flanks. "Could be," she said. She stood and set the single onto his sternum, rubbed hard, then tickled the inside of his nostril with a straw to get that sneeze that felt like a gate. The lamb sneezed, shook, blinked into existence. "We’ll give her a minute."

A sound then, beyond even the wind—an electrical hum’s absence, an end-of-sentence. The lights dropped away, and the red glow winked into nothing. Ruth had been built for a kind of startle that could move without much heart movement, but her breath caught inside her as if she’d been underwater and was suddenly given another kind of water.

"Oh," Eli said, and was already standing. He moved with that suddenness that was still so much boy, so much of the child who had once run at a word, always running, never just walking toward something when he thought he could get there sooner. He grabbed the headlamp from the post and flicked it on. Its circle found them, mild, wavering as he adjusted the strap around his cap. "I’ll go," he said. "We got it. I’ll go."

Ruth wanted to grab the edge of his jacket, the seam of the sleeve, to say wait, two, three minutes, the road’s gone, the snow’s wrong, stay here where I can put my hands on you. But she swallowed that like a mother swallows out ahead of her words so that they don’t make children cowardly. "Get the primer lever," she said instead. "If it doesn’t catch on the first pull, don’t conk your shoulder out. Close the chute when you go out or Lark’ll be in with the ewes and we’ll have to kill him."

Eli grinned without his eyes. "You love Lark."

"I do," she said as the dog, on cue, pranced up and sniffed at the pen, held back by memory of being shamed away from new lambs. "Go."

The wind pushed him into the yard and the door banged, then settled on its latch. The shed exhaled into its new state. The ewes made low calls. The ones in labor turned their heads back, the private sound they made the same in any light. The red lamps were dead but the heat they’d made would ride the bodies for a while. The kerosene lantern hung by the door had dust on it like a minor sin. Ruth wiped the glass with a corner of her shirt and lit it. It coughed and then rose into a small, defiant mecca. She set it by the pens. Their shadows went taller and wavered on the sheets of tin.

She crouched again by the new lamb. He was already reaching his head, a maggot-white thing with ears still folded like leaves. The ewe’s tongue rasped him. She was working up along the spine with such attention that Ruth felt for a moment like a spy watching a woman dress her firstborn. There was a time when that had felt obscene, like stealing. By now there was nothing on that side of it. She had attended ewes like a midwife attends women who have no shame left for flesh.

She tested the ewe’s bag with two fingers, pressed lightly up toward the udder, saw the yellow bead of colostrum collect at the nipple. At the onset of the storm she had taken extra pint jars into the house and set them by the stove, defrosted the store they kept in the chest freezer for hard nights. The chest itself was quiet now. Hale of any discovery would be made quieter in a few hours when the heat bled into the snow. In the kitchen, six pints of the gold stuff waited, sweating. She’d set a towel over them against Lark’s curiosity in case the dog broke his promise to be good in the house.

The ewe tensed and looked back and bleated in that way that uses the lamb’s name before the lamb has a name. Her sides fluttered. The second sac showed and tore. This one was smaller. Ruth set her fingers in just to turn the new lamb's head and even him out. He slid easier than the first, came cold like a cold peach from the icebox. She rubbed him against her jeans, dipped his cord into the iodine, felt his pulse beat the orange brown up the umbilicus as she kept the dipper against the belly. The second's lungs were slow to declare. She rubbed his ribs, hard with the towel. After a long count—one-one-thousand, two-one-thousand—he opened his mouth and whole minutes went into the noise he made. The ewe dove for him, mothered them both as if she could do it with only the part of her body that was her tongue.

"Good girl," Ruth said. She reached to mark them with the spray. Nine and nine. "You got this, you just got this." She stood and rolled her shoulders. The habit of Billy running a heater through the shed in a past storm presented itself to her memory like a photograph. They had warmed lambs by the woodstove in that old storm, sat them in boxes with towels underfoot while she checked math homework and Sam built a new handle for the manure fork because it had split. She had laughed then at the ridiculousness of lambs in shoe boxes, modern life shrunk to what was needed most. She did not laugh now. She did not cry either.

The door moved and a gust brought stinging snow with it so suddenly the ewes penned closest to it stomped and huffed as if stung. Eli backed through, swearing without words, a series of hisses and exhalations. He knocked his shoulder into the post. His headlamp had gone a full white with the snow and made a glare like a holy thing, useless.

"It won’t stay lit," he said, not with an apology and not accusing. That was new this spring, the way he spoke without needing to be forgiven. "It turns over and dies. It’s the carb. Or the line or something I can’t see with ice doing rain on your face."

"Lamps," Ruth said. "The camp stove?"

"House is the fridge now." He took off his cap and knocked it against his thigh. Snow skated down and made tiny pings on his boots. He stood in the light of the lantern and went human again, his face freed from the headlamp’s false sun. There was a raw ring on his forehead where the strap had pressed.

"We're okay," he said, too quickly and then slower. "We got the propane. I can bring the one-Bar heater from the shop. It smells like a tire fire but it’s heat. Colostrum’s out. We got plenty. We just—"

"Keep them coming," Ruth said. "Keep the little ones dry and nursing."

They moved through the pens. The lambing jugs had been built from old pallets and cattle panels wired to T-posts, covered blanketed by chaff and in some cases an old coat hung to keep a draft from a particular corner. Sam had always refused to build forever things in a lambing shed. "You want it loose," he’d say, drawing an invisible line with his palm by his thigh. "You want to be able to change your mind." Ruth had thought sometimes that he had said that for something more than pens, for marriage, for life, and she’d laughed and called him a philosopher for it. "You married me for my metaphors," he’d said, and kissed the soft part under her jaw, lit by a red lamp that had made the whole thing as if it were happening under a stopped sun.

"She’s off," Eli said, pointing with his chin to a ewe that had shrunk herself against the far board and made her eyes small and mean. Her lamb—no, two, if you counted the one whose feet were out and not yet on the straw—lay already on his side, chest too quick, head bobbing.

"Get your sleeves," Ruth said. They had taken to slinging OB sleeves on a wire by the sink weeks ago. She could get them blind. Even in the dark the place had made itself familiar to her beyond words. She could walk to any post and tell he was not there because of the particular shape of emptiness that lay at the base of it. She had closed her hand, more than once, around nothing in the air and been guilty afterward for reaching.

Eli wrestled his arm into the long plastic and slid his hand. He made a moue face he thought she couldn’t see in the low light. "Go easy," she said. "What do you feel?"

"One out, one back," he said. His hand was covered and she saw the habit of gentleness that had been given to him by no one but had come as a graft from somewhere she did not know in her own body. "This one’s nose is back. I got a knee. I don’t like that."

"Push him back," she said. "You’re just a part of the wall. Send him home a little until you can get the nose." She caught the ewe by the halter and eased her up to a corner so that Eli’s arm would get purchase. The ewe fought once, then sort of settled, her eyes gone deep and old. In the hard light of the lantern one pink membrane looked the color of meat and Ruth’s stomach did a roll. She was always prepared and she was always somehow surprised by the commonness of it, the dailiness of flesh. Eli worked his hand inside, one thumb on the lamb’s mouth like Sam had taught and like she’d shown him again last week: you curl your thumb to a hook and lay the pad in the V of the jaw. You don’t break teeth on a newborn.

"Okay," he said, breath like cold laid against the air. "I got him. He’s small. His head is there. Okay—" He pulled on a contraction like he’d been told to, making himself stubborn in the way that wasn’t force, and the lamb slid free and the ewe looked at him over her shoulder as if he’d done something unnatural that she would forgive only because forgiveness came with the afterbirth.

"Good," Ruth said. She had the towel ready and cleared the face. The lamb was not so much a color as some pale between. He made no noise at first. She ran the straw end hard inside his nose. Eli frowned with his whole face and beat the chest with the flat of his hand the way he had watched on some video at the fair. "Lower," Ruth said. "The lungs there." She took the suction bulb and drew, thin and long. The lamb choked, sputtered, and then made his sound. They both blew out their held breath at the same time, and the held breath left the way something heavy leaves a bed and relief comes with it.

"We’re okay," Eli said again, but he didn’t say it for her but to anchor it in himself somehow, as if saying it made it truer.

They wore their gloves until their fingers were numb and then took them off and worked bare, and then put them on again as the wet fought the cold like a shivering in their very bones. The blizzard did what blizzards do: it made time collapse and stretch. They named edgeless spans by events like ships going by in the night. A ewe in pen three raised her lip in the Flehmen response at the smell of birth. The old bottle lamb from the year before, Missy—named by a neighbor child before Ruth could do anything about it—pressed her face against the panel and baaa’d into Ruth’s face any time Ruth bent to tie fresh twine. "Go on, nosy," Ruth said, and pushed her big awkward head aside. When Ruth broke the crust of a water hole with a boot heel, a wet splashed onto her sock inside her boot and made an ache at her knuckle bones.

Lark did as he was told for thirty minutes and then broke faith, slid under a panel when Eli had his head in a ewe and came into the pen to nosily sniff a lamb’s wet ear. Ruth took him by the collar and set his chin between his shoulders with two fingers, the way Sam did, and said his name once in a voice that sounded to herself like somebody else’s. The dog cried almost softly to go back to doing the job he wanted like any creature, to arrange the moving of things into a shape that made a kind of sense. She let him out, and he tore around the yard in circles so that his joy had a place to burn out of him.

"Eat something," Ruth told Eli, handing him the thermos. The coffee had gone lukewarm but there was sugar in it like always. He took a long pull and then made a face because he had forgotten for a second that there was sugar in it. She had never known if he actually didn’t like sugar in coffee or if he felt he oughtn’t because Sam had always drunk it bitter as sin, black on the tongue, scalding and hot enough to knock a man over. He passed the cup back and did not comment. She folded a cracker into her mouth and let it soften and be more salt than food.

There was one that went wrong, close and then not. It could not be said better than that: wrong. The ewe was small and had more look of a goat in the face, and inside her was a lamb too big by half, both legs back and the head coming, and she had already strained so hard that her eyes had little bloody freckles at the rim. Eli tried first and then she took the place and slid her arm in. Everything in there was close and slick and refusing them. She waited through contraction after contraction so that what she was doing and what the ewe was doing could be folded into the same act. "Now," she said, and she pushed his skull back with a pressure like a prayer because all she had was that thumb and her forearm. The lamb slid away a little and she found a fetlock with two fingers, no more. That was enough to make a world. Once you had that you could build something.

"Got you," she said. "Light you are. You’re getting lighter."

To get the second leg, she had to go in up to her shoulder, the cold like fire against the part of her arm wet from sleeve. The earth angled sideways for a moment with the caving of the tin when the wind took a new tack. She thought, of all things, of Sam’s hand on the back of her neck once at the kitchen sink when she had been crying for a thing that was small—milk spilled, bread burned—and how his hand had fit there then and how now inside this animal there was that same space where her body knew to set itself and hold. She was gentle with her fingernails and then she was suddenly not because time was a thing with teeth. She hooked the second leg and the lamb slid amid the rush of fluid and smell and deep sound. It was a big male, broad in the face, his tongue lolling like he was a dog at the end of a run. He was shock white where enough wool had come that it would color later, and his ears were bloodied at the edge from the narrow he’d been. She worked the jaw and cleaned him and set a tube and sent four ounces of colostrum into the stomach where it could do the trick that it did, give a thing born into this world the ability to hold it off with his own body. The ewe was exhausted and looked at them like enemies.

"She’ll claim?" Eli asked, already halfway to making a pen for her with the bottle-lamb Missy’s old board. He could not do a thing without trying to do the other half of three other things.

"We’ll see," Ruth said. "Hobble her if she won’t. Don’t let him freeze. Put him down my shirt if you have to. I don’t care if you’ve got hair on your chest now."

"I do not," he said, automatic teenage shock, and then blushed so deep it was a thing to warm one in winter. Ruth, who knew full well and did not, for that knowledge, need to think a second further on it, grinned into the lantern glass and caught a reflection there of a woman who had been younger than this, who had been pregnant and held a lamb to her chest under her jacket in another storm and had not, even then, had anything like the words to tell the man beside her what it had meant that his hands had been there in that other winter for their first one.

The heater Eli brought stank like burning rubber and sent a worm of warmth into the first ten feet of air. Ruth hated the way her eyes watered and then loved that she could work and not feel her knuckles throb. The storm hiked itself up another notch toward midnight. The pasture was a plate turned to the wind. Their windbreak rows had gone to shadows and the evergreen needle tips harbored little clots of ice that clicked and dropped. Somewhere, Ruth assumed, the road had given up even pretending to exist. The plow guy would not call until morning because he was no fool. They were on their island now. When she went to the door to empty a bucket quick so that the urine did not slick their footing, she heard a hollow space under the snow, the way it holds air and promises a sink. She knew already where she would step in six hours and how the snow would give and make her curse aloud and no one would hear it but the dog. The last Caldwells who had had this place had, in legend, lost a sheep in a drift for two days and found her again like a relic. Her father-in-law had lived on that story for twenty winters, never mind that his own fences were a different kind and his weather a different brand.

Sometime after two the ewe in the end under the loose board began a noise that was only sometimes a bleat. It came out of her like a human cry pulled tight and thin. Eli’s head came up like a hunted thing's. "That’s not," he said. "That’s not right."

"No," Ruth said. The hair on her arms prickled at the sound. It had only a little of fear in it and all the rest desperation. She had heard it twice before in her life and been given a lesson in humility after. She went down the slab with the lantern and found that it wasn’t pain. It was anger. The ewe would not have her lamb and made that fierce complaint about it to God and the walls. The lamb she had had—small, delicate, a ewe-lamb with her face—stood and wagged her tail and cried to a mother who would not bend to her. Rejection. In another time they might have turned their back on that truth and called it something else. Tonight it had to be what it was.

Eli had the shepherd’s crook from the post and came into the pen to put the crook over the ewe’s neck and hold her still. "Get her back foot," Ruth said. "Just a minute." She moved the ewe into the corner, and the ewe kicked like she had an enemy. They tied a halter. They hobbled a back leg. They held her and pressed her into the corner of the world so that the world would be smaller than a refusal. Ruth stripped the ewe and milked the warm spray into a cup, her hands working that motion that was older than thought. Eli did not look away, did not say they should pour for a bottle because it would be easier; he had learned already what easier ran ahead of and she loved him for it in the same breath she cursed that he had learned it.

Ruth knelt on her mudded knees. She found in a pocket the small plastic feeding tube, yellowed now, the printed ounce lines rubbed by the years. She slid it into the lamb’s mouth, past the point where a cough would mean wrong way, down to a transmission that did not involve tongues or nipples but did involve life. The ewe stood against the wall and made her private hate for this bend of the universe. Ruth sent the ounces down, slow, measured, the way Sam had liked to narrate, "There he goes: four. Six. Eight. A little more, he’s got it. Stop. Pull like a string. There, see? Easy," and she had never forgiven him for being kind to even the tube and now could not help imitating the cadence of that old narration in her head while she watched her son’s hand on the halter, his knuckles gone white.

They kept them penned together. They left hay within nose of the ewe so she could choose to lower her head toward the lamb as if by accident. In an hour the ewe was less an enemy and more a creature surprised by a fact she had wanted to avoid. In two, the lamb walked under her belly and was rejected again but with less venom. Some of Ruth’s strength—too much of it—went to that pair, because she could not stand the thought of that ewe-lamb learning the world without what the world should offer from its first minute. It was her own weakness, and she knew it. The red lamps dark and the snow in the door mouth made weaknesses show.

"You should sleep," Eli said without derision. "This one is slow. I’ll sit here."

"And the others?" she asked.

"They're quiet," he said. "For now. You sleep in the corner. I’ll wake you if we got another Breech."

"Breach."

He shrugged. "It’s a breach of the peace either way." He gave her his still-warm place on the overturned tub and she looked at him and did not say the thing that had bloomed in her mouth, which was that he was making jokes that were good now, he was making a little light. She folded her coat under her head and performed the act of closing her eyes. Her body ached. The heel of her left hand had a bruise where a hoof had kicked. The tendon in her right groin sang like a wire drawn tight. When she let go of her jaw at last a dampness collected inside her mouth and made a taste of iron. She slept for no more than nine minutes, by the marks of the lantern on the post, and in that nine minutes dreamed not of Sam but of string—of the particular blue poly twine that came around hay bales and stayed on shoes or fences until they turned to hair. In the dream the string had gone around the whole world and she had unspooled one end and could not get it to do anything but knot on itself.

Eli’s boot nudged her. "Hey," he said, and his "hey" had a tremor in it that she recognized from when he had been six and found a fledgling on the porch that had fallen from the nest and he had been dismantled by the fact of it not flying. "We got one outside."

She was a body before she was a person. She was up, shaking straw from her coat, then catching the lantern. Something in the door was thudding in a steady interval. Snow had slipped under the door again and made a thin slid place. Eli had his own lamp on again and was already beyond the light with Lark at heel.

"How?" she said stupidly to no one. "How outside?"

"Gate," he said. "Loose board. I wired it but she must have pushed. She’s there—" He gestured into the white which was not white but the knowledge of how to read what white made of shapes. The ewe had turned the wrong way on her own sense, gone to the wind’s lee side of the shed. She was a lump under an overhang where the roof threw a drift down all at once and then again and again until what had been a slope became a wall. The lamb’s feet were already out of her and frozen to a pale glass. The ewe made a sound like she had swallowed something sharp and it had caught in her voice.

Ruth waded thigh-deep and felt the weight of the snow collapse into her boot tops, the wicked sear of it at the ankle bone. "No," she said to the ewe, not as if the ewe was at fault but as if the world were, but she said it to the ewe because the ewe was there and the world was not. Eli was ahead of her, his boy’s body in its long bones moving through drift like it had been imagined to do this and this only, and he caught the ewe by the wool and yanked. The ewe came not willingly but because something in the yanking was finally easier than the way she had made things be.

They dragged her by the armpits, as it were, and pulled her into the back stall where the heater still breathed and the bodies of other ewes made a weather different than any outside. She went straight onto her knees, rolled to her side, and labored without dignity. The lamb’s little black hooves were white with rime. Ruth pulled her tool from her pocket and unhooked the legs, brought them slick. The lamb slid but was heavy as if frost had weight. He came into her hands and did not move as if all the parts of moving had gone into the snow and she had collected only the rest. He was black, or would be. He had a white star on his forehead that would have been a thing to joke about later as luck.

Ruth did not think about anything. She put his long body under her coat, under her shirt, against the hot dry of the skin over her belly. The shock of him was a burning down her chest, all small hairs on her arms standing for it. Eli was already on his knees at the heater with the towels and scooping the snow from under the hoof prints with his hands so that it would not melt under the heat and make their feet slick later. He made curses at a low tone that would not scare the ewe.

"You come on," Ruth told the lamb into her own skin. He was a cold fish, a long muscle. She rubbed as if she could rub an entire blizzard out of him. "You come on, you above free range. Come on." Eli had gotten her phrase wrong when he was drunk on some YouTube farming video once and had called their lambs "free range above the stove" and she had laughed until it had hurt. She said it now like a charm.

"Warm water?" Eli said. "We can bag him."

"And boil his heart out of him?" she said, not unkindly. "This is quicker. This is what God gave us. He’s going white."

"His tongue…" Eli started.

"He’ll do," she said, as much for the words as for truth. She pinched the inside of his mouth. If it had been white beyond white she’d have put him back into the making things of this place and not thrown hers after him. It was pale. It was not dead. She pushed his legs against her belly. She made herself a kind of barn for a thing she had never made except the once they had made a boy. She felt him twitch. Or she wanted to have felt him and therefore likely had. She would take it. She would take the lie and let it live as an invitation.

Eli pushed the ewe to the jug. She did not fight them now. Her eyes had gone silly with being wrong and then right. He pulled what he could of warmth into that pen from the rest of the shed by the oldest trick: he hung feed sacks on the wire. He set one, two, into the corner they had stolen a heat from. He shoved straw as if straw were insulation like in a house, as if this were days and weeks and not one hour. He spoke to the ewe with no words but his breath. Ruth kept the lamb and then, when her own belly had ceased its complaint at the cold and begun to be numb, she set him out and rubbed him again and put his face under the ewe and stripped her once, twice, let the pearls of colostrum shine and fall and be collected by his mouth. He was slow. He wanted to die. She would not let him. The patent unreason of it buoyed her. She set the tube again because sometimes bodies won’t know what to do with ease until you teach them that something will go in them and make them stand up with it later.

Eli leaned his forehead against the panel for a second and then lifted it again. "Why did she—" he started.

"Go outside?" Ruth finished. "Because the world’s impossible. Because the wind was wrong in her ear. Because some part of her that wanted to be alone had more say than the part that wanted help."

"Like you," he said, and she did not flinch.

"Like me," she said. She had gone out into the yard after the call from the hospital had said there was a crash of heart and not to hurry because there was nothing to be done by hurrying, and she had walked into the pasture because she had not known where to set her body otherwise. She had stood for maybe a minute, maybe an hour, under the ash and thought the particular, pointless thought that had been that there had been too much rain for weeks when the soil did not want it and now there would be not enough when the corn was wanting. She had not cried then. She had waited for the crying as if it were a turn under the weather, something on a barometer somewhere. It had been months coming and now came sometimes when she carried a bucket through a door and the bucket caught the jamb and sloshed and got her jeans cold and she had to change into dry. Grief made a treaty with no useful hour.

The black lamb took to life as a punishment and then as a task and then, in a moment with no particular trumpet, as a life. He raised his head and it quivered and he pointed his nose in the direction of what he would later remember always as smell, as home. He did not stand. He put his mouth to the teat and let the ewe’s sound—a sound now low and cooing and not the anger voice—sing above him as he did the first of all earthly tasks: getting milk into a throat. Ruth put her hand on his ribs and felt them move. She let the warmth of him override the burn on her belly where his cold had been, then let that burn become a remembered part of her.

"Mark him," she said. Eli, obedient in the old ways that were important, stained the ewe’s flank with a wobbled eleven and the lamb’s backside with that same stamp. His blue paint was a different shade against black wool. He blew on his fingers to rid them of paint and then wiped them on his pants because there was, in the end, no other place to put anything now.

They carried a dead lamb out just before four, wrapped in an old grain sack that had yellow letters for a mill not three miles away that was now a wine tasting room. Eli wanted to make the grave feel like a thing besides calculation. He wanted to go to the orchard. He wanted to dig where Sam would have. The snow had other laws. They rigged a corner of the compost pile, opened it with the fork, and set the weight in and covered him with what could be had: hay that was rotted and warm. The steam rose like a little ghost and vanished. Ruth stood with the fork in her hand and did not bring to her mind the thought of apocalypse or heaven. She kept herself small. Eli leaned the grain sack against the post and rubbed his cold hands together and then tucked them under his arms the way she used to tell him not to do on account of making bad circulation worse. She did not say it now. She knew the small joy pain brought when it bit.

When they went back in a ewe was down in ten with a placenta half out. Eli stuck his tongue in his cheek like Sam while he thought what to do. "Don’t pull the whole thing," he said, testing her. That might have been a test on his tongue as much for himself as for her. "Tie it so it don’t get trod. Let her finish it."

"Look at you," she said. "Saying wisdom like it came from right in the way of you."

He blinked. "Like Dad," he said, too quickly and then like he could not unspool it back into his mouth, "I mean—not that I am—"

"You’re Eli," Ruth said. She tied the membrane in a knot, gently, and tucked the bundle so that it wouldn’t be stepped on when the ewe rose. "And you are this. Whatever other thoughts you have about that, you let the snow take them for now."

He nodded. Snow and ewes and the heat of small bodies took the excess thought. The night became an eldest thing.

The battery on the good headlamp guttered and went down to a pulsing whenever Eli moved his head. The kerosene lantern ran low and made an excited dancing when they bumped the post with a hip. Dawn, if you could call it that, arrived by degrees, as though the light had to ask permission. The wind burned itself down to a constant speech and then into talk and then into breath. The shed took on its other form, that gray that took away all edges and made everything nothing in particular. Ruth poured the last cup of coffee into the thermos lid and the coffee was a separate beast now, not even lukewarm, something like the memory of a drink.

They had borne twenty-one lambs in twelve hours. Two had not made the trip, one the black-starred who had in the end come back and set Ruth’s belly alight, one pale and pretty who had been beautiful for the ten minutes of her being and then been gone, taken back by nothing that could be named. Nine of them had been helped with hands and seven had done it like they were born for it, which they were. The reject ewe-lamb, the one who had been refused, now lay under the mother and sucked as if nothing had ever been otherwise. That particular forgiveness, of both creature and time, undid Ruth more than any remembered kiss.

She went past the board where they had chalked the number alive and the number dead since the start of this season and chalked again. She had not taken down the bit of Sam’s writing that said Patience in a hand like a plow with dignity; that had been from the year before, before the world had hardened into something she’d had to bite. Chalk dust clung to the meat of her thumb. She went to set the chalk back in the coffee can and stopped, the old tin scratching against the wood loud in the sudden relative silence when the storm had lowered itself.

She went to the door and pulled it open. The drift outside made a white cliff. The orchard was gone, the creek a shadow-line. The ash they had been married under looked like a man stooping in a wind. The geese that had flown over yesterday afternoon, shouting their perpetual astonishment that geography held, were long gone on the other side of it. The voice of the sump pump never came because the power was still a thing that belonged to some other world. Eli came to her shoulder, put his shoulder to the door to push it wider against its humility, and they both stepped into a world that, under the dying beat of the wind, was as quiet as if it had never intended to be otherwise.

"You see that?" Eli said, his hand pointing to the slant of the yard where the fence had been lowered by the weight and a new shape had formed that would today be a hazard and next week be a joke. "It’s like a hill. We could—"

"Sledding later," she said. The absurdity of sledding, of giving her body to a slope and gravity for the sake of nothing useful, floated before her like a balloon on a string. She did not dismiss it.

In the shed, lambs butted at the world for more. In number three, nine's second-birthed finally stood like his legs had been his all along. He shook himself, his ears went out from his head in ends like spoons, and he sneezed. He made a complaint at nothing and then a declaration at his mother’s teats. The ewe made a sound like a lullaby and in it was every oldness the world had ever offered. Eli tucked his hands into the pockets of his father’s jacket and pressed his shoulders in and for a second, in the poor dawn, looked like Sam. Ruth let that double-vision stand and did not swat it down with pain or words. He turned, and his face was only his, and that was better and worse.

"Hey," he said, like starting a new sentence. "Do you remember—"

"I remember," she said, and she did. She remembered the first time with a lamb in this shed when Sam had stood with a hand on the dog’s head to keep him back and Eli had been three and sleeping in the old metal washbasin because it had been all they had and he had, even then, refused to stay asleep anywhere ordinary. She remembered the year the drifts had climbed the fence and they’d had to cut holes in them with a chainsaw to get air to the ewes. She remembered Sam laughing at nothing because sometimes that had been what you did to stay in love with work. She remembered nothing and then everything so quickly that she had to catch a post with her hand so that the shed would not tilt.

"You okay?" Eli asked, not unafraid, not a stoic pretending that adults did not have rooms inside themselves full of furniture that crash sometimes in storms.

"I am," she said. She took his head in her hand like he was a lamb and set her palm at the place where he’d used to like his hair ruffled and rotated him forward. He leaned into it for a second and then pretended he had not.

Lark, chastened by his scolding, lay with his head on his paws on the straw, his nose twitching as if smelling both the past and the future and doubting neither. Eli went to get more straw from the bales, set them on his shoulder and carried them as if he owned both the load and the language for it. Ruth knelt again in the quiet and watched a lamb find its balance and, once upright, do the wobble-knock-step that stumbled and then steadied and then, with absolute assurance, got where it needed to go. The white of the blizzard light softened through the walls and made the lanolin shine on some places and go matte on others.

The snow would keep the road closed another day. There would be taxes and death certificates and notations in the ledger with the wrong hand and calls to neighbors who were probably themselves sitting with wet towels over lambs on their knees. There would be the matter of the generator to look at when the wind had lost its teeth, and there would be a milk bucket to scrub, and the red lamps to unhang and check for broken filaments when the power came back with a pop and then a hum. There would be sleep like a trick, and the ache that told her nothing solid had been made simple. There would be grief, sneaking through in the way a lamb crawled under a fence: head down, wriggle, and then standing on the wrong side before you had finished snapping the bucket down.

Now there was this—this season’s first blizzard that had blown their lights and made their bodies their only heat, this leaning into a ewe’s side, this boy that was a man in a coat that belonged to the one she had loved and would always love and could no longer turn to, this steady. The black-starred lamb knocked into the udder like a drunk and drew a mouthful and raised his head and chewed and let it go down. The rejected ewe-lamb, still soft at the crown like a peach, paused between swallows and listened with an ear turned in that tiny way for tiny noise and then went back to the work she had found by accident and via strategy both.

Ruth sat back on her heels. The heater’s stink had become part of the air, folded into ammonia and wool and iodine and newest breath. A quiet fell in her chest that had nothing to do with the storm’s shift. She put her hand on the board where Sam’s word sat. Patience. It was not a prayer and not not one. Eli slipped on a patch of slick and righted himself and grinned like he had wanted to make her laugh and maybe had. She laughed. It came not out of relief exactly but out of something like hunger that had found a bite of bread.

"We’ll do," he said, his father’s words without apology and then his own.

"We’ll do," she said back, and in front of them the ewes kept coming, and the day kept coming, and the fine thread between all of it held.
