He packed the case the way he always had, rolling the felt, slipping the rubber wedges into their loops, listening to the click of the tools against one another settle into a pattern he could carry in his head. The hammer’s handle, worn smooth by his palm, nested against the spine of the kit like a long bone. He checked the old paper stock of business cards though he would not need them today. The van’s heater coughed. A pale frost made the gravel at the edge of his driveway look salted. The sun was low and thin in the east, threaded through the leafless branches of the ash, and the only vibrant color in the yard was the red of the fuel can for his mower, turned upside down to keep the rain from crawling inside. He stood on the porch a moment with the kit open on the step and thought of how many mornings had started like this. He could count the routes by smoke stacking from chimneys, by the miles of road that unwound past him like a long string he kept tightening and tightening each year he went out.

He sat in the driver’s seat and tilted the rearview. His reflection was a map of small changes he’d measured the way he measured beats in a slow third. The left eyebrow had grown sparser. The skin where the sideburn met his ear was thin. His hearing aids looked like flesh-colored seeds tucked into his ears. He took them out and placed them in the case on the seat beside him. To tune by ear he couldn’t stand the way the little machines made the piano sound like it was holding its nose. He had always liked the texture of the actual sound that came up out of the cabinet and washed back toward him, the complications of it. There was honesty in the roughness. He preferred to find a note by circling it, hearing its neighbors argue, then patting their shoulders until they agreed.

The logbook lay under the meter on the seat, its cover softened by use. He had told them, the ones still on his list, that he was making his last round. It had felt like writing an apology across the top of winter. They had nodded, those who’d faced him in doorways with their breath coming out in smoke, the ones with their hands knuckled white on the backs of chairs, their sons or daughters or hired men shifting behind them, a dog pushing into their calves. He saw now the names in his tidy script, each with its little note: stovewood stacked; deep porch; bring light, a reminder of the parlor at the Miller farm that had shipped out its good bulbs to the barn one year and left him to work in a faded, old-lace dusk.

He put the truck in gear. The gravel moaned. The road out of his place was two ruts, straight as a taut string for a bit and then falling into a lazy S that followed the creek he’d never once seen go completely dry. The fields were pale, not brown or green, bleached like soldiers’ coats after too many summers. The stubble left from the corn hung on to its own shadow. Geese crossed the sky in a drawn-out V, the back wingmen lagging and then catching up, their bodies straining with each beat. Their noise reached him dimly, tinny in his bare ears. The world on days like this felt thin, as if he could push a finger through it and touch the wooden back of it, feel its brace.

He stopped at the end of his drive and read the road, the way a man reads a passage he’s known forever. A trickle of cars from the bigger town would be heading into the smaller town for the bank or the post office. A school bus would be trundling the other way. He waited until there was nothing, then eased onto the macadam. His shoes, with their worn soft toes, found their place on the pedals.

The first house was the Rileys’. It was not their house anymore—Mr. Riley had gone two summers ago, and the daughter had a husband who was in trucking and seldom home—but the piano was there, a Wurlitzer spinet that had sat in their front room with the same courage as their marriage and their old Christmas pictures. He pulled to the side of the drive under a walnut tree. The leaves, what remained of them, were curled like fists and full of insect scribble. The key was under the milk can as always, wrapped in a bit of black plastic to keep the rust away from its teeth. He let himself in, calling hello as he had countless times before. The house gave him the silence of a building waiting for a voice. He stood a second to let his eyes adjust to the inside dim, to breathe the slow, closed smell of the place, which he recognized in its parts—old wool and stale paper, the iron in well water, the faint, flat sweetness of furniture wax.

The spinet crouched against the wall with the photographs climbing it, thin lace runners on top, a brass ship’s clock tipped to one side. He opened the fallboard and ran his hand lightly over the keys as if to introduce himself. A mouse had left a peppering of droppings behind middle C; he brushed them aside gently, like a couple of pepper flakes spilling on a counter. He set his kit on the sofa edge and unrolled the red felt. The strip spilled across his lap and the memory of the movement pulled a thought across his chest like drawing a bow. How when Micah was small, he’d let him push the rubber wedges into the gaps like a game, each wedge with its little tongue, and the boy had stuck out his own tongue as he concentrated, copying the tools.

He slid the top panel off, lifted it onto the runner. The insides of a spinet were ungenerous—there was an economy to them that made him chuckle grimly, the flung-up action peeking out like a child from under a blanket. The pins were dusty. There were moth grazes on the damper felt. He put the temperament strip in and began to kill the unisons.

He could hear as he always had how years had bent this thing. The whole instrument sat a half-step low, or nearly. A spinet didn’t like to be hauled up in one visit; raise it too quick and you’d pay with the brittle stone voice of a broken string, the ugly breathless thunk of a note that could no longer tell its own story. He tuned it to itself. He picked his middle A and went down to F and up to B and felt for the sweet spot in the beatings like learning the rhythm of a nervous heart. A ripple there, that slow shimmer—push it, coax it, until it smoothed and sat with confidence like a bird on a wire.

He thought as he worked: this key had a stick when he was here in spring three years ago. Had he left the spinet any better than he’d found it? He tuned octaves, checking them open, then narrowed them again, each slender line he drew between registers like a wire rope he was laying across a small canyon. He leaned into the hammer with the caution he would use in speaking to someone hot without their knowing it.

He felt his fingertips burn from the friction of the wedges. He listened for the coarse sizzle of a false beat on the old wires, the way light catches on scratched glass and shows you a secondary sun. He felt for a moment that old, peaked alertness that had kept him young far longer than his face did—a state where his attention narrowed until his breath could be counted against how the note grew and died. He moved down to the bass. The first E was flabby. He set it a hair sharp against its neighbor and pulled the pair into their little agreement. He thought of the hands that had pressed these keys. There had been girls here once, the Riley girls in their woolen socks and long hair that smelled like the inside of an apple. There had been hymns thumped out at Thanksgiving, and then, in a winter when Mr. Riley hurt his back and no one thought about music, there had been slow creep of damp up the wall and the strings had relaxed like a tired man in a chair getting comfortable enough to groan.

He finished slowly. He wiped the keytops with a clean cloth as a courtesy. He looked up to the photographs. The brass ship’s clock had stopped at twenty past three. He could not remember if it had been that time last season too. Time had its own mind in houses like this.

He left a small note—just tuned, left at old pitch, be well—on the keys, and he closed the fallboard gently, as if the thing might wake. He locked up and put the key back under the milk can. It was warmer now. The frost had pulled back from the grass like a tide, leaving its strange sea-line across the yard. He sat in the truck and wrote in his book and then he looked up at what had been Micah’s road to the school and felt the ache for the old way his boy used to run with his bag tapping his hip and his trumpet case banging his shin, jumping two steps at a time over the ditch, his serious face eating the morning with its mouth set.

The next stop was the elementary school in Dalton. He liked the building because it was stubborn; it wore its bricks like a man wears a suit two decades after it’s gone out of fashion. The flag was already up on the pole and wrong for the wind. He went in through the side door, nodding at the sign that demanded parents check in, and he found the custodian, who had a skin like a scraped knee and a manner that was somehow both sleepy and frantic.

“Oh, hey, Mr. Wills,” the man said. “She’s back there, same as ever. Nobody’s been beating her up none. Nobody’s been loving her neither.” He wadded his paper cup and shot it into the trash can with a little flick of his wrist that showed he’d once been proud of that motion.

The music room smelled like dry books and the sour sweets kids sneak into their sleeves. Posters of instruments with their names were curling in the corners. The piano was a Baldwin, a small one, lacquer crazed on the lid by too much afternoon sun. He had first been asked to take care of it the fall the music teacher retired and the one after her had not lasted through spring. He lifted the lid. The action was light, like work meant for a day you thought you might also have time to rake leaves and fix the mailbox and sit with your wife on the step without anyone calling for you. He laid his strip in and thought for a moment of the boy in this town whose father had stopped him once in the grocery store to say thank you for pushing a little air into their house with his tuning, for making the old, square piano’s iron breath come easy so the kid could teach himself two chords and some notion of how to be less angry.

The custodian—Stan—leaned on the door and watched him, then wandered away, his keys rattling like a small creek behind him. He liked working with the background of a building alive: a buzzer halfway down the hall; a teacher’s laugh that had been the laugh she’d had since she was twelve; a kid who walked on his heels so he sounded bigger. He thought of when he had brought Micah here to watch him fix the sticky C in the old days. Micah had looked at the posters, the long-nosed bassoon, the flute like a silver tube of breath, and said, That one doesn’t get to vote, does it, and Harlan had laughed, surprised, because his son did not often bend things with humor. But then there had been a time he’d found the boy standing on the stool at the family piano, pressing his ear to the wood above the keys to hear the strings shudder. “It’s like when you put a seashell to your ear and it’s the sea,” Micah had said, serious as a funeral. “Only this one is the sea without all the fish.”

He did his work in a pleasant fugue. A scratched gear in his lower back began to complain and then quieted. The middle of the keyboard settled under his hand like something that had been waiting for this. He corrected some slack in the dampers that had come from the dry fall air. He tightened a few action screws that had loosened without telling anyone. He played a run with his left hand and remembered that in this room once he’d practiced a hymn with the lid closed while the after-school program kids had cut colored paper leaves and taped them to a paper tree to make the room know it was October.

When he finished he closed the lid and wrote on the flip chart with a marker. Play it now, for old time’s sake, he wrote. A teacher would likely pass it without seeing. Or maybe one would read it and sigh and think of what she had meant to do with her life. He looked around before he left, the way a man looks around a room he once lived in to make sure he hasn’t left more than he intends. He took his cap off as he walked past the flag in the main hall. He thought of children singing in fours, their small mouths open so wide he could see the hopes of their parents inside.

In the parking lot he opened the van door and sat with his feet on the pavement. The cold made his arches ache. He bent and rubbed them through the leather with his knuckles. His hands were still reliable, though in the morning sometimes he saw a little tremor in the right that reminded him of the thin line on water when the wind barely thinks of it. He remembered the first time he had noticed it for real, in a kitchen where a woman had offered him tea and he had spilled a little of it in the saucer and her face had fluttered as if he had spilled dignity instead, and he’d gone out to his van and put both hands on the wheel and spoken aloud to no one. I can still do it. I know where the note is even if my blood spills.

By noon he was at Ada Simms’s place. She had a front room that remembered people the way bread remembers heat after it’s cooled. Her house sat at the corner of two quiet roads, and her porch sagged in the way that gives you the urge to shoulder it up and say, There now. He had tuned her Star upright since she and Herbie were still pretending the farm would save itself. She had his picture in a frame on the piano beside a photograph of her grandson with his mouth sauce-red. In his picture he was twenty years younger and his hair had been determined in the way that men’s hair can be determined when the rest of them is unsure.

Ada met him with a dish towel over one shoulder. “Harlan,” she said in her gravelly voice. “I wondered if you’d quit on me. I baked those apple bars you like. Though I suppose this is you telling me you’ll be turning your back on me with your knapsack tied up in a handkerchief at the end of a stick.”

He smiled. “It’s my last round, Ada. I’m not running away from home.”

“You’re allowed. I’ve had my run of running. It’s not as fun as boys make it seem.” She leaned and peered at his face. “You all right? You look winter-bold.”

“I’m all right.” He wanted to put his hand on her shoulder the way he did sometimes with his own sister before she had moved away and become a voice on a telephone that said things like It’s so different here. “I brought you the little oil bottle you asked for. For the pedal screw.”

She laughed. “It’s like you bring scripture. You bring oil.”

He went to the Star and put his kit down. The finish had gone gummy decades before and accepted fingerprints the way a dog accepts burrs, with a sort of shrug. He had helped Herbie move this piano in and then again when they put down the new carpet and then again after Herbie had a stroke and he, Harlan, had become used to lifting more of the piano than any man wants to lift by himself. There was one note at the far left that went to sleep when you wanted it, and he had tried every year to wake it up for good.

Ada sat on the sofa and watched him, then stood and went to the kitchen and rattled dishes and came back with a plate of apple bars and two cups of coffee that wore their steam like little hats. The coffee smelled burned no matter what she poured into it, as if she had brewed it on a campfire.

“How’s that boy of yours,” she said in the casual way country people ask after grief when the person they’re asking has never spoken it in public. The question had weight wrapped in flannel. “The one with the Biblical name. What is it. Malachi? He was the one played that thing you like. What’s it called. All those tiny notes like a mouse having convulsions on the keys.”

“Chopin,” he said. “Micah. He was good at Chopin. He liked how it was all seriousness in the right hand and a man trying to remember his left hand had a purpose.”

Ada chewed. “He ever come back around? I know you were waiting like a dog on a front step you won’t admit is your whole faith.”

He paused with the hammer in his hand. The strings rang a little in their sleep. “He calls,” he said. “He’s downriver. He’s fine, he says. He works in the city and tells me about bridges as if I’ve never driven over one.”

“And you don’t push,” she said, and it was not a question. “You used to be a pusher.”

“I used to be a pusher,” he agreed, and the little tremor in his hand put a faint sway into his wrist that he harnessed to roll a pin up instead of down. “I’ve learned to pull when there’s nothing left in front. The road ends and you either push them into the ditch or you stand and watch them walk away where there isn’t a road.”

Ada made a little harrumph that was equal parts admiration and annoyance. “I never had that one,” she said. “When my boy told me he’d stay on in the city when he got out of the service, I told him I hoped the city ate him because that would be the only thing that would ever fill him up.”

He played a little melody for her when he finished, the one she had, between jars of preserves and doctor visits, named as her favorite—Abide with Me, which had the right amount of sadness and the right number of places to land. Ada’s face folded into itself like a paper fan. “Play it like that for me when I’m gone,” she said, and when he told her he would not be playing much more at all she pinched his arm. “Then teach me to tune,” she said. “I’ve got nothing but time and two ears yet work, though my left ear flirts.”

“Teaching is for when it’s the beginning,” he said gently. “I don’t know that I know how to midwife an end.”

“Lord, you got to get poetical the day you retire. Eat your bar.”

He ate the bar and savored the cold gelatinous feel of the topping. He took the little jar of oil out of his case and showed her how two drops meant more than groaning and less was greed. He wrote in his book and with a little heaviness about him. Ada leaned in and hugged him hard, bones and the smell of her cardigan, a sour ghost of onion from her kitchen.

In the van he sat and let the heat blast his thighs for a minute. The sky had gone that pale, topaz color it gets on a day when a good frost warns of a hard one coming. He put his fingers on the wheel and tapped out, absently, the opening of a nocturne his son had used to play and then he stopped because thinking of it that way made it a thing in a time before they had been careful with each other.

He drove on to Ridge Hall, the Grange building that used to host dances where men became fools and women became either mercies or plans. Now they had pancake breakfasts and collectible shows and one-year there had been a craft fair that had made him feel a little like he had accidentally walked in on somebody getting dressed. The piano there was a square grand someone had loved so much they’d kept moving it when they had moved their life, and then when their life had thinned the instrument had found its way to the Hall as a kind of slow bequest. It had exposed strings with a rust color like dried blood. The smoothness around the tuning pins had that old coveting look of iron that liked a hand. He had worked on it a handful of times over the years and always left with a sense he had spent an afternoon taking the hand of an old woman in a bed and telling her stories of her youth while she concentrated on breathing.

He unlocked the Hall himself with the key he had from committee days. The emptiness had a sound in its throat. He hauled a space heater from behind the stage curtain and turned it on low, giving the wood a chance to remember its joints. He did not turn on the overheads—the sunlight through the high windows was enough. Dust danced like a premonition. He set up his work and pulled the covers off the instrument and for a time he courted it as if it could be brought to readiness by coaxing rather than torque. That was a piano lesson he had learned early and then had to relearn: some things can only be begged to pitch, but others will only obey if you grip them with authority and make your intention known like you are a creek making its case to a stone.

He mumbled to it as he went, the way he did sometimes without knowing. You’ve got more in you than that, he said. Or, Look there at you, you stubborn mule, give me that last little bit. His breath made small clear crescents on the cold plate. He realized at one point he had said his own name out loud as if to remind himself he was there more solidly than this instrument was, and that thought clipped something in his chest so he stopped and shook his shoulders like he did after a long phone call that had gone worse than expected.

There had been that night in the church. The sanctuary had a crispness like clean sheets. He had tuned all afternoon in respect for both the boy’s nervousness and the seriousness of anyone entering a hall to parse joy. Micah had been fourteen, a year younger than most boys he had seen risk their confidence in that way, but he had that long, solemn face and that old gift that some children have that forces adults to lower their voices near them out of either awe or suspicion. He had practiced above the clatter of dinner and through the long summer afternoons when the only relief from the heat was to soak a rag in the sink and lay it across the back of his neck. He always took care in a way that felt to Harlan both thrilling and alarming. He took his shirts down from the line with the clothespins arranged in pairs around his wrist. He threw balls to the dog and called the pitches. He cradled fledglings and trusted them to leave him clean. He asked serious questions about why humidity mangled a soundboard the way it did and why laying a good third took more of a man’s pride than laying a fifth. What Harlan could answer with his tongue he did so; what he could answer with his hands he showed him. He told himself he was giving something, not taking.

He tuned for him that afternoon with the intensity of a groomer. He went over unisons twice because the little beating he could live with, his boy would not. He stretched by little increments. He checked fifths that he would have let live if he was tuning for a barn dance. He made it sing because that was what you give when you’re asked to be at a beginning.

People had come. Their good clothes looked like themselves with an extra coat of thick. The boy stood there at the front with his cuffs too short because no one had remembered to check, and Harlan had wanted to cuff the back of his neck just to lay a claim on him in amid the town. Then the playing. The opening of the program had been two pieces chosen by the music teacher, straightforward, narrow lines a boy could walk on with his arms out for balance. Then something harder that he had taken on because Harlan had recklessly suggested it in the way fathers sometimes think that raising the bar for their sons will teach them the right way to clear it.

Micah had delivered the first two like a man delivering a baby through a window. He had played the piece Harlan had chosen with love but with his eyes in a place that was not on the page, then had moved to something he had learned by ear in the deep nights, some winged, rivering thing, a piece that had in it the small excitements of water and the drop of it down a stoneside. He had looked to his father at the back of the church not to ask permission but to see if what he was about to do would be taken, and Harlan, whose heart had gone to its knees with pride at that look, had made the tiniest shake of his head because he had wanted the program to stay a program and the evening to satisfy what it was supposed to satisfy. He had wanted to give the music teacher her moment to be sure she was in front of it.

The boy had stilled. He had placed his hands on the keys and then lifted them again and placed them again like a man trying to set a table without scraping a plate. He had played four phrases and then had stopped, the small muscles in his face sparked with nerves. He had removed his hands and put them on his knees. He had looked at his father a second time, and Harlan had held his gaze with an that will be enough set to his mouth that he would spend years afterward trying to justify as necessary and unable finally to do. The boy had sat then with his fingers pinched between his thighs as if to keep them from flying away, and had said, quiet enough that not all could hear, “The A’s are a hair high. I can’t make them sit.” He had gotten up and left, his steps so careful on the runners that the only sound he made in the sanctuary was not a sound at all but the absence of one. Only later, when Harlan would allow himself to pick past the shame of that night, would he realize how extraordinary a thing his boy had tried to describe; that the alchemy he had set in the strings had blazed too perfect for him, and that if he had left the smallest freedom of wobble, the boy might have danced.

Harlan had stood there until his neck felt like a too-thin tree in a windy field. The pastor had cleared his throat and spoke words about nerves and shoulders and had asked the congregation to bow their heads for a minute not of prayer but of mercy. Harlan had found his boy outside under the big oak whose roots had crushed the paving and had taken him home, the two of them silent as an unplugged room. Micah had not played in public after that. He had played in the kitchen late some nights after Harlan had gone to bed and if Harlan crept to the kitchen and stood in the doorway he saw not his son but a form, a shadow with the violence of attention shining around it like a pale fire. He had told himself that was proof enough of the rightness of protecting the boy from people. He had told himself that was evidence of a private genius man should not be greedy for. A year later Micah had left to live with a girl who did not mind that the clock where he worked went home with him always, and Harlan had stood in the yard and watched dust settle after the car had gone and had turned to go back in the house and had seen that he had been standing in exactly the spreading shape of his own regret.

As he left Ridge Hall, Harlan paused on the step and put his hands on the railing and stood there listening to the autumn like it was an orchestra warming. A crow, then another. A man at the feed store throwing what he knew and loved onto a scale. A truck with a rattle in its bed. If he had closed his eyes he might have mistaken this for another year. But in his mouth he could taste winter. There was a cigar-ash taste he always got when the trees completely gave up.

He drove. The truck clicked as it cooled on the side of the road half-an-hour’s drive later at the church. It was the church where they had married him and Anne, where he had bent his head with the force of the idea of her. It was where they had counted money at long tables with the smell of well-worn hinges in their noses. It was where he had tuned and tuned until he knew the piano there better than he knew some members of his own family. The building gave and it took. It let the bats in through the louvers to live behind the beam. It kept the windows tight as a miser’s fist except for the one in the sacristy that welcomed in a sly lollygag of a breeze.

The sanctuary was unlocked as it often was. He pushed the door and it resisted as if reluctant to have his company. The nave was as he remembered it, the pews shined with butter wax, the hymnals spined all the same. The light at this hour came in blue. The glass that depicted the ship in the storm looked worn by decades of salt, though Harlan had always thought that perhaps the way the artist had fired the glass had had a hand in the mood. He stood at the back and smelled what church always smelled like—the cold breath of wood and stone and the linen smell of hymnals and in it all the faintest cinnamon of some long-forgotten fellowship pie. He took off his hat and ran his hand over his head, a habit. He did not have to check that they had not moved the instrument; that kind of meddling had been argued in committee long ago and lost to people who were gone.

The piano sat to the left of the chancel, a baby grand with a finish that took the light like a sad river, the faint whitening where sleeves had rubbed. He approached and lifted the lid. The stick went into its notch with a click that he had dreamed, he was pretty sure, more times than he wanted to count. He ran his hand under the bevel where the dust collects. He had tuned this piano for weddings and for a funeral when a farmer who had never sung at all had left word he wanted Abide with Me sung and there had been no keyboardist except himself and so he’d played with his heart thudding while the farmer’s children wept. He had tuned it for the night of his boy, bringing unisons into the center of themselves like he was tying a double knot.

He set his kit on the bench and took a breath. He had thought during the drive of making his last tuning there perfect as an apology made too late, perfect as a youth he could no longer deliver to the world. But he’d learned enough finally. He had learned to confess without flourish. He laid the strip in place with a gentleness that honored the memory but did not worship it. He set a single A and feathered it with his hammer until it resembled the pitch he knew, then checked against the old tuning fork he had kept in his pocket since he was twenty-five, his father’s before him, dinged on his kneecap until the air could hear it. The fork sang, fat as a bee. He smiled. Why he smiled he did not know. Maybe at the sameness of it; maybe at the little rebellion he had always felt using a fork in a world of brilliant, contemptuous machines. He set his temperament. He held one note down and brushed its neighbor and waited for what had always told him the truth.

Somewhere a car crunched on loose stone and then went away. He tuned the middle with his ears not fighting the hearing aid but alone and unhelped. His right ear gave him a little fit on a third that wanted to argue and he let the argument live a half-breath longer than he would have when he was younger. He stretched his octaves just enough that the piano felt hopeful about singing up into air. He didn’t push the bass hard; it had its own story. He saw in the corner of his view the spot on the carpet where Anne had stood with her flowers, her eyes burning not out of dramatics but out of belief, and for a second he lost the thread of the beat and had to go back and enter it again.

He had a ritual at the end of a tuning, when no one was watching. He would put the hammer down and he would place his hand on the case, the wood warm a little under the light, and he would apologize for anything he’d done that the instrument would have preferred he did not. It was a small superstition and a housebreaking of a sort. He did it now. He played a C with his left hand and let it ring in the room like the very beginning of the idea of something.

He sat on the bench and looked out at the pews. He could see the shape of his boy as he had seen him those nights when he came down late and played in the kitchen, the small light on the piano making him look like a lamplighter himself. There had been a night, months after the recital, when he had apologized to Micah in words that he had dragged like a net. He had told him he had wanted to set up a line he could walk on and had forgotten that a boy sometimes wants to find his own rope and throw it. He had told him that if he wanted to play in a closet with the shoes while the rest of the world did its foolish business, he, Harlan, would set him up with a sandwich and a bottle of water in a sack and tell no one. Micah had nodded and had said something quiet about the color of sound, which only later would Harlan admit meant nothing and everything, and then later still he had simply left. They did not have a fight they could put a date on. They had a slow unbraiding that at moments gave them the illusion of fresh rope.

The sound of the piano now, tuned to itself, filled the sanctuary the way good food fills a house—by leaping into the corners and then settling in the middle. He drew his hands to the keys and thought of Chopin and did not play him. He played instead the hymn Ada preferred. His hands found the old changes slightly different than his head wanted them to be, as if that morning his brain had rearranged one small drawer and then put it back and some things had come to rest in a place where they made more sense now. He played with his left hand steady and his right hand exploring lightly the upper register, feeling into his old habits of slight disobedience. He pulled the sustain pedal very gently with his foot as if he were leading a small animal into confidence after a long period of mistrust. The sound rose, spread, lay down.

He paused and then went on to something he had not meant to, to a small bit of a nocturne his boy had loved because it stepped down in perfect little stairs and then leapt up as if to prove that stairs are not the only way. He did not know all of it. He did not have the right to the middle. It didn’t matter. He played what he had, and the sound of that constraint felt very much like forgiveness to him.

Outside, the tree that had thrown his boy’s shadow across the stones in the walk clicked a little in a climbing wind. He closed the fallboard and put his hand flat on it and let the wood draw some of the heat from his skin. He packed his felt slowly, rolling and rolling until the red formed a tight, obedient cylinder. He cleaned his wedges and put them in their loops and put the little bottle of oil away as if to bless it. He took the tuning fork and set it not in his pocket but in the tool roll in a place where it would not rattle and would not sit against the metal. He left the hammer out.

He reached into the inner pocket of his coat and took out a card he had carried for months. On it was written a number for Micah that had been on the backs of envelopes and on podcasts and inside his head. He had called it when he was full of whiskey he had not earned and he had called it when he was full of longing that felt more honest than the whiskey and he had called it when the price of roofing had gone up beyond what he thought fair. He set the card on the keys under the fallboard just enough that it would not blow off if someone opened a window and just enough that it would show if anyone came along to lift the board for the first time in weeks. Then he took it back, because he felt suddenly that his boy might be a person who did not want to be found by paper. He put the card back in his pocket and laughed once into the empty room, the small sound of a man catching himself in his own trick.

He stood and took up the hammer. It traveled in his hand the way it always had, like a small animal that knew where to put its head. He looked at it. Its shaft was nicked and its head had that dull sheen old metal wears, the kind that looks like the surface of a pond at night. He had thought he would leave it at the church. He had thought that would be a story someone could tell when they found it, the way stories sometimes keep a person alive longer than his body does. But the thought of this tool in an office drawer or rattling in a closet with a Christmas wreath hollowed something in him, so he put it back in the kit. He took instead from the bottom of the kit the weight, a small steel mass he had used to hold down keys when he worked alone on dampers and he had always called his third hand. Its bottom was padded with leather long since hard as a book cover. He had painted a dot on it at some point to know which face had a burr. He set it on the far edge of the music desk where only a person who was thinking about such things would see it. Not a relic. A useful thing that might irritate someone enough to find him and hand it back and make him talk.

He turned to go and then turned back and sat and played the opening chords of something he had in his fingers he could not now name. It was not God he asked to listen. It was not even his son. It was something like a man putting a question to the only thing he had in common with everyone in the room, past and present. Do we go on tuned, he thought, or do we go on out of tune and brave. Does anyone know which is the better way.

He closed the lid and allowed the felt of the cover to kiss the wood the way it always had. He took his cap from the pew and fitted it on. He went down the aisle with his eyes on the place where the runner had a snag from some long-ago heel. He stepped around it. At the back door he stopped like a man who remembers halfway through the night that there is water out there and he does not have to die of thirst. He turned and looked long and he did not cry. He did not confess anything out loud. He made a small vow to himself to answer if the boy ever knocked and to knock no more if he did not.

Outside the sun had thinned, and the air had the edge it keeps for a night that will bite. He lifted the collar of his coat. He walked to the truck and set the kit on the seat and sat with his hands on the wheel. The geese came again, making their letters against the rinsed sky. He put his hearing aids in and the sound of the engine took on its nasal whine and he smiled because he was a man again among machines and not a fugitive in an orchard of notes. He started the truck. He checked the mirrors and then pulled out slow onto the road, the long, laid string of it humming under him. He drove past the cemetery where he had taken the better part of an afternoon once to tune a pump organ for fun and then had gone home and kissed his wife, and he allowed the day to fold in on itself the way workdays do when they have been good enough. He slotted the van’s kit between his thigh and the seat so it could ride there like a small animal that had gotten tired and fallen asleep.

He glanced in the rearview as he took the corner at the bottom of the hill and saw in it the church already smaller, the plane of its roof a hard line against the sky. Beyond it the ridge held the last of the sun like a held breath. The fields were down to stubble and to gold that wasn’t bright but a kind of calm, and he thought that maybe this was the proper end of things—you do them until you’re the last one who knows how and then you stop not because no one needs it any longer but because you have within yourself the good sense to lay your work down and walk the rest of the road with your hands empty. He tapped once, twice, on the steering wheel with his knuckles and the sound was a little harmless music that rode with him a mile and then faded, leaving him with the engine and the wind against the window and the thin, brave sound of his own breath.
