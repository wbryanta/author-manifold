By the time the sun pushed up behind the blue-gum windbreak and threw pale bars of light between the trunks, my shirt was already sticking to my back. Cal had the glue can open and the lid clenched in his teeth, purple primer staining his fingers. He had always been the one who didn’t mind looking like he belonged to a job. He knelt over the trench he’d scratched out with the shovel, wedging his knee into the baked furrow while he worked the fresh coupler onto the end of the pipe. The faint elastic squeal of PVC as it slid over barbs—it was a sound I’d forgotten, and that our father had insisted we come to recognize, like the bite of a dog we’d have to hold anyway.

“You’re not holding it,” Cal said around the lid. “Gonna back out if you don’t hold it.”

I pressed my palm against the top of the pipe where it bent and wanted to spring back, and the heat of the morning went into my skin. The trench water was already drying, making laces of mud along the walls. A grasshopper dove into the shadow and crackled out, and somewhere up in the blue already hawks were mewing like kittens. Between the rows a strip of bare ground stretched, white with dust. The apricot trees held up their leaves like green hands, edges already crisping, little thumbs of fruit hanging pale and hard, not yet blushed. It had not rained enough all winter. The well had coughed air at us last week, and the pump had had to be primed twice before it settled into its choke of steady labor. The orchard could feel the difference between belief and water.

Cal twisted the pipe with his big hands, giving it that quarter turn our father had taught him—to smear the cement instead of scraping it, to encourage a marriage instead of a fight—and held it, face close, mouth open as he sucked in air that smelled of glue and clean, hot plastic. He counted under his breath. I knew the rhythm. We’d learned to do everything here to a rhythm: lay tape, pull brush, prune low branches, lift, set, tie, for hours until the repetition reached inside your bones and made you hum even when you lay down at night.

When he let go, the joint held. He rocked back on his heels and wiped the vine of hair off his forehead with his wrist, leaving a violet bruise of primer above his eyebrow. “All right,” he said to nobody. He glanced toward the end of the row, where the main line disappeared into weeds and the low hill where the well-house sat beneath a chin of old eucalyptus. “Next.”

The day would go that way, all the way down to the road: dig, cut, glue; crawl on our knees to where a mouse gnaw or a gopher chew or our father’s impatience had split a seam. Test the run, listen for the far-off hiss of a leak. A map of small failures to hang the bigger ones on.

“I should have brought a hat,” I said.

Cal blew air through his nose. “Shoulda,” he said, and hung his head into the trench as if to hide a smile or a frown. His hair had thinned on top and gone the color of the moon. He was only three years older than me, but the valley had taken a rake to him that my city had not taken to me. My jeans were too clean. My boots were new, because I’d had to buy them this week. The soles still smelled like China. He had on the same battered red wing loggers we’d both worn when we were twenty. He never had much use for a wardrobe that changed.

We had not spoken yet about selling. We had not spoken yet about buyers. We had not spoken yet about the way this day would end, which we both knew was somewhere beyond the reach of mid-morning. The orchard hummed as it warmed, insects cranking up, a sound it had always made when we were boys picking up windfall in the summer before dawn, our father’s flashlight swaying along the rows like an old comet. A swallow came skimming between the trees and banked a foot away from my face, so close I could see its throat like a spar of rust.

When we were kids, we’d flood irrigated. Our father had opened the head gates the night before and water had rolled down the alleys of the orchard like bronze, so cold you could drown your hands in it and cry. In the morning there would be clean silt around the trunks and frogs waiting under the leaves. When the water district tightened its thumb and the neighbors started putting in micro-sprinklers, our father installed them grudgingly, apricot after apricot wearing a little gray spider at its feet. He hated the sound and the look and he said you could taste the difference in the fruit when the water came up slowly from the earth instead of being laid on top, like the way your mother touches you versus a stranger, but he was practical enough to keep the trees alive. He would never have admitted out loud which side of admission he stood on. The pipes had been his to lay, and then mine and Cal’s, and then finally Cal’s.

The smell of PVC cement always took me right into the well-house—the little concrete-block shed a few acres up the hill, cool as a cellar and wet as a pocket, where the pump hummed in the dark and the pressure tank clicked like a big insect when it reached some mark we’d learned by heart. The swallows nested on the rafters, mud cups like the hands of potters. Sometimes a snake would be sleeping around the tank, and we’d tap our boots against the floor and tell the snake in jokes that we were just passing through. The way you speak to a power that lives in the floor beneath your house.

“Pull that weed cloth back some,” Cal said. “You’re gonna glue dirt.”

I drew the frayed black cloth back from the trench, and the loamy smell reached up under the metallic stink of the glue, a smell like secrets… or maybe, if I was being less romantic, like rot. My upbringing made me think rot and love were versions of each other: slow, hot, complete. Somewhere up above the windbreak the freeway made its soft moan. The sound wasn’t there when we were small. Now it was the horizon.

“Do you hear from her?” Cal asked, as if it were the simplest question you could ask, as if there were only one “her” in the world. He cupped his eye with his wrist again and squinted at the line under his hand as if he were lining up a pool shot.

“Sometimes,” I said. I pressed the pipe while he wrestled the new T fitting into what had been there for thirty-one years—that was how long ago we’d converted to micro-sprinklers; I could count my life by the orchard’s decisions.

“She still in Seattle?”

“She’s in a hospital shift so deep it’s like a cave. Nights now. She sleeps like someone cut the power.” The way the phrasing came out, you could tell I was proud that I could say such things calmly.

Cal didn’t nod. He had opinions he wouldn’t give a voice to because they had too much capital behind them. He wound a strip of Teflon tape around the threads of a riser and tamped it in place with his thumb. I could see the creases in his knuckles, fine as an avocado seedling’s veins. Our father’s hands were like that, and our mother’s too. My hands were hands that had learned keyboards late enough they never got callused from them.

We moved down the row. Cal’s pace set the meter, steady as his breathing, not too fast to invite trouble, not too slow to respect the size of the job. Our father used to say, You stop because it gets dark, not because you’re tired. If you stopped because you were tired, you learned the shape of your fatigue, and then it owned you. What he did not say was that dark came at different times for some men than it did for other men.

By the third leak I had a violet crescent glued into my thumbnail. We had words we saved for when something fought us—words that had very little to do with God but that we liked holding in our mouths like small birds. We flung them at the pipe, at each other, at the groundhogs. A quail called from the brush on the north fence and the sound made me feel eight. He called ha! and ha! and ha!, incredulous, as if we were telling a lie he would not forgive us for.

“You could sell,” Cal said to the pipe. He had a habit of speaking into whatever his hands were on, as if that made it easier on a man. “You could take your half and take off. Forget all this.”

“And you?” I held the joint for him, and my breath fell into time with his.

“Me.”

“You could take your half and take off.”

He smiled at the dirt. “Me,” he said again. The lie was a small and kind one. We both knew he could not. Not because some ancient vow tied him here, not because the dead lived at the foot of the eucalyptus and you couldn’t leave them. Because he knew the orchard the way you know an old injury. You don’t leave it. You manage it.

We took a break at nine. You could start to see the white of it then, the heat getting organized, the promise of small hallucinations by afternoon. My shirt was two shades darker where it stuck to me and my knee had found the one piece of barbed weed left in the world. Cal kicked away a stone and squinted toward the tank house.

“You want me to run the pump?” I asked.

“You remember how?”

“As if there’s another way.”

He stood and arched his back as if his spine weren’t sure it still extended in both directions. The trees on the west end had lost leaves in a patch last fall, yellowing three weeks early in that bruised, brown way that reminded you of cigarettes. We had thought maybe mites. Or one of the new viruses the neighbors were scaring each other with. Really, it was the well coming up dry between noon and three every day. The trees had been trying to make do without telling anybody.

“I’ll do it,” he said. “I like the walk.”

He meant he liked being alone with the orchard when it made its first noise for the day, when the pump’s cough turned into a growl and then into a body tone you could feel in your sacrum. He liked the way the first sprinklers spun in their small circles and then got their bearings. That was another thing our father had taught us to find religious: the moment something finds its bearings.

I knelt and picked the filth out of the threads on a valve with the point of my knife. The little bit of attention the orchard still allowed a man to pay it felt like permission. Permission had been harder to come by in my life this last year. In the well-house Cal would pull the cord that killed the pump switch, loosen the priming port with an adjustable, pour water down that throat, heave his weight into the wheel to close the far line so the pressure came up in the main, bleed the air out of the tank with a cracked valve. He would do this with the instinct of someone stepping around a sleeping animal. If it didn’t catch he would wait with his palm on the case, feeling if the motor was getting warm in a way that meant money. If it did catch, the whole orchard would receive the news at once—the far lines first, bursting out with little hacks, the near ones wheezing like an old man waking. After that, it would be a day of inventory, of running the line in sections while we walked, listening for the hiss and the gurgle, leaning down to feel the barest damp patch in the dust as if we were tracking an animal. In the beginning of each summer day like this we were fugitives from something very particular and very called-down: the ledger, the bank, the man in Fresno with the three rings and the uneasy smile who had shepherded me and Cal through the paperwork after our father fell out of the ladder and did not stand up. In the afternoons like this we became someone else, someone competent and unphased.

I heard the pump catch. It sounded like the well was choking on the air in it before it found its lung. A whirr turned into a throb and then into the bass that vibrated the weeds. By the time Cal came back down the row, the little gray spiders were trembling. He walked without hurrying, pleased the way a man is when something he counted on is still here.

“You bring the pressure up all the way?” I asked.

“Not yet. Section at a time. Don’t want to blow the whole line out in one go.” He cracked his knee with his knuckles, sat down on the tailgate where we’d left the jug, took a long pull, spat a trickle to the side. “Anyway, I like to listen.”

We carried the 5-gallon jug down the row, set it in the cleft of a root and watched the first micro-sprinklers throw their beads. They knocked small holes in the dust and the smell rose up like a rumor—wet soil, sap, anise from the wild fennel under the fence that made our tongues licorice when we were kids. The beads of water made quick stars, and if you were reckless or in love you might believe that this was how a god saw rain, individually, each piece hung in the air by his attention.

“You tell the realtor we’d call him back?” Cal said.

“I threw his card away,” I lied. I had it in the glove compartment in a slot with the insurance and an old bank receipt. The realtor had given me the card in the grocery store parking lot, standing by my car with the engine on, as if he were a man who sold you a cold cure or a dead uncle’s watch. The card had weight; it had a textured edge. The firm was from out of county. The card was soft on your fingers the way suede is. He had a way of looking that said he had been born yesterday morning but also last decade. He—his kind—had eaten the land north of town and the almond orchards in the bend of the river and the fallow fields by the freeway and turned them into little cubes where men wore polo shirts to work.

“He came back?” Cal asked. “He come out here?”

“He came to mom’s grave on Sunday,” I said, and I could feel the lie creep in real time and take its line under the truth.

Cal laughed, a sound that shook everything off him like a dog. “He did not.”

“He didn’t,” I said. “But he might as well have. He parked on Willow and took a picture of the gate. He wants to put ‘gently sloping orchard’ in his listing. ‘Mature trees. Panoramic views of the valley and foothills.’ He’ll say something about hillside breezes, as if that makes electricity.”

Cal took a long breath and closed his eyes to the water’s plash. “We could sell,” he said, as if it were nothing but a sentence. “We could not sell. We could just keep coming out here, doing this, and we’ll die out here like a couple of deer under a fence.”

“You always did have a flair for the handsome ending,” I said.

“I don’t have a flair. I have an eye.” He let his head lie back on the bark of the tree. His hair had caught a sheet spider and it made a crown behind his ear. “You know how much a new pump is? If this one goes this summer the whole orchard’s a piece of theater. You know how much it costs to drill to four hundred feet? You know how many cherries the little guy can afford to be and still sleep at night?”

“We’re apricots.”

“Not a metaphor, city man.”

“I hate that you call me that.”

“Then come back.”

“What am I doing now?” I asked, and listened to the unsayable dice rattle around inside him. He was being fair to me in ten different ways that had nothing to do with this day, and I knew it. “If we sell, it won’t be to a family who puts in tomatoes and puts up in Ball jars and calls it Eden. It’ll be to a man with satellite imagery and cheap money. He’ll push the trees out. He’ll put in almonds. He’ll built a hardpan with his tire tracks and he’ll call it efficiency.”

“No, he’ll sell the water and he’ll put up solar and he’ll call it a farm.”

“He can reach the bank we can’t. He can hold an empty season with change in his couch cushions. He can fail in public and be called a visionary.”

Cal put his palms up like I’d robbed him. “So sell it to nobody?” he said. “Give it to the town and make it a museum of bad decisions?”

“I don’t know.” I finished my water and put the jug against the base of the tree, letting it leave a ringing when it hit the dirt. “I just want to not be the thing that kills something our parents woke up to every day of their lives.”

Cal looked at me for that, a good, long, unkind look that had his whole history behind it. “Who said we had that kind of power?” he said. “You think we’re the ones that make it die? That’s generous of you. This place is in a line of its own. We’re standing in front of a train and telling it our names.”

“My name is Jonah,” I said, as if that were an answer, and he laughed, because we had always loved the one time when something lined up on accident.

A dark thing moved in the weeds by the valve box, and Cal pulled his legs up out of the trench. For a second my chest did a thing it had not done since I was twelve and had reached into an irrigation ditch to grab for a can of worms and pulled a live thing instead. The gopher snake slid out of the box and across the dust and into the trunk shadow of the apricot without lifting its tongue. It was longer than my leg and cool as an idea. A pair of jays scolded it the whole way, telling stories about it as if they had seen it eat their children. The snake paused on the far side of the tree and lifted its head two inches into the air, tasting the morning, and then it went on. When I was a child I killed one with a shovel because I had mistaken it for something else. I had never told anybody. Sometimes I wondered who or what I had been protecting.

“You’re getting sentimental,” Cal said then, though I had not spoken, which is one of the small gifts of knowing somebody too long.

“Don’t you ever,” I said, and he smiled without moving his mouth.

We moved down the line again, cutting the sections that had split where our father had rammed his rig between the trunks with six inches to spare, hitting the riser with his bumper and then swearing for ten minutes about Chinese trash. We kept our eyes down and our hands dirty. The line had more holes than a case for a murder. We patched and listened, patched and listened.

At noon the heat let go of pretense and staggered into you with its mouth open. I sat under the old walnut down by the pump house, back against the stump of a limb that had been sawed out twenty years ago because it grew inconveniently low, and looked up into its cage of leaves. You could fall into the shape of a day like this and believe it was the only thing that needed seeing. The hills rose tiredly to the east. Dust hung like gauze above the road. In the well-house a hummingbird had found its way in and was banging itself into corners and then with intelligence into the only brighter square of light—the window the size of a book—without imagining the door. Cal went in and stood as still as posts until the bird banged into his head once and then, embarrassed, found its way out.

“It’s not going to last,” I said when he was back and had sat down in the dust where there was a patch of shade if you counted optimistically.

“It never did,” he said.

“You know what I mean.” I reached into the paper bag and found the sticky ham sandwich I had bought at dawn from the gas station and that had warmed itself into nauseousness in the bed of the truck. I peeled the plastic away and ate it anyway, because my body counted calories but my brain counted actions that looked like tradition.

Cal had taken to drinking sweet tea in the last few years, a thing that made me think of our mother and her summers in Alabama and the way she sweetened everything so that we would never have to taste a certain bitterness. He poured it into his mouth and then let a dollop slide onto his shirt because life was not something he was careful about.

“You ever think about the pole beans?” I asked, and it was the one time all day the air seemed to drop ten degrees.

“Haven’t thought about those in thirty years,” he said, which was the lie we both decided we could live with. “Why?”

“Because every year we’d plant ten poles on the south side of the coop and Mom would say this is what you do with a piece of dirt,” I said. “And you could just go out there in the morning and in the afternoon and you had a bowlful. And once you pulled the vines down in September the whole thing smelled like the inside of the word August.”

He sat for a minute with his wrist on his forehead. His wedding band—it hadn’t been there eight years ago; he had met her the year the canal was dry and married her the winter our mother’s lungs quit her; some days are tally sheets, some days are coins—had caught a line of sun and lit that finger up like a small saint. “We still could do that,” he said. “If that’s what you want to do, we still could.”

“I’m not sure that’s the point,” I said. “I think the point is that it made sense. This doesn’t make sense.”

“Making sense isn’t in the contract we signed,” Cal said. “The trees don’t care if we make sense. They care about water and heat units and their own particular demons. You act like the land recorded a set of intentions and we have to honor them. It didn’t. It just shows you who you are.”

“And who are we?”

“Men who can fix a leak in June,” he said. “Sons of a man who taught us how to do it and whose mistakes we are going to make better every day we live. Men who will not get thanked by much. Men who, excuse the poetry, know how to put our shoulder against a thing and feel it move.”

I could see my own bootprints in the dust, double-stitched and pretended. The sprinklers shivered on the near line, a rhythm that could put you to sleep if sleep were a thing a day like this allowed.

“You remember Dad telling us not to stand in the trench?” I said.

“Because when the long line blows you won’t hear it before it hits you,” Cal said. “He was talking about water. He was talking about patience.”

“He was talking about himself,” I said. “He was always talking about himself.”

“Everybody is.”

A truck passed on the road and threw up a fan of dust toward the orchard. The driver didn’t look in; nobody ever does. The road was just a road and the orchard was just an orchard and the houses were some trick of the mind people allowed for themselves to stay asleep at the wheel. The hawks circled high and lazy, heat holding them up by their undersides. A crow had found a fig in the neighbor’s tree and was announcing it in a language that said the fig had arrived since Adam and that he had been born to receive it.

When we went back to work, the heat climbed inside our clothes like a third brother. The primer made me a little drunk. Every time I bent to reach a connection, sweat fell out of me and peppered the dust and little prints appeared like a fairy’s footsteps. I had the feeling sometimes—when the air was right, when the smell of glue rode the smells of dirt and the trees were whispering in that particular leaf language—that our father would just come down the row, his step like a still young man’s, his hat flattened from too much time in the truck cab, his voice turned in good directions. He would set his hands on his hips and cock to the left. He would point with his chin at the leak we had missed. He would bark. He would be right. I did not believe in ghosts—I had made it a rule in adolescence not to—but I believed in repetition, which is a kinder form of haunting.

By two the freckles under my brother’s eyes had become dark coins. We made less conversation. The job got its jaw around us and put its teeth in. The newly fixed section was running clean, the whips rotating their little arcs in unison, and the faulty sections were obvious by their silence, which is one of those things you can’t explain to someone new. That’s nothing like a spiritual lesson at all, I told myself. It’s just pipes and pressure. The orchard was a collection of systems laid on top of older systems. We could line the pieces up to make some of the lines straight. We would never roll the crooked out of it.

The argument about selling, when it came again, came diagonal to the line, not down the neat furrows where you could get a good pace. It came when a standing puddle had appeared somewhere we had not ever seen one, a pool of impossible water in a little depression under a tree that had lost half its leaves over the winter. The water was grey and shining, an eye for something that was no longer here to see. We had cracked a big line somewhere under the run of roots. Cal and I looked at each other and then at our hands and then at the shovel and then at the small excavation we were going to make while the sun explained itself. He set the blade down into the ground and the old shovel groaned from its weld like a man standing up. We dug. The shovel clinked on a rock and on a buried piece of drip tape and on a little bottle that had somehow found a way to be here. The water only got deeper, feeding itself from a blood we had not expected. The old root buckled and spat amber into the pool.

“I don’t want to be here in October,” Cal said.

“We’ll finish the run before then,” I said, because jokes are what a body has defense to make when grief approaches.

“You know what I mean.” He sat back on his heels; his knees were stained heat rash red. “I don’t want to be here in October when the fruit failed by half because the well dropped to an old floor. I don’t want to be here in January when the guy comes with the check to buy the paper we live on. I don’t want to be here when the bank says what it says. I’d like to be here now and be here never again.”

“We could sell next week,” I said. “We could say yes.”

“We could,” he said, and plunged the shovel in. “Do you want to?”

“I don’t not want to,” I said. The shovel found plastic. “There,” I said, and we both lay flat and reached into the hole with our fingers like raccoons, groping for what was making the world wet. My hand found mud up to the wrist and then the slick cold line of the pipe and then the jagged mouth where it had cracked because the root had said that is enough. We would have to wedge a split repair over the mouth or cut the section out and glue a new and hope the root didn’t keep growing into it with the grim insistence of anything that knows itself to be a body and not a part.

“Hold it,” he said, and I held it. He cut. The tool made the weirdly cheerful squeak of PVC giving itself up. He primed. We both breathed like dogs. He glued. He made the marriage with his hands and with the pressure the joined worlds required. When you have stood with your hands in a living thing’s throat you do not expect to be forgiven. The split hushed. The pool calmed. The ground began to take the new truth in and make a pattern with it.

“I lie awake,” Cal said then, because there is a kind of confession that can only be made while a man’s hands are full and the sun is looking and nobody in the world would choose to hear it. “I lie awake and I can hear the pump through the house and I think about Dad in the kitchen with a cigarette over the sink, like that would make the smoke rise nicer. I think about the day we went and picked him up from the hospital and he said he’d never drink again and then he took a pull in the truck before we got out of the parking lot, and I think about the way he held our hands that one time we were little and we were scared in the thunder and he told us a story about riding the train on hobo passes and the sweet smell of Pittsburgh. I think about his hands on my shoulders the year I was sixteen and I told him I was leaving and he said over my breath and your mother’s grave. I think about how he looks under the dirt now and if he is a clean thing or a thing the worms can get themselves around. I think about the trees. I think about you in Oakland, in some apartment with a woman who can leave a note and not have to explain herself. I think about Mom’s last breath and what it did to the walls. I think about a realtor’s forearms and the way his shirt is always a little damp between his shoulder blades like he just had to jump for something. I think about my wife asleep with her back to me because heat is like an animal that climbed into bed. I think about crickets and the way a house cracks once an hour to remind you of its shape. And I want to stand up and go and do anything that is not exactly what I do the moment the sun comes up.”

He held the new joint for the count and I held my breath as if that counted toward something. The water silenced itself the way a child falls into silence when it remembers how to swallow.

“We’re not old yet,” I said. “But the decisions are.”

He released the pipe. It held. He sat back and wiped the solid curve of his jaw. “I can’t keep doing it,” he said, and in those words was also the thing he could not keep not doing.

We leaned against the roots and let the breath come back into us. The hum at the edges of the world grew louder, a choir of invisible engines. The smell of glue made me think of storms on the Gulf shore and our mother’s stories of night-swimming in water so warm you could be born again in it and never cry.

“My wife wants me to sell,” he said. “She wants to take the money and buy a small thing with two bedrooms nearer town, with a thermostat you control from your phone, and a little piece of dirt big enough for you to put tomatoes in a pot. She wants our kid to walk to school and not have to ride the bus an hour with the last names she will have to learn to spell. She wants to not have to take a shower with sand in it when the wind comes up from the west because the neighbor tore up his ground and never bothered to lay it down again. She wants me to come home without the sun printed into my face and the smell of a motor in my shirt.”

“And you?” I said.

“I want the orchard,” he said. “And I don’t.” He said it as if it were the one version of the truth a man had a right to.

“Who do you think we are if we sell?” I asked, and I wanted him to be better at answer than I was.

“We become people who do not carry around a map of this place in our head so exact you can lay it over your heart and see where the ridges line up,” he said. “We become men with a different map. We become people who tell the story of this place from a bar stool or a backyard three towns away instead of turning a valve. We are not less ourselves. We are the selves who left. You are not average, Jonah. You are you. You can survive the other version.”

“And you?” I said.

He looked at his hands. “I can too,” he said. “But I would be angry for a long time. And then I’d make a story about it and it would be true because I’d say it enough.”

We stood, because standing is easier than talking. We moved to the next sound. I found a leak by a tee and Cal shook his head and said he’d put that in when he was nineteen and he could not believe it had lasted so long, like anything.

By four the light began to turn monstrous, throwing hard shadows and making everything into a version of itself so literal you wanted to look away. We were three rows from the road then, starting to feel the pull of the end of the day like a string in your chest. I could see the mailbox from there, the little red flag, the sticker with our parents’ names faded to a pink you could read if you squinted. My mind took to listing the contents of that box over the years: seed catalog, water bill, letter from my college with the words we are happy to, postcard with a picture of a beach from a woman who would have married me if I had known enough to ask then, an advertisement for mattresses with a woman smiling on a bed her life had never touched, a letter from the state about nitrates in the well, a newspaper with the story of the neighbor’s kid who had gone to war and then not come back in a way the paper could write about.

The last big leak was under the dumbest of trees, the one our father always said should have been pushed out the year before it was planted. The branch angles were bad and the crotches tore and the fruit came on late and it attracted small soft rot the way some men attract soft want. It had lost a limb in the last wind and what was left of it was canted toward the road as if apologizing. The leak glimmered in the light. We did the work of the day on it, slow and right. Cal’s back made a shape I had seen my whole life and at the same time had never registered until now. I had the sudden urge to put my hands flat between his shoulders. I did it and felt him freeze and then breathe. We didn’t say anything. We did the thing in front of us to do.

By six the light had turned golden in the exact way that tricks you into loving all this. The apricots, the ones that would make it, had begun to put their blush on. They looked ridiculous, indecent, like people at a dance, each of them with its own intention. If I had known how to pray I would have prayed then for moderation: not too much love, not too little.

We made our way back up to the well-house and turned the pressure up to where it ought to be for the whole system to breathe. We walked the rows together, side by side, not because we needed both of us but because the sight of the work held had a specific pleasure. The sprinklers made their circles so fast they looked still. The occasional spit of an air bubble made its way to us and I rejoiced in it the way you rejoice in a stumbling rendition of a song you know too well. The ground between the rows drank and then looked indistinguishable from dry in the span of half an hour. The orchard did not care what we had done; it accepted the drink in silence and did not promise us anything for it.

“I’ll call the guy,” I said, when we were almost back at the truck.

He stopped. He wiped his face. He put both hands on the small of his back and bent like a hinge. “Which guy?”

“The realtor,” I said. I didn’t look at him. I looked at the line of trees and then away. “I’ll tell him to come out and walk it and tell us what it’s worth. We’ll listen. We won’t say yes. We won’t say no. We’ll listen.”

“And then?”

“We’ll fix the rest of the line,” I said. “We’ll pick. We’ll take it through to July. We’ll meet in August and lie to each other a little more and then we’ll tell the truth.”

He was quiet long enough that I could hear a tractor two properties over and the clapping of a crow’s wings. Finally: “All right,” he said. “You call him if you want. I won’t be nice. But I won’t be rude in a way that costs us money.”

“I’d expect nothing less.”

He looked at the well-house the way you look at a house you grew up in after you sign the papers and the new family pulls up with a golden retriever you will always call by the wrong name. The hummingbird zipped back in, saw us, and zipped back out without ceremony. The sky above the windbreak was a blue so violent it was like a kind of acid in your eyes.

“You know what my girl said the other night?” he asked, and I had to think to remember which girl, there being just the one, the little comet who carried our whole lineage forward without ever having wanted to. “She said, ‘Dad, what’s irrigation?’” He grinned. “I told her, it’s you don’t die.”

We climbed up on the tailgate with our last beers and sat with our boots out and our shoulders not quite touching. The orchard breathed its evening smell, green and moldy and sweet. The first bats of the year showed themselves like little fists in the air. I thought of Oakland, the noises that came through our window, a man who played trumpet in the alley as if all the air belonged to him, a woman whose smoker’s cough was a metronome at midnight, the fog that came in fat and cool and made your face a wet map. I thought of the small kitchen where we had painted the cabinets the color of a wet feather and where we had fought twice about nothing the week before she left and once about something afterward and once for silence. I thought of her sleeping on her side with her hair exactly where you wanted it to be. I thought of the realtor, his shirt tucked so neat you could cut your finger on its line. I thought of our father, the way his face looked when we were eight and important and seventeen and not, and of our mother in the morning when she put the beans on to simmer and you could hear them click in the pot that meant they were thinking about their lives.

The pump shuddered once, a little shrug that let us know someone had flushed somewhere, had turned a thing that should not be turned. The pressure steadied. Above us, the sky deepened until it made an explanation out of everything.

“You can stay the night,” Cal said without looking at me. “You don’t have to keep driving back and forth like a man who doesn’t know where to go.”

“I was planning on it,” I said. “I’ll sleep on the old couch in the room with the woodstove.”

“You’ll die,” he said. “It’s June. You can take my kid’s room. She can sleep with her mom. They don’t mind. You can stay.”

I said nothing, because I was counting how many times he had said you can and not you should or you will or I need you to. The difference is sometimes all a man needs to hear to believe in his own life.

On the road two teenagers drifted by in a rusted sedan with the windows down and music so heavy it made the dust bounce on the gravel oiled where the county had stopped to pretend it was useful. They had their hands out the window like they were offering little sacrifices to the air. They did not look in. They didn’t need to. This wasn’t for them, yet.

We slid down off the tailgate in one practiced motion. The pumps would turn themselves off on their own—with the floats, with the timer, with the forgiveness we had engineered into the system to make up for the forgiveness we did not have in ourselves. We could turn them off too, with one flick, and the silence would fall like a quilt over the whole valley, but tonight we let them run into the dusk, letting the arcs of water throw their small planets into the last of the light. We walked a little way out into the rows and stood with our hands half out, the spray beading our forearms, cool dots that did not ask anything of us. The ground gave a little under our boots, a shallow gratitude you couldn’t spend anywhere else.

“Remember when we’d run through this when he wasn’t watching?” I said.

Cal turned his face into the spray and closed his eyes like a dog. The water caught on the lashes there and made his eyes jeweled even when they were shut. “We never did anything when he wasn’t watching,” he said. “He saw everything.”

“Not everything,” I said.

He laughed, and we didn’t make a catalog. We didn’t have to. There had been woman and whiskey and snakes and the time we took the ladder out to the road and leaned it against the sign that had my name on it and erased it with a spray can, and then the time afterward when we leaned it back and wrote it again.

The dark came on in a line you could feel pass through your body. The trees became silhouettes and then absent presences. The sprinklers made a sound like sewing machines on the other side of a door. We let our arms fall. We walked back up the slight hill to the truck. The well-house ticked and clung to its coolness like an animal to a rock. The jug was empty. The glue cap was crusted. The shovel was back in the bed like a still piece of a man’s idea about how to survive.

As we crested the last rise we could see our father’s grave at the back of the little family plot, a ring of river stones our mother had insisted on, because she believed in circles. The dirt had sunk by an inch since winter; everything settles. There were walnuts scattered on it by a squirrel who would always be forgiven, and one small, bright plastic toy someone had left—a soldier maybe, or a horse; I couldn’t tell in the half-light—our niece’s fingers knowing what to put where we could not.

“We’ll walk over after dinner,” Cal said. “We’ll tell him what we’re going to do. He’ll have something to say about it, and then he’ll keep his mouth shut like he always did when somebody did what they were going to do anyway.”

“Okay,” I said. I was tired in the part of you that gives words shape.

“And we can go look at the pole beans,” he added, as if he had been thinking about them all day after all. “She planted some in a pot off the back porch. They’re coming up like they always do.”

“Of course they are,” I said, and let the part of me that believed in such things carry some of the weight the rest of me was tired of.

We climbed into the truck. The seat was hot and made a little fart of dust. When Cal turned the key the engine came up obedient as a dog that has been trained hard but forgives you everything. We sat a second with it idling, looking straight ahead at the rows like a man looks into a mirror without moving his face. Then he pulled the knob that turned on the headlights. The beams reached into the trees and made the water lines shine. The orchard looked like a city in miniature, plumbed and live. Cal did not put it into gear right away. He let his hand rest on the shifter like he was taking the pulse of a body.

“Tomorrow,” he said.

“Tomorrow,” I said.

We drove slow down the row, careful not to wash wheels in the soft spots. On the last bend before the road we both looked up at the sky the way our mother had taught us. She had eyes for weather that made the men laugh at her until it had rained only on the day she said it would and keep from raining on weddings when she had sworn she would take their vows from the sky. The blue held no cloud and promised nothing. It didn’t have to. The orchard was still shining under our low beams like a thing that believes in its own life. The pump hummed. The well was a throat that had given us its sound for half a century and might, if we stood listening long enough, tell us how much longer it meant to do it. In the meantime there were nights. In the meantime there were mornings. In the meantime there were holes to dig and glue to sniff and someone else’s money to be indifferent to. In the meantime there were two brothers with their hands on the wheel and the thing in front of them solved only until the next time it was not.

The truck rolled through the gate. The chain slapped the post. The sprinkler heads kept spinning their same small convictions behind us, cutting the air in ways our father had always insisted would amount, at the end of the day, to rainfall. We let ourselves be convinced, for the length of the drive at least, that it would.
