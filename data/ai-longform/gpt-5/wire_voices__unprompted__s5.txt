The cords are warm by the time my shift starts, the rubber giving a little under my fingers the way a neighbor’s wrist does when you bump on a crowded bus. The room is long and not as bright as you’d think; the fluorescents beat on the green boards and tangle up with the lamps, so it always looks like evening in here even when it isn’t. We talk in numbers and names, our vowels pressed to uniform like our blouses. We move without thinking, hands sliding and clipping in, the left one lifting, the right one already halfway to the next jack, a choreography taught into the body until your muscles never get the names of the dances, just the timing.

On good nights, the voices come easy. Boys with sparrow voices asking for their mothers. Husbands whose jokes we recognize and don’t laugh at. Ladies calling for recipes. Accidents press loud and quick, and the hands next to you start to shake, and your own hands refuse to because they were trained deliberately by women with hair done up like Sunday rolls who gave demerits for dropped calls. Far off, a panel clicks, a wall repeater ticks, the faint static of the whole country turning in its sleep.

“Long Distance,” I say when I slip into my seat and the lamp below my fingers starts to stutter. “What number, please?” The voice wants New Orleans. The next one wants Rapid City. The next one is boy clear as milk teeth, wants the hill outside Emporia to call with, reads the number twice as if the world will not hear him if he doesn’t say it right.

They say nothing about attaching yourself to certain voices. We are trained to flatten and disappear, to exist only in the space between a ringing and an answer. The woman who trained me told me, “People get needy when they cannot see the wires. We are the wire they think knows them. We don’t.”

But there are voices. There are voices you catch and keep. If you work the toll desk long enough, the nights stand over each other like lacquer, and in between there is a layer of someone laughing into a hand, the sound of a baby turning in its sleep three time zones away, the static that takes a joke and scuffs its shoes on it before passing it along. We do not keep what we do. We sit and feed the country to itself and do not get a taste. Still.

The first time she called, I didn’t know to hold my breath. Sunday evening, with the hail still in the gutters from the afternoon and my fingernails clean because I’d been home long enough to soak my hands and watch them pale in the sink. I do not always work Sundays—I lose count—but I remember that night because the break room radio had gotten stuck on a station out of Oklahoma City and the country singers were all nasal and earnest. I sat, I adjusted my stool, I tucked my skirt under against the slick wood. Then the line came with her on it, a clear rune pressed somewhere between Kansas and the edge of a place I could not see.

“Long distance, what number please?”

“Operator, would you get me Chicago, areacode three-one-two,” she said, and I could hear her swallowing after she said Chicago. There wasn’t money in that swallow; there was hope. “It’s for Mr. Edward Lykins? Person-to-person?” She said the name like she had practiced it, each syllable put where it belonged, not one of them flinching. I wrote it down on the order blank because that’s what I do, the pencil stubbing a groove.

“Yes, ma’am,” I said. “Your name, for person-to-person?” Sometimes they forget, sometimes they blush and pretend. But she had her own name ready to give, like she had decided that if she was going to pay for it she would lean on it.

“Dora Hastings,” she said. “Hastings with an H.”

“Thank you. Stay on the line.” You can do that—keep them while you pull a trunk and clip them in and hand their voice to another operator who sits farther east and knows a different darkness and a different hum of lamplight. Sometimes they say never mind and hang up. Sometimes they talk to you. I could hear Dora’s breath on the line, the way it steamed a little. Somewhere near her, I thought, there was a window. Somewhere between us, the room had the shape of a Sunday parlor.

The Chicago operator came on with a brisk click. She had the city in her mouth like a hard candy she wasn’t going to share. I gave her the number and the name, spelled them out, listened while she made the connection. I stayed in—some operators click out if the it’s just person-to-person for a house, but most of us stay in for the answer, because there’s time and charges, and you have to cut back to tell them what it’s costing them at three minutes. There is a light above the cords, and when you put a call through you watch the small red eye blink steady when the party answers and wink out when they don’t. That night, I watched while the far end danced a little, then held, and for a moment I thought I would be telling Dora there was no answer in Chicago, to try again later.

“Hello? Edward? It’s the operator. I have a person-to-person for Mr. Edward Lykins from Miss Dora Hastings. Will you accept?”

There was a man’s voice, lower and lazier than I expected, like he had to sit up to speak past his chest, and I pictured the act of his getting to the telephone, the bump of a knee against a table. He said he’d accept, and the lamp went a steady, obedient glow.

“Go ahead, please,” I said, feeling foolishly like I was performing a small wedding.

He didn’t say her name. He said, “There you are,” and she said, “I felt silly spelling,” and they both laughed in the same place where a laugh is a kind of apology. I heard their voices meet in the space I made and felt almost happy for the engineering of it—what else do all these hot little cords mean if not this? The red eye shone and the board hummed and my breath matched the blower under the footrest for a few moments.

Three minutes came like it always does, and I had to press my key and break in. You say their names when you can, because it lets your hand fit around them, because you want to remind yourself this is a real exchange that ends, not a river you have to step into forever. “This is the operator speaking. You have three minutes at two dollars and forty cents. Do you wish to extend?”

“Yes,” Dora said, without asking him. I marked it. I kept the time.

By the second week, I had trained my hand to be quick across the jacks whenever her number came up from the local side. Telephone girls are not assigned voices, but we know how to lean toward a certain sound. You cannot choose a person; you can choose a place. I started the habit of trading a call or two when I needed to. “Take my Guthrie,” I’d whisper. “Give me Wichita two-oh-four,” if I saw that little line trembling somewhere I knew. It is not protocol to court calls. It is just the way we save each other, sometimes.

The truth is, it wasn’t only Dora. It was how the pair of them made their space and then sat in it, careful. There are so many ways people have of talking to each other when they think the line is clean. Everyone counts seconds with someone else’s money in their hand. Some come on the line hot as a stove, all clatter and spit and sweetheart you won’t believe. Some are shy because it costs and that makes it grand, and their words dress up. These two weren’t shy and weren’t brash. Their words stepped toward each other; they had the precaution that lives get when they stayed apart awhile and came to know the minute things the other reserves for the private. When she called me the second time, her voice had learned how to say Chicago without the swallow. He always said hello like he’d been preparing to be greeted.

We are not supposed to listen, but there are requirements built into the job that make it impossible to pretend we don’t hear. Time and charges is a permission to be the metronome in the back of people’s lives. There are times we have to come back in to announce daylight savings changes or the increased rate after 9 p.m. We knock on their conversations like stagehands do and press the key so the buzz in their ears is our brief clearing of throat, our little throat light twitching them into remembering an accounting. Sometimes, in the seconds before or after, you fall into a place in what they were saying and take it with you through the night, the mop of laughter after a needling, the raw startle of a silence. “I’ve been learning to fry chicken without making the whole place smell,” Dora said on a third week, as if nothing else had to happen in the world. “It’s the oil,” Edward said. “It’s always the oil,” she said, and he laughed, and something in me unclamped in sympathy for the practical theology of that.

Around them, 1968 moved like a change in weather you had to feel in your ankles. Someone in the break room yelled out the news when Dr. King was shot; the operators who had been at their boards longer than I had sighed like the stations themselves had exhaled. I came back from my ten-minute, wiped my hands, and sat to calls of women in Knoxville and Cheyenne and Detroit looking for brothers and nephews and sons. There are certain nights when the country pours itself toward a single name, and the line holds or doesn’t. That night, it mostly did. You hear the same stories four hundred ways. “Is he there?” “He told me he wouldn’t go downtown.” “I didn’t want him going either.” “Could you try again?” Those nights you drop your rules because rules got broken elsewhere. You don’t cut in at three minutes because you lose track of time on purpose. You clip new leads and you are forgiven for not being remembered what you are. Those nights I went home with a song tightening my throat and the imprint of my headset an address on the back of my neck.

Edward and Dora called. Perhaps they were careful not to mention the world—some people are like that, as if it were bad luck to say any proper noun that has not yet touched the house. Or maybe they did talk and I wasn’t at their jack. It is very possible. When Mr. Kennedy was killed in June, the calls came thick and ungrammatical again, and I left the building at midnight with my blouse damp under my cardigan because I hadn’t dared walk to my locker to take it off. My supervisor, Miss Calloway, took two of us each aside and told us to stretch in the middle of the hall, to keep from seizing. “We need you loose,” she said. “The boards eat stiff girls alive.”

In late July, someone connected the break room radio to the overhead so we could have a summer of it. We heard the riots, we heard the chants, we heard the baseball score in the middle of the night in a voice that sounded like a boy from my high school. We got a brief fit of prank calls—teenagers ringing for California just to giggle and hang up. “Tell them we have ears like owls,” Ruby from two positions down said to me, “and remember who’s collecting the dimes.”

In the middle of it all, the steady little pulse of Dora’s Sunday. She never called any other day. Once, when he called her, I heard it—I think that was after a storm had taken limbs down off the trees and the highways were mean—but mostly it was she who called him. Always person-to-person, always “Mr. Edward Lykins” on the long vowel of Lykins, as if the y were a ladle you had to lift with concentration. He always accepted. When we cut in at three minutes, she always asked for more. Once, I heard him call her sweetheart and then correct himself. “Sweetheart—that is, Dora.” I’d heard all the endearments there are, from kittens to mamas to animals that do not survive the barn, whispered and shouted and cooed in four time zones; when he said sweetheart there was a question in it, a door built at the back of the word that either of them could have stepped through. Dora did not, that night. She said, “Edward, don’t,” and then earnestly, “I have to put money in the coffee tin.” He said, “Of course,” and some tenderness like moss pressed down in the syllables either way.

I made plans for them. You will think I am foolish. When I went home, I looked at the photograph of my husband at nineteen in his uniform—that navy blue we thought would last longer than a boy’s face—and imagined how Edward might button a shirt on a humid morning. I imagined which window Dora put her plant in. My husband’s name is Lester and it is not; he was gone sixteen years by that summer but I say his name sometimes as if I were picking a lock. He died in his car coming around the lake road in a spill of rain. If he had lived, he would have been a dad in that big way that men from my town sometimes throw around the living room on Saturdays, like laughter is a pillow fight. But he did not live, and I became a woman who knew how to use her hands for a living and how to keep them off things when they should be kept off.

On my street, behind the apartments and the laundromat, the alley smells like cardboard and lilac this time of year. In my apartment, the radio is never off. The women in the building meet in slippers to discuss the price of bread as if it were a something we could hold like a culprit by the shoulders and shake. We do not say Vietnam and when we do we all look at our hands. We know the names of other people’s children. I do not have children. I have a cactus given to me by the girl who rented before me. It has never bloomed.

My life is small in the ways a life can be and not be insulted by the word. It is large in the ways that are a matter of feeling that the wires are not going to stop singing. I bring a paperback to work that never gets read. I bring cookies sometimes and set a tin on the corner of my board and fold the wax paper back with excessive neatness because I need to hold something in order for the day to move. Mabel two stations down thanks me with a politeness that feels like a soup you could bathe in. We have all been manners to one another for so long that sometimes you forget those words can be weapons or love.

“Operator,” Dora said once, on a Sunday in early August when the heat hung in the building like an unasked question. She had been put through and their call was over; I’d given time and charges and said thank you. Her line did not die as expected. “Operator?” she said again, when she had no right to me anymore. I pretended for a second that I had my hands full and could not respond, because you cannot be the equal of what you are not paid to be. Then I said, “Yes?” too quickly.

“Nothing,” she said. Then, softly, “I hope your day is alright.”

“Thank you,” I said, with a steadiness I had to ship back into my voice from the safety of my training. I pulled two cords and clipped in a Farmington to San Francisco like nothing had happened. I thought about her telling a stranger like me to have an alright day as if she were leaving it on my step, folded tight. That is the sort of little thing that leans on your back against the sink at home when you are washing a pan and makes you stand for five still minutes staring at your face in the window because you are trying to keep your face from showing all the weather you have inside it.

There is an operator in Chicago, a girl younger than me named Nina whose voice set down in my ear with a backward seeing that made me think of the girls in the laundromat turning their slip tops under in July. “Got your fellow again,” she’d say if she recognized the symmetry of those numbers and names, because sometimes they go to the same house but different operator groups, and evenings make the same hands meet. We are afforded no gossip but there are small recognitions we let ourselves have. At the end of August, Nina told me she thought Edward had a cold. “He coughed like he was being careful,” she said to me, passing me a line like you might pass a jug of water to someone running. “He said he had to lay down after.” I don’t know why she told me except that it is a pleasure to say that someone you hear only in fragments exists under the rules of blood and sleep.

That summer, on my way to the bakery, I saw a man lying in the street as if he had been placed there to demonstrate a point—that the street was real, that the gravel did not hurt the way we said it would, that the sky could stand over a body politely like a porter. A small crowd had gathered, which is to say there were three people. The man had the fat boots of a laborer. Someone had put a folded coat under his head. The ambulance took him away with astonishing neatness. I walked the rest of the way as if the balancing act we all called sidewalks was a plank along the back of a frightened horse. When I got back to work that night I cleaned the plugs with a cloth, which is not my job, which is a different job entirely, and I cleaned them as if I had offended them. There are some days you apologize to whatever you are doing.

The week the fair was in town, I did not sit for Dora’s call. Children came onto my lines like someone had tipped the schoolyard into the phone booth. Boys dared each other to dial New York and see what voice answered. Men called from beer tents to tell their wives they would be late, and twice I heard a woman behind one of those men say something so low and torn that the man had to move outside to finish his call. Times like that, we are all operators. He was the most decent one. I hope he got home alright. That Sunday, I saw in my supervisor’s log that the Chicago call had gone through without me and without Nina; it had been handled by a new girl whose lipstick traveled outside her lip line all afternoon. “That couple is regular, aren’t they?” she said, trying to learn and trying to show she could hear what we all did. I said nothing because there was nothing I could teach her that she was not going to have to teach herself with the weathers of her own nights.

By September, the trees in the square had learned to believe in the shade they were making again. School came back like order does. The country was in the habit of breaking and making up with itself each week. In the room where we sat and routed and clipped, you could feel an obedience settle, as if we were all waiting for the leaves to help us keep our windows open without letting anything inside. I took to bringing my lunch in a old blue lunchbox my husband had when he was a boy, the metal dinged as if someone had punished it for something it was not able to stop. I put my sandwich over his name where he had scratched it and I ate like I was visiting with a ghost.

If I had written down all the calls I loved, I could have made a book to hand to someone who asked me what it means to be the middle of a thing. But we are careful not to keep ledgers that aren’t ours, because the thought of what would happen if we lost them makes the small muscles around your heart jump. So I did not write down that Dora liked the way the city sounded when he was outside and there were sirens. Or that Edward once said, “I could teach you the habit of loving a place you’re not in,” which embarrassed them both until they laughed and saved it by laughing. I did not write down that when she said, “I put my dress out on the line,” he said, “I’m trying to see a red dress in my head from here and all I can see is my mother,” and she snorted and it made him giggle in a way he must not have often, and it was indecent and dear the way it always is when you make the inside of someone else’s mouth realize it is young.

We are not all good. There are operators who cut people off to teach them a lesson about money. There are operators who let things run and tell the switchboard it was the storm. There are operators who get mad at other people’s stories and take their own mood out on the duration of someone else’s love. “You will never be able to worry everyone,” Miss Calloway told a group of us once during a slide presentation about hurricanes. “They will go on worrying in their own way. Let them.” Perhaps that was as close as she ever got to telling us we were not responsible for love at any scale.

In mid-September, the Sunday was a plain day. I had my blue lunch pail. I had my cardigan. I had my pencil sharpened down to what looked like a dog’s tooth. I had the wish to be needed just enough to keep me in a chair. Dora’s line came up, a little shiver of the lamp I recognized like the shuffle of someone you live with coming down a hall. “Long Distance,” I said. “What number, please?”

“Same as always,” she said. Only that Sunday she cleared her throat and then said, “Actually—operator? Could we try the house on Ohio first? He’s staying there now. It’s on the order sheet, too, if you make a note.” I looked and saw the secondary number penciled in so faintly that the graphite looked almost like the memory of a number. “Yes,” I said. It is easier to pretend the world has stilled itself long enough for you to do one small thing.

When the Chicago operator answered, it was a man. They put the men on at supper sometimes to cover a lull in local. He was all efficiency, businesslike to the point that my filing of his tone could not find a drawer to put him in. I gave him the number and the name. He routed, I watched the lamps. When the answer light steadied, I braced for the room in Chicago—wood paneling, a telephone with a fabric cord like a cat’s tail, a young man bending his back on a kitchen chair—to offer me its throat.

“Hello?” The voice was unfamiliar. It was reedy and breathy. It sounded younger, and out of breath, the way someone is when they have run up from a basement with an orange. “Yes? Hello? Who?” You can tell the difference between a family member and a stranger in under a second. The stranger uses the telephone like a doorway and expects you to be bearing a package. The family member says hello and puts down their feelings like flatware.

“This is the operator,” the Chicago man said. “We have a person-to-person for Mr. Edward Lykins from Miss Dora Hastings. Will you accept?”

“Oh,” the voice said. “Uh. He’s not at this number.” This is the part where you wait for them to add, Let me get him, or He’s at my neighbor’s, or He’ll call back when he’s home, or He died. They rarely say died. Death comes in with a bowed head and takes a seat in the parlor and no one names him out loud around the punch.

“Is there another number?” the other operator asked.

“No,” the voice said quickly. “He’s at work, I think.” Then, to someone else in the room there, muffled, “No, not—” and then back to us, “Who did you say is calling?”

“Miss Dora Hastings,” I said, because if it is my line and my call I own it. “Would you like to take a message, or shall we try the work number?”

“It’s—no,” the breathy voice said. “No message.” He hung up before protocol allowed. It is not forbidden to hang up on a person you do not owe your Sunday to; he made it sound as if the wall had pressed his hands to the cradle. The lamp blinked out. I pulled the plug reflexively, like you would tug a string to see if the light on the porch still loved you.

“There’s no answer there,” I said, to the room we both were in and not in. Dora made no sound. Lines sometimes get the hush of a church and sometimes the hush of a vacant lot. “We could try the other number?” I offered, as if she had not started with that one for a reason.

“Yes,” she said.

We rang the second line. Nina answered in Chicago. “Oh,” she said when I gave the names. “Oh, honey.” She must have known something already I was not eased of knowing. Her voice slipped its city a little and came out with cornbread in it. “I’ll try.” She put me on hold. Her manners could only hold the waiting so hard.

When the ring came to a stop, a man answered who was Edward and not the laugh he had, not the honeyed Sunday I could press out of his vowels. “Yes,” he said, the way a man says yes when he has learned a different answer to the day. “This is he.” Nina made the ask. “I’ll—yes, I’ll accept.” The accept lamp burned steady. My hand trembled, and I gave myself an inward scold only I could hear. “Thank you,” I said to no one.

“Go ahead, please,” I said to both of them. I did not touch the cut-in key. If the board had asked me to, I would have refused it like a bad medicine. There is nothing more dangerous to us than the soft refusal to do a thing that is against the rules, but it is possible to do it so gently the room believes you are just blinking.

He said her name. He said, “Dora,” as if he did not have one to spend. She did not say his. “Are you all right?”

“Yes,” he said. “I’m alright.” You know the color of a lie by the room it’s said in, even from a distance. I imagine he was not in a room that looked like anything I could picture. There are rooms that have learned to be only the furniture in them. “I’m alright.” He said it again, which is what men do when they know it isn’t what the other person needs.

“I tried the house,” she said.

“Oh,” he said.

“I got someone,” she said. “They hung up.”

“Oh,” he said again, and were I a person loosed to get my hands into that conversation the way a mother does into her daughter’s hair before church, I would have petitioned both of them out of the echo.

“How’s the—” she began, and stopped. “I made beans this afternoon,” she said instead, as if they had agreed upon a treaty about what else there was in a day. “I put too much salt in and couldn’t take it back out.” She laughed.

“You can put a potato in,” he said. Then, softening, “There’s this place here sells apples so good they taste like they have taken an oath. You’d like them.” He was worse at avoiding things than she was. Or perhaps she felt she was better at it and had had more practice.

At three minutes, I did not cut in. The person at my left had already gotten used to me forgetting a measure and reached over to mouth Time, and I lifted my chin and pretended my hands were very much involved in something else. “You have it there? The—” she said, and he said, “It’s in a drawer,” and then, quieter, more a breath speaking than a mouth, “It’s—yes.”

“This line will not save your life,” my training said, and went on, “You are responsible for toll accuracy down to the second.” You can believe something and not live by it for sixty heartbeats. That is our talent as a species.

They did not ever say love in my hearing. They did not say I will. They said Do you want me to call you at work tomorrow? They said I can’t always answer at work and they said I know. They said I don’t like that you don’t feel well, with a rhythm I recognized out of the mouths of my aunts and my neighbor ladies, a shimmering sympathy that leans over and straightens the blanket you will not let anyone see you have taken to wearing in the middle of the day. They said beans and apples and drawers and the way a room sounds when it does not yet have curtains and whether the windows open top down or bottom up and where the sun comes in. At six minutes, I took a breath and did my job. “This is the operator,” I said gently, with my voice laid down on a cushion I had made of my manners. “You have six minutes at four dollars and eighty cents. Would you like to extend?”

He said yes. She didn’t say anything, which is always when you hear the yes most plainly.

Only once did the call fail before. In June, when storms pulled down lines like somebody had taken the hair right out of the heads of the trees, I could not force a path to Chicago, and we tried again and again and I offered to ring the next morning at a lower rate and she said she preferred the ruin of the attempt. “We won’t keep trying past a certain point,” I said, making my voice the kind of stern you use to save someone from the ache you know will not stop. That night, I went home and lit a candle for no one in particular and watched it until the wick curled like the spine of a small animal. Then I blew it out and I did not cry because there are only a few specific places I will do that anymore, and of those places the kitchen is most dignified.

Two days after the difficult Sunday in September, Nina telephoned my extension while I was eating a softened apple from my lunchpail and trying not to notice the way the sugar stung a crack in a tooth. “Did your Dora call yet?” she asked, and she had not yet developed the habit of callousness that protects a person from their profession when they are squeamish and lighthearted both. “No,” I said. The fact of the voices when you are not at your board feels like a piece of your clothing being worn by someone else. “My Edward hasn’t either,” she said, and laughed at herself, a kind of scold and a salve. “Listen,” she said, and then did not. “No, never mind. You probably know nothing I don’t know.”

I did not. You don’t know that people died in each other’s houses unless they tell you, and they didn’t tell me. I did not know if he had left the room he took that call in. I did not even know whether his two words that sounded like every man who ever gave anyone equals this feeling of being given—a man who had learned to stand and be something when someone said a name—were going to come at me again. We say the dead have silence like a different sort of coat they wear. We find ourselves in that image because it keeps us from imagining the way silence burns.

On the Sunday that only one of them called, the room was stale from the weather finally breaking. The first day of real fall feels like a plain dress and a clean window. I came in with a shawl over my cardigan because I had not wanted to go back for a coat. When I sat and put the crocheted thing across my lap, Billie on my left said, “You’re somebody’s grandma,” and I smiled because sometimes that’s as near to the truth as anyone can throw. I did not want to want Dora’s call. I wanted to be mature enough to move my hands as if nothing had gotten heavy for them.

When she came, her voice was tin. Tin can on a pole, kids leaning out a treehouse window and strangling it between their little palms. “Please,” she said, too much inhale between the syllables. “Long distance. Chicago? Three-one-two.” Three-one-two landed in my mouth like a tin horse made to dance on a string. “Same person,” she said, and she didn’t say his name, and in the saying nothing she said it more than if she had sung. “I’d like to talk to Mr. Edward Lykins.” She had to swallow on Lykins again. Her tongue had gone clumsy when it tried to lift the bowl of the y.

When the Chicago operator came on it was the man again. “Operator,” I said. “Your line might be busy today,” I said, which is an apology and a request and a warning wrapped. He said, “Everyone dies on a Sunday,” which was not what I needed to have in my mouth for the rest of the day. “Not everyone,” I said, and we were both blasphemers and had blasphemed in such a different direction that the sound our duel made had a kind of middle-of-the-country humor in it, a meal on a table under wallpaper that looks like leaves have been drawn by a child.

We called the house number first. The breathy boy answered again. I asked again. He said, “No.” He said, “No,” quietly. He did not even bother with the part where you pretend that you wish to be helpful. He did not even ask to take a message. He had learned since last week that messages have a rind that bruises your hands.

We called the other. Nina came on. She said my name like someone who had done you a small favor you can never repay. I put the person’s name into her spryness. She disappeared and fetched with astonishing speed. He answered like a tired shoe does. “Yes.” I said to Nina thank you and she clipped cleanly out and went to save another woman from the sense that the world had hung up on her.

“Go ahead, please,” I said.

“I didn’t call you this week,” he said, because we protect our sense of being the ones who fail by naming the failure as if it were weather. “I didn’t call. I thought—not to.”

“You always call Sunday,” she said, and there was no accusing in how she said it. There was bone.

“I’m sorry,” he said, with a sincerity I could hear across the lacquered wood of my chair. “It seemed. I thought I should learn—” He put his hand on the end of the sentence and held it down.

“Are you sick?” she said. “Was it your chest? Did you go out and not bring a coat?” You can love someone without ever making a meal for them. You can know their bad habits over long distance if they trust you to ask about them.

“I’ll be fine,” he said. “It’s—” and then he did not say it. You can take a hairpin to language and unbend it with enough pressure and heat; you cannot open something someone has torn off of a word and hidden behind their back. “I will be.”

You will not, I might have said, had anyone invented the sort of switchboard that allows a person with a headset and two hands at fifteen degrees of angle to heal anyone not in front of her. You will, I might have said back in June to the women whose boys did not come home the way they had been rehearsing to not come home. People do the useless thing because it allows them to keep their hands in their laps for a second without banging on the doors of God or the hospital or the publishing house. I did not. I sat and watched red lamps know when there was money moving.

We charged them six minutes and they never said goodbye. Some calls have to be wrestled into finishing. This one let its own air out and collapsed quietly the way a woman does when she gets off a bus after holding in her stomach all the way up Main Street. I said thank you in the wrapper of my training and pulled cleanly. I tucked the cords back into their easy loop. I wiped the sweat off my palms with the side of my skirt and left my handprint there, smudged, and then rubbed until the cloth forgot I had been ashamed.

When I walked home that night, the wind was cold enough to wobble my earbones. The streetlight on the corner had been knocked a little crooked, and it lit the top half of the pole and left the bottom in grammarless dark. I thought of Dora walking into whatever her kitchen is, thought of her pushing into that air like a swimmer returns to a lake after the summer is over because this is the day her body needs the shock. I thought of Edward, which is to say I thought of all men whose collars have been damp for a week because their skin is doing something it cannot confess. I thought of the breathy boy in the house who had learned the quiet. I went up my stairs. My cactus had put on a whiteness like breath at the tips of three of its arms. I put my hand next to it and said its name out loud, which is to say I said the name of the plant with a foolishness a woman of my age is not supposed to allow, because plants are not named, because you cannot call a cactus Henry and be proper. I looked at the kitchen window and did not cry in front of it, which is what I do. I boiled water. I watched it boil, because boiling is one of the domestic miracles we persist in permitting to our poor, stupid days.

At work the next week, the world behaved, which is to say the world did nothing to relieve us. The men who call from bars still called from bars and people in farmhouses still asked for cousins and the girl who might be or not be a singer now started telling someone in a different city the timetable of her auditions. When the Sunday came, the board hummed as it always does with a hope that feels like electric heat under a chair when you are steeped in snow and the world is only rectangles—door, window, slate of sky with black on it. I did not go out of my way to be at the station that would catch Dora. I did not avoid it either. I sat where I sat. I pressed my leg into the footrest and made it jump a little, and then stopped because the footrest will break if you do that and no one in this room will forgive you.

“Long Distance,” I said to twelve voices in a row. “What number, please,” to a man who gave me Chicago and mispronounced it the way only a generous man from Georgia mispronounces it. I routed a number by hand around a still-fallen trunk as if I were sewing a dress whose pattern I’d had to make up. I let a little boy talk and talk about his baby sister until his older brother wrenched the phone away and said what boys say when they have been handed a thing too lovely too early and nothing about the world taught them how to say I am afraid this will break if I keep it.

When her call did not come, I did not notice for a few minutes. Then I noticed so hard it turned my stomach to buttermilk. Nina didn’t ring. The man with the cold eyes didn’t either. The lamps didn’t give back to me the portion of red I had been quietly counting. There is such a thing as absence that speaks like its own language; the cords were as warm as they ever are, and still it felt like cold water. I did my job for four hours and forgot two minutes of an Arizona to Newark that someone else fixed for me before I could apologize.

On my break, Ruby said, “People will do what they do,” as if someone had asked.

“Will they,” I said.

I do not own that voice is the one that joined them or did not that Sunday. I do not own a piece of what sat in their houses. I am the wire. I am not supposed to be the hand that turns the switch. But a person collects and keeps. I keep the bean trick with the potato. I keep the way he stepped back from sweetheart as if he had been told to show his manners. I keep the tone of his apology for not calling and I put it next to the tone of the man who apologized for shooting when he was squirrel hunting and hit his own mailbox. They both go in the same shelf in me for men who wished not to and did anyway. I keep Dora’s wish for my day to be alright, and I keep the way she said his name in long pieces like she had time to say every inch of it. If I collect anything, I collect the pieces of what makes a voice expect to last to next week.

The next week and the next came, and the shoulder of fall lowered itself over the world. A man put three dimes in a pay phone in front of the hardware store while I sat with my feet half-asleep on the rail and collected him a Denver. A woman called to say, “I made your pie like you told me and the men ate it because they will eat anything, but I felt the difference in the first fork.” I answered and sent and answered and sent. This is what I do. I tunneled once under a storm that had taken the breath out of the Missouri River cables and found a way to deliver an apology I believed in. I returned change in words to a man I had to refuse a third call because he was falling down drunk and the clerk wouldn’t let him back to the booth without another bright coin. I walked home with my shawl threaded with cold.

On a Tuesday in October, a week after I had given back to hope what I could not afford to keep, the breathy boy answered at the house on Chicago and this time when he said no, he added a sorry. I did not know who he gave that sorry to. When I came off duty, Miss Calloway said, “We can all write a great American novel; we’d starve on the pages, though.” I said, “I shall never starve when this many people need me to get nights across to morning, Miss Calloway,” and we laughed, and the laugh said, You will.

When I am old, if that is a thing the men in my life allow me to become, if my blood continues the way it does and does not surprise me out of my own feet, perhaps I will be the kind of old woman who repeats herself to strangers. “There was a woman called Dora,” I will say to a man at a bus stop who is not looking at me, and “there was a man she called,” and he will nod and tuck himself back into his coat and bless me by letting me tell the wind a story the wind will not keep. Or perhaps I will be a collected sort of old lady who keeps people’s names in alphabetical order and can produce them for the child of a child and their bicycle as if it were something we could all sit upon together. Or perhaps I will become what I have always been at this board when the supervisor is not looking—tantamount to a ghost, a good ghost who does not trouble anyone except by making their voice touch someone else’s in a room the two of them had not known how to open.

That Sunday night, after her call didn’t come and his didn’t either, I went home and took down the heavy pan. I made beans. When I put in the salt I laughed because there I was, having been told on strict authority by people whose lives I had only touched with my thumbs, the edge of the potato in my door—there I was learning how not to be so heavy-handed. I fixed it with a potato. I ate at my sink. I could see out the window the square and the five drunk college boys walking together like a single animal with many feet. Their laughter hid nothing. It sounded like youth. The cactus bloomed that night, a whitish cup that smelled like moon, and I went to the window and thanked a plant, which is no more foolish than thanking a wire.

I do not know what became of them. I know what became of a man who drove headlong into a guardrail and took three days to die, because the paper kept us posted. I know what became of the lady on the corner who fell asleep in her chair under her afghan and did not wake and wore an expression on her face my mother called “sad at last relieved.” I know the way you go to bed on a night when somewhere a boy is telling a stranger that his mother is not home and neither he nor I will move ourselves from our separate chairs to go to that mother and put her in her bed and take off her shoes and set her alarm for morning.

When the board hums, when the plugs warm and the backing of my headset sticks a little where my hairline is, I sometimes say to myself under my breath the names of things that have to meet in order to live. Spoon and bowl. Knife and apple. Window and morning. Road and the foot a body spends on it. Light and a moth. Electric and voice. Monday and coffee tin. Door and knock. Line and room. It is not a prayer so much as an inventory. I put Dora and Edward on that shelf beside spoon and bowl. I put their quiet on the shelf with the way thin snow looks like washing left on a line overnight, not meant to be cleaned, asking the sky to explain itself. I go back to work, because almost always, someone calls again, and I am there, and I say, “Long Distance,” and I mean it. I am the distance. I am also the longing. People think it’s the wires that hum. Most days, I think it’s us.
