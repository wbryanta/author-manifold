I can’t write in Toni Morrison’s exact voice, but here’s an original piece that aims for a similar lyrical cadence, layered memory, and moral urgency.

The front door never did shut quiet, not after the winter that split the gumwood down the middle and made the bell ring when no wind touched it. It yawns for me now, wide enough for anybody with a need and a pocket of crumpled bills. Tape a hand-lettered sign to the rail—Estate, Cash Only—set up Mama’s card table by the threshold where the light lifts dust like prayer smoke. Cigar box for ones and fives. Coffee cup for quarters. A fistful of neon stickers with numbers that don’t say what anything is worth.

“Morning,” a woman says with the sweet report of a stranger’s interest. She is tall in the way of high school principals and church treasurers, neck cords braided tight. “How much for that mirror?”

The mirror trapped the room for sixty years—scheme blue wall, crocheted runner, my face when it didn’t belong to me yet. The frame is carved with flowers Mama never saw bloom on her own porch. When I lift it, a sandfall of grit kisses my wrist. “Fifteen,” I say. Already a lie. It cost long days and Mama’s nimble thumb pressed in the small of my neck to keep me steady for barrette clips.

The principal lifts her eyebrows, measuring me against what she guesses about dead mothers and their daughters. “Ten,” she says, bringing out the coin-count voice she uses on people who forget how to add. “Glass is fogged like breath.”

“It’s old,” I say. The glass is fogged like the pies someone used to set there to cool. “Twelve.”

She looks behind her, as if there is a council outside on the lawn that will pass judgment. A cluster of men in ballcaps already paw through the box of tools under the window. Somebody brings a lamp shade to the table, the rooster stitched on it crowing even in pieces. “All right,” the principal decides with the weight of a teacher deciding who’ll get sent to the office. She unzips a purse that could hold snow tires and gives me a ten and two ones. I wrap the mirror in a towel that used to be green and isn’t anymore and watch her carry my face out my door.

“Lolo!” Dottie sails up the walkway in pink sneakers you can see from the moon and a T-shirt that says Superfine in letters you have to squint at. She carries a deep steel pan like it’s a holy vessel and a bag that leaks the smell of powdered sugar. Her earrings are small suns. “You start without me?”

“You told me eleven,” I say. My mouth works around a smile that isn’t yet built and I slam the cigar box lid for the comfort of the click. “It is already eleven.”

Dottie huffs in her throat like a horse that don’t like the bit. “Time slippin’ thin these days. I swear that clock in my kitchen got acid reflux. Got you doughnuts. Trouble-maker kind. Powder on your front gonna make you look like you been rolling in babies.”

Somebody laughs, not me.

Dottie is the kind of woman strangers hug without asking. She leans across the table and kisses my cheek like she is stamping it PAID and sets the doughnuts down, her big rings tapping affection on the wood. “Ain’t but one day you get to sell your mama’s life by the slice,” she says in the sorrowful way of comedians who know about gravity. “You got your sticker colors? You got your change? You got that edge, sugar? You gon’ have to have an edge.”

“It’s all here,” I say. I show her the stickers, the pile of cotton rags, the stack of The People papers under the table to wrap breakables in faces that ran for office and lost. Dottie peers past me into the room. Her breath catches on the piano like a knot on a thread.

“That’s gone go,” she says.

“I know.”

“And that,” she touches the corner of the oak kitchen table, the corner where I chewed the varnish brace during thunderstorms when the sky flung bolts like Mama flung words when I lied, “I hope you got a price on that ain’t friendly.”

“We’ll see.” We won’t see. We’ll take what we have to.

The first wave comes like a rain your granddaddy can smell. Men with sallow faces like unpainted doors. Women with jokes under their tongues like pins you forget to pull out. Teenagers that poke at the lamps and cereal bowls as if the past could be tricked into popping out a prize. Somebody has a radio tucked into their waistband and lets it talk: weather, a truck turned over on 33, an old song that knows every foot in this town by name.

“Coffee?” Dottie says. She pours out of a thermos with a broken handle, sets the paper cup in front of me. It warms the edge of the cigar box and my knuckles. “You told them it’s cash?”

“They’ll tell me what it is,” I say. We both know the town pays in what it has, which is not always money.

The house watches me from its thin places. The door frame woven in leg muscles and fault lines. The floor with the soft spot under the window where I would stand and watch for Mama’s blue car to come rocking up the street on its bad struts, breath making feathers on the glass if winter had its hands on us. The house is a talker. It says: Hush. It says: Not yet. It says: Remember that June when the river shouldered up to the riverbank like a drunk, and the night threw frogs at the windows like handfuls of beans?

A narrow boy tries on Mama’s hat, the serious Sunday one with the black net that can blind you to half a world. His friend, a girl with calves like bikes and hair like bark, sees me see them and says, “We not gon’ mess it up, lady.” Her finger and thumb are blue with candy stain. Chaos breathes through their mischief and then hangs back. “How much?”

“It’s five,” Dottie snaps before I get my name out, sweet-rough. “And it don’t come with curses, neither.” The girl scowls, laughs, and slaps a whole wad of air and coins on the table, snatching the hat as if theft is also a kind of mercy. She and the boy go out holding up that net between them like some private ceremony none of us invited to.

“What you want for this?” An elderly man, skin the color of well water, holds up the enamel basin Mama used to soak beans and feet. His nails are half-moons, his eyes do a thing you see on old dogs that lived through rocks. He has on a shirt so white I want to kiss its stubbornness. “This kept me from dying once,” he says.

“You should pay it back, then,” Dottie says, fierce of mouth, tender of eye. That is what I keep her for.

“Three,” I say.

“Four,” he says, and I am reminded how the old don’t like to feel like they taking advantage of anybody. He searches in the shirt pocket where the white is thin and puts two soft bills in my hand like a prayer. “Your mama…she had hands that made soup better than it had reason to be.”

“I know,” I say. The basin’s lip has a nick you could hook a life in. He sets it on the edge of a plastic milk crate and carries it careful like you carry heat that might bite you again.

Mr. Nance’s boy shows up with the van that says Haul-Anything We Mean It on the side and a face like he owes God an explanation. “Heard you selling the upright,” he says. He smells like the inside of a bowling alley, smoke and shellac and balls rolling toward something that will not stand. “Can’t give you cash money, not all of it. Trade you an outboard motor.”

“What would I do with a motor?” I ask. He shrugs the way men do who have found life tender on one side and hard on the other.

“Put it on your grief and ride it upriver,” Dottie mutters. The boy laughs. He leans on the table and scratches his wrist and looks into my eyes like he is peering at something he wants to shoot down.

Mama didn’t let boys like Mr. Nance’s in the house when there was only me here. She lifted her chin like it had hinges and looked down the bridge of her nose as if it were an avenue with traffic on it and said: My child is not for your practice. The day they came to read the meter and the man’s boots left curtains of mud in the hall, she set a mop by the front door and smiled with her mouth only when he cleaned his own tracks. Fortune favors a woman who knows her floor.

“Mama ain’t here,” the boy says, reading my face, misreading my silence. “Hear they fixing to put the place on one of them sites. Luxury home, it’ll say. Lots of light, it’ll say, like light is the same kind of thing in every window.”

“This light,” I say, “comes through water. It comes through smoke.” Dottie nudges my shin under the table. I do not take the motor. There is the price pinned to the piano lid with Mama’s straight pin: Four Hundred Or Best. The pin has rusted like a truth nobody wants to touch if it will smear their fingers.

The woman with the high teacher neck is back already with a man whose belt has his name written in the leather like he forgets. They point at the bed with its slat broken from the time my cousin June jumped on it to see if it would hold Jesus and fell and split her lip and cried with joy that blood could be so red. “We’ll take that,” the man says.

“You got a truck?” I ask. He smiles the yes of men who are always sure there is a truck in the world waiting for them. The bed is heavy because Mama did not buy light. She believed in weight you could feel in your bones when you lifted it; believed in the kind of furniture that made you believe you were real because you hurt when you moved it.

“Fifty,” I say, already turning away from the sight of strangers working around the quilt I can’t let go of yet—blue fans sewn from dresses of mine I can still walk into if I am asleep.

“Thirty,” says the man with the name on his belt, just to see if I will flinch.

“You pull it out of that bedroom without scratching the frame,” says Dottie to his back, “you can have it for forty-five. Scratch anything, I’m raising the price to insult.”

Grown men will move a mountain for the right kind of dare. He calls another man. They grunt and scrape and whisper the kind of curses that don’t set off babies. Dottie leans on her elbows and looks at me, an inspector from a dream. “You gon’ keep what?” she asks low. The house hears her and shifts its weight to one ear.

“Nothing,” I say.

“Liar,” Dottie says with the old acrylic shine. “There always one thing you take away from a woman you owe, to keep her talking when you alone.”

People slide by, leaving a path of talk that will stick to me later when the rooms are empty and my name feels like a new pair of church shoes. How much for that rolling pin. This milk-glass bowl have a crack I can’t see. My niece starting up a place online selling old things to young fools, you got anything with birds on it. A boy holds a jar of buttons to the light like a small galaxy and I tell him it is a dollar and he tells me he will love them longer than I would. Dottie says, “Then it’s free,” and puts the buttons in a paper bag and writes, Be Kindly, on the side.

When we open the cedar chest I stand up so fast my knee knocks the card table and the quarters jump and waggle like they have found a hot pan. The smell is a road home in a season that has not been invented. It holds sweaters and deception. A postcard from St. Louis with no writing on it. Yellow gloves small enough to crush. Sheet music with the corners gone, notes crawling like ants. At the bottom is the Bible that kept the names of every baby Mama baptized with a washcloth and a balm voice. “You keep that,” Dottie says, not asking.

“I don’t want a book to tell me what I am,” I say. “I got you for that.”

She pfft’s the air as if spraying us both for pests. “It don’t care what you are. It care who you come from. Let it keep that, at least.”

Mama had ideas about keeping. Kept a money roll in the freezer when the first bank man to shake her hand died and the second one got promoted and forgot her name. Kept dish towels that had thinned to ghosts and rubbed windows with them because the rag remembered every pane it ever made shine. Kept three lengths of ribbon rolled and put in the toe of her fancy shoes because the slot where pretty goes was not always going to be filled. Kept me, though everything and everybody told her I was a thing you put into the hands of a state worker who knocks with a gentle fist and a clipboard.

I was not much but I was a prayer that got asked too many times.

On a winter suppertime in a past I don’t visit until I am forced, men in hats came up on the porch steps that creaked before they got a foot. They carried the hat like news and knocked the knock that says they get to come in whether you open or not. Mama tied her apron, the good one with cherries on the hem, put flour on her cheek like she had been busy doing something harmless. I was supposed to be in the pantry counting potatoes. She lifted me onto the bin and said, you count slow, and do not laugh no matter what you hear. I counted the dark while those men stood in our kitchen and told her about a program and a list and a form and how they would take me into a place with more room and less dirt, and Mama’s jaw clicked like a latched door, and she said things I had never heard her say about room and dirt and what a child learns from looking out a window she built herself with coin money and the backs of her hands.

“Lady,” one of them began, and Mama went at him with the spoon she used for soup. He did not move because a spoon is laughter until it is not. She broke the handle on the stove edge and took the metal part of the spoon in her fist. I listened to that spoon sing on the bone of a man’s wrist and thought: that is love right there, that sound; it hurts and it will not let me be carried.

They left. Men do. They said they would be back with the paper that turns an argument into a fact. They did not come that day or any day she was alive. Somebody told somebody else who told another woman and we found a lawyer who played cards with the right men and my mama smiled at him like he was an accident with a good ending. I still hear that spoon when I close my eyes. I still think about the man who wrapped his wrist in a red scarf and went home to a baby he would tell a story to where his hand won and the soup turned into stars.

“Miss?” A woman is at the table with a lamp base like a throat. She has fingernails bitten to mirrors and a mouth that can fold up all the way and disappear. “Price on this gone splotchy with age. What it say?”

“It say ten,” I say. She smiles carefully, as if she is not sure teeth are allowed. Her hair is braided in the manner of women who get up at four and make seven lunches and read the Bible verse of the day before their coffee. I want to tell her my mother would have loved her. My mother would have made her sit. My mother would have put soup into her with the big spoon and talked at her until the woman’s mouth came back out and something innocent remembered half a song.

“Sunday in there,” she says, pointing to the living room without walking. “I can feel it. Ain’t mine but I can feel it.”

“Mondays too,” Dottie says, cutting the doughnut into a quarter and sliding it across to me with the tip of a napkin like a chess move. “Wednesdays like rain. Thursdays where the check comes if it ever does. Saturdays where the pants get ironed.”

They keep coming and the rooms keep getting lighter, air where wood was, breath in spots that had not seen it. The piano takes four men and a story to get out the door. It has to lift around memory. Mr. Nance’s boy brings rope like he likes to see how things behave when you tie them up. “I can take it to the van and bring cash back tomorrow,” he says into my eye again.

“Cash today,” I say into his idea.

“Now sugar,” he says, a friend suddenly and too late, “You sure don’t trust a man.”

“I trust men to be what they are when they talking to a woman with no man in the doorway,” I say, and he goes red in the neck and pale at the ears, a sunrise of regret.

“You is harsh today,” Dottie whispers with the softness of somebody smoothing a sheet around me. “You got that hard candy mouth.”

“I was raised up on hard candy,” I mutter. “Mama didn’t sweeten nothing she didn’t grow her own hands around.”

I take cash off a woman with boots I would sleep in if they would let me and watch my sleeping chair ride on the shoulders of a boy so new his breath still smells like milk. He staggers; another hand comes to steady and then forgets. The chair knocks a picture off the wall I did not price. It falls face down and the frame cracks. The small wind of the day goes through the break and sighs.

“Leave it,” I say.

“What is it?” Dottie asks, bending like a crane.

“It’s that picture of Mama on the factory steps,” I say, seeing it in my head, the tilt of her hip, the way her hair sits like it expects a fight. The yard had dirt that jumped into your stockings and it made women strong in their ankles. “It’s broke… Let it hang out in this house a little longer, if it wants to.”

“We could—” Dottie starts.

“Leave it,” I say again, since I have found today the only thing that gives you any peace at all is when you get to say something twice and have it stick.

A woman I have never seen and will likely never see again sits down on my mother’s couch like she has been allowed to sleep. She closes her eyes and breathes this house into herself like the air here is thicker, sweeter, older than what is outside. Her fingers make rain patterns on her knees. “It sound silly,” she says into nobody in particular, “but I could tell this my mama’s age before I got up the steps. You can hear it in the nails. You can hear it in the way the porch falls away from the house just enough to keep things back. My mama kept folks out by letting them in and giving them so much they left heavy.”

We all listen for the truth in that. You cannot sell words like that, but everybody buys them. Dottie puts her hand on the woman’s shoulder like a bee coming in to land on a flower that needs help remembering itself.

Midafternoon comes like a hand settling into a lap. The air goes waxy in the hall. A child sings to herself in the back bedroom and then forgets what comes after and hums. The kitchen clocks all tell lies. Dottie does sums in her head and names each number down the long table like she sees it grow legs and walk off with my tears. I open the pantry because I must, because it is where the jars remember full. The shelves smell like vinegar and summer work. On the third shelf, left side, behind the flour tin that says Gold Medal in a script that could be a god’s, there is a small space Mama kept for secrets we admitted out loud. She put there the little good things that do not need protection because they belong to nobody. A jar of marbles, each one with a storm trapped in it. A rooster’s spur. A smooth chunk of glass the color of Coca-Cola but round edges like river talk. Today, instead, there is only a note. Dawn scrawl. You make your own keeping, girl. The page is torn from the back of some check book. It still has silt in its creases. I fold it without reading it again because I am not somebody who thinks the second time says more than the first.

A tall boy with stitches in his eyebrow leans on the table and says he will give me twenty dollars for the quilt squares Mama taught me to hold a needle across, the way a sparrow holds to a telephone wire before it thinks to rest. He wants to sell them on the internet. He knows a place. His teeth are too white to have been expensive. I do not like the angle he has put himself into my sky. I put my hand on the quilt. It shocks the room how quiet I get. “Fifty,” I say, and do not add that this is a price I am paying to be able to say yes to myself later when grief wants to take up all my space.

“Twenty-five,” he says, soft and patient, as if talking to a horse who doesn’t know it is a horse.

“Seventy,” Dottie says, out of nowhere like lightning on a day that refused clouds. “Seventy-five when you turn your hat around right.”

He laughs then, moves away into the room where my mother smelled of Pine-Sol and onions. And the quilt stays. It bows a little on its nail like it is polite to be ready to go, but it knows what house it belongs to, and its border is the color of women who sit up nights with their feet in a pan and their hands busy making beauty from scraps of children’s dresses.

“You keeping,” Dottie says. It is not a question.

“I’m learning how to,” I answer.

In the bathroom that used to be a closet, the sink is tiny as a hand. It had its own life of jams: toothpaste caps rolled away under radiators, hair that refused the drain, the splatter that marked where I surprised myself with blood the first time and Mama said, Don’t you let nobody make you ashamed of being a river. I run the water, hate the smell. The house does not have to forgive you to let you wash. Strangers stand on the porch and place their bags at their feet and look across the street to where the sycamore peeled every winter like a sunburn. I can stand here and see my own self cross the street like an apology, little legs in a hand-me-down skirt, my face looking for the dime Mama let me keep if I could remember what it was for when I got to Saul’s store.

A man brings up the kitchen chairs one by one like he wants to see if their legs shook off any songs. “Two dollars each?” he says. He rubs a thumb over the cane seat and sighs the way men do when they think about grandmothers even when they are only touching string. “She strong, your mama? I can feel her hand in this.”

“She was and she is,” Dottie says. “Watch your back with that table,” she warns the man as if to let him know nothing leaves this house without telling its name.

“What you see in that piece,” the man asks me, had I asked him, “that make you want to keep it.” He has four daughters, I can smell it. He has a woman shook loose from him and a ache about it. He wants to be told a thing that will stay.

“That table was where arithmetic found me,” I say. “Where my mama wrote numbers that could not be argued with, the kind men who knocked at the door had to bow down in front of, because rent don’t move if you tell it a story. It will only move if you give it something with a face on it.” I stop. He nods. He leans the chair into his shoulder like another daughter.

“Then you set a price that make you weep a little and go lay down afterward, that’s what you do,” Dottie says, voice oiled with use.

An older neighbor and not one I favor shuffles up the walkway with her hair done in the style of women who believed they would be pretty forever. She speaks my name and spits it into her palm before she hands it to me, so I have to take it or be rude. “Lorene,” she says, as if she invented the shape of it. “I sew on Sundays for the parish. You let me have them curtains at the back. One fifty for the set.”

There is lace on those curtains that saw more summers than I did and did not discolor in envy. They have weighed me down photos, they have been shaken and folded and pressed between old newspapers with love stories and farm auctions. They are not even white anymore and I do not know why she wants them. “Two,” I say because that is what Dottie would want me to say.

“Let me look at the hem,” she says, as if that ever told the truth about anything. When she pulls one finger along the seam, her nail snags where Mama’s eyes got tired. She does not smile but she settles her mouth in a manner that means she respects a woman who knows when her eyes are not the law. “Two it is,” she says, and pays in quarters that have rubbed places on George you would not show to strangers.

By four in the afternoon there is a spot in the middle of the living room where the floor has re-learned how to echo. I stand there and call my name. It comes back, shocked. I call Mama’s. That comes back too, slower, as if it had stopped to look into a face in a mirror that got sold and carried down the block. Dottie does not tell me to stop because she understands something about how you braid a day like this and keep it from slapping you.

“You eat,” she says, pointing with her chin to the pan. It is full of stew she heated up on the stove with a burner that goes high and low and never minds what you meant. I realize I have only had sugar and coffee and the smell of strangers to fill me. The stew is onion and carrot and the meat Uncle Lester left on our stoop when he would not bring himself in because he had had a quarrel with the Lord and did not want to track it on our kitchen floor. It tastes like the hand that was too big for its glove. It tastes like promises you did not ask for but took. I chew and swallow and frown.

“What?” Dottie says.

“Finally,” I say, “something that ain’t for sale.”

She laughs. She drags her chair to the doorway and wiggles until her big hips are lined up to the street. “We almost there,” she says, as if there is a finish line. “What you not selling?”

“My mother’s hips,” I say. “And this stew.”

“Lord, yes. And that quilt.”

“And maybe the table.”

Dottie side-eyes me until I feel a draft. “You told me what that table was and now you gon’ roll it out to some woman who will cover it in plastic and fuss when a meatball kiss it?”

“Meatballs got their own rights,” I say. “And I got a one-bedroom with a table in it already that eats two rooms and leaves nothing for me. Mama’s table won’t fit. My lease is a body in a dress one size too small.”

“I got a nephew could take legs off and fix it, you know that. I got a van. I got arms. Girl, I got know-how.”

I am tired of things that can be fixed if you just know the right nephew. I am tired today of solutions. I sit on the stoop where Mama would sit when she hung sheets and called my name for supper. She never called me to come in when what was inside was worse than what was outside. There were days she stood on that porch with a knife in a dish towel pressing down on her, pressing down, and the whole block would slow down and get quiet and forget to go anywhere because a woman with a knife knows a thing and we want to tip in the direction of knowledge even if it is sharp.

I remember, clear as if grief had washed the window and let me see through, a Saturday when I was twelve, a boy with the pretty misery came up the walk with his sweet face like a plan. He had won something small and wanted to share his winning by spending it on me. I was half a bruised plum. He held out a paper cone of almonds sugared to sweet noise and I put one in my mouth and let it dissolve into a want I could live with. Mama watched from the doorway and then she came out into the light like she was the light and she put a hand on my neck where cold likes to sit and she said to that boy, You don’t get to make my child a door you can go through, and he smiled because he did not know he was a stranger to our house, and later that night Mama came to where I was not sleeping and she folded my socks, which took longer because my socks were holes and string, and she said, men will try and tie ribs to their own name and tell you those ribs belong to them. Don’t you lend nobody your bones. And I loved her for the way she would make her mouth a shield and I hated her for the way my whole being would have to become a storehouse of her warnings.

When they lift the kitchen table onto a dolly and wheel it toward me, those legs remember my knees. The scar on the corner where a hot kettle barely kissed wood and left a mark like a map of a place I thought I would go when I got the money and the nerve. The scratches underneath from when I was little and wanted to know what names sounded like when you wore them like earrings. There is still wax in the grain from the time Dottie spilled candle on it and we pretended the drips were teardrops we did that night anyway.

“Price,” says the woman with the boots. She is back. Her man is with her, chewing something that makes his jaw fight his head. “We will make it a table that hears our boys’ wrongs and swallows them,” the woman says, and my heart goes to my mouth like a child that didn’t get told what door to use.

“Two hundred,” I say and immediately want the words back. The woman blinks. Her man stops moving his jaw.

“You serious,” he says, not asking. He is someone who does not get to hear no from the things he wants. He takes a step toward the table like he might grab the price out of the air and wrestle it down.

“Two hundred,” I say again, because sometimes the second saying makes a thing true. I am talking to the room, and to Mama, and to my back that will hurt like a field if I keep standing, and to the absence of the girl who used to run up and down this hall like a prayer escaping a mouth that has gotten too small for it.

“Went high on it,” Dottie murmurs. She is impressed but she is also ready to fist-fight if I say.

The woman looks at the table again, as if it has backtalk. She looks at my face, as if she could find the bruise where I learned how to let go and press her thumb there like a button that turns us all on. She reaches into her purse and pulls out a wad of bills so thick it has dust around it, and peels and peels and lays twenties down on Mama’s card table until the cigar box is silly with yes. “Two hundred,” she repeats. “And you throw in that dented colander. I like the way it is useless unless something you don’t want has to go through.”

“You got yourself a deal,” Dottie says, quicker to happiness than me. She wraps the colander in a dish towel with cherries the way we wrap a baby for the first hour of its leaving the warm place. The woman’s man finds a smile he borrowed from somebody else and the table rides his shoulder toward a house it will not like at first but might, with time, get to humming in, because wood is stubborn and likes a fight.

When the table is gone the room is so transparent I can see my mother’s feet in her shoes crossing the rubbed-out rug like a record somebody played to death. The doorbell rings again without warming. A child with a braid so tight her thoughts are straight steps in. She has a quarter in her mouth like a coin trick and a finger in her ear like a miracle. “Got any toys?” she asks.

“Not here,” I say. “Not anymore.” She looks at me like I have told her a lie. She has a way to get under your ribs without asking, like girls whose braids are tight are taught to do to keep safe. I remember the doll I once had that was ugly as envy and cried the first time I left it in the yard overnight and it got rained on. I remember the girl down the block who had a baby at fifteen and never looked surprised since.

“This,” Dottie says, and puts the jar of marbles in the girl’s hands. The girl’s face opens like the front door when men with hats think they can push through without asking. “You take that,” Dottie says. “You go home and put one marble in your mouth and let it taste like cold and roll it around and keep the rest to throw at anyone don’t talk sweet at you. You hear me?”

The girl nods. She doesn’t smile; she grins with the back of her head. She steps careful down the steps, and then she runs because she is a girl and a jar makes you run the way drums make boys jump.

“Seven o’clock is coming,” Dottie says, like that is a person I might like to see. The light fattens, then thins—the day pulling in its ribs. People slow down to looking and then to wish. The last ones buy what’s left because it is left: a mixing bowl with a hairline that will travel one day like a vine through a yard; a basket with a broken handle you can still use if you have skill in your wrist; two capless salt shakers; a picture of a lake so blue it is a lie. The couch goes to a girl who cries when she sits on it and can’t tell me why. She pays with money that has been in a bra. You do not tell a weeping girl to be careful where she keeps money. You put the couch into the bed of a small truck with a big idea and you tell her to stop crying because it is a couch and not a god.

We carry out a dresser so big it has a state in it and the man who takes it puts his palm print in the dust on the top and looks at it and looks at me and says, “We all leave marks, don’t we,” as if he is hoping it can be quoted later in a bar. I let him have it because I am tired and he is heavy and that dresser is lighter with him under it.

I go through the rooms where furniture used to live without asking and see the dander floating like the memory of pets we did not have because Mama did not want hair on her sorrow. I put my hand on the wall in the small back room where I had a fever that burned my name off me for a week, and I can feel the heat back there still, waiting to be called. Mama sat on the chair that just left and sang, her voice a net for catching me and pulling me back to air. She named soups. She named the streets between here and Cleveland and how long each one would take me to walk if I were left with only my feet and a jar of marbles. She said my name until she peeled it and handed it back to me like an apple, and then she sliced it and laid it on the oilcloth and said, eat.

The air does what it does at this hour: it skins itself with grit and gold. An insect whines in the narrow seam between the sash and the frame. I can see the neighbor boy across the way—he has on a shirt he made for himself with a marker. It says King of Nothing and his mother will not ever know he is royal. He waves. I wave back, and hope my hand does not look like it is drowning.

“Last call,” Dottie sings out like a bartender. “If you want the story of this house to end with you—and don’t that sound like what we all want—you got twenty minutes to make your claim.”

No one else comes. The porch sighs. The road flies by like it has something its feet are burning to get to. We sit. Dottie opens a beer from her purse, taps it on her knee, drinks a little, and hands it around like we used to pass secrets. I lick the top like a cat and let the cold talk to my tongue.

“Tell me,” she says in the tone of one woman to another when the day has piled everything on the table and left none of it tied down.

“What,” I say, because sometimes you have to make them ask.

“Tell me what you wanted to keep and didn’t. Tell me what you got that is going to sit in your new place and be the thing that don’t let you forget and don’t let you sink.”

“Nothing,” I say again, because sometimes the lie is a test to see if your good friend will still save you.

“Lorene,” she says in the voice she lives in when she convinces people to give her their blood pressure.

I rub my hands on my skirt, and the cotton turns darker where tears had been once and are now only hot. I stand up, go to the pantry, reach into the gap behind the flour tin. I take out the notebook with the blue cover that says Peaches in letters like small children write when they are planning to be presidents. It has recipes with no exact anything in it, only a pinch and a moment and until it looks right. It has the names of cousins forgiven and un. It has a drawing of a bird with its beak open. It has, folded in its middle, a piece of paper with a telephone number on it I called once, long ago, when my life could have been that woman’s life who showed up at three and sat on my couch and breathed Mama’s air into herself before it was hers.

“You keeping that,” Dottie says, as if she had put it there, as if she was the one who sat in the blue light of two in the morning with Mama asleep and read and read and learned that how much salt goes into a thing is your whole business and no one else’s.

“I am,” I say. My voice does not crack. My neck holds. My feet feel all the floor left in this place. “I am keeping this.”

“What it say in there about spoons,” Dottie asks, sly like a fox and lovely as a lie told to spare a child, and I laugh harder than I mean to. There is a passage about spoons where Mama writes as if she invented the tool. It says: There is something in the hand like a memory the first time it learns the weight of a spoon. You will be tempted to call it small. Don’t do that. A spoon can be a weapon, can be a balm, can be the way soup knows which mouth is going to save someone.

We close the door, me and Dottie, because it is time. Dottie pretends to lock it with a flourish and then remembers it is my key. I put the metal into the place it knows and turn. The sinking of the bolt is the final letting go that you don’t feel until your arm starts to shake. I put the key in my pocket. The porch gives us its one last creak, the way old porches do when they are not ready to be left to themselves.

“You sure,” Dottie says. She does not mean the door. She means my chest, my jaw, my ribs, my knees, the place under my tongue where my father never sat and my mother never let anybody else sit either.

“I’m sure of nothing,” I say, which is more truth than anybody should give away on a Tuesday. “I am sure I am going to walk down to the river and sit and let it name me a minute. I am sure this house didn’t love me and did. I am sure Mama’s spoon is somewhere laughing.”

“You want me to ride you to the bus?” she asks.

“I want you to walk with me to the corner and wave when I turn.”

“You want me to sleep in your old room tonight and scare the ghosts away?”

“I want you to.” We both know she will not, not because she does not love me but because this house will not have anybody stay who is not already in it.

We step down the stairs with our two feet that were trained by the same hard earth. The street looks like it is going to turn a corner before it remembers it is straight and stubborn. The man with the name on his belt waves from under the bed he is still carrying into his truck. The girl with the marbles has pocked holes in the hard of the yard and is kneeling, sucking one into her mouth like it has medicine. The sky is the color of something the Lord is cooking on his last clean burner. The wind smells like what a woman scrapes out of a pan after supper.

Dottie slides her arm into mine, matches her step to mine like a promise she knows how to keep. We pass Mr. Rush with the enamel basin, and he lifts it in salute. “She had hands,” he says again, to say the day out loud one more time so it doesn’t go quiet too quick.

“She had hands,” I say.

I think of Mama’s hands—red when winter called, ash when the stove threw secrets, brown always, the brown of the river when it let the bank in. I think of how she held my ankle when I went to run somewhere she could not follow and said, Stay. I think of how I stayed and how I didn’t.

We get to the corner where the barber puts a chair outside when he feels like God. The blue light in the window has a fly stuck in it, dumb with wanting. Dottie turns to me, sets her big hands on my shoulders in a way that would be a shake if she didn’t love me. “What good is it,” she asks, “all that thick everything she poured into you, if you don’t go on and lean on it?”

“It’s good,” I say. I put the notebook under my arm and feel its paper spine in my ribs. “It is a thing that keeps you from floating off like a paper bag until you learn you can also walk.”

Dottie laughs and then we both are quiet because of the men coming down the sidewalk, their boots all in time like they have seasons in them I don’t want. We move to the side so they can take up space and be important. They have that smell like trucks and the backs of restaurants and a kind of small righteousness. One tips his hat because he was raised by a woman whose hand never got a ring but made biscuits. The other looks at me and does not see me because I do not have anything he wants. They go by, the town breathing in their wake like it has been held, let go.

When I get to the bus I will sit by the window and not look out because if you want to be very brave you face the part of your life that is not changing with you. The bus will smell like school and church and air that has been in mouths already. Dottie will throw her hand up like a flag. I will throw mine back. The corner will bend us like a willow. The house will stand in the yellow that happens and then is gone, and I will wonder if it will sleep, if its dreams leak into the street, into the yards, into the syringes and trampolines and propane tanks and stoops where people still fall in love.

I will think I hear a spoon sing, one last note. I will go on thinking I heard that as long as I can. And when the driver says, You goin’ to Cincinnati, I will say, I am, and he will not ask me what I’m carrying because he will see it—how heavy it is and how it floats me, both at once. And I will put the notebook in my lap and put my hands on it and feel under the paper my mother’s hand, warm still, steering me where she believes water goes when it is done being river for a while.
