The ferry’s throat opened on its hinges and swallowed the trucks that had been waiting all evening beside the warehouses. The men in orange jackets waved their red torches like slow windmills, letting one line of headlights creep and then halt, a game of patience and inches. He stood on the pedestrian deck with his paper ticket folded into a narrow blade in his pocket, watching the white registration numbers of each vehicle tilt and vanish as they moved up the ramp. There was diesel in the air and a sweeter smell, like tar heated by old sun. Pelts of rain blew then stopped. He waited with the others in their heavy coats and canvas bags. A child somewhere was speaking in a high voice about whales. It was the kind of night where light seemed a liquid that pooled and shifted. He had not eaten since noon.

The call came two days before at his kitchen table where there were three spoons in the jar, the morning peeled back like bark. This is the coroner’s office in Port Wellington. He had put the phone down on the table without thinking, so looking back he knows that his face must have been close to the wood as if listening into the grain. Approximately forty years of age, Caucasian male, recovered near the kelp beds of Sturgeon Point. A fisherman had thought he was a log at first and had thrown a rope in a practiced loop. Do you have a brother named Markus. The clerk’s voice was careful. There are markings, a surgical scar perhaps, an old break in the fourth metacarpal. You were the only name on a slip of paper in his jacket pocket, the paper waffled and gray from water and salt, your number still legible. There was a bad connection. He had said, Yes, and then after a silence unspooled like tape, he had said I haven’t seen him in years.

He would take the night ship across the strait.

On the passenger deck there was a low gallery of windows and the world beyond them, which was the harbor half built of signal lights and half of dark. He passed the arcade machines that made a thin electric laughter in their boards, and the bolted tables with their circles of padded stools, a man asleep already, cupping his cap on his belly. He heard bottles clink. Someone coughed. He felt the downward thud underfoot as the ship let go its mooring with a sound like a door shutting in a long house. Love songs, it may have been the radio of one of the truckers caged below, rose watery through the steel. He made a decision not to drink coffee because coffee would unravel his heart and he needed to think methodically. At the counter he asked for hot water and a tea bag and sat down facing the window.

There was a woman with her back to him, hair pinned in a way that showed the delicate salt-wrinkled skin at the back of her neck. She was sliding cards from a deck onto the table, three and then another three. She knew the rhythm in her fingers. He watched a moment and realized it was patience, that game one plays against oneself as if training for old age. When she lifted a card between her thumb and knuckle he saw that her hands bore dark freckles as if they had been rained on by earth. He thought then suddenly of Markus, his brother, how his brother’s hands had been nicked and cut by everything he had attempted. There was not a tool that had not left its address on him. The top of the first finger had been nearly bitten off by a dog at thirteen but stitched back by their mother on the flat oak table with fishing line and whiskey and the bareness of their poverty. Markus had watched her without a tear, impatient even then about the time squandered on repair. I can teach you to loop leather through a ring so strong it will never part, she had said. He had answered her with his eyes.

You could choose not to go, he had thought when he picked up the phone again and told the voice he would be there on the morning ship. He could choose and then the choosing would follow him for the rest of his days. The clerk had given him directions as if to the house of someone they both respected: walk up from the terminal, take the road past the cannery, turn right at the cedar factory, you will see the municipal building with its mural of migrating salmon. Between each instruction there was a breath for sympathy. He wrote the directions down and then sat with them turning light and heavy in his palm, as if paper had a tide.

He moved to the window when the woman’s cards fell all at once into a minor order. The ferry shouldered itself into the bigger water, the harbor’s protective arms falling away. The wash moved behind the ship like a wide comb. On the far breakwater figures seemed to stand and then were only palings of rock. It was as if leaving woke a small animal inside him. His throat tightened for a moment and he put a hand there, ashamed of the body’s clear signals. At the end of the breakwater there was a light that turned and turned, measuring. Beyond that the dark was full and so rich with the night that he could not separate the sea from the sky except for small tacks of light where the boats and the buoys declared themselves. He listened to the sound of the engines and imagined them netted by wires as thick as a boy’s wrist, the men inside among their dials and bolts, the warmth there, the smell of oil and rope soap. He had thought that if he came he would be disciplined, he would keep himself in a careful room like a patient, but it was all sheafs and floods at once. He looked down at the paper with the address and thought of the surgeon’s instrument that cut open the skin and had created the scar they would ask him to identify. He had never been in the room for any incision in his brother’s life. He had always walked outside its windows.

The woman spoke without turning.

– Are you going to sleep.

– I haven’t decided yet.

Her voice was a husk. – The seats fill up. You’ll have children’s feet on your shoulder if you wait.

– Then I’ll walk.

– You’ll be one of those men who do laps past the gift shop.

He heard the faint laugh that came out tight as if the humor was for her alone. He made the small bow that strangers make to each other and stood. When he was a boy, walking at night along the ditch after a summer storm, he had watched frogs sit up in the road startled by their own bodies every time carlights hit them. He moved toward the stern now in that way, startled and then pretending not to be.

He pushed out a door into the open and a cold like glass washed over his hands. He stood a moment between two coils of hemp as big as curled animals. The deck was slick. He could see the trail of churned water making a broad white path back to the city. There was nothing here that belonged to him. His town, his kitchen table, his coat cut in a city style that people here would read, his coins in his pocket, were all irrelevant in this weather. There was that ridiculous thought he had had as a child when he swam in the lake that the water you fell in was your own water. The lake taught him back and forth the truth and untruth of that idea all one July. He had learned how to cut the surface with his hands without splashing. His father had thrown a stick that looked like a black punctuation into the water and had said Bring that back. Markus had dived without thought and had held the stick upright as if it were an oar. He had watched his brother learn by accident, the way geese elect their leader by following the one who swims fastest toward the wrong shore.

If I had returned your last letter, he thought, this crossing would not be happening, or it would have a different temperature. Markus’s letter had been two winters ago and had been more drawing than words. His brother always drew like one who had imitated a teacher in an old book and then forgot the lesson and invented his instrument. There had been a shack drawn carefully with its boards hatched one way and then another, under them the faint remark in pencil You should visit. No address, only a place name, Sturgeon. At the bottom of the page a fish doodled like a blade. He had remembered the carpenter’s square they shared as boys and the small fights over whose it was, each claiming it while the other sharpened his blade on the red brick of the porch. He had thought of the way that square had an emblem stamped in its metal, an eagle or heron, and had adored the small exactness of that bird. He had folded the letter into the drawer where all the notices went: taxes, school photography of the year his daughter refused to open her mouth, the appointment card for a dentist who was almost a friend. The letter had been there like a moth pinned softly to cork. He had not gone to Sturgeon. He had not.

There had been a woman when they were men who were not yet tired, and she had been more in love with Markus or with the idea of him one shares only after a drink and a good winter. He remembered her hair in his hands as they both listened to the sounds on the stairs, hoping he would or would not come. They were in their twenties. The world’s brutality was a door they had not yet leaned their heads against. He and Markus had sat on the roof of their father’s house that autumn and thrown nails into the gutter just to see them gleam. That was after their mother had gone in a truck with a man named Jacques who smoked long brown cigarettes and bought them a toy each in an attempt to seal their mouths. His brother had chosen a compass and had broken it trying to pry it open farther than it was built to go. He had chosen a book of postage stamps then lost it. They had both been lost and tried to be bitter. It was not elegant and it did not make them clever.

He walked along the deck listening to his feet. He leaned on a low rail and looked into the spin of the propellers. A boy appeared beside him raising his chin to see as far as he could manage. The boy’s breath made a small white flag. The boy said nothing and then – Do you think we’ll see whales.

– At night.

– I saw them last time.

– Then you have more luck than me.

– They came like this, the boy said and drew his hand up through the air, turned it, and let it fall, the shape of a back and its curve, the place where water and skin divide.

You could live a whole life on the skeleton of a few gestures like that, he thought, and you could drown anyway.

He slept a while in a blue chair under a polygon of light. He realized when he woke that he had fallen into the one sleep he trusted, that short shutter of the body that takes nothing with it. A man in a wool hat had come and placed himself lengthwise on the carpets with his coat as a tent. A woman with a baby slid her fingers along the baby’s ear as if reading it. Somebody put coins into the machine and small chips of melody leaped a few feet then fell back like fish. He rubbed his eyes and they were dry. He had dreamed the ditch in the field behind their house with its slow water and the long crickets thrown like pinheads into the grass. In the dream he knelt to catch one and his hands came up black from the soil and he knew that he had dug too deep and disturbed something that wanted to be left where it was. He woke with that behind his eyes and drank the last mouthful of tea, now cold enough to give no pleasure.

When the crossing was half accomplished a man sat down where the woman had been solitaireing. He wore the coat of a factory supervisor, one notch better than the men he supervised but still the same smell. He took out cards as well and spread them. The table was now a place of repetition. The supervisor glanced at him and then at the window where now nothing was visible but their own reflections sliding like poor ghosts. He wanted to ask him whether he was going home or leaving it, but questions like that you can’t ask on a ship because everyone is in the middle of their life and it’s an obscenity to require them to answer at the precise center of it. He opened his mouth and then touched the paper with the directions under his hand and closed it. He had become a man who told himself to hush. He had become his father’s mouth.

When they were twelve and fourteen their father had taken them upriver in his truck to a dam and had spoken of the water with a caution he didn’t use with anything else. Don’t turn your back on that, he had said. Even the calm of it can roll you into a new family of stones. Markus had thrown a pebble and watched the rings extend and extend until they touched the concrete, and he had said That’s how long it is, which meant the world, he had meant the world. He could not say that now to his brother without being laughed at or hugged. It was always one or the other with Markus. He had remembered the night they broke into the school to see the prize lists posted early and Markus had stood in the hallway with a windowed case of trophies and had not moved for minutes, as if the power of looking was itself a form of theft. There was a ribbon hanging down from the case like a tongue. They had taken nothing. He had gotten out first and run so that Markus thought he had been abandoned and the thought was like a particle that moved under the room of their years and shifted the furniture a hair in the soundless nights.

The sea was steady now, or this ship was too heavy to notice its restlessness. He thought of the phone, the voice that had named his brother not with the softness of nickname or family, but flat as a hospital list. That sound had underscored the truth that you can live in other rooms and yet be found. He had not asked who had written his number on the slip of paper. Markus had never carried a wallet, he had wrapped what he needed into a red bandana like an actor in an old photograph, stubborn about his mythology even when his work dirtied it beyond use. Their last words were not kind. He tried not to embellish them. There had been a winter day, one of those days when the sunlight is a memory of another latitude and flat across the fields like brushed tin. Markus had arrived with two men he didn’t name and the three had loaded lumber into the back of a truck. The lumber was from a worksite that had claimed it would pay later, but that was a math Markus never trusted. He had said – It’s my wage. And he had said – It’s theft. Markus had thrown a length of wood into the truck bed and it made a sound like the answer to a question. You and your mouth, he had said. And he had laughed. It had been fine then. They had smoked three cigarettes between them. At the end he had said – Come in, have a meal. And Markus had said – I will, and there was a smile and another meaningless repetition of the way their bodies tended to lean against each other’s sentences. And then at the threshold Markus had taken his boots off because he remembered, and he had stepped on the mat with his big socks and left damp prints. They had eaten and his wife had had her small face guarded like a lighthouse. She had not liked Markus and then had liked him. It had been that winter. It had been longer ago than he wanted it to be. After that there were months when he heard of him by chance. Port work, a casino dealing cards without a drink in his hand, a season in the cannery, the notebook of a foreman’s wife where names were written down by habit and later crossed away. And then silence. The silence in which a brother becomes a weather.

Morning was something that gathered at the outlines of the windows and then ran along the baseboards of sky long before it had the courage to show itself. He watched the lint begin, the gray uncolor, then the finer shade that admits to blue. The supervisor at the table had fallen asleep with his head in the crook of his arm, the cards face-up like a set of untruths. The boy with the whales was curled inside a woman’s coat now, the coat too small. He stood and the blood came a little poorly to his legs. He pressed the cool of the glass with both hands as if he could measure their warmth against this water and know something. In the morning, he would walk up a road, and in a building under bright lights they would ask him to stand at a table and identify which parts of the world were his. He was one of those people who tries to be precise at the exact moment the world asks for emotion. He feared he would give them adjectives and no facts. He feared someone would write down that he had been unhelpful.

A man in coveralls came into the lounge and turned all the chairs back into their honest positions in a ritual that had no audience. He carried a plastic bucket in which a rag lived. He did not look at anyone. He walked by with a light in his eyes as if he were everywhere above it. These were the people he trusted because they never made a ceremony of doing what had to be done. At a certain hour in the port town, someone opened the shutters of the bakery and set the bread to cool and watch the street. A man walked to the pier carrying a short knife in his pocket and a flask for his tea. Their father had been one of these, long ago, his mouth a line that made water of a certain kind flee it. It is odd, he thought, that I am going to present myself as a man with a small precise weakness, a brother, and not the person I have grown to resemble.

He stood again on the open deck as dawn was sweating color into the east. The water here was not black any more but a blue worked by silver. The ship was high into the strait’s throat now, past the islands like low hats hung on pegs in the fog. A gull came like a clumsy minister and scolded nothing. He saw the first road of the other shore, a scar cut into a hill. A radio antenna stood its thin post against the light. He held the rail and let the wet anchoring cold tell him he was in a place where the truth of things was not decor, not choice. He said his brother’s name out loud and it struck the air and then was eaten by it. He said it again and imagined it alighting like a bird on the deck, ridiculous and living. He laughed because the mind makes these scenes to comfort itself.

The docking was less of a ceremony than the departure. Docking is a request, a negotiation with what does not want to hold you and yet agrees because that is its work. The ropes were thrown, gathered, worked into cleats by hands he could not see, there was a tremor in the body of the ship as if it had admitted its own weight and was not alarmed by it. He moved with the others to the ramp. The doors of the trucks were opened below and an engine caught like a cough then stabilized into its patient life. The woman with the cards was now holding a scarf, she wrapped it like a decision. He followed a family up and then down, his feet finding spaces where to step was allowed. The air was colder here than on the other shore and had a different sound in it, the particular ring of this harbor, the cry of the single gull that had not learned its place and was learning every morning. He walked with his bag, which had nothing in it that mattered except a shirt and a toothbrush as if he were a child who hoped to be invited to spend the night. There is a way the body carries itself when it is going toward a building like the one he was going to: smaller, as if it were ashamed of its height, hands visible, nothing in them, mouth closed.

He walked up from the terminal, the road with its wetness rising into his cuffs. The cedar factory was a long barn of iron siding and an open smell like rain itself is a timber. Men were at the far end sorting lengths on belts that brought them almost to their hands. The salmon painted on the municipal building had been done by a person who loved them with a mechanical love but nevertheless had done well their quickness and turned backs, their forwarding force. He went through the glass doors where there was a girl with hair the color of leaves that never grow here tucked above her ear. He said the name of the man who had called him and she said – He’ll be right out, as if he were a friend arriving from a smoke and not a man behind a door that said no public access. He sat in a chair that had been built for patience. A boy came in for a permit to cut a big tree and his mother repeated his answers like a translation. There was a bulletin board with sheets about whales and the way they get lost, about resuscitation techniques, about taxes, the usual liturgy of our age. He read them and put them down again.

A man with a tie sheened by years of rope burn came and stood with him.

– Mr. Keane.

– Yes.

– Thank you for coming.

They shook hands and the other man’s hand was two degrees warmer or colder than his and it made an impression which his nerves filed away. He followed him down a corridor that had doors every four feet as if the building had been built according to a rule and people had to live by that rule. On each door was an anodized plaque with a surname and an initial like in those office parks where life is immaculate and has no lint. He had the sensation that he was inside a ship, that the windows of the municipal building were only for show and the walls held the sky back from themselves.

– Let me say something before we go in.

– Yes.

– You may recognize him right away or you may not. The water changes the particulars. He has been… He is – the man corrected himself – in the cooler now for two days. A fisherman brought him in. He had a slip of paper with your name and number. There is on his left leg a set of lines that look like an operation, a metal plate perhaps. Might that be something you would know.

– He broke his thigh in a fall at the quarry when he was eighteen. Our father tied two boards to his leg and then we drove him to the clinic and they put him in a bed and told him to be still.

– And did he stay still.

– He read a book and pretended to be still.

The man’s face made humor of this. He pushed the next door and the world came in like a soundless engine. He had thought there would be white everywhere and perhaps a priest. There was light, perhaps too much. There was a noise as of a very low hum. There were instruments that were merely cupboards and a sink whose steel was thinner than at home. The table was like the tables in the meat shop where his father had bought their Sunday roast if they had one. He had expected the nakedness of the body to be an assault but it was covered to the chest by a clean towel and there was a neatness of the cheek that he knew could belong to anyone, to a sleeping teacher, to the man who sells shoes. He stepped until his breath made a part of the air move.

He was not sure it was Markus. He was not sure it was not.

They waited in a trapeze of silence. He asked – Could you – and the man nodded, and slid the towel to show the left thigh where there was a long white line like mathematics. He had run his thumb along that line once when they were young men and in the habit of being naked around their own lives and had felt the roughness, the places where bone had come near to the skin and gone back into itself. He had said to him then – You are lucky. Markus had said – I am luck. That unpunctuated grammar of his that announced his philosophy and could also be a stupidity.

He could not bring his eyes to the face until he had touched the thigh. He laid his four fingers lightly along the line, as if he were playing a cheap piano, as if he could coax something by the pressure of them. It was there, the truth of the leg, and it folded a portion of his ignorance away. He said – Yes – and then he said – Wait – because the mouth had ribs too.

He looked at the nose, the slant of it, at the scar that ran from the hairline to the place above the ear, not a wound he recognized because boys knock themselves in so many ways if there is a summer long enough. He looked at the second finger whose top had that slight flattening that meant the dog, yes, the dog. It was Markus. It wasn’t. The truth hovered between them like a coin wanted by two generous gamblers. He looked at the left eye, which was open a slit, gone cloudy now as if someone had blown ash across its surface. He had never looked this long at his brother’s face all at once. He had studied it over the years in small portions, as one reads a letter one hides after each line because it says so much it kills. He said the name again and this time in the room the air did not eat it but placed it somewhere on a shelf.

– I’m ninety percent. There has been time, he said.

– There has been time, the man answered as if he too were remembering a room.

– He called me once ten years ago from a pay phone. I could hear cars. He said he was coming from the east finally and would stand in our father’s yard as if he had never left it. He said this without a plan. He liked the way declarations sounded. He did not come.

The man listened as if he needed this not for his work but for the bread of his day.

– I think it is him. I think it is.

– We can do a set of prints. There are still records. And the dentists here like to keep their films as if the mouth is the archive of a man. But you can also say yes and we will believe you because it is a brother speaking.

– Yes.

– You can come again later if that changes.

He nodded. He stood for the duration of a whole minute with nowhere to place his hands and then he lifted one in the air and placed it on the sheet at the place of the knee, which was not scandal but a farewell. He did not try to kiss the forehead because he had never done that when the man was alive and such inventions he did not believe in.

When he left the room he thought he would be allowed to wander a while and then vanish. The clerk, the girl with the leaf-colored hair, looked at him with that mildness of the trained young. He wanted to take back the morning from everyone and keep it for himself to rot. He signed a form that was quarter of an inch thick and wrote his address in ink that was too blue. There was paper that said the date of the cremation on it as if a birthday had been scheduled. He did not make any sounds when he wrote. The man with the rope-sheened tie handed him a card with a place where he could pick up personal effects. There had been nothing in the jacket but a wadded cap, a slip of paper. The paper’s stuttering that had been his name. He signed where they asked. He left the building.

Some people when they walk out of a place like that are changed in the angle of their eyes. He could not tell whether this had happened to him. The road here sloped in a way his body did not trust, and his step adjusted as if the earth were a ship and he had been fooled. He went to the cannery for no reason except there were men there and he wanted their noises. He stood at a place where culled fish came across a belt like blunt stars. He remembered how he and Markus had thrown fish back into the river one year to save them from the heat as if hands could alter a season. He put his hand out on the rail and a man in rubber sleeves nodded, which was like a blessing. He waited in that smell until it had become the smell of something else.

He walked toward the point where the fishermen tied up their skiffs. There were white buckets and there were black boots. He stood at the end of the planks and the water there was the color of nail clippings. He leaned forward and saw his own face in it but wrinkled by fish spittle and wind. He felt tired in his eyelids. He had the sudden conviction that he could turn around and see Markus on the street scratching his head, alive, irritated by the whole show put on here. He might be half hiding behind the cedar stacks. The mind does these things to sustain the morning. You let it, or you kick it. He stood there a long time and then he walked.

At the small bar the ferry people go into between shifts there were piles of napkins with a blue anchor printed on them. He ordered a whiskey because he had not allowed himself to order coffee. He had a mouth then. He wondered whether it was possible to sleep here on a bench and wake up later with some exchange of blood accomplished. He thought of the letter with the shack drawn on it and went to the pocket of his coat like a man reaching for a boulder that isn’t there. He had left it in the drawer under the oven mitts. He had taught himself to put things in orderly places years ago when his daughter began to notice the world. It had been a way to tell her that life was not solely composed of lucky improvisations. He drank the whiskey and it walked quickly toward his chest. He set the glass down and watched the watery amber hold the reflection of the light above. In the mirror behind the bar there was his face and there were two old boaters and behind them the news crawling on a screen telling a story about a place where nothing here would be relevant.

The ferry would leave again in the late afternoon, a rich day of two sails hemming this port to his life elsewhere. He asked himself whether he would stay for the burning and make a ceremony or whether he would sit on that ship again with his small bag and return to his kitchen table where the wooden jar held three spoons. He said to himself the name of his brother’s daughter if he had one. He imagined a girl somewhere with a map of the islands in her heart and no father to teach her to throw the stick into the curve of the lake and bring it back. He saw that this was his theater mind again, making balconies and curtains appear. He threw his head back. His eyes closed and the lids showed him red. He slept and in the sleep the ditch came again but this time it was filled with glass and in the glass there were words moving under the surface as if they were fish and he could not catch any to read. When he woke, the barman was moving his fingers on the wet wood in a rhythm that was a tune he didn’t know.

He walked to the address the rope-tied man had given him. The place where personal effects come to rest. The house was behind a chain link fence laced with dead vines. A dog behind the house made a low complaint without hatred. He went inside and said the name and the name that he was given, the new fact. The woman there had her hair in one of those arrangements that women who have too little time to make a show tuck up because they want to be almost elegant. She put a plastic bin on a counter. He lifted the lid. Inside there was the cap, the paper folded like the wing of a moth, a lighter with the word Rita scratched into it as if a man had learned to write with flame, a key with tape around it on which someone had written Barge. He held each thing as if the holding could restore. He paid a fee in cash and wrote his name again as if he were his own secretary.

On the walk back to the harbor he did not take the direct road. There was a place near the schoolyard where kids had hammered beer caps into a post until the post was dressed in a metal coat and he stood and touched some, each had been pounded there by a boy who was a man now perhaps or a man who was gone. He saw the letter R on one of the caps and thought of the lighter. A boy flew past him on a bicycle, his mouth open as if forever saying Ha, and the back tire sprayed a line of water down the middle of the street that others would later use as a guide. He remembered the first time Markus had taught him how to ride with no hands. It had been on the hill behind the house and his mind had believed it and so the body had done it. It was a method we do not praise enough.

He went into the general store where there was a coffee that sat on a hot plate in a glass thing, the smell of it older than the day. He bought a sandwich wrapped in loud plastic and took it outside to eat on one of those iron chairs with a scroll for a back. He chewed as if he were in a hotel at a time when no friends were available. He fed the last corner to a bird and the bird dropped it as if offended. He laughed and then felt the shame of laughing at that moment and stopped. He thought he would talk to the priest at the small church because priests have a way of being silent even as you talk, which is as good as anything. He walked up to the church and into the vestibule and there was a man sleeping there with his feet in his shoes and his socks bitter. He left before he saw the altar. He began to have a pity for the whole organism of this town which had opened its mouth for him and given him what he needed without being asked to believe him.

Back at the harbor the ferry lay with its scars and names. The paintwork around the access doors was darkened by the hands of thousands. He went to the seawall and sat and watched the ducks cut their chevrons. A child ran up with a dog on a leash and the dog snapped twice at the water as if this was where it came from. He would go back in the afternoon and empty his bag of the shirt and toothbrush and the lighter with Rita and the key marked Barge, and he would lie on his bed and say words to the air until the air had had enough. He would let the body they had said was his brother go into the fire. He would bring back a box that was heavier than one expects, and he would place that box in a place in his house that had never been used before. He could not make any of this beautiful. He had spent years smoothing stories into shapes you could bear. It seemed to him now an impoliteness to do that.

When he was almost at the place where the ferry line begins, he saw a man standing on the curb with a bag in his hand and his head tilted as if the sky had given him a message he was transcribing in the black of his eye. It was the small tilt that his brother had when he was listening to instructions and was not obeying them. He looked hard at the man. He began to walk faster. He reached the corner where the man had been and there was no one.

Night was a page that had been folded down and now he smoothed it with his hand. He went onto the ship and it made its noises, then it left. The man with the wool cap lay on the carpet again in his particular shape. The woman with the cards was not there but a different woman with a scarf printed with birds sat in her place and was doing the same thing with precision; this is how the world organizes our dissolves so we never understand what is missing. He put his bag above him and sat with his hands palms-up on his knees as if he were a supplicant at a temple he had no permission to be at. He closed his eyes and saw a white room and a sheet and under the sheet a portion of someone’s brother. He opened his eyes and gazed at the reflection of a light in the plexiglas until he was bored of his own face and then the boredom erased the pictures. Outside, the dark water did its mindless thing and its brilliant thing and he could think of it as both and be forgiven.

A boy came and sat at the edge of the table and then slipped down and padded away with his sock absurd, the sock with some cartoon on it that will not exist in five years and somebody will ask what was that creature on your sock and there will be no answer and no one will be the poorer. He was tired. He might have wept if he were a man of crying. He remembered the last time he had held his brother’s head, which was a night under a basketball hoop with the river beside them and Markus had tried to box a man who had insulted him and then there had been a small red page of blood and he had placed the heel of his palm against his skull and said – Be still – and Markus had said – I can’t – and had not. It was the comedy of their lives. You lose men to this kind of comedy.

Hours pass on a ferry like this with a pace that is not of the land, and therefore the stories you tell yourself inside have a particular cadence, longer breath, a tolerance for return. He told them. He told the story of the day the letter with the drawing of the shack arrived and how he unfolded it without thinking and then folded it again with excessive care as if that could absolve him. He told the story of that winter when Markus had slept in the loft above the kitchen and had left his boots under the table wet all afternoon and no one had been angry because of a debt in him you paid with silence. He told the story of when the boy who later would be a priest had dared him to dive off the bridge and he had done it and afterward had been named in some small way the brave one, and Markus had smiled and his eyes had half-closed and he had lit a cigarette and taken a breath and blown the smoke across the river as if he had created that water and that water would take him wherever he chose.

Around midnight the ship was in the deepest part. He went to the stern again and stood with the others who could not sleep. The old men spoke in voices that had had the fear rasped out of them by years of motors. – She’s running quiet tonight. – A bit down at the bow. – Look at that, look at that. He thought of his daughter for a moment, her face when she sleeps, the way sleep erases the mémoire of the week, and he loved that for her. He thought of his father who had died in his chair with the television on, a show of animals, the sound low, the animals moving their mouths in a language no one could ever translate. He thought of the dog that had bitten Markus and saw the arc of it, the way surprise makes teeth truer than they want to be.

At the place where the wake loses its froth and becomes a single torn lane he saw a sudden churn and thought first of seals then of a log and then of nothing explainable except life. One of the men beside him pointed and did not speak, there are motions that require silence to complete. The man made a shape with his hands like a cradle and then let it go. He smiled without showing his teeth and the whole of his body, which had been built by work, rested. There is great kindness in seeing something beautiful when a stranger is there to be in the same hour with you.

Toward dawn he slept his second sleep, deeper, in which one sees no pictures. He woke to the announcement in a soft woman’s voice telling him the ship was arriving, that all passengers and their bags ought to be with them, a sentence that could be translated to every border one crosses. He put his hands on the bag and felt the coins, the lighter, the key. He stood and the blood arrived quickly enough, he was younger than he had feared all night. He made his way to the ramp and waited there, packed with the others, the smell of morning bread coming across the parking lot, a slight fog that made the trucks look taller than they were as if heaven had stretched them like toffee, and he let the smell enter his lungs and kept it there a moment, then let it out. He came to a point where he could see the line of the road that he lived on, beyond houses, beyond his decisions.

He did not imagine a future in that moment. He did not allow himself the prettiness of it. He stepped forward with the others, his foot finding the seam between ship and shore because someone else had made it soundly and with care. He walked and the wind that had not been there blew a small note across his ear. For one moment, standing at the bus shelter with the metal bench, he felt the weight of the key in his pocket and knew too exactly the feeling of the lighter and the paper folded into its quarter and he thought of all the boats in this harbor and the one named Rita and the way it was now his sad duty to have learned that name. He put his hand on the cold back of the bench and kept it there until the bus came, and he got on, and he sat, and through the large glass he saw a gull land upon a bollard and then lift off again as if even it had second thoughts, and the bus rolled and settled and the driver did not look back and he did not need him to, and he went toward the house where his spoon waited and the air would be his, and the day sat down in front of him and held its face in its hands and breathed.
