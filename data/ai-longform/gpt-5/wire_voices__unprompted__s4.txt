He always began with a throat cleared away from the mouthpiece, as if stepping up to a public stage. The finger click on the coin return, the slot swallowed, the long breath after her "Operator," and then: "Long distance, please. Station-to-station to Winona, Minnesota, River five eight nine-two-five. Mrs. Harriet Bloom, please." The voice was gentle in a way that made her picture a tie loosened, a ringed hand on the chrome edge of a motel desk, a hat brim held against the swell of a winter draft. Not old, not young. Somewhere the syllables were tired and civilized. It wasn't the words; it was his way with them. She could have identified him blind just from the lift over "please."

On Sundays in the late afternoons, when the newsroom door across Third Street belched cold air with men in shirtsleeves, when the switchboard was a rectangle of soft cities and row lamps and cords that lay obedient in their snaking grooves, he would come to her as surely as a distant light glows steady between branches. It wasn't always him first; sometimes the call was another woman's order, a father fretting, a girl with a nap of worry in her tone, a man with whiskey making him bold. The world came like a metronome and she answered the way she was trained, in a voice that promised nothing but a path through: "One moment. Area code?"

She didn't know his name that first month. Around him there were just the facts on her ticket: MN-Winona, River 5-8925, time-of-day 4:12 p.m., time and charges requested at completion. The pencil clamped into the metal bracket at her left throbbed with the bus underfoot trundling past outside; she used it to mark down each minute in quiet stripes as if she were etching a ribcage of the time they borrowed. Once connected she would flip the listening key off and lower the receiver from her cheek, but not all the way, not tonight and not with certain voices that warmed the neck of the line. She could not say what had made her adopt them. There was a superstition that if you stayed on for more than the necessary nods—"Your party? Go ahead"—you took something unseen and small from the caller. Someone had told her that in training. But later she learned there were also things the board gave back.

"River five eight nine-two-five," she repeated that first Sunday in March, because sometimes they got the Street and the River exchanges tangled and you didn't want to ring a woman's kitchen in La Crosse when her sister sat expecting in Winona with cookies on a napkin the daughter had sent from Minneapolis. He gave the number again, this time slower, as if reading it from a matchbook. Coins hit the gongish box within the payphone like a boy throwing rocks into ice. The lamps over trunks flared and died as she seized one, dialed the area code for Minnesota and the prefix for Winona like counting prayer beads, patched the cord into the right-hand jack for that desk's trunk, flicked on her ring. In her ear, after the hollowness of a line spanning two counties and a river came the local operator out of Winona, bright, half a laugh in every syllable the way the Wisconsin girls were. "Winona," she sang. "Long distance. River five eight nine-two-five," Lila said. She didn't add please. You didn't please your own. Winona took the number like a bite of good bread and set her own board to sing.

Three floors below where Lila sat between the wall Sinohry painting and the glassed-in offices with men bent over ledgers, street salt ate the shapes off people's shoes. In the mornings she could smell yeast from the bakery. Afternoons gave her machine oil and the wool of damp coats. The old building filtered everything that winter and gave it back faint as ghosts. When she cupped her hand around the earpiece after ringing, waiting for the woman to come, the headband of her set tugged at the flat of hair her morning pins had left smoothed. "River five eight nine-two-five," she said again, as one did, in case the distant woman had old wires that hummed and the words needed to cut a path.

"Hello?" the woman said. There were kitchens one could tell from the first breath taken by the phone. This one smelled to Lila of sugar on good aluminum. The first hello was always a little suspicious: The phones never rang for smell alone; they rang for women with catalogs to sell you a girdle, for stern men with badges, for neighbor girls inviting you to a potluck that turned out to be a meeting you didn't know you believed in until you found yourself nodding and halfway reaching for your checkbook. They rang for sisters.

"Long distance calling for Mrs. Harriet Bloom," Lila said, as if she did not already know the woman would say Yes and that her Yes would be quick. "This is Harriet." The motor hum of the refrigerator filled the second between their names. "Go ahead," Lila said, and cut herself out of their room.

He said her name the way one steps on a worn stair at home, as if the stair had a voice. "Harry," he said. He was already lighter to himself at the saying, as if a coat had come off.

"I didn’t set the roast," she said. "You were early."

"They started me late out of Mankato. The fellow ahead of me talked a woman into ten dresses she can't afford. I told myself I'd call before the doves went to roost."

He had traveled for as long as she would let herself imagine him. In her head that meant new towns each week but the same beige lamp, the transparent ice buckets with lipless tongues, the lobby chairs with their own leather smell, the floor carpets repeating hexagons so precise a man like this would not bother with them. She had known other traveling men. They grumbled at an operator in a public phone booth; they had loud shoulders and desired not to say their wives' names aloud where others could hear. But here he said "Harry," and the woman laughed as if the roast itself had ducked and run. Lila made a mark with her pencil down the slim ticket where minutes fell into their slots. Less than ten minutes cost less than fifteen. Most people bargained privately with the price of words. Winnie from the noon shift liked to say the company profited off miracles and half-truths. Lila had not laughed.

On her lap tonight, a red scarf she had thought she wanted when she went to College Fashion downtown on Wednesday lay with its frayed edge tickling her wrist as she wrote numbers. Down the aisle the other girls spoke around cigarettes cupped in one hand, lips breathing with enough politeness to look like exhaling prayer. "He thinks he's Charisma Sam himself," one of them said, and Lila thought of the posters the college boys carried downtown in March, the words End the War partly hidden under their coats. It was a year of words and waving. Her mother had asked if she intended to pick up a sign. "I'm busy," Lila had said. Her mother had said, "Phones matter, too," which was the kind of thing her mother believed and so it fluttered like a shade within Lila the rest of the day because she wanted it to be true.

On the line, Harriet was saying the neighbor girl's name for the second time in the same tone people used when they wanted to say God. They spoke of a neighbor who had taken to wearing her hair up because of a rash. They spoke of a barley cake she had managed without burning the edges. He told her that on Highway 14 the fields were threadbare with last summer's corn and there were crows in the numbers of small towns. "If you were here," he said, "I'd show you this hand-shaped cloud that sat over the road like a sign."

"And what would it be a sign of," she said, curiosity like a trick of light.

"That I was on my way home."

"Well you are, by way of," she said and Lila could hear the shape of her smile.

"Time and charges, operator," he said at about their eight-minute habit, as if not to be greedy even with what they had already made. She came in quickly; better to be graceful at it so they didn't end up resentful that you could end a conversation by report. "At completion or now?"

"Completion," he said. He always said completion. That was the first thing about him she wrote in the stolen way of her heart on the back of a separate slip: Completion. Also: Please. He said "please" as if it cracked something open that belonged to both of them.

"Did you fix the little lamp with the colored shade?" he was asking. "It wasn't the element; the switch was stubborn. The end table looked lonesome without it."

"You taught me to turn it just so until it comes to me," she said. "It came."

"You can teach a piece of tin to love you," he said.

"I didn't have your hands in the house," she said, and her tone broke into two pieces where something was pleased to be missed and something wasn't. Lila took a breath with her, without thinking. For a second she could see all three of their bodies: the man in a suit jacket fallen off a chair behind him, his shirt taking out the crease of the day as he rolled his shoulders; the woman at a telephone on a wall between a calendar and a row of aprons, the checkered dishcloth wrung once that morning for the day's use, water making a dark circle on the countertop; and herself, left finger touching a smooth hard ring on a panel of light, her back straight as girls were taught, a red scarf in her lap like a soft mistake.

At the end he said, "I'm watching a boy about fifteen with his hand to the window of the stationers', looking at pens like he's choosing one to write a surrender with." The woman said, "Don't talk like that," though there was a sound of wanting to be close to where he stood. "They carry those drafts in their pockets these days," she said. "They will take him like they took them all." And there the year took its place without asking anybody's permission. He said, "Not if I can help it," as if help were a thing one could mail ahead to the road to be waiting with sandwiches and the right country music. The boy in the window turned and wandered off; Lila didn't hear him go but she could feel the weight of the words on the glass.

"Your time is twelve minutes. Charges one dollar fifty-five, plus tax," Lila said as gently as care allowed. You never just dropped it; you lifted it enough so the ending didn't break anything they needed later. He thanked her as if she had delivered not the pinch of cost but the three candles of a small cake.

That first month and the next, when snow went slushy and the town wore the gray left by other people's feet, there were Sundays when he saw green in a ditch like a fortune and told her, when she told him her sister had called from Rochester with the news about warts coming back, when they said the words "frost heaves" like old people who have agreed to find pleasure in the ways the ground throws them a joke. There were also Sundays in April when the board lit like a theater and the words that came were not small. Dr. King was shot on a Thursday; on Sunday the men who called to their mothers in Peoria and Cedar Falls had a tremor in their talk like the lines themselves were struggling to stay in their holes. "It's a country built on breaking," one girl whispered into a cover of her shoulder the afternoon the lamps seemed like a fever. Lila had watched the supervisors' faces like one watches nurses when the body surprises you; the women had coats on because the company had turned down the heat with so many bulbs burning. That Sunday Harriet's kitchen was quieter than usual, the way rooms get when clocks stop. They did not mention the man in Memphis by name. They said things like, "Did you see the little ones on the news?" and "A white woman brought them glass of water" and "They said they'll be marching forever now." He said, "Not forever," then "As long as they have to." Their words were low and one leaned to the other over them. Lila, who had left her church pew the moment she had been old enough to walk out of it because there was an ache in the ritual that made her eyes smart, felt that odd thing her mother had named when she called telephones a kind of sacrament. Human speech had something in it bigger than human will. She set down her pencil and rested her hands in her lap and waited as one waits for bellringers to finish their changes so you can hear the silence when it comes.

She was twenty-seven and renting the upstairs of a house where the landlord’s wife did the curtains like magazine women did theirs. Lila had two dresses that made her skin hum and a good wool coat with a collar like something you might wear to a funeral even if you weren’t invited. She had a mother on North Lake Street who liked to divide any cake into quarters and then into eighths to show how fair she could be. There had been a boy when she was nineteen who said one thing with his hands and another with his jaw, and she had given away something she could not later name and only then had she felt it had been there all along like the presence of furniture you never notice until someone moves it into the hall. No one had married her and she had not married any of them. The telephone company had hired her when her father died and her mother had lifted both hands and said, "We will count the coins that enter the house like rice ready for a pot." In an era of dark headlines and boys in fatigues visible on the street at lunch, the work felt like a sentence spoken without raising your voice. Her supervisor had put a hand on Lila’s wrist once, early on, when Lila had gaped at the ranks upon ranks of jacks and lights and cords that waited. "It's knitting," she'd said. "You will learn the pattern. And there are days when you will unknit someone from something you cannot see."

By June the air in the room went heavy by noon. The girls rolled their hems. Fans chided the heat and the smell of cigarettes out the sash. Two of the younger operators set their bobby pins in coffee and made elaborate braids while waiting; the supervisors told them to mind their hair in the interest of the company. Bobby Kennedy was shot in a kitchen in California and the heat grew teeth. On those Sundays the world's gears sounded close even on the lines that did not carry it. Once Hattie Mae from up the row was humming "Abraham, Martin and John" and a man told his daughter about the pigeons in the church square at Noon Mass; he clipped the words as if any softness would level him. The same week Harriet and her husband—if he was that; he must have been; she called him "you who belong to me" once, while laughing—spoke about cucumbers and both laughed at a dirty joke about making pickles. There was so much to carry each day that the small things had to be folded carefully or they tore.

The routine was such that Lila began to mark her week by their rhythm. They did not waste stories on her; they spent none of themselves on the person who opened the door between them. But the presence of their call shaped the afternoon the way a table anchors a room and you begin to set things on it. When she tied her hair back with the red scarf in the mirror over her washbasin before work, she sometimes thought of the word "Harry," said with a drop as if on a warm afternoon men might pitch pebbles into water just to watch a thing be changed by touch. If she had to work a morning and the girls swapped shifts and someone said, "You get Sunday six to two," she would feel a calendar right itself.

The week he called from Sioux City he told her the train whistle there split him; his father had taken him to see the trains at a depot when he was seven and he still felt the iron patience in his bones when they crawled past, dragging their weight like an oath. He said the whistle sounded like his childhood teeth had chattered, and she laughed. Another week he called late and she heard the lobby radio behind him tinny with the Tigers game; he said thirteen innings; she said your heart will give out; he said only if I had one; she said hush.

On a Sunday that fell in August when the corn stood up and looked every sun in the face the world had ever made, he did not come. There were telephones from horseshoe clubs, angry women ignoring their assignment of anger, baby cries against mouthpieces. There was a man who wanted to be connected to a woman who had already told him to be a gentleman. She pretended not to know his voice around the third time. "The line is busy," she said. It wasn't; the line was living. And in the long pool of seconds in which she expected the coin clinks of her traveling man to begin there were none. At five past eight she checked her watch against the clock that belonged to all of them and felt the first small shrill of fear that had broken nothing yet.

It is a truth about long lines of any kind that they carry across them not only words but patterns. After a while you learn the pauses of people who speak to you. You learn the kind of silence that means the person you asked for is in the yard and the kind that means the person you asked for is in the ground. So when, on Tuesday afternoon at 1:10, an operator light flashed with the steady insistence of the hotel switchboard on the corner of Third and Vine, and she plugged in and said, "Operator," and from the other end came the very particular air of a house in Winona that made kitchen heat and the dust of flour and a clock tick cohere into a tone, and the woman said, "Long distance? I'd like to place a call," she knew the number without the woman saying and yet of course the number the woman spoke was not a number in Winona but something that hovered like the sound of an unmanned screen door. "Do you have the Belvedere Motor Lodge in Owatonna? I think he's there still? He said Owatonna." Words were careful and heavy as carefully handled glass.

It is against the rules to feel your heartbeat in your request for an out-of-town line, and yet the line will take it anyway. "One moment," Lila said, already reaching for the Minnesota trunk and the station-to-station listing. Belvedere Motor Lodge Owatonna; she could see the stiff thin letters of the sign, the capital loops like old ironwork. The Owatonna operator came on and Lila made her voice ordinary and repeated the request and waited with them while Owatonna rang a bell on a counter wood-polished by arms. It rang five times, and then the soft telephone voice of a woman at the desk said, "Belvedere," and her own voice seemed to carry smoke from the way the girls in Owatonna might live their evenings. "Room two oh eight, Mr. Bloom?" Lila heard Harriet swallow the letter of his name.

"One moment," said Belvedere. Lila's left hand held the pencil ready to make the first flick on a timesheet; with her right she held the plug anchored as if a tug from the other side might pull it free. The Owatonna woman came back. "Two oh eight is vacated. He checked out yesterday morning before nine." A car door had sounded, a suitcase had touched the earth. She could hear it like a sound traveling through wood.

"Do you know if he left a forwarding?" Lila asked without looking up to see if any supervisor was within the district of her hearing. "No, honey," the hotel clerk said. Again her voice was a special kind. "He said he was heading to Austin way." In that word were seventeen county roads and a sky that rarely admitted defeat. "Could you put me through to the room anyway?" Harriet's voice said, and in that question lived the small, ridiculous hope that you could ring emptiness and have it answer itself. "There's nobody to answer there," the woman at the desk said, not entirely unkindly. "Thank you," Lila said. She did not want the woman to say more and have to cut her like thread.

"Shall we try Austin?" Lila asked Harriet, and Harriet made that small sound women made when faced with a decision about a thing that should not have been a decision at all, like a man calling home. They tried Austin. It is a large thing and a long distance map to hold a town in your mouth and press and hope a man appears in a lobby there as if he had been inside your tongue all along. They made two calls: one to the Swan Court, one to a place called the Pines. Men with voices like linseed picked up and spoke around the cigarette in the corner of their lip. "No sir," one said when asked for Mr. Bloom as if the person on the other end were a man and not a woman slowly turning her ring. Women at front desks said names into yawning hallways; their voices lengthened with the distance they were being asked to cover: "Mister Blooooom?" No one answered.

"May we try Albert Lea?" Harriet asked very politely. Her please did not break anything open, but colored the room they were standing in a warmer paint. "We can," Lila said. She pulled one cord out and put another in as if by doing so she could rethread a line through time, put him back in the second where he had lifted a receiver and looked out through glass that held the reflection of his coat.

In the fourth attempt they reached a room that had a radio on and a man who had to put down the paper to get to the phone. He said hello as if he meant it and for a dizzy second Lila felt the arc of relief run through the wire like the warming of an iron stove beginning. Then his voice continued, younger and yawning: "No ma'am. I'm Tom." And in the background another man said, "Who's that, Tom?" which sounded like a life that had nothing to do with them. The hope in Harriet’s small laugh—"Oh. Of course."—widened and went out.

"We could leave word at the Belvedere that you called," Lila suggested. It would not do much, but it would mean a woman somewhere would have to say her name aloud and that might mean a God would hear. They left the word. It sat on a spindle by a phone on a fake brass with a round, where other words pinned there said Call your wife and Mr. Kealy from the route came by and Left tie clip and Do not take home the pens. Lila promised to ring again later; she promised nothing. She was the one at the window where you paid tickets for a train; she could only give you the seat, not the companion.

She thought then of all the lines laid across prairies like veins that would have nowhere to go if not for listening. She thought of her mother at the table counting coins. She thought of boys who put their hands to glass to look at pens. At her board the lamps flashed and waited and died. There was a woman with a party line who wanted to know if she could use it; there was a man who said I know you're on there listening, Mavis; there was a teen girl who asked her if the company had time as a service because she had forgotten to ask her mother when to be home. It was a day the city's engine ran ragged and the phones sucked at the air like ventilators. She worked until two and stayed until three because Dolly had a cousin who'd had a baby and wanted to go see it at four.

At five-thirty, when the light in the office windows across the street looked like cups half-filled with tea, the Belvedere called back to the Winona operator, and the Winona operator rang the toll line, and Hattie Mae said, "Lila, that's one of yours," passing a sheet of paper like a folded napkin, and Lila put in with the heel of her hand in a posture she recognized from women reaching into ovens for cakes. "Belvedere," said the hotel lady. There was the sound of a desk bell double-tapped. "You were looking for Mr. Bloom? He left his shaving kit. We have his home address on his registration, and a spare home telephone, a number in Winona, except—" she paused, gracious with it. "—we also have a notation he had a Dr. Joseph Moore in Albert Lea. We could give you that number." You were not to take addresses; you were not to take numbers; you were to connect two parties briefly and clear. "Please put me through to the party in Winona," the woman at the Belvedere said, as if Lila were her operator, which in this moment perhaps she was. Lila breathed. "Standing by," she said, and she went through the steps she knew so well that sometimes, in sleep, she woke with her hands in certain shapes.

"Mrs. Bloom," Lila said, though the training manuals warn against speaking names into living rooms, because you might get the housekeeper, who would tell people at church you were too close to the lives you rang through. "Belvedere Motor Lodge in Owatonna is calling you back." In the space it took Harriet to say "Thank you," you could have folded up a house and carried it on your back. The woman from the Belvedere came on the line with something like authority softened by cigarettes. "Mrs. Bloom? Your husband left a shaving kit with us; we see a note here that says if not reached at home, call Dr. Moore in Albert Lea. Did you want that number? Or we can ring through for you and ask if—"

"I'm here," Harriet said. "Please."

Dr. Moore's line was tracked down as if it had fled into tall grass. It rang and a woman with a voice older than all of them together put down a knitting needle to reach it. "Office of Dr. Moore," she said, while some hunt of small dogs barked far away. "Long distance calling for Dr. Moore from Mrs. Bloom," Lila said, and it was a thing like a ribbon flung from one hand to another above a parade, meant to make people feel they were being watched by saints. "He's out at the hospital this minute," the voice said, and Lila thought, Here it comes; the air changed around her, as rooms will when a door is opened and someone we have been expecting and fearing is there. "We can page him there," the voice said. In it was kindness that had learned to fix its shoes. They waited. The line held like a rope between boats.

When he came on, his "This is Joe" was the sound you put on when you were on the fifth call of the kind the day had ordered up and you had yet miles to go. "Dr. Moore," Harriet said, and now they were out of form entirely and their wires had become part of each other. "I'm calling about—"

"The sooner the better," he said, and then slowed. "Mrs. Bloom," he said. "We have your husband here. He had—" and when men are used to saying words that break other people's spines they learn to clear a path to the saying with decency that sounds like weather. "He had some trouble with his heart," he said, and in that sentence was the image of a man at a counter lowering his head until he could see only his own hands. "He is alive," he went on quickly. Lila's hand on the cord had become another part of the same body as the plug; she felt, across metal and distance, something like a mouse running through corn. "We have him stable. He asked to call you. We thought it best to get him settled before— There was a—he was on Highway 13, a minor— If you can come." He didn't say If you can come you should, because women heard command in invitation when it concerned someone they loved.

Harriet made the sound of a woman who was already tying what needed tying and turning off what needed offing. "How long is the drive," she asked, as if the time itself were a thing Dr. Moore could change. "An hour and a half," he said. "Take your time. Don't—" He paused. In the silence Lila saw the doctor's hand in the air, making a code sign to a nurse and then back again to hold the line. "Don't hurry beyond your lights," he said, which was a sentence all small towns have told women for years when they were going to the hospital. "May I speak to him," she said, and there was an ache to the word may like a bright and a whiff of Lake Peppin at night when the frogs practice a religion of noise. There was page-rustle; there was the hospital's hallway echo and the click of women's shoes. Someone took the handset and breathed as if reluctant to share their air with anyone. He came on: Harry turned into breath. "Harry," he said, which sounded like a lamp being switched on but with bad wiring. He wasn't prepared to have to say any more because saying a name often trips a man into silence when the silence is the thing he has been saving all his life for a time like this. "I will get the neighbor to sit with Doradee's boys," she said. "I will be there before you have a chance to hate the soup."

"Do not let them put you on this table," he said.

"Someone has to feed those boys," she said. "I'll bring them both a sandwich and sit them in the waiting room like two saints in corduroy."

"I wanted to talk to you before I closed my eyes," he said. He had not yet closed his eyes, and their having or not having closed seemed to matter less than the ritual had decided. "I'm right here," she said; she had always been there, at the other end of the Sunday, and Lila, sitting with a city balanced on her shoulders, felt as if she were also right there, as if her shoulder were under the same loaf.

"Your time is not being counted," Lila said, very carefully. It was not a thing she was allowed to do. Time is counted. Charges exist. Men pulled their lives along like carts that listing under the weight of cost. But in that second she leaned toward the mouthpiece with the foolish hope that words spoken on her small patch of plastic and wire could alter the accounting that the world had already decided was required. No one said Thank you; it was not a thing to be thanked for; it was a thing to not have to consider.

After she finished the connection, after Harriet said goodbye as if she were standing in a doorway with an overnight bag and had to get the last line completely folded so it would not catch on the doorknob, after the doctor made the sound of paper on a metal clipboard and then someone in the background said the word Pressure as if speaking to the air about the human's nature to go out like a tide, Lila cleared the cord and sat with her hands open on her lap. She did not leave paper evidence. There was the red scarf. It looked like something you would tie to a fence to mark the place where afterwards you came back with a shovel to find what you had left.

Hattie Mae had one elbow on the board and a look that said she had lived in three states and three marriages and knew this feeling and that it had made those states and marriages both better and worse. "Go home, girl," she said. "You look all patterned out." Lila shook her head; she had the two o'clock till ten. "I'll go at ten," she said. The supervisor walked past with the careful gait of a woman who has eaten lemon and does not intend for you to know it. "Ladies, DDD is going to change some of our traffic patterns," she said in the tone that said she had practiced it in her bathroom with the cold mirror and it still didn't fit her mouth. "We'll get you all trained up. The lines to Kansas City are going to move over to direct, but for rural exchanges we'll always need you." It was the sermon of the day. The operators looked at the board with a possessive love like people looking at fields they had harrowed and seeded and whose harvest they had come to count on with their own bodies.

On her break Lila took her coffee into the room where once there had been a closet and now there were two folding chairs and a table with someone's initials carved into it with a nail. Dolly and Winnie talked about a boy from the warehouse who came up to see them at noon. "His arms had letters," Dolly said, half in awe and half in a hope of scandal. "Like the navy," Winnie said. "Like a man writing his secrets where his mother can't see them." Lila took two sips from a cup that tasted of metal. She did not, could not, speak of what had transpired on her cords. Words were for connecting; you could not spare them for the telling or you used up something you needed for the work. She sat without her hands doing anything and so they looked uncertain, like birds between seeds. Hattie Mae, who had not left her board in twenty minutes, came in and sat down heavily and said to nobody, "They call for help because it’s invisible work," and to Lila, "You feel lit up because you didn't make it visible but it went through you anyway."

At nine, with lamps already wearing her eyes thin, the Winona operator flashed through again. "That's your Mrs. Bloom," she said, and she made a little shape with her mouth which could have been mercy or could have been be strong; over the line such shapes are ambiguous. "Long distance?" Harriet said, her voice now wrapped tight and careful as if any slippage could start the unravel. "If it's not too much trouble, could I ask to place a person-to-person to the St. Joseph Hospital in Albert Lea, room number Mr.—" there followed the last name as if she could put the name somewhere inside the room by speaking it and then the room would not be able to erase it and so he would have to remain. "One moment," Lila said. Her own "one moment" came out low. The hospital operator had the smooth tone of a woman who knew how to make laundry move from one bin to another without listening to the wet sadness of it. "Room two seventeen," she said. "I'll ring." The ring carried the distance like a jug and when it landed this time there was not the voice of a doctor nor the sigh of a man closing his eyes but the nearly inaudible sound of machines that were the same noise as summer locusts, that same regular insistence that made people ask if the world itself were humming and no one had noticed.

"Mrs. Bloom?" I can try to—" came a nurse's whisper. "He's resting. He took to it," she added in a code of old country wives, as if he had married the bed for an hour. "I will be there in the morning," Harriet said into the room of its hum, as if she were addressing the bed or the machine or God. "Tell him I am in the building, like a light."

"I'll write it," the nurse said. Lila could see it: a small square of paper, the kind they used for vitals and fevers, with a pencil line scrawled by a person who had not sat down since four.

After Harriet said goodbye "for now," in a voice that made the words cross a wide field with only a small hand to hold them steady, after the line cleared and Lila wrote on the ticket the time, two minutes, no charges recorded because it was in-house, she looked down her row and saw nothing changed for anyone else and everything changed behind her ribs. She lasted until ten with the steady dignity of a person on a funeral bench who refuses some kinds of gum offered.

If she thought of breaking rules then, it was not with a cry but with a tedious plainness: she thought, I will call in the morning and ask whether he made it to breakfast and whether there is anything our exchange can do to help. She thought of calling the Belvedere to ask them to mail the shaving kit to River 5-8925. She thought of putting aside one of the red roses her landlady cut from the municipal park with a sharp knife at dusk—"They plant them for us," the woman had said and smiled—and leaving it on the table as if that table were one of the places that could be counted by a country that did not count women for anything but births and phone bills.

There is a privacy that the wire teaches you. It is different from secrecy; it is not for hoarding; it is simply the courtesy of stepping out of a doorway so that the person carrying a heavy thing can pass through. In her bed in her sweet rental she lay and listened to the night run itself through its routines. There were men laughing late. There was the train. The sound of the train in June had been the sound of boys leaving. The sound of the train in September was the sound of women not yet going.

On the following Sunday he did not come at four, nor at eight. At nine that night, with the street fat with the smell of last light, Winona rang and there was Harriet, and she did not say please and she did not say long distance. "Operator," she said. "May I trouble you for the time?" In her voice was a particular grace, the way people fall into saying the wrong things to the right people in grief. "Nine oh four p.m.," Lila said. "Would you tell me again when the rates change," she asked, and then stopped. "It doesn't matter," she said. "I don't know why I asked that." "The nighttime rate is in effect," Lila said, which was both perfectly true and perfectly beside the point. "Thank you," said Harriet, and then, in a breath small and not meant for holding and yet Lila felt the shape of it enter her chest and sit there, "Our neighbor kept the boys all day. He said the sandwiches were better than church." Lila said something she was not trained to say. "He is not on a table any longer." She gripped the wood of her desk. "He is home somewhere inside you."

There was a pause in which the air filled and emptied three times. It could have been taken for a hesitation had it not been that it occurred this way in houses where something had just left and could not return. "Whom am I speaking to," Harriet asked softly, not suspicious, but in that tone old women use at bus stops with strangers when they say, and you, who are you traveling toward. "This is the long distance operator," Lila said, which was the truest thing of which she was certain. "Thank you," Harriet said, not in the way of thanks for an item in a store but as one says thank you to water after walking through dust.

After the click the wire went quiet in that way it does when it has been carrying a long story and needs to rest. Lila left her hands on the board and watched the small red jewels of light come and go. Machines do not cry. The women at them sometimes do, but not in the way of children. It is quieter. The heat of the room stuck to her neck. Someone coughed. Someone laughed elsewhere. The pencil in her bracket had been sharpened by a woman on the morning shift whose pencils always came to a beautiful pale cone that reminded Lila of the top of a mountain she had seen once only in a magazine.

Alone between calls, she unpinned the red scarf and laid it across her knees. It was bright in a way that did not match the hour. Yet it felt in her palm the way his "Harry" had felt in her ear the first of the year: a small heat that says you have been near a life that mattered. There are days you choose a thing to keep. The company would be doing away with some of the toll jobs by winter. There would be new machines. You would dial your own heartbreaks. You would call direct and say please to nobody. When that came she would still know the shape of a cord in her hand. She had become in these months the keeper of two voices which had touched each other weekly across counties like the exchange of coins through a ticket booth's little rectangle of glass. She did not have either of them. She had their pattern. It lives in you if you handle it enough.

On a Tuesday toward late September, just as the chill began again and you could smell something baked cooling two blocks away with your eyes closed, a package came up the freight elevator for the switchboard with Mrs. Bloom written in a hand she recognized because it had also made room three times in an afternoon for a signature with a shakingness to it on a hospital form. Hattie Mae scolded the delivery boy for bringing it to the wrong floor and laughed when she saw the name. "That the woman with the hospital man," she said to Lila under her breath. Lila lifted the package. It was heavier than a book, lighter than a bowling ball. The return address was a street in Owatonna. She dialed Winona with one hand and rested the parcel on her knees the way women hold babies they have no right to love and do anyway. "Long distance calling with a package for you," she said when the kitchen breath answered. She did not ask how he was; she had learned a respect for the small walls grief builds around the daily. "From Owatonna," she added. "The Belvedere Motor Lodge. It is your—" she did not know if the spiritual word was permitted—"your husband's shaving kit." She had almost said memory.

"I'll come by for it," Harriet said. "I can't think how it came to where you are." "It was misdirected to our building," Lila lied, automatically making the wire responsible for a kindness it had not had to do this time. "We are good at putting things where they go," she added. "So you are," Harriet said. "It's a good country for strings."

When she came to pick it up two days later, she came with a bag that had seen markets and Sunday potlucks and names stitched in the lining. Lila recognized her from her voice before her eyes agreed: hair pinned up without pride, cheekbones determined by storm and bread-kneading, a good dress. There is a look women in small river towns have when they have both been to a hospital and walked home again without anyone to hold their elbow. They seem already halfway past you and back to their kitchen. Harriet put the bag on the counter and smiled at Lila like people do when they recognize the shape of a lamp in a hotel they've never been to before. "I oughtn’t say it," she said, as women say at the start of sentences that want a small flame, "but I felt like I knew the skin of your voice." Lila nodded. "I'm not supposed to be known," she said. "But I think I am, sometimes."

"Thank you," Harriet said, and now it was in the tone of gratitude for a cup of water from a passing soldier. "Evidently I do not know where everything is supposed to be," she added, and reached into her bag and held up a jar. Inside were pickles, their brine lapping at the glass like the creek at the edge of a farm that you are about to leave in the hands of a cousin for a season. "I brought you one thing I can fix," she said. "I make them because when summer is gone I need proof something of it stays."

The rules said to accept no gifts because then the company could not be held responsible for your behavior. The rules did not say anything about pickles. Lila took the jar. It was heavy, real. A cord. "He tasted them before he—" Harriet began and then let the line go slack. Lila put the jar under the counter as if it were illicit and luminous all at once. "We will take good care of your papers," she said instead of anything wise. "We keep time for you. If you need hours, we'll write them down."

Later, after Harriet had gone and the board made its own little sea of gnat-lights and the men across the street came out in their shirtsleeves to complain about the mayor and the lack of raises and the cost of sending girls to school, Lila went into the small room with the folding chairs and unscrewed the lid from the pickle jar. The smell was exactly what the end of a season should smell like. She fished out a spear with a fork because Hattie Mae had brought a fork and a paper plate as if the act of giving and the act of eating were steps in a dance that had always existed. They both bit at once and made faces at the same time that meant good and sharp. "Time and charges," Hattie Mae said through crunch. "Completion," Lila said. They laughed like thieves. And through the wall thin with old plaster the small bells and voices continued.

That Sunday, the line did not light for him because he no longer needed her to say You may go ahead. The wire kept carrying other voices. A boy called his cousin from the locker room payphone for the score of a game. A girl whispered to someone that her penny loafers had cut a blister into her right heel. Men told women how to boil the cabbage with a pinch of nutmeg to make it tolerable. There were proposals you could recognize only by the sudden urgency with which a woman at the other end began to cough. There were angers forestalled by the ringing key going flat. Lila remembered making the small illicit pronouncement: Your time is not being counted. It had been a single true sentence in a job full of rehearsed ones, and it tasted of brine and of something clean.

When winter came down the river like an aunt with bad temper, she took her scarf out of the drawer again. She tied it twice to keep the coils of heat in the morning at her neck. She wore the coat with the good collar and once even pinned the red scarf at her throat because it was December and a girl should have warmth. In the glass old as the building she could see the angle of her jaw that her mother said would be respectable in a lawyer. At the board the lamps did not remember him particularly. That is another kind of rule. The wire holds no one long. But in the bubble of air between the earpiece and her ear, she could feel across counties a table where a woman answered on Sundays now when any of her people rang, and someone else at the neighbor's urged boys to chew properly. Every job has work you don't talk about. This was the part that had no name, that you could not put on a timesheet. It is very hard to explain to people who have never had their hands inside other people's hours what it is to be a small bridge, to be walked across without anyone looking down even once to see the water or call thank you to the bridge. But some afternoons, when she lifted the receiver and said "Operator," it felt like prayer enough.
