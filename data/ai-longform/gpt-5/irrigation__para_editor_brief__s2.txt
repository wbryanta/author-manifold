By the time the sun cleared the eucalyptus, I had my ear pressed to the black poly and the sound of water was like a distant whisper inside bone. The soil was already warm through my shirt. You could smell the dust lifting, the dry grass waking its brittle smell, the faint sweetness of the peaches that weren't ready yet but broadcast anyway. If I turned my head, the rows tilted into the light like a slow parade, trees gray-green and sad with the year. It made a wave in me you could call love, if you felt like being simple about it.

"You're going to fall asleep like that," Gabe said, stepping out of his truck with the takeaway cup balanced in his fingers. He crumpled the receipt in the cup holder with his knuckle still in it, like a man who can't quite believe a thing until it resists him.

"I can hear it," I said, even as I couldn't—nothing yet, not really, just an old dog dream of it. "You don't get closer you don't get wiser."

"You put your head down on this place long enough you're going to think you can hear it talk back," he said, but not unkindly. He set the coffee on the wellhead cap, looked at the bank of the ditch where the star thistle had come up in a shoulder-high army, and blew air out between his teeth.

It was June and already hot enough to sting the inside of your nose. June used to mean bearing down for the cherry run, getting nets up before the starlings taught their kids to be thieves, making the big burn piles safe enough that if the wind shifted you didn't strip the whole west forty. Our grandfather's decade was plums and prunes and a few acres of almonds you could pick in shirtsleeves with the September wind knocking the hulls off like secret doors. Dad's was cling peaches for the cannery and whatever else he could keep alive. Then the cannery shuttered and the big outfits put in water as if they were pumping from hell's own springs. The ditch ticks the roster with a paper schedule like a comedy that needs a straight man. Our well fell behind and the water came up tastier with clay.

"Primer?" I said, rocking back into a squat. The purple bottle rolled from under the truck seat when we braked this morning and now had a coat of dust; I thumbed it cleaner.

"Primer, glue, two couplers, T, twenty of half, twenty of inch," he recited, pulling the bag from the bed and turning it over like a magician palming coins. "And your favorite, the cutter you call a toe-eater. Where do you figure?"

"Right below the split in the old cement," I said, pointing toward the wet scar, then down. It hadn't run since last week but the soil still showed where the leak had flowered mud. "You can smell it."

"You can smell money leaving, sure," he said. He sipped his coffee and watched me drive the shovel in and make the first bite. It banged against a rock, and the sound was the dull heart-sound of this place.

"Mountains must have coughed," I said.

"They're full of things you can't live on," he said. "That was Dad, his whole life."

I didn't look up. If I looked at him too quick at eight in the morning, when his face was still his face and not a stranger's, I was more likely to say the one thing that would lay us both out. Our father had died in December with the furnace complaining through its ducts and the trees cut black as lines on a page. He'd kept his facts neat in a blue folder and written in his hand, sell if you must, though the must had always been a word he thought belonged to other people. We cleaned the house that night and found the cigar box under the sink, full of his childhood marbles and a bullet he said he'd dug out of a fencepost when the neighbors were shooting at quail and missed. He'd made a note on a paper card and slipped it in there: don't throw me away yet. You can love that kind of romance or you can call it poor man's procrastination.

"Hand me the hacksaw," I said. He kicked it with the toe of his boot and it skittered and bit the dirt. I laid it in the trench and made the small cuts precise, careful as if I were doing something delicate, and the smell of the plastic lifted—solvent and light like the smell of summer water in a black hose. We had put this irrigation in with Dad when we swapped from micro-sprayers to drip. He said, it's a different kind of mind to trust a drop. He didn't like it. He wanted to see his water move.

"Tom said he'd come after lunch," Gabe said. He said it like it was a curiosity and not something we'd argued about over his kitchen table until one in the morning with my nephew sleeping on the couch and the dog whining because it could hear outside better than us.

"I told you not to. He's not coming out to look at disease and bring casseroles. He wants to measure our sadness in square feet."

"He's a real estate agent," he said, as if that were all the words that fit the subject. "He's also our neighbor. He also delivered your second kid when you got stuck in that traffic on the 99."

I eased the cut section out and the line softened and sighed as if it had waited to let go. You don't think of a pipe as having memory until you pull an old one and it rests into the shape it had been forced to hold. The pebbles in the trench were red and white. One was marked like a fish's spine.

"And you know he has someone actually interested," he said, quieter. "Not just the solar boys. A family. Owns a small jewelry chain in Bakersfield, doesn't know better, wants to play at it. They see trees and a view and they think they're buying a postcard. We'd be forgiving them their education."

"You're a saint," I said.

He didn't purse his mouth or flinch or get ugly. He sat down on the tailgate and watched the road. A big swallow passed like a thought, making little grating syllables to itself. We had a neighbor with a rooster that wandered beyond his sense, crowing the way a drunk will deny a stop sign. I heard him to the south, punching his noise into the heat. The trees threw little disks of shade the size of dinner plates and the weeds made their own logic underfoot, not a path so much as a discouragement.

The coupler slid in plush as a confession. I painted primer, then glue. It stung the skin through my glove. Two minutes at least. I counted, not trusting either of us to talk and get time wrong.

"You're only one who still counts out loud," he said finally, and took the glove off to scratch the silver of his beard. He was two years older but he wore his years honestly, in a way I thought was brave until I realized it was merely speed. He did things quick, it added up. You do something quick enough you can call it decisive and not say you were afraid to look.

I set the other coupler, we tamped the dirt just enough to hold the pipe so it wouldn't torque before it set. We left it open to test. The pumphouse was a metal board nailed to a lean-to and a pump that had been new when Clinton was a fresh movie poster. The switch ticked when it turned on. It was a sound Dad used to hear from the kitchen and say, That thing has a spring in its voice like a high schooler. I flipped it. We went quiet to hear. Water banged its antique called note from up the line, the sound of it filling, the rattle and then the steady. A minute later I could smell it move. The emitter fifty feet north spit like a furious mouse and then softened into a steady smear. The leaves on that row's west side shook with the cold, like a bird bathing. All of that for one gulp.

"We need to run the set long today," I said. "Six hours. Get the basins filled, let it soak."

"If we had six," he said. "If we didn't have the blue folder saying June's note is the one that, if you don't pay, they'll come cut their own piece."

"You have to believe me when I tell you you sell in June, you'll get their greed discount. Wait until they need the water for their own almonds. Wait till the houses push, watch the map. Wait till July when everything catches fire. Then you tell them to meet you behind the equipment shed with a suitcase."

"Jesus," he said, not meaning it either as a prayer or a curse. He set the coffee down under the tailgate and it made a cool little ring on the dust. "We had this talk. I don't want to take it down to studs. I don't want to get cute and lose the chance. I'm not playing chicken with a guy who pays for things with a checkbook the size of the county."

From the pumphouse, water went out like good news to the south rows and like rumor to the far north. I walked the line and listened. In the fourth row, at the bend where Dad had made a joint with a kindness you don't get at scale, a fine wetness began to bead. It swelled and I could see where the crack, hair-thin at first, took itself seriously. Then it parted like cut skin and a bevel of water fanned onto the dust. I put my thumb over it and it held and then whistled and pushed me back. I looked up at the canopy. You learn to read thirst. The leaves had that down-turned, folded-tongue look. Heeled men talk about horses that way. A thirsty tree tries to tell you by making itself small, every flexible thing in it going limp as if to save the bones.

"Good," I said.

"Good means you're bleeding less than yesterday?" he said.

"Good means I know where. Hand me a clamp. We can get wild with it for now. Come back with a proper fitting this afternoon."

He gave me the clamp and the screwdriver and watched me get the steel jacket on and tight. You leave a little lip of the cut in place so the clamp has something to bite. If you worry the thing like a prayer, it holds. I wiped my hands on my jeans and they left the purple of primer and then it was just me with a violet hand on blue denim and a day hot enough to throw sparks.

"If you weren't who you were," he said, talking to the space over my shoulder, "I'd think you were stalling."

"I am stalling," I said, and the honesty felt like sticking a pin in my own thumb and then sucking at it like a child. "I'm stalling like a thing that loves what it's stalling for."

"You can't save it with clamps," he said, gentle, and for a minute I was tired enough to love him. "You clamp a life, you hold, what, a season, one more summer barbecue. Then the crack creeps under the band and makes a new smile a foot away. It wired you this way, I get it. It wired me different. I've said my speeches. You've said yours. I'm only asking: let the guy look."

The swallow made a low run in front of his chest with a moth in its mouth. It clicked its little hinge body and swung off. We had forty-three acres if you counted the corner by the road where the sumac had taken over like that stand had made a pact with the soil. Twenty-six in planted trees. Twelve still in fruit that anyone would buy. The rest a mix of things you could feed your enemies, and the back third a long-rotten experiment with pluots that never quite had their own mind. Dad had been stubborn and humble, something I had thought for years was a good combination until I understood how many ways such a man could be wrong.

"I'm eating," Gabe said, and went to the cooler and took out a hardboiled egg and a handful of grapes. The grapes were supermarket, Peruvian still in March and now California on the bag and this early in the season they tasted like wet. He rolled one around in his cheek and pretended it was good.

"You bring something for me?" I said.

"You have land," he said. "If you get hungry, take a bite."

He tossed the egg in the air, caught it, cracked it on the bumper, peeled it without looking. He used to peel oranges like that when we were boys, fast, making the peel come off in one long giraffe of skin, then hand half of the fruit to me as if we were two saints, as if he didn't hear our mother in the other room asking our father whether they could afford to pay the ditch fee late by one week and the man from the district saying in his flat way that everybody thought their own reason was good. Those years were their own museum now, all behind glass. There were days I could see through the glass clean. Other days everything fogged with my breath.

We walked another row, then another, making little sprints, listening for hiss, jouncing the lines to feel the tone. The heat rose two degrees inside one minute and then another two. The black poly winked its tired shine up at us. I squatted and the blood left my head and then came back. The smell of the soil was a loam library smell, deep under. On top the dust had a smashed mint smell where the weeds had been run down by the truck. We had mown last week, thinking the extra surface evaporation was costing us, and watched the lizards shoot out from the mower deck like cufflinks.

By eleven it was a contest not to be mean. We didn't say most of what we felt. We acted like men with a single useful thing to do. We cut a cracked elbow, we glued a new T. We dug with hands because the shovel, in that tight space, was less precise than your fingers. There is a certain scrawl your knuckles learn, an old script: where plastic will give, where it won't. It's something you can't translate for someone who hasn't knelt and done it. In that way, it's as private as prayer and as foolish as any devotion.

At eleven-thirty Tom slowed his truck at the shoulder and let it idle. He got out and did not walk straight toward us, but made a wide there-and-back, like a dog approaching a stranger's porch. He swung his hat like his head was hot and the hat needed to feel useful.

"You let him," I said without looking at Gabe. He didn't say he had. The fact was already standing there with boot squeak and a clipboard.

"You working yourselves into a second youth," Tom said. He was wider than he'd been five years ago. His chin came down to meet the tone of his voice in a friendly pitch. "I always said, the men in your family like to lift things they could carry easier in parts."

"Tape measure looks new," Gabe said. "How's it measure feelings?"

"With a conversion chart," Tom said, touching the brim of his hat. "I called first and your brother didn't pick up his phone."

"I was under the truck," I said.

"You were under pretending to be half your old man," Tom said. He had a way of saying things that were rude and tender at once, and I wanted to hate him for it because it was a talent that paid. He turned and looked up the slope and took in the view the way a real estate agent does: as a film of value laid over the scene like a second skin. "They're going to want to keep the big ones, if they even say the word keep. They'll pitch it like breakfast among the blossoms. They'll mean three Instagram months in a row and then, in the fourth, a guy with a chainsaw and a good horror-movie laugh."

"You come here to tell us you have a conscience?" I said.

"I came because your brother called me and asked me to put a number in my mouth and see how it tasted," he said, not looking embarrassed. "I came because I loved your old man and hated him in ways small men hate people who do not tell them what to do. I came because you both know the fact: the county's making noises about the road, and when roads change, values change. And because I have a buyer who might not be here in a month. He thinks this is Provence like he saw in a brochure at a dentist's office, and I'm hard-pressed to explain to him we have our own words and none of them are going to taste like that in his mouth."

"The Jewelry King," Gabe said.

"His wife wore a ring the size of a cheekbone," Tom said. "They wouldn't live here. They'd play here. They'd put up a rental and a wedding pad. There'd be barefoot brides up to their shins in irrigation slop, and they'd call it rustic and we would call it a matter for boots."

"We're in a drought," I said. "Do they think water gets conjured with vows?"

"Everybody thinks their own pitcher gets filled," Tom said. He took his hat off and pressed it to his chest. "And I understand if you tell me to go to hell. But you told me a year ago around your father's bed that you'd know it when you knew it, and that you'd ask me then to not keep you from doing the brave thing. I don't know which thing is brave. I know which thing is now."

"It isn't yours to know," I said. My throat had a heat in it I kept telling myself was thirst.

"I'll walk the line," he said, and then he did what some men do when they don't want to be told no. He started. He has been on this land all his life, and so I didn't tell him not to step here because there's a plastic fitting where the weeds look different, or not to lean his shin on that riser because it's brittle and will break where it meets the saddle. He knew. He stepped where we would have. He counted emitters in his head, calculating flow like water is an inventory you can line out on a page as if the list were the reservoir. He looked at the wellhead like a man does not look at a thing he intends to keep. He bent down and put his ear to the soil the way I had and closed his eyes and nodded and said, There you are, you stubborn thing, in a voice so soft I looked at him like I'd never seen him before. Then he stood, set his hat, and said, "I'll be in town if you want to talk when you've cleaned this section and yourselves. I'm not rushing you. I just know how long it takes men to come to a no when their hearts are set."

"There's no heart here," I said, and he smiled like I'd said the best joke of the week.

"Right," he said. "Go on lying to yourself tenderly, you two. It's as good a lie as any." He went back to his truck slow, to not kick dust over us, and left his smell in the afternoon for a minute—a cologne his wife bought him because he thought he didn't want to stink like him.

We worked. There is a holiness to it when you don't put a name on it. We moved down the row, up, back, making our little fixes, and after each fix I would look up into the tree and see it as if it were a head that had been buried to the nose in sand. The leaves on the immediate line perked and lifted in that convincing pantomime green things make, so quick you feel like a sucker if you call it hope. The heat ran us down in a way I had to respect. It wasn't just hard. It was a mental weight. It put one of its big hands on the top of your head and pushed. The sky was the color of a washer you left in the sun. We had not had a decent cloud in four weeks. The radio from the neighbor's shop was turned to a Spanish oldies station, and Amor was a word that bounced across the dry like a small bird made of soft rubber.

At one, we sat under the fig—one of the old giants, not planted for any profit but always cropping like it couldn't imagine a reason not to. The figs themselves were still small and green, a sort of defiant ornament. The shade was true. We ate warm sandwiches, the tomato mealy as a cheap kiss. Gabe told me my kid had told his kid he was going to be an astronaut. I told him his kid had told my kid you couldn't be a man if you didn't know how to climb onto the roof without a ladder. He said he had not told his kid that, but he had maybe thought it. We let the trees do the work of cooling and when we spoke it was in short syllables.

"I found Dad's map," he said after a while, touching his back pocket. "The one where he hand-drew the runs and marked the weirdness. The one with the little penciled X's where he said he never could find the flow, even when the ditch boss swore the gate was open."

"You found it?"

"He slid it into the instruction manual for the mixer," he said. "Which is a metaphor so strong I don't even know what it's for."

"Let me see," I said, and he handed me the folded paper. It was yellowed where the sun had got it, brown where his thumb had. It was Dad's hand, careful and leaning right. He'd drawn little dumb trees like the trees a kid draws, lollipops and stick trunks, and he had numbered the rows and written the numbers of emitters on each and then, with a dot of satisfaction in the decimal points, had calculated per section. He had written things like See also: squirrels and Ask Tommy re: muffler man—muffler man being a thing that was a joke between him and Tom, back when they were young men and the muffler shop had a statue out front with arms straight out and a handshake grip, because everything in those days had to hold something.

"You should have told me," I said. "I would have liked to see this earlier."

"I only found it last night, looking for the charger to the drill," he said. "I thought, this will be one of the things that saves us. Then I thought, that's a dumb thought. Paper doesn't wet trees. But it made me happy to hold it. It made me less called by the idea of somebody else walking around here with their jaw open."

We sat with that. Then we lay back on the dusty grass and looked up through the fig's whole-leafed harbor. A jay made its big-boned sound. A fly landed on my neck with all his opinions in his hands. I thought of my daughter at her piano lesson, of the way she hates the metronome and loves to get to the sad parts. I thought of my wife, who had said, Let me not be a problem in this decision. I'll follow you down whichever hallway. I thought of my mother in the city, in her apartment with the window that looks at more windows, with her good couch, saying, Boys, I don't need to die on that land again. Gabe put his hat over his face. He slept ten minutes like a hunted animal, his chest jerking when a mosquito bit the back of his knee.

When he woke, we got up like men pretending to be new men. We went to the pump house and felt the motor. It was hot as a fever. I nursed the valve slow to not kill it with our carelessness. The pressure wavered and then took a stand, and then in the far row to the west we both heard it: the little stream-lace chatter of water finding its places. I made a sound like a man might when he sees his team come back to win in the last minute. I wasn't embarrassed. There are things you're allowed to feel that way about.

"Third row's still dead," Gabe said, practical. He pointed with his wrist because his hand had a cramp that made every straight finger look like an old spoon. "We snake under the drive there, remember, and I think the sleeve got crushed the year we had the guy come in with too much truck."

"We dug it then."

"Heathen filled it back with all the big dirt from the trench walls instead of the good mellow stuff," he said. "It'll have settled wrong. And Dad never liked to compact because he said if it's got to find its own shape, let it. Which is poetry and also a way to strip your future self of options."

We walked to the drive where the gravel had sunk itself into the clay like a constellation. I could see where the trench had been by the way the fescue had never quite recolonized. We dug again. The shovel threw sparks like bits of mica when it hit rock. The sweat ran down my spine in a single serious line, then in two. A lizard jumped in and then out as if he had been dared. When the shovel hit the sleeve, the shovel stopped. We chipped around. The sleeve had, indeed, gotten a strange new egg shape. The line inside had kinked like a man with a bad back. We cut, measured, slid a new piece. The glue made its ghost milk. We set the new section in like it had asked to be born.

"I don't know how to say it without making you mad," I said, on my belly, my face a foot from the cut. "I can get through this season. I can talk to Menendez at the district and he can talk to his cousin and we can get our ten days. We don't run the upstairs bathroom or the washer for the duration. We get through the picking. We run the sums. After that, maybe we still sell and then we sell with a crop on the tree and we don't smile when they say we have to take their cash offer because they're doing us the favor of saving us from ourselves. I get to—"

"Say goodbye," he said, finishing it for me so I wouldn't have to beg. "You want to say goodbye on your feet."

"And put an ending you can live under," I said.

"I want you to say goodbye on your feet too," he said, and I could hear he meant it. "I also don't want to take a dollar sixty less on the dollar because we got noble. I have a kitchen that doesn't have a ceiling, Noah. My youngest has asthma we pretend is a choice. I have my mother face-timing me from a chair she hates with a nurse in the room we think is a guard."

The name, my name, when he said it, made something in me unclench that I had not known was clenched. For a second the field of argument narrowed to a strip of road and then fell away and there was only that morning, me on the pipe and him with his coffee, and all the days behind that, the two of us on the tractor as Dad drove in first gear and told us to watch the furrow as if it were a movie where a hero was about to appear.

"I know," I said. "I know what you carry. Don't act like I don't. Don't act like this is just—"

"An idea," he said, nodding, finishing everything. He drew his hands over his face like a man who has seen some things and found none of them edifying. When he spoke again it was with a little of the old black humor he had been born with, the thing that had kept me sometimes from punching him when we were fifteen. "If you don't fix that ball valve in the east row, it's going to go and kill a bird. And then you're going to have to make a speech to your daughter about how her father does not, in fact, kill birds."

"That valve was Dad's masterpiece," I said, because it was true. The handle had been a spoon once, and he had drilled it and adapted it, and the way it shut was like someone ending a sentence with a period and not an exclamation.

We set the new sleeve and tamped in the sand we had saved. I felt sentimental about that sand, which is a thing that would make sense only to me. It was good sand, not the red, not the gravel. It was the stuff that settled smart. We made a little dam of our hands and let the trickle go through and ease the pipe into place. If you love something wrongly, you get scolded. If you love a tool rightly it will do what you ask. Go figure.

We stood and the world swam a second and then righted. The pump had its own thought again. I went and kicked it—gently, because you don't want it to take offense—and it recognized me and didn't fall dead. In the east, the emitters started their small busy whisper. The smell came up, the mineral wet, the first sheet of dust turning to paste under the basins. The trees in the first row sighed, I swear to God they did, each in their own leaf-language. A mockingbird landed on the end post and sang one of his stolen songs, and I let him. He had to make a living too.

"Three," I said out loud, counting the hours we had left before the pump would put up its hand and say, Enough. "Four if I go shut off the back two now."

"Do it," he said. "Be the miser you were born to be."

We worked more. We said less. At four the heat backed off a little. The shadow under the fig stretched like a woman on a good bed. We found two more leaks and made three more clamps and one union. The neighbor's rooster found a new register and I thought, If I sell, I'm leaving him here. The ditch sang its dry noise. The air filled with the smell of something I have to call gratitude.

There is a way a day like that will end itself if you let it. It will draw you forward like an animal on a lead until you find yourself at the part of the orchard where the ground is not the ground anymore but the start of the old north fence, the posts rotted at the foot, the barbed wire making its own catenary of sadness between t-posts we had pounded as boys with a sledge so big it made me feel ten feet tall. There are beer bottles under the sumac, proof kids have their own way of making maps. There is a sign that once said Trespassers will be prosecuted. Somebody has written under it, Not by us. We're busy. There are two stones that beavers a hundred years ago maybe chewed in a river fifteen miles away and the river put down here because the river used to get to be interesting and then we made it not be.

We stood there with the sweat drying in a salt frost on our shirts and the orchard making its own music in our ears. I don't know that there's a lesson to tell. I could say that the land will forgive you everything if you kneel. That would be the kind of sentence men like me like to put in their mouths because it feels honest and cheap at once. But the land doesn't forgive you. It also doesn't care. It's a body. It does its processes. It fights when it wants to fight and it goes to sleep when it wants to be done. The love you feel? That's yours. That belongs to you. It is not a contract. It does not entitle you to an ending that makes your life feel tidy.

"What's that," Gabe said, and I followed his finger and saw it too. Far over on the west, a small plume of dust on the road that meant someone else had turned up our way. We waited with that little coil of fight in our bellies, like boys again, not trusting a thing until it was inside kicking range. The truck came up slow, polite. It was Harriet, who lives past the ditch, the woman with goats that won't be penned. She waved out her window and showed some teeth. She pulled into our drive and parked and came up without waiting to be asked, which is a way of behaving that I respected enough to dislike.

"You look like men from a calendar girls buy to fix them," she said by way of hello. "You want a beer that's been in a cooler all day? I have two. I was trying to give them away to the county survey boys and they acted like I'd invited them to church."

"We're not those boys," Gabe said, and she took the beers from the little chest she always carries with bungees in her bed. They sweated like they'd made an oath. She handed them to us and sat on the tailgate and swung her legs.

"I saw Tom's truck earlier," she said. "I'm sorry."

"Sorry for what," I said.

"Sorry you're at that part of the story where he shows up," she said. "It's not a judgment. It's just a place. 'I saw a coyote on my way to town.' 'I saw a vulture.' 'I saw Tom.' All of them mean there's a last piece of fruit on the table and somebody's about to say who it belongs to."

"We haven't said anything yet," I said.

"You don't have to tell me," she said. "I'm not your judge. I'm a person with goats that won't stop getting pregnant. But I wanted to tell you I saw two boys at my fence last night with wire cutters, and I told them if they want to steal, they can come in daylight and take what I have and look me in the face while they do it. They said, Lady, we've been to court for less. I told them: then go get a check and bring it here. They didn't like their options. They walked off. Point being: sometimes you talk men into decency and sometimes you only outlast them."

"You came here to call us men," Gabe said. "You must be in a generous mood."

"I'm in a mood where I watched the wind shift and bring the smell of the dairies from twenty miles away, and I thought, that's how you know something's wrong, when you're smelling other people's troubles in your kitchen," she said. "Point being two: if you sell, I will watch. If you don't, I will hoot into my kitchen. If you need somebody to sign a thing that says a road that doesn't help me will ruin me, I'm here. But I'm not swinging for you. I got my own fists full."

"Thank you," I said, because I meant the beer and the words both.

She looked out. The light was going soft at its edges, and the hill shadow was starting to eat the lower parts of the trunks like a spill. "Also," she said, "your pump sounds like a man who needs a heart doctor or a divorce." She jumped off the tailgate and slapped the bed like a man slaps a horse to make a point, then she walked back to her truck. She didn't wait to be thanked twice. She drove slow between the rows so she wouldn't throw the gravel rooster-tail that always thinks it's funny. When she hit the road, she gassed it and the dust rose like a sigh and then fell.

We stood there with the beers and didn't drink them at first. We held them because they were cold and we were hot and the exchange is sometimes the point. Then we opened and drank and felt that small balm. The east row trickled still, trying to be a thing. The west had a slow articulating language all its own. A coyote cried somewhere far enough it was a toy for our ears. A flight of geese went by, talkative, doing that practice loop geese do when the day wants to have one more item in it.

"You say after pick," Gabe said, as if he were making a dinner reservation. "We go to July. Maybe August. We shake hands with the men who show up with good boots and new money. We watch their eyes. We take the one who has a kid who wants to make jam, or the one who's a fool you think you can forgive. We do not take the swindler's cousin. We do not take the wedding pad. We do not take the man who says the word artisanal and cannot say the word pump without making a joke. We don't get to be gods. We just get to be a little mean and a little lucky. Then we sign, if that's where your heart can't ignore its own hands."

"And if it doesn't come," I said. "If the set runs and the line holds and August comes and I have money on the table and your kid can breathe because the ceiling is back and Mom stops calling you out of breath because her chair is on the wrong mat—"

"Then we get to have a bad fight in August," he said, and smiled, and for a second he looked our age again. "We get to say worse things. We get to swing in the driveway like two men without neighbors. The rooster comes to watch. Harriet pretends she cannot hear. Tom drives by checking his phone and takes a picture and never shows anyone. We get to make a mess like only a family can."

"I'd like to put that off," I said.

He didn't answer. We stood and let the evening do some work on us. The sound of the pump eased into a steadier voice now that the cooler air had taken some of the weight off. The first mosquitoes in truth started their little hunts around our ankles. I slapped, he didn't, his ankles a mess of old bites. We finished the beers. We gathered our tools without saying, as if they were animals we had to coax into the barn. We set things up safe so if a line blew in the night it would blow itself out and not make a geyser that ran the well dry. We turned the valve that carries water to the south orchard and we both watched the little gauge bounce and then settle. In the second row we saw the turnouts spit good, then better. The smell rose, stronger now, and I heard in it some version of our father's voice, and in it he was not telling me what to do. He was only speaking the way a man speaks when he is in love with a thing and has learned he cannot hold it, not in any way that makes the holding prove anything. He was telling me in that voice, Pay attention. Be kinder than you feel. Know you'll never forgive yourself right, whatever you do. Get the runs on in order. Eat. Sleep. Be ready to rise. There are worse lives.

We went back to the pump and I reached for the switch to set the timer. The light was nearly under all the way. The western hills were a black filling. In the house the kitchen light came on because my wife knows I like to see it on from the field, a lantern the size of a thumbnail by the time you get far enough. The dog barked once because it remembers everything in the world you teach it to forget. The switch was stiff. I pushed it, and it resisted like a man in an argument, and then it gave. The timer's red light came on. It burned there, small and brave. We stood and watched it a second longer than we needed to, as if our looking would alter it. Then we walked toward home, side by side, and it was easy for a little while, the way of it, our steps finding a matched rhythm the dirt itself, in its old patience, had tamped and kept.
