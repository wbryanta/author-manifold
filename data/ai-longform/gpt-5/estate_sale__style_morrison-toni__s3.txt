By noon the light had set itself on the porch swing and would not move, pinned there like a good brooch, like something you keep on the Sunday dress and do not wear out to the garden. Heat got itself in the shutters and hummed. The yard smelled of iron and clover where the city mower had thrown blades through patchy grass two days ago, trying to make tidy what never did conform. People—some from down the block, some from city neighbors two towns over who happened to pass a cardboard sign—were coming slow up the walk, wiping their necks with the backs of their hands, holding a folded list, a canvas bag, a biscuit of cash. The brown door was open and the screen breathed in and out and she, who had oiled the hinges herself this week so they would not cry when she made them do what they had done for forty-three years, stood just inside where shade set her face back into childhood.

They came in on a gentle swoop of talk, the way birds come on a telephone wire and make that wire sing. “Estate sale?” “You mind if I—” “Is that the price?” “We married and all my money go to paint. That’s a good looking pot.” All their mouths making soft interest and their eyes sharp little knives. She did not hate them. She had known them. She had been them, three houses over once when Miss Lula died and her boys could not bear to separate the quilts one from the other because each quilt had a corner of their lives stitched up in it. She had carried a lamp out then, the small trout-green one with the brass foot and the wobble in the neck like a man who has drunk grief but won’t give in, and had paid three dollars and four quarters for it because that is what it was marked and because it felt honest to be held accountable for taking it.

Now they moved through what had been her mother’s house and would be no one’s next week, because already the deed sat folded like stiff bread in somebody’s metal cabinet downtown. The living room snuck its smell of lemon and Pride under the smell of people. Bees fussed up in the locust tree, all sugar and boss. She had put bowls of rubber bands and tags on the dining table, and a yellow legal pad where she could write what went out and who took it and whether they haggled her down two dollars, which was not about the two dollars at all, which was always about how insistence can shave off a piece of your day and carry it away in its pocket. She wrote names sometimes so she would remember. Small gorgeous names that told her the buyers had a mother too. Evangeline. Junior. A man who called himself Dooley. A woman whose laugh made it never mind what her name was because that laugh would tell its own story wherever it went. The legal pad warmed under her wrist. The pencil glided.

Her mother had never been cheap. She had been exacting, which is a different thing if you are paying attention. “I don’t do wrong by pennies,” her mother said when the milkman tried to leave sour glass or when the landlord shorted them on heat one winter and the house took that as an affront, the windows clouding themselves and sulking. “If you set a thing in its right place, it can stand by itself.” She said it with a mouth that made prayer and trouble with the same soft. With a head that could hold so much the bones of it were called a casque by the brown nurse in the hospital who said it kind to her because she could see the line of her thoughts moving. That casque had turned back to dirt in May. Three women from Pilgrim Chapel raised up songs over her until her name took on the sound of split rain. She lay now in a rectangle with grass stitching over the top that would not be true grass until a whole other season apologized for now. And the house—Laurentian Street number 531—had a little ache in its joints every time a stranger lifted something that made it less house and more frame.

She had told herself to be stone. She said it last night when a moth kept beating the window and not choosing the hole in the screen that was right there like a permission. She said it this morning when the neighbor boy came with a wagon for the magazines because his daddy liked the look of old Rolling Stone. Stone, she said when she lifted the black skillet and set it belly-up with four dollars scotch-taped to the handle, because if she did not set it fat-side down she would remember the hissing meat of her mother’s economy there, the slick slide of Crisco, the law that says you don’t wash a good pan with soap, you let it learn you and you learn it. Be stone when the woman in the flowered blouse says, “Can I give you two for that?” about a pot whose lid had sealed Saturday soup like an absolution. Be stone when the man wants to carry the Davenport alone and you can see his spine say not without punishment. Be stone when the girl going out the side says, “I like the smell in here. It smell like somebody told you to hush.”

It came in on her sometimes like that, the voice that wasn’t any person’s voice. The house hummed it. The bedposts clicked it. The floorboards said oh-oh when a certain foot stepped on luck just wrong. She had been inside sounds like that since she had a sound of her own. She could still remember the thud of summer when a perfect red tomato rolled off the window sill because too fat to stay put, and hit the kitchen table and made a bruise it couldn’t help. The crooked tock of the metronome when her mother tried to teach somebody else’s child piano on Saturdays. The baby-high laughter that used to come down the hallway like a troop of velvet dancers back when the house was full and she, soft-necked with the dreaming of leaving, had not yet mailed for the college forms. Those laughs were her brothers then—Joel with his wrist always bandaged from foolish, and Otha with the part in his hair so straight it could slice bread if you dragged it through.

Now: her brothers spread. Joel out near Akron with his new family and his cough. Otha more god than kin these days, preaching up in Sandusky a holiness that made people put their tobacco in their shoes so the preacher wouldn’t see it in their pockets. He had not come, both of them had not come. “It’s your business, Sister,” Otha said on the phone when she told him Tuesday that the date had been set and the woman with the jelly shoes and the pretty clipboard would not give them a next week. “You were there last,” Joel said, working the apology into his truck’s dying engine, the phone on his knee while he called himself out across garlands of static. They were not wrong. She had been there last. She had sat through the body becoming nobody, through the back room shedding the smell of her mother’s rose oil until all that was left was a sheet folded crisp enough to make corners cut your thigh.

Somebody held up the picture frames and asked if the people came with them, meaning the old sepia faces. “That’s my great-grand,” she said, and the woman put them down gentle because she was a person and not a scavenger. Another woman wrapped salt shakers in newspaper and said she liked the six-point stars on them like people like certain skies. A boy with hair in his eyes found the marbles and said oh one of them got a bird in it. He rolled the cat’s-eye fixed so white and blue it made his own eyes envious. He looked at her without looking and she said take them, baby. The boy slipped them in his pocket like hunger does, and his mother said don’t you take. The boy’s mother had a look tried hard on by a world that don’t allow you to be so young a mother. “He can have them,” she said, and the mother said thank you without her throat opening. The boy’s hand around his new little world like he intended to learn it hard.

The piano. In it the secrets. The first thing the girl across the street learned was one finger three notes and then a fourth that nobody asked for but needed. In this piano a whole winter of a hand trying to make ten notes do what they did in church on Sunday. In this piano the blunt end of her own patience, losing itself and winning back. In 1974 her mother limped in a little rain to Mr. Kubac’s to pay on it the week he tried to cheat her by telling her the wood was swollen wrong and that would be extra. Her mother leaned on that counter like wheat leans, her shoulder under her dress looking like the head of a hammer. “Don’t tell me about wrong,” she said. “I stand it.” Dooley lifted the lid now and ran his fat fingers along the keys and the house shivered for the first time that day in a way that was not heat. “Been tuned?” he asked. “You gotta play it like you mean it to know whether it stay true,” she said, not to him, to it. He smiled at her like he knew, and she had to decide if she hated him for thinking his knowing got to be equal to hers. “I play at the lodge,” he said. “I can make room for this baby.” He squeezed a note out of it, then another. It came up like a sleeper comes up, grumpy and committed to not being nice. “I’ll pay the tag,” he said, which was inevitable because the tag had been written by a man by the name of Alvin who did not know that a dollar is not always money.

They took the sofa. They took the bed frame from the little front bedroom that had once been hers—the girl who stitched herself into a dress and walked out of here like a new seam toward a life—and then had been her mother’s when her mother could not walk the hallway to the back. They took the chenille. They tried to take the quilt from the cedar chest, and she said not that one, nothing from that chest is for you. They looked at her like a child told no by a cookie. “It ain’t on the list,” she said, but what she meant was not for fingers that don’t know how to read it. All the rag mouths from dresses of woman kin murmured in there. Her great aunt’s bridal hem. The feed sack from the year corn tried and the market said, we don’t want you this time. If they took one remedial stitch she would stand in that living room and be water on rock until the floor gave in. So she sat her hand on that chest and called herself mean because that’s what her brothers would say she was being. Her mother had let the air the last of her life and not enough came back in. She would not let the cedar surrender its breath for somebody else’s bed.

The kitchen wore a skirt of light. It cut itself on the knife set her father had brought home and put in the drawer like an apology for leaving. He had stood there once, light slicing on his knuckles where he felt at home being hurt because what else had his hands been born to? He taught those knives to mind potatoes instead of people. He cut bread with an attention his woman could trust, a child could love. Then he left, and none of that mattered because the story you mean to live and the one you get handed only meet up sometimes. Her mother put the knives in the bottom of the drawer and made roast anyway. She taught her own hand to leave out the onion for Otha and to put extra for herself since nobody was going to kiss her that night and smell it and say, Good. When the man in the browning golf shirt took those knives, she watched his fingers hold them like toys. “These ours now, Margaret,” he told a woman who had bought a stack of napkins for two dollars and the look on her face said, I wanted the knives. The man looked at her. “Ma’am, you take check?” She said, “I take green only,” and he looked offended like the way certain men get offended when the world is ordinary.

In the side yard Miss Eula from Pilgrim sat on a folding chair that had been brought up from the basement where it had slept its collapsible sleep for ten years because no one liked its orange enough to keep it awake. Miss Eula was ninety or so and did not apologize for it. She counted the people’s purchases with eyes that were cataract-cloud and hands that still bounced piano off the curves of her hips when the choir got too silly. “You drink water,” she told her every fifteen minutes, because she was a woman and you do not allow a woman to turn to salt just because summer has a mind. On the orange chair next to her an old man named Iron John who came to the pasture out back sometimes to see if the horses that no longer had a pasture were still there. Horses loved him. They walked to him even when he had no apple to trade. He had a smell of rain on him even when the sky thought better of that plan. “You got to set a thing free,” he said under his breath, not talking to her, not talking to anybody. “It ain’t yours once you call it yours.” He had taught that to himself about something so ugly it could only go by unspoken.

Afternoon went sideways. Late. It was that hour when drink sweats in its glass so much you have to wipe your hand every time you go and say you are thirsty. The man from the tax office came and stood by the street like a slender threat. He had on a shirt so white it wanted sunlight the way a thing wants what it used to be. He didn’t eat one of the butter cookies she had set out like good will. He asked her nothing. He counted the cars, he wrote on his pad, he left. She thought then, and in a smallness that made her ashamed, that if he wanted to count something, he should count the number of times his mama told him not to talk with that tone to a woman who could starve him in her evening prayer if she wanted. She would not do it, but she could.

How do you hold yourself still when they carry out what taught you to stand? When a girl of nineteen with a piercing in places that had no business has your mother’s dressing table mirror propped on her hip and is making remarks about whether her boyfriend going like it. The girl said without turning her head, “I know how to sand.” A boy with a knife paring the edge of his need stood behind her and nodded. “We redo everything,” the girl said. She looked in the mirror and her mouth, red as it was, went slack at what she did not see. She expected the mirror to lie and the mirror refused. Mirrors that have watched generations of women bury their hair and be born again do not lie for free. The girl took out her money without blinking, which was a kind of bravery. She turned back to the mirror and said, “We keep some stories, we scrape some off.” She did not know whose story she threatened and that was part of the harm and part of the mercy.

Over by the good fan—the one with the square body and a face that dared you—two women from the block fanned themselves while deciding whether to carry the big radio. “We grew up on that,” one said. It was a lie that had a feeling of truth because all radios are family when they are older than your intentions. She brought over napkins and a bottle of something in a glass so weak it forgot itself. The women said, “Girl, you sure?” and she said yes because she was. One of them had come the night her mother fell and kept calling up the steps to a woman who could not answer yet because she was trying to make a deal with the thing that pushed her breath up and up but could not coax it out. That woman had rubbed her back and said she always did have a way of finding trouble that could be shook and shaken. Later that night in the hospital the younger doctor had apologized correctly and the older nurse had carried the apology like a fat child. “You take what you want,” she told them now. “This fan will disappoint you if you ask for too much.” They laughed because disappointment compared to what else can be sometimes is a luxury item.

At the back of the hallway with the penny tile that had a lot of cents missing, the bathroom door had been shut as if a person lived still behind it. She opened it to grab that little blue rug because she could not abide the thought of a stranger’s feet standing there where her mother’s had twice a day. The sink stuttered when she turned it on, not with old pipes, but with a long memory of water. She rinsed her fingers and remembered her daughter, Naomi, all leg and sly when she was sixteen and wanted to use this sink because it made you look better than the one in the city apartment. Naomi came last night to help and had taken the jewelry that mattered and the photographs she chose, and had taken, to her credit, the ugly little doll with the pewter shoes that had been hers and had made fear for no good reason when she was six. Naomi had cried hard once and scared her mother, no, scared herself, maybe, with how full a woman can get without a place for her to go. “You sure,” she had said to Naomi then, over the glass coffee table they had pulled out onto the lawn to keep from making the house too much itself again. “You sure you don’t want the daybed?” Naomi was drinking a ginger ale and had the bones of a woman who would not suffer fools ever again. “I sleep fine here,” she had said. “I sleep fine anyplace I put my head that you called mine.” It made the room crack for a second because the words put a name to air and that is always wicked and always beautiful.

Sometimes while counting change, she would look up and her mother’s face would be in the hallway mirror where no mirror was, or her father’s hands would be unlocking the cellar door where the lock was not. She did not spook. She held herself the way people hold a fur you do not have permission to own. She could hear a loaf pan being set onto the stove and pop-corn starting wrong, she could hear the faraway train sliding town like a big fat iron eel going home. The house tried to speak to her and she was too tired to let it all the way. The last months had been a big ear leaned toward need and there is a particular silence that becomes yours when all those keening mouths stop. You have to teach yourself music again. You have to hold in your head a tone that somebody else cannot sing to you.

The man with the church tie made a joke about devil’s pots and bought the tureen for fifteen. A girl in a dress like a soda can fiddled with the icebox magnets and put one in her bra and then set it back because she was not that girl today. Somebody said, “Lord, I’d like to live in a place where my life could be this quiet and long.” She wanted to press that woman’s face to the floor and keep it there long enough to show her what the quiet had held. The day the police came and left their wet footprints on the entry rug because it was raining in the world and they did not know to wipe their sin on the mat; the day when Otha’s laugh tore up the walls and dragged them into its church. The evenings in August when the heat had grown hands and clutched the backs of their lungs and made them trick their own mouths with ice. The sound of the tornado that never touched down but made picnic plates do sit-ups in a shallow drawer. The little quiet when somebody said goodbye at the door and you knew they would not be back and knew none of the reasons they gave were the true one. Long is a thing with too much purpose to be sweet.

When the glass-front cabinet went, carried by two white boys who kept talking about their mother like a religion, she had to sit down. Not because of breath. Because of honey. The jar of honey that had sat on that shelf for twenty years with a spoon in it because her mother could not abide looking for the spoon every morning when the small hunger that comes after breakfast came like a song you can’t shake. That spoon had been a gift from somebody with an idea of taste; little knot on its handle, silver leaf carved out of an imitation. One day soon after her mother’s ankle made itself a liar and would not hold her, she had found a spoon like that in the basement of the church under three hymnals and a bowl of buttons, and had brought it home and made the jar look like a lady. The honey had sugared and sugared until it was a city of itself. There are things you cannot go into and cannot ask come out, and they live anyway. When they took the cabinet she looked past their faces and saw the missing circles, all the little ghosts where cups sat. She put her thumb in one and when she took it away, it left a spot of cleaner wood like a pale kiss. Her thumb said, That. That. That and that. She did not say sorry to anything because sorry had swallowed its own breath weeks ago.

Sun shifted at last. It staggered a foot like a man carrying a pig. The locust had put its leaves into applause. Somebody’s car door slammed. Somebody called, “You wanted four? I got three fifty.” She took it. She took all such commonsense defeats. She kept her meanness in the cedar chest where it could lay its head and not be paraded on the porch for anybody to admire. There was a shriek when the little white dog got free and leaped up on the sofa they had set half in the yard to take its photo for something called Listing. Its paws printed new rules on the dull rose fabric that had pressed her thighs a thousand Sundays when she was trying to be forgiven for something she had already outlived. A young man with hair bead-plaited to his neck smiled at her and said, “You know, I can relieve you of that donkey.” He nodded at the coat rack by the front door that had three bent nails in it and a little porcelain dog off to the left. It had been her mother’s also. Her mother did not allow a person to put a hat on it. “That stray don’t carry another stray on his back,” she would say. The boy now did not know that; he saw something liked itself. “You can have it,” she said, and picked it up herself to give to him. The nails left their long tongues in the air a minute and then sighed. He handled it with the awe some men can still give to a small thing, and she forgave him for what he would do later when someone’s girl made his hands do what they knew.

They took the bed her mother had died in. She made them take it. She had made herself measure that bed for the Good Year of someone else. She took the mattress off and her eyes caught on a coin that had lived there in the seam maybe since 1983 when somebody had needed bus and not had. It was a quarter with an eagle on it, not these new ones with histories all over them like stickers. She set it in her pocket. She did not perform her sadness. She did not hold the bed rail and mouth Lord. She asked the men if they needed water, if they needed to set the frame down and rest. They did. Men do. They rest when they are asked. She poured and brought the glass to them and did not try to rush what needed going slow. Her mother had sighed into that bed for months, the sigh that says the body has its own schedule and you are just the land it moves across. The bed had asked the floor to help it because the bed could not be what it was without the floor, and the floor had asked the earth to help it, and the earth had asked the tree roots, and together they had held her mother up in that last theatre of dignity where a leg insists on being a leg and an arrow intends still to fly even lodged in someone’s wood. When they took it away, the square of lighter floor underneath was like the rectangle on the cemetery. As if the absence when you move a thing is the only honest mark it makes.

People said all the talk we said to her, the talk any of us would, if we born to a language that offers balm in place of cure, breath when remedy is too far to reach. “She lived a long one.” “She kept her mind.” “She ain’t got to worry no more about outside cats.” “You doing good.” All of those true on a day when truth sat on the porch and took its shoes off. Someone asked her if she had kept the papers, and she pointed to the little desk with the leather top that had ink stains like men who shave their cheeks too fast. She had the envelope there from the lawyer. She had the card from the nurse. She had a receipt for the ham their neighbor sent because death needs salt. She did not yet have the papers that would make her an orphan in the eyes of the state, but she knew the way that word came down hard and made its own home between your ribs. A woman fifty-two years old holding a box of church fans and calling herself new.

The smell from the basement rose. Damp. A little mold where the fruit had thought about it and decided no. A note of that late boy’s socks, the one who had moved in after he had outstayed somebody else’s mother. He wrote music down there that rattled under your plates. He said he needed a place, and her mother said your song get somewhere, and when he had taken his brown duffel out of there one morning with a shame in it you could hold like a cat, he had left two string of words behind taped to the wall: Nobody told me how to want. That was his truth and also not enough to bring him home. The stairs were vexed as a ladder, and the jar with the pennies—her mother’s other bank—lay knocked over and the coins spread like seeds that had never expected to be given a ground that greedy. She had told herself just today she would scoop them up and put them in the Folgers tin that had held their cookies for forty years. She said it three times thinking of the Bible way of that. But now a girl with two bracelets of bells on her ankles saw the copper on the concrete and bent to pick up one. “Wheat penny,” she said, and had the nerve to have her mouth fall open like the coin had whispered what it had seen. “Take the whole thing, baby,” she told her. “Take every year.” The girl’s eyes said, For real? and her feet sang the way bells do when they are asked to say yes to air.

There was a way to get through this that her mother would have liked. You put a rag in your apron waistband. You put clean plates under where the dirty used to be. You stand in the door for a second and watch who comes in so you can put the right thing in the right hand. You mind you do not let anybody leave with an ache you could have eaten yourself. She tried. A woman asked for the wool blanket and, in taking it off the line yesterday from the cedar smell that could not hold it now, she had seen on the hem the tiny scar where, years ago, something took a bite and her mother backstitched her initials over the missing. She saw the beige thread making the M look like a river tries to when the rock says no. The woman held the blanket up and she saw the window through the weave and remembered when her father had come home with this very thing because somebody at the factory had lost it off their truck and men who did not go home with something ached more than men who did. “It’s heavy,” the woman said. “That’s how it keep you,” she told her. “It know its own job.” The woman nodded like they met there in that sentence for a minute.

Some had the nerve to ask what happened here. Did she die here. Was she really eighty-two. Did she have to go on oxygen, like that curiosity is a parallel to care. “She went the way she came: taking her time,” she said, closing the conversation like a box. She was tired of explaining what bodies decide in the great council where blood and lung and heart gather and say, I have been yours and now I will be mine. Tired too of the way sorrow needs telling to find its room. People think it moves in when you say its name, when really it has been sleeping on your couch since Tuesday with its shoes on, leaving its heel-print on every inch it lies. She had been sitting in her mother’s chair by the window the last night when the light had a little blue in it and the air broke into a thousand little graces, and her mother had said, “I am going to sleep now,” and everyone in the room had lied or told the truth in their way and she had held a hand that had slung itself on her neck when she was first walking and had kept her head straight when she was trying too hard to be mean, and she had said, “All right.”

Sometimes when a thing goes, it takes the need for it with it. The teapot, a lady with a potbelly that made you love her. It went. She watched it go and felt no arrow. The raw wood box her father had made to sit by the stove for kindling. Her mother had used it after for white potatoes and dirty carrots and sometimes for letters too hard to open. It went and took away the particular dread of reaching into it and finding a thing she had not shaped. The gravy boat with a chip like a small crime. It went and she smiled with her mouth and let the rest of her stay still. Other girls had wept for spoons. She knew the difference between crying because the spoon remembers you and crying so someone will tell you the spoon loved you. She put a rag on the table for folks to lay their coins on. They left them hot. She lifted them and counted and felt every burn. Her thumb kept the shape of eagles and heads all afternoon.

When the man came for the bed they did not know was sacred, he had with him a boy with a sullen shoulder. Boy had a scar on his jaw like somebody had told him once, You can’t, and he had said, Let me show you. He did not look at her, would not as he carried one end of the bed frame and let his father hold his part and the middle do what it wanted. Boys are a fatigue and a joy. She wanted one and had made do with many, and she let them take that bed and did not flinch at the idea of young foolish in it soon. There are grace mushrooms grow in the dark. There are prayers planted by accident. The boy’s shoulder knocked her mother’s wall and made a mark that now would never get painted over. She held the doorknob with her palm as if it could still hear temperature. Her mother’s picture in its tarnished frame watched. In it her mother was thirty and narrow and worked at Murray’s Cleaners and sang with a lowline like steel that don’t bend but it can hum. That picture had never left the hallway. It came now, unexpected, to the dining room because she carried it to keep someone from thinking it was for the taking. “Miss Mae always liked that one,” Eula said and reached to touch it and pulled her finger back like flame had teased. “She looked like she lost and found everything.” “You tell the truth,” she said, and Eula’s head cocked like a choir.

The day turned at its hinge into the slant that holds you. The boy who had taken the marbles went home to roll them under a bed where his mother forbid long before she had a child. Dooley came back with a sneer and truck and a rope and two brown twins to pick up the piano. They had brought quilts to ease its skin. He counted the steps with his mouth as if numbers were an opera. She stood on the little porch and watched the rope cut into the banister her father had sanded with a mercy and a patience unlike his love life. No one cried in any way that made news. It all happened as if it were ordinary to unhouse a memory you had eaten sitting down. The twins refused her water and she set the sweat on their necks in her mind to pray over later. The truck went. The sound of a piano on wheels is not a piano. It’s an elephant apologizing to gravel. Eula said, “You want me to stay through to the end?” and she said, “I want you to go home before your bones ask you why you played with them.” Eula patted the air and left. Iron John had gone without going, the way old men do when their leave takes place all over their body and not just at the door.

Room by room, each became more about edge than about middle. In every place where a thing had stood there was a lightness. It was not joy. It was not grief. It was that third thing you have no name for. The front room held its palm up and asked for a gift. The dining room rinsed its oilcloth face and stared the blank down. In the back room, she stood for a minute and recognized a smell so old anyone else would have called it new. It was lemon and starch and iron and the tired that filled her mother every Friday night when she had ironed so many collars her shoulder had been stamped with a row of men. It lifted from the closet and made her images of white shirts marching into a war they never think is theirs. In that closet was a dress her mother had not worn since 1981 when there was a hurricane on a coast that had never been her coast and she said, “We got to send them a dress that won’t need ironing.” She had sent two and kept one. It hung now with its thread bare in the arm. She took it down and put it to her lips. There are swallows you do not chew.

People started saying they going to come back tomorrow since not everything had gone. She said yes as if she had planned a two-day. The company with the pretty clipboard had said to her, If things don’t go, we have a man who buys in bulk and you won’t have to worry. She had nodded like she believed in men who save you from worry. She knew better. Worry is a mother if you treat her right. You put a bow on her and put her on your head and walk the yard and let the men wave at you. You go back in and take the last jar of pear preserves off the shelf and decide whether it is a comfort or a dare. She chose dare. She opened it and called to Naomi’s old classmate, who was picking through the spices with his thumb and sniffing each like it was a girl and he an awful boy. “Get us some spoons,” she told him, and he laughed and came back with two. The preserves were teeth-shocking sweet. She felt old as a kitchen table for a second and new as screen in spring. She put a scoop into her mouth and it worked on her like a story does.

A child came with his father, late, at the hour when everyone is too tired to lie. He looked like a boy she had known once who told her in lightning flashes that he loved her and then got jobs at being someone’s scarecrow. The father asked about the ladder, which was wood and had two rungs replaced by slats of a chair. She said five dollars. He said two. She said four. He said three. She said I am tired, and he folded. The boy had that look of someone who had made a home inside confusion and was going to defend it. He put his face up into everything until it said boo. He found a penny in the crack by the vent and took it. She did not call him thief. He would learn other words for himself. He asked about the ghost. “What ghost?” the father said in a voice you use when you choose normal on purpose. “The one that sit in that. The chair.” He pointed at the wicker chair by the window. The chair had done its job so long it squeaked a hello when you put it down. “Only ghost is the one you bring,” she said, and it is the kind of thing a boy will hold in his pocket forever like a rock. The father stared at the rug. The boy touched the wicker with a finger as if it were skin. There is a love some children have for the old. It is not pity. It is recognition. He said goodbye to the chair when his father picked up the ladder and he meant it, and that was more than any of them needed.

As five became not five, the air changed. Cicadas egged the world on. Somewhere a mother shouted Larry with a sweetness that meant she was not in dread. A cream truck with silly bells went by and no children ran because this block’s children were grown in another town and sweet had become expensive. A woman in white pants walked up the sidewalk and asked nothing. She stood and looked at the house with a grace that hinted at a deal already made in another room. A realtor’s smell. She did not hate her. She did not. She wished to tell her, Be gentle. Wear quiet. When you bring men with electric hammers in here, let them ask permission. Don’t let them tear up the kitchen. You can scrub a sin; you cannot sweeten a man’s idea of you. The woman folded her arms and hummed once, a note that did not land. “I called you,” the woman said. She said, “I got your message,” and the woman nodded as if prayer were a transaction. “We’ll be doing a walk-through,” she said. “Tomorrow,” and her pale sandals flashed away down the walk. She wondered if the woman would come back with a measuring tape and a man who read off numbers like football. She would be here. She would hear it. She would hold her hands still while they did what men do when they get to say what wood is worth.

She let the last woman in the house be the woman with the bracelets. The woman went back through the rooms slow like goodbye done right. She picked up a small Bible with a bent spine and she said, “I didn’t come to get this but it came to get me,” and she laid down four ones and took it. “No charge,” she said. “That one got the wrong names in it.” The woman looked at her as if what she said had told a thing it did not know. “I can get something even if the names ain’t mine,” she said. “You know that.” She did. She did. She waved her out the door like a field waves at wind. She looked one more time into the kitchen and saw that there was one cup left on the drain board and a rag folded like tomorrow. She went to the window and pushed up the sash and let a mosquito doubt itself about coming and then let it come anyway because she did not want to be the only mouth in the room.

She sat in the doorway—one foot in the living room, one in the light—and chewed the inside of her cheek the way a girl does when she means to be good and the world laughs at her sweet face. She picked the legal pad up and saw her mother’s words on the underside where, last year, her mother had written a recipe for yams in grease pencil, calling for butter like it was rain. She touched the ant-path at the lip of the porch and said, “Go around.” She thought about the river she had dipped in as a girl, not this city’s trickle but the one two counties over that snakes itself in the corn and knows how to turn the winter off men. She saw herself jumping from a rock into a shallow that had decided to be deep that day. She heard her mother call her name just once, full and beautiful, and saw herself climb up the bank with shame like a cat around her ankles and saw her mother reach for her and pull her close with a mouth that whispered, Look where you going, and let her go, and when she closed her eyes the porch steps hummed that note you can lie down on.

Ana, from down the way, came to stand at the bottom of the steps with a grocery sack under her arm fat with what she would not list. “You fed?” she asked. “I am and I am not,” she said because a woman can say more than one thing true in a sentence. “Come get stew,” Ana said. “You like my girl’s stew. She put elbow in it.” “I like your girl anything,” she said, and they laughed out of the hollows behind their collarbones. “We can bring a pot to you later. Or you come to us. Either way God get the credit.” “I will come,” she said. She had to move the blue rug and lock the door and then loan that lock a prayer. She had to find a place for the cedar to sleep where her hands would not wake it by accident. She had to call Joel and say nothing interesting. She had to take a bath in a water that had known her already and forgive it for its knowledge. She had to let Naomi talk her into the thing she wanted to say more than she wanted to hear her say it. She had to look at her feet and give them something kind. She had to put her mother’s face where it would not judge her back. She had to sit with the empty and call it a friend and listen when it said what it said.

She walked through the rooms and the floors said oh-oh oh-oh, not from hurt, from memory. She held the little thin spoon that had been in the honey and tucked it in her pocket because no law said she could not. She put the quilts back in the cedar and closed it and set the shoe polish on top so nobody would mistake the world for generous. She took the picture of her mother off the piano bench where she had set it when the rope came and held it under her arm like a problem. The porch had its shadow now full. The fan in the living room worked stupid like a man who has double-dared himself. The yellow legal pad had writing on it that looked like a river map and she knew in the morning she would not be able to tell what they meant. Her pocket had in it a quarter with an eagle and a spoon with a leaf and a little piece of chalk.

She turned off the kitchen light and under the sound of switch the whole house made the sigh a house makes when it decides to belong to itself a night. There is a debt all houses owe the people who shelled peas in them. There is a payment every woman deserves who lifts a pan off a stove and asks the air that it smell better than it does in there. She stepped onto the porch and the air did her favor. The street had the busy highway noise from over the way and the evening’s easy racket and a woman calling her boy and a sound like a television had married a man’s laugh. The locust leaves wore evening. A brown spider threw the first of her nets in the porch corner daring the door to be open and take her along for a ride. She let it be. She sat one more minute and said to the wood, It was good, and the wood said, I know, and she closed the door slow, and when it clicked it felt like a ring sliding on a finger it had belonged to all its life, even if today was the first day it knew it.

On the walk back to Ana’s, her shoes took in little stones and gave them back. The jar of pear on her tongue changed to bitterness in a way that tart can when it remembers labor. She let the boys on the porch nod at her and did not stop to talk and did not refuse to. The street had a name and she thought it to herself three times. It held different each time. It broke morning in it. It split night. It had in it a woman’s laughter so steady she believed for a second it came from her own body. The world held under her feet like a man who finally got there in time. She stopped at the gate of Pilgrim where the sign at the top had been painted that morning by the deacon’s cousin and the P lost in its curve. She put her hand flat on it where it bowed and did not straighten it. She needed a field. She needed stew. She needed to tell somebody, to tell herself: I am the daughter of a woman who did not do wrong by pennies. She needed to hear the night say Amen, and the night did. A fairness there was in that that she would fold and put in a drawer to take out sometime when the morning came on mean.

When she came back with the pot that had little crosses all over it for critters not to decide it theirs, the house had squatted into itself and was not yet another woman’s and was still, along the baseboards, along the inside hush, this one’s mother’s. She lifted the latch and went in and the moth that had beat itself on the screen did not go dumb. It found the hole. It left. She set the pot on the stove that did not belong to stove anymore and pulled two bowls and thought she would wash them after and put them back to wait through a night of after, and then she sat on the little footstool, the one with the embroidered branch that never yielded winter, and spooned stew into her mouth until her mouth said Now. And when her mouth said Now she lay her spoon down and reached for the cupboard and did not weep. If a tear came, it came like rain that will not brag about itself tomorrow; it came like a kind thing you set on the table and let be taken.

Neighbors passed by slow with their tongues in their mouths. The sky did its pink trick. A train took its time over by the tracks and sang only to those who had something worse to remember and needed the train to be the second worst. She sat and listened to that, and to the soft return of her own breath since there was at last room for it, a whole room, each room, and she heard, in the high part of her ear where the echo lives, her mother say once like a thing you can button in or take off your shoulders depending, Girls like you live. And because it had been said once and she wanted it now, she said back the only word that honored and refused that sentence all at once, the only word that could hold an argument and a song in the same mouth: I do. She rose slow and her ankles whispered to their God, and the house did not keep her when she stood. Under the light on the porch, a moth kept itself in the dark like a good man. She took the last tag off the table and stuck it to the back of her hand and it said $1 and she laughed at it, at her, at the mean and the mercy spooled up together like the thread left and the thread spent. Tomorrow more would go. Tomorrow polish would show itself on a new woman’s hands. Tomorrow names would be said by people who had never had to spell them for a judge. If a hum stayed on in the house with her through the night, it would be the kind that doesn’t tell you what to do, only tells you where you are, and how long you been there, and where else, if you are everybody’s child and your mother’s both, you might be called to stand.
